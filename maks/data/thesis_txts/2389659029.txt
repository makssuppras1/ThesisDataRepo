Abstract  
More and more enterprises choose Product/Service-Systems (PSS) as their business 
strategy, since it can improve the competitiveness and make more profits. This business 
model seems to dematerialize, but it does not necessarily lead to lower environmental 
impacts. In order to assess the actual environmental performance of PSS, a set of guidelines 
based on the LCA technique is proposed. The guidelines are still in a state of continuous 
improvement. The purpose of this thesis is to test the guidelines on a concrete PSS case 
and then make an evaluation of the guidelines. Research methods are adopted throughout 
the thesis, including literature review, case study, application and evaluation. Due to time 
and data limits, the study focuses on applying the first four steps of the guidelines. After 
each step, there is a brief subjective reflection. At the end, an overall objective evaluation of 
the guidelines is made. The results of this thesis will be used by academia to improve the 
guidelines in future research.

1. 
Introduction 
Product/Service-Systems (PSS) is a developing concept that contains two important 
elements: products and services. Its aim is to meet diverse user needs while 
reducing environment impacts through additional services. However, due to the 
added services may cause extra environmental problems, PSS does not necessarily 
have positive impacts on the environment. Therefore, a systematic approach is 
needed to assess the environmental performance of PSS in order to test whether it is 
sustainable. The goal of this thesis is to test such an assessment method by applying 
it to a concrete PSS case. At the end, an evaluation of the approach will be provided. 
 
1.1. What is Product/Service-Systems? 
With the development of society, individuals are willing to show their personalities 
through their distinctive lifestyles, especially consumption behaviors. It stimulates the 
enterprises to create new business models instead of persisting with the traditionally 
product-focused business model to meet the diverse user needs. Thus, for the sake 
of enhancing their competitiveness and generating higher profits, more and more 
companies provide integrated solutions to their customers by combining products 
with services. A business model containing products and services is called a 
Product/Service-System. 
 
In addition to making more profits for the enterprises, social transformation and 
environmental sustainability are influential in the formation of PSS as well. From a 
social point of view, the focus of social development shifts away from agriculture and 
manufacturing to services. In order to reduce resource consumption and accelerate 
economic development, more and more countries encourage labor force to engage in 
service industry (Central Intelligence Agency, 2017). Promoting PSS can create more 
job opportunities in services than traditional business models. Therefore, it is an 
important step in the worldwide social transformation. 
 
From an environmental perspective, energy consumption throughout the entire 
lifecycle is an important criterion for judging whether a product is environmentally 
sustainable (Pavlovskaia, 2014). Normally, enterprises are only responsible for the 
resource consumption during production process. However, greater energy 
consumption may exist in the use- or disposal stage of the product. Fortunately, 
adopting PSS can address such a problem. In this business model, the enterprises 
offer services to support their products, such as providing guidance to the customers 
during the use-stage or helping them recycle the products. The PSS requires the 
companies to expand their scope of responsibility, thus it has the potential to 
optimize the product lifetime and improve the possibilities of product recycling. 
 
To sum up, PSS as a comprehensive concept, is influenced by the many factors, 
such as customer needs, social push and sustainability. It is necessary to further 
explain the definition of PSS in order to get a better understanding of the concept. 
Due to different focuses of PSS research, the PSS does not have a consistent 
definition. Haase, Pigosso, & McAloone (2017) conducted a study of PSS definitions 
for purpose of getting an overview of the interrelationships between them. Some

According to Haase et al.’s research (2017), the general understanding of PSS is that 
it is a system combining products with services in order to meet customer needs, 
which is highlighted as economic characteristic in the table. However, as mentioned

environmental characteristic should be taken into account in the definitions as well. 
As we can see in the table, there are only 2 of the 10 definitions include both meeting

Combining these two definitions, the term of "PSS" in this study could be defined as: 
A system contains products and services in order to become more competitive, meet 
customer needs and have lower environmental impacts.

1.2. PSS categorization  
PSS exist all over the world. For example, customers could use Uber to share a ride 
with another passenger; travelers could use Airbnb to rent local lodging. Both of them 
are forms of PSS. However, their business models are different, which lead to 
divergent sustainable potentials. Therefore, it is necessary to classify the PSS into 
different types in order to evaluate their environmental performance.  
 
According to Tukker’s typology (2004), PSS are classified into three categories 
based on various economic and environmental characteristics: product-oriented, use-
oriented and result-oriented. 
 
Figure 1 Three main categories of PSS. (Tukker, 2004) 
 
● Product-oriented PSS 
Products are the core of this type of PSS and extra services are provided 
around the products. 
 
● Use-oriented PSS 
In this type of PSS, customers do not have the ownership of the tangible 
products but pay for the use of them. 
 
● Result-oriented PSS 
There are no pre-determined products or services in this type pf PSS. The 
customers pay for the outcomes and the providers deliver the result as their 
pre-set agreements.

2. 
Literature review 
2.1. Positive impacts of PSS 
As mentioned in section 1.1, PSS has the potential to promote economic, social and 
environmental sustainability. It encourages many authors to study the field of PSS. 
 
2.1.1. Economic benefits 
From the outset, the purpose of the enterprises provide services or outcomes rather 
than a single product to their customers is to meet their needs and obtain greater 
profits (Mohanbir Sawhney, 2004). According to Van Ostaeyen's thesis (2014), 
commercial companies can profit from the following four aspects by applying a PSS: 
 
Cost reduction 
Based on Mont’s interviews (2002b), some Swedish companies offer supporting 
services to their customers in order to prolong their product lifespan. In this way, they 
can reduce the cost of producing new products. 
 
Value increase 
As more services are added to the existing products, the value of the integrated 
solution should be higher than the traditional one. 
 
Competitiveness improvement 
Products can be mass-produced, but services are usually provided for specific users. 
Therefore, services as intangible commodities, are more difficult to be imitated by 
competitors (Olivia et al., 2003). 
 
Customer base expansion 
By providing tailor-made services to users, companies can strengthen their 
relationships with existing customers. At the same time, because of the lower access 
costs, it can attract new customer segments as well. For example, customers who 
cannot afford to buy a car can get the right to use the car through a rental service. 
 
2.1.2. Social benefits 
According to Mont (2002), PSS is valuable for the society. First, PSS requires more 
labor than traditional business model, since there are some additional services such 
as maintenance and repair. Therefore, PSS can create more job opportunities. 
 
On the other hand, PSS is a business model that has the potential to improve the 
environment. If it is actively promoted in the society, it is likely to have a positive 
impact on the promotion of more sustainable lifestyles. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
2.1.3. Environmental benefits 
PSS can reduce resource use and waste by manufacturing fewer products (UNEP, 
2002). Reducing the total amount of production and material consumption is 
achieved through the following three ways: 
 
Extended products life cycle 
In some PSS cases, the providers has the responsibility for the product throughout 
the entire life cycle (UNEP, 2003). In order to reduce the costs of recycling and 
producing new products, they are willing to extend the product life cycle through 
maintenance or repair, thus reducing the impacts on the environment while achieving 
greater profits. 
 
Intensive use 
Products can be used more intensively through renting or sharing services (Tukker, 
2004). This kind of PSS needs fewer materials per functional unit than the traditional 
business model. 
 
Increasing recycling 
Adopting a PSS makes the providers become more responsible for its products, not 
only within the use-stage, but also for disposal stage (Baines et al., 2007). In some 
PSS cases, enterprises provide recycling services. On the one hand, it can reduce 
waste. On the other hand, the valuable parts of the products can be re-used, thereby 
reducing the use of virgin materials. 
 
2.2. Potentially negative impacts of PSS 
While PSS has benefits to the society, economy and environment, it is hard to avoid 
potential negative effects in practical application. These undesirable impacts caused 
by intruducing services into the system is called rebound effects (UNEP, 2003). The 
consumer behavior and the extra activities added to support the PSS are likely to 
have such an impact. 
 
Consumer behavior 
According to Mont (2002b) and Tukker (2004), consumers may not properly use the 
products when they do not have the ownership of the product. In this way, intensive 
use will shorten the product life cycle, resulting in greater environmental impacts. 
 
Added activities 
Introducing the service into the system is accompanied by new activities. For 
example, some PSS include rental services, but in the way of borrowing and return 
may have additional environmental impacts (UNEP, 2003). Especially in car rental, 
fuel consumption and pollution emissions will increase.

2.3. Evaluating environmental performance of PSS 
Through the above literature review, it can be seen that the initial purpose of PSS 
development is to reduce environmental impacts, but its effect in practical application 
still need to be verified. Over the last decades, environmental issues have been 
increasingly concerned about. Various methods of environmental evaluation are 
emerging, such as Environmental impact assessment (EIA), Ecological footprint, 
Health impact assessment (HIA), Alternatives assessment, Life Cycle Assessment 
(LCA), etc. However, the emphasis and discipline of each method is different. Which 
of them is suitable for evaluating the environmental performance of a PSS or is there 
any new approaches that are specifically designed for a PSS? These are the 
questions will be discussed in this section. 
 
In order to know the differences and connections between the mentioned 
assessment methods, a comparison is made. All the approaches mentioned above 
can be positioned in the framework proposed by Hacking and Guthrie (2008). The 
framework is established to compare different assessment techniques by their 
features, at the same time, build a “bridge” between them. The framework is defined 
by three axes (Figure 2): the comprehensiveness of the sustainable development 
(SD) coverage; the degree of “integration” of the techniques and themes; and the 
strategicness of the scope. 
 
Comprehensiveness of SD coverage  
Here the scale of coverage is from purely biophysical concern to a holistic 
perspective. In other words, the emphasis of the assessment approach is from the 
environmental aspect to comprehensive dimensions of the sustainability, including 
social, economic and so on. 
 
Integratedness of techniques and themes 
Sometimes the assessment may include multiple themes, for example, evaluating the 
environmental, social and economic sustainability of a project simultaneously. In this 
case, a single assessment technique is not enough to conduct a comprehensive 
evaluation. Therefore, it is need to integrate a wide range of assessment techniques 
to complete the evaluation. It can be seen that the theme integration can drive the 
technique integration. The sencond axis in the the framework represents the degree 
of “integration” of these techniques and themes. 
 
Strategicness of the focus 
In order to have a more strategic perspective, techniques with broader focus may be 
used to assess impacts. Here the strategicness is determined by the features of the 
assessment technique instead of the purpose of the assessment. According to 
Hacking and Guthrie (2008), “the key features that determine the strategicness of 
assessments are: the explicit goal (whether consider the trade-offs between positive 
and negative impacts); the ‘benchmark’ used (internationally recognized standards, 
reference systems, etc.); the spatial and temporal coverage; and the extent to which 
alternatives, cumulative impacts, and uncertainty are considered.”

Ecological Footprint is used to measure how human beings impact the 
ecosystem and how much natural resources needed to develop human 
economy (“Ecological Footprint - Global Footprint Network,” n.d.). This 
assessment method is relatively macroscopic and abstract. Both the scale of 
impact categories and their influencing areas are very large. The assessment 
normally involves the Ecological Footprint of a whole country or city.  
 
l 
Alternatives Assessment focus on improving the environmental performance 
of a project by comparing a number of potential solutions for it. The initial 
application of the method is to compare different chemical substances. There 
are multiple chemicals alternatives assessment methods in use around the 
world, such as the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)’s DfE program, 
CPA’s GreenScreent and MBDC’s Cradle to Cradles paradigms, while their 
common goal is to protect the environment and human beings (Whittaker & 
Heine, 2013). A comprehensive alternatives assessment may relate to cost, 
avaiability and social attributes as well. 
 
l 
Health Impact Assessment (HIA) is used to predict the potential impacts of 
policies, plans and projects on health. Here the “health” is a more generalized 
concept, which includes not only the impacts on human wellness, but also the 
possible effects on social and environmental health. For example, how to make 
a “health transort policies” that can help reduce traffic injuries, air pollution,  
noise and promote walking and cycling (“WHO | Health Impact Assessment,” 
2014). As a method created specifically for health assessment, there are two 
main reasons making HIA have a relatively high degree of integration. First, 
because of its special theme, some internatianl agencies, such as the World 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
Health Organization, can provide support and guidance. Second, since some 
methods, such as EIA, and HIA  have overlapping data requirements, they can 
be carried out at the same time (Hacking & Guthrie, 2008). 
 
l 
Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is a widely used method for 
environmental impacts evaluation, even applied in the USA and EU Member 
States as legislation (Glasson, Therivel, & Chadwick, 2013). The aim of the 
assessment is to make a more sustainable decision on whether a project should 
be implemented by collecting relevant environmental information. The 
assessment could be applied to policies, plans, programmes or projects. 
However, no matter what scale of the object is, the focus of the assessment is 
always on the environmental impacts rather than social, economic or other 
potential impacts. 
 
l 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a method used to assessment the 
environmental performance of the entire life cycle of a product or a service, from 
material processing to disposal. As a developed assessment method, the 
procedures of LCA are interpreted in the ISO 14040 and 14044 standards. 
There are four main phases: Goal and scope definition, Inventory analysis, 
Impact assessment and Interpretation (European Commission -- Joint Research 
Centre -- Institute for Environment and Sustainability, 2010).  
 
After introducing the five methods, it is time to figure out which of them is more 
suitable for evaluating the environmental performance of a PSS. Beginning with the 
more comprehensive ones, Ecological Footprint and Alternatives Assessment: the 
scope of application of Ecological Footprint is too large and it is often used to assess 
the impact of a certain economic sector on a particular category of resource; 
Alternatives Assessment was originally used for the assessment of chemical 
substances. It mainly concerns significant hazards caused by different substances, 
but is not for ordinary PSS assessment. Although HIA is a highly integrated method 
that can be supported by WHO and combined with other assessment techniques, it is 
more focused on the influences on the endpoints. In addition, these effects must be 
"health" related, but the PSS may not have this kind of damage. As can be seen from 
Figure 2, both EIA and LCA are specifically developed for the environmental impacts 
assessment. EIA evaluates the overall environmental performance of a project, while 
LCA is an assessment of one product or service, here the product or service can fit 
into a PSS. Moreover, LCA has a lifecycle perspective, while EIA is usually more 
concerned about the project itself rather than its upstream and downstream impacts, 
that's why LCA has a higher degree of strategicness. It can be said that LCA is more 
suitable for assessing environmental impacts of a system that consists of a product 
or service. 
 
Although LCA is a mature method for environmental impacts assessment of systems, 
it is primarily used to evaluate tangible products. For complex systems, like PSS 
which may include multiple products and/or services, the use of LCA may have some 
limitations. Accroding to Louise L. Kjaer, Pagoropoulos, Schmidt and McAloone 
(2016), three overall challenges were identified when applying LCA to the PSS: 
defining the reference system, identifying the function unit and determining the 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
system boundary. In order to address the challenges and develop a special method 
which fits into a PSS, Louise L. Kjaer, Pigosso and McAloone (2016) proposed a few 
guidelines for assessing it. The guidelines are based on LCA method and incorporate 
the valuable parts of other assessment methods. The emphasis of the guidelines is 
to compare the PSS with other alternatives and evaluate the PSS in a quantitative 
way. As LCA is the foundation of the guidelines, the steps of the guidelines have 
inextricable link to LCA procedures (Figure 3). It can be seen that all the above three 
challenges occur in the first step “Goal and scope definition” of LCA, so in the PSS 
evaluation guidelines, this step is broken down into four steps: Study goal definition, 
PSS and reference system exploration, Comparability assessment and Process 
mapping. They are the biggest differences between the PSS evaluation guidelines 
and the normal LCA implementation. 
 
However, could the specifically designed guidelines assess the environmental 
performance of PSS very well?  This is the research question of this thesis. In the 
next chapters, the guidelines will be applied in a “real” case step by step in order to 
see if the guidelines can run smoothly. It should be noted that since Step 5 and Step 
6 of the guidelines are similar to the last two steps in LCA, these two steps will just 
be briefly described in this thesis rather than implementing them. The focus will be 
the application of the first four steps of the giudelines.

2.4. Guidelines for evaluating the environmental performance of PSS  
According to the guidelines, the exploration of PSS is a very important step for the 
assessment (See Step 2 of the guidelines). Here, PSS are divided into three levels 
for analysis: business model, type and strategy (Figure 4). As can be seen from 
Figure 4, the PSS business model and the PSS type may interact with each other, 
since in some cases PSS type could be determined at the starting point, which could 
help define the business model later. 
 
 
 
Figure 4 From PSS business model to PSS strategy 
 
As stated in the guidelines, PSS can be distinguished into three categories according 
to the business model: Result-oriented, Product-oriented and Use-oriented. It is 
not difficult to see that this classification is based on Tukker’s (2004) typology (See 
Section 1.2). Afterwards, the PSS business models lead to three different types of 
PSS: Activity-based, Product-based and Platform-based.  
 
● Activity-based PSS 
This type of PSS performs activities for the customers’ need. 
 
● Product-based PSS 
In this type of PSS, products are supported by extra services or the use of the 
products could be transferred as services. 
 
● Platform-based PSS 
In this type of PSS, products and/or services are provided on a platform in 
order to let the customers get access to them. 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
The result-oriented business model includes various products and services in 
order to accomplish a pre-set result. The PSS that aims to complete this activity is 
called activity-based PSS.  
 
In the product-oriented business model, services are provided throughout one or 
more life cycle stages of the core products. If the services only contribute to the use 
stage of the products, then the PSS can be classified into activity-based PSS. 
Another situation is that the services are provided throughout the whole life cycle of 
the products or they have intentions to extend the life cycle of the products. This kind 
of PSS is defined as product-based PSS, as the product itself is more important than 
its outcome in this system. 
 
The functions of the products are the core of use-oriented business model. Two 
types of PSS are related to this kind of business model. The first one is when 
customers only pay for the functions of the products instead of the ownership, then 
the PSS still belongs to product-based. The second one is that the products and/or 
services are provided on a platform in order to fulfil customers’ need. This type of 
PSS is called platform-based PSS. 
 
Although different PSS have different environmental performance, some general 
impacts can be estimated based on the type of PSS. There are six PSS strategies 
deriving from the three types of PSS. Their sustainable potentials are as follows: 
 
(1) Operational support (Activity-based) 
Operational support means that supportive services are offered in the use-
stage of the products in order to keep them running in the most efficient way. 
 
(2) Optimised result (Activity-based) 
When the customers and the PSS providers have an agreement with the 
result, it could be assumed that the providers will find the most efficient way to 
reach there, by choosing better equipments or optimal processes. This kind of 
PSS strategy is called optimised result.  
 
(3) Product sharing (Product-based) 
Product sharing strategy can increase the utilization of idle products. In this 
way, fewer products are needed to meet the same customers’ need. 
 
(4) Maintenance (Product-based) 
Maintenance can be performed as after-sales services, such as repairs and 
upgrades, or it can be involved throughout the use-stage like predictive 
maintenance. They have the same purposes: increasing lifetime of products 
and reducing energy consumption during use-stage.  
 
(5) Take-back for reuse, remanufacturing or recycling (Product-based) 
In some PSS cases, the providers have the ownership of the products during 
the whole life cycle. Therefore, they are responsible for taking care of the 
products from cradle to grave, including the disposal stage. Thus, the 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
products are able to be reused, remanufactured or recycled in order to 
improve the environment. 
 
(6) Sharing platform (Platform-based) 
The sharing platform strategy is more complex than others, since it is 
relatively new to the customers and normally contains multiple products and 
services. Thus, the environmental potentials of this PSS strategy largely 
depend on their substitutes.

3. 
Methodology 
The purpose of this study is to test the guidelines proposed by Louise L. Kjaer et al. 
(2016) on a concrete PSS case. Accordingly, there are two main parts in this thesis: 
application of the guidelines step-by-step and the overall evaluation of it.  Research 
methods are used throughout the whole process. Before applying the guidelines, a 
literature review of Product/Service-Systems (PSS) is conducted. Afterwards, a 
concrete PSS case is chosen to test the guidelines. The application of the guidelines 
shows how well it works. It is the basis of the evaluation of the guidelines.  
 
 
 
 
3.1. Literature review of PSS 
As mentioned in Section 2.3, the guidelines are created to evaluate the 
environmental performance of PSS. Therefore, background knowledge of PSS is 
essential. Exploring existing articles about PSS provides a deeper understanding of 
this topic. It gives insights into the definition, motivations, types, advantages and 
disadvantages of PSS. Especially in sustainable aspect, PSS do not necessarily lead 
to environmental benefits (See Section 2.2). Thus, how to evaluate the environmental 
performance of PSS is a critical research area. There are several ways to assess the 
sustainability of traditional products. However, there is a blank space in evaluating 
the more complex systems (PSS). A set of guidelines, based on LCA method, is 
developed to fill the blank. During the literature review phase, a small gap of PSS 
strategies in the guidelines is found. There are six types of PSS strategies based on 
the guidelines. Each of them has an example to support, except the “maintenance” 
strategy. This gives a direction for the further work on this thesis. In order to improve 
the guidelines and fill the gap, “maintenance” strategy is determined to be the key 
criterion for case selection of the thesis. 
 
3.2. A PSS case study 
A concrete PSS case was selected to show and test how the guidelines works. At the 
same time, it serves another crucial purpose as well: to fill the blank of “maintenance” 
strategy case in the guidelines. 
 
3.2.1. Three types of maintenance 
As mentioned previously, PSS are ubiquitous today. The first step in finding a 
maintenance-related case is to get the knowledge of the types and characteristics of 
maintenance. Maintenance as support service is common in industry sectors. There 
are two dominant categories of maintenance in use. The first one is corrective or 
reactive maintenance, which is carried out after the equipment or system has broken 
down. The other one is preventive maintenance, which is executed routinely based 
on a predetermined schedule. The purpose of scheduled maintenance is to avoid 
unexpected breakdowns. However, this type of maintenance strategy maybe lead to 
waste, since the maintenance sticks to the schedule, which means it will be 
implemented no matter whether the equipment or machine needs it (Mobley, 2002). 
In order to improve the efficiency of maintenance, another type of strategy emerges: 
predictive maintenance. Predictive maintenance is a condition-based maintenance 
approach, which normally relies on a monitoring system. It can predict possible 
failures based on the data collected by the monitoring system, and then prevent the 
failures by repair, maintenance or upgrade.

According to the table and Mobley’s research (2002), predictive maintenance has 
higher competitivity and value-creation than the other two manitennace strategies. 
From an environmental perspective, predictive maintenance can keep the equipment 
or machine in a good condition, which leads to a longer lifetime and lower energy 
cost than an aging equipment. In terms of the economy, predictive maintenance is 
executed just before the failure occurs, which means that the machine has minimal 
downtime and can restore production as soon as possible. In addition, the spare 
parts needed are prepared in advance, so the company does not need to spend extra 
money on urgent shipping. Although the monitoring system is normally expensive, it 
could be offset by increasing the equipment availability through performing predictive 
maintenance (Mobley, 2002).  
 
3.2.2. Predictive maintenance and PSS 
Although predictive maintenance is a valuable maintenance strategy, before starting 
further case screening, it needs to be confirmed whether it is a PSS. This is the 
definition of PSS in this thesis: “A PSS is a system which contains (1) products and 
services in order to become (2) more competitive, (3) meet customer needs and have 
(4) lower environmental impacts.” (see Section 1.1)  
 
(1) Products and Services 
In predictive maintenance, the product is the device or facility that needs to be 
maintained, and the service is the data monitoring, maintenance or management 
assistance provided for the product owner. Therefore, it can be said that predictive 
maintenance contains the two most important elements of PSS: product and service. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
19 
(2) More competitive 
Mobley (2002) made a figure to show the degree of competitivity of mainstream 
maintenance strategies (Figure 5).  
 
  
Figure 5 The path to maturity for equipment maintenance (Mobley, 2002) 
 
As can be seen from the figure, predictive maintenance has relatively high 
competitivity. Although preventive maintenance can reduce the number of 
breakdowns by scheduled maintenance, it is not sufficient to prevent non-time-based 
failures. However, through monitoring and assessing the condition, predictive 
maintenance can make the overall availability of the equipment reach 90% of the 
intended design. If not implement predictive maintenance, the figure is only 50% 
(Mobley, 2002). This is the reason why adopting the predictive maintenance can 
improve the competitiveness of enterprises. 
 
(3) Meet customer needs 
For enterprises that adopt predictive maintenance, their need is to reduce the 
frequency and duration of failures, thus minimizing the overall costs. As mentioned 
above, predictive maintenance can guarantee high equipment availability, so it can 
meet this demand. In addition, since equipment requires different amounts of 
maintenance, the traditional scheduled maintenance cannot be tailored for each 
piece of equipment. However, predictive maintenance can modify the maintenance 
plan by analyzing the data collected by the monitoring system, which will meet more 
specific customer needs. 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
(4) Lower environmental impacts 
In general, the beneficial effects of predictive maintenance on the environment are 
achieved by keeping the equipment in good condition. According to Mobley’s (2002) 
research, the equipment performance follows a sawtooth pattern: “which does well 
after maintenance and then degrades until the failure is noticed and brought back up 
to a high level.” (Figure 6)  
 
 
Figure 6 Preventive (predictive) maitenance to keep acceptable performance. (Mobley, 2002) 
 
When the performance of the equipment bottoms out, it will not only threaten the 
safety of the operators, but also release more pollutants. It can be seen from the 
figure that the implementation of predictive maintenance can slow the degradation 
rate of the equipment, thereby extending the overall life cycle of the equipment and 
reducing the environmental impacts.

4.2. MICHELIN® TIRE CARE 
In 2015, MICHELIN TIRE CARE solution was launched by Michelin Croup in the USA 
(Michelin, 2015a). At the beginning, the solution is only offered in truck product line in 
North America. Now it has expanded to Europe and other geographic zones of 
Michelin Group. The solution relies on a set of sensing devices, which is able to 
collect and transmit real-time condition data of the tires. The customers can check 
these information through their smartphone, PC or other devices that can get access 
to MICHELIN TIRE CARE cloud. After analysis, the system will provide maintenance 
advice. The aim of the solution is to optimise the maintenance operations, prolong 
the tire lifetime and reduce resource consumption. Through the TIRE CARE solution, 
Michelin provides not only tires but also high-level tire-related service. For the sake of 
different user needs, the services provided in North America and Europe are 
different.

trackable throughout the whole life cycle.  
 
In North America, MICHELIN TIRE CARE provides two types of programs: Fleet

3) Actionable reports

5.2. Step 2 PSS and reference system exploration 
It is important to define the scope of the study and its reference system in an early 
stage, because they determine the boundaries for the following steps. The business 
model, type and environmental potentials of the PSS case will be interpreted in this 
step. Moreover, the relevant environmental impact categories need to be considered 
from this step, which will lay the foundation for step 4 and 5. 
 
5.2.1. Scope and reference system 
According to the guidelines (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016), there are three possible 
scopes for a PSS: PSS consequences, PSS comparison and PSS optimisation 
(Figure 11). The scope depends on the purpose of the study, and each of them 
corresponds to a different reference system.  
 
 
Figure 11 Three scopes for environmental assessment of PSS (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016) 
 
● PSS consequences 
Here the study focuses on the influences caused by introducing the PSS into 
the reference system. Normally, this is a system-level assessment related to 
government and society, e.g. evaluating the impacts of bike sharing on the 
entire urban transport system. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
33 
● PSS comparison 
Here the comparison is between the chosen PSS and its reference system, 
the system could be a traditional product-oriented solution or a different PSS 
solution. The two comparable systems should have a common functional unit. 
 
● PSS optimisation 
The purpose of this type of scope is to choose an optimal solution by 
comparing different design elements within the PSS. 
 
In the case of Michelin, the purpose is to assess the environmental advantages of the 
TIRE CARE solution by comparing with traditional tire maintenenace service. It does 
not involve the design elements within the PSS, so the scope does not belong to the 
third one. In addition, the comparison does not involve the impacts on the whole truck 
tire industry. Therefore, the scope of this study should be PSS comparison. And the 
reference system is the traditional tire maintenance service without using TIRE CARE 
solution. 
 
5.2.2. PSS business model 
As mentioned in Section 2.4, the guidelines divide PSS into three types of business 
models: Product-oriented, Use-oriented and Result-oriented. Here the MICHELIN 
TIRE CARE solution is offered to support their truck product line, so the business 
model of the PSS can be defined as product-oriented. The corresponding PSS type 
would be judged in the subsequent section. 
 
5.2.3. PSS type 
Each of the three business models may link to different PSS types, depending on 
what factor is affected in the system (see Section 2.4). A product-oriented business 
model may derive two types of PSS: Activity-based PSS or Product-based PSS 
(Figure 12). The difference between these two types is that the influence of the 
service in the product life cycle. For an activity-based PSS, the service only influence 
the use-stage of the product, while the product-based PSS affect the whole life cycle 
of the product, e.g. offering take-back service or having an intention to extend the 
product lifetime. 
 
 
Figure 12 Relationships between PSS business model and type of PSS (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016) 
 
==PAGE:35==
 
34 
As we can see in the Michelin case, truck tire is their central product, and the TIRE 
CARE service is part of their post-sales service to support the product. The purpose 
of the service is not only to monitor the condition of tires during the use-stage, but 
also to provide tire maintenance services to their customers in the subsequent 
stages. The aim of the maintenance service is to prolong the product life time. 
Therefore, the MICHELIN TIRE CARE solution can be defined as a Product-based 
PSS. 
 
5.2.4. Substitutions 
In this sub-step, the alternative system of the PSS is identified. According to the 
guidelines, the substitutions could be multiple product systems, a single product 
system or a single life cycle stage of a product system(Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016). 
Which of them is more suitable for the study depends on the PSS type identified in 
the previous step. There is a chart in the guidelines, which shows the relationship 
between the alternative systems and their PSS types (Figure 13). 
 
 
Figure 13 Relationship between substitutions and PSS types (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016) 
 
● Platform-based PSS and Multiple product systems 
As most platform-based PSS provide new consumption patterns to the 
customers, this type of PSS may involve more than one product system. For 
example, Uber may not only affect the operation of traditional taxis, but also 
the consumption habits in other public transport systems. Therefore, for this 
type of PSS that has an impact on multiple product systems, the key to the 
study is to determine the consumer segments and dominant scenarios. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
35 
 
● Product-based PSS and Single product system 
A product-based PSS usually involves only one single system associated with 
its central product. In the evaluation process, it is important to outline the 
whole life cycle of the product, analysis the life cycle stages one by one and 
then find out the environmental hotspots. It is worth mentioning that this type 
of PSS may involve multiple product systems when the PSS not only affect 
the use-stage of the product but also its reusing or remanufacturing at the 
end-of-life stage. Because the recycled products may be shifted to other 
product systems. 
 
● Activity-based PSS and Single life cycle stage 
Normally an activity-based PSS provides customers a solution to a specific 
problem, which appears to affect only the use-stage of the product. However, 
it is likely to have indirect impacts on other life cycle stages. For example, HP 
Instant Ink service, which is a printing subscription, not only help their 
customers reduce their ink consumption but also provides cartridges recycling 
services (HP, n.d.). Therefore, for the environmental assessment of this type 
of PSS, it is recommended to pay attention to the whole life cycle instead of 
only one stage of the product. 
 
From Section 5.2.3 we can see, the MICHELIN TIRE CARE solution belongs to 
product-based PSS. The services include fleet evaluation, tire monitoring, actionable 
report and tire maintenance. However, they do not involve material shift in the end-of-
life stage of the tires. Thus, the substitution of the TIRE CARE solution can be 
regarded as a single product system. 
 
5.2.5. PSS potentials 
Although a PSS does not necessarily lead to environmental improvements, its 
potential impacts can be qualitatively predicted based on the PSS strategies. In the 
guidelines, the PSS environmental impacts are divided into two categories: primary 
and secondary impacts (Figure 14).  
 
 
Figure 14 “Comparing a PSS with a reference system in terms of environmental impact. The dotted area 
represents the potential impact savings. Rebound effects (illustrated by the red arrow) will lead to a 
decrease in anticipated savings, while secondary benefits (illustrated by the green arrow) lead to an 
increase in anticipated savings.” (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016)  
 
==PAGE:37==
 
36 
The primary impacts include avoided and induced impacts. The reduction caused by 
introducing a PSS is defined as avoided impacts. And the induced impacts are a 
number of additional environmental impacts that may arise from the new products, 
services or systems. If the avoided impacts are greater than the induced ones, then 
the PSS can be considered to have impacts savings.  
 
At the same time, the PSS may also have some secondary impacts in the  
implementation, which are caused by the changes of consumer behavior and values. 
The positive impacts caused by these changes are called secondary benefits and the 
negative ones are defined as rebound effects. These secondary impacts need to be 
taken into account as well when assessing the environmental performance. 
 
According to the guidelines, these impacts will be quantitatively analyzed in Step 5 
(Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016). However, based on the commonality of the PSS 
strategies, some of their potential impacts can be estimated in this step. As 
mentioned in Section 2.4, the PSS strategies are derived from their PSS types. Since 
the MICHELIN TIRE CARE case was chosen to support the “maintenance” strategy, 
it belongs to product-based PSS and has the features of this type. The core of TIRE 
CARE solution is the predictive maintenance for truck tires. Normally, conducting 
maintenance has the possibilities to increase lifetime of the products and reduce 
energy consumption during use-stage, as it can keep the products running in a good 
condition. However, unnecessary energy consumption may be caused by too 
frequent maintenance. The monitoring system of  the predictive maintenance can 
help solve this problem. Therefore, it can be assumed that the MICHELIN TIRE 
CARE solution has the potential to reduce the environmental cost.

the scope of the system is defined as PSS comparison, which means that the 
evaluation focuses on the differences between MICHELIN TIRE CARE solution and 
their traditional tire maitenance service (Figure 15).

greatest impact on the use-stage. The maintenance can make the tires work in the

5.2.7. Reflection of Step 2 
For step 2.1 
The guidelines refers to three types of PSS scopes: Consequences, Comparison and 
Optimisation. The third one, system optimisation, is easy to distinguish with the 
former two, since it only involves the evaluation of the elements inside the system. 
However, the first two are easy to confuse in the application. When people making a 
PSS consequences assessment, will naturally think of whether to compare the PSS 
with the baseline situation, which will mislead people to judge a consequences 
assessment as a comparison assessment. 
 
Suggestion: 
It is recommended that a simple case description be added for each scope in the 
guidelines, in order to help the evaluator understand the difference between these 
three scopes. 
 
For step 2.2 and 2.3 
Steps 2.2 and 2.3 are interrelated. As the classification of PSS had been studied 
through literature review in the first two chapters, these two steps were smooth-
running. MICHELIN TIRE CARE solution is obviously created for extend lifespan of 
their truck tires, so there is no doubt that it belongs to the product-based type of PSS 
towards product-oriented. 
 
Suggestion: 
It is easy to determine the business model and type of PSS through the descriptions 
and figure in the guidelines. No improvement suggestions. 
 
For step 2.4 
Although each type of PSS has a corresponding alternative, in practical applications 
it should be noted that in some cases, one type of PSS may correspond to more than 
one kind of substitutions. For example, the product-based PSS may link to multiple 
product systems rather than single product system, because of the material reuse in 
the disposal stage. 
 
Suggestion: 
As long as the evaluators carefully read the description of the substitutions in the 
guidelines, they will not encounter problems. No improvement suggestions.

For step 2.5 
Except the “Maintenance” strategy, the guidelines explain all other five PSS 
strategies through cases, which helps the assessor figure out which strategy the PSS

Suggestion:

5.3. Step 3 Comparability assessment 
The aim of this step is to ensure that the PSS and the reference system are 
comparable. First, a common functionality of the chosen systems is defined. Second, 
the overall functionality is divided into several sub-functions, in order to compare the 
differences between the PSS and the reference system. Lastly, the rebound effects 
that may be triggered by the different consumption factors (money, time, space, etc.) 
of the compared systems are described. 
 
5.3.1. Functionality an Functional Unit 
Here the functionality is the common functional outcome that the PSS and its 
substitutions can provide to the consumers. In order to make the analyzed systems 
comparable, it is necessary to define the functional unit (FU) of them, like what LCA 
does. The FU is based on the common functionality determined. According to the 
guidelines (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016), the three types of substitutions have their 
corresponding functional units: 
==PAGE:40==
 
39 
 
● Multiple product systems 
For multiple product systems, the FU should be a common functional outcome 
of all the analyzed systems that can be quantitatively compare. Since such 
comparisons involve multiple systems, even some solutions may contain 
multiple products, it is necessary to use sub-functions to ensure that the 
details of different alternative systems can be considered comprehensive. 
 
● Single product system life cycle 
Figure 16 shows the four stages of the life cycle of a product/service/system: 
Materials extraction and production, Manufacture, Use and maintenance and 
Recycling and disposal.  
 
 
Figure 16 The life cycle of a product/service/system. 
 
For a single product system, if one of stages in the life cycle has changed, it 
may affect the other stages. For example, prolonging the use-stage of a 
product, it can reduce the total production of the product. However, it may 
increase energy consumption and pollutant emissions during use phase. 
Therefore, when considering the FU of a single product system life cycle, all 
lifecycle stages should be taken into account so that the FU can be used to 
measure the impacts on the other phases caused by changes from a certain 
stage. 
 
● Single life cycle stage 
For a single life cycle stage, the FU focuses on a specific activity, which can 
be used to measure the changes of a certain stage of the life cycle. When 
analyzing this type of substitution, it is assumed that the other stages are not 
affected by the PSS or the impacts are very small so that they can be ignored. 
 
As mentioned in Section 5.2.4, the substitution of the Michelin case is a single 
product system, so the entire life cycle should be taken into account when defining 
the FU. In MICHELIN TIRE CARE, the core product is the truck tire. The 
maintenance services can extend use-stage of the tires, while influence the demand 
for raw materials, the amount of tire production and waste.  
 
On the other hand, the FU is determined based on the functionality of the PSS as 
well. In the TIRE CARE solution, the function offered by Michelin is to reduce the tire 
downtime so that improve the truck productivity per mileage. It can be seen that if the 
truck is disengaged, the tire itself cannot meet the customer's desire to increase the 
productivity. Therefore, in this case, the functional unit needs to be reflected by the 
truck's transport function, which can be defined as:

After the overall functional unit is determined, it should be devided into several sub-
functions, i.e. what processes are needed to achieve the above function unit. In this 
study, the functional unit of the two compared systems is to transport goods with a 
truck (see Section 5.3.1). However, the core product of the systems is MICHELIN® X 
One® tire. Therefore, the production of the tires should be considered as well when 
defining the sub-functions. Figure 19 shows the sub-functions needed to fulfill the 
functional unit (High resolution figure is shown in Appendix 3). 
 
 
Figure 19 Sub-functions of the two compared systems. The TIRE CARE solution is illustrated by the pink 
dotted line, while the traditional maintenance is illustrated by the black dotted line. 
 
It can be seen from the figure, the biggest difference between the Michelin TIRE 
CARE solution and the traditional maintenance service is the added tire monitoring 
system and the fault prediction based on the collected data, which also affects the 
tire repair phase. The traditional maintenance service requires regular inspection and 
maintenance of all the tires, while the TIRE CARE solution can predict the 
breakdowns based on the real condition of each tire and help the customer make a 
judgment as to whether or not a repair is needed. 
 
5.3.3. Utility, value and rebound effects 
The previous two sub-steps have studied how the PSS and its reference system fulfil 
the functional units with specific processes, while this sub-step will explore how well 
the compared systems work for the customer needs. It is affected by two main 
factors: the utility and the value.  
 
The utility is the objective and practical nature of the system. As mentioned in the 
guidelines (Louise L. Kjaer et al., 2016), usually cars are more suitable for long-
distance commuters than bicycles, since driving a car is more time-saving. 
 
The value is how the customers understand the value of the system, which is 
subjective and vary from segment to segment. Take the example of commuting 
again, if the customer segment likes a healthy lifestyle, then they will be in favour of 
riding bikes instead of driving cars. 
 
The utility and the value may be affected by many consumption factors, such as 
money, time, space, etc. (more factors see Appendix 4). Only when the utility and 
value of the system fit well with the customer values, they will choose it. In turn, when 
the customers choose a new PSS, their consumption habits may be affected as well. 
==PAGE:44==
 
43 
The negative impacts caused by the changes are called rebound effects (see Section 
5.2.5). The rebound effects can be distinguished into two types: direct and indirect 
(Figure 20). 
 
 
Figure 20 The differences between the direct and indirect rebound effects. (Evans & Hunt, 2009) 
 
Direct rebound effects mean that the impacts of changes are within the system. For 
example, a customer bought a car with lower fuel consumption, but because the cost 
of fuel per kilometre becomes cheaper, it may encourage the user to drive more 
mileage, which results in greater environmental impacts. 
 
Indirect rebound effects are the negative impacts that may have on other areas. As 
shown in the figure, if the customer spends the money saved through using a fuel-
efficient car on buying other products or services, it may cause greater environmental 
impact. 
 
In this study, the following consumption factors may affect the utility and value of the 
compared systems: 
 
● Money 
Depending on the size of the fleet, the cost of the subscription of TIRE CARE 
service per month for each truck ranges from $ 2 - $ 10. Although this 
expenditure is generated by introducing the PSS, it can bring four times profits 
than before (Michelin, 2015a). The benefits mainly come from the reduction in 
tire failure rates, since it will cost about $ 200 each time to handle a tire-
related event (“Michelin® Tire Care | Michelin Truck,” n.d.). 
 
In this case, the impact of the PSS is determined by the direction of the 
customer's investment. If the fleet will use the saved money to subscribe TIRE 
CARE service for more trucks, then it might generate secondary benefit. If the 
company uses the excess funds for purchasing more vehicles, it may have an 
indirect rebound effect. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:45==
 
44 
 
● Time 
As mentioned in one of the user feedbacks, before using TIRE CARE, the 
fleet had about three to four tire-related events per week; now the figure is 
about one event per month (“Michelin® Tire Care | Michelin Truck,” n.d.). 
Therefore, it can be said that adopting TIRE CARE program can reduce the 
tire's downtime, but it also means that the truck will have more time to run on 
the road, consume more fuel and release more pollution. Therefore, reducing 
the failure time may lead to a direct rebound effect. 
 
● Access 
Trucks that have subscribed the TIRE CARE solution can be inspected and 
maintained at any one of Michelin's maintenance shops (Figure 21). While the 
traditional maintenance service must be executed at the maintenance plant 
that cooperates with the fleet. This means that it can shorten the distance to 
the maintenance plant, thereby reducing fuel consumption and pollutant 
emissions, which leads to secondary benefit. 
 
 
Figure 21 Locations of 249 Love’s TirePass in the United States of America. (Michelin, n.d.) 
 
● Convenience 
Since the analysis results of the TIRE CARE solution will be uploaded to the 
cloud, customers can log on to check their tire condition at any time. They 
only need to take an action when they receive a repair reminder instead of 
having to drive to the repair shop on a regular basis. Therefore, it is similar to 
the “Access”, which can also reduce the impacts on the environment and 
bring secondary benefit. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:46==
 
45 
● Risk 
As mentioned above, subscribing the TIRE CARE service can reduce the 
frequency of accidents; that is to say, the times of roadside assistance can be 
reduced. As we all know, roadside assistances require trailers and a range of 
tools. Reducing the frequency of roadside assistances is equal to avoiding the 
consumption of the extra vehicles and equipment, which might bring 
secondary benefit.  
 
5.3.4. Reflection of Step 3 
For step 3.1 
In the process of performing this sub-step, the biggest difficulty is how to determine 
the functional unit according to the functionality. Although the guidelines has 
pointed out the main points that should be included in the functional unit based on 
the three different substitutions, when encountering a practical case, the situation is 
more complicated than imagined. For example, in this thesis, it was clear that the 
substitution of the Michelin TIRE CARE solution is a single product system. 
However, it is difficult to integrate the maintenance services, the tires, the trucks 
and their transport function into one functional unit. Because the tire itself cannot 
meet the customer needs, it must be put on the vehicles to achieve the transport 
function. And the maintenance service directly applies to the tires rather than the 
trucks. It can be seen that although the alternative system of the PSS is a single 
product system, it involves multiple products. Therefore, the evaluators need to pay 
special attention to how to integrate all the elements of the systems together during 
the implementation, so that a proper functional unit can be gotten.

show the steps of derivation - How can get the functional units from the user's

typical PSS cases given in the guidelines (free-floating or laundry service), if diverse 
cases can be made, it will help the assessors, especially the novices, understand 
how to implement this step. 
 
For step 3.2 
It should be noted that the core product of the system – the tire - should be taken 
into account in this step, since no matter the Mechilin TIRE CARE solution or the 
ordinary maintenance service, directly affects the tire’s lifespan. When comparing 
the differences between the two systems, we first used the flow chart to sort out the 
sub-functions of the traditional maintenance system, and then added or marked the 
differences of the TIRE CARE solution on the diagram. In this way the 
implementation of this step is very clear, did not encounter big obstacles. 
 
Suggestion: 
The explanation of the sub-functions is very clear in the guidelines. It would be 
better if add some suggestions on how to compare the sub-functions of the different 
systems, such as the flowcharts used in this study.

46 
For step 3.3 
When implementing this sub-step, the assessors should pay attention to the 
meaning of the rebound effects, which are not the advantages and disadvantages 
of the consumption factors of the systems, but the possible impacts on the 
environment. Such as the TIRE CARE case, it can reduce the frequency of tire-
related events, which is economically beneficial to the customers. However, it may 
release more pollutants because of increasing the running time of the trucks. 
 
Suggestion: 
It is very meaningful that the guidelines takes these non-functional factors into

5.4.2. System boundary 
Although the flowcharts seem to cover a wide range of processes, they can be 
simplified: excluding the same or similar processes of the compared systems. In the 
Michelin case, the similar process is the tire distribution and mounting. Michelin truck 
tire mainly has two types of market: the original equipment market and the 
replacement market. Original equipment is sold to truck manufacturers as original 
tires. Replacement tire is sold to the distributors. In North America, Michelin mainly 
distributes truck tires through road transport. They designed a system to optimize the 
transport capacity of the vehicles, so the number of tires loaded on each vehicle is 
fixed (Michelin, 2008). Whether or not to reduce the tire consumption in a certain 
fleet, the environmental impacts of the tire distribution and mounting process of the 
two systems should be similar. It can be eliminated outside the boundary. 
 
Another way to simplify the flowchart is to set cut-off criteria, which is to exclude the 
process that has insignificant impact on the environment. For the TIRE CARE 
program, this process is the devices that customers used to check the tire condition. 
Compared with the reference system, these devices are additional and may cause 
induced impact. But in practical terms, the customers can log on the TIRE CARE 
==PAGE:49==
 
48 
cloud through their existing smart phones, tablets or PCs instead of buying a lot of 
extra devices. Therefore, it can be assumed that the mobile devices and PCs 
process can be left out. 
 
The approach chosen to handle the multifunctional processes may influence the 
system boundary as well. According to ILCD handbook (European Commission -- 
Joint Research Centre -- Institute for Environment and Sustainability, 2010), there are 
three approaches to solve multifunctionality: 
 
● Subdivision of multifunctional processes 
Divide the multifunctional process into several mono-functional processes and 
then calculate their environmental impact separately. 
 
● System expansion 
System expansion means that adding other functions or subtracting 
unnecessary functions in order to make the systems comparable. 
 
● Allocation 
Allocation is to partition the inputs and outputs between the co-funtions based 
on some allocation criteria, such as element content, energy content, mass, 
market price, etc.  
 
In this study, there are two mainly multifunctional processes: fuel consumption during 
the use phase and the end-of-life tires.  
 
Fuel consumption 
Because not all of the fuel used by the truck will be consumed in driving the tires, the 
allocation approach should be used to calculate the exact tire fuel consumption. The 
fuel consumption of the tire during use phase is linked to its rolling resistance, which 
is the force resisting the motion when the tire rolls on the road. According to 
Michelin's statistics, about 30 to 40% of the total fuel consumption is rolling 
resistance consumption (Michelin, 2008). Based on the proportion and the total fuel 
consumption required for a truck, the amount of fuel consumed by the tire can be 
calculated. 
 
End-of-life tires 
The tire is composed of a variety of materials, so in the final stage it needs different 
disposal approaches. In North America, Michelin’s end-of-life tire recovery rates are: 
23% of tires are reused or retreaded; 24% of tires are used for material recovery; 
36% of tires are used for energy recovery; 17% of tires are disposed to landfill 
(Michelin, 2008). These percentages can be used as the basis for allocation.

5.5. Step 5 Quantification and Step 6 Interpretation and recommendations 
As mentioned in Section 2.3, the main differences between the guidelines for PSS 
evaluation and the LCA method are concentrated in the first phase: study goal 
definition, reference system exploration, comparability assessment and process 
mapping. Step 5 and 6 of the guidelines are basically same as the last two steps of 
LCA. The thesis should focus on the differences of the two methods. Moreover, as 
mentioned previously, the assessment is not an official commission from Michelin, so 
the data available is very limited. If use these data to do the quantitative assessment, 
the results will have a great uncertainty. Therefore, this thesis will only briefly 
describe the methodology of the last two steps based on the case instead of 
implementing them. 
 
The flowcharts needed for quantitative assessment have been established in Step 4. 
In Step 5, the assessors will collect processes relevant data, in order to model them 
in LCA software, such as SimPro, which can be used for LCIA as well. Here the 
ReCiPe method is chosen to conduct the LCIA. There are two levels of indicators in 
this method: midpoint and endpoint (see Appendix 5). In this study, the midpoint level 
will be selected, since it fits well with Michelin’s environmental performance criteria. 
The criteria focuses on the resources consumption exhaust emissions and waste 
disposal, which are related to the midpoint indicators in ReCiPe method. It should be 
noted that the ReCiPe method launched in 2008 includes 18 midpoint indicators; 
while in 2016, the experts revised a new version of ReCiPe that integrated the impact 
==PAGE:54==
 
53 
categories into 17 (Goedkoop et al., 2009; Huijbregts et al., 2017). They impact 
categories can be found in Appendix 5. In this assessment, the ReCiPe 2016 will be 
chosen, since it added and updated several novel damage pathways.   
 
Step 6 is to analyze the results of the quantitative assessment. For the Michelin case, 
due to lack of data, quantitative assesement is skipped in this thesis. However, it can 
be learned from the experience in the first few steps, that the biggest difference 
between a PSS evaluation and a normal product LCA is that there are additional 
products and/or services in the system. This new business model may affect the 
user's consumption habits, which will lead to rebound effects. When interpreting the 
results to the audience, the assessors should display all the relevant impacts to their 
intended audience, including the induced impacts, avoided impacts, rebound effects 
and secendary benefits. In this way, they can finally get an overview of the 
environmental performance of the PSS.

6. 
An overall evaluation of the guidelines 
Another important purpose of this thesis is to evaluate the guidelines. During the 
application phase, there is a brief reflection after each sub-step. In this chapter, the 
overall performance of the guidelines will be evaluated by using the three-
dimensional framework mentioned in Section 2.2.   
 
The evaluation of the guidelines will be considered from the three aspects of the 
framework proposed by Hacking and Guthrie (2008), each of which will be rated. The 
rating is divided into three levels: from low to medium to high. The rating criteria are 
explained in Appendix 6. The result is as follows: 
 
Table 8 Comprehensiceness, integratedness and strategicnees rating of PSS guidelines. 
 
Comprehensiveness of SD coverageMEDIUM 
Although the main purpose of the guidelines is to assess the environmental 
performance of PSS, it involves some other socio-economic factors as well. For 
example, adopting a PSS may influence the customers’ values and lead to rebound 
effects or secondary benefits, which is the social science aspect. Moreover, when 
considering the consumption factors, the customers might use the saved money to 
buy extra products, which will cause rebound effects. This is the performance of 
environment-economy interaction. The definition of “Medium” is a combination of 
environmental and socio-economic matters. Therefore, it can be said that the 
guidelines has medium degree of comprehensiveness. 
 
Integratedness of techniques and themesMEDIUM 
Here the definition of “Medium” is that the practitioners are from various disciplines 
and the method combines two or more assessment techniques, but focus on a 
specific theme. There are three levels of practitioners in the guidelines: the strategic 
decision-maker, the PSS designer and the LCA practitioner; which meets the first 
criterion. Secondly, the guidelines associate with LCA technique. It is sort of an 
approach using multiple assessment techniques. However, the guidelines are 
primarily created for environmental assessment rather than applying to other themes, 
so the rating is medium. 
 
Strategicness of the focusMEDIUM to HIGH 
There are six key features may affect the strategicness of the assessment approach: 
the explicit goal; the ‘benchmark’ used; the spatial and temporal coverage; and the 
extent to which alternatives, cumulative impacts, and uncertainty are considered 
(Hacking & Guthrie, 2008). The rating of each feature is different, which results in the 
middle to high degree of strategicness. For the assessment goal, the guidelines 
takes the tradeoffs between the positive and negative effects into account, from the 
avoided impacts to rebound effects. The assessment “benchmark” is based on 
different PSS scopes. It could be a system-level (PSS consequences) or a PSS 
comparison. The spatial and temporal scales of the guidelines are unlimited, 
==PAGE:56==
 
55 
depending on the scenarios. The alternative systems and the evaluation benchmarks 
are similar, which could be a single or multiple alternatives, depending on the 
scenarios and customer segments. The cumulative impacts depend on the type of 
assessment. If it is a pre-implementation assessment, then the result can be used for 
future PSS design. If it is a post-implementation assessment, then the method is has 
low cumulative impacts. The step 6 of the guidelines includes completeness checks, 
sensitivity checks and consistency checks. These methods can reduce the 
uncertainty of the guidelines. To sum up, the guidelines has good performance in 
most of the above six features, so the overall rating is middle to high.

7. 
Conclusions 
PSS is a system contains products and services in order to become more competitive, 
meet customer needs and have lower environmental impacts. However, the extra 
services and/or products may lead to unnecessary environmental impacts, so the PSS 
may not be able to achieve environmental sustainability. Therefore, a systematic 
approach is needed to assess the actual environmental performance of the PSS. In the 
literature review, Hacking and Guthrie’s framework (2008) was used to compare several 
commonly used environmental assessment methods. Among them, LCA seems an ideal 
way to evaluate a product or a system’s environmental performance from a life cycle  
perspective. Nonetheless, LCA is usually applicable to assessment of a single product 
or a simple system. Conducting a LCA on complex systems, like the PSS that may 
contain multiple products and/or services, will encounter some challenges. According to 
Louise L. Kjaer et al.'s research (2016), the three main challenges are reference system 
definition, functional unit definition and system boundary establishment. In order to 
overcome these difficulties, Louise L. Kjaer et al. proposed several guidelines (2016), 
which is an assessment approach based on LCA technique and developed specifically 
for evaluating the environmental performance of PSS. This guidelines is still in the stage 
of continuous improvement. The purpose of this thesis is to test the guidelines on a 
concrete PSS case and then make an evaluation of the guidelines. 
 
The reason why chose the Michelin TIRE CARE solution as the case is to fill the case 
vacancy on the “Maintenance” strategy in the guidelines. In Section 3.2, the three 
commonly used maintenance strategies are compared, and the predictive maintenance 
as the most advantageous one was selected as the research object. The relationship 
between the predictive maintenace and the PSS was discussed, from the three aspects: 
competitiveness improvement, customer needs satisfaction and environmental 
sustainability. The TIRE CARE solution is a predictive maintenance service offered by 
Michelin Truck. Therefore, it could be said that to the Michelin TIRE CARE solution is a 
representative case of PSS with maintenance strategy. 
 
So how well the guidelines works on a specific case? Due to the time and data 
constraints, the study focused on the challenges encountered in the first few steps of the 
guidelines. Because of the complexity of the PSS, the guidelines split the first LCA 
phase “Goal and scope definition” into four steps: Study goal definition, PSS and 
reference system exploration, Comparability assessment and Process mapping. In this 
thesis, these four steps were applied to the TIRE CARE case one by one. After 
completing each step, the author made a brief reflection. These reflections directly 
describe the problems or advantages that the author encountered in practice, which are 
relatively subjective . Since the author does not have long-term LCA assessment 
experience, these subjective evaluations may be more appropriate for novel PSS 
designers or LCA practitioners, while the LCA experts may not necessarily have such 
reflections when implementing these steps. Therefore, in Chapter 6, the author took the 
three-dimentions Hacking and Guthrie’s framework (2008) again to make an objective 
evaluation of the guidelines. These ratings of comprehensiveness, integratedness and 
strategicness of the PSS guidelines are medium, medium and medium to high. The 
author believes that the level of the rating itself cannot explain the pros and cons of the 
guidelilnes, since the evaluation should be combined with the intention of the guidelines. 
For example, the comprehensiceness rating of the guidelines is medium, which means 
==PAGE:58==
 
57 
that the assessment approach is focus on the environmental assessment, but also take 
into account the socio-economic matters. Does this explain that the guidelines is not 
perfect? Do the experts need to modify until it can reach a high level of 
comprehensiveness? This is not necessarily. Because from the purpose of the 
guidelines, is to assess the environmental performance of the PSS, there is no problem  
focusing on the environmental issues. At the same time, since the PSS are complex,  
especially the service element involves a range of human factors, from the changes of 
the customer’s consumption habits to providers cultural background, it is neccesary to 
consider the socio-economic factors. Therefore, the evaluation of a PSS should not just 
quantify the environmental data, like LCA, but also consider other themes. From this 
point of view, the moderately comprehensive guidelines is suitable for PSS 
environmental assessment. 
 
The last question is that “Can adopting a PSS really improve the environmental impact?” 
Through a range of research and practice, the answer is still not necessarily. Because 
there are too many elements in this complex system, as pointed out in the guidelines: 
the induced impacts, the avoided impacts,  the rebound effects and the secondary 
benefits. These inpacts are difficult to qualitatively estimate. A systematic approach 
must be used, from study goal determination, to reference system exploration, to system 
processes mapping, and then to quantitative analysis. Finally show the environmental 
figures to the intended audience and tell them whether the PSS is environmentally 
sustainable. This is the value of the guidelines.

Appendix 4 – Some common consuption factors  
 
Money: when the PSS is more or less costly than the alternative  
Time: when the PSS is more or less time consuming than the alternative  
Space: when the (elements of) the PSS takes up more or less space than the alternative  
Technology: when the PSS affects the availability of specific technologies or materials (e.g. 
when streaming services lead to people stop owning CD or DVD players)  
Skills: When the PSS requires more or less skills than the alternative.  
Information: When the PSS requires more or less information than the alternative.  
Access: When the PSS is more or less available than the alternative.  
Responsibility: When the user feels more or less responsible for the PSS than the alternative  
Convenience/comfort: When the PSS is more or less convenient or comfortable than the 
alternative  
Risk and safety: When the PSS is considered more or less risky or safe than the alternative  
Perception/image: When the PSS is considered more or less attractive than the alternative  
Source: Guidelines for evaluating the environmental performence of Product/Service-Systems. (Louise L. Kjaer et 
al., 2016)
