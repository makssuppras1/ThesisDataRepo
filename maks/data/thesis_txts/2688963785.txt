Abstract
The stability of human serum Transferrin (Tf) was investigated with isothermal chemical denat-
uration (ICD) and size exclusion chromatography - multi light angle scattering (SEC-MALS) to
characterize Tfs aggregation tendencies. In ICD it was found that Tf was the most stable when
formulated in 10 mM histidine pH 6.5, following 10 mM histidine pH 5.0, 10 mM acetate pH
5.0, and Tf formulated in 10 mM acetate pH 4.0 was the least stable. It was found through the
experiments, that stability parameters are the same within standard error, when Tf was excited
at 280 nm and 290 nm. The incubation time had a large impact on stability parameters and
after 4 hours of incubation, errors had been reduced by a minimum of 50 %. The aggregation
tendency was analysed by formulating Tf at diﬀerent concentrations (mg/ml) and comparing
the Gibbs Free energy (∆G) at each concentration. It was found that Tf in 10 mM histidine pH
6.5, 10 mM histidine pH 5.0 and 10 mM acetate pH 5.0 all stabilized the formation of native
state aggregates. Whereas Tf in 10 mM acetate pH 4.0, induced denatured state aggregates.

1
Introduction
Iron ions are essential for the human body, and normal levels of iron ions range from 50-60mg/kg
body weight[1, 2] which corresponds to 3-5 g of iron[3]. Due to the abundance of iron ions and
their ability to change between Fe+2 and Fe+3, iron ions are part of many biological processes
in living cells. The majority of iron ions are used in red blood cells for hemoglobin, which
amounts to 2/3 of the total iron in the body[3, 4]. Most of the remaining iron ions are found
in the reticuloendothelial macrophages (∼600 mg) as part of the immune system, or they are
stored in hepatocytes (∼1000 mg) in ferritin. Ferritin is a Fe3+ storage protein[3, 2]. A smaller
fraction of iron ions are bound to myoglobin in the muscles (∼300 mg), and only a minuscule
amount is in other cellular iron containing proteins and enzymes[3]. The majority of iron comes
from recycled red blood cells, but about 1-2 mg of iron is needed from the diet everyday. As
1-2 mg of the iron is lost every day due to normal sloughing of skin cells [4, 3]. The sloughing
of skin is also the only mechanism that removes iron from the body, and therefore the body
does not have a good iron regulator once the capacity is ﬁlled.[4]
Free ﬂoating iron ions in water are toxic since they can generate reactive oxygen species via the
Fenton reaction, where Fe2+ react with hydrogen peroxide and generates OH·[5]. The reactive
oxygen species readily reacts with anything, destroying any nearby tissue [6]. Therefore, iron
ions have to be bound by proteins. The protein responsible for collecting and transporting
iron around in the blood stream is human serum transferrin (Tf) and the iron storage protein
ferritin[6, 2].
1.1
Human serum transferrin
Tf is a 79 kDa protein consisting of two lobes that are connected via a linker[7]. Each lobe is
capable of binding one Fe3+. When iron binds to Tf, the lobes close around Fe3+ binding it
with a binding constant of 1020[8, 9, 3].
Plasma concentration of Tf is about 2 to 3 mg/ml, where about 30 % of Tf is saturated with
iron[7]. Tf can assume 3 forms:(i) apo-Tf - no iron bound, (ii) monoferric Tf - one iron is bound
and (iii) diferric (holo-Tf) - two iron is bound to Tf[8, 10]. When referring to these structural
forms later, apo-Tf will be the open conformation (see ﬁgure 1D), monoferric-Tf will be the
semi-open conformation or intermediate state (see ﬁgure 1C) and diferric will be the closed
conformation (see ﬁgure 1B).
1.2
Structure
Tf consists of a single polypeptide chain of 679 amino acids with two lobes referred to as the
N-lobe (residues 1-331) and the C-lobe (residues 339-679)[12]. The two lobes share an approx-
imately 40 % sequence homolgy [13].

1.3
Tf circulation in vivo
Tf is primarily produced by hepatocytes, but is also produced by the sertoli, ependymal and
oligodendroglial[7]. The iron bound to plasma Tf is 0.1 % of the total iron in the body ( 3
mg), but Tf transports between 20-30 mg of iron to the bone marrow everyday, resulting in
each Tf delivering iron on average 10 times a day to the bone marrow[3]. Tf receptors(TfR)
are responsible for extracting iron from Tf, this whole process can be seen in ﬁgure 2. At
physiological pH, TfR has an aﬃnity for holo-Tf that is 30- and 500-fold higher compared to
monoferric- and apo-Tfs, respectively[3]. TfR is located on cell membrane in proportion to cell
iron requirement, and ensure iron transport into the cell[15].
Holo-Tf and TfR create a Tf/TfR complex. This complex then undergoes endocytosis through
clathring-coated pits. The endosoe is then acidiﬁed by proton pumps ATPase to reach a pH
of 5.5, where a conformational change is triggered in Tf resulting in a release of both Fe3+[3,
10]. Throughout the release of iron from Tf, Tf remains bound to TfR, because of the acidic

There are two TfRs (TfR1 and TfR2). TfR1 is the most common type and is present in all cells,
while TfR2 is only expressed on liver cells. Both TfR1 and TfR2 are involved in regulating
hepcidin production, which is responsible for regulating iron storage. High hepcidin levels sig-
nal to import iron for storage in the liver, where low hepcidin levels signal to export iron from
the liver[3]. TfR1 and TfR2 control the hepcidin levels and thereby the iron load in the body,
through interactions with high Fe protein[3]. TfR1 interacts with high Fe protein, resulting in
the suppression of hepcidin production. When holo-Tf binds to TfR1 the interaction between
TfR and high Fe protein is broken, and the production of hepcidin is no longer suppressed[3].
Whereas, TfR2 interacts with high Fe protein, only when TfR2 is in a complex with Tf. The
Tf/TfR/high Fe Protein complex promotes production of hepcidin, thereby lowering circulat-
ing levels of iron. Furthermore, TfR1 have a 25 times higher aﬃnity for holo-Tf compared to
TfR2[3].
1.3.1
Non transferrin bound iron
In iron overload disorder such as hereditary hemochromatosis, the iron in plasma often exceeds
the carrying capacity of Tf, and gives rise to non-transferrin-bound iron (NTBI). NTBI is ef-
fectively cleared by the liver, pancreas, heart and various other organs, where it contributes
signiﬁcantly to tissue iron accumulation, and thereby increasing risk of iron-related pathol-
ogy[16]. There are two proteins that are responsible for the transport of NTBI zip8 and zip14,
and both of these transport Fe2+. Fe3+ therefore has to be reduced by plasma membrane pro-
teins before it can be transported across the cell membrane by zip8 and zip14[16].
It is also possible for holo-TF to transport iron across the cell membrane by use of the NTBI
transport system. Furthermore, this is an eﬃcient route of iron transport and requires less
energy than the endosomal incorporation of Tf/TfR complex[17]. When holo-TF binds to the
TfR1 receptor, the reduction potential of Fe3+ drops, allowing it to easier be reduced to Fe2+.
The oxidation protein STEAP2/3 on the cell membrane takes advantage of this and thus re-
duces the Fe3+ to Fe2+, which prompt the release of the iron from Tf. Zip8 and zip14 can now
transport the Fe2+ into the cell through the cell membrane.[17].

1.4
Blood Brain barrier and Tf
Millions of people worldwide suﬀer from neurological disorders, such as Alzheimer and Parkin-
son diseases. The possibility for treating these diseases are hampered greatly by the blood brain
barrier (BBB). The BBB regulates the ﬂuxes of molecules between the systemic circulation and
the brain[18].
Tf has been proposed as a possible carrier protein to transport drugs over the BBB, and also
through the use of the TfR1 receptor located on the BBB[18].
TfR1 has the ability to get Tf into the BBB[18]. This results in TfR1 becoming an attractive
target for conjugated drugs, either as Tf conjugated with a therapeutic drug or conjugated
to antibodies that have been tuned for binding to TfR1[18]. However, neither Tf nor TfR1
are transported through the BBB. As when iron is released from Tf, the Tf/TfR complex is
recycled back to the BBB surface [18]. Therefore, if conjugation with Tf is made, there is a
chance that the conjugate will not cross into the BBB[17].
For more than 25 years, studies have been carried out on the TfR1, Tf and antibodies with
aﬃnity for the TfR on the BBB. So a new method that allows for import of large drugs into the
brain could be developed[18]. The initial idea behind using the TfR1 as a gateway to the brain
arises from Tf’s presence inside the brain, which suggests that Tf can cross completely over the
BBB. However, that is where the problem arises, as mentioned above, Tf/TfR1 complex only
penetrates one side of the BBB, which can leave the drug inside the BBB, but a technique to
have it exit the BBB into the brain is still needed[18].
Studies have been carried out investigating if gold nano particles conjugated with holo-Tf could
penetrate the BBB. It was found that a small percentage of the gold nano particles crossed
BBB[18]. Furthermore, studies have also been done with antibodies that were speciﬁcally tuned
for the TfR1. These bound strongly to TfR1 away from the Tf binding site, which resulted
in the receptor becoming dysfunctional and then labeled for destruction. Thereby strongly
reducing the quantity of TfR1 receptor present on the surface of the BBB, which lowered the
brains ability to receive iron[18].
Once the BBB has been traversed, the drug will need to reach the area of disease. Under
the BBB lays a protein meshwork that nanoparticle (size of 100 nm and larger) get stuck in,
resulting in a size limit on the drug[18].
1.5
Tf as a drug
Even though Tf as a drug carrier for getting drugs over the BBB has many challenges, there
are other avenues that can be pursued, where Tf can be used as a drug and drug carrier.
First, it can be used to treat atransferrinemia, which is a rare condition, that is characterized
by anemia, iron overload, growth retardation and increased incidence of infection. By infusing
1-2 g of apo-Tf in patients every three to four months, the patient showed stabilization and
reduced symptoms of atransferrinemia[7].
Also, due to Tfs high iron uptake, it limits the bioavailability of iron for foreign pathogens, and
therefore has an immuneboosting eﬀect [3].
An other area in pharmaceutics, where targeting the TfR1 with either Tf or antibodies might

Cancer cells divide uncontrolled and thereby have a large need for nutrients, including iron.

1.6
Motivation for the project
With all these applications of Tf, a thorough study of Tf’s stability and behavior is needed
to ensure that Tf is stable to store and administer. This report will shed some light on Tf’s
stability at diﬀerent concentrations, diﬀerent pH and diﬀerent buﬀer systems.
The stability will be assessed through isothermal chemical denaturation (ICD) experiments and
supported by size exclusion chromatography coupled to multi angle light scattering detector
(SEC-MALS). ICD’s advantage is that the gibbs free energy of unfolding, ∆G can be deter-
mined. This is an indicator of protein stability. ICD can be measured at storage conditions, and
therefore experiments will give an indication of what condition the proteins should be stored
in, to have a long shelf life.
The aggregation tendencies of Tf will be investigated via the ICD experiments[19]. This is im-
portant for storage, because once a protein starts aggregating it will: 1) lose all of it’s biological
function. 2) Reduce the protein concentration, hereby losing medical eﬃcacy. 3) Aggregates
can become toxic and cause immune reactions, where the patient can develop immunity to the
drug[20]. SEC-MALS is useful for investigating the oligomeric state of Tf in solution, i.e. as
monomer, dimer, larger oligomers or aggregates.

2.1
Stability
When considering the stability of proteins there are generally two types: (1) thermodynamic
stability, usually characterized by the Gibbs free energy (∆G) which relates to how favorable
it is for the protein to be folded and (2) kinetic stability related to how fast a protein unfolds
once it is thermodynamic unstable, thereby the size of the free-energy barrier (∆G̸ =)[21].
The thermodynamic stability expressed through ∆G is a measurement of the equilibrium be-
tween 2 forms. The simplest form of chemical denaturation is the direct unfolding, where the
protein unfolds directly from native (folded) form to the denatured (unfolded) form[22, 23].

protein is boiled, just like boiling an egg. There, the egg white will not transform by cooling.
However, chemical denaturation is a reversible process, and therefore allows determination of
∆Go. Reversibility can be tested by diluting the already unfolded protein sample in a denatu-
rant solution with buﬀer, and look at the changes in ﬂuorescence[25].

unfold at the same concentration of denaturant[26]. When either of these situations arise, the
stability parameters get aﬀected. In case (i) the reported ∆Gtotal will be reduced, and in case
(ii) the reported ∆Gtotal will be higher than the ∆G reported for each isolated domain in the
protein[26].
2.2
Chemical denaturation
In chemical denaturation, the protein is unfolded with chemicals (choatropes/denaturants)[25,
27] that bind to the protein backbones. The most commonly used denaturants are urea or
guanidine hydrochloride (GuHCl), but other denaturants such as guanidine thiocyanate and
thiourea can also be used[22]. Urea and thiourea are non-ionic, while GuHCl and guanidine
thiocyanate are ionic. In literature it is often assumed that urea and GuHCl result in the
same ∆G[27], but it has been shown that GuHCl aﬀects electrostatic interactions in solution
and, therefore, has an inﬂuence on the calculated ∆G[28]. Four diﬀerent model proteins were
investigated to test GuHCl and urea’s interactions with the electrostatic interactions. GuHCl
resulted in the same ∆G for all four model proteins, where urea resulted in ∆G depending on
the amount of repulsive and attractive interactions[28].
As urea is not a strong denaturant, it is often required to use GuHCl to denature the protein.
Another option would be to use thiourea, but since thiourea is not UV transparent, its presence
will interfere with ﬂuorescence measurements, limiting its use in regular chemical denaturation
experiment. Thiourea can however be used in chemical denaturation experiments measured
through NMR[22].
The most common probe for chemical denaturation experiments is to monitor the change in
ﬂuorescence of tryptophan by exciting it 280 nm to 295 nm and then monitoring the absorbance
at 2 diﬀerent wave lengths between 300 nm to 500 nm[29, 25]. In our experiments excitation
was done at 280 nm and 290 nm, while absorbance for both of these was measured at 330
nm and 350 nm which corresponds to maximum absorbance of folded and unfolded protein,
respectively for tryptophan[30]. Excitation at 290 nm were done to see if noise from tyrosine
impacts the denaturation curves, because at 290 nm only tryptophan is an active ﬂuorophore[31,
32]. Unfolding curves were analysed as the ratio 350/330 [33, 23, 22, 25] (below the 350/330

2.3
Size-Exclusion Chromatography with Multi-angle Light Scatter-
ing
Liquid Chromatography is a widely used technique in identiﬁcation and separation of molecules.
Size-exclusion Chromatography (SEC) is one of 5 techniques used in liquid chromatography,
normally referred to as High Pressure Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)[37].
In HPLC systems, separation is done by having a stationary phase and a mobile phase, where
the stationary phase consists of a material that is capable of interacting with the sample either
chemically or physically. The mobile phase is a solution that helps to separate the compounds
from the original sample. The stationary phase is typically retained inside of a column and
the sample runs through the column and then elutes at the other side, separated into fractions
each ideally containing a single protein[37].
Apart from the HPLC equipment’s ability to generate pressures up to 350 bar, it also contains
17
==PAGE:19==
Nikolaj V. Eliesen
April 16, 2021
an UV-Vis detector, which is located after the column. This allows the HPLC instrument
to measure the quantity and time of eluting protein, resulting in a chromatogram[38, 37]. A
peak will be shown each time a protein elutes from the column in the chromatogram. The
chromatogram can then be analysed to quantify how well each protein was separate from other
proteins in the column.
The resolution of the chromatogram worsens the more the peaks
overlap[37].
In SEC, the stationary phase is a porous support that is completely inert towards the sample
and solution. It is these pores in the stationary phase that separate the sample based on size.
The pore size can be controlled, and if the hydrodynamic radius of the protein is smaller than
the pore size, the protein will disperse into the pores. This lengthen the route for the proteins
before they elute out of the column[37, 38]. It is also possible estimate the molar mass of a
protein with SEC by using standards of known protein with a certain molar mass and thus
making a relative scale of elution time vs molar mass on a log scale. However, this only works
for globular proteins, and linear molecules like DNA or denatured proteins will have a much
larger hydrodynamic volume mass ratio compared to globular proteins[37, 38].
In order to determine precise M w of the protein, SEC can be coupled to a Multi Angle Light
Scattering (MALS) detector. MALS allows calculation of the absolute molar mass. In equation
25, it is shown how to calculate the molar mass:

molar mass and Rg of each of these species. It is of great importance that each protein species
is well separated from the other protein species. Otherwise, the weighted average molar mass
will be determined, and not the true molar mass of each of the species that eluted at the same
time[39, 38].

6.1
Unfolding pathway
The shape of the unfolding curves diﬀers for Tf in diﬀerent conditions (see ﬁgure 20).
In
histidine buﬀer at pH 6.5, Tf undergoes a 2-state unfolding process, where Tf unfolds directly
from native to unfolded state without an intermediate state.
A
B
Figure 20: Unfolding curves for Tf in diﬀerent formulations after 4 hours of incubation for 10
mM acetate pH 4.0 (blue), 10 mM acetate pH 5.0 (yellow) and 10 mM histidine pH 5.0 (green)
and 24 hours of incubation for 10 mM histidine pH 6.5 (red). All data is from experiments
with excitation at 280 nm. The concentration of Tf in (A) cTF = 0.4 mg/ml and (B) cTF =

At pH 5.0 and 4.0 all three of the curves indicate that Tf goes through a 3-state unfolding
process, which indicates the presence of an intermediate state, where Tf is partially unfolded.
(see ﬁgure 20 and 21). According to the SAXS studies performed by Kulakova et.al. [34], the
conformation of Tf changes at diﬀerent pH. At pH 6.5, both the C- and N-lobe exist in the
closed conformation[34]. Furthermore, their sequence homology is around 40 %, and they are
nearly superimposable[13], resulting in the lobes unfolding almost simultaneously and therefore
only 1 transition is observed (a schematic of this concept can be seen in ﬁgure 22).
When pH is lowered from 6.5 to 5.0, the N-lobe opens[9], and its stability is lowered when
compared to the C-lobe, which remains close at this pH[9]. Therefore, during chemical unfold-
ing, the N-lobe unfolds ﬁrst, forming a partially unfolded intermediate state, where you could
imagine that the N-lobe is fully unfolded and the C-lobe is fully closed (As shown in ﬁgure 21).
When the pH is then further lowered to 4.0, Tf continues to unfold as a 3-state system, but
has signiﬁcantly lower stability, when compared to pH 5.0. This happens due to more dras-
tic conformational changes: In addition to 50-60 % of Tf in partially open conformation[34],
around 15 % of Tf is also present in the fully open conformation[34]. With both lobes open, Tf
becomes even less stable and, therefore, it is unfolded by much lower concentrations of GuHCl.
At pH 4.0 unfolding with urea were needed to get meaningful data.

Overview of ∆G data
The overview of the obtained ∆G values is shown in table 5 along with a 3-state approach for

stable in histidine than in acetate buﬀer, even though they are mostly within the statistical
uncertainties of each other.
The histidine buﬀer is aﬀected more by changes in concentration than the acetate buﬀer, and
the acetate buﬀer seems to overall stabilize an increase in concentration of Tf. Therefore, at
higher concentrations the acetate buﬀer might better stabilize Tf than histidine. At a concen-
tration of 10 mg/ml Tf in SAXS experiments by Kulakova et.al[34], the author found that 30
% of Tf was in the partially open conformation and 50 % of Tf was in the closed conformation
for Tf in acetate pH 5.0. In 10 mM histidine pH 5.0 it was 50 % of Tf in the partially open
conformation and 30 % of Tf in the closed conformation[34]. This ties into the assumption
that the closed conformation is more structurally stable than open conformation. Furthermore,
acetate is capable of reacting with Fe3+ to form insoluble Fe(III) acetate, removing iron from
the solution once Tf opens and releases the iron.

compared to other formulation conditions. This means that Tf is more stable at pH 4.0, which
is not in agreement with previous conclusions[34]. One possible explanation for the high ∆G

Aggregation tendencies
Figure 24: Changes in ∆Gtotal with cTF. Tf formulated in 10 mM histidine pH 6.5 and incubated

In order to characterize the Tf aggregation proﬁle, ∆G trend experiments were performed[36,
19]. When ∆G increases with cTF (∆G ↑cTF ↑) it is an indication of native state aggregation,
while if ∆G decreases with cTF (∆G ↓cTF ↑), it points to aggregation in denatured state[36].
A schematic representation of this can be seen on ﬁgure 6.
10 mM acetate pH 4.0 shows a decrease in ∆G with increasing cTF (shown in blue in ﬁgure 24),
meaning that Tf form aggregates in the denatured state. In both acetate and histidine pH 5.0,
∆Gtotal is increasing with increasing cTF (shown in violet and green in ﬁgure 24) pointing to
that the native state tends to form aggregates. The same is observed for Tf in 10 mM histidine
pH 6.5 (shown in yellow in ﬁgure 24), where ∆G increases with increasing cTF, suggesting
formation of aggregates in the native state. This points to the fact that Tf at pH 4.0 is fully
open and therefore tends to unfold easily and to aggregate from there.
Kulakova et.al.[34] performed SEC-MALS experiments on Tf at pH 6.5 and saw the presence
of 12% dimer and 2% trimer, supporting the notion that the native state forms aggregates at
pH 6.5 [34].

6.4
Challenges in ICD data analysis
ICD data analysis presents a few challenges. First and foremost, it is important to have well
deﬁned denaturation curves, with enough points in the transition interval, in order to get a
good ﬁt for the sigmoidal curve and obtain c1/2 and m-value. The reason for noisy data mainly
lies in pipetting, and the use of pipetting robots, such as the Unchained Labs HUNK, reduce
this issue since the samples are prepared automatically.[25]
Calculating ∆G also requires the protein to unfold reversible, this can often not be achieved in
thermal denaturation, but in chemical denaturation it can be achieved by removing the denat-
urant again by dilutions [25]. However, the author did not test if Tf would actually refold by
diluting the sample after denaturation, as this had already been shown by the Kulakova et.al.
[34]
Incubation time can drastically impact the transition interval, and therefore impacts c1/2, m-
value and ∆G as seen previously, because the protein ﬁnds a new equilibrium between folded
and unfolded state.[33]
The choice of denaturant can also aﬀect ∆G, as is was shown with urea and GuHCl. Therefore
it is often advised that ∆G should be calculated through another denaturation technique, for
examples diﬀerential scanning calorimetry or high hydrostatic pressure unfolding[24].
Moreover, multidomain proteins or proteins in diﬀerent oligomeric states have additional data
analysis challenges. Presence of diﬀerent oligomers or diﬀerent stability of individual domains
can lead to a multi-state unfolding with poorly deﬁned transitions. In Batey et.al. [26] the
authors review a number of behaviors that the protein can show during denaturation. The best
way to single out these incidents is to analyse each domain independently, by recombinantly
producing each domain separately[26]. This may be possible for Tf since we have shown that
each domain in Tf unfolds under diﬀerent concentrations of denaturant at pH 5.0 and 4.0,
showing that the isolated domains are stable.

7
Conclusion
It was found that analysing data excited at 280 nm and 290 nm yield the same results in terms
of ∆G, c1/2 and m-values. Furthermore, It was found that incubation time strongly aﬀected
stability parameters and that it greatly reduced standard error, by allowing Tf to incubate for
4 hours after mixing.
Two diﬀerent ﬁtting programs were used in this report, and PyFolding[40] proved to be ﬂexible
and improve analysing speed and allowed for much greater automation.
The analysis of Tf’s stability showed that Tf unfolded without an intermediate state at pH 6.5,
and at lower pH Tf unfolded with an intermediate state. It was diﬃcult to compare ∆G values
between pH, as pH 4.0 had the largest ∆G and pH 6.5 had the lowest ∆G. However, from c1/2
it was found that Tf is the most stable at pH 6.5, followed by pH 5.0 and ﬁnally in pH 4.0
being the least stable.
Within each condition it was found that Tf formulated in 10 mM acetate pH 4.0 had a tendency
to form denatured state aggregates, where at pH 5.0 and 6.5 it was found that Tf forms native
state aggregates.
