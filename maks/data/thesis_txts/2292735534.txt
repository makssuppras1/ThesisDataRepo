Abstract
The project is motivated by the increased interest and importance of computed tomography
(CT) as a measuring technique in industry over the last years. There are several advantages of
CT compared to coordinate measuring machines (CMMs). For example, it is a non-touching
and non-destructive measuring method, which can be used to investigate inner surfaces.
The major beneﬁt of CT to the traditional CMM inspection methods is its capability to
measure multi-material assemblies in the assembled state.
However, this can sometimes
prove to be complicated, since it might be diﬃcult to ﬁnd an adequate threshold, due to
the increased image noise and scattering artifacts, which have a negative inﬂuence on the
measuring procedure.
The present thesis contains an investigation related to optimization of scanning parameters
on CT measurements of calibrated multi-material assemblies. This study is based on analysis
of the eﬀect of diﬀerent scanning parameters and post-processing workﬂows on CT measure-
ments in terms of systematic errors and deviations. The experimental work was carried out
at DTU using a polymer assembly with simple geometries. The assembly, composed from a
female and male part, was scanned ﬁrst using four varying scanning parameters – 65, 70, 80
and 90 kV. The obtained 3D X-ray image was post-processed with ﬁve diﬀerent approaches.
After the preliminary work the assembly was re-scanned in another conﬁguration using two
best scanning parameters.
Measurement uncertainty was used as a parameter for quantiﬁcation of the selected inﬂu-
encing parameters and approaches. The diameter and roundness expanded uncertainties for
the female part lied in the range of 13-22 and 12-28 µm, respectively. For the male part, the
diameter and roundness yielded expanded uncertainties in the range of 15-22 and 12-28 µm,
respectively.
In addition, the best scanning parameters were used for the tolerance veriﬁcation of an
industrial part. It was found that CT suits well for diameter veriﬁcation on assemblies, but
there are still some problems related to undetectable systematic errors and deviations with
length and roundness veriﬁcation.

1
Introduction
This chapter gives an introduction to X-ray Computed Tomography (CT), a technique to
produce 3D picture of a scanned object with external and internal structures. Further, an
explanation of the problem and thesis structure are presented.
1.1
Application of computed tomography
In the modern industrial world, X-ray Computed Tomography ﬁnds application in three
major ﬁelds:
• CT was ﬁrst mostly used for medical purposes. Clinical CT was developed for imaging
by Hounsﬁeld in 1969.
• CT systems for material analysis become popular in the 1980’s.
The devices and
procedures for this non-destructive testing of objects diﬀer signiﬁcantly from the ones
used for medical imaging of patients.
• Most recently, CT scanners have made a breakthrough in dimensional metrology. This
CT application ﬁeld is considered as a third revolutionary development in coordinate
metrology, following the tactile coordinate measuring machines (CMMs) and optical
3D scanners [1,2].
As a result of more complex design, higher functional integration and new manufacturing
technologies, such as 3D printing, the part complexity increases and that is why more and
more eﬀort is put into CT dimensional metrology research and development.
It is the
only technology for industrial quality control and analysis of complex internal and external
geometries of components without the need of cutting and therefore, destroying the part
(non-destructive testing, NDT) [1].
Moreover, small parts of 5 mm and smaller can be measured with CT, while with traditional
coordinate measuring machines the measurements are time consuming, diﬃcult and in some
cases impossible. Another advantage of CT is that deformable objects, which are sensitive
to the touching force of the tactile methods, can be measured. While with optical CMMs,
the touching force problem is removed and problems from the reﬂective or transparent com-
ponents arises instead [9]. Using not-reﬂective sprays might help, but then the object gets
contaminated.
CT allows to measure components in the assembled state. This is important because the
dimensions of the component may vary in the assembled and non-assembled state [5]. Espe-
cially in case of interference ﬁt of materials with low Youngs’s modulus. On the other hand,
the measuring uncertainty of the CT technique is still quite high and adjustments are re-
quired to improve the accuracy. In addition, there are problems with scanning multi-material
objects [3,4].
1.2
Problem of the thesis
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the accuracy of computed tomography for multi-
material assemblies. A speciﬁc focus is set on understanding the inﬂuence of diﬀerent setup
parameters during the scanning and the post-processing workﬂow on the measurement re-
sults, bias and uncertainties, which give the same metrological characteristics as accuracy,
but with direct indication. During the post-processing, it is investigated if the deviation
7
==PAGE:8==
between CT and reference measurements can be enhanced by removing the noise errors by
doing surface determination of the assembly before ﬁltering or vice versa.
1.3
Structure of the thesis
After the introduction, a literature review about industrial CT is given in chapter 2. Chapter
2 presents the hardware of CT e.g. X-ray source and detectors and their inﬂuence on the
quality of the images. Furthermore, a sub-chapter Software gives a description of some of
the error sources, like beam hardening, in CT scanning and how they can be corrected with
the right settings in the reconstruction software or image analysis programs. This chapter
also describes the process chain for dimensional measurements with CT.
Chapter 3 discusses traceability of CT and presents the common inﬂuence factors, which are
divided into ﬁve groups. Four most relevant inﬂuence factors for the experimental part of
this thesis are described in more detail.
Chapter 4 presents the experimental part of this thesis, which is subdivided into three parts:
• Measurements on a totally assembled cylindrical multi-material assembly.
• Validation of the measurements results on the cylindrical multi-material object using
diﬀerent assembly conﬁguration.
• Tolerance veriﬁcation of industrial component. All of the sections investigate the inﬂu-
ence of diﬀerent voltage values and measuring strategy. The uncertainties estimated for
CMM and CT measurements based on ISO 14253-2 are also presented in this chapter.

2
Industrial CT
This chapter describes the basic principles of computed tomography and the technical sys-
tems and components, e.g. hardware and software, which have an inﬂuence on the resolution
of CT images and accuracy of CT measurements. In addition, a general process chain for
dimensional measurement with CT is given.
2.1
General overview of industrial computed tomography
X-ray CT has been used in medical routine from the seventies, while the industrial 3D
computed tomography for the non-destructive testing became common in the last decade.
In the clinical CT scanner, X-ray source is rotated around patient, while the object or patient
is laying still. In comparison with industrial CT scanners, the X-ray source is ﬁxed and the
part attached to the rotary plate is moving. In addition, the rotational axis in industrial CT
scanners is vertical rather than horizontal.
Additionally, the material types are diﬀerent. While tissue, blood and bone are the main
materials investigated in medical CT, the polymers, wood, concrete, ceramics, metals and
composites are scanned with industrial CT.
Furthermore, to obtain a 3D image of the scanned body, the current and voltage applied to
the electron beam that hits the target and generates the X-ray, has to be limited. This is
to protect the patient and also because the requirements on the accuracy are much lower
compared to the demands applied to technical CT scanners [14].
The principle of common industrial CT scanner is shown on Figure 1. The source generates
X-rays, which propagate through the specimen placed on a rotary plate. At each angular
position of 360 degree turn a penetration image is recorded by the detector. The greyvalue
images show the attenuation of the X-rays. The X-rays are attenuated due to absorption or
scattering in the material [9]. These recorded multiple views are reconstructed by a powerful
data processing unit connected to the CT system to obtain a 3D model that can later be
quantitatively analysed.

2.2
Hardware
2.2.1
X-ray source
An X-ray source shown on Figure 1 consists of an anode and a cathode located inside a
vacuum tube. When the cathode ﬁlament is heated, the electrons from the ﬁlament surface
are being ejected and accelerated towards the anode due to the electrical potential between
the anode and cathode. When these electrons hit the target (e.g. tungsten target), they
interact with the present atoms and transfer their kinetic energy to the anode. As these
interactions take place, the electrons are slowed down and therefore, they can be conducted
through the anode assembly out into the associated electronic circuitry. The area, where the
electrons are directed on the anode is called focal spot. The electron is being combined with
either the orbital electrons or the nuclei of the metal atoms. Thanks to these interactions
the kinetic energy is converted into thermal energy and electromagnetic energy in the form
of X-rays [6].
Most of the of the industrial CT systems use electron guns, although radiation sources are
also linear accelerators and synchrotrons [7]. Many factors inﬂuence the selection of a CT
scanner for a certain task. First of all, the focal spot size sets the limits to the object size
to be characterized. Focal spot size is the segment over the focal spot of an X-ray tube,
measured parallel to the plane of the detector [8]. Moreover, the image quality is dependant
from the focal spot size as well. For example, the 225 kV micro-focus source has a focal spot
size with a diameter of 5-300 µm. With this setup, relatively small specimens with diameter
values down to 5.0 mm can be characterised, reaching resolution values up to 5 µm. On the
other hand, the 450 kV macro-focus sources have a focal spot size of 2.5 mm and this can be
used for imaging parts with higher densities and dimensions, but the disadvantage is that
the spatial resolution with the macro-focus setup is only 150 µm [9].
Secondly, the voltage applied to the electron beam that generates the X-rays deﬁne the
penetration power of the X-rays. Furthermore, the attenuation in materials with diﬀerent
densities and geometries also depends on the choice of the proper voltage and current settings.
Higher voltage values result in increased photon energies, which penetrate more eﬀectively
than the low-energy ones. The intensity (amount of radiation) of the X-rays is inﬂuenced
by the current applied to the electron beam. Higher intensity values usually lead to better
image clarity [1, 2]. In the following Table 1 some examples of diﬀerent CT-systems, like
micro- and sub-micro X-ray CT, and their properties are brought out.

Another important characteristic, which has an inﬂuence on the X-ray spectrum, is the target
material. The atomic number (Z) of the target material (i.e. Tungsten Z = 74) inﬂuences
the the quality and quantity of the X-rays produced.
Increasing the atomic number of
the target will change the intensity of the continuous spectrum and also the quality of the
radiation. Figure 2 shows the spectrum of target material with a low atomic number and
high atomic number, respectively. The target can be classiﬁed as a reﬂective or transmitting
target. The diﬀerence of the direct emitter (reﬂective target) or transmission targets is in
the available radiation power. Transmitting targets are used in low power CT sources, while
reﬂective targets are used in high power CT sources. The target type and material aﬀects
the measurement time and the minimum focal spot size [5,11].

2.2.2
X-ray attenuation
As mentioned before, the X-rays are attenuated due to absorption and scattering. The atten-
uation of electromagnetic radiation through a homogeneous medium is deﬁned by Lambert-
Beer’s law:
I = I0 ⋅e−µ⋅x
(1)
11
==PAGE:12==
Where, I0 and I are the initial and ﬁnal X-ray intensity, respectively. x is the traveling
distance in an absorbing medium. µ is the attenuation coeﬃcient of the material.
The
attenuation coeﬃcient describes the fraction of a beam of X-rays that is absorbed or scattered
per unit thickness of the material.
If a non-homogeneous object is scanned, then the above equation becomes:
I = I0 ⋅e−R µ(x) dx
(2)
The line integral is taken along the direction of propagation and µ(x) is the linear attenuation
coeﬃcient at each point on the ray path [4].
The way X-rays are attenuated are described mainly with two mechanisms: photoelectric
eﬀect and Compton (inelastic) scattering. Both of the eﬀects are energy dependant. The
photoelectric absorption occurs when the total energy of an incoming X-ray photon is trans-
ferred to an inner electron, which is then ejected. In Compton scattering, the incoming
photon interacts with the free electron in an outer cell. Compton scattering is an ionizing
event, where the free electron is scattered and gains some energy from the scattered photon.
The practical importance of the diﬀerentiation of these two dominant physical processes is
that photoelectric eﬀect is proportional to Z4−5, where Z is the atomic number of an atom
in the scanned material. At the same time, Compton scattering is proportional only to
Z. Therefore, low-energy X-rays are more aﬀected by the change in the composition than
higher-energy ones [10].
2.2.3
X-ray detection
After some of the X-rays have been scattered, absorbed or transmitted, an X-ray detector
is used to capture the photons and create a 2D grey image. The X-ray sensors are available
as line detectors and area (panel) detectors. The line detectors are used in 2D-CT systems
having a fan beam source (Figure 3a). The line sensors yield a higher accuracy due to the
perpendicular synchronized movement of the X-ray source and the line detector relative to
the rotation axis of the object. This enables that the section plane created by collimated
x-rays through the workpiece is perpendicular. In addition, there is less pixel interaction and
scatter. On the other hand, this system has a long scanning time as only one slice is captured
during one rotation of the specimen. For example, the measurement of a workpiece with 100
mm height and interslice distance of 100 µm, results in 1000 lines which requires 1000 times
the measurement time compared to using an area sensor (if all the other parameters are the
same).
12
==PAGE:13==
(a) Fan beam CT using a line sensor.
(b) Cone beam CT using a ﬂat area de-
tector.
Figure 3: CT scan geometries [4].
The scanning times are shorter with 3D-CT systems, which consist of a ﬂat area detector
and a cone beam source that are shown on Figure 3b. With this system several section
planes can be captured at once. However, other problems arise. Since the cone beam has to
be used instead of fan beam, a wider angle of material is exposed to the X-rays, which could
produce even more scattered radiation than with fan-beam scanner and thereby worsen the
image quality [5, 23]. But the main disadvantage is that the reproduced specimen section
planes, except the central slice, are not perpendicular to the rotation axis. This results in
incomplete data of the top and bottom regions of the specimen. The scattered radiation
increases the unwanted signals and therefore, rises the formation of artifacts. Artifacts are
artiﬁcial structures in the scan result, which do not represent the reality. The measurement
errors during the reconstruction of the 3D data from the 2D images must be corrected [9,11].
The diﬀerent types of artifacts are described in section 2.4.
Another parameter that inﬂuences the necessary measuring time, is the number of projec-
tions. On the other hand, the higher the number of projections, the better is the quality of
the resulting volumetric model and the measurement accuracy as well. The total time to
perform a CT scan also depends on the detector exposure (or integration) time. By increas-
ing the detector exposure time also the total scanning time increases, but at the same time,
the image noise is reduced. The number of remaining X-ray photons that reach the a single
detector element raises with the increase of integration time [14].
2.3
Software
On the Figure 4 a schematic presentation of typical CT workﬂow including scanning the
object, reconstructing the 2D projection images to a volumetric model and analysis with a
post-processing program is given.
13
==PAGE:14==
Figure 4: A typical industrial computer tomography process chain.
Software plays an important role to achieve good quality reconstructed volume models, which
are used to analyse parameters such as diameter, roundness and orientation for each feature
(for example, a cavity, pore, grain, or cell) inside the specimen being scanned. Reconstruction
is done by a computer cluster connected to the CT system. Together with cone beam source
the algorithm according to Feldkamp, Davis and Kress is used. The algorithm takes into
account the fact, that voxels (see Figure 5) do not stay in the same horizontal projection plane
while the specimen rotates [5]. In order to increase the 2D image quality and thus, the quality
of resulting volumetric model (made of voxels), the detected grey value proﬁles are ﬁltered
before the reconstruction.
That is why this method is called ”ﬁltered back projection”.
The volume is modelled as 3D matrix of voxels. The ﬁlters applied to the projections can
be used to remove some of the artifacts, such as beam hardening and scattered radiation
(see Sections 2.4.1 and 2.4.2). Other post-processing steps that inﬂuence the quality of the
measuring results are surface determination (Section 2.3.1) and ﬁltering (Section 2.3.2) done
on the reconstructed volume.

2.3.1
Surface determination
After the 3D reconstruction with a certain software (usually provided by the CT scanner
producer), the volume is opened in additional speciﬁc software for image analysis.
The
subsequent procedure after 3D reconstruction is the surface detection or segmentation de-
termining the boundaries of the solid material and surrounding air or the interface between
two diﬀerent solid materials. The surface determination converts 3D voxel data into 3D
surface data [5]. The quality of the dimensional measurement results depends strongly of
the phase segmentation technique selected.
The most common method to identify a threshold gray value is the ”iso-50%” value. This
parameter is obtained from the gray value histogram where the y-axis shows the number of
voxels and x-axis the gray values. Figure 6 shows a gray value histogram for a multi-material
object scan (two bodies from diﬀerent materials that are assembled, the two peaks on the
right). Iso-50% takes the middle of the peaks as a threshold value and this usually results in
rather weak surface to use for accurate measurements, especially for multi-material objects.
This is because the global threshold method creates an ”imaginary” layer of less attenuating
material around the edge of more attenuating material [2].
In order to obtain more accurate material edge, a local adaptive thresholding is used. This
method starts form rough global contour determination and searches for the sharpest gray
value change in its neighbourhood. However, the local adaptive thresholding also has some
drawbacks. For example, when the assembly parts are made of similar material, their peaks
or tails in the gray value histogram merge with each other.

face is determined between background and one material (polyoxymethylene – POM). If the global
threshold would be used, the second material (polypropylene – peak in the middle) would create an

In the comparison of diﬀerent surface detection methods to evaluate cone beam computed

segmentation can be done by using a CAD model with the component structures as basis [5].

2.3.2
Data ﬁltering
In order to analyse the grayscale volume or ROI, the image noise has to be removed. The
noise voxels can have the same gray values as the surface under investigation and thus, it is
diﬃcult to remove the noise during phase segmentation. Excessive image noise in the source
images will signiﬁcantly limit the quality and utility of 3D rendered images particularly for
low resolution images, making it diﬃcult to distinguish between two surfaces [16]. In order
to suppress the unwanted signal while preserving the useful signal, ﬁlters are applied to the
voxel data or to the already segmented phase to suppress the unwanted signals [18].
All commercial software include several ﬁlters that can be easily applied to reduce the noise
of the images. There are ﬁlters which are present but can not be modiﬁed by the user.
But more interesting are the ﬁlters that can be changed by the user. For example, the 2D
median ﬁlter that can be applied to the projection image, ﬁlters working on the reconstructed
volume data e.g. Gaussian ﬁlter or 3D median ﬁlter, ﬁlters during thresholding process, and
statistical outlier ﬁlter for ﬁtting of a cylinder to surface data points.
In general, the ﬁlters can be divided into two categories – low-pass ﬁlters and edge-preserving
ﬁlters. The low-pass ﬁlters are also called linear ﬁlters and they are well understood and fast
to compute. The example is Gaussian Kernel Smoothing, which will create satisfactorily
smoothed homogeneous areas.
However, this will lead to more diﬃcult edge detection,
rounded corners, blurred crinkles, and extended edge regions due to the blurred gray value
edges. Therefore, these kind of ﬁlters are not good for multi-material assemblies, where two
or more bodies are to be segmented.
The edge-preserving ﬁlters are better for feature extraction. One can use non-linear algo-
rithms, such as the Median Filter. The median ﬁlter replaces each pixel by the mean of pixel
values in the cube centered around that pixel. The median ﬁlters can have diﬀerent sizes.
Larger cubes with size 9x9x9 produce more smoothing, and small features might be lost, but
not to the same extent as with the linear ﬁlters.
The edge-preserving ﬁlters are not additive: repeated application of a median ﬁlter is not
equivalent to a single application of a median ﬁltering using a diﬀerent kernel size. Kernel is
also referred to as ﬁlter or algorithm by some vendors. Applying non-linear smoothing ﬁlter
with e.g. kernel size 3x3x3 several times, has sometimes more eﬀect on noise reduction while
keeping the information about small features, than using a large kernel size. Figures 7b and
7d show the results after 5 iterations of the median ﬁlters corresponding to single 3x3 and
5x5 size ﬁltering inﬂuence on Figures 7a and 7c, respectively.

Basically, each repetition produces less change in the image than the previous one until, after

with kernel size above 3 showed negative results on the dimensions of the measurements.
While the 2D ﬁlters were applied to the projections, the 3D median ﬁlters were run on the
CT volume.

2.4
Image artifacts
One of the most critical problems that industrial CT is facing, are the artifacts and their
eﬀect on the quantitative analysis. Scanning artifacts can complicate the investigation of
features in interest or cause the CT value of a single material to vary in diﬀerent sections of
an image, not to mention the problems that arise with multi-material assemblies. The most
inﬂuencing parameters to the propagation of the unwanted eﬀects are: object’s geometry,
penetration lengths, positioning of the specimen on the rotary table, the setup parameters,
and the material of the specimen [9]. The description of most common types of artifacts is
given in the following subsections.
2.4.1
Beam hardening
The most common artifact in CT scanning is beam hardening, which causes the edges of a
specimen appear brighter than the core even though the material is the same throughout.
The phenomena derives its name from the fact that when the polychromatic radiation moves
through the object, low energy photons are absorbed or scattered, raising the mean X-ray
energy or ”hardening” the beam even though the overall intensity decreases. As a result,
interior parts are penetrated by higher energy X-rays than the outer skin of the workpiece
and this makes the edges more attenuating than the core areas.
For objects with circular cross-section this artifact appears clearly with brighter outer skin
of the workpiece and darker core and background. However, for asymmetrical objects it is
more diﬃcult to make diﬀerence between the beam hardening artifact and actual material
variation [5, 10]. Therefore, the threshold determination and measurements of density and
resolution become more complicated, which might result in higher measurement errors if no
beam hardening correction is applied.
There are many methods to address the beam hardening artifact. The most common ap-
proach is to use pre-ﬁltering by using an aluminium or copper plate between the source and
workpiece to remove the low energy X-rays and run the CT scan with high-energy X-rays.
The Figure 8 shows how Cu or Al plate between the source and workpiece ﬁlters out the low
energy X-Rays [5]. Pre-ﬁltering diminishes the artifacts, but on the other hand, to achieve
good quality images the scanning time has to be increased since the high energy X-rays are
also reduced [21]. Another method is to use a correction during the image reconstruction.
18
==PAGE:19==
Figure 8: Spectra for 50 kV source with and without ﬁlter [5].
The basic idea to correct the beam hardening eﬀect is to use a linearisation technique.
A relation between a propagated path length within the scanned object and a resulting
weakened intensity is estimated. The obtained characteristic line can be used to calculate
beam hardening corrected intensity values, which allow to reconstruct an artifact free CT
image. The line can be deﬁned by performing reference measurements using reference objects
or it can be found out from the reconstructed CT image itself. The second method has the
advantage that additional reference measurements are not needed and it has shown good
results for most of homogeneous specimens [22].
For the correction during the reconstruction, a software tool Iterative Artifact Reduction
(IAR) exists [23]. As the name suggests, it is an iterative process, where several modules for
homogeneous and heterogeneous objects are applied to the reconstructed volume in order to
calculate the beam hardening correction separately of any reference object [4].
2.4.2
Scattered radiation
Scattered radiation occurs because of a photoelectric eﬀect in the scanned object when the
energy of the incoming X-ray photon is transferred to an electron and afterwards ejected and
scattered [24]. A schematic illustration of a CT setup and the scattering from the object to
the surrounding and to the detector is shown on Figure 9.
Scattered radiation is the main source of image quality worsening in industrial X-ray cone-
beam computed tomography, which leads to the formation of scatter artifacts in the recon-
structed 3D volume. These artifacts include streaks between contrast details and general
loss of contrast. They impede the qualitative and quantitative analysis of the reconstructed
voxel models. In addition, the dimensional measurements are hindered by scatter, since it
blurs sharp edges and renders ﬂat surfaces curved in the surface determination step [25].
19
==PAGE:20==
Figure 9: Schematic illustration of a cone-beam CT setup, where incident primary radiation (green)
is scattered by the sample. A part of this scattered radiation (red) reaches also the detector, where
it gives rise to scatter signals [25].
Although diﬀerent in its physical origin, the appearance of scatter artifacts on the detected
images is similar to artifacts caused by beam-hardening. For example, halo without a ded-
icated structure at the location of the workpiece. Scattering artifacts can be reduced by
using methods and techniques such as Monte Carlo, anti-scatter grids and slit scans. It was
concluded in [26] that the Monte Carlo based method and the anti-scatter grid show the
best results in scatter reduction and their practical usage. The scattering eﬀect can also be
minimized by proper thresholding [5].
2.5
Magniﬁcation
In the previous sections, several parameters were mentioned which need to be taken into
account in order to obtain CT images with good resolution. For example, energy of the
source determined by the current and voltage, detector integration time, post-processing
with the software to remove the image artifacts such as beam hardening and scatter artifacts
and in addition, to do the segmentation and surface determination to be able to perform
dimensional measurements of the scanned part.
Another important parameter that inﬂuences the accuracy of CT measurements, is the
geometrical magniﬁcation determined by the position of the specimen with respect to the
X-ray source and detector. Higher magniﬁcation is achieved by placing the object under
investigation closer to the X-ray source. With higher magniﬁcation a smaller voxel size is
obtained and thus, a better resolution of the CT scan is achieved. Although with a higher
magniﬁcation better resolution is achieved, the cone beam angle is higher closer to the source
and this increases the measurement errors, since there are more slices detected that are not
perpendicular to the rotary axis. The geometrical magniﬁcation m is calculated as the ratio

• Secondly, the set of obtained 2D projections is reconstructed into 3D volume by a

attenuation coeﬃcient of the scanned specimen i.e.
to each voxel a gray value is

During the reconstruction,
some correction methods can be applied on the 2D projections in order to minimize

• Last but not least, measurement results are obtained and the error analysis is done

3.1
Traceability
Metrological performance veriﬁcation of CT systems is needed to ensure accuracy of measure-
ments and comparability with other measuring systems. Similarly to coordinate measuring
machines (CMMs), CT systems must be regularly tested to establish measurements with
suﬃcient quality [39]. Measurements can not be compared either among themselves or with
reference values without a documented traceability.
The Vocabulary of Metrology (VIM) deﬁnition of traceability is: ”the property of the result
of a measurement or the value of a standard whereby it can be related to stated references,
usually national or international standards, through an unbroken chain of comparisons all
having stated uncertainties”. Therefore, to establish traceability of dimensional CT scanners
all the measurement results obtained by CT have to be detectable to the unit length through
calibration chain [4,27].
Estimation of the measurement uncertainty is very important due to traceability reasons.
Traceability establishment requires estimation of task-speciﬁc uncertainty.
However, all there is now to verify accuracy of the performance of CT systems used for
coordinate metrology is a German guideline VDI/VDE 2630, which is intended for tactile
and optical CMMs, but can also be applied for CT evaluation.
The measurement traceability in CT can be achieved by following three steps:
• Developing / using reference objects to correct the system errors of a given CT scanner.
• Understanding of the inﬂuence factors.
• Assessing methods for measurement uncertainty estimation.
An overview of valid methods for the assessment of measurement uncertainty in CT is given
in the following list:
• Methods requiring a model equation – GUM method (JCGM 100:2008) [28], ISO 14253-
2 [29].
• Empirical methods – use of calibrated workpieces or standards ISO 15530-3 [30]. Use
of multiple measurement strategies in calibration artifacts ISO/DTS 15530-2 [31].
• Computer simulation – Suppl. 1 to GUM (JCGM 101:2008) [32], VDI/VDE 2617-7 [33]
and ISO/TS15530-4 [34].
• Combination of mentioned methods.

object to be measured and the speciﬁc parameters chosen for the measurement process. But
the the number of factors that inﬂuence the overall accuracy of CT is bigger compared
to classical CMM measurements and therefore, the users are typically not able to provide
appropriate statements of CT measurement uncertainties [2]. Some of the typical factors
inﬂuencing the overall performance of CT can be categorized into ﬁve groups (see Figure 12:

Scale errors
Determination of scale error is an important factor in dimensional metrology. In CT, the
correction of scale error depends on correction of the original voxel size by the use of reference
objects like ball plate or other objects with cylindrical or spherical shapes, independent of
threshold. The method for correction of the scale error using the ball plate developed at
DTU is done by comparing distance measured by CT with tactile CMM measurements [46].
The ball plate features a regular 5x5 array of ruby spheres glued on a carbon ﬁbre 13. The
distance between the balls is easy to measure on CT data points as the distance between
two ﬁtted spheres. Furthermore, since the distance between the centres is measured, the
threshold applied during the surface determination does not inﬂuence the obtained results
almost at all.

3.2.4
Problems with multi-material objects
Multi-material components with high density within low density material (e.g. plastic con-
nector with metallic pins) rise a major problem to dimensional measurement using 3D CT.
The generated scans suﬀer from severe artifacts and increased noise, which prevent reliable
measurements. However, a lot of industrial objects consist of more than one material, at
least after assembly [9].
The ﬁrst problem is to ﬁnd a reliable threshold for correct surface determination.
For
example, for a multi-material assembly with press-ﬁt connection an overlapping of the gray
values of two diﬀerent material between the two peeks in the gray value histogram may occur.
Therefore, it might be diﬃcult to distinguish between the shaft and the hub material and
the shaft and air that is also present. Another problem relates to proper selection of current
and voltage values for the scanning of diﬀerent materials in assembly. For high absorbing
materials, a higher energy exposure is needed so that suﬃcient photons reach the detector,
but these settings do not allow visualizing low absorbing materials. On the other hand, lower
current and voltage values allow visualizing the low absorbing materials like polymers, but
yield artifacts or total disappearance due to the presence of e.g metals or ceramics [2].
Often, the metrology technicians disassemble the multi-material assembly, like they would
do for CMMs. Each material is measured in a separate scan using optimal X-ray param-
eters. This procedure is time consuming and in many cases the part has to be destroyed
for separation. In addition, for assemblies this disassembling approach will only give infor-
mation about the individual components and does not guarantee proper functioning of the
assembly [9].
Another possibility that CT manufacturers oﬀer to handle the problems with multi-material
assemblies is using multi-spectra scans, including:
• Diﬀerent target materials e.g. using multi-target head.
• Diﬀerent sources, for example CT system with two diﬀerent tubes.
• Energy-sensitive sandwich detectors. The energy-sensitive photon counting detectors
are able to sort out energies and thus, energy-selective imaging with a single X-ray
exposure can be achieved [2].
3.3
Measurement uncertainty
All measurement result should be given together with the statement about uncertainty.
This ensures the documented traceability of measurement results to the SI unit. A complete
statement of a measurement result should be expressed as shown in equation 12:
Y = y ± U
(11)
Moreover, a dimensional CT measurement has to include a a correct statement of task speciﬁc
uncertainty. Task speciﬁc uncertainty requires a model, that covers all variables inﬂuencing
the value of measurand. The uncertainty should also describe the eﬀect on the measurand
of any variances in the inﬂuencing variables [4,13].
27
==PAGE:28==
The general method for the assessment of measurement uncertainty is outlined in GUM.
However, due to many inﬂuence factors in CT, it is a challenging task to establish the
equation for uncertainty estimation according to GUM. The estimation of measurement
uncertainties can be done using two approaches:
• Type A evaluation: estimated by statistical analysis of observations (usually from
repeated readings).
• Type B evaluation: calculated from assigned probability distributions, from past mea-
surements experience, from calibrated certiﬁcates, manufacture’s speciﬁcations, from
calculations, from published information and, ﬁnally, from common sense.
Once the diﬀerent contributors of uncertainty are estimated, the combined standard
uncertainty uc is calculated as the estimated standard deviation associated with the
result. It is equal to the positive square root of the combined variance obtained from
all variance-covariance components. The expanded uncertainty U is obtained by mul-
tiplying the uc by a coverage factor k. The intended purpose of U is to provide an
interval for result of a measurement that may be expected to enclose a large fraction
of the distribution of values, that could be reasonably be attributed to the measurand.
The coverage factor k is chosen on the basis of the desired level of conﬁdence [38].
In cases where compensation of bias eﬀect is not possible or one wants to point out
the quantity of the bias, the measurement result should be expressed as following [24]:
Y = y −b ± U
(12)
Where, b is the bias or systematic error:

(13)

male part. The female part is made from high attenuation material and thus, it has to be
deﬁned ﬁrst. On the gray value histogram, there is an overlapping of the gray values for
POM and the second material PP. When a local adaptive thresholding is applied for the
segmentation of the female part, then some of the female part remains on the male part.
Therefore, there is a layer of female part around the male part and this changes the surface
of the male part and thus, also the roundness values are aﬀected.

6
Conclusion
Nowadays, computed tomography has an increasing number of industrial applications. One
of the high potential applications of CT is the dimensional quality control for multi-material
assemblies. However, there are considerable diﬃculties in this area such as the requirement
for high measurement accuracy. The main objective of this thesis was to investigate the
inﬂuence of diﬀerent scanning parameters and image post-processing workﬂows in order to
improve the performance of CT measurements for multi-material assemblies.
Firstly, a literature overview about industrial computed tomography was given. This in-
cluded a discussion about the inﬂuence of diﬀerent components, like hardware and software,
to the image quality and thereby, to the quality of coordinate measurements. This chapter
also gave an insight to the general process chain on how to the measurement results with CT
are obtained. In addition, an overview of the existing guidelines and standards was given,
which included a more detailed description about some of the inﬂuence factors that cause
systematic errors in the dimensional measurements with CT.
The experimental part was divided into three parts:
• Investigation of the inﬂuence of four diﬀerent scanning parameters and ﬁve diﬀerent
post-processing workﬂows to the diameter and roundness measurements was carried
out. The object under focus was a cylindrical multi-material assembly, consisting of
two parts (female and male). For the diameter, no apparent inﬂuence from the dif-
ferent approaches was noticed at ﬁrst, but with the robust statistical analysis it was
shown that the best repeatability of the measurements was obtained using scanning
parameters 75 kV and 90 kV. Smallest roundness deviations were obtained at 90 kV. It
was later found that the smallest diﬀerence between CT and CMM measurements was
with the fourth and ﬁfth approach, which were F+SD(3x3x3) and F+SD(5x5x5), re-
spectively. The diameter and roundness expanded uncertainties for the female part lied
in the range of 15.1-17.4 and 11.7-19.5 µm, respectively. The diameter and roundness
expanded uncertainties for the male part were in the range of 15.8-21.4 and 12.4-28.0
µm, respectively. The En number showed valid uncertainty estimations associated with
the female and male part diameter, and roundness measurements.
• The validation of the results was done using two scanning parameters: 75 an 80 kV.
The part was assembled in a conﬁguration so that information about the free and
assembled section of the female and male part could be obtained. The diameter results
from the ﬁrst scan were compared with the results obtained from the assembled section
and it was concluded that the repeatability of the CT is problematic. A maximum
deviations between CT and CMM of 36 µm for dimensional and 51 µm for geometrical
measurements was obtained. In general, smaller deviations were achieved with applying
the ﬁltering before surface determination. Although the uncertainties were in the same
range as for the ﬁrst scan, the deviations were larger and therefore, less than 30% of
the En numbers were less than one.
• Finally, a tolerance veriﬁcation was carried out on the insulin pen dose button man-
ufactured by Novo Nordisk A/S. This time, the scanning parameters 75 and 90 kV
were used. Since the specimen had a clearance ﬁt and the wall thickness was smaller,
the deviations for the diameter and roundness yielded better. The length deviations
reached up to 100 µm, which could be explained with the wrong decision to use glue
as a ﬁxture during the calibration with CMM. In general, the deviations were smaller
using the scanning parameter 90 kV and a decreasing trend was noticed with higher
62
==PAGE:63==
ﬁlters and applying ﬁltering before surface determination. Although the deviations of
the diameter and roundness values were smaller, the average uncertainties were still in
the range of 12 to 20 µ. To conclude, if CT is suitable for tolerance veriﬁcation, the
CMM measurements should be repeated and more repeated measurements with CT
are needed to obtain repeatability values and new tests to decrease the uncertainty
component coming from the voxel are required as well.
6.1
Future work
Based on this experimental work it is suggested that further investigation should be done
about using ﬁltering during reconstruction as the ﬁlters applied to the voxel model do not
seem to be suﬃcient enough to reduce the image noise and scattering artifacts.
A suggestion for the software improvement to ensure metrological compatibility and trace-
ability is to add a function that allows to deﬁne the threshold value by inserting a numerical
value instead of the manual dragging of the line on the gray value histogram.
Further investigations of CT are needed for the geometrical measurements deviation and
expanded uncertainty improvements.
