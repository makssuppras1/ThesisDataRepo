Abstract
The use of compost biofilters is a promising approach for mitigating methane (CH4) emissions
by utilizing methane­oxidizing bacteria (MOB) to consume the gas. While most studies focus on
MOB performance under mesophilic conditions (20­35°C), as this is widely considered their opti­
mum, less is known about their potential in thermophilic environments (>45°C). This thesis aimed
to characterize compost from a full­scale biofilter treating manure gas at thermophilic conditions
and assess its CH4 oxidation potential under different conditions. Batch experiments with compost
from the biofilter were conducted at 50°C. Results showed significant spatial variability in CH4
oxidation rates horizontally and vertically within the filter, highlighting the heterogeneous nature
of such a biofilter. Oxidation potentials were higher at the bottom of the filter, closer to the gas
distribution layer. Additionally, certain areas within the filter exhibited a greater response to nutri­
ent addition than others. In the literature, compost used for CH4 biofiltration has been observed to
experience nutrient depletion over time. This is a significant issue because nutrients are crucial for
maintaining MOB activity. Batch tests revealed that nitrogen is consumed in considerable amounts
compared to other nutrients. In certain locations of the filter, 18 different trace elements saw an
average decrease of 35% in concentrations between September 2023 and May 2024, showing no­
table nutrient depletion in full­scale biofilters. In addition to CH4 removal, this thesis explored
the ability of MOB to degrade organic micropollutants (OMP). Cultures of MOB have previously
been suggested to co­metabolize OMPs in mesophilic conditions, however, this potential remained
unexplored not only in thermophilic conditions, but also using compost. During 48­hour batch
tests, the compost demonstrated successful degradation of several compounds during CH4 load­
ing. Sulfamethoxazole and Benzotriazole showed biological removal rates of 78.5% and 64.4%,
respectively. Dimethyl sulfide, 2,6 Dichlorobenzamide, Alachlor ESA, and Diclofenac showed
biological removal rates between 15.2% and 27.9%. These results suggest that compost may not
only offer a cost­effective method for CH4 removal, but can also contribute to OMP removal under
CH4 loading in thermophilic conditions.

Introduction
Methane (CH4) has increased 2.5 times in atmospheric concentrations since preindustrial times. It
is a potent greenhouse gas (GHG) with approximately 30 times the warming potential of CO2, and
its presence negatively affects atmospheric chemistry by altering ozone (O3) levels, amongst other
factors (Dlugokencky et al., 2011;Jackson et al., 2021). In total, 600 Tg CH4×yr­1 are released
globally where 235 Tg CH4×yr­1 are released naturally from environmental sources and 365 Tg
CH4×yr­1 are released from anthropogenic sources, of which approximately two­thirds (227 Tg
CH4×yr­1) are from agriculture­related activities (Jackson et al., 2021). In natural as well as an­
thropogenic methane­containing environments, methane oxidizing bacteria, often referred to as
methanotrophs, are present. The term methanotroph covers a wide range of bacterial species and
some archaea. They are capable of utilizing methane as their sole carbon and energy source, and
oxidize it into H2O and CO2, the latter being a significantly less potent GHG. This ability has led
to an interest in applying methanotrophs to engineered solutions for mitigating methane emissions
from anthropogenic environments, such as landfills, coal mines, and animal husbandry (Jiang et
al., 2010;Melse and van der Werf, 2005).
A widely adopted approach for practical implementation is constructing biofilters that provide op­
timal conditions for microbial communities to degrade pollutants. They are considered a less costly
and energy­demanding option compared to physical and chemical treatment alternatives (La et al.,
2018a). Biological filters consist of a filter medium that serves as a habitat for microbial commu­
nities. Gas treatment works by conveying polluted gas through the filter medium, where microbes
break down pollutants (figure 1.1). Biofilters have in some cases produced another potent GHG,
nitrous oxide (N2O) (La et al., 2018b). Therefore, these emissions should be monitored, especially
if there is ammonia (NH3) in the inflow stream, as it can happen due to incomplete nitrification/­
denitrification (Melse & van der Werf, 2005). Many filter configurations exist, where some are
supplied gas via mechanical pumping, and some rely on passive aeration without external pres­
sure. They can be open to the atmosphere, or in closed containers. As a filter medium, a wide
range of materials can be used, including organic materials (e.g., wood, compost, peat, and soil);
inert inorganic materials (e.g., as sand, rocks, gravel, perlite, and vermiculite); and inert organic
materials (e.g., various plastics, polyurethane, and polypropylene) (La et al., 2018b). It is com­
mon practice to use either a single material for the entire filter, or to combine multiple materials
to utilize the advantages of each individual component. Organic materials generally have a high
surface attachment area, and naturally contains nutrients, which is particularly important for gas
treatment where nutrients are limited in the inflow stream (Cabrera et al., 2019). For methane
removal, compost is often considered due to high surface area, high fluid permeability, nutrient
contents, being inexpensive, and has shown methane degradation rates 2­3 times higher than soil
(Cabrera et al., 2019;Wilshusen et al., 2004). Another advantage is that compost can be supplied
from local areas, with typical sources like gardening waste and municipal solid waste. Although
there are benefits of using biofilters with organic filter medium such as compost, many reduce in
performance over time. This has primarily been attributed to filter compaction and biomass accu­
mulation, which causes gas pressure drops, and to nutrient depletion due to microbial consumption,
which can happen rapidly depending on the material source (La et al., 2018a;La et al., 2018b).

Nutrients greatly control methanotrophic activity, particularly nitrogen (N) and phosphorous (P)
(La et al., 2018b), but also trace elements like copper (Cu) and iron (Fe) amongst others (Glass &
Orphan, 2012). Cu and Fe are important as they are essential components of the methane monooxy­
genase (MMO) enzyme in methanotrophs, which plays a big role in CH4 oxidation (Chidambaram­
padmavathy et al., 2015).
While most methanotrophs are mesophilic, psycrophilic and thermophilic methanotrophs can also
be found in the environment. In fact, methanotrophs have shown to grow in a wide range of con­
ditions in pH values from 1­8, temperature ranges of 4­95°C, and in both aerobic and anaerobic
environments (Guerrero­Cruz et al., 2021). This gives methanotrophs a great potential in bio­
engineered solutions, as they can function effectively across a diverse range of conditions. When
conditions shift towards more extreme environments, such as a low pH values, a methanotrophic
group might displace other methanotrophic groups present and take over growth in biofilters (La
et al., 2018b). However, even though they can grow in a wide range of conditions, according to
literature, optimal temperatures for growth are in the range of 20­35°C and optimal pH values are
neutral to slightly acidic in the range of 5­8 (La et al., 2018b). Due to reported optimal temperatures
being in mesophilic conditions, most studies on methanotrophic performance are conducted in this
temperature range. Less is known about their potential in thermophilic conditions, especially in
full­scale biofilters.
Methanotrophic oxidation of methane is an exothermic reaction, resulting in increased biofilter
temperatures during operation. In filters such as landfill covers, which are typically not aerated, the
filter temperature commonly fluctuate greatly throughout the year, which reduces efficiency dur­
ing colder months (La et al., 2018b;Reddy et al., 2019). However, it has been possible to achieve
thermophilic conditions in full­scale biofilters, even during winter months, which shows poten­
tial in utilizing thermophilic methanotrophs for engineered solutions regarding methane emission
reductions (Scheutz et al., 2017;Falk et al., 2024a).
Alongside methane oxidation, methanotrophs are being studied for other purposes. It has been
hypothesized that methanotrophs are able to degrade organic micropollutants (OMPs) via co­
metabolism, meaning they are able degrade other organic compounds in the presence of methane
without using them as a carbon or energy source (Benner et al., 2015). This ability has a great
potential as many OMPs are not removed efficiently by conventional wastewater treatment plants,
threatening soil and drinking water qualities when they enter the water cycle untreated (Benner et
al., 2015). In cases where OMPs have escaped to the environment, particularly into the groundwa­
ter, current treatment alternatives at drinking water treatment plants involve chemical and physical
treatment. These are often associated with high investment and energy costs, and can in some cases
3
==PAGE:8==
lead to toxic transformation products (Wang et al., 2022). Studies have focused on degradation
of pharmaceuticals, pesticides and chemical additives. Degradation varies greatly between com­
pounds, where some show great degradation, some slow, and some compounds not at all (Benner
et al., 2015;Wang et al., 2022). More research is needed to identify which compounds can de­
grade by methanotrophs, as a limited number of OMPs have been tested to date. Research so far
has focused on OMP degradation in mesophilic conditions, which are suggested to be the most
favourable for methanotrophs. OMP degradation potential remains unknown in thermophilic con­
ditions. Furthermore, previous research has been conducted using either methanotrophic cultures
in liquid, or mixed with solids in batch test experiments. However, the potential of using compost
as a growth medium in liquid to degrade pollutants under CH4 load has not yet been investigated.
This thesis seeks to characterize compost from a thermophilic full­scale biofilter constructed to
reduce methane emissions from a manure tank, including physicochemical characterization and
microbial analysis. Furthermore, methane oxidation rates of the compost under different condi­
tions are investigated to reveal the potential of thermophilic methanotrophs. Lastly, the OMP
degradation potential of the compost is evaluated to see if they are able to degrade pollutants in
thermophilic conditions. Compost will be used as growth medium, potentially offering a cost­
effective and sustainable way of mitigating pollutants.

Materials and methods
2.1
Compost sampling
Compost from a full­scale operating biofilter treating swine manure gas in Gilleleje, Denmark
was sampled to be characterized and used as inoculum for incubations. It was constructed in
February 2022 with dimensions of approximately 30 x 17 x 0.8 meters (L x W x H). The compost
in the biofilter stems from gardening waste and was composted under aerobic conditions. It was
at least 6 months old by the time the filter was constructed (Falk et al., 2024a). The filter treats
gas collected from the headspace of a covered manure tank and is distributed with a mechanical
pump (figure 2.1). The gas is distributed via pipes to a distribution layer beneath the filter, after
which it is sent in an upwards direction through the compost for biological treatment. The manure
tank has a capacity of 4400m3 (Falk et al., 2024a) and has an average retention time of 7 months
(Falk, 2024b). This quantity of manure results in an estimated methane (CH4) load of 42 tonnes
CH4×y­1 to the biofilter, which fluctuates throughout the year due to variations in manure volume
and temperature. Due to high loading rate and high methanotrophic exothermic activity, the filter
operates under thermophilic conditions at around 45­60°C, and in periods even higher. It is reported
that during stable methane loading, the filter operates around 60°C (Falk et al., 2024a).
Figure 2.1 shows an orthophoto of the filter with the different sampling locations. Samples A­B
were collected by a previous researcher in September 2023 and kept until analysis in May 2024.
Samples C­F were collected as a part of this thesis in May 2024. The samples were taken at different
depths, where A­B were taken at 30cm and 45cm below surface and C­F were taken at 30cm and
60cm below surface. For a full overview of sample data and naming method, refer to table 2.1.
Compost was extracted from the filter with a manual spiral drill meant for soil. The samples were
stored in plastic bags at 10°C and in dark conditions whenever not in use for laboratory experiments.

2.1.1
Compost characterization
Chemical composition and bioavailability
For solids characterization, compost from all sample locations were tested in duplicates for nutri­
ents, metals, total nitrogen and total organic carbon (TOC) contents. Small compost subsamples
(<20g WW) were placed in ceramic beakers and put into a 105°C Memmert model 100­800 oven
for 18+ hours. The dry compost was then stored in 15mL polypropylene falcon tubes (Sarstedt,
Germany) until testing.
A leaching experiment was performed to determine how the nutrients and metals dissolve from the
compost, which were assumed to be bioavailable for growth. To do so, 8g DW compost from each
location was put into 250mL glass serum bottles and mixed with 50mL DI water. The bottles were
then sealed with rubber stoppers and tightened with 20mm aluminium tear­off caps (Thermo Fisher
Scientific, USA). The bottles were then flushed with ≥99.999% N2 gas (Air Liquide, Denmark)
using 0.8mm hypodermic needles as inlet and 0.6mm hypodermic needles (Chirana T. Injecta,
Slovak Republic) as outlet through the rubber stoppers. They were flushed for circa 20 minutes at
around 1.4 bar to ensure the headspace consisted entirely of N2 gas to minimize biological activity
within the bottles. After flushing, they were left over night (20+ hours) on a 50°C KS 4000 i
control incubator shaker (IKA, Germany) shaking at 125 RPM. The following day, they were put
6
==PAGE:11==
into 50mL polypropylene falcon tubes (Sarstedt, Germany) and centrifuged 3­4 minutes at 11000g
using a Heraeus Multifuge X3R centrifuge (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The supernatant was
subsequently filtered using 0.2µm nylon filters (Agilent Technologies, USA) before being tested
for metals and nutrients.
2.2
Experimental setup
Multiple methane oxidation experiments, each with slightly different setups, were conducted through­
out the thesis (table 2.2). All experiments, except for the first incubation experiment, were con­
ducted in 250mL glass serum bottles, where raw compost was mixed with liquid to form a slurry
(figure A.1). Liquids used were DI water, nutrient stock, sodium azide (NaN3) and OMP stock,
depending on the experiment. As a nutrient stock, diluted ammonium mineral salt (dAMS) was
used. The dAMS media recipe was adapted from Whittenbury et al. (1970a) and can be seen in
appendix A.1. After compost and liquid were added to the bottles, they were sealed with rubber
stoppers and tightened with 20mm aluminium tear­off caps (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Af­
ter properly closing the bottles, they were each inserted with a 0.6mm and a 0.8mm hypodermic
needle (Chirana T. Injecta, Slovak Republic) through the rubber stoppers. To flush the headspace,
≥99.999% N2 gas (Air Liquide, Denmark) was injected through the 0.8mm needle, which served
as the inlet, while the 0.6mm needle was the outlet. The bottles were flushed 15­20 minutes with
around 1.3­1.5 bar overpressure to ensure the entire headspace consisted of N2 gas. After flushing,
the headspace pressure was neutralized and 50­75mL removed. The bottles were then injected
with ≥99.995% O2 gas (Air Liquide, Denmark) and pure CH4 gas (Air Liquide, Denmark) in a
ratio of 60:40 O2:CH4. Gas was injected to reach 20% to 30% CH4 V/V, depending on the exper­
iment. For the initial oxidation experiment, the starting overpressure after injecting CH4 and O2
was 23mL (1.13atm). For nutrient availability and OMP degradation experiments the overpressure
was 50mL (1.27 and 1.33atm respectively) After injection of gas, the bottles were put on a 50°C
KS 4000 i control incubator shaker (IKA, Germany) shaking at 125 RPM. The glass part of the
incubator shaker was covered in tinfoil on the outside to avoid photodegradation and temperature
alterations within the serum bottles (figure A.2).

2.2.1
Initial methane oxidation experiment
Incubation before initial oxidation experiment
A test was conducted to see if the thermophilic methanotrophs could be cultivated in laboratory
conditions using compost submerged in liquid. This was done using the compost from locations
A30, A45, B30, and B45 (2023 compost). In total, four bottles were prepared, one for each loca­
tion. The goal of this experiment was to check whether it was possible to reactivate the microbial
community after 7+ months of dormancy. As this setup was new, this experiment laid the foun­
dation for compost amount and warm­up time for subsequent experiments. After this experiment,
the compost slurry was transferred to smaller bottles to perform the initial oxidation test.
Initial oxidation experiment
To conduct the first oxidation experiment, the slurry from the previously mentioned reactivation
bottles were distributed to 250mL glass serum bottles (location A30, A45, B30, B45). Even though
it was already a well­mixed slurry, it was attempted to transfer the equivalent of 8g DW compost
to each bottle. In total, 14 bottles were prepared in the following configuration; duplicates from
each location, two A45 abiotic controls and two B45 abiotic controls. The abiotic controls were
autoclaved at 121°C for 15 min, with 10 min venting at 115°C using a Certoclav Multicontrol
autoclave (Certoclav, Austria). The last two bottles were from location B30 and B45, however,
unlike the rest of the bottles, they were placed horizontally rather than vertically in the incubator
shaker. The hypothesis was that by placing them horizontally, the increased contact area between
the liquid and gas would cause an increased mass transfer from the gas phase to the liquid phase.
After the slurry was added to each bottle, 50mL dAMS was added on top of the dAMS that was
already present in the slurry (from the previous incubation) to conduct the experiment. After the
oxidation experiment was done, the TS and liquid content was measured. Subsequent experiments
had compost inserted directly into the bottles based on moisture content, which was a more reliable
way of achieving a desired dry weight and liquid content.
After this initial oxidation experiment, all other experiments conducted in the thesis involving glass
serum bottles on the incubator shaker had the bottles placed horizontally (figure A.2 in appendix
A.2).
2.2.2
Impact of nutrient availability
To test whether nutrient addition had an impact on methane oxidation capabilities, one batch of
bottles was prepared with dAMS and subsequently evaluated against a batch prepared with DI
water.
C30 vs F30
Ten bottles were prepared, of which five were from location C30 and five from location F30. For
both locations, the bottles were prepared in the following configuration; two with 50mL dAMS,
two with 50mL DI water, and one abiotic control. The abiotic controls were prepared with 50mL DI
water and were autoclaved at 134°C for 20 min, with 10 min venting at 115°C following a second
autoclavation at 121°C for 15 min, with 10 min venting at 115°C using a Certoclav Multicontrol
autoclave (Certoclav, Austria). They were cooled in between the two heating phases.
C60 vs E60
A second test was conducted with compost from deeper layers in the filter at location C60 and
E60 to assess the effect of location and depth on methane oxidation rates. The setup has minor
modifications compared to the previous test. The abiotic control bottles were autoclaved at 121°C
for 15 min, with 10 min venting at 115°C following a second autoclave at 134°C for 20 min, with
10 min venting at 115°C and with cooling in between. The abiotic bottles warmed up for 11+
hours, compared to 17+ hours for the biotic ones.

2.2.3
Organic micropollutant degradation
To test the organic micropollutant degradation (OMP) potential of the compost, two experiments
were conducted. First, an abiotic sorption experiment was conducted to evaluate how much the
OMPs sorb to the compost slurry under abiotic conditions. Thereafter, a biotic degradation exper­
iment was conducted to determine the degradation potential under biotic conditions. In terms of
setup, the experiments followed the same procedure as earlier oxidation experiments, with addi­
tional steps.
Sorption experiment
The sorption test should be done under abiotic conditions to eliminate biotransformation of OMPs.
Autoclaving under high temperature and pressure can destroy soil structure, which might change
the sorption potential of OMPs in the compost (Lees et al., 2018;Trevors, 1996). Instead, the
highly toxic biocide, sodium azide (NaN3), was used. A concentration of 500mg/L was used
based on Rattier et al. (2014). The experiments were conducted in 250mL glass serum bottles.
Other researchers in the department use a working volume of 100mL to conduct OMP experiments.
Therefore, it was decided to aim for a working volume of 100mL, which was higher than previous
experiments, to make data using compost comparable to future research. Although the working
volume was higher, the liquid:solid ratio was kept in the same range (table 2.2). To calculate an
end volume of 100mL, it is assumed that the compost has a bulk density of 700kg ×m­3 based on
Sullivan and Miller (2001).
Fourteen different OMPs were added (table 2.3). The concentrations used were based on the subse­
quent biological OMP degradation experiment to ensure consistency between the two experiments.
It was aimed to reach the highest concentrations possible without inhibiting the methanotrophs.
Based on literature, a non­toxic concentration was estimated to be 500µg ×L ­1 for a range of
OMPs, so this concentration was used in both experiments (Benner et al., 2015;Wang et al., 2022).
Furthermore, for the sorption experiment, 1000 µg ×L ­1 was also tested to assess the impact of
initial concentration on the extent of sorption.

Analytical methods
To measure gas levels in the bottles, a Trace 1310 gas chromatograph (GC) was used (Thermo
Fisher Scientific, USA). Two machines were run in parallel where one measured O2 and the other
measured CH4 and CO2 with a column setup similar to Rasouli et al. (2018). N2O was measured
on the same column and machine as O2. Gases were measured by extracting 0.2mL air directly
from the headspace of the bottles, through the rubber stoppers, using 0.6mm hypodermic needles
and 1mL syringes. During times of GC measuring, the machines were mostly calibrated at least
once per day.
Liquid samples from the first ’impact of nutrient availability’ experiment (section 2.2.2) were col­
lected by opening the bottles and pouring the contents through a 47mm diameter glass fiber filter
using a water­driven comfort Heto master jet SUE30Q vacuum pump (Heto­Holten, Denmark).
The glass fiber filtering was carried out with different sizes down to 0.3 µm (Whatman, United
Kingdom; Advantec, Japan). However, this was a rather inconvenient process as the compost
slurry clogged the filter fast, demanding many filter changes to be necessary.
Instead, remaining liquid samples were taken from the bottles through the rubber stopper. The
bottles were shaken and held upside down, from where the compost slurry could be extracted using
a 1.2mm needle. During a previous test using 0.6, 0.8, 0.9 and 1.2mm needles to extract liquid, it
was learned that only 0.9 and 1.2mm needles were suitable for the task. The 0.9mm needles were
more prone to being clogged, however.
After liquid was extracted, samples that were meant for nutrient, trace element, and TOC analysis
were put in 15mL or 50mL polypropylene falcon tubes (Sarstedt, Germany) and centrifuged 3­4
minutes at 11000g using a Heraeus Multifuge X3R centrifuge (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA).
The supernatant was then extracted and filtered through 0.2µm nylon syringe filters (Captiva
Econofilter, Agilent Technologies, USA),
Samples that were meant for OMP analysis were put in 1.5mL or 2mL Eppendorf Safe­Lock tubes
(Eppendorf, Germany) and centrifuged at 14000g for 3 minutes using an Eppendorf MiniSpin
plus centrifuge (Eppendorf, Germany). The supernatant was then extracted and filtered through
0.45µm PTFE syringe filters, also known as teflon filters (Captiva Econofilter, Agilent Technolo­
gies, USA). They were filtered using PTFE filters, because studies have shown using the wrong
filter material can interfere with results (Dong et al., 2022;Ejerssa et al., 2024).
Analysis of PO43­, NOx, NH3, and NO2­, were measured using a San++ segmented flow analyzer
(Skalar, The Netherlands). Analysis of TOC, IC, TC, TNb were measured using a Multi N/C 3100
analyzer (Analytik Jena, Germany). Conductivity was measured using a WTW LF 539 micropro­
cessor conductivity meter (Wissenschaftlich­Technische Werkstätten, Germany).

Calculations
CH4 oxidation rates, O2 consumption rates, and CO2 production rates were calculated as follows;
Rate = Rate% · Vair · P · M · 106
R · T · m(DW)
(2.3)
where rate is the amount of gas consumed or produced in µg×h­1×g DW compost­1; Rate% is the
amount of %v/v gas consumed or produced in the headspace per hour in vol%/h; Vair is the volume
of the headspace in the bottle in L; P is the pressure in the headspace in atmospheres (atm); M is the
molar mass of the gas in g×mol­1; R is the gas constant in L×atm×K­1×mol­1; T is the temperature
in K; m(DW) is the dry weight mass of the compost in the bottle in g. The 106 is the conversion
factor from g to µg.
To find rate%, the method by Olesen et al. (2018) was used, which assumes zero­order kinetics
between measurement points during high methane degradation. Lag phases, determined by flat
CH4 trends at the start of incubations, and low oxygen limitation (O2 <3% v/v) were excluded
from calculations. Based on a linear trendline, the R2 was calculated to determine the quality of
the fit.
In beginning of experiments, where the headspace volume was known and a known amount of gas
was injected, the headspace pressure was estimated using Boyle’s Law, which states the pressure
of a certain mass of gas is inversely proportional to its volume;
P1 · V1 = P2 · V2
(2.4)
where P1 and P2 are the pressures in the bottle in atm; V1 is the original volume of the bottle
headspace in L, and V2 is the original volume plus injected gas volume in L.
Due to limitations, a simplified sorption modelling strategy was chosen. Two experiments were
conducted, one abiotic sorption experiments and one biological degradation experiment. The bi­
ological experiment was corrected using the results of the sorption experiment, so only biological
removal was used in calculations. The corrected curve was then used to calculate k and later on
Kbio.
OMP biodegradation is assumed to follow first order kinetics with the analytical solution;
C(t) = C0 · e−k·t
(2.5)
where C(t) is the concentration of OMPs at a given timestep in µg×L­1; C0 is the initial concentra­
tion of OMPs in µg×L­1; k is the first order rate constant of degradation in day­1; t is the time in
days.
The biological degradation rate constant, Kbio is calculated by normalizing k to the concentration
of biomass, which is compost in this case;
Kbio = k
X
where Kbio is the biological degradation rate constant in L×g­1×d­1; k is the first order rate constant
of degradation in day­1; X is the compost concentration in g×L­1.

For statistical analysis, Excel (Microsoft, USA) was used. The T.test was conducted to determine
statistical significant differences between samples. In cases where there was a hypothesis of one
group of measurements being significantly higher than the other, a one­tailed T.test was conducted.
If there was no such hypothesis, a two two­tailed t.test was conducted. In the T.test, a P≥0.05
assumes no statistical significant differences and P<0.05 assumes statistical significant differences
between the samples. Before a T.test was carried out, the sample set was tested for differences
in variance using the F.test where P≥0.05 assumes the dataset to have equal variance and P<0.05
to have unequal variance. Equal variances used a homoscedastic t.test whereas unequal variances
used a heteroscedastic t.test. Standard error of the mean is calculated on the basis of standard
deviation (SD), SEM = SD×√n­1, to show variations around the mean of the data.

Moisture
The deeper layers of the filter have significantly higher moisture contents than shallower layers
(P=0.0025) in the 2024 compost. The 30cm layer has an average moisture of 33.1 ± 1.07% and
the 60cm layer has an average of 41.0 ± 1.15%. In the 2023 compost, the 45cm layer does not
statistically have higher moisture than the 30cm layer (P=0.46). This suggests that either 45cm was
not deep enough to measure a significant difference, or with time water has started to accumulate
towards the lower layers of the filter. For the 30cm layer, there is no significant difference between
2023 and 2024 compost (P=0.54).
Unlike soil, which has been studied intensively for optimal moisture conditions in biofilters such as
landcover fills, the information is scarcer for compost, especially in thermophilic conditions. It has
been reported that yard waste compost has the highest optimal moisture content for CH4 oxidation
between different medias at >30% w/w (Chiemchaisri et al., 2012). Other literature has reported
the optimal range to be 25­50% for biowaste compost (Humer & Lechner, 1999). According to
this, the filter appears to be in favorable conditions.
VS content
When comparing A30+B30 from the 2023 compost to C30+D30 from 2024, the average VS con­
tent has fallen from 15.9±0.3% to 13.6±1.2%. This suggests a decrease of VS content in the filter
over time, although the 2024 compost is not statistically higher than the 2023 compost (P=0.153)
(table 3.1). Comparing depths, there is no statistical difference between 30cm and 60cm layers
(P=0.206) in the 2024 compost.
Volatile solids content, an estimation of organic matter, typically decreases with time in a biofilter
due to organic carbon being broken down by microorganisms. It is typical for compost to lose
around half of its initial organic matter during the composting process itself (Sullivan & Miller,
2001).
15
==PAGE:20==
The VS content in the filter ranges from 12.0 to 16.3% for the 30cm layer and 13.5 to 22% in the
60cm layer (table 3.1). VS content depends on the compost source, but higher values are generally
reported. One study with material from a full­scale biofilter consisting of compost and sand had a
VS content between 25.3 ­ 34.3% (Frasi et al., 2020). Two composting facilities with ≈24 months
old garden waste compost in Denmark, had VS content of 19% and 26% (Olesen et al., 2018).
Although the latter has not been used for biofiltration, the compost source and age are comparable.
Since the values are higher than in the CH4 biofilter, it suggests that using the compost for CH4
biofiltration further decreases VS content.
A recommended quality criteria of >15% volatile solids has been suggested for media in biocover
systems used for methane oxidation (Kjeldsen & Scheutz, 2017). Measurements show that 7 out
of 12 samples are below this criteria, and would not be suitable to be used in a new biofilter. The
decrease in VS is most likely due to age, high microbial activity, and permanent aerobic conditions
due to mechanical pumping, which passive compost filters do not experience.

3.1.3
Nutrients in solid phase
Due to the heterogeneous nature of compost, and the fact that a very small amount of compost is
needed for solid sampling, this experiment turned out to be rather unreliable (figure A.5). Between

duplicates, it is not uncommon to see one part being 100% higher than the other. These inconsis­
tencies make it hard to compare individual locations with each other, and suggests that methods
involving more compost, such as liquid experiments, are necessary. However, average values for
the most important nutrients for methanotrophs, Fe, Cu, P, N, can still be compared to garden waste
compost in literature .
The average value for copper (Cu), which is important for methanotrophic growth, is 25.2µg×g­1
in the biofilter. An analysis of 129 different green waste compost samples in Germany showed an
average value of 46.5µg×g­1 (Biala & Wynen, 1998). Other sources have reported values in the
range of 3.1 ­ 40µg×g­1, where only a few are in the lower range (Chaney et al., 2000;Haynes et al.,
2015). Comparing to the extensive data from Germany, the copper levels are around 46% lower
than what you can expect from similar compost, but still within a comparable range.
The average value for iron (Fe) is 8.4mg×g­1. One source found values ranging from 1.1 to
11.4×g­1, also suggesting comparable concentrations (Haynes et al., 2015).
Total nitrogen (N) is on average 0.76 w/w%. Similar compost in literature are mainly around
0.6 to 2.1% with few showing lower values (Haynes et al., 2015;Sarwar et al., 2010;Tong et al.,
2018;Biala and Wynen, 1998). The biofilter is towards the lower end of this range, indicating that
loss of N since start­up of the filter is a possibility. Since the feed is manure gas, NH3 is supplied
to the filter. However, this feed is not enough to see elevated concentrations in the compost solids.
Phosphorous (P) has an average value of 1.53mg×g­1, and values in literature for similar compost
range from 1.8 to 4.43mg×g­1 (Sarwar et al., 2010;Biala and Wynen, 1998;Tong et al., 2018). The
biofilter compost is below the typical ranges for green compost, suggesting it is highly likely that
the biofilter has reduced in P content over time.
In terms of the macronutrients N and P, the filter is low compared to compost that has not been
used in biofilters. This suggests that original concentrations in the compost are being depleted
when used in a CH4 biofilter context. In terms of micronutrients, copper is lower than expected,
but still in a comparable range to the literature. Iron seems to be present in healthy concentrations.
Methanotrophic activity heavily relies on nutrients, especially N and P (La et al., 2018b). Eventual
depletion will therefore reduce efficiency of the filter.

DNA quantification
Extracted DNA was sent for 16s rRNA amplicon sequencing, but failed. The likely cause appears
to be the DNA quality and purity. Values for 260/280nm absorbance ratio are generally considered
pure at values higher than ~1.8, while the secondary indicator of purity, the 260/230 ratio is con­
sidered pure between 2.0­2.2 (Lucena­Aguilar et al., 2016). Looking at the main purity indicator,
the 260/280nm absorbance ratio, the values are generally below what is considered pure, but in
most cases not considerably (table A.5 in appendix A.6). However, the 260/230 ratio is noticeably
lower than what is considered pure, which can be because of contaminants such as proteins.
Looking at DNA concentrations, the average is 38.9 ±2.7 µg ×g WW compost­1, which is com­
parable to literature values for compost with reported values at 10 ­ 83µg ×g­1 (Wu et al., 2009).
However, DNA concentration depends on the DNA extraction method (Wu et al., 2009), the age
of the compost (Reuter et al., 2009), and the compost source.
3.2
Initial oxidation experiment
Incubation before initial oxidation experiment
The results showed CH4 degradation in less than 24 hours, even after storage in plastic bags at
10°C, in darkness, without addition of O2 and CH4, for longer than 7 months. This experiment
highlighted the potential of growing methanotrophs in liquid under thermophilic conditions.
Initial oxidation experiment
From a first glance, it is obvious that the abiotic controls were unsuccessful as they consumed CH4
and O2, and produced CO2 in a similar fashion to the rest of the bottles (figure 3.2). This suggests
that autoclaving at 121°C for 15 min, with 10 min venting at 115°C is not sufficient to inactivate
the compost microbiome. Future experiments changed protocol to having multiple autoclavations,
as it has been shown to be more effective in soil (Choudhary et al., 2022;Dietrich et al., 2020). In
some cases bacteria survive autoclavation due to production of spores (O’Sullivan et al., 2015),
which is the most dormant state a bacteria can be in to protect itself (Basta & Annamaraju, 2023).
Methanotrophs are known to form spores, especially exospores (Hanson and Hanson, 1996;Sem­
rau et al., 2010). While exospores in general are less heat resistant than endospores (Ramírez et
al., 2023), which can survive temperatures up to 150°C (Basta & Annamaraju, 2023), exospores
in methanotrophic bacteria are heat resistant (Whittenbury et al., 1970b). Future experiments in­
corporated a cooling phase in­between autoclavation cycles. This approach follows the idea of
tyndallization, a process where spores are reverting back into vegetative cells during cooling and
then exterminated through reheating (Basta & Annamaraju, 2023). Four days of warm­up with
CH4 and O2 supply was enough to regain substantial microbial activity and show similar oxidation
rates to biological bottles. Oxidation calculations can be seen in appendix A.7.

It can be observed that double autoclaving the bottles considerably enhanced the reliability of
the abiotic control bottles compared to the initial oxidation experiment. There are no signs of
CH4 reduction throughout the period. This increases certainty in the oxidation test and its rates,
because losses of CH4 due to sampling or leaking bottles are negligible. Looking at O2, there is
an overall trend of slight decrease in the abiotic control bottles. Dissolution based on partitioning
coefficients between gas and liquid phase is unlikely to play a role, as O2 has similar solubility
to CH4. Henry’s Law constant for O2 is generally reported to be 1.25 mol×m­3×Pa­1 at 25°C
where CH4 is slightly higher at 1.4 mol×m­3×Pa­1 (Sander, 2023), which should therefore see a
similar decline. It is a possibility that methanotrophs were eliminated, but some microbes survived
the double autoclavation cycle. The slight decrease in O2 and increase in CO2 could stem from
respiration of the compost whereby organic matter is broken down. O2 consumption is considered
a good measure of compost respiration and stability (Gómez et al., 2006).

Observing CH4 oxidation rates, it is immediately evident that all locations benefit greatly from

thriving microbial community than elsewhere.

For all locations, the dAMS bottle uses less O2 per CH4 than their water counterpart. Thus adding
dAMS favours methanotrophs more than the microbes responsible for respiration. Comparing
locations, it can be seen that location C on average has higher O2 consumption and higher CO2

consumption in compost from aerobic respiration can increase due to accumulation of biomass
after longer periods of CH4 exposure (Scheutz et al., 2011), which can be the case as location C
presumably is a high activity location central in the filter. Location E and F show lower values of
O2:CH4 indicating smaller presence of other microbial communities.
The general stoichiometry for methane oxidation is CH4 + 2O2 →CO2 + 2H2O. However, O2
consumption is often lower in practice, as some of the carbon is diverted from complete oxidation
and instead incorporated into biomass (La et al., 2018b).

of the experiment, and/or biological hydrolysis increased the amount of PO43­ in the liquid. The
bottles consumed NH3 and saw a small amount of nitrate, indicating there is nitrification occur­

and can be formed in noticeable amounts in biofilters (La et al., 2018b). The bottles from one of
the nutrient experiments were tested for N2O after the experiment was conducted to see potential
production. In all cases the N2O were under the detection limit of the GC machine, suggesting
negligible production and/or it is unsuitable to be monitored in batch experiments like this.
3.4
OMP degradation
Biological removal was calculated for all compounds (figure A.18). Compounds with removal of
>15% were used to calculate Kbio, which were DMS, SMX, BTA, BAM, ALA­ESA, and DCF
(figure 3.5). Kbio values are not normalized to biomass, but to a compost concentration of 120g/L,
which makes the values rather low. There are, to the author’s knowledge, not any batch studies
with a similar setup to compare Kbio values to. Across all samples, degradation is higher in C60
compared to C30, which correlates to the fact that C60 showed higher CH4 oxidation rates in all
instances (figure A.20 and A.21 in appendix A.9). Relatively speaking, the difference between Kbio
for BTA in C60 and C30 is much lower than other compounds, suggesting that other activities, such
as heterotrophic degradation, is occurring.
In both Kbio and biological removal %, SMX and BTA show the highest degradation. Methane­
oxidizing cultures have shown degradation of these two compounds under mesophilic conditions
(Benner et al., 2015). The total removal rate is in a comparable range to this experiment (A.18).
This highlights the potential of OMP removal using compost in thermophilic conditions.

Evaluation of CH4 oxidation potential
Across all CH4 oxidation experiments conducted in the thesis, rates were found in the range of
32 to 220µg ×h­1 ×g DW­1. Based on extensive literature, values are reported in the range of 9 to
581µg ×h­1 ×g DW­1, with the majority being in the range of 25 to 250µg ×h­1 ×g DW­1 (Pedersen,
2010). All of these reported rates were conducted in mesophilic conditions. One study found CH4
oxidation rates in the range of 57 to 79µg ×h­1 ×g DW­1 (Scheutz et al., 2017). The results indicate
that the rates are in a comparable range to those reported in the literature, and compost­liquid slurry
provides a suitable growth habitat for methanotrophs in thermophilic conditions.

4
Conclusion
This thesis demonstrated that compost from a full­scale thermophilic biofilter treating manure gas,
when tested in batch experiments with liquid, achieved high CH4 oxidation rates. These results
demonstrate significant potential for the utilization of methanotrophs under thermophilic condi­
tions and possible utilization in compost­liquid slurries. CH4 oxidation rates showed high variabil­
ity within the filter horizontally and vertically, indicating a heterogeneous nature in such a full­scale
filter. High nitrogen consumption combined with nutrient depletion over time could affect long­
term biofilter performance. Furthermore, this research demonstrates the potential to degrade OMPs
using compost under CH4 loading. Out of fourteen compounds tested, six saw biological removal
rates of >15%. Sulfamethoxazole and Benzotriazole saw removal rates much higher compared to
the other compounds tested. Using compost in thermophilic conditions presents a cost­effective
approach to effectively mitigate CH4 emissions, and has potential to degrade OMPs in liquid.
Future research should test additional environmentally relevant OMPs, as the degradation effi­
ciency, as shown in this thesis, varies a lot from compound to compound. To evaluate practical
implementation, the OMP removal process should be explored in a reactor setup, potentially on­
site, to assess its feasibility. More in­depth sorption modelling should be conducted to assess
biokinetics on a deeper level. A DNA extraction method that has been adapted to compost should
be carried out to understand the microbial communities and their variability within the thermophilic
filter. Nutrient concentrations in the filter should be monitored to assess how often action has to
be taken to mitigate nutrient depletion. The costs associated with this should be estimated to see
long­term costs of running a thermophilic biofilter.
