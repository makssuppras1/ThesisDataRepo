Abstract (English)
This thesis investigates catalytic Power-to-Gas methanation from a socio- and
private economic evaluation point of view. The technology appears as one pos-
sible solution for the complex integration of fluctuating renewable electricity
sources into the Danish energy system as it produces storable gas from electric-
ity. The evaluation is based on a feasibility study conducted on a model plant
in Northern Jutland. Changing future economic conditions and technological
progress on the model are considered through two examination periods: 2025-
2044 and 2040-2059. Catalytic Power-to-Gas methanation in the Danish energy
system is found to be economically unfeasible within the next 40 years. Main pa-
rameters that lead to unfeasibility in both examined terms are: low efficiency of
the Power-to-Gas methanation technologies, electricity costs and low forecasted
natural gas prices that compete directly with the synthetic gas. Biomethane
production that precedes Power-to-Gas methanation in this study has greater
potential. This technology is expected to become less dependent on subsidies
in the near future and could help Denmark implement a green transformation
in the natural gas network as well.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The United Nations (UN) identify the increase of the renewable energy share
in the global energy mix and the increase in the rate of improvement of en-
ergy efficiency as crucial steps in ensuring global ”access to affordable, reliable,
sustainable and modern” energy (United Nations Department of Economic and
Social Affairs (UN DESA), 2018). The significance of this goal becomes even
more crucial, since global temperatures have steadily increased over the last
decades due to the anthropogenic emission of greenhouse gases (GHG) to the
atmosphere. The GHG release is a result of human kinds usage of mainly fossil
fuels to gain energy.
According to the Danish Energy Agency’s (ENS) Energy Statistics 2017 (Danish
Energy Agency, 2018a), around 64% of electricity supplied in Denmark stemmed
from renewable sources, mostly from domestic windmills, followed by biomass.
A different picture emerges when focusing on the domestic gas production
(North Sea exploration and upgraded gas from biomass), where in 2017 only
3.7% and in 2018 4.9% originated from biogas upgrading including a total gas
production growth of 4.2% in the same time.(Energinet, 2019)
To further increase this share of green gas in the grid, more biogas upgrading
facilities could be built and additionally incorporate a Power-to-Gas (P2G) unit.
This unit produces hydrogen from electricity when the latter one is cheap and
combines it in a catalytic methanation process with carbon dioxide from the bio-
gas facility to synthetic methane, ready to be fed into the gas grid. This P2G
system could serve as an electricity balancing and energy storage component
==PAGE:18==

in the future energy system. Energinet.dk, the Danish Transmission System
Operator (TSO) for electricity and gas, expects greater variations of electric-
ity generation in the future due to fluctuating renewable energy sources (RES)
driven by wind and solar (Energinet.dk, 2018). The TSO argues in the same
source, that batteries are probably not made for long-term electricity storage
since they need to be very big to store energy over the seasons which would
come with very high costs. This is especially remarkable for solar power, which
is due to Denmark’s location strong in summer, but contributes little in winter.
On the other hand, gas can be used for electricity generation in gas turbines or
in the long term, in fuel cells that provide higher conversion efficiency (Butera
et al., 2019). Furthermore, gas can serve as a fuel, especially in the heavy trans-
portation and shipping sector (Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2014).
A downside of the P2G methanation system is the early technological develop-
ment. The contained electrolysers come with low efficiency, some electrolysis
technologies are in their infancy, contain expensive rare earths and some compo-
nents’ lifetime is unknown. (Götz et al., 2016) A drawback of using biomass for
energy production is the limited availability, as the primary material can also
be used as a source for food or animal feed. Additionally, it competes for land
with alternative uses such as forests, fields for food production and its amenity
value. An intelligent regulation should be applied to mitigate this complication.
A second disadvantage is the continuous input stream of raw biomass to keep
the biomass fermentation process operating. (Tabatabaei & Ghanavati, 2018)
Figure 1.1 shows a basic flow chart of a biomethane generation plant with a
P2G methanation process. Energy plants like this appear attractive for coun-
tries like Denmark, where energy demand is higher at one part of the country,
namely Zealand, but agriculture concentrated at the opposite side of the land
in Jutland. It could create opportunities for the western part of Denmark as
the generation of jobs, expertise and increase the self-reliance on energy for the
country.
It would also enable the possibility for future energy collaboration
projects with developing countries to mitigate GHG emissions and would thus
contribute positively to Denmark’s image as a pioneer of renewable energies.

2.1
Technological State of the Art and Future
Potential
According to Götz et al. (2016), several catalytic P2G methanation demonstra-
tion plants are already in operation as in Japan, Denmark and Germany. The
currently largest facility of this kind is located in Werlte, Germany. The authors
of this article argue, that the P2G system might be an important instrument in
the future, but for being commercially successful, this system needs to overcome
economical barriers first. These obstacles are mostly caused by low efficiency of
the process components, since some of the P2G technologies are in their infancy.
This is confirmed by Qadrdan et al. (2015) who stress that the P2G concept
is nowadays far from being economically feasible due to high investment costs
==PAGE:22==
6
Background
for the contained P2G components, followed by electricity costs and finally due
to the gas selling price. However, Qadrdan et al. (2015) underline, that the
concept could be worth investing in, when other value sources are included, as
electricity grid regulation, production of oxygen, etc.
Energinet.dk (2018) identifies P2G as a potentially interesting component of
the future energy system. If used flexibly, the electrolysis process can consume
electricity when it is cheap and thus support the integration of wind and solar
power. This could also increase the efficient usage of the existing electricity
infrastructure, which could potentially lead to less network infrastructure ex-
pansion. This advantage arises, as energy would not be transported in form of
electricity, but as gas via the existing gas grid. Due to the particular importance
of district heating in the Danish energy system, occurring waste heat from the
electrolysis and methanation process could be effectively used in it.
From an environmental perspective, Reiter & Lindorfer (2015) conclude in a
conducted Life Cycle Analysis (LCA), that the P2G process offers significant
potential to reduce the Global Warming Potential (GWP) of gases compared
to natural resources, but only, if the used electricity for the process stems from
renewable sources. The authors investigated a catalytic P2G methanation pro-
cess and concluded that the electrical power used for the contained electrolysis
must not exceed 113g CO2 emissions per kWh; otherwise the usage of natural
gas resources would emit less CO2 into the environment.

2.2.3
Anaerobic Fermentation
Based on biomass, microorganisms produce biogas and valuable fertiliser in the
form of digestive residues in an oxygen-free atmosphere. The produced gas is a
steam-saturated mixture of mainly methane (CH4), carbon dioxide (CO2) and
traces of nitrogen (N2), hydrogen (H2), hydrogen sulphide (H2S) and ammonia
(NH3). Naturally, this process occurs i.a. in moors, soils as well as in paunch
of ruminant animals. Anthropogenic processes that create biogas are i.a. do-
mestic waste landfill, liquid manure tanks etc. Here, generation of gas is not
intended in contrast to biogas or sewage gas plants. These facilities’ purpose is
the efficient creation of energetic usable biogas.
The anaerobic fermentation to create the biogas depends on a complex mixture
of organisms that all depend differently on each other, but have their own ac-
tivities and growth rate. Due to this, the degradation process of the biomass
depends on the slowest microorganism group. Over the fermentation process,
which is shown in Figure 2.2, the microorganisms’ variety decreases, as the

2.2.5
Biogas Upgrading
The biogas generated by an anaerobic digestion process consists, as mentioned
and explained in Subsection 2.2.3 of mostly CH4 (50-70% in Denmark), (ENS,
Energinet.dk, 2017) but also other gases, mainly CO2. By removing the other
gases from biogas but CH4, a higher energetic and caloric value is achieved,
which turns biogas into biomethane. This is more attractive as it can be fed
into the natural gas grid or be further processed in other chemical reactions.
(Tabatabaei & Ghanavati, 2018) For this, several methods for biogas upgrading
according to ENS, Energinet.dk (2017) exist:
Water Scrubbing - CH4 recovery of >96%
Mature technology, which is based on the higher aqueous solubility of CO2
compared to CH4, but considered as a method less sensitive to biogas impurities.
The implemented configuration is dependent on the water quality and low-cost
availability. H2S is often removed in active carbon filters. (Muñoz et al., 2015)
Chemical Scrubbing - CH4 recovery of 99.5-99.9%
Technology similar to water scrubbing, but uses chemicals.
This makes the
process simpler and more effective, due to the higher CO2 reaction of chemicals
such as alcanol amines, alkali aqueous solutions or organic solvents compared
to water.
Prior H2S removal is highly recommended to prevent e.g.
amine
poisoning. The loss of chemicals is problematic. (Muñoz et al., 2015)
==PAGE:27==
2.2 Technological Background of the Analysed System
11
Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA) - CH4 recovery of 96-98%
Based on selective adsorption of CO2 over CH4 onto porous adsorbents with
high specific surface area such as activated carbon, silica-gel, etc. The 3.8 Å
CO2 molecules are mostly trapped in the 3.7 Å pores, whereas the 3.4 Å CH4
molecules can pass through the material. The biogas flows into the columns with
a pressure of 4-10 bars. The cycle of adsorption and regeneration (Skarstorm
cycle) lasts for 2-10 min. (Muñoz et al., 2015)
Membrane Separation - CH4 recovery >97%
Based on the principle of selective permeation of biogas components through
a semipermeable membrane.
Conventional membranes retain CH4 and N2
whereas they facilitate O2, H2O, CO2 and H2S. Preferred membrane materi-
als are polymeric origin as they have lower manufacturing costs due to easier
manufacturing and easy scalability. Biogas is fed into the process at 20-40 bar.
(Muñoz et al., 2015)
According to ENS, all presented commercial separation methods have currently
(June 2017) the same biomethane production costs. (ENS, Energinet.dk, 2017)
In the future, (ENS, Energinet.dk (2017) expects from 2030 on) two further
technologies will be available:
Cryogenic Separation - CH4 recovery >97%
Uses the different liquefaction and solidification temperatures of the biogas com-
ponents. This process allows a selective separation of the components while
stepwise cooling the biogas down and furthermore, the production of liquefied
biomethane between -162 and -182◦C. This procedure can be performed at 10
bars constant pressure. Regardless of synergy effects with the liquefaction pro-
cess, the technology is not reliably commercialised at full scale. (Muñoz et al.,
2015)
Enzymatic Separation
Captures CO2 in a non-volatile solvent with a biocatalyst in an absorber column.
The biocatalyst accelerates the CO2 absorption using enzymes. Then the CO2
is removed in a stripper column. A reduction in biogas upgrading cost by 25%
is expected. (ENS, Energinet.dk, 2017)

2.3
Feasibility Study
The general purpose of a feasibility study is to analyse the viability of an idea.
In this context, ideas are normally associated with proposed business ventures.
Due to the analysis of a proposed business concept, statements about cash-
flow generation and profits, withstand against experienced risks, viability over
the long term and if the set goals can be achieved, might be answered. This
approach saves time and money for business idea developers. Business ventures
cover a start-up business, a purchase of an existing business, expansion of current
business operations or a new enterprise for an existing business. The outcome
of a feasibility study should give deep insights in the different scenarios analysed
as well as the impacts, strengths and weaknesses of each. (Hofstrand, 2009)
Within the feasibility study conducted in this report, the following instruments
are used and elaborated on:
• Free Cash Flow (FCF) Analysis
• Discount Rate/Cost of Capital
• Net Present Value (NPV)
• Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
• Loss Carry Forward (LCF)
Fabozzi & Drake Peteron (2009) describe the cash flow analysis as ”assessing
the ability of the company to satisfy its contractual obligations and maintain
current dividends and capital expenditure policy without relying on external
financing.” According to the authors, a cash flow without adjustment may be
misleading because it does not reflect the cash outflows necessary for the future
existence of an enterprise. An alternative measure is the free cash flow. In
==PAGE:32==

Background
theory, the free cash flow is the cash flow that remains after the company has
financed all positive net present value projects. Positive net present value
projects describe those capital investment projects where the present value of
expected future cash flows exceeds the present value of project expenditures, all
of which are discounted using the cost of capital. Cost of capital are the costs
that the company has to pay for funding from creditors and shareholders. If a
project returns more than its costs of capital, it is a profitable project.

2.3.1
Socio-Economic Evaluation
The question that is attempted to be answered in a socio-economic (SE) evalu-
ation is if the project is beneficial for the society as a whole. This means that
the outcome of such an evaluation can lead to decisions about which projects
or technologies should be supported politically, e.g. through specific targets or
financial support schemes. This shall finally contribute to the social welfare
contribution in a country.
Therefore, the focus is set on actual costs, meaning that inflation is not appli-
cable, as well as taxation, depreciation, etc. A socio-determined discount rate
is applied. Differently to the private-economic evaluations (Subsection 2.3.2),
externalities which are not included in costs and prices, are taken into account.
(Kitzing et al., 2017b)
2.3.2
Private-Economic Evaluation
A private-economic (PE) evaluation tries to provide private investors informa-
tion for their investment decisions. This is therefore an important source for
investment decisions.
As a basis for the evaluation, costs and prices are used as seen by the market
place, contrary to the SE evaluation (Subsection 2.3.1). This means, that nom-
inal prices, tax payments, depreciation, market determined discount rates etc.
are included. (Kitzing et al., 2017b)

subject to uncertainty, this sensitivity analysis explores the development of the
NPVs from the different electrolysation technology cases within their operation
time frame. The P2G methanation unit is set to run 50% of the year. In this
sensitivity, reduction of electricity prices is considered only, as an increase of
electricity prices would lead to rising system infeasibility.

4.1
Summary of the Results
• The results of the base case scenario show that in both evaluation methods
and operational time frames an investment would not be profitable.
• Analysed scenarios in OT2 result in a less negative NPV than the OT1
case.
• Facilities without P2G process are close to a positive NPV, since the P2G
process comes with high OPEX, mainly electricity costs.
• All scenarios have large summed OPEX and small CAPEX.
• A private connection to a wind park would minimise PE losses compared
to a public grid connection, but would still result in negative NPVs.
• The yearly operational time threshold at which the P2G methanation unit
becomes district a heat producer, is forecasted to increase.
• high EUA certificate prices might lead to a feasible P2G methanation
process.
• The sensitivity analysis reveals that PEM and SOEC electrolysis comes
with high future development uncertainty.

5.4
Discussion on the Model
The created model evaluates the fermentation & biogas upgrading and the P2G
methanation unit on a yearly financial basis. The resolution of the results is
therefore severely limited. Changes in electricity prices on an hourly basis would
give a better picture of reality than applying a yearly average price, as performed
in the model. The used method to account for electricity price variations within
all forecasted years relies on the PDC of 2015 in both, the OT1 and OT2. The
future energy system might due to a higher share of variable RES as wind and
solar be faced to more extreme energy prices than 2015’s PDC reflects. There-
fore, future years might not be represented properly. Further, fixed district heat
prices are assumed which could be an inaccuracy for the future. Gas prices that
deviate from the future forecast could be included more in the model.
The P2G methanation unit in this model is assumed to either run on its full
capacity or be turned off. Therefore, the methanation unit has a size that is
able to process the whole H2 with CO2 to CH4 and would running idle in times
with no H2 production. This leads to additional expenses since the methanation
unit causes stand by costs. A cost reducing solution could here be a storage that
buffers the hydrogen when it is produced and would enable a continuous metha-
nation with a smaller methanation unit. As shown in Table 2.1, the electrolysis
technologies used have different cold start ramp-up times. Depending on the
==PAGE:78==
62

technology applied, these range from seconds to hours. Within the model these
cold start times have been neglected.
The model assumes a continuous supply of raw biomass for the fermenter.
Changes in the supply situation during the year, i.a. due to seasons, are not
taken into account. A changed biomass composition could have an influence on
the amount of biogas produced.
5.5
Potential Revenue Sources of the Power-to-
Gas Process
The P2G methanation process’ revenue situation could be improved by tapping
further revenue sources. As already mentioned in Subsection 2.1, the relatively
pure oxygen occurring at the electrolysis of water could be sold. Furthermore,
revenue could be generated due to electricity net balancing services, as described
by Qadrdan et al. (2015).
Besides the introduced points, the not methanised CO2 from the biogas upgrad-
ing process could be captured and stored. This principle is called bio-energy
with carbon capture and storage (BECCS) and creates negative CO2 emissions
(Honegger & Reiner, 2017). Such a negative emission technology (NET) could
create a SE benefit as it contributes to climate change mitigation.
If NETs
would be payed in the form of tradeable EUA certificates, the usage of BECCS
could be an additional revenue source in PE cases for the investigated system
of this paper. According to Bui et al. (2018), the BECCS technology is seen
as an important building block against global warming. However, considering
the maturity of the technology, it is not yet widely used. Further investigations
could be made within this field.

6.1
Conclusion
This thesis analyses the economic feasibility of catalytic Power-to-Gas (P2G)
methanation in the future danish energy system.
It is performed on a hy-
pothetical biomethane generation process that incorporates a P2G methanation
module, located in Northern Jutland. In order to achieve the best possible prox-
imity to reality, future prediction values for natural gas and electricity prices in
Denmark are taken into account. The availability of biomass in this region and
its transportation costs are considered. To account for the technological and
economical conditions in the future, two examination periods are constructed:
the years 2025-2044 and 2040-2059. On these periods, a socio -and private eco-
nomic analysis is conducted, as society and private investors experience different
impacts on their feasibility of catalytic Power-to-Gas methanation.
Under expected future conditions for technological progress and the economi-
cal environment as forecasted by the Danish Energy Agency (ENS), catalytic
P2G methanation operating at the years 50% cheapest electricity price hours
will not be feasible within the two considered time periods. This is valid for

the socio-and private economic perspective. For both economical viewpoints,
low technological efficiency within the P2G process and high electricity prices
as well as competition due to the lower expected natural gas price compared to
the produced gas are found to be the largest burdens.
A ”base case” without P2G methanation process is found to financially perform
significantly better. Even if the process is not yet self-sustaining in the defined
scenario framework, it is developing towards a promising direction. In the socio-
economic perspective, higher future CO2 emission allowance (EUA) certificate
prices could turn the balance. Within the private-economic analysis, it is found
that the ”base case” without P2G will become profitable with less and less sub-
sidies or higher EUA certificate prices. In both economic approaches, this is due
on the one hand to technical development (especially within the biogas upgrad-
ing) and on the other hand to increasing natural gas price future expectations,
which makes biomethane more competitive.
A feasible case for P2G containing systems within the socio-economic evalua-

best technological development and a large increase of EUA certificate prices
compared to today. Even though, the needed EUA certificate price for the later
investigated future time frame is determined to be lower than in the earlier
frame, it will probably not be covered by International Energy Agency (IEA)
(2016) future EUA certificate market price expectations in the examined pe-

infancy or exist on a smaller scale only, forecasted values are associated with
great uncertainty.
In any case, private-economic investors would additionally be faced with elec-
tricity grid tariffs. To avoid these, a private direct grid connection to a fictive
near by wind park could be an option. Due to a fixed electricity price agreement,
the feasibility study becomes less negative compared to a direct grid connection.
Electricity from a wind park would further contribute with low CO2 equivalent
to an environmental friendly synthetic gas product. This benefit is probably rel-
evant in the beginning of the first examined operational time frame only. The
electricity’s CO2 equivalent in the later operational scenario from 2040-2059 is
expected to be low, as Denmarks electricity will originate from renewable sources
(Energinet.dk, 2018). A drawback of the direct connection scenario could be the
limited electricity utilisation due to the wind parks capacity factor.
As the electricity price is subject market variations, a yearly operation time
of the P2G methanation process according to these market price variations is
analysed. The results show that the lower the electricity price and the lower
the P2G unit’s yearly operational time, the less negative the economical result.
This also does not vary with a varying P2G methanation unit size change. Both
investigations show again the great influence of the electricity price on the sys-
tem feasibility.

The financial analysis in a socio- and private economic view has shown that
under the given circumstances the catalytic P2G methanation is unprofitable.
This is mainly due to high electricity costs, low efficiency of the P2G process
components and competition of the produced gas with low natural gas prices.
Sophisticated environmental policies to mitigate climate change could support
the technology. An active improvement of the European Union Emissions Trad-
ing System that lead to more stable and higher EUA prices would support the
feasibility of the catalytic P2G methanation. Besides this, the integration of fur-
ther irregular renewable electricity sources demands for energy storage. Here,
the P2G methanation could be an interesting approach as the synthetic gas is
storable, also over long the long term. The possibility of negative emissions
by collecting the stripped CO2 from the biogas upgrading facility could help to
mitigate climate change and create further socio-economic value.
In conclusion, all forecasts are subject to uncertainty and the model itself is
based on many assumptions. Even though several sensitivity analyses for the
described technology have been carried out, the right future development might
not be covered by those.
6.2
Future Work
As the model conducts a financial analysis based on relatively inaccurate aver-
ages for electricity prices, the analysis could be conducted in a more precise way
by incorporating hourly price fluctuations. This would also include electricity
price scenarios with more extreme values as it can be expected for the future due
to an increase of variable renewable electricity sources. Furthermore, changing
future prices for district heat could be considered within the model instead of
fixed ones. Gas prices that may differ from the forecast of the Danish Energy
Agency may be included in future studies. An analysis on how the catalytic P2G
methanation process combined with a biomethane production can be beneficial
for the future national energy system as a whole would deliver more valuable
insights on the technology’s future role. Furthermore, externalities as the influ-
ence of crop harvesting to use them for biogas production could be taken into
the socio-economic evaluation. Political factors such as the wish to mitigate en-
ergetic dependence on other countries could also be considered as an externality
in further studies, as this system could be one solution.
