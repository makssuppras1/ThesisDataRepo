Abstract  
 
One of the major issues regarding wastewater treatment is disposal of the sludge 
(Uggetti, et al., 2010). Sludge treatment reed beds (STRBs) are environmentally friendly 
alternatives to conventional sludge treatment methods. Biological degradation of organ-
ic matter in STRBs produces greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2), 
methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O), depending on system management, sludge 
composition and climatic factors (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). With STRB gaining momentum 
it is important to know their potential contribution to global warming.  
The aim of this study was to assess seasonal variations of CH4, CO2 and N2O emissions 
from an STRB in Helsinge and to determine its annual global warming potential (GWP). 
A field work campaign was carried out from January to March 2016 to quantify the GHG 
emissions from two different basins (Basin 4 and 2) during winter using the closed 
chamber method. Spatial variations of the fluxes and temporal variations of interpolated 
data were analysed. Results from Basin 4 and 2 were compared, and results from Basin 
4 were compared to data collected during spring, summer and fall of the same basin. At 
last an assessment was made on whether STRBs are environmentally friendly alterna-
tives to conventional sludge treatment methods.  
The interpolated CO2 emissions ranged from 694 to 2068 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 4 and from 
197 to 600 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 2. The CH4 emissions were between 9 and 62 mg∙m-2∙h-1 
in Basin 4 and between 2 and 22 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 2. The N2O emissions ranged from 2 
to 19 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 4 and from 0 to 8 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 2.  
A strong correlation between fluxes of CO2 and N2O (R2=0.99) in Basin 4 during winter 
indicated that CO2 is anaerobically produced as a by-product of denitrification. Methane 
fluxes that also peak on the same days indicated anaerobic production of all three GHGs. 
Anaerobic conditions in the STRB are due to constant water saturation because of lower 
evapotranspiration and drainage rates. Variations between seasons were significant 
with highest CO2 and CH4 emissions during summer and lowest during winter and high-
est N2O emissions during fall and lowest during spring. Seasonal variations are caused 
by climatic factors such as temperature and oxygen availability in the sludge.  
The GWP (2014) for a 100 year time frame is found to be approximately 60 kg∙m-2∙yr-1 
(21.5 kg CO2-eq∙PE-1∙yr-1), which is considerably lower than conventional sludge treat-
ment methods. Annually, CH4 and N2O contribute equally to global warming, and future 
studies should therefore include both GHGs. Based on the study it seems that STRBs are 
environmentally friendly alternatives to conventional sludge treatment methods.

Resumé   
 
En af de helt store udfordringer med hensyn til spildevandsrensning er bortskaffelse af 
slammet (Uggetti, et al., 2010). Biologiske slamanlæg er miljøvenlige alternativer til 
konventionelle slambehandlingsmetoder. Biologisk nedbrydning af organisk materiale i 
de biologiske slamanlæg producerer drivhusgasemissionerne kuldioxid (CO2), metan 
(CH4) og nitrogenoxid (N2O), afhængigt af driften af anlægget, slamsammensætningen og 
klimaparametre (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). I takt med at biologiske slamanlæg flere steder 
bliver den foretrukne slambehandlingsmetode, er det vigtigt at kende deres bidrag til 
global opvarmning.  
Formålet med dette studie var at vurdere sæsonvariationerne i emissioner af CH4, CO2 
og N2O fra et biologisk slamanlæg i Helsinge, og at bestemme dets årlige bidrag til global 
opvarmning. I perioden fra januar til marts 2016 blev der udført feltarbejde med henblik 
på at kvantificerer drivhusgasemissionerne fra to forskellige bede (Bed 4 og 2) for en 
vinterperiode ved brug af ”aflukket kammer”-metoden. Spatiale variationer i fluxene og 
temporale variationer i det interpolerede data blev analyseret. Resultaterne fra Bed 4 og 
2 blev sammenlignet, og resultater fra Bed 4 blev sammenlignet med data, der var ind-
samlet fra samme bed under feltarbejder udført i foråret, sommeren og efteråret. Til 
sidst blev det vurderet om biologiske slamanlæg er miljøvenlige alternativer til konven-
tionelle slambehandlingsmetoder.  
De interpolerede CO2 emissioner rangerede fra 694 til 2068 mg∙m-2∙t-1 i Bed 4 og fra 197 
til 600 mg∙m-2∙t-1 i Bed 2. Metanemissionerne rangerede imellem 9 og 62 mg∙m-2∙t-1 i Bed 
4 og imellem 2 og 22 mg∙m-2∙t-1 i Bed 2. Nitrogenoxidemissioner var imellem 2 og 19 
mg∙m-2∙t-1 i Bed 4 og imellem 0 og 8 mg∙m-2∙t-1 i Bed 2.  
En markant sammenhæng mellem CO2 og N2O fluxe (R2=0.99) i Bed 4 viser, at CO2 er 
produceret anaerobisk som et biprodukt af dinitrifikation. Metanfluxe der også peaker 
de samme dage tyder på, at alle tre gasser er produceret anaerobisk. Anaerobiske for-
hold i slammet skyldes konstant vandmætning grundet lavere evapotranspiration og 
afvanding. Sæsonvariationerne var statistisk signifikante med højeste CO2 og CH4 emis-
sioner om sommeren og laveste om vinteren, og højeste N2O emissioner om efteråret og 
lavest om foråret. Sæsonvariationerne skyldes klimaparametre som temperatur samt 
oxygenindholdet i slammet.  
Potentialet for global opvarmning for 100 års tidshorisont blev udregnet til ca. 60 kg∙m-
2∙år-1 (21.5 kg CO2-eq∙PE-1∙år-1), hvilket er betydeligt lavere end konventionelle slambe-
handlingsmetoder. På årsbasis bidrager CH4 og N2O lige meget til den globale opvarm-
ning, og videre studier burde derfor fokuserer på begge drivhusgasser. Baseret på resul-
taterne fra dette studie, tyder det på, at biologiske slamanlæg er miljøvenlige alternati-
ver til konventionelle slambehandlingsmetoder.

1 Introduction 
Wastewater can be defined as excess water from households or industry that in any way 
is contaminated. It is treated before discharged to the environment to avoid health is-
sues for humans and the environment. The wastewater contains nutrients such as car-
bon, nitrogen and phosphorus that mainly originate from stool and urea (Metcalf & 
Eddy, 2003). The wastewater treatment in Denmark includes mechanical treatment, 
biologically enhanced nitrogen removal by activated sludge processes and chemically 
and biologically phosphorus removal. Wastewater treatment by biologically activated 
sludge processes produces considerable amounts of surplus sludge as a by-product 
(Olsson, et al., 2014). One of the major issues regarding wastewater treatment is the dis-
posal of the sludge since it is a complex procedure (Uggetti, et al., 2010). If the nutrients 
in the sludge are preserved, it can be used as fertiliser after treatment. Treatment of ex-
cess sludge from wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) includes dewatering and miner-
alization.  
Sludge treatment reed beds (STRB) are an alternative, low cost and low energy con-
sumption sludge treatment method compared to conventional methods, such as centri-
fuging or transportation and storage (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). They are furthermore easy 
to integrate into existing WWTP, and the technique relies on solar radiation, gravity and 
biological processes rather than mechanics or addition of chemicals or polymers 
(DeMaeseneer, 1997).  
The first STRB in Denmark was established in 1988 (Nielsen & Willoughby, 2005). Since 
then, several municipalities have introduced one or more STRBs and in 2009 there were 
more than 140 operating systems in Denmark (Uggetti, et al., 2010). The purpose is long 
term reduction and concentration of the sludge by natural dewatering and mineralisa-
tion.  
Dewatering occurs partly by evapotranspiration from the sludge surface and the reeds 
(Phragmites australis), and partly by drainage through gravity. Mineralisation takes 
place due to biological degradation of organic matter. The biological degradation pro-
cesses include aerobic respiration, fermentation processes, nitrification/denitrification, 
methanogenesis and methane oxidation, and they result in production of the greenhouse 
gases (GHGs) carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O).  
The processes and thus GHG production depend on system management, sludge compo-
sition and climatic factors (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). With STRB gaining momentum it is 
important to know their potential contribution to global warming, however only limited 
studies currently exist.

1.1 Aim  
The primary objectives of this study are to assess seasonal variations of CH4, CO2 and 
N2O emissions from a STRB in Helsinge and to determine its annual contribution to 
global warming.  
This is achieved by carrying out a field work campaign from January to March 2016 to 
collect data using the closed chamber method for quantification of GHG emissions dur-
ing winter. Data from two basins (Basin 4 and 2) are collected.  
Furthermore spatial variations of the fluxes and temporal variations of interpolated data 
are analysed, and the results are compared to data collected during spring, summer and 
fall of the same STRB to identify the global warming potential (GWP).  
At last an assessment is made on whether STRBs are environmentally friendly alterna-
tives to conventional sludge treatment methods.

2 Background  
There are a number of technologies and processes that are important to understand in 
order to assess the contribution of STRBs to global warming. First of all, the reason why 
GHG emissions from STRBs are important to quantify is their contribution to global 
warming, and thus their impact on the climate. The origin of GHGs from STRBs is the 
biological processes that happen in the sludge. The processes are connected to the 
sludge composition, and the design and operation of the STRBs.  
The following sections cover GHG emissions, their sources within STRBs, and all the 
steps in the sludge cycle from initial black- and grey wastewater to the final biosolids 
that are used as fertiliser.  
2.1 Greenhouse gas emissions  
The climate is constantly changing. The last 30 years have been the warmest period of 
the last 1400 years in the Northern hemisphere. Anthropogenic GHG emissions are at 
their highest recorded levels, resulting in exceptionally high concentrations of CO2, CH4, 
and N2O in the atmosphere (IPCC, 2014).  
Greenhouse gases are the term for gasses that absorb and emit infrared radiation at spe-
cific wavelengths. The main greenhouse gases are CO2, CH4, N2O, water (H2O) and ozone 
(O3) (IPCC, 2014). These gases trap energy in the form of heat between the atmosphere 
and the surface of the earth, which causes the temperature of the earth to rise (global 
warming). This property is why they are called GHGs, because the effect is similar to the 
one happening in greenhouses (IPCC, 2014).  
2.1.1 Globing warming potentials  
The GHGs do not contribute equally to global warming. The GHGs differ from each other 
in two main ways; their radiative efficiency (their ability to absorb energy) and their 
lifetime (how long they will stay in the atmosphere). Carbon dioxide lasts for thousands 
of years in the atmosphere, CH4 for a decade and N2O for about 100 years (IPCC, 2014).  
Carbon dioxide is the most commonly known GHG because of the public awareness of 
CO2 emissions, and people’s effort in trying to decrease their own carbon footprint. 
However, CO2 is not the most contributing GHG to global warming. The contribution of 
each GHG is called its “global warming potential” (GWP). The GWPs are given in relation 
to CO2 in CO2-equivalents (CO2-eq), and represent the effects of the accumulation of the 
GHGs in the atmosphere over varying time spans, and their contribution of radiative 
forcing. Carbon dioxide equivalents are calculated as the amount of CO2 emissions that 
would cause the same radiative forcing as the GHG in focus over a given period of time. 
(IPCC, 2014). This standard is a useful way of associating their different potential con-

Biogenic CO2 emissions have been previously fixed from the atmosphere through plants 
photosynthesis, and are therefore atmospherically neutral. Biogenic CO2 emissions are 
defined as those related to the natural carbon cycle, and thus produced by decomposi-
tion of organic matter (IPCC, 2007), as well as those resulting from the production, har-
vest, combustion, digestion, fermentation, decomposition, and processing of biologically 
based materials (EPA , 2014). Therefore CO2 emissions from STRBs are not included in 
the calculations of the potential contribution to global warming.  
Anthropogenic non-CO2 emissions were estimated to be 10500 Mt CO2-eq on a global 
scale in 2010, of which 963 Mt CO2-eq (≈7 %) originates from wastewater treatment (≈6 
% from CH4 and ≈1 % from N2O) (EPA, 2006).  
2.2 Reed beds: Sludge reduction   
Sludge treatment reed beds are used for long-term reduction of sludge. The sludge re-
duction processes consists of dewatering (drainage, evapotranspiration) and minerali-
sation of organic matter in the sludge by biological processes (Nielsen & Willoughby, 
2005).  
This section will focus on the biological processes that are relevant to the measured 
GHGs (CO2, N2O and CH4). The inorganic gaseous compounds are released through mi-
crobial reactions going on in the sludge during degradation of organic matter. These 
processes include nitrification/denitrification, aerobic respiration, sulphate reduction,

(Teiter & Mander, 2005).

2.2.3 Methanogenesis  
The formation of CH4 by microbes (methanogens) under anaerobic conditions is called 
methanogenesis. The methanogens uses carbon sources to respire (Kadlec & Wallace, 
2008), and depending on the source different products are produced. Two examples are 
shown in Eq. 6 and Eq. 7:

The process depends on temperature, and on the oxygen availability of the soil, and thus 
how well the soil is drained (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). Independent on the carbon source 
CH4 is always produced; however in some cases also CO2 is produced.  
2.2.4 Methane oxidation   
Methane can be oxidized to CO2 by methanotrophs under aerobic conditions. Methane is 
oxidised to methanol (CH3OH), formaldehyde (CH2O), formate (HCOO-) and CO2

Methane oxidation results in production of CO2 and negative fluxes of CH4.  
2.2.5 Accumulation in sludge   
Other important substances that however are not covered in this study are phosphorus 
and heavy metals. They are bound to the sludge residue and tend to accumulate in the 
reed beds. Concentrations have been proven to increase over time and depth (Nielsen & 
Bruun, 2014).

2.3.1 Composition of wastewater and biosolids    
Sewer systems are either separated or combined with respect to household wastewater 
and stormwater. Wastewater in a separate system is defined as liquid waste and water 
carried waste that is produced in households, industries or institutions. It is mainly con-
stituted by excess water from the community. In a combined system also stormwater, 
surface water and groundwater may be present (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003). Water carried 
wastes include for instance cotton buds, agglutinated grease particles, and stools. The 
disease-causing microorganisms that live in human intestines are called pathogens, and 
they are released with bowel movement to the sewer system. Pathogens include among 
others Escherichia coli and salmonella (Madigan, et al., 2012).  
The nutrients found in wastewater comprise carbon, nitrogen and phosphorus, and they 
mainly origins from stools and urea. Other compounds found in wastewater are biode-
gradable organic matter measured as biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) or chemical 
oxygen demand (COD), suspended and dissolved solids, and xenobiotics such as heavy 
metals. (Winther, et al., 2011).  
A wide-spread method to determine the composition of the influent wastewater and 
biosolids to the WWTP is fractionation. The composition of the influent is important 
when designing the treatment facilities in the WWTP to avoid exceeding levels of con-
taminants in the effluent (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  
2.3.2 Danish wastewater legislation  
Wastewater cannot be discharged directly to the environment due to its composition of 
pathogens, organic matter, nutrients and possible toxic, mutagenic, and carcinogenic 
compounds (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003). This is mainly due to prevention of diseases in the 
community by avoiding direct (discharge to the sea, lakes, streams or soil) and indirect 
(through drinking water) contact with the wastewater (Miljøstyrelsen, 2003). If the 
wastewater is discharged to the environment without preliminary treatment, the nutri-
ents will lead to algal bloom in the receiving aquatic environment, and thereby cause 
local oxygen depletion. Additionally, anaerobic decomposition of organic matter in the 
wastewater may lead to nuisances such as production of unpleasant odours due to re-
duction of sulphate to sulphide by anaerobic microorganisms (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  
The Danish government has therefore legislated that all wastewater produced in Den-
mark has to be collected and treated before discharge. They also state that utilisation of 
wastewater sludge on agricultural lands should be done in a way where pathogenic mi-
croorganisms are not transmitted to humans (Miljøstyrelsen, 2003).  
2.3.3 Wastewater treatment and sludge production  
Wastewater treatment is composed of various unit operations and unit processes. These 
consist of treatment by physical-, biological- and chemical means respectively. Unit op-
erations and unit processes are overall terms that cover the different levels of 
==PAGE:23==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 8 - 
 
wastewater treatment in a WWTP. These are: preliminary, primary, advanced primary, 
secondary (with and without nutrient removal), tertiary and advanced treatment. 
(Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  
2.3.3.1 Preliminary treatment  
Preliminary treatment removes all the coarser materials in the influent, such as cotton 
buds or sanitary towels that may cause operational problems if stuck upstream in the 
system. This is done by mechanical filtration through screenings or grids to allow only 
particles of a certain size enter the WWTP. The removed solids are either grinded, and 
returned to the wastewater, or compressed until dewatered and incinerated. (Metcalf & 
Eddy, 2003). Due to preliminary treatment, coarse materials are not expected in the 
sludge that is loaded to the STRB.  
2.3.3.2 Primary- and advanced primary treatment  
Primary treatment is the term for gravitational removal of TSS and organic matter from 
the wastewater. This especially includes sand and gravel to avoid unnecessary abrasion 
of the system. The primary settling is performed in a primary settling tank (or grit 
chamber) by removal of readily settable solids and floating particles (Metcalf & Eddy, 
2003). When the water flows into the settling tank that is already filled with water, some 
of the water will have an upward flow direction and some will flow downward. Settling 
occurs due to gravity when the velocity of the upward flow direction (Vw) is smaller than 
the settling velocity of the solids (Vs). To the contrary, if Vw > Vs the solids will flush out 
of the tank and onward into the system (Grum & Vezzaro, 2015). The expression “ad-
vanced primary treatment” refers to physical- and chemical enhanced clarification, for 
instance the addition of chemicals for flocculation, which binds the particles together to 
provide a greater mass and thus a higher settling velocity (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  
2.3.3.3 Secondary treatment  
Secondary treatment includes the chemical, biological -, and physical unit processes go-
ing on in a WWTP. These are the processes that happen in the activated sludge tanks, for 
instance nitrification/denitrification, phosphor removal, and final settling of particles in 
the secondary clarifier (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  
The nitrification/denitrification processes in the WWTP corresponds to the processes in 
the STRBs that are already explained. Nitrogen enters the WWTP mainly in the form of 
ammonium, and is oxidised to nitrate by nitrification. Total nitrogen in the surplus acti-
vated sludge that is loaded to the STRB consist therefore primarily of ammonium and 
nitrate.  
Phosphor removal is performed by incorporation of phosphor in TSS and can be both 
biologically- or chemically enhanced by microorganisms or by chemical precipitates re-
spectively. Biologically enhanced phosphorus removal takes place in the activated 
sludge tanks under alternating aerobic- and anaerobic conditions (Henze, et al., 2008). 
Chemical precipitation for phosphorus removal is done by adding salts of multivalent 
==PAGE:24==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 9 - 
 
ions to form precipitants of phosphates (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003). Both types of phosphor 
removal lead to accumulation of phosphorous in the activated sludge that eventually is 
pumped to the STRB.  
Final settling of the solids in the wastewater takes place in a secondary settling tank (or 
secondary clarifier). The principle is the same as for the primary treatment. After sepa-
ration, some of the settled sludge is returned to the activated sludge tank. This fraction 
is called “return activated sludge”. The other fraction is pumped to disposal. (Henze, et 
al., 2008).  
2.3.3.4 Sludge disposal  
A great concern with regards to wastewater treatment is disposal of the sludge in a sus-
tainable way (Henze, et al., 2008). Sludge disposal is one of the most expensive and diffi-
cult issues in wastewater engineering. There are different ways to dispose the sludge, 
both as liquid biosolids or dried (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  Liquid sludge is applied to ag-
ricultural land by vehicles where it is allowed to partially dry before it is incorporated 
into the soil by disking or ploughing. Limitations regarding utilization of liquid biosolids 
are related to the vehicular transportation. The vehicles may ruin crop yield due to com-
pression of the soil. Furthermore, the total mass of liquid and solids is much higher 
compared to dried sludge, which demands a bigger storage capacity of the trucks 
(Metcalf & Eddy, 2003).  
A research subject that becomes more and more in focus is dewatering and dewaterabil-
ity of the sludge (Henze, et al., 2008), as dewatering of the sludge reduce the costs of 
sludge handling, transport and final disposal (Uggetti, et al., 2010). One widely used 
method for dewatering is high solids centrifuges that increase the extraction of water 
and thus the concentration of solids. Application of centrifuged sludge is similar to that 
of liquid (Metcalf & Eddy, 2003), however the energy requirement and operation- and 
maintenance costs are higher (Uggetti, et al., 2010).  
Sludge treatment reed beds combine sludge storage, dewatering and mineralisation 
(Nielsen, 2007), and transportation related issues do not exist, as the sludge is pumped 
directly from the WWTP to the STRB. A well designed and functioning STRB can reach a 
dry matter content of 20-30 % (Uggetti, et al., 2010), contrary to conventional sludge 
treatment technologies, such as centrifuging, that typically reaches a dry matter content 
of 20 % (Olsson, et al., 2014).  
2.4 Reed beds: System design   
The system design creates the settings for the mineralization processes going on in the 
sludge, and is therefore connected to the production of GHG emissions. The design of the 
system determines for instance the dewatering rate that is influenced by evapotranspi-
ration (reed density) and drainage (porosity of filter layer). Anaerobic conditions lead to 
the production of CO2 (denitrification, methanogenesis), CH4 (methanogenesis) and N2O 
==PAGE:25==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 10 - 
 
(denitrification). Aerobic conditions are favoured and lead to CO2 (respiration, methane 
oxidation).  
Each basin consists of a membrane, filter layer, sludge residue, reeds, a sludge loading 
system and a reject water and aeration system. Figure 1 illustrates the design and com-
position of a reed bed. Each basin is furnished with a waterproof membrane in the bot-
tom for preventing leakage (Uggetti, et al., 2010).  
 
Figure 1: Design of reed bed systems (Orbicon, 2016)  
2.4.1 Filter  
The filter is placed on top of the reject water and aeration system. The filter is 55 – 60 
cm before sludge loading and built up of different layers: 30 – 45 cm of pebble gravel, 
geotextile, 15 cm sand and a growth layer (Nielsen, 2007). The coarser layer has a high-
er porosity and a larger pore volume, which results in faster drainage of water com-
pared to the sludge layer (Stefanakis & Tsihrintzis, 2011), and water is not stagnated in 
the bottom and anaerobic conditions are therefore minimised.  
2.4.2 Sludge loading system  
The sludge loading system pumps sludge from the WWTP with a loading rate of 50 – 60 
kg DM∙m-2∙yr-1 onto the surface of the reed beds. The hydraulic and dry matter load is 
uniform across the surface of the basins to minimize spatial variations. This is secured 
by dimensioning of the basin to the pumping capacity, which depends on sludge viscosi-
ty, pump performance, infiltration velocity of the water through the filter, and the num-
ber of sludge distributors (Nielsen, 2007). The sludge loading rate is important for the 
performance of the STRB, as it affects the processes taking place. A high loading rate in-
creases the plant biomass which increases the evapotranspiration. Furthermore, the 
==PAGE:26==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 11 - 
 
amount of water that finally has to be dewatered increases with the hydraulic load 
(Stefanakis & Tsihrintzis, 2011).  
2.4.3 Reject water and aeration system  
The reject water and aeration system consists of a perforated drain pipe on top of the 
membrane, and primarily returns the filtered water to the WWTP. Furthermore it works 
as ventilation to avoid anaerobic conditions in the lower sludge residue layers. The pipes 
are over dimensioned to avoid retention of water in the basin, which also causes anaer-
obic conditions, and to avoid flooding the roots of the reeds. The dewatering capacity is 
triggered by a variety of parameters such as sludge production, quality and climate 
(Nielsen, 2007).  
2.4.4 The reeds  
The purpose of the reeds is to increase the dewatering by evapotranspiration and by 
mechanical interference in the sludge residue, caused by movement of the stems. The 
movements maintain porosity around the roots and crack the dried sludge, which allow 
oxygen diffusion. Decay of the roots additionally provides pores for water infiltration 
and aeration throughout the sludge layers (Nielsen, 2007).  
2.5 Reed beds: System operation  
Operation of the system is as important for the processes as the design, and therefore 
also influences the production of GHGs. The operation cycle of a sludge reed bed system 
is on average ten years and the total lifetime of a system is approximately 30 years. The 
operation cycle (or phase) consists of a number of periods: commissioning, normal load-
ing, emptying and reestablishment of the system (Nielsen & Willoughby, 2005).  
2.5.1 Commissioning  
The first part of the phase is a commissioning period of two years, which consist of the 
planting season and the two following growing seasons. In this period the loading is 
adapted to the development of the reeds to avoid replanting. The purpose of this period 
is to provide optimal growing conditions for the newly seeded reeds (Nielsen & 
Willoughby, 2005). If the reeds do not have time to develop due to over loading in the 
commissioning period, they will not grow and spread to a sufficient density in the basin, 
which will lead to inefficient dewatering and anaerobic conditions.  
2.5.2 Normal loading   
The second part of the phase is the loading and dewatering beginning in the third or 
fourth year of the operation cycle when the vegetation is fully developed. The loading is 
increased to keep up with the amounts of sludge produced in the WWTP (Nielsen & 
Willoughby, 2005). The operation of the STRBs alternates between loading and a resting 
period to secure efficient dewatering. Each basin is loaded one to three times per day for 
==PAGE:27==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 12 - 
 
three to eight days, followed by a resting period of 40 – 50 days. Also in this period over 
loading will result in anaerobic conditions (Nielsen, 2011).  
2.5.3 Emptying and reestablishment   
An emptying prognosis assures that the overall sludge treatment plant runs smoothly by 
avoiding overload of some basins (Nielsen & Willoughby, 2005). Typically, one or two 
basins are emptied at a time and are thus excluded from receiving sludge from 1 – 2 
months to 2 years before emptying. The purpose is to improve dryness and mineralisa-
tion (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). After a basin is emptied, the reestablishment begins, and the 
cycle starts over with commissioning. However, the reed is expected to re-establish itself 
more quickly compared to the first commissioning stage (Nielsen & Willoughby, 2005). 
After emptying, the sludge is deposited and allowed to mature until it is ready to be dis-
tributed on agricultural land.  
2.6 Climatic factors   
The various processes going on in a STRB are impacted by climatic factors, such as solar 
radiation, precipitation, wind speed, temperature, humidity and barometric pressure 
(Stefanakis & Tsihrintzis, 2011). Evapotranspiration is the main contributing process to 
dewatering of the reed beds. A low dewatering rate due to low solar radiation, high hu-
midity or high precipitation rate results in anaerobic conditions in the sludge and thus 
the production of CO2, CH4 and N2O. Evapotranspiration during winter is expected to 
take place to a limited degree compared to other seasons, whereas increased plant activ-
ity during summer due to higher temperatures increases the evapotranspiration and 
thus dewatering of the sludge (Stefanakis & Tsihrintzis, 2011). A higher dewatering rate 
of the sludge is expected to lead to more favourable aerobic conditions, and a higher 
production of CO2 relative to CH4 and N2O. Higher temperatures also enhance microbial 
activity, leading to increased GHG emissions compared to the colder months.

3 Methodology  
Greenhouse gas emissions from Helsinge STRB have been measured three times before; 
ten days during spring 2015 (March – April), ten days summer 2015 (July – August) and 
ten days duringfall 2014 (October – November). Results from the spring campaign have 
not been published, and the results from summer and fall have been published by stu-
dents in a master thesis (Labeau, 2015) and a bachelor thesis (Andersen & Schultz, 
2015), respectively. The campaign in the present study was carried out over eleven days 
during winter 2016 (January – February) to achieve flux data for the last season. In all 
campaigns the same methodology was used with minor adjustments. The methodology 
composes of fieldwork and data treatment, and is described in the following sections.  
3.1 Site description  
3.1.1 Helsinge STRB  
Helsinge STRB (56°00'42.5"N; 12°11'38.1"E) was established in 1996 with ten basins of 
1050 m2 each (Nielsen & Willoughby, 2005). In 2014 four additional basins were im-
plemented to decrease loading on the remaining basins. The current capacity of Helsinge 
STRB is 630 metric tonnes dry solids per year and the maximum area loading rate is 60 
kg ds∙m-2∙yr-1, which is recommended for STRBs receiving activated sludge (Nielsen & 
Willoughby, 2005). The established plants are common reed (Phragmites australis) that 
are able to grow (to some extent) in watery, muddy and anaerobic conditions with 
changing water levels, pH and salinity (DeMaeseneer, 1997).  
The sludge that is loaded to the STRB is surplus activated sludge and sludge from final 
settling tanks in Helsinge WWTP. The WWTP receive water corresponding to 42000 PE, 
and the treatment covers mechanical treatment, biologically nitrification/denitrification 
and chemical phosphorus removal (Gribvand Spildevand, 2014). The annual sludge pro-
duction volume of the WWTP is 500 – 600 tonnes of dry solids that are loaded to the 
STRB. Another sludge fraction that is loaded with a random frequency is concentrated 
activated sludge from four smaller WWTPs in Helsinge that is dewatered in a centrifuge. 
The two types of sludge are collected in a buffer tank and mixed prior added to the STRB 
(Nielsen & Willoughby, 2005). When the basins are emptied, the bottom 20 cm, approx-
imately, is left in the basin to avoid destruction of the filter layer, resulting in a constant 
layer of original sludge from 1996.

3.3.2 Time weighted average  
When computing the average flux for the winter season, it was taken into account that 
the intervals between measuring days were not similar. This was done by weighing the

measuring day were weighed by the number of days since loading stopped. This method 
secures that the entire period is covered, and therefore the average fluxes are presented 
as time weighted averages (TWA).

3.3.4 Statistical analyses  
A number of correlations are determined throughout the report. To identify correlations

cant difference between the variance within the datasets and the variance of the means 
between the data sets. This is described more thoroughly in Appendix C. The ANOVA

4 Results   
The field work campaign resulted in accumulated gas concentrations of CO2, CH4 and 
N2O from both Basin 4 and Basin 2. The gas concentrations were converted into fluxes 
that were analysed regarding spatial variations and interpolated to analyse seasonal 
variations and to compare the two basins. First, the fluxes from Basin 4 alone are pre-
sented and spatial variations are analysed. Second, the interpolated results from Basin 4 
are shown, and the seasonal and annual variations are analysed. Then the fluxes and 
interpolated results from Basin 2 are presented, and at last a comparison between the 
two basins is made.

5 Discussion    
In the following sections, the results will be discussed. First, the spatial variations of 
GHG emissions within the two basins will be explained, followed by a discussion of the 
interpolated emissions from both basins. Then, the seasonal variations will be analysed 
and the yearly emissions assessed. Lastly, the methodology and uncertainties are dis-
cussed.  
5.1 Spatial variations of GHG emissions (Basin 2 and 4)  
The spatial variations are statistical non-significant, which means that the deviations 
between the data sets are smaller than the deviations within one data set. As the mini-
mum and maximum values vary considerably, the spatial variations should still be dis-
cussed. The small variations between the fluxes that peak at the same time are consid-
ered spatial variations, and when the emissions from a few chambers differ from the 
other chambers. For instance, the peak in Chamber 3 and 5 during day 23 is considered 
a spatial variation, whereas the peak in all seven chambers during day 9-12 is not, since 
they are more likely caused by parameters that affect the entire basin, such as climatic 
conditions. However, the fact that the peaks are not constituted by the same flux value is 
a spatial variation.  
5.1.1 Correlation between CO2 and N2O emissions  
Methane is produced by anaerobic methanogenesis. Carbon dioxide is produced either 
by aerobic respiration or anaerobically by methanogenesis or denitrification, and N2O is 
produced as a bi-product of aerobic nitrification and anaerobic denitrification.  
The correlation between CO2 and N2O in Basin 4 (R2=0.99) strongly indicate that CO2 is 
produced as a by-product of denitrification, hence anaerobically. This is corroborated by 
the tendency that CH4 emissions peak at the same time as CO2 and N2O indicating that all 
three gases are anaerobically produced. The same conclusion is made by Madsen (2016) 
in a study of Basin 2 in Helsinge WWTP during the fall of 2015. However, it should be 
noted that such a strong correlation might be a quincidence.  
The anaerobically produced CO2 can be explained by constant water saturation of the 
sludge due a low dewatering rate. The sludge was frozen much of the time, assumably 
resulting in a lower drainage rate (not measured). Furthermore, it was cloudy during 
most of the measuring days and the humidity was high due to high precipitation rate, 
which causes a lower evaporation.

Spatial variations in STRBs can be explained by local conditions around the chambers 
such as reed density, temperature, pressure, water saturation of the sludge and sludge

distribution. In this case, the variations are most likely caused by differences in reed 
density and sludge conditions. The chamber temperature was notated upon every 
measurement, and did generally not vary more than 1-2 degrees. The barometric pres-
sure was not measured; however it is not expected to vary significantly from chamber to 
chamber.  
Reed density means the amount of reed stubbles in the chamber. The reeds secure high-
er transpiration of water from the sludge, and higher oxygen diffusion from the roots. 
Less water in the sludge means better aeration which benefits the aerobic biological 
processes that produce CO2 (Stefanakis & Tsihrintzis, 2011), on the contrary a lower 
reed density would result in higher CH4 and N2O emissions due to anaerobic conditions. 
The reed density of each chamber is however constant during the field work period, and 
spatial variations caused by differences in reed densities would consequently be con-
stant too. For instance, the CO2 emissions in Chamber 4 are generally lower than in the 
other chambers, and Chamber 4 has a lower TWA with regard to CO2. This could be 
caused by a lower reed density, and it is corroborated by pictures of the chambers found 
in Appendix D. From the pictures in Appendix D it was not possible to quantify the 
amounts of reeds in the chambers because some of them were bended. However in 
Chamber 4 (also 2 and 7) it was possible to see the sludge through the reeds, unlike the 
other chambers, which lead to the assumption that there were fewer reeds in these 
chambers. Additionally, Chamber 22 and 24 (Basin 2) emits considerable higher CH4 
emissions than the other chambers, and the pictures in Appendix D show clearly that the 
reed densities of these two chambers are lower than the other two chambers in Basin 2.  
Another explanation to the spatial variations is the sludge composition and condition. 
The sludge composition is inhomogeneous with regard to content of water and organic 
matter. Uneven water distribution can cause anaerobic pockets of methanogens that 
transform organic matter into CH4. Suddenly drainage or cracks by movements in the 
sludge surface can release the CH4. Such release could be incited by the wind moving the 
reed stems or by walking on the sludge to reach the flux chambers, even though the dis-
turbance was minimized by placing wooden sheets to step on. A release of gases like that 
will show as peaks in the flux chambers. This could explain the peaks in all three gases in 
Chamber 5 (Basin 4) during day 23. Furthermore, the content of organic matter or ni-
trate might be unequally distributed. A higher content of organic matter affects the pro-
duction of CO2 and CH4 positively, and nitrification/denitrification is strongly dependent 
on the availability of nitrate.  
5.1.3 Correlation between GHG emissions and distance to basin edge  
The reed density was observed to be closer in the middle of the basin compared to the 
sides, and hence aerobic processes were expected to dominate. Therefore, a correlation 
was made between GHG fluxes and their distance to the nearest edge of the basin; how-
ever, no correlation was found. The missing correlation may be explained by the CO2

being produced anaerobically by denitrification due to water saturation of the sludge. It 
could also be explained by local conditions, for instance even though the plant density 
generally appeared to be larger in the centre of the basin, it might not be homogeneously 
distributed. This is supported by the fact that there are fewer reeds in Chamber 2, 4 and 
7, which produce the lowest CO2 and N2O emissions. Again, it is an assumption that there 
is a lower reed density in Chamber 2, 4 and 7, based on the fact that it was possible to 
see the sludge through the reeds.  
5.2 Interpolated GHG emissions (Basin 4 and 2)  
The flux chambers were distributed over the area of Basin 4 to cover spatial variations, 
whereupon interpolation of the fluxes to the entire basin was done to find the best ap-
proximation to the total contribution of GHG emissions from Basin 4.  
The CO2 emissions ranged from 694 to 2068 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 4 and from 197 to 600 
mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 2. This is within the range recorded by Olsson, et al. (2014) that 
varied between 40 and 3000 mg∙m-2∙h-1 during fall. Another study measured CO2 emis-
sions from constructed wetlands for wastewater treatment during winter in Estonia, 
Norway, Poland and Finland (Søvik, et al., 2006), and found CO2 emissions of 210 – 3900 
mg∙m-2∙d-1 (8.75 – 162.5 mg∙m-2∙h-1), which is considerably lower than the fluxes 
recorded in the present study.  
The CH4 emissions ranged between 9 and 62 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 4 and between 2 and 22 
mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 2. This corresponds to the CH4 emissions that Uggetti, et al. (2012) 
recorded from sludge treatment wetlands, which were in the range of 10 to 5400 mg∙m-
2∙d-1 (0.42 – 225 mg∙m-2∙h-1). It should be noted that Uggetti, et al. (2012) measured 
fluxes in a summer period where the fluxes are expected to be higher compared to 
winter. Olsson, et al. (2014) recorded CH4 emissions during fall from -0.03 to 5 mg∙m-2∙h-
1, which corresponds to the values measured in Basin 2, however are considerably lower 
than the values from Basin 4. The same applies for Søvik, et al. (2006), that recorded CH4 
emissions of -1.5 – 190 mg∙m-2∙d-1 (-0.06 – 7.8 mg∙m-2∙h-1). Negative CH4 emissions nor-
mally occur in well drained soils with high oxygen availability, due to CH4 oxidation 
(Uggetti, et al., 2012a). Negative CH4 fluxes are common in agricultural soils (Maljanen, 
et al., 2004), but are also observed previously in constructed wetlands for wastewater 
treatment (Søvik, et al., 2006) (Ström, et al., 2006 ).  
The N2O emissions ranged between 2 and 19 mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 4 and between 0 and 8 
mg∙m-2∙h-1 in Basin 2. As was the case for CH4 emission, the N2O emissions are compara-
ble to those measured in Uggetti, et al. (2012), that recorded N2O emissions in the range 
from 20 to 950 mg∙m-2∙d-1 (0.83 – 40 mg∙m-2∙h-1), however during summer. Ström, et al. 
(2006) measured N2O emissions in the range of -13.9 – 31.5 mg∙m-2∙d-1 (-0.58 – 1.31 
mg∙m-2∙h-1), which is considerably lower than N2O emissions measured in this study. 
This is also the case for N2O emissions recorded by Søvik, et al. (2006) during winter 
(negative – 0.22 mg∙m-2∙h-1). Negative N2O fluxes are previously recorded (Søvik, et al., 
==PAGE:50==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 35 - 
 
2006) (Teiter & Mander, 2005), however no explanation were given to wetlands work-
ing as sinks for N2O.  
Differences in literature are most likely due to climatic or seasonal differences (tempera-
ture, precipitation), sludge composition (content of organic matter, nitrate, water con-
tent), operational differences (loading frequency and amount) or measuring methods 
(directly from chambers on site or from extracted samples transported to a lab).  
The interpolated data show, in correspondence with fluxes from the individual cham-
bers, that all three gases peak at the same time, indicating that they are anaerobically 
produced. This is contrary to previous studies that found CH4 emissions to drop when 
CO2 and N2O increases (Madsen, 2016) (Uggetti, et al., 2012a) (Cui, et al., 2015). The in-
verted patterns were in these studies explained by enhanced anaerobic conditions due 
to sludge loading and a low availability of NO3-. However, if the dewatering rate during 
winter is lower compared the other seasons, as it is discussed earlier, the sludge might 
already be saturated with water when loading begins, and the microorganisms in the 
sludge will not experience a sudden change in environment. At highly anaerobic condi-
tions, a part of N2O is reduced to N2 through the denitrification process (Uggetti, et al., 
2012a).  
5.2.1 Global warming potential  
The GHGs have different integrated radiative forcing over a period of time, which makes 
it difficult to compare their contribution to global warming. Therefore GWP is a useful 
way of associating their different potential contributions to global warming, and it al-
lows addition of the GHGs. This is expressed in the comparison between the graphs that 
show the relative distributions of GHGs over time with and without accounting for GWP. 
Considering the distribution of GHGs as fluxes, CO2 constitutes almost everything, and 
CH4 and N2O only a few percentages. However, taking their GWP into account, N2O be-
comes the main contributor to global warming in Basin 4. Even though CO2 makes up the 
second largest GHG in Basin 4 and the largest in Basin 2, it is biogenic, and thus neutral 
in the contribution to global warming.  
5.2.2 Comparison between Basin 2 and 4  
There is a weak correlation between peaks from Basin 2 and from Basin 4. As is the case 
for the spatial variations, similarities in the fluctuations of the emissions are assumed to 
be caused by climatic factors. This is corroborated by the fact that loading of the two 
basins were staggered by nine days (see loading dates in Appendix A), and since the 
time variable of the graphs in the figure comparing the two basins are presented per 
date, peaking of the two graphs at the same time is more likely to indicate climatic influ-
ence rather than temporal variations due to sludge loading.  
Differences in GHG emissions from Basin 4 and Basin 2 are due to differences in reed 
density, sludge depths and availability of carbon and nitrate. The sludge depth was in 
==PAGE:51==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 36 - 
 
Basin 4 1.3 meters and in Basin 2 0.8 meters. The reed density was higher in Basin 4 
compared to Basin 2.  
It is already discussed, that a higher reed density enhances aerobic conditions in the 
sludge. Taking this into account, the GHG emissions from Basin 4 should indicate more 
aerobic conditions than Basin 2; lower CH4 and higher CO2. On the other hand, studies 
have found that evaporation from the unplanted parts of the reed beds are enhanced by 
solar radiation. The dark colour of the sludge increases absorption of radiant heat from 
the sun, and the uneven surface provide a larger available area for evaporation com-
pared to an open water surface. However, the same studies found that the total evapo-
transpiration is significantly higher in beds with reeds than without. (Stefanakis & 
Tsihrintzis, 2011).  
Considering the sludge depths a more shallow depth means enhanced aerobic condi-
tions. This is due to a larger fraction of surface area, and that the distance between sur-
face and filter is smaller, and it leads to the opposite conclusion compared to reed densi-
ty, that the GHG emissions from Basin 2 should indicate more aerobic conditions than 
Basin 4.  
The reed density is assumed to be the main process distinguishing the two basins, be-
cause CH4 emissions in CO2-eq in general constitute a larger fraction of the emitted GHGs 
from Basin 2 compared to Basin 4, indicating more aerobic conditions in Basin 4. The 
emission rates are generally higher in Basin 4 compared to Basin 2, which is explained 
by increased microbial activity in Basin 4 due to a larger amount of sludge, and thus 
more active biomass. The microbial activity, and thus GHG production, depends fur-
thermore on the availability of carbon and nitrate.  
5.3 Seasonal variations of GHG emissions (Basin 4)  
According to a statistical analysis of variance, the variations between the seasons are 
significant. Carbon dioxide and CH4 emissions were highest during summer and lowest 
during winter. Especially CH4 emissions have a strong seasonal dependency, which was 
also observed by Uggetti, et al. (2012). Fluctuations in CH4 emissions are affected by pa-
rameters such as change in pH, substrate availability and soil temperature. Even though 
only the air temperature was recorded, a strong correlation between seasonal averages 
of CO2 (R2=0.94) and CH4 (R2=0.68) emissions as a function of temperature were ob-
served (see Appendix G).  
Another parameter that affects CH4 and CO2 emissions are the availability of oxygen de-
pending on water content in the sludge. Previous studies have found that CH4 emissions 
increase with anaerobic conditions caused by a higher water table in constructed wet-
lands for wastewater treatment (Uggetti, et al., 2012a). This could be another explana-
tion for the higher CH4 emissions during winter, however the water content of the 
sludge was not analysed. There are some indications though, that this is the case. The 
==PAGE:52==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 37 - 
 
first indicator is the pipes that were installed to measure gas compositions in depth, and 
the fact that only concentrations of atmospheric gasses were measured. The pipes were 
filled with water to the same level as the surrounding sludge, indicating water saturation 
of the sludge. Another indication is that the sludge height did not decrease in the cham-
bers during the field work period. A decrease in sludge height indicates dewatering, and 
thus no dewatering occurred. The last indication is the average precipitation recorded 
during the field work campaigns that are approximately three times higher during fall 
and winter compared to spring and summer, however no correlations between seasonal 
average of precipitation and fluxes were found for CH4 (R2=0.47) and CO2 (R2=0.13) (see 
Appendix G). A negative correlation was expected for CO2, since the production of CO2 
increases as the water content of the sludge decreases, due to higher decomposition of 
organic matter. A higher precipitation rate means lower evapotranspiration due to high-
er humidity, and therefore a lower dewatering rate. Even though the precipitation rate 
was higher during summer compared to winter, the temperatures were correspondingly 
higher, resulting in a higher evapotranspiration rate. During winter the plants are with-
ered, which also causes lower evapotranspiration.  
The N2O emissions were highest during fall and lowest during spring, indicating less 
temperature dependency. This corresponds to results from previous studies that 
demonstrated a low dependency of N2O emissions on temperature by recording higher 
N2O fluxes during winter compared to summer (Augustin, et al., 1996). In general the 
low dependency is corroborated by varying results, as other studies found N2O emis-
sions to be higher during summer compared to winter (Søvik, et al., 2006). The N2O 
emissions seem to rather depend on the water saturation of the sludge. A correlation 
between seasonal average of precipitation and fluxes was found for N2O (R2=0.63) (see 
Appendix G). This observation is confirmed by several studies that revealed an increase 
in N2O emissions with a lowering water table (Martikainen, et al., 1993) (Teiter & 
Mander, 2005).  
Methane emissions are high during spring and summer when N2O emissions are low and 
low during fall and winter when N2O emissions are high. This suggests that the two gas-
es compete with each other. The distribution of GHGs are similar during spring and 
summer and during fall and winter; however, the emissions are approximately twice as 
high in summer compared to spring, and the same applies for the fall and winter fluxes. 
The N2O production dependends on nitrate from aerobic nitrification, which means that 
completely anaerobic conditions will lead to reduction in the N2O emissions (Uggetti, et 
al., 2012a). Anaerobic conditions however favours the production of CH4, and thus 
reverse fluctuations in emissions of N2O and CH4.  
No correlations were found between barometric pressure and GHG emissions (see Ap-
pendix G).

5.4 Annual GHG emissions from Basin 4  
The CO2 emissions are not included in the GWP of Basin 4, because they are produced 
during decomposition of organic matter, and due to photosynthesis considered a neutral 
contribution to global warming.  
The annual GWP (2014) of Basin 4 based on total emissions of CH4 and N2O is 60.2 kg 
CO2-eq∙m-2∙yr-1 over 100 year time horizon (GWP 2007 = 61.3 kg CO2-eq∙m-2∙yr-1). Tak-
ing the dimensions of the basin into account (1050 m2, 3000 PE) this corresponds to 
21.1 kg CO2-eq∙PE-1∙yr-1 (GWP 2007 = 21.5 kg CO2-eq∙PE-1∙yr-1).  
The emission is similar to a GWP (2007) of 60.95 kg CO2-eq∙m-2∙yr-1 (17 kg CO2-eq∙PE-
1∙yr-1) from sludge treatment wetlands recorded by Uggetti, et al. (2012). However, 
Uggetti, et al. (2012) based the annual GWP on measurements from a summer campaign, 
and would assumably get a lower GWP if campaigns were carried out for each season. 
Furthermore, the basin dimensions of are marketly smaller (210 m2, 1000 PE), why a 
lower GWP was expected.  
Another study found GWPs (2007) from conventional methods such as centrifuge (36 kg 
CO2-eq∙PE-1∙yr-1) caused by electrical consumption and sludge transportation to a 
WWTP (untreated) (162 kg CO2-eq∙PE-1∙yr-1) caused by fossil fuel consumption (Uggetti, 
et al., 2011). Compared to conventional methods the STRB in Helsinge has a considera-
ble lower GWP than centrifuge and sludge transportation. As the GHG emissions from 
the STRB in Helsinge in general were in the higher range compared to previous studies, 
STRBs seem to be a favourable alternative to conventional methods in terms of contribu-
tion to global warming.  
The change in GWPs from 2007 to 2014 does not change the overall assessment of the 
STRB.   
5.5 Limitations, uncertainties and further studies  
The limitations and uncertainties that are related to this study are discussed in the fol-
lowing section. Additionally, suggestions to improvements and further studies are in-
cluded where suitable.  
5.5.1 Comparison to literature  
Comparison of results from this study to literature holds a number of uncertainties, be-
cause a critical selection of the reviewed literature was not possible, due to limited 
amount of reference studies. Seasonal variations of CO2, CH4 and N2O fluxes from STRBs 
have not been covered all together in the same study before, and only few studies have 
covered one or two of the GHGs. The selected studies have either one or more GHG in 
common, the methodology or the site type (constructed wetland for wastewater treat-
ment, STRB). Due to this lack of comparability, the results vary significantly between 
studies.  
==PAGE:54==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 39 - 
 
Climatic factors, such as temperature and precipitation, and sludge composition have 
proven to play important roles, and therefore results from Spain may not be comparable 
to results from Denmark.  
Another factor is operation of the different study sites. Even though STRBs are overall 
run according to the same operation model that is described previously in this study, 
they are managed and maintained by plant workers with limited technical knowledge. 
Deviations from the operational procedure may not be recorded, because they are con-
sidered trivial. For instance, during measurements on day 12 after loading (04/02/16), 
Basin 4 was unintentionally loaded with sludge during measurements in Chamber 5. The 
plant workers were immediately contacted, and the sludge loading only lasted for ten 
minutes. However, such an event could occur between measuring days without it being 
communicated to the researchers. Therefore communication with the plant workers 
during measuring campaigns is important.  
5.5.2 Weather data  
The average temperature recorded during the winter campaign was 0.9oC higher than 
normal. It can therefore be discussed whether the campaign carried out during this 
study is representative for a winter period. At least, the temperature differences should 
be taken into account. As already mentioned, the CO2 and CH4 fluxes are highly tempera-
ture dependent, and the emissions measured during this period might be increased 
compared to a representative winter.  
Since historical weather data from the field work periods were not available from Hel-
singe WWTP, the nearest reference spot was chosen. The nearest place with available 
data on precipitation, temperature and barometric pressure were in Copenhagen Air-
port, which is located approximately 50 km south of Helsinge WWTP. There are uncer-
tainties related to the distance between study site and where the weather data was ob-
tained. The change in temperature over 50 km is assumed to be limited, whereas the 
change in precipitation can be significant. The difference in annual precipitation be-
tween Copenhagen Airport and Helsinge WWTP is 50 – 100 mm, however the difference 
in single events is even more significant (Winther, et al., 2011). Therefore, correlations 
based on the recorded weather data should be used only as an indication of a potential 
outcome.  
5.5.3 Time weighted averages  
The TWA was in all cases closer to the measured minimum flux than it was to the maxi-
mum flux. This is explained by the fact that the maximum fluxes consist of the largest 
positive peaks that were measured. There are only a few positive peaks, and no large 
negative peaks, which return a TWA closer to the minimum.  
Additionally, there were shorter intervals between the measurements in the beginning 
of the fieldwork period than toward the end. The fluxes were higher in the beginning of 
==PAGE:55==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 40 - 
 
the period; however, there could have been more peaks in the end of the period between 
the measuring days that were not recorded.  
The reason for choosing TWA instead of non-weighted averages is to take the different 
measuring intervals into account. A TWA is more accurate at smaller intervals, as the 
method assumes that the measured flux has been constant since last measurement and 
therefore larger intervals accounts for more. If a peak is measured and the measuring 
interval is three days, it will count less than if the measuring interval is seven days, and 
thus the uncertainty increases with the measuring interval. Therefore smaller meas-
urement intervals are recommended.  
5.5.4 Surfer 13 (Kriging) 
The Kriging method was used in Surfer 13 to interpolate the calculated fluxes of each 
chamber to the entire basin. Negative fluxes of CH4 and N2O were achieved on some of 
the days. However, Surfer 13 does not accept input files containing negative fluxes. 
Therefore the input files were manipulated and negative fluxes were replaced by 0.001 
mg∙m-2∙h-1. As an uncertainty analysis the fluxes in Surfer 13 were also replaced by 0.01 
mg∙m-2∙h-1 and 0.1 mg∙m-2∙h-1, however the final interpolated flux did not change signifi-
cantly (in the order of 10-4).  
Since the interpolated results are harmonic means of the basins, and thus a type of aver-
age, changing negative values to a positive will increase the final value. However, in all 
three cases where data was manipulated the harmonic mean was found to be 0 mg∙m-
2∙h-1, which are also the lowest observed fluxes. It is therefore not assumed to cause 
great uncertainty.  
As negative fluxes were measured in Basin 2 only, this uncertainty does not affect the 
results and discussion of seasonal variations and annual contribution to global warming 
of Basin 4.  
5.5.5 Field work  
The present study provides the potential contribution to global warming of a single ba-
sin. In order to decrease uncertainties and/or to make a complete environmental as-
sessment there are more things that should be included.   
The comparison between the two basins reveals how much differences in sludge height 
and reed density affect the physical and biological processes in the STRBs, and thus the 
production of GHGs. Therefore, multiplying the GWP of Basin 4 by the total number of 
basins to find the total GWP of Helsinge STRB would result in great uncertainties, and 
the result was thus presented per area and per PE instead. However, the annual contri-
bution to global warming is still based on a single basin only, and as Basin 4 is one of the 
basins with deepest sludge layer and the most reeds, the result can be considered as a 
worst case scenario.  
==PAGE:56==
M. E. H. Nielsen | Master thesis | June 2016 
- 41 - 
 
Literature on the influence of temperature on the biological processes refers to sludge 
temperature. In this study, only the air temperature was recorded. A more accurate as-
sessment of the correlation between temperature and fluxes can be made knowing the 
sludge temperature. Also the humidity of the air would be interesting to measure, as a 
correlation between humidity, evapotranspiration and GHG emission most likely will 
exist.  
The assumption was made, that the dewatering rate is lower during winter compared to 
the other seasons. This is reasoned by lower evapotranspiration due to high humidity 
and withered reeds, and lower drainage rates due to frozen sludge. It would be interest-
ing to confirm the assumption by measuring rates of drainage and evapotranspiration. 
Dewatering mechanisms have been evaluated before in experiment of a pilot-scale STRB 
(Stefanakis & Tsihrintzis, 2011); however no documentation for a full-scale study was 
found.  
From this study it was found that CH4 and N2O contributed equally to the potential glob-
al warming. Therefore, further studies should focus on emissions of both GHGs.  
5.5.6 System performance  
In deed it is relevant to decrease CH4 and N2O emissions from STRBs. Nevertheless, GHG 
emissions are inevitable products of mineralisation of organic matter, and they cannot 
be avoided completely. It is however possible, to some extent, to find the optimal bal-
ance between the different emissions. Highly anaerobic conditions lead to the produc-
tion of N2 instead of N2O, which is more favourable, but it also increases CH4 emissions. 
Therefore aerobic conditions are desired. Such solutions include optimisation of system 
operation or development of system design, both with the incentive to increase dewater-
ing.  
Optimisation of system operation could include education of the plant workers, so that 
they know the consequences of a malfunctioning STRB, and the resting periods are kept. 
It should be noted, that Helsinge STRB is quite well functioning (Nielsen, et al., 2014).  
Development of the system design could for instance include coverage of the basins dur-
ing winter to avoid precipitation reaching the basins. The cover should still allow solar 
radiation and air to reach the sludge surface and the reeds. Furthermore, an environ-
mental assessment, for instance an LCA, should be conducted to estimate whether the 
improvements are environmentally beneficial or increase the environmental impact. 
The GHG emissions from the operational phase of the total life cycle of an STRB only 
constitute a minor contribution to the total carbon footprint (Uggetti, et al., 2012b). 
Therefore, improvements of the system should only be implemented if they improve the 
total environmental impact. The results from this study can advantageously be used to 
conduct a full life cycle assessment of the STRB in Helsinge.

5.5.7 Further studies  
This section presents some ideas to further studies that are outside the scope of this 
study.  
The quality of the sludge is not covered in this study, it is however important for the en-
vironmental assessment. One of the reasons that STRBs are considered environmentally 
friendly is that the sludge is used as fertilizer in agriculture. Concurrently with a strong-
er focus on the negative effects of utilizing sludge in agriculture, especially the addition 
of heavy metals, the legislation is made stricter with lower limit values (Nielsen, 2007). 
Therefore, methods to reduce the amounts of heavy metals and other hazardous com-
pounds are of interest.  
It could be argued that the wastewater composition changes over a year, and therefore 
also the treatment efficiency of the WWTP and the sludge composition. It could be rele-
vant to include seasonal variations of sludge composition in further studies.  
Additionally, quantification of nitrogen gas (N2) could provide a more detailed estimate 
of the oxygen availability in the sludge, as the production of N2:N2O ratio increases with 
decreasing oxygen level.

6 Conclusion  
Greenhouse gas emissions of CH4, CO2 and N2O from an STRB in Helsinge have been 
quantified during a winter field work campaign. Spatial variations of the fluxes and tem-
poral variations of interpolated data were analysed, and the results were compared to 
data collected during spring, summer and fall of the same STRB to determine the GWP. 
At last an assessment was made on whether STRBs are environmental friendly alterna-
tives to conventional sludge treatment methods. The conclusions made based on this 
study are:  
 A strong correlation between fluxes of CO2 and N2O (R2=0.99) in Basin 4 indi-
cate that CO2 is anaerobically produced as a by-product of denitrification, and 
CH4 fluxes that peak the same days indicated anaerobic production of all 
three GHGs.  
 Anaerobic conditions in the STRB during winter are due to constant water 
saturation because of lower evapotranspiration and drainage rates. Evapo-
transpiration is decreased due to high humidity caused by high precipitation 
rates and withered reeds. A lower drainage rate is a result of frozen sludge.  
 Spatial variations are caused by differences in reed density and sludge com-
position. They are however statistically insignificant.  
 Interpolated emissions reveal that N2O is the largest GHG emission in Basin 4 
and CO2 in Basin 2. The differences between emissions from Basins 4 and 2 
are caused by different reed densities, sludge depths and availability of car-
bon and nitrate.  
 Variations between seasons are found to be statistically significant with 
highest CO2 and CH4 emissions during summer and lowest during winter and 
highest N2O emissions during fall and lowest during spring.  
 Seasonal variations are caused by climatic factors such as temperature and 
oxygen availability in the sludge.  
 Annually, CH4 and N2O contribute equally to global warming, and future stud-
ies should therefore include both GHGs.    
 GWP for a 100 year time frame based on emissions of CH4 and N2O is consid-
erably lower than conventional sludge treatment methods.  
Based on the study it seems that STRBs can be considered environmentally friendly al-
ternatives to conventional sludge treatment methods.

Appendix name: Statistical Analysis 
Appendix C  
 
This appendix presents a statistical analysis of variance of seasonal variations of interpolated 
data.  
Analysis of variance (ANOVA):  
An ANOVA test was performed using Excel to test the variance of data sets from the four 
seasons.  
The requirements for the data sets to be analysed by an ANOVA test are:  
 All sets of data are normally distributed  
 The data sets have identical dispersion  
As the ANOVA test is not highly sensitive to the requirements, the evaluation of the criteria is 
visually performed by means of the following figure that shows the fluxes up the Y-axes and 
the seasons out the X-axes (1=Fall, 2=Spring, 3=Summer and 4=winter). The averages are 
marked with crosses, with the minimum and maximum fluxes as end points of the error bars.  
 
 
The aim of the ANOVA test is to see if the mean values of the datasets are similar (null-
hypothesis, H0), or if at least one of the means are different from the others (H1). The mean is 
denoted with μ. The two hypotheses are thus:

Appendix F  
 
In the main report it was mentioned, that new GWPs was presented in 2014. This appendix 
presents the results taking GWP 2007 into account.  
In the main report, only results that are taking GWP 2014 into account are presented, as these 
values are the most representative for the current environmental impact. However, as 
literature before 2014 are using 2007 data, the results from the present study are now 
presented accounting for GWP 2007.  
Methane has a higher GWP in 2014 than in 2007and N2O a lower, however the results do not 
change markedly, and the same assessments made in the main report apply to the results 
below.

Climate Data 
Appendix G  
 
This appendix shows the correlation between temperature, barometric pressure and 
precipitation as a function of GHG emission from Basin 4 over time.  
The climate data was achieved from www.wunderground.com from the nearest possible 
location (Copenhagen Airport). The fluxes used in the correlation are the interpolated. The 
graphs below show the results.  
For temperature and precipitation: First, correlations between daily fluxes and temperatures 
are presented per GHG. Second, correlations between seasonal averages are presented. For 
pressure only daily fluxes as function of temperatures are presented.  
Strong correlation between seasonal averages of CO2 and CH4 emissions and temperature was 
found, with R2 of 0.94 and 0.68, respectively. And correlation between seasonal N2O emission 
and precipitation (R2=0.63) was observed.
