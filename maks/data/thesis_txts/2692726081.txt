Abstract
The current increase of renewable energy production coupled with the increasing popularity of smart
meters creates a perfect opportunity for operating a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) with
near-real-time scheduling. This work carries out a comparative assessment of five operation policies
for a PV-BESS system with varying objectives and information quality. Namely, a Self-consumption
maximisation policy (SCM), a deterministic MPC (DMPC), a scenario-based stochastic MPC (SMPC)
and two data-driven policies respectively based on a Random Forest (RF) algorithm and a K-nearest
neighbours (KNN). The evaluation results suggest that, depending on the system’s characteristics, each
policy could perform the best. The SCM shines in poor prediction environments when the decision model
can be eﬀiciently approximated with deterministic rules. The deterministic MPC performs the best when
accurate forecasts can be provided. Otherwise, in an imperfect information case, the stochastic MPC
performs better by an uncertainty model. Finally, the data-driven methods have shown promising in
approximating the DMPC performances with lower execution times, but should be further refined in
future work.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Energy is the backbone of today’s society, it has become essential in every aspect of our lives. Trans-
portation, healthcare, construction, communication, all of our modern life is powered by converted
energies.
As such, energy demand has been growing at an unprecedented speed for the past decades and
is expected to continue in this trend. In its 2019 international energy outlook [20], the US Energy
Information Administration predicts that world energy consumption will grow by nearly 50% between
2018 and 2050, mostly due to energy-intensive economic growth coupled with global population growth.
However, a large part of this energy is produced through non-renewable and hydrocarbon-based fuels
such as natural gas, coal, oil that heavily contribute to carbon emissions and climate change. To follow
the 2015 Paris climate agreement’s “to keep global warming to within 1.5°C of pre-industrial level by
2050”, the International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), in its 2018 special report, highly encourages
countries to bring carbon emissions to “net-zero” by 2050. This change cannot happen without a shift
of the energy balance to carbon-free energy sources.
Figure 1.1: Global share of total final consumption by source 2018 [28]
Renewable energies such as wind and solar offer very promising solutions. In operation, they produce
carbon-free energy and have become highly cost-eﬀicient in recent years. Those sources virtually produce
“free” energy after installation as the natural resources they use (wind, sunlight) can be harvested
without cost. According to the IEA, Solar PV has become consistently cheaper than new coal or gas
power plants in most countries [29].
Despite all their benefits, renewable energies remain dependent on weather conditions, leading to
uncertain availability and varying power quality [38]. These uncertainties hinders their integration to
the grid as energy providers must still be able to satisfy the real-time demand of their users while
maintaining the grid’s stability.
==PAGE:9==
1.2 Background
2
Combining intermittent energies with energy storage systems is one method currently used to achieve
better energy dispatch. Among the existing storage solutions (fly-wheel, compressed air storage, heat
storage, water reservoirs), the battery energy storage systems (BESS) offer versatile solutions with
high eﬀiciency for storage and conversion. With the steady decline of battery prices over the past decade
[47], BESS makes for a competitive cost-eﬀicient component widely used for integrating intermittent
sources into the grid.
Figure 1.2: Suitability of different electricity storage technologies for different applications [18]
Nonetheless, a proper BESS operation strategy must be used to optimise the operation of these
systems. In the future, these strategies will also be referenced as policy. Many policies have been
employed over the years to achieve higher cost savings, higher self-consumption or a better lifetime for
the battery. They can be categorized into three types:
• Myopic/heuristic policies where the operation will be based on control rules reacting to real-time
data from the system’s sensors or exogenous information.
• Optimisation policies where the system will find the optimal decisions based on partial knowledge
of future events.
• Data-driven policies where the system learns an approximate optimal decisions policy based on
historical data.
Additionally, the energy sector has recently seen a growing popularity for smart meters in diverse
facilities [55]. This led to a rising amount of data being available for operating systems on a near-real-
time scale. In this context, the present work will pursue the following research question:
What are the most effective operation policies for a BESS scheduling in a near-real-time
environment?
1.2
Background
Decision policies for BESS operation have been a problem at the intersection of multiple research
communities, leading to a plethora of methods. In reference [52], W. Powell proposes to gather those
results under a unified framework for sequential decisions and applies it on an energy storage problem in
reference [53]. Reference [4] also reviews the major types of energy management of PV-battery systems.
Overall, three decision policy communities seem to emerge: Rule-based policies, optimisation policies
and data-driven policies.
==PAGE:10==
1.2 Background
3
1.2.1
Rule-based policies
Rule-based policies, also denoted as heuristic or myopic policies in the literature, are strategies using
expert deterministic rules to control the battery and are a common strategy for energy storage [58, 2,
46, 63, 39, 62, 4]. They have the benefit of being simple, resilient and with very little computation
needed. On the other hand, it is challenging to use them to model more complex behaviours (i.e. battery
degradation) and give sub-optimal cost minimisation when compared to optimisation approaches [2]. A
typical strategy is the self-consumption maximization (SCM) [46, 58, 63, 39, 62]. It basically uses as
much self-produced energy as possible to sustain the load and stores any excess energy in the battery.
When the production is not enough, the rest of the energy is first provided by the BESS and then by
the grid. Another commonly used strategy is the “buy low, sell high” strategy applied to BESS. As
indicated by its name, the battery will charge when the grid energy price is lower than a defined low
set-point and will discharge when the price is higher than a high set-point. A mix of both strategies
has also been implemented in the following references as a benchmark for their strategies [2, 4].
1.2.2
Optimisation policies
Another classic approach is to use optimisation models to schedule the BESS operation. Several com-
munities of researchers have proposed their approach to the problem including:
• Stochastic programming [59]: This type of optimisation model introduces random variable in the
deterministic linear programming approach. It often approximates the problem as a “Two-stage
stochastic problem”, meaning that the decisions are split between the first stage “here-and-now”
decisions and the second stage decisions which can be updated based on the first stage results.
These two references [12, 48] find optimal bidding for combined renewable energy and BESS. In
reference [12], the author co-optimises the offering with a linear decision rule reacting to forecast
errors in operation. Reference [67] uses Sample Average Approximation to take into account the
uncertainty related to the amount of power generation from PV units in an energy consumption
scheduling problem.
• Dynamic programming: This method simplifies an intricate and large problem by dividing it into
smaller solvable problems. Based on Bellman’s equation, it often requires using approximations
to reach a computationally tractable solution. This method has been subject to many papers for
its ability to incorporate uncertainties and non-linearities. Reference [31] uses forward dynamic
programming on a combined PV and battery system by approximating uncertainties on load and
PV generation. [68, 2, 37, 61, 56] uses backward approximate dynamic programming to different
types of combined generator and battery systems.
Additionally, to reduce the computational
burden, [68] uses a data-driven approach based on historical data to approximate their cost-to-go.
And [61] uses a sampled set of future outcomes to approximate exogenous information.
• Model predictive control (MPC): This method takes a decision at time t by solving a model over
horizon (t, t + H). While the common approach of MPC uses a deterministic approach for future
values, the framework can be applied to stochastic settings by drawing inspiration from other
fields such as stochastic programming or dynamic programming. For instance, References [50, 41]
uses a scenario-based MPC to take into account uncertainties in heating systems and PV-battery
systems, respectively.
1.2.3
Data-driven strategies
Data-driven strategies use historical data to learn behaviours using standard machine learning algo-
rithms. [68] uses a k-nearest neighbours method to approximate the future value expectation from Bell-
man equation in a dynamic programming framework. Reference [4] approximates dynamic programming
decisions with a Neural Network. Compared to optimisation strategies, data-driven strategies require
training data and learning time before being deployed. However, the execution time in operation is
generally faster than optimisation. Making them competitive contenders for near-real-time operation
of BESS.

1.3 Contribution of the Thesis
4
1.3
Contribution of the Thesis
In this work, the author carries out a comparative assessment of different operation policies for a PV-
BESS system. Different strategies will be explored and evaluated on a series of performance indicators
and simulated on a domestic dataset. This study complements other references doing similar work [4,
61, 53] by considering variations on the policies and performance indicators.
The major contributions of this thesis can be summarized with the following points:
• The combined PV and BESS system was modelled as a sequential problem
• Five different policies for PV-BESS scheduling were implemented, comprising:
– A rule-based policy: Self-consumption maximisation (SCM),
– Two optimisation based policies: deterministic MPC, stochastic MPC,
– Two data-driven policies: Random forest, K-nearest neighbours.
• Variations on the objective function of the problem were introduced to include CO2 reduction
and battery degradation reduction to the initial cost minimization objective.
• Four indicators (execution time, cost savings, average CO2 emissions, battery degradation) were
introduced to evaluate the performance of each policy under perfect and imperfect forecasts.
• The policies were evaluated using the load and production profiles from Ausgrid Solar Home
Electricity Data with the electricity price and carbon emissions from DK1. This study found
advantages for each policy depending on the quality of the available predictions, the required
execution time and the objective of the system, which concurs with previous works’ findings.
1.4
Organisation of the Thesis
The rest of this paper is organized as follows:
Chapter 2 describes the system’s base model in seven subsections inspired by reference [51]: pa-
rameters, state variable, exogenous information, decision variables, transition function, objective
function, and uncertainties. Variations of the objective function will then be introduced to take
into account a CO2 emissions cost and a battery degradation cost.
Chapter 3 presents the different strategies implemented under three categories: Rule-based, optimi-
sation based, data-driven.
Chapter 4 first introduces the materials and parameters chosen for the evaluation, followed by the

Chapter 5 presents and discusses the evaluation results.
Chapter 6 Concludes this study and introduces possible future work.

CHAPTER 2
Problem description
The energy storage problem studied in this paper is illustrated in figure 2.1. It considers four energy
nodes linked to each other: a photovoltaic energy source (PV), a battery energy storage system (BESS),
the electrical grid and the demand load that needs to be satisfied. This problem counts two sources
of uncertainty, the PV generation and load. Prices, on the other hand, are considered to be known 12
hours ahead with a Time of Use tariff based on the Nordpool market day-ahead prices.

The problem will be further described in the following sections. The first section presents the base

2.1
Base model
This PV-BESS problem is described using seven key components inspired by Powell’s modelling frame-
work [52]: parameters, state variable, decision variables, exogenous information, transition function,
objective function and uncertainties. This framework describes the problem as an optimal decision
policy (Xπ(St)) search.

The capacity of the battery Cnom is considered constant in this problem, but in real life, this

In this thesis, the term energy level will be used to describe the amount of energy stored in
the battery in kWh as opposed to the state of charge (SOC) expressed in percent. Emin and Emax
correspond to the lower and higher bounds of the battery energy level. The battery usually degrades
faster when fully charged or discharged [44, 64, 26, 14, 33]. To prevent this behaviour, it is common
practice to keep the SOC away from those values, for instance, keep the SOC between 20% and 80%.
The selling price of electricity πs is considered constant in this thesis, but depending on the electricity
providers, this price can be time dependent.
The look-ahead parameter H depends on the data availability and how far in the future can the
forecast be usable. In this paper’s case study, this parameter will be defined according to the availability
of the day-ahead electricity price.

2.2.1.1
Degradation model
A battery is usually considered to have reached its end of life when its capacity degrades to 80% of
its original value. In its early stage, a battery usually has a slow degradation rate, but when its state
of health reaches around 80%, the degradation rate accelerates as shown in figure 2.3. Batteries are
generally replaced or re-purposed when reaching this elbow point. The number of cycles necessary to
reach this stage is generally equivalent to the manufacturer’s nominal cycle life denoted Lcyc in this
paper.

This degradation rate of the battery cell, also referenced as aging rate, depends on multiple stress
factors such as the depth of charging/discharging, the state of charge, the current rate, the temperature
and the state of health of the battery. It is also a non-linear process and depends on the battery’s
technology.
A commonly accepted theory divides the degradation between calendar aging and cycle aging [65,
27, 25, 40, 26, 14, 33, 2, 57]. The calendar aging correspond to the degradation naturally happening
over time even when the battery is not actively charging or discharging. It mainly depends on the SOC
and the temperature. On the other hand, cycle aging represents the degradation that happens on each
charge/discharge cycle. Its main parameters are the depth of discharge, the average state of charge, the
current rate and the cell temperature during the occurring cycle. Generally, the following factors lead
to a quicker degradation of the battery:
• Extreme operation temperature (cold or hot).
• High Depth of Discharge.
• Low or High state of charge. The calendar aging rate grows with higher state of charge [35, 57].
However, cycle aging accelerates both at low state of charge and high state of charge. Moreover,
cycles centered around the SOC of reference (usually 0.4 or 0.5) have a lower aging rate [35, 65,
14].
• High current/power rates. In the battery field, the C-rate is the most commonly used unit. It
compares the battery’s maximal capacity (Ah) to the current (A) used to charge/discharge. A
1C rate means a full discharge of the battery in an hour.
Operating a BESS considering these parameters can be beneficial to extend the battery lifespan.
Many papers were written to include the degradation of the battery into their decision process. Refer-
ences [27, 26] reviews the field of degradation approximation and cluster the methods between model-
based, data-driven and hybrid methods. References [33, 60, 44] all integrates battery degradation in
a control algorithm using polynomial functions fitted to a degradation dataset. The first uses mixed
integer quadratic programming on an MPC framework to operate a battery for peak shaving. It takes
into consideration the depth of discharge aging, power aging and SOC aging. The second reference
proposes an offline and online method for operating a BESS using a polynomial function to represent
the influence of the DOD. The last reference combines the temperature and current stress factors under
one polynomial function, then combines the SOC and DOD factors under a quadratic function.
In this thesis, the degradation is modelled using the following approximations:
• The temperature is considered constant (Active cooling system).
• The voltage is considered constant similarly to reference [40]. In reality, the voltage is not constant
but at least function of the state of charge.
• On a discretised control with fixed voltage, the C-rate can be directly related to the DOD of each
decision. Therefore, only the DOD stress factor is kept.
• The capacity of the battery is constant during the decision process. The look-ahead period (less
than a day) is supposed to be too small to have a significant impact on the capacity.
• The system operates only between 20% and 80% SOC similarly to [33] to prevent exponential
degradation happening at high and low SOC.
With these approximations, the degradation model only depends on the SOC and the DOD which
can both be controlled by the BESS’ operation policy. Their impact on degradation is often modelled
using quadratic terms [65, 33] or exponential terms [43].
In this thesis, a simplified version of the
quadratic degradation function from reference [33] is used with the power stress term being ignored.

The base model of the problem being defined, this chapter now focuses on the different decision policies

3.1
Rule-based policy
Rule-based policies are deterministic functions defined to follow deterministic rules for operating the
battery.
Those policies can takes different names in the literature, such as set-point controller [2],
heuristic strategy [4] and belong to the Policy Function Approximation group in Powell’s framework
[52]. Those methods have been largely used as benchmarks in the energy management systems literature
[2, 4, 58, 46]. In this paper, a self-consumption maximisation strategy (SCM) is implemented.
The battery charges whenever the PV production exceeds the consumption and discharges when the
consumption exceeds the production as described in the following algorithm:

3.2
Optimisation-based policies
Optimisation based policies group the policies using an optimisation algorithm for operating the battery.
In a sequential problem, at time t, the decisions for [t, t + H] are computed for the future based on the
state St of the system. However, only a few of the computed decisions will be operated before updating
the state of the model with new inputs. Once the state is updated, another optimisation should also be
run to update the decisions. This method naturally emerges from the base model definition when trying
to apply an optimisation method, but also has its own dedicated research field called Model Predictive
Control (MPC). In the next sections, the Model Predictive Control framework will be reviewed before
properly stating the deterministic and stochastic MPC.
3.2.1
Model Predictive Control
Model predictive control is a methodology that computes an optimal decision based on a model of a
dynamic system and a belief state of the future. This method is formulated like a classic optimisation
problem but will periodically compute a new set of decisions.
After each optimisation, only a few
decisions will be operated before computing a new set of decisions. In some cases, the decisions are
applied for a single timestep but in other cases, decisions can be applied for multiple time-steps before
computing a new set of decisions.
The period between each optimisation usually depends on the

3.2.3
Stochastic optimisation
This section implements a two stage stochastic programming model. This method approximate the
impact of a decision taken at time t on the future of the system. It uses two common approximations
used in direct look-ahead policies [51]:
• Stage aggregation: It approximates the decision process into two stages. The first stage decision
takes a decision xt here-and-now before the realization of uncertain variables , (Lτ, Rτ) ∀τ ∈
{t, ..., t + H} in this problem. Once the first stage has unfolded, the information on the rest of
the dataset [t + 1, H] is supposed to be revealed and the second stage decisions xt+1, ..., xt+H are
optimized in a deterministic manner. The second stage variables are also called recourse variables
as they can be updated when the uncertain variables are unveiled. This approximation can achieve
better accuracy by using more than two stages, with each stage unveiling new information before
taking the following decisions. However, those multi-stage models are often too computationally
expensive to be used in practice.
• Stochastic process approximation: Instead of using a full representation of a probabilistic model,
the uncertainties are replaced by samples representing possible future events.
Those samples
are often referred as scenarios ω ∈Ω. Generating scenarios can be done in multiple ways. The
Sample Average Approximation method [6, 67] uses Monte Carlo sampling. The boostrap sampling
method directly sample from historical data. Scenarios can be simulated using time series models,
such as the ARIMA model [5, 66]. In this thesis, the past weeks are directly sampled as scenarios.

3.3.4
K-Nearest Neighbours
The k-nearest neighbour algorithm assumes that a similar situation St leads to similar output yt. From
this assumption, the idea behind this algorithm is to find in the dataset the k elements most similar to
the input features and return their average label value. A KNN is not a parametric model, which means
that it does not need a training phase as is expected from other machine learning algorithms. It will
compute the distance from each dataset point to the point to predict and choose the k closest points.
KNN’s simplicity makes it a good benchmark for other algorithms and can perform well in some cases.
One of its disadvantages lies in its execution time growth when the volume of data increases. Only two
hyperparameters need to be tuned: the number of neighbours k and the type of distance used. In this
thesis, they were both chosen using a grid-search cross-validation.
3.3.5
Random Forest
The Random Forest algorithm is part of the ensemble method family which combines predictions from
multiple simpler models to get a more accurate prediction than an individual one. More precisely, a
RF model uses multiple decision trees trained on datasets obtained by resampling the original dataset.
This training procedure for ensemble method is called bagging and should reduce the variance of the
original model. A decision tree usually performs with low bias but high variance, which makes it the
perfect candidate to use the bagging method leading to the RF method.
Feature selection
The obvious downside of this method comes with the additional computational demand and
training time to train multiple trees instead of one. However, one can reduce the complexity
of the problem by selecting the most important features to train on. Indeed, when training a
random forest model, each feature importance can be generated, as described in reference [9], by
computing the expected decrease in node impurity over the forest. These scores can then be used
to select the most important features. This method is used in this thesis as a simple approach for
feature selection.
Hyperparameters tuning
A RF model has more hyperparameter than the KNN, making the tuning more time consuming.
The main ones are:
• The number of trees in the forest (n_estimators)
• The maximum number of features to consider for splitting a node (max_features)
• The maximum depth of trees (max_depth)
• The minimum number of samples necessary for a split to occur (min_samples_split)
• The minimum number of samples per leaf node (min_samples_leaf)
Based on the Scikit learn documentation, the most important parameters to tune are the number
of trees and the size of the number of features to consider when splitting a node. In theory [10],
based on the law of large numbers the more trees in the forest, the better the variance becomes.
However, after reaching a certain amount of trees, the results will not get significantly better.
Regarding the maximum number of features, lower values usually lead to a lower variance but
higher bias. For regression trees, a good empirical value is to use 33% of the features [21] but the
best value is usually chosen through cross-validation. The last three parameters are mainly used
to limit the size of the model by constraining the growth of a tree. They can be chosen based on
the size of the dataset or selected by cross-validation. In a single tree, those parameters also play
a role in preventing overfitting, but in a RF model, the bagging method makes it harder to overfit.
For instance, deep trees will have more splits and therefore more information about the dataset
==PAGE:31==

but are more prone to overfitting. A small number of samples per leaf and a small number of
samples per split could also lead to overfitting.
Tuning these hyperparameters can quickly become a tedious process. Training on every combina-
tion of hyperparameters can rapidly become unmanageable. Considering only 4 possible values
for each parameter, it would require nfit = 45 = 1024 training. For an average training time
of 30s, it would lead to a 8.5h of total training. The resolution of the tuning process therefore
depends on the balance between available resources and expected benefits.
In this thesis, four hyperparameters are tuned sequentially (one by one) by order of importance
(n_estimators, max_features, max_depth, min_samples_split). This method allows to greatly
reduce the number of hyperparameter combinations but leads to sub-optimal models.
It still
allows to extract the overall value of a data-driven policy and can be changed in operation when
the algorithm needs to be trained less frequently.
3.3.6
Cross-validation on time series
To evaluate a machine learning model, it is common practice to use a two-layer cross-validation. The
outer layer is used to approximate the generalization error of the model while the inner layer is used
to get the best model for the training set. In general, the methods used to split the dataset between
training and test (i.e. K-Fold) consider the data independent and identically distributed. However, this
thesis’ problem depends on time series, so typical split functions have to be used with caution. The order
of the data is significant and should not be shuffled without thinking recklessly. Methods dedicated
to time series can be used instead, such as a blocked cross-validation (figure 3.2b) or a walk-forward
cross-validation (figure 3.2c) [54]. The first method uses the most recent data as a train set and ignores
data points older than a defined period or happening after the test set. The second one ignores data
points happening after the test set but uses all the data available before the test set for training.

The evaluation will be carried out in two steps, a preliminary evaluation then a complementary evalu-

production are both influenced by the seasons and further work could be done on a bigger dataset. In

the entire year and led to similar conclusions.
To simulate the performance of the policies both in an advantageous and disadvantageous environ-

section 2.1.7 but also using a perfect forecast (PF) over the look-ahead domain [t, t + H]. It should
be noted that the forecasts are also changed in the training features of the data-driven policies. Using
perfect forecasts for a stochastic MPC does not make sense and will therefore not be included in the
PF comparison.

Optimisation based policies
The results of optimisation based policies heavily depend on the quality of the forecast. A deterministic

It proves to have slightly better results than using a DMPC with imperfect information, reaching savings
of 18% against 10% for a deterministic MPC.
Optimisation based policies can be designed to take into account more complex behaviours than a
rule-based policy. On this financial objective, the optimisation algorithm takes advantage of low market
prices (i.e. at t = 150) and charges the battery outside overproduction periods. However, combined
with poor forecasts, this feature can also penalize the policy. For instance, at t = 325 in figure 5.2, due
to a poor belief state, the DMPC and SMPC both charge the battery while there is no overproduction
or low prices. The SCM policy and other rule-based policies do not face this problem as they rely on
real-time inputs and do not have the uncertainties linked to a belief state.
Regarding the execution time, as expected, the DMPC is quicker than the SMPC as it does not
need to optimise variables over multiple scenarios. But compared to the SCM, the optimisation policies
are a lot slower (1e4 times slower). Additional scenarios would also lead to a greater execution time for
the SMPC. Using 4 scenarios, the execution time was 150ms and 600ms using 40 scenarios.

Data-driven policies
Data-driven policies were trained with labels from a DMPC with perfect forecast on the look-ahead
period. The Random Forest policy seems to returns a smoothed version of the DMPCPF. In figure 5.3,
the trajectory of the RF policy stays close to the DMPCPF’s but with smoother slopes. This translates

lower than the stochastic MPC policy but higher than the DMPC. Using perfect forecast it produces
better financial results, smaller than the DMPC but higher than the SMPC.
On the other hand, the KNN policy’s results does not seem to depend on the quality of the forecast.
The trajectories in figure 5.3 are very similar and leads to less improvement (+4% points) on the savings
than for the RF policy (+8% points). The KNN policy also seems to replicate the average SOC profile
of the battery everyday. This behaviour could derive from the periodic nature of the dataset and the
way a KNN model works. Indeed, a KNN algorithm takes the K most similar data points and return
their average label, which could lead to a trajectory similar to an average daily profile. The great
number of features with the same weight could also encourage this type of behaviour by limiting the
importance of each feature.
In term of execution time, the data-driven policies can both perform better than the optimisation
based methods. Depending on the chosen hyperparameters, the random forest execution time ranged
from 122ms to 2220ms using the Scikit-learn library. The KNN algorithm had faster execution time
averaging 6ms per decision, also using the Scikit-learn implementation.
The tuning and training process are not accounted in the execution time but are still important
considerations. The KNN policy had a very fast deployment with only a minute to tune and training
required. On the other hand, an RF model was slower to be tuned and trained. In this thesis, searching
through 32 combinations in a 3-fold cross validation on 14304 data points took between 15 minutes and
45 minutes. Once the hyperparameters chosen, training on a single fold took less than a minute. This
training time could still be easily acceptable if the model was to be retrained on a weekly or monthly
basis. Moreover, tuning the RF model did not significantly improve the performance of the policy and
could potentially be used without tuning depending on the data.

Degradation
When no degradation is taken into account in the policies’ objective, the DMPC and SMPC policies
produces the most damage. In figure 5.5, the DMPC and SMPC have frequent and deep cycles to
minimize the objective, which both accelerate the degradation of the battery. The SCM policy still has
deep cycles but cannot charge outside overproduction periods, leading to a smaller cycle frequency and
a smaller degradation. The RF policy is a naturally smoother version of the DMPC policy, inducing
less time spent at min/max SOC and therefore less degradation. The smoother slope also induces a
smaller power rate which is beneficial for a battery lifetime, but this stress factor was not considered in
the current degradation per cycle function. The KNN policy have smaller cycles and is centered around
the SOC of reference, which makes for a slower degradation.
Adding a degradation term to the objective has a good impact on decreasing the battery aging for
the data-driven and optimisation policies. Turning the SCM policy the worst policy on this performance.
The degradation term also does not seem to depend on the quality of the forecast and actively decrease
the wearing of the battery both in the imperfect information case and the perfect information case.
However, improving the degradation performance clearly has drawbacks on other performances. Except
for the KNNPF policy, the other performances decrease when adding the degradation term to the
objective. Adding a degradation term allows to have better control on the cycle life of the battery and
could increase the return on investment, but could also lead to a longer payback period. Depending on
the priorities of the consumer, one approach could be more beneficial than the other.

in reality they are subject to uncertainties, the CO2 reduction could therefore be overestimated here.

5.3
Discussion
The results demonstrated in this chapter can be summarized with the following points:
SCM : The SCM policy outperform all other policies in the execution time and gets the best financial
performance when using imperfect information. It still scores as the second best financial value
in a perfect information case. The CO2 emissions being correlated to the electricity price, the
SCM also happened to have a low carbon emission profile. This policy however is less suited for
complex objective functions. When taking into account the degradation of the battery, the SCM
is outperformed by the more versatile data-driven and optimisation policies. This comes as no
surprise as the SCM was not designed for this purpose. It should be stated that this policy is
very well suited for the dataset used in this thesis. The demand mainly happens in the morning
and the evening while the PV production happens during the day. The planning of the battery is
therefore quite simple and mainly requires to charge the battery during the day to discharge it in
the evening, which works well for a this type of short-sighted policy with no planning capability.
Deterministic MPC : The deterministic optimisation both has the best financial indicator in a
perfect information case and the worst indicator in an imperfect information case. This was to be
expected as this policy makes decisions by blindly optimising on a deterministic future. When fed
wrong information, the policy will have no means to deal with this error. However, with accurate
forecasts, it performs better than a SCM policy and can be extended to more complex objectives.
Unlike an SCM policy, it can choose to store low price energy for later use and at the same time
take into account a CO2 and battery degradation cost. Optimisation based methods are, however,
the slowest policies among the tested ones.
Stochastic MPC : The stochastic MPC has the same features as the DMPC but can take into account
uncertainties through the use of scenarios. It is versatile enough to add a CO2 and degradation
term but performs better than the DMPC and the derived data-driven policies in case of bad
forecasts. The degradation cost seemed to have a heavy influence on its financial performance, as it
gets very close to the DMPC results. Considering that the SMPC has one of the lowest degradation
indicator, it can be presumed that the degradation coeﬀicients in the SMPC’s objective function
might be inappropriate for this policy. Regarding the execution time, the SMPC is slower than
the DMPC. This result was expected as using scenarios leads to a higher number of decision
variables. This execution time should also grow with the number of scenarios and, more generally,
==PAGE:45==
5.3 Discussion
38
the execution time should grow with the complexity of the optimisation model. Similarly as using
better predictions for the DMPC, having a better model for the uncertainties will certainly grow
the performances of this policy. It should be noted that this policy is actually quite diﬀicult to
compare with the other ones as it does not rely on the exact same state variable. However, this
method does get better performances than a DMPC using the average of the scenarios (persistence
forecast output).
Random forest : The random forest policy manage to replicate complex behaviours from the deter-
ministic MPC with a faster execution time. It is therefore a good alternative to the MPC when
the execution time needs to be reduced to the maximum and the behaviour cannot be approxi-
mated with a rule-based method. This result is consistent with the work done in reference [4] for
a neural network model. However, the algorithm showed signs of overfitting during training. In
future work, a better feature selection could reduce the issue, but trying other learning methods
should also be considered. A common limitation to data-driven methods is the availability of
data. This limitation can partially be solved through the use of transfer learning when there is
little data on the consumer but other customer’s data are available. Another improvement on
data-driven policies could be carried out on the loss function. In this work, the mean squared
error was used as a loss function to fit to the DMPC decisions. However, the evaluation is done
using other performance indicators. Similarly to reference [16], future work could be done on
integrating those indicators in the loss function.
KNN : This method has a fast execution time but initially did not provide good financial results. The
KNN was found to produce SOC trajectories close to the average profile of the DMPC leading
to unremarkable results. It also showed signs of underfitting, which indicates that the model is
not appropriate for the task. However, by training it on the DMPC decisions with a degradation
term, its financial performance actually increased, in opposition to all the other policies behaviour.
The KNN leap in performance could be explained by the fact that the DMPC with a degradation
term has a profile closer to being periodic more easily replicable for the KNN model.
Execution time: All policies tested in this thesis had an execution time inferior to 1 second. Consid-
ering that the current time resolutions for load/generation smart meters are generally between
15 minutes and 60 minutes, all the policies can be considered fit for near-real-time scheduling.
One limitation of this result is that the decisions were taken for a discretised time space based
on the dataset resolution. While most papers uses this approximation to have tractable solutions,
it should still be reminded that in real-life decisions are taken on a continuous time space. Hav-
ing smaller decision time-steps would only make sense if the information flow had better time
resolution but would also generally require more computational power to get results over the
same period. Besides, having more raw features does not always lead to better predictions for
data-driven policies.
Best policy: From the evaluation results, the best performing policy seems to be function of the
characteristics and requirements of the system, which confirms the results from similar work [53,
61, 4].
It should be reminded that the evaluation results were computed using a dataset of a single domestic
system over a single month. It means that the results should not be generalized for the entire year.
Further work should be done with a longer dataset to get enough data to train the data-driven policies
and still get a test set equivalent to a year. Moreover, the experiment was carried out on a single
consumer, making the results biased towards his consumption profile. Carrying the same experiment
on multiple customer with different consumption profiles should be considered to get generalized results.
It should also be pointed out that the load/generation profiles and the price profile do not belong to the
same country or the same time period, which removes potential correlation between the two datasets.
The battery parameters used in this thesis should also be taken with caution. While being based on
real batteries [33, 19], it does not represent a specific commercially available product.
The battery model is also a limitation to this work. Some parameters (capacity, voltage, eﬀiciency)
were considered constant while, in reality, they are non-linear variables. This might lead to some overop-
timistic results. More accurate but computationally expensive policies such as dynamic programming
==PAGE:46==
5.3 Discussion
39
were not considered in this thesis as it did not fit into the near-real-time computing environment. How-
ever, combining it with learning algorithm like in reference [4] could solve the computational problem.
Another limitations concerns the degradation model (section 2.2.1). While the degradation cost was
designed as a rough approximation that could quickly be solved by modern solvers, the degradation
indicator also uses the same function to evaluate the damage done on the battery. As the indicator
does not require a fast execution time, a more time-consuming degradation function could instead be
used to generate an accurate evaluation while keeping the simplified degradation cost in the policies.
Moreover, the degradation function is using coeﬀicients from reference [33] but should ideally be fit to
a more recent battery degradation dataset. It should finally be noted that a simulation cannot replace
a real world experiment, which should also be considered to validate this evaluation’s results.
The CO2 emissions impact on the decisions were not significant on this dataset. The electricity
cost and the degradation cost seemed to prevail before the CO2 emissions cost for different policies.
However, the CO2 price in the European market has been rising this year 2.6 and is bound to continue
its growth as additional carbon taxes should be implemented in the coming years [32]. The share of
the CO2 emissions cost in the objective function should therefore increase in the near future but could
also be increased in the simulation as a preventive approach. It should also be reminded that the CO2
emissions computed are an optimistic approach which considers perfect information on the next 12
hours emissions.
Finally, the list of policies experimented in this thesis is not exhaustive and could be completed
with other methods. Either by improving the current policies or by adding different methods. Hybrid
policies [13, 52, 16] combining benefits from decision rules, optimisation and data-driven methods would
be interesting for future work.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to review different BESS operation policies for near-real-time scheduling.
This paper first described the considered PV-BESS problem (chapter 2), including the parameters, the
variables, the uncertainties and the different objectives that will be used. Then the decision policies
considered in this thesis were presented in chapter 3, including one rule-based policy (SCM), two op-
timisation based policies (DMPC, SMPC) and two data-driven policies (RF, KNN). Each policy had
variations on their objective function to either consider a financial cost, a degradation cost, and/or a
CO2 emissions cost. These policies were then compared using consumption, generation, price and CO2
profiles from open source datasets (section 4.1) using variations on the policies’ objective function and
the quality of the predictions. A preliminary evaluation was first carried out to extract the main charac-
teristics of the policies with a straightforward financial objective. Then a complementary evaluation on
CO2 emissions and battery degradation was completed using all four performance indicators: execution
time per decision, financial savings, battery degradation, and CO2 emissions per kWh. An overview of
the results for each policies under different problem variations can be found in table 5.2.
When the predicted load and PV generation is accurate, the optimisation based method had the
best performance but with a higher execution time. The SCM policy still performed very well in term
of cost savings being close to optimality with the smallest execution time out of all the policies. The RF
policy trained to replicate the DMPC decisions manage to get decent cost savings while reducing the
execution time of the DMPC. It still performed worse than the SCM both in execution time and savings.
However, when taking into account the battery degradation, the RF manages to replicate the DMPC
behaviour while keeping a low execution time. Making this policy a good alternative for cutting down
time for time-consuming optimisation methods. However, the RF policy did not manage to catch the
minor changes in the DMPC decisions when adding a CO2 emissions cost. The KNN method performed
poorly on the first tests but surprisingly got very good scores when trained on the more periodic DMPC
labels with degradation term.
In case of poor quality forecasts, the DMPC policy performed very poorly. While it managed to
keep a low degradation when included in the objective, it never achieved good financial performances.
That was to be expected for a deterministic optimisation with no mean of dealing with prediction errors.
Using data-driven methods manage to get slightly better cost savings but still far from the SCM results.
The stochastic optimisation approach proved to be effective in getting better economic performance
than its deterministic counterpart by making use of uncertainties modelled with scenarios.
Concerning the execution time, all policies tested in this thesis took less than 1 second per decision.
Considering that the current time resolutions for energy smart meters are generally between 15 minutes
and 60 minutes, all the policies can be considered fit for near-real-time scheduling.
Finally, similarly to references [53, 61, 4], the carried evaluation results indicate that the choice of
the best policy depends on the system’s characteristics and requirements. Rule-based methods have fast
execution time and perform well when correctly designed for the problem. For complex behaviour with
good quality forecasts, a DMPC policy will have the best objective performance but can induce a higher
execution time. A data-driven policy, such as a random forest model, can be used to approximate the
DMPC behaviour while reducing the execution time. Finally, for complex behaviours with uncertainties,
using a stochastic MPC could get better results than the previous deterministic approach but with a
longer execution time. A good scenario generation technique will also bring better results to an SMPC.
