Abstract
Increasing adoption of the Automatic Identification System (AIS) provides the autonomous
ability to locate and identify fishing vessels. With the immense near real­time AIS data
sets comes the opportunity to develop software that automatically processes the data
and provides actionable insights. In particular, detecting the anomalous activities of fish­
ing vessels is valuable since it could include unlawful activities, accidents, or threats. This
thesis constructs trajectory­based models to discriminate between legitimate fishing ves­
sel activities and anomalous events based on historical AIS data. The proposed approach
uses a variational recurrent neural network that learns a distribution representing the data
that can state the reconstruction likelihood of AIS trajectories. Then for anomaly detec­
tion, a geographically­dependent a contrario detection rule is used to account for the fact
that the learnt distribution may be location­dependent.
Two regions in Danish waters are examined to see how well­performing models for the
small region around Bornholm generalise to a larger region around Skagen, which has
more fishing vessel traffic. Additionally, this thesis analyses the impact of different input
AIS data representations, particularly moving from discrete to continuous inputs. Experi­
mental results show that the number and types of anomalies detected with the continuous
diagonal Gaussian­based model is more reasonable in the fishing vessel context for the
region around Bornholm. However, the discrete Bernoulli­based model works better for
the region around Skagen. Both models detect interesting outliers, but they falsely de­
tect too many anomalies. Further improvements and parameter tuning could make the
models even more helpful in practical anomaly detection applications.
Resumé
Den stigende anvendelse af ”Automatic Identification System” (AIS), gør det muligt au­
tonomt at lokalisere og identificere fiskefartøjer. Med de enorme AIS­datasæt, får man
mulighed for at udvikle software der automatisk behandler dataene og giver praktisk ind­
sigt. I særdeleshed, påvisning af fiskerfartøjers unormale aktiviteter kan give brugbar
informationer om fiskefartøjers uregelmæssige adfærd og potentielt føre til afsløringen af
ulovlige aktiviteter, ulykker eller trusler. Dette projekt konstruerer modeller til at skelne
mellem lovlige fiskefartøjes aktiviteter og unormale hændelser baseret på historiske AIS­
data. Den foreslåede metode bruger et ”variational recurrent neural network”, der lærer
en fordeling, der repræsenterer de data, der kan give rekonstruktion­”likelihood” for AIS­
baner. For at påvise anomalier, bruges en geografisk­afhængig ”a contrario”­regel for at
tage hensyn til, at den tillærte fordeling kan være lokation afhængig.
To områder i det danske farvand undersøges, for at se hvordan velfungerende modeller
for det lille havområde omkring Bornholm kan overføres til en større område omkring Sk­
agen som har mere fiskefartøjer trafik. Derudover, analyserer projekted indflydelsen af
forskellige AIS­data input repræsentationer, især fra diskret inputs til kontinuerte inputs.
Eksperimentelle resultater viser, at antallet og typer af anomalier påvist med den kontin­
uerlige ”diagonal Gaussian”­baserede model er mere brugbar til området omkring Born­
holm, mens den diskret ”Bernoulli”­baserede model er bedre egnet til området omkring
Skagen. Begge modeller påviser interessante afvigelser, men de viser for mange falske
anomalier. Videreudvikling af modellerne kan gøre dem endnu mere nyttig i praktiske
anomalidetektion applikationer.

Introduction
Illegal, unreported and unregulated (IUU) fishing remains a significant problem, contribut­
ing to overfishing, threatening the sustainability of global fisheries and food security, and
with severe economic consequences (Agnew et al. 2009; Petrossian 2014; Cabral et al.
2018). Suspicious fishing activities are sometimes the subject of mass media articles that
can help raise awareness of this critical issue. A particularly striking Danish article was
recently published describing how foreign ”ghost” vessels vacuum Danish waters (Her­
schend 2021). It uncovers Dutch vessels, some over 40 meters long, deliberately hiding
their location while in Danish waters by turning off their Automatic Identification System
(AIS). Alarmingly, some of these fishing trawlers deploy and pull large and heavy metal
chains across the seabed that can cause significant ecological damage. In Iceland, AIS
data reportedly show attempted whaling inside whale watching regions, which regula­
tions prohibit (“Sagðir hafa stundað hvalveiðar á hvalaskoðunarsvæði” 2011). Another
Icelandic news article reported fishing activity in a restricted area, where the vessel in
question was forced back to port (Proppé 2021).
The AIS is an automatic information system that provides the autonomous ability to locate
and identify AIS broadcasting vessels. Increasing adoption of the AIS enables estimation
of global fishing vessel activities at the individual vessel level in near real­time. Processing
data sets containing billions of AIS messages identified more than 70,000 fishing vessels
from 2012 to 2016 (D. A. Kroodsma et al. 2018) and around 60,000 fishing vessels in 2017
(Taconet, D. Kroodsma, and Fernandes 2019). With such large near real­time data sets
comes the need for software that automatically processes the data and provides action­
able insight. In particular, detecting the anomalous activity of fishing vessels is of interest
since it might include accidents, threats, or unlawful activities. These include vessel cap­
sizing, collisions, loss of navigation, engine failure, smuggling, military manoeuvres, and
IUU fishing (Martineau and Roy 2011; Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021). The overarching
goal of this project is to have a positive impact on the sustainability of global fisheries by
assisting with the identification of IUU fishing. This is very much in line with the United
Nations (UN) 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), in particular goal 14 that has
to do with life below water (Cormier and Elliott 2017).
1.1
Scope and Objectives
The historically gathered AIS data has been used extensively to improve maritime safety,
security, and efficiency, with tasks such as collision risk assessment, route estimation,
vessel path planning, and anomaly detection (Tu et al. 2017). Since the available data for
this project consists of inputs without any corresponding ground truth outcome measure­
ments, the focus is necessarily on unsupervised anomaly detection techniques. There
are many challenging aspects of performing anomaly detection in general and even more
so when it comes to fishing vessel trajectory­based anomaly detection. These types of
challenges include
• The notion of normalcy may evolve over time. This is especially true for fishing
vessels, where seasonality could be expected.
• As is the case for this project, the lack of labelled data for training, validation, and
testing anomaly detection models is usually a significant issue.

• Anomalies are different depending on the application. In some domains, small
deviations are anomalous, but in others, they might be expected. For example, a
relatively small deviation when a vessel is located along sea or shipping lanes may
be an anomaly, but outside those, its general manoeuvring can be very different
(e.g. the vessel is fishing), and a similar deviation could be normal.
• Data sets can contain noise that looks similar to an actual anomaly, such that an
observation is statistically unusual without its behaviours being suspicious. Effects
like sea surface and wind conditions not included in the model can cause variation
in the AIS data streams. Then, for example, in rough weather, a ship’s behaviour
could be completely different and seemingly abnormal without being suspicious.
In general, anomaly detection is nontrivial to solve, and many techniques solve a spe­
cific problem that depends on the characteristics of the data, the availability of a labelled
data set, and the type of anomalies that should be detected. In addition, for data such
as sequence data and spatial data, observations are related to each other, which must
be considered when choosing an appropriate anomaly detection technique (Chandola,
Banerjee, and Kumar 2009).
The main high­level idea in this project is to construct normalcy models to discriminate
between legitimate fishing vessel activities and anomalous events based on historical AIS
data. It is then possible to use the models to detect deviations from the normalcy to clas­
sify new AIS trajectories as normal or abnormal. There are many different approaches to
defining a normalcy model, but this project chiefly investigates deep learning methods for
vessel trajectory­based anomaly detection. The main goals are to reconstruct, extend,
test, and evaluate a very promising approach that has shown relevant detection of ab­
normal behaviours for cargo and tanker vessels (Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021). It is a
probabilistic approach based on a variational recurrent neural network (VRNN) that learns
a distribution representing the data and can state the reconstruction likelihood of AIS tra­
jectories. Additionally, it employs a geographically­dependent a contrario detection rule
to account for the fact that the learnt distribution may be location­dependent.
Of particular interest is investigating model performance on regions of interest (ROIs) in
Danish waters and on fishing vessels that generally have very complex moving patterns.
The hope is that AIS­based deep learning fishing vessel trajectory anomaly detection can
provide insights that make Danish waters safer and positively impact the sustainability of
Danish fisheries. To that end, this thesis will
• Analyse the VRNN­based model performance for fishing vessels around Denmark.
• Evaluate the generalisation of the VRNN­based model to a larger ROI.
• Examine the impact of different input AIS data representations, as discussed in
chapter 3, in particular of moving from discrete to continuous inputs.
• Assess the impact of switching from a diagonal multivariate Gaussian distribution to
a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) for the generating distribution of the VRNN.

Related Work
This chapter summarises and evaluates work done in literature related to this project,
identifying the successes and limitations of proposed solutions to similar problems in the
maritime domain and other contexts. Section 2.1 introduces and broadly touches on gen­
eral anomaly detection, section 2.2 focuses on maritime specific anomaly detection, with
an empathises on traditional machine learning­based methods, and section 2.3 dives into
deep learning methods for anomaly detection.
2.1
Anomaly Detection
Anomaly detection concerns finding patterns in data that deviate from expected normal
behaviour. Anomalies and outliers are two terms most commonly used, sometimes inter­
changeably, in the literature for such patterns. Anomaly detection has been researched
within numerous fields and is of interest since anomalies translate to significant and even
critical actionable information in many practical applications (Chandola, Banerjee, and Ku­
mar 2009). For example, anomaly detection finds use in fraud detection for credit cards
(Aleskerov, Freisleben, and Rao 1997), taxation (Vanhoeyveld, Martens, and Peeters
2020), and cryptocurrencies (Monamo, Marivate, and Twala 2016), insider trading de­
tection (Kulkarni, Mani, and Domeniconi 2017), cyber security (Hong, Liu, and Govin­
darasu 2014), fault detection in spacecrafts (Fujimaki, Yairi, and Machida 2005), military
surveillance for enemy activities (Bhattacharyya and Kalita 2019), and maritime situational
awareness (Martineau and Roy 2011).
Supervised anomaly detection assumes the availability of labelled training data for nor­
mal and anomalous observations. However, no well established common labelled bench­
mark data set for maritime anomaly detection exists (Laxhammar 2008), and the available
project data has no labelled training data. Then supervised methods, such as decision
trees, support vector machines, and nearest neighbour classification, cannot be used
(Omar, Ngadi, and Jebur 2013). Therefore, unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms
for multivariate data are of primary interest in this project.
Unsupervised anomaly detection techniques are the most widely applicable and require
no training labels. With such techniques, only the internal structure of the data set is taken
into account, and the implicit assumption is that normal observations are by far more fre­
quent than anomalies (Kalinichenko, Shanin, and Taraban 2014). Goldstein and Uchida
2016 present a comparative evaluation of unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms
for multivariate data, where 19 different unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms are
evaluated on ten data sets from multiple application domains. The approaches all have
different strengths and weaknesses that depend on the specific task or application, the
nature of the anomalies in the data set, and the available computational power.
2.2
Maritime Anomaly Detection
Many maritime specific anomaly detection papers have been published in recent years
(Riveiro, Pallotta, and Vespe 2018), generally studying the following three main types of
vessel anomalies:
• Positional anomaly, where the vessel is located in unexpected, restricted, or for­
bidden regions.

• Speed anomaly, where the vessel’s speed is above or below some threshold that
can be considered normal for a vessel with similar characteristics.
• Time anomaly, where the time of a vessel’s voyage is unusual or unexpected.
Different techniques can detect different types of anomalies, but positional and speed
anomalies are most commonly researched and detected (Tu et al. 2017).
Maritime anomaly detection methods can broadly be classified into data­driven/learning­
based methods and signature/rule­based expert systems (Riveiro, Pallotta, and Vespe
2018). Rule­based anomaly detection uses a set of expert rules to indicate when an
anomaly has occurred. It has the advantage of being interpretable and easy to under­
stand, but the disadvantage that an exhaustive list of anomalous activities for all scenar­
ios must be predefined (Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021). Rule­based expert systems in the
maritime domain have been proposed by Edlund et al. 2006, Roy 2010, and Kazemi et al.
2013. However, defining an exhaustive list can be quite a daunting task, if not impossible,
particularly for fishing vessel trajectory­based anomaly detection. Therefore, this project
focuses on learning­based anomaly detection, where this list of rules is learned implicitly
using historical data.
The most popular non­parametric maritime anomaly detection method is the KDE, where
most papers adopt a Gaussian kernel. For parametric methods, the GMM is the most
popular, and for clustering methods, the density­based spatial clustering of applications
with noise (DBSCAN) is the most popular (Riveiro, Pallotta, and Vespe 2018). Other more
sophisticated methods have been proposed, such as Gaussian Processes (Will, Peel, and
Claxton 2011; Kowalska and Peel 2012), Bayesian Networks (Johansson and Falkman
2007; Mascaro, Nicholso, and Korb 2014), and Hidden Markov Models (Toloue and Ja­
han 2018), that attempt to capture the sequential structure of AIS vessel trajectories. The
application of unsupervised deep learning methods for maritime anomaly detection is rel­
atively sparse, but Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 propose a VRNN­based architecture as
discussed in more detail in the next section. The basic idea for all these approaches is
that vessels of the same type should have similar behaviours in a small region.
For maritime anomaly detection, DBSCAN has been applied to cluster critical points of
the AIS trajectory into points where the vessel enters an ROI, where it exits the ROI, and
where it stops (Varlamis et al. 2019). However, since fishing vessel AIS trajectory patterns
do not follow relatively simple route­based patterns typical for cargo and tanker vessels,
DBSCAN has little hope in generalising to fishing vessels, so it is not appropriate as a
standalone method for this project (Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2018).
Both KDE and GMM are powerful density estimation tools that often have good perfor­
mance but can have high computational costs and require a large amount of normalcy
training data to produce reliable estimations (Tu et al. 2017). Comparing KDE and GMM
for statistical anomaly detection in sea traffic found that KDE for normalcy modelling can
perform better than GMM by capturing more details in the normal data, for example, re­
lated to the spatial distribution of vessel location along sea lanes (Laxhammar, Falkman,
and Sviestins 2009). However, similar to the issue with DBSCAN, it is not reasonable to
assume that fishing vessel traffic more or less follows sea lanes that can be described as
sequences of straight­line segments. That is, KDE’s advantage over GMM regarding the
ability to accurately model arbitrary sea lanes is not directly of interest for this project.
Laxhammar, Falkman, and Sviestins 2009 made a KDE and GMM comparison by using
both methods to estimate the probability density function of a position­velocity vector of
normal vessel trajectories, with performance measured based on simulated anomalous

2.3
Deep Learning Methods for Anomaly Detection
There is a great deal of literature on deep learning and artificial neural networks in gen­
eral. Experimental results show state­of­the­art performance using deep learning meth­
ods in various application domains, especially when dealing with large­scale data sets
(LeCun, Bengio, and Hinton 2015; Alom et al. 2019). In recent years, deep learning­
based anomaly detection has shown great success in tackling challenging issues, as
documented in several survey and review articles that discuss opportunities, challenges,
and different deep learning anomaly detection methods (Chalapathy and Chawla 2019;
Pang et al. 2021). One of the biggest criticisms of deep learning is that the process can
be very obscure and cannot provide the operator with explanations (Martineau and Roy
2011). Therefore, developing inherently interpretable deep learning anomaly detection
models is important, but balancing their interpretability and effectiveness remains a chal­
lenge (Pang et al. 2021).
Deep learning methods for anomaly detection excel on diverse data types, such as multi­
variate data, sequences, and images, by learning intricate structures and relations tailored
for anomaly detection using neural networks. There are essentially three ways to perform
deep learning­based anomaly detection, as shown in fig. 2.1 and presented by Pang et
al. 2021. At one extreme, deep learning can be used purely for feature extraction or di­
mensionality reduction, with independent anomaly scoring. The disadvantage of such an
approach is that entirely disjointed feature extraction and anomaly scoring frequently lead
to suboptimal anomaly scores. At the other extreme, deep learning finds use for end­to­
end anomaly score learning, where a neural network is involved that directly learns the
anomaly scores. However, such approaches often require novel loss functions to drive
the anomaly scoring network, as is done by Sultani, C. Chen, and Shah 2018.
Finally, deep learning can be used to learn feature representations of normality, where
the learned representations can improve anomaly detection since it is forced to capture
fundamental underlying data structures. In this context, autoencoders (AEs) (Jiang et al.
2014; Ionescu et al. 2019), sparse AEs (Makhzani and Frey 2013), and VAEs (Doersch
2016) have been proposed, where the assumption is that normal observations are easier
to reconstruct than anomalies from the compressed space. However, standalone, these
methods lack the notion of temporal structure in a sequence. Anomaly detection based
on generative adversarial networks (GANs) have also been proposed (Schlegl et al. 2017;
Li, D. Chen, Goh, et al. 2018; Li, D. Chen, Jin, et al. 2019), where the assumption is that
normal data are easier to generate than anomalies from the latent feature space of the
generative network. However, training of GANs can fail to converge or suffer from mode
collapse (Metz et al. 2016), and for complex data distributions, the generator network can
be misled and generate observations outside the normal data manifold (Pang et al. 2021).
The choice of network architecture primarily depends on the nature of the input data (Cha­
lapathy and Chawla 2019). The AIS vessel trajectories are sequential time series data,
and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) can capture features of such data. Standard
RNNs process data and cyclically perform computations in a forward direction, i.e. from
the past to the future. Bidirectional RNNs (Schuster and Paliwal 1997) provide an archi­
tecture where the calculations are conducted in both the forward and backward directions.

However, this may not be intuitive for vessel trajectories (Murray and Perera 2021), and
for near real­time vessel trajectory anomaly detection, a bidirectional architecture cannot
be used. The limitation of RNNs is that they fail to capture the context of long sentences,
but Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) attempt to solve
that. LSTMs are a type of RNNs that include a memory cell to store information from pre­
vious time steps (Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le 2014), and GRUs use gates to control the
information flow (Chung, Gulcehre, et al. 2014). These types of methods find great use
in literature and different architecture combinations have been successfully leveraged for
sequence data (Nanduri and Sherry 2016; Shipmon et al. 2017; Munir et al. 2018; Had­
dadPajouh et al. 2018).
A stacked LSTM architecture for anomaly detection in time series proposed by Malho­
tra, Vig, et al. 2015 trained a network on normal data and used it as a predictor over a
number of time steps. For assessing the likelihood of anomalous observations, the predic­
tion errors were modelled as a multivariate Gaussian distribution. Similarly, a deep RNN
architecture with LSTM units was used as a predictive model for healthy electrocardiog­
raphy signals, where the probability distribution of the prediction errors from the recurrent
models was used to indicate normal or abnormal behaviour (Chauhan and Vig 2015).
RNNs expressive power and ability to train using error backpropagation are the critical
reasons for their popularity as generative models for highly structured sequential data.
However, the only source of output variability is in the conditional observation probability
density function. That could be a problem when trying to model the variability observed in
highly structured data characterised by complex dependencies among the observations
at different time steps (Chung, Kastner, et al. 2015). With that in mind, neural network
architecture structures that combine the elements of RNNs and AEs have been proposed
(Malhotra, Ramakrishnan, et al. 2016; W. Lu et al. 2017; Kieu et al. 2019).

VRNNs take this a step further by combining elements of RNNs and VAEs. VRNNs are
recurrent latent variable models for sequential data that include latent random variables
into the hidden states of RNNs using elements from VAEs. This model should have im­
proved representational power that can help model the kind of variability present in highly
structured multidimensional sequences (Chung, Kastner, et al. 2015). In this project,
sources of such variations beyond the behavioural patterns of the vessels include the fact
that vessel trajectories may reflect sea surface and wind conditions and the quality of AIS
broadcasted signals, which may depend on the metocean conditions and interference in
densely populated traffic regions (Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021).
VRNN inspired approaches have found use in applications related to machine transla­
tion (Su et al. 2018), anomaly detection in videos (Y. Lu et al. 2019), clinical time series
forecasting (Ullah et al. 2020), and autonomous driving (Eskandar et al. 2021). In 2020,
a generative model for multimodal trajectory prediction for interacting pedestrians based
on a VRRN was proposed, treating the trajectory prediction problem as modelling the
joint probability distribution over sequences (Brito et al. 2020). The objective was to de­
velop a prediction model suitable for interaction aware autonomous navigation. Their
method achieved faster convergence than GAN­based approaches and, by using a time­
dependent prior over the latent space, enabled the model to achieve state­of­the­art per­
formance and generate diverse trajectories. To predict the trajectories, they model the
network output as a GMM to account for the multimodality of pedestrian motion.
Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 published a paper proposing a maritime anomaly detector
where deep learning is used to learn feature representations of normality. They used a
VRNN­based probabilistic neural network representation of AIS trajectories and a con­
trario detection. The model assumes that most of the vessel behaviours in the training
set are normal and that it can learn the dynamics of those trajectories and capture the
high complexity of AIS data using a VRNN. Since the density and behaviours of vessels
are not geographically homogeneous, the model employs a geographically­dependent a
contrario detection rule. It works by dividing the ROI into small cells and applying a local
threshold to state the normality of each AIS message in the given cell. The paper is an
extended version of their previous work presented in (Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2018), and
a third paper exists analysing the proposed maritime anomaly detector in an operational
context (Nguyen, Simonin, et al. 2020).
The work done by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 is of utmost interest for this project and
forms the basis for much of the work done in the project. Specifically, they deal with AIS
data in an unsupervised manner for anomaly detection, with the possibility of processing
real AIS data in a streaming context. The results show relevant detection of abnormal
behaviours for cargo vessels and tankers, but some fishing vessels that went fishing were
detected as abnormal because there were not enough similar AIS trajectories in their
training set. So, trajectories can be statistically abnormal without their behaviours being
suspicious.
Interestingly, Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 do not represent an AIS message using a four­
dimensional real­valued vector (two dimensions for position, one for speed, and one for
course, i.e. [lat, lon, SOG, COG]). They argue that this representation is not suitable for
neural network­based methods since it is difficult for a neural network to disentangle the
underlying geospatial meaning. Instead, as part of preprocessing, they map the continu­
ous real­valued trajectory points to a ”four­hot vector”, where there are precisely four ones
and zeros everywhere else. Additionally, they re­sample trajectories to a resolution of ten
minutes using linear interpolation so that the time between observations is equidistant.

Applying a four­hot encoding to the data comes with its own set of issues as it reduces
the resolution of the predictions (Murray and Perera 2021), drastically increases the di­
mensionality of the input space, and introduces additional hyper­parameters related to
the resolution of each bin in the one­hot vectors. Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 note
that when the resolution is too high, the whole network becomes too bulky and requires
extensive computational resources to run, and may lead to overfitting.
On the other
hand, critical information may be lost if the resolution is too low. They further state that
for anomaly detection, very accurate position and velocity features may not be needed
(Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021). For the complex moving patterns of fishing vessels, the
four­hot representation is seemingly limiting, so it is of interest to further investigate the
generalisation of the VRNN­based model to fishing vessels and to evaluate the impact of
moving from a discrete four­hot representation to continuous four­dimensional inputs (i.e.
[lat, lon, SOG, COG]).
Finally, Ding et al. 2020 present a novel approach to vessel trajectory prediction based
on a variational LSTM where the state vector of a vessel at time t is represented by
[∆time, ∆lat, ∆lon, SOG, COG]. They do not use any interpolation methods when pro­
cessing trajectories and instead calculate the time increment between two continuous
data points. Furthermore, instead of the actual latitude and longitude, they propose us­
ing the differences ∆lat = latt −latt−1 and ∆lon = lont −lont−1. Lastly, to weaken the
influence of the magnitude difference between the input data, they normalised the input
data using min­max normalisation. This additional possible AIS data representation is in­
teresting to consider and could be a part of further experimentation in future work. Since
there is no single agreed­upon approach that works best in all situations for representing
AIS data and constructing trajectories for modelling, experimentation with different data
representation and VRNN generating distribution is warranted.

AIS Data and Data Representation
This chapter details the raw AIS data, different input data representation, ROIs, and data
preprocessing steps. As such, section 3.1 provides details on the primary information
included in AIS messages and their possible shortcomings. Raw continuous AIS inputs
and a mapping to a discrete representation are discussed and illustrated in section 3.2.
Two ROIs ranging from relatively small to large in size are presented in section 3.3, with
an exploratory data summary in section 3.4. Finally, section 3.5 outlines preprocessing
steps taken to define the training data sets.
3.1
Automatic Identification System
Vessel AIS data sets are based on transponders located onboard vessels broadcast­
ing messages that contain the vessel’s Global Positioning System (GPS) coordinates,
its unique Maritime Mobile Service Identity (MMSI) number, course­over­ground (COG),
speed­over­ground (SOG), heading, and other information about the vessel and voyage.
These messages provide information to identify, distinguish, and locate vessels for colli­
sion avoidance, navigation, monitoring, and historical analysis of suitably equipped ves­
sels (Bole, Wall, and Norris 2013). The AIS transponder devices can be classified into
class A, class B, and class B+. Vessels broadcast messages at different frequencies
depending on their current speed and transponder class, with class A generally sending
more frequent messages than the others. The frequency ranges from two seconds (for
class A while underway using the engine) to three minutes (for class A at anchor or class
B vessels going less than 2 knots) (Taconet, D. Kroodsma, and Fernandes 2019).
There are some notable shortcomings when it comes to monitoring fishing vessel activity
using AIS data. First, only a tiny fraction of fishing vessels in the world are equipped to
broadcast AIS messages. Additionally, AIS messages are susceptible to jamming, and
vessels can turn off their AIS and even send false reports. Finally, regulations gener­
ally place more requirements on larger vessels, making the AIS data skewed towards
them. For example, the European Union has fairly stringent AIS regulation, requiring all
vessels over 15 m to broadcast AIS messages (Taconet, D. Kroodsma, and Fernandes
2019). That being said, the number of active AIS broadcasting non­Chinese fishing ves­
sels (dominated mainly by European countries) has been increasing, with some seasonal
fluctuations (D. A. Kroodsma et al. 2018; Taconet, D. Kroodsma, and Fernandes 2019).
3.2

Theory
This chapter outlines the theoretical foundation of the main building blocks of the proposed
approach. Section 4.1 introduces a generative model for non­sequential data called vari­
ational autoencoders. Section 4.2 moves to sequence modelling with a recurrent neural
network. Section 4.3 combines elements of the first two sections by presenting a varia­
tional RNN for sequential data. Since some experimentation will be done using GMMs
as the generating distribution of the VRNN, section 4.3.1 briefly introduces the GMM. Fi­
nally, in section 4.4, properties of a contrario detection are presented and discussed. The
section also contains a brief theoretical introduction to KDE, which is used as part of the
a contrario detection in this project.
While all these methods find general use in literature, the idea throughout this chapter is
to have the application to vessel trajectories in mind and how the methods can find use
in this project. The notation used in this chapter is inspired by Bishop 2006.
4.1

In this section the focus is not on entire trajectories, but instead on a data set {x1, x2,
. . . , xN} containing N i.i.d. samples of some continuous or discrete variable x. This can
be seen as the first stepping stone towards the VRNN. One can, for example, think of this
as N AIS messages that are either represented with raw inputs (continuous) or four­hot
encoded (discrete). VAEs introduce a latent random variable z, designed to capture the
variations in the observed variable x. The assumption is that the data is generated by a
random process that involves a continuous unobserved random variable z, such that x

distribution of x and z can be factorised as

4.2
Recurrent Neural Network
The second stepping stone towards the VRNN model are RNNs. They are a family of
neural networks that can handle sequential data with variable­length input and output.
The focus is now on a specific trajectory training example xi

RNNs process data and cyclically perform computations by recursively processing each
sample while advancing an internal hidden state h ∈IRM. The parameters governing the
connections are the same for each step. With parameter sharing across different model
parts, RNNs can generalise to sequence lengths not seen during training. At each time
step t, the hidden state is updated with

sequence of x values as a sequence of one step probabilistic predictions, one can capture
the full joint distribution across the entire sequence (Chung, Kastner, et al. 2015).

termines how to advance the hidden internal state and a mapping g from the hidden state
to the output. RNNs expressive power and ability to train using error backpropagation are
the key reasons for their popularity as generative models for highly structured sequen­
tial data. However, the only source of output variability is in the conditional observation
probability density function. That could be a problem when trying to model the variability
observed in highly structured data characterised by complex dependencies among the
observations (Chung, Kastner, et al. 2015; Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville 2016).

5.1
General Setup
This section first outlines the frameworks, data splits, and feature standardisation used
in this project. Then the training and model hyperparameter values are presented where
most are kept at fixed values since the primary focus is more broadly on the investigation
of different experimental setups, as detailed in section 5.2.
5.1.1
Code and Frameworks
Results presented in this thesis are produced using Python 3.9.6 and PyTorch 1.10.0.
The PyTorch code runs on DTU High­Performance Computing (HPC) services using Tesla
A100 16 GB during training. All code related to this project is available on GitHub:
https://github.com/ThordurPall/vessel­trajectory­anomaly­detection.
5.1.2
Data Splits and Feature Standardisation
The available data set is randomly split into 80% for training, 10% for validation, and 10%
for testing. Before using these data sets, the input features are standardised. With the dis­
crete four­hot representation, the inputs are just centred by the training means. However,
when using the continuous four­dimensional inputs, the inputs are on different scales and
are standardised by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation.
5.1.3
Training and Model Hyperparameters
There are quite a lot of hyperparameters that come with the proposed approach, and the
values used in this project are inspired by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 and their accompa­
nying Git repository available on GitHub: https://github.com/CIA­Oceanix/GeoTrackNet.
These values showed promising results for discrete four­hot encoded inputs in their work.
The batch size is fixed at 32, and the Adam optimiser is used, so one optimisation step is
taken every 32 observations processed. The LSTM has a single hidden layer of size 100,
and the dimension of the real­valued latent variable z is also 100. Three fully connected
networks with one hidden layer of size 100 and a ReLU activation function are used as the
feature extractor of the joint inputs, the feature extractor of the latent random variables,
and for the prior distribution representation. Similarly, the approximate posterior and the
generating distribution are parameterised by two fully connected networks with one hidden
layer of size 100. The learning rate used by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 is 0.0003,
but that is not well suited for all model combinations in this project, so experiments are
performed using different learning rates. When visual examination of learning curves
indicates that a model has not trained well, learning rate schedulers are introduced in an
attempt to improve training (see section 5.2).
Since the focus is more broadly on investigating different model setups and generalisa­
tion, thorough hyperparameter tuning (such as using Bayesian optimisation) will not be
done. Additionally, note that the task at hand is not to develop a model that does perfect
reconstructions but to use the model as part of an anomaly detection framework. As such,

the model should perform well with most trajectories but poorly for anomalies such that it
can show a difference between normal and anomalous trajectories.
5.2
Experimental Setups
This project performs a number of experiments with different combinations of the following:
• ROIs: Both the Bornholm and Skagen ROIs are considered and compared.
• Data Representation: Experiments are done using both a discrete four­hot repre­
sentation and using the actual continuous four­dimensional inputs. With different
input representation comes the need for different generating distributions for the
VRNN. This project experiments with the following distributions:
– Bernoulli: As is done by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021, Bernoulli distributions
are always used for the four­hot encoded input bins.
– Diagonal Gaussian or GMM: A multivariate Gaussian with a diagonal variance­
covariance matrix (parameterised by a mean and a variance vector) or a GMM
with M ∈{2, 3, 4} diagonal Gaussians is used for continuous inputs. Further
experimenting with more components, isotropic Gaussian, and a full variance­
covariance matrix could be considered but is not done in this thesis.
• Training data comprising of different vessel types: Experiments are performed
for models trained with a training data set comprising only fishing vessels, only cargo
and tankers, and using all three vessel types (fishing vessels, cargo, and tankers).
However, the primary focus is on models trained only on fishing vessels.
• Injecting trajectories into small data sets: Experiments are done for the Born­
holm ROI, where cargo and tanker trajectories are injected into the training set in
proportion to the fishing vessel only training set. The idea is that the small Born­
holm fishing vessel training set could benefit from additional trajectories to assist
in learning general vessel manoeuvring. For example, a model called ”Fishing +
200%” contains 1/3 fishing and 2/3 cargo and tanker trajectories.
• Generative bias: A bias can be added to the raw output of the fully connected
network that parameterises the generating distribution. This has proven helpful for
initialising the distribution to a sensible starting point when using a Bernoulli generat­
ing distribution. For discrete four­hot encoded inputs, the generative bias is always
set to the training data mean, but for the actual continuous four­dimensional inputs,
experiments are done in Bornholm with and without a generative bias.
• Different learning rate: Not every model works well with the same learning rate,
so some learning rate tweaking is done.
• Scheduler: For models that are challenging to train, a scheduler is used to ad­
just the learning rate based on the number of epochs. Trials were done where the
scheduler decays the learning rate by a factor γ every epoch, but it either decreases
the learning rate too much and converges to a solution with a relatively high loss,
or does not decrease it enough, and the loss blows up. So instead, the schedulers
used decay the learning rate by γi once the number of epoch reaches milestone i.
Experimental results with different setup combinations are presented in chapter 6. Since
this thesis focuses on reconstruction errors and their use for anomaly detection, both the
loss and reconstruction log­likelihood learning curves are shown in chapter 6.

6.2.1.1 Continuous Models Without Generative bias
The initial idea is to train continuous models without adding a mean value bias to the
raw output of the fully connected network that parameterises the generating distribution.
Without such a bias, the network needs to learn to find logical mean value vectors en­
tirely on its own and without a good starting position. After running many experiments
with different learning rates and data sets, the conclusion is that the continuous Born­
holm models are much more challenging to train than the discrete models presented in
section 6.1.1. To give the general idea, fig. 6.14 presents learning curves validated on
a validation set comprising of only fishing vessels for a small subset of the experiments
performed. The model trained on all three vessel types has a learning rate of 0.0001, and
the GMMs presented here have four components. These experiments show the typical
observed training behaviour, where the learning curves are very noisy. The only learning
curves where such a pattern was not observed was when the learning rate was too low,
and the model converged to a solution with a relatively high loss.
Considering the learning curves in fig. 6.14 trials were done where a scheduler decays
the learning rate by a factor γ every epoch. Figure B.1 in appendix B shows a few such
experiments, but either the learning rate decreases too much and converges to a solution
with a relatively high loss, or does not decrease enough, and the loss blows up. So
instead, the scheduler used in fig. 6.15 decays the learning rate by γi once the number
of epochs reaches milestone i. The figure shows the learning curves for the chosen
Bornholm diagonal Gaussian model when a generative bias is not used and is trained
only on fishing vessels. The initial learning rate is 0.00005, schedule milestones = [500,
700, 1000, 1300], and γ = [0.5, 0.5, 0.7, 0.6]. The location of the milestones where the
learning rate is decreased by a factor γi is shown in fig. 6.15a as black vertical lines.

0.6, 0.6]. The diagonal Gaussian model is the one shown in fig. 6.15 and is plotted again

7
Discussion and Future Work
With lessons learned from chapter 6, this chapter discusses the results and makes rec­
ommendations for future work related to VRNN trajectory­based fishing vessel anomaly
detection. Section 7.1 broadly explains the overall results and how they relate to work
done in literature. The section also points out the main model limitations. Section 7.2 then
makes recommendations on where first to place implementation effort going forward.
7.1
Discussion
The approach taken in this thesis was based on work done by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al.
2021. Their work showed promising results for cargo and tanker vessels, but this thesis
investigated the generalisation to regions of interest in Danish waters and on fishing ves­
sels that generally have very complex moving patterns. Two ROIs were examined and
compared as part of this thesis. Firstly, a small region around Bornholm that has similar
characteristics as the ROI used by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021. Secondly, a relatively
large region around Skagen that has significantly more fishing vessel traffic and is used to
evaluate the generalisation of the VRNN­based approach to a larger ROI. While Nguyen,
Vadaine, et al. 2021 use a four­hot encoded input representation and a Bernoulli gener­
ating distribution, this thesis also analysed the impact of moving to continuous inputs.
Experimental results showed that the models with continuous inputs were much more
challenging to train than the discrete models. So, a benefit of the discrete four­hot encod­
ing presented by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021 was the ease of training. However, since
there were so many empty cells in the Bornholm training set, it seemed that the Bernoulli­
based models could not reasonably generalise to new trajectories and the Bornholm di­
agonal Gaussian­based models were more promising for anomaly detection. Therefore
a continuous­based generalisation of the approach proposed by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al.
2021 could find use for smaller training sets (relative to the size of the ROI) containing
complex trajectories. For Skagen, which is much more densely populated with fishing
vessels, experimental results indicated that the number and types of anomalies detected
with the discrete Bernoulli­based model is more reasonable in the fishing vessel context.
After a close examination of the most extreme detected outliers (the worst reconstructions
by reconstruction likelihood) in chapter 6 and a private conversation with a fisherman (see
appendix A), the chosen models did detect fascinating outliers. Overall, the conclusion
was that the most extreme detected outliers seem rather strange or abnormal in the fishing
vessel context. They sail over land, show cargo­like behaviour, go up to relatively high
speed, or are in very sparsely populated areas. So, it appears that the proposed approach
can generalise to fishing vessels around Denmark and larger regions, but it requires the
region to be densely populated with fishing vessels. However, the chosen models have
two main limitations. Firstly, they have a tendency to reconstruct AIS updates by copying
the previous actual values. This could result from the number of trajectories with low
fluctuating inputs since the models can then reconstruct the AIS updates almost perfectly
and incur very little loss simply by using the previous observation. There are, for example,
many fishing vessels in the data set that are static in harbour. Secondly, the models
seemingly falsely detect too many anomalies to be completely reliable in practical anomaly
detection applications. For instance, as was the case with Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021,
some fishing vessels that went fishing are detected as abnormal because there are just
not enough similar AIS trajectories in the training set. Finally, note that the proposed

approach is unsupervised, so the detected anomalies are statistically unusual but may
not involve suspicious events or have malicious intent.
7.2
Future Work
Preprocessing steps should not remove trajectories out at sea, but a better filter is needed
to detect when vessels are static in harbour. Currently, filtering is based on the naviga­
tional status, but there are fishing vessels where the last status is ”Engaged in fishing”,
and still, they are constantly sending AIS messages while in harbour. Ongoing experimen­
tation involves investigating the impact of removing static points by adding a naive filter
that removes AIS updates with a speed less than 0.2 knots. However, this will remove too
many points, so a more sophisticated filter is required for production­ready applications.
Many preprocessing parameters come with the preprocessing steps taken, and it is worth
experimenting with those. In particular, the design choice to split trajectories that are
longer than 24 hours into roughly equal parts so that the sequence length does not get
overly large. For the Skagen ROI, where around 22% of trajectories are longer than 24
hours, experiments should be done where the maximum 24­hour threshold is increased.
This could help the models detect when vessels are turning off their AIS while out at sea.
There is a clear benefit from the additional fishing vessel training data examples in the Sk­
agen ROI. For example, the chosen Bornholm Bernoulli­based model presented in fig. 6.1
starts to overfit relatively quickly, while the Skagen Bernoulli­based models in fig. 6.8 do
not run into such an issue. So, the Bornholm models would most likely benefit from ad­
ditional fishing vessel training trajectories. As such, assuming there is no substantial
seasonal effect, it seems advantageous to include data over more months or years.
In literature, no single agreed­upon approach works best in all situations for represent­
ing AIS data and constructing trajectories for modelling, so other data representations
could be considered.
For example, Ding et al. 2020 represent the state vector of a
vessel by [∆time, ∆lat, ∆lon, SOG, COG] for trajectory predictions based on a variational
LSTM. Preliminary Bornholm diagonal Gaussian­based learning curve results where the
AIS state vectors are represented by [∆lat, ∆lon, SOG, COG] are plotted in fig. B.6 in ap­
pendix B. The hope was that this representation would ease training somewhat, but the
initial learning curves are still very noisy. It is also interesting to consider other comple­
mentary information such as weather conditions or (as done by Ding et al. 2020) add an
input that tells the model the time difference between points and forgo the interpolation.
Experimentation could be done with regards to the a contrario detection. With the current
setup, the a contrario detection works in the same way for discrete and continuous inputs.
However, one could, for instance, consider a different approach for the continuous inputs
where the reconstructions to compare with are taken from a small neighbourhood around
the vessel’s position. Another thing to consider is the ϵ parameter value used. Throughout
this project, ϵ is fixed at 10−9 inspired by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al. 2021, but one way to
detect fewer anomalies is to lower this value. So, as done by Nguyen, Vadaine, et al.
2021, running experiments gradually decreasing the value of ϵ to reduce the number of
false positives while keeping all the true detections are warranted moving forward.
Finally, one could use the actual continuous inputs where it worked best and use the dis­
crete one­hot encoded vectors for inputs where it worked best. Specifically, a Gaussian­
based model could be used for the position and possibly speed, but a Bernoulli­based
model could be used for the course or speed and course. After improvements to the
models such that they detect fewer false anomalies, a more detailed analysis of the types
of anomalies detected by the models on a trajectory­based level would be interesting.

8
Conclusion
This thesis constructed a trajectory­based model aimed at discriminating between legit­
imate fishing vessel activities and anomalous events based on historical AIS data. The
proposed approach consists of two main parts. Firstly, it utilises a variational recurrent
neural network that learns a distribution representing the data that can state the recon­
struction likelihood of AIS trajectories. Secondly, it employs a geographically­dependent a
contrario detection rule to account for the fact that the learnt distribution may be location­
dependent. The main idea is that if an AIS message has a lower likelihood than other
messages in the same local region, it should be flagged as abnormal. Then an entire
vessel trajectory is considered an anomaly if it contains many abnormal AIS messages.
The proposed approach has shown promising results in literature for cargo and tanker
vessels, but this thesis investigated the generalisation to regions of interest in Danish
waters and on fishing vessels that generally have very complex moving patterns. Two
regions of interest were examined to see how well­performing models used for the small
region around Bornholm generalise to Skagen, a larger region with more fishing vessel
traffic. Finally, this thesis analysed the impact of different input AIS data representations,
in particular of moving from discrete to continuous inputs. With different input representa­
tion comes the need for different generating distributions of the VRNN. Experiments were
performed using Bernoulli generating distributions, diagonal Gaussians, and GMMs.
Experimental results showed that the models with continuous inputs were much more
challenging to train than the discrete models. When using the continuous inputs, the
learning curves were generally very noisy or converged to solutions with a relatively high
loss. Different schedulers were introduced to improve training of the continuous mod­
els that decay the learning rate at specific epochs. The training was very sensitive to
the particular scheduler values chosen, and other values that were seemingly close of­
ten resulted in very different learning curves. Experiments performed using GMM­based
models all ended up using the same mixing component with a mixing probability of effec­
tively 1.0. This indicates that the GMM­based models considered did not find a way to
utilise the added complexity to improve performance.
For the region around Bornholm, the best performing diagonal Gaussian­based model
performed well when it came to reconstructing the latitude, longitude, and speed but failed
to capture the course. In fact, the resulting course Gaussian distribution was centered
around the empirical mean and covered the entire range of course values. The Bernoulli­
based Bornholm model had difficulty reconstructing the vessel position but generally did a
fine job reconstructing the speed and course. All things considered, the Bornholm diago­
nal Gaussian­based model was more promising for anomaly detection than the Bernoulli­
based model. With the diagonal Gaussian­based model, around 26% of test trajectories
were detected as anomalous, which is seemingly too high but could prove useful for some
initial filtering of trajectories. Both models detected interesting outliers, but since the Born­
holm fishing vessel training set is so small, many trajectories were detected as anomalous
because of a lack of training examples in the corresponding cells. Additionally, since this
is an unsupervised learning­based approach, the detected anomalies are statistically un­
usual but may not involve events that are suspicious or have malicious intent.
For the larger region around Skagen, the Bernoulli­based and diagonal Gaussian­based
models were both fairly good at reconstructing the position and speed. However, the
Vessel Trajectory Anomaly Detection
59
==PAGE:68==
Gaussian­based model was again very uncertain when reconstructing the course. The
Bernoulli­based model was reasonably successful in reconstructing all four inputs, and
using that model, the proportion of Skagen test trajectories detected as anomalous was
much lower (around 5.5%) than for Bornholm. So, it appears that the proposed approach
can generalise to fishing vessels around Denmark and a larger region, but it requires the
region to be densely populated with fishing vessels.
Whereas the number and types of anomalies detected with the continuous diagonal Gaus­
sian­based model appeared more reasonable in the fishing vessel context for Bornholm,
the opposite was true for Skagen. Overall, the results showed promise, and the most
extreme detected outliers seemed rather odd or abnormal in the fishing vessel context.
However, the models seemingly falsely detected too many anomalies to be completely
reliable in practical anomaly detection applications. One way to detect fewer anomalies
and reduce the number of false positives is to lower the value of the predefined a contrario
threshold parameter ϵ. Experimentation with different values of ϵ is warranted as part
of future work. After improvements to the models to detect fewer false anomalies, the
following steps could include finding examples of or simulating illegal, unreported and
unregulated fishing to see whether the models can assist with identifying such trajectories.
When ignoring the entire generating distributions and just looking at point­based recon­
structions, the chosen models seemed to have a tendency to copy the previous actual
values. This could result from the number of trajectories with low fluctuating inputs since
the models can then reconstruct the AIS updates almost perfectly and incur very little loss
simply by using the previous observation. There are, for example, many fishing vessels
in the data set that are static in harbour even though their navigational status indicates
that they are not static. Ongoing experiments involve investigating the impact of remov­
ing static points by adding a simple preprocessing step that removes AIS updates with
minimal speed. This can, however, remove AIS points for vessels that are out at sea, so
a more sophisticated filter would be needed for a production­ready application.

Private Conversation With a Fisherman
This chapter provides a summary of a private conversations with Fjalar Rikhardsson. He
is the owner and captain of a 7.5­meter fishing vessel and has worked on fishing vessels
of different sizes.
Date of conversation: 23.11.2021.
The first conversation’s main aim was to understand better fishing vessel manoeuvring
and how quickly fishing vessels tend to move. Information provided helps put some ex­
perimental and data preprocessing design choices into context and gauge whether they
are logical. The main conclusion is that when re­sampling trajectories, a resolution of 10
minutes seems a bit too high. However, it depends on the fishing vessel and the type
of fishing activity it is engaged in. In general, fishing vessels do not make fast and com­
plex turns unless something is seriously wrong (e.g. the vessel is about to collide with
something). To more generally capture fishing manoeuvres, a resolution of somewhere
between one to three minutes seems more appropriate than 10 minutes.
Date of conversation: 24.01.2022.
The second conversation involved discussions about the most extreme outliers that the
different models detect. Overall, the most extreme cases do seem rather strange and
abnormal in the fishing vessel context, but that does not mean that the vessels have
malicious intent. Of particular interest was to understand better what could be going on
with the vessel in fig. 6.9a and fig. 6.9b. After some discussion, the conclusion is that this
behaviour looks weird for a fishing vessel. In particular, the vessel is almost still for such
a long time and without drifting much. Then the vessel goes up to a relatively high speed
(for a fishing vessel) while still inside a small area. It could be that this is a fishing trawler
that had their net stuck for the first hours, but then it is strange that it was for such a long
time and that they then continue to fish in the same small area.
