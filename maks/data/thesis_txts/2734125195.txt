Abstract (English) 
 
This thesis focused on investigating non-technical solutions that do not rely on energy to address 
the issue of enhancing IEQ and sleep quality in residential buildings without only depending on 
mechanical ventilation systems. The findings were described in the form of a manual which is aimed 
at people working in the construction industry such as engineers or architects who can implement 
the solutions in a building project, and residents who can integrate some recommendations in their 
everyday lives. 
This project was completed by adopting four distinct methods: literature review of studies about the 
topics covered in the thesis, field measurements to test and show real-life examples of two non-
technical solutions, feedback obtained through an online survey by the potential targets this manual 
is crafted for, and dissemination to demonstrate some possible steps that can be taken to make this 
work available to the public. 
The solutions were divided into two chapters. One dedicated to engineers and architects in which 
several non-technical alternatives with one real-life example of trickle vents were discussed that can 
be incorporated in projects with the goal of shining a light on options that can contribute to 
improving both IEQ and the residents’ sleep quality without the need to install energy-consuming 
technology. The other one intended for the occupants of a house encapsules suggestions, an 
example of indoor plants in a real setting, and recommendations informing them about multiple 
habits that can be detrimental to IEQ and their sleep quality, and simple decisions that can be made 
everyday to maintain a healthy indoor environment in the long term. 
Moreover, the answers obtained from the feedback revealed that most responders not only found 
the content of the thesis useful but would also be willing to incorporate some solutions in real 
building projects, and recommendations in their daily routine, thus further validating the potential 
of the manual. 
Although the solutions contribute to a certain extent to enhancing IEQ and sleep quality, they can 
not fully replace conventional energy-intensive technologies since they are not able to fulfill the 
same functions and provide the same services of an average mechanical ventilation system when 
they are needed, such as maintaining an adequate thermal comfort in winter while supplying the 
house with filtered fresh air coming from outside on a rather polluted day, which is why the 
combination of non-technical solutions and mechanical ventilations systems can be the most 
optimal compromise to enhance IEQ and sleep quality by taking advantage of both concepts. 
Furthermore, not all non-technical solutions are feasible and can be implemented in projects or 
someone’s everyday life due to multiple reasons like strict regulations depending on the territory or 
addictions that might prevent some residents from making the right decisions. 
In the end, it is the designers’ responsibility to make the right decisions about which non-technical 
solutions to incorporate in the project to place more emphasis on IEQ and sleep quality based on 
their experience and legal restrictions. In addition, the residents should be encouraged by designers 
or by the occupants themselves after reading this manual to try to remove detrimental behaviors 
and habits while adopting as many beneficial ones as possible in their everyday life.

Chapter 1: Introduction  
 
Humans tend to spend up to 90% of their time indoors [1] and between 6.15 and 7.5 hours a day 
are spent in the bedroom sleeping, depending on the country [2], making it the perfect place for 
harmful pollutants to proliferate which, if not properly eliminated or minimized, can degrade the 
occupants’ quality of sleep. 
Although sleeping is such a fundamental part of our lives, not so much attention is placed upon sleep 
quality and the impact IEQ has on it, something unacceptable when considering that an inadequate 
sleep environment can lead to lack of sleep which can subsequently result in two main issues: 
detriments and loss of benefits. The detriments include impaired cognitive and behavioral 
performance, partly from diminished attention and arousal. The loss of benefits includes missing 
sleep-dependent cognitive processing such as memory consolidation and insight formation [3]. 
Improving IEQ through some solutions explained in this project could contribute to maintaining a 
sustainable level of IEQ in bedrooms and therefore help residents sleep better which can avoid the 
disadvantages mentioned before and have a positive impact on their mental health [4]. 
Not only sleeping well in an environment with adequate air quality would be beneficial to our own 
health, but also to the economy. Especially in developed countries, people do not sleep enough 
which hurts the economic activity; in fact, as it is shown in the table below, countries lose countess 
hours of work due to absenteeism and sickness caused by lack of sleep which translates into an 
economic loss of tens of billions of dollars (411 for the US, 138 for Japan, 60 for Germany, 50 for the 
UK and 21.4 for Canada  [5].

Objective and research questions 
 
Solutions to address the common issue of poor IEQ already exist such as ACs, mechanical ventilation 
systems, and air purifiers; nevertheless, those typical technologies have one aspect in common. They 
are all energy-dependent, implying that they rely on the use of electricity in order to function which 
can be a problem given the recent global necessity to reduce energy consumption to preserve the 
environment and combat climate change. 
Not only this master's thesis objective was to shine a light on different non-energy-consuming 
solutions to improve indoor environmental quality and sleep quality, but also to frame it in the form 
of a manual which can be consulted by architects and engineers who are the main characters in 
charge of designing the sleep environments we all sleep in, and thus have the opportunity to play a 
vital role in tackling poor IEQ in bedrooms. Furthermore, the manual provides insights about 
decisions and actions the occupants can take to enhance IEQ in the long term which should be 
recommended by the designers themselves.  
Lastly, in order to validate the content of this manual, feedback from the main target audience was 
necessary to verify whether they would be willing to adopt some of the solutions described or not, 
while also receiving useful comments that can be used to sharpen this project. 
The main research questions this thesis tried to answer are the following: 
1. What are some non-technical solutions that can be implemented in a residential building by 
either technical experts in the construction industry or residents to enhance IEQ and sleep 
quality without relying on energy? 
 
2. Can they replace an average mechanical ventilation system and its functions? 
 
Who is this manual for? 
 
This manual is designed to serve one purpose: inform engineers, architects and whoever oversees 
designing sleep environments in residential buildings about some non-energy dependent solutions 
that can be employed to either minimize the pollutants in the air or cleanse the environment in 
order to keep it as healthy as possible for the occupants to improve their sleep quality at night. 
Unfortunately, during the design process of a building little to no attention is put on sleep quality 
and how it can be influenced by IEQ, a lack that grows even further when we consider non-technical 
solutions that can be beneficial in bedrooms to improve IAQ. This manual is meant to fix that 
problem by educating designers not only to take IEQ and sleep quality into consideration when 
working on a building, specifically the bedroom, but also to provide them with the knowledge they 
need in order to take actions on this wide topic. 
Although designers are the main characters who can fix the issue, they are not the only ones; for 
instance, they could design the most sophisticated non-energy dependant bedroom capable of 
==PAGE:10==
6 
 
reaching ideal values in terms of IEQ which would be beneficial to the occupants’ sleep quality, but 
what would be the point if the residents sleeping in such environment decided to smoke cigarettes, 
not ventilate the room whatsoever and take actions that would completely compromise the indoor 
quality and diminish the effort put in by engineers or architects?  
That is why engaging with the people living in the environments designed by professionals is 
essential in order to inform them on measures they can take to maintain adequate levels of IEQ in 
the long term, an area that is emphasized in “Chapter 5: Sleep-enhancing design strategies for users”. 
In other words, everybody must play a role in improving IEQ, which can subsequently impact sleep 
quality, in all stages as also advocated by S.-S. Kim, D.-H. Kang, D.-H. Choi, M.-S. Yeo and K.-W. Kim 
who stated that “building professionals such as architects and construction engineers or occupants 
should take one or more measures appropriate in the building life cycle such as design, construction, 
or occupancy” [6]. 
 
Why non-technical solutions? 
As previously mentioned, we already spend a lot of time indoors in environments with insufficient 
air quality [1], while at the same time the world is moving towards a sustainable way of living which 
includes the need of reducing emissions and energy consumption to tackle challenges such as 
climate change. What if we completely removed energy-hungry technologies from our house used 
to improve IAQ such as ACs and HVAC systems, therefore contributing to reducing energy 
consumption, and replaced them with elements that do not consume energy at all but are able to 
minimize the level of harmful pollutants in the bedroom, thus allowing people to sleep better at 
night?  
It is relevant to mention that HVAC systems account for 32% of the energy consumed by residential 
buildings worldwide making them a big contributor of the global energy consumption [7] and in 
some developed nations the figure can even reach 50 % [8]. Moreover, despite them being 
considered an efficient way of reducing indoor pollution and improving IAQ, HVAC systems can also 
become sources of indoor pollutants themselves if not properly maintained and cleaned regularly 
[9].  
These are some of the main reasons why the topic of this project was chosen and like Z. Shayegan, 
M. Bahri and F. Haghighat also stated in their study “To promote energy conservation in buildings, it 
is important to develop zero/low-energy air cleaning technologies that do not rely on HVAC systems” 
[10]. 
However, there are other motives that can justify the relevance of this interesting and unknown 
topic: 
• 
Having non-technical solutions installed in the bedrooms that can improve IEQ and sleep 
quality are not only sustainable options due to their absent consumption of energy, but that 
==PAGE:11==
7 
 
also implies they do not rely on the electricity grid; therefore, they can operate and function 
regardless of the status and reliability of it.  
This means the solutions implemented can keep being operative and fulfill their purpose 
even during a power outage or a blackout which are quite often in developing countries but 
can also occur in developed nations from time to time, whereas conventional energy-based 
systems would stop working [11]. 
 
 
Figure 1: Number of power outages per month by country [11]. 
• Most people around the world do not have access to ACs or mechanical ventilation systems 
or simply do not have them installed in their house; for instance, only 1 European out of 10 
has AC at home [12]. In developing countries, it is mainly due to financial reasons, but in 
wealthy nations such as Europe, it is due to high electricity costs and cultural reasons. Some 
non-technical solutions might be an alternative to more conventional ways to maintain 
sufficient levels of IEQ that most people around the globe do not possess without the need 
to pay higher electricity bills. 
 
• Another relevant point that can be argued is maintenance. Typical energy-dependent 
elements such as ACs and HVAC systems require frequent maintenance of once or twice a 
year depending on the product, to ensure their capacity and efficiency remain optimal but 
also to extend their lifespan and reduce the risk of a sudden failure. These reparations are 
not only frequent, but can also be rather expensive, especially for low-income families. 
However, using non-technical solutions that do not have electronic components which are 
more susceptible to breakdowns, would be beneficial since they need less complex 
maintenance which translates into more money saved by the residents.

Methods 
 
The writing process of this manual includes three main methods: 
- 
Study and literature review. A multitude of scientific articles and studies were analyzed 
regarding different topics that are covered in this manual.  
They were mainly used to gather knowledge and information about non-technical solutions 
and any other aspect related to it, which are then used to support what is argued and 
explained in this project. 
 
- 
Measurements. Two separate measurements were conducted to enrich the thesis by testing 
two non-technical solutions discussed in the manual and present their findings as examples 
of the potential they may have in a real-life scenario. 
In chapter 4, measurements were carried out in three bedrooms to study the contribution 
trickle vents on top of windows can have in improving IAQ, specifically about the reduction 
of CO2. 
Moreover, in chapter 5 other measurements were conducted in a single bedroom to 
investigate if three different types of plants can influence the IAQ at night by removing CO2 
and ensuring its concentration does not reach high values. 
 
- 
Feedback. A draft of the manual until chapter 5, specifically number 4 and 5 about non-
technical solutions and actions users can take to improve IEQ and sleep quality in the long 
run, was sent to different parties that might be interested in the content of this project such 
as engineers, architects, students, and residents living in residential buildings.  
The objective was to receive feedback from the target group of this manual, specifically the 
areas that would affect them. For instance, architects and engineers are mainly interested in 
chapter 4 and 5 while the occupants only in chapter 5. The feedback, their criticisms and 
opinions were subsequently used to further validate this project to ensure it fits their needs 
and enhances their knowledge in those subjects in a simple and straightforward way. 
 
- 
Dissemination. This manual was seen since its inception and the first stages of its production 
as a potential product that can be realistically sold and made available to the public and not 
just a master’s thesis.  
For this reason, a list of options is available to demonstrate the possible phases that it would 
require to promote the project and sell it to the wide market.

Chapter 2: Fundamentals of bedroom 
design 
 
Since this project revolves around IEQ and sleep quality in bedrooms, it is relevant to provide an 
overview of some key design aspects of such rooms. 
As mentioned in the first chapter, architects and engineers play a key role in shaping bedroom 
design, such as the general layout, materials, furniture selection, and more. Nevertheless, in some 
circumstances, a few of these elements such as furniture or paint can be selected alongside the 
residents or can be modified or replaced by them in a future moment; this implies that some design 
elements described in this chapter are not permanent but are likely to be altered or changed by the 
people living in the environments envisioned by the designers. 
It is worth noting that around the world there are plenty of different types of general designs of 
bedrooms which differ from one another for multiple reasons; here are a few features that 
characterize houses around the globe and shape the environment in bedrooms: 
 
- 
House size. Some countries have generally more space than others per capita, therefore they 
tend to construct bigger houses; for instance, Australia has one of the lowest population 
densities in the world of only 3 people per square kilometer [13], which could explain why 
they have the largest average house size of around 214 sq. meters [14]. Whereas, Hong Kong 
has one of the highest population density rates of 7125 people per square kilometer [13], 
but the average house size is just 45 sq. meters [14]; thus, on average Australian designers 
have more space to work with which allows them to make bedrooms more spacious than the 
ones found in densely populated places like Hong Kong. 
 
Space is not the only reason why some countries construct bigger houses than others, there 
are plenty of other explanations such as different cultures, policies, regulations, and building 
traditions. 
 
- 
Materials. Bedrooms, like any other building, are not designed with the same materials 
around the world. Historically, civilizations used local materials to construct their shelters 
due to their availability and ease to acquire [15]. This explains why in some nations like the 
US, wood is widely more used than concrete or steel, simply because it is abundant, but it is 
also deep-rooted in the construction tradition and in the building sector providing jobs to 
millions of people [16].  
 
Another parameter to take into consideration is the location of the building; in fact, houses 
in colder areas tend to have more wood and bricks given their insulation capacities compared 
to hot areas where other materials are generally used, like mud clay [15]. 
Although construction history, traditions, and location play an important role in this topic, 
there are other relevant reasons influencing the materials utilized in buildings, such as: 
==PAGE:14==
10 
 
functionality, unique architectural styles typical of each region, personal preference, and 
many more. 
 
- 
Furniture. Each house in each country has multiple pieces of furniture with different 
materials and styles that changed and evolved for hundreds of years. It is no coincidence that 
when IKEA was preparing to open its first store in India, they decided to conduct surveys and 
tests inside Indian households with plenty of furniture, home appliances and more, to adapt 
its catalogue to the Indian furniture market due to its differences in terms of style, size, and 
even climate which is hotter and more humid than Europe’s [17].  
 
This anecdote showed that each nation has specific types of furniture that fits its climate, 
architectural and even its people’s traditions. For instance, Indians prefer sleeping on harder 
mattresses compared to other nations, which is why it is common to find them stuffed with 
coconut coir, something that IKEA had to incorporate in order to suit Indian customers [17]. 
 
Since there are too many types of bedroom design around the world and describing all of them 
would fall outside the scope of this project, the next subchapters will only focus on some key 
information regarding bedroom design in one nation as an example, Italy, to provide an overview of 
some key architectural elements present in many sleep environments which might be useful to learn 
and remember before reading the remaining chapters of this manual considering it focusses on IEQ 
and sleep environment in bedrooms. However, as described above, countries do not share the same 
building components or traditions with one another, therefore what is discussed in the next 
paragraphs might not necessarily apply to other nations. 
 
General design  
 
Italy is a country with a diverse built environment which stretches from wooden pitched-roof houses 
in the north and on mountains, to concrete flats in urban areas. The average size of an Italian house 
is 81 sq. meters, lower than other European counterparts such as Denmark which stands at 137 sq. 
meters, one of the largest in the EU [14]. The vast majority of the buildings are apartment complexes, 
one of the highest rates in the EU [18], due to the housing rush of the post-war era (see table below) 
to build as many houses as possible in a short amount of time and, since flats are duplicable and can 
accommodate dozens of families, it was the typology chosen [19]. 
==PAGE:15==
11 
 
 
Table 2: Number of residential buildings constructed in Italy per year [19]. 
Like every single country, Italy has minimum parameters and requirements that need to be met 
when designing a building; in this case, the Ministerial Decree of July 5th, 1975 set the first minimum 
standards for residential constructions, such as the ceiling height of at least 2.70 m and the size of a 
single bedroom must be at least 9 m2 and 14 m2 for a double room which must also be separated 
from the rest of the house for health purposes, with the exception of studio apartments, making it 
stricter than other European countries’ codes, specifically considering that other nations like 
England and Denmark do not even have minimum standard sizes for different rooms [18]. Another 
relevant requirement of the Ministerial Decree of 1975 is the RAI (rapporti aeroilluminanti) which 
states that the minimum size of a window in any living environment where it must be installed like 
bedrooms should be 1/8 of the floor size of the room [20]. Another document worth mentioning is 
the Testo Unico Edilizia (dpr 380/2001); it regulates the entire building industry from constructing a 
new complex, to renovating a part of a house, which is soon going to be replaced by a new version 
[21]. It is crucial to know that every regulation mentioned above applies to the entire territory of 
Italy; however, every town typically has its own local minimum requirements and building regulation 
which could differ from the national ones, therefore it is important for designers to check and verify 
the local building rules of the area where they operate. 
It must be noted that although the vast majority of bedrooms are residential, there are also other 
typologies of sleep environments such as dorms for students which can be one room apartments 
that serve as kitchen and bedroom for one person without any wall dividing the two environments 
or bedrooms with a separate or even communal kitchen, hotel rooms where a similar space planning 
situation can be found, and hostels that are rooms with normal beds or bunkbeds designed to 
accommodate even over 10 people. Nonetheless, most of the solutions proposed and the studies 
analyzed in this manual focus primarily on residential bedrooms with a few exceptions about dorms, 
thus the main focus area of this project are bedrooms in residential buildings. 
Besides the minimum size requirements for bedrooms mentioned above, there are reference 
measurements that designers can consult when working on the furniture which may vary depending 
on the source since they are indicative. Typically, an average one-person bedroom in Italy has: a

single bed (90 x 200 cm), one bedside table (45 x 45 cm), two closet modules (one module is 60 x 60 
cm), and a desk (120 x 60 cm). A double bedroom has: a bed (160/180 x 200 cm), two bedside tables 
(45 x 45 cm), five closet modules (60 x 300 cm), one chest of drawers (120 x 60 cm), and a chair or 
small couch if there is enough space.  
Another indicative aspect that engineers or architects should consider is the use space (spazi d’uso 
in Italian) which is how much space there should be between pieces of furniture to ensure full 
accessibility, avoid clashes between people in the room, and guarantee an optimal level of livability 
in each room. As it can be seen in the figure below, it is indicated the minimum distance there should 
be between the bed and the window (90 cm), a number that is the addition of 45 to 60 cm of space 
dedicated to opening the window and 30 cm for a standing person. This guarantees that a person 
can get out of bed and open the window without worrying about a lack of available space when it is 
open, enhancing the accessibility of the room [22].

the bed and a wall there should be at least 60 cm allowing anybody to walk freely without

When it comes to the orientation of a typical Italian residential building, there are different schools 
of thought depending on the location and use of the building. In general, it is recommended to either 
build the house parallelly to the solar thermal axis or follow the E-W orientation with the main sides 
facing the north and south [23].  
The point is to locate in the northern part of the building rooms that do not need sunlight and are

the bedroom, it should be located in the south or east unless the layout of the house does not allow 
that or the space is limited, in this case it can be positioned in the north if there are no other 
alternatives [23].

We have already discussed why sleeping in an environment with an adequate IEQ is important and 
beneficial to our cognitive performance [3] and economy [5]; unfortunately, there are some 
environmental parameters in our bedrooms that can affect our sleep quality and be detrimental to 
our health, thus they are an essential aspect engineers and architects should know and remember 
when designing.  
In this chapter, the main pollutants and parameters are explained and what role they play in 
compromising sleep quality. 
 
IEQ parameters 
 
There are multiple parameters that can influence sleep quality and can be studied to assess the 
conditions of an indoor environment. Here is the description of the main IEQ parameters: 
• Temperature. Also known as dry bulb air temperature, it is one of the most complicated and 
important parameters to analyze since it affects people differently given each person’s 
preference, and due to its role in defining the thermal comfort along with relative humidity 
of the air, air velocity, mean radiant temperature, human metabolism, and clothing level [35]. 
In general, the ideal air temperature range that would satisfy the thermal comfort of an 
average human being is between 18°C and 23°C which may vary from person to person since 
it is a very subjective indicator [36].  
 
This parameter vividly impacts someone’s productivity, in fact between 21°C and 25°C people 
tend to work efficiently but anything above that will start corroding productivity; for every 
1°C increase, productivity drops by roughly 2% [35].  
As mentioned, not only thermal comfort depends on everyone’s preferences, but it also 
affects people differently based on body mass index, clothes, and sex. For instance, women 
tend to perceive temperature drops or differences to a greater extent than men and are more 
susceptible to cold temperatures [35].  
 
It must be noted that like any other parameter, the temperature of an indoor environment is 
not constant, but it fluctuates due to several factors such as ACs, thermal insulation of the 
building, outside temperature, number of people inside the room, and more. 
When it comes to assessing thermal comfort in a specific environment, the Fanger's comfort 
model is widely used which combines two indexes: PMV (predicted mean vote) and PPD 
(percentage of dissatisfied occupants).  The first represents a scale from -3 to +3 of the 
==PAGE:23==
19 
 
thermal sensation of an individual, too cold, or too hot, while the second identifies the rate 
of dissatisfaction caused by the thermal sensation. If the two are plotted, they would appear 
like it is illustrated in the figure below [37], although new methods have been developed 
using machine learning algorithms to take into account parameters that are not included in 
the Fanger's comfort model such as body mass index, and sex [35].

CO2 comes from different sources, mainly from burning fossil fuels like coal or oil, factories, 
and manufacturing facilities but also from human respiration, thus the more people there 
are in a non-ventilated enclosed environment, the faster the concentration will increase. 
 
As mentioned in “IEQ in bedrooms based on current international guidelines”, there are no fixed 
international limits regarding the indoor CO2 concentration; however, figures between 1000

reduce learning and memory capabilities, and accentuate the symptoms of sick building

It is essential to monitor the CO2 concentration in indoor environments in order to keep it 
under control and prevent it from reaching high limits which could jeopardize the occupants’

• PM, particulate matter. As the name suggests, PM are particles that are made of a mix of 
both solid and liquid matter; they can be distinguished in categories depending on their size: 
PM10 have a diameter of less than 10 μm, PM2.5 are smaller than 2.5 μm and finally PM1.0 
which includes everything between 0.1 and 1.0 μm [42]. 
These particles can generate outdoor from several activities such as construction, industries, 
vehicles, and many more, or indoor by cooking, candles, animals, and more [42]. Since they

are microscopic, they can easily penetrate buildings through various ways like windows, 
cracks in the outdoor walls or leakages in the ventilation system with no filters making them 
complex to deal with [43]. 
Due to their small size, not only they can infiltrate buildings, but can also easily enter our 
system, including lungs and bloodstream. Exposure to PM can negatively impact people’s 
health, specifically individuals who suffer from hearth and lung diseases; moreover, they can 
amplify symptoms in asthmatic people and children resulting in respiratory issues [42]. 
 
• VOCs, volatile organic compounds. These pollutants are gasses produced by certain solids 
and liquids, can evaporate rapidly, contain carbon, and are quite common to find indoors; in 
fact, their concentration can be two to five times higher than it is outside with some activities 
that can drastically increase it to even 1000 times higher than outdoor levels [44].  
The list of all the volatile organic compounds is quite extensive and includes: acetone, 
benzene, formaldehyde, vinyl chloride, gasoline for automotive and several more elements 
[45]. 
 
VOCs tend to originate from multiple sources, for instance smoking (either outside or inside), 
some cleaning products, pesticides and repellents, fuels, paint, cooking, buildings 
components, furniture, and many more sources [46].  
A prolonged exposure to high concentrations of certain VOCs, specifically by people with 
allergies, asthma, or other comorbidities, can lead to respiratory issues and cause headaches, 
nausea, irritations and more. Since certain VOCs are toxic and cancerous for both animals 
and humans, in grim cases they can also damage specific organs like kidneys and liver or 
result in cancer [44]. 
 
• O3, ozone. This is a gas that is fundamentally beneficial to our planet when it is found in the 
upper atmosphere; however, the same can not be said for ozone found in urban 
environments which is produced by the combination of NOx and VOCs coming from vehicles 
or factories reacting with the sunlight, thus amplifying the O3 concentration during sunny 
days [47]. This pollutant does not only come from outside but can also originate from indoor 
sources such as air purifiers [48]. 
 
Exposure to O3 can have an adverse effect on anybody, including healthy adults, but 
especially kinds due to their lungs not being fully developed. Health effects are related to the 
respiratory system such as asthmatic attacks and difficulties in breathing [47]. Moreover, 
some impacts on people’s health can also occur when O3 interacts with pieces of furniture 
commonly present in buildings like carpets, clothing, and linoleum [48]. 
 
• NO2, nitrogen dioxide. It is a pollutant produced by the combustion of fossil fuels such as 
vehicles and industrial facilities which is why it is more commonly concentrated in big urban 
centers rather than small rural towns, although it can also come from indoor sources like 
cooking and smoking [49].  
The health impact of NO2 can include some respiratory issues, irritations to eyes and nose, 
and lung complications [50]. Children are the most affected due to their metabolic rate and 
amount of air they breath that exceeds an average adult’s based on their body size;

naturally by volcanos; however, humans play a role in its production when burning anything 
that has a high sulfur content, such as fossil fuels and some manufacturing processes, but 
also elements like unvented kerosene space heaters that can compromise IAQ [51].  
SO2 mostly affects the respiratory system causing issues such as difficulty in beathing and 
lung complications which can be accelerated in sensitive individuals with comorbidities like 
asthma [52]. 
 
• Radon. It is a colorless and tasteless gas with no distinct odor which makes it impossible to 
detect for humans. It forms underground form the decay process of uranium which is found 
in rocks; it migrates upwards generally infiltrating buildings through cracks and the 
foundations [53]. 
It is a harmful pollutant that has negative health effects on residents. Long exposure to radon 
can cause lung cancer; in fact, it is estimated that this dangerous gas is responsible for 3% to 
14% of all lung cancers in a year [53]. 
It is a hazardous pollutant that must be dealt with to minimize its exposure and protect 
residents’ health indoor.

temperature, basically the quantity of water in the air compared to the maximum amount

in a study conducted in a bedroom, it is important to also gather data and personal opinions from 
the occupants themselves through questionnaires or surveys which can guide and improve our 
understanding of sensations and personal discomfort of each resident. 
 
How IEQ can affect sleep quality 
 
Before diving into different scenarios in which some IEQ parameters mentioned previously can affect 
sleep quality, it is necessary to shine a light on what happens to our body and mind when we sleep. 
Sleeping occurs in four different stages called sleep cycles; an average person goes through four to 
six cycles (see figure below), depending on the individual and the sleep environment, which vary in 
length and get progressively longer throughout the night [61].  
 
 
Figure 8: Sleep cycles [61]. 
==PAGE:29==
25 
 
The four sleep cycles are the following: 
- 
Stage 1, N1. The first step consists of the body and mind that begin to relax which is an 
indication that we are falling asleep, a process that can take up to 7 minutes on average, a 
time in which someone can easily be woken up by some disturbance [61]. 
 
- 
Stage 2, N2. This is by far the longest phase since it takes up 50% of the entire night of sleep 
with each N2 stage lasting around 10 to 25 minutes; the body and brain continue to relax, 
although the activity of the mind can spike in some moments [61]. 
 
- 
Stage 3, N3. During this stage, the body continues to relax allowing it to recuperate and 
regenerate, for instance by improving the immune system or repairing bones and muscles; 
furthermore, the brain’s activity is characterized by delta waves, which is why this sleep cycle 
is also called slow-wave sleep (SWS) [62]. 
We spend roughly 25% of our time in this phase and given the level of relaxation our mind 
and body experience, this is the hardest moment in which someone can be awoken and if it 
happens, we can feel confused, have a foggy brain, and be affected by impaired mental 
performance for up to one hour [62]. 
 
- 
REM. This final cycle that stands for rapid eye movement (REM) takes up around 25% of our 
sleeping time, which is when our body is completely paralyzed with the exception of our eyes 
and respiratory system; whereas, our mind is almost as active as it is when we are awake 
allowing us to dream vividly but also enhance our cognitive functions and memory [61]. 
 
An important aspect that should be mentioned is sleep hygiene. It is the conglomeration of habits 
and behaviors adopted by the residents before going to bed that promote sleep quality which can 
be both negative and positive depending on the activities and how they impact sleep. Not all 
activities performed before bed are associated with sleep quality, but some do have a relation with 
it; for instance, going to bed and waking up at the same time or sleeping for the same amount of 
hours every day are typically perceived as beneficial, whereas going to sleep while hungry or being 
exposed to blue light before bed are seen as detrimental [63]. More information about beneficial 
sleep hygiene can be found in “Chapter 5: Sleep-enhancing design strategies for users”. 
Overall, following the cycle just described would be ideal and beneficial to our overall health, but it 
is not always possible due to a variety of IEQ factors. 
An ideal bedroom with all optimal IEQ parameters does not exist and it would not be objective given 
how subjective some indicators are as mentioned in the beginning of this chapter. Nevertheless, 
some studies tried to assess the perfect sleep environment that would please most people and 
maximize sleep quality from a thermal, lighting, acoustic and air quality point of view [64]. The 
results point out that all external noises should be minimized to below 35 dB through adequate 
acoustic insulation; moreover, the temperature range should be between 17°C and 28°C with a 
relative humidity of 40 to 60% with the possibility of controlling it through a thermostat, the 
bedroom should be pitch black which can be enabled by dark curtains, and the level of oxygen should 
be increased while CO2 concentrations should decrease [64].  
==PAGE:30==
26 
 
Similarly, an experiment was conducted to find the perfect combination of temperature, relative 
humidity, and pre-sleep and pre-awakening illuminance aimed at optimizing sleep quality among 9 
different groups of people. It was discovered that the option 20°С, 55%, 150 lux, 30 lux worked the 
best at satisfying objectively and subjectively all the groups tested [65]. Furthermore, temperature 
was the parameter that impacted sleep quality the most compared to RH and illuminance which 
were barely perceptible; 17°C felt too cold for some people, whereas 23°C felt too warm for others, 
thus 20°C was the closest option to thermal neutrality [65]. It must be noted that this experiment 
was conducted in chambers that recreated some indoor environmental conditions but not all the 
realistic conditions present in an average bedroom, meaning that the interaction between other 
factors was not considered.  
Such sleep environments, as beneficial as they would be to our health and sleep quality do not often 
reflect the situation found in the vast majority of bedrooms in the real world; in fact, a survey in 
Portugal revealed that only 31% of occupants had a good night of sleep and a study conducted in 
student dorms in Lisbon showed that the indoor temperature was outside the recommended 
comfort range for almost half the time spent in bed while levels of CO2 and VOCs were higher than 
the legislation allows even with some rooms having mechanical ventilation systems, a phenomenon 
that worsens with the increase in the number of people sleeping in a room since they exhale CO2 
and VOCs [66].  
The study also managed to prove that an increase level of CO2 and RH was associated with a 
deterioration of sleep quality, specifically in terms of the number of awakenings throughout the 
night; this implies that not only CO2 can indeed affect sleep quality, but the higher it is, the more 
impactful its consequences are on sleep cycles. Therefore, a reduction in the carbon dioxide 
concentration in bedrooms improves sleep quality, decreases the number of awakenings, and allows 
people to feel energized the following day [66].  
Another study that took place in dorms in winter in Beijing, China reveled that temperature was one 
of the main dissatisfactory reasons why people had trouble sleeping matching with the study 
mentioned above, along with noise; the ideal temperature that satisfied most individuals was 
24.2°C. Moreover, the CO2 level averaged 1750 ppm which is higher than recommended while noise 
levels were around 40 dB [67]. An interesting result coming from this work in terms of IEQ and sleep 
quality is the difference between females and males. Men tend to be less satisfied with their sleep 
quality than women, although females are more susceptible to noises and temperature differences 
and also prefer warmer environments [67] [35]. 
The same trend does not only apply to dorms, but also to normal households. A similar correlation 
was illustrated in a study performed in Sydney, Australia where they analyzed the connection 
between some IEQ parameters and sleep quality and found that when the temperature in a bedroom 
rises by 1 K, sleep efficiency and REM sleep percentage dropped by 1.036% and 1.647%, respectively, 
implying that on average the higher the temperature is, the harder it is to sleep and the lower REM 
sleep is possible to achieve [68]. Moreover, another parameter that compromised sleep quality was 
CO2. It was found that an increase in the mean CO2 concentration by 100 ppm resulted in a 
degradation of deep sleep by 4.3% that translated into a worse sleep quality reported by the 
occupants themselves, fully demonstrating how IEQ can impact sleep quality [68].  
==PAGE:31==
27 
 
Something relatable can be seen in a study carried out in summer in Shanghai, China in which IEQ 
parameters were monitored in different residential apartments to find out the factors affecting sleep 
quality. It was found that while CO2 levels were around 1000 ppm which is acceptable, the average 
noise level was 46 dB and goes beyond the limit; moreover, most occupants felt comfortable with a 
temperature of 24.8°C and RH of 64% which is close to the Chinese results described above [69]. It 
was noticed that the higher the temperature, CO2, and noise levels were, the worse the sleep quality 
was specifically the SWS, showing how a poor IEQ can lead to a degradation of sleep quality. 
Interestingly, even in this case it was revealed that temperature and CO2 impacted females more 
than males, although the overall satisfaction of sleep quality was slightly higher for women than 
men [69].  
Although CO2 is a common pollutant and it is used to assess the general IAQ of an environment and 
it can influence sleep quality as it was demonstrated above, it is not the only pollutant that can have 
adverse effects on sleep quality.  
Some studies have shown that pollutants such as PM2.5 and PM10, besides having detrimental 
effects on people’s health, also have an impact on sleep quality; specifically, an increase in their 
concentration in the air and long exposure can translate into sleep issues like sleep-disordered 
breathing (SDB) and nocturnal hypoxemia which can make breathing much more complicated than 
it is for an average person, and impact the respiratory system at night due to their small size that 
allows them to penetrate our lungs [70]. Another study in China pointed out that long exposure to 
PM2.5 and NO2 can negatively influence sleep quality which was especially impactful for men below 
the age of 40 [71]. 
Moreover, since being exposed to PM and NO2 impacts the respiratory system causing sleep apnea 
at night, consequently it reduces sleep time by around half an hour, thus preventing people from 
getting enough sleep [72]. 
Unfortunately, there is also one more pollutant that is commonly found in bedrooms due to the lack 
of awareness and attention to IAQ, VOCs [73]. They are so widespread that IAQ measurements in 
some Portuguese houses reported the presence of VOCs in every single dwelling, expect for one, 
with levels exceeding the national regulations by 18 folds, a pattern that matches with other studies 
conducted in other nations [74]. 
Besides the hazardous health effects VOCs have on humans mentioned in the section “IEQ 
parameters”, they are capable of disrupting our sleep due to the respiratory irritations they can 
trigger such as sneezing, coughing, noise discomfort, snoring, and general difficulties in breathing 
which can complicate and therefore extend the process of falling asleep (N1), jeopardizing deep 
sleep [75].   
From the results obtained from all these studies, it is possible to distinguish the factors that affect 
sleep quality the most, temperature and CO2 level; nonetheless, it is also important to shine a light 
on one element that comes right after them, noise.  
A comprehensive review study discusses multiple relations between noise and sleep quality which 
states that people who live next to airports, railways line or busy roads tend to report a non 
satisfactory night of sleep; in fact, they are more prone to experience insomnia, stress, and 
headaches but also have their sleep schedule disrupted since they take more time to fall asleep, an 
==PAGE:32==
28 
 
effect that increases with the noise level, but can decrease with the installation of double-glazed 
windows. Furthermore, exposure to loud noises before sleep affects people more than in other 
moments of the sleep cycles [76].  
Noise does not affect us all the same way, but rather varies depending on age and sex. Exposure to 
noises at night mostly interferes with stage 3 and 4 of the sleep cycle in young people, whereas it is 
correlated with a deterioration of mainly REM in older individuals. Moreover, continuous noises 
impact the REM stage while intermittent noises mostly affect SWS [76].  
As shown, noise can disrupt people’s sleep quality in multiple ways, from external noise such as a 
busy road or railway line, to internal noises like the mechanical ventilation if it is not properly 
designed [73], which can worsen their sleep schedule and quality of sleep that subsequently impacts 
the performance of the next day compromising their productivity; that is why it is vital to minimize 
noises and ensure people sleep in quiet rooms. 
An IEQ parameter that influences sleep quality is relative humidity. High humidity levels can reduce 
the time spent in SWS and REM, which can subsequently lead to bodily recovery and memory 
consolidation issues. Moreover, humidity can fuel the proliferation of bacteria and mold that can 
impact people’s health by triggering allergic reactions specifically in vulnerable individuals suffering 
from asthma. The ideal humidity level to enhance sleep quality should be between 30% and 50% 
and not exceed 60% for the reasons explained above [77]. 
Another parameter that can impact sleep quality is light. It can alter someone’s sleep depending on 
multiple factors such as intensity, exposure, and time of exposure; in fact, the effect of pre-bedtime 
light can be beneficial or detrimental based on the type of color temperature lighting. The higher 
the color temperature is like blue light, the worse is the quality of sleep resulting in increased sleep 
latency and reduced REM sleep; whereas, exposure to a red light before bed tends to enhance sleep 
quality and sleep duration which is also confirmed by the occupants themselves thanks to a self-
report assessment of sleep [64].  
The negative impact of light exposure is also reported during sleep. Not having adequate blinders or 
curtains allows the light coming from outside sources such as light poles, building, and cars or inside 
sources like a dim lamp and light bulbs to penetrate the bedroom affecting the sleep of the residents 
who tend to be less satisfied, in fact even a light of just 40 lux can result into longer times to fall 
asleep, more frequent awakening throughout the night, and reduced SWS [64]. 
Lastly, even the first light people see in the morning is a game changer. Being exposed to a bright 
light over 1000 lux early in the morning is perceived as beneficial compared to later exposures; in 
fact, it can reduce the number of times people wake up during the night in the following sleep cycles, 
thus optimizing sleep quality [64]. 
Overall, IEQ parameters can affect sleep quality in different ways. Some are more common and 
considered more impactful than others such as CO2; nevertheless, they all affect sleep quality 
positively or negatively to a certain extent, which is why it is essential to acknowledge them and take 
proper actions to ensure they do not compromise sleep environments that are supposed to be as 
healthy as possible not just to protect the occupants’ health, but also to enhance sleep quality. In 
other words, improving IEQ can also lead to an indirect positive effect on sleep quality.

-

-

- 
The body self-regenerates while the mind is characterized by delta waves

-

In another study, 20°С, 55%, 150 lux, 30 lux worked the best at satisfying

Temperature 
It is one of the IEQ parameters that affects sleep quality the most [67]. 
In a study it was found that for every by 1 K increase in temperature, sleep

CO2 
It is another parameter that greatly influences sleep quality. A study revealed 
that every increase in mean CO2 concentration by 100 ppm resulted in a

Chapter 4: Non-technical design choices 
for optimal sleep environment  
 
The information provided in this chapter is aimed at technical professionals in the construction 
industry to inform them about the contribution they can make to tackle poor indoor environmental 
quality in sleep environments to enhance sleep quality without the use of technology that replies 
on energy, but only non-technical solutions. 
Addressing and solving issues related to the indoor environmental quality of a sleep environment in 
order to improve people’s health and sleep quality is a difficult task which can not be completed 
with just one solution but a multitude given the variety of IEQ parameters that can impact such 
places in different ways as described in “Chapter 3: Relation between indoor environmental quality (IEQ) 
and sleep quality”. 
An aspect that does not help the case discussed in this thesis is the general lack of awareness that 
involves professional figures such as architects or engineers who tend to overlook the topic of IAQ. 
As P. M. Bluyssen stated in her study “Awareness of the problem is of utmost importance. We see 
that for example with architects and housing corporations, but also product producers and the end-
users (occupants) themselves, this awareness with respect to IAQ in general is not present. Indoor 
air is thought to be the same as indoor climate, and therefore related to thermal comfort aspects 
such as too warm and too cold. We have a long way to go in that perspective” [9].  
This is why this manual could help some professionals learn about the topic of IEQ, its relation with 
sleep quality, and some solutions that can be implemented without relying on energy-based 
technologies like mechanical ventilation systems for the reasons explained in the paragraph “Why 
non-technical solutions?” which can also become sources of indoor pollutants, specifically ducts and 
filters if not properly assembled and especially maintained [9].  
 
Non-technical solutions 
 
It must be noted that the following list of non-technical solutions is purely for the purpose of 
informing engineers and architects working on building projects about certain choices that can be 
made in order to minimize the detrimental effects some IEQ parameters have on residents as seen 
in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality” due to for instance the exposure to pollutants, thus improving 
IEQ which can subsequently have an indirect beneficial effect on the occupants’ sleep quality. 
It is not mandatory to employ any of the solutions described below since they are just 
recommendations and suggestions which may not even be possible to adopt in specific 
circumstances or locations; in the end, it is the technical professionals’ job and responsibility to make 
their own design decisions based on their own experience. 
==PAGE:35==
31 
 
There are plenty of non-technical solutions that can be employed to minimize pollutants and 
improve IEQ and sleep quality. Here is a list of all choices that can be made by technical professionals, 
and recommendations that can be implemented divided into four subgroups based on their main 
purpose. 
 
Source control 
 
This category involves non-energy intensive implementations that mostly rely on controlling and 
managing the source of pollution, substances, or agents to prevent them from affecting the 
residents’ health or comfort with the objective of enhancing IEQ and sleep quality. 
• Insulation and blinds. The envelope of a building is a fundamental part of any residential 
building; it combines plenty of layers that are made of many different materials, from 
perforated bricks, to autoclaved aerated concrete blocks and many more, with each one of 
them having specific properties and functions. However, a crucial layer that must be 
considered is insulation since it contributes to guaranteeing an adequate level of thermal 
and acoustic comfort for the residents, a vital part of IEQ that affects sleep quality. 
 
Insulation: 
 
Thermal and acoustic insulation can sometimes be achieved by one layer depending on its 
properties, but this is not always the case as explained in the test conducted by A. Di Bella, 
N. Granzotto and C. Pavarin who stated that “In some cases it is possible to identify 
intersections between sound reduction and thermal transmittance trends as a function of 
insulating materials properties, but in general, the increase in sound insulation is not directly 
dependent on the performance of thermal insulation provided by materials” [78]. Generally, 
thermal insulation is accomplished with materials that have a low thermal conductivity, low 
porosity, low density, or a closed-cell structure with small volumes filled with a gas such as 
air. Whereas acoustic insulation is achieved through materials that are porous, elastic, and 
soft, have a high density, or based on the way they are connected to one another. 
 
Some studies were conducted evaluating the thermal and acoustic properties of a few quite 
common materials used as insulation, including EPS (expanded polystyrene insulation) 
mineral wool, and coating (plaster). It was found that mineral wool applied on a wall seems 
to increase its acoustic insulation by 4 dB while EPS has no effect; however, the use of the 
coating (plaster) on the two thermal insulators resulted in an increase in acoustic insulation 
by 3 to 7 dB. In summary, the thermal insulation depends only on the layer chosen, while the 
acoustic insulation varies depending on the thermal insulators applied, their thickness and 
whether there is a coating involved or not [79]. 
 
Regardless of the materials chosen by the designers, it is necessary to mention the 
importance of structural joints and the way they are positioned and assembled in the 
construction site since if the insulators are not properly connected to one another in the 
proximity of joints, that might cause a thermal bridge which can degrade the thermal 
==PAGE:36==
32 
 
insulation and therefore the thermal comfort of the residents, and compromise the sound 
reduction of the element, undermining the acoustic insulation and comfort of the indoor 
environment [78].  
 
Insulating walls is critical, but it is essential to investigate another component present in 
every building from an acoustic and thermal point of view, windows. Such elements are not 
simply relevant because of natural ventilation and visual comfort as seen in the section urban 
vegetation in “Air cleaning”, but also due to their impact on the thermal insulation since they 
disperse 4 times more heat than insulated walls [80] and their impact on acoustics.  
 
A window component that can be a thermal bridge is the glass. In the past, most windows 
were single glazed which was not enough to provide adequate insulation to a house; 
nevertheless, nowadays the industry has adapted to today’s needs since now it is widely 
possible to find double if not even triple-glazed windows with different types of gases in the 
cavity such as argon or xenon allowing them to lower their thermal conductivity and improve 
acoustic performance [81]. 
Another part that evolved is the frame. Now it is mostly made of aluminum, PVC, and wood 
or a combination or them which all have excellent insulation properties also thanks to the 
modern frame profiles that have an insulator material inside like polyamide allowing them 
to have low thermal conductivity values separating the cold side (outside) from the warm 
side (inside), known as thermal break [82].  
 
Windows let solar radiation into the building which can reduce the reliance on heating 
systems depending on the orientation; on the other hand, they can increase the heat 
demand of a building in winter due to their thermal capacities being lower than those of 
insulated walls and in summer the solar radiation can cause overheating that can disrupt 
thermal comfort by increasing the indoor temperature [83]. Moreover, windows can let too 
much light coming from artificial sources like light bulbs at night into the bedroom which can 
disrupt the quality of sleep of the occupants. This is why the windows must be accompanied 
by one more component, blinds. 
 
Blinds:  
 
Besides providing some privacy, blinds have the goal of controlling daylight to either reduce 
overheating or reduce the brightness of the indoor environment specifically in the bedroom 
at night where it is supposed to be completely dark to avoid disrupting sleep quality as 
explained in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality”.  
 
The most common way of controlling daylight exposure is through curtains; they are 
produced in different colors and levels of transparency, from sheer or semi-sheer that allow 
a substantial amount of sunlight to pass through which are not ideal in sleep environments 
and should not be used in such rooms but rather in living rooms or bathrooms, to room 
darkening or blackout curtains that are opaque enough to block most if not all the sunlight 
which is why they can be used in bedrooms.  
==PAGE:37==
33 
 
Although curtains are common and relatively cheap, they are not the only solution that can 
tackle overheating and exposure to excessive light at night; one alternative is the use of 
venetian blind. 
 
Compared to normal curtains, they are designed with slats that can be adjusted by the 
occupants to control the amount of sunlight passing through based on their preference. 
Moreover, a study showed that when the slats are fully closed at ϕ = 90°, a venetian blind 
can enhance the energy performance of a single or even double-glazed window by 
minimizing the heat transfer rate and the thermal radiation from the indoor glazing [84].  
 
Overall, these types of blinds can greatly reduce overheating and improve the energy 
performance of the window when they are closed, be adjusted based on the residents’ 
preferences, and they are also easy to clean. On the other hand, since the amount of sunlight 
passing through can be controlled by the slats, this implies that they are not as effective as 
blackout curtains at darkening a sleep environment, therefore it is important to assess the 
pros and cons of such blinds before deciding on where they can be installed in a residential 
house. 
 
A better solution from a thermal insulation point of view are roller blinds. Not only these 
types of blinds can be made of wood, PVC or even aluminum filled with polyurethane, and 
can be adjustable based on how much privacy and sunlight the residents want to achieve 
similarly to venetian blinds, but can also be installed either on the external or internal side 
of the window to provide an extra layer of insulation which can be extremely important given 
that openings tend to have more heat losses than insulated walls as mentioned above.  
A study carried out in winter involving a double-glazed window demonstrated that when 
roller blinds are fully closed (rolled down) and positioned on the internal side, they are 
capable of reducing heat losses by 33%, a figure that further increases to an astonishing 45% 
when they are on the external side of the window, thus it is optimal to install them on the 
outside and leave 20 mm of spacing in between the glass and the blind [85].  
 
Furthermore, when roller blinds are completely closed, not only they can contribute to 
enhancing the acoustic capability of the window, but they may have the same darkening 
effect of blackout curtains, thus they are a better solution since they combine positive 
features of both venetian and curtains while also adding an extra layer of security to the 
building if they incorporate a mechanism allowing them to immediately shut in case of a 
break-in. 
A part that must be considered about roller blinds is the element installed above the frame 
inside the wall that accommodates the roller blinds when they are rolled up called roller blind 
box. A mistake often made is to forget to insulate it adequately, something that can 
compromise the acoustic insulation and cause a thermal bridge diminishing the thermal 
effect of the blinds themselves. It is vital to add thermal insulation inside the box to prevent 
heat losses and fully take advantage of the benefits of roller blinds. 
 
Overall, insulation of walls or windows is a fundamental part of the design phase that can 
optimize the thermal and acoustic capabilities of the envelope allowing the residents to enjoy 
an adequate thermal and acoustic comfort, thus improving IEQ. Moreover, blinds are a 
==PAGE:38==
34 
 
critical element that can contribute to minimizing heat losses while also ensuring complete 
darkness in bedrooms at night which is a key aspect of ensuring that the light coming from 
external artificial sources or the Sun early in the morning does not compromise or disrupt 
the residents’ quality of sleep. 
 
• Whole-day lighting scheme. As already discussed in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality”, lighting 
can affect sleep quality in a positive or negative way. However, since it is impossible to control 
natural daylight, what designers can implement is a whole-day lighting scheme that 
automatically adjusts the illuminance level and the correlated color temperature (CCT) 
following the natural light-dark cycle to enhance sleep quality. 
 
This was precisely the objective of a study conducted in winter in which the illuminance was 
at its peak of 500 lux in the morning between 8:00 and 12:00 since it can reduce the number 
of awakenings at night and in the following sleep cycles [64], and it slowly dimmed reaching 
100 lux in the evening simulating the natural lighting cycle of the Sun; moreover, in one 
scenario the CCT was kept stable at 2700 K, while in the second case it fluctuated between 
6500 K – 2700 K [86].  
The results showed that the second scenario of diminishing CCT was the optimal way of 
improving sleep quality. Objective data and subjective answers coming from the people 
exposed to the lighting scheme revealed that their sleep duration increased by almost 35 
minutes while in the first scenario it improved by roughly 15 minutes, and sleep onset latency 
decreased as well [86]. 
 
Overall, both scenarios had a positive affect on sleep quality; however, it must be noted that 
the study required people to stay indoors and only involved older people who are less likely 
to go outside; furthermore, adjusting the light indoor is possible only if natural light is 
obstructed by blinds or other devices otherwise it would interfere with the illuminance and 
color of the artificial light [86]. 
Although this is a solution that requires the use of electricity to modify the indoor lighting, it 
was deemed useful to mention in this manual since houses in developing and developed 
countries have lights by default, therefore this could be a tool designers could take advantage 
of when it comes to designing better indoor environments, specifically for older people who 
tend to spend more time at home, to improve sleep quality.

Air cleaning 
 
This subgroup primarily refers to elements that can be added to a building project to cleanse the air 
of pollutants and other harmful substances to improve IAQ and sleep quality. 
• Materials. The decision regarding the materials that are utilized in a building, whether it is 
for the envelope or the indoor environment, is fundamental from both an engineering and 
architectural point of view; they are typically chosen based on multiple factors ranging from 
their physical properties and local availability to their aesthetic and environmental 
performance. However, what is often neglected is the ability of certain materials to 
contribute tackling indoor pollutants which may help enhance the residents’ health and 
quality of sleep. 
 
PRMs: 
 
Some of these options are known as passive removal materials (PRMs) and their main goal 
is to reduce pollutants which can be done in two different ways depending on their removal 
properties that can be distinguished into two categories [10]: 
 
1. PCO-based PRMs. This type of PRMs is based on the process of photocatalytic 
degradation in which some pollutants are degraded into less aggressive elements thanks 
to light energy. Titanium dioxide (TiO2) is a common photocatalyst which is nowadays 
used to produce multiple PRMs that can remove pollutants under UV radiation and 
normal visible light if it is properly altered through different techniques to ensure its 
properties can be utilized in both outdoor and indoor environments. Moreover, due to 
its hydrophilic features, materials built using TiO2 are also self-cleaning which contributes 
to making the surfaces easy to clean and reducing the cost of maintenance [10].  
 
2. Sorptive-based PRMs. This kind of PRMs have porous on their surface which are used to 
capture pollutants inside due to their adsorbate and adsorbent capabilities. However, 
pollutants are not kept concealed forever since they are slowly expelled as by-products 
which is why ventilation and cleaning need to be taken into account to maintain a high 
level of functionality and efficiency [10]. 
The use of sorptive building materials applied on walls in a study showed that the average 
level of pollutants decreased specifically toluene, a type of VOC, implying that the 
installation of such products contributed to improving IAQ; it must be mentioned that 
their removal performance was also influenced by ventilation [87].  
 
The following materials that incorporate features described above of PRMs can have many 
different applications and combat different types of pollutants, a characteristic that should 
be considered when designing a building and specifically sleep environment: 
 
- 
Clay. It is used to manufacture bricks but can also be utilized to make clay plaster walls; it has 
a high sorption capacity of VOCs and can also minimize ozone. 
==PAGE:40==
36 
 
A study conducted in a building with bricks demonstrated that they contributed to the 
reduction of VOCs and formaldehyde by 27% and 9.4% [88]; whereas, another experiment 
involving clay plaster panels showed a significant decrease of ozone in the indoor 
environment [10].  
 
The potential of clay plaster coverings was further emphasized in a study that measured 
ozone, C5–C10 saturated n-aldehydes, two aromatic aldehydes, and the air quality perceived 
by 24 people in a chamber containing ozone, a carpet, and clay plaster surfaces in different 
combinations to assess their contribution to enhance IAQ. The results demonstrate that the 
best scenario in terms of perceived IAQ is when either just clay plaster or both clay plaster 
and carpet are in the test chamber with no ozone, meaning that it plays a robust role in 
improving IAQ. Moreover, the worst conditions were recorded when there was no clay 
plaster in the chamber but only the carpet and ozone; however, when it was added, the IAQ 
was enhanced, and the levels of aldehyde dropped significantly [89]. 
Clay is a useful material to combat poor IAQ and should be promoted in the design process 
of indoor environments due to its removal capabilities. 
 
- 
Paint. Although it is a common product used to protect layers and modify the colors of the 
surfaces of a room, it can also incorporate TiO2 or adsorbent agents as pigments to acquire 
VOC removal capabilities; in fact, a study performed in a room painted with adsorptive paint 
resulted in the removal of an astounding 96.3% of toluene [10]. Another study tested the 
efficiency of latex paint of absorbing formaldehyde and found that it was acceptable 
depending on the conditions; 25°C and 50% RH were ideal in which the absorption rate was 
the highest, but the further the temperature and humidity increased, the less absorption was 
recorded [90]. 
The paint industry is also moving forward to improve its manufacturing process in order to 
reduce the VOCs emitted; in fact, the study made by A. M. J. López and G. A. H. Llanos 
confirms and illustrates “the commitment of the paint industry to reduce its impacts by 
formulating coatings to avoid the evaporation of volatile solvents in the drying process” [91].   
 
It must be mentioned that the addition of photocatalytic paint on a wall is not always 
beneficial to the perceived air quality. In a study, a photocatalytic paint layer on a gypsum 
panel was tested in a room in which 35 people had to assess the perceived air quality with 
different combination of UV and visible light, and the two main sources of pollution: building 
materials like chipboard, linoleum, a 30-year-old carpet, and human bio effluents. The results 
demonstrated that when the room had only building materials as sources of pollutants with 
the UV and visible light illuminating the paint, the responders rated the air quality as poor or 
unsatisfactory, potentially due to the emission of undesired by-products from the paint itself 
because of incomplete oxidation of the primary pollutants in the air which led to a decrease 
in perception of air quality in the room [92]. Furthermore, since it utilizes UV or visible light, 
it does not perform efficiently at night. 
 
In summary, choosing treated paints to absorb VOCs and low-VOC paints is optimal and 
recommended in order to minimize those pollutants and therefore improve the IAQ which 
would be beneficial to the sleep quality of the occupants; nevertheless, it is important to 
==PAGE:41==
37 
 
keep in mind that they might also release unwanted by-products which can compromise the 
perceived air quality. 
 
- 
Cement and mortar. Common elements used for coating surfaces, the integration of TiO2 in 
the mix which may vary based on size and preparation, leads to an increase of the indoor 
pollutants removal capacity that has been utilized to reduce the concentrations of NO [10].  
A study involving 11 types of mortars mixed with TiO2 tested the removal efficiency of VOCs; 
the results showed positive photocatalytic properties which were effective at combatting 
certain VOCs such as 2-propanol [93]. 
A similar effect can be seen in cement or mortar mixed with sorptive elements as aggregates 
which may tackle different pollutants depending on agents added. For instance, the 
implementation of activated charcoal in the mix translated into an improvement in NO2 
removal efficiency [10]. Moreover, mixes of mortar with the aggregate lime-zeolite resulted 
in the reduction of methyl-ethyl-ketone (MEK), a type of VOC, by a staggering 85% in 24 hours 
[94]. 
 
Another test looked into sensory (odor assessment made by 25 people) and chemical 
measurements involving a cement-based mortar and a cement-ash-based mortar tested in 
stainless steel climate chambers with two main sources of pollutants: linoleum and a carpet. 
The study illustrates that although the odour intensity increased with the addition of loading 
of the two mortars, there was no difference regarding sensory and chemical emissions 
recorded from both mortars; in some cases, even though the addition of linoleum 
compromised the odor intensity, increasing the loading of mortars resulted in a less invasive 
odor, meaning that the more the loading was increased, and the better the odor intensity 
was. Furthermore, they acted as sinks for organic acids, which is beneficial to IAQ since they 
can absorb or neutralize some compounds, like the ones released by linoleum which would 
explain why the more mortar was added, the better it was in terms of odor intensity  [95]. 
Given the widespread usage of cement and mortar in the construction industry, adding TiO2 
or specific aggregates to the mix before applying it on outdoor or indoor surfaces as coating 
is certainly a positive action that can be taken by designers to tackle pollutants and enhance 
IAQ. 
 
- 
Gypsum board and plywood. This is another common element used in construction which 
can have sorptive abilities depending on the category of sorptive agent.  
Adding inorganic or carbon-based sorbents such as activated carbon results in greater 
removal rates of toluene than in normal gypsum boards which has also the risk of remitting 
a lot of the toluene absorbed [10]. 
 
In addition, gypsum boards that have chemical agents that react with VOCs can counter 
formaldehyde and toluene. In fact, a study conducted with this type of gypsum board treated 
with chemical elements showed that not only they can remove the two pollutants mentioned 
earlier, but they can eliminate formaldehyde at three times faster than toluene [10]. 
A similar analogy can be drawn with plywood which can have sorptive capabilities depending 
on how it is treated. A test conduced on micro-carbonized plywood showed that it was 
perfectly capable of reducing the risk for humans of inhaling formaldehyde at a higher rate 
==PAGE:42==
38 
 
than latex paint mentioned before since it was not influenced by high temperatures, with its 
absorption reaching 50.5% corresponding to a ventilation rate of 0.50 h‒1 [90].  
Treated plywood and gypsum boards are great ways to reduce VOCs and can be good 
elements to adopt in indoor environments.  
 
Low-emitting materials:  
 
Besides choosing PRMs to remove pollutants, it can be beneficial to choose low-emitting 
materials that release less harmful pollutants than conventional materials not only when 
they are applied in a building, but also during the entire construction process, an attribute 
that falls under the category of “Source control” since it focuses on minimizing the pollutants 
emitted rather than absorbing them. 
 
A Korean study tested the emissions of pollutants from low-emitting finishing materials 
installed in various parts of the building during and after its construction; the measurements 
demonstrated that all the materials chosen had low emissions of different types of VOCs 
throughout the entire construction phase and after, with all the figures staying within the 
maximum levels set by the regulation with one material, diatomite, standing out due to its 
positive impact at emitting 80% less formaldehyde and toluene than conventional ones. 
However, it must be noted that the Korean regulation during the study had generously high 
limits for pollutants compared to the WHO or other nations like Japan [96]. 
 
Not only installing low-emitting materials can minimize the occupants’ exposure to pollutants 
due to their reduced emission rates of harmful elements that can improve IEQ but can also 
be a financially smart decision. In fact, a study carried out a cost-benefit analysis on three 
different cases, high, low, and very low emitting materials, and it found that the installation 
of low and very low emitting materials would enable the residents to save up to 13,500€ over 
a 10-year period of running costs. This is because if the materials release very low amounts 
of pollutants, the ventilation rates required to have the same IAQ in the three cases are lower, 
therefore reducing the energy consumption needed for ventilation and allowing the 
occupants to save money [97]. 
Engineers and architects can rely on different labels like LEED or DGNB to identify low-
emitting materials on the market that meet the criteria and requirements or utilize different 
database available online to find the best products like greenbuildingproducts.eu [98]. 
 
Overall, materials play a crucial role in shaping an outdoor but also an indoor environment 
which is why engineers and architects should put more emphasis on the positive impact 
materials can have on the indoor environment and how beneficial they can be in terms of 
reduction of pollutants that can subsequently optimize sleep quality in bedrooms.  
It must be addressed that these materials can have some drawbacks that need to be 
considered; the use of such new technology is still not widespread and well known given the 
emphasis manufacturers place on their materials’ technical and environmental characteristic 
while often overlooking the negative or beneficial effect they may have on the indoor 
environment. Nevertheless, as mentioned in the case of the low-VOCs paint industry that is 
moving forward readapting and modernizing its production of paints [91], the trend seems 
to be changing with more and more awareness being spread about indoor environment. 
==PAGE:43==
39 
 
Although the presence of companies manufacturing PRMs or low emission materials strongly 
depends on the location and dynamics of the construction industry in every nation and 
region of the globe, more firms are emerging like the Italian Ariostea which produces active 
porcelain panels which are self-cleaning and can be installed either on the façade or indoor 
and contribute to reducing pollutants such as VOCs and NO2, odors but also bacteria thanks 
to their antibacterial and antiviral properties [99].  
 
However, it must be noted that photocatalytic PRMs work efficiently when they are exposed 
to sunlight or UV light, something that is not guaranteed indoor in certain situations like at 
night which can undermine their pollutants removal capabilities. Moreover, some of these 
materials can release some unwanted by-products like in the case of certain paints 
mentioned before which can affect people’s health, especially if they have allergies [92]. 
 
Another point to make about PRMs or low emitting materials is the price. Since they are not 
widespread as mentioned above, their supply is limited depending on the area which drives 
up the prices, making them more expensive than conventional ones. Although it is possible 
to make up for the high price over a long period of time thanks to the reduced energy needed 
to ventilate the house with a mechanical ventilation system as shown by a study cited above 
[97], they are still more expensive than commonly used materials which is something to keep 
in mind when calculating the construction expenses of a building.   
As designers, it is important to check the local manufacturers near the site of the building to 
find materials with some of the features mentioned in this section in order to reduce 
pollutants in indoor environments, specifically sleep environments to improve the residents’ 
sleep quality. 
 
• Urban vegetation. These terms refer to any type of greenery from trees and bushes to grass 
that is present in the surrounding area of a house or residential building. Although it is not 
an integral part of the indoor environment because it is outside, it is in its vicinity and the 
two can be connected to one another when windows are open which can contribute to 
enhancing IEQ and have a positive impact on the residents’ sleep quality. 
 
Having vegetation around a dwelling is extremely beneficial to the outdoor air quality since 
plants can purify the air by removing particulate matter, VOCs, and other pollutants which is 
particularly desirable when the residents open the windows letting outside air into the 
rooms, thus contributing to making natural ventilation an ideal source of fresh air that 
improves IEQ [100]. 
 
Not only outdoor vegetation can have a positive impact on both outdoor and indoor air 
quality but can also be useful from a thermal point of view. Greenery can cool down an area 
such as around a building through solar shading and water evaporation which is optimal in 
summer or during hot days. It is preferred by people to live in green areas during the hot 
season due to the thermal effect vegetation has on a house which can reduce the indoor 
temperature and, in some cases, even reduce the use of air conditioning and therefore the 
energy consumption [100]. 
 
==PAGE:44==
40 
 
Furthermore, greenery around residential buildings is effective at minimizing the heat island 
effect which is common in urban areas with little to no vegetation that amplifies the 
temperature making it hotter than it normally would by 1 to 5°C; vegetation is able to reduce 
the heat island effect by 0.6°C for every 10% increase in greenery [100]. An interesting 
feature of vegetation is that it can be used in the surrounding area of a building, but also on 
top of it in the form of green roofs or on the façade which can provide an extra layer of 
insulation that is capable of keeping the house cool in summer and warm in winter [100] 
without undermining its aesthetic, on the contrary it enriches its architectural value. 
 
Living in areas with plenty of greenery does not simply have a positive impact on air quality 
and thermal comfort, it is also beneficial to the residents’ mental health. In fact, some studies 
have shown the importance of being exposed to vegetation, whether it is a physical contact 
or just looking at some vegetation from the window, since it translates into lower anxiety, 
depression, and stress levels, three conditions that are unfortunately quite common 
nowadays specifically in developed nations [101]. Moreover, exposure to green areas has 
been found to be correlated with slower aging, meaning that people living in places 
surrounded by vegetation tend to live longer than individuals who do not have this privilege 
such as people with socio-economic issues [102]. 
 
Mental health is greatly connected to sleep quality since feeling stressed or anxious can 
directly affect someone’s sleep by making it harder to get enough sleep and altering the four 
cycles described in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality”, a loop that can impact other moments 
of someone’s life as it can be seen in the figure below [103].

Therefore, an aspect that can alleviate the symptoms of such issue can be exposure to 
vegetation which can reduce stress levels, depression, and anxiety, improve someone’s well-
being and restore mental health which can subsequently be beneficial to the residents’ sleep 
quality at night [100] [101].  
 
A study analyzed the psychological effects that green coverage ratio and spaciousness 
(subjective visual perception of openness of an indoor environment) have on people, and 
they found that they impact someone’s mental health differently. While the exposure to both 
greenery and spaciousness resulted in improved visual comfort, mood, and productivity, the 
presence of just vegetation had a positive impact on other aspects such as alertness, 
concentration, and recovery. In other words, it is more beneficial to people’s mental health 
to be exposed to greenery in a spacious area with large windows rather than small ones, 
which might cause psychological distress due to reduced prospects and visibility, since they 
have more advantages related to the well-being of residents [104].  
 
It must be acknowledged that although increasing the volume of vegetation around a 
residential building is highly recommended due to its benefits regarding IEQ and sleep 
quality, it is not always a feasible solution since many properties and plots of land are already 
urbanized and lack in available space to add greenery; a solution for existing dwellings can 
be a refurbishment revolving around green rooftops and green facades which do not need 
to be at ground zero making them ideal for highly urbanized and densely populated zones. 
However, if there is enough space or it is a new urbanization project of an area, it is optimal 
to adopt measures to ensure the residents can enjoy the benefits described above of being 
surrounded by as much vegetation as possible, something that can be further facilitated by 
collaboration between engineers and urban planners.

Ventilation 
 
This category focuses on solutions that involve a natural air flow between the outside and inside of 
the building to remove pollution and safeguard occupants’ health to optimize IEQ and sleep quality. 
• Pre-occupancy measurements. Once a residential project is completed and the necessary 
legal documents are signed and submitted to the authorities such as the report filled out by 
a certified and qualified home inspector after conducting a physical inspection to ensure all 
the requirements of the building code are met and it is safe for habitation, the building is 
ready to be placed on the market and be inhabited. However, one aspect that is sometime 
overlooked is the state of the indoor air quality after construction. 
 
A study conducted in multiple newly built residential buildings right after construction and 
after 6 to 12 months revealed that the level of pollutants in the indoor air is rather high in 
the first few months after the completion of the project; on the other hand, the 
concentration falls to acceptable levels 6 to 12 months after the construction depending on 
the materials chosen [105]. In fact, if low-emitting materials and PRMs are prioritized over 
conventional ones as explained in “Air cleaning” with the addition of natural ventilation, it is 
possible to further reduce the waiting time necessary to have a reasonable and adequate 
level of IAQ. 
Another study conducted in newly constructed Korean apartments had similar results. It was 
found that ventilating the apartment at the pre-occupancy stage is an effective and simple 
strategy to reduce VOCs since it prevented some materials from emitting and reabsorbing 
them, thus minimizing the pollutants in the indoor environment making them more suitable 
for living purposes in a short period of time [6]. 
 
This is why it is important to suggest designers to conduct some measurements of the IAQ 
prior to the occupancy stage to guarantee the indoor environment does not have a high 
concentration of pollutants generated during the construction phase which would be a good 
practice before residents move into their new house. 
 
• Radon removal solutions. Radon is a dangerous gas that typically penetrates the foundation 
entering the indoor environment. Designers must deal with this issue present in all sites to a 
certain extent depending on the soil and location with the necessary tools to safeguard 
people’s health and preserve IAQ; it is so fundamental that it is also mentioned in some green 
certifications like GBC Home. 
 
An easy and efficient way to reduce the infiltration of radon is by sealing with different 
materials like polyurethane caulk all potential cracks and joints of the foundation which could 
be highways radon can take advantage of and penetrate indoor environments [106]. 
Another more technical way to minimize radon infiltration is through gravel and foundation 
ventilation pipes. Gravel placed below the slab serves as a volume in which radon can move 
freely and create an air flow layer; thanks to this air movement, the ventilation pipe 
connecting the gravel layer to the outdoor, as seen in the construction detail below, redirects 
all the radon accumulated in that layer towards the outside of the building along with other 
==PAGE:47==
43 
 
soil gasses and humidity which can damage the foundation if not properly eliminated, thus 
preventing any pollutant found underground from penetrating into the foundation, as long 
as there are no cracks or weak spots which should be sealed as explained earlier.

This concept can be further optimized by adding ventilated PVC iglù which create large 
cavities below that can generate enough air flows capable of redirecting any soil gas like 
radon and humidity to the outdoor through ventilation pipes connecting the cavities with the

• Trickle vents. A device that designers can adopt to promote natural ventilation which can 
improve IAQ are trickle vents. They are small openings typically installed on the frame of the 
window at a height of at least 1.7 m which can be closed or opened manually (known as 
fixed) or automatically based on humidity, pressure, pollutants or even temperature (called 
controlled) or have an extract fan integrated, and their main goal is to provide a constant 
flow of fresh air from outside without opening the window [108]. 
 
Often the occupants of a house forget to open the windows which could compromise IAQ; 
however, a study showed that generally once the trickle vents are positioned either open or 
closed, people tend to leave them as they are, implying that if they are left open, they are 
likely to remain in that position guaranteeing a reliable and constant flow of fresh air [109]. 
 
Some studies discussed the positive affects of having open trickle vents in a house. In a 
refurbishment of an old home from the 1930s, it was found that the installation of trickle 
vents improved air change rate by over 12% [109], although that varies depending on the 
size of the vents. Some building codes recommend a minimum of 4 cm2 of opening per m2 
for a maximum of 8 to 10 m2 per occupant in offices, something that was validated in a study 
carried out in an office in which those requirement resulted in good IAQ in winter with levels 
of CO2 lower than 1000 ppm with no negative effect like draught perceived by the employees 
[110]; in fact, unlike windows, draught is not necessarily an issue in trickle vents due to their 
height and design, something proven by some surveys carried out in which people did not 
complain about cold draught or feel any significant discomfort [111] [112]. 
Not only they can contribute to keeping the air fresh, but also to tackling excess of condense 
which, if not properly dealt with, can fuel the growth of mould that can subsequently have a 
detrimental effect on the residents’ health and trigger allergic reactions [111].  
 
Fixed trickle vents are beneficial, but as mentioned before, some occupants might forget 
them either opened or closed [109], a behavior that would diminish the purpose of such 
devices; this is why a study was conducted comparing fixed vents with a hybrid alternative, 
basically controlled trickle vents opened and closed based on RH and pressure to prevent 
overventilation which could jeopardize the energy efficiency of the house. The results 
demonstrated the superiority of hybrid trickle vents over fixed ones since they were able to 
adjust the openings on their own decreasing ventilation heat loss by almost 40% without 
relying on human activity [113]. 
 
When choosing the type and size of trickle vents in a residential building, it is always essential 
to check the national or local building code and guidelines which might regulate some 
parameters. For instance, in the UK the Building Regulations 2010 states that trickle vents in 
habitable rooms like bedrooms should be a minimum of 5000 mm2 and 2500 mm2 in the 
rest of the indoor environments like kitchens or bathrooms; moreover, it regulates the area 
of trickle vents in the entire dwelling based on the area of the floors and the number of 
bedrooms as it is shown in the table below [112].

Not only trickle vents can be beneficial to the IAQ but can also help residents save money 
since they can contribute ventilating an environment without the use of mechanical 
ventilation systems which would consume a lot of energy and therefore money. A 
comparison between two offices, one with trickle vents and the other without showed that 
they managed to save up to 46% in the scenario with trickle vents accounting for a total of 
$100,000; another study conducted in a house in winter reveled that it is possible to save up 
to £1,000 per year since trickle vents rely of natural ventilation and not energy-consuming 
technologies [112]. 
 
Overall, trickle vents can be an ingenious addition to windows in a dwelling that can be 
helpful in keeping a constant flow of fresh air coming from outside, without relying on 
mechanical ventilation systems or compromising comfort due to draught, which enhances 
IAQ and therefore be beneficial to sleep quality in bedrooms, and it can contribute to saving 
up money. Even though they are a rather simple implementation, it is vital for engineers and 
architects to check the national or local regulations about their size, type, and ventilation 
requirements, evaluate the feasibility of such solutions from multiple points of view while 
taking into consideration some of their properties such as heat loss, although it is small and 
can be optimized by using hybrid vents instead of conventional one, they can still be 
contributors of heat losses given their thermal transmittance ranges between 2.1 W/m2 K 
and 2.8 W/m2 K, and acoustic resistance which is typically between 27 dB and 40 dB when 
the vents are open and 33 dB and 49 dB when they are closed [112].

environments, it was decided to conduct some field measurements to enrich the content of 
this manual and provide a real-life example of one of the non-technical solutions mentioned 
in this chapter.

Other solutions 
 
This last part refers to other types of solutions which do not share the same purpose as the ones 
mentioned in the previous categories. 
• Green building certifications and standardization. Something that could guide designers 
towards an approach that involves IEQ, which can subsequently be beneficial to sleep quality, 
is the use of green building certifications.  
 
These are programs aimed at emphasizing and promoting the design of green buildings, 
essentially sustainable buildings constructed by taking into consideration the environmental 
impacts of each phase, the use of resources and their life cycle. They are usually divided into 
different categories, each one of them specializing in a specific aspect of the building, for 
instance its envelope, use of resources, materials, and so on which have a point-based system 
in which credits are received by fulfilling the requirements expressed by each category; some 
certifications have mandatory requirements that must be met per category while others do 
not, implying that the decision of what requirements to satisfy is up to the designers. In the 
end, the designers must demonstrate how they met the requirements chosen and the 
summary of all points becomes the total score awarded to the building; the higher it is, the 
more green or environmentally friendly the dwelling is labeled [114]. 
There are plenty of certifications available on the market, from DGNB in Germany to LEED in 
the US, which can help raise awareness about the complex topic of IAQ since they have 
categories or parts of them dedicated to IEQ ranging from 3% to 11% of the total points 
obtainable, with requirements to follow in order to get credits. Some of the requirements 
attributed to IAQ could include choosing low emitting materials like the ones mentioned in 
“Air cleaning”, ventilation systems, and measuring IEQ parameters [114].  
 
Although green certifications are not single non-technical solutions like materials or trickle 
vents described in other paragraphs, they are still relevant to mentioned since they can guide 
engineers and architects towards a different approach that places a lot of emphasis on IEQ, 
to ensure they do not neglect it. 
Generally, studies have shown that people living in buildings constructed by following the 
IEQ requirements of a green building certification tend to perceive the indoor air as 
satisfactory since they experience less symptoms related to exposure to poor IAQ like 
allergies, coughing, and sneezing. However, in a few cases the IEQ was similar or even worse 
than in conventional buildings [114].  
 
It must be said that obtaining a green certification is not synonymous with having a building 
with perfect IEQ since in some cases designers can avoid focussing on the category dedicated 
to this topic and still get enough credits by fulfilling other sections to receive the certificate. 
Furthermore, some of the points obtainable require the use of energy-consuming 
technologies like a mechanical ventilation system which would not be adequate for the scope 
of this project; nevertheless, not all solutions need to be technical, like the low-emitting 
materials mentioned earlier, and it can still be a useful tool that can guide engineers and 
==PAGE:59==
55 
 
architects in placing some emphasis on IEQ which is beneficial to the residents’ health and 
sleep quality.  
 
These certifications are standardized, meaning they have a template that must be followed 
with requirements and limits that must be met by the designers which apply to specific 
buildings. However, the same thing can not be said in some countries for specific IEQ 
parameters and different types of pollutants which do not have limit values to meet in indoor 
places like it is explained in “IEQ in bedrooms based on current international guidelines”.  
An international, national template or comprehensive standardized regulation would be a 
guide and a base line upon which engineers and architects can rely to design indoor 
environments that would be required to have a sufficient IEQ that would have to be 
demonstrated, exactly like it is in a green building certification.  
As J. Saffell and S. Nehr mentioned in their study, designers along with scientists and 
politician should work together to draw up some kind of standardized regulation for indoor 
environments “to define the limit values for pollutants that are neglected health risks” [115]. 
 
In summary, although green building certifications are not necessarily a single direct solution, 
they can be a helpful tool for designers to guide the design process towards a new approach 
that involves IEQ in order to receive the final certificate; moreover, engineers and architects 
should work together with the local municipalities or even national authorities to integrate 
IEQ in the standard building regulations to set limits to certain indoor pollutants harmful to 
human health and minimum requirements to meet in order to prevent designers from 
neglecting IEQ in the design phase, which is also strongly connected to sleep quality as seen 
in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality”. 
 
These were the main non-technical solutions that can be employed by engineers and architects 
when designing a building that have the purpose of minimizing pollutants, improving IEQ and 
optimizing sleep quality. 
The topics covered and solutions that can be applied are diverse and involve different parts of an 
average residential house. From materials that focus on both indoor design and outdoor like the 
façade, urban vegetation which mostly encompasses the area surrounding the building, to specific 
components of the building such as trickle vents and the iglù foundation to remove pollutants.  
Designers can take advantage of the solutions and recommendations mentioned to design better 
buildings with a special emphasis on the indoor environment, specifically IEQ and sleep quality, two 
aspects that are not often considered in the process and that can be easily affected by multiple 
pollutants as explained in “Chapter 3: Relation between indoor environmental quality (IEQ) and sleep 
quality”.

• 
Insulation and blinds. There are different types of materials used to insulate the outer walls

Blinds are fundamental to prevent overheating in summer and thermal losses in winter.

• Trickle vents. They are small openings typically installed on the frame of the window at a 
height of at least 1.7 m which can be closed or opened manually (known as fixed) or 
automatically based on humidity, pressure, pollutants or even temperature (called 
controlled) or have an extract fan integrated, and their main goal is to provide a constant 
flow of fresh air from outside without opening windows, thus contributing to improving IAQ. 
Moreover, they can be helpful in removing pollutants and condense from indoor 
environments without causing significant draught problems and compromising the 
occupants’ thermal comfort. However, the increase in air change is not comparable to 
opening windows or having a mechanical ventilation system, and it is important to verify if 
they produce acoustic or thermal losses, something that can be minimized by adopting 
controlled trickle vents rather than conventional ones.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Ventilation”. 
 
• Urban vegetation. Living in residential areas immersed in vegetation is highly beneficial for 
multiple reasons. Greenery helps cleanse the air from pollutants, it can provide shading in 
summer while also reducing the heat island effect, and green rooftops or layers on the façade 
add extra thermal insulation to the building.  
Moreover, being exposed to vegetation physically or by looking at it from the window has 
been shown to be highly effective at improving residents’ mental health with significant 
effects in the reduction of stress and anxiety, which can subsequently be helpful at enhancing 
sleep quality. For more information about this topic, go to “Air cleaning”. 
 
• Green building certifications and standardization. Certifications can be useful design tools 
for engineers or architects to guide the design phase towards a new approach that involves 
IEQ in order to receive the final environmental award. Although this topic is embedded in all 
certifications, in some cases it is possible to receive enough points to obtain the certificate 
without completing the requirements about IEQ, which would diminish the use of such tool. 
==PAGE:61==
57 
 
Engineers and architects should work together with the local municipalities or even national 
authorities to advocate for the integration of IEQ in the standard building regulations to set 
limits to certain indoor pollutants harmful to human health and minimum requirements to 
meet in order to prevent designers from neglecting IEQ and sleep quality in the design phase. 
For more information about this topic, go to “Other solutions”. 
 
• Pre-occupancy measurements. Newly constructed buildings have high concentrations of 
pollutants, specifically in the first few months after their completion; the levels decrease to 
safe limits after 6 to 12 months, depending on the location and materials used. 
It is important to conduct measurements in the pre-occupancy stage to ensure the building 
is safe to be inhabited by new residents from an IAQ point of view, and if it is not, natural 
ventilation or using low-emitting materials can be a way of speeding up the process. 
For more information about this topic, go to “Ventilation”. 
 
• Radon removal solutions. There are multiple non-energy intensive solutions that can be 
implemented to prevent the infiltration of radon from the foundation into the indoor 
environment.  
Sealing cracks and weak spots in the foundation is necessary to minimize any infiltration; 
however, a thorough solution is the addition of either gravel or PVC iglù in the foundation to 
create a volume in which radon, humidity, and other soli gasses can be trapped and directed 
outside through ventilation pipes connecting that layer to the outside of the building. For 
more information about this topic, go to “Ventilation”. 
 
• Materials. PRMs are special materials that can remove or absorb pollutants in the air thanks 
to their photocatalytic properties (PCO-based PRMs) or porous surfaces that serve as traps 
for pollution (sorptive-based PRMs). These features can be implemented in different 
construction elements such as clay, paint, cement and mortar, and gypsum board and 
plywood. 
However, PCO-based PRMs may not be effective if there is no visible or UV light while 
sorptive-based PRMs need to be cleaned in order to remove the pollutants trapped in their 
porous; moreover, some undesired by-products could be released in certain conditions. 
In addition, some materials can minimize the pollutants they release, called low-emitting 
materials which tend to emit less harmful pollutants than conventional options during the 
production process and normal service life.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Air cleaning”. 
 
• Whole-day lighting scheme. It is possible to use light bulbs in the house to automatically 
adjust the illuminance level and the correlated color temperature (CCT) following the natural 
light-dark cycle to enhance sleep quality, specifically sleep duration and decrease onsen sleep 
latency. 
However, this method only works if people stay indoor and if it is possible to control natural 
lighting coming from outside, meaning that it is more fit for elderly individuals who tend to 
be more sedentary than the youth.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Source control”.

Chapter 5: Sleep-enhancing design 
strategies for users 
 
This chapter is written to inform residents of possible behaviors or habits that can optimize or 
compromise IEQ and sleep quality. The knowledge is also dedicated to technical professionals who 
are residents themselves, but especially because they can provide future occupants of dwellings that 
they design with information they may need to contribute maintaining a healthy IEQ in the long term 
through some sort of short instruction guide drawn up by designers or by suggesting the reading of 
this chapter to spread awareness. 
Architects and engineers are the main characters who can play a huge role in enhancing IEQ and 
sleep quality thanks to the non-technical solutions that can be implemented in their projects as 
explained in “Chapter 4: Non-technical design choices for optimal sleep environment”. Nevertheless, to 
guarantee a healthy sleep environment in the long term, it is fundamental that they collaborate with 
one more actor, the residents. 
The people living in residential buildings designed by engineers and architects have certain habits 
and behaviors that can immensely affect the IEQ and sleep quality positively or negatively. This is 
why the aim of this project can not be achieved without the involvement of the users, especially 
after the construction of the buildings they move in which is when the designers’ jurisdiction ends 
and the occupants’ begins. In other words, during the design phase, professional designers can 
choose specific implementations aimed at improving IEQ, but this would be useless if, upon moving 
into the house, the residents did not collaborate and adopted behaviours that degrade the indoor 
environmental quality of the sleep environments in the long run, thus diminishing or reducing the 
effectiveness of the actions taken by technical experts making their work go to waste. 
In the following paragraphs, actions taken by residents that can affect the IEQ and consequently 
sleep quality are described. 
 
Recommendations to residents that can influence IEQ and 
sleep quality 
 
The purpose of this paragraph is not to force any change in people’s lifestyles, but to inform of some 
unconscious or conscious habits or decisions that might have a positive or negative impact on IEQ 
and sleep quality. Possessing this knowledge can persuade people to start adopting a new approach 
which could hopefully result in the creation of new habits overtime and more awareness about the 
consequences of certain actions at home. 
Each person has their own behaviors and habits that are ingrained in their brain and can come from 
different sources like the environment they grew up in, the people around them and their actions, 
and more. Some people’s behaviors or decisions can positively or negatively affect IEQ and 
==PAGE:63==
59 
 
subsequently sleep quality at night. Here are the main points that residents should consider and 
learn about to minimize the exposure to pollutants and enhance IEQ and sleep quality divided into 
three subgroups based on their context. 
 
Ventilation related solutions 
 
This part is about natural ventilation, benefits, and drawbacks it can have in a residential house that 
occupants should be aware of in order to optimize its use and improve IEQ and sleep quality at night. 
• Natural ventilation (window opening). This is the most common and ancient method to 
refresh the indoor stale air to improve IAQ and cool down the dwelling dating back to the 
Greeks and Romans who knew that polluted indoor air could have an impact on us. 
Throughout the centuries, our knowledge about this topic grew drastically; in fact, in the 
medieval era people were aware that poorly ventilated places were more likely to spread 
diseases and feel uncomfortable making them not ideal for breathing [116]. 
 
The simple concept of opening windows continued existing, and it is still practiced today for 
multiple reasons. In a study conducted in 15 Danish houses during winter, spring, and 
summer aimed at analyzing the window opening behavior, it was found that the two main 
reasons why people open windows are CO2 and temperature [117]. In other words, when 
the air felt stale, people were more likely to result to opening windows to refresh the air, 
similarly to what was done centuries ago as explained above, with the difference that now 
we have the understanding of CO2 which is considered an indicator of the quality of the air 
since the higher its concentration is, the more discomfort is felt which incentives people to 
open windows to reduce its levels; moreover, if the outdoor temperature was low, people 
were less encouraged to open windows due to the risk of excessively cooling down the house 
and amplifying heat losses [117]. 
 
Another survey looked into the same topic regarding window opening and it revealed that 
people open windows for multiple reasons: IAQ, noise, solar radiation, and more; however, 
the most prevalent response was outdoor temperature, similarly to the study mentioned 
before. In fact, this is a trend that has been occurring since the 1950s when some experts 
found that in 60% to 70% of cases, windows were opened due to weather-related factors 
such as the temperature [118]. 
This is not surprising since residents prefer having control over their house, a criteria 
windows meet given they are opened manually by the occupants when they think it is 
necessary [118]. 
 
Another study carried out in German offices for an entire year assessing the users’ window 
opening pattern revealed that the behavior changes depending on the season; in summer 
the windows were opened more often than in winter in which they were opened for a short 
period of time and only partially rather than completely open due to temperature 
differences. Whereas the highest number of times windows were closed and opened was 
==PAGE:64==
60 
 
during autumn and spring because of sudden changes in temperature. Moreover, windows 
were never fully opened at night [119]. 
 
The window opening behavior is not only dependant on IEQ parameters like CO2 or 
temperature, but also on income and ethnicity. A survey carried out in the US shows that 
people with a low income are more likely to open windows than wealthy individuals who rely 
more heavily on the mechanical ventilation system since they have the financial means to 
purchase and maintain it; furthermore, some categories belonging to the Hispanic and Asian 
community open windows more often than other groups [120]. 
This reveals that opening windows is an action that changes depending on the climate and 
therefore the location, season, income, and someone’s habits and cultural background which 
vary depending on the ethnicity, a practice that is probably taught by the parents when 
growing up and passed from generation to generation. 
 
Besides the action of opening windows, it is crucial to understand the role natural ventilation 
plays in the context of IEQ. 
Many houses rely on natural ventilation to refresh the indoor air and reduce pollutants 
depending on the nation; around 59% of Swedish houses involved in a survey were naturally 
ventilated, 40% in Denmark and a staggering 75% in Finland demonstrating that it is a quite 
prevalent practice even in cold regions [121]. 
The role of natural ventilation achieved by opening windows is discussed in multiple studies, 
like one conducted in Copenhagen, Denmark between September and December in which 
84 candidates had to sleep in their normal conditions at night for a week and then change 
them the following week by opening windows and bedroom doors to assess the effectiveness 
of such activities. It was found that while opening windows was rather beneficial to IAQ since 
it decreased CO2 concentrations which had a positive impact on sleep quality, opening doors 
was not, potentially due to the fact that some pollutants like PM, NO2, and O3 can come 
from within the house through cooking which then migrate to the bedroom at night, and 
effect that is exacerbated when the bedroom doors are open at night [121]. 
 
A similar study analyzed the effect opening windows and/or doors has on IAQ during winter 
in 17 households. The lowest mean CO2 concentration was found in bedrooms that had 
windows open at only 658 ppm which is totally acceptable and even below some countries 
recommended guidelines, whereas the highest value of 1150 ppm was recorded in bedrooms 
with closed windows; in this case, opening doors and/or windows had a slight positive effect, 
but not significant. The temperature range during the experiment was between 18°C and 
22°C and humidity was in line with normal conditions [122]. 
 
In another test carried out in dormitories with natural ventilation in which data was collected 
with windows closed and opened during autumn and winter shows the gap between the two 
scenarios and the positive influence of simply opening the window at night. While with 
windows open the CO2 values ranged between 525 ppm to 840 ppm, with windows closed 
the situation changed since the range was between 1730 ppm and 3900 ppm, numbers that 
should not be recorded in a bedroom; in fact, the people who slept in such poorly ventilated 
environments had trouble sleeping and had a worse next-day performance than individuals 
who opened windows at night [122]. 
==PAGE:65==
61 
 
The same pattern can be found in an Australian study conducted during summer. The 
measurements revealed once again that the best IAQ scenario is related to the bedrooms 
with windows open at night which had an average CO2 concentration of roughly 542 ppm, 
outperforming the case in which people slept with windows or door open, and even when 
the AC was on, a setting that recorded values between 721 ppm and 955 ppm. Furthermore, 
the temperature was around 25°C/26°C with windows open or AC on, implying that they had 
relatively the same effect of cooling down the environments at night. The individuals who 
did not sleep in the naturally ventilated rooms had a lower self-reported quality of sleep than 
the ones who had windows open because of the higher CO2 concentrations that were 
measured [68].  
 
Natural ventilation seems to be a good solution to reduce pollutants in the bedroom at night, 
but how does it compare to energy-consuming ventilation systems? 
A study conducted in a rather cold area of China, Urumqi, over a one-year period revealed 
that while the mechanical ventilation system reduced the temperature by 1.6 K, the humidity 
by 3%, and was capable of reducing the level of CO2 more efficiently, natural ventilation had 
a sharper drop in temperature after 2 hours, but subsequently stabilized at 20°C on average, 
and was able to decrease CO2 at a slower rate than the energy-consuming system probably 
due to outdoor pollution being an issue in that area in winter [123]. 
Moreover, in winter residents preferred to use the mechanical ventilation system, whereas 
in summer opening windows was the optimal solution since the temperatures were not too 
hot or cold and the level of pollution was relatively low. In both cases, humidity dropped to 
low levels, even 20% in some cases which is not acceptable, potentially due to the dry climate 
of the city; this implies that humidification was necessary in order to keep relative humidity 
at a sustainable range [123]. 
 
In other studies comparing natural and mechanical ventilation systems, it was discussed that 
while the second one may have a higher ventilation rate [124] and be able to reduce 
pollutants more effectively than open windows since residents claim that they were less 
disturbed by stuffy air and more satisfied from a thermal comfort point of view [125], it is 
also relevant to mention that in other circumstances the mechanical ventilation system did 
not perform adequately due to a lack of maintenance that it requires periodically in order to 
function properly without which it could potentially become a source of indoor pollution 
contradicting its predefined purpose of improving IAQ as discussed in “Why non-technical 
solutions?” [9] [126]. 
 
Although opening windows at night is beneficia to IAQ, many people decide to keep them 
closed at night; 70% of responders in a survey in Denmark, 41% in China, and 69% in Norway 
did not open any window during sleep [121]; in many cases, besides noise and security, one 
of the main reasons why people decide not to open them lies in the outdoor temperature 
and the fear that, especially in the winter season, it might compromise their thermal comfort 
since the higher the opening area of the window is, the higher the natural ventilation rate is, 
which is ideal for IAQ, but the lower the temperature can drop in the bedroom as reported 
in some of the studies mentioned above [121]. 
==PAGE:66==
62 
 
Nevertheless, a study conducted in Beijing during winter with -9°C outside demonstrated 
that opening windows at night does not necessarily have to result in a poor thermal comfort. 
They tried to measure temperature and CO2 with different opening areas to find the most 
ideal that would satisfy the residents from an IAQ and thermal point of view; it was found 
that an opening area below 0.033 m2 was not adequate to keep the CO2 concentration under 
1000 ppm, whereas 0.077 m2 was sufficient to control the level of carbon dioxide, but was 
detrimental temperature wise since they recorded less than 20°C after only 4 hours of sleep 
and the humidity dropped below 30% which is not in line with the Chinese guidelines. 
However, 0.055 m2 was an acceptable area from both points of view, something that was 
also validated by the residents who were satisfied with the results [127]. 
 
Essentially, if residents are afraid that fully opening the windows at night in winter might end 
up jeopardizing thermal comfort, perhaps the solution lies in adjusting the opening area 
depending on the liking of the occupants to ensure the indoor temperature is within their 
acceptable range while ensuring that the CO2 concentration drops to healthy levels.  
Although heat losses are a problem that could occur when opening windows especially in 
cold seasons, there is an aspect that must be taken into account by the residents before 
deciding to naturally ventilate their house, outdoor pollution. 
 
In a study it was shown that almost 80% of people think they live in an area with clean air 
and over 70% of responders living more than a mile from a highway, train station or industrial 
area believe the same or that it is ok. However, in reality those are all sources of pollution 
which can subsequently flow into any building with windows opened, thus compromising 
both IAQ and sleep quality instead of improving them [128]. 
 
This was further examined in a study conducted in Portugal where different pollutants were 
measured under different window and door settings at night, similarly to what was done in 
some studies mentioned above. Although it was found that the best scenario with the lowest 
CO2 was when the windows were open, in this case VOCs, formaldehyde, and PM 
concentrations were higher than the Portuguese legislation allows in the settings with 
opened windows or doors. This not only demonstrated that certain pollutants originated in 
other parts of the house like the kitchen can migrate to the bedrooms with open doors, but 
that opening windows may not always be beneficial to IAQ since there could be outdoor 
sources of pollution which can produce elements like PM that can infiltrate into the sleep 
environments through the windows like shown in this study carried out near a road with 
moderate traffic, undermining IAQ and sleep quality [129]. 
While opening windows is generally a good, simple non-energy dependant way of removing 
pollutants from bedrooms, it is not always true since it depends on the location of the 
dwelling and the potential sources of pollutants that may be in the proximity of the building. 
 
Overall, the essential action of opening windows to improve IEQ, something that can also be 
achieved to a certain extent by opening trickle vents when they are installed, check 
paragraph in “Ventilation”, it is a simple action that can have a huge impact on the indoor 
climate. It has its drawbacks which emerge specifically in the case of noisy or polluted areas 
depending on the season, residents can feel discouraged to open windows at night when it 
is cold outside, and it may not always be as effective as mechanical ventilation systems at

removing pollutants. However, it is still true that it does make a difference when the outdoor 
conditions are right, purifying the air and cooling down the house in hot seasons without 
using any energy or regular maintenance, making it a cost-effective solution to improve IEQ 
and subsequently sleep quality.

Behaviors/habits related solutions 
 
This final section groups together all information and insights about behaviors and habits adopted 
by residents that could influence negatively or positively IEQ and sleep quality. 
• Smoking. This is unfortunately a common habit many people pick up during their life. 
Statistics paint a different picture depending on the country; for instance, in Denmark 23 % 
percent of the population uses products containing nicotine or tobacco, with 14% of them 
stating that they smoke indoor [130]. Although the figures show that the trend is in decline 
in most nations compared to 50 or 60 years ago, it is still a huge problem that is responsible 
for 7 million deaths worldwide with over 1.3 million caused by being in contact with cigarette 
smoke (second-hand smoke) [131]. 
 
Nowadays, it is widely known that smokers and the people around them especially in indoor 
environments are exposed to multiple pollutants that could lead to cancer. Several studies 
have demonstrated that smoking indoor can lead to levels of pollutants like PM2.5 to be over 
10 times higher than the level in a smoke-free house; to put it into prospective, the 
concentration of pollutants in smokers’ homes was equivalent or even greater than the 
outdoor levels of rather polluted cities like Beijing or Delhi [132]. However, some studies have 
demonstrated that smoking outdoor and then going back inside does not necessarily 
improve the situation from an IAQ point of view; in fact, a paper showed the difference 
between rooms with non-smokers, and people who smoked outdoor and went back in and 
the results reveal that the IAQ in smokers’ sleep environments was rather poor and 
insufficient [133].  
 
Smokers’ bedrooms exceeded the national limit of CO2 by 67% and CH2O, PM, and VOCs 
concentrations were between 33 and 100% higher than they should have been while non-
smokers’ rooms had normal values except for VOCs. The study shines a light on the effect 
known as thirdhand smoke, basically the particles of cigarettes that stick to walls and clothes 
or are emitted from breathing after smoking, which is precisely what explains why smoking 
outdoor and then walking back into the house does not reduce the pollutants people are 
exposed to that degrade IAQ which subsequently also affect sleep quality, specifically in 
individuals with asthma or respiratory issues as described in “IEQ parameters” [133].  
This is why not only smoking indoor is a mistake, but so is smoking outdoor and then going 
back inside the dwelling. Ideally, residents should quit smoking, but if it is not a feasible 
solution due to addiction, at least it would be beneficial not to smoke in general in the hours 
preceding bedtime to ensure the exposure to thirdhand smoke is minimized as much as 
possible to prevent having high levels of CO2, PM, VOCs, and other harmful pollutants in the 
air at night. 
 
• Candles. These common items are often found in many houses around the world and come 
in multiple shapes, designs, and scents. Although people usually purchase them to embellish 
rooms due to their physical appearance and the aesthetic contribution they have, there is 
one aspect that is neglected which can pose a risk to the IAQ and therefore sleep quality of 
residents, they are a source of indoor pollution. 
==PAGE:69==
65 
 
Multiple studies have been conducted to assess the pollutants candles can emit when 
burning, like one involving five types of wax-wick compositions. The results demonstrated 
that they could emit multiple harmful pollutants such as soot, PM2.5, particle PAHs, NOx, 
and more when the wick was burning; it was found that they reached a peak, after which 
their concentration decreased while the level of soot increased. The pollution produced by 
candles did not end with the extinguishment, but it continued a little bit further, specifically 
soot [134]. 
Moreover, in some studies it was revealed that a reduced cognitive capacity, and lung and 
cardiovascular-related health issues are some problems that can arise as a result of burning 
candles in indoor environments, jeopardizing IAQ [134]. 
Similar findings have been suggested by other studies which highlighted the indoor pollution 
produced by candles, resulting in the emission of CO, SO2, BTEX, PAHs, PM, and more, all 
elements that can have an adverse effect on IAQ [135]. 
 
Although burning candles can be a net contributor of indoor pollutants, it is not the same as 
having a chimney in the room, meaning that it does not necessarily result in a potential threat 
to human health. In fact, in a study the emissions of some pollutants caused by candles were 
analyzed and compared with limits after which they become dangerous to humans; it was 
found that, with the exception of PM, the concentration of other pollutants detected like 
VOCs did not exceed the maximum established limit [136].  
 
Overall, candles are sources of indoor pollution when they burn. While they do not 
necessarily pose an immediate risk to human health since they are typically not used 
everyday, the exposure changes depending on the ventilation conditions, the time they burn, 
and other factors that are not taken into account in some studies conducted in chambers 
[136], it is still important to remember that candles do release harmful pollutants and, as 
seen in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality”, along with other sources of pollution, they play a 
role in compromising IAQ which subsequently also impacts sleep quality.  
For this reason, it is advised not to use any candle at all in indoor environments, specifically 
in bedrooms; if this is not possible, it is suggested to limit the amount of candles lit and the 
exposure by not burning candles regularly but only during celebrations while remembering 
to ventilate during or at least after the candles extinguish in order to minimize as much as 
possible the impact some pollutants emitted by the burning process have on IEQ. 
 
• Music. Although sleeping is such an important aspect of our lives and beneficial to both our 
health and our economy as explained in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality” and “Chapter 1: 
Introduction”, many people have trouble sleeping. One way to directly intervene on this issue 
and improve sleep quality is through music. 
This solution has a healing effect on the human mind since it is used in therapy sessions to 
relieve pain, psychological issues, and problems related to lack of sleep. Music can be an 
emotional self-regulator influencing our nervous system and enhancing our emotional state 
making us feel more positive, thus relieving symptoms related to emotional disorders like 
depression; moreover, soothing music can have a physiological and psychological effect 
which slows down our hearth and respiratory rate, therefore preparing us in the phase 
preceding sleeping [137]. 
==PAGE:70==
66 
 
There are multiple studies that investigated the effect music has on sleep quality. One test 
conducted in Taiwan on older adults in which people who had sleep issues related to 
depression, cognitive impairment, and more had to listen to sedative music for 45 minutes 
before going to sleep for three weeks. The results indicated that after the session, the 
responders had a much better sleep quality than before, specifically regarding the time it 
took them to fall asleep, sleep efficiency, and reported fewer daytime dysfunctions the 
following day [138]. 
 A similar conclusion can be drawn from many other studies on elderlies, in which it can be 
stated that exposure to music before sleep improves many parameters related to sleep 
quality such as sleep latency and duration, sleep efficiency, and overall they felt more 
energetic and functional the following day thanks to a healthy night of sleep [137]. 
Older people are not the only ones suffering from sleep-related issues who can benefit from 
music exposure at night. Students are unfortunately another group of people who are 
considered poor sleepers, in fact between 37% to 80% of students have trouble sleeping 
depending on the country [139]. 
 
A study conducted in Japan showed that music can also have a positive impact on young 
adults; in this experiment lasting 4 weeks, students between the age of 18 and 30 who had 
sleep issues were categorized into three groups [139]: 
 
1. Prescribed music group, basically individuals who were assigned a list of soothing 
songs they had to listen to for 40 minutes before going to sleep. 
2. Preferred music group in which they were given the liberty to choose whatever songs 
they liked that they had to listen to for 40 minutes. Most of them opted for pop music. 
3. No-music group, students who did not have to listen to any song before bedtime. 
 
After the session, it was found that not only the groups that listened to music reported a 
better quality of sleep compared to the no-music group which did not report any 
improvement, but it also revealed that it is not necessary to listen to only soothing songs 
since the options chosen by the students produced a positive effect as well, allowing them 
to relax, and reduce stress and anxiety [139]. 
 
Overall, music can be a useful and simple tool everybody has access to that can improve 
sleep quality thanks to its effect it has on our emotions and mind. It is recommended for 
residents to test this solution for long periods of time to see the impact it has since it 
possesses the potential to be incorporated into their sleep routine as a way of enhancing 
sleep quality.  
 
• Sleep hygiene. This practice is the combination of bedroom environment and routines/habits 
that someone can adopt to improve their overall sleep quality [140].  
 
As seen in this project, sleeping is such an important part of our lives that strongly influences 
our productivity, physical and mental health, quality of life, and more aspects which is why it 
is important to enhance sleep quality, something that anyone can achieve by simply changing 
habits and routines [140]. Basic hygiene like washing our hands can prevent us from getting 
==PAGE:71==
67 
 
sick which can have a consequence on our health; the same concept can be applied to 
sleeping, which takes the name of sleep hygiene, simple instructions that can have a positive 
impact on someone’s sleep quality. 
 
There are multiple actions that can be implemented every day to improve sleep quality which 
were studied in different papers involving a variety of subjects, from average people to even 
students who are a group of individuals suffering from lack of sleep, with some getting even 
less than 6 hours of sleep per day. Nevertheless, with the right habits before going to bed 
mentioned below, even students recorded an increase in overall quality of sleep [141]. 
 
These are the activities that are incentivized due to their positive effect on sleep quality: 
ventilating the bedroom, exercising during the day, eating light meals before sleep time, 
avoiding using any electronic device in the hour preceding bedtime, purchasing comfortable 
mattresses, using relaxing technique suited to each resident to promote sleep quality like 
music as explained in another paragraph in this chapter, and more. However, one simple and 
effective implementation in everyone’s everyday life is the adoption of a consistent sleep-
wake schedule, basically going to sleep and waking up the next day at the same time in the 
same conditions since it becomes part of each day, a routine, and it programs the brain to 
perform the same activities every time, thus making it more likely to get enough sleep; this 
also includes the steps taken in the moments prior to going to sleep such as brushing teeth 
and wearing a pajama which consolidate the habit and prepare the mind to sleep time [140] 
[141]. 
 
On the other hand, it is essential to stop some behaviors many people adopt in their life 
before going to bed.  
Going to bed hungry or thirsty, smoking, drinking alcohol and caffeine in the hours before 
going to sleep, feeling anxious, nervous or in general being stressed and worrying about the 
following day’s problems, not getting enough exposure to sunlight during the day, and more 
have all been negatively associated with sleep quality [63] [140]. 
It must be acknowledged that while it is feasible and relatively easy to remove some poor 
behaviors such as going to sleep hungry, it is not always possible to eliminate certain 
behaviors or habits, specifically when there is an addiction involved (caffeine and alcohol 
abuse); in fact, in one study it was found that even if people are informed about the negative 
consequences that drinking coffee and alcohol have at night on sleep quality, they will 
continue practicing these habits [141]. This is why it is not suggested to stop such poor 
behaviors for the sake of improving sleep quality, but rather limit them or consume them not 
in the proximity of bedtime. 
 
Overall, knowing about these small actions that can have a positive or negative impact on 
sleep quality is useful, but it is even more effective to practice them; as F. C. Brown, W. C. 
Buboltz and B. Soper stated in their study “ knowing about proper habits does not necessarily 
influence sleep quality, whereas practicing proper habits is strongly related to good overall 
sleep quality” [141]. 
As hard as changing regular habits might be, it would be beneficial to adopt some behaviors 
mentioned above and eliminate or at least minimize habits that are detrimental to sleep 
quality.

Building related solutions 
 
This category is related to elements or objects and their correct use that can be integrated into a 
house to improve IEQ and sleep quality. 
• Blind use. Blinds come in different shapes and with different purposes: privacy, energy 
efficiency, lighting, and more. However, it is important to understand how to properly use 
blinds in order to fully utilize their advantages. 
 
People operate blinds differently depending on a multitude of factors. Some studies revealed 
that, regardless of the type of blinds they use like curtains or roller blinds, residents tend to 
close them more often on the south façade since the heat and light coming from the Sun is 
stronger and more intense throughout the year compared to the  north where the light is 
more diffuse and not direct [142]. 
 
The blind use pattern changes depending on the season as well. During summer, the 
percentage of blinds closed is much higher than in winter; this is not surprising since on hot 
days indoor environments can quickly overheat, an effect that can be combatted by closing 
the blinds, whereas in winter it is necessary to take advantage of the limited sunlight 
available to naturally warm up the house, something that encourages people to keep the 
windows coverings open [142]. 
It is also worth mentioning the part of the windows that is occluded by the blind and the 
effects it has. Covering only the upper area of the window, a common practice if roller blinds 
are installed in the house, translates into blocking sunlight more effectively than covering the 
bottom part of the window, an action that results in limiting the view more consistently than 
the upper part, thus providing more privacy than a shield from the sun. In other words, 
depending on what section of the window is covered which is also dependant on the type of 
blinds that are used, roller blinds slide from top to bottom while conventional curtains slide 
form left to right or vice versa, it is possible to achieve a different effect in terms of privacy 
and sunlight exposure [142]. 
 
Overall, blinds can be an ideal simple tool residents can use to have more privacy, optimize 
their thermal comfort based on the season and location, and darken the sleep environments 
at night, thus enhancing IEQ and sleep quality. 
For more information about blinds, see paragraph insulation and blinds in “Source control”.  
 
• Green cleaning products. Cleaning is a fundamental task that any resident needs to complete 
in order to keep the house tidy and remove dust and bacteria which might cause health 
issues and trigger allergies. However, something that is often overlooked are the negative 
health consequences of using conventional cleaning products and the impact they have on 
IAQ. 
 
Research has shown that people who use conventional cleaning products at home or at work 
tend to have a reduced lung function; furthermore, the exposure resulted in a 50% increase 
in the risk of asthma and an increase in developing respiratory diseases [143]. 
==PAGE:74==
70 
 
Cleaning products also contain considerable amounts of VOCs that, as explained in “IEQ 
parameters”, can be detrimental to human health, IAQ and sleep quality; some VOCs are not 
even listed on the ingredient labels on the products, some of which can be toxic, result in 
respiratory issues and even be carcinogenic [143]. 
 
An easy alternative that would allow residents to clean while at the same time minimize the 
impact such activity has on IAQ, and their health is the adoption of green cleaning products 
which utilize more eco-friendly ingredients rather than toxic elements such as heavy metals 
and more chemical compounds. This is not only better from a sustainability perspective since 
making green products, although it may still have an environmental impact on categories 
such as water consumption during use and disposal [144], it pollutes less than conventional 
ones as some LCA studies showed, but it is also beneficial to people’s health [145]. 
Green cleaning products have such a positive impact on people’s health and IAQ that 85% of 
office workers would like them to be utilized to clean their workplaces instead of 
conventional ones; moreover, the use of green alternatives could improve people’s 
productivity by 5% while simultaneously reducing the number of sick days due to poor air 
quality at work since they do not use harmful ingredients [145].  
 
To confirm the validity of such improvements regarding the reduction of toxic elements and 
VOCs present in green cleaning products, a study tested in a chamber the following products 
available on the US market: 16 conventional, 9 green with fragrance and 7 fragrance-free 
green. The results clearly illustrated in the figure below demonstrate that out of the 736 
VOCs identified, 75.5% came from the conventional items tested, 21% from the green 
products with fragrance and only 3.8% from fragrance-free green cleaning products. 
Moreover, none of the top 10 most impactful VOCs detected came from fragrance-free green 
products tested [143].

superior than their conventional counterparts since they emit lower levels of VOCs;

furthermore, the best option are the green products that do not have any fragrance since 
they did not have almost any harmful VOCs, thus they are suggested as the best cleaning 
products on the market [143]. 
A similar experiment involving 9 conventional cleaning products and 7 with a green label was 
conducted in both a laboratory and a real case scenario in which people used them to clean 
their house. It was found that 75% of the highest VOCs emissions came from conventional 
products; in fact, subjects who used them during cleaning were exposed to much higher 
concentrations of VOCs compared to individuals using green items [146]. 
A survey was carried out in Malaysia analyzing the benefits of using green cleaning products 
and it revealed that the adoption of such items would improve IAQ, reduce waste, provide a 
healthy environment, improve productivity, be more environmentally friendly, and have 
more benefits [147].  
 
Overall, due to the use of more environmentally friendly and less harmful ingredients, green 
cleaning products and specifically the ones without a fragrance are an acceptable option to 
conventional items. Since the price of the two alternatives has declined over the past years 
making them as expensive as their conventional counterparts [145], the adoption of green 
labeled products is cost effective while at the same time more optimal from an IAQ point of 
view given the reduced concentration of VOCs emitted, thus residents are encouraged to 
check the label on the bottle of the cleaning products and prioritize the purchase of green 
alternatives. 
 
• Sleep microenvironments. These terms refer to bedding (pillows and pillowcases, 
bedsheets, blankets, and bed frame) along with the volume of air surrounding it, including 
the so-called BZ (breathing zone); in other words, it is the area where we sleep at night.  
When it comes to elements that can compromise IEQ and interfere with sleep quality, people 
tend to refer to different types of sources; nevertheless, this sleep microenvironment is often 
overlooked when it comes to acknowledging that it can be a source of pollutants as 
illustrated in the figure below [148].  
 
 
Figure 22: Sources of pollutants in sleep microenvironment [148]. 
==PAGE:76==

If not thoroughly washed, bedding is a common source of dust which accumulates overtime 
containing different types of biological particles since people sleep on it which could cause 
irritation to the respiratory system, specifically to people allergic to dust. The situation 
worsens if considering that the particles can begin to float in the room because of the 
movements made during sleep; in fact, the more movements are made throughout the night, 
the more resuspension there will be causing more particles to become airborne [148]. 
Sleeping on a bed naturally leads to more bacteria originating from human skin, oral, and 
more that can cause dermal problems, weaken the immune system, and trigger allergic 
reactions; moreover, sweating can increase moisture generating the ideal environment for 
fungi to proliferate which can be a source of skin irritation and itchiness or in some cases the 
exposure to it can lead to the development of infections like athlete’s foot [149]. 
 
Bedding can also be a source of multiple VOCs such as plasticizers and formaldehyde from 
the bed frame in it is made of wood, which are more likely to propagate when moving at 
night, but are also fueled by the human body temperature and moisture, two elements that 
accelerate the emission rate since people are directly in contact with all these sources of 
VOCs [148]; a study reinforced this concept with an experiment in which it was found that 
the concentration of pollutants under the bedsheet was 30 times higher than in the middle 
of the room; sleeping with the head under the covers can increase the intake of pollutants 
by 24 times with a rebreathing rate of 60%, a value high enough to potentially induce hypoxia 
[150]. 
 
Overall, sleep microenvironments are sources of pollutants mostly due to dust they 
accumulate and VOCs coming from the materials of the bedding which makes it worse 
considering that people sleep in contact with such sources with their noses at a few 
centimeters from them.  
In order to combat the pollutants on the sources described, it is important to choose low-
emitting materials to minimize the VOCs emitted, wash the different elements composing 
the bedding regularly, at least once a week or every 3 to 4 days if there are pets sleeping on 
the bed to prevent the accumulation of dust and other particles that might undermine 
breathing and therefore sleep quality [151]. Furthermore, ventilation is another aspect that 
could promote the dilution of pollutants and clear the air in the bedroom. 
 
• Indoor plants. Many residents tend to buy potted plants to position around the house, 
typically in living rooms where they spend most of their time during the day; this is mostly 
done for decoration purposes or if they have the green thumb. However, what some might 
not take into consideration is that indoor plants can influence IEQ. 
Generally, plants have a few abilities that can be beneficial from an indoor environment point 
of view: 
 
- 
Photosynthesis. This process most people know about involves the absorption of CO2 in a 
room to then produce oxygen (O2) as a by-product at a rate that changes depending on the 
plant and the optimal lighting exposure it needs [152]. 
==PAGE:77==
73 
 
- 
Transpiration. This feature consists of water moving from the roots of the plant to the leaves 
which is then turned into water vapor and released into the room. Light, temperature, and 
humidity can influence transpiration [152]. 
- 
Phytoremediation. This useful tool that certain plants have can serve as a biological filter 
cleaning the air in a room by removing VOCs, CO, and CO2 [152]. 
 
The abilities of some plants to cleanse the air from CO, CO2, VOCs, and PM is quite complex 
with different studies analyzing specific laboratory tests or real-life scenario tests. 
Since they require light in order to initiate the photosynthesis process, a study tested the 
absorption of CO2 in two settings with different lighting intensity to see if it would optimize 
the removal; the results showed that the higher the intensity is, the more CO2 reduction it is 
expected to occur in a room. It must be noted that this experiment was carried out in a 
laboratory and not in an actual building [152]. 
While there is some evidence indicating that plants can help reduce CO2 which is an selling 
point for many florists, it must be acknowledged that this contribution is highly dependent 
on the type of plant and its exposure to sunlight, implying that they would be used in living 
rooms but not in bedrooms since they have to be as dark as possible in order to facilitate and 
guarantee an adequate sleep quality as mentioned in “How IEQ can affect sleep quality”. 
Furthermore, most mixes used in potted plants have decomposing organic matter which can 
be producers of CO2 if not properly illuminated, thus compromising IAQ instead of improving 
it [153]. 
 
VOCs and PM were also the targets of some studies, like the one that was carried out in an 
office showed that the seven plants were able to keep the concentration of VOCs below 100 
ppb which is a healthy level. A similar result was found in a test conducted in another office 
with certain plants; it was found that the rooms with plants had a 47% to 69% reduction in 
VOCs which were kept below 100 ppm in this case as well [153]. Another test in a chamber 
in which two species were tested, Zamioculcas and Aglaonema, and it was found that after 
48 hours, the benzene present was fully removed [154].  
Exactly like the CO2 tests mentioned earlier, the VOCs removal capabilities of plants are 
dependant on the light intensity. An experiment revealed that the formaldehyde removal 
abilities for spider plants, aloe-soil system, and golden-soil system were drastically different 
between 14% and 95% depending on the light intensity [152]. 
 
More measurements were gathered from newly built apartments in South Korea that had 
the following plants: rubber trees, rhapis, and happy tree. While the results were positive 
regarding VOCs with a substantial decrease between 36% and 85% in every single pollutant 
tested (benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, and xylene) except for benzene which recorded a 
decrement of only 9%, the same pattern could not be found for PM since no relevant 
decrease was detected probably due to a high concentration of PM outdoor during the test 
which might have penetrated into the building disrupting the results [155].  
Although plants show a significant ability to remove VOCs from indoor places, it must be 
mentioned that they do not replace the removal rate of a well-ventilated room due to the 
exchange of air between outdoor and indoor of around 1 h-1 ; in fact, it would take a total of 
10 to 1000 plants per m2 to achieve such figure depending on the species chosen [156]. 
==PAGE:78==
74 
 
Besides their direct effect on IAQ, plants can also have an impact on the human well-being 
and mind.  
Indoor plants can help people feel in touch with nature and be beneficial to their mental 
health. A survey was conducted analyzing the influence plants have on a group of people and 
it was found that the following species epipremnum, ficus sphere, palm, and ficus column 
had the most impact on their mental health and used the following terms to describe their 
feelings: uplifting, interesting, relaxing and beautiful [157]. 
 
In other words, just the presence of some plants in the indoor environment can help the 
residents feel less stressed, uplift their mood, lower anxiety and stress due to their 
appearance but especially the healthiness and canopy density which contributed the most 
to the responders’ well-being; this implies that plants that do not seem healthy can 
undermine the perception of indoor environment while lush green plants are beneficial [152] 
[157]. Furthermore, beautiful plants positively affect the overall comfort and health; a test 
conducted in an office showed that the presence of plants reduced complains about 
coughing and fatigue by over 30% [152]. 
Indoor plants have the same psychological effect on humans as urban vegetation described 
in “Air cleaning” and since mental health issues can disrupt sleep quality due to their relation 
[103], having a lot of plants that appeal the residents can help them feel more relaxed and 
reduce any stress related symptoms which can subsequently have a beneficial impact on 
their sleep [152]. 
 
Overall, indoor plants are an interesting solution that can be implemented in an indoor 
environment due to their removal capabilities of CO, CO2, PM, and VOCs; although their 
abilities may vary depending on the species and lighting conditions which prevents them 
from being positioned in dark rooms like sleep environments but rather in the living areas, 
they can still contribute not only to removing certain pollutants and improving IAQ, but also 
have a positive impact on the residents’ mental health serving as a tool to embellish and 
make the environment more relaxing to be in, thus minimizing their stress, anxiety, and other 
issues which would normally compromise their sleep quality. 
 
Nevertheless, there are plants that are known not only for their remarkable capabilities of 
removing VOCs like toluene and formaldehyde, but also for their ability to remove CO2 with 
a very low light intensity. In a study that tested in a chamber the IAQ benefits of two plants, 
zamioculcas zamiifolia and sansevieria trifasciata, revealed that they were able to remove 
95% of toluene and formaldehyde in 48 hours while also reducing he CO2 concentration from 
410 ppm to 160 ppm [158].

above have when it comes to reducing the CO2 levels at night and in the morning in a real-
life scenario, in was decided to conduct some measurements to enrich the content of this

range of CO2 above 1250 ppm while the night before without plants averaged 1175 
ppm. Night 8 with plants reached a range of around 1430 ppm while the night before 
without plants did not exceed 1350 ppm. This increase in the concentration of carbon 
dioxide when the plants were in the bedroom compared to the nights before without 
plants was likely caused by the two fiddle-leaf fig since this trend occurred for four 
consecutive days instead of only one night, indicating that the possibility of it being 
an anomaly fueled by outside sources is low. 
Considering that the conditions in the room were not changed, the explanation for the 
distinct behaviors reported in the two phases is unknown.

Conclusions: 
 
Although the first three tests with the 10 plants capable of initiating the photosynthesis 
process even in areas with low light intensity such as at night showed positive results in terms 
of concentration of CO2 in the last 30 minutes of sleep that was reduced by 70 – 100 ppm 
compared to nights without plants depending on the case, it must be said that there were 
occasions in which this effect did not occur, on the contrary a few nights with plants had a 
higher CO2 range than the previous nights with no plants in the room. The reason for such 
behavior is uncertain and might depend on either the performance of the 10 plants or 
outside factors like infiltration of CO2 through the door. 
Another aspect to consider is that even when the concentration of carbon dioxide with plants 
in the room was lower than the night before without plants, it was never below 1000 ppm 
which is the recommended by some regulation and guidelines. 
Moreover, the data suggests that neither the 10 plants nor the two normal plants (fiddle-leaf 
fig) were able to accelerate the decay of CO2 in the morning right after awakening. 
 
It can be concluded that the 10 plants utilized in the first three tests (four zamioculcas 
zamiifolia, one sansevieria trifasciata, and five platycerium bifurcatum) can be useful at 
slightly reducing the CO2 concentration at night by roughly 70 – 100 ppm in a room with the 
same size as the one in the tests, but more measurements and analysis are necessary to 
assess their effectiveness depending on the number of plants, the type of room, its size, and 
the season. Since the contribution of the plants is very minimal at reducing CO2 at night, it 
must be reminded that they can always be utilized for uplifting the residents’ mental health, 
meaning that the presence of these lush green plants can have a positive effect on their 
mood, stress, anxiety, and other mental disorders [152] [157]. 
 
In future studies, it would be interesting to measure the ability of the plants at removing 
other types of pollutants in the bedroom like PM and VOCs and not simply CO2 like it was 
done in this test. 
The limitations of the measurements can be found in “Limitations”.

The list briefly illustrates all the non-technical solutions prioritized based on their usefulness: 
• Green cleaning products. Certain harmful pollutants like VOCs are emitted by cleaning 
products which could jeopardize people’s health and IAQ. 
To minimize that, the adoption of green cleaning alternatives is suggested since they contain 
natural ingredients rather than chemical ones and emit fewer pollutants than their 
conventional counterparts, specifically products that do not have any fragrance. 
For more information about this topic, go to “Building related solutions”. 
 
• Natural ventilation (window opening). Opening windows at night can be an effective and 
convenient non-energy dependant solution to remove pollutants, cool down indoor 
environments, and therefore enhance sleep quality. 
Although it may not be more efficient or versatile than mechanical ventilation systems and it 
can compromise both thermal comfort and IAQ if it is too cold outside and polluted, it is still 
a great solution that can be easily utilized and adjusted by the residents based on their likings 
and preferences to optimize IEQ and sleep quality.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Ventilation related solutions”.  
 
• Indoor plants. Thanks to the abilities of certain plants to remove pollutants when exposed 
to sunlight, they can be useful tool to integrate in a house to improve IAQ. Moreover, due to 
their appearance and lushness, they can have a positive impact on the residents’ mental 
health, similarly to urban vegetation in “Air cleaning”, which can subsequently alleviate stress 
and anxiety, thus enhancing their sleep quality. 
However, it must be said that, although plants can contribute to improving IAQ to a certain 
extent, they do not have the same capacity to cleanse the air of pollutants as natural or 
mechanical ventilation systems, their soil can be a source of pollutants itself, and they require 
sunlight in order to start the photosynthesis process, except for a few plants mentioned in 
the case study, zamioculcas zamiifolia, sansevieria trifasciata, and platycerium bifurcatum; 
although their CO2 removal capabilities were minimal and variable, they can always be used 
for improving the residents’ mental health which can subsequently contribute to enhancing 
sleep quality.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Building related solutions”. 
 
• Smoking. This rather detrimental habit can lead to serious consequences not only to 
someone’s health but also to IAQ. Smoking indoor or being exposed to second-hand and 
third-hand smoke can all generate substantial amounts of pollutants that can permeate a 
sleep environment, thus damaging both IAQ and sleep quality. 
Quitting would be the ideal option, but if it is not feasible due to addiction issues, it would 
be preferable not to smoke in the hours preceding bedtime to minimize the concentration of 
harmful pollutants in the bedroom at night.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Behaviors/habits related solutions”. 
 
==PAGE:92==
88 
 
• Candles. They can be sources of indoor pollution due to the harmful elements released 
during the burning process like PM, VOCs, and soot. Although they do not pose an immediate 
threat to human health because the exposure is usually limited since they are not lighted 
everyday, they are still a source of pollutants that can affect IAQ and sleep quality, which is 
why it is suggested not to use them or at least ventilate the environments after they 
extinguish. For more information about this topic, go to “Behaviors/habits related solutions”. 
 
• Music. Listening to music at night before going to sleep has been found to be helpful at 
enhancing quality of sleep. It is a simple tool we all have access to that can be beneficial to 
anyone regardless of their age, from young students to elderlies, and type of music, from 
soothing songs to pop.  
It is suggested to test it for long periods of time with different genres of music to see if it has 
a positive effect on sleep quality. 
For more information about this topic, go to “Behaviors/habits related solutions”. 
 
• Blind use. These can be useful implementations that can have a positive effect on privacy, 
thermal comfort, lighting, and more.  
Typically, it is suggested to keep the blinds slightly or fully closed in summer especially on the 
south façade to avoid overheating, while in winter the opposite should be done to naturally 
heat the house. Moreover, it is important to block the sunlight coming from the upper part 
of the window rather than the bottom, something possible with roller blinds.  
For more information about this topic, go to “Building related solutions” and “Source control”. 
 
• Sleep microenvironments. It represents the area where we sleep, including the bedding and 
the air around it. This environment can be a source of multiple pollutants due to the secretion 
of sweat, human skin, saliva, and more that can turn the bedding into the perfect 
environment for fungi; moreover, the materials that compose the bedding may emit VOCs 
and accumulate dust, something that is made worse by our movements at night which can 
resuspend the particles and make them airborne. 
To prevent this, it is important to frequently clean the bedding, choose low-emitting 
materials, and ventilate the bedroom throughout the day. 
For more information about this topic, go to “Building related solutions”. 
 
• Sleep hygiene. These are norms everyone should integrate in their everyday life to improve 
sleep quality.  
Some habits should be promoted like creating a sleep schedule and sticking to it everyday, 
not going to sleep hungry, exercising during the day, and more due to their positive impact 
on sleep quality. On the other hand, others should be discouraged like using electronic 
devices before bedtime, smoking, drinking alcohol, and more because of their potential at 
jeopardizing the residents’ quality of sleep. 
For more information about this topic, go to “Behaviors/habits related solutions”.

Chapter 8: Discussion 
 
As it was described in “Non-technical solutions” and “Recommendations to residents that can influence 
IEQ and sleep quality”, there are multiple non-technical solutions that building designers can 
implement, and actions and decisions residents can make to minimize the indoor pollution and 
improve IEQ and subsequently sleep quality. 
Even though those considerations have a positive impact on IEQ and sleep quality without relying 
on energy, there are issues and limitations that come with them that need to be discussed. 
 
Combination of non-technical and technical solutions 
 
Although all the solutions explained in terms of supplying fresh air through natural ventilation like 
window opening and trickle vents have a positive effect on IEQ to a certain extent, they would not 
be able to fully replace a traditional mechanical ventilation system on their own since it has features 
that can not be replicated by non-energy consuming technologies, such as ability to filter the outside 
air before supplying it into the indoor environment especially when it has high levels of pollutants 
like PM, something that can not be done by normal natural ventilation systems like windows, a 
disadvantage that would just end up compromising IEQ instead of improving it [128] [129], except 
in the case of trickle vents in which a small filter can be installed to minimize the amount of outdoor 
pollution flowing into the indoor environments, but it must be changed frequently in order to keep 
it functional and effective. 
Moreover, natural ventilation might not be an optimal option if the outside temperature is low, a 
decision that could jeopardize the thermal comfort of the residents [121]; although this negative 
effect can be minimized by adjusting the opening area of the window based on the liking of the 
residents [127], it can generate heat losses and an increase in energy expenditure if the radiators 
are set to their maximum capacity to satisfy the thermal comfort of the occupants. The problem can 
easily be solved by some mechanical systems that are able to heat up the outside air before 
supplying it into the house, thus guaranteeing an adequate level of thermal comfort. 
Regarding the issue about keeping an indoor environment warm when the outside temperature is 
low, it must be mentioned that there are other non-energy consuming options that can be chosen 
to address it like passive heating systems. Assuming that the house is properly thermally insulated, 
both walls and windows, during sunny days in winter engineers or architects could take advantage 
of large windows specifically on the south façade to amplify the solar heat that can be stored in 
thermal mass elements such as masonry, stone, concrete, and more, and then slowly released at 
night when it is needed by the residents [159]. However, the issue with this method is that not only 
it requires sunlight during the day which is not often available in winter during cloudy days, but it 
also may not provide the same level of warmth as active heating systems; moreover, even if it 
releases some stored heat at night, it does not solve the problem of supplying fresh heated air from 
==PAGE:102==
98 
 
outside like a mechanical ventilation system, meaning that it may satisfy the thermal comfort of the 
residents but not improve IAQ. 
On the other hand, there are also flaws regarding HVAC systems. Besides the fact that they account 
for 32% of the energy consumed by residential buildings worldwide making them a big contributor 
of the global energy consumption [7] as described in “Why non-technical solutions?”.  They can be a 
source of indoor noise pollution if they are outdated or not properly designed [73], and, unlike some 
non-technical solutions like trickle vents or simple window opening, HVAC systems require 
maintenance periodically, particularly focused on filters every 6 or 12 months, in order to function 
efficiently without which they could potentially become a source of indoor pollution contradicting 
their predefined purpose of improving IAQ [9] [126], thus becoming a yearly financial burden for the 
residents who also have to remember to perform such maintenance. In other words, while HVAC 
systems need maintenance every year which can be a financial constraint for some individuals like 
low-income residents, most non-technical solutions do not, and if it is necessary, it is usually 
performed less frequently due to the lack of electronic components or filters.

In general, both non-technical solutions relying on natural ventilation and mechanical ventilation 
systems (technical solutions) have pros and cons as seen in the table above; perhaps, in order to 
maximize their individual potential, it would be optimal to combine the two solutions to enhance 
IEQ while the rest of the non-technical solutions can contribute making the indoor environment 
healthy which can improve sleep quality. 
In fact, a hybrid ventilation system, an indoor environment that is naturally and mechanically 
ventilated, not only would be able to take advantage of the positive aspects of both systems but 
would also be able to make up for each other’s weaknesses, thus making it a more versatile and 
==PAGE:103==
99 
 
adaptable solution for every situation. For example, if it is too cold outside or too polluted, while 
opening the windows might not be the best option, using the mechanical ventilation system would 
be able to warm up the air flowing into the building and filter it, improving IEQ. Whereas, if the 
conditions are ideal outside, meaning that the temperatures are not too low and the outdoor air 
quality is at a safe level, natural ventilation would be the preferable solution. 
In a study carried out in Singapore that lasted two months, the overall IEQ and perceived sleep 
quality were compared in two different settings: one with natural and mechanical ventilation 
systems (NMV), and the other with AC. The results revealed that not only people preferred the NMV 
configurations over the AC bedrooms since they were considered better sleep environments, but 
the CO2 level was substantially higher in AC bedrooms, between 500 and 1100 ppm, than in NMV 
settings in which it ranged between 420 and 560 ppm [160]. It must be mentioned that it was carried 
out in a hot tropical city, so its results may differ in other locations. 
Another study investigated the differences in terms of IEQ over summer, autumn, and winter among 
naturally ventilated rooms, mechanically ventilated environments, and houses ventilated through 
hybrid systems, basically combining both NV and MV. Although opening windows was sufficient 
during summer at achieving low CO2 concentration indoor, it was inadequate during winter where 
peaks of even 5000 ppm were recorded, potentially due to the presence of a busy road, and the fact 
that outdoor pollution levels tend to be higher in cold seasons; moreover, the lowest temperature 
was found in cases with NV in winter of around 18.9°C while relative humidity was higher in naturally 
ventilated rooms which could lead to the formation of mold or fungi [161]. On the other hand, 
houses with mechanical ventilation systems had lower CO2 levels, temperatures were above 20°C in 
all seasons but in some moments had insufficient levels of relative humidity below 30% [161]. 
The case with both natural and mechanical ventilation, hybrid system, seems to be the most stable 
scenario. Not only it recorded low concentrations of CO2 and acceptable levels of relative humidity 
of around 35% in winter which is within the limits as mentioned in “IEQ parameters”, but it was able 
to maintain the temperature at around 22°C throughout the entire period of three seasons. As M. 
B. Narożny and M. Kostka stated “it is possible to effectively ventilate rooms with the use of hybrid 
ventilation systems, combining mechanical and natural systems” [161]. 
While it is true that the adoption of a hybrid system, the combination of natural and mechanical 
ventilation technologies, would involve the use of energy when the MV is utilized, it must be 
mentioned that there are new types of systems being developed that consume less power than 
conventional solutions, with some able to save up between 64% and 73% of heat energy [8]. 
Moreover, the world is moving towards the implementation of more green technologies able to 
produce energy in a sustainable way from renewable sources as seen in the figure below, which will 
account for 35% of the electricity production by 2025 [162]. This entails that, although nowadays 
many new buildings are required to have some type of mechanical ventilation system and the 
proposed comprise of a hybrid ventilation system involves the use of electricity, if the trend seen in 
the figure below continues to grow, the use of energy-consuming technologies should not be a 
massive problem from an environmental point of view, especially if the source of energy is 
renewable.

Limitations 
 
In this project, the following limitations can be identified: 
• Testing of non-technical solution, trickle vents (case study). During the measurements 
conducted in the three houses described in “Ventilation”, a few limitations can be identified. 
 
Firstly, as mentioned in the description of the measurements, the room of case one was not 
an average sized bedroom with a door like it was in case two and three, but rather a large 
room consisting of the living room turned into a temporary bedroom for the test and the 
bedroom of the first subject’s roommate divided by curtains, both connected to the kitchen 
through a short corridor. This means that the CO2 could dilute and travel around a large 
environment making it harder to compare the results of case one with case two and three. 
 
Secondly, the bedroom door in case two, which was supposed to be closed in phase one and 
two, was frequently opened during the night by a cat. This might have altered the results 
which would explain why some nights had more variable and inconsistent data not aligned 
with their counterparts. 
 
Finally, the entire test only involved three subjects due to lack of time and difficulties 
encountered in the process of finding potential candidates; although it was enough to 
comprehend that the CO2 concentration was at its lowest point when both trickle vents and 
==PAGE:105==
101 
 
doors were open, the number was too low to draw any comprehensive conclusion regarding 
their potential.  
 
In future studies, it would be interesting to investigate the efficacy of trickle vents on a large 
scale in dozens of different bedrooms to have a more homogenous idea of their contribution 
when it comes to reducing carbon dioxide when they are open. Moreover, it could be useful 
to measure not only CO2, but also other pollutants like VOCs to assess whether the trickle 
vents can minimize their concentration during the night or not, something that was not 
evaluated in this project. 
 
• Testing of non-technical solution, indoor plants (case study). During the measurements 
carried out to analyze the potential of three types of plants, zamioculcas zamiifolia, 
sansevieria trifasciata, and platycerium bifurcatum, of removing pollutants like CO2 from a 
single bedroom described in “Building related solutions”, a few limitations can be identified. 
 
Firstly, the bedroom window and door were supposed to be kept always closed, except right 
before going to sleep when the door was opened to place the plants in or out of the room 
depending on the day, in order to control the indoor environment as much as possible to 
prevent potential infiltrations of air or CO2 from altering the measurements. Although the 
conditions were not changed, it must be noted that the window and the door were not fully 
airtight which might have unintentionally influenced or affected the indoor environment in 
the bedroom, a disadvantage that could explain why some days the CO2 concentration was 
off or varied drastically compared to the day before.  
 
Secondly, even though the plants used in the test showed some positive results compared to 
nights when they were not in the bedroom, it would be interesting to conduct a similar 
experiment while changing the number of plants to see if there is any improvement. For 
instance, if the 10 plants generally reduced the CO2 by roughly 70 to 100 ppm depending on 
the case, what would 20 plants do? Would they be able to double the reduction of carbon 
dioxide recorded in the test? If so, would only 5 plants record around half of the reduction?  
However, it must be acknowledged that simply the presence of 10 plants in the bedroom 
obstructed the freedom of movement of the subject; this implies that, regardless of the 
positive effect the plants might have on IAQ and sleep quality, they might also get in the way 
or limit the available walkable space inside the room, a problem that worsens in cases of 
even smaller bedrooms than the one utilized to carry out the measurements in the four tests. 
 
Lastly, similar to the measurements about trickle vents, the tests were conducted in only one 
bedroom, making it impossible to draw universal conclusions regarding the potential of the 
plants given the air tightness of the house and potential infiltrations of air that might have 
altered the data.  
Future studies should investigate this topic in multiple bedrooms with different sizes and 
occupants to acquire a more thorough and comprehensive idea of their capabilities. 
Furthermore, it could be useful to measure not only CO2, but also other pollutants like VOCs 
to quantify the removal rate in bedrooms and the contribution plants can have on improving 
IAQ and sleep quality.

• Residents’ contribution. Chapter 5 was dedicated to residents to highlight the contribution 
and role they can play themselves to enhance both IEQ and sleep quality. Although the points 
listed are not mandatory and are not all meant to be followed, regardless of whether the 
pieces of advice and recommendations are provided to the occupants by 
engineers/architects or by this manual, it is not guaranteed that people will comply with any 
of the suggestions described.  
 
People mainly tend to follow the rules because of the disciplinary consequences of breaking 
the law [163]. Neglecting the points described in “Chapter 5: Sleep-enhancing design strategies 
for users” does not result in legal consequences since they are not rules nor mandatory to 
fulfill. 
However, there is another way of convincing people to follow a specific rule, in this case some 
recommendations about the correct behavior and decisions to make to improve IEQ and 
sleep quality, which is to clearly argue and explain why a rule is important or socially correct 
and the negative consequences of not complying rather than simply communicate the rule 
[164]. This is precisely what was done in this project; it was not just mentioned the actions 
or correct behaviors to adopt, on the contrary a comprehensive explanation was provided 
for each topic accompanied by evidence-based research and health consequences of not 
complying with the recommendations. This was done to hopefully shape the residents’ 
behaviors and influence their decision-making process to emphasize the importance of IEQ 
and sleep quality which can be both affected by the occupants themselves.  
 
It must be said that, in “Chapter 6: Feedback”, the responses given by the residents suggested 
that they would be willing to follow some recommendations which is a positive sign 
indicating that the descriptions and explanations of this manual based on the points 
mentioned above may be helpful at convincing people to change their behaviors and habits 
to improve IEQ and sleep quality in the long term. 
Nevertheless, it is impossible to predict whether residents will actually follow some of the 
recommendations or not, making it a limitation of this project. 
 
• Feedback. “Chapter 6: Feedback” was a useful part of the project to validate the work done 
until chapter 5, obtain opinions from the potential target audience about the manual to test 
their perception of it, and if they found the topics described useful or not. Nevertheless, 
some flaws can be identified in the process. 
 
Firstly, the number of responders is not high enough to fully acquire a general idea of what 
most of the target audience thinks of this manual, specifically regarding technical experts 
such as architects or engineers since only 10 participated. In future surveys, it would be 
relevant to try to reach a wider pool of individuals from multiple countries around the world 
to ensure the feedback realistically reflects what most think of this project. Perhaps, if the 
steps described in “Chapter 7: Dissemination” are taken, then social media platforms can be 
utilized to reach a larger audience to acquire more diverse feedback from a variety of 
individuals. 
 
Secondly, although a few questions requested an explanation for some of the answers by 
including the word “why?” at the end of the questions such as “Which are your favorite topics 
==PAGE:107==
103 
 
and why?”, most responders only provided a short answer but not the explanation, 
something that would have been useful to know. Moreover, the two questions that were not 
mandatory to answer because the decision was left to the individuals not to force them to 
write responses that they would have likely not meant “Do you think there are topics we 
missed in the manual? If so, could you please explain them?” and “Do you have any 
suggestion on how to improve the manual?”, almost no one typed a response, an important 
part of the feedback that would have been relevant to analyze.  
 
Finally, another limitation was the time available to conduct the survey. The period in which 
the survey was carried out, beginning of December 2023 to beginning of January 2024, might 
have been too limited for some people to read over 50 pages of the manual until chapter 5 
due to the Christmas holidays, which would explain the relatively low number of responses. 
In future surveys, not only it is necessary to increase the sample size as mentioned before, 
but it is also important to leave the link of the online survey active for a longer period of time, 
3 to 6 months rather than just one, to allow people to find some spare time to go through 
the entire document and carefully examine it in order to ensure they provide honest answers 
instead of a rushed feedback.

Chapter 4” and “Summary of Chapter 5”. Lastly, “Chapter 7: Dissemination” was a look at future paths 
that can be chosen to turn this work into a manual that can be made available to the public and 
guarantee its full fruition. 
Regarding the second research question in “Objective and research questions”, although the solutions 
discussed in chapter 4 and 5 do contribute to a certain extent to minimizing indoor pollution and 
improving IEQ along with sleep quality as demonstrated by the studies mentioned in this project, 
and the feedback indicated that most responders who read the draft would be interested in 
embracing some recommendations in their projects for the case of designers and in their everyday 
life for the case of residents, it must also be acknowledged that they are not intended to fully replace 
a conventional mechanical ventilation system as explained in “Chapter 8: Discussion” since the non-
technical solutions can not provide the same services that mechanical ventilation systems offer in 
the right moment they are needed, such as maintaining an adequate thermal comfort in winter while 
supplying the house with filtered fresh air coming from outside on a rather polluted day. 
Moreover, some non-technical integration involving designers simply cannot be approved in certain 
conditions or locations, such as the addition of urban vegetation if the plot of land either does not 
allow it or is not large enough. Similarly, some changes in residents’ behaviors or habits that would 
be beneficial to their IEQ and sleep quality are hard to achieve, especially if they are fueled by 
addictions like smoking, or other reasons which can prevent people from integrating some positive 
behaviors in the long term such as lack of commitment, willingness to adopt the correct habits, time 
to improve their routine, and more factors. 
In conclusion, while non-technical solution can enhance IEQ and sleep quality to a certain extent if 
they are integrated in the building project or in the habits of residents, the combination of both non-
technical solutions and a mechanical ventilation system is the most optimal option since they can 
make up for each other’s weaknesses and it is possible to take advantage of the positive sides of 
both systems rather than just one of them, thus having a more versatile and effective tool in each 
house.  
It must be reminded that it is the designers’ responsibility to make the right decisions about which 
non-technical solutions to incorporate in the project to place more emphasis on IEQ and sleep 
quality based on the national and local regulations, budget, type of house, location, and overall 
design of the building. Regarding the residents’ contribution to keeping an adequate IEQ and sleep 
quality in the long term, it should be encouraged by designers or by the occupants themselves after 
reading this manual to try to remove detrimental behaviors and habits while adopting as many 
beneficial ones as possible in their everyday life.
