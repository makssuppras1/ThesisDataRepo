Data Analysis of AI-assisted Biodiversity Monitoring 
Abstract 
This thesis investigates how biodiversity metrics can be applied to underwater video data 
generated by AI-powered camera systems developed by Anemo Robotics. The goal was to bridge 
the gap between automated species detection and ecological insight by selecting, testing, and 
structuring biodiversity metrics in a way that supports scientific and operational use. A literature 
review and stakeholder analysis led to a phased metric framework, with core metrics including 
abundance, diversity, and behaviour-based indicators. 
 
These metrics were applied to data from the MOSAR project, a real-world artificial reef 
deployment in a Danish harbour. The outputs revealed differences between control and reef sites 
across all core metrics. However, full ecological interpretation is left out of the thesis and reserved 
to professional marine biologist. Ultimately, the system delivers a rich, structured dataset that is 
designed to support further interpretation by domain experts. 
 
While not intended as a biological validation, the project provides a reproducible framework for 
integrating biodiversity metrics into future Anemo deployments. The thesis highlights the potential 
of Anemo Robotics approach to contribute significantly to biodiversity assessment in both 
regulatory and scientific contexts.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Problem statement 
 
We’re in the midst of the biggest biodiversity crisis marked by an unprecedented reduction in 
wildlife populations, with a 73% decrease reported over the las 50 years. Scientists are 
recognizing this crisis as the sixth mass extinction event, and it is the first primarily driven by 
human activities. The acceleration of species loss through habitat destruction, pollution, 
overexploitation, and climate change has detrimental impacts on ecosystem services, which are 
vital for human survival (WWF, 2024). Marine ecosystems are particularly vulnerable. 
 
Luckily both companies and governments are trying to fix this with biodiversity net positive by 
2030 goals. Research indicates that local-scale marine biodiversity outcomes are heavily 
influenced by human impact. Whenever we build a man-made structure in the ocean we impact 
biodiversity. It is important to note that if we take the right precautions we can increase marine 
biodiversity around offshore structures (Elahi et al., 2015).  
 
In order to stop and reverse biodiversity loss it is important to keep track of what is happening 
under the water, however current methods are inadequate. They are often intrusive, expensive, 
and irregular (Payne et al., 2016). Emerging technologies, such as autonomous monitoring 
systems, offer promising solutions to both of those challenges. 
 
 
1.2. Anemo Robotics 
 
Founded at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), Anemo Robotics is making innovations 
for assessing marine biodiversity. The company’s first product is a combination of underwater 
camera systems (AnemoCam) and computer vision AI models designed to recognize and count 
fish species (AnemoAI). 
 
The conception of Anemo Robotics was inspired by the expanding scale of marine construction, 
particularly the surge in offshore wind farm development, where they discovered a critical niche 
in measuring marine biodiversity, a big component of offshore construction. Research indicated 
that, with proper management, constructing offshore facilities could actually enhance marine 
biodiversity.  
 
One significant challenge in the industry was the manual and labour-intensive process required 
to track and report biodiversity impacts to regulatory authorities. This sector, like many traditional 
industries, has been slow to adopt new technologies. The founders of Anemo Robotics saw an 
opportunity to address this gap by providing more precise and accessible biodiversity data to 
companies engaged in ocean construction. 
 
 
AnemoCam: Longterm Underwater Camera System 
 
AnemoCam is an autonomous underwater camera system built for long-term marine biodiversity 
monitoring. Designed to operate for up to three months without human intervention, AnemoCam 
is equipped with high-capacity batteries, automated antifouling systems, and high-intensity 
lighting that ensures visibility even in low-light or turbid conditions, down to 50 meters of depth. It 
provides continuous, high-definition footage with settings tailored for AI-based species detection.

AnemoAI: Streamlined Species Detection 
 
A major challenge in marine biodiversity assessment via video is the analysis phase, which 
traditionally is done manually and consumes up to five hours for every hour of footage (DTU Aqua, 
internal communication, 2023). Given that scientists may gather over a hundred hours of footage 
for a single project, the analysis phase can become excessively time-consuming to the point 
where it is extremely impractical or simply impossible. AnemoAI addresses this by significantly 
reducing the time required for video assessments, with the use of automated species detection 
models. 
 
Anemo does not anticipate that a universal AI model capable of recognizing all fish species 
globally will be available any time soon. This is why Anemo is focusing on creating a system that 
can be rapidly adjusted and allows for fine-tuning to new environments. This is achieved using a 
custom-trained YOLO v8 computer vision model as a foundational element, which is then re-
trained through an active learning process. This approach has already reduced internal model 
training time by tenfold and continues to improve as more data is gathered. 
 
 
1.3. Project objective of this Master’s Thesis  
 
This Master’s Thesis contributes to one of the many ongoing development areas within Anemo 
Robotics: translating the output of AnemoAI into biodiversity-relevant formats that are useful for 
marine stakeholders. With Anemo’s current technology in place, the next step is to explore how 
the data generated can be translated into biodiversity metrics that are relevant, understandable, 
and useful for biologists, regulators, and clients working in marine environments. 
 
This thesis explores new opportunities for how Anemo’s data can align with recognized 
biodiversity assessment methods. As the company grows, so does the need to ensure that our 
outputs can be interpreted within ecological frameworks. This work is therefore not intended as a 
biological validation of ecosystem changes, but as a foundation to show how Anemo’s technology 
can produce relevant and valuable biodiversity data.  
 
The scope of this thesis is threefold:  
 
First, to identify biodiversity metrics and standards currently used in both academia and industry, 
with a focus on those suited for video-based underwater monitoring. This involves reviewing 
scientific literature, and common practices in marine ecology to understand which metrics are 
considered valuable, and how they are applied. The goal is not only to list existing metrics but to 
analyze which ones are most relevant to the type of data AnemoAI generates. 
 
Second, to apply those metrics to data collected from Anemo’s flagship project, the MOSAR 
(Monitoring Of Small Artificial Reefs) project. The aim of this application is not to perform a full 
biological or ecological analysis, but to demonstrate the type of outputs Anemo’s system can 
provide. This includes showcasing the richness, consistency, and usability of the data, and 
illustrating how it can support expert interpretation by marine biologists, ecologists, or regulators. 
 
Third, based on this work, the thesis proposes a set of key biodiversity metrics and analysis 
methods that Anemo could adopt as part of its future data pipeline. This includes defining what 
Anemo projects should aim to deliver, and recommending how these can be standardized for 
broader application. The idea is to support a first step towards a future where biodiversity data 
from AnemoAI is compatible with scientific and industry expectations. 
 
By addressing the scope, guided by the research questions found in Appendix A, the thesis 
supports Anemo’s mission to build a robust, versatile, and scientifically grounded platform for 
marine biodiversity monitoring, that can serve both commercial applications and broader 
ecological understanding.

2. Understanding Biodiversity Metrics 
Monitoring biodiversity requires more than simply counting species. It involves examining 
patterns, ecological roles, and species interactions across time and space. In both academic 
research and applied marine monitoring, biodiversity metrics serve as tools to translate raw 
observations into interpretable indicators of ecosystem health.  
 
As offshore development and nature-inclusive design expand, the need for standardized 
biodiversity monitoring methods has grown. Metrics offer a common framework for researchers, 
engineers, ecologists, and policymakers.  
 
This section explores how biodiversity is defined and measured, which types of metrics are 
commonly used, and how these approaches inform both scientific understanding and practical 
decision-making in industry contexts. 
 
 
2.1. Why Monitor Marine Biodiversity? 
 
Marine biodiversity plays a foundational role in the health, resilience, and productivity of ocean 
ecosystems. It supports ecosystem functions such as nutrient cycling, primary production, and 
food web dynamics, all of which are critical to both marine life and human industries. As 
emphasized by WWF and the Living Planet Index, biodiversity is not only about the number of 
species, but also about the variation within species, between species, and among ecosystems 
(WWF, 2024) 
 
Monitoring biodiversity is crucial for understanding the status and trends of ecosystems, 
especially in the context of climate change, habitat loss, and increasing human activity. Effective 
monitoring enables the identification of ecological degradation, the success of restoration or 
mitigation efforts, and the long-term sustainability of resources. 
 
Various international initiatives and regulatory frameworks, including the post-2020 Kunming-
Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, the EU Marine Strategy Framework Directive, and the 
CSRD and TNFD reporting standards, are pushing for clearer, more standardized assessments 
of biodiversity. These frameworks emphasize not just protecting biodiversity, but documenting its 
changes over time with reliable, comparable indicators (WWF, 2022; IUCN, 2022; Newbold et al., 
2016). 
 
For all these reasons, biodiversity monitoring is increasingly seen not only as an ecological 
responsibility, but also as a regulatory necessity and a practical decision-support tool. As 
emphasized in the Living Planet Report (WWF, 2022), the global decline in biodiversity has 
elevated the importance of not only documenting loss, but also of measuring ecological 
improvements. This is particularly relevant in the context of new offshore developments, which 
are now expected to demonstrate positive biodiversity outcomes. Monitoring provides the tools 
needed to detect these changes and assess whether interventions are contributing to ecological 
enhancement (UNEP, 2022). 
 
 
2.2. Why Metrics Matter 
 
Biodiversity metrics play a central role in turning complex ecological data into something 
structured, interpretable, and actionable. Without metrics, biodiversity remains a qualitative 
concept, difficult to communicate or compare. Metrics create a common framework for tracking 
changes, evaluating interventions, and supporting evidence-based decisions in both scientific and 
policy contexts.

From an industry perspective, the importance of biodiversity metrics has only grown. Metrics have 
become not just scientific tools but also reporting tools. With increasing regulatory pressure 
companies must report measurable outcomes. 
 
This emphasis was highlighted during our interview with Lars O. Mortensen from DHI, who 
highlighted that biodiversity monitoring is moving toward a model where developers are expected 
to quantify outcomes of "net positive impact" or ecological enhancement. Moreover, there is 
growing agreement that we cannot wait for perfect standards; instead, scientifically grounded 
metrics should already be used to support robust, defensible reporting. (Schrameyer, 2024). 
Biodiversity indicators provide companies and governments with a framework to align more 
effectively with international conservation targets and standardized reporting schemes 
(Mortensen et al., 2024). 
 
Metrics matter because they provide structure, comparability, and relevance. They allow raw 
observations, like fish passing by a camera, to be transformed into insights that support science, 
regulation, and industry action (Newbold et al., 2016). 
 
 
2.3. Navigating the World of Biodiversity Metrics 
 
Biodiversity metrics are essential, yet navigating the world of biodiversity metrics is far from 
straightforward. In reviewing the available literature and speaking with industry professionals, it 
quickly became clear that there is no single, universal way to measure biodiversity. Instead, 
dozens of possible metrics, each reflecting different aspects, are used depending on context, data 
availability, and monitoring goals. 
 
The challenge lies in navigating this landscape and understanding which metrics are relevant for 
each case. This section reflects the process of exploring that complex space. It combines 
academic research, industry practices, and internal design considerations from Anemo to 
understand how biodiversity is currently assessed and how it can be applied to automated, video-
based monitoring.  
 
 
2.3.1. The Complexity of Measuring Biodiversity 
Biodiversity is a multi-dimensional concept, spanning through many different aspects in a single 
space. It’s an intricate and intangible concept, and many existing frameworks lack detailed 
guidance for how to quantify it meaningfully across different contexts (Mortensen et al., 2024). 
Unlike physical parameters such as temperature or salinity, it cannot be measured with a single 
instrument or summarized with a single number. This makes monitoring complex, as each metric 
captures only a subset of the ecological picture, and the ecological dimensions that define 
biodiversity, such as function, interaction, and resilience, are not always captured by traditional 
indicators. 
This complexity is further amplified in marine environments, where monitoring is logistically 
difficult and direct observations are limited. As a result, marine biodiversity assessments often 
rely on indirect data. The ability to select and apply and interpret the right biodiversity metrics to 
this kind of data requires not only an understanding of ecology, but also of the methods used to 
collect the data, and the limitations that come with them. 
For instance, seeing more fish in a site might look like a good sign, but it doesn’t necessarily 
indicate improved biodiversity. Higher fish abundance can actually reflect reduced ecological 
quality if the increase is due to a few opportunistic species dominating the system (Schrameyer, 
2024). Without understanding species identity, roles, and ecosystem context, metrics like simple 
abundance can easily mislead conclusions. This shows why biodiversity monitoring is not just 
about counting fish, but accounting for a mesh of data inputs that require a thoughtful ecology 
and diversity understanding and interpretation to get meaningful monitoring insights.

In the context of this thesis, understanding this complexity was a necessary first step toward 
identifying which metrics can be meaningfully applied to AI-enhanced underwater video. It also 
made clear that while no single metric can fully represent biodiversity, a carefully selected 
combination can offer powerful ecological insights. 
 
 
2.3.2. Mapping the Metrics Landscape 
 
Given the complexity, navigating biodiversity metrics required a deliberate exploration. Several 
layers of research were undertaken to understand how biodiversity is currently measured, and 
which of those metrics are compatible with Anemo’s technological context. 
 
The academic literature has been a foundational component of this work. Scientific publications 
from general marine-focused biodiversity research, restoration science, AI-based monitoring, 
including contributions from DTU Aqua and international research groups, were analysed. These 
sources offered strong theoretical frameworks and use cases, often targeting specific habitats or 
metric formulations. They also provided practical applications of various metrics relevant to 
studies using underwater video and AI models, ensuring alignment with Anemo’s methods. 
However, it was also observed that the level of detail and specificity common in academic settings 
sometimes proved difficult to translate into practical applications. Real-world monitoring often 
involves time and resource constraints that limit the feasibility of highly granular metric 
formulations. 
 
To understand how biodiversity metrics are applied in practice, an overview of application in 
industry settings was performed from different sources. Publicly available material was collected 
from companies and consultancies involved in biodiversity monitoring, such as DHI, 
NatureMetrics, and WSP. These resources were reviewed to assess how biodiversity indicators 
are described, prioritized, and implemented in applied settings. This was complemented by 
internal project knowledge at Anemo, offering insight into what clients and partners (e.g., 
ECOncrete, DTU Aqua) expect or value in biodiversity data and how they intend to incorporate 
metric-based assessments into their workflows. 
 
To complement these perspectives, an in-depth interview was conducted with Lars O. Mortensen, 
Ph.D. in Environmental Sciences and Principal Scientist in Offshore Biodiversity and Ecology at 
DHI (Danish Hydraulic Institute). This exchange offered valuable context on global trends in 
biodiversity monitoring, industry challenges, and the relevance of automated monitoring tools. 
The discussion also helped validate and challenge elements of the evolving methodology 
developed throughout this project. 
 
In addition, the current regulatory space was reviewed to identify which biodiversity metrics are 
being emphasized or required as part of emerging standards. While this was not the central focus 
of the thesis, it reinforced the need for measurable, structured biodiversity indicators aligned with 
broader environmental reporting frameworks. 
 
 
2.3.3. Overview of Relevant Biodiversity Metrics 
 
2.3.3.1. Literature Insights 
 
A wide range of biodiversity metrics emerged from the academic literature, often reflecting the 
ecological goals, environments, and data collection methods used in each study. These studies 
provided a scientific backbone to this thesis, offering a set of relevant metrics as well as their  
limitations. While the selection and naming of specific metrics varied, certain categories and 
indicators were consistently present across papers and case studies. What follows is a synthesis 
of the most frequently used or discussed biodiversity metric dimensions, based on this literature 
review.

Abundance 
 
Abundance is the most foundational dimensions for measuring marine biodiversity. It reflect how 
many individuals are present in a system, crucial for understanding ecosystem structure, 
productivity, and resource availability. These metrics are especially relevant for underwater video 
data, where individuals can be visually tracked and, in some cases, sized. 
 
True abundance is practically impossible to obtain in marine environments, or any non-controlled 
environment, even using traditional methods like trawling or diver-based surveys. As such, all 
studies reviewed rely on relative abundance metrics that estimate trends or comparative values 
rather than exact counts. Relative abundance is a key component of Biodiversity, which measures 
how common a species is relative to other species of the community. 
 
Several studies based on video monitoring consistently relied on relative abundance metrics like 
MaxN and MeanCount. Both are amongst the most commonly applied abundance tools in and 
are widely adopted in both academic and applied studies due to their compatibility with visual 
data, scalability across time, and their capacity to provide standardized comparisons (Cappo et 
al., 2003; Logan et al., 2017; Baker, R, 2022). 
 
● 
MaxN, the maximum number of individuals of a species observed simultaneously in a 
video frame, was widely used in both academic and applied contexts for its simplicity and 
repeatability (Cappo et al., 2003; Logan et al., 2017). Its main strength lies in its ease of 
computation and low risk of double-counting individuals that move in and out of the field 
of view. However, MaxN is known to be a conservative measure, it avoids overcounting 
but tends to underestimate actual abundance, especially for mobile or schooling species. 
 
● 
MeanCount, on the other hand, involves averaging counts across sampled frames or 
time periods and is often considered a more robust estimate of relative abundance. While 
it introduces more variability due to differences in detectability or sampling conditions, it 
can provide a clearer picture of average species presence when applied thoughtfully. 
Literature suggests that in cases where the goal is to track community shifts over time 
rather than absolute numbers, MeanCount may offer a better signal (Campbell et al., 
2015; Connolly et al., 2024). 
 
● 
In addition to simple counts, several studies emphasized the importance of understanding 
density, the number of individuals per unit area or volume. This metric provides more 
ecological context than raw counts, as it accounts for spatial variation in detectability and 
habitat coverage. In video-based studies, calculating density often requires adjustments 
based on field of view, camera positioning, and visibility (Baker, R, 2022). Common 
approaches include estimating the camera’s observational area using reference scaling, 
stereo-video calibration, or known object sizes within the habitat. 
 
Some studies also proposed the integration of habitat complexity or visibility scores into 
density estimations to adjust for uneven detection rates across environments (Logan et 
al., 2017). For instance, methods that include visual obstruction or structural complexity 
as correction factors provide more realistic density values, especially in reef settings. 
These methods are particularly important in comparing structurally diverse habitats or 
assessing restoration success, where spatial arrangement can heavily influence species 
visibility and detectability. 
 
Collectively, these metrics not only quantify who is present in a habitat but also suggest how well 
those populations are functioning, growing, and contributing to ecological processes. As a result, 
they form a foundational layer in the broader landscape of biodiversity assessment.

Diversity 
 
Diversity is one of the most fundamental concepts in ecology and is often used as a shorthand 
for ecological health, complexity, and stability. In the context of marine biodiversity, diversity 
generally refers to two interlinked ideas: taxonomic diversity, which relates to the number and 
distribution of species; and functional diversity, which describes the range of ecological roles, 
traits, and interactions present in a community. 
 
Diversity, while intuitively appealing, is not a single measurable concept but rather a collection of 
related indicators. Most studies use a combination of taxonomic and functional indicators, 
sometimes augmented with behavioural or interaction-based metrics, to interpret community 
composition and system-level dynamics. 
 
 
Taxonomic Diversity 
 
Taxonomic diversity is the most frequently used category of biodiversity metrics in marine 
monitoring studies. It is built on the identification and enumeration of species, providing a 
snapshot of how many distinct taxa are present in a given system and how they are distributed. 
This form of diversity is widely adopted due to its accessibility, requiring only species-level 
identification, and its broad use in both ecological research and regulatory reporting frameworks. 
Its value lies in offering a foundational measure of biodiversity that is relatively simple to compute 
and widely interpretable. 
 
● 
Its core and simplest metric includes species richness, the number of different species 
observed in a given sample or location. This metric is intuitive, widely understood, and 
simple to compute. However, species richness alone does not account for the relative 
abundance of species. A community dominated by one species and a community where 
all species are equally abundant may have the same richness but differ substantially in 
ecological character. 
 
To address this, studies often rely on diversity indices that incorporate both richness and 
evenness, offering a more comprehensive perspective on community structure. 
 
● 
The Shannon Index is particularly popular in ecological research due to its ability to 
balance the influence of both rare and common species. It is especially useful in 
community comparisons where overall species presence is relatively consistent, but 
abundance distributions vary. Several marine monitoring studies, especially those 
involving restoration sites or artificial reefs, use the Shannon Index to evaluate whether 
ecological interventions lead to more even and complex communities. Its sensitivity to 
rare species makes it effective in detecting early signs of species arrival or turnover 
following habitat enhancement. 
 
● 
The Simpson Index, in contrast, gives more weight to dominant species. This makes it 
particularly robust to sampling noise and ideal for systems where dominant species 
structure ecosystem function. It is frequently applied in long-term monitoring or 
comparative studies across gradients of human impact, where resilience or dominance 
patterns are a focus. Its lower sensitivity to rare species can be seen as a limitation in 
early detection contexts, but a strength in quantifying stable, functional communities.  
 
Both indices are commonly found in literature evaluating artificial structures and reef complexity 
(Torquato et al., 2017; Karwowski et al., 2024), and together they provide complementary 
perspectives on taxonomic diversity. 
 
A number of papers also refer to the Hill numbers framework, which unifies richness, Shannon, 
and Simpson under a single diversity profile that can be interpreted consistently across studies

(Jost et al., 2006). This approach is becoming more common, especially in studies aiming for

Overall, taxonomic diversity metrics remain the most accessible and standardized way to evaluate 
biodiversity, particularly when full trait-level or behaviour-level data is not available. However, 
taxonomic diversity alone may overlook critical functional differences among species, 
underscoring the need for complementary functional diversity metrics in holistic biodiversity 
assessments. 
 
 
Functional Diversity 
 
Functional diversity expands the understanding of biodiversity by focusing on species' roles within 
ecosystems: their behaviours, traits, and interactions that influence how ecosystems function and 
respond to change. These roles govern processes like nutrient cycling, biomass production, 
predation, and resilience to disturbance. Unlike taxonomic diversity, which treats all species as 
equal, functional diversity finds the contribution that each species makes to the ecosystem. 
 
This perspective is particularly powerful in marine environments, where ecosystems are complex 
and rapidly changing. It helps explain why two habitats with the same number of species might 
behave entirely differently, because the composition of ecological functions is not the same. 
Functional diversity is especially relevant for restoration, conservation, and infrastructure projects, 
where the goal is not just to increase species numbers, but to rebuild or enhance ecological 
functions. 
 
In the literature, functional diversity is approached in several ways, each with different levels of 
complexity and data requirements. 
 
● 
Trait-based approaches to functional diversity assess how species differ based on 
biological traits that reflect their ecological roles, such as: feeding mode, locomotion, 
reproductive strategy, and habitat use. These traits serve as proxies for understanding 
how species interact with their environment and contribute to ecosystem functioning. In 
marine ecology, this method is widely used to evaluate ecosystem structure across both 
natural and artificial habitats., and showed particularly useful in studies of artificial reefs 
and offshore platforms (Torquato et al., 2017). 
 
Researchers typically compile trait data from sources like FishBase or SeaLifeBase 
(https://www.fishbase.org/, https://www.sealifebase.org/) or species-level studies, 
focusing on attributes such as body shape, trophic level, and depth range. Species are 
positioned within a multidimensional functional space based on these traits, allowing 
analysis of patterns related to community assembly, resilience, and the effects of 
disturbance.  
 
While trait-based metrics are flexible and powerful, they rely on accurate species 
identification and trait data, which can be difficult to obtain in video-based studies, 
especially in turbid or complex environments. Nonetheless, they remain a vital tool for 
linking species-level observations to broader ecosystem processes. 
 
 
● 
Functional groups is a widely used approach in functional diversity research that 
clusters species into ecological guilds based on shared roles or behaviors, such as 
feeding strategy, mobility, or habitat use. Instead of examining individual traits, this 
method simplifies complexity by focusing on broader ecological functions, commonly 
grouping species as herbivores, piscivores, invertivores, or generalists. This allows 
researchers to assess whether key functional roles are present within a community.

This approach has been applied in studies of offshore platforms and restoration sites, 
where the presence or return of specific functional groups is often used as an indicator of 
ecological integrity or recovery (Karwowski et al., 2024). Functional groups are also well-
suited for applied monitoring because they align closely with ecosystem service goals 
and are easier to communicate to non-specialist stakeholders. As with trait-based 
methods, however, their reliability depends on accurate species identification and a 
strong ecological understanding of the target system. 
 
 
● 
Key functional species are individual species whose presence, absence, or abundance 
signals important ecological conditions or processes. These species often hold 
disproportionate influence in their ecosystems, whether as top predators, habitat 
engineers, or sensitive indicators of change. Unlike trait- or group-based approaches, this 
method focuses on species-specific insights that can reveal habitat quality, trophic 
structure, or resilience with high specificity. 
 
In several monitoring contexts, especially those connected to restoration or infrastructure 
impact assessments, specific species are prioritized for their ecological significance. For 
example, in the Baltic Sea region, the Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) is frequently 
highlighted as a key species due to its role as a top predator and indicator of healthy 
benthic habitats. Experts from DTU Aqua emphasize how the presence of cod on restored 
habitats is interpreted as evidence of ecological success, even when sample sizes were 
limited (DTU Aqua, internal communication, 2024). However, this method requires deep 
ecological knowledge and carries the risk of oversimplifying community assessments by 
focusing too narrowly on a few species. 
 
 
● 
Metric-based indices are quantitative tools used to measure functional diversity within 
a trait-based framework. They are especially valuable when a detailed, statistically 
grounded understanding of community structure and ecosystem function is needed. 
Commonly used indices include Functional Richness (FRic), Functional Dispersion 
(FDis), and Functional Divergence (FDiv), each capturing different aspects of trait 
space. FRic quantifies the total range of functions present, FDis measures the spread of 
traits around a community’s centroid, and FDiv assesses whether extreme traits dominate 
(Torquato et al., 2017). 
 
Another widely applied metric is Rao’s Q, which combines species abundance with 
functional dissimilarity to evaluate the average trait difference between individuals in a 
community (Torquato et al., 2017). This index has proven useful in studies assessing 
restoration success and comparing artificial versus natural reefs (Karwowski et al., 2024).  
 
These metrics help identify whether communities are functionally diverse, dominated by 
specialists, or constrained by habitat filtering. However, they too rely on detailed trait and 
abundance data.  
 
Functional diversity offers a richer, more nuanced understanding of biodiversity that extends well 
beyond species counts. Through traits, functional groups, key species, and statistical indices, it 
allows to evaluate how ecosystems actually work, and whether they are functioning in healthy, 
resilient ways. Especially in marine environments influenced by infrastructure or restoration, this 
lens is critical to understanding not just what is present, but what is possible. 
 
At the same time, functional diversity is not easy to automate. Many of the approaches found in 
the literature require detailed ecological knowledge, robust species-level identification, and 
contextual understanding of the ecosystem in question. This makes functional diversity more 
challenging to implement using generalized AI-based systems or standard video surveys.

Behavioural and Spatial Metrics 
 
Behavioural and spatial metrics represent a rapidly emerging frontier in biodiversity monitoring, 
especially in the context of video-based observation. Unlike taxonomic or functional diversity 
metrics that describe who is present and what traits they bring, behavioural metrics aim to capture 
how species behave and interact with their environment over time. 
 
These types of metrics are historically underused in traditional biodiversity surveys such as diver-
based visual censuses, extractive trawls, or short-term point sampling. The main reason is simple: 
these methods provide only snapshots of biodiversity, missing the dynamic patterns that occur 
on hourly, daily, or seasonal cycles. However, long-term, static underwater video, particularly 
when enhanced with AI detection, has opened the door to observing behavioural and spatial 
patterns at unprecedented resolution. 
 
In the literature, behavioural metrics have been especially useful for understanding species' 
habitat preferences, competition, foraging strategies, and responses to habitat modification or 
restoration (Villnäs et al., 2011; Logan et al., 2017; Connolly et al., 2024). They also contribute 
significantly to assessments of ecosystem function, resilience, and spatial partitioning. As a result, 
they are becoming increasingly relevant in biodiversity studies where infrastructure plays a role 
in shaping marine life. 
 
Anemo's own long-term monitoring setup is particularly well suited to this metric dimension. Its 
ability to record continuously over weeks or months allows for the identification of patterns that 
would otherwise go unnoticed, and creates opportunities to explore biodiversity not just as a static 
measure, but as a dynamic process shaped by space, time, and structure. 
 
 
● 
Diel activity metrics capture how species' behaviour varies between day and night 
cycles. These patterns reflect fundamental ecological processes, such as feeding, 
resting, or predator avoidance, and can vary significantly between species and habitats.  
 
In the literature, diel activity has been used to assess community composition across 
time, and to detect shifts in species behaviour in response to changes in habitat structure 
or external stressors (Connolly et al., 2024). Static video allows for accurate recording of 
these rhythms, especially when recordings are paired with light sensors or time-stamped 
detections. The presence of consistent day-night activity patterns can indicate ecological 
stability, while disruptions may signal anthropogenic disturbance or shifts in trophic 
relationships. 
 
 
● 
Habitat use metrics capture how species interact spatially with their environment. These 
behaviours include vertical distribution in the water column, proximity to habitat features, 
use of specific microhabitats, and movement between impact and control zones. 
 
The literature highlights vertical distribution as a particularly informative metric, with some 
species consistently observed close to substrate while others occupy midwater or pelagic 
zones. These patterns are often linked to feeding strategies, shelter use, or predator 
avoidance (Torquato et al., 2017). Distance-to-structure metrics also provide insight into 
whether species are actively using artificial elements or merely passing through the area. 
For Anemo, these metrics are particularly promising because the camera's fixed position 
makes it well suited to detecting positional trends over time. Zonation frameworks, such 
as dividing the camera field of view into distance bands or vertical layers, can be applied 
to track how different species use space. With enough temporal coverage, this can yield 
insights into how habitat use changes across seasons, tidal cycles, or developmental 
stages.

Behavioural and spatial metrics provide a unique window into the dynamic aspects of marine 
biodiversity. They go beyond static species inventories to reveal how organisms move, interact, 
and respond to habitat features and temporal cycles. Enabled by long-term video data, these 
metrics are especially valuable in marine environments shaped by infrastructure or restoration 
efforts, where behaviour can reveal more than species counts alone. 
 
 
Productivity and Growth Metrics 
 
Productivity and growth metrics aim to capture the capacity of marine ecosystems to generate 
and sustain life over time. These metrics go beyond simply identifying which species are present. 
They provide insights into how populations grow, reproduce, and contribute to energy transfer 
across trophic levels. In other words, they reflect not just the structure of ecosystems, but their 
function and vitality. 
 
These indicators are commonly used in fisheries science, restoration ecology, and increasingly 
in marine biodiversity monitoring, particularly when it comes to assessing the ecological impact 
or success of infrastructure interventions. Metrics such as biomass production, size structure, 
juvenile recruitment, and energy flow are especially valuable because they offer a quantifiable 
way to evaluate outcomes (Gillanders et al., 2020; Connolly et al., 2024).  
 
They help bridge the gap between biodiversity enhancement and measurable return on 
investment (ROI). In fact, this was one of the most compelling aspects of these metrics during 
this thesis: their potential to help stakeholders justify and evaluate the effectiveness of nature-
based solutions and ecological restoration. 
 
While fewer studies have applied these metrics to passive video monitoring directly, the 
theoretical foundation is strong, and examples like those from Connolly et al. (2024) show how 
these concepts are increasingly being adapted to automated and visual-based biodiversity tools. 
 
● 
Biomass and growth-based metrics provide insight into the amount of living biological 
material in an ecosystem and how it changes over time. These metrics are foundational 
in understanding ecosystem productivity, particularly in restoration and habitat 
enhancement contexts where demonstrating biological gains is key. 
 
Standing biomass is typically calculated using species-specific length–weight 
relationships, often sourced from databases. In video-based studies, especially those 
employing stereo cameras or calibrated scaling, it is possible to estimate the size of 
individual fish and extrapolate their weight. This allows for the calculation of total biomass 
per unit area, an important indicator of ecosystem output. 
 
In restoration-focused literature, biomass production is frequently used to assess 
ecological performance over time. For example, Connolly et al. (2024) quantified the 
biomass produced by fish communities on restored shellfish reefs, capturing both 
standing biomass and the annual growth of individuals. This metric helps move beyond 
simple presence or abundance, offering a functional understanding of how much 
biological energy is being generated by a system. 
 
● 
Recruitment and size structure metrics offer a view into the population dynamics and 
regeneration capacity of marine communities. It helps assess how populations are 
replenishing, how age classes are distributed, and whether a habitat supports long-term 
ecosystem function. 
 
Recruitment metrics focus on the earliest stages of life, tracking the presence and survival 
of juvenile individuals. These are particularly valuable in restoration and impact studies, 
where juvenile presence can signal habitat suitability and potential for recovery. High 
juvenile abundance is often interpreted as a sign of successful reproduction, favourable

environmental conditions, or habitat connectivity. For example, Connolly et al. (2024) 
used juvenile density and biomass to assess fish productivity on restored shellfish reefs, 
revealing strong early-stage ecosystem performance. 
 
Size structure, meanwhile, provides a snapshot of how individuals are distributed across 
age or life stages. Healthy populations typically show a spectrum of sizes, from juveniles 
to large adults, indicating stable recruitment and low fishing or predation pressure.  
 
Productivity and growth metrics offer a functional lens on marine biodiversity, connecting 
monitoring efforts with ecosystem performance and long-term value. While they require more 
advanced tools, these metrics will become very powerful when Anemo integrates stereo cameras 
into its systems, unlocking accurate length estimation and enabling broader application of growth 
and productivity assessments. 
 
 
Habitat Context Metrics 
 
The physical and environmental conditions of a habitat directly influence which species are 
present, how they behave, and how ecosystems function. Metrics that reflect habitat 
characteristics, such as structural complexity, substrate type, light conditions, temperature, and 
visibility, are therefore essential components of biodiversity assessment. They provide context for 
interpreting biological data and can also act as independent indicators of habitat quality or stress. 
 
In marine environments, these environmental and structural metrics are frequently used to 
evaluate the suitability of habitats for supporting diverse and functional fish communities. From 
natural reefs to artificial substrates, subtle differences in habitat composition or complexity can 
lead to major changes in species richness, abundance, or behaviour. 
 
● 
Structural complexity metrics describe the physical arrangement and variability of the 
habitat. This complexity can be natural or artificial, such as artificial reefs. Greater 
complexity is often associated with higher biodiversity because it creates more ecological 
niches. 
 
In restoration and artificial reef studies, researchers frequently use measures such as 
substrate rugosity, percent macroalgae cover, or the ratio of soft to hard surfaces to 
quantify complexity. For instance, Casabona et al. (2024) correlated macroalgae cover 
with the presence of Atlantic cod, suggesting that increased vegetative complexity may 
enhance habitat suitability for certain functional species.  
 
 
● 
Environmental parameters such as temperature, salinity, visibility, and light availability 
are key drivers of marine biodiversity. These factors influence species distribution, 
physiological performance, reproductive timing, and habitat preference. As such, they are 
frequently used as explanatory variables in ecological models and biodiversity 
assessments. 
 
In several marine monitoring studies, temperature has been identified as one of the most 
important variables shaping fish assemblages (Casabona et al. 2024). DTU Aqua 
routinely incorporates temperature monitoring in reef studies to interpret shifts in species 
presence, particularly for cold-water species such as cod. 
 
Visibility and turbidity are also important, especially in the context of underwater video. 
Low visibility reduces detectability of individuals, but also influences species behaviour 
and habitat use. Metrics like functional visibility (e.g., maximum distance at which fish are 
identifiable) and turbidity index are used in some studies to normalize video-based 
observations across sites or sampling events.

2.3.3.2. Industry Insights 
 
While biodiversity metrics have a rich academic foundation, their application in real-world projects 
often differs in scope, complexity, and intent. In industry, the need for actionable, scalable, and 
communicable insights often outweighs the demand for academic precision. As Anemo Robotics 
moves from technical feasibility to commercial implementation, understanding how biodiversity is 
currently measured and used in practice becomes essential. 
 
This section presents an overview of how biodiversity metrics are used across the private sector, 
with a focus on three domains: Environmental consultancies building standardized metrics; 
Biodiversity data companies; and direct collaborators and clients. These different perspectives 
are informed by public documentation, internal knowledge from Anemo projects, and direct 
conversations, including an interview with Lars O Mortensen, Ph.D. in Environmental Sciences 
and Principal Scientist in Offshore Biodiversity and Ecology at DHI (Danish Hydraulic Institute). 
 
 
Consultancies and Frameworks for Biodiversity Standardization 
 
Environmental consultancies play a central role in assessing biodiversity for offshore 
infrastructure projects such as wind farms and restoration initiatives. These firms are responsible 
for designing and conducting environmental monitoring campaigns, analysing collected data, and 
producing standardized reports that guide infrastructure design and evaluate project success. In 
recent years, some environmental consultancies have made major efforts to develop scalable 
biodiversity assessment frameworks that go beyond one-off site surveys. 
 
In Denmark, consultancy companies such as DHI  (https://www.dhigroup.com/) and WSP 
(https://www.wsp.com/) have responded by building structured approaches to biodiversity metrics 
that simplify complexity without sacrificing ecological meaning. This motivation was echoed in an 
interview with Lars from DHI, who noted that all consultancies are actively trying to create 
approaches that make biodiversity more operational: "Everyone (all environmental consultancies) 
wants to do something in that direction”. 
 
According to Lars, this shift gained momentum in the last couple of years, particularly as 
biodiversity has become a more prominent theme in offshore infrastructure planning. This 
increasing focus is not only driven by regulation, but also by industry leadership, such as Ørsted’s 
development of a Net Positive Biodiversity Framework (Ørsted, 2024), a trend followed by all 
the major offshore operators, with most of them setting internal goals of being Biodiversity Net 
Positive by 2030. 
 
For consultancies like DHI, this presents both a challenge and an opportunity. All major 
companies set goals towards Biodiversity Positiveness, but none of them has indices on how to 
measure it, since it is not part of the regular Environmental Impact Assessments (L. Mortensen, 
interview, 2025). The challenge comes to create biodiversity metric frameworks that are 
scientifically reliable with statistical structured methods, but also compatible with commercial 
project planning and stakeholder expectation. 
 
It is important to point out that while this tools and systems are getting importance and 
prioritisation across these consultancies and the sector, they are still a work in progress and not 
publicly available yet, shifting and adapting to the market, still figuring out what is relevant for 
Biodiversity Positive measurement. It also represents an opportunity for Anemo to join some of 
the developments to integrate the technology as relevant data acquisitions for the tools.

DHI: Biodiversity State Indicator 
 
DHI (Danish Hydraulic Institute) is an international independent research and consultancy 
organization specializing in water environments. With a strong foundation in hydrodynamic 
modelling and environmental engineering, DHI works across fields such as coastal infrastructure, 
marine ecosystems, water quality, and resource management. DHI is one of the leading 
environmental consultancies working in offshore infrastructure and marine biodiversity, has also 
been a close collaborator with Anemo Robotics. 
 
Over the past years, DHI has been among the first in the sector to actively develop structured 
approaches to biodiversity monitoring that respond to growing industry demand. Their work goes 
beyond traditional impact assessments, aiming to create standardized, comparable, and scalable 
biodiversity metrics that can be applied across wind farm sites, restoration projects, and nature-
inclusive designs. 
 
DHI has responded to this challenge by developing a structured, multi-dimensional framework for 
biodiversity assessment, culminating in the Biodiversity State Indicator (BSI). The BSI emerged 
from a gap in available biodiversity metrics for marine infrastructure. As Lars explained: “Nobody 
really knew how Biodiversity Net Positiveness was measured, because that’s not what you do in 
your own impact assessments, you monitor for negative consequences”, “There was a metric 
lagging, and we used that” (L. Mortensen, interview, 2025). 
 
The BSI was designed to fill this gap by integrating different ecological dimensions into one metric. 
Lars elaborated: “The theory behind it was that we could create a metric that could embrace other 
dimensions of the ecosystem, such as functional diversity, interactions between species, and, of 
course, the species themselves. But maybe in one metric and measure it by that.” Importantly, 
the BSI is not based on new or experimental formulas; it uses established ecological indices, 
structured into a new framework that allows for broader interpretation and standardized reporting. 
 
DHI’s Biodiversity State Indicator (BSI) is built around three core pillars: 
 
- 
Taxonomic Diversity – Measures species richness and distribution using classical 
ecological indices such as species richness, Simpson, Shannon, and Pielou’s 
Evenness. These metrics provide a snapshot of community composition and dominance 
structures. 
 
- 
Functional Diversity – Focuses on the ecological roles and traits that describe the 
species contained in the ecosystem. Metrics used here include Community Weighted 
Mean (CWM), Functional Richness, Evenness, Divergence, Dispersion, and Rao’s 
Q. The indices considered for this pillar can give information about the productivity and 
stability of ecosystem processes. These allow DHI to assess not just who is present, but 
what functions they perform.  
 
- 
Interactive Diversity – Captures species relationships and dependencies. This includes 
more advanced ecological network metrics like Trophic Guild Ratios, Adjacency 
Analysis, Connection Ratio, Network Density, and Average Path Length, all 
indicators of ecosystem complexity and resilience. 
 
Each of these pillars is computed using key data inputs: species identity, abundance, and 
monitoring station designation (e.g., control vs. impact). These values are then normalized 
and compared to referenced conditions or target states, producing a Performance Indicator. 
 
A critical component of enabling these metrics in practice is access to high-quality trait data, 
especially regarding roles and functionality of species in ecosystems. As Lars noted in the 
interview, DHI is actively working on building a trait database tailored to the species most 
frequently encountered in their marine monitoring work. This involves collecting, validating, and

used in metric calculations.  
 
 
WSP: Biodiversity Monitoring Standard 
 
WSP is a prominent environmental consultancy working across Europe with a growing focus on 
biodiversity monitoring in marine infrastructure. Their approach has emerged from years of 
experience delivering assessments for coastal developments, offshore construction, and habitat 
enhancement initiatives. 
 
This ambition is most clearly reflected in their ongoing development a national biodiversity 
monitoring standard being designed to support restoration and offshore initiatives throughout 
Denmark, for which Anemo has been asked to participate. 
 
The initiative surges from the lack of standardized biodiversity scores on marine ecosystems. 
While on land a biodiversity score has been developed for companies to report on the ESG targets 
in the green accounts, there is currently no standardized biodiversity score for marine biodiversity, 
limiting companies to choose investments in marine biodiversity. 
 
The main barriers to more corporate investment in marine biodiversity are:  
- 
Lack of standard for reporting marine biodiversity: Common prerequisite to 
document that biodiversity projects can be remunerated, and thus whether companies 
choose at investing such. 
- 
Measurements of effects on marine ecosystems are often expensive.  
- 
Consulting firms can rarely point to concrete projects in the sea, without special 
preliminary investigations being carried out. Many private actors want specific project 
sketches presented, before they consider supporting marine projects.  
- 
Missing catalogue of concrete project types and their possibility to be included in the 
reporting of ESG targets.  
 
On a European level, development of a marine biodiversity score is also very relevant, since this 
is EU legislation on green accounts. 
 
The initiative of the company focuses on using functional species groups and key indicator 
species, particularly cod and macroalgae, as proxies for ecological condition and restoration 
success. As noted in internal meetings, the goal is not just to count species, but to understand 
how ecosystems function and whether restoration efforts produce meaningful biological change. 
 
The goal is to create a cost-effective and standardized framework for biodiversity impact 
assessment, especially suitable for restoration projects and biodiversity positive goals. The 
project’s scope includes both developing a scoring system for biodiversity quality and determining 
the minimum monitoring effort required to produce robust conclusions. 
 
One key element in the methodology is the focus on functional biodiversity over rare species 
inventories. Functional groups of macroalgae are categorized based on their ecological roles and 
morphology. On the fish side, Atlantic cod is treated as a primary indicator species due to its 
ecological and socio-economic relevance in Danish waters. Several internal conversations 
emphasized the importance of behavioural insights, such as how long cod spend in restored 
habitats, as a potential indicator of habitat preference and functionality. 
 
At the heart of the framework is a quality score, designed to be reported either as a 1-to-5 scale 
or via categorical descriptors of habitat status. This score is not a raw biodiversity index, but rather 
a proxy for habitat quality and ecological function, supported by underlying metrics such as MaxN 
counts of indicator species, macroalgae coverage, and functional group presence.

Regulatory Context and Drivers 
 
A key reason such consultancies are investing in the standardization of marine biodiversity 
metrics is the increasing demand for credible reporting frameworks, not only from clients and 
investors, but also due to growing regulatory momentum. These frameworks are quickly becoming 
foundational for how nature-based projects are measured, compared, and justified. While interest 
in biodiversity reporting has grown over the past decade, there is now a marked shift toward 
mandatory disclosure and standardized methods, especially in Europe, the UK, and Australia 
(Schrameyer, 2024). 
 
The following initiatives represent the most relevant regulatory and policy frameworks driving 
biodiversity metric development (Schrameyer, 2024): 
 
- 
CSRD (EU Directive – ESRS E4): A mandatory corporate sustainability directive for 
companies operating in the EU. From 2025, biodiversity and ecosystem impacts will need 
to be reported and tied to financial performance. 
 
- 
EU Taxonomy: A classification tool that defines criteria for sustainable economic 
activities. It is particularly relevant for infrastructure and development projects claiming 
environmental benefits. 
 
- 
EU Nature Restoration Law: A central pillar of the EU Biodiversity Strategy requiring 
each member state to submit restoration plans by mid-2026, with measurable progress 
toward protecting and restoring 30% of marine and terrestrial ecosystems by 2030. 
 
- 
TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures): A voluntary framework, 
gaining traction as a tool for assessing nature-related risks and guiding companies in 
aligning with EU regulations. 
 
- 
Global Reporting Initiative (GRI 101): A more robust follow-up to GRI 304, focused 
specifically on biodiversity loss and impact. It encourages structured reporting across 
sectors and geographies. 
 
- 
ISO TC 331: An international technical standard under development, with specific 
working groups exploring metrics for marine biodiversity assessment. 
 
- 
The Biodiversity Plan (Convention on Biological Diversity): Requires all signatory 
countries to develop national biodiversity strategies and action plans, creating 
downstream expectations for industry participation and reporting. 
 
Together, these frameworks are actively influencing how biodiversity projects are funded, 
implemented, and measured. For consultancies, developing tools for biodiversity measurement 
standardization is a way to stay ahead of regulatory requirements while also creating practical 
instruments for clients to use in ESG disclosure and performance tracking.

As biodiversity becomes a critical reporting and investment domain, companies are seeking 
scalable, standardized methods to quantify nature-related impacts. In recent years, there has 
been a rapid rise in companies offering biodiversity data services aimed at supporting 
environmental reporting, conservation efforts, and ESG compliance. These providers often 
position themselves as enablers of "nature-positive" action, offering standardized biodiversity

metrics that are accessible to businesses, developers, and governments. Among the leaders in 
this space is NatureMetrics (https://www.naturemetrics.com/), a UK-based company that 
specializes in biodiversity monitoring through environmental DNA (eDNA) sampling. 
 
eDNA has emerged as one of the dominant technologies in this landscape because of its ability 
to detect species presence quickly and across wide spatial scales without the need for visual 
observation or extensive fieldwork. However, as we will explore, while eDNA and services like 
those from NatureMetrics offer important advantages, they also come with specific limitations, 
particularly when it comes to capturing ecosystem functionality, behavioural dynamics, and 
habitat usage. 
 
Deeping into NatureMetrics services, the company offers a suite of biodiversity metrics derived 
from eDNA samples, which are collected from water, soil, or sediment and analysed to detect the 
presence of species. Their service portfolio includes several high-level diversity indicators: 
 
- 
Species Richness: The total number of species detected in a sample. This is one of the 
most straightforward biodiversity metrics and is used to track changes in species 
presence over time. 
 
- 
Food Chain Integrity: Assesses the presence of higher trophic level species to infer the 
completeness of the food web. The return of top predators is treated as a positive 
indicator of ecological recovery. 
 
- 
Evolutionary Diversity: Measures the variety of species types present in a community, 
often interpreted as a proxy for ecosystem integrity and resilience. Higher scores suggest 
greater functional redundancy and robustness against environmental change. 
 
- 
Commercial Value: Evaluates the economic importance of fish species detected, 
offering insights into the potential commercial or conservation value of an area. 
 
- 
Prevalence of Sensitive Species: Measures the proportion of species considered 
vulnerable to human disturbance, serving as an indicator of environmental health. 
 
The standardized outputs are positioned to align with ESG reporting needs, restoration project 
tracking, and biodiversity net gain initiatives. 
 
While NatureMetrics' approach offers significant strengths, particularly in scalability and ease of 
sampling, it also has notable limitations. eDNA methods primarily capture species presence or 
absence, without providing information on behaviour, habitat use, or even abundance. 
Furthermore, detection probabilities can vary depending on environmental conditions, species 
shedding rates, and sampling methods, introducing layers of uncertainty that must be carefully 
interpreted. 
 
All metrics presented by NatureMetrics, while relevant for initial and repetitive biodiversity 
monitoring, are based in a basic interpretation of the species found in their samples. From only 
one true primary data source, species presence, they express a series of metrics extracted 
through their databases and biology expertise.

Comparison to Standard Methods: ECOncrete benchmarks its ecological concrete

2.4. Selecting the Right Metrics for Anemo 
 
After carefully reviewing a set of different Biodiversity Metrics and how they are applied to 
research and industry, an initial design of how they should be applied to Anemo Robotics data is 
performed, focusing in the relevance of the metrics and the capabilities of Anemo’s technology.  
 
Considering a realistic scope of this thesis, a 3-phased approach was designed for an escalated 
implementation of the metrics into Anemo’s data.  
 
The selection of metrics for this thesis carefully balances three key factors: (i) biological relevance, 
(ii) feasibility within Anemo’s current data structure and processing capabilities, and (iii) the 
practical constraints of the project’s timeline and scope. The phased approach ensures a 
progressive integration of increasingly complex ecological indicators as Anemo's internal 
capabilities and hardware evolve. 
 
 
Phase 1 – Initial Metrics: Implemented in This Thesis 
The first phase focuses on metrics that are immediately implementable on existing Anemo 
datasets. These metrics are chosen for their strong ecological relevance and straightforward data 
requirements, allowing the project to deliver direct biodiversity insights from the current 
deployments. 
 
- 
Taxonomic Biodiversity Metrics 
- 
Abundance Metrics: MaxN, MeanCount, Frequency of Occurrence, and Density 
estimates, using area calibrations where possible. 
- 
Diversity Metrics (Hill Numbers Framework): Including Species Richness (q=0), 
Shannon Diversity (q=1), and Simpson Diversity (q=2), providing complementary 
views of biodiversity sensitivity to commonness and rarity. 
 
- 
Behavioural Biodiversity Metrics 
- 
Activity Patterns: Assessment of diel (day–night) and seasonal changes in fish 
assemblages. 
- 
Habitat Use Patterns: Analyses of fish proximity to structures and vertical 
distributions in the water column. 
- 
Control–Impact Comparisons: When control zones are available, relative 
comparisons can be drawn between impacted artificial reef areas and natural 
controls.

Phase 1 – Initial Metrics 
 
The initial metrics chosen for this thesis are well-suited to the available data and are easy to 
implement, given Anemo's technological capabilities. These metrics are fundamental and 
essential for biodiversity assessments, commonly used in various biodiversity monitoring projects 
within both industry and academia, particularly in many of DTU Aqua's restoration studies, which 
Anemo collaborates with and bases most of the Biological work. Additionally, the selected metrics 
align well with indicators from DHI, WSP, and NatureMetrics, making integration and comparison 
straightforward. 
 
 
Taxonomic Biodiversity Metrics 
 
Taxonomic metrics form the foundation of any biodiversity monitoring effort. They quantify 
biodiversity by identifying and classifying organisms based on their species identity, providing the 
most direct and interpretable view of community structure. These metrics allow for comparisons 
across space, time, and habitat types, and are essential for tracking ecological changes. 
 
 
Abundance Metrics 
 
Abundance is the metric that best matches Anemo’s technological strengths. The combination of 
underwater video and automated AI-based detections is particularly well-suited for generating 
standardized counts of fish. Relative abundance metrics are among the most widely used 
indicators in marine biodiversity monitoring programs, and represent a base for most of the 
assessments. It is also the one of the main metrics used across DTU Aqua’s restoration studies. 
Additionally, client feedback has consistently highlighted a strong interest in straightforward 
metrics like, making abundance an ideal early focus for delivering immediate and practical 
insights.

● 
MaxN was selected as a core abundance metric because it perfectly matches the 
technical strengths of Anemo’s current setup: fixed-position underwater cameras 
combined with AI-based detections. Its simplicity ensures fast processing and 
comparability across sites and time, which is essential for building a scalable monitoring 
system. Furthermore, MaxN is heavily used by DTU Aqua and international partners in 
restoration monitoring, ensuring Anemo’s outputs can be directly benchmarked against 
industry-standard datasets and methodologies. 
 
● 
MeanCount was selected as a complementary abundance metric to MaxN because it 
captures additional information about species persistence and regularity of presence 
throughout video recordings. This metric is particularly useful for differentiating between 
species that appear frequently but in small numbers versus species that may only trigger 
a MaxN spike. Including MeanCount improves the sensitivity of Anemo’s biodiversity 
assessments without significantly increasing computational complexity, especially when 
visibility conditions allow reliable frame sampling. 
 
● 
Frequency of Occurrence was chosen for its robustness and its ability to provide 
valuable insights even in videos with varying quality or limited field of view. FoO ensures 
that presence/absence trends remain reliable, and is the most basic approach used 
currently in many monitoring projects. 
 
 
Diversity Metrics 
 
Diversity metrics provide insight not only into how many species are present, but also into how 
individuals are distributed across species, offering important signals about ecosystem health and 
stability. They are simple to compute from taxonomic data but provide powerful foundations for 
interpreting ecological change. Diversity metrics form the essential baseline for more advanced 
biodiversity assessments, including functional diversity and resilience analyses that Anemo may 
pursue in the future. 
 
In this thesis, diversity metrics are framed within the Hill Numbers framework, a widely accepted 
system that unifies different measures of biodiversity under a single conceptual approach. Hill 
Numbers express diversity as the "effective number of species," meaning the number of equally 
abundant species that would be needed to produce the observed level of diversity. This 
interpretation makes biodiversity values more intuitive and directly comparable across studies. 
 
The strength of the Hill Numbers framework lies in its ability to adjust the sensitivity of the diversity 
measure depending on the parameter q: 
 
● 
Species Richness (q = 0) was selected because it provides the most direct and intuitive 
measure of biodiversity. It simply counts the number of different species observed. It is 
widely used in restoration ecology as an understandable indicator of habitat health. 
Richness is fast to compute from Anemo’s video outputs, making it ideal for scalable 
reporting across multiple sites and time periods. 
 
● 
The Shannon index (q = 1) not only how many species are present, but also how 
individuals are distributed among those species. This metric aligns with standard 
biodiversity assessment protocols used internationally and allows Anemo to deliver more 
nuanced insights about habitat quality and ecological resilience. 
 
● 
The Simpson index (q = 2) complements Richness and Shannon by emphasizing 
dominant species and reducing the weight of rare species. It provides a stability-oriented 
view of the community, which is particularly important in early stages of reef colonization 
and restoration projects. By incorporating Simpson, Anemo ensures that biodiversity 
==PAGE:29==

29 
assessments can address both species richness and community dominance, offering a 
fuller ecological interpretation to clients and partners. 
● 
At q = ∞, the Hill Number reduces to the inverse of the Berger-Parker dominance 
index, focusing entirely on the most abundant species and ignoring all others. Although 
this extreme case is rarely used in practical biodiversity monitoring, it provides theoretical 
insight into dominance patterns within a community. In this thesis, q = ∞ is acknowledged 
for completeness but is not computed routinely, as the focus remains on q = 0, 1, and 2 
for ecological interpretation. 
 
 
Behavioural Metrics 
 
Behavioural metrics offer important insights into how different species are using the artificial 
structures over time. These metrics capture ecological processes that static species lists cannot 
detect, such as daily activity patterns, habitat preferences, and responses to habitat interventions. 
Incorporating behavioural indicators is particularly important for Anemo, as one of the key selling 
points of underwater video technology is the ability to non-invasively observe natural fish 
behaviours. Behavioural metrics not only strengthen biodiversity assessments but also add a 
functional dimension to evaluating structures impact. 
 
● 
Diel activity patterns provide insights into how fish communities vary between day and 
night periods, an aspect particularly well captured by continuous underwater video 
monitoring. Many marine species display strong diel behaviours which are critical for 
understanding ecosystem functionality. Detecting diel shifts is important for Anemo to 
fully document biodiversity use of the structures across time, not just during daylight 
hours. Analysing activity patterns is low-cost to implement once video timestamps are 
available, making it an efficient behavioural layer to add to Anemo’s biodiversity 
assessments. 
 
● 
Seasonal presence trends provide insights into how fish communities change over 
longer timescales, influenced by environmental cycles, reproductive behaviour, and 
species-specific features. For Anemo, detecting these temporal patterns helps assess 
how artificial structures are used throughout different stages of the year, and whether 
their ecological function persists across seasons. 
 
● 
Environmental correlation metrics evaluate how species presence and abundance 
respond to external physical variables such as temperature, salinity, and light availability. 
These metrics are valuable for distinguishing between species responding to habitat 
structure versus broader environmental conditions. In the context of Anemo, they help 
clarify whether observed biodiversity changes are due to the artificial structure itself or 
external environmental drivers. 
 
● 
Species co-occurrence metrics assess how often different species appear together 
within the same sampling unit. These patterns provide insight into community structure, 
potential species interactions, and shared habitat preferences. For Anemo, co-
occurrence metrics offer a way to go beyond individual species observations and assess 
broader ecological assemblages. This is particularly relevant for understanding how 
artificial structures may facilitate or alter existing community dynamics. 
 
● 
Habitat use metrics capture how fish interact spatially with the structures. Whether they 
stay close to the reef, use vertical space differently, or occupy surrounding open areas. 
This information is essential for understanding the ecological role of structures, beyond 
simple species presence. This represents one of the unique aspects of Anemo’s 
technology, seen shortly in literature due technological constraints, it can give biologists 
a vital tool for understanding species interactions and behaviours, and to capture how 
species use the structures, whether are being used as shelter, feeding grounds, or transit 
corridors.

● 
Control–impact comparisons allow direct evaluation of how structures influence local 
biodiversity compared to nearby unaltered habitats. By comparing fish activity and 
abundance in impact zones (artificial reefs) versus control zones (natural seabed), 
Anemo can generate strong, evidence-based assessments of ecological enhancement. 
This approach is widely used in restoration ecology and is expected by many clients and 
regulatory frameworks when evaluating habitat interventions. 
 
 
Phase 2 – Secondary Metrics 
 
Phase 2 centres around the integration of functional biodiversity metrics, which are arguably the 
most important indicators for detecting meaningful changes in ecosystem health and function. 
Unlike purely taxonomic measures, functional metrics assess how species traits contribute to 
ecological processes. They provide insights into whether an ecosystem is resilient, redundant, or 
vulnerable to disturbances, offering a much deeper understanding structure impact. Including 
functional biodiversity is increasingly recognized as best practice in restoration monitoring and 
marine spatial planning. 
 
Despite their high relevance, functional biodiversity metrics are currently out of scope for this 
thesis. Implementing them requires detailed biological trait data for each observed species, as 
well as significant ecological expertise to properly interpret functional group structures and 
community dynamics. Given the limited time frame, focus on initial biodiversity baselines, and the 
current resources available, this level of ecological analysis would not be feasible within the scope 
of this project. 
 
However, the thesis design intentionally prepares Anemo for future integration of functional 
biodiversity. By structuring species observation outputs in a standardized and clean format, 
Anemo’s datasets will be ready to connect with trait databases to output functional metrics on 
biodiversity. In particular, DHI are building a comprehensive marine functional trait database, 
which will enable seamless matching between Anemo’s taxonomic data and trait-based functional 
classifications.  
 
 
Phase 3 – Hardware-Dependent Metrics 
 
Phase 3 focuses on productivity metrics that will become available once Anemo’s hardware 
advances, particularly through the integration of stereo camera systems. Stereo vision technology 
enables accurate measurements of fish size, a critical step for estimating biomass, size structure, 
recruitment rates, and energy flow within reef ecosystems. These metrics are highly valued for 
assessing ecosystem productivity and sustainability, offering a powerful extension beyond 
traditional abundance or diversity measures. 
 
Currently, precise size-based metrics are not feasible with Anemo’s existing camera setups, 
which lack the calibrated depth perception required for reliable fish length measurements. 
Although preliminary observations on fish size distributions can be made visually, assessments 
on biomass and fish production require the depth-resolving capabilities that only stereo cameras 
provide. For this reason, metrics like individual size, biomass, production modelling, and juvenile 
recruitment tracking are deferred to a future phase once hardware upgrades are implemented. 
 
However, Anemo's ongoing camera development roadmap already includes plans for integrating 
stereo capabilities. Once available, size data can be directly linked to growth models and 
ecological production functions, allowing Anemo to deliver advanced indicators such as total 
biomass per area, size class distributions, recruitment monitoring, and ecosystem energy transfer 
estimates. These metrics will significantly enhance the biological value of Anemo’s monitoring 
programs, opening the door to productivity-based KPIs and more advanced ecological reporting 
for clients and research collaborators.

3. Applying Metrics to the MOSAR Project 
With the selection of biodiversity metrics completed, the next step is to put them into practice. The 
MOSAR Project offers an ideal opportunity to do so, providing structured, high-quality underwater 
video data collected specifically to track biodiversity changes around artificial reef modules. This 
chapter marks the transition from theoretical design to real-world application, using the selected 
Phase 1 metrics to evaluate how fish communities interact with newly deployed structures. It 
serves as the first test of how Anemo’s monitoring tools perform when applied to real project 
conditions. 
 
 
3.1. Overview of the MOSAR Project 
 
The MOSAR Project ("Monitoring Of Small Artificial Reefs in a Danish Harbour") is a 
collaborative initiative aimed at addressing the degradation of marine ecosystems in Denmark. 
The project brings together three main partners: ReefCircular, Anemo Robotics, and DTU 
Aqua, and is sponsored by the Karl Pedersen Fond from Dansk Industri. Each of the partners 
contributed their expertise to design, deploy, and monitor innovative artificial reef solutions in a 
Danish Harbour.  
 
The primary goal of MOSAR is to construct sustainable reef modules from biodegradable 
composites and bioconcrete, deploy them in degraded marine environments, and monitor their 
effectiveness in enhancing biodiversity over a full year of field observation with Anemo Robotics’ 
automated video technology. Putting to test both reef and monitoring technologies, on real 
conditions and settings for the first time. 
 
In the summer of 2024, reef modules were deployed in the Port of Hundested, at DHI’s facilities, 
alongside underwater camera systems developed by Anemo Robotics. Three cameras were 
installed: two adjacent to reef modules and one at a control site without artificial structures. Anemo 
Robotics is responsible for ongoing data collection and system maintenance, while also leading 
the development of AI-based species identification models, with biological validation support from 
DTU Aqua. 
 
A total of 330 hours of usable footage was collected throughout the period between July 2024 
and February 2025. 
 
A set of different models was trained from a dataset of 5997 labelled frames extracted from 
footage recorded between July 2024 and October 2024. The dataset used is labelled by two 
marine Biologist from DTU Aqua, within 10 different species classes.  
 
The structured setup of MOSAR data provides an ideal opportunity to apply the Phase 1 
biodiversity metrics selected earlier, testing their performance under real-world conditions while 
benefiting from a controlled experimental design.

the data was generated, collected, processed, and prepared for analysis. This section outlines 
the full experimental and technical context behind the MOSAR monitoring activities. From camera 
deployment and data collection protocols to the training and development of AI-based detection 
models.

Initially, two kind of structures from ReefCircular were installed: ReefTiles attached to the quay

Cameras were initially placed at fixed positions and angles to ensure consistent field-of-view 
coverage across monitoring periods. However, given that the cameras were battery-powered and 
required retrieval and redeployment every two months for maintenance and data collection, 
maintaining exact consistency in framing across deployments proved challenging. Small shifts in 
camera position and orientation were inevitable, particularly under changing sea conditions, 
manual handling and no visual confirmation of the Field of View (FOV) of the cameras.  
 
To improve consistency, starting from the second deployment onwards, divers were used during 
installation to manually adjust and optimize camera positioning relative to the reef modules as 
accurately as possible. Even with diver support, it was not possible to fully replicating the exact 
camera position and field of view. Divers did not have direct feedback on the camera view 
underwater, and positioning relied on approximate external references.  
 
In addition, environmental factors like currents and harbour conditions occasionally shifted the 
cameras after deployments. Starting in November 2024, additional weights were added to the 
camera systems to improve stability and reduce drift between deployments. Due to strong 
currents and other circumstances, the dataset contains some gaps and inconsistencies. In

Experimental Setup and Recording Settings 
 
Three specific locations were chosen for the Boulder Reef experiment, at the bottom of the sea 
bed along the quay walls at DHI’s premises, following a basic Control and Impact study. The three 
locations were separated by 20 meters each, and had all similar conditions: Placed on Hard 
sandy bottom, at 1 meter from the quay wall, 8 meters deep. The three locations were 
separated by one control and two impact locations. Impact locations had five Boulder Reefs from 
ReefCircular anchored on the sea bed, and the control location had no artificial structure. 
 
The monitoring was performed by three battery-powered AnemoCam cameras from Anemo 
Robotics, with the labels Anemo-01, Anemo-05 and Anemo-06. The cameras are based on the 
12-megapixel Sony IMX708 image sensor from Raspberry Pi Camera Module 3 Wide, with a 
consistent resolution of 1080p at 30fps. 
 
Camera Anemo-01 was located at the control site, while Anemo-05 and Anemo-06 were located 
at both impact locations looking towards the Boulder Reefs. Cameras were positioned 1 meter 
above the ground and 1 meter away from the reef modules. 
 
Cameras were initially placed with an intended field of view designed to capture approximately 
25% reef module, 25% harbour wall, 25% sandy bottom, and 25% water column within each 
image, providing a balanced view of different habitat types. 
 
Cameras were programmed to record 1-minute video clips every 30 minutes throughout the 
deployment periods. This sampling strategy was proposed by DTU Aqua, following common 
practices in academia which helps on balancing the need to capture diel variations in fish activity 
while optimizing battery life. The sampling frequency allowed for up to 2 months of full day-night 
cycle coverage with manageable data volumes. 
 
The artificial light from the AnemoCam cameras was used for night monitoring. It was set up 
depending on the lighting conditions throughout the seasons, and updated on every 
redeployment. Given the depth in which the cameras were exposed at, the light was scheduled 
to start 1 hour before dusk and to finish 1 hour after dawn. Ensuring the most usable data as 
possible.

Data Preparation 
 
Preparing the raw video footage for AI model development required extracting individual frames 
that could later be manually labelled and used for training and validating the detection models. In 
a computer vision workflow, training a model to detect marine species relies on static images 
where objects of interest (in this case, fish) are clearly visible and annotated with bounding boxes. 
Therefore, building a high-quality frame dataset is a crucial first step in the machine learning 
pipeline. 
 
Frames were sampled systematically from the MOSAR video recordings across all three cameras 
(Anemo-01, Anemo-05, Anemo-06). The sampling approach aimed to cover a broad range of 
times of day (morning, midday, evening, night) to capture variability in lighting conditions, species 
behaviour, and fish visibility. Most of the training frames were taken from the months between 
July 2024 and October 2024. 
 
A total of 5997 frames were extracted from videos recorded between July 2024 and October 2024, 
and were used for the training of the models. 
 
 
Data Labelling and Training Dataset 
 
After preparing the extracted frame dataset, the next step involved manually annotating the 
images to create the training and validation sets for AI model development. This step involves 
drawing bounding boxes around each visible individual and assigning each annotation to a 
specific species or group. 
 
Labelling was performed using Label Studio (https://labelstud.io/), an open-source annotation 
platform that allows for precise bounding box creation and class assignment. The labelling 
process was carried out by two marine biologists from DTU Aqua, ensuring a high level of 
biological expertise and consistency. Fish were classified to the lowest possible taxonomic level 
==PAGE:35==

35 
based on image quality and visibility. When species-level identification was not possible due to 
poor resolution, individuals were labelled more generally as "fish" without specific species 
attribution. 
 
The final labelled dataset consisted of 5997 frames, covering ten target species or functional 
groups identified during the MOSAR monitoring period. However, a threshold was applied for 
inclusion into AI model development: only species with more than 500 individual annotations were 
selected for training dedicated detection models. This threshold was determined to ensure 
sufficient sample size for robust model training and to avoid poor performance associated with 
underrepresented classes. Consequently, while more species were observed across the 
recordings, only a subset was included in the modelling phase based on data availability. 
 
The labelled dataset was split into two subsets: 80% of the frames were assigned to the training 
set, used for optimizing model parameters, and 20% to the validation set, used for independent 
performance evaluation. Care was taken to ensure diversity in lighting conditions, camera 
perspectives, and environmental variability across both subsets, improving the model’s ability to 
generalize across new, unseen conditions.

Model Training and Validation 
 
Model development followed a two-step training workflow designed to support both data quality 
control and downstream biodiversity metric analysis. Rather than starting directly with species-
level classification, an initial binary classification model ("Fish" vs. "No Fish") was trained early in 
the labelling phase to evaluate the reliability of annotations and assist in identifying unlabelled 
frames that were likely to contain fish. 
 
This binary model was trained on a smaller initial dataset, using a standard 80/20 train-validation 
split. While not used in final inference, it played an important role in guiding the labelling process. 
Inspired by active learning principles, the model was used to highlight potentially informative 
==PAGE:37==
Data Analysis of AI-assisted Biodiversity Monitoring 
37 
frames, helping the annotation team prioritize their efforts. This approach allowed the dataset to 
grow more efficiently and with greater diversity, supporting better generalization in the later model. 
 
Once the labelled dataset reached sufficient size and quality, a final multiclass object detection 
model was trained to detect and classify individual fish across the ten selected species. Both the 
binary and multiclass models were developed using YOLOv8 (https://yolov8.com/), a state-of-the-
art real-time object detection architecture, which was finetuned for the MOSAR data and 
optimized for underwater data. Only species with more than 500 annotated instances were 
included in the multiclass training, ensuring enough data representation for effective learning. 
 
A set of models were trained for parameter optimization. Models of YOLO large and medium were 
trained, with very similar results. Although the models trained with large YOLO framework  were 
gave a better performance, the difference was not worth the increase in power. 
 
 
Model Performance 
 
Despite challenges such as variable visibility, partial occlusion, and inconsistent lighting, models 
demonstrated robust and consistent detection performance across the selected species. These 
model outputs formed the basis for the biodiversity metrics applied in the following sections. 
 
The performance of the trained YOLOv8 models was evaluated using standard object detection 
metrics, including F1-score, mean Average Precision (mAP), and per-class precision and recall. 
Model validation was conducted on a 20% held-out subset of the labelled dataset, using the 
manually annotated bounding boxes as ground truth.

3.2.1 Abundance Metrics 
 
Abundance metrics quantify how many individuals of each species were observed in the 
monitoring dataset. These metrics are fundamental in ecological assessments, as they provide 
insight into species presence, habitat usage, and temporal dynamics. For the MOSAR project, 
the structured detection outputs generated by the models allowed consistent and repeatable 
calculation of abundance metrics across all video samples. 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
40 
Data Analysis of AI-assisted Biodiversity Monitoring 
The following metrics were calculated: 
 
- 
MaxN: Maximum number of individuals of a species observed in a single frame. 
- 
MeanCount: Average number of individuals observed per frame. 
- 
Frequency of Occurrence: Proportion of video samples in which a species appeared. 
 
 
Each metric was calculated independently for each species, camera, and 1-minute video 
segment. Below, we describe the computation method for each metric. 
 
 
MaxN 
MaxN is the most important metric used in this thesis and serves as the foundation for several 
downstream analyses. It provides a standardized, conservative estimate of fish abundance from 
underwater video data and is particularly well-suited to stationary, unbaited camera deployments. 
 
Definition and context 
 
MaxN is defined as the highest number of individuals of a given species observed simultaneously 
in a single frame during a discrete time segment, in this case a 1-minute video. Originally 
introduced in Cappo et al. (2003) and further developed in large-scale survey frameworks 
(Campbell et al., 2015), MaxN is widely accepted across both academic and applied marine 
monitoring contexts, including projects by DTU Aqua. 
 
The strength of MaxN lies in its repeatability, simplicity, and resistance to double-counting,  
particularly important when individuals frequently re-enter the field of view. However, its 
conservative nature often underestimates true abundance, especially for schooling or transient 
species that may not be fully visible in a single frame. 
 
Application in this study 
 
MaxN was calculated using the structured detection outputs produced by the models, which 
provided per-frame predictions with species labels, confidence scores, and bounding boxes. Each 
1-minute video segment, recorded at 1 frame per second, consisted of 60 frames. For every 
species detected in a given video segment, all detections were filtered to include only those with 
a confidence score of 0.4 or higher. 
 
The detection records were grouped by: 
 
- 
Video segment (1-minute interval), 
- 
Camera ID (Anemo-01, Anemo-05, Anemo-06), 
- 
Species class (as defined in the labeled dataset). 
 
For each species in each video segment, the number of detected individuals was computed for 
every frame. The standard MaxN value corresponds to the highest count observed in any single 
frame during that segment. 
 
To reduce the impact of transient false positives or inconsistent single-frame detections, a 
consistency-filtered variant was implemented. In this approach, the MaxN value was only 
accepted if the same or higher number of individuals was detected in at least two consecutive 
frames (i.e., sustained for 2 seconds). This approach was inspired by Simon Madsen, previous 
CoFounder of Anemo Robotics, in his MSc Thesis (2023), who applied a 10-frame consistency 
rule, though that threshold was not feasible here due to the 1 fps sampling rate and shorter 
observation windows. 
 
==PAGE:41==
Data Analysis of AI-assisted Biodiversity Monitoring 
41 
In cases where individual fish were detected in one frame but not in the next, and no consistent 
count could be confirmed, it was considered a false positive and thus not counted for MaxN 
computation. 
 
For statistical analysis, Mann-Whitney U Test was applied for comparison between control and 
impact sites, using the available functions from Python’s SciPy library. 
 
 
MeanCount 
 
Definition and context 
 
MeanCount represents the average number of individuals of a given species observed per frame 
during a defined observation period. In this case, 1-minute video segments. It differs from MaxN 
in that it accounts for species presence and persistence across the entire observation window 
rather than capturing only the peak abundance in a single frame. As such, it can offer a more 
nuanced view of relative abundance, especially for species that appear intermittently or are 
consistently present in small numbers. 
 
While MaxN is often favoured for its simplicity and resistance to overcounting, several studies 
have highlighted the complementary value of MeanCount. Notably, Campbell et al. (2015) 
demonstrated that MeanCount tends to have a more linear relationship with true fish abundance 
when compared to MaxN, especially in habitats where fish are not highly mobile or schooling. 
However, the metric is also more sensitive to variation in detection quality and consistency, 
requiring a larger number of frames or sampling events to produce reliable estimates. 
 
Application in this study 
 
MeanCount was calculated for each species within every 1-minute video segment, using the 
structured detection outputs produced by the models inference. Each segment, sampled at 1 
frame per second, consisted of 60 frames. Detections were first filtered to retain only individuals 
with a model confidence score of 0.5 or higher. 
 
For each frame, the number of individuals of each species was recorded. The MeanCount for a 
species within a segment was then calculated as the average number of individuals across all 60 
frames: 
 
 
 
Earlier studies, including Campbell et al. (2015), often calculated MeanCount from a subset of 10 
randomly selected frames per video to reduce manual labor. However, the use of automated 
detection in this thesis allowed for calculation over the entire available frame set. This removed 
sampling randomness and improved the stability of the metric, particularly for species with 
sporadic movement patterns. 
 
For statistical analysis, Mann-Whitney U Test was applied for comparison between control and 
impact sites, using the available functions from Python’s SciPy library.

Definition and context 
 
Frequency of Occurrence (FoO) is defined as the proportion of observation periods (in this case, 
1-minute video segments) in which a given species was detected at least once. It is a binary 
presence/absence metric: a species is either present in a video (regardless of how many

3.2.2 Diversity Metrics 
 
While abundance metrics quantify the number of individuals observed, diversity metrics provide 
insight into the structure and balance of species composition within a community. These metrics 
are essential for assessing ecological health, particularly in the context of artificial reef 
performance, where changes in species diversity over time are key indicators of ecosystem 
development and stability. 
 
In this thesis, diversity was analysed using the Hill Numbers framework, which provides a unified 
approach to calculating diversity indices based on species richness and relative abundances. 
This framework allows comparison across different orders of sensitivity to species abundance: 
 
- 
q = 0 emphasizes species count (richness), 
- 
q = 1 corresponds to Shannon diversity (sensitive to evenness), 
- 
q = 2 corresponds to Simpson diversity (emphasizing dominance). 
- 
q = ∞ corresponds to the inverse Berger-Parker index (pure dominance).  
 
All diversity calculations were performed using the hill() function from the scikit-bio Python library 
(https://scikit.bio/docs/dev/generated/skbio.diversity.alpha.hill.html), 
which 
implements 
Hill 
numbers efficiently from species count vectors. The input for these functions was the aggregated 
species detection data (MaxN per species) from each camera and deployment period.

3.2.3 Behavioural Metrics 
 
While abundance and diversity metrics describe what species are present and in what numbers, 
behavioural metrics offer insight into how species use the habitat over time and space, as well as 
to provide vital data on their functionality. 
 
Because the AnemoCams collect timestamped, spatially consistent video data, they enable the 
extraction of multiple behaviourally relevant patterns from species detections. This includes short-
term activity rhythms, seasonal trends, responses to environmental variation, and spatial use of 
the reef environment. While behavioural metrics are less standardized than diversity indices, they 
offer valuable context to interpret how and why fish are using artificial reef structures. 
 
 
Diel Activity Patterns 
 
Definition and Context 
 
Diel activity patterns describe how fish presence or behaviour shifts over a 24-hour cycle, often 
reflecting underlying ecological strategies such as foraging, predation avoidance, or diel vertical 
migration. Many reef-associated and demersal species exhibit strong temporal preferences, 
making this a valuable behavioural indicator in monitoring studies. The approach in this thesis 
was adapted from Torquato et al. (2017), who used time-segmented detection data to analyse 
diel patterns at offshore platforms in the Gulf. 
 
Application in this study 
 
Since the AnemoCam includes an artificial light mounted on the device, and its capability to record 
during all day, data was collected during day and night for the monitoring duration. Light was 
automatically turned on between dusk and dawn. 
 
Each detection event in the structured model output included a timestamp, allowing assignment 
to diel time categories. Local sunrise and sunset times for Hundested, Denmark, were 
automatically 
calculated 
for 
each 
date 
using 
the 
Astral 
Python 
library 
(https://astral.readthedocs.io/en/latest/), accounting for shifting photoperiods throughout the year.  
 
Detection records were binned into four standardized diel periods: 
 
- 
Dawn: 1 hour before to 1 hour after astronomical sunrise (sun at −18°), 
- 
Daylight: from 1 hour post-sunrise to 1 hour pre-sunset, 
- 
Dusk: 1 hour before to 1 hour after astronomical sunset, 
- 
Night: the remaining hours (fully dark period).

comparison of activity across these diel phases. This approach permitted identification of species 
with daytime or nocturnal tendencies, and highlighted transitions at twilight periods. 
 
For statistical analysis, Kruskal-Wallis correlations were calculated between species abundance 
and diel activity slots (Night, Dusk, Day, Dawn), using the available functions from Python’s SciPy 
library. 
 
 
Seasonal Presence Trends 
 
Definition and context 
 
Seasonal presence trends reflect how species occurrence and abundance shift over longer 
temporal scales. These patterns are influenced by factors such as spawning migrations, 
temperature-dependent behaviour, or seasonal food availability. 
 
Application in this study 
 
Each detection in the structured dataset included a timestamp, allowing it to be assigned to a 
specific month and season. Detections were grouped by calendar month (July 2024 to January 
2025), and by seasons. Species-level MaxN values were aggregated per camera and deployment 
period. Trends in total species presence (richness) and individual species abundance were then 
compared across months. 
 
For statistical analysis, Kruskal-Wallis correlations were calculated between species abundance 
along seasons, and in along months, using the available functions from Python’s SciPy library. 
 
 
Environmental Correlations 
 
Definition and context 
 
Environmental correlation metrics explore how species presence and abundance vary in relation 
to environmental factors such as water temperature, salinity, and light availability. These 
relationships offer insight into behavioural responses to changing physical conditions and are 
critical in understanding whether observed biodiversity patterns are driven by local habitat use or 
broader oceanographic cycles. 
 
In marine restoration monitoring, these correlations help distinguish between reef-attracted 
species and those simply following favourable environmental conditions. 
 
Application in this study 
 
Species detection data was temporally aligned with daily environmental data obtained from the 
Copernicus Marine Service, using the Global Ocean Physics Analysis and Forecast product 
(GLOBAL_ANALYSISFORECAST_PHY_001_024). Attempts were made to access reanalysis

-

Data was retrieved as daily means and interpolated to match the timestamped detection periods. 
Species-specific MaxN values were then aggregated by day, and correlation analyses were 
conducted between species abundance and environmental parameters. Correlation coefficients

Habitat Use 
 
Definition and context 
 
Habitat use metrics describe where within the visible environment species tend to appear. For 
example, whether they are more often found near the reef structure, along the wall, or in the open 
water column. This provides insight into how artificial reefs are being used: as shelter, foraging 
ground, transit area, or simply part of the general environment. 
 
While traditional diver-based studies define habitat zones spatially in the field, this project used a 
camera-centric approach, leveraging the consistent framing of AnemoCams to define spatial 
zones directly within each video image. This enabled analysis of species-specific microhabitat 
use across different reef types, locations, and seasons. 
 
Application in this study 
 
For each camera deployment, a representative still frame was selected and used to define spatial 
habitat zones manually using the online tool image-map.net. Polygon zones were defined 
consistently across cameras and seasons to represent different physical substrates and reef 
elements, including: 
 
- 
Reef structure (artificial module), 
- 
Harbor wall (vertical substrate), 
- 
Sandy bottom, 
- 
Water column (above or away from hard substrate), 
 
For each detection (bounding box), the pixel coordinates were used to assign it to one of the pre-
defined polygon zones. This assignment was done for all species, cameras, and deployment 
months (Summer, October, November, January), allowing both seasonal and spatial 
comparisons. 
 
To visualize raw behavioural use, species-specific heatmaps were generated by plotting all 
detection coordinates over each still frame. These maps reflect detection density (not relative 
abundance) and served as a first exploratory layer. Then, for each species and camera, the 
proportion of total detections occurring in each zone was calculated using Kruskal-Wallis 
Correlation, allowing a more formal quantification of habitat use, using the available functions 
from Python’s SciPy library. 
 
 
Species Co-occurrence Patterns 
 
Definition and context 
 
Species co-occurrence patterns describe the extent to which different species are detected 
together within the same sample unit. These patterns can suggest possible ecological 
interactions, such as habitat sharing, predator–prey dynamics, or simply overlapping habitat 
preferences. While co-occurrence does not confirm interaction, it offers a useful window into 
community structure and potential behavioural or functional relationships among species. 
 
Application in this study 
 
For each 1-minute video segment, the presence or absence of each species was recorded based 
on MaxN values (presence = MaxN ≥ 1). Co-occurrence matrices were then constructed by 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
46 
Data Analysis of AI-assisted Biodiversity Monitoring 
comparing species pairs across all segments. The number of co-occurrence events (both species 
detected in the same video) was counted and normalized by the number of segments in which 
either species occurred, producing a pairwise co-occurrence index. 
 
To quantify the strength of these associations, Spearman rank correlation was applied across the 
full set of segment-level species presence values, capturing monotonic relationships between 
species: for example, whether species tend to appear more often in the same segments (positive 
correlation) or are typically exclusive (negative correlation). 
 
 
Control and Impact Comparison 
 
Definition and context 
 
Control–impact comparisons are a core method in ecological monitoring to assess the effect of 
an installation of artificial reef structures. By comparing abundance and diversity metrics at reef-
deployed cameras ("impact") versus a structurally identical area without artificial habitat 
("control"), it is possible to infer whether observed changes are likely due to the reef structures 
themselves. 
 
Application in this study 
 
To assess the influence of the Boulder Reef modules on local fish abundance and diversity, 
multiple biodiversity metrics were compared between the control site Anemo-01 (no artificial reef) 
and the impact sites Anemo-05 and Anemo-06 (adjacent to reef structures). Abundance metrics 
included MaxN, MeanCount, and Frequency of Occurrence (FoO), while diversity was assessed 
using Hill numbers across three orders (q = 0, 1, 2). 
 
For each metric, values were grouped by camera and aggregated across all valid 1-minute video 
segments. Given the non-normal distribution typical of ecological count data, non-parametric 
Mann–Whitney U tests were used to compare both overall and species-specific distributions 
between: 
 
- 
Control (Anemo-01) vs. Anemo-05, and 
- 
Control (Anemo-01) vs. Anemo-06 
 
This statistical approach allows for robust comparison of fish presence, abundance, and diversity 
between zones, without assuming equal variance or normality. It provides a straightforward way 
to detect potential ecological enhancements associated with the artificial structures.

3.3.2 Diversity Metrics

metrics, based on the MaxN counts, provide a unified framework for comparing species richness 
(q = 0), typical species diversity (q = 1), dominant species emphasis (q = 2), and maximum 
dominance (q = ∞). Weekly values were computed for each camera and used to explore how 
diversity patterns varied between reef and control sites over the monitoring period.

evenness. Weekly averages showed a consistent pattern of higher diversity at the reef-associated 
cameras (Anemo-05 and Anemo-06) compared to the control site (Anemo-01). Richness values 
at the reef sites peaked early after deployment and gradually declined through winter, while the 
control site remained more stable but consistently lower throughout the monitoring period (Figure

Control and Impact Comparison 
 
One of the central aims of this study was to assess the ecological influence of artificial reef 
structures on the surrounding marine community. To do so, we compared a comprehensive set 
of ecological metrics between two reef-associated camera locations (Anemo-05 and Anemo-06) 
and a nearby control location without reef intervention (Anemo-01). The comparison spans 
abundance, diversity, spatial behaviour, and presence metrics, providing a multidimensional view 
of reef impact. All statistical analyses were conducted using Mann–Whitney U tests, and detailed 
results are presented in Appendix C. 
 
Across all abundance-related indicators (MaxN, MeanCount, and Frequency of Occurrence), reef-
associated sites consistently exhibited significantly higher values than the control. Total MaxN 
showed a marked difference, with median values at reef cameras reaching 6.00 (Anemo-05) and 
5.00 (Anemo-06), compared to just 1.00 at the control site (p < 0.001). Similarly, MeanCount 
displayed elevated values at the reef locations (medians of 3.75 and 3.28) relative to 0.30 at the 
control. Frequency of Occurrence also reflected this trend, with higher weekly detection rates at 
the reef sites. Species-specific comparisons supported these results: taxa such as A. rubens 
(starfish), C. maenas (crab) or G. niger (black goby), showed significantly greater presence and 
abundance on reef sites. 
 
Diversity metrics, as measured through Hill numbers, further supported these findings. All Hill 
diversity indices (q = 0, 1, 2, ∞) indicated significantly greater diversity at reef locations. Species 
richness (Hill q = 0) was higher at reefs (median = 5.00) than at the control (median = 4.00, p < 
0.001). Similarly, Shannon entropy (q = 1) and Simpson diversity (q = 2) values were elevated at 
reef sites, suggesting more even community composition. The Berger–Parker dominance index 
(q = ∞) also showed significantly lower dominance by a single species at reef sites, indicating 
more balanced community structure overall. 
 
 
 
3.4. Discussion and Considerations 
 
This thesis does not aim to provide new ecological interpretations or theoretical insights into 
marine biodiversity. As the author is not a marine biologist, the focus has been deliberately placed 
on the development and testing of a metric-based methodology using AI-processed underwater 
video data. The primary objective has been to demonstrate whether meaningful biodiversity 
metrics can be extracted from such data, supporting future work by experts who can interpret the 
ecological patterns in detail. 
 
Using computer vision and biodiversity metrics to process and interpret underwater video data,  
the work shows that Anemo’s system can generate structured, interpretable outputs that reflect 
meaningful biological patterns. By turning large volumes of raw video into standardized ecological 
indicators, the pipeline supports collaboration between technologists and marine scientists and 
opens the door for scalable, data-driven biodiversity assessments.

Abundance Metrics 
 
From a data and processing standpoint, the abundance metrics produced results that were 
coherent, interpretable, and largely aligned with expectations. The clear differences observed 
between control and reef sites were also confirmed during manual review of video segments, 
which helped validate that the system was capturing meaningful ecological signals. Findings 
mirror similar results from other studies using abundance metrics in Control and Impact 
experiments (Gilby et al., 2021). 
 
These metrics worked especially well for sedentary or highly visible species, which tended to 
dominate the counts. However, they were less reliable for rarer or fast-moving species, where 
detections were more sporadic or inconsistent. Those observation also reported by Campbell et 
al. (2015), who found that MeanCount and MaxN were more reliable for low-mobility species, and  
underestimated abundance in more mobile fish. 
 
One anomaly worth noting was the apparent peak in abundance during October. This may have 
been influenced by the fact that several frames from this period were included in the manually 
labelled training data, potentially skewing detection frequency. Additionally, the strong increase 
in starfish detections during that time was driven by Camera 1, which was oriented directly toward 
the harbour wall, an area where starfish were more concentrated. 
 
The absence of data from September also left a significant gap in temporal coverage, limiting the 
ability to fully interpret seasonal dynamics. 
 
 
Diversity Metrics 
 
The diversity metrics generally followed the patterns seen in the abundance data. Since they are 
mathematically derived from species presence and relative abundance, any issues or biases in 
the underlying abundance metrics likely carried through to diversity calculations.  
 
That said, species richness was consistently higher in the reef sites, which aligns with visual 
review of the footage, where a broader range of species was clearly observed compared to the 
control zone. Similar patterns have also been reported in other studies of artificial and restored 
reef systems (eg. Gilby et al., 2021). It is also possible that the models performed slightly better 
on reef footage due to greater species representation in the training data, and that affected the 
identification of different species. 
 
 
Behavioral Metrics 
 
The behavioural metrics implemented in this project were among the most informative, yet also 
the most difficult to interpret without deeper ecological context. Diel activity patterns, particularly 
for species like Gadus morhua (cod), aligned well with known literature describing crepuscular 
behaviour, which gave additional confidence in the metric outputs. 
 
Seasonal shifts in presence also followed intuitive trends and were easily observable when 
reviewing the detection outputs over time. Species like Gadus morhua (cod), that have seasonal 
residency appeared in warmer periods and dropped in colder months, as expected. 
 
Habitat use analysis, while still early in its development, showed strong potential to reveal how 
different species interact spatially with artificial structures. However, the method applied here was 
a first attempt, and there is clear room for refinement in how zones are defined and how movement 
is quantified.

3.4.2 Value for Marine Biology 
 
The structured data and metric outputs generated in this project offer a rich foundation for 
ecological interpretation, with a great value of the dataset for marine biologists. What makes this 
work so powerful is the structure, resolution, and repeatability of the data. Every detection is 
timestamped, geolocated, and associated with specific metrics of abundance, diversity, 
behaviour, transforming video footage from an often underutilized resource into a dense, dataset 
that can support a wide range of scientific questions. 
 
For biologists, this means that species-specific patterns can be explored easily in a much more 
detail than manual analysis datasets. Questions that would typically require expensive and time-
consuming field surveys can now be investigated in a matter of minutes using the outputs from a 
single deployment.  
 
The value lies in how integrated and standardized these metrics are: they were computed 
systematically across space and time, meaning that comparisons between sites, species, or 
deployment periods are not only possible but scientifically robust. In many existing monitoring 
programs, such insights would require labour-intensive fieldwork and video review—often leading 
to limited temporal coverage or simplified presence/absence summaries. Here, those same 
insights are already built in, distilled from thousands of video frames through automated 
processing and metric computation. 
 
One of the most distinctive aspects of this project is the inclusion of behavioural and spatial-use 
metrics, which are extremely rare in conventional biodiversity monitoring. These dimensions are 
typically excluded from ecological assessments due to the difficulty of collecting and analysing 
such data manually. The automated video pipeline enables these metrics to be derived 
consistently and at high temporal resolution, unlocking entirely new layers of ecological insight 
that are often inaccessible to manual methods. For marine biologists, this opens the door to 
behavioural ecology and habitat functionality analysis within standard monitoring campaigns. 
 
Beyond the quality of the results, the automation and structure of the analysis itself represent a 
fundamental shift in how marine biodiversity can be studied. Traditional video analysis is labour-
intensive and time-limited; each new question (e.g., about diel variation, habitat preference, or 
abundance trends) requires rewatching, reannotating, and reanalysing footage. In automatic 
methods like Anemo’s, any number of metrics can be calculated from the same source. Instantly, 
repeatably, and at scale. This opens the door to iterative experimentation, where biologists can 
revisit the data to test new hypotheses without restarting from scratch. 
 
Not only does automation make analysis faster and more scalable, it also improves the accuracy 
and consistency of the data itself. Manual workflows tend to have inconsistencies and strategies 
across observers. By contrast, automated approaches standardize methodology ensuring metrics 
are computed uniformly. This is especially evident in metrics like MeanCount, while traditionally 
calculated using a small number of manually selected frames, automation enables this metric to 
be derived from every single valid frame in a video segment. The result is a far more accurate 
and representative measure of abundance, particularly for mobile species that may appear 
intermittently. 
3.5. Challenges and Limitations 
 
The MOSAR project represented the first full-scale deployment of Anemo Robotics’ underwater 
video monitoring system in a real-world marine environment. Using novel technology in dynamic 
and unpredictable field conditions such as marine environments often comes with many 
challenges. The project faced a number of technical and logistical challenges that shaped the 
dataset and analyses. Many aspects were being tested under operational conditions for the first 
time, leading to critical learnings but also a range of limitations in data consistency, coverage, and 
accuracy.

These challenges allowed for iterative improvements in both the technology and methodology, 
and represents a valuable experience for Anemo Robotics. It is important to note that MOSAR 
was a sponsored project meant for testing and validating the technology, and to get learnings and 
developments are more relevant than getting factual data. 
 
 
Deployment Constraints 
 
One of the primary limitations of this study lies in the constraints surrounding the underwater 
camera deployments. Several factors effected to the consistency on data collection.  
 
- 
Redeployments if the cameras meant that cameras did not have a exact same position  
throughout the months, and was only done with the aid of divers from November onwards. 
Some deployments had reefs not completely on frame, and it was especially effected on 
the control camera (Anemo-01). 
 
This meant that deployments didn’t have a consistent FOV, and the desired equal split 
(25% reef, 25% quay wall, 25% sandy bottom, 25% water column) was hardly achieved 
in some deployments.  
 
- 
Currents and storms also provoked some camera movements throughout the 
monitoring period, causing cameras to get completely unusable FOVs for some periods. 
From November onwards, additional weights were added to the camera stands, with good 
results where the cameras didn’t suffer from movements anymore.. 
  
- 
Technical issues, especially in malfunctioning lights, resulted in periods without night 
footage, that limits the completeness of diel activity data and may have biased the 
temporal analyses. 
 
- 
Iterations on hardware updates also effected on the consistency of the data collection. 
Integration of better biofouling solutions along the project, such as the wipers, favourably 
affected the data collection after iterations. Same happen with light and camera settings. 
 
- 
Missing data such as the whole month of September is, arguably, one of the biggest 
limitation of the project. Most of it is due to unusable data given current drag, since the 
cameras worked and recorded on all of the deployments.  
 
Future studies should implement tighter controls for deployment consistency, possibly by using 
fixed mounting points to ensure a consistent FOV, and routine maintenance to ensure stable 
conditions across all monitoring periods. 
 
 
Labelling Bias 
 
Several important sources of bias were introduced through the composition and methodology of 
the labelling dataset used to train the computer vision models. 
 
- 
Temporal distribution of labelled data was uneven, with most of the annotations drawn 
from July, August, and October. While these months offered relatively high visibility and 
species activity, they do not represent the full range of seasonal dynamics. This bias may 
have reduced model performance during other periods where certain species appeared 
but were underrepresented or missing in the training set. 
 
- 
Spatial imbalance in labelling was inevitable. Given that Anemo-01 (control site) had 
lower species abundances overall, resulted in fewer detectable events and, 
consequently, fewer labelled segments. This underrepresentation may have affected on 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
66

the effectiveness of the model on the control site, and introducing some level of bias in 
assessing control-impact differences. 
 
- 
The active learning strategy used in the iterative development of the Fish/No-Fish 
classifier introduced an exclusion bias. Species that were not present or confidently 
detected in the early model iterations were less likely to be labelled in subsequent rounds. 
This created a feedback loop in which already rare or less detectable species had fewer 
opportunities to enter the labelled dataset, limiting the CV system's ability to detect and 
quantify them reliably. 
 
Future Anemo projects will have to follow a more standardized labelling protocols to ensure 
balanced data among time, sites and species. Introducing randomised approaches as well as 
methods for enhancing species with lower presence. . 
 
 
False Positives and MaxN Accuracy 
 
A critical limitation in this study the sensitivity of MaxN metric to false positive detections. Given 
that most of the metrics computed derived from initial MaxN calculations, it becomes very 
important to ensure the reliability of such. 
 
Since MaxN captures the highest count of individuals observed in a single frame within each 
videos, any erroneous detection can inflate the metric, especially false positives. To mitigate this, 
a conservative filter was applied requiring detections to appear in at least two consecutive frames 
before contributing to the MaxN tally. While this step reduced noise, it did not fully eliminate the 
risk of overestimation. 
 
Another challenge related to MaxN accuracy arises from the video sampling rate. To enable faster 
computation in inference, detections were made on 1 frame per second each second, rather than 
at the original 30 FPS. Although this allowed for efficient processing across large datasets, it may 
have missed some species. This concern is particularly relevant for species that appear briefly or 
are highly mobile. 
 
To quantify the extent of this issue, a MaxN accuracy test was conducted comparing model-
generated MaxN values at multiple downsampled frame rates (30 FPS, 10 FPS, 1 FPS, 0.2 FPS, 
0.1 FPS) against a "ground truth" derived from manual annotations by a marine biologist in a 
traditional manner. The results show that while some species like A. rubens (starfish) and G. niger 
(black goby) maintained relatively accurate MaxN estimates even at 1 FPS, other more mobile or 
less distinct species like C. rupestris (wrasse) and P. flavescens (two-spotted goby) experienced 
substantial drops in accuracy at lower sampling rates. Results can be found on Appendix C. 
 
 
Lack of a “true” Ground Truth 
 
One of the biggest limitations of this study is the absence of a real ground truth. The MOSAR 
project included ground truth derived of MaxN counts done by experienced marine biologists in a 
traditional way, a process that is both time-consuming and highly subjective.  
 
The traditional MaxN approach involves scanning through video footage to record the single frame 
in which the maximum number of individuals appears. However, this method is inherently limited 
by human attention span, interpretive bias, and the inability to replay detections across multiple 
frames.  
 
As a result, even “ground truth” values used for benchmarking AI models carry inherent 
uncertainty, and complicates the validation process for new monitoring technologies.

4. Learnings from MOSAR and Future Monitoring 
Design 
The thesis help get foundation on applied biodiversity metrics for underwater camera systems 
with automatised species detection. The next step is to transform these analyses into a 
standardized monitoring framework, repeatable system that can be used across future Anemo 
projects. Such a framework would ensure consistency in how biodiversity is measured, reported, 
and interpreted, while maintaining flexibility to adapt to different environments, clients, or 
regulatory needs. 
 
 
4.1 Recommendations for Future Monitoring Design 
 
The MOSAR project, from which most of this Thesis practical work has been based of,  highlighted 
several important lessons that can help the design of future monitoring campaigns using Anemo’s 
underwater video system. While the core data collection and metric computation processes gave 
good indications, there are clear areas where refinement would enhance consistency, data 
quality, and interpretability of the results. 
 
● 
Camera placement and orientation is a critical factor influencing the results. 
Inconsistencies in framing significantly metrics values and habitat-use interpretation. 
Standardizing deployment protocols, through fixed mounting frames or visual feedback 
during setup, would ensure more consistent an therefore comparable data across 
locations and time. 
 
Designing experiments with standardized distribution of type of habitats in frame, and 
keeping it consistent across time and locations would enhance exponentially the 
accuracy of the results and possibilities of habitat use understanding. Depending on the 
project and experiment success criteria, different distributions should be implemented.  
 
Anemo Robotics is slowly transitioning from AnemoCams (battery-powered cameras) to 
AnemoLive (new cameras connected to external power) is proven to be a good direction. 
Since this new cameras are connected to both power and internet, they can be properly 
fixed, avoiding the need for redeployments and thus ensuring consistent camera position 
throughout all experiments. 
 
● 
The labelling strategy is one of the most important parts of automatization solution and 
need to be balanced and intentional. The AI model used in this project was trained on a 
dataset with slightly uneven temporal and spatial representation, which may have biased 
detection performance.  
 
Future labelling efforts should aim for even coverage across seasons, sites, and habitat 
types, and include deliberate sampling of rarer or underrepresented species. Different 
approaches including randomized methods and more labels need to be implemented to 
ensure a robust labelled dataset. This may mean increasing the labelling effort, but it 
would also improve both model generalizability and ecological completeness. 
 
● 
Inference sampling rate is worth revisiting and performing more experiments to 
determine the best approach. While the 1 FPS used in this study was computationally 
efficient, it limited the system’s ability to capture short-duration events or fast-moving 
individuals, and was heavily effected by false-positive cases. A moderate increase in 
frame sampling (e.g., 5–10 FPS) could yield much more detailed and accurate data 
without overwhelming storage or processing capacity.

● 
Functional trait-based indicators could be more easily integrated by aligning with the 
trait classifications and ecological groupings already used by DHI, and other entities, in 
their biodiversity indicator frameworks. Anemo’s outputs should map detected species to 
existing trophic groups, mobility classes, or habitat associations based on standard 
databases using DHI’s specific categorizations. This would ensure compatibility, allowing 
Anemo’s data to plug directly into established biodiversity scoring systems and contribute 
to a broader, standardized understanding of ecological function. 
 
 
4.2 Towards a Standardized Monitoring Framework for Anemo 
 
As proposed earlier in the thesis, the three-phased structure offers a progression for adding 
biodiversity metrics in stages, based on technical feasibility. However, for future Anemo projects, 
it is important to define a standardized package of outputs that should be implemented by default 
in any project, regardless of location or client. 
 
This core suite should include: 
 
● 
Abundance metrics: Standardized MaxN, MeanCount, and Frequency of Occurrence, 
which offer complementary views of species presence and relative activity. Where 
possible, these should be converted to density values to account for differences in 
camera angle, habitat complexity, or field of view. This standardization is especially 
important for comparing data across deployments, seasons, or environmental conditions. 
 
● 
Diversity metrics: Hill numbers (q = 0, 1, 2) provide a unified and interpretable 
framework for describing community diversity and are considered a basic requirement in 
any biodiversity assessment. This method captures species richness, evenness-
weighted diversity, and dominance patterns in a scalable and mathematically consistent 
way. Because these indices are derived directly from existing abundance data, they can 
be implemented with minimal effort and offer a richer ecological narrative than species 
counts alone. 
 
● 
Temporal activity patterns: Diel and seasonal presence trends are easily computed 
from timestamped detections and offer valuable behavioural insight with no added data 
collection burden. These metrics reveal when species are most active or present, and 
can inform clients about usage cycles around structures. Their integration adds a dynamic 
temporal dimension to otherwise static species lists or counts. 
 
● 
Habitat use analysis should be treated as a strategic priority. Anemo’s ability to capture 
spatial interactions within the camera frame is one of its most important differentiators 
compared to traditional and other monitoring tools. The inclusion of spatial behaviour 
metrics can provide clients with insights that are otherwise extremely difficult to obtain, 
but that provide huge value for ecological understanding. 
 
Finally, to ensure scientific and industry relevance, all core metrics should be designed with 
compatibility with the biodiversity indicators and trait-based classification systems used by 
partners such as DHI. By adapting species labels and structuring outputs to align with these 
external frameworks, Anemo can position itself as not only a data provider, but a direct contributor 
to broader ecosystem assessments and regulatory processes.

5. Conclusion 
The work carried out in this thesis aimed to explore how biodiversity metrics can be applied to the 
kind of data generated by Anemo Robotics' technology. Through a mix of metric exploration, real-
world data application, and reflection on broader use cases, the project generated key learnings 
not only for this specific case, but also for how Anemo Robotics can position its technology in the 
evolving field of marine biodiversity monitoring. 
 
 
5.1 Summary of the Thesis 
 
This thesis examined how biodiversity metrics can be integrated into Anemo Robotics’ AI-
powered underwater monitoring system. The intention was to bridge AnemoAI's technical outputs 
with biodiversity standards used in ecological and regulatory contexts, increasing the scientific 
relevance and usability of the data produced. 
 
Through a comprehensive review of biodiversity metrics and practices across academia and 
industry, a curated set of metrics was identified that align well with the type of data AnemoAI 
generates, and that are scientifically and industry relevant. This was followed by an application of 
those metrics to real-world data from the MOSAR (Monitoring Of Small Artificial Reefs) project. 
Although the data presented inconsistencies common in early-stage, real-world monitoring 
deployments, it demonstrated that Anemo's technology is capable of generating valuable 
biodiversity-related insights. 
 
While the thesis briefly touched upon an standardized analysis plan for future Anemo projects, it 
laid important groundwork by exploring which metrics are feasible, relevant, and interpretable. It 
also highlighted technical and conceptual considerations for future refinement. 
 
Overall, this thesis provides a first practical step towards integrating biodiversity metrics into 
Anemo’s product and technology. It validates the opportunity for Anemo to contribute 
meaningfully to the broader challenge of marine biodiversity monitoring, and opens up several 
clear directions for future development. 
 
 
 
5.2 Key Findings and Learnings 
 
This project reinforced on the complexity of marine biodiversity monitoring. Across literature, 
industry interviews, and hands-on analysis, it became clear that biodiversity is not a single 
variable to measure, but a multi-dimensional concept involving many different aspects of the 
ecosystems. While Anemo’s technology does not aim to capture every layer of biodiversity, the 
thesis shows how it can contribute to critical and highly relevant pieces, particularly around 
abundance, behaviour, and long-term trends, with automatized analysis. 
 
One important learning is the challenge of translating AI-detected fish data into formats that 
marine biologists or industry stakeholders can meaningfully use. Applying metrics like MaxN, 
MeanCount, frequency of occurrence, and Hill numbers helped bridge that gap. These metrics 
proved easy to compute, widely used in practice, and well-suited to the kind of continuous, non-
invasive video data Anemo generates. While the results from MOSAR were not always consistent, 
due to variable data quality, detection accuracy, and natural ecosystem variability, they still 
provided valuable insights into patterns of fish presence, habitat use, and behavioural insights. 
 
The analysis also highlighted the value of behaviour-related metrics. Anemo’s static, long-term 
deployment proved especially useful in analysing diel activity and habitat interaction patterns, 
areas that are often underexplored in traditional biodiversity surveys. The ability to extract

temporal and spatial patterns opens up new ways of understanding how marine life responds to 
artificial habitats. 
 
While the thesis focused on a first layer of biodiversity metrics, it also proposed paths for future 
development. The next steps include integrating functional biodiversity metrics, such as trait-
based or guild-based indicators, and exploring size-based assessments by improving Anemo’s 
technology with stereo cameras. These additions will allow for more advanced ecological 
indicators such as biomass, productivity, and functional richness. Furthermore, aligning metric 
outputs with regulatory frameworks and client-specific reporting needs will be an important 
direction to increase the applicability and impact of Anemo’s data. 
 
In short, this work showed that Anemo’s technology can deliver structured, repeatable biodiversity 
data, and that it already provides a valuable layer of information in the broader biodiversity 
monitoring landscape. The challenge ahead lies in building on this foundation, refining how the 
data is processed and presented, expanding into more advanced ecological metrics, and ensuring 
outputs remain interpretable and useful for marine scientists and stakeholders. Rather than a final 
product, this thesis represents a starting point, offering grounded insights and directions for 
Anemo’s future development in biodiversity assessment. 
 
Summary: Answers to the initial Research Questions: 
 
RQ1: What are the recognized standards and best practices for biodiversity assessment 
using underwater video data? 
The thesis reviewed scientific literature and industry practice, identifying abundance, 
taxonomic, and presence–absence measures as commonly used. With functional diversity 
being very relevant for deeper ecological understanding and gaining importance across the 
academia and industry, in different analysis methods. Biomass estimations metrics are also 
found to be heavily used for production analysis, although mostly in research-based studies 
using stereo cameras. 
 
RQ2: Has there been an improvement in marine biodiversity in the MOSAR project’s 
studied site? 
Although results are to be reviewed by biology experts, there are indications that suggest an 
improvement of key biodiversity metrics in the studied site. All core abundance and diversity 
metrics showed consistently higher values at reef sites compared to the control, suggesting a 
positive local effect of the artificial structures on biodiversity.  
 
RQ3: What additional insights can Anemo Robotics' technology provide that current 
methods may not capture? 
Anemo’s system enables the automated capture of temporal and behavioural patterns that are 
not included in traditional monitoring due to their labour demands, and technology limitations. 
These include analysis of diel activity, zone-based habitat use, and co-occurrence insights, 
which its integration adds valuable functional context to biodiversity data. The thesis showed 
that Anemo’s technology can provide breakthrough tools for valuable understanding of marine 
ecosystems. 
 
RQ4: How can the findings from this project be standardized into a reproducible and 
scientifically robust data analysis plan for Anemo Robotics? 
The thesis serves as a foundation for future Anemo Robotics analysis approach. It proposes a 
suite of repeatable metrics and standard structure that allows Anemo to scale monitoring efforts 
across deployments while maintaining scientific rigor and compatibility with external 
biodiversity frameworks. The thesis proposes a staged approach to include relevant metrics as 
part of the development for more accurate and standardized analysis, including incorporation 
of trait-based databases for functional diversity metrics, and development of stereo cameras 
for size estimation dependant metrics.

5.3 Implications of the Thesis 
 
5.3.1 Implications for Anemo Robotics 
 
The findings of this thesis have several implications for the development and strategic positioning 
of Anemo Robotics. First, it confirms that the data collected by Anemo’s systems can produce 
biodiversity insights that are both valuable and increasingly expected by clients, researchers, and 
regulators. 
 
The successful application of biodiversity metrics into Anemo data demonstrates the potential to 
deliver structured ecological information that supports environmental reporting, restoration 
monitoring, and nature-based investment assessments. Particularly for marine scientists, the 
richness and structure of Anemo’s dataset, its temporal resolution, behavioural insights, and 
spatial consistency, offer a unique and highly valuable resource that traditional short-term or diver-
based monitoring often cannot provide, given Anemo’s technology a competitive advantage for 
future monitorings. 
 
The thesis lays a foundation for a standardized approach to biodiversity monitoring using Anemo’s 
systems, that has direct value in improving the scalability of Anemo’s offerings. With future 
developments, such as integration of functional metrics and stereo cameras, Anemo has the 
potential to position itself as a key enabler in marine ecological monitoring and restoration 
initiatives.  
 
Notably, when the results from this thesis were shared with external collaborators and potential 
partners, the feedback was overwhelmingly positive, especially from specialists on marine 
biology. The behavioural components in particular, such as diel activity and habitat interaction 
heatmaps, sparked strong interest and were recognized as a significant differentiator, resulting in 
new kind of data that moves forward the understanding of marine species. These elements further 
reinforce how Anemo’s data can open up new questions and insights for researchers, while also 
satisfying the practical needs of clients looking for scalable and repeatable biodiversity indicators. 
 
 
5.3.2 Implications for the Marine Biology Community 
 
For marine biologists, this thesis offers a new approach to accessing high-resolution biodiversity 
data that is typically unavailable through conventional monitoring. Traditional surveys are often 
limited in temporal resolution, spatial coverage, or feasibility over long durations (Novak, 2024). 
In contrast, this work shows that AI-powered video systems like Anemo’s can generate structured, 
multi-metric datasets over extended periods with minimal human intervention. 
 
Some of the outputs approached in this thesis, such as continuous diel activity patterns, habitat 
use distributions, and species co-occurrence, are rarely captured in small-scale reef monitoring 
projects due to the manual labour and time constraints involved. By automating the process and 
delivering these metrics as part of a standardized package, Anemo's system provides marine 
scientists with a new source of behavioural and spatial data that complements existing methods. 
 
In addition to its immediate relevance for marine biologists, this thesis also contributes to the 
broader movement toward standardized, automated biodiversity monitoring. It offers a model that 
can be replicated across different projects and environments, ensuring outputs that are 
scientifically robust and can be comparable across time, space, and specific project needs. 
 
The thesis illustrates a pathway for shifting biodiversity monitoring away from highly manual, time-
limited workflows toward scalable, automated systems. The combination of AI-based detection 
and standardized metrics demonstrates that meaningful analysis can be achieved at scale. In 
doing so, this thesis provides a bridge between technological potential and ecological application, 
pushing forward what’s possible in the emerging field of automated biodiversity assessment.
