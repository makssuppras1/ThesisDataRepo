Abstract
Exposure to endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs) during fetal development may disrupt ovarian develop-
ment, leading to adverse effects on reproduction later in life. While most research has focused on direct effects
of EDCs on the ovary, secondary effects mediated through disruption of the thyroid hormone system are less
explored. During fetal development, an adequate supply of thyroid hormone (TH) is essential for proper ovary
development, and insufficient TH levels have been suggested to adversely affect reproductive function and fer-
tility in both animals and humans. However, establishing a link between developmental TH insufficiency and
long-term reproductive effects is challenging, partly due to the significant lag time between fetal exposure and
manifestation of outcomes in adulthood. Identifying early molecular mechanisms affected by TH insufficiency
is key to bridging this gap. The aim of the thesis was to identify molecular biomarkers of effect on postnatal
day (PND)6 ovaries following 6-Propyl-2-thiouracil (PTU)-induced hypothyroidism during development.
Pregnant rat dams were exposed to two concentrations of PTU through gestation and lactation, and ovaries were
collected on PND6 and PND80. Early morphological changes were assessed by semi-quantitative estimations
of germ cell nests and follicles in PND6 ovaries. No significant differences were observed, though a tendency
towards increased nest counts in the high-dose group suggested a possible delay in nest breakdown. Bulk RNA
Barcoding and sequencing (BRB-seq), carried out on both PND6 and PND80 ovaries, identified more than
3000 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) distinguishing the two age groups, while PTU exposure resulted
in fewer than 50 DEGs per dose group on both PND6 and PND80.
The two genes, activin A receptor type 2B (Acvr2b) and fatty acid binding protein (Fabp4), were selected for
further histological analysis with in situ hybridization. Both genes were downregulated in PND6 ovaries, with
Fabp4 also downregulated on PND80. Acvr2b was localized in both granulosa cells and oocytes, while Fabp4
was expressed in oocytes and in stromal cells. Semi-quantitative analysis suggested reduced Acvr2b expression
in both dose-groups, though not statistically significant, while the Fabp4 downregulation was not confirmed in
situ, highlighting the need for increased statistical power.
Given their localization and potential importance for ovary development, Acvr2b and Fabp4 may serve as early
molecular biomarkers of hypothyroidism-induced effects on the ovary. Acvr2b downregulation could influence
meiotic oocyte maturation, follicle assembly, and folliculogenesis, while Fabp4 may influence follicular atresia,
and consequently nest breakdown and follicle assembly. Further research is needed to establish their potential
as predictive molecular markers of adverse effects.
In conclusion, this study identified two relevant genes as potential early molecular biomarkers of secondary
effects of hypothyroidism, which may contribute to adverse effects on ovary development and consequently
female reproductive health.

Introduction
Endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs) have been recognized for their adverse effects on both the male and
female reproductive systems. In females, exposure to EDCs has been linked to a variety of effects, such
as disrupted timing of puberty, irregular estrous cycles, ovulatory dysfunction, infertility, and adverse birth
outcomes (Gore et al. 2015). Animal studies have identified several adverse effects of exposure to EDCs
in the ovaries, including effects on follicle assembly- and recruitment, increased follicular atresia, altered
steroidogenesis, and oocyte numbers (Gore et al. 2015; Johansson et al. 2017; Buck Louis et al. 2011).
While most research has focused on direct effects of EDCs on the reproductive organs, secondary effects
mediated through disruption of the thyroid hormone system are less explored. Normal function of the thyroid
gland and the synthesis of thyroid hormones (THs) is essential for several physiological processes in the body,
regulating among other metabolism, heart rate, and muscle function (Shahid et al. 2023). Additionally, THs
are essential for ovary development and function, and disruptions in the thyroid hormone system have been
suggested to adversely affect reproductive function and fertility (Silva et al. 2018; Fedail et al. 2014; Lukovi´c
et al. 2016). In women with inadequate TH levels, irregular menstrual cycles, ovulatory dysfunction, fertility
problems, and an increased risk of adverse pregnancy outcomes are more commonly observed (Krassas 2000;
Redmond 2004; Silva et al. 2018). These observations are supported by results in animal studies, where
irregular estrus cycles, decreased ovulation, disruptions to folliculogenesis, and increased follicular atresia
have been linked to TH insufficiency (Lukovi´c et al. 2016; Radovanovi´c et al. 2012; Hatsuta et al. 2004;
Armada-Dias et al. 2001).
Since the fetus is dependent on maternal TH supply during pregnancy, it is hypothesized that TH insufficiency
during this period may lead to adverse effects in the reproductive system that could cause reproductive disor-
ders later in life (Zoeller et al. 2004). However, linking developmental TH insufficiency and long-term effects
is challenging due the delayed manifestation of outcomes. Understanding this link relies on identifying early
mechanisms affected by TH insufficiency, but studies on early effects are limited. Moreover, most research
has focused on physiological endpoints, which provide valuable insights but offer limited explanation of the
mechanisms underlying the observed effects. Consequently, little is known about how developmental TH in-
sufficiency impacts the offspring’s reproductive system early in life or the molecular mechanisms involved.
Next-generation sequencing (NGS) methods, including transcriptomics, have proved to be efficient ways for
identifying mechanisms behind observed phenotypes by identifying differentially expressed genes and path-
ways. Additionally, NGS can be used to identify molecular effect biomarkers of e.g., disease and environmental
exposures, and has among others been used widely in cancer research (Satam et al. 2023; Desai et al. 2015;
Kugathas et al. 2023). Though it has not, to our knowledge, been previously investigated, it may also be useful
in identifying early molecular effect biomarkers of developmental TH insufficiency in the ovaries.
The purpose of this project is to identify potential molecular effect biomarkers of thyroid hormone disruption in
postnatal day (PND)6 rat ovaries from offspring of rat dams with induced hypothyroidism during the perinatal
period. More specifically the aim is to answer the following questions: i) How does developmental hypothy-

roidism impact differential gene expression at postnatal day 6 in offspring, as revealed by Bulk RNA Barcoding
and Sequencing (BRB-seq)? ii) Do differential gene expression patterns observed in BRB-seq correlate with
expression levels in ovarian tissue, as assessed by in situ hybridization (ISH), and where are the transcripts
localized in the ovary? and iii) Which of the differentially expressed genes may potentially serve as molecular
effect biomarkers, and what implications could these have for ovarian function?

Background
2.1
Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals
During the last few decades, a steady rise in reproductive disorders has been observed in both males and
females. These include, among others, malformed genitals, disrupted timing of puberty, and an increase in
reproductive site cancers rates (Woodruff 2011). In the same period, an increasing number of people are expe-
riencing fertility problems. The reasons for these changes are likely due to a multitude of factors, however, as
the changes have occurred relatively rapidly, it cannot be explained by genetic changes. One of the suggested
main contributing factors to the development is the exposure to EDCs (Gore et al. 2015). Increasing evidence
suggests that exposure to EDCs, especially during early gonadal development, leads to reproductive disorders
later in life (Gore et al. 2015; Johansson et al. 2017; Skakkebaek et al. 2015). According to the World Health
Organization, an EDC is ”...an exogenous substance or mixture that alters function(s) of the endocrine system
and consequently causes adverse health effects in an intact organism, or its progeny, or (sub) populations.”
(Bergman et al. 2013), and several classes of environmental chemicals with EDC activity have been identified.
Some of the most studied are Bisphenol A (BPA), phthalates, polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), and different
pesticides. EDCs interfere with the action of hormones in the body and may cause adverse effects through-
out the entire lifespan from fetal development to adulthood, through varying mechanisms. These include by
binding to hormone receptors, acting as agonists or antagonists, affecting hormone synthesis and metabolism,
altering hormone receptor numbers, or affecting circulating hormone concentrations (Gore et al. 2015).
One of the most well-known and studied examples of an EDC affecting female reproduction is the synthetic
estrogen diethylstilbestrol (DES), which was prescribed to pregnant women from the 1940s to the 1970s to
reduce the risk of pregnancy complications. It was, however, later discovered that females exposed to DES
in utero had an increased risk of adverse reproductive effects later in life such as vaginal cancer, earlier age
at menopause, endometriosis, reproductive site cancer, and infertility (Hatch et al. 2006; Hoover et al. 2012;
Palmer et al. 2001). Later, the ovarian dysgenesis syndrome hypothesis has been formed, proposing a common
fetal origin for an array of female reproductive disorders later in life, and the possible role of exposure to EDCs
during fetal life. These reproductive disorders include polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS), premature ovarian
insufficiency (POI), reproductive site cancers, and reduced fertility (Johansson et al. 2017; Gore et al. 2015).
Besides effects on the reproductive system, EDCs can also interfere with the thyroid hormone system, leading
to altered TH levels or action, which in itself may lead to adverse effects (Gore et al. 2015). However, while
both effects on the female reproductive system and the thyroid hormone system have been studied separately,
little is known about the interplay between the two, and only few studies have investigated secondary effects
of TH disruption during development of the female reproductive system (Radovanovi´c et al. 2012).
2.2
The Thyroid Hormone System
The thyroid hormone system is a complex system, involving three main endocrine organs, the hypothalamus,
the pituitary gland, and the thyroid gland, referred to as the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis (Figure
3
==PAGE:11==
1). THs, mainly triiodothyronine (T3) and thyroxine (T4), synthesized and secreted by the thyroid gland, play
an essential role in multiple processes in the body, including regulation of metabolism, growth, and muscle
function, and they have a critical role in fetal development, especially in development of the brain (Itoh 2019).
The synthesis and secretion of T3 and T4 are regulated through the HPT-axis. Thyrotropin-releasing hormone
(TRH) is produced in the hypothalamus and stimulates the secretion of thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)
from the anterior pituitary, which in turn stimulates the follicular cells in the thyroid gland to synthesize and
release T3 and T4. The maintenance of normal thyroid function depends on a negative feedback control sys-
tem, where circulating T3 and T4 levels inhibit both the hypothalamus and the pituitary, thereby reducing the
synthesis and secretion of TRH and TSH (Figure 1) (Silva et al. 2018). Biosynthesis of THs is a complex
process, involving several different enzymes as well as iodide. Iodide is transported into the thyroid follicle
through the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS) and pendrin. In the follicle, iodide is oxidized by thyroperoxidase
(TPO) and incorporated into the glycoprotein thyroglobulin, to form iodotyrosine scaffolds and subsequently
to T3 and T4, which are transported to the bloodstream. The pro-hormone T4 is the main hormone secreted by
the thyroid into the bloodstream and serves as a precursor to T3, which is the biologically active hormone. T4
is actively transported into cells, where deiodinase enzymes convert it to T3. (Silva et al. 2018).

sis and secretion of T3 and T4 from the thyroid gland. TRH

THs exert their effects by binding to TH receptors (TRs) in virtually all tissues of the body. The nuclear
receptors TRα and TRβ become activated upon TH binding. This activation regulates transcription factors in
the respective tissues, leading to the transcription of downstream genes (Shahid et al. 2023). Consequently,
4
==PAGE:12==
when TH levels are low, normal gene expression and tissue function may be impaired. This is particularly
critical during pregnancy, where the developing fetus is dependent on maternal thyroid hormone supply, as
the thyroid gland of the fetus is not fully functional until week 12 of gestation in humans and until GD21 in
rodents, making the maternal TH status crucial for fetal development (B´arez-L´opez et al. 2017; Zoeller et al.
2004). Maternal TH insufficiency can severely affect brain development in the offspring potentially leading
to retarded maturation, intellectual deficits and neurological impairment (Bernal 2022). Additionally, THs are
essential for the female reproductive system, and TH disruptions can lead to subfertility or infertility in both
humans and animals (Silva et al. 2018). Though the effects and mechanisms are not fully established, evidence
suggests that not only can TH disruptions lead to reproductive disorders in adult life, but that adequate supply
of THs during fetal development through the mother is essential for proper development of the reproductive
system, like it is the case with brain development (Luis et al. 2019).
2.3
Ovary Development
Development of the ovary begins during fetal life, and female reproductive health is therefore to a certain
degree determined at this life stage. Primordial germ cells migrate to the early gonadal structures that, at this
point, have still not developed down either a female or male path. The germ cells divide through mitosis and
begin to form clusters of cells, termed germ cell nests, and eventually differentiate into oogonia. In the 10th
gestation week in humans, 13.5 days post coitum (dpc) in mice, oogonia enter meiotic prophase, marking
the first apparent morphological ovarian differentiation (Aatsha et al. 2023). In females, meiosis arrests in
prophase I until puberty. Around 2-3 days before birth in mice, the germ cell nests begin breaking apart and
oocytes are surrounded by a single layer of somatic pre-granulosa cells, forming primordial follicles, in the
process termed follicle assembly (Johansson et al. 2017). Simultaneously, around two-thirds of the oocytes
undergo programmed cell death (Pepling 2012). The process of follicle assembly proceeds from the medullary
region of the ovaries to the cortex and continues until PND6 in rodents. Here, almost all remaining oocytes are
enclosed by granulosa cells, forming primordial follicles (Figure 2) (Aatsha et al. 2023; Johansson et al. 2017).
In humans, follicle assembly occurs during mid-gestation, however, the processes are similar to that of rodents
(Aatsha et al. 2023).
Before and throughout reproductive life, growth of some of the follicles is initiated in a process called initial
recruitment. The follicles develop through primordial, primary, and secondary stages before becoming antral.
At the antral stage most follicles undergo degeneration, or atresia. At sexual maturity under optimal hormone
stimulation with follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) during each reproductive cycle, a few of the growing folli-
cles are rescued and reach the preovulatory stage in a process called cyclical recruitment (Figure 3). Eventually
the follicle pool is depleted, which leads to reproductive senescence (McGee et al. 2000). EDCs can poten-
tially interfere with any of these processes (Johansson et al. 2017), and though the mechanisms are not fully
established insufficient TH levels may also contribute to adverse secondary effects in the ovaries (Aghajanova
et al. 2009; Wakim et al. 1993).

2.4
Thyroid-Disrupting Chemicals
Several chemicals, like polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), some pesticides, and bisphenol A (BPA), have been
shown to disrupt the thyroid hormone system through different mechanisms (Ramhøj et al. 2022; Gore et al.
2015). These include: i) interfering with regulation of the HPT-axis, ii) interfering with iodine uptake necessary
for TH synthesis through the NIS, iii) inhibiting the TPO enzyme responsible for iodination of thyroglobulin
for TH synthesis, iv) binding to TH distributing proteins in blood, v) interfering with TH metabolism by e.g.,
targeting deiodinase enzymes, and vi) increasing TH clearance from the blood by activating enzymes in the
liver (Gilbert et al. 2020; Gore et al. 2015). Chemicals can thus interfere with TH production, and by one or
more of the above-mentioned mechanisms cause alterations in circulating levels of TH (Figure 4). Altered TH
levels are known to cause several effects on the female reproductive system (see section 2.6 and 2.7). These
effects can be due to both direct actions of THs in the ovaries, but also to effects of THs on the bioavailability
of other hormones and growth factors that are essential to the female reproductive system (Silva et al. 2018).

2.5
Effect of Thyroid Hormones on Other Hormones
As the TH system is not a closed system, THs and the HPT-axis influence the availability of other hormones,
including ones involved in female reproduction, through e.g., interfering with transport, synthesis, and clear-
ance. Hence, when TH levels are altered, this can cause other hormone levels to either increase or decrease.
Hypothyroidism has been shown to increase levels of prolactin (PRL) in both human and animal studies (Silva
7
==PAGE:15==
et al. 2018). This is likely related to increased TRH levels under hypothyroid conditions, as lactotrophic cells
in the pituitary gland, like thyrotropic cells, express membrane receptors for TRH, resulting in increased pro-
lactin secretion (Pan et al. 1990). Excess PRL, or hyperprolactinemia, subsequently inhibits the pulsatile LH
secretion that happens before ovulation through inhibition of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) neurons
(Grattan et al. 2007).
Another way in which THs affect other hormones is by altering production of hepatic sex hormone-binding
globulin (SHBG), a transporter protein responsible for blood transport of sex steroids, testosterone, dihy-
drotestosterone, and estradiol (E2). Low TH levels is correlated with a reduction in SHBG, and consequently
lower levels of total circulating sex steroids, and an increase in the free fraction, while the opposite effect oc-
curs when TH levels are too high. Additionally, the rate of metabolic clearance of steroid hormones is reduced
both when TH levels are too low and too high. In addition to affecting transport and clearance, THs also seem
to influence the synthesis of sex steroids, E2 and testosterone, with reduced or elevated synthesis occurring
when TH levels are too low or too high, respectively. (Redmond 2004). These changes in hormone levels may
contribute to some of the adverse effects occurring because of TH disruptions (Silva et al. 2018).
2.6
Thyroid Disruptions in Female Reproduction
Even though the mechanisms are not yet fully established it has become clear that the female reproductive
system is dependent on adequate levels of THs. T3 and T4 have been identified in human follicular fluid,
and TRs are expressed by granulosa cells, oocytes, and ovarian stromal cells, highlighting the role of THs
in female reproduction, by directly targeting ovarian tissue (Aghajanova et al. 2009; Wakim et al. 1993). In
vitro studies have demonstrated how both growth of preantral follicles and the ovulatory rate are stimulated
by THs, and that T3, when combined with FSH, can enhance proliferation and reduce apoptosis in ovarian
granulosa cells (Kobayashi et al. 2009; Maruo et al. 1987; Silva et al. 2018). Additionally, the interaction
between gonadotropins and T3 has been shown to inhibit excessive androgen production by theca cells and
stimulate estrogen production through aromatization of androgens in granulosa cells (Gregoraszczuk et al.
1996). Hence, THs are involved in several physiological processes, like steroidogenesis, follicular develop-
ment and differentiation, as well as ovulation (Luis et al. 2019). However, the relationship between female
reproductive physiology and thyroid function is complex, and results are inconsistent.
One of the most common and studied instances of disorders of the thyroid hormone system is hypothyroidism,
which is characterized by low levels of circulating T3 and T4, and consequently high levels of TSH, as the
negative HPT-axis feedback loop fails to downregulate TSH production from the pituitary (Feldt-Rasmussen
et al. 2021). In human epidemiological studies, hypothyroidism is associated with, among others, irregular
menstrual cycles and premature ovarian failure (POF), and severe hypothyroidism is associated with failure of
ovulation (Krassas 2000; Redmond 2004; Kalantaridou et al. 1998). Additionally, hypothyroidism beginning
before puberty is associated with delays in the onset of puberty, accompanied by subsequent anovulatory
cycles (Krassas 2000). Pregnant women with untreated hypothyroidism have a higher risk of abortions in the
first trimester, stillbirth, or premature births, and untreated hypothyroidism may also lead to infertility (Figure
8
==PAGE:16==
5) (Concepci´on-Zavaleta et al. 2023). Thus, it is obvious that inadequate levels of TH lead to adverse effects
on female reproduction in humans. The mechanisms behind, however, are not well explored in human studies.
2.7
Hypothyroidism in Animal Studies
Like in human studies, animal studies investigating hypothyroidism and subsequent effects on ovary develop-
ment show differing results. This is probably due to different protocols, with differences in the way to induce
hypothyroidism, timing of induction and investigation, as well as methodology to evaluate the results. There is,
however, a wide consensus that TH insufficiency causes adverse effects in the ovaries. A summary of different
studies investigating effects of hypothyroidism in the ovaries is displayed in Tables 2, 3, and 4, with exposure
periods divided into pubertal- and adult exposure, perinatal exposure, and pre- and postnatal exposure, respec-
tively. The effects are also summarized in Figure 5.

2.7.1
Later Life Hypothyroidism
Several studies have focused on the effects of hypothyroidism in pubertal- and adult animals (Table 2), likely
due to observations in humans where hypothyroidism, as previously mentioned, results in ovulation- and fer-
tility problems, as well as disruptions in the timing of puberty (Krassas 2000; Concepci´on-Zavaleta et al. 2023;
Redmond 2004). Similar effects have been observed in animal models, with observations of irregular estrus
cycles potentially resulting from altered gonadotropin levels (Hatsuta et al. 2004; Armada-Dias et al. 2001).
While some studies report reduced LH levels, which could affect the estrus cycle (Hatsuta et al. 2004; Meng
et al. 2017), others find no effect on LH levels (Hapon et al. 2010; Armada-Dias et al. 2001). This discrepancy
could be due to differences in the timing of measurements, as LH levels fluctuate throughout the estrus cycle.
Multiple studies have also reported increased PRL levels in hypothyroid animals, which is likely due to the rise
in TRH associated with hypothyroidism, which in turn inhibits the preovulatory LH secretion (Hapon et al.
2010; Armada-Dias et al. 2001). Furthermore, while some studies show decreased ovulation rates as a conse-
quence of hypothyroidism (Mattheij et al. 1995), others report no effect on the ovulation rate despite reductions
in LH levels (Hapon et al. 2010).
In studies investigating follicular development in hypothyroid animals, the results are also inconsistent. For
instance, while one study found decreased numbers of antral follicles and corpora lutea (CL) (Armada-Dias
et al. 2001), another found no effect on these parameters, but did, however, observe a decrease in primordial,
primary and preantral follicles. At the same the latter showed no effect on the percentage of atretic follicles
in the hypothyroid animals, suggesting that the decrease in follicle numbers was not due to follicular atresia
(Meng et al. 2017). Hence, from observations in pubertal- and adult animals, hypothyroidism does cause
effects in the female reproductive system (Figure 5), though very varying, likely due to different treatment
regimes. Combined with the knowledge that the ovary express TRs during development, it is presumable to
believe that developmental hypothyroidism may also cause effects in the female reproductive system that could
have long-lasting consequences.

2.7.2
Developmental and Postnatal Hypothyroidism
Even though there is limited evidence as to how TH insufficiency affects the developing ovary, some studies
have looked at different endpoints in the ovaries of animals with hypothyroid mothers during gestation and
in the neonatal period (Table 3 and 4). Lukovi´c et al. investigated developmental hypothyroidism from the
beginning of gestation through lactation, and observed a decrease in the number of nests, oogonia, and oocytes
per nest, as well as an increase in primordial follicles, and a decrease in primary and secondary follicles in
rats on PND4. On PND7, an increase in primary, secondary, and preantral follicles was observed alongside an
increase in proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) expression in oocytes and granulosa cells of primordial
follicles, pointing towards increased cell proliferation in primordial follicles of hypothyroid animals. All this
points towards an acceleration of primordial follicle formation, as well as premature folliculogenesis due to
developmental hypothyroidism (Lukovi´c et al. 2016).
Overall, developmental and neonatal hypothyroidism appears to affect folliculogenesis, by either accelerating
or impairing it, with observations of altered numbers of primordial, primary, secondary, or antral follicles.
These changes have been observed in studies with the time of investigation in neonatal and infantile life-stages
(Lukovi´c et al. 2016; Fedail et al. 2014; Chan et al. 1995), as well as in older animals (Radovanovi´c et al.
2012; Meng et al. 2016), indicating both immediate and long-lasting effects. It should, however, be noted that
in other studies there does not seem to be an effect on folliculogenesis (Park et al. 2021; Zertashia et al. 2002),
which again could be due to differences in the methodology of the studies.
Additionally, follicular atresia also seems to be affected, with some studies showing increased follicular atresia
when the time of investigation is in later life stages (Radovanovi´c et al. 2012; Meng et al. 2016; Dijkstra et al.
1996), suggesting that effects may persist into adulthood. However, no effect on atresia or apoptosis markers
was observed when the time of investigation was in the neonatal period (Lukovi´c et al. 2016). Results from
animal studies also point toward effects on ovulation, including a reduction in the number and size of antral
follicles and CL in pubertal and adult animals following both developmental and neonatal hypothyroidism, as
it is the case when hypothyroidism is induced at later life stages (Radovanovi´c et al. 2012; Meng et al. 2016;
Dijkstra et al. 1996).

2.7.3
Molecular Changes in the Ovaries
Most research has mainly focused on hormonal and morphological changes in the ovaries, and while this
provides valuable insights, it does not offer explanations of the mechanisms behind. For this purpose, investi-
gating effects on a molecular level may act alongside physiological endpoints to unveil underlying mechanisms
of effect (Kugathas et al. 2023).
A few studies have looked at molecular effects alongside physiological endpoints. Saha et al. investigated
the extracellular matrix, which is an important factor for normal ovary function, following postnatal hypothy-
roidism. They looked at extracellular matrix proteins, including collagen, and found that gene levels of pro-
collagen lysine 2-oxoglutarate 5-dioxygenase (Plod), the key enzyme for collagen biosynthesis, as well as
collagen I and III protein levels, were decreased. Additionally, they found an increase in the gene levels of
12
==PAGE:20==
matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), which are proteinases capable of degrading components of the extracel-
lular matrix. It was hypothesized that the simultaneous downregulation of Plod isoforms and upregulation
of MMPs, result in disturbances in the collagen status, which could potentially disrupt the ovarian structure,
follicular development, and steroidogenesis, which are all dependent on the extracellular matrix (Saha et al.
2005).
Other studies have investigated oxidative stress markers in the ovaries of animals after developmental hypothy-
roidism. Meng et al. found an increased expression of mRNA and protein of SOD1, a producer of reactive
H2O2, as well as a decreased expression of several antioxidant genes. Alongside these findings, they found
that 4-hydroxynonenal, an oxidative stress marker, seemed to be enhanced in hypothyroid ovaries. From this
it seems that hypothyroidism somehow disturbs the redox balance, resulting in oxidative damage, which could
lead to an increased level of follicular atresia (Meng et al. 2016).

Though it is difficult to draw conclusions as to how hypothyroidism affects development and function of the
ovary due to different study methodologies and, therefore, varying results, it is certain that TH insufficiency
causes adverse reproductive effects. Effects on folliculogenesis, steroidogenesis, ovulation, ovarian morphol-
ogy, oxidative stress, and the ovarian extracellular matrix has been suggested. However, most of the results
highlighted here are from animal studies either with induction of hypothyroidism after some of the critical
windows in ovary development highlighted in Section 2.3, or with a time of investigation at later life stages.
The need for investigation into immediate effects of hypothyroidism during development is therefore much
needed.
There is a lack of evidence from a molecular level, as most studies have focused on physiological endpoints
when trying to establish effects of hypothyroidism in the ovaries. Changes at a molecular level, such as gene
expression or protein levels, may not only serve as explanations for underlying mechanisms of observed effects,
but also have the potential to serve as molecular effect biomarkers, which might be useful in future testing
strategies (Kugathas et al. 2023). This study aims to fill in some of the current research gaps by identifying
molecular effect biomarkers of hypothyroidism and the effect on ovary development, potentially shedding light
on how underlying mechanisms influence reproductive outcomes in both early and later life stages.

Materials and Methods
3.1
Study Design
To investigate the effects of developmental hypothyroidism on ovarian development and identify potential early
molecular biomarkers of effect, different methods were applied. To induce developmental hypothyroidism,
an animal study was conducted prior to this project using the TPO-inhibitor 6-Propyl-2-thiouracil (PTU) in
pregnant rat dams. Ovaries were collected from offspring at PND6 and PND80 for analysis. The following
methods were applied:
1. Histological analysis in PND6 ovaries to assess early morphological changes:
a) H&E staining followed by RNAscope in situ hybridization targeting Ddx4 mRNA as an oocyte
marker, to semi-quantitatively assess follicle counts in PTU-exposed vs. control ovaries.
b) Immunofluorescence staining (IF) of DDX4 protein to identify and semi-quantitatively assess germ
cell nest counts in PTU-exposed vs. control ovaries.
2. BRB-seq in PND6 and PND80 ovaries to identify transcriptional changes associated with developmental
hypothyroidism. Candidate genes for early molecular effect biomarkers were chosen for follow-up in
situ analysis in PND6 ovaries.

3. RNAscope Multiplex Fluorescent assay in PND6 ovaries to localize selected candidate genes in relation
to oocytes and granulosa cells and semi-quantitatively assess their differential expression in situ.
3.2
Animals and Exposure
The animal study was carried out prior to this project at the animal facilities at the National Food Institute,
Technical University of Denmark (DTU Food). Adult Sprague-Dawley rats (Charles River Laboratories, Ger-
many) were time-mated and supplied to DTU on gestation day (GD)3. All animals were kept under 12 h
light/12 h dark cycle by artificial illumination at 22 °C ± 1 °, humidity 55% ±5, and ventilation changing air
10 times per hour. They were fed a commercial diet (soy and lucerne free based on Altromin 1314) and tap
water ad libitum.
The animals were exposed to PTU in vehicle at either 1.25 mg/kg bodyweight (bw)/day or 2.50 mg/kg bw/day.
The control group received 2 mL/kg bw/day of corn oil, used as vehicle of exposure. The animals were dosed
by gavage from GD7-PND28, except for GD22 and the day after birth. Pups were weaned at PND28 and
dissections conducted on either PND6 or PND80 (Figure 6), where the animals were weighed and both ovaries
were collected and trimmed from the fat pad. One ovary was snap frozen in liquid nitrogen and hereafter stored
at -80 °C and the other placed in 10% formaldehyde and processed for embedding in paraffin (Figure 6). The
experiment was approved by the Danish Animal Experiments Inspectorate and monitored by the DTU in-house
Animal Welfare Committee.

3.3
Bulk RNA Barcoding and Sequencing
To identify transcriptional effects of hypothyroidism in the PND6 and PND80 ovaries, 3’ bulk RNA barcod-
ing and sequencing (BRB-seq) was performed on the snap frozen ovaries (Alpern et al. 2019). The analysis
was performed by Associate Professor Fr´ed´eric Chalmel, INSERM, Rennes, France, prior to this project. The
method relies on sample barcoding and multiplexing principles for bulk RNA profiling, allowing for efficient
and cost-effective RNA sequencing (Dainese 2022) (Figure 7). During the first step of complementary DNA
(cDNA) library preparation, barcoded oligo (dT) primers are added to the samples to uniquely tag each indi-
vidual RNA sample. The barcoded primers consist of an adaptor sequence for primer annealing, an identifier
barcode, unique for each RNA sample, and a random unique molecular identifier (UMI) for each mRNA
molecule. After reverse transcription into cDNA, RNA samples are pooled to shorten the time and cost of
library preparation, by carrying out the remaining steps in bulk. Following reverse transcription, double-strand
15
==PAGE:23==
(ds) cDNA is synthesized. The ds cDNA is subsequently fragmented and tagged using Tn5 transposase, which
cleaves the cDNA and ligates adapters for amplification. The fragmented cDNA is then indexed and amplified
using a unique dual indexing strategy (P5 and P7 in Figure 7) that minimizes the risk of misassigning barcodes
during sequencing. Lastly, the final cDNA library is sequenced, and differential gene expression analysis is
performed to identify differentially expressed genes between different groups (Alpern et al. 2019; Dainese
2022).
Figure 7 – BRB-seq workflow: Each well of the BRB-seq plate contains a distinct barcode adaptor label that is unique
for the RNA sample. After reverse transcription, the barcoded samples are pooled for the cDNA library preparation
and library sequencing. After pooling of the samples, double-stranded cDNA is synthesized by PCR. Afterwards the
cDNA undergoes tagmentation with Tn5, in which DS cDNA is fragmented and tagged by ligating adaptors. The cDNA
library is subsequently indexed and amplified. (Image created with BioRender.com, adapted from (Alpern et al. 2019)

ter (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and RNA quality was assessed using a 2100 Bioanalyzer Instrument (Agilent
Technologies, CA, USA). Samples with an RNA integrity number (RIN)-score > 8 were included for subse-

3.3.2
Experimental set-up
The BRB-seq experiments were performed as previously described (Kugathas et al. 2023) on RNA samples
from PND6 and PND80 ovaries. A first step reverse transcription and template switching were performed
using 4 µL RNA with a concentration of 2.5 ng/µL and sample-specific barcoded oligo-dT. cDNA was then
pooled, purified, and ds cDNA was synthesized by PCR. The sequencing library was subsequently generated
by tagmentation using 50 ng of ds cDNA with the Illumina Nextera XT Kit. The library was sequenced
using a NovaSeq sequencing system and reads were aligned to the rat reference transcriptome. Differentially
expressed genes (DEGs) were identified based on the statistical comparisons: i) PND6 controls vs PND80
controls; ii) controls vs PTU-exposed samples at PND6 at each dose; iii) controls vs PTU-exposed samples at
PND80 at each dose. For each comparison, sequential statistical filtration steps were applied: i) the median
gene expression value of all samples (1.078) was used as background cutoff; ii) a fold-change of at least
1.3; and, iii) a p-value cutoff of 0.5 adjusted with the Banjamini and Hochberg method (Benjaminit et al.
1995). A gene ontology term enrichment analysis was performed using the Annotation Mapping Expression
and Network (AMEN) suite tools. Annotation terms in a gene cluster were considered enriched when the false
discovery rate (FDR)-adjusted p-value was ≤0.05. Fold changes were calculated from the expression dataset
in Appendix E.
3.3.3
Selection of Genes for Further Analysis
To identify genes of interest for further analysis with in situ hybridization, a set of criteria was determined to
create a list of potential candidates. As minimum, one of the following should be fulfilled: i) genes differen-
tially expressed at both PND6 and PND80; ii) genes differentially expressed in both dose-groups within the
same age group in a dose-response manner; or iii) genes with a fold change > 1.80 or ≤0.60. Subsequently,
genes with low expression levels (≤1.5 rlog-transformed expression data (counts)) in either dose-group were
discarded, as we hypothesized, they may be difficult to detect in a histological assay. The remaining genes were
screened for relevant information, with emphasis on expected functions that could be of relevance for ovary
development or function, using NCBI GenBank (NCBI 2022). Lastly, two genes were selected for further
analysis based on their possible relevance for ovary function: activin A receptor type 2B (Acvr2b) and fatty
acid binding protein 4 (Fabp4).
3.4
Histological Staining
The paraffin embedded ovaries from PND6 pups were sectioned at 5µm on a HM450 microtome, and 16
sections from the middle of the ovaries were mounted on glass slides. During sectioning the sections were
placed in a cold ion-exchange water bath with 5-10 mL 70 % ethanol, and subsequently in a 41 °C water bath
to ensure flat sections. The sections were transferred to SuperFrost+ glass slides and dried overnight at 37 °C
and then stored at -20 °C.
3.4.1
Hematoxylin & Eosin Staining
H&E staining was performed on ovary sections from PND6 pups (n = 6) to assess ovarian morphology. First,
the paraffin was removed from the sections by deparaffinization to ensure that the staining reagents properly
adhere to the tissue. The sections were heat treated at 60 °C for 60 minutes, and subsequently washed in
17
==PAGE:25==
petroleum. The tissue sections were rehydrated through a graded ethanol-water series (99% to 70 %), to
replace paraffin molecules with water molecules in the tissue. The slides were stained with hematoxylin for 1
minute, placed in a running water bath for 5 minutes, and then counterstained with eosin for 2 minutes. The
slides were then dehydrated through a graded ethanol-water series (70 % to 99 %). Lastly, the slides were
mounted with Eukitt. The detailed protocol is found in Appendix A.
3.5
Immunofluorescence Staining
Immunofluorescence Staining (IF) was performed to visualize germ cell nests, through protein staining of
DDX4, identifying oocytes, and DAPI to stain the cell nuclei. IF is an immunochemical technique used in
various biological research areas to, among others, visualize the distribution and localization of proteins within
tissues. The method relies on fluorescently labeled antibodies binding to target proteins with high specificity.
In this study indirect IF is employed, which ensures high sensitivity and allows for detection of more than one
target. Indirect IF relies on primary antibodies binding to the target epitope and secondary fluorophore-tagged
secondary antibodies, binding to the primary antibodies (Figure 10). Signal is detected using a fluorescent
microscope (Im et al. 2019).

microscope (Image created with BioRender.com).

min. The slides were washed in PBS for 3 x 5 min and transferred to a Sequenza slide rack. Blocking was

rabbit – polyclonal), was diluted in 1% BSA in PBS at 1:300. 250 µL primary antibody was added per slide,
which were left to incubate overnight at 4 °C. The next day the slides were left for 30 min at room tempera-

3.6
RNAscope in situ Hybridization
The RNAscope® assay allows for visualization of single RNA molecules per cells in tissue samples mounted on
slides through ISH, while preserving tissue morphology, using a novel hybridization-based signal amplification
system and unique probe design (Figure 9). Two different in situ hybridization (ISH) RNAscope assays were
applied for different purposes. The RNAscope® 2.5 RED assay was performed to identify oocytes using the
marker gene Ddx4 and to spatially visualize and count follicles in the ovaries, while the RNAscope® Multiplex
Fluorescent assay was applied to quantify the expression of the two selected genes, Acvr2b and Fabp4, as well
as to localize the two genes in the ovaries in relation to oocytes and granulosa cells. Additionally, a follow-up
RED assay was performed to verify results from the fluorescent assay with one of the genes, Acvr2b. While
the 2.5 RED assay only allows for detection of a single mRNA target, the Multiplex Fluorescent assay allows
for detection of up to four mRNA targets simultaneously. Each target probe is designed to be detected with
channel-specific amplification systems, C1, C2, C3, and C4, and different fluorophores are assigned to each
channel making it possible to separate them with a fluorescent microscope (ACDBio 2019).
3.6.1
Oocyte Marker, Ddx4, and Acvr2b mRNA Detection with 2.5 RED Assay
RNAscope® 2.5 RED assay was performed on ovary sections from PND6 pups (n = 6) using the reagents
and protocol from ACD Bio (ACDBio 2019). Before treatment with probes and detection reagents, sections
were deparaffinized with petroleum followed by hydration with 99% ethanol. The sections were subsequently
incubated with Hydrogen Peroxide for 10 minutes to block peroxidase activity within the tissue. The tissue was
then boiled for 15 minutes in a microwave in a solution of RNAscope Target Retrieval Reagents to expose the
target mRNA in the tissue. A hydrophobic barrier was created around each tissue section and dried overnight
at room temperature. On day 2, the sections were incubated with RNAscope® Protease Plus for 15 minutes at
40 °C in a HybEZ™II Oven (ACD Bio) to permeabilize cell membranes in the tissue and expose target mRNA.
The sections were then incubated with the probe against the gene of interest at 40 °C in the HybEZ™II Oven for
two hours. The assay was verified with a positive control probe targeting the housekeeping gene cyclophilin B
(Ppib) and a negative control probe targeting the bacterial gene dihydrodipicolinate reductase (DapB). Sections
were then incubated with different amplifiers (AMP 1-6) at either 40 °C or at room temperature for 15 or 30
minutes with a washing step with wash buffer in between each hybridization. To detect signal, the sections
were incubated with fluoro-labelled probes using a mixture of 1:60 Fast RED-B to Fast RED-A for 10 minutes
at room temperature. The sections were counterstained with Mayer’s Hematoxylin for ∼5 seconds to lightly
stain the cell nuclei. Lastly, the sections were mounted with VectaMount. The detailed protocol and a list of
reagents and probes are found in Appendix B.

and a negative control probe targeting DapB in all four channels. Sections were then incubated with different

wash buffer in between each hybridization. The three dyes, TSA Vivid Fluorophore- 520, 570, and 650, were

3.7
Semi-Quantitative Estimation of Follicle Counts and Germ Cell Nests
Sections were imaged at 40x in a Panoramic Midi II digital slide scanner (3DHISTECH Ltd., Budapest Hun-
gary) and images were evaluated with the CaseViewer Software (version 2.4) from 3DHISTECH. Primordial,
primary and secondary follicles were counted in one section per animal using the sections stained for Ddx4
in the RNAscope® RED assay. Primordial follicles were characterized as an oocyte surrounded by squamous
pre-granulosa cells, primary follicles were characterized as an oocyte surrounded by one layer of cuboidal
granulosa cells, and secondary follicles were characterized as an oocyte surrounded by more than one layer of
cuboidal granulosa cells. Only follicles with a visible oocyte and with positive staining for Ddx4 were counted.
The counting was carried out blinded with respect to dose group. The area for each ovary section was mea-
sured using the imaging software QuPath (version 0.5.1) and the follicle counts were normalized relative to the
measured area.
The tissue sections stained for DDX4 protein with IF were investigated in QuPath. Germ cell nests were
characterized as more than two oocytes in a cluster not separated by granulosa cells. Only oocytes stained red
for DDX4 and with visible nuclei stained with DAPI were included. Nests were annotated and counted in the
control and PTU-exposed groups including two sections per animal. Counting was carried out blinded with
respect to dose group.
3.8
Imaging and Quantification of Acvr2b and Fabp4
For fluorescent slides, the exposure times for the different channels were optimized for each of the experiments
before imaging. The exposure times were fixed for all sections within the same experiment. Percent positive
signal area within whole ovary sections from the RNAscope® Multiplex assay was quantified using Python
(version 3.13). The Acvr2b and Fabp4 signal channels were separated from the marker gene and DAPI channels
using CaseViewer, creating grayscale images, which were then loaded into Python. To quantify the image data,
the channel image was converted into a numpy array, and it was verified that the images were single channel
images. A threshold was set based on a selected percentile of pixel intensity values, and a binary mask was
created. Pixels above the threshold were assigned a value of 1, and all others were set to 0. The threshold
was fine-tuned by visually comparing the original image with the binary mask to best isolate true signals while
removing background noise. The total signal area was calculated by counting the signal pixels (those with
a value above 1) and multiplying this count by the known area per pixel (0.029876 µm2). This approach
provided a quantitative measure of the signal area in square micrometers. The area of the whole section was
measured to calculate % positive signal area within each section. The negative control slides were analyzed in
the same way, and the % positive area of the negative control was subtracted from the area of the sample slides
before analysis. The two marker genes, Ddx4 in oocytes and Foxl2 in granulosa cells, were quantified in the
same manner. This was done to ensure that a potential difference between groups was not due to a difference in
21
==PAGE:29==
the distribution of cell types in the tissue. The Python code is provided in Appendix F.1. The quantified signal
areas were averaged for the two slides to get signal per animal (See data in Appendix F.3 and F.5).
The RNAscope® RED assay with the Acvr2b gene was analyzed using QuPath (version 0.5.1). As with the
Multiplex Assay, the gene expression was quantified by measuring the area of the probe. Representative color
values for hematoxylin, background and probe were identified and loaded into QuPath. Using the ”Create
thresholder” function in the program, the percentage of red-stained tissue versus non-stained tissue was quan-
tified. Additionally, the number of cells were counted in each section. The signal area and cells count were
averaged for the two slides per animal. See Appendix F.2 for a detailed description of the method and Appendix
F.4 for the data.
3.9
Statistical Analysis
Statistical analyses were carried out using R (version 4.4.2). P-values ≤0.05 were considered statistically
significant. Normality was tested with QQ-plots as well as Shapiro-Wilks tests. Homogeneity of variance
was inspected visually by plotting the residuals in the data alongside simulated residuals with homogeneous
variance.
Comparisons of primordial and secondary follicle counts between control and PTU-exposed groups were per-
formed using one-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s post hoc test. Since primary follicle counts did not
appear to follow a normal distribution, they were analyzed using a non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test with
Dunn’s post hoc test. Similarly, germ cell nest counts were analyzed using the Kruskal-Wallis test with Dunn’s
post hoc test due to their non-normal distribution. For Acvr2b and Fabp4 signal area, the data seemed to follow
normality and homogeneity of variance. Therefore, comparisons between control and PTU-exposed animals
were conducted using one-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s post hoc test.

Results
4.1
Induction of Hypothyroidism through PTU Exposure
Though not a part of this project, TH levels were measured to confirm hypothyroidism in offspring on GD21
and PND16. On GD21 T4 levels were reduced by 31% and 97%, and on PND16 T4 levels were reduced by 78%
and 99% in the PTU-1.25 and PTU-2.50 groups, respectively (Strand et al., unpublished). This is supported
by Axelstad et al., reporting significant decreases in T4 levels and increase in relative thyroid gland weights
at PND16 after exposure to 0.8, 1.6, or 2.4 mg/kg bw/day (Axelstad et al. 2008), and in newer, unpublished
studies also conducted by the Research group for Molecular and Reproductive Toxicology.
4.2
Decreased Body Weight Following PTU Exposure
Body weight was assessed in the PTU-exposed pups on PND6 (data not shown). The bw decreased by 16.5%
from 13,2 g in controls to 11.1 g in the PTU-2.50 group (p = 0.0066). Although significant, the decreased
bw may not be due to toxicity, but rather a result of reduced growth and delayed development, caused by
reduction in THs. No effect was observed in pups from the PTU-1.25 group, and PTU exposure did not cause
overt toxicity effects in either dams or offspring, supporting that the lower bw is due to reduced growth and
delayed development. In the PND80 animals, the effect on body weight in the PTU-2.50 group was no longer
observed.
4.3
Germ Cell Nest and Follicle Counts
PND6 ovaries were stained for the DDX4 protein in oocytes to assess the number of germ cell nests (Figure
10A). There were generally few nests, with a large proportion of the animals containing no nests. There was
no significant difference between control and PTU-exposed animals, however, there seemed to be a tendency
towards more nests in the PTU-2.50 group (Figure 10B).
PND6 ovary sections were stained with H&E, to determine whether the developmental hypothyroidism affected
follicle numbers, but the heterogeneous structure of the tissue at this stage made it difficult to separate oocytes
from other cell types. An RNAscope ® 2.5 RED assay was therefore applied using the Ddx4 mRNA as an oocyte
marker (Figure 12). Primordial, primary, and secondary follicles were subsequently counted in one section per
animal and compared between control and PTU-exposed groups. There were no effects on primordial, primary,
or secondary follicle counts between groups (Figure 11).

Discussion
The aim of this project was to investigate the effects of hypothyroidism on PND6 ovaries to help identify
molecular biomarkers of effect associated with ovarian disruption due to thyroid-disrupting chemicals. This
was investigated by assessing early morphological effects through semi-quantitative assessments of germ cell
nest and follicle counts. Additionally, transcriptional effects in PND6 and PND80 ovaries were analyzed by
BRB-seq, and two genes, Acvr2b and Fabp4, were selected as potential biomarker candidates and followed up
with RNAscope in situ hybridization in PND6 ovaries.
5.1
Acvr2b Expression and Potential Effects of Downregulation
The activin receptor plays a major role in ovarian function during adulthood and development as it facilitates
signaling of both activins and inhibins. This is evident in activin receptor type II knockout female mice, which
have suppressed pituitary and serum FSH concentrations, follicle development blocked at the antral stage and
are infertile (Nishimori et al. 1996). In the present study, Acvr2b mRNA was expressed in both oocytes and
granulosa cells, consistent with previous findings in both human and rodent ovaries (Wijayarathna et al. 2016;
Drummond et al. 2002). The findings from BRB-seq were not quantitatively confirmed by RNAscope assays,
which is perhaps not surprising as BRB-seq, being highly sensitive, can detect low expression levels, whereas
RNAscope is a semi-quantitative analysis. However, even with only two randomly chosen sections from each
ovary, the quantification was close to statistical significance in the fluorescent assay, providing further support
for the BRB-seq findings. Given its role and localization in both granulosa cells and oocytes, downregulation
could have adverse effects on ovarian function and reproduction.
Both activin and inhibin act as autocrine and paracrine regulators of folliculogenesis (Findlay et al. 2001;
Findlay 1993). During postnatal follicular development, expression patterns of activin receptors and signaling
pathway components, including Smads, along with their co-localization with activin in growing and antral
follicles, indicate that activin A promotes follicle differentiation (Drummond et al. 2002). Furthermore, it
seems that activin, possibly with FSH, has an autocrine action on granulosa cells, promoting differentiation
during secondary and antral stages of folliculogenesis (Findlay 1993). Interestingly, the hypothesized effect of
Acvr2b downregulation on folliculogenesis aligns with research on developmental hypothyroidism’s impact on
the ovary. Previous studies suggest that developmental hypothyroidism may influence folliculogenesis, though
findings vary, with reports of both impairment and acceleration (Fedail et al. 2014; Lukovi´c et al. 2016).
Although this study found no effect on follicle counts in PND6 ovaries, further research is needed to assess
whether folliculogenesis could be affected early on with a more robust quantification using a larger group size.
Additionally, evidence suggests activin to play a role in the earliest stages of follicle formation, as admin-
istration of excess activin increases the number of primordial follicles in mice (Bristol-Gould et al. 2006).
Moreover, interrupted activin signaling in transgenic mice models, leads to a range of ovarian pathologies,
including formation of multi-oocyte follicles (MOFs). This indicates an important role of activin in the estab-
lishment of the early follicle pool, possibly playing a role in nest breakdown, as MOFs occur during the early
stages of follicle formation (Pepling et al. 2018; Kipp et al. 2007). A tendency towards delayed nest breakdown
was observed in the present study, with seemingly more nests in the PTU-2.50 group, however, not statistically
significant. The lack of statistical significance is likely due to the group size, and while no nests were observed
in half the animals, the others contained more nests than the control and PTU-1.25 groups, indicating differ-
ences in sensitivity to exposure. A larger group size is therefore needed to dismiss an effect on delayed nest
32
==PAGE:40==
breakdown. It should be mentioned that the possible delay in nest breakdown could be due to the reduction in
body weight observed in the PTU-2.50 group, which suggests a delay in growth and development, rather than
to Acvr2b downregulation. This makes it difficult to separate effects of transcriptional changes and decreases
in body weight. Limited research exists on nest breakdown and follicle assembly following developmental
hypothyroidism, though Lukovi´c et al. reported fewer nests and oocytes per nest on PND4 after perinatal PTU
exposure (Lukovi´c et al. 2016), contrasting with our findings. Although this study shows no significant effects
on follicle counts or germ cell nests, previous research highlights the need for a more in-depth analysis of
these physiological endpoints and their correlation with Acvr2b expression. Notably, as the BRB-seq analy-
sis revealed a downregulation of Acvr2b RNA in PND6 pups. Acvr2b expression was not downregulated in
PND80 animals. However, its early-life downregulation could contribute to lasting effects on the follicle pool
and folliculogenesis into puberty or adulthood, potentially leading to conditions like POI, early reproductive
senescence, or reduced fertility (Johansson et al. 2017).
Besides its role in follicle formation and folliculogenesis, activin is thought to be involved in oocyte maturation.
Activin A seems to accelerate meiotic oocyte maturation in vitro in both rats and humans (Sadatsuki et al. 1993;
Alak et al. 1998). Silva et al. examined the expression patterns of activin subunits and receptors in fetal human
ovaries at 14-21 weeks gestation, finding both activin and its receptors to be expressed in oogonia. They also
demonstrated that stimulating ovarian fragment cultures with activin A increased proliferation and survival
of oogonia, highlighting activin as an autocrine and paracrine regulator of germ cell proliferation during the
period of development leading up to primordial follicle formation (Silva et al. 2004). This is supported by the
present study, as Acvr2b transcripts were primarily located in oocytes in the RNAscope RED assay, suggesting
the receptor to play an important role here, and that downregulation could have important implications for the
oocyte. Although this study has focused on postnatal and adult expression of the Acvr2b, hypothyroidism may
also affect its expression during gestation, potentially impacting meiosis. The initiation of meiosis is a key event
during development, as it triggers the subsequent processes of nest breakdown and follicle assembly (Johansson
et al. 2017). The observed tendency of delays in nest breakdown could therefore also stem from disruptions
in meiosis. Follow-up experiments using ovaries from earlier developmental stages, before primordial follicle
assembly (e.g., GD17-21), after maternal PTU exposure could help determine whether Acvr2b downregulation
occurs prior to PND6, with potential consequences for meiotic oocyte maturation and survival.
Together, these findings emphasize the crucial role of activin and its receptors in ovarian development. Acvr2b
downregulation as a consequence of developmental hypothyroidism could impair meiotic oocyte maturation,
follicle formation, and folliculogenesis, potentially affecting reproductive function both immediately and long-
term (Johansson et al. 2017).
5.2
Fabp4 Expression and Potential Effects of Downregulation
Fabp4 was downregulated in both PND6 and PND80 ovaries following developmental hypothyroidism, sug-
gesting a lasting effect, and potential impacts of this downregulation are therefore interesting to consider. The
gene encodes a protein, which is a part of the fatty acid binding protein family, consisting of highly conserved,
cytoplasmic proteins that bind long-chain fatty acids and other hydrophobic ligands (NCBI 2022). The fatty
acid-binding proteins are all involved in transport and metabolism of intracellular free fatty acids and facilitate
the transfer of fatty acids and lipid regulons within cells, aiding the regulation of intracellular signaling path-
ways (Wang et al. 2009). Given its strong association to adipose tissue and metabolism (Wang et al. 2009),
it can be speculated that the observed Fabp4 downregulation in the PND6 ovaries is linked to lower body
33
==PAGE:41==
weights of the PTU-exposed pups, although the weight reduction might not necessarily reflect a reduction in
fat tissue. However, since downregulation persisted in PND80 ovaries despite normalized body weights, other
mechanisms are likely involved.
Fabp4 mRNA was present in granulosa cells, consistent with prior studies in human ovaries (Hu et al. 2011).
Additionally, Fabp4 mRNA was localized in clusters in what appeared to be stromal cells, due to their localiza-
tion in the periphery of the ovaries and not around the follicles, which would have suggested location in theca
cells. The presence of Fabp4 mRNA in the ovarian stroma has, to our knowledge, not previously been reported.
Most studies in ovarian biology have focused on the functional units of the ovaries, the follicles, and the role of
the ovarian stroma and its function is therefore not well understood (Kinnear et al. 2020). The effect of Fabp4
downregulation in the ovarian stroma is therefore difficult to predict. Deconvolution analysis of the BRB-seq
data would be an interesting follow-up to this study, as it could help determine whether Fabp4 downregulation
primarily occurs in granulosa or stromal cells.
Fatty acids may be involved in modulating ovarian functions, including apoptosis of granulosa cells. FABP4
has been localized in atretic antral follicles in mature mice, predominantly in apoptotic granulosa cells. Though
its mechanisms and role in granulosa cell apoptosis is unclear, FABP4 has been suggested to interact with per-
oxisome proliferator activated receptor (PPAR) by acting as a vehicle of long chain fatty acids, the ligands of
PPARγ. PPARγ is also expressed in granulosa cells and appears to modulate both apoptosis and prolifera-
tion of these cells (Nourani et al. 2005). Apoptosis is an important process during nest breakdown and follicle
assembly, where nest breakdown and oocyte loss occur simultaneously, but the mechanisms are not well under-
stood (Pepling 2012). It could be speculated that a decrease in Fabp4 expression might reduce apoptosis in the
ovaries of PTU-2.50 exposed animals. However, this seems to conflict with the present study, as the seeming
delayed development in this group suggests that apoptosis remains necessary for nest breakdown. Investigating
apoptosis in PND6 ovaries could clarify whether Fabp4 downregulation affects this process. Overall, findings
indicate that Fabp4 influences female reproduction, particularly follicular atresia, but further research is needed
to establish effects of its downregulation in both early development and adulthood.
5.3
Imaging and Quantification Limitations
The RNAscope Multiplex Fluorescent assay was used in an attempt to simultaneously localize and quantify
the expression of Acvr2b and Fabp4. Fluorescent staining techniques, including RNAscope, are widely applied
for visualization and localization of cell structures, proteins, and genes (Alhede et al. 2018). The RNAscope
Fluorescent Multiplex assay was selected for its high specificity in detecting single mRNA molecules, making
it an ideal follow-up to RNA-sequencing. While BRB-seq provides sequencing data from the whole ovary,
it lacks information on localization of genes. However, RNAscope Fluorescent assay has previously mainly
been applied for localization and visualization rather than quantification, making validation of the quantitative
results challenging, as established methodologies for comparisons are absent.
Before scanning and analyzing the slides, negative control slides were inspected under a fluorescent micro-
scope, revealing unexpected signal in both the red and yellow fluorophore channels across all negative controls.
Autofluorescence was ruled out as no signal was detected in control experiments without the red fluorophore.
Similarly, pixel intensity comparisons showed no difference between negative controls and sample slides, ex-
cluding contrast-enhancement from the software as a cause. Spectral overlap from the red to the yellow channel
was observed, but additional tests indicated that this alone could not explain the yellow signal in negative con-
34
==PAGE:42==
trols. The cause remains uncertain, and additional troubleshooting is required to determine whether the issue
may originate from tissue binding of the fluorophores or the negative control probe itself. Staining experiments
without the negative control probe may clarify the observed artifacts.
Naturally, the analysis and interpretation of the fluorescent assay should be approached cautiously, as the
presence of signal in the negative control slides raises the possibility of false positive signals in the sample
slides. When comparing Acvr2b gene expression between the fluorescent and RED assays, larger signal areas
were detected in the fluorescent assay, though whether this discrepancy reflects false positive signals or higher
sensitivity of the fluorescent assay remains unclear. Localization of the Ddx4 marker in the sample slides
were restricted to oocytes as expected, whereas negative controls displayed scattered red signal throughout the
tissue. This suggests that false positive signals may not impact sample slides, but for Acvr2b conclusions are
challenging to draw, as the expression pattern cannot be independently verified with certainty. The localization
to oocytes and granulosa cells were, however, verified with the RED assay, and the main concern is therefore
enlargement of the quantitative results.
To ensure consistent quantification, exposure times for Ddx4, Foxl2, and either Acvr2b or Fabp4 channels
were standardized within each experiment. However, brightness variations were observed both within and
across sections. These variations were attributed to scanner artifacts rather than signal strength differences,
as brightness in certain regions was visibly enhanced in some images. These brightness variations were at-
tempted accounted for during quantification. In RNAscope, each dot represents a single mRNA molecule, and
theoretically brightness of the dot should not impact its detection (ACDBio 2019). However, brightness incon-
sistencies affected signal detection. A percentile-based thresholding method was applied to optimize detection
while minimizing background noise. Fixed-threshold methods were unsuitable due to the inconsistent bright-
ness distributions, leading to inaccurate signal patterns. While the percentile-based thresholding reduced these
inconsistencies by focusing on the brightest dots in each section, it struggled with within-tissue brightness
variation, occasionally missing signals in the less bright regions. An adaptive thresholding approach, which
considered local pixel intensity, was tested but proved less effective. Future optimization, potentially includ-
ing image smoothing before quantification, may improve accuracy when quantifying expression in RNAscope
Fluorescent assays. Overall, the assay works well as a follow-up to BRB-seq, providing additional information
on gene localization that cannot be provided by BRB-seq. For quantification purposes, a few optimization
and troubleshooting steps, as well as an increase in group size, could also make it a valuable tool to verify
quantitative sequencing results in situ.
5.4
Molecular Effect Biomarkers of Hypothyroidism
Establishing a relationship between EDC exposure and reproductive effects in models such as the rat seems to
be more challenging in females than in males. This is true for well-known human EDCs, with direct effects
on the human reproductive system, such as DES, which cannot be replicated in the rat, likely due to a lack of
sensitive endpoints (Johansson et al. 2021). Adding to the complexity, there seem to be secondary effects of
thyroid-disrupting chemicals on the reproductive system, but these also remain poorly understood. This study
aimed to investigate the effects of hypothyroidism on PND6 ovaries to help identify molecular biomarkers of
effect associated with ovarian disruption due to thyroid-disrupting chemicals. In addition to molecular markers,
potential morphological biomarkers were evaluated. However, given that follicle and germ cell nest counts are
time-consuming, molecular biomarkers would be preferable for practical applications.
35
==PAGE:43==
Early molecular effects of developmental hypothyroidism have, to our knowledge, not previously been ex-
plored. Saha et al. examined the expression of collagen biosynthesis genes and extracellular matrix-degrading
proteinases in immature rats following postnatal hypothyroidism (Saha et al. 2005), while Meng et al. re-
ported increased expression of oxidative stress marker genes due to developmental hypothyroidism, though in
120-day -old rats (Meng et al. 2016). With differences in time of exposure and investigation, this limits their
applicability for identifying early-stage biomarkers of developmental hypothyroidism. Identifying early effect
biomarkers is particularly critical for assessing EDCs, as many female reproductive disorders that manifest
later in life may originate from developmental EDC exposure (Johansson et al. 2017).
The two investigated genes, Acvr2b and Fabp4, are both interesting candidates for early biomarkers of effect.
However, an essential characteristic of a molecular effect biomarker is its ability to predict adverse effects at
the molecular level, linking chemical exposure and its early and late effects (Hern´andez-Jerez et al. 2024). For
Acvr2b, evidence supports its role in ovarian development, where activins influence meiotic oocyte maturation,
follicle assembly and folliculogenesis. However, further research on its protein expression and mechanisms in
early life is needed to establish whether its downregulation may serve as an early indicator of adverse effects,
and thus serve as a molecular biomarker. The role of Fabp4 in ovary development and function remains unclear,
and whether its downregulation may predict or lead to adverse effects in early or later life is uncertain. However,
it is particularly interesting to consider, as its altered expression was observed in both PND6 and PND80,
suggesting a lasting downregulation across ages. Investigating intermediate timepoints could help determine
whether its downregulation is stable, providing further justification for studying its effects and mechanisms.
Additionally, validating the investigated genes as molecular effect biomarkers requires considering the mech-
anisms of PTU compared to other thyroid-disrupting compounds. PTU inhibits TPO, preventing iodide ox-
idation and thereby reduces T3 and T4 production. Additionally, it inhibits the enzyme deiodinase 1, which
converts T4 to the active hormone T3, primarily in peripheral tissues. As a result, PTU exposure not only
reduces serum TH levels, but may also alter the T4/T3 ratio (Ramhøj et al. 2022). Although several envi-
ronmental thyroid-disrupting chemicals also inhibit TPO (Gore et al. 2015), the mechanisms underlying the
observed effects in this study may be specific to PTU, while exposure to other thyroid disruptors could lead to
different phenotypes.
Next steps in validating the two genes as molecular effect biomarkers could involve confirming their down-
regulation with other methods. It would also be valuable to conduct experiments with other thyroid-disrupting
chemicals with known modes of action, looking at both molecular and histological levels. Observing down-
regulation of Acvr2b and Fabp4 after exposure to other thyroid-disrupting chemicals would further strengthen
their potential as molecular effect biomarkers. In addition, comparing the molecular effects of thyroid disrup-
tion with those induced by other known EDCs that affect female reproduction, such as DES, BPA, or phtalates
could help clarify whether the observed effects are specific to thyroid-disrupting chemicals, or if they share a
common mechanism with other EDCs that directly impact female reproduction more broadly. This would help
determine whether Acvr2b and Fabp4 downregulation are specific markers of thyroid disruption, or part of a
more general response to endocrine disruption.
Moreover, the applicability of molecular biomarkers of effect should be considered. The BRB-seq method used
in this study is expensive, time-consuming, and not widely available, so exploring alternative, more accessible
test methods to confirm transcriptional changes is essential. Ultimately, development of in vitro models for
prediction of ED disruption would be of special interest to reduce cost and increase throughput, enabling faster
36
==PAGE:44==
and more affordable compound screening, as well as to reduce animal use. In vitro testing would require
appropriate assays that are both sensitive and reproducible, and due to the complexity of endocrine disruption
and for regulatory purposes, a combination of multiple complementary assays are likely needed.
For risk assessment purposes, integrating potential molecular effect biomarkers of hypothyroidism into ad-
verse outcome pathways (AOP) could help bridge the gap between exposure to thyroid-disrupting chemicals
and effects on female reproduction. AOPs describe a sequence of events from an early molecular trigger to
an adverse outcome, focusing on key mechanistic events (Johansson et al. 2020). Although extensive research
is needed, establishing AOPs that link exposure to effect — potentially with Acvr2b and Fabp4 downregula-
tion as intermediate molecular key events — would enhance our understanding of how developmental thyroid
disruption affects female reproduction. Incorporating these biomarkers into AOPs would not only provide
mechanistic insights but also strengthen their regulatory relevance by linking molecular changes to adverse
outcomes, supporting more efficient chemical screening and prioritization in regulatory assessments.
5.5
Perspectives
Acvr2b and Fabp4 are both candidates for molecular biomarkers of developmental hypothyroidism. Their
downregulation was not confirmed by ISH, emphasizing the need for histological follow-up experiments with
greater statistical power. Additionally, while RNAscope allowed for localization of gene expression, artifacts
such as signal presence in negative controls indicate the need for further validation. To complement findings
on gene-level, future studies assessing whether FABP4 and ACVR2B proteins are also downregulated using
immunohistochemistry, western blotting, or proteomics, would provide additional insights. Since gene expres-
sion does not always reflect protein expression, this would provide a more comprehensive understanding of
hypothyroidism’s effects on ovarian development and function.
A critical area for future studies is linking molecular changes to ovarian pathologies. It would be of particu-
lar interest to investigate whether nest breakdown, follicle assembly, folliculogenesis, and atresia are affected
in early development following developmental hypothyroidism. This would additional assays and histolog-
ical assessments using a larger sample size than the present study. Furthermore, investigating whether sim-
ilar gene expression changes occur following exposure to other TPO-inhibitors, such as methimazole or the
pesticide amitrole, could determine whether the PTU-induced gene expression changes are broadly applica-
ble to thyroid-disrupting chemicals. However, expecting that a common biomarker exists for all effects of
thyroid-disrupting chemicals on the female reproductive system is unlikely. Instead, searching for an array of
biomarkers representing secondary effects of thyroid disruptions in the ovary may be a more viable approach.
Although ovarian biomarkers are valuable for understanding molecular mechanisms and potentially useful
for regulation and screening of thyroid-disrupting chemicals, they can be challenging to measure. Ideally, a
molecular biomarker of effect that is detectable in a less invasive way, such as in blood, would be preferable.
This would not only simplify detection but could potentially also have broader applicability to human studies.
Finally, the complex interplay between the thyroid hormone system and reproductive system is not well under-
stood, and there is a need to fill these basic knowledge gaps to enable progress in areas such as identification of
molecular biomarkers of effect and development of AOPs, which ultimately could aid in regulation of thyroid-
disrupting chemicals.

6
Conclusion
To assess secondary effects of thyroid-disrupting chemicals on female reproduction, early-life molecular biomark-
ers of effect would be valuable. BRB-seq showed that PTU-induced hypothyroidism altered expression lev-
els of 73 and 83 gene transcripts in PND6 and PND80 animals, respectively, with 15 of particular interest.
Downregulation of Acvr2b and Fabp4 was further investigated but could not be confirmed by in situ hybridiza-
tion. Given their localization and potential importance for ovary development they could serve as molecular
biomarkers of hypothyroidism. Acvr2b downregulation may affect meiotic oocyte maturation, follicle assem-
bly, and folliculogenesis, while Fabp4 downregulation may influence follicular atresia, and consequently nest
breakdown. Further research is needed to validate these genes as molecular effect biomarkers and to further
elucidate the secondary effects of thyroid-disrupting chemicals on female reproduction.

H&E Protocol
1. Deparaffinize Sections
(1) Warm slides at 60°C for 60 min
(2) Transfer slides to petroleum for 10 min
(3) Transfer slides to a second dish with petroleum for 10 min
2. Rehydrate Sections
(1) Transfer slides to 99% ethanol for 5 min
(2) Transfer slides to 96% ethanol for 5 min
(3) Transfer slides to 70% ethanol for 5 min
(4) Transfer slides to running tap water for 5 min
3. Staining and Counterstaining
(1) Transfer slides to Mayers Haematoxylin for 2 min to stain cell nuclei
(2) Transfer slides to running tap water for 5 min
(3) Transfer slides to Eosin for 2 min to stain extracellular matrix and cytoplasm
4. Derydrate Sections
(1) Quickly wash the sections in running tap water by dipping them up and down approximately 10
times
(2) Quickly dip the sections in 70% ethanol approximately 10 times up and down
(3) Quickly dip the sections in 96% ethanol approximately 10 times up and down
(4) Transfer slides to 99% ethanol for 30 sec
(5) Transfer slides to another 99% ethanol dish for 5 min
(6) Transfer slides to another 99% ethanol for 5 min
(7) Transfer slides to completely fresh 99% ethanol for 5 min
5. Mounting
(1) Put a small line of Eukitt on a coverslip and gently place on the slide. Avoid trapping air bubbles.
(2) Let the slides dry overnight in fume hood at room temperature

# Check if the image is multi-channel and reduce to one active channel 
if yellow_array.ndim == 3:  # Multi-channel image 
    yellow_array = yellow_array[:, :, 0]  # Extract one active channel (R, G, or B)
