Abstract
The integration of precise geometric modeling with numerical analysis is fundamental
in modern design and engineering. This thesis explores the application of Iso-geometric
Analysis (IGA) within the context of shape optimization. IGA extends the traditional
isoparametric concept found in Finite Element Analysis (FEA) by employing Non-Uniform
Rational B-Splines (NURBS) as basis functions, which allow for the exact representation
of geometries and enhanced numerical analysis. This approach offers significant advan-
tages in managing complex geometries, enabling simultaneous design and analysis, and
delivering higher accuracy in numerical simulations.
A single-patch 2D IGA formulation is presented, demonstrating superior accuracy and
faster convergence for the study of a simple cantilever beam compared to standard isopara-
metric Q4 Finite Element Methods (FEM). Furthermore, a multi-patch extension is ap-
plied to address more complex geometries that cannot be represented by a single patch,
revealing a trade-off between system matrix size and convergence speed when compared
to the single-patch approach.
As IGA effectively bridges the gap between design and analysis, it naturally extends to
shape optimization, where both processes are integrated. However, implementing shape
optimization within the IGA framework present challenges, particularly in maintaining
structural stability to avoid non-physical solutions. This is achieved through the use of
Coons patches to preserve internal coherence, alongside constraints such as convexity and
corner constraints to ensure stability at the patch boundaries. While the proposed frame-
work proves effective for simple geometries and configurations, like a simple cantilever
beam and an L-shape beam, further research is needed for its analysis in more complex
scenarios.

1 Introduction
Iso-Geometric Analysis (IGA) is a computational approach that employs the same ba-
sis functions used in computer-aided design (CAD) for precise geometric modeling as the
solution space in numerical analysis. This innovative method enhances the traditional
isoparametric concept of Finite Element Analysis (FEA) by utilizing Non-Uniform Ra-
tional B-Splines (NURBS) as shape functions. Unlike traditional FEA, where geometry
and solution fields are approximated by simple polynomial shape functions, IGA utilizes
NURBS to accurately model geometries and directly solve the numerical methods.
Figure 1.1: Elastic bar torsion - comparison of FEM & IGA simulations (Vavourakis,
2018)
==PAGE:14==

Figure 1.1 shows an example where the solution of a square cross-section bar under a
torsion load is shown with both IGA (left) and FEM (right) formulations. One of the
main differences in the solution is that, with IGA the geometry has a higher degree con-
tinuity, leading to a more accurate representation of the physical behavior. Additionally,
the higher-order continuity and smoothness of NURBS in IGA allow for a more efficient
representation of the solution field, often requiring fewer degrees of freedom (DOF) com-
pared to the traditional nodes in FEM.
The primary focus of this thesis is to explore the application of IGA in the context
of structural shape optimization. Structural shape optimization is a process in engineer-
ing design, aiming to achieve configurations that meet specific performance criteria while
maintaining structural integrity. However, conventional FEA-based approaches often face
challenges in accurately modeling complex geometries and require a high number of de-
grees of freedom (DOF), leading to increased computational cost.
IGA, with its ability to represent complex geometries using fewer DOF compared to
traditional FEA, offers a promising alternative for shape optimization. By integrating
design and analysis processes, IGA can potentially lead to more efficient computations
and improved design outcomes. This thesis investigates these potential advantages and
proposes a novel framework for implementing shape optimization within the IGA context.
Specifically, this work addresses the challenges associated with maintaining geometric
fidelity and structural stability during the optimization process. It also explores both
single-patch and multi-patch IGA configurations to determine their effectiveness in han-
dling complex geometries.
1.1
State of the art
This section reviews the development and application of Iso-Geometric Analysis (IGA)
within the context of shape optimization, highlighting key advancements and current
challenges.
1.1.1
Development of a NURBS based FEA
One of the first attempts for a general formulation of B-Splines in FEM can be found
in Kagan et al., 1998 with their study of B-Spline Finite Element (BSFE). Here they try
to bridge the gap between CAD and FEA, and show the potential of this formulation to
provide "superior precision" when compared to spectral elements. Later, Hughes et al.,
2005 continued this line of research, extending the formulation to use NURBS, and thus
generating what is now called IGA.
One of the primary advantages of IGA is its ability to represent exact geometries, unlike
FEA, which typically relies on an approximation of the geometry through meshing. This
exact representation in IGA reduces geometric discretization errors and enables higher
==PAGE:15==
Chapter 1. Introduction
3
accuracy in the analysis of complex structures. IGA has been applied across a wide range
of mechanical engineering problems, including structural mechanics, thermal analysis,
fluid-structure interactions, and more. Various studies have demonstrated the superior
accuracy and efficiency of IGA compared to traditional FEA, particularly in problems
involving complex geometries or where higher-order continuity is required (Gupta et al.,
2023).
In structural mechanics, IGA has been effectively used for the analysis of beams, plates,
and shells, showing higher accuracy in vibration analysis and buckling problems. The
technique has also been applied to thermal analysis, where IGA’s ability to handle complex
boundary conditions has been advantageous. Additionally, IGA has shown promise in
optimization and fracture mechanics, where its precise geometry representation is critical
(Gupta et al., 2023).
Furthermore, IGA has been extended through various refinement techniques such as
k-refinement, which is unique to IGA and allows for the enrichment of the NURBS basis
functions without altering the geometry, a feature not available in classical FEA (Cottrell
et al., 2009). Despite its advantages, IGA also presents challenges, particularly in the
imposition of boundary conditions due to the non-interpolatory nature of NURBS. How-
ever, alternative methods such as Lagrange multipliers and penalty methods have been
developed to address these issues (Hughes et al., 2010).
1.1.2
Shape optimization
The theory of shape optimization is a mathematical field that lies at the crossroads of
calculus of variations and free boundary value problems. Its roots can be traced back to
Newton’s work in 1685, particularly his investigation into the optimal shape of a body
moving through a fluid with minimal resistance (Newton, 1685). Over the years, this
theory has evolved considerably, broadening its scope to encompass applications in various
areas such as solid mechanics, fluid dynamics, and inverse problems.
In shape optimization, the goal is typically to determine the most favorable shape of
a domain, often represented as Ω, that either minimizes or maximizes a given objective
function f(Ω). The domain Ωis typically situated within a Euclidean space Rd, where d
is either 2 or 3. Within this space, an inclusion Ωi ⊂Ωmust be identified, the shape of
which is unknown and must be found simultaneously with the solution of the governing
boundary value problem (Plotnikov & Sokolowski, 2023). These problems are often set in
contexts involving different materials, such as solid elastic substances, fluids, or physical
fields, all modeled using partial differential equations (PDEs) with appropriate boundary
conditions.

1.1.3
IGA in Shape Optimization
Early approaches to shape optimization, when applied to standard FEA, used the coor-
dinates of finite element boundary nodes as design variables. However, this often resulted
in unrealistic and irregular shapes due to the independent movement of these nodes (Fran-
cavilla et al., 1975). The need for smooth, realistic designs, as well as the challenges of
mesh dependency and maintaining a consistent FEM mesh, underscored the limitations
of this approach.
To overcome these challenges, it became common practice to separate the design model
from the analysis model. Typically, the design model employed a coarser geometric de-
scription than the analysis model. Various boundary representations, such as polynomials,
Bézier curves, B-splines, and NURBS, have been utilized to this end. Spline representa-
tions, in particular, have proven effective due to their smoothness and ability to maintain
regular boundaries (Braibant & Fleury, 1984; Haftka & Grandhi, 1986). However, ensur-
ing consistency between the design and analysis models during optimization has remained
a persistent challenge.
IGA significantly mitigates these issues by eliminating the separation between design
and analysis models, offering substantial potential for shape optimization (Wall et al.,
2008). Despite its advantages, IGA is not free of challenges, particularly regarding in-
ternal coherence. In IGA-based shape optimization, the method distinguishes between
"outer/boundary DOF", which define the structure’s shape, and "inner DOF", which are
essential for analysis. Proper interpolation of these inner DOF is crucial to avoid non-
physical shapes and singularities.
Recent advancements propose promising solutions to these challenges. For instance,
efforts by (Qian & Sigmund, 2011) and (Limkilde et al., 2021) focus on stabilizing the Ja-
cobian determinant during the optimization process, ensuring that the geometries remain
physically valid throughout the analysis.
1.2
Objective and outline of the thesis
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop and validate a robust methodology
for shape optimization using IGA, presented for those with a basic FEM knowledge. This
includes the derivation of sensitivities, the implementation of boundary conditions, and
the handling of constraints related to geometric continuity and physical realism. This
work aims to contribute to the growing body of knowledge on IGA by demonstrating its
applicability in structural optimization, potentially offering a more integrated and efficient
alternative to conventional methods.

2 Fundamental of IGA shape functions
2.1
IGA vs FEM: shape function
The core difference between these two methods resides in the shape functions. While
traditional FEM defines the physical domain with nodes interpolated by Lagrange poly-
nomials, IGA defines it with control points interpolated by NURBS basis functions. This
difference can be easily appreciated in Figure 1.1, where in the IGA example, the control
mesh lies outside the physical geometry except for corners and flat faces. At the same
time, this control mesh generates a continuous geometry representation of the deformed
shape of the bar, which differs from the block construction of FEM. Lastly, it is important
to notice that the number of control points (IGA DOF) used in IGA is considerably less
than the number of nodes (FEM DOF) used in FEM.
The reason for these differences can be explained by examining the NURBS (Equa-
tion 2.10) against Lagrange basis functions. For example, cubic basis functions for the
interpolation of 10 points are shown in Figure 2.1. Figure 2.1a presents a standard cubic
Lagrange basis function generating three elements, producing only C0 continuity between
the elements. Figure 2.1b shows the same example with the Bernstein basis functions,
the most simple type of spline basis function. These can only have a degree p = n−1 = 9
with n the number of points interpolated giving it a Cp−1 = C8.
The Bernstein basis functions are a special case of the B-Spline basis functions, where
degree p is maximum. B-Spline basis functions allow the degree p ∈[0, n −1] which
increases the flexibility of the curve and allow to focus the local control of the curve.
This is possible thanks to the introduction of the knot vector (Equation 2.4), which can
be uniform like in Figure 2.1c or non-uniform like in Figure 2.1d. This last provides the
option of increase the resolution of parts of the curves, or reduce the continuity of the
curve by repeating the knots ξi of the knot vector
Finally, non-uniform B-Spline basis functions can be extended by making them ratio-
nal, leading to NURBS basis functions by adding a last parameter called Weights W.
This allow a more precise control of the effect of the basis functions, like it is shown in
Figure 2.1e where the basis functions are the same as in Figure 2.1d but weighted based
on W = [1 1 5 1 1 1 2 1 1 3].

2.2
NURBS for geometry representation
NURBS is a mathematical model that uses basis splines (B-Splines) and is commonly
used for representing curves and surfaces.
NURBS has its origins with Bezier curves
(Bézier, 1972), which are a natural starting point to explain NURBS and its elements.

The control points P are the key elements that define the shape and position of a
Bezier curve. Each point Pi is a vector in a d-dimensional space, typically R2 or R3, and
influences the curve locally. The union of the linear interpolations of the control points
Pi −Pi+1 define the control polygon as shown in Figure 2.2. Some of its properties are:
• Position Influence: Each control point influences the curve’s position in the space.
The first and last control points P0 and Pp are interpolated by the curve, meaning
the curve starts at P0 and ends at Pp.
• Convex Hull: The curve lies within the convex hull of its control points, ensuring
that the curve maintains a predictable shape.

Properties of Bernstein Basis Functions
Bernstein basis functions have several important properties:
• Non-negativity: Bp
i (ξ) ≥0 for all i, n and 0 ≤ξ ≤1. This is crucial for the

B-Spline vs Bezier curves
B-Splines are build over Bezier curves adding:
1. Scalability: Bezier curves require a degree p = n −1 curve for n control points. B-
Splines enable the use of multiple lower-degree p ∈[0, 1, . . . , n−1] polynomials "stitched
together".
2. Local Control: When working with lower degree polynomial, B-Splines allow for
local control. Meaning that adjusting a control point in a B-Spline affects only a portion
of the curve.
3. Continuity: B-Splines provide Cp−1 continuity across the entire curve, where p is
the polynomial degree. This ensures smooth transitions between curve segments.

Uniform knot vector
The key component of B-Splines is the knot vector KV , which is a sequence of non-
decreasing parameter values that defines a partition of the parameter space. A uniform
knot vector has evenly spaced knots (ξi −ξi+1 = C, with C constant), providing equal
influence of each control point over the curve segments. The uniform knot vector for a
B-Spline of degree p with n control points is:
KV = {ξ1, ξ2, . . . , ξn+p+1},
(2.4)

knot vector KV is said to be open if its first and last p + 1 knots are repeated, generating
B-Splines that are interpolatory at their start and end points.

3.1.3
Differences between IGA and FEM matrix systems
Previously it was mentions that one of the most important features of NURBS is the
use of higher order polynomials, this feature have important consequences in the stiffness
matrix previously introduced. Just as with standard FEM, the bandwidth b of the stiffness
matrix of IGA is b = 2p+1. This means that the sparsity of the stiffness matrix in IGA can
be significantly decreased with the increase of the degree of the NURBS basis functions p.
In particular, standard FEM uses linear interpolations with a bandwidth of b = 2+1 = 3,
while for NURBS it can easily reach higher values.

4 Multi-Patch IGA
4.1
Multi-Patch implementation
Generally, the geometries generated in CAD are composed by different patches "snitched"
together. For the visual scope of CAD, the multi-patch formulation has no big complex-
ity, but for the IGA formulation it is an important factor. In most practical situations,
it’s often necessary to describe domains using multiple NURBS patches. This approach is
particularly useful when different materials or physical models are applied in various parts
of the domain, as it simplifies the process by using distinct patches for each subdomain.
Additionally, when using multiple processors in parallel computing, it is advantageous
to avoid splitting a single patch across processors. This method is especially beneficial
when dealing with complex geometries that cannot be easily represented by a single tensor
product structure, making multiple patches a more suitable solution (Cottrell et al., 2009
section 2.3).
To ensure a sufficient basic convergence, some constraints will be define that ensure the
C0 continuity across the patch boundary between patches. To achieve this in a simple
way (Cottrell et al., 2009 Chapter 3), the constraints impose are:
• Only full curves in the patch boundary can be bounded.
• The curves bounded must be equal, meaning that they need to share the same
control points and have the same degree and knot vector.

edges are relevant for optimization. Therefore, to maintain the physical stability of the
structure, the inner points must be uniformly interpolated based on the positions of the
outer control points.

Chapter 6. Conclusion

6 Conclusion
This thesis presents a investigation into the application of IGA for structural shape
optimization. The research conducted has presented the potential of IGA to offer high ac-
curacy and efficiency in comparison to traditional FEA, particularly in scenarios involving
complex geometries and higher-order continuity requirements. In the initial stages of this
work, a detailed exploration of NURBS and their advantages over classical polynomial-
based shape functions was shown. This foundational understanding provided the basis for
the development and implementation of both single-patch and multi-patch IGA frame-
works.
The single-patch IGA demonstrated the method’s capability to precisely model struc-
tural responses with fewer degrees of freedom and to provide a fast solution convergence
when compare with traditional FEA. The multi-patch extension, while introducing some
trade-offs in accuracy compare to its single-patch counterpart, are the key to generate
complex shapes and allow a stable discretization of these and being beneficial in scenarios
requiring localized refinement.
The core contribution of this thesis lies in the development of a shape optimization
framework that takes profit of IGA’s precise geometric representation, specifically the
application of special constraints that maintains the physical stability during the opti-
mization process. By integrating Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) and employ-
ing analytical sensitivity derivation, the framework was shown to efficiently navigate the
design space, achieving optimized shapes that maintain high geometric fidelity and struc-
tural performance. The results from the numerical examples shows the effectiveness of
the proposed framework in finding optimal shapes for a variety of simple cases.
This research has established an effective framework for shape optimization of simple
geometries using IGA. The findings suggest that IGA holds significant promise for future
applications in engineering design. However, challenges such as the imposition of more
complex boundary conditions and computational efficiency studies remain areas for fur-
ther research. Future work could explore the development of more advanced multi-patch
techniques, integration with other optimization algorithms, and the application of IGA
in more complex, real-world engineering problems.
