Resumé: 
Indenfor vindenergi er det naturligvis afgørende at kunne beregne hvor meget 
energi en vindmølle udtrækker af vinden. Dette kan blandt andet udregnes 
ved at opstille impulsbevarelse omkring vindmøllen, hvilket er idéen bag 
Blade Element Momentum (BEM) modellen, som første gang blev foreslået af 
Glauert i 1935. Selvom om modellen har vist sig at være utrolig brugbar, 
bygger den på en række antagelser, som er blevet genstand for fornyet 
interesse indenfor de senere år. I den forbindelse er en korrigeret model 
blevet foreslået af J.N. Sørensen (2015), hvori der korrigeres for nogle af de 
flow effekter der opstår som en konsekvens af rotation i slipstrømmen. Målet 
med dette projekt er at validere den korrigerede model ved at sammenholde 
resultaterne med en Aktuator Disk (AD) simulering, som løser den fulde 
strømning omkring møllen.  
Det viste sig at den korrigerede model stemmer vældig godt overens med AD 
simuleringen. På den inderste del af rotoren undervurderer Glauerts BEM  
model effekten, mens den korrigerede model er ekstremt tæt på resultaterne 
fra AD-simuleringen. Dette gælder især for rotorer designet til at køre ved et 
lavt tiphastighedsforhold og når rotoren kører ved det tiphastighedsforhold 
den er designet til. For en optimum Glauert-rotor designet til et 
tiphastighedforhold på 2, er effekten i Glauerts BEM model undervurderet 
med 7,3%, mens effekten i den korrigerede model kun er 0,8% fra AD-
resultatet. Lignende resultater er opnået for Tjæreborgmøllen, hvor Glauert’s 
BEM undervurderer effekten med 2,8%, mens den korrigerede model er 1,4% 
fra AD-resultatet.  
Alt i alt har den korrigerede model en række fordele. Først og fremmest er 
modellen udledt analytisk på baggrund af fysiske overvejelser. Dertil komemr 
at modellen er ligeså simpel og hurtig som Glauerts BEM model. Sidst, men 
ikke mindst, giver den korrigerede model altid resultater der enten er bedre 
en Glauerts BEM model eller som er uændrede i forhold til Glauerts BEM-
model. Det vil sige at den korrigerede model aldrig udmønter sig i resultater 
der er mindre pålidelige end Glauerts BEM-model. På baggrund af 
resultaterne i dette projekt konkluderes det derfor at den korrigerede model 
med fordel kan bruges frem for Glauerts BEM-model.

Abstract
The momentum theory in the form of the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) model is widely used to
compute the power extracted by a wind turbine. First introduced by Glauert in 1935, the BEM model
builds on approximations which might aﬀect the validity of the results. Among these are ﬂow eﬀects due
to rotation in the wake, which are neglected in the BEM model. Recently, a corrected BEM model was
suggested by J.N. Sørensen, which accounts for ﬂow eﬀects due to wake rotation. The corrected model is
derived analytically and consists of a correction term to Glauert’s BEM model. In this thesis, the corrected
model is validated against Actuator Disc (AD) simulations, in which the full ﬂow ﬁeld around the wind
turbine is solved. It is found that the corrected model agrees remarkably well with AD results. Thus, while
the BEM model underestimates the power coeﬃcient on the inboard of the rotor, the corrected model leads
to an increment in the power coeﬃcient close to the root, and thus obtains results in very good agreement
with AD results. Thus, for an optimum ’Glauert’ rotor designed for a tip speed ratio of 2, Glauert’s BEM
model underestimates the gobal power coeﬃcient by 7.3 %, while the corrected model is within 0.8% of the
AD result. The eﬀect of applying the corrected model increases for small tip speed ratios and when the
rotor is operated at the tip speed ratio it is designed for. When applied on a real rotor, in this case the
Tjæreborg wind turbine, the corrected model performs better than the BEM model as well. Thus, the BEM
model underestimates the power coeﬃcient by 2.8%, while the corrected model is 1.4% below the AD result
at the design tip speed ratio. In conclusion, the corrected model has some clear advantages. First of all it
is derived analytically, based on physical considerations, which means it is self-contained. Furthermore, it is
as simple and fast as Glauert’s BEM model and provides results impressively close to AD results. Last, but
not least, the corrected model always provides results that are better or un-changed from the BEM results.
This means that implementing the corrected model never leads to results less reliable than Glauert’s BEM
model. Based on the analysis in this thesis, it is therefore concluded that the corrected model can be used
in preference of Glauert’s BEM model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
An essential question in wind energy is naturally how much power a wind turbine generates. That is, given
the design of the wind turbine and the nature of the incoming wind, how much energy does the wind turbine
extract from the wind. As the wind energy industry expands and the technology becomes more and more
sophisticated, it is now the small deviations between predicted and actual performance that matter.
Wind is not a novel source of energy. Already one centure ago, it was shown that by a simple momentum
balance around a wind turbine, it is possible to estimate the power extracted by the wind turbine rotors.
This was shown by Rankine (1865) [21], W. Froude (1878) [8], R. E. Froude (1889), [7], Joukowsky (1912)
[14] and Betz (1920) [2].
Later, in 1935, Glauert [9] extended the analysis by two modiﬁcations. Firstly, Glauert added rotation
in the momentum balance by regarding the rotors as a rotating disc. Thereby Glauert considered not only
how much the wind turbine slows down the wind, but also to what degree the wind is set into rotation.
Secondly, Glauert divided the blade into a number of elements and then solved the momentum balance for
each element, assuming that the ﬂow through each element is independent of the others. Glauert then used
experiments to ﬁgure out the lift and drag on each blade element, given the local angle of attack. This is an
iterative proces, since the angle of attack depends on the ﬂow conditions, and the ﬂow conditions depend on
the lift and drag, i.e. the loads on the blade. This is called the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method,
and it has proven extremely powerful in estimating the power extracted by the rotors. However, the BEM
model builds on a number of assumptions, which Glauert himself adresses [9]. Some of the assumptions have
been accounted for by corrections to the original model, e.g. Prandtl’s tip loss correction [1] and Glauert’s
correction for heavily loaded rotors [9]. However, other assumptions remain essential in the BEM model.
Today, the wind energy industry still uses the BEM model to compute the power extracted by a wind
turbine. As en example, the BEM model is exploited to design and optimize wind turbine blades. Therefore
the accuracy of the BEM model is highly important.
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, the BEM model and its underlying assumptions have been the object of renewed interest.
One recent example is due to the German wind turbine manufacturer Enercon, who reported that some of
their turbines exhibited high power eﬃciencies on the inner part of the blade [6]. At this part of the blade,
Enercon claimed to exceed the Betz limit of CP ∼= 59.3%, which is the upper limit of the BEM model. This
observation sparked a renewed interest in investigating the BEM model and its underlying assumptions.
Recently, a corrected BEM model was suggested by Sørensen (2015)[27], in which rotation in the wake
is accounted for in a diﬀerent way than proposed by Glauert. The corrected model is based on physical
considerations and was derived analytically. The corrected model is elegant, since it simply introduces an
additional term in the usual BEM routine. Therefore, the model is easily implemented and it is hoped that
the corrected model improves the ability to correctly predict the power output of a wind turbine, when
9
==PAGE:12==
compared to Glauert’s BEM model. The motivation of this thesis is thus to investigate the corrected BEM
model suggested by Sørensen (2015)[27].
Furthermore, with the computer power available today, the committed error in Glauert’s BEM model
can be inspected closely by use of CFD models. This way, the original approximations by Glauert can be
quantiﬁed and the validity of the BEM model can be further investigated. Even though CFD models are
more precise, the BEM model is still currently the only model used by the industry. The reason for this is
that the BEM model is far easier to implement and requires much less computational power.
1.2
Problem Formulation
Given the hypothesis stated above, the purpose of this thesis is to investigate whether the corrected BEM
model improves the ability of the BEM model to correctly predict the power output of a wind turbine. To
do so, we need an accurate measure of the actual power output. For this purpose, the Actuator Disc (AD)
model is used, as developed by Sørensen, Mikkelsen, among others [24] [29] [28] [30] [17]. The AD model
computes the entire ﬂow ﬁeld around the wind turbine and therefore does not neglect any of the ﬂow eﬀects
which could aﬀect the performance of the wind turbine. An additional purpose of this thesis is to evaluate
the error committed in the BEM model by using the AD model to quantify the omitted terms. In this thesis,
The AD code refers to a Fortran code developed by Mikkelsen, among others, and which is documented in
e.g. [17] [18].
1.3
Thesis Objective
The corrected model suggested by Sørensen in fact consists of three diﬀerent proposals of a correction term
to the BEM model. The primary goal of this thesis is to validate each of the three corrected BEM models
against the AD model, thereby determining whether or not the correction terms improve the BEM model
and which of the three correction terms is more suitable. Several part-goals that are more speciﬁc have been
formulated:
• To do a mutual comparison of the three corrected BEM models in terms of global quantities as well as
local quantities along the blade. The corrected BEM models are investigated on three diﬀerent rotors,
which are introduced in Chapter 6: 1) An optimum Glauert rotor, 2) a restrained Glauert rotor and
3) the Tjæreborg wind turbine. The goal is to determine how the corrections alter the results of the
BEM model. The results are reported in Chapter 8
• To validate the corrected BEM models against Navier-Stokes/Actuator Disc simulations in order to
determine if the correction terms improve the predictions of the BEM model. The analysis is carried
out on two diﬀerent rotors 1) A restrained Glauert rotor 2) the Tjæreborg wind turbine. This is done
in Chapter 9
• To evaluate the errors due to fundamental assumptions in the BEM model, by quantifying the omitted
terms via the AD model, Chapter 10.
Before the analysis is carried out, the State of the Art in momentum theory is presented in Chapter 2
and the necessary theory needed to derive the correction terms is presented in Chapters 3-4: In Chapter 3
the Momentum Theory is introduced and Chapter 4 introduces the BEM model as developed by Glauert
(1935). The three rotor designs used in the analysis of the corrected models are presented in 6 and Chapter
7 brieﬂy describes the AD model. After the results are presented in Chapters 8-10, the conclusion and
recommendations are given in Chapter 11.

State of the Art in Momentum
Theory
In order to place the work of the current thesis relative to the existing research body, it is relevant to map
out the State of the Art in momentum theory in general and BEM theory speciﬁcally. Recalling the problem
formulation of this thesis, the two main questions posed in this chapter are the following:
• How big an error do basic assumptions in the BEM model introduce?
• What corrections to the BEM model have been suggested that account for rotation in the wake?

The frame for the literature search
Before starting the project, a literature review was carried out in order to map previous research eﬀorts in

• Literature was sought that challenged Glauert’s BEM model in the way that the basic assumptions
were questioned
• Literature concerning quantiﬁcation of the errors committed in the BEM was sought as well
• When searching for literature regarding the BEM model the main focus was on corrections for wake
rotation and/or wake expansion
The databases used in the literature search was Web Of Science, scholar.google and DTU Findit.

An Invalid Equation in the General Momentum Theory of the Actuator Disk
P.M. Goorijan, AIAA Journal, 10:543-544, 1972 [10]
11
==PAGE:14==
Overview:
This technical note is one of the ﬁrst works that questions the underlying assumptions in the
General Momentum theory. It is written for helicopters, but the analysis applies for wind turbine rotors as
well.
Methods:
Goorijan performs an analytical study of the basic equations in the General Momentum theory,
by establishing the axial momentum balance around the wind turbine.
Results:
In this paper, Goorijan proves that applying the axial momentum equation in a simple diﬀerential
form is incorrect. This adresses the approximation that the pressure acting on the control volume can be
neglected.
Comments:
Goorijan is very clear in his point: that the BEM model relies on an equation that is incorrect.
However, he does not mention the fact that in most cases the inaccuracies display only a limited inﬂuence
on the results, therefore BEM in its simple form is still a valid technique to compute performance and loads
on the rotors.
Fluid Dynamic Aspects of Wind Energy Conversion
OM de Vries, AGARD Advisory Group for Aerospace Research and Development, AGARD-AG-243:C4-
C9, July 1979[4]
Overview:
In this paper, de Vries investigates the consequences of neglecting pressure due to wake rotation.
Method:
The investigation is purely analytical. As in Goorijan (1972), de Vries establishes the momentum
balance around a wind turbine.
Results:
At low tip speed ratio, de Vries shows that energy is lost in setting the wake into rotation, however
the wake rotation also lowers the pressure which causes a greater air ﬂow. De Vries shows that these two
consequences of wake rotation largely balance each other out.
Comments:
De Vries argues that the analysis is carried out for a constant circulation along the blade
span, and therefore the conclusions cannot immediately be applied to an arbitrartry loading of the rotor.
A General Momentum Theory Applied to an Energy-Extracting Actuator Disc
DJ Sharpe, Wind Energy, 7:177-188, 2004 [22]
Overview:
In this paper, Sharpe seeks to formulate a more general momentum balance by including the
eﬀects of wake rotation and wake expansion.
Method:
The method is fully analytic.
Sharpe studies how inclusion of the wake rotation and wake
expansion aﬀect the momentum balance around an actuator disc. He assumes radial uniform circulation.
Results:
Sharpe ﬁnds that rotation of the wake is accompanied by a fall in the static pressure behind the
rotors. This means that the pressure drop over the rotors is increased and thus the energy extracted by the
rotors increase. The eﬀect is small for large tip speed ratios, but signiﬁcant for very small tip speed ratios
below λ = 3. Sharpe also ﬁnds that wind turbines designed at very low tip speed ratios can produce power
coeﬃcients which can even exceed the Betz limit. Sharpe argues that in principle, the theory establishes
that there is no loss of eﬃciency associated with the rotating wake, and therefore it should be possible to
exceed the Betz limit, at least in theory.
12
==PAGE:15==
Comments:
The theory proposed by Sharpe implies that the velocity goes to inﬁnity at the centreline of
the wake. Sharpe argues, however, that this does not pose a practical problem, since the blade root has a
ﬁnite radius in the case of a real rotor and therefore the velocity is also ﬁnite. In Glauert [9], the model
was presented as a special case of the general momentum theory. However, Glauert pointed out that for the
assumption of constant circulation to be fulﬁlled, the angular velocity close to the root would exceed that
of the rotor itself. It was argued by e.g. Glauert (1935) [9] and de Vries [4] that this solution is unphysical
at small tip speed ratios, exactly because this would lead to angular velocities. In any case, at this point, it
was debated whether ot not Sharpe’s conclusion was fully true to the ﬂow physics.
General Momentum Theory for Wind Turbines at Low Tip Speed Ratios
Jens N. Sørensen and Gijs AM van Kuik, Wind Energy, (14):821-839, 2011 [26]
Overview:
The paper studies the behavior of the free vortex wake model of Joukowsky in order to explain
why the power coeﬃcients exceed the Betz limit at all tip speed ratios and tend to inﬁnity at small tip speed
ratios, as adressed in e.g. Sharpe (2004). That is, instead of arguing why or why not it is possible to exceed
the Betz limit, Sørensen and Kuik seek an explanation for why it happens.
Method:
The general momentum theory is analysed in order to understand anomaly behaviour at small
tip speed ratios. In line with the free vortex model of Joukwosky, circulation is assumed to be constant.
The method is fully analytic using the concept of a disc ﬂow introduced by Joukowsky [14]. The momentum
balance for the ﬂow is established and wake rotation and expansion are included, as in Sharpe [22]. To
verify if the strange behavior at small tip speed ratios is related to the lack of the inﬂuence of the lateral
pressure, Sørensen and Kuik include this term in the equations. The term is modelled by a proportionality
with the pressure drop over the rotor ∆p and the area expansion A1 −A. Solving the equations for diﬀerent
proportionality constants ǫ, they ﬁnd that for all ǫ ̸= 0, the power coeﬃcient becomes exactly zero for tip
speed ratio going to zero. Even for ǫ = 10−7, the power coeﬃcient is zero when the tip speed ratio tends to
zero.
Results:
It is concluded that the most likely explanation for the high power coeﬃcients is that the force
contribution from lateral pressure and friction is ignored. A corrected model is proposed, which accounts
for the inﬂuence of the pressure acting on the lateral boundary of the control volume. The corrected model
ensures that the power coeﬃcient does not exceed the Betz limit at low tip speed ratios. Furthermore, the
corrected model predicts a power coeﬃcient that tends to zero at zero tip speed ratio. However the actual
magnitude of the proportionality constant ǫ is not determined
Comments:
The idea behind the correction is very similar to the correction model investigated in the this
thesis.

Overview:
In this work Madsen et al.
compare the BEM model against results from an AD model.
Furthermore, two corrections to the BEM model are suggested, based on the ﬁndings of the comparison.
Firstly, Madsen et al. analyse the rotor ﬂow by using a vortex model described by Øye [19]. We will not

the ﬂow using the vortex model, Madsen et al. ﬁnd that the pressure due to centrifugal acceleration in the

The full set of Navier-Stokes equations are solved in the AD model via the commercial CFD

corrections to the BEM model:

Aerodynamic Optimization of Wind Turbine Rotors using a Blade Element Mo-

M. Døssing, Helge Aa. Madsen and Christian Bak, Wind Energy, (15):563-573, 2012 [5]
Overview:
This paper investigates the validity of the corrected BEM model suggested by Madsen et al.
(2007) [16] and (2010) [15] by comparing the results with AD results. Furthermore, Døssing et al. seek
to optimize the design of rotor blades by using the corrected BEM model. However, the main interest in
relation to this thesis is how the corrected BEM model performed compared to AD results.
Method:
The rotor used for the test is an optimum rotor designed for λ = 8 using the Risø B1-15 airfoils
with design CL = 1.4 and a lift-to-drag ratio of 110. The AD code used is described in a diﬀerent paper by
Johansen et al. [13].
Results:
Døssing et al. ﬁnd that both axial and angular velocities in the corrected BEM model shows
good agreement with the AD results from Johansen et al. [13]. It is therefore concluded that the corrected
BEM model shows accuracy comparable to the one found in AD models.

Even though the corrected BEM model shows good agreement with AD results, it still has

The General Momentum Theory
Jens Nørkær Sørensen, draft of chapters 4 and 7, 2015 [27]
Overview:
This is the most important of the presented papers, since it introduces the three correction
models that are investigated in this thesis. In this paper, Sørensen derives three diﬀerent corrections to the
BEM model which account for rotation in the wake. The corrections are derived analytically, and are based
on the assumption that circulation is conserved along a streamline.
16
==PAGE:19==
Method:
The three corrected models are implemented in the BEM code and a comparative study of the
models is performed on an optimum Glauert rotor designed for 1) λ = 6 and 2) λ = 3. The rotor designed
for λ = 6 is run for tip speed ratios in the range 3 ≤λ ≤9, while the rotor designed for λ = 3 is run for tip
speed ratios in the range 1 ≤λ ≤5.
Results:
The results of the comparative study of the three corrected models revealed the following:
• Errors comitted in Glauert’s BEM model: In the paper, Sørensen compares the induction factor
a from Glauert’s BEM model to AD results. It is found that the deviation increases with the loading
CT , especially at the inner part of the blade, where BEM overestimates a by around 25%. Error proﬁles
on an optimum rotor are also reported, and it is found that ǫ3 ∼= 0, while ǫ1 and ǫ2 are within 2% on
the majority of the blade and increase to around 10% at the tip.
• Global properties: Sørensen ﬁnds that the three models result in integral thrust coeﬃcients that are
almost equal, whereas they are all higher than those computed using the Glauert model. The biggest
eﬀect of the correction models is found when the two rotors operate at the tip speed ratio they are
designed for. Thus, when the rotor designed for λ = 6 is operated at λ = 6, the thrust coeﬃcient CT
is increased by around 2% and the power coeﬃcient CP is increased by 2.5%. For the rotor designed
for λ = 3, the eﬀect of the corrected model is increased. Thus, the thrust coeﬃcient is altered by 6%
while CP is altered by 8% when compared to Glauert’s BEM model. The eﬀect of applying Model 2
is slightly smaller than Model 1 and 3.
• Local properties: The biggest eﬀect of the corrected models is at the inner 60% of the blade. Also
locally, there are barely any diﬀerence between the three corrected models at λ = 6. At λ = 3, Sørensen
ﬁnds that Model 2 acts as mixed model between Model 3 and Glauert’s BEM model: At the root, the
Model 2 is identical to Model 3 and at the tip, Model 2 is identical to Glauert’s BEM model.
Sørensen argues that the ﬁndings illustrate the importance of including rotational terms at the inner part of
the rotor and at small tip speed ratios.
Comments:
The optimum Glauert rotors are based on the same airfoil as in this thesis, and therefore the
results are directly comparable.
2.4
Summary
The purpose of this chapter was ﬁrst and foremost to map the existing literature in corrections to the BEM
model and the error comitted when neglecting wake rotation. The most important work is naturally The
General Momentum Theory by Sørensen (2015) in which the corrected BEM models investigated in this
thesis are suggested. The papers described in this chapter are listed in Tables 2.1 and 2.2.
Comments on the error comitted due to the approximations in BEM
In this literature search,
focus was kept on the error committed due to approximations concerning the rotation in the wake. The
analytical studies by Goorijan (1972) [10], de Vries (1979) [4], Sharpe (2004) [22], and Sørensen & van
Kuik (2011) [26] all revealed that neglecting the pressure due to rotation in the wake lead to an error in
the momentum theory. The magnitude of the errors committed in the BEM model were investigated by
Sørensen and Mikkelsen (2001) [25].
Comments on the correction models:
I basically found only two correction models which account for
rotation in the wake: The correction model suggested by Madsen et al. and Døssing et al. and the recently
suggested correction model by Sørensen (2015). The correction model suggested by Madsen, Døssing et al.
is well-performing and shows very good agreement with AD results. However, the correction model is not
self-contained and relies on AD results for calibration. This is a serious draw-back, since calibration against

a numerical model is never fully true to the ﬂow physics. The correction model suggested by Sørensen is
derived analytically, but needs to be compared against against e.g. numerical results in order to validate the
correction. This is the purpose of this thesis.

3.1
1D Momentum theory
The simplest way to estimate the power output of a wind turbine is by considering a one-dimensional
momentum balance around the rotors. This was e.g. done by Rankine in 1865 [21], W Froude (1878) [8],
RE Froude (1889), [7] and Betz [2].
The entire wind turbine is reduced to a set of rotors and we ignore the tower, the ground on which the
turbine stands, the nacelle and everything else but the rotors. The rotors are then modelled as a permeable
disc, which acts as a simple drag device by slowing down the incoming wind. In fact, this is the same as
considering a wind turbine with an inﬁnite number of blades. A correction to this approximation was later
derived and will be introduced in 4.2.1. Since the problem is considered one-dimensional there is no rotation
of neither the disc nor the wake behind the turbine rotors.
The three governing equations for this problem are the momentum equation, the energy equation and

3.5
Summary
In this chapter we have learned that from a simple 1D momentum balance around the wind turbine, an upper
limit of the available power can be determined. This is the Betz limit, which states that CP,max ∼= 59.3%.
We have also seen that the momentum balance can be extended to include angular forces. The general
momentum balance is captured by the two general equations eq. (3.17) and eq. (3.28), ﬁrstly derived by
Sørensen (2015) [27].
Furthermore, the general expression for the power extracted by the turbine was derived, as presented in
eq. (3.31). This expression is complicated by the fact the we need to know the distribution of velocity in the
far wake u1. However, if we apply conservation of circulation, it is possible to establish a constant axial ﬂow
in the wake in two diﬀerent ways: 1) A solid body rotation in the wake or 2) A free vortex ﬂow in the wake.
Depending on whether the wake acts as a solid body or a free vortex, the power will be given by either eq.
(3.40) or eq. (3.43). It is also seen that neglecting the pressure acting on the control volume will always
cause an error in the momentum balance.

Summary
In this chapter we have seen that the BEM model links the 1D momentum theory with the aerodynamic

• The ﬂow can be divided into annular independent streamtubes
• Conservation of circulation can be ignored
• The pressure in the ultimate wake equals the pressure far upstream p0 = p1
• The induced velocity in the rotor plane is equal to one half of the induced velocity in the far wake,

In this thesis, these assumptions will be challenged.

eﬀects and lateral pressure on the stream surfaces in the control volume.

8.1
Optimum Glauert rotor with CT,design = 8/9
Firstly, the corrected models are used to compute the induction factors on an optimum Glauert rotor. A
total of eight optimum rotors were designed for tip speed ratios in the range 2 ≤λ ≤9. In Fig. 8.1, the
induction factors a and a′ are compared for an optimum rotor designed at the smallest tip speed ratio, λ = 2.
It is seen that the axial induction is strongly aﬀected by the correction on the inner 60% of the blade, where
the induction is decreased dramatically. At the very tip, the correction does not change the induction factor
since tip loss correction is applied throughout. Close to the root there are barely any diﬀerences between the
three correction models. In the central part of the blade, however, Model 1 and Model 3 result in a slightly
lower induction factor than Model 2. In fact, Model 1 and Model 3 give extremely similar results over the
entire blade. The angular induction factor a′ is slightly increased in the corrected models and no diﬀerence
between the three corrected models is visible.
The corresponding thrust and power coeﬃcients are plotted over the optimum rotor in Fig. 8.2. In all
three corrected models, the thrust coeﬃcient is increased dramatically at the inner part of the rotor. Even
more interestingly, while Glauert’s BEM model predicts an almost constant thrust coeﬃcient over the inner
60% of the blade, the corrected models predict an increasing thrust as r/R tends to zero. This means that
according to the corrected models the wind turbine is more eﬃcient in extracting energy from the wind on
the inner half of the blade than what Glauert’s BEM model predicts. This is reﬂected in the power coeﬃcient
as well, where CP is increased at the inner part of the rotor due to the correction terms.

8.4
Summary
In this chapter, the three corrected models are compared to Glauert’s BEM model. The comparison lead to
the following conclusions:
• The correction terms lead to smaller axial induction factors, but increased CT and CP on the inner
part of the blade compared to Glauert’s BEM model
• Model 1 and Model 3 are identical to the third digit for the global quantities and show no visible
diﬀerence in the local quantities.
51
==PAGE:54==
• As expected, Model 2 acts as a mixed model between Model 3 (or Model 1) and Glauert’s BEM model.
From the local quantites obtained on both the optimum and restrained Glauert rotors, it is seen that
Model 2 follows Glauert’s BEM model at the outer 15% of the blade and coincides with Model 1 and
3 at the root of the blade.
• From the analysis of the optimum and restrained Glauert rotors it was found that the correction terms
have biggest impact at small design tip speed ratios.
• For the Tjæreborg wind turbine, the biggest impact of the corrections is seen when the turbine is
operated close to the design tip speed ratio or above.
These ﬁndings agree well with results found by Sørensen (2015) [27]. Model 1 and Model 3 are found to give
practically identical results. At the blade element closest to the tip, however, Model 3 sometimes caused
unphysically high induction factors which results in unreliable power coeﬃcients etc. This might be an issue
of the tip loss correction. Therefore, since Model 1 and Model 3 exhibit results that are practically identical,
it is preferred to use Model 1 in preference of Model 3. And since Model 1 and Model 3 are nearly identical,
the results from Model 3 is omitted from the remaining part of the thesis for clarity.

Summary
In this chapter, the results of the corrected models are compared to AD results for the restrained Glauert
rotor and for the Tjæreborg wind turbine. The conclusions are listed here:
• For the Glauert rotor it is found that the local quantities in the corrected models agree considerably
better with AD results than Glauert’s BEM model does, especially at the inner part of the blade.
• For the restrained Glauert rotor, the global properties in the corrected models agree extremely well
with the AD results, especially at small design tip speed ratios. Thus, for λ = 2, Model 1 gives a power

power coeﬃcent by 7.3%.
• For the Tjæreborg wind turbine, the largest eﬀect of the corrections is seen when the wind turbine is
operated close to the design tip speed ratio, thus at λ = 7, the power coeﬃcient in Model 1 is 1.4%
below the AD result, while Glauert’s BEM model is 2.8% below. The same tendency was found for
the restrained Glauert rotor when operating away from the design tip speed ratio.
One very important thing to note is that the corrections are always better or unchanged when compared to
Glauert’s BEM model. This means that in all cases, using the corrected models never gives results that are
less reliable than Glauert’s BEM model.

10.2
Summary
In this chapter, error proﬁles computed from AD simulations were used to quantify the error committed in
Glauert’s BEM model. The following conclusions can be drawn from the analysis of the error proﬁles:
• For all the considered rotor cases, the error increases with the tip speed ratio.
• The error function ǫ1 is within 3% on the majority of the blade, but increases considerably at the tip.
The largest errors are for Tjæreborg rotor at λ = 6, where the error is around 12%.
• The error function ǫ3 is negligible for all the rotors considered
• The error function ǫ2 is within 5% (except for the tip of the Tjæreborg rotor at λ = 6)
• In the error function ǫ2, which accounts for the pressure acting on the control volume, the pressure
acting on the lateral boundary ∆Y/∆X is the main contributor. This is especially the case for the
Tjæreborg wind turbine.
This is somewhat surprising since this term is normally assumed to be
insigniﬁcant.
In conclusion, the error functions cannot fully explain the large impact of the corrected models on the inner
part of the blade. Additionally, the error functions show that neglecting ∆Y/∆T leads to an error, especially
in the case of the Tjæreborg wind turbine, for which ∆Y/∆T is far the biggest contributor to ∆X/∆T.

Chapter 11
Conclusion and recommendations
In this thesis, the Blade Element Momentum model is investigated, and particular emphasis is placed on the
basic assumptions which form the foundation of the model. Since the BEM model neglects certain terms
in the momentum balance, it is inevitable that errors are committed. Recently, three corrected model were
suggested by Sørensen (2015). The corrected models account for ﬂow eﬀects due to rotation, which are
ignored in Glauert’s BEM model. At this point, the reader is reminded of the primary goals of the thesis:
• To validate each of the three corrected models against the Actuator Disc model, thereby determining
how the corrected models perform and which of the three corrected models is more suitable
• To quantify the errors committed due to the approximations in Glauert’s BEM model
The three corrections are implemented in the BEM routine and investigated on two diﬀerent Glauert rotors
and on the Tjæreborg wind turbine. Subsequently, the results are compared to Actuator Disc (AD) sim-
ulations, which is considered to have a very high degree of accuracy. Based on the results, the following
conclusions are drawn:
• It was found that all three corrections do indeed improve the BEM model, as the outcome is consider-
ably closer to the AD results. In fact Model 1 is seen to achieve CT and CP -values within a few percent
of the AD-result. Thus, for an optimum ’Glauert’ rotor designed for a tip speed ratio of 2, Glauert’s
BEM model underestimates the gobal power coeﬃcient by 7.3%, while Model 1 is within 0.8% of the
AD result
• The biggest eﬀect of the corrections is found at the inner part of the rotor, and for rotors designed for
low tip speed ratios. It is found that the corrected model correctly predicts a higher thrust- and power
coeﬃcient at the inboard of the rotor.
• The biggest impact of the corrections is found when the rotor is operated close to the tip speed ratio
it is designed for. Thus, for the Tjæreborg wind turbine operated at a tip speed ratio of λ = 7, Model
1 is within 1.4% of the AD result, while Glauert’s BEM model underestimates the power coeﬃcient by
2.8%.
• The errors committed in the BEM model were quantiﬁed via the AD simulation by the use of three
error functions, ǫ1, ǫ2 and ǫ3. In general, it was found that the biggest errors are committed at the
rotor tip and that the error increases with the tip speed ratio. The error committed by assuming
p0 = p1 and u2
theta1 = u2
θ , ǫ3, was negligible for all the considered rotors. The error due to the pressure
acting on the control volume, ǫ2, is within 4%. The error committed by assuming uR = 1/2(U0 + u1),
ǫ1, is within 3% on the majority of the rotor but amounts to 12% at the tip of the Tjæreborg rotor at
λ = 6. The pressure acting on the lateral boundary ∆Y/∆X was found to be the main contributor to
the error due to pressure, ǫ2. Even though the error functions do reveal important properties of the
ﬂow, they cannot fully explain the large impact of the corrected models on the inner part of the blade.
65
==PAGE:68==
All three correction models were found to improve the BEM model, however Model 1 is found to be in
the better agreement with AD results.
In conclusion, the corrected model (Model 1) has some clear advantages. First of all the model is derived
analytically, based on physical considerations. This means that the model is self-contained and does not rely
on e.g. calibrations via other models. Very importantly, the model has proven to provide results impressively
close to AD simulations, which is considered a solid model for validation. Furthermore, it is as simple to
implement and as fast as Glauert’s BEM model, which is crucial in e.g. big design procedures, that rely
on a huge number of BEM computations. Last, but not least, the corrected model always provides results
that are better or un-changed from the BEM results. This means that implementing the corrected model
never leads to results that are less reliable than results obtained by using Glauert’s BEM model. Based on
the analysis in this thesis, it is therefore concluded that the corrected model can be used in preference of
Glauert’s BEM model.
11.1
Recommendations for future investigations
The following suggestions are made for future investigations:
• It is found that the force contribution from pressure acting on the lateral boundary ∆Y/∆T plays a
larger role than expected. Therefore it would be interesting to see if a correction for this term can be
found. Since ∆Y/∆T relies on ﬂow behavior over the entire control volume, it might not be possible to
express a correction in terms of known quantities in the BEM model. Therefore an empiric correction
might be on interest, e.g. based on AD results.
• Design of optimum rotors based on the corrected model. How much more power can be extracted from
the inner part of the blade?
• Further validation of the corrected model against accurate CFD simulations is needed. Such investi-
gations should include tests on real blades and incorporation of common engineering corrections for
e.g. yaw, dynamic wake, tip correction etc. Is it indeed true that the corrected model always provides
results which are better or unchanged from Glauert’s BEM results?
• During this project, a correction for the velocity in the rotor plane uR was derived analytically, based
on the presented theory in this thesis. It is possible to derive an expression for the pressure contribution
∆X/∆T by neglecting ∆Y/∆T, however, implementing the correction in the BEM routine never worked
satisfyingly. It would be very interesting to see how such a correction performs when implemented
succesfully, and more investigations into this correction is therefore suggested.
