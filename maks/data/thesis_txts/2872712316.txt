Abstract
The present study aims to explore the use of earth materials in construction, focusing specif-
ically on compressed earth blocks (CEB) and adobe bricks. The core objective is to evaluate
how biogas fiber, an organic cascade from NGF Nature Energy Biogas A/S’s [1] biogas pro-
duction, can enhance the structural and physical properties of clay sourced from Egernsund
Wienerberger A/S [2].
To understand the fiber’s properties, a site visit to the biogas facility was conducted. This will
provide insight into the biogas fiber’s origin, production process, and potential pretreatments
to improve its bonding ability. Microscopy analyses will further investigate the structural
integration of the fiber within the earth mixtures.
This leads to the production of earth mixtures incorporating 1% and 2% biogas fiber into
a base composition of clay, sand, and water. These mixtures are shaped into cylindrical
specimens (CEB), which are subjected to compression and splitting tests, following European
standards. In addition, the specimens will be tested at different stages throughout their
drying and acclimatization process to evaluate how the mechanical properties change over
time as the moisture content decreases. This will provide a clearer understanding of the
material’s behavior during curing and its readiness for use in real construction contexts.
In the later phase of the project, adobe bricks will be produced using the optimized mixtures
that performed best for CEB. These bricks will be evaluated for water content, durability, and
overall material performance, offering insights into their suitability for real-world applications.
Compression and flexural tests will be conducted to analyze the bricks’ mechanical properties,
following European standards. This hands-on phase is crucial for assessing the practicality
of deploying such sustainable building material in the construction sector.
The findings in the study resulted in a need to further process the fibers. Fourteen days
of curing in plastic film for CEBs containing untreated fibers resulted in fungal and mold
growth. As a result, fiber treatment and a revised drying process were introduced. Specimens
produced with treated fibers did not exhibit the same extent of fungal or mold development.
The CEB tests showed an increase in strength ranging from 5-11% in compression and 10-
16% in splitting, depending on the curing or drying conditions, when 2% treated fibers were
used.
Positive outcomes were also observed for adobe bricks. In terms of flexural strength, the
inclusion of 2% untreated fibers led to a 19% increase compared to specimens made with the
same earth mixture but without fibers. Additionally, the projects adobe bricks demonstrated
a reduction in shrinkage, with the best results achieved using 2% treated biogas fibers.

1 | Introduction
The Danish construction industry generates around 15 million tons of waste each year, two-
thirds of it being surplus soil [3]. This material is often removed during excavation and is
typically transported to disposal sites, leading to significant energy consumption and con-
tributing to environmental inefficiencies. This linear approach to soil waste management is
increasingly at odds with Denmark’s sustainability and circular economy ambitions [4]. Dan-
mark’s ambition is to achieve a 70% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 compared
to 1990 and achieve climate neutrality by 2050 [5].
Given the clay-rich nature of Danish soil, there is untapped potential in reusing this surplus
material locally for construction purposes.
A promising direction involves further development in unfired clay-based building materials
reinforced with natural fibers to enhance their structural and functional performance [6].
Natural fibers can enhance the mechanical properties of clay, including tensile and compres-
sive strength, while also improving moisture regulation, thermal insulation, and resistance to
cracking [6].
This project investigates the structural potential of combining clay-rich earth with locally
sourced natural fibers, with the aim of developing a low-impact building material. At this
stage, the focus is solely on exploring the material’s potential, particularly its mechanical
performance and the effects of fiber reinforcement. If successful, this material could reduce
reliance on high-emission construction products such as concrete and fired bricks, while po-
tentially offering the added benefit of recyclability. The integration of low-energy production
techniques and the use of natural or industrial byproducts supports Denmark’s goals for
environmental responsibility and sustainable innovation in the construction sector.

2.1
Earth as a resource
Interest in building with earth has grown steadily in recent years [6]. This renewed attention
has prompted communities and researchers to explore its potential as an alternative to con-
crete and other carbon-intensive materials. It is important to distinguish between the terms
soil and earth, which are often used interchangeably.
Soil refers to the natural, loose material that makes up the uppermost layer of the ground.
It forms gradually over hundreds of years through weathering processes, which include me-
chanical weathering, such as temperature fluctuations, freezing and thawing, erosion, and
glaciation, that physically break down rocks, as well as chemical weathering, which involves
reactions like oxidation, reduction, and carbonation that decompose the minerals in rocks
[7].
Earth, by contrast, refers to soil that is either naturally suitable or has been engineered for
use as a construction material. In this context, earth signifies the intentional use of soil in
building, whether in its natural state or after it has been modified [8, 9]. The distinction
between common ground material and engineered earth is important when considering its
use in construction.
Figure 2: Soil & Earth [9]
The construction industry produced 15 million tons of waste in 2022, shown in Figure 3.
Two-thirds of the industry’s waste was soil from construction projects such as the metro line
in Copenhagen [3]. As shown in the figure, there is an increasing trend in soil waste. Out of
the 10 million soil waste, approximately 6 million tons were uncontaminated and 4 million
tons were contaminated. While this number gives a clear indication of the scale, it must be
noted that there is a gray area in how soil waste is defined and recorded in Denmark [10]. Not
all excavated material is registered uniformly, and the categorization between waste, reusable
soil, and surplus resource is not always consistent. Nevertheless, there is still great potential
in utilizing resources and reducing the consumption of primary raw materials through the

2.1.1
Building with earth
Building with earth is one of the oldest construction methods and has recently regained
interest due to its sustainability, low energy use, and recyclability. Earth is often locally
sourced, minimizing transport and environmental impact [11].
Earth cannot compete with steel or concrete in mechanical properties, but has proven suitable
for 1-3 story buildings, with potential for larger structures [12]. Earth buildings are seen all
around the world.
Different environments have led to the development of region-specific
construction methods. Mediterranean countries rely on covering their earth walls with a
layer of lime plaster or decorative fired clay tiles, resistant to water [13]. In more northern
climates, techniques were adapted to suit wetter and cooler conditions. In England, cob, a mix
of clay, straw, and water, was built in thick, monolithic walls, while in Germany, wellerwände
used compacted layers of moist earth without formwork. Protective elements like wide roof
overhangs and lime renders helped these structures withstand moisture, demonstrating the
viability of earth building even in temperate regions.

Earth can be a recyclable material, and its use may reduce manufacturing costs when locally
sourced. However, the feasibility of on-site reuse depends significantly on the soil’s compo-
sition, particularly the presence of suitable clay or sand content. Contemporary techniques
such as compressed earth blocks (CEB) and adobe bricks vary in production and composition
but have the advantages of breathability, thermal mass, and low embodied carbon [12].
Building with earth offers environmental benefits, but it also comes with notable limitations.
One major drawback is its sensitivity to water, requiring careful protection against moisture
to prevent erosion and structural damage [6]. Earth has low tensile and flexural strength,
making reinforcement necessary, especially in seismic areas [15]. Construction is often labor-
intensive and depends on skilled workers, while material quality can vary due to inconsistent
soil composition [8]. Additionally, building codes in many regions lack provisions for earth
construction, complicating approval processes [16]. Weather constraints and the need for
thick walls and ongoing maintenance further limit its practicality.
The mechanical strength of earth is the result of friction between grains [6] due to the
increasing number of adhesive and frictional contact spots between particles, emphasizing
the importance of compaction of the brick. Therefore Earth becomes stronger and stiffer
with increasing density [17], and the strength of earth follows the increasing compaction
effort [17].

2.1.1.1
Compressed earth block
Compressed earth block (CEB) is the most widespread contemporary technology of con-
struction with raw earth [17]. The blocks are manufactured with a manual, mechanical, or
hydraulic press, resulting in identical bricks, shown in Figure 5. The earth used for CEB is
often classified as clayey sandy soils, with 10-20% clay, 10-20% silt, and up to 50-70% sand
and coarser particles [17].
CEBs are formed using earth with a moisture content of 5–15%, compacted in a dry state
under slow, steady pressure to ensure uniform density. Densities achieved are in the range

2.1.1.2
Adobe brick
Adobe is a building technique that uses rectangular, sun-dried, unfired clay bricks to build
entire walls and buildings. A traditional exterior load-bearing wall is 50-60 cm thick, and
interior walls can be thinner. The bricks are laid with clay mortar, which is the same material
that bricks are made of, combined with runners and binders as traditional brick walls. [13].
Adobe bricks consist of clay, gravel, and water, and from old analyses, the clay only makes up
25-30% of the dry mass, whereas sand, silt, and crushed stones make up 70-75% [13, 17]. In
Denmark, the moraine soil has almost the same mixing ratio, making the clayed soil suitable
for adobe bricks [13].
Adobe bricks are molded in a soft or plastic state with a water content between 5-40%,
typically in a wooden mold without top or bottom [17].
It must be pressed or rammed
together so it becomes as compact as possible. This process makes adobe bricks less dense,
weaker, and dimensionally uneven compared to compressed earth blocks. Fibers from animal
hair, hemp, flax, and fine straw strips may be included to increase certain properties [13].
After drying, the bricks are knocked out of their molds, where they must dry while protected
from sun and rain [13]. Figure 6 shows there is greater variation in the finished adobe bricks
compared to CEB.
The drying process is relatively long before the adobe bricks can be utilized, making the
production of the bricks longer compared with other earth bricks [17].

2.2
Organic fibers
Throughout history, natural fibers have been added to earthen construction materials to
improve their mechanical and physical properties. Fibers such as straw, animal hair, and
plant stems have traditionally been mixed with clay to enhance tensile strength, reduce
shrinkage, and limit cracking during drying [11, 6]. These additions made earthen structures
more durable, breathable, and better suited to their local climates.
The traditional methods continue to inspire modern approaches. In this project, that legacy
is carried forward through the use of biogas fibers, sourced from a Danish biogas company,
NGF Nature Energy Biogas A/S. This creates a link between time-tested building knowledge
and contemporary efforts to develop environmentally responsible, low-impact construction
materials.
2.2.1
NGF Nature Energy Biogas A/S
NGF Nature Energy Biogas A/S is a Danish company that has 13 biogas facilities in Denmark.
In 2023, their biogas facilities processed more than 5.1 million tons of biomass waste from
agriculture, industry, and Danish citizens. From 5.1 million tons of waste, 207 million m3 of
biogas was produced, corresponding to annually heating 180.000 households [18].
Biogas is a methane gas created through the anaerobic breakdown of organic waste and
residues. As a renewable energy source, biogas is crucial to the green transition, with bio-
methane serving as a sustainable alternative to fossil natural gas [19].
In 2023, biogas accounted for nearly 40% of Denmark’s gas system, and by 2030, it is projected
to account for 100% [19]. According to the Danish Energy Agency, straw plays an important
role in achieving the goal, where today straws account for 2% of the input. It is estimated
that an increase to 9% straw input is necessary to reach the goal by 2030 [19] and therefore
increase the amount of cascade products such as biogas fibers.
In Denmark, biogas and biofuels accounted for 9.22 % of the total production of primary
energy in 2023 [20]. Figure 7 shows that the production of biogas has more than tripled from
2015 to 2023. This increase is largely due to support schemes that have aimed to promote
the use of biogas for electricity production, process purposes, and heat production [19].

In this project, a collaboration with NGF Nature Energy Biogas A/S (Nature Engergy) has
made it possible to conduct experiments with sponsored biogas fibers from the facility in
Videbæk, Denmark.
The biogas production is based on manure and deep bedding from local farmers and after
the anaerobic breakdown, the farmers collect the digestate waste as fertilizer. Nature energy
keeps records of the in- and output of Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Potassium since the Danish
Agricultural Board requires fertilizer accounting from farmers [22].
To comply with the
quotas and the demand for fertilizer, Nature Energy mechanically separates and removes
these phosphorus-rich fibers, resulting in a cascade product, biogas fibers.
Today, the fibers are a costly waste stream rather than a resource for Nature Energy A/S.
A promising alternative is to use the fibers as part of a bio-based building material, helping
increase the circular economy and thereby contributing positively to the green transition in
Denmark.

1. Manure and deep bedding delivered by nearby farmers are stored separately in two
designated storage areas at the biogas facility.
2. Automated crane handles the deep bedding, which can have a length of up to 1 meter.
3. A portion of deep bedding (measured as dry matter) is combined with a corresponding
amount of manure in a mixer, where the materials are thoroughly blended.
4. The rota-blocker forces larger and tougher objects out before entering the reactors.
5. The rota blocker chops/cuts the straw from the deep bedding into smaller pieces, which
are sent further through a 35 mm sieve.
6. The same process is then repeated, with a 25 mm sieve.
7. The mixture is transferred to a 9000 m3 reactor, where it is stored for 20 days at 50 ◦C,
during which methane is produced.
8. The mixture and methane are sent to a new reactor for 7 days at 50 ◦C. This extra
reactor ensures most bacteria go into hibernation.
9. Raw gas (Methane) of 4-5000 m3 per hour is sent to a sulfur purifier. This ensures the
last Salmonella bacteria are removed.
10. Finally, the raw gas undergoes a final quality check to ensure that the CO2 content
remains within the acceptable range of 2–7% ppm. Once approved, an odorant is added
to the gas, as methane is naturally odorless.
11. The biogas is sent to the main grid.

3.1
Mechanical properties of soil
The mechanical behavior of soil is very complex and is due to the micro- and macrostruc-
ture, variability, and the physical and chemical interactions with air and water in soil voids.
The mechanical behavior of soil is that of a nonlinear, non-conservative, anisotropic, and
viscoelastic-plastic material [7]. Formulating an exact and general mathematical description
of stress-strain-time behavior of soil can prove difficult, which is also the reason why labo-
ratory test, field tests and empirical correlations is used. Complex mechanical behavior is
typically idealized and simplified, a linear response is often assumed for small strains, with
uniform material properties and direction-independent behavior generally taken for granted
[7].
Due to poor engineering properties of soil, improvement or stabilization may be required,
typically through chemical or mechanical methods.
Chemical stabilizing may be mixing
materials such as cement, lime and other chemicals into the soil. Mechanical stabilization
with compression removes the air within the soil without a significant change in water content.
This will increase the strength and decrease compressibility and permeability [7].
3.1.1
Clay minerals and soil composition
Soil consists mainly of mineral matter, but it also contains organic material, including living
plants, animals (e.g. bacteria, fungi, worms), animal waste, and decomposing organic matter
[6]. The predominant minerals in rocks and therefore in soils, and particularly in clay are
silica, mica, feldspar, amphiboles, and pyroxenes[7, 6].
The crystals of clay minerals are very small, with an average diameter of 1-2 µm or less, and
they have a plate-like shape [7]. The most frequent clay minerals in soil are kaolinite, illite,
and montmorillonite [6] shown in Figure 10.
(a) Kaolinite

The structure of the clay minerals consists of layers made up of oxygen tetrahedrons with
a silicon center (siliceous tetrahedral layer: white shade) and a layer of oxygen octahedrons
with an aluminum center (aluminous octahedron layer: gray shaded) [6].

Kaolinite sheets are a double-layered structure and are negatively charged at the edge of the
sheets with a low ion-binding, and there is a constant distance of 7Å between the sheets [11].
Illites have a three-layer structure, with a siliceous tetrahedral layer as the outer layer and an
aluminous octahedron layer in between, which creates an ion binding capacity. The sheet is
non-saturated and is boned by K ions that balance the negative charges. There is a distance
of 10Å between the sheets, and illites are stable in contact with water [11].
Montmorillonites resemble the Illites but are not electrically neutral and are weakly linked.
The ions between the sheet are exchangeable cations (Na, Ca) and water molecules. When
in contact with water, the mineral is not stable and suffers severely from swelling. Therefore,
the distance between the sheets is in a range of 14-20Å [6, 11].
3.1.2
Unconfined compressive strength vs. dry density
A well-established relationship in earthen construction is the positive correlation between
dry density and unconfined compressive strength.
As dry density increases, the particle
packing improves, reducing internal voids and enhancing interparticle friction and cohesion.
These effects translate directly into higher compressive strength, making dry density a critical
indicator of structural performance in both laboratory and field settings.
Multiple studies across a range of earthen materials, like adobe, rammed earth, and CEB,
confirm that increasing dry density yields consistently higher compressive strength values.
Minke (2006) [11] and Houben Guillaud (1994) [6] both report that increasing compaction
effort, which raises dry density, can double or even triple compressive strength depending on
soil type and moisture content as shown in Figure 11.

In an experimental study on stabilized adobe, a 10% increase in dry density led to a 30–50%
increase in compressive strength under standard curing conditions [11]. This relationship is
seen in fine-grained soils where clay and silt fractions dominate, as these materials benefit
from denser packing and improved matrix integrity [11]. In rammed earth construction, dry
densities exceeding 2000 kg/m3 have been linked to compressive strengths of over 3 MPa
without additives, compared to values below 2 MPa for specimens below 1700 kg/m3 [23, 16].
The increase in strength is not only due to reduced void ratio, but also to the enhancement
of interparticle contact forces during compaction.
It is important to note that the density–strength correlation may level or even reverse at
excessively high compaction levels, particularly if they lead to over-drying, cracking, or dis-
ruption of fiber reinforcements. Therefore, while maximizing dry density is a common design
goal, optimal compaction must balance density with workability, moisture control, and rein-
forcement [6].
In CEB production, mechanical compaction is a central process.
The aim is to achieve
high dry densities through controlled and repeatable application of pressure, often exceeding
2 MPa, to produce blocks with minimal voids and high uniformity.
This dense packing
increases interparticle friction and reduces moisture sensitivity, thereby improving strength
and durability. Uniform compaction also ensures that test samples accurately represent full
blocks, making the mechanical performance of the units more predictable and structurally
reliable.

In contrast, adobe bricks rely primarily on gravity and limited manual compression during
casting. Rather than maximizing compaction force, adobe production prioritizes even dis-
tribution of the mix within the mold. The flowability of the fresh earth mix is critical: it
must be cohesive enough to prevent segregation, but plastic enough to fill corners and edges
uniformly [24]. In this context, uniformity of material distribution, rather than maximum
density, becomes the key to producing consistent and testable bricks. Uneven settling or
differential drying can introduce weak zones, so managing the internal homogeneity of the
brick is more important than maximizing compressive strength.
3.1.3
Plasticity
The engineering behavior of fine-grained soils has little relation to texture, grain size and
shape, or grain size distribution. It is more influenced by electrochemical than gravitational
forces. Therefore, the plasticity and cohesiveness of the clay are highly influenced by the
water surrounding the clay crystals and pores [7, 6]. Well hydrated cohesive soil can, after it
has reached its elastic limit, deform without breaking due to plasticity from the lubricating
effect of films of water between the grains [6]. The water content of soil can be compared
to certain standards of engineering behavior, such as Atterberg limits, which gives a good
indication of the soil behavior. The mechanical behavior of soil depends on its water content
and can range from a brittle solid to liquid [7, 6] shown in Figure 6.

3.2
Fiber
Incorporating certain fibers into clay-rich earth composites has emerged as an effective strat-
egy to mitigate drying-shrinkage cracking and to enhance post-peak ductility under bending
and splitting loads. Fibers bridge micro-cracks during drying, redistribute stresses under flex-
ural or tensile action, and provide energy dissipation through pull-out mechanisms. Studies
have shown results for the following categories.
• Animal Fibers (e.g., wool, hair): Offer high tensile strength (170 MPa) and moderate
elongation (35%), with natural scales that enhance mechanical interlock.
Optimal
dosages (0.3-0.5%) improve both compressive and flexural strengths by 5-10% and
markedly reduce shrinkage cracks [25, 26].
• Plant Fibers (e.g., straw, sisal, palm): Low-cost and biodegradable; hollow geometry
yields good thermal performance but lower tensile strength (Table 2). At 0.5-1% con-
tent, they reduce crack width and improve toughness, though variability in dimensions
necessitates careful selection and treatment [27].
• Synthetic Fibers (e.g., polypropylene, polyethylene): Provide uniform geometry, high
durability, and consistent bond characteristics.
At 0.5-1%, they yield the greatest
improvements in post-peak flexural strength (up to 50%) and control plastic shrinkage
effectively, albeit at a higher cost and environmental footprint [28].

bond. High aspect-ratio fibers (aspect ratio > 100) ensure efficient stress transfer and crack-
bridging capacity, but may entangle during mixing if too long (lengths > 30 mm).

4.1.1
Processing of earth
To ensure consistent and controlled properties of the earth used in testing and production,
a standardized processing method is followed. This method aims to eliminate natural vari-
ability such as moisture content and particle agglomeration, and to produce a homogeneous
fine-grained earth suitable for mixing with additives like sand and fibers.
The clayed-soil is first delivered in large big bags and contains a natural moisture content that
must be removed. The clayed-soil is spread onto metal containers and dried in a laboratory
oven at 50 ◦C for a minimum of 72 hours. After drying, the earth is immediately sealed in
plastic containers to prevent moisture uptake from the ambient environment.

to break down clumps and create a finer, more uniform material. Approximately 10 kg of

revolutions. This ensures thorough disintegration of aggregated particles. The ground soil is
then sieved through a 0.4 mm sieve to remove oversized fractions and ensure size consistency.
Finally, the sieved material is transferred to a new sealed plastic container and stored until

4.3.1
Optimum moisture content
The optimum moisture content (OMC) is based on the same technique as Geyti (2023) [41].
The study discussed using the proctor method to determine the OMC. Based on studies
from Boussaa et al. [47] and Abbou et al. [48], the proctor method is unfit for determining
the OMC when working with CEB. This is because the proctor compaction method differs
significantly from the compaction technique used in the production of CEB’s. As a result,
the correlation between water content and dry density obtained through the proctor method
does not accurately reflect the actual relationship observed in the final compressed specimens.
Therefore, a more suitable approach to determining the OMC was adopted by applying static
compression using a hydraulic press, consistent with the method used to produce the CEB
specimens for final testing. This approach was also used to evaluate whether the reference
earth mixture could be directly compared (1:1) to the results obtained by Geyti (2023) [41].
The OMC was investigated for all earth mixtures listed in Table 10, including REF, UF1,
UF2, TF1, and TF2. A hydraulic press, EME-052-Instron-6022 was used to produce the test
specimens, with water contents ranging from 8-18%.
Each specimen used approximately 350g ± 1g of the mixture. For REF, this weight is from
soil, sand and water. Included in this weight is 3,5g fiber for UF1 and TF1 and 7g fiber for
UF2 and TF2, as mentioned in section 4.3 and shown in table 8.
The earth was mixed with water by hand and sealed in a plastic container overnight, to
ensure uniform moisture distribution. After producing the specimens for each water content,
the actual moisture content was determined by drying three 20 g samples of the mixture at
105C◦for a minimum of 24 hours.

The standard DS/EN 1015-3 [53] is used for determining the consistency of fresh mortar.
Due to the high range of water content that is investigated in the adobe bricks, the method

in adobe bricks. In addition, the adobe bricks are going to be cast in the same molds as
mortar specimens to test compression and flexural bending, described in standards DS/EN
12390-3:2019 [50] and DS/EN 12390-5:2019 [54].
The flow value of mixture TF2 with a water content ranging from 20-40% is determined by
the procedure, shown in Figure 35. A truncated conical brass mold, with an internal diameter
of 100 mm in the bottom and 70 mm at the top, is placed inside the 100 mm circle on the
flow table, after which another truncated conical brass mold is placed on top to ensure that
the mixture gets into the first mold [35a]. The mold is then half filled with the mixture and
compacted with a tamper with 10 short strokes [35b]. The mold is then filled and compacted
again with 10 strokes with the tamper [35b]. The upper mold is removed, and the top of the
mixture can now be scraped off with a palette knife [35c]. The lower conical mold is removed,
and the handle is turned 15 times to make the mixture flow out [35d]. The diameter of the
mixture is then measured – vertically from the handle and horizontally at a 90-degree angle.
This process is done twice for the same mixture, resulting in an average flow value.

It is expected that the true water content deviated from the theoretical value, as multiple
factors can influence moisture levels. The earth mixture was combined with water and stored
in an airtight container overnight, with the container weighed before and after to confirm no
significant water loss due to evaporation. Nevertheless, environmental conditions—such as
high ambient temperatures, may have affected the specimens. From the time the specimens
were produced, measured, weighed, wrapped in plastic, and set aside for curing, some water
evaporation may have occurred.
Additionally, differences in the soil itself could affect the results. The soil used in this study
was sourced from Egensund and delivered in big bags; given the two-year gap between the
two projects, the exact same clayed-soil was not used. Although a full characterization of
the soil used in this project might reveal some differences from the material used by Geyti
(2023) [41], these variations are not expected to have a major impact on the overall results.

choosing the 40–50% window, still safely on the linear, elastic part of the curve, the modulus
becomes not only higher but also much more consistent from one specimen to the next. The

6.3
Biogas fibers in earth construction
Based on the sections above, treated fibers have demonstrated a positive impact on both
CEB and adobe bricks. In CEB bricks, they contributed to improved strength and reduced
density, while in adobe bricks, they helped reduce shrinkage and enhance flexural strength. As
a result, the fiber-reinforced earth mixtures produce a strong yet lightweight material. This
is advantageous not only for reducing physical handling efforts and transportation emissions
but also for contributing to a more sustainable construction approach.
However, the inclusion of treated fibers introduces additional steps in the production of fin-
ished CEB or adobe bricks, which in turn can increase the overall environmental impact.
During the fiber treatment, the fibers were washed with clean water, increasing water con-
sumption. The resulting wastewater, contaminated with bacteria and manure residues, must
either be treated in a wastewater facility or repurposed for other uses, both options poten-
tially having negative sustainability implications. Following washing, the fibers undergo heat
treatment, which demands significant energy input and further contributes to environmental
impact. Additionally, treated fibers tend to become more entangled than untreated ones,
requiring an extra step to separate them, which delays production and reduces processing
efficiency.
This project aims to contribute to a more sustainable construction industry, and the biogas
fibers should contribute to the green transition, but before one can assess whether it is worth
the increase in strength, an LCA must be prepared to assess the entire process of making
bricks with and without fibers.
Without this comparison, one can only conclude on the
mechanical properties and only assess the sustainable results.

7 | Conclusion
This project investigates the possibility of utilizing biogas fibers in earth construction by
looking into how biogas fibers influence the mechanical and physical performance of the
compressed earth blocks (CEB) and adobe bricks. The fibers were evaluated by comparing
specimens with and without fibers. In addition, the effect of fiber treatment was investigated
by comparing specimens with treated and untreated fibers. The decision to treat the fibers
was prompted by early observations of fungal growth on specimens containing untreated
fibers. Post-treatment, a visible reduction in fungal growth was observed, indicating the
effectiveness of the treatment process.
This study is divided into two parts: compressed earth blocks (CEB) and adobe bricks. This
distinction is based on differences in production methods, and as such, the results from each
cannot be directly compared. However, the motivation for producing both lies in applying
insights gained from CEB testing to the development of adobe bricks. Since CEBs are more
densely compacted, they are expected to yield higher mechanical performance.

In conclusion, fiber addition improved mechanical properties and reduced material weight
in compressed earth blocks. For adobe bricks, fibers enhanced flexural strength and helped
minimize shrinkage. This study serves as an initial exploration, and the results suggest a
promising potential for biogas fibers. Therefore, further research into their application in
earth construction is recommended.

8 | Further work
There are several aspects of further work that can be looked into. Below is a prioritized list
of future projects.
Material: Sustainability evaluation
An important direction for future work is the evaluation of earth materials through compre-
hensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). Comparing unfired earth materials, such as adobe or
compressed earth blocks (CEB), with conventional building materials like concrete, brick,
or wood can help quantify their environmental performance. Key LCA indicators—such as
embodied energy, carbon footprint, end-of-life reuse potential, and material toxicity, can re-
veal both the advantages and limitations of earth-based materials. This approach provides
a robust foundation for understanding their role in sustainable construction and supports
evidence-based decisions in material selection for environmentally responsible building prac-
tices.
CEB and adobe: Durability testing under water exposure
Long-term durability should be assessed through exposure tests like dripping (drop test),
capillary suction, and immersion. These tests would simulate real-life weathering conditions
and indicate how well the bricks maintain structural integrity when subjected to moisture,
which is particularly relevant for outdoor applications.
Increased fiber Content (1–5%)
Some studies suggest that increasing fiber content up to 5% improves tensile strength, though
often at the cost of compressive strength.
Exploring this trade-off in a controlled study
would clarify the mechanical limits and optimal balance for structural and non-structural
applications.
Fiber treatment and processing techniques
Further research into fiber treatment and processing is essential to improve both hygiene
and mechanical performance. Exploring different cleaning methods—such as ethanol, UV
light, lime, or water—could help reduce mold growth while maintaining sustainability. At
the same time, investigating how physical modifications (e.g., fraying or splitting) influence
fiber-soil interaction may enhance mechanical bonding, stress transfer, and ultimately, tensile
and flexural strength. These effects could be supported through microscopic analysis, along
with measurements of pH and water absorption before and after treatment. Additionally,
the byproducts from fiber processing warrant further examination.
CEB and fibers: Aspect ratio
The aspect ratio, both of test specimens and the fibers themselves, can significantly influence
test results. Standardizing fiber length and specimen dimensions according to accepted norms
could improve or worsen consistant results. Additionally, testing the effect of halving the fiber
length (e.g., by cutting or shredding) could show how shorter fibers affect mechanical bonding
and crack resistance.
Full recyclability
Given the emphasis on sustainability, a critical area for future work is the experimental

evaluation of the material’s recyclability. Although unfired earth materials are often described
as fully recyclable, this assumption should be tested under practical conditions. Investigations
could include the mechanical breakdown of used adobe specimens, their rehydration and
remixing, and the reformation into new bricks. Subsequent testing of strength, shrinkage, and
durability in the recycled bricks would help determine the extent to which the material retains
its performance over multiple life cycles. Additionally, examining whether the presence of
natural fibers—especially those treated or degraded—affects recyclability could offer insights
into how fiber type, treatment, and content influence reuse potential.
Adobe: Formwork removal and early load-bearing capacity
Future studies should explore optimal timing for removing adobe bricks from molds. Un-
derstanding how soon the bricks can bear weight without deformation or cracking is key
for efficient construction. Testing the structural integrity after different curing periods (e.g.,
1, 2, 5 days) can simulate realistic building phases where newly laid bricks must support
subsequent layers without delay.
