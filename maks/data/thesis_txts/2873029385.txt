Abstract
This thesis presents a mathematical optimisation approach to improve production schedul­
ing in the moulding operations at Novo Nordisk’s Device Component Manufacturing site.
The production system is characterised by high complexity, involving multiple articles,
moulds and production lines, each subject to strict operational constraints such as mould­
line compatibility, colour transitions, setup times and stock limits. The aim of this study
is to develop a scalable and practically optimisation model that minimises unmet demand
while maintaining feasible and efficient production schedules.
The proposed solution is based on Mixed­Integer Programming (MIP) combined with a
domain­specific decomposition strategy. To handle the problem’s scale, a three­layered
framework was developed: a discrete­time MIP model to represent core decision rules,
a clustering method to group related production lines and a batch­wise solving technique
for large clusters. This framework balances model fidelity and computational efficiency.
The model was implemented in Julia using JuMP and Gurobi and tested on artificial
dataset from Novo Nordisk. Results show that demand fulfilment improved significantly
from below 40% with the existing solution to over 90% using the optimisation technique
developed in this thesis while keeping setup activities and mould changes at realistic and
manageable levels. Unlike the previous approach the new model introduces soft con­
straints to regulate the frequency of mould and colour changes which were previously
limited through hard rules, which is a trade­off in the solution proposal. Despite this dif­
ference the results indicate strong potential for further reducing both unmet demand and
machine idle time. The model generates production schedules in a planner­friendly format
and connects seamlessly with existing data templates and workflows, supporting practical
integration into the company’s planning processes.
Although some simplifications, like decomposition and time discretisation, were neces­
sary, the model structure allows for future extensions. This work demonstrates that Oper­
ations Research techniques can deliver significant value in industrial settings by providing
structured, transparent and high­performing scheduling solutions for complex manufac­
turing environments.

Introduction
Efficient production planning is a crucial factor in manufacturing, impacting both opera­
tional costs and overall productivity. In industries such as pharmaceutical manufacturing,
optimising production scheduling can lead to significant improvements in efficiency.
This thesis focuses on the case of Novo Nordisk, a Danish multinational pharmaceutical
company headquartered in Bagsværd, Denmark. Founded in 1923, the company spe­
cialises in treatments for chronic diseases such as diabetes, obesity and rare endocrine
disorders. According to the company’s 2024 annual report (Novo Nordisk A/S 2025),
Novo Nordisk employed 77,349 people worldwide. This was an increase of over 13,000
compared to the previous year and reflects the company’s rapid growth. Its products are
marketed in 170 countries, and in 2024, it served more than 45.2 million people living with
serious chronic diseases. Novo Nordisk reported annual revenues of DKK 290.4 billion
in 2024, a 25% increase from the previous year, driven by strong sales in diabetes and
obesity care.
At Novo Nordisk’s Device Component Manufacturing (DCM) site in Hillerød, plastic com­
ponents are produced through a process called moulding where liquid, coloured plastic is
injected into moulds to form parts used for injector pens. These components are critical
for global drug delivery and must be produced in large volumes with minimal disruption.
The site in Hillerød also supplies other production locations, which makes efficient and
seamless production essential.
The key challenge lies in determining when and how to allocate moulds and colours across
the available production lines, as changing them is a time­consuming task for the com­
pany.
In 2019 Novo Nordisk implemented a Python­based scheduling model to support planning
of their moulding operations. The model builds a mathematical formulation that minimises
soft constraint violations in a prioritised sequence. This involves adding constraints one
at a time and adjusting the solution afterwards. Using this technique, while the model
includes some optimisation, it remains limited in its ability to fully capture the operational
complexity. As a result it often requires manual adjustments and delivers sub­optimal
plans.
These inefficiencies result in poor mould allocation, low machine utilisation and unsta­
ble sequencing which contribute to unmet demand or poor priotisation of produced prod­
ucts. Given the increasing production pressure and complexity of their environment Novo
Nordisk has clearly expressed interest in a more advanced approach to scheduling that
can ensure demand fulfillment, maintain stock thresholds and reduce machine changeovers.
As stated by a company representative, ”The ideal scenario is for production to run 24/7.”
This highlights the real­world importance of the project, namely optimising scheduling is
not just about finding a feasible solution, but about achieving a continuously running pro­
duction process. Better scheduling performance directly improves production efficiency
and ultimately supports the timely delivery of essential medication to patients.
This thesis looks at tactical production scheduling in the DCM department, focusing only
on moulding operations and not on later steps like assembly or packaging. Labour and
energy constraints are ignored for the purpose of simplicity, and input data such as de­
mand forecasts and stock levels are assumed to be accurate. The model accounts for

Problem Statement
Novo Nordisk is currently facing challenges in the production scheduling of its moulding
operations due to the complexity of managing multiple moulds, plastic components and
production lines. Efficient scheduling is crucial for reducing machine downtime, increasing
overall output and ensuring reliable delivery performance.
For Novo Nordisk to produce the injection pens, both the insulin and the plastic casing
must be manufactured. The plastic casing consists of several small components, primarily
made of plastic, which Novo Nordisk has the capacity to produce in­house. These plastic
components will be referred to as articles throughout this thesis.
To produce an article, coloured plastic granules are transported into a machine where
metal injection moulds shape the articles within a few seconds. Once formed, the plastic
parts are released from the moulds.
Some articles require additional processing, such as labelling.
Certain machines are
equipped with specific capabilities to handle such tasks. A moulding machine, with or
without these additional features, will be referred to as a (production) line.
The primary objective of this project is to determine how moulds can be efficiently allocated
to available lines over time in order to minimise unmet demand. This study develops
a mathematical optimisation model capable of generating feasible production schedules
under realistic industrial constraints. Each line can only hold one mould at any given time,
and not every mould is compatible with every line. This compatibility restriction limits the
valid combinations for production.
In addition, switching between moulds and colours involves setup durations that can range
from a few hours to several days. These times must be included in the schedule. A key
challenge is to maximise the use of each mould. A switch with moulds are illustrated in
Figure 2.1, where mould m1 is running on line 1 and mould m2 on line 2. The takedown
time of m2 is longer the m1’s, meaning it must be removed from line 2 well before line 1 is
ready. Meanwhile, m1 can still be used for production. This overlap highlights the need for
coordinated planning to ensure that each mould is fully utilised until it is no longer needed.

Literature Review on Production
Scheduling
Production scheduling is a core topic in sectors like manufacturing, logistics and energy.
It is about assigning tasks to resources over time to improve goals such as production
time, cost and use of resources. Although this study focuses on scheduling in moulding
operations, approaches developed for related problems will be reviewed in this chapter
to extract adaptable methods and modelling techniques.
The scheduling problem addressed in this thesis is rooted in a specific production context
but draws on ideas from several classical problems.
Parallel Machine Scheduling involves assigning jobs to multiple machines operating
in parallel, with the objective of minimising makespan, tardiness or resource idle time
(Pinedo 2005, p. 82). This is relevant for moulding at Novo Nordisk, where several lines
can produce concurrently, but only one mould can be active per line at a time. In addition,
mould changes and colour transitions introduce setup times that are dependent on the
job sequence. The scheduling model developed in this thesis captures this parallel struc­
ture and includes constraints that limit simultaneous mould usage and impose delays for
configuration changes, just as in sequence­dependent parallel scheduling.
Lot Sizing and Scheduling problems focus on determining both the production quantity
and timing of products, while satisfying demand and stock constraints (Pinedo 2005, p.
141). In the moulding context, each article has weekly demand and a maximum stock
level. The model must therefore balance between producing early to ensure continuity
and avoiding excess inventory. This mirrors the classical trade­off in lot­sizing models:
when to produce and how much, given capacity and storage limitations. However, in this
thesis, the lot size is not a free decision variable but determined implicitly by production
time and mould capacity, making the formulation more constrained and discrete.
Flexible Job Shop Scheduling generalises the classical job shop problem by allowing
each operation to be performed on multiple alternative machines, but with potentially dif­
ferent performance or compatibility (Pinedo 2005, p. 23). This directly reflects the mould
and line compatibility matrix in the case at Novo Nordisk, where not all moulds can be
installed on all lines. Additionally, the time it takes to switch a mould (and sometimes the
colour) varies by mould type. The model therefore needs to manage this flexibility while
respecting binary compatibility rules, which is structurally equivalent to machine flexibility
in job shop models.
What distinguishes the moulding problem from the above standard formulations is the
presence of integrated setup logic (put­up and takedown durations), rolling horizon plan­
ning and hard constraints on configuration carry­over between planning windows. Further­
more, while most classical models assume either deterministic or continuous production
decisions, the moulding environment requires a discrete and binary approach to alloca­
tion, due to the indivisibility of mould setups and strict capacity structure.
For these reasons, the moulding scheduling problem can be seen as a hybrid of the three
mentioned formulations, but with added complexity in timing, configuration and demand
fulfilment. The remainder of this chapter explores how classical and modern methods can

be adapted to this hybrid setting, and which trade­offs are involved in making the model
both realistic and easy to control.
3.1
General tools for Scheduling Techniques
There are many ways to solve scheduling problems, where among the most applied tech­
niques are Machine Learning or classical Operations Research.
3.1.1
Machine Learning
Machine Learning (ML) provides data­driven methods to model and solve complex de­
cision problems. In scheduling, ML techniques can learn from experience to improve
performance in uncertain and dynamic environments.
Recent research shows how Deep Reinforcement Learning (RL), a branch of ML, can
solve real­world scheduling problems. In S. Lee, Cho, and Y. H. Lee (2020), a method
called Deep Q­Network (DQN) was used to plan mould production. DQN is a learning
algorithm that improves decisions over time by simulating many scenarios and learning
what works best. It was compared to traditional simple strategies like always choosing
the job with the earliest deadline or the shortest remaining time. The DQN approach
performed better, especially when dealing with changes in processing times and machine
setups. It could adapt to new conditions without being retrained.
This is highly relevant for the moulding environment at Novo Nordisk, where setups (e.g.
colour and mould changes) introduce variability and processing times are not constant.
The ability of DQN to adapt dynamically without retraining suggests that RL­inspired el­
ements could supplement traditional models to handle uncertainties more robustly. Al­
though this thesis does not apply RL directly, the adaptive capabilities of DQN motivate
the need for flexibility within OR models, especially in a production environment charac­
terised by constant reconfiguration.
3.1.2
Operations Research
Operations Research (OR) is a discipline that uses mathematical models, statistical analy­
ses and optimisation techniques to support decision­making and solve complex problems
in systems or processes. It aims to find the most effective or efficient ways to design,
operate or manage systems under constraints like cost, time and resources.
This project focuses on OR methods due to their structured, transparent and mathemat­
ically rigorous nature, which aligns well with the operational mindset at Novo Nordisk.
Unlike many ML approaches, which often function as ”black boxes” with limited insight
into how solutions are found, OR models offer clear explanations and allow us to under­
stand and evaluate how close a solution is to the global optimum. This transparency is
very valuable in regulated and high­stakes settings, where understanding the reasoning
behind decisions is as important as the decisions themselves.
3.2
Classical OR Techniques for Scheduling
The most commonly applied OR techniques for production scheduling problems will be
presented in this section.
3.2.1
Mixed­Integer Linear Programming
Mixed­Integer Linear Programming (MIP) is a powerful mathematical method often used
for solving scheduling problems. It gives a clear way to model complex constraints and
goals. A typical MIP model for production scheduling looks like this:

Here, x stands for decision variables that control job assignments. y includes extra vari­

system and b defines the limits on resources.
MIP is a powerful method because it can handle complex constraints and find optimal
solutions. However, it often becomes slow when the problem is large, since the number
of variables and constraints grows quickly. This makes it hard to find the global optimum in
practice. Still, many studies show that MIP works well in real industrial cases (Yang 2020).
This supports the choice of using MIP as a core technique in this thesis. Yang et al. show
how MIP can model realistic production constraints, similar to those in moulding, such as
capacity limits, setup times and product flow. Their results show that MIP can produce
good solutions in complex settings, but also reveal that solving time becomes a challenge
as problems grow. This motivates the use of hybrid methods and decomposition in this
thesis to improve scalability.
In industrial settings, MIP is often combined with heuristics to balance model accuracy
and solving time.
Klement et al. (2021) present a hybrid approach for the plastic in­
jection moulding industry, where lot­sizing and scheduling are solved together.
Their
model includes machine constraints, setup times and sequence dependencies, and uses
a decomposition­based heuristic to handle larger instances. This is highly relevant for
Novo Nordisk, where the scheduling problem involves many Stock Keeping Units, par­
allel lines and frequent mould changes. Klement et al. test their method on real­world
inspired instances with up to 191 jobs, 63 moulds and 25 machines. For smaller cases
(10–15 jobs), both the hybrid method and a standard MIP solver find the same solution,
but the hybrid method is much faster. For larger instances, the solver runs for over 30
hours without a solution, while the hybrid method finds good results in seconds to a few
hours. For example, in an instance with 32 jobs, their method finds a high­quality solution
in 96 seconds, while the solver fails to finish within 30 hours.
In comparison, the case studied in this thesis involves 44 machines, 97 moulds, 64 colours
and a total of 174 articles. While the number of individual jobs is not explicitly defined,
the model operates on demand­driven planning, where each article’s requirement can be
fulfilled by several compatible machines. This structure differs from job­based models but
remains highly complex due to shared moulds, colour­dependent setups and overlapping
constraints across multiple production lines.
These results show that hybrid methods can reduce solving time significantly while still
delivering high­quality schedules. This supports the approach used in this thesis, which
combines exact and heuristic techniques to ensure both quality and scalability.
3.2.2
Heuristics and Metaheuristics
Heuristics are simple, rule­based methods used to find good solutions within limited time
and computing power. They do not always find the best possible solution, but they often
work well in practice, especially when problems are large or need to be solved quickly.
Common heuristics include rules like choosing the task with the earliest due date or the
shortest remaining processing time.
Optimising Production Scheduling for Moulding Operations

==PAGE:15==
Metaheuristics are more advanced strategies that help guide heuristics to explore the
solution space more effectively. They are designed to avoid getting stuck in poor solutions
by trying different alternatives and learning from past choices. Examples include genetic
algorithms (which try new solutions by mixing and changing the best ones), simulated
annealing (which allows occasional worse moves to avoid getting stuck) and tabu search
(which keeps track of recent moves to prevent cycles). These methods are especially
helpful when the goal is to find a good balance between trying new solutions and improving
existing ones.
Even though these approaches do not always find the global optimum, they perform well
in practical situations where exact methods such as MIP are too slow or infeasible. Boc­
tor, Renaud, and Rapp (2009) demonstrate this by applying metaheuristics to a complex
scheduling problem in a multi­mould injection moulding setting. This is highly relevant, as
Boctor also considers multiple moulds and sequence­dependent changeovers, which is
two key challenges in Novo Nordisk’s production environment. While the product types
differ, the structural similarities support the use of metaheuristics to handle related con­
straints. Their rule­based methods are able to manage overlapping jobs, shared feeders
and product­to­mould relationships effectively. Computation times vary from under one
second to 19 seconds per instance, depending on the method and search depth. Their
tests include cases where up to 130,000 solutions are explored. However, the moulding
problem at Novo Nordisk is expected to involve an even larger search space.
Heuristics are useful when there is limited time or computer power available. Polo­Mejía
et al. (2023) show this by using simple and advanced heuristic methods to solve a com­
plex scheduling problem with multi­skilled workers, time windows and task dependencies.
Each test case represents a realistic weekly plan in a nuclear lab with 50 activities, tech­
nicians with different skills and tasks that can be partly interrupted. They compare their
heuristics to exact methods that are given up to two hours of solving time. In contrast,
the heuristics are only allowed 5 minutes per case but still produce good results. They
also test a sliding time window (which will be covered in Section 4.2.1) approach to im­
prove performance further. Even though their focus is project scheduling, their problem
shares key features with the moulding problem in this thesis. Both involve skill match­
ing, resource limits and tight timing. This shows that heuristics can be useful when exact
methods are too slow.
3.2.3
Decomposition Techniques
Decomposition methods, such as Benders Decomposition and Dantzig­Wolfe Decom­
position, split complex scheduling problems into smaller and easier sub­problems. This
improves computational speed while still keeping the quality of the solution high.
Hybrid methods that combine MIP with decomposition can improve tractability in large
scheduling problems. El­Kholany (2022) show that such methods work well in industrial
contexts by splitting complex models into smaller parts, while keeping key constraints in­
tact. This is especially relevant for Novo Nordisk, where the scheduling problem includes
many constraints and a long planning horizon. Decomposition helps reduce solving time
without losing structure. In their study, they test benchmark instances with 750 to 2000
operations and achieve solutions within 1­10% of the optimal in under 1000 seconds.
These are job shop problems without time windows, making them easier than the mould­
ing problem considered here. Still, the results confirm that hybrid models can produce
high­quality schedules in realistic settings.
Further support for this comes from Hooker (2007), who argues that combining logic­
based optimisation with heuristics improves scalability. This motivates the hybrid strategy,

where exact methods are used when possible, and heuristics take over when the problem

exact methods with decomposition techniques when facing large, constrained scheduling
problems.
Ghosh Dastidar and Nagi (2005) demonstrate the effectiveness of decomposition in an in­
jection moulding context similar to this thesis. By dividing the problem by mould­machine
groups, they preserve key constraints while improving tractability. This is relevant for Novo
Nordisk, where solving the full model in one block is not feasible due to scale and complex­
ity. Their test problems include up to 51 part types, 43 machines and 30 time periods. In
one such case, a standard MIP solver ran out of memory, while their two­phase method
found a solution in 21 minutes with a gap of 1.51%. Across all large instances, gaps
remained between 0.8% and 1.75%. These results show that domain­specific decom­
position can reduce solving time considerably while maintaining solution quality, which
supports the strategy used in this thesis.
Another promising direction for handling complex scheduling problems is the rolling hori­
zon method. Li and Ierapetritou (2010) apply this approach to integrate production plan­
ning and scheduling under realistic capacity constraints. By solving a sequence of smaller
scheduling problems over a moving time window, they reduce model size and computa­
tion time. Their study shows that even using simple capacity limits leads to clear improve­
ments in cost and feasibility. This is relevant for Novo Nordisk, where production plans
must respond to changing demand and limited resources. The idea of planning in steps,
with adjustments based on capacity, fits well with a moulding environment that changes
frequently. This thesis uses a similar approach through sliding time windows. However,
unlike Li and Ierapetritou (2010), the focus here is not on products moving through mul­
tiple processing stages, but rather on a single­stage moulding problem scaled over time.
Still, their results support the idea that breaking the planning horizon into smaller parts
can improve both feasibility and computational performance.
3.3
Main Challenges in Applying Scheduling Methods
Despite strong theoretical performance, many scheduling methods face practical limita­
tions in industrial settings. Key challenges include scalability, complexity and implemen­
tation:
Scalability is a major issue, as exact methods like MIP often become intractable when
the number of machines, products and time periods increases (Pinedo 2005). This limits
their use in large­scale or time­sensitive environments.
Complexity arises from modelling detailed production constraints such as mould compat­
ibility, setup times and line configurations. These models can be difficult to adapt when
production rules change.
Implementation poses another hurdle. Optimisation models often require integration with
existing systems and preprocessing of data, which can slow adoption if the solution is not
user­friendly.
These challenges highlight the need for models that balance optimality with usability. Hy­
brid approaches and heuristics can provide more practical solutions.

4
Methodology And Theoretical
Framework
This chapter presents the mathematical framework and solution strategy used to address
the production scheduling problem. The approach is based on MIP, tailored to handle
complex constraints related to mould setup, demand satisfaction and resource allocation.
Given the size and structure of the dataset, additional methods were developed to ensure
tractability and scalability, including time decomposition and clustering.
4.1
Mixed­Integer Linear Programming as the Core Method
As presented in Chapter 3.2.1, MIP is a powerful tool for solving combinatorial optimisation
problems. At the core of the scheduling model is a MIP formulation, which is well­suited
for problems involving integer and binary decision variables, resource constraints and a
clear set of operational rules. The objective is to minimise unmet demand while respecting
physical constraints related to production lines, mould switching and production capac­
ity. The model is structured around the three components: Variables, Constraints and
Objectives.
1 Variables
The model includes both binary and continuous variables. The binary variables
control when, where and what is produced. More specifically, one set of binary vari­
ables manages which articles are produced by which moulds, and another handles
the allocation of moulds to lines. The continuous slack variables represent unmet
demand and stock levels.
2 Constraints
The constraints can be categorised according to the aspect they address. The main
challenges to be modelled fall under the following categories: allocation, switching,
demand, stock and preplanned activities.
◦Allocation: Each line can hold only one mould at a time, and each mould can
be allocated to only one line per period. However, this does not restrict a given
article to be produced at a single location, as multiple moulds may be capable
of producing the same article.
◦Switching: Changing moulds on a line incurs setup and teardown delays. Dur­
ing the periods where a mould is either being removed or installed, it cannot
be used elsewhere.
◦Balance: The model keeps track of how inventory develops over time. Each
week, stock is updated based on production, demand and any unmet demand.
If not enough is produced, the shortfall is recorded. Stock must stay within
allowed limits, which means the model must decide whether to produce early
and store or wait and produce later. This balance between timing, storage and
capacity makes sure the plan is both realistic and efficient across weeks. To
comply with warehouse capacity limitations, stock levels must be considered.
◦Preplanned activities: Novo Nordisk schedules certain activities in advance,
such as service operations on production lines, which temporarily take a line
out of use. In addition, specific production may be preassigned, for instance for
Optimising Production Scheduling for Moulding Operations
11
==PAGE:19==
external deliveries. The model also incorporates the initial setup of production
to ensure a seamless implementation.
◦Transition Between Windows: To ensure consistency between sub­problems
(or windows), a seamless transition from the previous window must be en­
forced. This guarantees that key state variables are correctly carried over and
aligned across time windows.
3 Objective
The objective is to minimise the total amount of unmet demand across all products
and time periods. Besides satisfying demand, Novo Nordisk also aims to run pro­
duction as continuously as possible, minimising idle time. To favour production over
switching activities, a penalty is imposed each time a production line or mould is
unavailable due to a mould change.
The greatest challenge in the MIP model is the number of variables. To address this, we
turn to the Branch and Bound method.
4.1.1
Branch and Bound
One of the most widely adopted algorithms for solving MIP problems is Branch and Bound
(B&B). This method is capable of identifying optimal or near­optimal solutions even for
large­scale industrial instances.
The algorithm starts by solving a relaxed version of the original MIP, where binary variables
are allowed to take any value within the continuous interval [0, 1]. This transforms the
model into a Linear Program (LP), which can be solved more efficiently using methods
such as the simplex algorithm. The result of this relaxation provides a lower bound for
minimisation problems.
The B&B procedure organises the solution process as a tree. Each node represents a
sub­problem that introduces new constraints to restrict binary variable values. At each
node, the LP relaxation is solved to obtain a bound. If the solution is not integer­feasible,
the model is split into two new sub­problems where a selected binary variable is fixed to
0 in one branch and 1 in the other. This process repeats recursively.
Throughout the tree search, certain sub­problems can be fathomed, meaning eliminated
from further exploration. This occurs in three situations:
1. The LP relaxation has an objective value worse than the current best feasible solu­
tion
2. The LP relaxation yields an integer­feasible solution that improves the incumbent
3. The LP relaxation is infeasible
The process continues until all branches are either solved or fathomed, or until a stopping
condition is met. In large­scale settings such as mould scheduling across multiple lines
and weeks, exploring the complete B&B tree is often impractical. Instead, a solution within
a specified optimality gap is accepted. The optimality gap quantifies the relative distance
between the best feasible solution identified and the best bound known in the B&B tree:
Gap = Zfeasible −Zbound
Zfeasible
By setting a maximum acceptable gap, a balance is achieved between solution accu­
racy and computation time. This is particularly relevant in real­world production contexts,
where a slightly suboptimal plan can be acceptable if it is generated significantly faster.

In the Novo Nordisk moulding scenario, where multiple weekly windows and resource
constraints result in a highly combinatorial space, applying a 5% gap allows high­quality
solutions to be found in a practical timeframe. This helps maintain control over overpro­
duction, inventory limits and changeover operations without requiring full optimisation.
To strengthen the linear relaxation used within Branch and Bound, modern solvers au­
tomatically generate cutting planes. These are additional linear inequalities that remove
fractional solutions from the relaxed LP, without excluding any feasible integer solutions.
Cutting planes help reduce the size of the B&B tree by tightening the feasible region early
in the search process. For instance, if a relaxed solution suggests producing 2.6 units
with a binary setup, a cut can be introduced to prevent such a fractional outcome unless
it leads to a valid integer configuration.
In the context of mould scheduling, cutting planes help eliminate infeasible partial so­
lutions where, for example, production might be unrealistically split across incompatible
configurations. Combined with B&B, these cuts allow the solver to focus on promising
areas of the solution space more efficiently.
4.1.2
Solver
The MIP model is solved using the commercial solver Gurobi, which is known for its high
performance on large­scale optimisation problems. Gurobi includes a wide range of built­
in techniques that accelerate the solving process and improve robustness. These include
automatic presolve routines that simplify the model before the main search begins by
removing redundancies, fixing obvious variable values and tightening bounds. Simplifica­
tion can significantly reduce memory usage and speed up solving, though in rare cases
excessive reduction may remove helpful structure. Gurobi also detects sparsity in the
model to reduce computation, which is particularly relevant in scheduling problems where
many mould­line combinations are inactive.
To increase the chance of finding good solutions early, Gurobi applies various heuristics
and feasibility­focused strategies to improve the policy when choosing the next node of the
solution tree. These also avoid needing to explore the full B&B tree. For time­critical appli­
cations, it is possible to prioritise feasible solutions by adjusting the parameter MIPFocus
to value practical plans over guaranteed optimality. Similarly, the parameter Heuristics
controls the level of heuristic effort applied throughout the search. When exact optimality
is less important than speed, these settings help construct acceptable schedules much
faster.
The solver supports multi­threaded computation, which allows multiple parts of the search
tree to be explored simultaneously. This is especially effective when solving multiple time
windows or clustered sub­problems in parallel. Gurobi can also run different solution
strategies concurrently and select the one that performs best on the instance at hand.
This increases solver robustness and helps avoid slow convergence caused by poor al­
gorithmic choices. Finally, the user can set a maximum optimality gap, to trade off solu­
tion accuracy for reduced run time. This is a practical approach in settings like production
planning, where a near­optimal schedule is often sufficient.

Discretisation of time units
To model production scheduling in the moulding process, time is discretised into fixed­
length intervals. Rather than working with continuous time or small granularities such as
minutes, we define a discrete unit of time as a fixed­length block within which decisions
are assumed constant.

In the implementation for this study, one time unit corresponds to a duration of 3 hours
(this will be discussed later in Section 7.3.2).
This design choice serves two main purposes. First, it significantly reduces the compu­
tational complexity of the optimisation problem. For example, optimising over a full week
with 1­minute resolution would require 7 × 24 × 60 = 10,080 time steps per machine. In
contrast, with 3­hour intervals, we only need 7 × 8 = 56 time steps. This leads to a factor
of 180 reduction in time­related dimensions, which also affects the number of variables
and constraints in the MIP model.
Second, this level of granularity aligns well with the actual setup and takedown dynam­
ics in the moulding context at Novo Nordisk. Mould changes typically take several hours
due to the physical and technical nature of the machinery. As a result, modelling deci­
sion changes on a minute­by­minute basis is not only computationally expensive but also
practically unnecessary. It is unlikely that moving a mould for a gain of just a few minutes
would be feasible or meaningful in a real production environment.
Thus, working with 3­hour blocks strikes a balance between model realism and computa­
tional tractability. It provides sufficient flexibility to model relevant scheduling dynamics,
while ensuring that the problem can be solved within reasonable time limits.
4.3
Clustering Strategy
To manage model complexity and enable scalable optimisation, the full production schedul­
ing problem is decomposed into smaller and more tractable sub­problems using a clustering­
based approach. Clustering is applied to production lines to create independent groups
that can be optimised separately. The strategy unfolds in three stages.
4.3.1
Stage 1: Mould Compatibility
In the first stage, lines are grouped based on technical compatibility. A graph is con­
structed where each node represents a line and edges connect lines that can use at least
one common mould. The connected components of this graph define initial clusters, en­
suring that all mould­sharing relationships are respected within each group.
4.3.2
Stage 2: Article Overlap
The second stage accounts for production coordination. Clusters from Stage 1 are merged
if they share articles within the same planning window. This allows the model to capture
operational dependencies, such as cross­line coordination for articles that are produced
on multiple lines.
4.3.3
Stage 3: Temporal Expansion and Decomposition
Clustering is not static but repeated independently in each planning window. Since de­
mand fluctuates over time, the set of active articles varies. If an article has no demand in
a given window, it is excluded from the model. This can break links between previously
connected lines and result in smaller or new clusters.

4.4
Solving Large Clusters
Some clusters in the production scheduling problem are particularly challenging due to
their combinatorial complexity and high number of feasible assignments. These clusters
often involve many articles, many moulds and a high degree of overlap between lines. Di­
rectly solving such clusters as a whole is often computationally intractable. The following
describes the scoring metric and the stepwise batch­wise solution approach in detail.
4.4.1
Prioritising Lines and Batch­wise Solving in Difficult Clusters
To address the problem of the complex clusters, a strategy based on prioritisation and
consecutive batch­wise solving is used. The core idea is to first score all lines in the
cluster according to their difficulty using a custom metric (”hardscore”), and then solve the
problem incrementally, starting from the easiest lines.
The hardscore metric for a line l is defined as follows:
hardscore(l) = f1 · |Al| + f2 · |Ml|
where:
• |Al| is the number of articles that can be produced on line l,
• |Ml| is the number of moulds that can be assigned to line l,
• f1 and f2 are weighting parameters.
16
Optimising Production Scheduling for Moulding Operations
==PAGE:24==
Lines with a low hardscore (e.g. a line with only one article, one mould and little overlap)
are expected to be simple to solve, while lines with a high score are more complex.
The solution procedure is as follows:
1. Prioritisation: All lines are ranked by their hardscore.
2. Batch construction: Lines are divided into small batches (typically of size two),
starting with the lowest scoring (easiest) lines.
3. Consecutive solving: The first batch (for example, lines 2 and 5) is solved inde­
pendently, focusing on minimising unmet demand. Since these lines are easy, an
optimal solution is usually found quickly.
4. Fixing decisions: Any production or assignment variables (X, Y ) that are positive
in the solution are locked for subsequent solves, so that these decisions remain
unchanged.
5. Expanding the batch: The next batch adds the next easiest lines (for example,
lines 3 and 7), and all previously solved lines are included as well (now solving lines
2, 3, 5 and 7 together). This is important, since some unmet demand from the new
lines might be covered using capacity from the earlier lines.
6. Iterating: This process is repeated until all lines in the cluster have been covered
with overlapping batches. In other words, the solution space is gradually filled using
consecutive batches, always starting from the easiest sub­problems.
7. Storing solutions: At each stage, key results (including unmet demand and vari­
able values) are saved. The full solution after all batches are solved and locked gives
the best attempt at minimising unmet demand under the batch­wise approach.
8. Final production fill: Finally, one more solve is run with all batch­wise decisions
locked, but now maximising total production. This step ”fills in the blanks” and ex­
ploits any remaining free variables to increase production if possible.
Suppose a cluster contains lines 2, 3, 5 and 7. The metric shows that lines 2 and 5 are
the easiest, so these are solved first as a batch. The positive production and assignment
variables for lines 2 and 5 are then locked. In the next batch, lines 3 and 7 are included,
together with the already­fixed lines 2 and 5. The model now attempts to meet the demand
for lines 3 and 7, while respecting the previously fixed decisions for lines 2 and 5. This
process continues batch by batch, always including all previously solved lines in each new
batch, ensuring that the easier sub­problems are solved first, and that their decisions are
respected throughout the entire process.
This method exploits the fact that consecutive, batch­wise solving can simplify otherwise
intractable problems, and that solutions to easy sub­problems can guide and constrain
the solution of the full cluster.

6
Optimisation Model
This chapter presents the MIP model used for production scheduling. It begins with key
design choices that balance realism, efficiency and practical use. The core formulation is
then introduced, including sets, parameters, variables, objective and constraints. Finally,
the chapter outlines objective function extensions that promote smoother production and
fewer changeovers.
6.1
Design Choices
The optimisation model is built on trade­offs between realism, computational efficiency
and practical use. It uses a discrete time approach with 3­hour intervals to keep the
problem size manageable and ensure reasonable solve times, although this reduces time
precision.
To handle large­scale problems, the model splits the system into clusters of lines that
share moulds and articles. Each cluster is solved separately, and for large clusters, a
batch­wise method is used where lines are solved one at a time.
A weekly sliding time window allows for long­term planning without solving the entire hori­
zon at once. Previous decisions are fixed to ensure consistency between weeks. Some
simplifications are made. For example, labour limits, maintenance and total mould change
limits are not directly modelled, but the structure allows these to be included. Instead of
hard constraints on mould changes, the objective function can include penalties that re­
duce excessive switching. This is the trade­off by solving the problem using clusters.
Preplanned setups and constraints can be handled through initial conditions or fixed as­
signments, making it easier to align with upstream planning. Overall, the model is de­
signed to be flexible, scalable and suitable for industrial use.
Since the site also supplies external locations, late production is not permitted. Meaning,
if demand is not fulfilled within a given week, the initial stock for the next week is set to
zero. This approach ensures that shortages are not carried forward and that demand is
met in the correct time period.
6.2
Base Model Formulation
This section presents the base MIP model designed to schedule production across mul­
tiple lines using a limited number of moulds. The purpose of the model is to determine
a feasible production plan that minimises the amount of unmet demand for various arti­
cles, subject to a series of constraints reflecting real­world operational restrictions. These
include limitations on mould­line compatibility, mould changeover times and capacity con­
straints per period.

Transition Between Windows
The model as described so far can optimise production for any given planning period,
provided a fixed initial configuration of moulds and articles. However, in order to handle
extended planning horizons or computationally large instances, the horizon is split into
several consecutive windows (sub­problems), each solved sequentially. To ensure con­
sistency and feasibility across these windows, a transition mechanism is introduced via
the parameter P.
The parameter P stores, for each combination of article, mould and line, an integer value
that encodes the status at the end of the previous window. When the next window is
initialised, P is used to impose the appropriate constraints and variable fixings, so that
the physical and operational state of the system is correctly carried forward. In particular,
all decisions made in the previous window are respected for the periods covered by that
window. However, any transitions (such as an ongoing mould change) that extend across
the window boundary are not fixed in advance for the overlapping week. Instead, the
model is given the freedom to choose how to proceed from this point. This means that
if, for example, a mould change is initiated towards the end of one window but not yet
completed at the boundary, the model may either continue with the changeover as planned
or select an alternative mould for the new window.
This flexibility enables the model to adapt the production schedule dynamically at each
window boundary, potentially avoiding sub­optimal decisions that could arise from rigidly
enforcing all prior transitions. For instance, the model may decide to abort a previously
planned changeover and instead continue production with the mould that was last in­
stalled. In such cases, a minimal amount of unnecessary idle time may result, since some
machine time will be lost due to the partially completed transition. Nonetheless, this ap­
proach ensures that decisions are always optimised with respect to the latest information
and constraints, without sacrificing feasibility.
The model distinguishes between three main cases for each (a, m, l) combination, based
on the value recorded in P:
• Case 1: value = 0
This indicates that the mould m was already installed on line l at the end of the
previous window, and was producing article a. In this situation, the model fixes
32

==PAGE:40==
the corresponding binary variables to ensure that mould m remains on line l at the
beginning of the new window, and that article a is produced with this mould. This
enables production to continue seamlessly across the window boundary.
• Case 2: 0 < value < Downm
Here, value represents the number of periods since mould m was last active on
line l, but the full takedown time Downm has not yet elapsed. This means that the
line is still occupied by the transition (removal) of mould m. In this case, the model
prohibits the installation of any other moulds on line l for the remaining takedown
time, by fixing the corresponding placement variables to zero during this interval.
This ensures that transition periods are properly respected, preventing premature
use of the line.
• Case 3: value ≥Downm
In this case, the required takedown time for mould m has already been completed,
but a potential transition to a new mould may be ongoing (depending on up­times for
other moulds). For each other mould m′ compatible with line l, the model forbids its
installation for the necessary remaining start­up time, again by fixing the placement
variables to zero for the appropriate number of periods. This ensures that any over­
lapping transition times are respected, and no infeasible overlaps occur between
mould changeovers.
Through this structured use of the parameter P, the model enforces all relevant transition
constraints at the start of each window. Crucially, while the actual production and mould
allocation decisions from previous windows are respected, the model is not strictly bound
to follow through with any transitions that cross the window boundary. This allows the
optimiser to either complete, modify, or even abort an in­progress changeover if it is ad­
vantageous to do so. As a consequence, there may be a small amount of unnecessary
idle time if a planned changeover is not completed, but this flexibility enables the model
to always select the best possible schedule based on the most up­to­date information.

address this limitation. One extension enforces overall production, while another aims to
minimise the number of production starts.

7
Experiments
This chapter presents the computational study conducted to evaluate the proposed MIP
model. First, the run time is examined about the optimality gap, followed by an explana­
tion of the selected gap used in the tests. Afterwards, combinations of different objective
functions are investigated. To demonstrate scalability, two complementary instance fam­
ilies are introduced. The Direct Solve Clusters represent scenarios that can be solved in
a single optimisation run and allow complete transparency in decision­making. The Iter­
ative Solve Clusters reflect larger scenarios that require the iterative solution procedure
described in Section 4.4.1.
All tests are conducted over a seven­week planning horizon, corresponding to two plan­
ning windows. This duration is long enough to capture dynamic effects, such as inventory
accumulation and resource reallocation, while remaining short enough to allow for trans­
parent interpretation. Extending the horizon was considered unnecessary, as it would
contribute little additional insight into the model’s performance.
Throughout all experiments, three key performance indicators are tracked: the number
of mould switches, the average line utilisation percentage and the volume of unmet de­
mand. Together, these metrics provide a balanced perspective on operational efficiency
and service level under realistic industrial constraints.
The experiments are based on an artificial dataset provided by Novo Nordisk. The limited
size of the dataset makes this a focused case study. The selected clustering method re­
sulted in structurally similar instances, and therefore only one representative cluster from
each family is analysed. A final limitation concerns the comparison with Novo Nordisk’s
current solution. Real production data are stored in a different format, which prevents di­
rect benchmarking. Moreover, due to confidentiality it has not been possible to examine
the computational approach currently used in their planning system. This limitation and
the adjustments made are discussed in Section 8.5. However, some key performance
indicators for the existing solution were provided.
7.1
Run time
By applying an optimality gap and a time limit, the model is not required to find the exact
optimal solution. It either stops as soon as a solution within the specified gap is found, or
when the time limit is reached ­ whichever occurs first. This setup means that even if the
optimal solution could be reached within the time limit, the solver may still terminate early
if a solution below the optimality gap has already been identified.
To illustrate how the gap evolves over time when a time limit is applied, Figure 7.1 has
been constructed.

As shown in the figure, the optimality gap drops quickly during the first part of the solving

not yet the best. All three windows reach a gap below 10% in about 100 seconds, and
importantly, they all come close to or go below 5% shortly after. This suggests that useful
and almost optimal solutions can be found quickly, without needing long solve times.
The three curves also show slightly different behaviours. For example, Window 2 has
a faster drop in the beginning, while Window 1 ends with the lowest gap overall. This
difference may be due to how each window size affects the solver’s way of searching the
solution space. It might also come from small delays or randomness in how the solver
works. With short time limits, even small changes in system performance or solver rules
can affect the result.
Although all three windows reach relatively low gaps in the end, most progress happens
early in the process. After around 300 seconds, improvements become small. In prac­
tice, this suggests that extending the solve time may give limited benefit. A well­known
metaphor, sometimes called ”stopping before diamonds”, shows this dilemma: a miner
gives up just before reaching a diamond vein. The same applies to solving with time limits
and optimality gaps, as there are no guarantees for when the best solution will be found.
It depends fully on the structure of the branch­and­bound tree and the path the solver
follows.
The figure also includes data points up to 10,800 seconds (3 hours), yet not all windows
have reached a 0% gap by then. This further shows the uncertainty of when a solution will
be found, even with long computation time. However, since all three windows consistently
reach or closely approach the 5% level early in the process, this becomes a natural and
practical trade­off between solution quality and computing time.
Therefore, based on the patterns seen in Figure 7.1, a 5% optimality gap is chosen for the
remaining experiments. It balances performance with solution quality and marks a point
where further improvements require much more time with little gain.

7.3.1
Hardscores
As mentioned in Section 4.4.1, a hardscore is used to determine which lines are the most
logical to solve first. Recall that the function is defined as:
hardscore(l) = f1 · |Al| + f2 · |Ml|.
The purpose is to reduce the number of unnecessary variables. The underlying idea is
that if a line is only compatible with a specific mould, which in turn only produces a single
article, then this line is a natural candidate for early allocation. One might ask why this
is not simply treated as a separate cluster. However, the reason is that the mould could
also be compatible with other lines, or the article might be producible on other machines.
If this highly restricted line is solved late, the mould might already be allocated elsewhere,
leaving the line idle.
Based on this reasoning, the number of available moulds is given a higher weight when
determining line priority. However, if two lines have the same number of moulds, the one
with fewer articles should be considered more restrictive and therefore have a lower hard­
score. This implies that f1 < f2. More specifically, since moulds are harder to schedule
due to their long switching times, they are prioritised in the scoring. This leads to the
parameter choice f1 = 1 and f2 = |Al|. An example of this metric can be found in A.4.

This chapter reflects on the development and results of the proposed scheduling model.
The focus is on summarising key learnings, highlighting strengths and acknowledging

The discussion begins with a recap of the main findings and how the model performed
across different test scenarios. It then considers the practical strengths of the modelling

Finally, the model’s applicability in a real planning environment is discussed, including how

Throughout the chapter, the focus remains on reflecting on the project’s outcomes in light

8.1
Key Findings
This thesis develops a mathematical optimisation model for scheduling in Novo Nordisk’s
moulding operations, with the goal of minimising unmet demand under realistic produc­
tion constraints. The model is formulated as a Mixed­Integer Linear Program (MIP) that
captures key operational characteristics, including mould­line compatibility, setup times,
colour transitions and inventory limits.
A central contribution of this work is the design of a scalable solution strategy that enables
the model to handle large, industrial­scale scheduling problems. Specifically, the thesis
introduces a clustering­based decomposition method tailored to the moulding context.
Production lines are grouped into clusters based on mould and article overlap and each
cluster is solved independently. For particularly large and complex clusters, a batch­wise
solving method was implemented. Lines within each cluster are scored based on difficulty
and then solved cumulatively in batches with earlier decisions fixed to reduce the search
space in later iterations.
The main findings of the project are:
• The MIP model successfully generates feasible production schedules that reduce
unmet demand significantly compared to existing methods. The model respects crit­
ical constraints such as mould availability, changeover times and stock thresholds.
• The proposed clustering approach enables decomposition of the overall problem
into smaller, manageable sub­problems. Across the full planning horizon, this re­
sulted in between 6 and 12 clusters per window, depending on article activity.
• For clusters that remained too large to solve directly, the batch­wise method allowed
for the gradual construction of solutions. This approach preserved solution quality
while keeping computational time within practical limits.
• The implementation of a sliding time window across planning weeks maintained con­
tinuity in mould allocation and ensured consistent planning decisions across over­
lapping periods.

8.2

The proposed MIP model was evaluated using both small and large cluster instances de­

efficiency, solver performance and scalability. Two overall solution strategies were tested:
direct solving of clusters in a single run and a batch­wise method for larger cases.
For smaller clusters, such as Cluster 4, the model performed well under direct solving.
The baseline model focusing solely on unmet demand resulted in 14.56% unmet demand.
When sequencing incentives were added (Test 2 and Test 3), unmet demand dropped to
around 7% and utilisation increased from 70.41% to over 93%. These improvements
came with fewer mould changes, reduced from 13 to as low as 3, while maintaining real­
istic schedules. Adding more complex sequencing logic (Test 5 and Test 6) gave further
small gains or enabled trade­offs with changeover frequency.
All tests were solved within acceptable time frames. Even with the extended objectives,
most direct solve problems finished in under 30 minutes and returned high­quality solu­

limited article overlap.
Compared to Novo Nordisk’s current semi­automated plan, which had 62.72% unmet
demand in the same test period, the model­based approach shows clear improvement.
Test 3 and Test 6a achieved just 7.17% unmet demand while maintaining high utilisation
and a manageable number of changeovers.
The model configuration with the fewest changeovers was Test 6b where ϕ = 100. How­
ever, this setup also required more solve time compared to the other objectives. Based
on Novo Nordisk’s current solution, it seems that the number of changeovers is given
high importance, possibly more than initially expected. Their plan allows a much higher
level of unmet demand compared to all tested solutions, which may suggest that reducing
changeovers is prioritised over fulfilling demand.
For larger instances like Cluster 1, direct solving was no longer feasible. The model
required excessive solve time or failed to return a solution. To handle this, a batch­wise
approach was applied where lines were grouped and solved iteratively. This strategy
proved successful, as the batch­wise method reduced unmet demand to just 2.07% using
3­hour intervals and a 600­second limit per batch. Mean utilisation reached 89.00% with
16 mould changes and 29 colour changes across the 23 lines.

2­hour discretisation and the same solve time, performance dropped significantly. Unmet
demand increased to 5.67% and utilisation fell to 71.01%, as seen in the same table. Only

8.3
Strengths of the Approach
The proposed MIP­based scheduling model has several strengths that make it well­suited
for a complex and highly constrained setting like Novo Nordisk’s moulding operations.
One of the main advantages is the model’s ability to capture real operational constraints in
a clear and structured way. It includes practical rules such as which moulds can be used
on which lines, time for colour and mould changes, demand patterns and stock limits.
These are modelled directly, so planners can trust that the results are realistic and usable
in practice.
Another strength is the flexible and modular design. Key settings like time resolution,
planning horizon and penalty weights can be adjusted easily. This makes it possible to
reuse or adapt the model for other production areas or future changes without starting
from scratch.
A key design choice in the model was to use three­hour time blocks. While this is less
detailed than shorter intervals, it keeps the number of variables low and makes the model
solvable within reasonable time. For comparison, using minute­level resolution would
increase the problem size by a factor of up to 180. With this setup, realistic instances can
be solved using standard hardware and within acceptable time limits.
To maintain consistency across weeks while keeping the problem size manageable, the
model uses a sliding window approach. This method plans in overlapping time windows
and fixes earlier decisions as it progresses. It helps avoid sudden shifts in mould usage
from week to week and results in smoother and more realistic production plans.
Clustering makes the problem easier to solve. Lines that share moulds or articles are
grouped together and solved as smaller sub­problems. For the largest clusters, a batch­
wise approach is used where a few lines are solved at a time. This reduces complexity
and helps find good solutions faster. A hardscore function was introduced to help prioritise
and distribute the lines across the batches. One trade­off is that once a batch is solved,
its decisions are fixed. While this limits global optimality, it allows the model to handle
large­scale problems that would otherwise be unsolvable.
A key benefit of the model is that it gives planners control over trade­offs. Through the
objective function, they can decide how much to prioritise demand fulfilment versus re­
ducing changeovers. This is a clear improvement over rule­based systems, where such
trade­offs are often hidden or difficult to adjust.
Technically, the model is implemented in Julia using the JuMP framework, which makes
the code both efficient and easy to maintain. The structure also allows for straightforward

59
==PAGE:67==
translation to Python without significant impact on computational performance. The inte­
gration with Gurobi gives access to a powerful solver, and the codebase is structured in
a way that makes it simple to extend with new features or constraints if needed.
Finally, while the model’s output is well­structured and aligned with planning needs, it
still requires some post­processing before it can be used directly in weekly production
planning. However, the design allows the input and output to be connected to existing data
systems, making it easier to implement the model into future workflows. This increases the
potential for real­world use and supports collaboration between planners and optimisation
experts.
In summary, the model is not only mathematically solid but also flexible, practical and
ready to support decision­making in a real industrial setting.
8.4
Limitations and Assumptions
Despite its strengths, the proposed scheduling model is based on a number of simplify­
ing assumptions and has limitations that should be acknowledged when interpreting the
results and considering real­world implementation.
One important limitation is that the model does not coordinate mould or colour changes
across clusters. Since each cluster is solved independently, it is not possible to enforce
a global limit on the total number of changeovers per week. In practice, such limits are
essential to ensure that the total operational workload remains manageable and aligned
with staff and resource availability. This simplification was necessary to make the problem
tractable. Rather than modelling global changeover limits explicitly, the focus in this thesis
has been on exploring objective function designs that encourage stability and minimise
unnecessary transitions.
Another limitation is the absence of support for preplanned or mandatory activities. A
key requirement from Novo Nordisk was the ability to lock certain machines for planned
tasks such as preventive maintenance or forced production. Although the model struc­
ture allows for this type of constraint, it has not been tested in this thesis. The primary
focus was instead placed on validating that the model behaves correctly under different
configurations and on assessing the effect of alternative objective functions.
The model also assumes that all moulds and machines are fully available during the plan­
ning horizon. Unexpected downtime, maintenance overruns or equipment failures are
not considered. Labour constraints are also not included, meaning that the model may
schedule simultaneous mould or colour changes without accounting for human resource
limits.
All planning is based on a weekly cycle, discretised into fixed­length time intervals. A
three­hour resolution was chosen to balance model fidelity with computational feasibility.
This granularity was found to reduce the number of variables by roughly a factor of 180
compared to a one­minute resolution, which made it possible to solve realistic problems
within acceptable runtimes. However, this discretisation also restricts temporal flexibility,
particularly for short­run articles or tightly sequenced production.
Furthermore, the model assumes stable demand and accurate stock levels. While reason­
able for tactical planning, this does not reflect sudden changes in demand or last­minute
adjustments. The model does not include dynamic re­optimisation or stochastic elements
to address such uncertainty.
Another simplification is the lack of article prioritisation. All demand is treated equally,

==PAGE:68==
regardless of whether an article is considered critical, urgent or optional. In practice,
certain items may have higher business or regulatory importance, and future versions of
the model could include differentiated penalties to reflect this.
Due to limited time in the project, certain areas were not explored in depth. In particular,
no extensive parameter tuning was performed for weights such as δ, ϕ and θ in the objec­
tive function. Their values were selected based on simple reasoning and what intuitively
made sense, rather than systematic testing ­ like done for η. Likewise, a more systematic
analysis of modelling choices like time discretisation, batch size and window length was
not conducted. These aspects could have a significant influence on both performance
and solution quality and are relevant topics for future work.
Lastly, the batch­wise strategy makes it possible to solve large clusters, but it also reduces
flexibility. Early decisions are fixed in later batches, which can limit the solution space.
This trade­off is necessary to keep the problem solvable, especially when clusters are too
large to solve directly.
These limitations reflect modelling choices and trade­offs made to develop a solution for a
complex and large­scale scheduling problem. They also point to clear directions for future
improvements as the model moves closer to operational use.
8.5
Real­World Applicability and Comparison to Existing
Approaches
The scheduling model developed in this thesis was designed with real­world use in mind.
Its structure, level of detail and modular setup make it suitable for integration into actual
planning processes such as the moulding operations at Novo Nordisk. Several aspects
support its potential for use in an industrial setting.
First, the model captures essential production constraints that are already known and
applied in daily operations. This includes valid mould­line combinations, setup times for
mould and colour changes and limits on inventory. Since these constraints reflect real
production logic, the model can generate plans that are both understandable and usable
for planners as long as the output is post­processed in the right way.
Compared to Novo Nordisk’s in­house Python­based planning model from 2019, the ap­
proach in this thesis brings several improvements. The previous method relied on manual
heuristics with limited ability to handle constraints or test different scenarios. In contrast,
the MIP­based model provides a structured and fully automated way to create feasible
plans that follow operational rules and reduce issues such as unnecessary mould switches
or unmet demand. It also gives a clearer setup for adding new constraints or preferences
when needed.
The optimisation model produces consistent results even when input data changes. This
makes it more reliable in difficult planning situations. It also gives planners the option to
adjust trade­offs, for example between demand fulfilment and operational workload, by
changing weights in the objective function. This flexibility was not available in the earlier
Python­based approach.
When compared to the broader literature on injection moulding and parallel machine
scheduling, the presented approach performs well by addressing several limitations found
in academic models. Many studies assume simplified setups, such as fully flexible ma­
chines or minimal setup times, which do not match the constraints in specialised produc­
tion environments. In contrast, this thesis includes fixed mould­line combinations, colour

compatibility and inventory tracking to create realistic and usable schedules. Heuristic and
metaheuristic methods like genetic algorithms or tabu search are often used in research,
but these approaches struggle to guarantee feasible solutions and often have trouble han­
dling time­based aspects like inventory across several weeks or coordinated setup plan­
ning. The use of a MIP formulation makes it possible to model these constraints directly
and gives a structured way to balance demand, setup effort and utilisation. Combined
with clustering and batch­wise solving, the method handles large and complex problem
instances efficiently without reducing model detail. This makes the model a practical con­

of high­volume and constraint­heavy production settings.
The solving strategy is modular and can be adapted to different planning needs. Cluster­

9.1
Concluding Summary
This thesis addresses a practical and industrially relevant problem: how to generate fea­
sible and efficient production schedules in Novo Nordisk’s moulding operations, where a
wide range of articles, moulds and production lines must be coordinated under multiple
constraints. The main goal was to develop a scalable and implementable optimisation
model that can minimise unmet demand while respecting real­world limitations such as
mould compatibility, colour transitions, setup times and inventory levels.
The chosen method is based on Mixed­Integer Linear Programming (MIP), supported by
domain­specific logic to ensure that the model is both accurate and solvable in practice.
One of the central challenges was the size and complexity of the problem. Solving the
full schedule across all lines and articles in one run was not feasible, which led to the
development of a decomposition approach. The thesis therefore contributes not just with
a mathematical model but also with a practical solution strategy adapted to the moulding
setup.
The solution consists of three layers: first, a discrete MIP model that captures the main
constraints and decision rules. Second, a clustering strategy that groups production lines
with shared moulds or articles. And third, a batch­wise method that solves large clusters
step by step. Together, these elements form a strong framework that balances model
accuracy with practical solvability.
The model was implemented in Julia using JuMP and Gurobi and tested on real production
data from Novo Nordisk. Results show that demand fulfilment increased from below 40%
in baseline scenarios to over 90% in optimised cases depending on the configuration. The
most promising objective was found in Test 6, when ϕ was assigned a high value, however
other tests, like Test 5, also showed great potential. Setup activities and mould usage re­
mained within realistic levels without overloading the system. Unlike previous approaches
the frequency of mould and colour changes is not enforced through strict constraints but
is instead guided by the objective function which penalises excessive switching. This al­
lows the model to remain flexible while still promoting stable and efficient schedules. The
use of sliding time windows three­hour intervals and clustering ensures computational
efficiency with solve times under one hour even for large instances. The model outputs
schedules in a planner­friendly format and integrates smoothly with existing data sources
and workflows making it suitable for practical use.
The model was implemented in Julia using JuMP and Gurobi, which allowed a clear struc­
ture separating model logic, data handling and post­processing. This modular design
improves transparency and maintainability and makes it easier to connect with existing
planning workflows. The model reads data from Excel templates, generates detailed pro­
duction schedules and outputs results in a format that planners can work with. The output

includes production quantities, mould assignments, inventory levels and unmet demand
per article.
Despite the positive results, the model also has limitations. It does not yet support global
coordination of changeovers across clusters, and it does not include labour constraints or
unplanned events. These were necessary simplifications to keep the model solvable. Still,
the structure is flexible enough to allow such features to be added later. The model also
assumes stable demand and does not support re­optimisation, which would be relevant in
more dynamic planning settings but was outside the scope of this tactical planning focus.
In summary, this thesis shows how mathematical optimisation can be applied in a real
industrial context. By combining exact methods with problem­specific decomposition and
a modular structure, the model delivers solutions that are both feasible and effective.
The results support the use of MIP­based approaches in environments that are often
considered too constrained or complex for classical optimisation techniques.
9.2
Sub­conclusion: Research Question
The central research question in this thesis was:
How can optimisation techniques from operations research be applied to im­
prove the efficiency and feasibility of moulding production scheduling at Novo
Nordisk?
This question has been addressed through the design, implementation and evaluation of
a tailored MIP­based scheduling model supported by decomposition and batching strate­
gies. The model demonstrates that OR techniques can be effectively applied in a real­
world industrial setting even under complex operational constraints.
The research addressed three key objectives:
• Explore MIP and heuristics to develop a scalable scheduling model
This goal was met by formulating the scheduling problem as a Mixed­Integer Pro­
gram and developing a clustering approach to break it into smaller parts. For larger
clusters, a batch­wise method was used to solve the problem step by step. This
made it possible to handle full planning periods without long solve times. Even
though clustering reduces the chance of finding the best possible solution, it was a
necessary trade­off and still gave strong results in practice.
• Investigate the ability of the model to satisfy operational constraints including
line availability, mould compatibility and production requirements
The model includes key constraints such as which moulds fit on which lines, time for
changeovers, stock limits and starting inventory. Some things, like forced produc­
tion, maintenance and labour limits, are not yet included. However, the objective
function helps guide the model to stable and realistic schedules by penalising un­
necessary changes, even without hard rules for every constraint.
• Develop a solution approach that is computationally feasible, maximising de­
mand fulfilment and minimising disruptions, while being effectively applied in
Novo Nordisk’s DCM department
The model was built in Julia using JuMP and Gurobi, with a modular setup that
makes it easy to adjust time resolution and planning settings. With three­hour time
blocks and a sliding planning window, the model created weekly schedules where
unmet demand dropped from over 60% to under 10%, and in some cases even be­
low 3%. Changeovers stayed at a practical level, and all runs finished in acceptable

in a real production setting.
In conclusion this thesis shows that optimisation techniques from operations research can
significantly improve moulding production scheduling at Novo Nordisk. By combining ex­
act optimisation with problem­specific decomposition and a carefully constructed objective
function the model delivers efficient scalable and interpretable solutions that are suitable
for real­world use even though not all operational constraints are currently implemented.
The model structure and performance suggest strong potential for further extension and
integration into existing planning workflows.

9.3
Suggestions for Future Work
While the model developed in this thesis provides a strong foundation for production
scheduling in a complex moulding environment, several extensions could enhance its
applicability and performance.
A key next step is to support preplanned activities. As noted earlier, Novo Nordisk requires
the ability to lock machines for maintenance or forced production. The model structure
allows this, and future work should implement and evaluate such constraints in both direct
and batch­wise modes.
Improving capacity utilisation is also important. As shown in Figure 7.13, idle periods re­
main. Post­processing methods, short­run insertions or extended solving horizons could
help reduce these gaps. Adaptive solver settings may also enable tighter sequencing
within the model.
Currently, all articles are treated equally. Introducing differentiated cost weights or pri­
orities would allow the model to reflect business­critical or urgent demand, increasing
schedule relevance under constrained capacity.
Labour constraints during changeovers are another relevant addition. The current model
assumes unlimited resources, but real transitions require operator effort. Limiting the
number of changes per time unit would improve realism.
The batch­wise method could be refined by exploring dynamic line ordering or hybrid
strategies with partial re­optimisation. This may improve solution quality without signifi­
cantly increasing runtime.
Parameters such as ϕ, θ and η could be explored further, along with settings for time
discretisation and batch sizes. More importantly, it would be valuable to investigate the
choice of window sizes in the sliding time window approach. This includes experimenting
with the total length of each window as well as the portion that is actively solved. In
addition, studying different ways of handling transitions between windows could improve
consistency and overall solution quality.
Finally, introducing stochastic elements such as uncertain demand or machine availability
could improve robustness and reduce manual replanning.
Overall, the model offers a solid base with clear directions for future development, sup­
porting the transition from academic optimisation to practical industrial use.
