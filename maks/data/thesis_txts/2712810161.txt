Abstract
The optimal design of stepped scarf repair has been a complex problem due its dependency on
various geometric values and material mechanical properties, giving huge variation of effects in design
loading capacity and strength in the repair. Therefore, an automated method named Multi-Parameter
Stepped Scarf (MPSS), has been introduced for designing stepped-scarf repair for composite laminate
structure. The repair scheme has been presented to accommodate different parameters such as layup
stacking and geometrical inputs including ply thickness, resin thickness, over-lamina, overlapping
length, ply-by-ply material match and mismatch, according to the user-defined inputs and loading
scenario of tension and bending. The MPSS is able to generate 2D meshed model written in
an ABAQUS input file for both stepped-scarf repair and pristine laminate, enabling to perform
comparison of global elastic modulus and flexural rigidity before and after repair for preliminary
design investigation of the repair. The automated method will ease computational modelling during
more complex analysis using damage mechanics and parametric studies. Extensions for future work
are also discussed in relation to strength restoration of the stepped scarf repair.
Keywords: wind turbine blade; finite element model; stepped scarf repair; bending; tensile; stiffness;
flexural rigidity

According to the Global Wind Turbine Maintenance, Repair and Overhaul Market (MRO) report [1],
the MRO market is expected to increase to USD 200.81 billion by 2026, from USD 81.80 billion in
2020, even after the COVID-19 outbreak. As no significant impact has affected the market due to
uninterrupted operations of wind farms and maintenance activities, except on sudden halt in first
half of 2020, but immediately recovering from the second half of 2020.
The rising demand in renewable energy has been witnessed in efforts to reduce the global reliance
in fossil fuel, increasing the share of wind power in global energy production. As a consequence, a
resulted boost for maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) services is seen.

The year 2019 was the biggest growth year to date for offshore wind energy, which has installed 29
GW capacity globally. Despite COVID-19 outbreak, Global Wind Energy Council (GWEC) predicts
strong growth in 2020 depicted in Figure 1.1, and substantial job creation over the next decade. The
offshore wind industry witnessed major installations in 2020. For instance, China installed a 3 GW
offshore wind in a single year, followed by the Netherlands (installed 1.5 GW), Belgium (installed 706
MW), the United Kingdom (installed 483 MW), and Germany (237 MW). While Europe continues to
lead in overall offshore capacity, China shows biggest growth rates and high number of installations,
together with other Asian economies on the rise.
A total investment of USD 107.2 billion was made in wind power globally in 2021, making it one
of the fastest growing energy market. Although most of the wind power is coming from onshore,
offshore wind farms are gaining wide attention, which is expected to increase the demand for the
wind turbine significantly. As offshore wind energy deployment escalates, it is likely that it will drive
global wind turbine maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) market during forecasted period.
Moreover, more countries tend to invest in wind energy in the Middle-East and Africa region, especially
in the Asia-Pacific, which is forecasted to be the largest and fastest market increasing global wind
power, owing to the contribution of China with around 278.32 GW capacity as of 2020. On the
other hand, India, the second-largest country in the Asia-Pacific region in terms of wind energy
installed capacity, has a capacity of 38.625 GW as of 2020. Accordingly, the Indian government has
set a target of 140 GW coming from wind-based energy generation. South Korea also aims to have
approximately 18 GW of wind power by 2030. This, in turn, is expected to present Asia-Pacific as an
excellent business destination for players involved in the global wind turbine maintenance, repair and
overhaul business during the forecast period.
As different locations are being deployed for wind turbines (WT), the more challenge there regarding
concerns on environmental factors, putting additional pressure on wind turbine components, resulting
to a premature failure causing significant downtime in wind farms. Furthermore, the costs involved
in providing MRO services are much higher for offshore than onshore sites, due to access difficulties.
Such risks, confines the growth compared to onshore.

1.1 Overview of Repair in Composites
After the accelerated growth of wind energy, the majority of built wind farms in early 2000s are
reaching their limited age, boosting the market for repair and maintenance strategies. Wind turbine
blade repairs are quite expensive, on which poses risks in cost during operation.
1.1.1 Frequency of blade failures
According to the Global Wind Energy Council (GWEC), at the end of 2016 approximately more
than 341,000 wind turbines were spinning and generating energy, which signifies that approximately
1,023,000 blades were in operation. WT blades can fail at different damage modes but the damage
progression differs for each blade design. Most common damage seen on wind turbine blades includes
[2-3]:
• Manufacturing
defects
and poor manufacturing
quality control
• Transportation damage
• Assembly damage
• Installation damage
• Lightning strikes
• Environmental wear
• Icing build-up and impacts
• Leading edge (LE) erosion
• Fatigue
• Mechanical failure
• Failure of a control system
to detect vibration, imbal-
ance, or insufficient power
• Workmanship
Carroll et al. [4] reported that the average failure rate for an offshore WT is at approximately 10
failures per turbine in a year. With 80% of those repairs being minor repairs (such as application of
protection tapes and shields), 17.5% major repairs (such as structural component repair) and 2.5%
major replacements [5]. Furthermore, it has been observed that there is a trend of rising average
failure rates with rising average wind speeds in onshore wind farms. This shows high correlation
in failures occurring in offshore wind farms as it is subjected to higher wind speeds than onshore.
Moreover, Dao et al. [6] showed that offshore WT has 4 times higher in failure rate than the onshore
WT when it comes for rotor blades and hub.
1.2 Repair in Composites
In order to make wind industry competitive and attractive with respect to other renewable energy
available, a call for optimization of WT blade repairs is an important consideration for the WT blade
repairs as the wind energy market expands and develops globally in the coming next decade. Therefore,
a cheaper and more efficient blade repair is required with the aim of restoring the performance of the
blade at the same level as initial structural capacity, and will be able to uphold until wind turbines
reaches its approximate 20-25 years of lifetime expectancy. [5]

During the last decades, technologies in composite repaired structures has been deepen, due to
increased application of lightweight material in aerospace industry, influencing the development
of repairs in wind energy. Such knowledge and technologies are less mature compared to aircraft
constructions. As a consequence, some aerospace techniques are adopted for composite application.
[7]
The application of the repair method is dependent on (a) severity of damage, and (b) region of
damage, and (c) aerodynamic/flush requirements. Depending on damage severity and type, the
repair methods can be grouped as follows [5]:
1. Erosion repair and protection, typically by coatings, tapes, shields,
2. Non-structural matrix cracks, cosmetic repairs. Filling and sealing, resin injection for small
surface cracks or small delaminations,
3. Structural damage: Plug/patch and scarf repair.
In order to restore load-carrying capacity of the blade after a structural damage, scarf repairs are
commonly applied, where the ply-by-ply layers should match the original material structure [8]. In
industrial application, this is also known as ’ply matrix’. In fact for composite adherends, maximum
stress occurs adjacent the ends of plies [9-10]. It was observed that the composite layup and lamina
thickness influences the stress concentration. With various stacking of different type of lamina layers,
a disparity in stiffness is present in between adhesive and plies, inducing stress variation.

1.3 Objective of The Project
The objective of the project is to provide an automated algorithm to design stepped-scarf repair
in composite structures. The Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf (MPSS) repair scheme is introduced
to include design parameters in the repair in term of material, layup stacking and geometrical
according to loading scenario (tensile or bending). These are then taken into account during 2D
mesh generation, and imported into an ABAQUS input file.
This will allow design flexibility and higher working efficiency in model generation in different repair
simulation studies granting the possibility for further investigation and understanding of the effect of
different parameters on the joint performance.

2.1 Stepped-Scarf Repair
This section will highlight how the idea and development of the Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf Repair
(MPSS) came, especially why the design parameters are considered in the automated algorithm and
their importance.
Fig. 2.1.: Scarf repair working procedure. [11]
7
==PAGE:19==
In a blade structural damage, composite repair is applied in order to restore strength and stiffness of
the blade. Bonded scarf or stepped repairs are used in composite structures when higher strength
recovery is required.
Fig. 2.2.: Upper: Cross-sectional views scarf repairs; Lower: Cross-sectional view of stepped repair. [14]
Scarf repairs are complex to design and require the removal of significant parent material. Moreover,
an optimal scarf repair design is non-existent due to multiple number of geometric and material
parameters that impacts the bonded repair joint performance. The stepped scarf repair involves
removal of damaged fibre and be replaced by reinforcing layers to restore the strength and stiffness
capacity of the blade. In Figure 2.1, a stepped scarf repair procedural steps are shown. The process
is reflected from industrial practices currently applied [11].
Figure 2.2 shows two types of scarf repair, tapered and stepped scarf. Tapered scarf repair can be
difficult to apply in practice depending on repair configuration, while stepped scarf repair joints offer
an easier, efficient and less time consuming to produce according to Wang et al. [14]. Furthermore,
their study revealed that stepped repairs can be designed to be rivalled to the tapered scarf repair´s
performance, due to similar stress concentrations behaviours at ply terminations. For these reasons,
stepped scarf repair is modelled using Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf (MPSS) repair scheme to
accommodate different design variables that affects the repair joint performance.
As Hart [8] suggested, a strong optimum-designed of stepped joint will have a failure occurring in the
adherend on the parent laminate outside of the joint, instead on the adhesive bond line where peel or
shear failures may occur. But designing such optimum stepped-scarf repair is complex due to high
number of material and geometric parameters that must be taken into consideration during design
process that will influence the joint performance. Adding the orthotropic nature of the composite
laminate, the complexity increases as the stiffnesses of the bonded materials vary.

Odi and Friend [31] expressed that techniques suggested in open literature for modelling composite
stepped-scarf repairs are not entirely adequate. When considering isotropic adherends in a bonded
joints, the design and studies has now relatively matured according to Adams et al., [13], in compari-
son to orthotropic adherends.
Bendemra et al. [15] examined the influence of joint parameters on peak stresses in the adhesive
bond line through linear finite element analysis to perform parametric study with focus on joint
design parameters including ply thickness, adhesive thickness, scarfing, stacking sequence, over ply
layup, and over ply lap length. The outcome shows that the stepped joint is affected by parameters
including ply thickness, scarfing, and stacking sequence. Therefore, in the developed automated
method, such parameters are considered.
In fact, the importance of the addition of the stacking sequence in the developed method is that,
in the variation of stacking sequence, the location of 0◦creates localized peak stress concentration
when 0◦ply is applied on near or free edges of laminate. The adhesive bond line adjacent to 0◦
lamina are more likely to be subjected to stress concentration. This is due to the majority of the
load transfer arising at the region of stiffer plies during tensile loading [15]. This observation accord
with published results from Wang et al. [20] and Breitzman et al. [21].
Due to concern over the stress concentration, the effect of application of the over ply layer on the
repair is also investigated in previous works. Bendemra et al. [15] concluded that stress concentration
in stepped-lap joints can be mitigated with the introduction of over plies and appropriate design
parameters. The advantage of adding over-laminate was also noted by Gunnion and Herszberg [10],
on which a significant drop in peak peel and peak shear stresses was seen, though different stacking
layup sequence and increase of thickness of the over-laminate has little effect on the peak stresses.
Therefore such addition is worth-taking into consideration in the developed automated method, to
help future studies in repair strength restoration.

The previously cited references applied tensile loading in their respective FE models, but this does
not limit the importance of investigating stepped-scarf repair under bending since wind turbine
blade structures are being subjected to various loading scenario. In fact, Liu et al. [28] stated that
such case has not been investigated sufficiently. Furthermore, Vaidya et al.[29] observed that the
out-of-plane load causes higher peel stress concentration than in-plane load case in their studies on
impact response. In order to extend the developed method’s capability, both tensile and bending are
treated.
As these previously mentioned studies suggest, there are huge material, geometrical and layup stacking
consideration to be taken into account, complicating the achievement of an optimal repair design.
In order to investigate the variation, finite element (FE) model is typically used. To broaden such
investigation, a method is proposed in this study to accommodate different designs and user-defined
parameters, named Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf Repair (MPSS) scheme.

2.2 Modelling Stepped Scarf Repair using MPSS
Two dimensional stepped scarf repair geometry is built as shown in Figure 2.6. The construction
of the stepped scarf repair considers adhesive thickness in between each fibre ply layer that act as
resin interface, ta,i, and in between parent and repair laminate, ta. The Multi-Parameter Stepped
Scarf (MPSS) repair scheme is developed in MATLAB software to generate the 2D meshed stepped
scarf geometry, material properties, loading and boundary conditions. These will be printed out in an
ABAQUS input file (.inp) for the software to run.
It has been examined that adhesive bond lines is an important factor when designing scarf bonded
joints [10; 15], therefore, material properties of resin interfaces can be changed, together with the
UD and BIAX fibre material. Wang and Duong [12] suggested that there are generally five methods
when it comes to designing bonded joints for composite repair structures. These methods includes i)
average stress method, ii) maximum stress method, iii) linear elastic fracture mechanics method, iv)
virtual crack closure theory (VCCT) and v) cohesive zone modelling (CZM) technique. The CZM
technique is implemented as one of the ABAQUS model tree set ups of MPSS. Therefore, MPSS is
able to generate resin interfaces into cohesive elements or solid elements in ABAQUS, depending on
the complexity of the future study that will be performed by the user.
Different parameters are possible to be inputted in the MPSS in order to generate the .inp file
and these are summed up in Table 2.1. The MPSS has the ability to consider two material types
which includes UD and BIAX material with mechanical properties enlisted in Table A.14-A.17. An
additional material type, for example TRIAX, could be added, but it will require more lines in MPSS
code by applying same logic used for UD and BIAX.

It is important to note that due to the high variation of set-up in repair design, there are several
Abaqus functions to accommodate them. But in general, a common model tree set-up is used,
except on the Sets. In this section, the function Abaqus is tackled in the following points to enlist

• Element Type, Material Properties, Section
All materials are printed out in the .inp file listed from Table A.14 to A.17. For the repair
design, a static 2D plane strain linear analysis was used. Both the adherends and adhesive
were modelled using linear 4-noded plane strain elements with reduced integration, i.e. CPE4R
elements. But if an option of cohesive elements has been chosen, then the resin elements will
have COH2D4 elements. These can be manually changed if other 2D element type is required.

Solid elements are used to design the scarf joints to facilitate simple calculation comparison and
to allow lower computational time. Such technique in designing and analyzing scarf repair is
chosen for FE methodology, considering that the strategy was applied successfully in published
studies [10; 18]. Additionally, studies using cohesive elements in resin has been also available
in order to consider damage mechanism in the joints [17; 30]. Therefore, both options have
been considered in MPSS.
• Boundary Conditions and Applied Displacements
The boundary conditions applied to the stepped scarf repair are shown in Figure 3.3. Two set
edges, right and left, of the 2D model contains collection of nodes and elements whose motion
are governed by the motion of a single node, named rigid body reference node, which in these
study are called RP-1 and RP-2 for right and left edge set respectively as illustrated in Figure
3.3).
For tension, right reference node, RP-1, is fixed for ux = uy = uz = 0, while left reference node,
RP-2, is subjected to x-direction displacement of ux = xdisp [m], while fixed in uy = uz = 0.
While bending is applied through application of rotational displacement about z-axis in both
RP-1 and RP-2 reference nodes, with urz = −thetarot [rad] and urz = thetarot [rad] respec-
tively. RP-1 is fixed for ux = uy = uz = 0, while RP-2 is fixed for uy = uz = 0.

5.1 Conclusions
An automated method for designing stepped-scarf repair for composite laminate structure has been
created based from different FE models and parametric considerations highlighted in Section 2.1.
The Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf (MPSS) repair scheme has been presented to accommodate
different parameters enlisted in Table 2.1 on which affects the repair design performance under tensile
and bending scenario.
During the model verification through analytical calculation using Classical Laminate theory and
ABAQUS calculation using Euler-Bernoulli equations, to compute global elastic modulus and flexural
rigidity of a pristine model, results shown for three difference layups from Table 4.1 and 4.2, that not
a significant percentage difference is observed with respect to loading under tension and bending.
The Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf (MPSS) repair scheme is advantageous to have a preliminary
understanding of laminate’s stiffness restoration after repair. The MPSS scheme is introduced as a
natural initial step to move forward to more complex finite element analysis in future studies. Its
ability to accommodate geometrical and material parameters will help the working efficiency and
design flexibility in future studies due to its automated algorithm facilitating succeeding research
endeavour.

5.2 Recommended Future work
This section will highlight recommended future works using the Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf
(MPSS) repair scheme.
• Bonded composite repairs requires strength and stiffness restoration close to the original prop-
erties as possible. But in order to achieve a good repair, a good design is required. Therefore,
stepped-scarf repair design require comprehensive analysis of stress phenomena around the
adhesive interface and two adherends. A parametric sensitivity analysis would be advantageous
to widen the study of the effect of various parameters in the stepped-scarf joint performance in
terms of stiffness and stress variation.
This will enable the analysis of occurring stress concentration as may contribute to the failure
in composite [10], and the complexity increases as it varies with along the joint, depending on
layup sequence, geometrical parameters and material properties [9].
• The mechanical property of the resin should also be considered, as brittle adhesives tend to be
sensitive to porosity and stress concentration, while adhesives with high ductility and lesser
modulus would be able to reduce bond line stress concentration improving the joint strength
capacity [22-24], though a decrease in stiffness would be expected [25].
• To recognize undesirable failure process in the adhesive joint, a detailed progressive failure
analysis based on fracture mechanics through cohesive zone modelling (CZM) would be an
advantage for the repair design and would provide further insights into the damage mechanisms.
• An experimental test is proposed to be carried out to further investigate the validation of the
Multi-Parameter Stepped Scarf (MPSS) repair scheme, including other loading scenario such
as bending, compression and fatigue, to gain insights on strength concentration and stiffness
restoration through pristine and repaired specimens.
