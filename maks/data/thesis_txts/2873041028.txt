Abstract—This paper introduces a novel soft-robotic mecha-
nism and reports on the development and experimental validation
of its Variable Stiffness Link (VSL) system. The VSL comprises a
series of rigid, vertebral segments linked by an internal tensioning
cable, with an electric motor modulating the cable tension to
dynamically adjust the structure’s bending stiffness. A custom
test bench with integrated sensors was used to evaluate the VSL’s
performance on a 1.5kg suspended payload under multiple stiff-
ness settings. Results show that increasing the link’s stiffness via
higher tension signiﬁcantly reduces payload oscillation amplitude
and settling time. A MATLAB/Simulink dynamic model of the
VSL was developed and calibrated against experimental data,
achieving a high level of accuracy in predicting the system’s
mechanical response. The validated simulation framework and
control architecture enable precise real-time stiffness control and
provide insight into stiffness–force relationships, conﬁrming the
VSL’s effectiveness as an adaptive bridge between rigid and soft
links.
Index Terms—Variable stiffness structure, Adjustable stiffness
structure, Soft robotics
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern robotic systems must increasingly operate in un-
structured environments and interact physically with humans,
demanding a delicate balance between precision, strength,
and compliance. Conventional rigid robots are designed with
high-stiffness materials and joints, enabling accurate motion,
repeatability, and substantial load-bearing capabilities [1], [2].
These attributes make them ideal for industrial applications
where tasks are repetitive and environments are predictable
[3]. However, the very rigidity that confers strength also
introduces risks: rigid structures are poorly suited to safe
physical interaction [4] and lack the mechanical adaptability
needed for uncertain or dynamic settings.
Conversely, the ﬁeld of soft robotics introduces highly
compliant structures capable of large deformations [5]. By
using ﬂexible materials such as elastomers, textiles, or cable
networks, soft robots naturally conform to complex geometries
[6], absorb impacts [7], and reduce contact forces during
interaction [8]. These characteristics make them particularly
advantageous for tasks involving fragile objects [9], variable
terrains [10], or close human-robot collaboration [11]. Yet, the
inherent compliance of soft systems often limits their capacity
for precise control [12] and high-force output [13], restricting
their utility in tasks that require both dexterity and structural
integrity.
To bridge the gap between these two paradigms, researchers
have increasingly turned to variable stiffness mechanisms,
which enable robotic structures to dynamically tune their me-
chanical compliance in response to task demands or environ-
mental disturbances [14]. By allowing a robot to be soft when
safety or adaptability is prioritized, and stiff when accuracy or
force is required, such systems promise to unify the beneﬁts
of both rigid and soft robotics. Variable stiffness is particularly
advantageous in applications involving physical interaction,
adaptive locomotion, or manipulation under changing loads,
where ﬁxed compliance can either compromise safety or
degrade performance.
Within this context, Variable Stiffness Actuators (VSAs)
have emerged as a dominant design approach, actuator where
the output stiffness as perceived at the load shaft can be varied
through one or multiple internal actuators, enabling control-
lable stiffness at the joint level. One representative example
is the actuator developed by Sun et al. [15], which employs a
variable stiffness mechanism based on an antagonistic variable
radius principle. The system integrates a foldable Hoberman
linkage and spiral-cam-based quadratic springs arranged in
an antagonistic conﬁguration. By simultaneously adjusting the
Hoberman linkage radius and spring preload using two rotary
motors, the actuator achieves a high range of stiffness change
while allowing independent control of stiffness and position.
This design enables real-time stiffness modulation with a
compact structure and high tracking performance. However,
it introduces mechanical complexity through components like
spiral cams, a bidirectional screw mechanism, and multiple
elastic transmission elements, leading to increased size, mass,
and potential hysteresis.
Another approach to address stiffness problem is designing
Variable Stiffness Link, the stiffness change is distributed
throughout the entire link structure. Differently from VSA, a
VSL is a passive link element, enabling controllable stiffness
at the joint level. In one approach, Stilli et al. [16] proposed
the use of Variable Stiffness Links (VSLs) to achieve safer
human-robot interactions through pneumatic actuation. Their
hybrid design employs silicone, plastic mesh, and fabric layers
to form ﬂexible links whose stiffness can be modulated by
inﬂating or deﬂating internal chambers. This method allows
the robotic links to transition between soft and rigid states,
thereby adapting to diverse manipulation scenarios with min-
imal mechanical intrusion. The pneumatic system provides
==PAGE:2==
smooth stiffness transitions and inherent compliance, which is
particularly beneﬁcial in close-proximity human-robot collab-
orative tasks. However, it uses pneumatic activation, introduces
massive mass and system complexity, as it requires an air
pump system.
In this work, we propose a new approach based on elec-
trically actuated cable twisting to achieve distributed stiffness
modulation in soft robotic structures. Speciﬁcally, we intro-
duce a novel Variable Stiffness Link (VSL): a cable-driven
mechanism that adjusts its mechanical stiffness in real time
via motor-controlled twisting of internal tendons. This method
enables a smooth and reversible transition from a compliant
to a stiff conﬁguration without relying on external pressur-
ization or segmented designs. The VSL prototype integrates
a DC motor with embedded sensing and a closed-loop PID
controller to precisely modulate cable tension, allowing for
real-time control of stiffness in response to external loads or
disturbances.
To validate the proposed concept, we constructed a ded-
icated experimental platform that subjects the VSL to both
static and dynamic loading conditions. Results demonstrate
a wide tunable stiffness range and effective damping of os-
cillatory payload motion, such as swing in cable-suspended
systems. Complementary simulations developed in MAT-
LAB/Simulink corroborate the experimental ﬁndings, offering
a predictive model of force–deformation behavior under vary-
ing stiffness states.
The results suggest that electrically actuated, cable-based
stiffness modulation offers a novel design that is lightweight,
distributed, and compatible with soft robotic architectures.
This approach opens new pathways for robotic systems that
must adapt their morphology in real time to meet the diverse
demands of modern human-centric and unstructured environ-
ments.
II. METHOD
The Variable Stiffness Link (VSL), a patent pending me-
chanical system, depicted in the accompanying technical draw-
ings represents a novel mechanical system engineered for real-
time modulation of bending stiffness through internal tension
control. The VSL is composed of a series of rigid segments
shown in Figure 1 (2) enclosed within a compliant inner shell
(4) and aligned along a central axis (C). These segments are
threaded by a tensile element shown in Figure 1 (3)—such
as a twisted string or wire—anchored between two end caps
Figure 1 (2A, 2B). When tension is applied, typically via
motor actuation or similar means, the axial force compresses
the rigid elements against one another, increasing frictional
and geometric coupling between interfacial surfaces, as shown
in the bent conﬁguration in Figure 1, thereby raising the overall
structural stiffness.
In the absence of tension, as seen in the rest conﬁguration,
the segments maintain minimal contact, allowing the structure
to bend and ﬂex under external forces. This tunable mechanism
allows for a wide range of stiffness states without changing the
overall geometry. The design of the VSL integrates mechanical

simplicity with functional versatility, enabling stiffness control
with minimal actuation power, potentially without dedicated
sensors, and suitable for integration in weight- and space-
constrained environments such as drones, wearable robotics,
and soft manipulators.
III. MECHANICAL DESIGN
The VSL itself is constructed as a series of solid 3D-
printed segments or “vertebrae” linked in series. In each
segment, interlocking protrusions shown in Fig 2 (1) and
complementary pits shown in Fig 2 (2) constrain the relative
rotation to a safe range, avoiding unwanted free spinning. A
central through-hole in Fig 2 (3) in each segment carries the
tensile actuator cable, a twisted-string wire, along the length
of the rope, while peripheral guide holes shown in Fig 2 (4)
accommodate rigid alignment wires that maintain structural
continuity, without signiﬁcantly affecting stiffness. The outer
surface of each segment is sloped shown in Fig 2 (5) so
that adjacent segments always pivot about a consistent edge;
this controlled articulation edge helps conﬁne bending to the
designed degree of freedom. These segments are assembled

IV. EXPERIMENT SETUP
To actuate the structure and tune its stiffness, a 12V DC
geared motor (DFRobot GB37Y3530) with an integrated en-
coder was employed. Internal wires are attached to the motor
axis and to the end-unit of the rope mechanism, in a way that
the wires (at least two) can be twisted. By twisting the internal
wires, the motor applies tension between each consecutive
units, which creates the effect of increasing the structure’s
stiffness.
To measure the mechanical response of the system, two
types of load sensors were deployed on a bench setup: 1)TE
FX29 compression load cells to capture the pulling force
applied by the motor. Three TE FX29 sensors are positioned
between the motor and the VSL base to measure the force
exchanged between the base plate and the ﬁrst disk unit of the
VSL. 2) 20 Kg load cells with Joy-it SEN-HX711 ampliﬁer to
measure the external forces at the end effector. The external
force is applied by hand by the test operator to the last disk
unit, in a direction orthogonal to the VSL axial direction at
rest. The sensor is cabable of measuring up to 20 kg.
For the purpose of motion tracking and deformation analy-
sis, infrared light-emitting diodes (LEDs; Vishay TSHF5210)
were afﬁxed to the lateral surface of the Variable Stiffness
Limb (VSL). These markers were monitored using a calibrated
vision system comprising a Raspberry Pi (RPi)-based record-
ing unit equipped with a Global Shutter Camera. The camera’s
standard infrared (IR) ﬁlter was replaced with an IR-pass ﬁlter
to ensure visibility of the LED emissions, thereby enabling
accurate tracking of pixel displacements associated with each
marker. This setup facilitated frame-by-frame analysis of seg-
ment positions throughout actuation and external loading. The

V. MODELLING AND SIMULATION
To complement the experimental analysis, a simulation
model of the VSL was developed using MATLAB Simulink
in conjunction with Simscape Multibody. Each segment of the
VSL is represented as a rigid body, interconnected through
revolute joints that emulate rotational ﬂexibility and mechan-
ical contact between components. While these joints permit
only one-dimensional rotation about the z-axis, the simulation
is conducted within a full three-dimensional environment to
more accurately capture realistic deformations.
Rather than explicitly modeling the twisted-string actuation
mechanism and the internal rigid wire of the VSL, the simu-
lation connects individual elements via revolute joints, which
serve to approximate the contact interactions that occur under
applied tensile forces. As a result, the system is constrained
to one-dimensional rotational motion; however, the movement
is visualized and quantiﬁed in three-dimensional space. This
modeling approach reﬂects the current capabilities of the

To enable bidirectional movement, two rotational joints are

model, as illustrated in Fig. 4A.
The simulation takes as input an external load and a pulling
force, which respectively follow the global and the local frame
on the last element. The external load, which is illustrated in
Fig 4 B, can be applied either in the centre of the last elements
or on the edge, depending on the data that is needed. One can
also choose if the external force is in the x-direction or in
the y-direction, or a torque around the z-axis. It can also be a
combination of those loads.
The pulling force, illustrated in Fig 4 C, represents the
force which with the twisted-string actuation is pulling on
the parts. Rather than directly being represented by a force
in the “external force” block available in MATLAB Simscape
like the external load, the pulling force is represented by the
stiffness; a higher pulling force results in a stiffer system.

B. Dynamic Load Test
To evaluate the dynamic behavior of the VSL under real-
world conditions, a slung load experiment was conducted. This
test simulates the VSL acting as a suspended cable carrying a
payload, providing insights into its damping behavior, swing
stability, and time to convergence under different stiffness
conﬁgurations.
A 1.5 kg mass was attached to the bottom of the VSL
structure, and the system was left free to oscillate. Three
stiffness levels were applied:
• From 0s to 35s, the motor generated a 3 kg pulling force,
producing a more compliant (soft) conﬁguration.
• From 35s to 55s, the pulling force was increased to 12
kg, resulting in a much stiffer rope-like behaviour.
• From 55s to 70s, the pulling force was increased to 20
kg, resulting in an higher stiffness rod behaviour.

VII. DISCUSSIONS
The work presented in this report demonstrates the feasibil-
ity and effectiveness of the proposed Variable Stiffness Link
(VSL) system for bridging between rigid and soft robotics. The
test bench design, hardware implementation, and integrated
sensor suite enabled thorough investigation of the VSL’s
performance under both static and dynamic loading conditions.
The main ﬁndings can be summarized as follows:
• The VSL exhibits tunable stiffness properties, with a
signiﬁcant change inmechanical behavior under varying
pulling forces, as conﬁrmed through both load cell mea-
surements and angle deformation tracking.
• The control architecture allows for precise force applica-
tion via a PID-regulated DC motor, while sensor integra-
tion provides real-time feedback on internal tension and
external loading.
• The Simulink-based simulation model, calibrated against
experimental data, achieves a high level of accuracy in
predicting the mechanical response of the VSL. It enables
systematic exploration of stiffness-to-force relationships
and can support future integration tasks.
• The slung mass test reveals how the VSL modulates dy-
namic response characteristics such as oscillation damp-
ing and swing recovery.

Abstract—This project focuses on the design and implemen-
tation of a quad-copter frame incorporating variable stiffness
links, with the goal of enhancing impact absorption and enabling
structural recovery following collisions with the environment. It
addresses the mechanical realization of variable stiffness mecha-
nisms within an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV), the development
of low-level control systems to operate these mechanisms, and
their seamless integration with the ﬂight control architecture.
Furthermore, the project explores control strategies for dynam-
ically tuning structural stiffness to maximize impact absorption.
The ultimate objective is to deliver an aerial robot equipped with
a variable stiffness structure and control algorithms speciﬁcally
designed for effective impact mitigation.
Index Terms—UAV, variable stiffness links, impact absorption,
structural recovery
I. INTRODUCTION
Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) have grown increasingly
popular in ﬁelds involving environmental monitoring [1] [2],
search and rescue [3] [4], and infrastructure inspection [5]
[6], highlighting their revolutionary potential for traversing
dynamic and complicated environments. But a signiﬁcant
drawback still exists: conventional rigid frame drones are still
vulnerable to accidents and collisions [7], which can result
in catastrophic structural damage, mission failure, or even
safety risks during missions near people [8]. This challenge
intensiﬁes in congested or unstructured environments, where
conﬁned spaces and unexpected obstacles demand both pre-
cise navigation and contact resilience. Existing UAV designs
largely prioritize lightweight construction [9] and aerodynamic
efﬁciency [10], these drone frames excel in stable ﬂight but
lack the adaptability to absorb impacts or mitigate damage
during unintended contact with obstacles. This limitation risks
mission failure and elevates maintenance costs and downtime,
especially in contexts where human repair is impractical or
hazardous.
Most prior research on UAV collision prevention has
concentrated on software-based collision avoidance systems
(CAS) that leverage onboard sensors and algorithms to detect
obstacles and avert crashes. A comprehensive survey of UAV
collision avoidance strategies highlights that these approaches
typically integrate various sensing modalities (active sensors
like radar/LiDAR and passive cameras) with real-time nav-
igation algorithms for obstacle detection and evasion [11].
Methods range from geometric path-planning and force-ﬁeld
(potential ﬁeld) techniques to optimization-based and reactive
sense-and-avoid maneuvers, all aimed at ensuring the drone
Fig. 1. A quadcopter integrated with variable stiffness links.
maintains a safe distance from obstacles [11]. This software-
driven paradigm has become the dominant approach in UAV
safety, effectively reducing collisions by planning around
hazards before impact [11].
However, even the most advanced CAS cannot guarantee
that every collision will be avoided – sensor limitations,
latency, or unforeseen obstacles can still lead to impacts. This
realization has spurred interest in hardware-based collision
resilience, which shifts focus from merely avoiding collisions
to surviving collisions. Instead of relying solely on sensors and
control logic, hardware-centric solutions enhance the drone’s
physical ability to withstand crashes, providing a complemen-
tary safety net when avoidance fails.
A notable example of this approach is the collision-resilient
quad-copter CogniFly introduced by Azambuja et al. [12].
CogniFly is an under-250g UAV equipped with a bio-inspired
semi-rigid frame that mimics arthropods’ exoskeletons [12]. Its
airframe combines rigid carbon-ﬁber rods with ﬂexible 3D-
printed joints, a design that allows the structure to deform
upon impact and absorb collision forces [12]. This exoskeletal
architecture protects critical components and provides passive
impact damping, enabling the drone to survive multiple col-
lisions at speeds up to around 7m/s with minimal damage
[12]. Such a hardware-driven strategy for collision tolerance,
==PAGE:9==
although far less common than software CAS, represents
a promising alternative for enhancing UAV robustness. In
essence, while conventional solutions prioritize avoiding colli-
sions via sophisticated sensing and algorithms [11], emerging
designs like CogniFly demonstrate that resilient structural
design can greatly mitigate damage when collisions occur [12].
Traditional approaches to drone crash resilience have relied
on adding extra protective structures or using inherently ﬂex-
ible materials, but each comes with trade-offs. For example,
enclosing a drone in a rigid exoskeleton or cage can shield
sensitive parts, yet this adds bulk and weight to the aircraft
[13]. Heavier drones lose payload and agility and must juggle
protection versus practicality. Elastic frames absorb shocks,
but too much ﬂex shifts motor angles and breaks the rigid
geometry ﬂight controllers rely on for stability. In essence,
a frame that is always pliable can cause oscillations and
vibrations, which makes the autopilot work harder to keep the
aircraft stable and wastes energy. Past research on fully ﬂexible
drone frames also found that such designs often only protect
against certain collision directions and can leave critical com-
ponents, e.g. propellers, exposed, all while being difﬁcult to
manufacture without special materials. These limitations moti-
vate a solution that provides protection without the downsides
of permanent exoskeletons or always-soft structures.
Variable Stiffness Link (VSL) technology addresses this
need by embedding adaptability directly into the drone’s
frame. Instead of attaching a separate crash cage or making the
entire frame continuously compliant, the VSL approach allows
the structure itself to dynamically switch between stiff and
ﬂexible states. This means the UAV can remain rigid during
normal ﬂight, preserving precise controllability and efﬁciency,
and then momentarily soften upon impact or in anticipation of
a collision to absorb energy.
By avoiding a bulky external exoskeleton, the design stays
lightweight and aerodynamic, and by not relying on purely
elastic materials, it avoids the constant ﬂight penalties of a
ﬂoppy frame. Conceptually, the VSL achieves this balance
through its cable-twisting mechanism: tightening the inter-
nal tendon increases the link’s stiffness, while loosening it
makes the link compliant on demand. This smooth, reversible
stiffness modulation grants the drone immediate post-collision
recovery—once the impact is over, the frame can be re-
stiffened to its original shape, restoring structural integrity for
continued ﬂight.
II. METHOD
The Variable Stiffness Link (VSL), a patent pending me-
chanical system designed, depicted in the accompanying tech-
nical drawings represents a novel mechanical system engi-
neered for real-time modulation of bending stiffness through
internal tension control. The VSL is composed of a series
of rigid segments shown in Figure 2 (2) enclosed within a
compliant inner shell (4) and aligned along a central axis
(C). These segments are threaded by a tensile element shown
in Figure 2 (3)—such as a twisted string or wire—anchored
between two end caps Figure 2 (2A, 2B). When tension

conﬁguration.
is applied, typically via motor actuation or similar means,
the axial force compresses the rigid elements against one
another, increasing frictional and geometric coupling between
interfacial surfaces, as shown in the bent conﬁguration in
Figure 2, thereby raising the overall structural stiffness.
In the absence of tension, as seen in the rest conﬁgura-

structure to bend and ﬂex under external forces. This tunable
mechanism allows for a wide range of stiffness states with-
out changing the overall geometry, preserving aerodynamic
efﬁciency—especially valuable in aerial robotic systems. The
design of the VSL integrates mechanical simplicity with func-
tional versatility, enabling stiffness control with minimal actua-
tion power, potentially without dedicated sensors, and suitable
for integration in weight- and space-constrained environments
such as drones, wearable robotics, and soft manipulators.

To enable rapid and cost-effective prototyping of the pro-

IV. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULT ANALYSIS
Prior to integrating the variable stiffness limb (VSL) into
the drone platform, preliminary testing was conducted on a
custom testbed to evaluate the mechanical load capacity of a
single VSL module under propeller-induced thrust. The setup
included a 20 kg load cell (equipped with a Joy-it HX711
ampliﬁer) for force measurement, as well as two infrared
light-emitting diodes (TSHF5210, 890 nm) serving as visual
markers for angle estimation, as shown in Fig. 6.
The infrared LEDs were positioned along the length of the
VSL to act as reference points. As the propeller generated
upward thrust, the VSL experienced a tilt, causing a change
in orientation of the markers. A camera-based computer vision
system was employed to detect the LEDs, ﬁt a straight line
between them, and calculate the tilt angle ω with respect to
the horizontal plane.
The load cell, mounted beneath the blue clamp-like connec-
tor, measured the vertical force F transmitted through the VSL
from the motor. The net thrust T generated by the propeller
was then estimated using the following equation:
T = F/cosω

where ω is the inclination angle of the VSL relative to the
horizontal. This method allowed for an indirect but practical
estimation of the propeller thrust that the VSL could withstand
under realistic operational conditions.

V. DISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSION
The results of this work demonstrate a successful implemen-
tation of variable stiffness links (VSLs) as drone arms, mark-
ing a signiﬁcant step toward collision-resilient UAV design.
We achieved our primary goal of creating a quadrotor arm
that can switch between rigid and ﬂexible states on demand,
using an internal tendon-tensioning mechanism. This is in
contrast to most existing drones that are either entirely rigid or
permanently ﬂexible. By integrating adaptive stiffness directly
into the frame, our approach bridges the gap between stability
and compliance. In normal ﬂight (stiff mode), the VSL arms
maintain a rigid geometry for efﬁcient control, while in a
collision event (compliant mode), they can ﬂex to absorb
energy and prevent damage. Our work contributes to this
emerging area by providing a compact, mechanical solution
for stiffness modulation using a twisted tendon – to our
knowledge, one of the ﬁrst variable-stiffness implementations
integrated into a standard quadcopter frame.
Experimental evaluation of a single VSL-based arm con-
ﬁrms the feasibility of the design under realistic thrust loads.
In bench tests, a single arm withstood approximately 0.37kg
of upward force before signiﬁcant deﬂection, corresponding
to an estimated total lift of 1.5kg for a quadcopter with four
such arms. This payload capacity, while modest, demonstrates
that the VSL arms can support a small drone in principle. The
tests conﬁrmed that the VSL concept is mechanically sound
and that it meets the basic requirement of functioning as a
drone arm under load, while offering the intended ﬂexibility.
Despite these promising achievements, our prototype also
revealed signiﬁcant challenges and limitations that must be
addressed moving forward. When we assembled a full quad-
copter using four VSL arms, shown in Fig 1, the current
design struggled to carry the vehicle’s weight. The drone’s
actual takeoff weight was higher than anticipated, and as each
motor throttled up to generate the required lift, the VSL arms
bent at unexpectedly large angles. In stiff mode, the arms did
not remain fully rigid; instead, they exhibited considerable
compliance and even oscillation under high thrust. Under
high tension, the twine can stretch or creep, which introduced
unplanned ﬂexibility just when the arms were supposed to be
rigid. Uncertainties in the arm stiffness and motion introduced
by the VSLs mean that advanced control strategies will be
needed to fully utilize the concept in free ﬂight.
Moving ahead, we are exploring several improvements to
overcome these limitations. First and foremost, we plan to re-
place the elastic twine tendons with low-stretch, high-modulus
cables or wires. Using a non-elastic tendon will drastically
increase the stiff-state rigidity of the VSL, ensuring the arms
behave like true rigid members when locked. Secondly, we
recognize the need for feedback control of stiffness. In the
current prototype, stiffness is adjusted open-loop (with no
sensor on the tendon or arm). Adding sensors to measure ten-
don tension or arm bending would enable active regulation of
stiffness in real time. Lastly, extensive real-world testing will
be critical. We plan to conduct controlled collision experiments
==PAGE:13==
(e.g. ﬂying the drone into obstacles in both rigid and ﬂexible
arm modes) to quantify impact absorption, damage reduction,
and post-collision recovery.
In conclusion, this work has demonstrated the feasibility and
potential beneﬁts of a variable-stiffness drone arm for collision
tolerance. Our VSL prototype has validated the concept on a
fundamental level, but it also underscored the challenges of
implementing such adaptability in practice. We have identiﬁed
immediate improvements (like using inelastic tendons) that
are expected to signiﬁcantly enhance performance. Looking
forward, incorporating sensing and adaptive control will be key
steps toward a fully robust system. With these enhancements,
we anticipate that variable stiffness arms can substantially
improve drone resilience – enabling drones to survive impacts,
recover quickly, and continue their missions with minimal
intervention.
