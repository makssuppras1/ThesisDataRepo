Abstract 
This project aims to examine low temperature decomposition of biomass by TGA instrument in isothermal 
setting. Tests involved thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of 8 biomass samples. Results were partly used to 
study the behavior of low temperature biomass decay, and partly to model pyrolysis kinetic for organic 
matter (dry and ash-free) in low temperature. Experiments were conducted by two STA instruments under 
four thermal configurations. The first three of them included isothermal stages of 150°C, 170°C, 190°C, 210°C 
and 230°C, heating rate of 10 K min-1 and atmospheres of 0% O2 (Condition 2), 20% O2 (Condition 3), 80% O2 
(Condition 1). For modeling of pyrolysis kinetics, availability of reactive organic matter for pyrolysis were 
modeled as a function of temperature, with low heating rates of 0.2, 1 and 2 K min-1 and temperature range 
from 150°C to 600°C in non-isothermal setting (Condition 4). 
    TG results showed that decomposition of wood particles were distinctly different depending on 
temperature, mineral composition, atmosphere and thermal configuration. Based on isothermal TG data, 
two stages of decomposition was observed. Stage one were mass loss related to moisture-evaporation in a 
temperature range of 25°C – 120°C for all atmospheres. Stage two were mass loss due to devolatilization 
seen from around T>120°C for pyrolysis processes. Observations indicated that the net effect of oxidation + 
pyrolysis was the same as only pyrolysis at T<190°C. Two possibilities for oxidation processes in biomass were 
suggested; 1) oxidation processes in biomass occur at higher temperatures compared to pyrolysis, namely at 
T>190°C or 2) oxidation occurs from T>120°C at the expense of pyrolysis and at a similar rate. Mineral matter 
(wet mixing) was seen to increase mass loss rate under all atmospheres, suggesting that calcium and 
potassium affects devolatilization rate. 
    A custom kinetic model was developed for pyrolysis kinetics of the reactive fraction only (dry and ash-free 
basis). Initially, pine doped with KCl appeared to have the highest reaction rate compared to the rest, while 
increasing slowly towards 230°C. Wood pellet, wheat straw and beech wood appeared to have lowest 
reaction rate initially corresponding into lower activation energies. Although, beech wood and wheat straw 
seemed to increase much more at higher temperatures which was also predicted with higher pre-exponential 
factors. Wood pellet was predicted to be the least reactive material. The model also predicted that the 
highest reaction rate is to that of beech wood at 230°C. Considering ignition risk, fuels with higher activation 
energies towards pyrolysis was suggested to be much more stable under slightly elevated temperature in 
isothermal setting. Pine with the lowest activation energy was suggested to present highest ignition risk upon 
exposure to slightly elevated temperatures. 
    Finally, pyrolysis kinetic model were tested for reproducibility. Good agreement was generally observed 
between pyrolysis experiments and model. Although, modeled data (dry and ash-free basis) for some 
materials seemed to be marginally scattered at elevated temperatures between 170°C – 190°C and at 230°C.

1 Project description 
1.1 Background 
Converting coal-fired power plants to biomass pellet firing is an effective strategy to increase the share of 
renewable energy. However, handling of biomass fuels, especially in pulverized form, creates new challenges 
as biomass powders have a tendency to self-ignite at comparatively low temperatures. This has a drastic 
effect on the mills of pulverized fuel fired boilers. Here, air is used to dry and transport particles from mill to 
burner. High temperatures are desired to increase overall plant efficiency. On the other hand, the risk of mill 
fires significantly increases with temperature in the mills. There is currently no sufficient knowledge on how 
to determine operating parameters that are both safe and efficient. 
Results from an ongoing study at CHEC Research Center suggest that mill fires originate from settled dust 
accumulations, which begin to smolder and then switch to flaming combustion as temperature increases. 
The influence of various fuel and process parameters is however not yet well understood. 
1.2 Objective 
To understand low-temperature self-heating and ignition in biomass, the objective is to investigate low 
temperature decomposition kinetics of biomass by TGA instrument. Preliminary results from the literature 
suggest that composition of different materials as mineral matter in the biomass have an influence on 
transition from smoldering to flaming. However, there are only few studies on this topic. Therefore, a 
systematic experimental investigation should be carried out to better understand the mechanisms at play in 
low temperature ignition. 
1.3 Content 
The project is to include the following work:  
 
A critical review of the literature on low temperature ignition of biomass. 
 
Tests on TGA instrument to determine pyrolysis kinetics and oxidation conversion of biomass 
samples. The study should comprise the following sample types: 
o Different woody materials. 
o Effect of mineral matter (potassium chloride (KCl)) on kinetics. 
 
A comparison of pyrolysis and combustion kinetics will be planned if possible. 
 
The findings shall be reported in form of a kinetic model.

2 Introduction 
The increasing energy demand has motivated the search for alternative energy sources, and it is especially 
renewable energy sources such as biomass [1]. The belief that the world's resources are infinite is no longer 
widespread. The development of new energy sources cannot keep the pace with the growing consumption 
and demand. Developed countries find it difficult to compensate the energy demand, even after the increase 
in energy production due to growth pressures. 
Currently, there is a need for alternative energy sources that are cheap, renewable and non-polluting. 
Therefore, the emphasis is on renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, heat, hydro, biomass etc. The 
great interest came, however, only when it created political interest. In conjunction, the Kyoto Protocol was 
established in 1997 [2], with the intention to reduce global emissions of greenhouse gases. Several of COP 
(Conference of the Parties) summits, had unfortunately not the best outcome, but managed by subsequent 
COP meetings to establish goals for reducing emissions. During last COP18 meeting in Doha, Qatar in 2012, 
the Kyoto protocol was extended for the second commitment period to the year 2020. 
Denmark already exploited a considerable amount of renewable energy, as well as sources that previously 
were considered waste products delivered now by the Danish Agriculture and its accompanying industries. 
It is everything from straw, wood chips, firewood, biodiesel, biogas from manure and organic waste to 
produce electricity and heat. In 2003, the Danish consumption of biomass was around 55 PJ out of a total 
energy consumption of approximately 830 PJ [3]. Under proper conditions, the development potential is big 
on biomass utilization and biotechnology concepts for the production of bioenergy. 
Biomass is a general term for all the organic matter formed by photosynthesis. The primary drivers for a 
greater share of bioenergy in the national energy profile are regularly two closely connected motives; one 
being the potential reduction in greenhouse emissions and the second towards establishing a more 
sustainable energy system [4]. Bioenergy, as an energy source also has a clear advantage as it can be stored 
and used for regulating power from intermittent sources such as solar and wind renewable energy 
technologies. Biomass has also the ability to be processed into fuels for the automotive industry, aviation 
and heavy industries. However, biomass is not a free resource as the like of wind and solar energies. It 
requires continuous input of fuel to produce, transports and process and eventually biomass end uses in 
agriculture and ecology for i.e. food production may ethically complicate the use of it. Policies should 
therefore be in place to ensure biomass scarcity and sourcing in a more economically, environmentally and 
socially sustainable way.

3 Literature study 
3.1 Background 
 Biomass in power plants 
In the pre-industrial times, Denmark´s energy production was dominated by imported petroleum, but since 
the oil crisis in 1970s, the country has been compelled to rethink its energy portfolio, hence the promotion 
of renewable energy. Incentives were introduced to promote biomass for bioenergy as a renewable energy 
source and as a fossil fuel replacement/alternative.  
Biomass is the largest source of renewable energy in Denmark if you look at all energy purposes. According 
to the Danish Energy Agency's energy statistics, in 2014 approximately 98 PJ biomass was used in Denmark 
for different purposes distributed between 19% straw, 17% wood chips, wood waste 7%, 19% wood and 38% 
wood pellets. A total of 39 co-generation plants (CHP) utilized biomass as fuel in 2015, corresponding to a 
total consumption of about 2.6 million ton biomass [5]. Most recently, the Danish Climate and Energy 
Ministry has initiated “Energy Strategy 2050” which is a huge step towards realizing the government's vision 
of Danish independence of coal, oil and gas, hereby bioenergy most likely will play a key role [6].  
According to the Annual Energy Outlook 2012 by US. Department of Energy (DoE) and Energy Information 
Administration (EIA), bioenergy production will have the highest annual growth (peak 3.3%) from 2010 to 
2035, compared to the other types of energy sources, i.e. petroleum, oil, natural gas, coal and nuclear [7]. 
 
Figure 1: Projected global outlook for wood pellet consumption in 2020 [8] 
 
To compensate for such a significant annual growth, the global wood pellet consumption is expected to 
increase with 60% from 2015 to 2020, as reported by Pöyry Consulting in Figure 1 [8]. 
Currently, Denmark utilizes wood pellets, wood chips and wheat straw as biomass resource. The generated 
bioenergy is predominantly used for combined heat and power (CHP) in power plants. The nation is also 
==PAGE:15==
 
Page 15 of 91 
 
heavily dependent on wood pellet imports of nearly 90% of the annual demand. Hence, prices are predicted 
to increase in the future [4]. 
Now, biomass shares many attributes and properties with conventional coal, however biomass exhibits some 
disadvantageous characteristics, especially related to the high tendency for low temperature oxidation and 
ignition during storage and processing, and the hazard of self-ignition/spontaneous combustion associated 
with it. In addition, coal is much less hydrophilic than biomass making it more suitable for open-air storage 
whereas biomass usually is stored indoors in silos. Indoor storage of biomass in for example silos, needs 
proper ventilation due to biological decomposition and chemical process that might promote low-
temperature oxidation leading to off-gassing (release of combustible gasses i.e. CH4 and CO) and eventually 
ignition. Biomass is friable meaning that dust layer ignition is a major issue during conveying and milling of 
wood pellets, when dust accumulates on hot surface such as lamps and machinery. Also biomass has a higher 
burning rate than coal, thus any ignition flame would propagate much faster [9]. This has been evident as to 
several instances of fires and explosions have occurred during storage, conveying and milling of biomass [10]. 
 
 
 
Figure 2: Loesche hammer mills (MPS) for wood pellet milling in power plants [11]. DONG Energy utilizes Loesche mills for 
wood pellet milling in AVV2 with a capacity of > 300.000 tons wood pellets [12]. 
 
During the lifetime of a pellet or biomass particle under storage, transportation, handling, milling etc. within 
a power plant, it may encounter various atmospheric conditions as humidity level, temperature and oxygen 
concentration. Also, biomass particles come in different sizes so that situations of potential ignition hazard 
vary, necessitating the need for quick laboratory methods for assessing risk of ignition under all conditions 
(handling, storage, milling etc.) where dust layers on hot surfaces become a real hazard. The risk of self-
heating and low-temperature self-ignition, has eventually led to many studies in the field to understand these 
phenomena’s [13]–[17].

 Handling of biomass 
During handling of biomass, issues can emerge associated to dust formation, off gassing, self-heating and 
biohazards, thus decreasing the quality of biomass.  
Relying upon biomass origin, size, shape, composition and moisture content distinctive complications can 
occur amid storage, handling and processing. The most well-known issues are outlined as [18], (see Figure 
3): 
 
a) Self-heating and self-ignition 
b) Off-gassing and oxygen depletion 
c) Dust formation 
d) Biohazards 
 
Self-heating of biomass occurs by either two reasons; 1) chemical oxidation reactions and 2) microbiological 
decomposition. The fresher the biomass and the higher the moisture content, the more prominent is the 
hazard for self-heating and eventually self-ignition/spontaneous combustion [18]. Oxidation reactions 
require oxygen; hence biomass oxidation rate appears to rely upon the age of the biomass and for the most 
part decreases with time as oxygen depletes if in storage. Oxygen depletion is another hazard for plant and 
storage operators.  
There is a lack of literature on the mechanisms behind the low-temperature oxidation reactions in wood 
pellets, meaning that they are yet to be fully understood. It is proposed that it is likely associated with the 
biomass extractives [19]. Heat production because of microbiological decomposition depends to a (huge) 
degree upon the moisture content and the surface area [19]. 
 
 
Figure 3: Fire and explosion factors illustrated as The Fire Triangle and The Explosion Pentagon [19, p. 139]. 
 
==PAGE:17==
 
Page 17 of 91 
 
Moreover, biomass exhibits off-gassing as it discharges CO and CO2 while consuming oxygen in chemical 
oxidation and –microbial processes. Even though CO and CO2 are odorless, CO are otherwise very toxic and 
can be deadly at low concentrations. CO is considered to be dangerous at a concentration as low as 1,500-
2,000 ppm after 1 hour of exposure [20], [21]. Low oxygen concentrations can prompt to suffocation of 
operating personnel handling biomass in storage without proper ventilation. As both CO and CO2 are heavier 
than oxygen, they usually accumulate at the bottom of the storage, posing significant health hazard. Besides, 
biomass contains volatile organic compounds (VOC) as terpenes, terpenoids, ethers, aldehydes and esters 
that may create a health and safety hazard if evaporated and accumulated in high concentrations. 
Beside self-heating and off-gassing, biomass can liberate significant amounts of dust, especially for dry solid 
biomass fuels, increasing the risk of explosion. Biomass dust ignition occurs at the interphase between 
biomass and air, which makes it more flammable then bulk material. Considering, that biomass dust has a 
very large surface area compared to its mass the result could be fatally explosive. Explosive suspensions of 
dust forms at different mass-to-oxygen ratios; depending on biomass type, size, and shape of particles the 
threat level varies. Then, the suspensions can ignite anytime by i.e. friction in mills, hot surfaces or 
electrostatic discharges and can result in a fatal explosion [18]. 
Biohazard mostly refers to the risk of bio-decay of biomass. Depending on the properties of biomass, 
moisture content, composition and temperature, fungal infections can digest biomass and form huge 
colonies commonly known as mold. These colonies produce airborne mycotoxins that can cause irritations 
and allergic reactions along the respiratory system [18]. 
In general, the road for combustible fuels for power generation is filled with ignition sources that can quickly 
evolve dramatically. Special risk areas should be ensured including hammer mills, transport systems, silo 
systems, filter units, cyclones, flue gas cleaning, filters and storage areas/containers of materials with a risk 
of spontaneous combustion. 
Electricity is one of the very dynamic forces in modern society. Therefore, it is essential that the power plants 
have optimal reliability - and therefore understanding low-temperature self-heating and self-ignition of 
woody materials, would provide optimal fire protection to prevent fires and explosions that could cause 
serious consequences.

3.2 Low-temperature ignition of biomass 
 Quality, chemical and physical properties of wood pellets 
In general, the increasing pellet demand by the domestic heating sector, coupled with an insufficient supply 
and availability of raw material, drive the pellet market from local to global context. To compensate for the 
demand, a clear quality and sustainable growth of pellet market is required to harmonize pellet utilization 
[22]. In fact, high quality is required by pellet machinery and boilers/furnaces to achieve high efficiency and 
low emissions. Overall, biomass quality is crucial for thermal utilization and depends on chemical, mechanical 
and physical properties. Some parameters could, however, be related to the origin of raw materials whereas 
others related to manufacturing process and handling [23].  
Most commonly, wood pellets are manufactured from lingo-cellulosic sawdust, wood chips, bark, and planer 
shavings into fine powder. The ground material is further processed by compression and extrusion to produce 
fine cylindrical shaped pellets in the size of 6 to 8 mm in diameter. Some manufacturers might process ground 
material by drying, comminution, densification, cooling and dust removal. During compression, woody 
material can be exposed to temperatures around 100°C, which causes lignin to plasticize allowing it to act as 
glue for cohesion of pellets [24]. The chemical composition of wood pellets is essentially cellulose, 
hemicellulose, lignin and extractives. However, variation between species of raw material, soil conditions, 
age, and raw material composition are reasons for varying pellet compositions, as they are usually 
manufactured from a blend of feedstock. Table 1 lists typical composition of wood pellet feedstock.

 Decomposition of woody materials 
Fire and explosion hazards of wood pellets due to biomass decomposition in storage and during handling are 
very common, with several incidents reported over the years [31]–[33]. They can take place along the whole 
supply chain of wood pellet production, delivery and at the consumer site (power plants). Source of ignition 
ranges from hot surfaces, overheating motors, friction from bearings in mills and conveyer belts. Improper 
cleaning, maintenance and countermeasures of dust and fines accumulation is another source of ignition, 
increasing the risk of fires and dust explosion [18].  
In closed environments, wood pellets have been observed to heat up due to microbial decay and chemical 
degradation. With bulk mass acting as insulation, heat usually accumulates deep inside the bulk while 
maintaining normal surface appearance making it harder to detect self-heating by visual inspection. Microbial 
decay is usually related to moisture content, and can be problematic with high levels increasing microbial 
activity [18].  
Microbiological decay leads to heat release in stored compartments, increasing temperatures. Peak 
temperatures were reported to be up to 80°C depending on the type of biomass [34]. Chemical degradation 
is another factor implicating self-heating. It usually starts to have an influence at about 40°C and at ≥ 50°C 
chemical degradation processes will exceed the biological conditions [34]. With poor heat transfer within the 
bulk mass coupled with insulating properties of biomass, heat accumulates inside the bulk resulting in self-
ignition. Self-heating of wood pellets in storage, depends mainly on ambient temperature, moisture content, 
wood age, size of bulk and density [18].  
 
3.2.2.1 
Self-heating and self-ignition 
Bulky amounts of biomass in storages display a high tendency of decomposition subject to storage conditions, 
leading to heat emissions and toxic gases [35]–[38]. Larsson et al. [39] reported in 2012 a significant 
temperature increase pattern in a 29.1 m diameter wood pellet storage silo. The temperature increase rate 
were recorded at a maximum of 1.7°C/h with a maximum temperature peak of 60°C at the center of the 
pellet pile. Others were also able to discover significant temperature increases due to self-heating in biomass 
storage, with recorded temperatures of 60-70°C for fresh wood [36] where thermophilic fungi actually can 
survive [40]. In some cases, temperatures higher than 70°C were not seen and the risk of self-ignition were 
non-existing. However, energy loss were observed in the biomass [34].  
Kubler et al. [34] investigated self-heating phenomenon on different sawdust and pellets types, in the range 
of 20 – 60°C with micro calorimetry [41]. The investigation showed peak energy production of nearly ~250 
mW/kg after approx. 10 days of storage at 50°C. Beyond that, the released heat decreased and remained at 
==PAGE:21==
 
Page 21 of 91 
 
a lower constant for 74 days. In Sweden, similar constant heat production from wood pellets were found at 
50 – 90°C [42]. The increasing spontaneous fire accidents, has led to more research into this field suggesting 
hazard control [43], [44], i.e. ventilation and air-cooling [45], [46]. However, limited data on self-ignition 
because of self-heating in storages and pellet mills are available in the literature and is needed to be 
investigated. 
Guo et al. [47] investigated recently heat release of stored wood pellets at temperatures of 30 – 50°C with 
TAM (Thermal Activity Monitor) air isothermal calorimeter. She recorded a rapidly increasing heat release 
with a peak of 0.33 mW g-1 at 50°C and 0.04 mW g-1 at 30°C, and slowly decreased with time due to oxygen 
depletion (Figure 4). He concluded that heat release rate from decomposition of stored pellets strongly 
depends on storage temperature and the age of pellets. Heat release rate increased with increasing storage 
temperatures, but was insensitive to pellet moisture content.

3.2.2.2 
Off-gassing from biomass 
Off-gassing is another critical hazard caused by decomposition of biomass and/or wood pellets. An incident 
in the Port of Helsingborg, Sweden 2006 killed one seaman and seriously injured a stevedore [50]. Pellet 
storing and transportation in confined spaces may produce oxygen deficient atmosphere with lethal amounts 
of CO, limiting work access due to life threatening hazards [51].  
An investigation into biomass off-gassing by UBC Biomass and Bioenergy Research Group (BBRG) showed 
that CO, CO2 and CH4 were the major gases emitted from stored wood pellets at different temperatures [52]–
[54], with CO2 concentration being the highest and CH4 the lowest (Figure 7).

3.2.2.3 
Microbial metabolism 
Biological decay of biomass is a complex process involving various bio- and chemical reactions. Microbial 
metabolism is probably one of the critical causes of self-heating and decomposition at low-temperatures 
[61]. Glucose oxidation, for example, is a fundamental exergonic reaction (Eq. 3.1)) that releases 2880 kJ of 
heat energy plus 6 moles of CO2 by oxidizing one mole of glucose. 
 
C6H12O6 + 6O2 →6CO2 + 6H2O 
(3.1)
 
The reaction in equation 3.1 represent the oxidation of glucose and production of heat and water and for 
microorganisms, this process enhances the environmental factors favoring further microbial activities [36]. 
By decomposition of glucose, biomass quality deteriorates resulting in energy losses and potentially into self-
heating. The combination of other heat sources in the biomass bulk coupled with self-heating could lead to 
self-ignition point, erupting fires and explosions. Furthermore, toxic gas release of VOC’s [52]–[54] and 
oxygen depletion [51] from microbial decay complicates health hazards.  
Water content in wood has a critical effect on microbial activities. A study disclosed that the temperature in 
a pile depended on the initial moisture content [36]. The lower the water content, the lower the microbial 
activity. Even after drying of wood, rewetting, can in some cases, revive dormant microbial spores[62].  
Another study by Lehtikangas [44] investigated the storage effect of dry wood pellets, and concluded that 
temperature development, moisture content increase and microbial growth were observed after five months 
of storage. Consequently, the increased activity resulted in quality deterioration of wood pellets especially 
durability. Some scholars presented work linking humidity to accelerating biomass decay by heat release and 
gas emissions, thus accelerating the road to self-ignition [36], [38], [63], [64]

3.2.2.4 
Chemically assisted degradation 
Chemical degradation is another factor implicating self-heating. It usually starts to have an influence at about 
40°C and at ≥ 50°C chemical degradation processes will exceed the biological ones [37]. Chemical degradation 
of woody materials consists of a series of oxidation reactions such as hydrolysis and pyrolysis.  
Hydrolysis is a chemical reaction with the purpose of breaking down long chain-molecules, cellulose, 
hemicellulose and lignin, while consuming water. Cellulose hydrolysis is generally a rate-limited reaction with 
slow conversion. In a study, two types of hemicelluloses reaction rate were observed; 1) a fast hydrolyzing 
rate and 2) slow hydrolyzing rate [65]. The lower degree of polymerization (DP) of hemicelluloses means that 
they decompose much easier than cellulose. The celluloses crystalline structure together with the physical 
protection by hemicellulose and lignin, hydrolytic activity of cellulose is limited [66]. However, the non-
crystalline portion of cellulose, decompose easily by generating glucose which further degrades into i.e. CO2 
[67], [68]. Pyrolysis reactions are more substantial in the high temperature oxygen-deficient regions.  
Degradation of biomass happens when oxygen binds to the biomass surface, this generates heat, solid char, 
and volatile compounds like CH4, CO2, and CO [69], [70]. In Yang et al. [70], they discovered different pyrolysis 
behaviors for three main components (hemicellulose, cellulose and lignin) of biomass. The easiest and fastest 
to be pyrolyzed among the components are hemicelluloses, with a significant reaction rate beyond 220°C. 
On the other hand, pyrolysis of cellulose is very difficult due to its crystalline structure [28]. The pyrolysis of 
lignin is different from the other two compounds due to the aromatic rings within the lignin, that result in an 
extremely wide reaction temperature range of 100- 900°C [70]. Fast pyrolysis is considered as an important 
approach of producing charcoal and biofuel [69], [71]. While slow pyrolysis is the significant cause for 
spontaneous ignition and woody biomass smoldering combustion [63], [72], [73]. In general, pyrolysis is 
considered as a very complex thermochemical process during which biomass goes through a series of 
reactions [74], [75].

3.3 Biomass combustion 
 Principles of combustion 
The three elements a fire requires to ignite are, heat, fuel, and oxygen. In case of one of the three elements 
are removed, burning stops. A fire naturally occurs when all three elements are present and combined in the 
right correct proportion, combustion is self-sustaining, because the fuel releases excess heat to initiate 
further burning [76]. 
In order to obtain complete biomass combustion requires a certain amount of air, and by volume air contains 
about 79% nitrogen and 21% oxygen. Thus, the stoichiometric combustion product of biomass in air, reaction 
Eq. (3.2), will include CO2, H2O, and N2. This reaction is generating heat [77]. 
 
Biomass + Air →Carbon dioxide + Water vapor + Nitrogen + Heat 
(3.2)
The above equation is incomplete because it does not consider a number of factors, such as biomass 
composition may contain many more elements (e.g. heavy metals that significantly influence the 
management of ashes, with respect to its final destination) than those shown. Another factor could be the 
moisture content of the fuel that may be varying, and may even limit the overall combustion process. If the 
moister content is very high, spontaneous reaction of the fuel will not occur, and therefore requires the 
addition of another fuel [78].  The solid fuels combustion take place through a sequence of steps in parallel 
or sequentially, which include essentially, heating and drying, devolatilization (happens in the absence of O2), 
combustion of volatile and combustion of char [79].       
Biomass is difficult to mill due to its fibrous structure. During biomass combustion in industrial power plants, 
the size of particles in the furnaces is normally larger than that of coal particles and their shape is non-
spherical. In addition, surrounding temperature varies within combustors implying that the particles are 
exposed to different heating conditions as they burn within the furnace [80].

 Combustion characteristics of biomass 
3.3.2.1 
Smoldering and flaming combustion   
As an alternative of burn directly cellulose materials (e.g. woody materials), decompose and generate a 
mixture of volatile components, highly reactive char and tarry components under strong heat flux [28]. 
Burning wood materials is governed by a series of oxidation reaction that depend on the oxidation rate and 
the air flow rate. In case of low air flow rates, the gas phase production may be limited or diluted, which 
cannot maintain the gas phase combustion [82]. However, if the oxidation rate is high, the combustion of 
tarry components and flammable volatiles forms flaming combustion. Under certain conditions, the 
oxidation of solid phase forms glowing and smoldering combustion [73]. Throughout this stage, the 
temperature of the solid phase increases, while toxic gases and heat are released from the combustion, this 
event appears in flameless form [83]. The smoldering reaction phenomena is invisible and can develop into 
a rapid spreading combustion when oxygen flow increases or other conditions changes that might increase 
the oxidation rate [48], [49]. The oxygen flow rate is a significant factor for both flame combustion and 
smoldering combustion. Smoldering propagation rate increases under higher oxygen flow rate and the 
development to flaming combustion takes place much faster [83]. 
Because of low permeability of solid wood as the oxidation zone remains close to surface of the solid wood 
[83]. On the contrary, wood pellets have faster oxygen diffusion, meaning greater permeability [46] than 
solids wood. As a suggestion wood pellet might have a bigger chance to smolder with a faster smoldering 
spread rate than wood.      
 
3.3.2.2 
Spontaneous combustion 
Smoldering is considered as one of the most essential phenomena that may follow spontaneous combustion. 
When the solid phase starts charring when the pyrolysis reaction rate is high enough, which generates large 
amount of heat and flammable gases. Spontaneous ignition arises when the smoldering develops into flame 
combustion. Spontaneous ignition of biomass and wood residues occurs due to self-heating (temperature 
increase due to exothermic internal reactions) followed by thermal runaway (self-heating which rapidly 
accelerates to high temperatures) and finally, autoignition. This phenomenon has been covered in numbers 
of studies [63], [72], [84], [85], and the results showed that wood started to pyrolyze and become charred at 
temperatures above 100°C. In case of a pile of stored material is exposed to oxygen environment during the 
current pyrolysis, the smoldering will turn into an overt fire. Smoldering is the slow, low-temperature, 
flameless form of combustion that emits heat but no light. The stating point of this type of combustion is 
undefined. The glowing combustion generates more heat and some light, while flaming combustion is visually 
==PAGE:29==
 
Page 29 of 91 
 
seen under fire. Ignition temperature is defined as the temperature at which light is emitted [86]. Studies 
showed that hay has an ignition temperature point at or close to 200 °C, and the rate of combustion depend 
on the relative humidity and air temperature [84]. According to Yang [63], the ignition temperature of wood 
was around 300°C, and by increasing the density of wood the time to reach ignition could be increased. It 
was also found that the dust ignition from wood pellets occurred at 450°C in ambient conditions with limited 
oxygen supply, tested according to the ASTM E 1491 standard. However, for a stationary sample of the same 
dust and with ample air supply, the ignition temperature was determined to 225°C with respect to the Bureau 
of Mines RI 5624 Standard [87].   
Liodakis [85] showed that retardant, like (NH4)2SO4 (AS) and (NH4)2HPO4 (DAP) reduced considerably the 
ignition properties of the forest species, affecting their combustion time (ignition delay), and fire point 
values1. 
 
3.3.2.3 
Dust combustion and explosion 
Sawdust is a by-product of cutting and bulk wood pellets always contain certain amount of sawdust (wood 
dust). The amount of sawdust increases during handling and transportation due to the breakage of pellets 
[46]. The size of the produced dust from wood pellets ranges from 500 to a few micrometer in diameter [87]. 
The dust could cause a number of health and safety issues during handling. As an example, dust increases 
the resistance to airflow causing unequal temperature distribution [46]. It may become a potential health 
problem when fine dust (< 100μm), from processes such as sanding, become airborne and are inhaled [87], 
[88]. 
The most dangerous hazard is dust explosion which involves fine powders. This is a rapid combustion and 
often seen during the handling and operating of combustible materials such as wood dust, coal, and sulfur 
powders [89]–[91]. This phenomenon is seen when rapid combustion occurs in a confined space, enormous 
overpressure will be generated due to hot release gases, which finally lead to the dust explosion [89]. The 
only way to prevent dust explosion is by controlling the source of dust and oxygen. The dust limit for safe 
operation is provided in the MSDS of the wood pellets [88]. The ignition source is a considerable factor for 
the dust explosion, which could be mechanically generated sparks, lightning, hot surfaces, flames or electric 
currents. Table 3 encompass results for the explosibility of wood pellets dust under different condition using 
Canadian pellets [87], [88]. The results shows that even though the auto-ignition temperature of dust (450°C)

 Self-heating parameters 
Wood pellets have overcame the ones from another kind of waste due to their better characteristics 
compared to biomass from agro-industry or grass [93], [94]. Primarily, wood pellets have less ash content 
and higher value of high heating than most industrial wastes, such as spruce and straw [95]. Miscanthus and 
bioethanol waste [96], residues from beech or grape pomace [94], among others. Normally, the most 
significant factor is the pellet quality and the consumers have increasingly demand for high quality products 
that reflects the current standard requirements. However, depending on the product and its processing, 
pellets might present considerable differences. 
As mentioned earlier, wood materials often decompose and degrade due to biological and chemical reactions 
that may cause hazard by producing heat and gases. The decomposition process is affected by different 
parameters such as, oxygen concentration, moisture content, temperature, and other environmental 
conditions. The self-heating rate is explained by use of the kinetic parameters, activation energy (E), reaction 
enthalpy (ΔHR), and pre-exponential factor (A) that can be experimentally measured for woody materials 
[42], [97], [98]. These parameters vary differently depending on the material and its decomposition 
mechanisms [99]. For the measurement of self-heating rate, two approaches are found in the literature, 
micro scale experiment (calorimetric method) [100]–[102] and lab scale experiment. 
A typical plot for heat release rate as a function of time is given in Figure 8. The heat release rate for sample 
2 from group 1 (fresh pellet B with 8% w.b. moisture content) in a closed ampoule using the TAM air 
isothermal calorimeter at 30, 40 and 50°C. The heat release data shows that the temperature in which the 
test was performed has a significant influence on the heat release rate. The rate of heat release is higher at 
the increased temperature. A maximum was observed for the self-heating release rate of 0.33 mW/g at 50°C. 
To begin with, the rate of heat release reaches a peak value of 0.33 mW/g at 50°C and 0.04 mW/g at 30°C, 
before it slowly decreases. They wondered if the heat release rate decreased with time after the peak was 
reached due to the declining oxygen concentration, and to finish at a constant value after a time period of 
50 hours (1.8 ∙105 s) this corresponds to the heat release rate of the oxygen independent reactions.       
Initially, the heat production from the pellets appears to be at its highest, which may indicate that the 
system was governed by oxygen dependent reactions. However, at the end where the heat production 
decreases and the oxygen is consumed, the heat is mainly released from oxygen independent reactions. 
This observation can be confirmed by Kuang's [53] research, which was about off-gassing emission. They 
witnessed a high reaction rate to begin with that slowly decreased after the initial period, and an 
equilibrium stage at the end where the CO2 and CO concentrations stopped increasing. In addition, they

also observed that the oxygen level was exponentially decreasing, which was similar to the decrease in 
the heat release rate observed from TAM experiments.

As reported by Kuang et al. [52]–[54], the decomposition reaction of wood pellets can be assumed to undergo 
reactions of 1-order with the reaction products; CO2, CO and CH4. The overall reaction is then given as, 
Pellets + O2 →CO2 + CO + CH4 + q 
(3.7)
By assuming that the pellets decomposition reactions can be divided into oxygen independent reactions and 
oxygen dependent reactions. The heat production (q) from the two reactions is expressed as, 
q = qox + qnox 
(3.8)
Where; qox and qnox are the heat released from oxygen dependent reactions (in J) and the heat released from 
oxygen independent reactions (in Jules), respectively.    
The study was conducted in a close ampoule starting with atmospheric air. As the oxygen was used due to 
the reaction, the heat release from oxygen-dependent reactions decreased while the heat released from 
oxygen independent reactions remained unchanged. Later in the processes, there was no or little oxygen left 
in the ampoule since most of the oxygen was consumed by the reactions. The measured heat release could 
have something to do with the heat release from oxygen independent reactions. The release of heat from 
the oxygen independent reactions (qnox) was therefore obtained, as shown in Figure 8.

 TG reaction kinetics 
There are a number of approaches for modelling the complex decomposition process. The simplest is the 
empirical model, which employs global kinetics, where the Arrhenius expression is used to correlate the rates 
of mass loss with temperature. The decomposition process of wood particles can be expressed by the 
following reaction [108], 
Biomass + Oxygen
k1→Volatiles + Char + CO2 + H2O  (Combustion) 
 (R1)
Biomass
k2→Volatiles + Char   (Pyrolysis) 
 (R2)
Both reactions are thermochemical biomass decomposition processes. In combustion process (R1) at low 
temperatures, both pyrolysis and oxidation occur simultaneously, whereas for oxygen-deficient 
atmospheres, only pyrolysis occurs (R2). 
A number of methods in the literature have been developed for deriving kinetic parameters from dynamic 
TG experiments [109]. Generally, the methods can be divided into integral methods such as those of Coats 
and Redfern [110] and Ozawa [111] and differential methods such as those of Kissinger [112], Friedman [113], 
Freeman and Carroll [114] and Borchardt and Daniels [115]. Integral methods and some differential methods 
such as Freeman and Carroll [29] and Borchardt and Daniels [115] are based on a single heating rate 
experiment, which may bring errors into the kinetic parameters for complex reactions. Friedman’s [113] and 
Kissinger’s methods [112] use multi-heating rates. Friedman’s method calculates the kinetic parameters at 
various conversions and then takes the average as the parameters of the overall reaction. The DAEM model 
regresses the activation energy with conversion [116]–[120] instead of taking the average. Kissinger’s 
method derives the activation energy using the peak temperature at which the maximum reaction rate 
occurs and the order of reaction using the shape of the mass loss–time curve [109]. 
There is an extensive literature into kinetic modeling from TG, although the models mentioned are limited in 
their application with the proposed TG configurations in the project (low temperature configurations). Firstly, 
many reports in the literature intends the use of models for high temperature iso- and non-isothermal 
processes, doubting whether these models will be applicable for low-temperature decomposition reactions 
[103], [121]–[124]. Secondly, TG configurations in the project included multiple intermediate isothermal 
stages in each configuration. That further complicates the use of these models reported in the literature. In 
so, a custom model had to be derived.

Verdict from TG results 
The studied biomass materials in TG instrument showed from initial stand point that,  
i. 
All materials underwent water-evaporation in a temperature range of 25°C – 120°C. That is true for 
100% N2, 20% O2 and 80% O2 atmospheres. 
ii. 
Mass loss due to devolatilization were seen from fairly low temperature of around >120°C for 
pyrolysis processes. 
iii. 
Mineral matter was seen to increase mass loss rate, suggesting that calcium and potassium affects 
devolatilization rate. That is true for 100% N2, 20% O2 and 80% O2 atmospheres. 
iv. 
Higher oxygen concentration correlated into higher mass losses. 
v. 
Coal were seen to absorb mass under oxidizing conditions in the low temperature range.  
Mass loss were indeed recorded and observed by TG instrument. However, it is difficult to see whether or 
not the samples would reach critical temperature. Critical temperature is the lowest temperature for which 
self-heating occurs in biomass to a point where ignition happens. That would require isothermal experiments 
to run for many days, before reaching critical temperature. Here, mass loss rates would continue with in 
isothermal setting. Though, that is true to some extent, at some point the available reactive material would 
run out and the decomposition process stops. 
In perspective, Jones et al. investigated in a study low temperature ignition of biomass [10]. An ignition delay 
time was observed as the fuel pyrolyzed, showing the time, it takes a fuel to ignite at isothermal setting. It 
was seen to increase exponentially as the isothermal temperature decreased. An empirical relationship from 
single particle and dust layer tests was derived to estimate ignition delay times for low temperatures 
regardless of fuel type. For example, ignition delay times for fuels held isothermally at 70°C or 150°C were 
predicted to be 423 and 16 days, respectively. Although, he argued that model accuracy and robustness was 
in question.

In Figure 17, a difference between oxidation and pyrolysis at T>190°C, is seen. In comparison, pyrolysis 
process (Condition 2) was seen at T>120°C, suggesting the possibility that oxidation processes in biomass 
occur at higher temperatures compared to pyrolysis. Although, one cannot completely rule out oxidation 
below this temperature. Observation indicates that the net effect of oxidation + pyrolysis (either Condition 1 
or 3) is the same as only pyrolysis (Condition 2). But it's a bit difficult to distinguish between the two. Another 
possible explanation is of course that oxidation occurs at the expense of pyrolysis and at a similar rate. 
 A closer look in the range of 190°C - 210°C, one notices that initial oxidation temperature differs slightly 
between the materials. It was seen at a temperature of ~190°C for wheat straw, beech wood, sunflower husk 
and pine with wet mixed KCl. In comparison, the remaining materials were seen above 190°C and more closer 
to 210°C. This could well in fact be a trend where higher mineral composition in biomass correlates into 
higher mass loss rates in rather lower temperatures (as seen before in Figure 12-15).  
Vamvuka et al. studied the effect of mineral matter on chemical activation of coal and biomass materials 
[128]. Experiments were performed by non-isothermal thermogravimetry, over a temperature range of 25–
850°C, at a heating rate of 10 K min-1 and with material particle sizes of 250 µm. The findings suggested that 
calcium, magnesium, potassium and silicon minerals generally acted as inert materials, slightly inhibiting the 
pyrolysis and combustion rates of the samples between 250°C and 400°C. However, they increased the 
sensitivity of the reactions by; 1) causing decomposition of materials to start at lower temperatures and 2) 
by lowering temperatures at which maximum decomposition rate occur.

5.2 Kinetic modeling of biomass pyrolysis 
A general step-by-step procedure is proposed for determining of the pyrolysis kinetics. 
Step 1: TG curves were modified into dry and ash-free data. 
Step 2: Determination of reactive organic fraction availability as a function of temperature for pyrolysis 
reactions (construction of S-curves with results from Condition 4). 
Step 3: Kinetic modeling of biomass pyrolysis at low temperatures (Condition 2). 
Step 4: Test of kinetic model reproducibility by re-construction of TG-curves. 
 Modeling equations of biomass pyrolysis from TG experiments 
Kinetic parameters are a way of describing the rate of reaction with respect to temperature. Mass loss with 
time (TG data) is used to determine reaction rates, and from these, the kinetic parameters. Kinetic analysis 
of decomposition process involves a reaction model and Arrhenius equation. A model was derived within 
internally discussions with main supervisors at CHEC Research Centre - DTU to analyze TG data. 
In typical high temperature pyrolysis, it is assumed that all organic non-char material is available for reaction. 
However, preliminary data indicated that at low temperature, only a smaller fraction of the dry and ash-free 
biomass was available for pyrolysis reaction. A temperature dependent reactive (and non-reactive) fraction 
is therefore determined. The pyrolysis kinetic is then determined for the reactive fraction only.

6 Conclusion 
Low temperature decomposition kinetics of biomass were studied by TGA instrument in isothermal setting. 
Decomposition of wood particles showed distinctly different decomposition curves depending on 
temperature, mineral composition, atmosphere and thermal configuration but the thermal decomposition 
routes were not fundamentally different, both being continuous and irreversible. 
Based on isothermal TG data, it was concluded that all materials underwent water-evaporation in a 
temperature range of 25°C – 120°C. That was true for 100% N2, 20% O2 and 80% O2 atmospheres. Mass loss 
due to devolatilization were seen from fairly low temperature of around T>120°C for pyrolysis processes. 
Mineral matter (wet mixing) was seen to increase mass loss rate, suggesting that calcium and potassium 
affects devolatilization rate. That was true under 100% N2, 20% O2 and 80% O2 atmospheres. Higher oxygen 
concentration correlated into higher mass losses. Coal were seen to absorb mass under oxidizing conditions 
in the low temperature range. During non-isothermal pyrolysis, with decrease in heating rate a shift in 
conversion curves was observed due to longer exposure time to temperatures with lower heating rates.  
The study further suggested the possibility that oxidation processes in biomass occur at higher temperatures 
compared to pyrolysis, namely at T>190°C. Although, one cannot completely rule out oxidation below this 
temperature. Observation indicated that the net effect of oxidation + pyrolysis (either Condition 1 or 3) was 
the same as only pyrolysis (Condition 2) at T<190°C. But it's a bit difficult to distinguish between the two. 
Another possible explanation is of course that oxidation occurs at the expense of pyrolysis and at a similar 
rate. Nevertheless, future research into low temperature oxidation is recommended for further insight into 
biomass self-heating and self-ignition. 
TG data of Condition 2 were used to determine pyrolysis kinetic using custom isothermal model. The reason 
was due to the way TG configurations were designed. They included multiple intermediate isothermal stages 
in each configuration, which complicated the use of other models reported in the literature. In typical high 
temperature pyrolysis, it is assumed that all organic non-char material is available for reaction. However, 
initial data indicated that at low temperature, only a smaller fraction of the dry and ash-free biomass was 
available for pyrolysis reaction. A temperature dependent reactive (and non-reactive) fraction was therefore 
determined. Pyrolysis kinetic was then determined for the reactive fraction only. 
From 120°C, pine wet mixed with KCl appeared to have the highest reaction rate compared to the rest, while 
increasing slowly towards 230°C. Wood pellet, wheat straw and beech wood appeared to have lowest 
reaction rate initially corresponding into lower activation energies. Although, beech wood and wheat straw 
seemed to increase much more at higher temperatures which was also predicted with higher pre-exponential 
==PAGE:66==
 
Page 66 of 91 
 
factors. On the other hand, wood pellet was predicted to be the least reactive material. The model also 
predicted that the highest reaction rate is to that of beech wood at 230°C compared to the rest. Wheat straw 
follows in suit, whereas sunflower husk appears to peak along with pine with wet mixed KCl. Dry mixing of 
KCl with pine did not appear have any effect on pyrolysis kinetics. 
The importance of pyrolysis activation energy when considering ignition risk, was highlighted. Fuels with 
higher activation energies towards pyrolysis was suggested to be much more stable under slightly elevated 
temperature in isothermal setting. Pine, whose weight loss data relates to low activation energy is suggested 
to present highest ignition risk upon exposure to slightly elevated temperatures. 
Finally, pyrolysis kinetic model were tested for TG curve reproducibility. Good agreement was generally 
observed between slow pyrolysis experiments and model. Although, modeled data (dry and ash-free basis) 
for some materials seemed to be marginally scattered at elevated temperatures between 170°C – 190°C and 
at 230°C. Clearly more data is required to validate the empirical correlation. The proposed model is valid and 
applicable for describing pyrolysis conversion in temperature range of 120-230°C.

7 Future outlook 
For future work, it is recommended to quantify the kinetics of oxidation in the combustion curves (Figure 
12&14). Preliminary discussion were held with supervisors at CHEC Research Centre on the topic. Modeling 
was recognized to be not as simple or straightforward as it seems. Mass loss decomposition in combustion 
processes includes both pyrolysis and oxidation simultaneously, and to model oxidation alone one need to 
exclude pyrolysis-related mass loss, which further complicates kinetic modeling. It would require an 
ingenious way to model oxidation by excluding pyrolysis part and express oxidation alone. 
Some suggestions have been made so far in internal discussions with main supervisors at CHEC Research 
Centre, on possible ways of how to do it. Although, related literature is currently very limited. The proposals 
are: 
 
Proposal 1: Modeling of oxidation kinetics, by modeling global combustion kinetics (Condition 1&3) 
and subtracting that with pyrolysis kinetics (Condition 2). 
 
Proposal 2: Modify combustion TG curves (Condition 1&3) into displaying only oxidation process, by 
subtracting pyrolysis curve (Condition 2) from it. 
 
Proposal 3: Modeling of oxidation with the assumption of that all material is available for oxidation 
from the beginning and throughout the experiment. Though, this proposal introduces uncertainties 
and could prove wrong. Results so far has indicated that both pyrolysis and oxidation occur 
simultaneously. 
Nevertheless, future research into low temperature oxidation is recommended for further insight into 
biomass self-heating and self-ignition.
