Abstract 
Concrete is the most used construction material in the world. The building industry is big and complex with 
multiple projects going simultaneously. It is important to optimize the construction process as well as possible 
in order to minimize the cost and time. One area is the precast industry, where it is crucial to know the prop-
erties of concrete in order to produce the precast elements as efficiently as possible. The setting time of concrete 
is one of the aspects that is important to know e.g. curing and form stripping. This study will examine what 
causes the difference in setting and the nature of the Vicat method for determination of setting will be studied. 
In addition, a comparison of different methods for determination of setting will be investigated. Based on this 
study, the following conclusions can be stated. 
The w/c ratio and temperature have an influence on setting time. In general, there is a good relation between 
manual and automatic Vicat. When it comes to determine the final setting time, a deviation is observed due to 
leaching and redistribution of phases. Furthermore, the mixing intensity shows small indications of difference 
in setting time, but nothing significantly. Regarding storing condition, it is observed that the graphs are differ-
ent around the final setting time due to the characteristics at the penetration surfaces due to storing condition. 
It has been shown that mortar has earlier setting time, and different particle sizes of sand have an influence on 
setting time. It is known that the used sand leaches chemical substances, but it is figured out that this is not the 
main contribution for a shorter setting time for mortar.  
It is observed that the initial setting time are different between Vicat and Proctor. Proctor has an earlier initial 
setting time for all four concrete mixes, while the final setting time for Vicat and Proctor are close to each 
other. Regarding the semi-adiabatic and isothermal calorimetry, it is difficult to verify a relation between the 
calorimetry and penetration methods.

1 Introduction 
Concrete stands as the most utilized construction material globally and will continue to do so for the foresee-
able future. It is therefore vital to optimize all aspects of this product in the construction industry, to save cost 
and accelerate construction speed in an ever-growing world. Time is therefore playing a pivotal role in this 
pursuit. One area in which speed plays a key role is in the precast industry where prefabricated construction 
elements are produced. Here the wet concrete is poured into the formwork which can only be demoulded when 
the concrete has, at least, reached its final setting time. The process of pouring the concrete into the mould can 
subsequently start again. The knowledge of setting time is therefore a crucial aspect of information in order to 
optimize this process. Another example of when the knowledge of setting time is relevant is during the casting 
of walls. In this example, the casting rate is directly tied to the setting time of the concrete, as it is crucial to 
not exceed a certain requirement of hydrostatic pressure, which is put onto the formwork.  
The setting time is split into two categories. The initial setting time is when concrete loses its workability. The 
final setting time is when the concrete begins to develop strength. The means of measuring these parameters 
are different depending on which method is used. A Vicat apparatus uses the penetration depth to estimate the 
initial and final setting time, while a Proctor needle measures the penetration resistance.  
The Vicat apparatus is developed by a bridge engineer, Louis Vicat, who established the right proportions of 
clay and limestone to make artificial cement and invented the Vicat apparatus [1]. The apparatus can be used 
for cement paste, mortar, and concrete. The method is based on observing the penetration of the needle into a 
specimen until it reaches a specified penetration depth. The specified depths indicate when the initial and final 
setting time is reached. 
The idea of Proctor method is to measure the strength development, by measuring the penetration resistance. 
From a specific value, the initial and final setting time can be estimated. This method is developed by Ralph 
Roscoe Proctor, who was a resident engineer who worked for the Los Angels Department of Water and Power. 
The method was used to determine the maximum density of a soil sample along with the optimum moisture 
content and later on, the method was implemented on concrete to determine the setting time [2]. The test is 
done on a mortar sample, which is sieved from fresh concrete, so the max particle size is 8 mm.

1.2 Alternative method 
In this project, the Vicat apparatus will be mainly used for determination of setting time. Other methods such 
as ultrasonic can also be used. In the following, a brief introduction of this method can be seen.  
1.2.1 
Ultrasonic method 
This method is written based on paper [3]. This method is called ultrasonic method, where an ultrasonic (US) 
apparatus, is used. The method is based on measuring the transit time of US pulse through the material. Thereby 
the velocity of the US wave propagation is calculated. Simultaneously with the measurement of US pulse 
transit time, the temperature of the specimen is obtained by using a temperature sensor.  
The setting time of the cement paste is obtained at specific points on the curve for the recorded internal tem-
perature (gray curve) and US pulse velocity (green curve). Some specific points are marked on the temperature 
curve, see Figure 1.1. These are max. and min. temperature, and largest positive and negative inclination. The 
points are then transferred to the US pulse velocity curve vertically, and this is marked by arrows. All men-
tioned points in the curves are shown in Figure 1.2. From the marked points the setting time is obtained by 
searching for intersections of tangent and secant lines as shown in Figure 1.2.

2 Setting time and hydration process 
After each section, the concerned reference of that section will be displayed. The term setting is based on 
solidification of the plastic cement paste, which is a complex process influenced by chemical and physical 
processes. The reaction process between cement and water is the primary reason of setting. [4] 
When dealing with setting, two essential terms are considered. The beginning of solidification is stated as 
initial set, meaning that the paste becomes unworkable. At this stage, operations such as concrete placement 
in formwork and compacting by vibration become difficult. Thus, the initial setting time determines the length 
of time where the cement mixes remain plastic and workable. As time goes on, the paste becomes more solid 
and after considerable time, it reaches its final set where the paste is fully rigid. Those two terms are crucial to 
know for practical importance since the knowledge of them are critical during the construction process. [4] 
2.1 Hydration process of cement 
Setting and hardening of cement are happening through the chemical reactions of cement with water. Thus, 
the chemistry behind this must be investigated to understand these processes. In this part of the report, the 
hydration of the different compounds will be described, namely calcium silicates (C3S and C2S), tricalcium 
aluminate (C3A), and the ferrite phase (C4AF). Firstly the hydration of each compound will be introduced and 
then the hydration of the cement as a whole will be considered. [5] 
2.1.1 
Calcium silicates 
A hydrolysis reaction occurs when the calcium silicates react with water. The products are calcium hydroxide 
and a less basic calcium silicate hydrate meaning that the C/S ratio is now lower compared to the anhydrous 
calcium silicate. From the hydrolysis reaction, a hydrate with the approximate composition of C3S2H3 is 
formed. The reactions for both calcium silicates may be stated as shown in eq. (2.1) and (2.2). [6] 
Reaction of tricalcium silicate: 
2(3CaO · SiO2) + 6H2O →3CaO · 2SiO2 · 3H2O + 3Ca(OH)2 
𝟐𝐂𝟑𝐒+ 𝟔𝐇→𝐂𝟑𝐒𝟐𝐇𝟑+ 𝟑𝐂𝐇 
(2.1) 
Reaction of dicalcium silicate: 
2(2CaO · SiO2) + 4H2O →3CaO · 2SiO2 · 3H2O + Ca(OH)2 
𝟐𝐂𝟐𝐒+ 𝟒𝐇→𝐂𝟑𝐒𝟐𝐇𝟑+ 𝐂𝐇 
(2.2) 
The produced hydrate is poorly crystallized and produces a porous solid, which is defined as a rigid gel. Since 
the chemical composition of the calcium silicate hydrate varies due to age, temperature, and water/solid ratio, 
it is preferable to refer it as “calcium silicate hydrate” (CSH). This notation is more general and does not imply 
any particular composition or structure. [6]   
Due to impurities in the alite and belite, the produced hydrate can have different compositions and properties. 
In general, the products are CSH gel with an average composition of C3S2H3 and Ca(OH)2. The calcium 
silicates make up to around 70% of the cement and therefore the setting of the cement mixes will mainly consist 
of their hydration products. [6] 
Another reaction product is Ca(OH)2, which makes the cement pastes highly alkaline. The alkaline environ-
ment with a high pH value ensures good protection to embedded steel against corrosion. [6]

2.1.2 
Tricalcium aluminate 
Tricalcium aluminate is the most reactive towards water. It is believed that it has a considerable influence on 
the early hydration and rheology of cement. The hydration behavior is like other minerals, namely depending 
on several factors. These are water to solid ratio, temperature, specific surface area of C3A, mixing procedure, 
size of the hydrating sample, and admixtures. [5] 
The presence of water leads to the hydration of C3A which produces hexagonal plate crystals consisting mostly 
of the hydrates C4AH19 and C2AH8. These hydrates are metastable and will later on transform into a less 
soluble and stable cubic hydrate which has the composition C3AH6. The formation of C3AH6 is very fast at 
room temperatures in pastes with low water/solid ratio, and this formation contributes to the heat evolution in 
the early ages. The reaction with water is displayed in eq. (2.3).[6] 
3CaO · Al2O3 + 6H2O →3CaO · Al2O3 · 6H2O 
𝐂𝟑𝐀+ 𝟔𝐇→𝐂𝟑𝐀𝐇𝟔 
(2.3) 
The hydration of C3A is different in cement due to the presence of calcium sulphate(CS · H2), which is added 
to control the setting. The first stable hydration product is needle-like crystals known as ettringite, and it con-
tinues to be formed as long as there is a sufficient amount of calcium sulphate available in the solution. The 
reaction is displayed in eq. (2.4). When there is a lack of calcium sulphate, ettringite becomes unstable and 
converts to platy monosulpho-aluminate as shown in eq. (2.5). [5] 
Step 1: Formation of ettringite: 
𝐂𝟑𝐀+ 𝟑𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟐+ 𝟐𝟔𝐇 →𝐂𝟑𝐀· 𝟑𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟑𝟐 
(2.4) 
Step 2: Formation of monosulpho-aluminate: 
𝐂𝟑𝐀· 𝟑𝐂𝑺· 𝐇𝟑𝟐+ 𝟐𝐂𝟑𝐀+ 𝟒𝐇→𝟑(𝐂𝟑𝐀· 𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟏𝟐) 
(2.5) 
In the absence of gypsum, many cements undergo the phenomenon “flash set”, There is a huge evolution of 
heat with a subsequent poor strength development, and the plasticity cannot be regained. This condition occurs 
due to increased early reaction of the aluminate and ferrite phase, and the formation of plates of monosulphate 
(AFm) phase in the cement paste. [7] 
In the other case, where there is too much presence of gypsum in the form of hemihydrate, the condition is 
called “false set”. There is no high evolution of heat and the plasticity can be regained by further mixing. In 
addition, the strength development is not that much affected. This setting happens by the interlocking of gyp-
sum crystals, which are tabular and can be up to 5-10 mm long. If the amount of hemihydrate is not too great, 
then the crystals redissolve when mixing and the reaction will follow its normal course. [7]  
2.1.3 
Ferrite 
The hydration for the ferrite phase is the same as for C3A, where the first phase is formation of ettringite until 
the gypsum is exhausted, and then it is converted into monosulpho-aluminate. The reaction steps are displayed 
in eq. (2.6) and (2.7). [6] 
Formation of ettringite: 
𝐂𝟒𝐀𝐅+ 𝟑𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟐+ 𝟑𝟎𝐇→𝐂𝟑𝐀· 𝟑𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟑𝟐+ 𝐂𝐇+ 𝐅𝐇𝟑 
(2.6) 
Formation of monosulpho-aluminate: 
𝟐𝐂𝟒𝐀𝐅+ 𝐂𝟑𝐀· 𝟑𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟑𝟐+ 𝟏𝟐𝐇→𝟑(𝐂𝟑𝐀· 𝐂𝐒· 𝐇𝟏𝟐) + 𝟐𝐂𝐇+ 𝟐𝐅𝐇𝟑 
(2.7)

2.2 Structure development 
In the early hydration process, Ca(OH)2 is formed during the hydrolysis reaction of calcium silicates, where a 
dense CSH gel coating is also formed on the cement grains. Ettringite is also formed by the reaction with C3A, 
and the ettringite is coating on the C3A grains.  As a result of coating from CSH gel and ettringite, the hydration 
process is retarded for a period, where the paste remains plastic and workable. This period is called for 
“dormant period”. The dormant period ends and the initial set is reached when the CSH coating breaks up and 
the hydration process continues. [6] 
The hydration products have a volume that is more than twice compared to the anhydrous cement. Thus, the 
hydration products fill in the spaces between the cement grains and form point of contact. This is attributable 
to the stiffening of the cement paste. As the hydration goes on, the concentration of hydration product and 
points of contact increases resulting in the paste becoming rigid. At this stage, the final set is reached. [6] 
 
Figure 2.1: Hydration and structure development. [6] 
In Figure 2.1, a schematic description of the hydration process is displayed, which shows the structure of the 
formed paste. During the dormant period, the cement grains are separated, and the hydration products are 
mainly Ca(OH)2 and ettringite. After around 1 hour, CSH gel is formed, where its particles have a shape of 
long fibers. The increase of the volume from the hydration products causes decreasing of the porosity while 
intergrowing causes stiffening. [6] 
After around 24 hours, the sulphate ions are depleted so that the formation of ettringite stops and the formation 
of monosulphate starts. Alumina and iron oxide containing phases such as 4CaO(Al2O3, Fe2O3) · aq begins to 
form. Furthermore, the hydration of calcium silicates continues, where the CSH particles now have short fibers. 
[6]

2.3 Factors affecting hydration 
Hydration of Portland cement depends on various factors, and the properties of the hardened paste depends to 
some extent on the hydration process. This part will consider the factors that affect the hydration of cement. 
2.3.1 
Cement composition 
The hydration is dependent on the composition of cement since the hydration of each cement constituent varies. 
C3A hydrates very fast and most of its hydration takes place within 24 hours. On the other hand, C2S reacts 
slowly with water, and its hydration takes longer time. In Figure 2.2, the hydration rate of each compound is 
displayed. It can be seen that 63 % of C3A is hydrated after 24 hours whereas the C2S is only at 17 %. Thus, 
the cement composition is crucial in the early stage of hydration. It can be stated that the hydration of the 
cement is determined by the hydration of the individual compounds since the water is in contact with all cement 
constituents, and therefore they are independent of each other. As the hydration goes on, the CSH gel layer is 
formed around the cement grains and it keeps increasing so that the hydration rate becomes dependent on the 
rate of water diffusion through this layer. [6]

2.3.2 
W/C ratio 
Tests show that the hydration degree is related to the w/c - ratio as presented in Figure 2.3, where the degree 
of hydration is displayed as a function of curing time. The degree of hydration is plotted as the weight of 
chemically bound water to the weight of anhydrous cement. It can be seen from the figure, that the different 
w/c - ratios flatten off at different hydration degrees. [6] 
 
Figure 2.3: Effect of w/c - ratio on the degree of hydration as a function of curing time. [10] 
2.3.3 
Temperature 
The rate of hydration is highly dependent on the temperature since a high temperature will accelerate the 
hydration rate, provided that the high temperature does not dry the paste, which in that case can slow the 
hydration rate and can even stop the hydration totally. The acceleration of the hydration rate is displayed in 
Figure 2.4, where the weight of chemically bound water to the weight of anhydrous cement is plotted as a 
function of curing time. It can be seen that this acceleration is maximum at early stages which indicates that 
the setting time is highly dependent on the temperature of the cement paste, but the hydration rate later on is 
not that much influenced by the temperature. This could be explained by the formed CSH gel layer that slows 
down the reaction rate since this layer retards the hydration so that the anhydrous cement cannot reach the 
water. [6]

3.1.5 
Vicat 
A well-known method to determine setting time is called Vicat, where a needle with a cross-section area of 1 
mm2 is used. The needle has a mass of 300 ± 1 g, which corresponds to an applied stress of 2.94 MPa. The 
method is based on observing the penetration of the needle into a specimen until it reaches a specified pene-
tration depth. The specified depths indicate when the initial and final setting time is reached.  
The apparatus can be used for cement paste, mortar, and concrete. For each mix type, there are different re-
quirements according to the DS/EN standards. These requirements are listed in Table 3.4. Note that the con-
crete uses the standard DS/EN 480-2 [13] with adjustments, which are sieving so that the aggregates larger 
than 4 mm are sieved away and using a water bath as described in DS/EN 196-3 [14] for cement paste. It is 
worth mentioning that a water bath is used as a storing condition for all tests in order to perform the tests on 
the same basis.

The used Vicat apparatus has a needle, that is used for the initial set. It has a form of a cylinder, which has an

Manual Vicat test 
The filled mould should be stored according to the standard as described in Table 3.4. After a suitable time, 
the first penetration can be done by lowering the needle gently until it reaches the surface of the specimen and 
then releasing it without adding initial velocity or forced acceleration. Then the scale can be read after waiting 
for 30 seconds or when the needle stops moving. The penetrations are repeated after a suitable time until the 
penetration requirement for initial set is reached. For initial set, the manual Vicat apparatus is displayed in 
Figure 3.8. 
In order to determine final setting time, the mould should be turned over. For cement paste mixes, the needle 
with ring attachment is used, as displayed in Figure 3.9. However, the needle from initial set can be used for 
mortar and concrete mixes. The test is done as before until the penetration requirement from Table 3.4, for 
estimating the final setting time, is reached. For all manual tests, the frequency of penetrations is 15 minutes.

Mixing procedure

3.3.3 
Ball milling and sieving 
As a starting point, quartz sand with a grain size fraction of 2-3 mm is acquired. Subsequently, this sand is 
ground to achieve five particle size fractions, and two additional buckets specified by specific surface areas 
(SSA). Consequently, there will be a total of seven different buckets of quartz sand, specified either by particle 
size fraction or SSA. The sand is ground down using a ball mill. The test is performed by filling the mill with 
quartz sand and porcelain balls as displayed in Figure 3.18. To optimize the grinding process, the balls should 
vary in size. Note that the porcelain balls are made of the same main substance as quartz sand. Thus, there 
should not be a problem regarding releasing chemical substances into the sand.  
The ball mill operates by rotating with an optimum rotation speed for a suitable time, see Figure 3.19. It is 
crucial to ensure that the rotation speed is at its optimal. This can be controlled by listening to the porcelain 
balls hit each other where the sound should be loudest as possible. The grinding happens when two porcelain 
balls hit each other and there is a sand particle in between. It is expected that the speed of the grinding process 
is something similar to an exponential function, meaning that the grinding is faster in the beginning compared 
to later on.

Cold water extraction  
The cold water extraction method will be utilized to investigate the concentration of chemical substances for

mL of the achieved liquid, 18 mL of demineralized water, and 280 μL of 32.5 % HNO3. The plastic tube is 
stored in a refrigerator until the ICP analysis is conducted by the laboratory assistant. Note that the cleaning of 
the used equipment should be done by using weak acid in order to eliminate the residues from the previous

3.4.5 
Penetration resistance method - Proctor 
This method is called penetration resistance test, which is done in accordance with DS 423.17 [22]. The idea 
of this method is to measure the strength development, by measuring the penetration resistance. From a specific 
value, the initial and final setting time can be estimated. The test is done on a mortar sample, which is sieved 
from fresh concrete, so the max particle size is 8 mm.  
The Proctor test is based on a loading apparatus, see Figure 3.30, which is used to provide and read the pressure 
applied on the mortar. The force can be read from 100 N and 600 N with a precision of ± 10 N. The equipment 
used to perform the method is as follows: A sieve with 8 mm. A container to hold the mortar with a minimum 
internal opening of 150 mm and depth of 120 mm. Cylindrical penetration needles with pressure surfaces of 
32, 65, 160, 326, and 651 mm2 are used, and they should be at least 25 mm in height. Note that these surface 
areas are deviating from the standard DS 423.17 [22], where it is stated that the needle should be 50, 100, 200, 
400, and 800 mm2. This is not an issue since the penetration resistance is converted into the applied pressure. 
Furthermore, a vibrating table is also used. Lastly, a temperature measuring device of thermocouple thermom-
eter with a precision of ± 0.1 ℃ is used, and the temperature of the laboratory should be 20 ± 2 ℃.

3.4.6 
Alternative methods for measuring setting time 
Alternative methods for estimating setting time can be used to investigate the relation between the calorimetry 
methods and penetration methods.  
Isothermal calorimetry 
This method is intended to measure the heat of hydration of cement mixes at different ages by isothermal 
conduction calorimetry, and the test is conducted in accordance with DS/EN 196-11 [24]. The principle is to 
keep the sample at a constant temperature and measure the heat flow. There must be one cell with the sample 
of interest and another cell called reference cell, which contains a blank sample that does not evolve heat. The 
used calorimeter for this project contains 8 cells having their own reference cells for each of the sample cells. 
Both sample and reference cells shall have the same heat capacity. 
The heat released by the sample is detected by the sensors into the heat sink. Thereby the voltage output from 
a measurement is obtained by the difference between voltages from the sample cell and reference cell. The 
released heat from the hydrating sample is removed fast enough that it can be assumed for practical purposes 
that the temperature remains constant. The temperature of the apparatus should be at 20 ± 0.2 ℃. The test 
setup is displayed in Figure 3.31. 
 
Figure 3.31: Isothermal calorimetry.

Semi-adiabatic calorimetry 
Throughout the hydration process, the concrete temperature increases due to the heat development. The heat 
development is measured in accordance with DS 423.37 [25] by using the quasi-adiabatic calorimetry. The 
sample is stored in an insulated vessel. Thereby the system has a heat loss to the surroundings, which means 
that the sample temperature will be lower compared to a fully adiabatic system.  
The sample volume is approximately 5 L and the concrete temperature and ambient temperature should be

4 Results, analysis and discussion 
This part of the report introduces the performed tests and thereby shows the obtained results. Based on the 
achieved results, a discussion will be performed. Further, this section is split into three subsections namely 
cement paste, mortar, and concrete.  
4.1 Cement paste 
Several factors might influence the setting time for cement paste, and this will be investigated. Two types of 
cement, Aalborg Solid cement and Aalborg White cement, will be used with a series of tests by using the Vicat 
apparatus. The first test investigates the influence of different w/c ratios, where different Vicat apparatuses 
will be compared in order to investigate the repeatability of the Vicat machines. The laboratory, where the tests 
are conducted is approximately 20 ℃, and by using a climate room, the setting time when the temperature is 
10 ℃ and 30 ℃ will be determined. 
Another aspect is the influence of mixing intensity, where three different mixing methods will be used. Those 
are hand mix, mortar mixer, and paste mixer. Those mixing methods have different mixing speeds, and the 
influence of this will be studied. Finally, alternative storing conditions will be studied, when the cement paste 
is either covered by a plastic foil or stored in a climate room at 20 ℃ with RH ≈95%. 
The following sections introduce the obtained results from the conducted tests for cement paste mixes, and 
thereby the observed result will be discussed.

the results are very similar. On the other hand, the measuring principle regarding final set has some differences 
such as the needle and penetration surface. After reaching the initial set the mould is inverted and a needle 
with a ring attachment is used for the manual measurements while the automatic Vicat continues without any 
further action compared to initial set. These differences in final set lead to that the penetration depths cannot 
be compared directly and therefore another method must be implemented.  
The statement of 0.5 mm into the specimen for final set is something that is used for the manual Vicat test. In 
DS/EN 196-3 [14], it is stated that the automatic Vicat apparatus might be calibrated against the reference 
method. This might include that the determination of final setting time with the automatic Vicat should be 
modified.

4.2 Mortar 
It is known that the setting time of mortar is different from cement paste when using the same w/c ratio and 
cement type. This means that the addition of sand influences the setting time. This will be investigated in this 
chapter, as well as what could be the cause of this difference. A series of tests are performed where different 
particle sizes of sand are used to investigate the influence of particle size on setting time. For all mortar mixes, 
Aalborg White cement is used. 
Pure quartz sand is used to conduct these tests. This is because pure quartz sand is well defined and should not 
influence the setting time by leaching any chemical substances into the mix. Further information about the 
used sand is already introduced under section 3.3.2. A bag of pure quartz sand has been acquired with a coarse 
particle size distribution between 2-4 mm. In order to acquire smaller particle sizes, the quartz sand has been 
ground down from the coarse particle size to smaller particle sizes. The chosen intervals for the particle sizes 
are displayed in Table 4.17. The particle sizes are verified by using a microscope, see Appendix G. The dif-
ferent particle sizes are also investigated by performing a Blaine test to measure the SSA, to get a more precise 
idea of what is used in the mortar mixes. Additionally, CWE is done to estimate which chemical substances is 
leached from the quartz sand. Further, those chemical substances are added to the paste by exposing the mixing 
water to the quartz sand, and the setting time is then measured for the cement paste. 
4.2.1 
Mix design   
The mix design should have the same ratio of cement, water, and sand for all tests. This is done so that all 
other parameters than the particle size of sand are kept constant. When having the smallest particle size the 
specific surface area is significantly larger than for the largest particle size, see Table 4.17. This means when 
adding water to the smallest particle size, the mix will appear drier and less workable, than the mix with the 
largest particle size. On the other hand, for a mix with a high w/c ratio, bleeding is likely to happen when using 
the larger particle size. So the optimal w/c ratio is when the mix is still workable when using the smallest 
particle size and bleeding does not occur for the largest particle size. 
Bleeding is more likely to happen to larger particle sizes than the smaller ones. This is due to the bulk w/c ratio 
in mortar being higher in the larger particle size, compared to the smaller. A series of experiments are per-
formed to determine the bleeding of Aalborg White cement with different w/c ratios. The test is performed by 
mixing cement paste and filling up a container that can be sealed, so no moisture can escape, see Figure 4.19.  
 
Figure 4.19: Cement paste - White cement. Test to estimate the amount of bleeding. Containers filled with cement paste 
at different w/c. 
The weight of the filled and empty container is measured. The containers are kept closed until initial set has 
been reached, where the weight of the bleeding water is measured. It was ensured to measure the weight before 
the cement paste reached the final setting time. This is done to get a result as close as possible to the scenario, 
when the Vicat test is performed. The results are plotted in Figure 4.20.

paste. The addition of sand with the largest particle size might be able to stabilize the specimen and thereby 
bleeding can be avoided.

and the calculation is based on a cylindrical form. This source of error is assessed to be lower compared to 
measuring the height manually since the measurements are small and require carefulness.

The SSA was investigated in different ways. The first two methods take the mean value of the particle size 
intervals. Then assume the shape of the particles. Firstly, a sphere was assumed, and then a cube. The third 
method was measuring the Blaine value according to DS/EN 196-6 [16]. The sphere has a smaller SSA than 
the cube, which is expected because a sphere has the smallest surface to a given volume. Both the sphere and 
cube have much smaller SSA than the Blaine values.  
The reason could be the chosen mean value of the particle size, does not obtain the correct value of the SSA. 
The SSA increases rapidly when decreasing the particle size. A better estimation could have been, the 1/3 
fraction of the particle size interval. This might have given a closer SSA to the Blaine values. For example, 
the interval 125-250 mm should have been 166.7 mm instead of 187.5 mm when calculating the SSA. The 
finer part of a particle size interval has more influence on SSA compared to the coarse part. This uncertainty 
is tried to keep as small as possible by having narrow particle size intervals. The SSA increases a lot when the 
volume is increased, and this is not a linear progression. Solid cement was also investigated where a reference 
value using Blaine is acquired from Aalborg Portland. It can be seen that the measured Blaine value is close 
to what Aalborg Portland has stated. This indicates that the used Blaine apparatus is calibrated correctly.  
The difference between Blaine values and calculated values can also be due to that the Blaine apparatus is 
mainly used for finer particle size. When using the coarse particle sizes, the filter paper becomes the dominat-
ing part for the Blaine test, and this could be the reason why the SSA obtained by the Blaine test is higher 
compared to the calculated methods. For the largest particle size, the measurement took around 4 seconds, and 
if it should agree with the calculated value, then it should only take 0.001 seconds. This is difficult to measure 
by a manual operator. It could be interesting to run the test by only having the filter paper and thereby the 
contribution of the filter paper could be determined.

4.2.3 
Setting time with mixing water of different exposure of sand. 
The addition of different particle sizes in mortar influences the setting time seen from the Vicat results. The 
reason for this phenomenon is still unclear. Thus, a new series of tests will be conducted to further investigate 
this. One option could be that the sand is leaching chemical substances into the mortar mix, and in that way 
influences the setting of the mortar. The first step is to identify which chemical substances are leached from 
the sand. This is considered by performing CWE on the used sand for the mortar mix and then sending the 
solution to an ICP analysis. The ICP results can be seen in Figure 4.27. 
 
 
Figure 4.27: Mortar - White cement - w/c = 0.45. Setting time with mixing water of different exposure of sand.  
Concentration as a function of Blaine value. ICP results after CWE. 
Figure 4.27 shows that the overall tendency of the ions is that the concentration increases as the Blaine value 
of the sand increases. This indicates that the sand leaches more ions to the mortar when it is of finer particle 
size. The concentrations start relatively small and then increase. At 300 m2/kg the concentrations become more 
stable and do not increase as the Blaine values increase. All curves behave as expected with no sudden spikes, 
except the Silicon concentration. Silicon has a sudden spike in concentration, which is much higher than any 
other concentration. This could be an error when performing the ICP. Silicon is poorly soluble in water, and 
the value should not be that high. 
Thus, another series of tests will be performed where pastes with w/c = 0.45 are used. The water used for the 
pastes will be exposed to the different particle sizes of sand beforehand. The procedure is the same as when 
doing the CWE with adjustments. Instead of mixing the sample for 5 min, the sample is now mixed for 1 hour. 
To avoid any addition of heat from the magnetic stirrer, the mixing is done manually every 5 minutes. The 
water is then extracted and by using the mixing water exposed to sand, the Vicat tests are performed to measure 
the setting time.

The results from the semi-adiabatic calorimetry are displayed in Figure 4.36. The concrete temperature in-
creases as the hydration process goes on, in particular at a specific time interval of 9 - 13 hours after water 
addition. It is observed that the temperature is increasing very fast. This might indicate that something is hap-
pening during this time interval. A paper [28] regarding setting time and semi-adiabatic calorimetry is studied. 
It states that the largest curvature indicates the initial setting time while the largest inclination indicates the 
final setting time. 
In Figure 4.36, the average values obtained by Proctor and Vicat test are shown as vertical broken lines. The 
initial setting time is marked close to the largest curvature. Regarding final setting time, it is observed that 
Proctor is close to the maximum inclination while Vicat is marked later and close to the upper largest curvature. 
Thereby, it can be stated that “Vicat - initial” and “Proctor - final” in Figure 4.36 are the ones that best fulfill 
the conditions stated in the paper. 
It is important to mention that the semi-adiabatic calorimetry accelerates the hydration process since the tem-
perature increases during the test. It is known that a high temperature influences setting and hydration rate. 
This might be considered when discussing the achieved results. In addition, the system has a heat exchange 
with the surroundings. Therefore, the obtained temperature might be lower than it should be. This is not inves-
tigated further since the focus is the characteristic of the curve in order to evaluate the setting time.

5 Conclusion 
During the project, several tests are performed, and the following statements are reached. 
The w/c ratio influences the setting time for Aalborg White and Aalborg Solid cement. It has been shown that 
decreasing the w/c ratio results in an earlier setting time and vice versa when increasing the w/c ratio. In 
general, there is a good relation between manual and automatic Vicat. When it comes to determining the final 
setting time, a deviation for the automatic Vicat is observed due to the penetration surface of the specimen. 
This happens as a result of bleeding and leaching on the top surface. Thereby an alternative approach is devel-
oped to determine the final setting time for the automatic Vicat. There is uncertainty when determining the 
final setting time for automatic Vicat caused by the fluctuations of the curves. Regarding temperature, it is 
observed that higher temperature leads to earlier setting times compared to lower temperatures. The activation 
energy based on setting time is obtained. It is close to the stated one from the Arrhenius function which is 
based on strength development. Furthermore, the mixing intensity is investigated. In total 4 different intensities 
are tested for from 60 rpm to 2500 rpm. There were small indications that the setting time was different for the 
different intensities, but nothing significantly larger than the uncertainty in the measurements from the Vicat. 
No apparent pattern could be observed on the setting time when increasing the mixing intensity. Regarding 
storing condition, it is observed that the specimen covered with plastic foil had some issues with the automatic 
Vicat. For the specimen stored in 𝑅𝐻≈95%, the specimen might dry out during the test, but this might be 
minor. For both alternative storing conditions, it is observed that the graphs are different around the final 
setting time due to the characteristics at the penetration surfaces. 
It has been shown that different particle sizes of sand influence the setting time. When decreasing the particle 
size the setting time decreases, until the particle size reaches <0.063 mm, where the setting time becomes 
constant for even smaller particle sizes and larger SSA. The reason for this has also been investigated. It was 
shown, from the ICP analysis, that the used quartz sand leaches chemical substances. Using mixing water, that 
has been exposed to the quartz sand, a paste mix was made and tested for setting time. It was observed that 
there was no significant change in setting time. The results using the exposed water had setting times close to 
the reference paste using normal distilled water. It can then be stated that the influence of leaching from the 
quartz sand did not have a significant influence on the setting time of the mortar. The reason for the phenom-
enon could then be explained by other things. One possibility could be heterogeneous nucleation. 
For concrete mixes, it is observed that the initial setting times are different between Vicat and Proctor. Proctor 
has an earlier initial setting time for all four concrete mixes, while the final setting times for Vicat and Proctor 
are similar to each other. When comparing the graphs, Proctor is capable of producing fewer fluctuating curves 
without much disturbance. There seems to be a higher repeatability of Proctor when a test is repeated. Regard-
ing the semi-adiabatic and isothermal calorimetry, there is no observed relation between the calorimetry and 
penetration methods. It is difficult to estimate the setting time based on the obtained results for the calorimetry 
methods. From the papers [28], [29], it is observed that max curvature and inclination might indicate the setting 
time, but this is not verified in this project.

6 Proposal 
For future investigations for setting of cement-based materials, it would be relevant to consider these sugges-
tions.  
• 
It is known that leaching is happening on the top surface for w/c=0.35. It could be interesting to in-
vestigate the influence of leaching for higher w/c ratio. This could also help when proposing an alter-
native method for estimating the final setting time for the automatic Vicat.  
 
• 
Another aspect is trying to avoid leaching by using a pore solution instead of demineralized water as 
a storing condition. Thereby the effect of leaching on penetration depth and setting time could be 
investigated. 
 
• 
When performing the test for cement paste at different temperatures, the heat development could be 
determined and thereby the activation energy based on strength development could be obtained and 
compared.  
 
• 
Regarding mixing intensity, it might be interesting to investigate the mixing efficiency when mixing 
for 1 min instead of 3 min.  
 
• 
As an alternative storing condition, curing membrane could be investigated since it might not affect 
the penetration surface.  
 
• 
For the test with mortar, it might be interesting to investigate the effect of heterogeneous nucleation. 
This can be done by using a material that has the same physical properties as quartz sand and thereby 
the effect of this phenomenon on setting time can be investigated. 
 
• 
During the test with concrete mixes, the temperature of the specimen for the proctor test could be 
measured several times so that better interpolation could be done. 
 
• 
Furthermore, sieving to a maximum of 4 mm and 8 mm is done for the Vicat and Proctor for the 
concrete mixes. The weight before and after could be noted and a comparison of the weighted and 
calculated mortar fractions could be performed so that the uncertainty of this could be estimated.
