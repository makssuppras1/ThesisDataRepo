Abstract
Over the past decades the importance of renewable energy has soared, and so has the eﬀort in
making better and cheaper solar cells, however, the current state-of-the-art thin-ﬁlm technolo-
gies all rely on rare or toxic elements. The objective of this project is to fabricate the absorbing
p-type material, Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS), with electrodeposition to use in ﬂexible thin-ﬁlm solar
cells, as it consists purely of earth-abundant and environmentally benign materials.
The synthesis of CZTS was done from stacked bilayer precursor (Cu-Zn/Cu-Sn) through elec-
trodeposition-anneal route. The stacked bilayer precursor (Cu-Zn/Cu-Sn) was formed through
sequential electrodeposition, in the order of Cu-Sn/Cu-Zn, onto bulk Mo and Moly foil sub-
strates.
The electrodeposition process was optimized for deposition of a Cu-Zn-Sn (CZT)
thin-ﬁlm that is around 350nm thick and slightly Cu-poor and Zn-rich, when compared to
the stoichiometric CZTS composition of 2Cu:1Zn:1Sn:4S. The morphology and composition of
the as-deposited samples were investigated using XRF and GI-XRD methods. Subsequently,
the stacked CZT percursor were soft annealed at 300◦C for 90min to improve intermixing of
the elements. Then, sulfurization was done at 585◦C for 15min, by adding a sulfur precursor
next to the CZT samples. The signiﬁcance of the soft anneal was investigated by comparison
when the process was omitted, while the morphology and composition of the sulfurized samples
were investigated using XRF, GI-XRD, Raman and SEM methods. The performance of the
CZTS layer was tested by fabrication into a complete solar cell with the structure: Mo/CZTS
(p-type)/CdS (n-type)/i-ZnO/Al:ZnO and measuring the IV curve.
XRD and Raman spectra shows the successful synthesis of thin-ﬁlm CZTS, with the highest
measured eﬃciency of 0.44% on bulk Mo substrates, while no functional cell was observed on
Moly foil substrates.

Chapter 1
Introduction
There is no doubt that human activity is having a signiﬁcant environmental impact on our
planet, causing various problems such as; pollution, deforestation, climate change, and de-
pletion of natural resources. The usage of fossil fuels is the leading cause for many of these
environmental issues, with climate change being the main concern in many parts of the world.
Fossil fuels are responsible for roughly 84.3% of the world’s global primary energy consumption
[1], so while completely stopping the usage of fossil fuels could solve many of these environmen-
tal issues, with such a large dependence on fossil fuels it is not something that can be easily
replaced. To halt consumption of fossil fuels, it would instead require discarding the modern
lifestyle, giving up the wonders of the industrial and electrical age, however, such drastic mea-
sures are not something most people are willing to take. Instead, the solution according to the
UN is ”sustainability” and sustainable development, which means ”development which meets
the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their
own needs”[2]. For this reason, great eﬀort is being put into creating and improving sustain-
able/renewable energy sources, for us to continue with our modern lifestyle without disrupting
the natural balance of things.
There are several energy sources that are classiﬁed as sustainable or renewable such as; solar,
wind, hydro, geothermal, and biomass, however, solar and wind energy are the only ones that
on their own can cover the current and future energy demand [3]. Both of these types of energy
are thus well worth exploring but out of the two, solar energy has the largest potential by far
as it is essentially unlimited.
1.1
Solar energy
Currently, silicon (Si) based solar cells are the leading photovoltaic (PV) technology for com-
mercial usage due to their high eﬃciency at a low cost, with crystalline silicon (c-Si) having a
market share in the range of 90 to 95% of the global market. This is mainly due to silicon be-
ing a very abundant material on earth with low toxicity and a very well-established production
method that has been improved over several decades. Even though c-Si photovoltaic modules
have been the standard for a long time, it seems unlikely to be the ﬁnal PV solution [4], at the
very least not on its own. Thin-ﬁlm PV (TFPV) oﬀers new applications and can also be used
in combination with c-Si to make cheap tandem cells. Thin-ﬁlm refers to a layer of material
ranging from a single monolayer of atoms to several micrometres in thickness. A Thin-ﬁlm solar

cell is thus a solar cell made by deposition of one or more layers of photovoltaic material onto a
substrate, allowing for a solar cell that is thin enough to be bent without causing deformation.
An important parameter when comparing solar cell technologies is the energy payback time
(EPBT). EPBT, as the name suggests, is the amount of time it takes for an energy system to
generate the amount of energy equivalent to the amount it took to produce the system. A solar
cell does not have a ﬁxed EPBT but needs to be calculated for each situation, as it depends on
various parameters such as the module’s geographical position. As an example, a PV system
in Northern Europe would need roughly 1.5 years to generate the amount of electricity needed
to produce it, whereas the same PV system in Southern Europe would need 1 year or less [5].
With an industry-standard lifetime of 25 to 30 years, it would generate between 16 to 30 times
the energy required for its fabrication during its lifetime.
1.1.1
Thin-ﬁlm solar cells
There are several advantages to thin-ﬁlm solar cells, such as having a lower EPBT than Si solar
cells [6] and the amount of material required is much less compared to the conventional c-Si
modules. For thin-ﬁlm solar cells only a thickness of roughly one micrometre is required for
suﬃcient absorption of light, hence the name ”thin-ﬁlm”, which is what makes it possible to
fabricate an eﬃcient solar cell that is suﬃciently thin to be bent without causing deformation.
The thinness, lightness and ﬂexibility of TFPV allow for diﬀerent applications such as more
sophisticated building-integrated installations, and solar panels that can be rolled out without
needing a mounting rack. Another advantage of TFPV is the thermal properties, except for
thin-ﬁlm c-Si, all thin-ﬁlm solar technologies exhibit a lower temperature coeﬃcient than c-Si
[7], thus less eﬃciency is lost at higher operating temperatures. Besides using TFPV for new
applications, using TF in combination with c-Si for tandem cells can allow for cheaper or more
eﬃcient solar cells.
The reason materials used for thin-ﬁlm solar cells only need roughly one micron to absorb
almost all the light, is because they have direct band gaps. For a direct band gap material,
photons can be directly absorbed as long as they have suﬃcient energy, however, for indirect
band gap materials like c-Si the absorption also requires a change in momentum, either by
absorption or emission of phonons. As the reaction involves both photons and phonons it is
a second-order reaction, which is much less likely to occur, thus material with in-direct band
gaps need to be much thicker to achieve the same level of absorption.
Currently, there are three main thin-ﬁlm PV technologies; CdTe, CuInxGa1−xS(Se)2 (CIGS),
and amorphous silicon (a-Si), each with their problems. Si thin-ﬁlm solar cells are relatively
underdeveloped due to low eﬃciency and instability from the Staebler-Wronski eﬀect, while
thin-ﬁlm technologies based on CdTe and CIGS are problematic as they rely on rare elements,
respectively tellurium and indium. Rare elements have a limited supply and thus widely ﬂuc-
tuating prices, making the technology both expensive while also limiting the market potential.
Additionally, for CdTe, many places have restrictions on the usage of heavy metals such as
cadmium (Cd).
As such in recent years there has been a great focus on the development
of thin-ﬁlm photovoltaic cells using inexpensive, earth-abundant, and environmentally benign
materials such as Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) which material properties are quite similar to CIGS.
The basic structure of the thin-ﬁlm solar cell fabricated in this thesis is illustrated in Fig. 1.1.
The cell consists of several diﬀerent layers/components: (i) Mo as the back contact, (ii) CZTS

1.2
CZTS
Simply being made of abundant and non-toxic elements is not enough in itself to make for a
good solar cell, as the most important part is how well it performs as a solar cell. What makes
CZTS so promising is exactly its optical and optoelectrical properties and high resemblance to
CIGS. For the optical properties, CZTS has an absorption coeﬃcient of 104cm−1 [8], meaning
that 99% of the light is absorbed within 1µm. Additionally, it is a direct band-gap material with
a band gap of 1.45−1.51 eV, which is very close to the optimal value for a single-junction solar
cell as calculated by Shockley-Queisser to be 1.34 eV [9], typically referred to as the SQ-limit.
At a band gap of 1.45−1.51 eV, the theoretical upper limit of eﬃciency for a solar cell is around
32 to 33%, while the theoretical maximum at 1.34eV has an eﬃciency of 33.7%. Besides the
optical properties, a good p-type absorber also needs to have good band alignment as well as
high carrier mobility and lifetime, it is expected that these properties can be tuned accordingly
for CZTS due to its high resemblance to CIGS. Coming together this could potentially allow
for a solar cell that is cheap, environmentally benign and with a performance comparable to
the current leading thin-ﬁlm technologies.
The importance of features such as earth abundance and environmentally benign might seem
trivial compared to high price eﬃciency, making as much sustainable energy as cheaply as

and in combination with agriculture, the risk of leakage of heavy metals is a natural concern.
1.2.1
State-of-the-art
Throughout this thesis CZTS is referring speciﬁcally to the quaternary compound Cu2ZnSnS4,
however, in literature, CZTS is also used interchangeably with Cu2ZnSnSe4 (CZTSe) and
Cu2ZnSn(S,Se)4 (CZTSSe). The current eﬃciency record for each type is 11% for CZTS de-
posited using sputtering [12], 12.6% for CZTSSe fabricated through the hydrazine-mediated
route [13], and 11.6% for CZTSe fabricated by evaporation [14]. The addition of Se is not
without advantages, as can be seen from the current record eﬃciencies, one of them being
the band gap of CZTSSe can be tuned as the presence of Se lowers the band gap, with pure
CZTSe having a band gap around 1 eV [18]. Even though regular CZTS without Se holds the
lowest record amongst them, it is the one I consider to be the most promising, as the addi-
tion of Se in CZTS works contrary to the main advantage of CZTS, namely only consisting of
earth-abundant elements and being environmentally benign, as Se is toxic and much rarer than
sulfur.

In this project, the CZTS is fabricated through an electrodeposition-anneal process. A Cu-Zn-
Sn (CZT) metallic precursor is deposited using electrodeposition followed by a high-temperature
sulfurization process transforming the CZT into CZTS. Many other ways to fabricate CZTS

Currently, the record eﬃciency for CZTS is 11%, achieved by Yan et al. in 2018 [12]. The
fabrication process used was a co-sputter deposition of Cu/SnS/ZnS precursors followed by a
high-temperature sulfurization process. Then chemical bath deposition (CBD) of CdS on top of
the CZTS absorber layer, immediately followed by heat treatment of the heterojunction. The
current record for CZTS using electrodeposition is 7.4% using simultaneous electrodeposition,
with a conﬁguration of Mo/CZTS/CdS/ZnO/AZO [16].

1.2.3
Defects and secondary phases
From the literature, it is known that CZTS ﬁlms will have higher eﬃciency when the compo-
sition is Cu-poor (Cu/(Zn+Sn)< 2) and Zn-rich (Zn/Sn> 1) compared to the stoichiometric
ﬁlm while maintaining kesterite structure [20]. To achieve high eﬃciency, it is thus important
to have the right type of defects while avoiding other types of defects and secondary phases.
The CuZn antisite has the lowest formation energy and is always the dominant defect in the
stable chemical region. With its high population, it can produce a signiﬁcant hole concentra-
tion, determining the intrinsic p-type conductivity of CZTS [21]. To avoid secondary phases,
accurate control over the composition is required. The CZTS composition needs to ﬁt into a
very narrow phase region that is typically visualized using a ternary phase diagram, where the
sulfur concentration is kept constant, as shown in Fig. 1.4.

1.3. ELECTRODEPOSITION
How a secondary phase will inﬂuence the performance of the CZTS absorber layer diﬀers
depending on which secondary phase, but they all impact the performance negatively, typically
either by acting as an insulator and reducing the devices active area or by aﬀecting the carrier
collection eﬃciency, thus these secondary phases are to be avoided.
1.3
Electrodeposition
In this project, the CZT precursor is fabricated through electrodeposition, a manufacturing
method that is capable of large-scale production at a low cost, which is of great importance for
the future, as otherwise, CZTS based solar cells using electrodeposition would not be viable for
commercial application. The ideal fabrication process of a cheap and environmentally-friendly
solar cell should also be cheap and environmentally friendly, therefore throughout this thesis, the
use of toxic chemicals such as cyanide has been avoided as much as possible. Electrodeposition
is a widespread method for the fabrication of coatings, mostly consisting of metal or metal
alloys but not exclusively. There are several advantages to electrodeposition from a commercial
viewpoint: (i) it can be conducted in ambient conditions, (ii) it uses relatively small amounts
of energy, and (iii) it is highly eﬃcient in terms of material utilisation, making it a very cost-
eﬃcient fabrication method.[23]
Generally, electrodeposition-based processes for fabrication of CZTS can be separated into two
categories: (i) Single-step co-electrodeposition of Cu, Zn, Sn, and S directly to form the CZTS
compound and (ii) a two-steps process where Cu, Zn, and Sn is electrodeposited as metal
precursors followed by a sulfurization process. In this project, the latter was used which also
can be categorized into two diﬀerent methods: (i) simultaneous electrodeposition of Cu-Zn-Sn
from a single electrolyte and (ii) sequential electrodeposition of Cu-Zn-Sn from diﬀerent elec-
trolytes. Due to the large reduction potential gaps between the metal ions, it is very diﬃcult
to obtain good Cu-Zn-Sn precursors using simultaneous electrodeposition, therefore sequen-
tial electrodeposition is preferred by many researchers. For the sequential electrodeposition
method, variations can also be made for the stacking order such as Cu/Zn/Sn and Zn/Sn/Cu
or in combination with alloy deposition such as Zn/Cu-Sn and Sn/Cu-Zn. For this thesis, the
sequential method was chosen with a speciﬁc focus on stacking brass (CuZn) on bronze (CuSn)
or vice-versa (Cu-Zn/Cu-Sn or Cu-Sn/Cu-Zn).
The stacking of CuZn and CuSn was chosen as not much investigation into this combination
has been made, for fabrication of CZTS, while electrodeposition of brass and bronze, in general,
have been studied greatly. However, plating of brass and bronze are both typically done using
cyanide baths, and no non-cyanide bath been commercially operated with much success [24][25].
The fabrication of CZTS from stacked Cu-Sn/Cu-Zn metallic precursor, as well as the method
used and developed for that purpose over the course of this thesis, is part of new research. The
method developed in itself is therefore also a part of the results, along with the results achieved
using the method.

Electrodeposition, also known as electroplating or electrochemical deposition (ECD), is an
electrochemical process similar to chemical plating. The main diﬀerence being electrochemical
deposition requires an external source of electrons, while chemical plating does not. For this
reason, chemical plating is typically referred to as electroless plating. The term plating can
be used for both electroplating and electroless plating, but throughout this project all plating
done is and will refer to electroplating.
The overall setup for ECD consists of four components: A power source, two electrodes con-
nected to the said power source (the anode and cathode), and an electrolyte. An electrolyte
is a substrate that will produce an electrically conducting solution when dissolved in a polar
solvent such as water, however, the term is often also used for the conducting solution, which
at times is also referred to as the ”bath”. Depending on the metal that is to be plated, the
electrolyte needs to fulﬁl a set of speciﬁc requirements [26]:
- The electrolyte must be a suﬃciently good electrical conductor to get a homogeneous
distribution of the deposited metal as well as to avoid excessive heating of the electrolyte.
- A suitable salt of the metal must be selected to dissolve in the electrolyte, in a suﬃcient
amount and at an appropriate temperature.
- The concentration of complexing agents in the electrolyte, as well as its pH-value, must
be kept within a certain range, for the reduction of metal to occur before the evolution
of hydrogen.
These requirements cannot be met for some metals when using a water-based electrolyte. Some
elements like titanium can only be plated using an organic electrolyte, while others such as
magnesium require the use of a molten salt electrolyte at 700◦C or higher. Materials with such
requirements are generally avoided, as they are much harder and more expensive to use. Metals
that can be plated using an aqueous solution is categorized into three groups: (i) electroplatable
metals, (ii) electroless-platable metals, and (iii) metals that can only be codeposited together
with a metal from the previous two categories [26]. The metals that can be reduced chemically
(electroless plating) can also be plated using electroplating.

2.2
Electrodeposition of alloys
The electrodeposition of alloys is a lot more complicated than the electrodeposition of a single
metal. The main reason for this is because all the metals in the alloy are reduced at the same
potential, even though the metals will have diﬀerent standard electrode potentials. Therefore,
the more metals are involved in the alloy plating, and the greater the diﬀerence between their
potentials, the more complex the deposition becomes. The standard electrode potential in-
ﬂuences how the metals deposits in relation to each other, however, many other factors also
inﬂuence the deposition and special phenomenons can occur, which is why alloy plating is
categorised into ﬁve systems.
2.2.1
Plating systems
The ﬁve types of alloy plating systems are:[29]
1. Regular: The plating process is controlled by diﬀusion.
2. Irregular: The plating process is controlled by the cathode potential.
3. Equilibrium: The metals composing the alloy are in chemical equilibrium with the bath.
4. Anomalous: The less noble metal deposits preferentially.
5. Induced: Elements that cannot be deposited by themselves, only through codeposition
with another metal.
The ﬁrst three systems; regular, irregular, and equilibrium are referred to as normal codepo-
sition. The last two systems; anomalous and induced are collectively referred to as abnormal
codeposition. For the regular and irregular types, the ratio between the more noble metal and

less noble metal is higher in the deposited alloy than in the bath, this is referred to as prefer-
ential deposition. The deposition of CuZn done as part of this thesis falls under the irregular
plating system, however, it behaves quite similarly to the regular alloy system within the scope
of this project. The deposition of CuSn done as part of this thesis falls under the regular plating
system. The main distinguishing characteristics for each of the systems are listed below.
Regular
Regular codeposition is characterized by the deposition being controlled by diﬀusion. To deposit
both the more and less noble metals, the noblest metal would only be present in small amounts,
so when applying a relatively high current density the limiting current for the noblest metal
will be exceeded, the potential changes and the less noble metal start depositing.
Irregular
Irregular codeposition is characterized by being controlled to a greater extent by the potentials
of the metals against the solution rather than diﬀusion. Plating variables also tend to have
a smaller impact on composition than for regular deposition and the eﬀect of some variables
on the composition can act contrary to diﬀusion theory. An example of irregular plating can
be achieved through the use of a complexing agent in the electrolyte. The agent forms strong
complexes with the noblest metal and changes its potential to be closer to that of the less noble
metal, if the change in potential is great enough then it becomes an irregular plating process.
Equilibrium
The equilibrium plating system is characterized by the metals in the solution being in chemical
equilibrium. It is a very unique system as the ratio of the metals deposited using a low current
density will be the same as the ratio of the metals in the bath.
Anomalous
Anomalous codeposition is quite rare and occurs only under speciﬁc conditions, concentrations,
and operating variables. It is characterized by having reverse preferential deposition, as in the
less noble metal deposits preferentially. Deposition of ZnNi alloy is known to exhibit anomalous
deposition
Induced
Induced codeposition is characterized by the deposition of alloys containing metals that cannot
be deposited alone, namely the elements with a light grey background in Fig.
2.1.
These
metals that cannot deposit by themselves are referred to as reluctant metals, while the metal
used together with the reluctant metal, in order to stimulate the deposition, is referred to as
the inducing metal. The eﬀect of plating variables on the composition for induced codeposition
is much more unpredictable than for the other plating systems.

2.2.2
Plating variables
The composition of an electrodeposited alloy depends on a great number of variables, with the
main variables generally separated into three groups; bath composition, bath operation, and
miscellaneous. Adapted from [30] the main variables are:
A. Variables of bath composition
1. Concentrations of depositable metals
a. Ratio of the concentrations of the depositable metals to each other
b. Total concentration of the depositable metal
2. Concentration of complexing agents
3. pH of plating bath
4. Presence of addition agents (typically referred to as additives)
5. Presence of indiﬀerent electrolytes or conducting salts
B. Variables of bath operation
1. Current density
2. Temperature
3. Agitation of bath or movement of cathode
C. Miscellaneous variables
1. Cathode current eﬃciency
2. Shape of cathode (composition dispersion)
3. Basis metal
4. Thickness of deposition
5. Type of current
Throughout this thesis, only the most inﬂuential and easily adjustable variables were adjusted,
speciﬁcally: The concentration of depositable metals and complexing agents, current density,
agitation, and the shape of the cathode. The eﬀect and relation of these variables will be brieﬂy
explained in the following subsections, with a focus on their eﬀect for regular and irregular
codeposition as is used in this project.
Concentrations of depositable metals and complexing agents
The most important relation for the deposited composition with alloy plating is the ratio of
depositable metals. Plotting the alloy composition over the bath composition, the curves for
each type of plating system is quite distinct. For regular codeposition the ratio of the metals
in the deposit changes very fast with change in the metal ratio of the bath, as an example; a
codeposition of copper-bismuth will change from 0 to 100% copper as the metal percentage of
copper in the bath changes from 0 to 20%.[30] All other types of codeposition have a less steep
curve. A general feature of codeposition is that deposition of purely the more noble metal can

==PAGE:22==

occur without the bath purely consisting of said noble metal, as in the example before, with
codeposition of copper-bismuth, this indicates that the bath must contain at least 80% bismuth
before it will co-deposit with copper at all. This is referred to as limiting bath composition.
For anomalous codeposition, the relation is less consistent but still vary apparent. Increasing
or decreasing the total amount of metal in the bath can also aﬀect the ally composition. For
regular codeposition, an increase in total metal content will result in an increase of the more
noble metal in the deposit, however, for irregular and anomalous it has very little inﬂuence.
After the metal ratio in the bath, the concentration of a complexing agent in the bath has
the largest impact on the alloy composition, however, it usually only has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on
”mixed” type plating baths. Alloy plating baths containing complex ions are separated into
two groups; ”single” and ”mixed”. In a ”single” complex bath the complex ions of all the
parent metals are formed from a single complexing agent, thus any change in concentration
would aﬀect all the metals similarly.
In a ”mixed” complex bath the complex ions of the
parent metals are formed from diﬀerent complexing agents, so a change in concentration of
the complexing agent would only aﬀect a speciﬁc metal. The general eﬀect when increasing
the concentration of a complexing agent is decreasing the deposition potential of the metal the
agent works on (typically the more noble metal), decreasing its preferential deposition thus
lowering the metal content in the deposit.
Current density
Current density is the most important variable among the operating variables and the underly-
ing mechanism can be viewed from two diﬀerent angles; diﬀusion control and cathode potential.
Considering the cathodic potential, then with an increase in current density the cathode po-
tential becomes more negative, making the plating conditions approach that of the less noble
metal, therefore this increases the ratio of the less noble metal in the alloy [31]. According
to diﬀusion theory, the deposition rate of any metal has an upper limit, determined by how
fast the ions can move through the cathodic diﬀusion layer. At any given current density the
deposition rate of the more noble metal will be closer to its limiting value than the less noble
metal, therefore an increase in the current density will increase the deposition rate of the less
noble metal by a greater amount. For regular codeposition, it can thus be said that an increase
in the current density will decrease the ratio of the more noble metal in the alloy, however, for
other types of codeposition this is not necessarily true. Some plating systems will show the
opposite relation, while others might exhibit a minimum or maximum deposition rate as the
current density is varied, but the change in composition from a variation of the current density
is only somewhat large for regular codeposition.
Agitation
Agitation has its pros and cons, plating without agitation can make for highly reproducible
depositions purely dependent on diﬀusion, however, for a heated bath getting a uniform tem-
perature across the bath without agitation is almost impossible. Purely diﬀusion controlled
plating is also much slower, which would usually be considered a downside but for deposition
of very thin layers it could be an advantage. The bigger issue for non heated plating is changes
in the solution across the cathode due to diﬀerent densities of the substrates. As an example,
in a still solution, the denser solution formed by dissolution of the anode will sink, while the

less dense depleted solution from the cathode will rise towards the surface, causing a diﬀerence
in the composition of the solution of occur. Such a diﬀerence will inﬂuence the deposition and
result in an uneven alloy composition, as the local concentrations of depositable metals will
diﬀer, making some agitation a necessity in most plating system. On the other hand, agitation
can result in diﬀerent ﬂow conditions that may result in local diﬀerences, also resulting in un-
even alloy compositions. It is generally true that as agitation is increased so does the amount of
metal in the deposit, for the metal with the lowest concentration in the bath. As such agitation
was kept to a minimum, while still preventing the solution to stratify, since increased deposition
of the metal with the lowest concentration was never desired throughout this thesis.
Shape of cathode
Typically a uniform alloy composition across the surface of the plated object is desired, to
achieve that the surface of the target must be homogeneous.
For example, an irregularly
shaped object will experience diﬀerent current densities across its surface, resulting in a non-
uniform composition.
The shape of a cathode can also be the cause of local variations in
agitation, temperature, and bath composition all of which may inﬂuence the composition.
Current density is usually the main cause behind non-uniformity due to the shape of the
cathode. The variation of cathode shape used throughout this thesis is quite minimal, with
all targets being homogeneous with no irregular shapes, all targets were more or less square or
rectangular with some variation in sizes. Therefore the inﬂuence of the cathode shape on the
alloy was quite consistent across all samples.
2.3
Material distribution
Even though the cathode might not have an irregular shape, the shape and size can still
inﬂuence the current density and in doing so alter the material distribution. For electrodes
with very high conductivity, it can be assumed that the charge will be distributed across the
surface of the electrodes instantly, resulting in the potential between anode and cathode being
the same everywhere. The current density at a speciﬁc location will therefore be a function of
the distance to the opposing electrode and this eﬀect is called the primary current distribution.
The distance between electrodes, their shape, size, and surroundings can thus inﬂuence the
material distribution, as shown in Fig. 2.3. The ﬁrst (left) example in the ﬁgure is the ideal
scenario, with an even current distribution across the cathode. In the second (middle) example,
due to the smaller size of the cathode, the outer part of the anode will act on the cathode edges,
resulting in a higher current density on the edges, and especially at corners. The high current
density increases the deposition rate, leading to a thicker layer which in turn then draws current
density away from the centre, resulting in an uneven material distribution with less in the centre
and more along the edges and on the corners. The third (right) example is a ”Hull-cell”, a
specially designed cell used for investigating the alloy thickness and composition as a function
of current density. Throughout this thesis a variety of cathodes have been used, however, they
all fall under the second example. Current distribution is a lot more complicated than what
is expected from the primary distribution, as the contribution due to overvoltages can be very
important depending on the electrolyte, however, that is outside the scope of this thesis.

2.4
Characterization of plated materials
Throughout this thesis, a combination of X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) and Scanning Electron
Microscopy (SEM) with Energy Dispersive X-Ray Analysis (EDX) have been used for the
characterization of the alloy composition. XRF was used as the main characterization method as
it is fast and was readily available, while SEM/EDX was only used occasionally as a supporting
characterization method. All SEM/EDX measurements were made by supervisor Sara Engberg,
as training for the equipment was very limited due to lockdown restrictions.
2.4.1
XRF
XRF is a non-destructive characterization method used for determining the composition of a
sample. The sample is exposed to a focused beam of high energy X-rays. If the X-ray has
suﬃcient energy when it collides with an atom it can be absorbed, exciting an electron into a
higher orbital, leaving the atom unstable. To regain stability an electron in the higher orbitals
will jump down to the vacancy left by the previous electron, and in doing so energy is released
in the form of a photon. The energy of the photon is the diﬀerence between orbitals, which
is very distinct for each element and is used to identify which elements are present in the
sample. By measuring how many counts of each photon energy is detected the composition
can be accurately determined, with some exceptions as there are elements with very similar
signatures, such as sulfur and molybdenum, which is why this method can not be used to
determine the sulfur content of a CZTS ﬁlm deposited on a Mo substrate. It is also possible
for the XRF to calculate the layer thickness, by setting up an application informing the system
which components are expected wherein the sample, it uses that together with the peak signals
to calculate the layer thickness.
2.4.2
SEM/EDX
SEM/EDX is another non-destructive characterization method, which combines the high-
resolution imaging of SEM with compositional analysis of EDX. SEM works by scanning a
focused beam of electrons across the surface of the sample and detecting the signal from sec-
ondary and backscattered electrons, providing information regarding the surface topography.

Chapter 3
Conversion of precursors into CZTS
As mentioned previously, the fabrication of CZTS in this thesis was achieved through a two-
steps process. Step 1: Electrodeposition of Cu, Zn, and Sn as metal precursors, with deposition
of CuZn and CuSn alloy speciﬁcally. Step 2: A sulfurization process to convert the metal
precursors into CZTS.
3.1
Phase formation
Depending on the operation conditions, the metal precursors will form diﬀerent phases already
during electrodeposition, additionally, alloy formation between the layers also takes place from
the moment the electrodeposition begins and continues afterwards at room temperature. The
heat treatment as part of the sulfurization process further accelerates the diﬀusion and can
lead to phase transitions depending on the temperature and relative stability of the phases. It
is possible to include a heat treatment before sulfurization to get a more intimately mixed and
homogeneous precursor, which may also reduce the evaporation of the more volatile elements
by the formation of more stable phases, this kind of heat treatment is often referred to as
a soft bake or soft-anneal. Furthermore, a soft bake of the metal precursors can reduce the
sulfurization time required and signiﬁcantly reduce element loss [32]. A disadvantage is the risk
of lateral segregation of the elements into islands of stable phases, resulting in a non-uniform
composition at small length scales [33]. The phases of the metallic precursors are of importance
as the reaction pathway to CZTS depends on them.
3.1.1
Binary alloy systems
The phase diagram for Cu-Zn and Cu-Sn is shown in Fig. 3.1. As can be seen from the phase
diagrams, the phases have more or less vertical regions of stability at room temperature for
both Cu-Zn and Cu-Sn. For Cu-Zn alloy plating a wt% of roughly 55 Zn was the target, thus a
mixture of β’- and γ-phases are expected. At room temperature Cu-Sn is expected to form the
hexagonal η-Cu6Sn5 phase, additionally with a wt% of roughly 70 Sn, it is expected to occur
alongside β-Sn (tetragonal) [33].

3.2
Sulfurisation
Sulfurization refers to a process in which the metal precursors are loaded into a furnace, with
sulfur being introduced into the system one way or another. In this thesis, sulfur was introduced
by loading solid sulfur into the reaction chamber along with the precursors. As the furnace
is heated the solid sulfur evaporates and will react with the metal precursors forming binary
and ternary phases. Once the metal sulﬁdes have been formed, the crystallization of the CZTS
phase occurs rapidly at high temperature, however, simply having the correct composition and
forming CZTS phases is not enough to create eﬃcient devices, as the morphology of the CZTS
absorber layer is also crucial to its performance. General the most important features are the
grain size, surface smoothness, and physical contact with the substrate. A relatively smooth
surface to avoid shorting from pinholes, and good physical contact to achieve good electronic
contact. Grain size is highly inﬂuenced by the process variables during the sulfurization, mak-
ing it possible to tune the process to form larger grains, which is important as larger grains are
good for carrier transport and reduces carrier recombination. On the other hand poorly cho-
sen sulfurization conditions can result in secondary phases either by preventing the formation

reactions to reach completion, thus getting stuck in the transient phases, or due to evaporation
altering the stoichiometry.
3.2.1
Process variables and eﬀects
The sulfurization process includes several variables, which can mainly be grouped as process
variables and other variables. The process variables are heating rate, max temperature, time,
cooling rate, pressure and process gas. Other variables are the amount and composition of
metal precursors and other precursors added to the system. The main process variables are
temperature, time, and pressure which have distinct eﬀects on the crystallization of the CZTS
phase.
Temperature
The phase formation evolution during sulfurization is very dependent on the temperature as
shown in Fig. 3.5. Depending on the phases present in the metal precursor, the formation
of diﬀerent binary and ternary sulﬁdes can occur at diﬀerent temperatures [33]. Some studies
show the formation of SnS2 and Cu2S prior to the formation of ZnS, while others only see
the formation of ZnS and Cu2S, with SnS2 never forming and instead forms CZTS and CTS
directly from liquid Sn, S, and the other binary phases [33]. For the formation of CZTS, without
secondary phases, a temperature of at least 550◦C is required [32].

Time
The standard sulfurization time used in literature is much longer than what is required simply
for the formation of CZTS phases [33]. The main reason why a longer sulfurization time is
used is that the increase in grain diameter is very slow compared to the sulfurization and
phase formation. A disadvantage to longer sulfurization time is increased element losses. Zn
can evaporate from its elemental form, but the rate of Zn-lose is decreased for Cu-Zn alloys,
additionally, once the precursors have been fully converted into sulﬁde materials there will be
no volatile Zn phases left and evaporation will stop [36]. Sn loss is seen even at high pressures,
but it occurs much faster at high temperature (> 550◦C and low pressures.
Pressure
The pressure is referring to the partial pressure of nitrogen introduced into the sealed system
before initializing the heat treatment, this is at times referred to as the background pressure.
The pressure inﬂuences both the ﬁnal composition, the rate of phase formation, and the grain
size.
Experimental research has shown that at low pressure (< 10mbar) the rate of phase
formation of the binary and ternary sulﬁdes is signiﬁcantly slower compared to slightly higher
pressures (> 100mbar), additionally the sulfurization process is also likely to end earlier due
to the sulfur vapour having left the area around the sample [36]. On the other hand, grain
size decreases as the pressure is increased, and the decrease in grain size is especially fast at
pressure < 150mbar. Lastly as mention earlier, Sn loss is seen even at high pressures, but it
occurs much faster at high temperature (> 550◦C) and low pressures [36].
3.3
Characterization of phases
To characterize the phases in the metal precursor before sulfurization as well as after the phases
formed after sulfurization two diﬀerent techniques were utilized: X-Ray Diﬀraction (XRD) and
Raman spectroscopy. The two techniques are used complementary as neither technique can
distinguish all of the phases from each other.
3.3.1
X-Ray Diﬀraction (XRD)
XRD is a non-destructive characterization method, commonly used for phase identiﬁcation and
stress measurements. For the characterization in this thesis, to minimize the signal from the
underlying substrate below the CZTS layer, a speciﬁc variation of XRD was utilized, namely
Grazing Incidence XRD (GI-XRD). Commonly for all XRD, a monochromatic beam of X-rays
with a wavelength of λ is directed at the sample, throughout this thesis a copper target was
used for the generation of X-rays wielding a Kα radiation of 1.54˚A. The X-ray hits the sample
at an incidence angle of θ and is scattered in all directions, at speciﬁc angles where the Bragg
condition; nλ = 2dsinθ is fulﬁlled the scattered beams will interfere constructively resulting in
high-intensity reﬂections, d being the lattice spacing and n is an integer. For regular XRD the
source and detector are both rotated around the sample, with an incidence beam angle of θ
while the detector is kept at an angle of 2θ. However, for GI-XRD the incident beam is kept
at a ﬁxed very low incidence angle and only the detector is rotated around the sample. The
24
==PAGE:32==
3.3. CHARACTERIZATION OF PHASES
advantage of GI-XRD is the probing depth is kept very low, and with an incidence angle of
2.5◦as used in this project, the estimated probing depth is roughly 800nm through the entire
measurement. An incidence angle of 2.5 was chosen as that is the approximate thickness of the
CZTS layer after sulfurization, minimizing the amount of signal from the underlying substrates.
XRD can in most cases accurately distinguish material phases, except when there are multiple
phases with similar symmetry and lattice parameters, which is the case for CZTS and some of
the secondary phases that can occur. Speciﬁcally, it is known that ZnS and Cu2SnS3 can be
indistinguishable from CZTS [33], which is why Raman spectroscopy is usually used together
with XRD as it can distinguish between those phases. Identiﬁcation of speciﬁc material phases
was done by manually matching them against references from the Inorganic Crystal Structure
Database (ICSD), with each reference having a unique ICSD collection code (cc).
3.3.2
Raman spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is another non-destructive characterization method, used to measure the
vibrational energy modes of a sample, it can provide both chemical and structural information
as well as identiﬁcation of substances through their characteristic Raman shifts. Raman spec-
troscopy works by illuminating a sample using a monochromatic laser beam, inelastic scattering
of the photons results in the photon energy either being shifted down or up, which is known as
the Stokes and anti-Stokes shift respectively, and the frequency diﬀerence between the incident
light and scattered light is called the Raman shift. By measuring the spectrum of wavelength
shifts and comparing it to know characteristic Raman shifts for CZTS and secondary phases, it
can be used to identify certain phases. The eﬀective penetration depth of the probe laser can
be estimated using dα =

coeﬃcient of > 104cm−1, giving a probe depth of 500nm or less. A list of characteristic Raman
shifts for CZTS and common secondary phases used for peak identiﬁcation can be seen in Fig.

Fabrication of CZT
The fabrication of the thin-ﬁlm Cu-Zn-Sn (CZT) metallic precursor was done using electrode-
position, speciﬁcally using a sequential deposition process. A CuZn alloy is deposited on the
substrate ﬁrst, followed by deposition of CuSn alloy or vice versa, forming a stacked bilayer
precursor (Cu-Sn/Cu-Sn). By varying the composition and/or thickness of each alloy the total
composition of the CZT is controlled.
4.1
Experimental details
In this section the experimental details are brieﬂy described, covering; the basic components
used for electrodeposition, the sample treatment before deposition, the deposition of the two
alloys CuZn and CuSn, and characterization of the stacked alloys.

Sample preparation
Stainless steel substrates
The SS substrates were ﬁrst washed using DI water and scrubbed down with ethanol by hand,
improve the tape adhesion used to cover the backside. A hole is punched through the substrate

(5 −10µm) nickel layer on top of the stainless steel substrate.

The bulk Mo substrate goes through the same treatment as the SS substrate, but the treatment
stops right after the cathodic degreasing and goes straight to electrodeposition.

4.2
Results and discussion
Diﬀerent investigations and results were made using each type of substrate, with the results on
bulk Mo building on top of those for SS and likewise, the results from Moly foil builds on the
results from bulk Mo.
4.2.1
Stainless steel substrates
A total of 53 samples were made on SS substrates, numbered #1 to #53, all of them being
CuZn plating. No CuSn plating was done on SS substrates due to a delay in receiving the tin(II)
oxalate required for the CuSn bath. As the CuSn system used was a regular plating system
while the CuZn system was irregular according to literature [24][25], conﬁrming the behaviour
and adjusting parameters for the CuZn bath was more relevant in case of any odd behaviour
in the irregular system, while the behaviour of the regular CuSn system was expected to be
consistent with the literature.
XRF
The composition of all 53 samples made on SS were measured using XRF. The plating of CuZn
was investigated varying four of the main plating variables: (i) concentration in the depositable
metals, (ii) the current density and (iii) the plating time. The brass baths in table 4.1 was mixed
and used in the order from left to right, with the ﬁrst three baths being used on SS substrates.
By reducing the amount of copper sulfate while keeping the zinc sulfate constant, both the ratio
of concentrations of the depositable metals and the total concentration of depositable metals
was changed simultaneously, however, the change in total metal content should have very little
inﬂuence on the CuZn plating system used. The change in alloy composition as a function of

4.3
Discussion and outlook
On bulk Mo substrates, the sequential use of a Glycerate-Zincate bath for CuZn deposition
followed by an Oxalate bath for CuSn deposition proved capable of reaching the targeted
composition and thickness for fabrication of CZTS. The average composition for each sample
was very consistent, allowing replicable fabrication of the samples, however, the homogeneity
of the composition across the samples was quite inconsistent. The main cause for the non-
homogeneity and inconsistency is thought to be a result of how the system was set up. With
the samples being placed and aligned manually by hanging the copper wire over the edge of
the side of the bath, some variation in placement and orientation during the electrodeposition
is very likely to have occurred.
For Moly foils substrates, deposition of CuZn in the targeted ratios and thickness was achieved.
Examination of CuSn deposition on Moly foil, had to be left unﬁnished, due to the contami-
nating Sn-K signal from the PVC spacer during XRF measurements. The source of the con-
taminating signal being the PVC spacer was not discovered until the very end of this project,
leaving no time for the examination to occur. Characterization of the electrodeposition of CuSn
alloy on Moly foil was thus never completed.
As the aim of this part of the thesis was to fabricate samples with a proper composition the
goal was partly achieved. Average compositions very close to the target was achieved on bulk
Mo, while keeping to a layer thickness around 300nm, reaching the ideal composition should be
possible with some minor adjustments to the plating variables. Examining and adjusting the
composition on Moly foil was not possible until it was too late, leaving this as only a partial
success.
Looking back, several types of improvements to the setup could have been made, both for
making the experimental work more eﬃcient, as well as to make the plating processes more
consistent and reliable. A holder for the samples could have been ordered/fabricated for each
of the baths, ensuring the samples would be position in a consistently accurate manner, thus
reducing the variation and improving the homogeneity of the samples. A hull cell could have
been introduced into the process at an early stage, decreasing the amount of time needed to
examine and determine a proper balance between composition and current density. Less time
could have been spent on SS substrates, but most importantly deposition and characterization
on Moly foil substrates should have been attempted much sooner, to test whether the achieved
composition on bulk Mo would translate over to Moly foil substrates. As the deposition rates
proved to be quite diﬀerent on Moly foil there was no need to ﬁne-tune the plating variables
for the proper composition on bulk Mo. Additionally, the problem with the PVC spacer would
have been encountered earlier, thus the solution might have been discovered in time for proper
Moly foil samples to have been achieved.

Anneal and sulfurization of CZT
The formation of CZTS, from the metallic precursors deposited using electrodeposition, is
achieved through a sulfurization process, as described in Sec. 3.2. The substrate with stacked
CuSn/CuZn/Mo alloys is placed into a furnace together with a solid sulfur precursor, melting
together and forming the CZTS phase under proper sulfurization conditions. A Sn precursor is
also typically added to counteract material loss during the sulfurization process. Heat treatment
of the metal precursors before sulfurization can improve the morphology of the ﬁnal CZTS and
reduce elemental loss by decreasing the annealing time at high temperatures.
5.1

The furnace used for the heat treatment of samples in this thesis was located at DTU Risø,
while samples were fabricated at DTU Lyngby. To undergo heat treatment, the samples would
thus be carefully transported in individual plastic sample bags to Risø. The furnace used is
shown in ﬁg. 5.1(a), with the sample boat shown in ﬁg. 5.1(b). The samples were placed into
a boat, together with any additional precursors placed in the hollows, after which the boat was
closed tightly by screwing the lid on. The boats would then be loaded into the furnace after
which the furnace is closed and sealed up airtight.

were made to examine either the diﬀerence in impacts or to adjust for other parameters. A
ramping rate of 20◦C/min and starting pressure of 175 mbar was used for all the procedures.

As diﬀerentiating between Mo and S is not possible using the XRF system, the compositional

5.3. DISCUSSION AND OUTLOOK
5.3
Discussion and outlook
XRF measurements of the second batch shortly after sulfurization, showed composition close
to the targeted composition, indicating the proper composition can be achieved through this
fabrication process. The non-homogeneity of the sample thickness and composition that was
observed through XRF mapping of sample #106, was still present after the annealing processes.
Local improvements to the homogeneity of the composition and thickness were observed, but
the global trend across sample #106 was still present. The resulting uncertainty of the average
composition is therefore of quite a signiﬁcant size, with some areas on a sample being within
the targeted compositional ratios and other areas quite far from them. However, as the non-
homogeneity of the sample thickness and composition was consistent between samples, at times
along a diﬀerent orientation, the samples were still reproducible.
The XRF mapping of sample #106, which had previously shown a good composition after the
sulfurization (using 10 point XRF measurements), showed signiﬁcant material loss of Sn and a
bit of Cu, moving the composition far away from the target. Considering the uneven loss, the
lost material must have consisted of Sn-S, Cu-S and/or Cu-Sn-S phases. This indicates that
the sulfurization process was insuﬃcient for a complete transformation of CZT into CZTS. The
XRD and Raman characterization was done shortly before the XRF mapping, and as such, the
material loss is believed to have already occurred at that time. Based on the XRD and Raman
spectra measured, the second batch consists of CZTS and possibly some ZnS phases, while the
third batch also has some Cu7.2S4 phases present. Based on this, after sulfurization, the samples
have consisted of CZTS and ZnS phases, and considering the change in composition over time,
SnS, CuS and Cu-Sn-S phases may have been present on the surface and subsequently ﬂaked
oﬀbefore XRD and Raman characterization took place. Leaving the remaining CZTS layer as
a mixture of CZTS and ZnS phases.
Considering the measured composition of the Moly foils with roughly 80% Cu, 15% Zn, and
5% Sn, less formation of the CZTS phase was expected, which was also reﬂected by a lower
intensity in the XRD spectra. The intensity of the Cu7.2S4 phase on Moly foil was also higher
as was expected considering the high concentration of Cu. Given the material loss of Sn on
the bulk Mo samples, it cannot be determined if the low concentration of Sn on the Moly foil
samples are due to a similar material loss or if the electrodeposition process was insuﬃcient to
achieve the proper composition. Further examination of the peak intensities could have revealed
additional details regarding how much CZTS was formed. And the use of a 785nm excitation
laser during Raman spectroscopy could also have given additional information regarding the
CZTS and ZnS phases. Other methods could also have been utilized to probe the sample such
as secondary ion mass spectrometry to examine the homogeneity of the composition through
the sample.
As the aim of the thesis was the formation of CZTS phases for the fabrication of ﬂexible thin-
ﬁlm solar cells, the results can be considered a partial success. Formation of CZTS phases
was achieved, however, the annealing processes have likely been insuﬃcient, resulting in the
formation of both CZTS and secondary phases. For further studying, particularly the formation
of CZTS phases through the annealing processes would be of interest, as the poor adhesion of
Sn after sulfurization indicates CZTS phases was not fully formed.
Looking back, more variations to the sulfurization process could have been attempted, however,
it would have required less time spent ﬁguring out the cause for the Sn-K contaminating signal
in the XRF.

IV curve
An artiﬁcial sunlight lamp is used for IV characterization. Calibration is done by measuring the
intensity below the lamp and locating the area where the intensity is roughly 1kW/m2, which
is then the spot where the sample is to be placed during measurement. A sample is placed
under the lamp and conducting probes are carefully placed onto the sample, one on the indium
connecting to the back contact layer, with the other placed on one of the cells in the grid as
the top contact, as shown in Fig. 6.1(b). The top contact probe needs to be placed carefully
to avoid damaging or penetrating the top layers. With the lamp on, the current through the
sample is measured over a range of voltages, creating the IV curve. The top probe is then lifted
and moved to a diﬀerent cell, repeating the measurement for each cell on a sample.

6.4. DISCUSSION AND OUTLOOK
6.4
Discussion and outlook
Working solar cells, with a peak eﬃciency of 0.44%, were made using the bulk Mo substrates.
Given the material loss that was observed after XRF mapping of sample #106, the same sample
the peak eﬃciency was measured on, the average composition of the samples made into solar
cells was quite poor, consisting of CZTS and ZnS phases, with the SnS phase having been etched
oﬀduring the fabrication of CZTS solar cell device. A peak eﬃciency of 0.44% could not have
been reached without a lot of CZTS phase and at most a bit of ZnS. Considering the variation
in composition seen during XRF mapping of sample #106, proper compositional ratios can be
found in some areas of the samples. The cells with eﬃciencies above 0.2% have likely been
found in such areas, consisting mainly of CZTS with maybe a bit of ZnS, while higher levels of
ZnS or possibly other secondary phases are present outside these areas. Considering that the
conversion eﬃciency drastically decreases once outside of the targeted composition ratios, as
shown in Fig. 3.4, the achieved eﬃciencies on average being around 0.05% is very reasonable.
With the Moly foil substrates having had a very poor composition combined with the failure
to deposition the AZO layer, the resulting IV curves being that of a resistor was expected.
As the aim of this part of the thesis was the fabrication of a working thin-ﬁlm solar cell, using
the fabricated CZTS layer, it can be considered a partial success. The thin-ﬁlm CZTS layer
for a ﬂexible thin-ﬁlm solar cell was successfully formed on bulk Mo substrates, but it was not
achieved on the Moly foil. No functional and ﬂexible thin-ﬁlm solar cell was therefore made,
leaving it as a partial success.
Looking back, the AZO deposition on Moly foil could have been done by ﬁxing the samples
more securely in place during the sputtering process, however, with the composition on the
Moly foil samples being even worse than those on the bulk Mo samples, it is unlikely to have
resulted in good working cells regardless.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was the development of a process using electrodeposition to fabricate
the absorbing p-type material CZTS for thin-ﬁlm solar cells. This was then to be tested by
using the fabricated CZTS layer to form a working thin-ﬁlm solar cell. This goal was achieved
on bulk Mo substrates, however, no ﬂexible thin-ﬁlm solar cell was made.
The ﬁrst step of the process was the development of an electrodeposition process for the de-
position of the Cu-Zn-Sn metallic precursor layers. At an early stage, it was decided to form
the metallic precursors through a sequential deposition of CuZn and CuSn in some order, as
another researcher was working on the simultaneous deposition of the metallic precursors. Ad-
ditionally, it was also decided to avoid the use of cyanide-based baths for the deposition, to
stay consistent with the environmentally benign property of CZTS, limiting the types of baths
to non-standard and less investigated plating systems. To save on the Moly foil for fabricating
thin-ﬁlm solar cells, the process was initially developed for deposition on bulk Mo. Optimiza-
tion of the electrodeposition method was done through repeated electrodeposition of CuZn and
CuSn on bulk Mo, varying the current density, deposition time, and the concentration of de-
positable metals in the electrolytes, followed by compositional characterization using XRF, until
a CZT layer of stacked CuSn/CuZn/Mo with total compositional ratios of Cu(Zn+Sn) = 0.93
and Zn/Sn = 1.23 was achieved while keeping to a layer thickness around 350nm. To better
understand how the composition was distributed over the sample, a complete mapping of a
single sample was done using the XRF, showing that a completely homogeneous alloy compo-
sition was not achieved. The expected main cause for the inhomogeneity is the low precision
in which the samples were placed during deposition. A working method for the fabrication
of the metallic precursor layer was successfully made for bulk Mo, however, the transference
of the fabrication process from bulk Mo to Moly foil was unsuccessful. The main reason for
that was a problem encountered during the characterization of the composition of the metallic
precursor layers using XRF. The PVC spacer used during the XRF measurements contained
Sn that interfered with the characterization of the composition in the Moly foil samples. The
PVC spacer was not found to be the problem until the end of the experimental work, thus the
fabrication process developed on bulk Mo was unable to be adjusted for the Moly foil samples.
The second step of the process was the transformation of the metallic precursor layer (CZT) into
CZTS. The formation of CZTS phases was conﬁrmed using both XRD and Raman spectroscopy,
while peaks from secondary phases were minor or completely absent indicating a successful
formation of the CZTS layer on both bulk Mo and Moly foil samples. However, by comparing
XRF measurements of sample #106 (bulk Mo) taken immediately after sulfurization, with

those taken later on the same sample, following the XRD and Raman characterization, a large
material loss of mainly Sn was discovered to have occurred within that period. As the XRD
and Raman spectra of sample #106 were consistent with the rest of the samples in the same
batch, a similar material loss is expected to have occurred for the entire batch. As the material
lost was mainly Sn, the top part of the CZTS layer on the samples after sulfurization likely
consisted of SnS, CuS and/or Cu-Sn-S secondary phases which subsequently ﬂaked oﬀbefore
XRD and Raman characterization took place. Leaving a remaining CZTS layer consisting of
CZTS and ZnS phases on the samples. A working method for the transformation of the CZT
layer into CZTS was therefore only partially achieved. Formation of CZTS did occur, but the
process was insuﬃcient for the entire CZT layer, resulting in the formation of secondary phases.
The third step of the process was measuring the performance of the CZTS solar cell. The
transition from CZTS to a solar cell was outside the scope of this thesis and was handled by
other members of the research team. Functional solar cells were achieved on bulk Mo, with the
highest eﬃciency obtained being 0.44%, however, no functional solar cell was made on Moly
foil. The peak eﬃciency of 0.44% on bulk Mo could not have been reached without a lot of
CZTS phase and at most a bit of ZnS. This was likely achieved due to the inhomogeneity of the
composition in the CZTS layer, as the XRF mapping shows areas where a proper composition
was maintained even after the material loss. Resulting in areas where more CZTS and less
ZnS phases were formed, allowing the cells in these areas to achieve eﬃciencies above 0.2%,
while the remaining cells would have higher levels of ZnS or possibly other secondary phases
present. The failure with the Moly foil samples was a combination of several issues. Proper
composition could not be made due to the interference from the PVC spacer during the ﬁrst
step. The material loss observed on bulk Mo during the second step could also have occurred
for the Moly foil samples, and in the third step, an incident occurred during the deposition of
the AZO layer on the Moly foil, with the Moly foil samples sticking onto the shutter due to
being very light and slightly magnetic.
In summary, a working process, but with signiﬁcant room for improvement, was developed
for the fabrication of CZTS on bulk Mo and resulted in some functional solar cells, with the
peak eﬃciency obtained being 0.44%, however, the results achieved on bulk Mo was unable to
be transferred over to the thin-ﬁlm Moly foil substrates, mainly due to signal contamination
during XRF compositional characterization.
7.1
Ideas for future work
With the ﬁrst major issue solved, being the contaminating signal from the PVC spacer during
XRF measurements, actual investigation and optimization of the electrodeposition process on
Moly foil substrates can be done.
Considering the issue of material loss encountered during the second phase, further examination
of the adhesion of the CZT layer as well as how it is transported are of interest, however, as
the loss was not seen until a while after the sulfurization process, an investigation into why the
process was insuﬃcient for complete formation of CZTS and subsequently optimize the process
for complete formation of CZTS would be imperative to the process.
