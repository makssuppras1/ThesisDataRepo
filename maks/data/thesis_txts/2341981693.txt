Preface
This thesis is the ﬁnal assignment of the MSc programme in the ﬁeld of Civil Engi-
neering and was prepared by Daniel Vejsgaard Laursen at the Technical University
of Denmark in collaboration with the consultancy ﬁrm Rambøll.
The thesis deals with the possibility of increasing the shear strength of precast
prestressed hollow core slabs by locally ﬁlling a number of the cores. The inter-
action between the precast prestressed hollow core slabs and the ﬁlled cores has
not been studied fully in the literature. Furthermore, the product standard, valid
for precast prestressed hollow core slabs, only gives a solution on how to account
for the ﬁlled cores, but not on how to account for addition of shear reinforcement.
These issues has been investigated by a number of full-scale shear tests on precast
prestressed hollow core slabs with a number of ﬁlled cores.

Abstract
By full-scale shear tests of twelve precast prestressed hollow core (PPHC) slabs with
two ﬁlled cores, it is determined by a comparison to the calculation method pre-
scribed in the product standard, DS/EN 1168 (2011), and the calculation method
recommodented in FIB-bulletin 6 (2000), whether or not it is reasonable to increase
the shear capacity of the PPHC slab with ﬁlled cores compared to the PPHC slab
without ﬁlled cores.
To investigate diﬀerent factors that may inﬂuence the shear capacity, diﬀerent
amount of shear reinforcement is added to the cores, and furthermore a special
wax is added to half of the PPHC slabs before ﬁlling the cores in order to simulate
the long time eﬀects of concrete shrinkage and thereby the lack of bond stresses to
build up in the interface between the PPHC slab and the ﬁlled cores.
The shear capacity is tested in three point bending and it is noticed that a mean
increase of approximately 63% is possible to achieve by ﬁlling two cores compared
to the PPHC slab without ﬁlled cores, but also that it is connected to a rather large
standard deviation compared to PPHC slabs without ﬁlled cores. Moreover it is
noticed that when the wax is added to the interface an increased shear resistance is
no longer present. From the experiments it is also observed that no eﬀect of adding
stirrups is achieved.
The PPHC slabs show two diﬀerent behaviours. A brittle behaviour is observed
for the PPHC slabs without ﬁlled cores while a much more ductile behaviour is
observed for the PPHC slabs with ﬁlled cores. It is observed though that when wax
is added to the interface the ductile behaviour completely vanishes, which is due to
the bond stresses in the interface going towards zero.
Moreover, diﬀerent crack developments are observed during the experiments. The
PPHC slabs with ﬁlled cores show an asymmetrical crack development while the
PPHC slabs without show an symmetrical development. This phenomenon is as-
sumed to be closely related to the ductile and brittle behaviour of the slabs.
Diﬀerent shear resistances are obtained when following the calculation methods
given by DS/EN 1168 (2011) and FIB-bulletin 6 (2000). Both methods predict an
anchorage failure and it is determined that DS/EN 1168 (2011) and FIB-bulletin 6
(2000) predict the shear resistance of the PPHC slabs without wax with a deviation
up to 8% and 35% respectively from the mean tested shear resistance. Therefore
the anchorage calculation of FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) is assumed to be very conserva-
tive.
Lastly, a set of new calculation considerations is developed, since none of the exist-
ing calculation methods were able to fully describe the physics within the composite
structure. Through the considerations it is assumed, that the ﬁlled cores can be
vii
==PAGE:8==
treated as a dowel rotating inside the PPHC slab. The considerations indicate that
a certain extra shear capacity, ∆P, per ﬁlled core can be calculated and added to
the shear capacity of the PPHC without ﬁlled cores, but that it should be added
to the shear-displacement curve at an unknown point of the PPHC slab after its
shear capacity has been reached.
Based on the experiences acquired through the project, it can not be recommended
to account for any extra shear capacity before the long time eﬀects on the shear
capacity from concrete shrinkage has been fully investigated. Avoiding the interface
between the ﬁlled cores and the PPHC slab by casting both parts together at the
same time could be an opportunity to achieve an extra shear capacity, but is not
practically allowed by the casting method of today.

Resumé
Ved en sammenligning mellem forskydningsbæreevnen bestemt vha. fuldskala-tests
på 12 præfabrikerede forspændte huldæk med to udstøbte kanaler og beregnings-
metoderne givet i DS/EN 1168 (2011) og FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) er det undersøgt,
hvorvidt det er rimeligt at øge disses forskydningsbæreevne i forhold til huldæk
uden udstøbte kanaler.
Det ønskes undersøgt hvorledes forskellige faktorer vil inﬂuere på forskydningsbæ-
reevnen, hvorfor forskellig mængde forskydningsarmering er tilføjet til kanalerne.
Ydermere er en specialvoks smurt i kanalerne på halvdelen af elementerne for at
undersøge langtidseﬀekterne forårsaget af betonsvind.
Forskydningsbæreevnen er opnået ved tre-punkts-bøjning, og det bemærkes at det
er muligt i gennemsnit at øge forskydningsbæreevnen med ca. 63% ved at udstøbe
2 kanaler sammenlignet med ikke at udstøbe nogen af kanalerne. Det bemærkes
dog også, at standardafvigelsen er væsentlig øget for forskydningsbæreevnen med
udstøbte kanaler. Ydermere bemærkes det, at når der tilføjes voks til kanalerne for-
svinder forskydningsbæreevneforøgelsen fuldstændigt. Det er ligeledes observeret,
at forskydningsbøjler ikke har nogen betydning for forskydningsbæreevnen.
Huldækkene med og uden udstøbning viser to vidt forskellige arbejdskurver. Der er
observeret en meget sprød opførsel for huldæk uden udstøbning, mens en meget sej
opførsel er observeret for huldæk med udstøbning. Ydermere viser arbejdskurven,
at når der tilføjes voks, vil der igen opnås en meget sprød opførsel.
Revnedannelsen viser sig også at variere mellem huldæk med og uden udstøbning.
Der observeres en meget mere ujævn revnedannelse for huldækkene med udstøb-
ning end for huldækkene uden. Dette antages at skyldes dækkenes duktilitet eller
mangel på samme.
Når man følger de to beregningsmetoder i DS/EN 1168 (2011) og FIB-bulletin
6 (2000) opnås der to meget forskellige forskydningsbæreevner. Begge metoder
forudsiger et forankringsbrud og en forskydningsbæreevne 8% over og 35% under
den test-ede forskydningsbæreevne for hhv. DS/EN 1168 (2011) og FIB-bulletin 6
(2000), hvorfor beregningsmetoden til forankring i FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) må anta-
ges at være overvejende konservativ at bruge.
Slutteligt er der udviklet et nyt set bereningsovervejelser til at håndtere udstøbning
af kanaler, da ingen af de eksisterende formår at beskrive fysikken bag brudmeka-
nismerne i huldækkene med udstøbte kanaler. Gennem bereningsovervejelserne er
det antaget, at kanalerne kan opfattes som dorne der rotere indeni kanalerne. Det
indikeres i bereningsovervejelserne, at der ﬁndes en forskydningsbæreevneforøgelse,
∆P, for hver kanal, der er udstøbt, som kan lægges til forskydningsbæreevnen af
huldækkene uden udstøbning. Dog ser det ud til at ∆P skal lægges til arbejdskur-
ix
==PAGE:10==
ven for huldækkene uden udstøbning efter de har nået deres bæreevnekapacitet.
På baggrund af erfaringerne opnået gennem projektet, kan det ikke anbefales at
regne med nogen forskydningsbæreevneforøgelse før langtidseﬀekterne foresaget af
betonsvind er fuld ud klarlagt. En mulighed for at opnå en forskydningsbæreev-
neforøgelse kunne være at eliminere støbeskellet mellem de udstøbte kanaler og
selve huldækket ved at støbe begge dele på samme tid, men dette er ikke praktisk
tilladeligt med de støbemetoder, der anvendes i dag.

Introduction
For decades prestressed precast hollow core (PPHC) slabs have been used as a
building material, mainly as ﬂoor and roof decks for multi-storey buildings. Not
only PPHC slabs, but many types of prestressed precast elements has been used in
building construction since the 1950s. A key feature of precast elements is quick
erection and thus reduction of on-site labor needs.
Using PPHC slabs in building construction sometimes requires an iterative de-
sign process where several opportunities are considered. This is not only the case
for PPHC slabs, but often an issue for many building components.
Designing new buildings is a matter of getting many diﬀerent professions to work
together to create the best solution for the client. This procedure involves a lot of
give and take between the professions in order to reach this goal. Therefore the
structural engineer is often given a limited space to ﬁt in the load carrying structure
to also leave some space for the technical building services. Due to this inconvenient
design procedure it can be necessary to increase the load carrying capacity of the
structure without increasing the height it.
A way of increasing the shear resistance of the structure when using PPHC slabs
is to ﬁll a number of the cores. The PPHC slabs are usually installed under simply
supported conditions, thus it is only necessary to ﬁll the cores locally at the slab
ends where the shear is largest considering uniformly loaded conditions.
The product standard, DS/EN 1168 (2011), which applies for design of precast
concrete products in i.a.
Denmark, does not hold a calculation method, which
fully covers the design of PPHC slabs with a number of ﬁlled cores. On this ba-
sis it is of interest to investigate whether or not the method predicts useful shear
capacities, and furthermore, to get an understanding of the interaction between
the precast and in-situ cast part. This includes the eﬀect of the interface where
large bond stresses need to build up for the composite structure to work as a whole.
To clarify as many inﬂuential parameters as possible, PPHC slabs with diﬀerent
compositions have been tested in a full-scale shear capacity test. The two param-
eters which have been varied are the amount of shear reinforcement added to the
cores and the shrinkage of the concrete, which has been simulated by adding wax
to the cores before casting.
A PPHC slab with a width and height of w = 1200 mm and h = 320 mm re-
spectively and a span of 4000 mm was used for the shear testing. The cross section
of the PPHC slabs used is shown in Figure 1.1 where the amount of prestressing

Calculation Methods
Based on the shear capacity of the twelve specimens it is determined whether or
not common calculation methods yield a safe design. As described below, the na-
tional product standard DS/EN 1168 (2011) applies for verifying the mechanical
resistance of PPHC slabs in Denmark, thus very essential to compare the results
to. Also the FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) has been found to hold a design method worth
comparing to. The method is very similar to DS/EN 1168 (2011) though, but in-
cludes some crucial parts that has been left out in the DS/EN 1168 (2011). Finally,
a plastic upper bound method, derived in Hoang et al. (2014) has been compared
to the results, since calculation methods proposed for design of structures are often
very conservative.
Below the three calculation methods are described.
Moreover, a method determining the load of which the cross section cracks and a
method determining the moment capacity of the PPHC slabs have been described.
The method giving the cracking load is important regarding the shear capacity and
the moment capacity is important when designing the PPHC slabs in order to avoid
moment failure.
2.1
Product Standard
In Denmark the mechanical resistance of concrete structures used in buildings has
to be veriﬁed according to DS/EN 1992-1-1 (2008), which is legally authorized
by the Danish building regulation that forms the basis of design and construction
of concrete structures. The Danish building regulation has its legal authorisation
through the Danish construction legislation.
DS/EN 1992-1-1 (2008) holds the overall design rules, but a number of national
standards on how to apply the rules in DS/EN 1992-1-1 (2008) has been published
by the European Committee for Standardization (CEN). The national standard
DS/EN 1168 (2011) applies for i.a. PPHC slabs, hence used in determination of
the theoretical shear capacity according to DS/EN 1992-1-1 (2008).
The shear capacity of PPHC slabs should according to DS/EN 1168 (2011), 4.3.3.2.2.1
be veriﬁed considering three failure modes. Shear failure can occur either in regions
cracked by bending or in regions uncracked by bending. Furthermore anchorage
failure can occur if a ﬂexural crack arises within the anchorage length of the re-
inforcement. The three failure modes has to be considered in the following way
(DS/EN 1168, 2011):

The plastic shear capacity is calculated using the method presented in Hoang et al.
(2014). The method is mainly a crack sliding method which has been supplemented
with a rotation failure mechanism. Both mechanisms have been derived from the
virtual work principle, thus an upper bound solution. Furthermore, the concrete in
both mechanisms is treated as a rigid plastic Modiﬁed Coulomb material.

the crack may turn into a yield line and cause sliding failure. According to Hoang
et al. (2014) the method was originally developed for reinforced concrete beams.
In Hoang et al. (2014) the method has been further developed to deal with PPHC
slabs.

Design of Experimental Set-up
When the mechanical resistance of precast concrete products is going to be ob-
tained by a full-scale test, it has to follow a certain guideline if the result should
be considered valid. Precast concrete products have to follow the guidelines given
by DS/CEN/TR 14862 (2004) on full-scale testing. The purpose of DS/CEN/TR
14862 (2004) is to give guidelines on how to incorporate full-scale testing in the
product standards. Therefore the requirements given for full-scale testing within
each product standard has to be followed. Since DS/EN 1168 (2011) applies for
PPHC slabs this has been followed in designing the experimental set-up. It is stated
in DS/EN 1168 (2011) that PPHC slabs should be submitted to full-scale testing
according to Appendix J in the same product standard, thus applied.
Mainly three things are considered: The support condition, the loading condition
and how the load is applied. Apart from that the measuring of deformation has
also been considered.

the cores, the pure shear bearing capacity of the PPHC slab without ﬁlled cores

been casted, as described in Chapter 4, allowing to perform a shear test in both
ends of each PPHC slab.

The 12 PPHC slabs with ﬁlled cores used in the project had to be manufactured
through a number of diﬀerent working procedures. First the casting of the PPHC
slabs had to take place. Afterwards they had to harden before the installation of
the reinforcing caves and the applying of the wax could take place. Lastly, before

casting at degrees below freezing point.

Casting of the PPHC Slabs
The PPHC slabs have been manufactured at the precast concrete element factory in
Vemmelev owned by the company Spæncom. The widely used extrusion technique
was used in the production of the PPHC slabs. It is a technique where the wires
are prestressed before long strips of slabs are extruded and afterwards saw-cut into
units with the speciﬁed length. Furthermore the casting takes place onto a 60¶C
warm casting bed allowing to create recesses after just 8 hours, at which point
the concrete has a maturity of 50 hours and a tensile strength of 2.0 MPa. The
specimens were produced with 4 recesses to be able to install the reinforcing caves
into the cavities and also to ensure a proper vibration of the concrete. See Figure

2 Large temperature induced stresses.

A guideline on how to asses whether damage of the concrete at a certain casting
situation should be expected or not is given in SBI-anvisning 125 (1982). When
assessing whether frost will occur at an early age, a number of parameters has to
be determined. The parameters then serve as input parameters to a large amount
of diagrams of which the temperature in the concrete at 15 maturity hours can be
determined. The parameters inﬂuencing whether frost will occur in the concrete
are the cement density, the air temperature, the air speed, the temperature of the
concrete at casting, how fast the concrete hardens and the cooling number.
All the parameters was chosen conservatively, since the parameters in SBI-anvisning
125 (1982) were provided as round numbers. The cement density and the concrete
temperature at casting have been given by Spæncom to 250 kg/m2 and approxi-
mately 12¶C respectively. The casting was performed when the weather forecast
predicted that the temperature would not drop below -5¶C and that the air speed
was not expected to exceed 2 m/s. The allowed air temperature was settled at -10¶C
and the wind speed at 5 m/s for design purpose. The last parameter, the cooling
number, a, was determined from SBI-anvisning 125 (1982), Table 1, as equivalent
to a square column with a mean side length of 200 mm and a 50 mm insulation
layer. The following design input parameters were used for design purpose:
- cement density = 250 kg/m2
- cement hardening speed: Normal
- concrete temperature at casting = 10¶C
- air temperature = -10¶C
- air speed = 5 m/s
- cooling number = 0.023
The input parameters were used to locate the hardening diagram of the speciﬁc
case (Figure 4.14). From this diagram it can be seen that the temperature in the
concrete is above 0¶C when 15 maturity hours is reached.
The second precaution against large temperature induced stresses is not relevant
for this case since, according to SBI-anvisning 125 (1982), it only becomes critical
when the insulation is removed during the ﬁrst maturitu hours. Therefore it serves
as a precaution relevant for a construction site where it is of interest to remove the
insulation as fast as possible. This assessment is also supported by the fact that
SBI-anvisning 125 (1982), Table 2, allows for temperature diﬀerences up to 56¶C in
the given case. It is clearly seen in Figure 4.14 that temperature diﬀerences larger
than approximately 20¶C will never occur for this case.

Grouping and Naming of the Specimens:
12 specimens have been manufactured as described in Chapter 4. In accordance
with Chapter 3 the 12 specimens have been subjected to a shear capacity test in
both ends of the slabs. As the cores have only been ﬁlled in one end of the PPHC
slabs, the tests have been divided into two groups. One group containing the 12
tests on the specimens with ﬁlled cores (called main tests) and one group containing
the 12 tests on the specimens without ﬁlled cores (called reference tests). The two
groups have subsequently been divided by the characteristics of the ﬁlled cores into
3 serials, which are characterised by:
- Serial 1:
Only concrete has been added to the cores
- Serial 2:
Concrete and stirrups with a spacing of 250 mm have been added
to the cores
- Serial 3:
Concrete and stirrups with a spacing of 100 mm have been added
to the cores
To be able to distinguish between the tests each test has been given a unique
name. The serials contain four specimens each and are therefore numbered by 1-4.
Besides that, 000, 250 or 100 has been added after the sequentially numbering of
the specimens, where 000 denotes no stirrups added, 250 denotes stirrups with a
spacing of 250 mm and 100 denotes stirrups with a spacing of 100 mm.

6.5.6
Discussion of Results
Before any of the tests were performed a very diﬀerent behaviour in several areas
was expected. The main thought behind the design of the reinforcing caves added
to the cores was a situation where an inclined crack formed from the edge of the
support to the point of the external force. It was furthermore assumed in the design
that the crack cut through both the PPHC slabs and the ﬁlled cores.
It was later experienced by the tests that the shear reinforcing caves could not
be activated during the testing. Therefore it is assessed that only the shear resis-
tance of the ﬁlled concrete contribute to the shear resistance, when bond stresses
are able to build up in the interface between the PPHC slabs and the cores. The
in-situ casted cores are therefore assumed, to some extend, to act as stiﬀdowels
rotating inside the cores. This correlates with the observations that the shear re-
sistances of the main tests with wax and the reference tests were almost equal, and
that the shear resistances of the main tests without wax were very varying. Since
the main tests with wax represent a situation where the concrete has shrunken, it
could be interesting to investigate if the use of expanding concrete would avoid the
problem.

served, since it is diﬀerent from test to test when and which cracks develop during
testing. Diﬀerent tendencies has been observed though, from which the presump-
tion of the physics behind he failure mechanism has been based.

Discussion of Comparison
Reference Tests
Based on the comparison between the three calculated shear resistances and the
tested shear resistance it is clear that none of the methods are able to describe the
physics in the PPHC slabs. The method proposed by DS/EN 1168 (2011) predicts
that a web shear failure will occur. This is also what was observed during the
testing, but the method is highly over-conservative, since it predicts a shear failure
in average 21% below the tested shear resistance. In contrary to this the plastic
method proposed by Hoang et al. (2014) predicts a shear resistance in average 29%
on the unsafe side and predicts a rotation failure with pull out of strands. The
last method, FIB-bulletin 6 (2000), is actually predicting a shear resistance very
close to the tested shear resistances with an average deviation of only 6%, on the
unsafe side though. The problem with FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) is that it predicts an
anchorage failure, which has not been observed for any of the reference tests.
The very large diﬀerence in the methods are assesed to be a result of a very complex
stress contribution due to the transfer of the prestressing to the concrete, which
has been investigated by Brunesi et al. (2015). He created a numerical FE model
predicting the tested shear resistance of 49 tests on average within 0.002% with a
standard deviation of 2.8%. According to Brunesi et al. (2015) the shear capacity
is not very sensitive to prestress losses, but very sensitive to the hollow core shape
and related to non-circularity of the voids and inherent web width variation along
the depth. He also found that the more non-circular the voids are the more brittle
behaviour of the PPHC slab should be expected.
It is a problem that non-inﬂuencing parameters as prestress losses are taken into ac-
count in the models, while highly inﬂuencing parameters like shape and web width
variation are not considered.

Discussion of Comparison
Comparison with Calculation Methods
It is explicitly stated in W. Beeby et al. (2008) that the shear resistance valid
for the uncracked regions should describe the shear resistance and not the cracked
expression, but it has been found by both Pajari (2005) and Bertagnoli and Mancini
(2009), who have tested 49 and 129 PPHC slabs respectively, that it delivers very
non-conservative results. This is probably why DS/EN 1168 (2011) requires also
to consider the cracked shear resistance, which is a purely empirical expression de-
rived for concrete beams and not slabs. The cracked shear resistance delivers, ac-
cording to Bertagnoli and Mancini (2009), very over-conservative results for short
spans and very non-conservative results for long spans. Bertagnoli and Mancini
(2009) adopted the same approach as proposed by DS/EN 1168 (2011) to investi-
gate whether or not the method was safe to use. Their conclusion was that it was
safe to use, and also that the cracking force, like in this project, should be taken as
the failure load for short span slabs.
It seams that no complete method has been proposed by any author, and therefore
a very conservative approach has been implemented in the design rules.
Main Tests
The comparison of the calculated shear resistance to the tested shear resistance
showed a satisfying correlation between the DS/EN 1168 (2011) and the tests with-
out wax, while a satisfying correlation between the FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) and the
tests with wax was found. Both methods predict an anchorage failure, but mainly
web shear failure or combined web shear and anchorage failure was observed in the
tests. In fact, both methods should correlate with the tests without wax, since
none of the methods take the concrete shrinkage, which the wax is simulating, into
account.
It seams odd that the shear resistance of DS/EN 1168 (2011) is deﬁned as the
uncracked shear resistance regardless of the actual condition of the cross section,
which is also why the method in FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) was considered, since it in-
cludes an expression for both cracked and uncracked regions. FIB-bulletin 6 (2000)
is based on the preliminary version of DS/EN 1168 (2011), and why the expression
for cracked regions later on has been expelled from the product standard is a mys-
tery.
The FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) is actually seen to predict the web shear resistance very
well, but is limited by a conservative anchorage approach, while DS/EN 1168 (2011)
is seen to overestimate the web shear resistance but gives reasonable results by the
anchorage failure.
Furthermore the interface between the ﬁlled cores and the PPHC slabs has been
determined to have a huge eﬀect on the shear resistance, since no extra shear re-
sistance was obtained when wax was added. Based on this, it can be questioned if
the method is valid for in-situ ﬁlled cores at all (Figure 7.3) or if the cores should
be precasted along with the casting of the PPHC slab for the method to be valid

Since the shear resistance of PPHC slabs with ﬁlled cores is based on the methods
for PPHC slabs without ﬁlled cores it was not expected to obtain calculated shear
resistances close to the tested shear resistances, but conservative calculated shear
resistances was expected. This was not obtained and it was very surprising that
the shrinkage of concrete potentially has such negative eﬀect on the shear resistance.
Also, the eﬀect of adding stirrups to the ﬁlled cores was surprisingly determined to
have no positive eﬀect on the shear resistance. In fact, the drop in shear resistance
between the specimens without stirrups and the specimens with stirrups could be
due to bad bond conditions but also potentially to the fact that the ﬁlled cores had
started to act like a dowel and thereby introduce a failure mechanism causing the
shear resistance to drop.
It is therefore assessed that the calculation method proposed by DS/EN 1168 (2011)
should be used very carefully and maybe completely removed from the product
standard until the subject has been completely covered.
Industry Approach
By an ongoing dialogue with Spæncom throughout the project period an insight
into the way they design their PPHC slabs was gained. From this dialogue it is
understood that they do not follow the product standard strictly.
When designing the web shear resistance an internal method is used, but they
have started to use the advanced method in DS/EN 1168 (2011), which in the be-
ginning had to be reduced to correlate with their testing results. After an ongoing
adjustment of the concrete composition they have managed to reach the web shear
resistances predicted by the advanced method. It is therefore experienced that the
concrete composition has a huge eﬀect on the shear resistance, hence two diﬀerent
concretes with the same compressive strength can deliver diﬀerent shear resistances.
Anchorage failure is normally not considered by Spæncom when designing a PPHC
slab, since their experience from dozens of tests has showed that this is not a
problem. This is in accordance with Elliott (2014) who experienced that the trans-

None of the calculation methods presented in Chapter 2 were able to predict the
shear capacity, and a large spreading of the main test results without wax has been
observed compared to the reference results and the main results with wax. By
simple equilibrium considerations a new model taking the inﬂuence of the interface

8.1. Discussion
The failure mechanism is for the PPHC slabs with ﬁlled cores very complex as
it does not fail globally at the approximate same load as the PPHC slabs without
ﬁlled cores did. Instead local failures occurred at diﬀerent locations to diﬀerent
loads in each test, hence the global failure develops over a certain load interval.
Therefore the "ﬁrst part" of the model (considering the ﬁlled cores as a dowel)
actually gives reasonable results when the m∆P is added to the shear resistance,
P, after it has failed, as sketched in Figure 8.22.
The "second part" of the model (considering the moment capacity of the cores)
showed that the larger bond stress the lower m∆P. This was not observed, thus
it is assumed that the correct moment distribution has not been obtained, and
thereby that the stress distribution of ‡1 and ‡2 would look diﬀerent than assumed.
A model able to predict the shear capacity better than the one above would have to
be developed by a number of experiments, where the internal stresses and crack for-
mations of the dowel along with the interface bond stresses were to be investigated.
It would be interesting to investigate the extend of y1 since the crack was observed
to develop from the slab end and slowly increasing its length. Furthermore the
transfer of shear forces through the vertical part of the interface, which has been
completely neglected, would also be interesting to investigate and incorporate in a
new model.

Conclusion
Throughout the project a great deal of experiences were acquired, both in the ex-
perimental part and in the theoretical part. Some of the experiences were expected
and some were not.
The testing of the PPHC slabs showed two very diﬀerent behaviours of the slabs
with and without ﬁlled cores. It showed that a much more ductile behaviour of the
PPHC slabs could be achieved by ﬁlling a number of the cores with concrete, but
also that this extra ductility completely vanishes, when the bond stresses build up
in the interface between the PPHC slab and the ﬁlled cores go through zero.
Furthermore an asymmetrical crack development was experienced for the slabs with
ﬁlled cores, while a very symmetrical and fast developing crack formation of the
slabs without ﬁlled cores was experienced. This was surely related to the ductile
and brittle behaviour of the slabs with and without ﬁlled cores respectively.
From the main purpose of the experiments, namely to determine the mean shear
resistance of the slabs, it can be concluded from following obtained shear resistances:
PPHC with ﬁlled cores,
without wax
VRm = 444 kN with a standard deviation of 53 kN
PPHC with ﬁlled cores,
with wax
VRm = 294 kN with a standard deviation of 18 kN
PPHC without ﬁlled cores
VRm = 273 kN with a standard deviation of 25 kN
that a mean increase of the shear resistance of approximately 63% is possible to
achieve by ﬁlling two cores with concrete, but also that this is connected to a rather
large standard deviation. When shrinkage is introduced, simulated by the wax, no
increase of the shear resistance was experienced. Moreover, no eﬀect of adding
stirrups to the cores was observed.
Comparing the testing results to the calculation method in DS/EN 1168 (2011),
prescribed to use in Denmark, and the calculation method in FIB-bulletin 6 (2000)
containing some parts, which has been left out in DS/EN 1168 (2011), diﬀerent
calculated shear resistances were obtained. Both methods predict an anchorage
failure and a shear resistance of 480 kN and 290 kN calculated by DS/EN 1168
(2011) and FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) respectively for PPHC slabs with ﬁlled cores. It
was noticed that DS/EN 1168 (2011) predicts the shear resistance of the PPHC
slabs without wax within 8% while FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) predicts within 35% of the
tested mean shear resistance VRm = 444 kN. Therefore the anchorage calculation
of FIB-bulletin 6 (2000) must be very conservative.
As the existing calculation methods were not able to describe the physics within the

composite structure, a set of new calculation considerations was developed. They
indicate that the ﬁlled cores act as a dowel and that a certain extra shear capacity,
∆P, per ﬁlled core can be calculated and added to the shear capacity of the PPHC
without ﬁlled cores, but that it should be added to the shear-displacement curve of

The overall conclusion to the project is therefore that before the long time ef-
fects on the shear capacity from concrete shrinkage, simulated with wax, have been
fully investigated, an extra shear capacity cannot be achieved by ﬁlling a number
of the cores. Avoiding the interface between the ﬁlled cores and the PPHC slab
by casting both parts together at the same time could be an opportunity to safely
increase the shear capacity, but is not practically allowed by the casting method of
today.

In the project three diﬀerent experiments where a deformation were measured has
been performed; the compressive strength test of the concrete cylinders, the tension
test of the reinforcing bars and the shear test of the PPHC slab. The deformation
has been used to determine the elastic modulus of the concrete cylinders and the re-
inforcing bars and furthermore the internal shear-displacement-curve of the PPHC
slab.
Regarding the measuring of the elastic modulus two diﬀerent so called extensome-

and the reinforcing bars respectively. They measure the axial displacement over 10
cm and 5 cm respectively with a maximum displacement of ± 2.5 mm.

core slab had to be used (Figure E.3). Six deformation measuring devices, DMD
1-DMD 6, were used, two at each support and two at the location of the load.
At the supports deformation measuring devices (DMD 1, DMD 3, DMD 4 and
DMD 6) which were able to measure a deformation up to 5 cm and at the location
of the load deformation measuring devices (DMD 2, DMD 5) which were able to
measure a deformation up to 10 cm were used.

Critical Buckling Moment
In Appendix I the moment capacity of the instability phenomenon lateral torsional
buckling has been considered and in this calculation the critical buckling moment,
Mcr is used. In this Appendix this critical buckling moment is determined both for
the UNP400 proﬁle and the HE300B proﬁle. It is often rather troublesome to de-
termine Mcr, thus the widely used program LTBeam has been used for the purpose.
The program consist of three windows where input parameters are speciﬁed and one
window where the calculation of Mcr is performed. In the following, screen dumps
of the four windows from the determination of Mcr for each proﬁle are provided.
