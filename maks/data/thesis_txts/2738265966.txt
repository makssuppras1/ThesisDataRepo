Executive Summary 
Are you German? If not, you should learn from our experience here. If so, have you been following the news 
in 2023? Then you probably were annoyed by the term "Heizungsgesetz" (Building Energy Act), right? So 
was I. Therefore, let us shed some scientific light on what happened during that legislation process and what 
we can learn from it. 
The background of this work is the need for impactful climate policy on the German federal level, focused 
on the governing parties. Three core aspects influence the studied case, the update of the Building Energy 
Act (“Gebäudenergiegesetz”, GEG): First, the German target to become climate neutral by 2045. Second, 
the coalition agreement from 2021 of the governing parties SPD (Social Democrats), Green Party, and FDP 
(Liberal Party) to decarbonize the heating sector. Third, a need for independence from fossil fuels due to the 
energy crisis caused by the Russian-Ukrainian war. Overall, the GEG caused a heated public debate in 2023 
on how to decarbonize the heating sector. 
The research question, therefore, focuses on the interrelationship between the GEG and the governing parties. 
What narrative components support/hinder a federal climate policy like the GEG in Germany? 
The theoretical structure for this study provides the Narrative Policy Framework (NPF). This is because not 
only facts but also stories influence human and, therefore, political behavior. The NPF analysis uncovers 
(in)congruences between the parties’ narratives about the GEG and points out enablers and barriers. 
However, the narrative focus does not mean this work merely investigates the communication during the GEG 
process. The NPF and the developed recommendations extend beyond that. 
The methodology applied in this work involves expert interviews. Since the GEG is a recent process, 
interviews collect direct impressions from experts involved in or observing it. These experts include politicians 
from the governing parties. 
The analysis emphasizes two key enablers: Firstly, the binding coalition agreement. Secondly, the GEG’s 
holistic orientation by interlinking with the Municipal Heat Planning and funding program in the end. On the 
contrary, the missing alignment between responsible ministries and governing parties, as well as the GEG’s 
complex, not-user-oriented design, hindered it. Negative communication through horror stories, especially 
from the Liberals, insufficient coordination within the governing parties, and underestimating the role of fossil-
based assets slowed down the process. 
In conclusion, this work recommends putting citizens and users at the center of a holistic legislation process. 
In addition, internal agreement and coordination within and between the governing parties and leading 
ministries ensure comprehensible communication. Moreover, the parties should aim for extensive alliances, 
including all key characters, to improve support for such legislation. 
The reflection of the research process illustrates three aspects: surprises, limitations, and potential for future 
research. The author is surprised that the unplanned publication of the GEG draft did not play the key role in 
the process. Furthermore, he expected a clearer strategy from the governing parties to create a holistic GEG 
and a more vital role of end users in the legislation process. Limitations concern especially the expert 
interviews where further perspectives from SPD and the ministries would have extended the findings. In the 
future, research should investigate similar climate policy processes and the interface between the ministry 
and parliament within them and develop emergency instruments to bring a heated debate like the GEG one 
back to a constructive level.

1 Introduction 
In 2023, many German citizens heard the term "heating law" many times. Between February and 
September, the law was an emotional and recurring topic in the news, on the street, and in conversations 
with friends and family. Why is that? Because this law affects us all. It affects how we will have a warm 
home in winter and how we and future generations will live on our planet. Therefore, this law and its 
legislative process offer much to learn from, especially for future climate policy processes. Hence, that 
is the aim of this work. 
This introductory chapter describes the boundaries of this work. In the beginning, 1.1 characterizes how 
climate change, previous climate policies in Germany, and narratives represent significant cornerstones 
of the case, briefly presented in 1.2. This work conducts a case study about the decarbonization of the 
German heating sector 1, intending to uncover narrative relationships as stated in 1.3. Lastly, 1.4 
describes the composition of this document. 
1.1 Framework of German Federal Climate Policy 
The following sections portray an overview of factors relevant to this work that influence German federal 
climate policy. 
1.1.1 
Global Warming and the German Democracy 
Climate change is one of our greatest and most urgent challenges. As of 2023, global energy-related 
emissions are still rising (IEA 2024). This causes two main challenges: First, a global warming humanity 
has not seen before. Between February 2023 and February 2024, our planet hit the threshold of 1.5° 
global warming within 12 months2 for the first time (Poynting 2024). Second, a rapid decarbonization of 
our global economies and societies based on the Paris Agreement (UN 2015). The decarbonization of 
our societies aims to limit global warming. 
 
This means major challenges for politicians and political parties in leading such change. In the German 
democracy (and many others), society and politicians are not on track to limit global warming in line 
with the Paris Agreement (Climate Action Tracker 2023). A wide diversity and hierarchy of actors 
involved in the decarbonization process complicate a fast reduction in GHG emissions: different 
legislative levels (EU, federal, states, municipalities), judicial boundaries (e.g., court decisions by the 
federal constitutional court), economy lobbying (e.g. from industry, agriculture, NGOs), and citizen 
perspectives (e.g. citizens' initiatives for and against renewable energy projects) are complex to align.
 
 
If we zoom into the political sphere of this wider picture, we find a specific challenge for a democracy: 
the governing parties focus on what voters find important (“riding the wave theory”; Klüver and 
Sagarzazu 2016). Research shows that long-term challenges such as global warming are unfavorable 
for humans and, therefore, voter behavior: we tend to focus on what we can see and feel today, not what

politicians in improving their policy process and their political collaboration. To do so, this work uses the 
example of a specific case about decarbonizing the German heating sector (see 1.2). 
1.1.2 
The Energiewende – History and Status Quo of German Climate Policy 
The energy system is a key sector where cooperation is necessary to decrease GHG emissions 
successfully. Cooperation across European countries on bilateral, regional, and European levels is 
essential for the effectful transformation of this system through effective climate policies (Lorenz, Egerer, 
and Gerbaulet 2018, 319, 342). This includes EU-wide and regional cooperation between countries 
(Lorenz, Egerer, and Gerbaulet 2018, 321–22). One example is the North Sea Countries Offshore Grid 
Initiative, which is among ten countries attempting to create a joint offshore grid (Lorenz, Egerer, and 
Gerbaulet 2018, 327).  
The transformation of the German energy system (“Energiewende”), with a focus on electricity, reaches 
back to the 1970s (Kemfert, Oei, and Von Hirschhausen 2018, 377–87). It was about independence 
from oil and nuclear power initially and then extended towards coal-based power (Kemfert, Oei, and 
Von Hirschhausen 2018, 377–87). During the process so far, it overcame the longstanding alignment 
between the energy industry and energy policies (Kemfert, Oei, and Von Hirschhausen 2018, 377–87). 
Subsequently, the fundamental idea became popular to connect large-scale climate action with 
economic growth (Eckersley et al. 2023, 152).  
During the 1990s, political debates started to discuss the coal dependence of the German electricity 
industry in the face of GHG emissions. Germany started to take on an initiative-taking role on the 
international level regarding climate policies. The law on renewable feed-in tariffs from 1990 was the 
foundation for the rise of renewable energies in the German energy system. In 1998, the Green Party

What followed was a population-backed decision in 2011 to phase out the use of nuclear power, 
followed by a consensus to end the use of coal power. In both cases, societal agreement prevailed 
against industry interests. In addition, this shows the holistic dimension of the Energiewende: it is not only 
a revolution in terms of modern technologies and a changing energy economy but also a revolution in 
terms of society and politics. This is because the process aims to make the energy system more 
transparent and allow citizens to (financially) participate in it, e.g., with their solar energy production. 
However, Kemfert et al. criticize the still-existing gap between energy prices for energy-intensive 
industries and households. Such a gap demonstrates the maintained power of these industries to 
influence energy policies. (Kemfert, Oei, and Von Hirschhausen 2018, 377–87)  
Overall, the case of the Energiewende supports individual policy solutions on various levels rather than 
one monocentric solution for an action like decarbonizing the energy sector. These solutions are an 
economically reasonable investment that will pay off. However, compared to other sectors, the 
electricity sector is comparably easy to decarbonize. Sectors like transport, households, industry, and 
agriculture miss roadmaps as clearly as Energiewende’s. (Kemfert, Oei, and Von Hirschhausen 2018, 
377–87) 
 
Most recent federal legislation set the German target for GHG reduction to -65% for 2030 and to -88% 
for 2040 (Bundesregierung 2024). In 2024, the federal environmental agency projected that meeting 
the 2030 target would be possible (Umweltbundesamt 2024, 8). However, challenges remain, especially 
in the hard-to-abate sectors of the mobility system and buildings, especially GHG emissions from traffic, 
which are not decreasing (Umweltbundesamt 2024, 8, 15). The case studied in this work deals with 
decarbonizing one of these challenging sectors, the building and heating sector. Therefore, it stands for 
a continuation of the Energiewende by extending it beyond the electricity sector (see 1.2).  
1.1.3 
Multi-level Climate Governance 
The German climate policy described above, i.e., the case examined in this paper, takes place in a 
complex system of legal levels that interact with each other. Therefore, it is important to address the 
fundamentals of this structure. However, it is a hierarchical system in which federal climate policy sets 
the boundaries for implementation at the state and municipal levels. Only UN5 and EU legislation can 
force the federal level to implement specific climate policies. (Eckersley et al. 2023, 156; Hustedt 2013, 
97, 99) 
EU Level 
Climate policy takes place on different vertical levels of climate governance, especially in Europe. 
Regulatory and non-regulatory measures on the EU level strive to mitigate global warming. Examples

are fixed GHG emission reduction goals, the regulatory EU Emission Trading System (EU ETS), and 
funding programs to foster actions, e.g., in municipalities. (Kemmerzell 2018, 39–40) 
 
The EU started to play a role in climate policy during the 1990s and 2000s, focusing on energy efficiency 
(Kemmerzell 2018, 41). Later, legislation like the “Climate and Energy Package” created binding 
country-level targets for emission reductions and renewable energies (Kemmerzell 2018, 40). Today, 
the EU Green Deal pushes forward the development of Europe as the first net-zero continent in 2050 
(Europäische Kommission 2021). It includes new emission reduction goals such as “Fit for 55”, aiming 
to cut EU GHG emissions by 55% in 2030 (Europäische Kommission 2023). Funding, especially for 
COVID-19 recovery, supports the transformation of the economy and society on different levels in the 
EU member states (European Commission 2024). Beyond that, the EU cannot force its member states 
to take specific climate action (Kemmerzell 2018, 43). Therefore, the effect of EU regulation on lower 
levels, like municipalities, is mostly indirect (Kemmerzell 2018, 45). 
 
On the national level, EU countries independently decide on aspects like their energy mix and specific 
decarbonization strategies. France focused on nuclear power, while Germany phased it out. This leads 
to a non-alignment of the energy system transformation in the EU, anchored on the national levels. At 
the same time, it needs EU-wide cooperation for aspects such as balancing renewable energies 
between regions. A common EU climate policy helps countries like Germany to pursue their 
decarbonization activities on a federal level. (Kemfert, Oei, and Von Hirschhausen 2018, 385–87) 
An example showing the complexity and interactions between different governance levels is the 
“Covenant of Mayors for Climate & Energy” (Covenant of Mayors 2024). This program pushes and 
supports EU cities toward a sustainable future (Covenant of Mayors 2024), bypassing the authority of 
the member states (Kemmerzell 2018, 43–44). At the same time, a French federal policy on local climate 
protection stressed that cities adopt this Covenant of Mayors for Climate & Energy from the EU 
(Kemmerzell 2018, 44 based on Bendlin 2016, 133).  
 
Overall, the interactions between the EU, federal, and municipal levels create high complexity. This 
complexity increases the difficulties (to a varying extent) of implementing effective and efficient climate 
policies on all political levels.  
Federal Level 
Germany and other EU countries develop its strategy and implementation to tackle its GHG emissions 
within the framework and legislative boundaries provided by the EU (Eckersley et al. 2023, 154). An 
important cornerstone for German climate policies represents a ruling by the Federal Constitutional 
Court from 2021: the court forced politicians to adopt more ambitious climate policies to protect the 
freedom of future generations (Bundesverfassungsgericht 2021). This led to adopting the Climate Action 
Act (Deutscher Bundestag 2021). Besides that, German climate policies are based on tight 
collaboration and joint approaches, especially across federal and state governments (Eckersley et al. 
2023, 167–68). An example is the phase-out of coal power generation: it moves 40 billion Euros paid 
by the federal government towards the affected regions, distributed by the responsible states 
(Bundesregierung 2023). To further raise German ambitions, the federal government might need to take 
on the lead because more complex, high-hanging fruits are the next level of measures (Eckersley et al. 
2023, 167–68).

State Level 
In German federalism, the states own significant power over climate policies – like the federal 
government. The “concurrent legislation principle” ensures that states cannot override existing federal 
climate policy. This leads to the situation that most state-relevant climate policies originate from the EU 
and the federal German government. In many cases, like renewable energy, (1) the EU creates a 
directive that (2) the German government translates into federal law, which (3) the states need to 
implement in practice. In addition, the federal government can explicitly pass legislation to the states, 
e.g., allowing them to introduce their climate action acts. That means states cannot set more progressive 
standards than what the federal government defines but can set standards where federal legislation 
stays blank. Eckersley et al. name the example of renewable energy requirements for buildings: a state 
cannot expand requirements regarding energy efficiency beyond federal boundaries. However, a state 
can define such requirements for old buildings not yet covered by federal laws. (Eckersley et al. 2023, 
155–57) 
 
More broadly, research defines the German federalism structure as “cooperative federalism” (e.g., 
Börzel 2003, 5). Different government levels cooperate often quite closely (Müller 1998, 467). An 
example is the strong veto power of the states, institutionalized in the “Bundesrat6” (Eckersley et al. 2023, 
158). At the same time, states can execute different approaches to reach the common objectives 
(Eckersley et al. 2023, 158). The COVID-19 pandemic represented a similar policy case. During the 
pandemic, public discussions arose about whether the differing health protection laws in individual 
states meant high bureaucratic complexity without significant effects on pandemic management 
(Knobbe 2021; Behnke 2020). Others argued that this state-specific approach allowed individual 
measures for individual pandemic situations across the country (Behnke 2020). For climate policy, states 
often adapt their climate policies towards the least ambitious state (Eckersley et al. 2023, 167). At the 
same time, it encourages states to aim for climate policy leadership within Germany (Eckersley et al. 
2023, 168). 
 
Analyzing the impact of these boundaries in practice, Eckersley et al. demonstrate the important 
consequences of German federalism regarding climate policy: 
- 
The (partial) difference between federal and state ambition: while the federal government 
enacted a new climate protection law with 1.75°-compatible ambition (Knopf and Geden 2022, 
13), most of the states still have lower ambitions (Eckersley et al. 2023, 153). 
- 
Differences between the states based on their reliance on a specific type of energy source: 
Eckersley et al. identify five types of states based on their reliance on coal energy, nuclear/solar 
energy, wind energy, imported energy, and city-states. (Eckersley et al. 2023, 159–61) 
- 
Governing parties play a role: states governed by the Green party normally are more ambitious 
(Eckersley et al. 2023, 166). 
- 
Industry lobbying: The state system allows additional entrance points for such activities, 
potentially slowing down climate ambitions from different angles (Eckersley et al. 2023, 166).

- 
Generally, more wealthy states perform better regarding ambitious climate policies (Eckersley 
et al. 2023, 159–61, 166–67). 
In sum, the state level adds another level where climate policy can be strengthened – or weakened. 
Municipal Level 
The municipal level possesses limited power regarding systemic climate action, such as climate targets. 
At the same time, municipalities need to implement various measures in practice. Therefore, they 
represent another cornerstone of effective climate action in Germany. 
 
The German federal government started supporting climate action on the municipal level with 
information guidelines in 1997 (Kemmerzell 2018, 44). Financial support, however, depends on specific 
funding programs until today. One example is the national climate protection initiative, which funds local 
projects, e.g., the energy efficiency of street lighting (Nationale Klimaschutzinitiative 2020). 
In this 
case, the federal government bypasses the states to ensure direct funding of municipalities (Eckersley 
et al. 2023, 157). In other cases, cities often use EU, federal, and state programs as an important funding 
source for climate policies (Kemmerzell 2018, 50). 
 
Besides that, mitigation and adaptation of global warming is a so-called “voluntary task” for German 
municipalities (Eckersley et al. 2023, 155). To strengthen municipal climate action, a state would first 
need to put legislation in place to assign these tasks to the municipalities and supply them with funding 
(Eckersley et al. 2023, 155, 157). The absence of this reliable funding leads to a division between 
German municipalities - some lack financial resources for these activities and need to focus on their 
mandatory duties. In contrast, others are more ambitious (Eckersley et al. 2023, 157). Lobbyist groups 
like Klima-Bündnis, therefore, demand that climate policies be made mandatory for German 
municipalities. This would lead to higher financial capacities for the corresponding activities, such as re-
designing the local transport system. At the same time, it would decrease dependence on federal 
funding programs. (Klima-Bündnis 2022, 2) 
 
In addition, German municipalities play another significant role in transforming the country. Meanwhile, 
they depict the place where climate protection is implemented and are powerful in blocking progress. 
For example, citizen initiatives on a local level have the power to delay or stop important projects like 
building new electricity grids through lawsuits. Solving this common procedure represents one of the key 
challenges for further climate action in Germany. (Reusswig, Lass, and Bock 2020, 142–43) 
 
 
Overall, significant power for effective climate action in Germany lies with the EU and federal level. 
These levels decide on the general level of ambition and provide resources for implementation. 
Furthermore, states and municipalities depend on the framework provided by the EU and federal levels 
but maintain relevant freedom in organizing – and blocking – the respective climate action. It is 
important to keep this in mind as this work progresses: Implementing the case presented in 1.2 requires 
considerable support from the municipalities. 
1.1.4 
Climate Policy within a Narrative-Based Environment 
To implement the necessary climate action described in 1.1.1 and relevant for the following case study, 
communication is a key element of human interaction and, therefore, a crucial part of climate policy 
processes. Following Fløttum and Gjerstad, using the right language is an essential part of

communicating global warming successfully (Fløttum and Gjerstad 2017, 1). In addition, the statement 
“We tell ourselves stories in order to live” from the novel author Joan Didion (Barthwal-Datta, Krystalli, 
and Shepherd 2022, 465 on Joan Didion 1979) summarizes the role of stories in human communication.  
This essential role of stories and narratives also accounts for political science (Barthwal-Datta, Krystalli, 
and Shepherd 2022, 465) and the field of climate policies (Donald, Young, and Mach 2022, 2). 
Therefore, narratives are highly important for making sense of (in) the actions and perspectives of 
politicians and other actors in this field (Barthwal-Datta, Krystalli, and Shepherd 2022, 466). This key 
role is why narratives of climate policy in the governing German parties represent an essential point of 
departure to improve these policies. In Germany, the governing parties possess highly varying narratives 
about what climate policy means: e.g., do we need to change our daily behavior, or does a holistic

2.1 Role of Narratives 
Human beings tell each other stories as an essential part of life, including politics (Barthwal-Datta, 
Krystalli, and Shepherd 2022, 465). Over the past years, narratives have become even more relevant 
to our society and political science (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 161). They are essential aspects of 
communication about the current major societal transformations (Leipprand, Flachsland, and Pahle 
2015, 2; Sangalang and Bloomfield 2018, 594). On a global scale, this is visible in the upcoming US 
elections as a polarized battle between Joe Biden and Donald Trump (Wolf 2024). On the German 
scale, this was visible in heated discussions about the “Last generation before the tipping points” (Letzte 
Generation 2024), blocking roads to fight for lower GHG emissions while others argued their right to 
individual freedom. Actors like politicians, lobbyists, and the media apply narratives strategically to 
shape this political landscape (Barthwal-Datta, Krystalli, and Shepherd 2022, 466). Narrative methods 
are also used to improve participation and inclusiveness of public policy debates on energy transitions 
(Miller et al. 2015, 8–9). 
Research about climate change communication indicates that a compelling narrative works regardless 
of political preference (Sangalang and Bloomfield 2018, 583, 594–95). Scientists suggest instead 
focusing on solution-oriented “win-win narratives” to solve challenges of global warming instead of 
communication “doom and gloom narratives” (Hinkel et al. 2020, 503). Furthermore, various 
researchers showed the importance of “language” and “frames” for the communication of global 
warming (Fløttum and Gjerstad 2017, 1–2). At the same time, it is essential not to conjure up a certain 
future by using a narrative like the “glacier-ruins narrative” describing a future without glaciers (Jackson 
2015, 489). 
 
Altogether, narratives are crucial to understanding how an individual or a group interprets a situation or 
a challenge like global warming (Patterson and Monroe 1998, 330; Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 
2014, 1). Jones et al. even argue that understanding these underlying narratives “has become an 
existential concern” (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 161). “Strategic narratives” are a powerful way of 
communicating someone’s interpretation of a situation: politicians strategically include their “interests 
and goals” in these narratives by describing how the future could look like and how to achieve it 
(Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 2014, 5). Therefore, the difference between a narrative and a 
strategic narrative is – as entailed by the name – the strategic approach of creating and shaping a 
narrative in a way that helps to achieve strategic goals. In addition, it is also essential to understand the 
aim of an actor who is forming a narrative: samples are “agenda setting, legitimation, diverting attention, 
securing acquiescence, enhancing popularity, and mobilization” (Meskimen, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 
2014, 8).

A general challenge to researching narratives represents the strong interrelations between narratives, 
their effects, and their environment (O’Loughlin, Miskimmon, and Roselle 2017, 25). Another one is 
characters without a potential lack of clear goals and strategy for a narrative and measurable effects 
of applying a narrative (O’Loughlin, Miskimmon, and Roselle 2017, 25). 
Dealing with these challenges is where the Narrative Policy Framework (NPF) comes into play. The NPF 
investigates the influence of narratives on public discussions and “how they reflect policy beliefs” 
(Cairney and Weible 2014, 368). It analyzes the role of narratives in political processes and is a 
relatively modern framework with more than 20 years of research (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 183). 
Understanding the narratives is highly relevant to making sense of policy processes and has helped to 
explain them better (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 162, 183). The NPF makes the political process less 
complex and analyzes it as a heuristic (McBeth, Lybecker, and Sargent 2022, 476). Therefore, it 
represents the ideal framework for this work because it can reach a broader understanding of climate 
change narratives (Fløttum and Gjerstad 2017, 12–13) on a federal German policy level. Specifically, 
the public debate about the GEG in Germany depended heavily on different narrative aspects. For 
example, specific media reports focused on the individual consequences of the law for housing owners 
(Rupprecht, Schäfer, and Sedlmayer 2023; Rupprecht, Schäfer, and Hellemann 2023). This made it 
difficult for other actors, like the law-developing minister, to convey their message. Subsequently, a 
journalist attested to the three governing parties' different narratives regarding their climate policies 
(Krohn 2024b). Therefore, NPF is the only framework to analyze the narrative interrelationships within 
this debate, specifically by dismantling them into individual narrative components. Thus, it remains vital 
throughout this research process to look beyond the perspective of narratives to realize narrative-
independent policy challenges. 
Narratives in the German Energiewende 
Narratives in German climate policies have been the subject of scientific investigation before. In their 
analysis of narratives in Germany about the Energiewende (German transition of the energy system), 
Leipprand et al. discovered two main narratives, with each three to four storylines10. The narrative of 
“Energy Transformation” illustrated the picture of a fully renewable energy system in Germany. Storylines 
contained aspects such as positive stories improving the triple bottom line of the system, a decentralized 
system with higher participation of society instead of large corporates, and the chance of becoming a 
global leader in this field. The second narrative of “Energy Mix” focused on villain stories about risks on 
the triple bottom line, the market situation questioning the costs of the EEG, and the call for international 
instead of national action to solve global warming. Leipprand et al. found that speeches of the party 
Christian Union (CDU/CSU) and the FDP switched from the Energy Mix narrative towards the Energy 
Transformation narrative between 1989 and 2011. However, they kept mentioning the Energy Mix 
narrative consistently. Meanwhile, the Green Party and the SPD increasingly adopted the Energy 
Transformation narrative. The SPD also decreased its use of the Energy Mix narrative in 2011. Thus, 
these developments do not necessarily show increasing support of the Energiewende by CDU/CSU and 
FDP: analysis of the storylines shows their increasing use of economic villains (see 2.3) in their speeches.

These parties especially mentioned the additional costs of the feed-in tariffs, impacting low-income

After their analysis from 2015, narratives changed: “Necessary technologies for the energy

Energiewende. Joint statements were aimed to prove that German society could not implement specific 
climate policies. These narratives claimed that the technology for use cases like energy storage was not 
ready yet. From a technology perspective, these arguments were not correct – but used to convince 
actors to act less. (Kemfert, Oei, and Von Hirschhausen 2018, 383–84)  
Furthermore, Kalt identified two narratives about the phase-out of coal power in Germany: the “anti-
coal narrative” and the “pro-coal counter-narrative”. Within the “anti-coal narrative,” policy demands 
spanned from more green growth towards more fundamental expectations of a post-capitalistic system. 
Meanwhile, they shared the common goal of phasing out the use of coal in Germany and set this in the 
context of a better future world with a stable climate. Over recent years, these actors started to point 
out the perspective of fairness on a German level, with young generations, villages, and nature being 
protected from further expansion of coal mining. On the contrary, the “pro-coal counter-narrative” 
focused mainly on job security. (Kalt 2021, 1140–45) 
 
Altogether, the relevant role of narratives in German discourse is the right starting point for this work's 
investigation of narratives in the GEG process further. This work builds on existing analyses of narratives 
in German climate policy and adds a new perspective. The NPF serves as the best theory to do so.

How Narratives Work 
The processes behind narratives and how they work have not been a focus of the NPF. Consulting the 
foundations of NPF research, three approaches exist to communication with strategic narratives 
(Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 2017, 9–10): 
- 
“Formation”: To understand how an agent creates a strategic narrative, one needs to know their 
“strategic goal and types of communication”. This could be something like solving an existing 
problem like inflation or focusing more on the future, like receiving a better election result at an 
election year distant. (Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 2014, 8) 
- 
“Projection”: Analyzing the projection of a narrative within our modern online society is essential. 
It complements the formation process or counteracts it with a focus on the user perspective. 
Aspects like broad access to information and social networks, shorter time-frames between 
events and media coverage, and wider variety and competition of available narratives shape 
how people understand a narrative. (Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 2014, 11) 
- 
“Reception”: Recent technologies forming new user behavior have changed the reception of 
narratives over recent years. With its just-in-time distribution of information and new interactivity 
on social media, the internet represents two significant factors within this development. In 
addition, “cultural context” and “political context” are regional aspects influencing how people 
receive narratives. (Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 2014, 11–12) 
Therefore, Jones et al. propose the two “narrative mechanisms” to explain narrative effects (M. D. Jones 
et al. 2023, 168–69): In both cases, a narrative activates specific reactions in the audience. These 
reactions build on “cognitive heuristics,” which the Homo Narrans use as a boundedly rational human 
being. These two mechanisms are: (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 168–69)

Core Assumptions 
Following Jones et al., the NPF is constructed around five assumptions. They serve as a starting point for 
applying NPF in research (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 162–63; 2022, 138–39): 
- 
“Social Construction”: Human beings have different perspectives on the world and how it 
functions. The term describes these “variable meanings (…) associated with public policy”. 
- 
“Bounded Relativity”: The different social constructions are “bounded” to “belief systems, 
ideologies, norms”. Therefore, they are relative to what an individual or group is bound to. 
- 
“Generalizable Structural Elements”: NPF assumes that it is possible to analyze general 
narrative “structures and substructures (…) across contexts”. 
- 
“Three Levels of Analysis”: The NPF categorizes narratives into “micro (individual), meso (group 
and coalition), and macro (cultural and institutional) level”. For more details, see 2.3. 
- 
“Homo Narrans”: For humans, narratives represent a key element in their way of thinking, 
behaving, and communicating (M. D. Jones et al. 2022, 139). The Homo Narrans is the 
centerpiece of the NPF. They are a boundedly rational12 human being (M. D. Jones et al. 2022, 
139). 
NPF research must comply with these assumptions. This work can only ensure a consistent research 
process based on scientific NPF fundamentals if it reflects and accepts these assumptions (M. D. Jones 
et al. 2022, 139). 
 
This work agrees with these five assumptions as a research ground layer. Human beings, including those 
involved in the GEG process, are boundedly rational individuals (Selten 1999, 2–3) who depend on 
stories (Barthwal-Datta, Krystalli, and Shepherd 2022, 465). Stories and narratives about the GEG 
process demonstrate that this behavior accounts for individuals and parties within the meso level (e.g., 
Pinzler 2023). Furthermore, generalizable components of narratives should be a valuable and 
necessary structure to analyze these different perspectives and derive transferable recommendations 
(see 5.4).

Policy Narrative Form 
Policy narratives take place within “public policy problems”. Four different components create such a 
narrative: (based on M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 163–65; 2022, 143–46; Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and

(1) “Setting”: It describes the “spatial, temporal and ideational” location of characters within the 
narrative (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 163–65; 2022, 143–46). “Focusing events” mean relevant 
events that influence the political importance of a topic (University of Massachusetts Lowell et 
al. 2021, 169 based on Birkland 1998, 53). 
(2) “Characters”: There are three main characters of a narrative: “victim”, “villain”, and “hero”. 
They can be a person or a group or represent a value. The villain causes the policy problem 
and threatens to harm the victim. Meanwhile, the hero helps to reduce the harm or entirely solves 
the policy problem. (Jones et al. 2023, 164, based on Jones and McBeth 2010). For climate 
change narratives, Jones proved heroes to be effectful characters (M. D. Jones 2014, 3). 
However, these characters are usually missing within climate action narratives (Fløttum and 
Gjerstad 2017, 13). 
 
If a character does not fulfill the requirements for one of the three types but still plays a 
responsible role in the process, the author introduces the role of a “key character.” 
 
(3) “Plot”: It describes how the characters relate to each other and within the specific setting (M. 
D. Jones et al. 2023, 165). Five sections describe a climate change narrative in more detail: 
“initial situation”, “complication”, “reaction”, “resolution”, and “final situation” (Fløttum and 
Gjerstad 2017, 3 on Adam 2008).

Policy Narrative Content 
The assumption of “narrative relativity” explains the challenge with narratives: each narrative is unique 
depending on the specific “context” (M. Jones, Shanahan, and McBeth 2014, 4–5). Therefore, Policy 
Narrative Content aims to deal with this challenge. Structuring a narrative into two respective categories 
allows the operationalizable analysis of the individual context (M. Jones, Shanahan, and McBeth 2014, 
4–5):  
(1) “Belief systems” exist to identify science-based “categories of people”. People of one category 
use similar ways of thinking to construct their view of the world socially. Understanding the belief 
system of a particular group of people is vital to explaining how this group understands a 
specific narrative. (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 165–66) 
(2) “Narrative strategies” explain how one uses the narrative components to achieve a specific 
goal. Following Jones, relevant NPF strategies are the following: (based on M. D. Jones et al. 
2023, 166–67) 
- 
“Scope of conflict”: Who and what is (not) involved? 
- 
“Narrative Causal Strategy”: Who is responsible for a conflict? 
- 
“Devel-Angel-Solidarity (DAS-Shifts)”: Who is to blame for a conflict (devil shift)? Who 
can solve a conflict (angel shift)? Who is harmed by a conflict (solidarity shift)? 
“Level of Analysis” 
While analyzing narrative form and content, NPF offers three interlinked levels of analysis, ranging from 
“Macro” over “Meso” down to “Micro level” (M. D. Jones et al. 2022, 140–41; Shanahan, Jones, and 
McBeth 2018, 334). The macro level analyzes narratives on global and national levels, the meso level 
on groups, and the micro level on single persons (M. D. Jones et al. 2022, 140–41; Shanahan, Jones, 
and McBeth 2018, 334). If such narratives on different levels are inconsistent, they can hinder each 
other’s effectiveness (Miskimmon, O’Loughlin, and Roselle 2017, 8). 
 
For this work, the meso level is the relevant space. It focuses on the group level during the GEG process 
(Shanahan, Jones, and McBeth 2018, 334). Examples could be parliamentary groups of the parties, 
lobbyist groups, and other interest groups. Therefore, this represents the right level for the presented 
research question that aims to generate more universally valid insights into the GEG policy process. On 
the contrary, a micro-level focus would be too narrow for this work13. Such an individual perspective 
would be too individual to provide findings on a broader level to learn from the GEG process narratives. 
Nevertheless, there might be overlaps between micro and meso levels. These are justified in the

“Narrator and Audience” 
The relationship between narrators and the audience of a narrative is an essential factor in its 
effectiveness (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 167). In this case study, the parliamentary groups of the three 
parties represent both (depending on the specific situation) narrators and the audience to each other.15 
Related to the research questions in 1.3, “narrator congruence with the audience” is a specifically 
relevant aspect here (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 167). Politicians and citizens, as a second potential 
audience, can better identify with a narrator sharing their perspective and, therefore, better accept their 
presented narrative. This leads to a vital role for the narrator, whether the audience listens to a narrative 
and whether it convinces the audience. With this, empathy seems to play a role. Scientists proved this 
for climate-change-skeptic people, where a farmer was the more convincing narrator than a mechanic 
(McBeth, Lybecker, and Sargent 2022, 477–78, 490–93; Idaho State University et al. 2021). 
 
At the same time, the ideological beliefs of the audience are also important. Research about recycling 
showed that ideology can play a role in how the audience perceives a narrative. How the narrator frames 
the story influences how convincing it is. For more details, see earlier sections of this sub-chapter. 
(Lybecker, McBeth, and Kusko 2013, 326–28) 
 
The described narrative components of form, content, meso level, and strategies explain the NPF's 
relevant interrelationships and modes of operation applied to the GEG case. Therefore, the analysis in 
4 relies on these aspects. 
2.4 Development of NPF Research Design 
Choosing the fitting research design marks a significant step towards answering the research questions. 
Here, research within the field of NPF offers two options: “experimental” and “non-experimental designs”. 
Experimental design compares the effects of an experiment between control groups or before and after 
a “narrative treatment” on a singular group. Non-experimental designs apply in other situations, e.g., 
for “case studies” analyzing a specific case and how narratives influence such cases. (Shanahan, Jones, 
and McBeth 2018, 337–38) 
 
Often, NPF researchers use case studies on the meso level16. This is why this work also chooses a meso-
level case study to analyze the policy process of the German GEG and the involved parties in depth 
(see 1.2). This non-experimental design does not compare the GEG process to different subjects. As no 
control group is available, this case would not serve the purpose of an experimental design. (based on 
Shanahan, Jones, and McBeth 2018, 338)

3 Expert Interviews as Research Methodology 
Building on the chosen research design of a case study, the research methodology provides data as the 
base for the research analysis. This is why 3.1 first elaborates on the impactful choice of expert interviews 
as methodology. Subsequently, the experts represent the essential data source for the further process. 
Therefore, 3.2 describes their selection in detail. Afterward, the interview guide in 3.3 needs to request 
information from the interviewees that they can provide and contribute to answering the research 
questions. Based on these preparations, 3.4 amplifies that the expert interviews took place well-
prepared and in similar conditions. Lastly, 3.5 explains the analytical approach, including the coding of 
the interviews. Based on these foundations, the analysis starts in the next chapter. 
3.1 Choice of Research Methodology 
The research methodology generally aims to gather data to answer and analyze the research question. 
This should happen so that other researchers can contest whether they would draw the same or different 
conclusions from the data. (Shanahan, Jones, and McBeth 2018, 338) 
The scientific methodology serves to generate qualitative data as the cornerstone of this analysis. 
Therefore, it needs to collect hands-on insights into the field of the GEG legislation process. To succeed, 
the scientific methodology needs to fulfill three criteria: 
(1) Gather data that is up to date about this recent policy process.17 
(2) Win specific in-depth insights as a foundation for further analysis (Kaiser 2014, 30–31). 
(3) Integrate experts who were actively involved in the policy process. 
This work could use expert “interviews” or further “content analysis” of other sources to gain valuable 
data about the GEG within the chosen non-experimental research design. A heterogeneous group of 
experts would help identify differences and similarities between the policy narratives for interviews. 
However, content analysis of other sources is the most crucial method within NPF research. It would 
investigate public information like “media accounts, social media, and speeches”. (Shanahan, Jones, 
and McBeth 2018, 338–40) 
 
The methodology of expert interviews is fulfilling the three previous criteria well: 
(1) Interviewing experts involved in the policy process allows immediate translation of their 
experiences into this research. It happens without the delay of other researchers analyzing the 
GEG process, which requires time to pass. This indicates an “explorative expert interview” 
(Kaiser 2014, 29).  
(2) Interviews as a qualitative methodology create in-depth insights into the topic (M. D. Jones et 
al. 2022, 151). This is specifically relevant to gain knowledge of informalized processes (Kaiser 
2014, 26) besides standardized parliament procedures.

(3) Expert interviews allow the collection of direct expert knowledge from people involved in the

In addition, the direct interaction with the experts through interviews could allow the interviewees to

This work uses expert interviews to analyze the German GEG's policy process. This represents a popular 
approach for NPF research (Schlaufer et al. 2022, 260; M. D. Jones et al. 2022, 151).  
At the same time, paying attention to the risks of interviews for the research process is essential. This 
work focuses on four essential risks: 
- 
In the presented case, one risk is that about a year has passed between the most active phase 
of the policy process and the expert interviews for this work. Experts, e.g., politicians, typically 
have a busy working schedule, dealing with several different topics every week. Memories about 
process details can get lost over time, especially with new topics calling for the experts' attention. 
The pre-briefing and beginning of the interview should address these circumstances by 
reminding the experts about essential milestones.  
 
- 
Another risk is that the time availability of parliament members and other renowned experts is 
often limited, leaving questions that experts would eventually be available to answer. 
- 
Furthermore, interviews always contain the risk of false information, whether provided 
intentionally or not. Politicians, especially, might fear public or political consequences when 
providing information that differs from the official party's position. This can lead to a reduced 
provision of information by them. (Kaiser 2014, 33–34) 
- 
The unforeseeable dynamics of an interview represent another aspect, making the results of 
interviews challenging to compare. (Kaiser 2014, 33–34) 
In sum, expert interviews provide a promising approach to gathering insights into the GEG process as a 
foundation for further analysis. The interview guide should address the risks of that methodology.

3.3 Preparation of Interview Guide 
The interviews should aim to uncover the narrative components of our GEG case (Shanahan, Jones, and 
McBeth 2018, 338–39). The interview guide translates the research questions (see 1.3) into specific 
questions for the experts (Kaiser 2014, 52). Therefore, it represents the starting point for gathering the 
new knowledge necessary to answer the research questions (Kaiser 2014, 52). 
Subsequently, a well-prepared interview guide is essential for gaining valuable insights from the 
interviews and counteracting the risks of interviews (see 3.1) as best as possible (Kaiser 2014, 34–35). 
Kaiser characterizes this as an “interview-guide-based expert interview” (Kaiser 2014, 34–35). In such 
interviews, more direct questions create more reliable data while their purpose might be evident to the 
respondents (Shanahan, Jones, and McBeth 2018, 338–39). Instead, fewer direct questions allow more 
open responses, leaving more room for interpretation (Shanahan, Jones, and McBeth 2018, 338–39). 
To combine both, interviews within NPF research often are semi-structured, allowing flexibility during the 
interviews (M. D. Jones et al. 2022, 151). 
 
Quality criteria for expert interviews reflect transparency about the theory-based research process and 
the researcher's unbiasedness toward (unexpected) results (Kaiser 2014, 9). Ideally, interviews take 
between 1.5 and 2 hours (Kaiser 2014, 52). However, based on the restricted time availability of 
politicians and renowned climate policy experts, the author requested interviews between 30 and 45 
minutes for this work.

Guiding Questions 
Guiding questions lead through the interview and structure it. They ensure that the interview covers all 
necessary topics and their timing. Beyond that, the questions guide from more general topics towards 
specific ones within the research question. This helps the expert to dive deep into the field. In the case 
of this work, two similar but slightly adjusted interview guides include broad questions for all experts and 
specific questions for the individual field of expertise (politicians vs non-politicians). During the interview, 
the interviewer can deviate from the original guide, serving the goal of gathering individual knowledge 
from each expert. In addition, the interview guide documents the researcher's knowledge to show the 
interviewee their expertise and enable an in-depth discussion. It also contains information about the 
research itself and data protection. (Kaiser 2014, 53–55, 67) 
 
In detail, the interview guide of this work requests information about the GEG process along with the 
structure of narrative form and narrative content of the NPF (see 2.3). It intends to deconstruct and 
analyze the underlying party narratives and focuses on the meso level19 of the process. The complete 
interview guide of this work is documented at 9.  
3.4 Conduction of Interviews 
To conduct the interviews, the author requested expert interviews with a brief email, including a project 
briefing document. These requests were made between February 16th and April 22nd. In case of no 
reply, the author followed up with a second email after two to four weeks and a phone call, if needed. 
In total, the author requested 55 expert interviews. 
 
Twelve of the requests led to interviews, which took place between April 15th and May 6th, 2024. They

Common approaches for content analysis represent “conceptual” and “relational analysis”. The first 
option investigates the presence and frequency of specific concepts within the expert interview. The 
second option looks into how different concepts relate to each other within the interview. (Columbia 
University 2024) 
 
To answer the research questions of this work, a combination of conceptual and relational analysis fits

Rules for Coding 
Coding of the transcripts is necessary to combine conceptual and relational analysis. It analyses the

describe the respective component. Chapter 4 documents this comprehensive analysis.

The present chapter builds on the data gathered from the expert interviews (see Table 1) to analyze the 
process based on the research questions (see 1.3). It, therefore, aspires to discover how congruent the 
GEG narratives of the three governing parties were and which enablers and barriers the experts 
experienced to make such climate policy happen. 
To proceed, the chapter analyzes the coding tree (see Figure 3) built from the NPF components. This 
integrated structure ensures consistency in chapters 2, 3, 4, and 5. First, 4.1 starts with the narrative form 
to describe and study the circumstances of the GEG narratives. Next, 4.2 dives into the parties’ narrative 
content by examining their belief systems and strategies. Each sub-chapter about one NPF component22

4.1 Narrative Form 
The present sub-chapter analyzes the narrative form of the GEG process, its setting, the parties’ moral 
of the story, the relevant characters, and the plot. 
4.1.1 
Setting 
The experts point out that three essential circumstances describe the setting where the GEG process 
took place. First, they analyze the structure of the German heating sector. Second, they refer to the 
political situation in Germany, which influenced how the GEG process developed. Third, past climate 
action in the heating sector combines the two previous aspects. 
German Heating Sector 
Germany was the most gas-dependent country in Europe before the Russian-Ukrainian war (Herrmann 
2024). Most heating systems today depend on burning natural gas (BMWK 2024). Therefore, many 
characters during the GEG process (see 4.1.3) were interested in keeping their business models alive 
(Herrmann 2024). 
 
Moreover, the setting of the heating sector was complicated: about 22 million buildings existed in 
Germany, and more than 16 million were single- and double-family homes (Bergmann 2024). Especially 
those over 50 years old with a high energy footprint who were challenged to decarbonize (Bergmann 
2024). It would have been necessary to renovate more than every second of these old buildings to 
increase their energy efficiency significantly (Mindrup 2024). This would not have been possible until 
2045 (Mindrup 2024). In addition, financially less powerful people, like young families and older people, 
lived in these houses (Bergmann 2024). A solely market-based approach for the heating sector would 
have caused significant social challenges in German society due to its unequal distribution of wealth 
(Bergmann 2024).

The Political Situation of the Governing Coalition 
In general, the GEG process happened during challenging political times. One challenge was the 
Russian war against Ukraine, which started in 2022 (bpd.de 2024). It represented an impactful focusing 
event because it caused significant political and economic challenges that the BMWK and the three 
governing parties needed to deal with simultaneously (Bergmann 2024). A second challenge embodied 
the German debt limit. The limit restricted the scope for financial and governmental support to 
implement the new GEG rules from the beginning (Herrmann 2024). A third challenge displayed the 
politics and economy’s focus on short-term actions without looking at long-term consequences like the 
rising costs of fossil-based heating within the next years (N. Höhne 2024). In sum, Bergman characterizes 
the situation as follows: 
„I simply think that this new constellation of the traffic light government was completely 
overwhelmed by the crises that came their way.” 
Kai Bergmann 
Since 2021, the Social Democratic Party, the Green Party, and the Liberal Party (“Ampel-Regierung”; 
traffic light government) have governed Germany (bpd.de 2021). The decarbonization of the heating 
sector played a significant role during the coalition negotiations in 2021 (Koch 2024), which, therefore, 
represented another focusing event for the decarbonization of the heating sector. Furthermore, the three 
parties contained different levels of power due to their different number of parliament members. The 
SPD had 207, the Green Party 117, and the FDP 91 parliament members (Deutscher Bundestag 2024c). 
This distribution led to varying numbers of parliament members from each party in the technical 
committees, causing different levels of expertise and capacity for a topic like the GEG (in der Beek 2024; 
Koch 2024).  
Climate Action in the German Heating Sector 
Zooming onto the heating sector, the GEG represented a cornerstone on the German path to GHG 
neutrality, building on the Climate Action Law (N. Höhne 2024; Bergmann 2024; Kochskämper 2024; 
Krohn 2024a; Schneller 2024). Within the Climate Action Law, the building sector was one of the most 
significant GHG sources and challenging to decarbonize, while little time remained for the necessary 
transition (Bergmann 2024; Kochskämper 2024; Krohn 2024a). The government’s initiative to fully 
decarbonize the heating sector started with the challenging circumstance of only having 22 years left 
till climate neutrality in 2045 (Krohn 2024a). The former version24 of the GEG banned new oil- and gas-
based heating from 2026 (Haberle 2020; Köhler 2024), which was not enough. To decarbonize the 
sector, the GEG focused on the source of the GHG emissions, which was the heating system (N. Höhne 
2024). All systems needed to be GHG neutral by 2045; therefore, it was necessary (due to their lifetime) 
to start replacing them now (N. Höhne 2024) instead of focusing on energy efficiency first (Bergmann 
2024)   
To do so, the two ministers, Habeck and Geywitz, announced necessary measures to reduce the building 
sector’s emissions in 2022 after the sector had missed its GHG targets (Bergmann 2024). Therefore, the 
German initiative for the GEG appeared quite late compared to other countries (Kochskämper 2024). 
For decades, GHG emission reductions were not relevant figures in this sector (Krohn 2024a).

This setting required the parties to define further how to implement the necessary decarbonization of the 
heating sector while shaping the transition socially equitably (Bergmann 2024).  
4.1.2 
Moral 
The moral of a policy narrative, 2023) describes why the governing parties wanted to update the GEG 
and which aspects of the law were important to them. The following sections demonstrate common goals 
and fundamental differences. 
SPD 
The experts possess two perspectives on whether a clear moral of the SPD existed. On the one hand, 
party-external experts question the existence of a clear moral for the GEG. Höhne describes a relatively 
passive SPD whose vision and policy solutions for the GEG became unclear (N. Höhne 2024). Krohn 
misses a clear social-democratic idea for climate action in Germany (Krohn 2024a). On the other hand, 
the party-internal experts describe that a moral existed. For the SPD, fossil fuels, including natural gas, 
popular for heating, needed to be phased out (Mindrup 2024). To do so, creating a binding agreement 
was an important motivation for the Social Democratic Party to support regulatory law (Schneller 2024). 
Such a law supplemented the existing CO2 price (Mindrup 2024). On the path there, the SPD aimed to 
minimize the impacts of the energy transition on the citizens' daily lives (Krohn 2024a; Schneller 2024). 
This was why they required the municipalities to define their future heating infrastructure first (see 4.1.4) 
before building owners needed to move (Köhler 2024; Schneller 2024). In addition, the Social 
Democrats intended to protect and support employees throughout the GEG process (Krohn 2024a), and 
stabilizing tenant rents also played a role (Mindrup 2024). Implementing the GEG adjusted to individual 
financial strength represented another focus of the SPD (Bergmann 2024). However, the question 
remained whether the transition dimension would allow people not to experience significant changes in 
their everyday lives (Krohn 2024a). 
Looking at the adopted GEG, an only-SPD-made GEG would have differed in certain parts only. In 
principle, such GEG would have contained the same deadlines. About the technical requirements, its 
focus on (blue) hydrogen and biomass would have been stronger. Besides that, the social balance would 
have improved: the funding program would have been more differentiated to support low-income 
housing owners. (Schneller 2024) 
Green Party 
The Green Party naturally had the most substantial focus on climate action. It wanted to implement this 
focus in the GEG by regulating technologies and social equity.

perspective, he found this a dangerous distraction and a waste of energy (Herrmann 2024). Limiting

Saleh 2024). Furthermore, the law becomes active later than the coalition agreement stated (Herrmann 
2024). Instead of 2024, the binding rules will be activated in 2026 and 2028, when municipalities need 
to have their KWP ready (Herrmann 2024). The Greens would have liked to prevent this delay.

(In)congruences of the Parties 
All parties shared a common moral for the GEG: their coalition agreement from 2021 was the critical 
starting point to revise and update the law (Bergmann 2024; SPD, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and FDP 
2021, 70). They intended to implement the German target of climate neutrality (Mindrup 2024; 
Herrmann 2024; Köhler 2024), representing a thematic framing (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 178). Moreover, 
the geo-political developments after the agreement formed a second shared moral. The Russian-
Ukrainian war, followed by an energy crisis in Germany due to high natural gas dependence, called for 
an earlier start of the GEG (Bergmann 2024; N. Höhne 2024; Kochskämper 2024; Krohn 2024a). This 
should help to depend more on renewables and less on gas (Bergmann 2024; N. Höhne 2024; 
Kochskämper 2024; Krohn 2024a). Thus, the GEG needed to clarify how to implement a share of 65% 
renewable energy for heating systems in practice (Köhler 2024). In a later stage, the parties agreed that 
the KWP should clarify the local future heating infrastructure first to reduce requirements towards the 
citizens further27 (Schneller 2024; Köhler 2024; Krohn 2024a). 
 
Nevertheless, Bergmann questions whether all three parties approached this with a clear moral 
(Bergmann 2024).

Narrative Enablers 
The commonalities of the three parties justify one enabler for an effectful GEG climate policy: the parties 
share a common starting point based on the coalition agreement, increasing coalitional glue and 
requiring them to implement the 65% rule (SPD, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and FDP 2021, 70). This rule 
represents the thematic framing of contributing to climate neutrality. The 65% requirement leaves limited 
room for disagreements, e.g., about the allowed technologies. The Liberal statements (Köhler 2024; 
Koch 2024) demonstrate that the coalition agreement was a core aspect of why the coalition agreed 
on the new GEG. 
Narrative Barriers 
On the side of the barriers, fundamentally different morals hinder successful climate policy. In the 
present case, preferences ranged from strict regulatory law mainly allowing heat pumps (Herrmann 
2024; Taher Saleh 2024) to no specific need for a law to enable maximum freedom (Köhler 2024; in 
der Beek 2024). According to the Liberals, the publicized draft did not fulfill the criteria of technology 
openness, which the governing parties aligned on in the coalition agreement (in der Beek 2024).

4.1.3 
Characters 
The following characters played roles with various levels of relevance, influence, and presence in the 
GEG process. This sub-chapter describes the three governing parties’ roles and then continues how the 
parties characterized the relevant ministries, media, and further characters. 
Parliament with SPD, Green Party, and FDP 
The GEG process involved parliament members of the governing parties (Mindrup 2024). These 
parliament members represented key characters because of their legislative power29. Many parliament 
members were part of the process because the multidisciplinary law affected different experts, e.g., in 
the housing and energy sectors (in der Beek 2024). The joint working group of the three governing parties 
(“Koalitionsausschuss”) functioned as the highest board for decision-making and was, therefore, 
powerful (Taher Saleh 2024). Mindrup argues that he would have also integrated experts from the 
opposition parties (Mindrup 2024). Such integration being not the case, parties like the conservatives 
(“CDU/CSU”) did not support the GEG and even announced to revise it if they came into power 
(Mindrup 2024). 
Diving deeper into the governing parties’ roles, the SPD did not generally communicate a solid public 
image of its climate action ambitions (Krohn 2024a), and its positions on the GEG were challenging to 
grasp (Krohn 2024a).   
During the GEG process, the Social Democrats owned a unique role because the chancellor, Olaf 
Scholz, belonged to their party (Bergmann 2024). Scholz acted as a facilitator in the government, often 
without taking a clear side (Bergmann 2024). Therefore, the SPD focused on internal alignments before 
positioning themselves to create a united image (Bergmann 2024). Correspondingly, the SPD did not fit 
into a clear NPF character type throughout the process (Jones et al. 2023, 164, based on Jones and 
McBeth 2010). Formally, the Social Democratic parliamentary rapporteurs were Timon Gremmels 
(Herrmann 2024; Schneller 2024) and Martin Diedenhofen (Taher Saleh 2024).  
Besides that, many of the SPD’s parliament members were directly elected in their district (Schröder 
2024). Schröder assumes that this led to a higher political reliance on the opinion of the citizens, 
reducing the political willpower to support legislation with significant everyday impact like the GEG 
(Schröder 2024). Moreover, the party had strong local roots in the municipalities and their local utility 
providers (Bergmann 2024). The municipalities were critical in their role as implementors of the KWP 
(Schneller 2024). That is why they advocated for the employees and their jobs during the GEG process 
(Bergmann 2024). Regarding NPF characters, the author argues that the SPD saw the employees and 
the local utility providers as victims because both were significantly affected by the GEG. Therefore, the

The reason could be that certain municipalities represented their interests directly on the EU level 
(Kochskämper 2024). 
 
The integration of the municipalities left room for improvement. This is reflected when the parties 
discussed the KWP, which was more relevant for the municipalities than the GEG, significantly later than 
the GEG (see 4.1.4). Especially at the beginning of public discussions, many aspects of the GEG were 
not apparent, like the later link introduced to the KWP. Municipalities who were part of the discussions 
early on could have prevented further public confusion and protest. An example is the VKU, which 
suggested interlinking GEG and KWP on March 1, 2023 (Rupprecht, Schäfer, and Sedlmayer 2023). 
When this connection finally emerged in the GEG process, SPD and FDP attempted to portray the 
municipalities and local utility providers as heroes who would limit the consequences and harm to 
building owners (Schneller 2024; Köhler 2024). 
NGOs for Welfare, Tenants, and Environmental Protection 
As the next character, welfare, tenant, and consumer organizations played a significant role in 
representing the interests of lower-income people and tenants (Taher Saleh 2024; Herrmann 2024). In 
addition, environmental NGOs embodied essential characteristics within the NGOs (Köhler 2024). They 
mainly acted in line with the Green Party (N. Höhne 2024), turning them into key characters without 
further NPF labels. Looking back, the Green Party would have included these characters more intensely 
(Taher Saleh 2024; Herrmann 2024).  
(In)congruences of the Parties

claim they consulted all other key characters (Herrmann 2024; Koch 2024; Köhler 2024; Schneller 2024; 
Taher Saleh 2024). Two public hearings32 of experts from various stakeholders happened during the 
process (Taher Saleh 2024; Deutscher Bundestag 2023c; 2023b).

Schröder 2024; Taher Saleh 2024; Köhler 2024). Again, no media reports indicate that this hero image 
overcame the public impression of Habeck as a villain. At the same time, none of the parties 
characterized building owners as core victims of the GEG (see Barriers of this sub-chapter). This did not 
happen even though this group was the group for which the GEG was made and who needed to cover 
its (financial) consequences. 
Overall, all three parties aimed to mainly communicate positively regarding characters: First, they 
intended to protect (different) victims from the negative consequences of the GEG. Second, they 
eventually tried to display themselves as heroes, minimizing that harm. It, however, stands out that the 
Liberals especially applied a form of negative communication by portraying Minster Habeck and the 
Green Party as villains, which influenced the public debate visibly. Beyond that, experts criticize that 
specific media characters supported this negative image.

Narrative Enablers 
Looking at enablers, the character section illustrates one strong climate policy enabler. This enabler 
represents the close and intense exchange between the characters after the GEG draft was published, 
e.g., between the BMWK and the associations (Koch 2024). The governing parties significantly adjusted 
the initial GEG draft to turn it into the final version (BMWK and BMWSB 2023; BMJ 2020). One result 
was the interlink between GEG and KWP, demanded by characters like the VKU (Rupprecht, Schäfer, 
and Sedlmayer 2023).  
However, it should be noted that this exchange should have happened earlier in specific cases, such as 
the late exchange with municipalities and utility providers (see the following section). 
Narrative Barriers 
Following the enablers, the analysis of the characters reveals four significant barriers in the GEG process. 
Firstly, negative-oriented communication hindered a constructive public debate about the GEG. 
Specifically, the FDP started on February 28, when the initial GEG draft became public, portraying 
Minister Habeck and his Green Party as villains who wanted to harm building owners (V. Höhne and 
Hackenbruch 2023). This set the tone for further negative communication, e.g., through negatively 
framed 101 questions, which aimed to question the entire GEG project weeks later (Der Spiegel 2023). 
The author argues that such blaming represented a non-constructive debate that slowed the GEG 
process. A strategic application of narrative elements to successfully create a strong hero image (M. D. 
Jones 2014, 3) would have increased the parties' persuasive power (Jones et al. 2023, 178 based on 
Crow and Lawlow 2016). The strong public presence of the FDPs and the media’s communication 
overshadowed the different attempts to position utility providers, Green and Liberal Party, as heroes 
within the narrative. 
Secondly, the interviews demonstrate that the inclusion of the municipalities was not a key focus of the 
parties. Firstly, the parties’ experts rarely mention the municipalities (Schneller 2024; in der Beek 2024; 
Köhler 2024; Schröder 2024; Taher Saleh 2024; Koch 2024). Despite being asked, the FDP and Green 
Party only stated that they integrated the municipalities into the process without providing further details. 
This indicates that municipalities were not one of the top present priorities. Subsequently, if the parties 
had better listened to the municipal level, then the call from the association of municipal companies on 
March 1, 2023, to closely connect GEG and KWP (Rupprecht, Schäfer, and Sedlmayer 2023) would 
have ensured an earlier reaction to that call. In fact, it took the parties until April to demand such an 
interlink (Koch 2024; Mindrup 2024). In addition, the municipalities could have provided their extended 
knowledge about the heating sector from the beginning: 
“In reality, the municipalities have always done much more than the federal legislature. [...] 
All in all, the municipalities saved the building sector from doing even worse [in terms of its 
carbon footprint].” 
Christina-Johanne Schröder, MdB 
To better include the municipalities, Krohn would have focused on detailed and clear roles and 
communication between the federal, state, and municipal administrations from the beginning (Krohn 
2024a). 
Thirdly, the experts implicate that the GEG process was not user-centric. Building owners who were most 
affected by the GEG should have been at the center of the design of the law (Bergmann 2024; Krohn 
==PAGE:49==

2024a). The fact that none of the parties’ experts mention building owners as a key character supports 
that this was not the case. The ministries and the governing parties focused on consulting important 
characters like associations who intended to represent the interests of building owners (and other 
characters). However, a direct integration of citizens affected by the new law did not seem to happen. 
Furthermore, the practice-oriented industry, local utilities, and potential investors should have integrated 
better (Bergmann 2024). Specifically, the Green Party would have included heating installers and 
chimney sweepers more intensely – despite challenges34 with their integration (Schröder 2024). 
Fourthly, another barrier represented the missing alignment of the GEG draft between the ministry and 
parliament. The Liberals criticize that the BMWK discussed a potential GEG with many associations but 
not the legislature itself before the draft was published. However, Koch states that his party read about 
the details of the draft in the press (Koch 2024). The Liberals considered the ministry responsible for 
contacting the relevant governing parliament members beforehand (Koch 2024; in der Beek 2024).  
Fifthly, the Green Party especially underestimated the role of devalued fossil-based assets through the 
GEG. Specifically, they undervalued the investment dimensions of gas grids (Herrmann 2024; Schröder 
2024). These represented private and municipal assets that lost significant value, which led to resistance 
against the GEG by the asset owners, e.g., local utility providers (Herrmann 2024; Schröder 2024).  
 
Overall, the analysis in this section illustrates positively that the parties included a wide range of 
characters in the process. Meanwhile, the parties failed to focus on some especially important 
characters. Moreover, the SPD and the Green Party intended to present narrative heroes. Nevertheless, 
the Liberal image of Habeck as a villain retained the most attention in the public debate. 
4.1.4 
Plot 
Based on the analysis of the setting, the moral, and the characters, this section examines how the plot 
of the GEG combines the previous narrative elements. The following analysis is based on Caspari et al. 
(2023) and the expert interviews. 
How the Law Became Important 
The initial narrative situation included two essential focusing events that started the main plot of the GEG 
process. The first focusing event, the coalition agreement of the new German federal government in 
2021, stated the goal of banning natural gas and oil heating from 2025. After that, the second focusing 
event of the Russian-Ukrainian war created a more urgent need for Germany to become independent 
from Russian natural gas. In March 2022, the government agreed on different measures to reduce the 
dependency on natural gas and energy prices due to the war and economic sanctions. Part of these 
measures was an agreement to move the start of the GEG forward by one year to 2024. (Caspari et al. 
2023)

Höhne 2024; Taher Saleh 2024).

suggests that a minimal arrangement before publishing it could have ensured a more constructive 
beginning of the public debate. 
Finding an Agreement 
In response to the public debate, a 30-hour meeting between the three governing parties at the end of 
March 2023 led to an agreement on adjustments to the draft. Now, every new heating system from 
January 1, 2024, should use 65% renewable energy – in a technologically open manner and with 
transition timeframes until 2028. The government should agree on the amended law one month later so 
that parliament can pass it before summer. In April, the cabinet passed the law, including new 
exemptions to make it more socially balanced. However, Liberal finance minister Christian Lindner 
added a note calling for “a practicable and financeable implementation.” In other words, the Liberal 
Party expected further adjustments to the GEG. This was also due to a party-internal proposal of the 
Liberals. (Caspari et al. 2023)

(Bergmann 2024). However, compliance infringements forced Minister Habeck to remove Graichen from 
his position in May 2023 after weeks of public discussions (Caspari et al. 2023). Laying off Graichen 
was also a move to reduce public pressure on Habeck (Bergmann 2024), which especially arose through

The parliament needed to start negotiations about the GEG at the end of May to pass the law before

their parliamentary group's disunity (Caspari et al. 2023). The creators among Frank Schäffler expected 
responses to these questions before they could continue negotiating the law (Der Spiegel 2023). Other 
Liberal parliament members assumed this was a fossil-motivated move to delay the legislation process

After further negotiations, the Liberals still blocked the law's introduction into parliamentary discussions

To begin with, all three parties formed a story of decline through global warming and the energy crisis 
(Krohn 2024a; Herrmann 2024; Schröder 2024; in der Beek 2024; Koch 2024). In the coalition 
agreement, they aligned that an update of the GEG would be their solution to that story (SPD, Bündnis

governing parliament members (Schneller 2024; Taher Saleh 2024; Köhler 2024). Therefore, they 
learned about the ministry's plans through BILD's unplanned publication of the GEG draft (Taher Saleh

The content of this draft established conflicts between the parties. Especially, the market-focused 
Liberals called for significant adjustments regarding allowed technology options (Köhler 2024; in der 
Beek 2024). To strengthen this negotiation position, they exaggerated the potential consequences of 
the law in their communication and built a horror story (V. Höhne and Hackenbruch 2023; Rupprecht, 
Schäfer, and Hellemann 2023), supported by fossil-fuel-related characters (Stöcker 2023; Lau 2023). 
Besides that, the SPD and Green Party demanded more social balance through financial support 
(Schneller 2024; Herrmann 2024). For them, this demand was intricately connected to their morals (see 
4.1.2).   
Looking back, the experts do not agree whether the unplanned publication of the GEG draft was a 
significant factor in the following debate (e.g., Schneller 2024; N. Höhne 2024). However, more time 
would have allowed the BMWK to align its draft with parliamentary requirements for issue containment. 
This suggests the conclusion that a later publication would have eased the following process.  
 
Beyond that, the interlink of GEG, KWP, and funding program represents the second major development 
throughout the plot. All three parties wanted this connection to pursue issue containment again (Mindrup 
2024; Schröder 2024; Köhler 2024). Due to the eventual importance of this triad of the laws, it remains 
unclear why it did not happen from the beginning. The experts suggest a lack of coordination between 
the ministries (Schneller 2024; Taher Saleh 2024), adjusted legislative timelines due to the energy crisis 
(Koch 2024), and political pressure from one of the governing parties (Schröder 2024; in der Beek 2024) 
as potential explanations for this situation.

Narrative Barriers 
Furthermore, two significant barriers to the result of the legislation process became clear through the 
plot analysis.  
On the one hand, the communication of a horror story created an unconstructive beginning of the public 
debate. The Liberals partially joined the sensationalist and fossil-related newspaper BILD (e.g.,

that would come up soon. The horror story significantly impacted the following public and parliamentary 
discussions. 
On the other hand, the coordination between the ministry and governing parties lacked preparation 
and coalitional glue. Bergmann and Höhne argue that the ministry, especially its Green parliament

4.2 Narrative Content 
To address narrative relativity, the following two sections examine the belief systems and strategies of 
the governing parties during the GEG process. The sub-chapter outlines how they helped and hindered 
the overall process. 
4.2.1 
Belief Systems 
Belief systems categorize people with similar beliefs into one group (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 165–66; 
2022, 145). In the present case, three options exist to reduce emissions in the heating sector: laws, 
funding incentives, and price-based mechanisms (Kochskämper 2024). The belief systems of the three 
parties developed along these three paths because the German government consisted of three 
fundamentally different parties (N. Höhne 2024). Thus, the line of conflict especially evolved along the 
relationship between the state and the individual (Köhler 2024; N. Höhne 2024). The approach and the 
instruments how to reach climate neutrality in 2045 represented the key challenge (Mindrup 2024): e.g., 
an entirely market-based approach that would enable a carbon-neutral heating sector in line with 
German climate targets would have brought massive social inequalities (N. Höhne 2024). Overall, a 
fundamental transformation in the heating sector needed more than one instrument to implement it 
(Kochskämper 2024). 
SPD 
Regarding the GEG, the Social Democrats believed in protecting employees and their wealth (Krohn 
2024a). They focused on social justice, employees, and worker’s unions (Bergmann 2024). Together with 
the Liberals, they wanted to protect the citizens from large changes in their daily lives due to the 
transformation towards a climate-neutral country (Krohn 2024a; Köhler 2024; Schneller 2024). At the 
same time, they believed that the instrument of regulatory law would enforce a binding success that 
other instruments cannot guarantee (Schneller 2024). Instead of choosing a market-based approach, 
the Social Democrats focused on implementing the GEG through regulatory law and defining climate-
friendly standards to create climate action (Bergmann 2024). 
Green Party 
The Greens preferred a mix of regulatory law, market-based instruments, and funding programs 
(Schröder 2024; Bergmann 2024). This is because a socially equitable transition can only occur by 
distributing its costs (Schröder 2024). Regarding the GEG, the Green Party aimed for a regulatory 
approach (N. Höhne 2024; Koch 2024; Krohn 2024a). They combined the regulation with a funding 
program to further support building owners in their heating system transition to implement their belief 
system (Herrmann 2024). Otherwise, many citizens could not finance their new heating system and 
potential insulation work (Herrmann 2024).

FDP 
A core perspective of the Liberals is freedom. Thus, the GEG should not reduce the freedom45 of an

regulatory law can enforce further climate action in the heating sector (Koch 2024).

Liberalized infrastructure, e.g., for the German train system (FDP 2024).

4.2.2 Strategies 
Between February and September 2023, the parties needed to apply certain narrative strategies: each 
of them wanted to implement their belief system and their morals into the GEG as best as possible. This 
section analyses these strategies in depth. 
SPD 
The SPD saw two fundamental issues with the initial GEG draft. The first issue was its strong focus on 
heat pumps as a solution for most buildings (Mindrup 2024). It was not flexible enough to adjust the 
heating system to individual needs, like the energy (in)efficiency of a building (Mindrup 2024; Schneller 
2024). Based on their local roots (Bergmann 2024), the so-called “district approach”46 had always 
played a dominant role for the party. Here, the SPD supported that strategy first to make the 
municipalities responsible for reducing the scope of conflict (based on M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 166). 
The state should clarify infrastructure for the citizens before requiring them to become active (Schneller 
2024). This strategy ensured higher support rates by the citizens (Mindrup 2024). Being part of the SPD’s 
moral, this focus already became part of the coalition agreement in 2021 (Mindrup 2024; SPD, Bündnis 
90/Die Grünen, and FDP 2021, 45). The second issue was social equity, where the party applied a 
solidarity shift (based on M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 166) to implement their moral. As a supplement to the 
draft, the Social Democrats were especially missing social aspects like financial solutions for 
economically weak citizens (Bergmann 2024). These characters represent potential victims (Cairney 
and Weible 2014, 166) of the costs of the GEG, and the SPD emphasized their potential loss of wealth 
(Bergmann 2024). Consumer and tenant protection are a core interest of the social democrats 
(Herrmann 2024). However, missing consumer protection regarding the costs of district heating and 
heating with biogas suggests that these topics were only present to a limited extent within the SPD’s 
strategy (Herrmann 2024). 
 
A further aspect represented the SPD’s handling of lobbying interests. The party intended to treat the 
relevant characters equally without being influenced by single interests (Schneller 2024). This strategy 
aimed to maintain control over the narrative. They aspired not to be influenced by individual companies 
and, therefore, not to talk to them (Schneller 2024). At the same time, the SPD naturally supported the 
public utility providers in order to secure local jobs (Bergmann 2024). All in all, they would not involve

(In)congruences of the Parties 
Comparing the parties’ strategies, the public debate in the first days of March seemed like the GEG 
would contain a large scope of conflict (e.g., Laschyk 2023). This was because the GEG was a law with 
a direct and potentially costly impact on the daily lives of many people, which made it difficult to 
convince people of its benefits (Koch 2024). Therefore, all three parties applied the same narrative 
strategy: reducing the scope of conflict. Moreover, they applied DAS-Shifts to highlight specific 
characters in the process. To accomplish both, they used different approaches. 
Firstly, all three parties focused on two aspects to decrease the scope of conflict. SPD and the Green 
Party aimed to socially balance the law by protecting tenants and low-income housing owners 
(Bergmann 2024; Herrmann 2024), which does not appear to be important for the Liberals. To do so, 
their instrument was the call for a supplementary funding program (Bergmann 2024; Herrmann 2024). 
It stands out that the Green Party’s experts, especially, repeatedly mention the role of consumer and 
tenant protection (Herrmann 2024; Schröder 2024), while the SPD’s experts do not. Here, the 
underrepresentation of the SPD during the interviews could play a role (see 3.2). However, Herrmann 
supports the impression that it did not play a key role during the negotiations (Herrmann 2024).  
Besides, SPD and FDP also demanded more technologically flexible options within the GEG (Mindrup 
2024; Köhler 2024; in der Beek 2024). The Green Party focused especially on heat pumps, perhaps too 
much, without leaving enough space in the debate for necessary alternatives (Bergmann 2024). In 
addition to more flexible technology requirements, SPD and FDP demanded that the state clarify its 
infrastructure first before housing owners need to become active (Mindrup 2024; Köhler 2024). The 
subsequent linking of GEG and KWP should further reduce the scope of conflict with building owners 
who did not want to install a heat pump or one of the other technological options (see 4.1.4). 
 
Secondly, the parties applied solidarity and devil shifts. Again, SPD and the Green Party used the same 
instrument to implement their moral of the GEG. Through their solidarity shift, both parties emphasized 
that citizens with low income and tenants need to be protected from the costs of new heating systems 
(Bergmann 2024; Herrmann 2024). Therefore, they called for an income-adjusted funding program as 
well as further tenant protection from rising rents (Bergmann 2024; Herrmann 2024). On the contrary, 
the Liberals blamed Minister Habeck for the impact the GEG would have on housing owners (Koch 2024). 
Such devil shift intended to shift (negative) public focus away from the Liberal Party, which had agreed 
to update the GEG in the coalition agreement with the ministry that was responsible for the first GEG 
draft.  
Throughout their strategies, the parties had one thing in common. They claimed that they equally 
integrated all relevant characters in the process and, therefore, did not miss relevant interests that might 
not be reflected in the GEG (Schneller 2024; Herrmann 2024; Koch 2024).

belief systems in the negotiations and the final GEG. To do so, they used forceful communication to 
separate from each other. However, Koch’s reflective statement supports that the parties’ demands were 
closer to each other than the public debate suggested. He describes that looking back, the Liberals 
understand why the ministry and the Green Party developed the draft of the GEG (Koch 2024), but they 
might have wanted too much (Koch 2024). 
Narrative Enablers 
The experts identified one enabler among the strategies: the continuous reduction of the scope of 
conflict represented a key enabler for the GEG. In the beginning, the separate focus on only the singular 
heating system of a building was a significant weakness of the GEG draft (Bergmann 2024; Mindrup 
2024): 
“First, we have to pass a law on heating. And then we need to talk about refurbishment. 
Moreover, I think that was one of the biggest mistakes, apart from the question of the 
subsidies.” 
Kai Bergmann 
Later, the negotiators interlinked GEG, KWP, and the funding program. This led to a decreased scope 
of conflict because the GEG then distributed the requirements between housing owners and municipal 
infrastructure providers. For further details, see Enablers of 4.1.4. 
Narrative Barriers 
On the side of the barriers, the experts describe eight aspects that they view as decelerating the success 
of the GEG process. These barriers put a strong focus on government-internal coordination, 
communication, and further factors. 
A significant barrier during this process represented the lack of coordination within the coalition. The 
governing parties should better coordinate the communication strategy from the early beginning to 
speak with one voice (Köhler 2024; Schneller 2024) and, therefore, strengthen the coalitional glue. In 
principle, such working groups exist, including the chairpersons of the parliamentary groups and the 
ministers (Köhler 2024). However, the needed coordination between the coalition and the parliamentary 
groups did not work after the publication of the GEG draft (Köhler 2024; Schneller 2024). This made the 
parties apply different narrative strategies against each other. One example represented the devil shift, 
where some Liberal parliament members blamed Minister Habeck: they publicly submitted 101 critical 
questions, even though they were not even involved in the process (Schröder 2024). Meanwhile, SPD 
and the Green Party applied a solidarity shift to put tenants and low-income building owners at the 
center of their communication (Herrmann 2024; Schröder 2024; Mindrup 2024). Additionally, better 
internal coordination between the three governing parties would have created a more constructive 
public debate (Kochskämper 2024). It would have transmitted the image that the decarbonization of 
the heating sector is a common project of the entire coalition (Taher Saleh 2024). This would especially 
have helped to deal with the upcoming negative media reports (Kochskämper 2024). 
Another factor describes the lack of coordination within a party: communication potential lies in the 
public reactions of the Liberal Party to the GEG process. More factual comments from their parliament 
members would have allowed further focus public attention on the Liberal’s solutions (in der Beek 2024).

The complex design of the law represented another barrier during the GEG process because it provided 
a large scope of conflict. The intense public debate about it supports that claim (see 4.1.4). Here, an 
interesting difference exists between the experts:  
 
On the one hand, Taher Saleh states that the juridical language was not avoidable in creating legal 
certainty (Taher Saleh 2024). In der Beek defends the complexity of the law because it was rather 
designed for experts, e.g., from utility providers, than for ordinary citizens (in der Beek 2024). Such a law 
needed to cover many potential use cases in order to be sufficient for further implementation (in der 
Beek 2024). If the GEG had not mentioned certain technologies, then they could not have been funded 
through a respective funding program (in der Beek 2024). Therefore, the potential use of many different 
technologies is required to expand the size of the GEG (in der Beek 2024). 
 
During the legislation process, the discussed law changed significantly. The adopted version of the GEG 
was easier to explain than the initial draft because the state defined the infrastructure boundaries first 
(Köhler 2024). Nevertheless, applying the law to the individual case was still a complex topic, as 
demonstrated in Figure 4. The decision tree by the official German Federal Environment Agency 
contained multiple conditions and yes/no questions to guide through the correct legal options 
(umweltbundesamt.de 2023).  On the other hand, Mindrup notes that laws like the GEG should be 
much simpler and easier to understand to reduce the scope of conflict (Mindrup 2024). In principle, the 
GEG could have only stated the requirement of renewable energy for new heating systems and a method 
to evaluate this criterion (Koch 2024). Moreover, the complexity of the juridical language, which even 
parliament members do not always entirely understand (Taher Saleh 2024), increased the risk of 
different, potentially even incorrect, understandings of the GEG. Journalist Krohn elaborates that if it 
was necessary to add numerous exemptions to the law as it happened in the GEG, this points toward 
fundamental issues within the law (Krohn 2024a). Learning from other countries, Mindrup describes the 
example of South Tirol:

The missing immediate integration of affected building owners in the GEG process, calling for a user-

The complexity of the GEG, however, pointed towards the fundamental setting (see 1.1.3) of the German 
legislative system. Here, the ministries usually create and draft new (complex) laws; the parliament 
adjusts them (Mindrup 2024). In the US, on the contrary, the parliament is responsible for law initiatives 
(Mindrup 2024). To do so, the American parliament members have more sophisticated staff because 
designing a law from the bottom up requires high capacities (Mindrup 2024). A solution could be to 
strengthen the staff of the German parliament members or parliamentary groups. The additional staff 
would need to have a clear purpose in identifying and drafting new law initiatives from the parliament. 
This would help to repeat the success of the parliament (Mindrup 2024) in the GEG process. 
 
Overall, the interviews demonstrate a disunity about whether the GEG could have been a much less 
complex law. At the same time, the experts agree that the law is highly complex, making it harder for 
ordinary citizens to understand it. In the future, a shift away from juridical lawmakers towards user-
focused laws and a strengthened parliament could help to make laws simpler. 
Moreover, the communication during the GEG process did not respond much to the needs of building 
owners who might need to afford high investments due to the new law (Krohn 2024a). Therefore, the 
process and its communication should have been more user-centric, using the building owner’s needs 
as a starting point. Krohn supports Bergmann’s previous claim by suggesting that the GEG 
communication should be more focused on the citizens’ and building owners’ needs instead of 
concentrating on how to explain the GEG to the citizens (Krohn 2024a). Thus, a more holistic 
communicative image of GEG would have made the approach of the governing parties easier to 
understand, more reasonable, and, therefore, more convincing. 
Furthermore, the acting parties should increase their honesty and transparency. The demands of the 
GEG would require many buildings to not only exchange their heating system but also increase energy 
efficiency (Bergmann 2024). Such broader renovation of buildings would cause higher investments: 
Minister Habeck admitted later that a full building renovation meant an investment of 200,000 € instead 
of 20,000 € for a new heat pump (Fabricius and Höfling 2023). It would have been especially the role 
of the Green Party and the Social Democratic Party to solve these social issues through a more holistic 
strategy (Bergmann 2024).

narrative approaches of the parties that have already become visible through the previous pages. Thus, 
it strengthens and extends the findings on why the parties acted the way they acted during the BEA 
process: they aimed to implement their fundamentally different belief systems as well as their morals 
into the law. To do so, they focused on limiting the scope of conflict and emphasizing the roles of specific 
characters, e.g., tenants and Minister Habeck. However, these strategies were not aligned and 
repeatedly conflicted with each other, which is why this chapter examines a list of enablers, especially 
barriers. These barriers include expandable communication, lack of alignment, and a limited focus on 
an understandable and user-centric law.

Climate Action 
Overall, the adopted GEG version represents progress towards the climate goals for 2045; however,

“From my perspective, it could have been even more, but at least we have taken a step

the final version of the law will cause higher sales of natural gas than the initial GEG draft would have 
caused (N. Höhne 2024).

Role of the Media 
The public debate emphasized the importance of independent information as a basis for citizens to form 
their own opinions. In the GEG debate, the “loud” voices were especially present, creating an 
informational disbalance. (N. Höhne 2024): 
“[...] that much damage has been done in the political debate. It is no longer about the 
result of the law but about the process. As a result, climate policy has been discredited 
unfavorably. Every further step in climate policy is now being approached very, very, very 
carefully.” 
Prof. Dr. Niklas Höhne 
An example is the missing differentiation of the media and how the policy process works (Laschyk 2023; 
Der SPIEGEL 2023). In March 2023, it became not easily clear to a newspaper-reading citizen that the 
debated draft of the GEG was merely a proposal of a ministry (BMWK and BMWSB 2023) without binding 
force. This is because the legislative power lies with the parliament as its fundamental adjustments to 
the law illustrated (Mindrup 2024). 
 
Kochskämper is concerned about the long-term effects of the public GEG debate. The sensationalistic 
media reports and political discussions are now especially connected to the Green Party (Kochskämper 
2024). This could have negative impacts on further climate action (Kochskämper 2024). Many people 
in Germany are worried about the consequences of the GEG until today, even though the law improved 
significantly (in der Beek 2024). 
Political System 
According to the experts, the negotiations demonstrate two aspects of the German political system. First, 
they illustrate the hierarchical decision tree in the German government. After not agreeing on a technical 
negotiation level, negotiations continued on the level of the parliamentary groups and the three party 
leaders Scholz, Habeck, and Lindner. Here, the technical experts and the parliament group leaders, 
respectively, were not involved. (in der Beek 2024; Köhler 2024). 
 
Second, they showed fundamental interrelationships within the German democratic system. During the 
public discussions, the process turned from an outcome-oriented one into a power-oriented one. This 
happened because the parties involved wanted to differentiate themselves from the other parties in light 
of upcoming future elections. As a political advisor, Bermann has experienced such developments in 
every political process. (Bergmann 2024)

5 Conclusion and Implications 
This work analyses the climate policy process of the German GEG in 2023 with a focus on the three 
governing parties SPD, Green Party, and FDP. Within this process, it investigates the climate policy 
narratives of the three parties based on the Narrative Policy Framework (NPF) by interviewing experts 
about the GEG process. Furthermore, this work identifies enablers and barriers for such climate policy 
processes.  
 
The following sub-chapters conclude on the respective analysis. In the beginning, 5.1 describes the key 
(in)congruences of the parties’ narratives. After that, 5.2 illustrates key enablers, followed by key barriers 
in 5.3. Subsequently, 5.4 turns these findings into practice-oriented recommendations as inputs to 
improve future climate policy processes in Germany. This is followed by 5.5, with a critical perspective 
on this work to consider its limitations transparently. Finally, subchapter 5.6 briefly discusses research 
directions that could build on this work. 
5.1 Congruence of the Three Parties’ Narratives 
SPD, Green Party, and FDP's narratives demonstrated congruence about the starting and end points of 
the GEG process as well as their primary strategy to reach a final agreement. The parties also 
heresthetically applied several narrative components in the process, but their narratives were 
inconsistent. Table 2 compares the parties’ narratives in more detail beyond the following summary.

On the one hand, the three parties shared the common moral that they wanted to reach climate 
neutrality in 2045 and better energy independence, especially from Russia. This thematic framing based 
on the coalition agreement of 2021. When the GEG process ended, the parties returned to their 
collective understanding and pointed out their contentment with the updated GEG. All three parties

agree that the holistic interlink between GEG, KWP, and the funding program meant a key adjustment 
to reduce pressure on building owners. Moreover, they support the allowed technology options for the 
most part. Altogether, the parties applied a main strategy that led to the definitive version of the GEG. 
Through different approaches, they reduced the scope of conflict that the initial GEG draft created for 
building owners. 
On the other hand, the three parties differed fundamentally in how they reduced this scope of conflict 
and in how to implement the 65% target for renewable energy of new heating systems, as well as their 
moral of the GEG. This incongruence appeared immediately after the unplanned publication of the GEG 
draft in February 2023. While most of the experts agree that the circumstance of the unexpected 
publication did not have an essential influence on the following process, a lack of internal alignments 
and, therefore, a lack of coalitional glue initiated differing reactions to the draft.  
 
Here, the market-based Liberals stood out, especially through a rather aggressive strategy and the 
application of a narrative horror story within media and coalition membership. Their respective 
connections suggest that this was due to links with the fossil fuel industry. Certain parliament members 
illustrated the GEG draft as a law that would harm building owners through a lack of freedom of choice 
and high costs. To support this image, they blamed especially the responsible minister, Habeck, as a 
villain who would want to harm the housing owners. In the future, the Liberals intend to allow as many 
technological options as possible within the GEG, e.g., hydrogen and biogas, while advocating for the 
transition of fossil-based infrastructure. This approached based on their belief system to enforce 
technologically open GHG emission reduction paths as well as protecting citizens from raising heating 
costs. Within the Liberals' belief system, the support for implementing regulatory law at all was generally 
low (instead of merely adjusting a CO2 price). Though, they did not promote a higher CO2 price 
prominently.  
 
In the meantime, the Social Democrats took a less present and more moderating role. Like the Liberals, 
they advocated for broader technology options, however implemented through regulatory law. Their 
broader technology focus was due to their strong municipal roots. In the municipalities, they wanted to 
protect jobs at the utility providers and protect their fossil-based assets. Furthermore, the Social 
Democrats aimed for a socially balanced approach by supporting tenants and low-income housing 
owners through a respective funding program.  
The Greens, on the contrary, tried to defend the energy-efficiency focus of the GEG created by the 
Green minister Habeck. They preferred strict regulatory law with a narrow focus on heat pumps, 
especially as energy-efficient technology. In addition, they demanded an implementation of the law that 
would protect tenants and low-income housing owners. Their social aspect overlapped with the Social 
Democrats and was even more prominent. Besides that, they emphasized the win-win narrative of energy 
independence from Russian gas. However, this morality was not particularly clear in the public debate. 
Altogether, the duration of the legislative process, along with the heated public debate, illustrated that 
the parties’ narratives were large incongruences compared to their commonalities. The analysis 
emphasizes the power of a horror story, the consequences of missing government-internal alignments, 
and a holistic legislation perspective. For more details, see the following sub-chapters.

5.2 Key Enablers during the GEG Process 
The analysis identifies numerous enablers and barriers that influenced the GEG process. Some of them 
overlap, different experts support various of them, and others are only mentioned in one or two interviews. 
This chapter focuses on the most prominent, thus repeatedly named, and appearing enablers that the 
analysis brings up. 
In sum, the congruence of the parties’ narratives described in the previous sub-chapter hints towards 
the two key enablers that helped the GEG process to succeed. 
- 
Firstly, the process demonstrated the essential role of binding agreements for implementing 
climate policy and increasing coalitional glue, especially with characters of fundamentally 
different belief systems. The creation of the common coalition agreement with the rule of 65% 
renewable energy for heating systems happened long before the actual GEG process started. 
Nevertheless, it represented a crucial focusing event. Without it, the GEG as regulatory law 
might not be in place today. This is because the Liberal experts state that they only agreed to 
this kind of regulation due to the coalition agreement. Otherwise, regulatory law would not have 
been a solution for them. 
- 
Secondly, the holistic focus of the final GEG through the interlink with KWP and the funding 
program represented a core improvement between the initial draft of the law and its adopted 
version. By including a broad range of affected characters like utility providers, associations, 
and ministries, this integrated approach successfully reduced the scope of conflict. It reduced 
potential (expensive) implications for housing owners and tenants. For example, the income-
adjusted funding program and the prior municipal clarification about future heating 
infrastructure created significant support from the state for potential victims. Thus, this approach 
led the path to the adoption of the law. 
Due to their key role in the process, the presented key enablers are an integral part of the 
recommendations in 5.4. Table 3 lists all further enablers based on the expert interviews. These enablers 
are not as present and dominant throughout the analysis and, therefore, are not described in detail in 
this section. 
5.3 Key Barriers during the GEG Process 
As with key enablers, 5.1 hints towards the most relevant aspects of the GEG process that hindered its 
progress. Therefore, this sub-chapter examines the most relevant key barriers as an outcome of the 
analysis51. Overall, this section elaborates on six key barriers:  
- 
To begin with, the heated start of the public debate about the GEG in February 2023 and the 
negative reactions of the Liberals point towards the first key barrier: missing alignment between 
the responsible ministry BMWK (law-developing executive) and the governing parties (law-
adopting legislature). At the time, none of the experts was prepared for the content of the

unplanned published draft. Various experts claim that pre-discussions would have clarified that 
not all three parties would have seen the draft as an acceptable foundation for further 
discussions. Therefore, an alignment between BMWK and the parliament members who would 
later be responsible for adopting the law would significantly reduce public pushback from the 
beginning. 
- 
A second key barrier represented the complex, not-user-centric design of the GEG. The focus 
on legal security resulted in a long and complicated law. It was designed to manage heating 
technologies for different buildings, not to give building owners comprehensible instruction on 
how to choose their future heating system. On the one hand, some experts argue that this level 
of legal security and diligence would be necessary in the German legal system to enable the 
supplementary funding program. On the other hand, other experts argue that a simpler design 
of such a law would be possible. Moreover, the analysis demonstrates that building owners, the 
end users of the law, were not at the center of the development process. For example, a 
comprehensible and transferable heating solution for the most common and energy-inefficient 
buildings would have been a user-centric starting point for developing the law.  
- 
The negative communication, together with horror stories, represents the third key barrier. The 
media and the Liberals focused on communicating threatening cases of buildings requiring 
extreme investments. This focus created an image that would overwhelm citizens, and this image 
overshadowed other attempts to present a hero for this story. Indications suggest that they also 
acted in the interests of fossil fuels. Together with a lack of clear communication and 
explanations from the other parties, this negative communication created strong opposition 
from the citizens. The governing parties failed to elaborate on what the law would mean in detail 
and what the actual consequences are, e.g., a rising CO2 price. They did not clarify the possible 
self-interests of some characters involved in the GEG debate. Furthermore, communication 
preparations in case of something like an unplanned publication of such an essential law would 
ensure a rather proactive instead of reactive communication approach. 
- 
Moreover, the experts missed internal coordination between the parties and the coalition. This 
caused uncoordinated public reactions, especially at the beginning of the process. The 
incoordination did not communicate a clear image to the public and, therefore, did not help to 
create a better understanding of the GEG for citizens. 
- 
In addition, the underestimation of the power of fossil-related assets represented a fifth barrier. 
In the beginning, the parties underestimated the extent to which the GEG would affect assets 
like gas infrastructure. The following opposition created another pushback in the GEG process, 
which could have been better anticipated. 
- 
Finally, the three parties' fundamentally different belief systems meant a sixth key barrier. The 
parties' different focus on regulatory law vs. market-based policy displayed a major point of 
dispute in the government-internal negotiations.  
The author expects the impact of these key barriers to be especially significant. Beyond them, Table 3 
contains all barriers identified in this work. Again, the experts and the analysis do not explicitly 
underscore the relevance of the other barriers.

5.4 Recommendation for Action to Improve Future Climate Policy Processes 
If the GEG process represented a soccer game52, there would be significant commonalities in how to 
improve the quality of the game for the next time. The following recommendations for action, based on 
the key enablers and key barriers from the GEG process, intend to improve the soccer game of future 
federal legislation processes in Germany. 
(1) Most soccer games are designed to entertain their spectators. The spectators form the center 
of all the following activities because, without their support and money, the game stops working. 
The same should account for climate policies: the citizens and users of a law like the GEG 
should be at the center of its holistic development and low-complexity application. This 
ensures support and practice-oriented implementation of the law. 
(2) To win, a coach needs to ensure aligned communications within their team. For climate policies 
like the GEG, agreement and coordinated communication within and between governing 
parties and responsible ministries are crucial. 
(3) During a time-sensitive soccer game in a loud stadium, the style of communication decides 
whether the team understands each other and wins the spectators over. Therefore, clear, 
transparent, and easy-to-understand communication with the presentation of a shared hero 
represents a key tactic to convince citizens and other relevant characters to support a climate 
policy like the GEG. 
(4) Of course, soccer games only work as a team. In a climate policy process, governing parties 
should build cross-shoulder alliances through binding arrangements like a coalition 
agreement while including all key characters in the process. 
(5) Defenders and attackers believe in different strengths to win a game. Likewise, different parties 
have different belief systems on how to implement climate policy. For such effectful 
implementation, the parties should (a) be aware of the different belief systems, (b) take on their 
perspectives, and (c) be willing to utilize their helpful approaches. 
These recommendations reduce the potential scopes of conflict in a climate policy process like the GEG 
and expand the parties’ common ground. Then, even fundamentally different parties can create climate-
action-focused policies.

During the process, three types of limitations arise regarding the NPF theory, the methodology of the 
expert interviews, and other limitations.  
First, the NPF framework brings along relevant restrictions as the foundation for the presented research. 
To begin with, NPF is a modern theory with a good fit for this study (see 2.1). It was officially named 13 
years ago (M. D. Jones et al. 2023, 183), which makes it an up-to-date framework to deal with modern 
challenges such as climate action policies. At the same time, this is a rather brief period, which leads to 
a lack of long-term evidence for the theory. In addition, researchers have mostly applied it in Western 
countries, often on the meso level (Schlaufer et al. 2022, 251). This limits new perspectives that the NPF 
could bring up to find new out-of-the-box solutions for effectful federal climate policies by parties. 
Indeed, the research builds upon a narrow number of very present researchers in the field, like Jones, 
Shanahan, and McBeth, giving them a substantial influence on this work. Furthermore, it is always 
necessary to mention the aspect of narrative relativity. This means that the analyzed GEG context is 
unique, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings54. Besides that, this work could have 
extended its analysis of the characters’ dimension beyond the basic types of victims, villains, and heroes. 
This could have enabled more differentiated findings within the characters. 
 
Second, the expert interviews leave room for further improvements. While they provided extensive 
insights on the GEG process, the Social Democrats were not represented by a current parliament 
member. In addition, interviews with BMWK and BMWSB would have helped better investigate the crucial 
interface between the executive and legislature. The ministries’ perspectives on the exchange between 
ministries and parliament members, as well as the triad of GEG, KWP, and funding program, would have 
been a valuable supplement 55. The same accounts for the perspective of state ministries 56 and 
municipalities whose relevance increased with the importance of the KWP. Based on the findings, 
additional interviews with two character groups would be valuable to make the law more user-oriented.

5.6 Directions for Future Research 
Based on its findings, this work delivers several starting points for further research to improve federal 
climate policy processes. 
Altogether, this work calls for timely and practice-oriented investigation of GEG-like processes in 
Germany. This would ensure just-in-time reflections and, therefore, allow the parties a continuous 
learning curve based on science. Moreover, it would serve to question or support the presented findings 
and extend to the key enablers and barriers. An example could be the 2024 update of the Climate 
Action Law, where the legislation process happened in a more constructive way (Bundesregierung 2024).
 
 
Another aspect represents the in-depth investigation of the GEG process regarding the role of the 
leading ministries BMWK and BMWSB and their interface to the governing parties. This work illustrates 
from the parties’ perspectives that a major potential of the GEG process lies in this (missing) exchange. 
Therefore, this interface should be investigated to strengthen it.  
Another next step could be, as researchers have done before and based on the findings of this work, to 
specifically improve the persuasiveness of components of the parties' analyzed narratives (Fløttum and 
Gjerstad 2017, 12). Then, researchers should test these enhanced narratives to identify the most
