Abstract
This project addresses the reactive synthesis problem for declarative process specifica-
tions, specifically within the context of DECLARE, a well-established declarative pattern-
based language grounded in Linear Temporal Logic over finite traces (LTLf) and com-
monly used in Business Process Management (BPM) tools. Reactive synthesis involves
constructing a finitely-representable controller, if one exists, that responds to the uncon-
trollable actions of the environment, ensuring that a given specification is consistently
met. In this work, the reactive synthesis problem for DECLARE is formalized and a solu-
tion is implemented based on the approach proposed in [1], which presents an EXPTIME
algorithm for DECLARE synthesis.
The pipeline SymbSyntDec was implemented to bridge the gap between theory and prac-
tice. This tool facilitates the translation of DECLARE specifications into Symbolic Deter-
ministic Finite Automata (SDFA). Further, it converts them into the AIGER format using
the novel translation tool SymDFA2AIGER, enabling integration with existing synthesis
tools. Implementing SymbSyntDec encodes enhanced DECLARE specifications into a
pure past Linear Temporal Logic over finite traces (LTLfp) formula. Utilizing a portion of
the proposed algorithm, an SDFA is constructed based on this formula, marking a signif-
icant achievement in creating an SDFA for any given LTLfp formula.
Although the implementation is complete, future work includes validation and refining the
strategy to enhance its readability for human users. This project substantially contributes
by providing a practical solution for reactive synthesis in DECLARE, laying the groundwork
for further advancements in the field.

Introduction
Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) has long been recognized as a fundamental tool in study-
ing time-based modal logics with applications spanning verification and synthesis tasks.
Given its significance, extensive research has been conducted on various variants, such
as LTLf [2] and LTLfp [3]. Unlike LTL, which is interpreted over infinite sequences of states,
also known as traces, LTLf and LTLfp are interpreted over finite sequences of states.
Since its introduction [4], LTL has proven indispensable for ensuring systems adhere to
desired behaviors. Verification involves checking if a dynamic system complies with an
LTL specification, while synthesis goes a step further. Synthesis involves crafting a system
that maintains these specification by constructing a correct-by-construction program [5]
that realizes the specification.
Significant research has explored different synthesis scenarios, particularly focusing on
open, also called reactive, systems [6]. In reactive synthesis, the system referred to as the
Controller, interacts with its surroundings, called the Environment, which can influence the
behavior of the Controller. This interaction can be conceptualized as a two-player game
where the Controller aims to fulfill a given specification, while the Environment seeks to
disrupt it. The goal of synthesis is to create a program that directs the Controller’s actions
to ensure that the specification is met, regardless of the Environment’s actions.
The reactive synthesis problem was initially addressed using specifications written in Se-
quential Calculus [7]. Subsequently, it was demonstrated to be solvable in 2EXPTIME-complete
for LTL specifications [8]. This original solution inspired the scientific community to explore
more efficient and less costly implementations across various variants of LTL.
In many domains, systems operate over finite sequences of events, making LTLf a more
suitable choice than LTL. LTLf has been extensively studied in fields such as AI, formal
methods, and BPM [9, 10, 11]. Recent research in LTLf aims to overcome the limitations
of LTL, with significant progress obtained in LTLf-based synthesis [1, 12, 13, 14, 15]. This
research focuses on creating programs that reliably terminate, unlike those for infinite
state systems.
Traditionally, reactive synthesis for LTLf involves three steps: converting the formula into
a Nondeterministic Finite Automaton (NFA), then into a Deterministic Finite Automaton
(DFA), and finally solving a reachability game to reach a final state. Although LTLf allows
for algorithmic optimizations, the synthesis problem remains 2EXPTIME-complete [14].
In the context of BPM, DECLARE is a widely studied declarative process modeling lan-
guage. It has been extensively researched for various reasoning and analysis tasks [12,
16, 17, 18]. DECLARE is pattern-based, with constraints defined through a set of unary
or binary templates formalized in LTLf and combined conjunctively.
Reactive synthesis in the context of DECLARE presents an intuitive challenge and has
been the subject of research in recent years [1, 12, 15]. This project aims to develop the
findings further and propose a reactive synthesis algorithm for DECLARE specifications
[1], using examples provided in [15] and basing the proofs on previous research [12].
Similar to general reactive synthesis, the reactive synthesis of DECLARE is based on a
system with two parties: the Controller, which represents the organization and its internal
resources responsible for executing the process, and the Environment, which consists

of external stakeholders making constrained, uncontrollable decisions that influence the
progression of execution. This scenario necessitates an assume-guarantee approach
for DECLARE: ”Given a declarative specification (assumption) regulating how the exter-
nal activities can be executed, together with a declarative specification (guarantee) con-
straining the execution of internal activities, the goal is to derive a program (orchestration)
indicating how to ensure that the guarantee is respected under the assumption that the
external stakeholders behave according to the assumption” [1].

SymbSyntDec algorithm
This project aims to implement the reactive synthesis prob-
lem for DECLARE, which has been shown to have a solution
in EXPTIME [1]. The referenced work presents a systematic
encoding of all DECLARE patterns into linear-sized LTLfp
formulas in a procedure they call ”pastification”. These for-
mulas are then translated into SDFA, enabling the resolution
of the problem in a symbolic form, which has known benefits
[19, 20].
The main drawback of the proposed algorithm is its lack of
implementation. This project addresses this gap by bridg-
ing the theoretical propositions with practical applications
through a tool named SymbSyntDec (Symbolic Synthesis for
DECLARE specifications). The tool’s structure is illustrated
in Figure 1.1, where its primary task is to translate enhanced
DECLARE specifications into SDFA. Enhanced DECLARE
specification consist of multiple DECLARE patterns and de-
fined actions, specified for the Controller or the Environment,
respectively.
The creation of an SDFA is made possible for the given LTLfp
formula generated from the Enhanced DECLARE specifica-
tion, which opens the possibility of generating an SDFA for
any LTLfp formula with a similar procedure.
To utilize existing off-the-shelf tools for reactive synthesis,
such as SafetySynth [21], the SDFA must be converted
into an AIGER file. Initially, this step was expected to use
an existing tool for the transformation. However, since no
open-source tool was available for constructing an AIGER
file from a given SDFA, this project proposes and implements
SymDFA2AIGER, a tool designed for this purpose.
Finally, to generate a solution to the problem, referred to
as a strategy in this context, the updated version of the
SafetySynth tool was used. This demonstrates the com-
plete pipeline of the algorithm, performing reactive synthesis
for the DECLARE specification.

Report Structure
The rest of the report is organized as follows:
In Chapter 2, an overview of the theoretical foundations underpinning this study is pre-
sented, including Reactive Synthesis on Finite Simple Traces for DECLARE.
Chapter 3 describes the proposed reactive synthesis solution from [1]. This section intro-
duces some of the main challenges related to the suggested implementation and outlines
the problems this project aims to address.
Chapter 4 delves into the technical aspects of the implementation. It covers the assump-
tions made, proposes the two algorithms SymbSyntDec and SymDFA2AIGER, explains them
step by step, and presents their implementation.
In Chapter 5, the practical applications are presented through various experiments, each
adding layers of complexity. First, two experiments using the SymDFA2AIGER tool are pro-
vided. Subsequently, a subpart of SymbSyntDec is used to create an SDFA from an LTLfp
formula, and lastly, an experiment using SymbSyntDec is given, depicting the full pipeline.

2.1
Propositional Logic
PL is the foundational building block for other logics, such as First-order Logic. It describes
the world in terms of a fixed, known set of propositions [9]. The constant symbols used in
the syntax of PL are defined such that each term belongs to a finite set X ∈{X1, . . . , Xn}.
Additionally, there are the following four logical symbols [22]:
1. ∼, also known as ¬ [reads ”not”], represents negation.
2. ∧[reads ”and”], represents conjunction.
3. ∨[reads ”or”], represents disjunction.
4. ⊃, also known as →[reads ”implies”], represents implication.
Another important symbol is ↔[reads ”if-and-only-if”], which denotes biconditional logic. It
is an abbreviation for (X →Y )∧(Y →X). The value of a proposition Xn can be mapped
to one of the elements in the set {True(t), False(f)}. In this project, we sometimes refer
to this as a Boolean formula.
Well-Formed Formula
An well-formed formula (WFF) in PL is constructed using the symbols presented here in
the following manner:
• A single proposition (e.g., X1) is a wff.
• If ϕ1 and ϕ2 are wffs, then any logical combination ϕ1 ⊗ϕ2,
where ⊗∈{¬, ∧, ∨, →, ↔}, is also a wff.
• If ϕ is a wff, then ¬ϕ is also a wff.
• If ϕ is a wff, then (ϕ) is also a wff.
No other string is allowed in the wff of PL.

2.2
LTLf and LTLfp
LTLf is a variation of LTL, specifically designed only for finite traces. LTLfp is a segment of
LTLf where only past modalities are used in its syntax. This section provides an overview
of LTLf, mentioning its history, related logics, and its significance in the verification of
finite-state systems. LTL is used to describe sequences of states in a system where
the progression of time is linear. It was introduced by Amir Pnueli in 1977 as a tool for
the formal verification of concurrent systems [4]. Unlike LTL, which is used to reason
about finite and infinite sequences, LTLf is tailored for applications where the behavior of
systems is naturally finite.
One of the common ways to check for the validity of a given temporal logic, such as LTL or
its segments, is with an automaton. The idea is that any given formula can be represented
by an automaton; this constructed automaton will accept only the models of the formula
[23]. In this chapter, we will present some automata that can be used for validation of LTLf
and LTLfp.
The concept of LTLf was introduced to address limitations of LTL when applied to finite
systems. Notable early works include those by De Giacomo and Vardi [2], who explored
the theoretical foundations and practical applications of LTLf in various computational con-
texts. Their research demonstrated the utility of LTLf in specifying and verifying properties
of systems where behaviors are inherently finite, such as business processes, workflows,
and finite-state machines.

2.2.2
Semantics
In this paper, we will strictly follow the definitions of LTLf and LTLfp as defined in [1, 12].
Definition 1. Finite trace: Formulas in LTLf over the alphabet Σ are evaluated over
finite sequences of states or words, specifically sequences in (2Σ)+. This interpretation is
referred to as general finite trace semantics. For a finite trace σ = ⟨σ0, . . . , σn−1⟩∈(2Σ)+,
the length of σ is denoted by |σ| = n. The notation σ[i, j] (where 0 ≤i ≤j < |σ|)
represents the subinterval ⟨σi, . . . , σj⟩of σ.
The satisfaction of an LTLf formula φ is defined inductively, by a sequence σ at a specific
time 0 ≤i < |σ|, with the notation σ, i |= φ, as follows:
• σ, i |= p if-and-only-if p ∈σi;
• σ, i |= ¬φ if-and-only-if σ, i ̸∈φ;
• σ, i |= φ ∨ψ if-and-only-if σ, i |= φ or σ, i |= ψ;
• σ, i |= φ ∧ψ if-and-only-if σ, i |= φ and σ, i |= ψ;
• σ, i |= φ →ψ if-and-only-if σ, i ̸∈φ or σ, i |= ψ; i.e φ →ψ ≡¬φ ∨ψ;
• σ, i |= Xφ if-and-only-if i + 1 < |n| and σ, i + 1 |= φ;
• σ, i |= e
Xφ if-and-only-if i + 1 = |n| and σ, i + 1 |= φ;
• σ, i |= Y φ if-and-only-if i > 0 and σ, i −1 |= ψ;
• σ, i |= Zφ if-and-only-if either i = 0 or σ, i −1 |= ψ;
• σ, i |= φUψ if-and-only-if there exists i ≤j < |σ| such that σ, j |= ψ, and σ, k |= φ
for all k with i ≤k < j;
• σ, i |= φSψ if-and-only-if there exists j ≤i such that σ, j |= ψ, and σ, k |= φ for all k
with j < k ≤i;
• σ, i |= φRψ if-and-only-if either σ, j |= ψ for all i ≤j < |σ|, or there exists i ≤k < |σ|
such that σ, k |= φ and σ, j |= ψ for all i ≤j ≤k;
• σ, i |= φTψ if-and-only-if either σ, j |= ψ for all 0 ≤j ≤i, or there exists k ≤i such
that σ, k |= φ and σ, j |= ψ for all i ≥j ≥k.

2.2.3
Pastification
In Cimatti et al. 2021 [24], as part of their work on introducing a new safety fragment of
LTL, they introduced a method called pastification on LTL. This allows them to view a
given system in a past tense, i.e., pure past LTL. Pastification is a very powerful tool, as
modeling a system from the current state backward allows one to represent it as a formula
corresponding to a deterministic automaton, given that “the past has already happened.”
In [1, 12], a relevant pastification method is presented for LTLf.
In this paper, we will use various tenses to describe the action of pastification, such as
”pastify” and ”pastified.” The term ”past” will also refer to the similar action of pastification.
2.3
Finite-State Automata
A finite automaton (FA), also known as FSA, is a type of automaton characterized by
having a finite number of states. Another restriction is that at any given point, the system
can be in only one of these finite states. Based on the system’s states, we can model and
analyze its properties. FSAs are integral to the automata-theoretic characterization of
LTLf [2]. FSAs can be represented in two ways: the classical explicit-state representation
and a symbolic representation.
Definition 2. word: For a given alphabet Σ, a finite word σ ∈Σ∗represents a finite
sequence of symbols from Σ. From this point onwards, when we refer to a word, we
mean a finite word unless explicitly stated otherwise.
2.3.1
Non-deterministic Finite Automaton and Deterministic Finite
Automata
NFA and DFA are fundamental models in Automata Theory and formal languages. They
are used to recognize patterns described by regular expressions and play a crucial role
in the study of regular languages [1, 25].
Definition 3. An NFA A is defined as a tuple (Σ, Q, δ, I, F), where:
• Σ is a finite alphabet of input symbols,
• Q is a finite set of states,
• δ : Q × Σ →2Q is the transition function,
• I ⊆Q is the set of initial states,
• F ⊆Q is the set of accepting states.
Definition 4. A DFA is an NFA where |I| = 1 and δ : Q × Σ →Q, ensuring that for each
state and input symbol, there is exactly one possible transition.
DFAs are deterministic because they have exactly one initial state and each transition
leads to a single specific state, distinguishing them from NFAs, which may have multiple
initial states and multiple possible transitions from a given state for a given input symbol.
A model of a given NFA A starts in one of the initial states I, where |I| ≥1. The transition
function δ specifies sets of possible next states for each state q ∈Q and

2.3.2
Symbolic Nondeterministic Finite Automaton and Symbolic
Deterministic Finite Automaton
The notion of symbolic representation is the distinguishing factor between Symbolic Non-
deterministic Finite Automaton (SNFA) and NFA, as well as SDFA and DFA. While explicit
automata operate with defined values, symbolic automata define everything at a symbolic
level. Whereas the explicit version can represent a small number of models within the
same formula, the symbolic version can represent many more models by symbolically
representing parts that fit multiple models into the same automaton. The benefits of the
symbolic approach have been researched and have well-known practical advantages [20,
19].
Definition 7. An SNFA over the alphabet Σ is a tuple S = (X∪Σ, I(X), T(X, Σ, X′), F(X, Σ)),
where:
• X is a set of state variables,
• Σ is the set of actions of the automaton,
• I(X) defines the initial state,
• T(X, Σ, X′) defines the transition relation, and
• F(X, Σ) defines the final state.
The initial state and transition relation are given by X′ = {x′ | x ∈X}, which are Boolean
formulas. The final state is defined by a set of Boolean formulas over X ∪Σ. |S| denotes
the sum of the number of symbols in I, T, and F.
Definition 8. State variable: The state variables, indicated by their ambiguously defined
formulas, symbolically represent a set of states. The state variable depicts the systems
propositional properties in a given time in the system.
The name X refers to its property, as it can represents one or more states (from the explicit
NFA or DFA) based on some given properties indicated by their variables.

events, activities, and decisions that collectively lead to outcomes beneficial to customers.

2.6
Reactive Synthesis
The reactive synthesis problem aims to determine whether, in a given system, controlled
actions can be chosen such that, regardless of any uncontrolled actions occurring in the
system, the desired system specifications can be enforced. The reactive synthesis prob-
lem was originally proposed in 1962 [30].
Within the scope of reactive synthesis, this problem can be modeled as a two-player zero-
sum game between a Controller and the Environment. The Controller’s goal is to satisfy
the system specifications, while the Environment’s goal is to violate them. The objective
of the synthesis task is to determine which actions the Controller should take such that, for
any sequence of uncontrolled actions taken by the Environment, the controllable actions
chosen by the Controller will uphold the specifications.
Multiple tools exist for addressing realizability and reactive synthesis problems, many of
which were developed as solutions for The Reactive Synthesis Competition (SYNTCOMP)
[31] and will be briefly mentioned.
Strategy
A strategy can be represented as either a Mealy or Moore machine [12]. In Mealy ma-
chines, the output is determined solely based on the current state, whereas in Moore
machines, the output is determined based on both the current state and the current input.
We adhere to the definitions of Realizability and Strategy as outlined in [12].
Definition 16. Strategy: Let Σ = C ∪U be a set of variables partitioned into controllable
C and uncontrollable U ones. A strategy for Controller is a function s : (2U)+ →2C
that, for any finite sequence U = ⟨U0, . . . , Un⟩of choices by Environment, determines the
choice Cn = s(U) of Controller (Definition 1 [12]).
A strategy for Controller is a function s : (2U)+ →2C that, for any finite sequence U =
⟨U0, . . . , Un⟩of choices by Environment, determines the choice Cn = s(U) of Controller.
Let s : (2U)+ →2C be a strategy and let U = ⟨U0, U1, . . .⟩∈(2U)ω be an infinite sequence
of choices by Environment. We denote by res(s, U) = ⟨U0 ∪s(⟨U0⟩), U1 ∪s(⟨U0, U1⟩), . . .⟩
the trace resulting from reacting to U according to s.
Realizability
The realizability test is also referred to in the literature as a consistency test or satisfi-
ability test. In very abstract terms, it involves determining whether a solution exists for
a given problem. In the context of reactive synthesis, the realizability check (for a given

Brief History of the AIGER Format
In 2006, a compact file format for model checking was discussed at the Alpine Verifica-
tion Meeting in Ascona. The name ”AIGER” was derived from the acronym And-Inverter
Graphs, and it was inspired by the nearby Swiss Alps mountain ’Eiger’ [37].
The AIGER structure has two formats: a binary format and an ASCII format. The ASCII
format is preferred when the system does not require the AIGER library to be saved. It is
simpler to generate and has fewer restrictions. The two formats can be easily identified
by their file endings and headers. The ASCII format begins with the header ’aag’ and is
saved with the ’.aag’ file extension. The name ’aag’ is an acronym for ”Ascii Aiger.” The
binary format, on the other hand, is saved with the ’.aig’ file extension and starts with the
’aig’ header.
AIGER supports both ASCII and binary versions, where the ASCII format is simpler and
easier to generate, while the binary format is more compact and preferred for benchmarks
and competitions.
The binary format is more restricted but also more compact, making it easier for tools to
read, which is why it is usually preferred for benchmarks and competitions. However, the
ASCII format is more human-readable, and there are multiple tools, such as ’aigtoaig,’
that can convert between the two formats. Given the focus of this project, only the ASCII
format will be presented.

Variables and Literals
Literals, which are constants or signed variables, are represented using unsigned inte-
gers. This integer represents their allocated place, often referred to as the index of the
variable. The least significant bit of an unsigned integer encoding a literal acts as the sign
bit, while the remaining bits indicate the variable index. To determine the sign of a literal,
one can perform a modulo 2 operation. If the result is one, the literal is negated; if zero,
it is not negated (e.g., 3 is the negated index of 2). These indexes can only be assigned
once and cannot be reassigned to different variables or literals.
AIGER exclusively models cycle-accurate systems. Latches are assumed to have a reset
state of zero, meaning they are initialized to zero without the need for a clock signal or
reset.

Each element is defined on a separate line. Its index integer defines it, and it is always
defined as an even number. There are four types of elements that can be defined: inputs,
latches, outputs, and And-gates. They must be declared in that specific order. There is
no limit to the number of elements defined, as long as they are syntactically correct, do
not create cycles, and adhere to the defined header.
Inputs are defined by a single integer, their index. Although they can be redefined with an
And-gate, they are usually kept simple as one Boolean variable, i.e., defined as AIGER
variables. They can be thought of as Boolean variables or actions, as in DFA.
Latches are defined with two integers; the first represents their index. Latches represent
a state function where the second integer defines the next state function of that given
latch. In other words, they represent a point in time in the modeled system. They are not
equivalent to, but they are the closest to, a state in an automaton.
Outputs are defined by a single integer. This integer can be either even or odd, with an
odd integer representing the negation of the corresponding even integer. It is the only
index that can be defined as the negation of itself, as demonstrated in Listing 2.5. An
AIGER file that represents any given deterministic automaton will always have only one
output.
And-gates are defined by three integers. The first integer defines the operation and
serves as its index, while the two following literals, given as integers, construct the AND
gate. These two literals can also be the same element, in which case the AND gate acts
as a pointer.

Comments
After the symbol table, an optional comment section may begin. This section starts with
a ’c’ character followed by a newline. The subsequent lines contain the comments. Each
comment starts at the beginning of a line and continues until the next newline character.
There may be no comment lines at all, but if present, the final comment must end with a

Some tools lack the ’\n’ character in the output AIGER, which requires the user to add

Syntax
A final note regarding the syntax of an AAG file: All integers must be positive. All integers
defining the literals are even numbers (except the output, which may be odd). A single
space separates two integers or symbols. There are no spaces between an integer and
the line separator ’\n’. Given in a text form, every line must end with ’\n’, including the last
line of the file. The file allows for duplication in the sense that elements can be defined
multiple times or the same declaration can appear more than once, as long as no cycles
are created.

Closure
Closure is a set containing all the elements of a given formula. It includes all the sub-
formulas, as well as atomic properties that are part of the original formula. The closure is
obtained by flattening the complex formula into its individual elements and placing them in
a new set of formulas. This process ensures that from this point onwards, we can refer to
the closure set with the assurance that it contains all the elements of the original formula.
The closure function of an LTLfp formula, denoted by C(ψ) over the alphabet Σ is defined
as the smallest set of formulas satisfying the following properties given in Equation (3.5)
recursively.
(1) ψ ∈C(ψ), and every sub-formula is also in C(ψ).
(2) For any proposition p ∈Σ, p ∈C(ψ) if and only if ¬p ∈C(ψ).
(3) For any formula of the temporal formula Since ϕ1Sϕ2, the formula Yesterday:
Y (ϕ1Sϕ2) should be added to C(ψ).

3.2.5
Time Complexity Improvement
Existing algorithms for the realizability of an LTLf formula have a 2EXPTIME complexity
[14]. DECLARE specifications can be translated into the LTLf formula Equation (2.3)
and realized through an LTLf solver, maintaining the 2EXPTIME complexity. The typical
approach for solving realizability and reactive synthesis for DECLARE is through an LTLf
solver, resulting in the same 2EXPTIME complexity.
The complexity of the algorithm presented is derived from the following components: Lin-
ear encoding of DECLARE specifications into LTLfp, achieved through a linear-size trans-
formation of DECLARE into LTLf, which was then translated into LTLfp. The system’s
actions have been shown to be pastified in linear size. Overall, it has been demonstrated
that the transformation of DECLARE specifications into SDFA can be done in singly ex-
ponential time and linear size.
Given that it has been shown that reachability games can be solved in PTIME with re-
spect to the size of the input [1, 40], demonstrates, using Algorithm 0, that DECLARE
can be pastified in polynomial size, allowing for the provision of a singly exponential-time
algorithm for the realizability problem.

3.3
Problem Statement
There are clear divergence between the current state of a proposed definitions and wished
state of a fully operational tool for reactive synthesis of DECLARE. We present the main
three gaps which we believe can produce an implementation for the suggested algorithm.
3.3.1
Construction of SDFA from LTLfp
The creation of an SDFA from a given LTLfp formula is a complex task that involves
multiple sub-definitions and calculations, which are fully described in the existing literature
but not yet implemented. If this implementation existed, one could create an SDFA from
any given LTLfp, allowing easy implementation of the suggested procedure. This would
enable reactive synthesis for any LTLfp in singly-exponential time, instead of the double-
exponential time used for LTLf.
3.3.2
Implementation of Reactive Synthesis for DECLARE
Described in Chapter 3 are the definitions necessary for creating an algorithm for the reac-
tive synthesis of enhanced DECLARE specification. To the best of our knowledge, there
is no existing implementation of a reactive synthesis algorithm for DECLARE. In other
words, neither the suggested definitions have been implemented, nor does any alterna-
tive algorithm or implementation exist. Additionally, there is a lack of implementation for
creating an SDFA from LTLfp formulas, as mentioned in section 3.3.1.
3.3.3
Apprehending SDFA into AIGER
The last step of algorithm 0 asks to perform a realizability check and extract a strategy
from the given DFA. One of the most common file types used for reactive synthesis is .aag,
i.e., ASCII AIGER. There exists no algorithm or implementation that, given an SDFA, can
produce a .aag file while preserving its deterministic properties.

right side of the Triggers formula, and on the right side, there is a disjunction of the com-
puted left side of the Triggers and the original Triggers formula, wrapped with a Weak

4.2.7
Closure
The closure formula definition Equation (3.5). In this section, we will elaborate on the
definition and explain how it should be implemented in Algorithm 6. The modified function
called earlier in Algorithm 4 has updated the formula to fit the scope of the closure defini-
tion. This modification ensures that we work with an equivalent formula that consists only
level of past modalities as defined in Section 2.2.1.
The closure formula of an LTLfp formula, denoted by C(ψ) over the alphabet Σ, is defined
as the smallest set of formulas satisfying the following properties recursively:
1. ψ is in C(ψ), and every sub-formula is also in C(ψ). A sub-formula is any formula in ψ
with the Boolean connectives or Temporal modalities, i.e., {¬, ∨, ∧, Y, Z, S, T}.
2. For any proposition p ∈Σ, p ∈C(ψ) if and only if ¬p ∈C(ψ), meaning that when a
proposition is added, its negation should also be added to the set of formulas.
3. For any temporal formula of the type Since ϕ1Sϕ2, the formula Yesterday: Y (ϕ1Sϕ2)
should be added to C(ψ). Similarly, for a formula of the type Triggers ϕ1Tϕ2, the formula
Weak Yesterday Z(ϕ1Tϕ2) is added to C(ψ).
Algorithm 6 takes the given LTLfp formula and strives to partition it into all its sub-formulas
through recursion. First, it constructs the set of closure C and adds the original formula
to that set.
As the base case, if the formula consists of an atomic action that is part of the given Σ, in
addition to the already added action, we include the negated version of the given action.
For the past modalities Yesterday and Weak Yesterday, in addition to adding them to the
set, their sub-formulas are also passed as parameters to the closure function. The same
applies to the logical operations (∧, ∨).
For the past modalities Since and Triggers, an additional operation is required. As be-

either an atom (variable), a literal (a latch defined to a constant), or by some ∧operations
on the preceding elements. This algorithm, commonly used with Algorithm 18, takes
a given list of indexes of declared variables and literals and constructs an AIGER And
operation. This declaration is then associated with a given defined literal.
Algorithm 17 is a recursive algorithm. Its base case occurs when the input list has a length
of 1. As long as the list has at least two elements, we create an And operation in AIGER.
This operation is saved to a new index. We then remove those two elements from the list
and append the new index to the end of the list. It is important to append it to the end of
the list to minimize the number of operations in our file.

value representing the amount of And operations i updated accordingly.

4.3.9
AIGER Final State
Any given SDFA has a final state formula by definition. In the extreme case where this
formula is empty, the system is not realizable. Therefore, this formula is assumed to be
non-empty. It should also have a representation of each of the state variables ion the
formula.
The given final state formula typically consists of a representation of multiple states, where
the system would be True in any one of them. This usually implies that the formula will
include some logical ∨operations.
The output index defined in Algorithm 16 will be used as the final state, meaning that the
result of the final state formula calculation will be mapped to that index. This is how we
will identify the automaton in the AIGER file.
For reactive synthesis, it is essential to specify that our defined init state is not part of
the final state of the system. Otherwise, in the uncontrolled system, there will always be
a state-the initial state (init)-that would not be realized. Given the requirements of the
AIGER file, the formula must be modified to a CNF form. This means that the final state
formula in the AIGER format looks as in Equation (4.2).
¬init ∧CNF(F(X, Σ))

4.4.2
Code Structure with singledispatch
The structure of several functions implemented in the tool utilizes singledispatch, initially
inspired by the rewrite function in the Plan4Past tool3. singledispatch allows writing
Python code in a functional way, or in other words, specifying which sub-function should
be addressed based on the type of the input. As mentioned in Section 4.4.1, parsing
formulas with pylogics_modalities to LTLfp enables the identification of the modality of
the object based on its class. We use this feature to our advantage in multiple parts of
the implementation of SymbSyntDec as well as in a few instances in SymDFA2Aiger.
The function for invoking this part of the implementation retains the main name of the func-
tion and is specified with the @singledispatch notation preceding it. If no sub-formula
can be called, this main function is executed. The sub-formulas are defined using the no-
tation @FunctionName.register (where FunctionName is the name of the main function)
preceding the function.
As previously mentioned, to obtain the sub-formula of a given formula, one needs to
use either the .operands or .argument operations. The .argument operation is used
for Not, Yesterday, and Weak Yesterday, all of which have in common only one sub-
formula.
In most of the implementations, rather than calling it directly, the functions
FunctionName_unaryop (where FunctionName is updated accordingly) are employed for
this task. An example of this can be seen in modify_unaryop presented in Listing 4.7.
On the other hand, .operands is used when there are at least two sub-formulas. Al-
though the classical definition typically involves only two sub-formulas for a given logical or
modality operator, pylogics and, consequently, pylogics_modalities are implemented
to ’flatten the tree,’ meaning that when possible, all functions with the same modalities
or logic are defined as a single tuple of formulas. For example, a ∧b ∧c is defined as
And(a,b,c) instead of (a ∧b) ∧c, or a ∧(b ∧c). An example of using this function can be
seen in the modify_or function in Listing 4.7.

State Variables
The method responsible for creating the state variables is state_variables, and it is
structured similarly to closure. It includes a call function named state_variables and
an operational call named state_variables_operands. It returns two dictionaries, one
with all state variables with their symbolic names as key and formula as value, alongside
with a comprehensive dictionary.
The comprehensive dictionary contains all available information about the set of state
variables. This includes the following mappings, where the left side indicates the key and
the right side the value:
• Symbolic name →formula
• Formula →symbolic name
• Modality →list of all symbolic names associated with that modality
This function is invoked with the output of closure, i.e., the Closure_set, as input. Recall
that the closure operation adds the formula and all its sub-formulas (some in negated
form) to the set. Additionally, some formulas are wrapped with the modalities Yesterday
and Weak Yesterday.
As defined in Equation (3.6) and Algorithm 7, a state variable is defined for each element
in the closure set that has the modality Yesterday or Weak Yesterday. Any other formula
in the closure that is not wrapped in these modalities should not be included in the set of
state variables.
When a state variable needs to be added to the set and dictionary, the helper function
add_variable is used. If the formula is not already present in the comprehensive dictio-
nary, it is first added to the simple dictionary. Subsequently, it is added to the compre-

for each input are detailed in Listing 4.24.
As mentioned, the tool utilizes the pylogics_modalities library, and all the formulas
correspond to the LTLfp definitions provided by it.
The state variables are provided as a set. This set includes the names of the given state
variables. It is defined as a set of formulas, but the implementation also accepts a set of
strings. These names must correspond with the other inputs in the subsequent definitions.

The set of actions, Σ, must be provided as two disjoint sets: one for controlled actions and
one for uncontrolled actions. The tool expects the controlled actions to be provided first,
followed by the uncontrolled ones. If these sets are not disjoint, an error will be raised,
and execution will be halted.
Initial state is a formula of type PLTLAnd constructed from the atomic values of each of
the defined state variables. Each state variable must appear exactly once, either in a
negated or non-negated form. If a state variable is defined but not included in the initial
state formula, the system will crash. Similarly, if a state variable is included in the initial
state but not defined in the set of states, the system will also fail.
The final state has requirements are simple. The final state formula must be a WFF PL
formula, where all its propositions are either part of the action sets or the set of state
variable. The tool will crash if a state variable is defined in the final state but not present
in the set of states.
This tool was implemented to follow the specifications outlined by SymbSyntDec. In this
context, the transition system is represented as a dictionary instead of a single AND for-
mula with multiple if-and-only-if sub-formulas. The key of this dictionary is the primed
state variable, and the value is a PL formula defined based on the actions and the state
variables. The formula is assumed to adhere to the definition in Equation (3.11). The key
should be formatted as STATE_VAR + ”_prime”, where STATE_VAR is the name of the
unprimed state variable. For example, x_var5_prime : x_var5 ∨skip ∨ship ∨cancel,
where x_var5 should be in the set of state variables.

by the tool. The default name is SymbDFA_AIGER.aag. If a name is provided without the
file type .aag, the tool will appended it before creating the file.

original formulas. As mentioned in Section 4.5.6, the symbolic names used do not repre-

an informational dictionary can be provided, with the symbolic name (i.e., the state vari-

comments are added to the AIGER file, which enhances its value and documentation,

This part of the implementation modifies the header, literals, and symbol.
The header section includes I the number of inputs, referred to here as act (for actions).
This represents the number of inputs defined in the system. For each new action defined,
whether controlled or uncontrolled, this value is incremented by one.
The literal part of the code, represented by a_action (where a stands for AIGER), is
defined in this function for the actions. Defining an action in the AIGER format is straight-
forward. It should be defined on a new line with an index identifying it. The index must
be an even number, and all indexes in the system will be sequential. The variable i2, as
explained in Section 4.6.1, is used.
After the action is defined, it is saved to the dictionary. The key is the name of the action,
and the value is its index, i.e., i2. Once saved, act is incremented. index.i is then
incremented for the next use of i2 or t2.
The symbol section, represented by s_action, is updated to include the necessary infor-
mation for reactive synthesis for this file. The defined action can be listed in the symbol
table. Each action which is added to the symbol table is added on a new line, starting
with the character i, followed by a serial number beginning at 0. This is then followed by
a space and an indication of whether the action is controlled or uncontrolled.
The uncontrolled actions have looser requirements, allowing for optional inclusion in the
symbol table. If included, their name should be specified after the space. For complete-
ness, this implementation includes all of them and specifies their names as the strings
representing the actions. e.g. ”i4 i_pay” for the uncontrolled action defined as the fifth
action with the name pay.
Controlled actions, however, have stricter requirements to facilitate reactive synthesis.
After the index and number, followed by a space, the string controllable_ must be in-
cluded. Although it might seem minor, the underscore character ”_” must be present.
Following this, there are flexible requirements. In this implementation, it is set
to controllable_NAME, where NAME represents the name of the action being defined. e.g.
i0 controllable_skip for the first controlled action with the action name skip.

actions must be non-empty. There is also safety measures implemented to ensure the
action sets are disjoint.

4.6.4
AIGER Latches
In the implementation of SymDFA2AIGER, latches are defined in three separate occasions.
They are first defined in the state variable function (aiger_state_variables), then when
the flag init is declared (aiger_init), and lastly when the transition relation is being
defined (aiger_transition). All three implementations follow the proposed solutions
presented in Algorithm 14, Algorithm 15, and Algorithm 20, respectively.
The latches are defined using the syntax specified in Section 2.6.2. All latches are defined
in both the literal section and the symbol section, adhering to the relevant syntax rules.
As previously mentioned, for a deterministic automaton represented in an AIGER file, it
is crucial that no unnecessary latches are defined. If unnecessary latches are included,
the AIGER system would become nondeterministic. When defining the state variable,
one latch is produced for each state variable. When defining init, one latch is defined.
Lastly, in the transition system, one latch is defined for each primed variable of the state
variables. Since there is only one primed variable for each state variable, the total number
of latches is 2 × |state variables| + 1.
All three functions define information for both the literal section and the symbol section.
None of them contribute to the comment section. As mentioned in Section 4.6.2, one of
the non-mandatory inputs can produce comments for the state variables.
The sequence in which these three functions are called is essential. The aiger_transition
function must be called last of the three, as it uses variables defined in the other two. As
shown in Listing 4.26, it relies on the index for the declared flag init and the indexes
saved for the primed variables. As explained in Section 4.2.8, the placeholder for the
primed variables was created earlier, given that the unprimed version of the state vari-
able points to the primed version. For example, var_x →var_x_prime or in code syntax,
12 →14.
The init flag is necessary even though it could be directly defined in the state as its value
1. The main purpose of this decision is based on the fact that the system is initializing all
latches to False. The init latch, is the one state in the system which in the initialization
is set to True, i.e represents the initial state of the system.
For the successful construction of the AIGER file, the different PL formulas must be in CNF
format. The aiger_transition function is one of those that uses this implementation.
The cnf function defined in the implementation utilize the singledispatch.

Conclusion and Discussion
In this project, SymbSyntDec was introduced as a pipeline designed for reactive synthesis
tailored to the Enhanced DECLARE specification. This tool is one of the primary contribu-
tions of this project, creating a concrete solution based on the assumptions and algorithm
proposed in [1], which is shown to possess EXPTIME complexity.
DECLARE, a well-established declarative, pattern-based language grounded in LTLf, is
extensively used in BPM. SymbSyntDec has proven capable of processing any enhanced
DECLARE specification and generate a strategy that addresses the reactive synthesis
problem for that specification.
This pipeline processes the specifications and transforms them into an LTLfp formula
through several steps of pastification. Subsequently, this LTLfp formula is converted into
an SDFA, demonstrating the pipeline’s effectiveness in translating any given LTLfp into
an SDFA.
A significant challenge encountered during the project was the unanticipated AIGER trans-
formation step. This crucial intermediate step, essential for the overall solution, had not
previously been implemented in open-source solutions. Threfore, a novel procedure for
translating SDFA into the AIGER format was presented. This procedure was developed
as a tool named SymbDFA2AIGER, which translates any SDFA into the AIGER format, and
it marks a key contribution of this thesis.
Both SymbSyntDec and SymbDFA2AIGER have been released as libraries on PyPI. Because
any solution for SYNTCOMP can be utilized for the realizability and reactive synthesis
tasks, the updated SafetySynth was employed.
Although the implementation is complete, further exhaustive testing, validation, and the
extraction of strategies in a human-readable format are still pending. These elements
highlight the additional work required to validate and fully enhance the solution. While
further work is necessary to ensure the procedure is fully operational and userfriendly,
this project lays a strong foundation for future research and development in the field of
reactive synthesis for SDFA and declarative process specifications.

Future Work
This chapter explores potential paths for extending and enhancing the current work if
time had allowed it. Future research could focus on addressing the gaps identified in this
project by optimizing and further developing the solutions presented. Another key de-
velopment area involves enhancing the accessibility and usability of the tools developed.
Additionally, and most importantly, further validation beyond the case testing is essential
to ensure the reliability of the implementation. Finally, expanding the applicability of the
approach to other domains and refining the strategy extraction process for better human
readability are promising directions for future exploration.
No-Op Operation
As mentioned in Section 2.7, based on a discussion from both [1, 12], it could be sug-
gested that a no-op operation be added to the system. Such an operation could be con-
sidered for inclusion in the actions of both the environment and the controller. Although
not introduced formally, adding a no-op operation would be a trivial step that would not
affect the outcome of the reactive synthesis problem.
On the other hand, adding such an operation to either set of actions could have more
complex implications. The first question to address is how to consistently add a no-op
operation to the action set while keeping the original constraints of the system intact.
Given that the suggested approach is to use the no-op only when it is the other player’s
turn, allowing that player to perform multiple actions during that round introduces two
properties that need to be validated: Firstly, does the added action alter the intended
specification of the system? Secondly, is it possible to misuse the added action, such as
passing the turn to the other party when any other action might lead to an unfavorable
outcome?
Introducing such an action to the parties might mean that in any state where a player must
act, it could choose to perform this no-op action if it anticipates that any other action might
lead to a losing state. It could potentially change the solution for a system that does not
include a no-op operation.
This process has not been tested in this project but could be an interesting subject for
further development.
Validation
Validation is critical in ensuring that the models, methods, and tools developed are accu-
rate, reliable, and applicable to real-world scenarios. The Achilles heel of this project is
the lack of comprehensive validation for the assumptions and solutions presented. The
best scenario would involve validating all the assumptions in this project while also validat-
ing some of the dependency assumptions from [1], on which the solutions of this project
heavily rely.
Although some syntactic checks have been performed using nuXmv by comparing the
SymDFA2AIGER output AIGER files with SMV files derived from the same SDFA, this cannot
be considered a complete validation of the solution.

Future work is suggested to validate the entire pipeline and tools, which should also in-
volve using different reactive synthesis tools to compare the results of the AIGER files
created.
Furthermore, validation is required for the assumption that any system without uncontrol-

Strategy
The final output of the SymbSyntDec pipeline is a strategy created with the SafetySynth
tool on the AIGER file. This strategy is supposed to follow Definition 16, defining which
controllable action should be taken based on the past uncontrollable action.
The strategies presented in Chapter 5 lack the essential property of being easily readable
and understood. This is one of the most significant gaps in this project, as it lacks a
human-readable description of the strategy, which would provide a clear depiction of which
controllable action should be taken at each timestamp.
A search was conducted to find a tool that could represent the strategy in a more rec-
ognizable way for workers from the BPMN field. The closest finding was BoSy1, which
allegedly, at the time of its deployment, could create a DOT Graph with the relevant ac-
tions that should be taken as labels. However, this tool has not been maintained, meaning
this feature is no longer functional.
Another path for validating the strategy, is to flip the system. i.e., check if a strategy exists
such that an en environment can find a strategy such that the system would always be
unrealizable. It calls for redefining the algorithm and procedure, creating the SDFA. Such
a system, should be assured or falsified if the system is realizable.
This highlights an important area for future development, both for this tool and in the
broader field, creating a tool that, when deployed, can take a specific or general strategy,
such as those produced by SafetySynth or any other tool from the SYNTCOMP, and
render it in a human-readable format.
Another segment for future work on the strategy has to do with its validation. Validation
can be suggested by flipping the system, i.e., to check if a strategy exists where the envi-
ronment can ensure that the system is always unrealizable. If such exists, with the right
settings, it is a clear indication that the original problem is not realizable. This approach
involves redefining the algorithm and procedure for creating the SDFA. Given such a
system, the realizability of the system can either be confirmed or refuted.

The function assures that the key is uniform for any set of two elements to ensure that
only one object exist for any two indexes. In other words, it checks that {x, y} and {y, x}
will have the same key in the dictionary. If the key exists, index representing the value
is returned, and otherwise, the value computed by the And function is used, as shown
in Listing 4.27, to write the code and create a new index, which is then saved to the
dictionary.
This function checks only two indexes at at time. It has its advantages, but it also has its
limitations. As an example: (a ∧b) ∧(c ∧d) ≡(a ∧c) ∧(b ∧d) create six And operations,
where ideally would have been three, given that the right and left sides are equal.
When the input is given to the program, it can create a list of all the lists that need to be
combined with an And operation. Those lists can be sorted in such a way that the amount
of operations needed is limited by reusing made operations. It will minimize the number
of And declarations to a minimum, but will require extensive memory and precalculations.
A proposed implementation for such a calculation could include two key elements: a data
structure that saves all computed values and a specific stepwise implementation.
A dictionary can be an effective data structure for this purpose. The dictionary, where the
keys are a hashing value of the indexes producing the And operation, and the index of
the calculated And operation or operations are stored as its values, would store both the
intermediate operations and the whole operation. This ensures that any two identical lists
provided will only be calculated once.
However, this dictionary alone is not sufficient, especially in scenarios where the aim is to
keep the And operations to the minimum. It is equally important to reorganize the list before
performing calculations. Given that the lists represent a conjunction operations, their
sequence has no significance. The most common occurrence within the lists and sub-
lists, should be identified to limit the overall number of operations, a notably demanding
task. A suggested implementation to a sub-problem of such has been found4, though it
does not resolve the entire problem.

Project Plan
Title: SymbSyntDec: An Automated Pipeline for Reactive
Synthesis for Declarative Specifications
Milestones for the Project:
Establishing the Background
This part includes writing the project plan and understanding all the background material
related to the reactive synthesis of DECLARE specifications. As the reference material,
the seminal paper on reactive synthesis for DECLARE should be used (link). In what
follows, we use [1] to refer to this paper. This step also includes a preliminary study of
reactive synthesis tools that are suitable for the pipeline described below.
Pipeline Creation
This milestone focuses on the steps that should be followed in order to generate strategies
from given DECLARE specifications.
Input Preparation
Given two DECLARE specifications (conjunctions of DECLARE patterns), one for Con-
troller and the other for Environment, perform a set of standard manipulation steps. These
steps include:
1. Transforming the DECLARE specifications into their LTLfp counterparts.
2. Introducing additional formulas needed to guarantee the assume-guarantee formu-
lation of the problem as well as simple-trace strategy creation (where choices by
Controller and Environment must strictly alternate). Information needed for (1) and
(2) has been fully established in [1].
At the end of this step, one obtains an LTLfp formula representing all the information from
above.
LTLfp Formula into a Symbolic DFA
This step relies on definitions from [1] and focuses on creating a complete algorithm for
translating LTLfp formulas into symbolic DFA. The algorithm must rely on constructive
definitions from [1]. The first outcome of this step is the algorithm in pseudo-code. The
second outcome of this step is the actual implementation of the algorithm. The input
and output formats for this algorithm will be initially based on the preliminary study done
before.
Integration of a Reactive Synthesis Tool
Decide on a reactive synthesis tool based on the preliminary study of the tools. Integrate
the tool into the pipeline and try to prioritize automation as much as possible (that is, all
steps should be connected in one program). The tool should output a strategy (if the
original specification is realizable). Otherwise, it should show the unrealizability result.
Writing the Thesis
This would be a big part of the delivery. It should include all the necessary background,
terms, and terminology reflecting the pipeline created and connecting it to the current
research in the process mining field. The writing will be done throughout the project and
not only in the final stages.
