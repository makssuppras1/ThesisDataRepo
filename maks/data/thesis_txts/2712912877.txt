Master Thesis
1
Abstract
Green hydrogen is increasingly cited as a solution to the decarbonisation of industry.
Its large-scale production is the subject of many questions, particularly on the economic
aspect. This thesis aims to create and explain a versatile cost model composed of several
modules to analyse the financial aspect of green hydrogen production from offshore wind
energy.
After acquaintance with the models already used in the literature, a schedule-based model
is created allowing to simulate the reliability, maintenance operations as well as the pro-
duction for both the wind farm and the hydrogen production system. Using a Monte-Carlo
loop, the failures of each of these systems are simulated and taken into account. The costs
involved in production are thus recorded and analysed.
For the offshore wind part, the electricity production is modelled and an LCoE of 101.3
e/MWh is obtained. This electricity is sent to the hydrogen production system with a
pre-determined set point and, when both systems are taken into account, an LCoH of 6.76
e/kg is calculated.
When a hybrid renewable energy system (HRES) producing electricity and hydrogen, a
characteristic point emerges at which it becomes more profitable to buy electricity from
the grid than to produce it from the farm.
Furthermore, the work on optimising the
lifetime of the stack shows that, under the conditions presented, a lifetime of 70,000 hours
gives the lowest LCoH.
The results show that green hydrogen is not currently competitive in the hydrogen market,
but that the technology has the potential to mature and compete with grey and blue
hydrogen in the future.

Introduction
3.1
Background
Global warming and the need to change our consumption patterns to reduce our impact on
the environment has led to the emergence of new low carbon energy production methods.
Indeed, the overuse of fossil fuels has significant damage to our atmosphere and their
limited supply makes them a fragile energy source that we must be able to do without
in the coming decades. To meet these demands, large-scale production of electricity from
renewable energy sources is a must, and in this context, offshore wind power seems to be
an unavoidable solution. The main disadvantage of renewable energies, and particularly
of offshore wind, is that electricity production is highly dependent on the location of the
wind farm and the time of year. Electricity can be stored in batteries but this solution
is bulky and difficult to transport. The use of hydrogen as an energy carrier can thus
respond to this problem. The benefits of using hydrogen is that it makes it much easier to
store and transport energy over long distances with minimal losses. Using the electricity
generated by offshore wind farms to produce large quantities of clean hydrogen can be a
way of meeting the industry’s ever-increasing demands for hydrogen while keeping carbon
emissions low.
Since the early 2000s, the use of offshore wind energy has increased considerably, from 0
MW in 1998 to 35,300 MW in 2021 [1]. Countries such as China, the USA and Japan
have recently accelerated the development of offshore energy on their territory, allowing
this still recent technology to gain greatly in maturity. The North Sea is nevertheless
very much involved in offshore energy production and Belgium in particular is producing
approximately 2,150 MW of offshore wind in 2021 [2], which is just over 6% of the world’s
offshore wind production[1].
The installation of more wind farms requires a lot of capital, a very large upstream invest-
ment and significant costs in the operational part. Adding hydrogen production from the
electricity supplied seems to be a way to create a lot of hydrogen, but the understanding
of the costs involved in such a realisation needs to be properly studied to ensure financial
profitability in comparison to other means of production.
The aim of this thesis project is therefore to develop a cost model that is sufficiently
detailed and flexible to quantify the profitability of the hydrogen production from offshore
wind energy in Belgium and to assess the competitiveness of such production on the
current hydrogen market.
3.2
Research Objectives
The objectives of this thesis project are
1. Develop a detailed and flexible cost model based on existing literature.
2. Use this cost model on a selected case study.
3. Assess the results in comparison with the similar analysis results found in the literature.

3.3
Research Question
• Is hydrogen production from offshore wind in Belgium competitive in the current
hydrogen market?
3.4
Approach
In order to understand the ins and outs of hydrogen production from offshore wind energy,
the author of this research first conducted a review of the existing literature. He chooses to
divide the cost calculation into capital expenditure, operational expenditure, and revenue
module. He then uses an existing model or develops one of his own to calculate the costs
of each of these parts and creates a versatile global model that takes into account the
majority of the costs involved. Finally, he uses this developed cost model to economically
model a case study and thus make a comparison with other ways of producing hydrogen.

4.1
Hydrogen Policy
4.1.1
In Europe
In order to combat global warming, Europe has set a number of targets to be achieved in
the coming years. The most important of these is the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions
by 40% in 2030 and by 80-95% in 2050 [3]. Part of this will therefore involve low-carbon
energy production. In the REDII report, it is indeed announced that the goal is to have
one third of the total energy consumption in 2030 coming from renewable energies [4].
This includes 14% of the total fuel consumption for roads and rails to be renewable. The
use of bio-fuels is limited to 7%, which opens the way to other energies such as hydrogen.
For maritime transport, the EU maritime directive announces an ambitious target of
a 55% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 [5].These targets therefore clearly
illustrate the European Union’s desire to decarbonise as many sectors as possible to greatly
encourage the energy transition. There is therefore a great need to increase hydrogen
production to meet these expectations and the EU wants to have 40 GW of renewable
hydrogen electrolysis by 2030 [6]. However, a legal framework is still lacking in order to
develop the recent hydrogen technology to its full potential, both from a safety, economic
and social point of view. Many legal barriers need to be removed and it is the HyLaw
project, led by 23 partners from 18 European countries, which could help to improve
this perspective [3]. Other international collaboration projects are also being set up by
the European Union, such as the Green Octopus project, which aims to have a European
backbone in 2030/2035 to facilitate the transport and supply of hydrogen between Belgium,
the Netherlands and Germany with possible links to France and Denmark, facilitated by
the ports [9].

4.1.2
In Belgium
From the Belgian point of view, a number of targets have also been announced to take
advantage of the country’s favourable weather conditions to develop renewable energies.
In its NECP (National Energy and Climate Plans), Belgium estimates its wind power
capacity at 6.5 GW in 2030 and targets between 400 MW (low scenario) and 2280 MW
(high scenario) of installed electrolysers for green hydrogen production [8]. This would
contribute between 1 and 5% of the country’s greenhouse gas emission reduction [7]. A
revision of the tax system is also envisaged to encourage this transition. Finally, the NECP
targets announce a use of offshore wind at 0.2 to 1.1 TWh/y and a hydrogen cost between
6.5EUR/kgH2 and 8.3EUR/kgH2. In Flanders, there are now at least 20 companies and
almost 18,000 employees with research activities in the field of hydrogen[14].

4.2
Hydrogen Production
4.2.1
Types of hydrogen
Generally, hydrogen is described in different colours depending on the origin of the energy
used to produce it and the treatment of the gases produced [10]. In particular, there is
grey, yellow, green and blue hydrogen. Yellow hydrogen is produced from nuclear energy
while grey and blue hydrogen is produced from fossil energy, the difference between the two
being that the carbon dioxide emitted is captured and stored for blue hydrogen. Finally,
the hydrogen studied in this research and which is said to be "clean" is the green hydrogen,

4.3.2
Capital Expenditure (CAPEX)
Capital expenditure, or CAPEX, is often considered the initial investment required to
build the system before hydrogen production has even begun. Normally, CAPEX does
not take into account financial costs such as interest rates, as well as the financing structure
such as debt or equity[15]. It can be divided into three parts, the first being the price of
the stack, the part allowing the conversion between chemical and electrical energy. The
second part is the Balance of Plant, written BoP, which concerns all the components
such as the electrolysis of water and its auxiliary systems but it does not include the
generation of electricity itself. It also concerns the transport and delivery of energy and
the price of infrastructure. The third and final part is the financial costs that may appear
as contingency, insurance or other peripheral costs.
The first component of CAPEX is therefore the cost of the stack, the part of the electrolyzer
that allows the conversion of chemical energy into electrical energy. It comes in different
Page 12 of 69
==PAGE:19==
Master Thesis
forms depending on the type of electrolysis chosen: PEM, AEL or SOE. The cost of the
stack depends firstly on the capacity of the chosen electrolyzer and will often be given in
EUR/kWh or EUR/MWh. As is often the case, the higher the capacity of the electrolyser
is, the lower the price per kilowatt-hour is. The main components of this price will be first
of all the price of the material used, then the price of the labor. Finally comes the process
price for the building, for the operational part and for the capital part[16]. Various data
is available in the bibliography, in particular IRENA[18] or the BEIS department of the
United Kingdom[17], the costs strongly depending on the year of the study, the chosen
assumptions and the studied manufacturers.
The second component of CAPEX is the Balance of Plant, which corresponds to auxil-
iary systems and the price of infrastructure. The BoP is the most expensive part of the
CAPEX of hydrogen production according to IRENA[18], on average about 55% of the
total CAPEX. This average varying according to the manufacturers and the producer’s
choices regarding production (onshore, offshore, distance from the coast, capacity, etc).
According to BEIS [17], this component includes the drier, the cooler, the de-ionizer, the
cost of infrastructure and the electricity grid connection. According to IRENA[18], BoP
also includes the power supply, the deionised water circulation, the hydrogen processing
and the cooling. Moreover, if the studied LCoH corresponds to the produced hydrogen,
these components are sufficient. However, within the framework of the study of the deliv-
ered hydrogen, it is necessary to add the compression part as well as the transport costs
of the hydrogen.
The third and final component of CAPEX is financial costs. This part is usually negotiated
between the stakeholders in a confidential manner and information about it is usually not
described in the literature. It is moreover difficult to see emerging hypotheses and trends
because these projects are not yet set up on a large scale, so the necessary hindsight for
banks and insurance companies to be able to analyze the risks of such a production is not
yet present. It is thus difficult for the insurance companies to set up precise insurances
taking into account the risks, just like for the banks and the investors to anticipate the
return on investment and to decide the cost of the debt.
Most of the cost models currently in use do not separate CAPEX into three parts as
described. They instead use a cost per megawatt of capacity to describe CAPEX in a very
simple way where the accuracy is debatable. As explained above, the larger the production
capacity, the lower the price per megawatt due to the economy of scale. Thus this method
must be adapted to the capacity, for example by estimating the percentage cost reduction
achieved for large-scale production as it is done in the Scottish Government study [19].
4.3.3
Operational Expenditure (OPEX)
In the field of energy and in particular hydrogen, operational expenditures, also commonly
called OPEX, concern the daily or, in our case, annual costs, allowing the company to
keep its hydrogen production operational. As was done in the CAPEX part, the OPEX
part can be divided into several parts:
• Maintenance cost
• Stack Replacement cost
Page 13 of 69
==PAGE:20==
Master Thesis
• Tap Water cost
• Electricity cost
The literature found on the maintenance costs and the stack replacement costs topics
shows that these costs are often separated into fixed and variable costs, the composition
of each also depending on the quality and accuracy sought in the study. Most of the
research reviewed estimates the OPEX part of the hydrogen production as a percentage
of the CAPEX [25][19][24]. This method is very simple and different components of the
operational costs are not visible. One of the goals of our research is to get a clear overview
of these costs in place in the production of scale from offshore wind energy. Therefore,
the OPEX of the hydrogen production needs to be modelled by using all operational
parameters such as availability, failures, etc.
The lack of experience with large scale hydrogen production prevent us to obtain accurate
costs for the OPEX part and in particular for maintenance. It was estimated that the
maintenance is about 15 days per year by a professional working in the hydrogen field [20]
even though this information is not based on the field experience. A similar value (14.6
days/year) was found in the literature as well [60].
The cost of maintenance changes dramatically depending on whether you have offshore or
onshore hydrogen production facility. This is due to the additional logistics involved in
offshore operations such as vessel rentals and finding the workable weather windows for
maintenance, etc. These factors make offshore operations more expensive.
When it comes to the water supply part, it is necessary to take into account the hydrogen
generation requirement in order to obtain the amount of water needed and finally to
obtain its cost. The water price depends on the geographical location but also on the
time of the year when the water is taken. For offshore hydrogen production, sea water
must be desalinated and purified, but it is nevertheless free and present in almost infinite
quantities.
For onshore hydrogen production, the system is usually connected to the
continental water network and the supply must be properly anticipated. According to
Song et al[21], it takes 9 liters of water to create 1 kilogram of hydrogen but, taking into
account possible evaporation during the process, the volume of pure water needed is 10
liters per kilogram of hydrogen produced.
Over the life of the hydrogen production system, it is possible that the stack may need to be
replaced if it is damaged or inefficient. This naturally requires maintenance, temporarily
stopping the production of hydrogen. In 2020, for a PEM electrolyzer, the lifetime of the
stack was between 30,000 and 90,000 hours while it is estimated to be between 60,000 and
90,000 hours for 2030[13]. This time remains approximate and will gain in precision with
the multiplication of this type of project and the improvement of the data available for
the production of hydrogen on a large scale with this type of electrolyzer.
Finally, the principle of water electrolysis is to use electricity to separate water molecules.
One of the operational costs is therefore the cost of the electricity required. Many models
are available to estimate the LCoE (Levelized cost of energy) for the electricity produced
by offshore wind farms. The goal will be to evaluate different cost models to find the
missing elements to include hydrogen generation capability. By anticipating the efficiency
of the stack and the various losses, it is possible to obtain the electrical power necessary to
produce one kilogram of hydrogen. Depending on the production scenario, the production

4.3.4
Decommissioning Expenditure (DECEX)
Decommissioning cost (DECEX) must be considered if the entire life cycle of the plant is
required to model because these costs occur when the system has finished its operational
life. Some models available in the literature are not very precise on this subject, but
decommissioning costs are supposed to include dismantling, decontamination, removal
and also the engineering necessary to prepare such actions [22].
4.4
State of the art of existing cost models
4.4.1
Current Approach
As previously stated, the concept of producing hydrogen from offshore wind energy is still
quite recent and the necessary hindsight is not yet present, especially to know some details
of the operational part, the lifetime and the deterioration of the efficiency of the stack for a
large scale production of hydrogen. For the moment, the models available in the literature
still lack fidelity but the studies are multiplying as well as the projects which will enable
us to assess the costs of the green hydrogen production more realistically.
4.4.2
Concept Consulting Model
Description and assumption
This study [23] focuses on reducing greenhouse gas emissions in New Zealand by using
green hydrogen in the main "energy services" such as transport, electricity generation
and heating to replace fossil fuels. This model has been developed for the global case
of renewable energy use but not for offshore wind in particular. The costs have been
calculated for 2019, with the addition of an estimate of the costs in 20 years to be able to
analyze the envisaged evolution of the technology.
Hydrogen compression and storage are parameters that are effectively taken into account
in this model. The CAPEX part of the electrolysis is given in $/kW and no information
is given on the electrolyser capacities for which such an assumption is relevant. Thus, the
phenomenon of economy of scale for very large electrolysis capacities is not clearly defined
whereas it seems to be able to have a non-negligible impact for a large scale hydrogen
production. Finally, the DECEX are not used in this 20 years project. The cost model is
therefore not usable over the entire life cycle of the production system but only until the
end of its use. The price of electricity for electrolysis is considered here to be fixed over
the entire life of the project based on the assumptions made.

Summary of Literature Review
Green hydrogen has a major place in the European and global race for decarbonisation
and thus the fight against climate change. Numerous targets at different scales confirm
the growing interest in cleaner production and consumption.
However, the field of large-scale green hydrogen generation is still very new and imma-
ture. The models outlined above are based on many assumptions that have not yet been
validated. This underlines the importance of ongoing and soon to be implemented pilot
Page 18 of 69
==PAGE:25==

projects that can help refine the models currently in use. A generic LCoH data is not
yet present to analyse the economic aspect and thus depends on the scope of the project,
e.g. whether the electricity comes from the wind farm or is bought from the grid, but also
whether the hydrogen production is analysed or the hydrogen ready to be transported.
There are already a few cost models available to estimate the LCOH however they are
not suitable for the goal of this study. CAPEX part of these models is either broken
down into subparts corresponding to each sub-system used, or expressed empirically as
cost per megawatt. To model the impact of the each part of the system on the CAPEX
figures, decomposition is necessary. The OPEX part is either calculated as a percentage of
CAPEX or is analysed as different components of OPEX and calculating them one after
another. However, none of these models simulate the components in their entire life cycle
with possible failures.
The model developed in this thesis will therefore include all major components and pro-
cesses as much as possible/available. For the OPEX part in particular, the model that will
be developed will model the failures of the various components and take into account their
impact on the financial component, but also on the availability of our various systems. In
addition, the production of hydrogen ready to be used for a fueling station in particular,
will be studied and calculated hour by hour, taking into account the different efficiencies
and the possible availability of our systems. The model developed will therefore be versa-
tile, it can be adapted for different wind farms, different capacities. It allows to calculate
different LCoH according to its use and to return the costs involved.

As presented in the literature review at the beginning of this report, it was decided to

the equipment and the necessary material to build the wind farm while the installation
costs concern the cost of constructing the wind farm.

Limitations of the model
The model used in this study has some limitations that need to be identified in order to
be improved in a possible future project. The first limitation is that all subsystems are
simulated in parallel. During the lifetime of the wind farm, two or more subsystems might
undergo a failure at the same time which is not taken into account. Thus, in this model,
one failure may mask another. Nevertheless, the cost of repair is taken into account.

may become more accurate in the future, and that are highly dependent on the geograph-

information is collected. Finally, the wake loss is approximated here by a coefficient but
its value may gain in accuracy by realistically modelling it.

production took place or not. When the repair takes place, the repair/replacement cost
and the logistic cost is calculated and added to the OPEX variable.

Sensitivity Analysis
In order to analyse the importance of several parameters used in the cost model and thus
to have leads for a potential optimisation and consequently produce green hydrogen at
the lowest possible cost, a sensitivity study was carried out.
It consists in modifying
some inputs of our model in order to raise the new LCoH obtained and to quantify the
importance of this parameter. For this study, the LCoH1 was used because it takes into
account the wind farm as well as the hydrogen production system while selling back the
part of the electricity not used during the unavailability of the hydrogen station. This
LCoH is the general idea of this study when the whole production is taken into account.
The parameters that were changed for this sensitivity analysis are:
• Discount rate: It allows to find the present value of future cash flows that are
changing due to the interest. It is used here to see the financial impact that its
modification may have.
• CAPEX Total: This parameter is expected to decrease in the future due to economies
of scale and technological advances. Modifying this parameter thus makes it possible
to begin to anticipate future production prices for green hydrogen.
• Stack Efficiency and Stack Degradation: Again, these parameters are likely to be
reduced in the future due to technological advances and the use of more resistant or
efficient materials.
• Stack Lifetime: This parameter is linked to the previous parameter and allows the
impact of a longer (or shorter) view of the stack to be quantified.
• Water Cost: Water is purchased from the Belgian network at a price for large cus-
tomers. Changing this parameter makes it possible to quantify its impact on the
final LCoH and thus to find an alternative if its impact is significant (e.g. sea water).
• Failure Rate: Many approximations had to be used for the failure rates of the
different subsystems for hydrogen production. Modifying this parameter allows to
know if these assumptions have a big impact on the final result or not.

It is therefore necessary to be particularly precise about the financial conditions of such a
project in order to have a relevant LCoH.
The total CAPEX also has a significant impact, although more limited than the discount
rate. Here, a range of plus or minus 10% of the original CAPEX translates to about 6%
change in LCoH. It can be seen that reducing the CAPEX clearly reduces the LCoH and
can therefore be a focus point to reduce the costs.
The efficiency of the stack has a direct impact on the LCoH. This is to be expected, as the
greater the efficiency of this element, the greater the output and thus the Revenue part of
the LCoH calculation will lower it. Furthermore, the fact that 16% decrease in efficiency
increases the LCoH 19% but 16% increase in efficiency decreases the LCoH about 14%
indicates that an optimum point for the stack efficiency can be found to have the biggest
LCoH gain.
This model also takes into account a degradation of stack efficiency over time, which
according to this analysis has a small impact on the LCoH value. Indeed, over a standard
viewing time of 85,000 hours, a degradation of 0.1%/1000h results in a final degradation
of approximately 8.5%. However, this final efficiency will only be reached gradually, as
for the other degradation values tested. Thus, the impact is lower than for a reduced or
increased efficiency at the start of production.
Finally on the stack parameters, its lifetime has also been modified to quantify its impor-
tance. Here, it can be seen that its impact is limited. On the other hand, the impact is not
linear which is shown in . This is mainly due to the fact that after a certain lifetime the
stack is replaced (additional costs so higher LCoH) and the new system will have higher
efficiency (lower LCoH). It is observed that a stack life of 70,000 hours for this degradation
results in the lowest LCoH. Although, the difference is small, such analysis can help to
target a lifetime for the stack that will work best for the benefit of LCoH and profitability.

The cost of purchasing the water used for electrolysis does not greatly impact LCoH

Finally, there is great uncertainty about the choice of failure rates for the hydrogen part

by the companies after the installation of their pilot projects and the analysis of their
results. However, by changing the failure rates, we observe that the LCoH change only
slightly. This means, the results are not very sensitive to these very uncertain values.
Nevertheless, when more accurate failure rate information is available, they need to be
updated in the model.
To conclude this section, improvement in all these parameters will have a positive impact
on the LCoH. But the largest impact comes from the stack efficiency and the discount
rate. It can be seen that the stack is the main axis of technological improvement of the
hydrogen production system to be competitive on the market.
7.8
Limitations of the model
This model was created by the author with the aim of being as faithful to reality as
possible. However, it does have some limitations, most of which can be quickly corrected
thanks to the versatility of the program. The limitations identified are as follows:
• Values used for the failure rates, failure costs and failure classification need to be
more precise
• Preventive maintenance can anticipate failures and avoid downtime but is not taken
into account here
• Behaviour of the electrolyser for large-scale production which is still not well known
Page 62 of 69
==PAGE:69==
Master Thesis
• CAPEX part of the wind farm is imprecise, especially the cost of transport and
logistics of components from the factory to the site
• Storage is not taken into account. It may lead to high costs
• The wake loss phenomenon can be more accurately modelled
• The transfer time of maintenance vessel could be more accurate with precise mete-
orological information
The approximation of some of the values used may slightly alter the values obtained.
Indeed, as presented before, the production of green hydrogen on a large scale is a very
recent subject of study, still largely theoretical. The necessary hindsight and the behaviour
of the different elements for such a production is not known and many assumptions had
to be made for the purpose of this study. The advantage of this model is that it can
be modified and the results of future pilot projects in Europe and worldwide can be
implemented to obtain even more reliable results.

Conclusion
8.1
Conclusion
In this master thesis, a versatile cost model has been developed, taking into account
the operation of the wind farm and the hydrogen production, particularly based on the
availability of the systems, the different losses and the weather conditions.
The main part of the cost model, concerning the calculation of OPEX and production,
simulates hour by hour the operation of the wind farm and then the hydrogen production
system, taking into account the failures of both systems according to different severity
categories. The notion of set point is defined, allowing the production of electricity to be
directed towards hydrogen or towards the grid, depending on the production requirements.
Firstly, for the case study carried out of the Mermaid wind farm in Belgium, an LCoE
of 101.3 e/MWh was obtained, which is about 28% higher than the actual value, mainly
due to inaccurate weather conditions and possible lower failure rates in the actual wind
farm. For the hydrogen part, several LCoHs have been calculated, including the value of
the optimised LCoH of the HRES with the surplus part of electricity sold, which has a
value of 6.76 e/kg. These values evolve notably by changing the set point or the study
scope. The influence of the set point leads to a significant result: LCoH is lower when
the grid electricity is used to create hydrogen for the set points less than 70%.
When the set point is more than 70% LCoH is further reduced by using the
electricity from the wind farm for this study case. Of course, this percentage can
change depending on the different parameters taken into account (farm location, farm and
hydrogen production capacity...). However, it should be noted that, even if these values
are changed, there is a characteristic point at which it becomes more interesting to use
grid electricity if the purpose is solely to reduce the LCoH.
Net Present Value (NPV) of the different systems also showed that currently hydrogen pro-
duction is not profitable for the scenario selected in this research. For the Mermaid wind
farm, an NPV of 1,487,422,697 euros was determined, whereas this NPV is 565,299,660
euros for a hybrid production of electricity and hydrogen. Nevertheless, the most prof-
itable option of operating hydrogen production facility is using it together with a wind
farm as a hybrid system in this case. These values are likely to change, particularly for
hybrid production, which will become more profitable as the technology used matures.
In addition, the model was used to describe the impact of stack life on the cost of hy-
drogen. Counter-intuitively, a longer lifetime did not lead to a lower hydrogen cost when
degradation is taken into account. For the case study chosen, it was found that a lifetime
of 70,000h allows good efficiency to be maintained while keeping the stack count low.
Thus, the LCoH is the lowest for this stack lifetime and increases even if the lifetime is
extended unless the stack degradation speed is reduced and/or the stack price is reduced
proportionally. This study showed that there is a need to optimize the stack parameters
to produce hydrogen at the lowest cost.
As a reminder, the aim of this report was to analyse the competitiveness of such hydrogen
production on the market. As discussed above, the value of LCoH is not currently com-
petitive with the production of blue hydrogen and grey hydrogen. Nevertheless, this case
Page 64 of 69
==PAGE:71==

study shows that the NECP’s goal of producing green hydrogen between 6.5EUR/kgH2
and 8.3EUR/kgH2 in Belgium [8] is reachable. These goals can be achieved by improving
the technologies used, including electrolysers, which have a significant impact on LCoH
when their efficiency, price and lifetime changes.
This work has provided a rigorous approach to the costs involved in producing green hy-
drogen from offshore wind, notably by modelling different scopes, and its versatility allows
it to be easily adapted in the future with new values to further analyse the competitiveness
of such hydrogen generation systems.
8.2
Future Works
For a potential reader who wants to work on this theme in a study, there are many ways
to advance this field. Firstly, this model focuses on the production of green hydrogen
from offshore wind. Other energy generation schemes can be modelled using the same
principles used in this model and approach. Indeed, hydrogen is considered green when
the source of electricity production is said to be renewable. Thus, a similar model based on
photovoltaic, onshore wind or hydro-electricity is also possible to be developed. Moreover,
the cost model can also be expanded to include the floating wind farms, as this type of
foundation will become more common in the future.
This research focuses on onshore hydrogen production, however there are other options in
terms of location for a potential future project. A few studies are available in the literature
dealing with the onshore, offshore or in-turbine (or on-floater) positioning of the hydrogen
production system but none of them use a detailed process-based approach as it is used in
this model. It therefore seems appropriate to expand the cost model to cover the location
of the electrolyser as a parameter to evaluate the impact of it to the LCoH.
Moreover, the notion of set point, which is rarely encountered in the reports studied in
the literature review, provides flexibility to optimize the production strategies. The use
of the set point can be further improved by converting it to a dynamic selection based
the desired outcome. In particular, dynamic set points that adapt to demand to produce
hydrogen at the most profitable time. As the demand for electricity is set to increase, it
is difficult to imagine having offshore wind farms entirely dedicated to the production of
hydrogen. Thus, the use of hybrid systems needs to be further explored to mature this
technology.
The author of this report can only encourage future studies in this area and will be
delighted to help research move forward on this exciting topic, which is sure to be a must
in the future.
