ABSTRACT 
Prolonged periods of overfishing have resulted in the depletion and collapse of many commercially 
and ecologically important fish populations worldwide, notably the once highly productive and 
viable stocks of the North Atlantic (NA) cod. Management actions to facilitate recovery of these 
stocks, primarily through gear restrictions and fishery closures have proven largely unsuccessful. 
Therefore, the key question is what factors causes this lack of recovery? The key mechanisms 
suggested to influence recovery are reproductive success, fishing and natural mortality, as well as 
growth. While the former factors have been given much attention, the role of growth and its effect 
on recovery in fish stocks is poorly understood. In this study, I use mixed modelling and a 
comprehensive collection of long-term stock assessment data on 14 NA cod stocks and the 
environmental conditions these are facing (i.e., in terms of temperature and its seasonality) to 
investigate the underlying drivers affecting cod growth. More specifically, I set out to answer the 
following questions: 1) What are the overall drivers of variation in growth (i.e. weight) for NA cod 
stocks? 2) Do the drivers or their relative importance differ between life history stages, represented 
by juveniles and adults? 3) Is there a regional effect causing changes in variation in growth across 
stocks? Based on the mixed models for juvenile and adult weights my results show that juvenile 
growth is primarily affected by external factors, including temperature and seasonality, while adult 
growth is regulated through internal, density-dependent processes, involving both cannibalism and 
intra-specific competition for food, occurring when juvenile and adult abundances are high, 
respectively. This demonstrates a shifting importance between external and internal regulation of 
cod growth throughout ontogeny. To that end, this study can add sufficient knowledge to future 
predictions of how growth of NA cod will change as a response to changes in its environment. This 
knowledge is important in developing future management strategies to sustainably use our marine 
living resources under imminent global change.  
 
Keywords: North Atlantic cod, growth, temperature, seasonality, density-dependence, metabolism, 
mixed intercept models.

1. Introduction 
Prolonged periods of overfishing has resulted in the depletion and collapse of many commercially 
and ecologically important fish populations worldwide, notably the once highly productive and 
viable stocks of the North Atlantic (NA) cod (Gadus morhua) (Myers et al. 1997; Hutchings 2000; 
Hutchings & Reynolds, 2004). Management actions  to facilitate recovery of these stocks, primarily 
through gear restrictions and fishery closures have proven largely unsuccessful (Hutchings & 
Reynolds 2004; Shelton et al. 2006;  Lilly et al. 2008). Consequently only four out of the 22 stocks 
(i.e., Iceland, North Sea, Kattegat and NE Arctic) are showing positive trends in biomass while the 
rest are still suffering from a lack of recovery (ICES 2016) and in the case of the western NA cod 
stocks it is even argued that these are “facing imminent extirpation or extinction” (COSEWIC 2010; 
COSEWIC 2016). Therefore, the key question is what factors causes this lack of recovery?  
Our limited knowledge on these issues has caused many concerns in the progress of developing 
sustainable management strategies for NA cod (Lindegren et al. 2009; Lindegren & Eero 2013). 
The key mechanisms suggested to influence recovery of fish stocks are reproduction (i.e. 
maturation), fishing and natural mortality, as well as growth (Hutchings & Reynolds 2004). While 
reproduction, fishing and natural mortality have been given much attention, the role of growth and 
its effect on recovery in fish stocks is poorly understood. Size and growth are two terminologies 
which are often confused with each other. In this thesis growth is defined as a process that at some 
point will lead to a given state (body size) depending on drivers affecting the growth process 
(Enberg et al. 2012). Growth influences many vital processes, such as reproduction (i.e. size affects 
maturity and fecundity) and survival (i.e. small fish are more vulnerable for predation than larger 
fish, while larger fish are more prone to being caught than smaller fish) (Köster et al. 2013; 
Lorenzen 2016). Pronounced differences in body size across cod stocks, as well as strong variation 
in body size over time is common for many NA cod stocks (App.3: fig. 3.1 and 3.2) but the 
underlying drivers causing this are not completely understood.

1.1 Definition of growth 
Growth is a continuous process that requires energy (Boeuf & Payan 2001). Metabolism refers to 
the processes involved in energy production which can either occur aerobically (with oxygen) or 
anaerobically (without oxygen) with aerobic metabolism being the most energy efficient (Cho & 
Kim 2011). The energy flow and thereby the potential surplus energy available for growth can be 
viewed by two processes. Firstly, acquisition of energy (e.g. ingestion and digestion of prey and 
energy production) and secondly, allocation of the energy output from the acquisition process (fig. 
1). It is important to point out that these two processes cannot be completely distinguished from 
each other as allocation of produced energy to acquisition of energy must take place due to the 
processes involved in ingestion and digestion. Acquisition of energy relies on prey availability, 
feeding and metabolism while allocation of energy depends on the state of the fish. Which of the 
processes (e.g. maturation, changes in morphology and storage) energy is allocated to is expected to 
take place in order to maximize fitness (competing processes listed; fig. 1) (Sheridan 2011; Enberg 
et al. 2012). The energy available for things such as movement, growth, and reproduction is 
reflected by the aerobic metabolic scope (AMS) which is defined by the difference between the 
minimum and the maximum energy use of fish, termed respectively the standard metabolic rate 
(SMR, the energy needed to sustain basic functions in order to survive) and the maximal metabolic 
rate (MMR) (fig. 2). Because temperature and dissolved oxygen affects metabolism, the magnitude 
of the AMS is affected by these factors (Claireaux et al. 2000; Pörtner et al. 2008, Tirsgaard et al 
2015).

In summary, variation in growth can be a result of changes in the following regulators: 1) 
mechanisms controlling the size of AMS, 2) acquisition of energy and 3) allocation of energy. Thus, 
changes within these three regulators can affect growth reflected by variation in the state of the fish 
(i.e. body size) (Wootton 2011). 
1.2 Drivers affecting growth 
Drivers acting on the three main regulators of growth described above can be defined as external 
(e.g. prey availability and climatic factors including temperature, seasonality and oxygen) or 
internal (e.g. density dependence involving both cannibalism and intra-specific competition for 
food). The most well studied factor affecting the metabolic rates (i.e. SMR and MMR) and thus 
controlling the size of AMS, is temperature. Fish are ectothermic (i.e. their body temperature 
mimics the ambient temperature) why their thermal environment have crucial effects on various life 
history parameters  (Brander 1995; Schmidt-Nielsen 1997; Pörtner & Farrell 2008). A species has a 
so-called ‘thermal window’ which defines the range of temperatures where it can produce energy 
(fig. 2).

Within the thermal window, the optimum temperature (Topt) is defined as the temperature where 
AMS is maximized (Pörtner 2002; Pörtner & Farrell 2008). Temperatures below or above Topt that 
cause a decline of AMS as a result of changes in SMR and MMR are defined as lower and upper 
pejus temperatures (pejus=getting worse)  (Pörtner et al. 2008; Tirsgaard et al. 2015). When 
temperature increases, so does both SMR and MMR. Notably though, MMR increases at a faster 
rate than SMR resulting in augmented AMS (MMR – SMR) when temperatures increase. However, 
MMR only increases until the upper thermal limit is reached where after it declines. Consequently, 
at this point AMS also starts to decrease and temperature now has a negative effect on the 
production and availability of energy (Claireaux et al. 2000; Rogers et al. 2011). Two critical 
temperature limits (Tcrit) (lower and upper) define at what point AMS is being zero and survival is 
time limited and relying on anaerobic energy production (fig. 2) (Pörtner & Knust 2007). At least

for some species (e.g. cod) it appears that the width of the thermal window may differ throughout 
ontogeny, suggestively narrowing with increasing body size, due to a lowering of the optimal 
temperature and the upper critical temperature (Björnsson et al. 2007; Pörtner et al. 2008; Tirsgaard 
et al. 2015). If this is the case, larger individuals may be more prone to changing their distribution 
patterns as compared to juveniles under a climate change scenario with increasing temperature 
(Bjørnsson et al. 2001; Anderson et al. 2002).  
The second regulator, acquisition of energy, is depending on food availability. The amount of 
energy ingested is determined by prey type, prey availability and intra- and inter-specific 
competition for food, where temperature can affect prey availability by changing prey distributions 
and length of the feeding season (Swain et al. 2003). In addition, density-dependent processes can 
cause increased competition for food, predation or space (Ohlberger et al. 2014). One type of 
density-dependence is cannibalism that can act as a self-regulatory mechanism in a stock. This is a 
well-known phenomenon in NA cod stocks where increases in length of adult cod and population 
density increase the magnitude of cannibalism, with the major effects on smaller size cod in age 
groups 0-3 (Hecht & Appelbaum 1988; Bogstad et al. 1994; Neuenfeldt & Koster 2000). 
The third regulator, allocation of energy, is more difficult to examine as it is very difficult to point 
out exactly what processes energy is favored towards. The best example in this context is 
maturation versus growth. Both processes are highly energy consuming and information about them 
can be obtained by maturity and weight at age data. A trend towards earlier age and smaller size at 
maturity has been found for many NA cod stocks in the last decade (Hutchings 2005; Ottersen et al. 
2006; Audzijonyte et al. 2013; Hunter et al. 2015). The strength of this trend seems to be depending 
on stock- or area specific characteristics such as temperature and fishing mortality (Drinkwater 
2005; Lilly et al. 2008; Nash et al. 2010). Earlier age and small size at maturity can have negative 
consequences for population growth as energy is allocated to maturation instead of growth 
(Hutchings 2005; Hunter et al. 2015). High fishing pressure is causing high mortality and 
individuals maturing earlier and at smaller size, potentially increase their fitness compared to larger 
individuals with higher probability of getting caught (Swain et al. 2015).  
In summary, all three regulators can affect growth, most likely not alone, but through complex 
interactions which can be difficult to disentangle. An intensive number of studies have examined 
variation in growth in individual NA cod stocks (Norwegian Coast: Berg & Albert 2003; Rogers et

al. 2011, NE Arctic: Mehl & Sunnanå 1991; Michalsen et al. 1998, Northern Gulf of St. Lawrence: 
Dutil et al. 1999, Georges Bank: Campana 1996, Northern: Krohn et al. 1997 and references within:  
ICES, 2005c) where different factors (temperature, density-dependence, year-class strength and 
prey-availability) were examined. These studies have provided important knowledge for each 
individual stock, but do not provide an overall picture of drivers affecting growth across NA cod 
stocks, in particular the marked differences in mean weight between stocks. In this context a 
comparative analysis across NA cod stocks is needed.  
Comparative studies on drivers affecting other important life history processes than growth have 
been very effective in generating knowledge about NA cod stock dynamics such as e.g. recruitment 
(Stige et al. 2006; Lilly et al. 2008; Mantzouni et al. 2010; Wang et al. 2014). Brander (1995) 
provided the first comparative study of temperature effects on growth. In this study a simple, but 
very descriptive (         exponential model describing the mean catch weight at age (2-4 year 
old) across 17 stocks as a function of the product of age and temperature was generated. Though, 
Brander (1995) pointed out that other drivers than temperature must be investigated to understand 
the complexity of drivers behind variation in growth. A very useful tool to carry out a comparative 
analysis is a mixed modeling approach which can account for variations in size and growth across 
stocks distributed over a large geographic area (Weisberg et al. 2010; Rogers et al. 2011).  
In this study, I use a comprehensive collection of long-term stock assessment data to investigate the 
underlying drivers affecting growth, reflected by variation in weight, of NA cod stocks using a 
mixed modelling approach. More specifically, I aim to answer the following questions: 1) What are 
the overall drivers of variation in growth (i.e. weight) for NA cod stocks? 2) Do the drivers or their 
relative importance differ between life history stages, represented by juveniles and adults? 3) Is 
there a regional effect causing changes in variation in growth across stocks? To further exemplify 
the complexity of drivers potentially affecting growth in NA cod a short review on Eastern Baltic 
cod growth is given. This stock has been chosen as a case study because it has a remarkable history 
regarding numbers and condition of fish. After large decline in numbers this stock actually 
recovered in number but with individuals being poor in condition (Eero et al. 2015). Examination of 
drivers causing this trend can be used to illustrate how some stocks might deviate from the overall 
growth models and to highlight how stocks in general are highly affected by multiple factors.

2 Material and Methods 
2.1 Study area 
The Atlantic cod is found in shallow coastal areas and deep waters throughout the North Atlantic. It 
is divided into several stocks ranging from the east coast of Canada, Greenland and Iceland to the 
coasts of Europe from the Bay of Biscay to the Barents sea (Cohen et al. 1990; ICES 2005, fig. 3). 
Temperatures at equal latitudes can differ throughout the Atlantic ocean due to hydrographic 
features (Lilly et al. 2008). However, cod stocks in both the western and eastern Atlantic experience 
pronounced thermal gradient from south to north. Maximum growth rates are found between 8-
15°C, depending on body size, but cod are distributed in a wide range of temperatures spanning 
from -1 to 19°C (Jobling 1988; Drinkwater 2005; Righton et al. 2010). As a result of the different 
habitats, the stocks show marked differences in growth, as well as maturity, diet and other life-
history related parameters (Brander 1995; Bjørnsson et al. 2001; Lilly et al. 2008; H. Wang et al. 
2014).

Weight, in kg, was either given as weight in catch or weight in stock. Weight in stock was used in 
this study and was mostly derived from observations, however some assessments used weight-
length relationship to calculate mean weight at age or converted weight-at-age in catch to weight-at-
age in stock (App. 2, table 2.1 and 2.2). In order to account for potential differences in the factors 
affecting growth throughout ontogeny I defined two life history stages corresponding to juveniles 
and adults. The mean weight of juveniles and adults for each year was calculated by using weight 
data from the corresponding age groups of juveniles and adults. Age groups were defined by 
calculation of mean maturity of age over years where percentage mature individuals for juvenile 
and adults were defined to be <25% or >75%, respectively (fig. 4, App. 2, table 2.3 and 2.4). The 
percentages of maturity for each group were chosen to assure that the two groups reflected two very 
distinct life history stages and did not overlap with each other. Division of data into two life history 
stages resulted in two new factors; number of years being juvenile and start age of adult. These 
factors could, if significant, take into account that the life history stages differ in age classes 
between stocks.

In order to represent some of the external (climate related) factors acting on juvenile and adult 
growth I collected sea surface temperature (SST) data from the NOAA Extended Reconstructed Sea 
Surface Temperature dataset (ERSST, www.ncdc.noaa.gov). Both annual mean SST and the annual 
difference between maximum and minimum SST values (seasonality) (°C) were calculated for each 
stocks´ area. Furthermore, another external factor, a prey/predator ratio, was calculated as a proxy 
for food availability for each stock. Prey species were defined as the main preferred pelagic prey 
item for each stock, including Atlantic herring (Clupea herringus L.), Capelin (Mallutus villosus), 
Sprat (Sprat sprattus) and Beaked Redfish (S. mentella and S. Fasciatus) (App. 2: table 2.5). The 
prey/predator ratio was calculated as the biomass ratio (using SSB) between the main prey species 
and each respective cod stock. Arguably, this provides a very rough estimate of food availability 
and does not take into account that the diet of cod is very diverse and differ depending on stock and 
size of the individual (Link et al. 2009; Pachur & Horbowy 2013). Numbers of juveniles and adults 
were used to account for potential density-dependent processes acting as internal drivers on growth 
of juveniles and adults.  
2.3 Statistical analysis 
Linear mixed intercept models were used to examine the effects of external and internal drivers on 
growth (i.e. weight of juvenile and adults). In this context the following drivers were examined: 
temperature, seasonality, prey availability (prey/predator ratio), density dependence (i.e. number of 
juveniles and adults), number of years being juvenile and start age of becoming adult. Stock ID was 
defined as a random factor. Logarithm of numbers of juveniles and adults and prey/predator ratio 
was done before modelling. Since weights represent values at the start of each year, the response 
variables were lagged by one year to accurately reflect the timing of the external and internal 
processes acting on growth during the preceding year. Mean weight was modelled for each group 
(i.e. juvenile and adult) in two separate models as follows:

The  LME function from the NLME package in R (Pinheiro et al. 2016; R Core Team 2016) was 
chosen for the modelling because the within-stock errors (residuals) could be allowed to vary 
between stocks by using varIdent variance structure. Before model fitting collinearity between 
predictors was assessed by using variance inflation factors (VIF) (Zuur et al. 2009). Then, model 
reduction regarding the fixed structure was performed using the maximum likelihood (ML) 
estimation procedure and a backward selection routine based on F tests in ANOVA. Log likelihood 
ratio test in ANOVA together with minimizing the Akaike´s Information Criterion (AIC; Burnham 
& Anderson 1998) were used in model comparison. Models with ΔAIC ranging between 0-2 were 
defined as being equivalent (van Denderen et al. 2014). To extract random effects from the models 
the best linear unbiased predictors (BLUP) were derived by using the ranef function (Pinheiro et al. 
2016). Model validation was done by checking up on the assumptions of normality and 
independence of residuals, as well as normality of the random effect. More detailed information 
about the theory behind the mixed modelling approach is given in appendix 1.

3 Results

explanatory variables (table 2). However, a test of collinearity, using variance inflation factors

5 Discussion 
Fish growth is affected by multiple internal (e.g. density-dependence) and external (e.g. temperature 
and seasonality) drivers (Brander 1995; Swain et al. 2003; Wang et al. 2014). Despite an extensive 
body of research, especially on NA cod growth (ICES 2005c), the effect of these drivers acting on 
growth across NA cod stocks, as well as the degree to which the relative importance of internal and 
external drivers change throughout ontogeny, is poorly known (P-Gandaras & Zamarro 1990; 
Chouinard et al. 2003; Swain et al. 2003; Hjermann et al. 2004). In this study, I collected an 
extensive long-term data set of growth (in terms of weight) across  NA cod stocks and used mixed 
modelling to investigate the relative importance of internal and external drivers on growth of 
juveniles and adults. The results show that juvenile growth is primarily affected by external factors, 
including temperature and seasonality, while adult growth is mostly regulated through internal, 
density-dependent processes, involving both cannibalism and intra-specific competition, but also 
the external driver seasonality. This demonstrated a shifting importance between external and 
internal regulation of cod growth throughout ontogeny.  
5.1 Temperature and Seasonality 
Positive effects of increase in temperature on growth is a common finding in most studies for NA 
cod (Brander 1995; references in: ICES 2005c; Wang et al. 2014). This may not be a surprise 
because temperature is known to be one of the most important drivers affecting growth as it directly 
and indirectly regulate important processes related to the acquisition and allocation of energy 
(Brander 1994; Brander 1995; Enberg et al. 2012). Acquisition of energy involves both 
physiological processes (e.g. metabolism) and ecological processes (e.g. density dependence) while 
allocation of energy rely on the output of acquisition of energy and the physiological state of the 
fish (fig. 9) (Enberg et al. 2012). 
Temperature is known to positively affect important physiological processes affecting acquisition of 
energy and thereby energy available for growth. Though, this only applies until the species upper or 
lower thermal limits are reached, at which points temperature tends to have a negative effect on 
energy production. NA cod are known to cope with a broad range of temperatures (-1 to 19 Celsius 
degree) and are exposed to large fluctuations in daily temperatures (Righton et al. 2010; Köster et 
al. 2013). Optimum temperatures regarding optimum growth rates have been found to range 
between 8-15°C (Jobling 1988; Righton et al. 2010). In this study, I found a significant positive 
==PAGE:26==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
28 
 
 
effect of temperature only on weight of juveniles and not adults, indicating that juvenile growth is 
more vulnerable to changes in temperature compared to adults. This is in agreement with individual 
growth studies on the NE Arctic and Icelandic cod where stronger positive temperature effects were 
found on smaller compared to larger fish (Björnsson & Steinarsson 2002; Hjermann et al. 2004). 
 
Figure 9 Schematic illustration of drivers affecting growth of juvenile and adult based on the two life history models 
from this study. Processes affecting the acquisition of energy differ between juvenile and adults regarding growth. The 
only common driver between juveniles and adults was seasonality. Weight of juveniles tend to only depend on external 
drivers (i.e. seasonality, temperature) while weight of adults depends mostly on internal drivers (i.e. density dependence 
like numbers of adults and juveniles) but also on the external  driver seasonality. Temperature affects SMR and MMR, 
setting the limits of AMS. The size of AMS together with feeding related processes (SDA) together affect the acquisition 
of energy and hereby the amount of energy left for growth that will depend on the state of the fish.  Density dependence 
will affect food availability and is therefore indirectly affecting SDA and the energy production. * indicates the drivers 
found to be significant for weight of juveniles and adults in this study. 
 
From a physiological perspective temperature is known to affect processes such as standard 
dynamic action (SDA), SMR and MMR, with the two latter setting the size of the aerobic metabolic 
scope (AMS=MMR-SMR), within which aerobic energy requiring processes (e.g. growth) must be 
accommodated. SDA is a metabolic response that refers to the energetic cost related to processes 
involved in feeding such as: ingestion, digestion, biosynthesis and assimilation. Meal size, 
temperature and ambient oxygen are the main drivers on the magnitude and duration of SDA  
(Secor 2009). Decline in oxygen availability prolong the duration of SDA (size of fish: 126.2-155.5 
g) (Jordan & Steffensen 2007) and the optimum temperature reflecting the shortest duration of SDA 
is found to be 10 °C, thus defined as the most "cost effective" temperature for SDA (size of fish: 
==PAGE:27==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
29 
 
 
162-196 g) (Tirsgaard et al. 2015). Like SDA also the gastric evacuation rate, reflecting the time it 
takes to empty the stomach after a meal, is affected by temperature. Gastric evacuation rate 
increases exponentially with increasing temperature until reaching an optimum temperature 
(~15.5°C) where after it declines (Andersen 2012). The positive effect of temperature on SMR and 
MMR (until an upper thermal limit is reached) theoretically increases the size of AMS and thereby 
the amount of available energy for growth depending on how energy is allocated. However, at the 
same time the increase in SMR as a result of increase in temperature call for a higher ingestion of 
food, to support this higher maintenance costs.  
In a recent laboratory study it has been suggested that the optimal temperature (Topt) found to 
maximize AMS decreases with size for NA cod. Tirsgaard et al. (2015) found that a 50, 200 and 
450 gram cod differed in Topt with 14.5, 11.8 and 10.9°C, respectively. In this study, the mean 
weight of juveniles ranged from 0.05-1.5 kg (App. 3, fig. 3.3) suggesting that Topt for the largest 
juveniles would be below or around 10°C (Tirsgaard et al. 2015). Hence, individual stocks 
experiencing temperatures below Topt (App. 3, fig. 3.3) would potentially benefit from increases in 
temperature as they still have the ability to increase AMS (Speers-Roesch & Norin 2016). The 
underlying reason for the present positive effect of temperature on weight of juvenile may therefore 
be due to increase in AMS because SMR and MMR respond positively to increase in temperature 
and/or a shorter duration or lower cost of SDA resulting in a higher energy output from the 
processes related to acquisition of energy.  
The other external driver examined in this study, seasonality, is reflecting the difference between 
minimum and maximum temperature throughout a year and can therefore indirectly also affect the 
physiological processes (SDA, SMR, MMR and AMS) described above. However, the primary 
effect of seasonality is thought to be primarily related to food availability as changes in seasonality 
is known to affect migration patterns, length of feeding periods and prey availability of NA cod 
(ICES 2005c). High seasonality might reflect long and cold winters or very long and hot summers 
or a combination of both. If high seasonality relates to long winters, the negative effect of 
seasonality on weight of adults could potentially be explained by a shortening of the feeding period 
as cod may leave their feeding grounds and migrate towards wintering grounds where feeding 
activity is reduced, to stay in areas with preferred temperatures (Castonguay et al. 1999; Anderson 
et al. 2002; ICES 2005c). In support of this, the Northern Gulf of St Lawrence stock is known to 
have different growth rates throughout the year with negative growth in the late winter and positive 
==PAGE:28==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
30 
 
 
growth in the late summer (ICES 2005c). Prey availability for Northern cod stock is known to be 
affected by temperature and it has been suggested that low seasonality (i.e. more constant 
temperature throughout year) provides access to food all year around while high seasonality causes 
low food availability during winter (Lilly 1982; ICES 2005c). On contrary to the adults seasonality 
had a positive effect on juveniles. This can potentially be a physiological respond (i.e. increase in 
magnitude of AMS) to increase in temperature, though only if food availability is high at the same 
time. However, it is most likely that seasonality is a proxy for prey availability as was the case for 
the adults. The fact that seasonality in the present study affected juveniles and adults in different 
ways might be caused by the different prey items that juveniles and adults mainly feed on, benthos 
and pelagic fish, respectively (Floeter & Temming 2003). Although purely speculative, one may 
suggest that seasonality has a positive effect on abundance of benthos and a negative effect on 
abundance of pelagic fish, i.e. a differential affect depending on the prey type in question.  
5.2 Density Dependence and food availability 
The experimentally well-established effects of temperature on cod physiology and growth are not 
necessarily the major driver behind the observed variation in growth in the present study. This is 
because food is often a limiting factor in the wild and NA cod stocks presumably do not feed close 
to their maximum consumption rates (Anderson et al. 2002; Björnsson & Steinarsson 2002). 
Instead, it may be argued that for the most part the effect of temperature on growth is more likely 
indirect, arising from its impact on prey availability on growth instead of physiological effects (e.g. 
metabolism). While weight of juveniles in the present study was affected by external drivers (i.e., 
temperature and seasonality), the adults were primarily affected by internal drivers such as the 
numbers of adults and juveniles and weight of juvenile. The negative effect of number of adults 
suggests that density-dependence is causing a negative effect on weight of adults probably as a 
result of competition for food.  
The effect of density dependence on growth of NA cod has been examined for 10 stocks with all 
studies showing that variability in growth to some extent can be explained by this internal driver 
(ICES 2005c). Inducement of density dependence in a stock results in increase in competition for 
prey and space possibly resulting in lower acquisition of energy. Cannibalism is a common feature 
for cod that is found to increase with body size  (Bogstad et al. 1994). The positive effect of number 
of juveniles found in the adult weight model therefore indicates that juvenile cod act as prey for 
==PAGE:29==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
31 
 
 
adult cod hereby potentially increasing the level of acquisition of energy. Along the same lines, 
studies on individual NA cod stocks have found positive relationships between prey biomass and 
growth. For instance, growth of the Icelandic, NE Arctic and Northern cod stocks have been found 
to be related to biomass of capelin (a major prey item of cod in these waters) with low biomass of 
capelin resulting in low size-at-age (Mehl & Sunnanå 1991; ICES 2015b; Rose & Rowe 2015). 
Condition of Eastern Baltic cod has also been related to sprat biomass, but only after the mid-1990s 
(Casini et al. 2016). While individual studies support the effect of food availability on growth, 
modelling prey availability as a prey/predator ratio across all cod stocks is very difficult as cod 
feeds on a broad range of prey items and exhibit shifts in diet throughout ontogeny (Link et al. 
2009). Small cod (<40 centimeter) feed on benthos (mainly crustaceans) where diversity of prey 
items has been found to be very high. Fish above approximately 40 centimeter start to feed on 
pelagic fish and the fraction of fish in the diet hereafter increases while the fraction of invertebrates 
decreases (Floeter & Temming 2003). Hence, the present overall prey/predator ratio included in the 
initial models should be considered as a first and very rough proxy of prey availability. Since this 
proxy turned out to have an ecologically unrealistic negative effect on weight I decided not to 
include it. However, providing a better and more robust estimate of prey availability, accounting for 
spatial (stock) differences in prey composition and preferences and the way these would change 
with size throughout ontogeny (e.g., Casini et al. 2016) would be recommended in future studies.  
5.3 Size-selection 
Fishing mortality (F) was not included in this study because the effect of F on weight can be very 
difficult to detect due to the delayed response to changes in F. Further, fishery is thought to cause 
selection of growth rates which cannot be reflected by examination of size-at-age but instead by 
examination of growth rates (Enberg et al. 2012). Fishery acts directly by removing fish from a 
population and this can exert a size-selective pressure on the population, which can result in a 
genetic change in response (Hanson & Chouinard 1992; Chouinard et al. 2003; Hutchings 2005; 
Swain et al. 2007). Selection might act on growth rates favoring slow-growers over fast growers or 
the other way around. The direction of selection on different growth rates caused by fishing 
mortality is complex and to date still debated. Slow growth rates can be beneficial as this will lead 
to lower probability of getting caught in fishery (i.e. due to inactivity or evasive behavior). This 
would trade-off against feeding and leads to slower growth.  On the contrary, fast growth rates 
might call for a faster life history (i.e. early maturation) to minimize the risk of getting caught, 
==PAGE:30==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
32 
 
 
before maturation takes place (Enberg et al. 2012). Being small when maturing may however imply 
decreased fecundity (i.e. lower number of eggs produced) as compared to cod maturing at a larger 
size (Ottersen et al. 2006). Shelton et al. (2006) examined 12 cod stocks and found that 10 out of 12 
stocks showed lower body growth rates resulting in lower spawning per recruit. This indirectly 
illustrates how selection of growth rates might negatively affect the recovery of NA cod stocks 
because recovery might be negatively affected by earlier and smaller size at maturity. The latter 
trend has been found for many NA cod stock (Hutchings 2005; Ottersen et al. 2006; Audzijonyte et 
al. 2013; Hunter et al. 2015). In this study it was clear from the different slopes of the factor: 
yearsjuv that early maturing stocks had lower mean weights compared to stocks that mature at older 
age. Number of years being juveniles seemed to decrease for stocks living in warmer areas than in 
colder areas (App. 3, fig. 3.4) indicating that these stocks are naturally maturing faster, though at a 
smaller size, than stocks living in colder waters. It is important to point out that this does not tell 
anything about whether growth rates are slower or faster between stocks. The factor yearsjuv only 
reveals that some stocks mature at a smaller size and that weight differs depending on the time 
being juvenile for each stock.   
5.4 Limitations of data 
Although assessment data is the best type of data existing for a comparative study of drivers on 
growth, the methods for data collection behind each assessment differ. This implies that some 
caution should be taken when analyzing this type of data. For instance, data on weight at age in 
stock has not been derived in the same way between stocks (App. 2, table 1), as data is based either 
on surveys, commercial fisheries, weight-length calculations or combinations of these. Further the 
methods behind maturity at age varied between stocks and in 3 stocks maturity at age has been kept 
constant throughout the assessment period resulting in no information about variability in maturity 
at age. Another feature which has been very difficult to take into account is potential differences or 
changes in mesh size and minimum landing size for each stock. However, mesh size will influence 
the size-range of fish caught in the fishery and assessment data is therefore not a 100 % 
representative of the real world (ICES 2015h). Regarding the environmental drivers, temperature 
(SST) is only an approximate of the surrounding temperature of fish as cod is considered mainly a 
demersal species. Further, some cod stocks might be exposed to large or smaller temperature 
gradients throughout the year, due to large seasonal migrations that are very difficult to account for 
(Michalsen et al. 1998; Anderson et al. 2002; Berg & Albert 2003).

6 Conclusion 
Our present lack of knowledge regarding the poor recovery of fish stocks is a serious concern, 
especially within fisheries management. This study focused on one of the least examined factors 
when it comes to the drivers behind the lack of recovery, namely growth. Growth was investigated 
by modelling variation in weight across NA cod stocks and over time. Not only did this study 
provide insight into the overall drivers of growth of NA cod, it also showed that drivers of growth 
differ depending on life history stages. The results showed that juvenile growth is primarily affected 
by external factors, including temperature and seasonality, while adult growth is regulated through 
internal, density-dependent processes, involving both cannibalism and intra-specific competition. 
This demonstrated a shifting importance between external and internal regulation of cod growth 
throughout ontogeny. To that end, this study can add sufficient knowledge to future predictions of 
how growth of NA cod will change as a response to changes in its environment. This knowledge 
can be important in future management perspectives as our oceans are facing new challenges as a 
result of climate change resulting in new environmental conditions for our fish stocks.

1.1 Statistical theory 
This section is not meant as an attempt to illustrate the mathematics behind the statistical models 
used in this study. It provides guidance in how two different "worlds" (i.e. biology and statistics) 
can communicate and work with each other in trying to optimize and extract as much information as 
possible from data that is already accessible for e.g. biologists to help increase knowledge about 
biological systems. This is done by providing more detailed statistical knowledge of the statistics 
used in this thesis (i.e. the mixed modelling approach) in relation to the biological aspect that the 
models in this study cover. In summary the overall aim in this study was to provide two overall 
models of drivers on weight of juvenile and adult NA cod.

model contains both fixed and random effects (explained in details later). A simple example of a

If the goal of this study had been to examine drivers on weight of juvenile and adult for each stock, 
then a linear fixed model could actually have been suitable to some extent. By including stock ID as 
a fixed factor and allow the slope to differ for each level of stock ID regarding the different 
explanatory variables (e.g. temperature, seasonality, numbers of juveniles and adults and 
prey/predator ratio) a linear model would have provided 14 different slopes one for each stock for 
each explanatory variable. Though, by doing this the number of regression parameters increases 
which in this study would mean that the total number of parameters would be 29 if only one

would only say something about these exact 14 stocks not a general trend of drivers on weight for 
juveniles and adults of NA cod. In this study it was therefore more comprehensive to use a mixed

1.3 The Mixed model 
A mixed model does not only include fixed effects but also random effects.  
                                                                                                                                
The term     reflects the random structure of the mixed model (model 1.2). From a biological 
perspective; what is a random effect? In this study stock ID was used as a random effect or more 
precisely defined as a random intercept in the two models to represent all NA cod stocks.  Each 
level of stock ID was therefore defined to have individual intercept values reflecting mean weight 
variation of stocks. By allowing a random intercept for each stock ID the residuals (ε) of the model 
was given more structure as some of the variance was captured by the random effect of stock ID. 
The variance of stock ID (random factor) in the final models could then provide information about 
the variability between each stock. Further, the model could also provide Best Linear Unbiased 
Predictors (BLUP) (also called the conditional mode) which biologically means; predictions by the 
models of which stocks are in general weighing less or more than the overall mean weight of all 
stocks in the juvenile and adult weight models (Bates et al. 2015). In summary the mixed intercept 
models in this study not only provided two overall models for drivers on weight of juvenile and 
adult NA cod, but also predictions of each individual stock levels regarding mean weight. The latter 
use full for the analysis of whether there exists a spatial effect of how the weight differs between 
either western and eastern or northern and southern stocks.

1.4 Model selection 
In the process of deriving the best models for predictions of drivers on weight of juveniles and 
adults NA cod certain steps were done before conclusions on the final models were taken:  
1. The full mixed model 
2. Reduction of the mixed model 
3. Model validation 
4. Use of different variance structures 
5. Final model 
Writing up the full model with all possible fixed effects was the first step. With the full fixed effect 
structure defined focus could be turned to the random structure. In this study the random structure 
was very simple, as no random slope was added and the models only contained one random factor; 
stock ID. However, in other cases where the random structure can be more complicated, reduction 
of the random structure must be done before reduction of the fixed structure.   
Step 2; reduction of the mixed model therefore includes two steps. Further it is important to point 
out that reduction of random and fixed structures should be done by using two different estimation 
procedures based on the likelihood methods. The goal of using likelihood tests is to find the 
parameters that make the observations most likely. The random structure should be reduced by 
restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation while the fixed structure should be reduced by 
using maximum likelihood (ML) estimation because REML requires the fixed structure to be 
exactly the same which is not the case in model reduction of the fixed effects. Though, when 
estimates of the final model are found this should be done by model estimation with REML because 
this type of estimation provides better estimation of the random variance than ML (Zuur et al. 
2009).  
In this study the Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was used as the statistical tool in the model 
reduction. ANOVA is an expansion of a t-test that only tests the difference between two means, as 
ANOVA can test the differences between two or more means. Test of means is done by analyzing 
variance, hereof the name of ANOVA (Gotelli & Ellison 2004). F statistics within ANOVA has 
been used to provide information about significance level of each explanatory variable included in 
the full models. Variables with low F values and high p values were excluded from the models. The 
stepwise model reduction was then done by a log likelihood ratio test an ANOVA and the Akaike

Information Criteria (AIC) was used to define the best model. AIC measures the goodness of fit 
based on the likelihood and the number of parameters within the model (Burnham & Anderson 
1998).  
When the final models were chosen for juvenile and adults regarding the fixed and random 
structures model validations were needed (step 3). This was done by examination of the 
corresponding residuals for each model. Normality and independent distribution of residuals and 
normality of the random effects should be accepted for both models. If these assumptions were not 
met the models either called for transformation of the response variable or use of different variance 
structure if the residuals showed violation of independent distribution. As the latter was the case for 
both the juvenile and adult models further examination of the variance of residuals for each stock 
was done. Plotting residuals for each stock showed that residuals differed between stocks indicating 
that the models should be allowed to have different variances between stocks (fig. 1).

1.5 Baltic Sea 
Cod  in the Baltic Sea are from a management perspective divided into a western and an eastern 
stock (Eero et al. 2014). Difficulty of age reading together with unaccounted natural mortality, 
vague measurements of discards and catchability in surveys have all together caused the assessment 
to fail and hence the status of the Eastern Baltic cod (EB cod) is to this date unknown (Eero et al. 
2015; Horbowy 2016; Hüssy et al. 2016).  Special attention has been paid to EB cod in recent years 
due to drastic changes in this stock. Since the mid-2000s weight at age has declined drastically 
resulting in poor condition of individuals while in the meantime number of fish has increased (Eero 
et al. 2015; ICES 2015j). Drivers behind this trend are highly debated and many hypothesizes have 
been put forward during the last ten years. EB cod inhabit a very unique ecosystem regarding 
hydrographic conditions causing a  very stratified water column that result in gradients in oxygen, 
temperature and salinity, abiotic factors all known to affect metabolism of fish (Drinkwater 2005; 
Hinrichsen et al. 2011; Lehmann et al. 2014). Unfortunately data from EB cod was not included in 
this study due to the uncertainty in age reading. However, it is interesting to review the potential 
drivers on growth for EB cod, especially given the unique hydrographic conditions this stock is 
experiencing.  
Two types of clupeids, herring (Clupea harengus) and sprat (Sprattus sprattus L.) are the preferred 
pelagic prey items for EB cod (ICES 2005b). Abundance of these species has decreased in the latest 
years in the area where EB cod are most presented (Eero et al. 2012). This lack in fish prey can 
cause changes in type of prey for EB cod resulting in changes in nutritional values. EB cod is also 
known to forage on benthic prey items (Neuenfeldt & Beyer 2003). Foraging for benthic prey  
involve excursions into severely hypoxic (low levels of dissolved oxygen) bottom waters prevailing 
in the area  (Neuenfeldt et al. 2009). This may result in an energetic cost related to changes in 
metabolism, as the fish has to rely on anaerobic metabolism resulting in an oxygen debt that has to 
be "paid" back after these excursions, hypothetically this leaves less energy available for growth 
(Plambech et al. 2013).  
Due to the stratification, renewal of oxygen in the bottom water depends on inflow of oxygenated 
water from Kattegat. Within the last decades a reduction in the rate of inflows has resulted in 
deteriorating bottom oxygen conditions  areas within Baltic Sea (Kabel et al. 2012; Lehmann et al. 
2014). This is thought to negatively affect the amount of benthic animals thereby reducing the 
==PAGE:47==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
49 
 
 
amount of benthic prey items available for EB cod. Density dependence might also be influenced by 
increase in hypoxic areas as this deprives the size of tolerable habitat for EB cod (Eero et al. 2015). 
This hypothesis has recently been tested  by Casini et al. (2016) providing a statistical analysis on 
the condition of EB cod ranging from 40-49 cm. In this study they found a strong negative 
correlation between increase in hypoxic areas and the condition of cod together with a positive 
correlation in biomass of sprat and the condition of cod after the mid-1990s. Before the mid-1990s 
the condition of cod was negatively correlated with cod abundance. As the number (or density) of 
small individuals has increased (presumably due to the habitat compression resulting of increased 
areas with hypoxia) this might induce density dependence leading to higher competition for energy 
(i.e. prey availability) and less energy available for growth.  
Density dependence can also be induced by fishing selectivity (Svedäng & Hornborg 2014). In 
fishery the large and fast growing fish are removed first resulting in higher numbers of smaller 
individuals left in the ecosystem. If these small individuals are not caught and reproduction is 
constant within the stock, fishery acts as an inducer of density-dependence (Svedäng & Hornborg 
2015). Thus, suggestively, intra-specific competition of food and space as a result of density 
dependence can maintain the stock in poor condition if gear selection is kept constant throughout 
time. 
Lastly, another, but still speculative, driver regarding growth is the significant increase in infections 
of EB cod with anisakid larvae. This driver has been hypothesized to correlate with the increase in 
numbers of grey seal (Halichoerus grypus) in the Baltic Sea where seals act as the final host for 
several parasites that use cod as intermediate host (Haarder et al. 2014; Mehrdana et al. 2014; 
Nadolna & Podolska 2014). Prevalence and intensity of the infection of anisakid larvae in EB cod is 
known to increase with body size (up to 70-80 cm) revealing that the infection possibly is a result of 
eating infected prey items, being another intermediate host of the parasites (Horbowy et al. 2016; 
Zuo et al. 2016). Infections of anisakid larvae result in tissue damage and penetration through vital 
organs or musculature (Noguera et al. 2009; Zuo et al. 2016). The effect of infections has in recent 
studies based on observations of EB cod showed that body condition decreased with increase in 
infection load of the parasites Pseudoterranova decipiens (cod worm) and Contracaecum osculatum 
(liver worm) (Mehrdana et al. 2014; Horbowy et al. 2016). Casini et al. (2016) also tested the effect 
of grey seal abundance in the Baltic Sea as a proxy for infections of parasites in EB cod and found 
only a minor effect on the condition of EB cod. Although field data thus suggests that infection with 
==PAGE:48==
Plambech M. (2016), Comparative Analysis of Growth Changes in North Atlantic Cod 
50 
 
 
liver and cod worm correlates negatively with condition of the individual, it is difficult from field 
data to disentangle any cofounding effects of e.g. food availability and hypoxia on growth and 
condition. Controlled experiments investigating the potential effects of parasites on physiology on 
EB cod is lacking to provide further understanding of why and if infection with parasites might 
affect growth of EB cod. 
In summary, inflow of oxygenated water from Kattegat has been limited in the last decades causing 
increase in hypoxic areas in the Baltic Sea that potentially can affect both directly (altering 
metabolism) and indirectly (density dependence: i.e. prey availability, habitat size) growth of EB 
cod. Together with increase in hypoxic areas and decrease in pelagic prey and possibly also benthic 
prey EB cod is probably facing a hard time related to energy to be able to increase in condition. 
Furthermore EB cod is experiencing increase in intensity of parasitic infections causing a possible 
reduction in condition. Taken into account that the Baltic Sea at the same time is increasing in 
hypoxic areas and in grey seal populations potentially resulting in higher infection pressure of EB 
cod, the growth conditions are presently not suitable for reversing the trend of poor condition of EB 
cod. This case study not only revealed how some stocks will deviate from the two weight models 
provided in this study, but also highlighted the complexity behind drivers on weight as these might 
differ over time but further also differ between stocks.
