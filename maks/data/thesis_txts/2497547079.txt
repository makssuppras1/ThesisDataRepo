Abstract 
The aim of the master thesis is to investigate dough and bread where brewers spent yeast, as a by-
product, is substituting baker’s yeast.  
To investigate the quality of the bread, texture analyses were conducted on both dough and baked 
buns where 11 different parameters of quality were measured. The proofing performance of the 
spent yeast was evaluated by the volume increase of the dough and a color analysis was performed 
to evaluate the color change of the crust. All data were subjected to one-way ANOVA to evaluate 
the parameters statistically. A literature review was conducted to obtain an overview of the current 
articles, findings and gaps within the area which lead to the working hypothesis of the thesis.  
To obtain a uniform recipe, the dry matter and viability of spent yeast and baker’s yeast was 
determined. The proofing time for baker’s yeast and spent yeast dough was 1 and 24 hours 
respectively. Through the thesis it was discovered that the salt content was the most dominant 
factor for inhibiting the proofing of the dough containing spent yeast. The salt content was reduced 
by 50 % in the recipe to ensure the spent yeast was capable of proofing the dough. Analysis of the 
dough and bun data indicated that the spent yeast is able to proof the buns to an acceptable size 
and that the baked spent yeast buns are eatable, and textures are acceptable. However, the baker’s 
yeast and spent yeast buns did not have the same quality and the data was varying. To ensure the 
same quality when using spent yeast instead of baker’s yeast further investigations and 
optimizations should be applied for spent yeast as a proofing agent in baked goods.  
To recover spent yeast from beer production and utilize it as a proofing agent in buns requires big 
capital investment and high daily operation costs. A turnover of investments within one year is not 
possible. The literature suggests other ways to increase the value of spent yeast by looking at the 
nutrition content as a source in higher value feed, microbial nutrition or as food ingredients.  
 
Keywords: Brewer’s spent yeast, Bread, Texture analysis, By-products

Formålet med kandidatspecialet er at undersøge dej og brød, hvor ølgær bliver anvendt som 
biprodukt efter ølproduktionen med det formål at erstatte bage gær. 
For at undersøge brødets kvalitet blev der udført teksturanalyser på både dej og bagte boller, hvor 
11 forskellige parametre for kvalitet er undersøgt. Rest ølgærens hæveevne blev evalueret ved at 
måle forøgelsen af dejens volumen under hævning, og der blev udført en farve analyse for at 
evaluere farveændringen på skorpen af bollen. Dataene blev bearbejdet med one-way ANOVA 
som statistisk analyse. Et litteraturstudie blev udarbejdet for at opbygge viden om aktuelle artikler, 
undersøgelser og mangler inden for emnet, hvorefter projektets arbejdshypoteser blev fastlagt. 
For at opnå en ensartet opskrift blev tørstofmængden og levedygtigheden af rest ølgær og bage 
gær bestemt. Hævetiden for dej med bagergær eller rest ølgær var henholdsvis 1 og 24 timer. Under 
projektet blev det konstateret, at saltindholdet var den mest dominerende faktor der hæmmede 
hæveevnen af dej som indeholdt rest ølgær. Saltindholdet blev reduceret med 50 % i opskriften for 
at sikre, at rest ølgæren var i stand til at hæve dejen. Analyserne af dej og bolle dataene indikerer, 
at rest ølgæren er i stand til at hæve bollerne til en acceptabel størrelse, og at de bagte boller er 
spiselige, og teksturen er ligeledes acceptabel. Bollerne med bage gær havde ikke den samme 
kvalitet som bollerne med rest ølgær, og dataene varierede meget. For at sikre den samme kvalitet, 
når rest ølgær er anvendes i stedet for bage gær, skal rest ølgær, som hævemiddel i bagværk, 
yderligere undersøges og optimeres.  
At genanvende ølgær fra ølproduktion og anvende det som hævemiddel i boller kræver store 
kapitalinvesteringer og høje daglige driftsomkostninger. Et investeringsafkast inden for et år er 
derfor ikke muligt. Litteraturen foreslår andre måder at øge værdien af rest ølgær, ved f.eks. at 
fokusere på næringsindholdet som værdiskabende grundlag i foder, mikrobiel ernæring eller som 
fødevareingredienser.

1. Introduction  
The earliest signs of both bread baking and beer making are dated back to the ancient Egyptians 
and it is expected that the knowledge about bread and beer travelled from Egypt to Europe through 
time. Baker’s and housewives were using spent yeast in baking from both breweries and distillers 
all over Europa not knowing what substance gave the bread its qualities. In 1775 Krünitz described 
the first substance causing fermentation in his encyclopedia. He stated that by mixing spent yeast, 
wheat flour and sugar together and stored it in a warm place, had an impact on fermentation where 
he concluded the importance of cultivating the yeast (Frey, 1930). In the 18th century yeast played 
a major role in the bread making industry and the production of baker’s yeast became through time 
more industrialized. The production of baker’s yeast is today monitored and controlled to ensure 
a high-quality product. Not only within yeast but in the whole food sector this has been the trend 
through time. As the production, industrialization and welfare increased so did the production 
waste. Throughout the 18th and 19th century the production waste was not recognized as a problem, 
but today the world population is projected to grow to 9.7 billion in 2050 (United Nations, 2019). 
This increase will demand more resources than the earth can supply. July 2019 the EU passed the 
overshoot day, which indicates that the European living standard is using the world’s resources 
faster than it can renew them (Vandermaesen et al., 2019). These facts push the industry to utilize 
resources in a more sustainable way. With an already increasing demand on resources and high 
climate impact further population growth is an issue that needs to be incorporated within decision-
making in countries and corporations all over the globe. Within industries it is important to 
evaluate, investigate and understand how a production can contribute to the global increasing 
demand of resources. Often this procedure is carried out for the final product, but it is equally 
important to investigate the by-products and how they can add value to the industry and support 
united nations 17 sustainable development goals. The global goals are established to secure a better 
future for everyone. The 12th goal relies on responsible consumption and production. This goal 
encourage industries to take actions to utilize resources in a more sustainable way (Global Goals, 
2015). By investigating productions and pushing the linear economy towards circular, there are 
both economic and environmental gains. A key aspect to circular economy, relies on recycling 
secondary raw materials to be reused for prolonging the lifetime of a product (Brears, 2018). In 
Denmark a possible conversion towards a circular economy is analysed in a report from Ellen

2. Literature review 
In the beverage industry beer is one of the most dominant alcoholic drinks and is the fifth most 
consumed beverages in the world. (Barth, 2013). When beer is brewed there are produced two 
major by-products, spent grains from the mashing process and spent yeast generated during the 
fermentation of the beer. Both spent yeast and grains are mainly used as animal feed, a low value 
product that is used for larger farm animals as, pigs, cattle and sheep (Huige, 1995). To investigate 
how greater value can be added to spent yeast and to obtain an overview of the current situation of 
spent yeast utilization the literature has been examined. The following articles state different 
approaches to gain higher value for spent yeast as a by-product.  
An article by Ferreira from 2010 evaluates how spent yeast as an ingredient in fish feed can provide 
higher value. The article states that it is possible to replace 50 % of the fishmeal protein with spent 
yeast protein with no negative performance in the fish. It is also stated that the spent yeast is 
capable of enhancing the immune response in the fish (Ferreira et al., 2010). Another study indicate 
that the spent yeast can be used as a flavor enhancer when the yeast is treated with enzymes (Chae 
et al., 2001). Brewer’s spent yeast also contains a high amount of β-Glucans that can be used in 
foods for nutritional purpose or as thickening agent. Multiple studies indicate that β-Glucans 
extracted from spent yeast can be used as a thickening agent in foods e.g. mayonnaise 
(Thammakiti, 2004), (Worrasinchai, 2006). A study by Bryant & Cohen investigate the amount of 
α-acids that can be retrieved form spent yeast. Research has shown that hop acids have antioxidant, 
anti-inflammatory, antibacterial and anticancer activities. They suggest the α-acids to be used as a 
high-value functional food ingredient (Bryant and Cohen, 2015).  
However, the yeast also has potential in other higher value applications besides feed and food. 
Multiple sources indicate an increased interest in using the spent yeast in microbial media as 
nutrient source because of the high levels of vitamin B complex and minerals (Chandrasekaran, 
2012) & (Ferreira et al., 2010). A study showed that the bacteria L. acidophilus had the highest 
growth rate in presence of spent yeast compared to baker’s yeast and a solution where the two was 
mixed (Champagne et al., 2003). A study by Lamoolphak evaluated growth of Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae in two growth mediums, one containing commercial yeast extract and one containing 
spent yeast extract. The two mediums showed the same growth rate (Lamoolphak et al., 2006). 
Also in bioethanol and biogas production, spent yeast and other waste products from beer

2.1. Working hypothesis  
Due to the increasing interest in by-products and how value can be added to them, together with 
the lack of information in the literature about spent yeast application in bread, the following 
hypothesis has been stated for further investigation.  
1) Main Hypothesis: Brewer’s spent yeast can be used as proofing agent in buns 
2) Hypothesis: Brewer’s spent yeast application does not change the texture of dough 
3) Hypothesis: Brewer’s spent yeast application does not change the texture of the crust and 
crumb in buns 
4) Hypothesis: Brewer’s spent yeast application does not change the crust color of the buns  
5) Hypothesis: Brewer’s spent yeast application does not change the texture of the crust and 
crumb in buns during the shelf life period 
Viability, dry matter and plate count experiments are not mentioned in the hypotheses, but they 
are carried out to invastiagte uniformity of the yeast to ensure the hypotheses can be evaluated.

3. Background 
To investigate the above stated working hypothesis the background knowledge of both ingredients 
and processing of bread and beer is stated. Parameters and procedures behind the two products 
should be in place to ensure a complete understanding of the processes and to either include 
important effect or exclude non-important effects, when application of brewer’s spent yeast in 
buns is evaluated.  
3.1. Bread 
Bread is a well-known food product that is consumed in many variations around the world. In 2016 
COOP stated that three out of four people consume bread for lunch and 33 % consume bread or 
buns for breakfast in Denmark (Roland et al., 2016). Most bread consists of four basic items, flour, 
water, salt and yeast. The ingredients are added together where yeast is dissolved in the wet 
ingredients before the dry ingredients are added and the dough is formed during mixing. The bread 
is proofed, where yeast is producing gas to ensure bread quality before baking.  
3.1.1. Flour 
Flour is used in both bread, buns and pastries. Flour can be based on many different types of grains 
or cereals to fulfill the purpose that is needed for the baked goods. Many baked goods consist of 
wheat flour as main ingredient, because wheat flour contains gluten proteins. 80 % of the proteins 
in wheat are gluten proteins that have the ability to form a gluten complex when the flour is mixed 
with water. The gluten complex consists of two proteins, gliadin and glutenin where gliadin is very 
stretchable and glutenin is more elastic. Together with the lipids naturally occurring in flour they 
can form a rubbery gluten complex (Hansen, 2018). The gluten proteins are increasing the volume 
of the bread because the gluten complex can trap and hold gas produced during fermentation. 
Different kinds of flour have different kind of starch composition and gluten content. Wheat, rye 
and barley contain gluten but in different amounts and the gluten proteins are also different from 
each other. Wheat contains the highest amount of gluten, here after rye and barley. Barley is mostly 
used in beer production, while both rye and wheat are commonly used in bread industries (Sharma 
and Rallabhandi, 2015). Besides gluten, starch is also an important factor in flour. During baking 
starch is gelatinized which is very important for the bread structure. The structural qualities and 
the composition of the starch are also very important for baked goods that contain yeast. Wheat

starch consist of the two polysaccharides amylose (25%) and Amylopectin (75%) (Hansen, 2018),

Wheat flour also contains free sugars as glucose, sucrose and maltose. The total amount of mono- 
and disaccharides in wheat flour is 4 mg/g where sucrose represents 50 % of the total soluble 
sugars (Gabrielǎ and Daniela, 2010). The yeast metabolizes the free sugars into CO2 to gain the 
airy and light quality of bread. Because of the relative low amount of free sugars available in the 
flour, enzymatic degradation of damaged starch is very important for the yeast to metabolize free 
sugars. Damaged starch is achieved through the milling process of the wheat. A high amount of 
damaged starch in flour will increase the water absorption, therefore the amount of damaged starch 
in flour should max. be 7 – 8 % (Hansen, 2018). When water is added to the flour the enzymes 
will begin to degrade the starch. Two main enzymes α and β amylases are very important for varies 
food productions. The α-amylase catalyze the degradation of the α(1,4)-glycosidic linkage in both 
amylose and amylopectin while the β-amylase catalyze the hydrolysis of the α(1,4)-glycosidic 
linkage in the non-reducing ends of the amylopectin. To degrade the α(1,6)-glycosidic linkage, α-
1,6 glucosidase also known as the debranching enzyme, has to be active, see Figure 3.1. Different 
grains contain different enzymatic activity and both harvest and storage condition of grains and 
flour will affect the activity. In grains and flour the enzymatic activity is often characterized by the 
falling number (FN). Flour with high (FN) have a reduced capacity to form fermentable sugars, 
because of a lower enzymatic activity. A low (FN) results in dough that is difficult to handle and 
with a sticky crumb, because of a higher enzymatic activity (Struyf et al., 2016). The falling

number (FN) is given in seconds, because of the test method where a metal rod is dropped into a 
suspension of flour and water and the time it takes for it to reach the bottom gives the falling 
number. FN should be between 220-300 seconds, if FN > 300 seconds addition of enzymes is 
needed. If FN < 200 seconds the enzyme activity is too high and the flour is unfit for baking 
purposes (Hansen, 2018).   
3.1.2. Water 
In bread water is a parameter that is very important for the bread to assemble. During baking it 
plays a major role for the bread to gain its structure and airiness in the end. An increase in water 
content will affect the bread because excess of water will inhibit the gluten complex and the air 
pockets will be too fragile to contain enough CO2 to obtain the characteristic soft texture (Struyf 
et al., 2017). A decrease in water content will affect both the yeast and the flour. With a decrease 
in water the flour cannot make a gluten complex or gelatinize the starch, and the yeast will not 
have optimal growth. This will result in a dry and porous consistency.  
3.1.3. Salt 
Salt is an important part of bread both in relation to structure and flavor. Bread without salt would 
be considered flat in taste and it is therefore a consumer preference. Together with sugar, salt 
reduces the water activity of the dough which can lead to reduced baking performance. Addition 
of salt can cause a decrease in yeast cell viability because of saline stress by cation toxicity and 
osmotic stress. This will cause a reduction in gas production from the yeast cells. Even though salt 
has negative impact on yeast performance, it has a strengthening effect on the gluten complex, 
making the complex more stable during proofing (Struyf et al., 2017). There is a salt balance 
between gluten strengthening and yeast viability that must be taken into account. 1 to 2 % salt 
based on the amount of flour is usually added (Brinsden et al., 2013). 
3.1.4. Yeast 
Baker’s yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most common microbe to use during fermentation 
in the bread industry. The high content of starch and water in wheat dough makes a great 
environment for the yeast to replicate and thrive. While the yeast metabolizes the free sugars, 
glucose and fructose in wheat flour, maltose needs to be transported into the cell where maltase is 
degrading maltose to glucose. The yeast cells also secrete invertase, a sugar degrading enzyme,

which degrades longer chains like sucrose and fructan into glucose and fructose. When the sugars 
are metabolized the yeast produce CO2 and ethanol, that are responsible for the viscoelastic dough 
to trap air bubbles and expand during proofing (Struyf et al., 2017). The yeast also produces 
volatile compounds that contribute to the bread aroma. The most important compounds include 
alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, esters, ethers, acids, hydrocarbons and furans (Pico et al., 2016). 
Fresh baker’s yeast has a high viability to ensure the above stated parameters are in place. The 
viability was between 90 – 100 % in 4 different plate count trials and 100 % in methylene blue 
staining test (Simard and Bouksaim, 1998).  
3.1.5. Additives 
In bread production additives are very important for the structure and quality of bread. There are 
many additives used in bread production to ensure stabilization, volume increase and prolonging 
of shelf life. Ascorbic acid (E300) is a reduction agent, that performs oxidation reactions with 
oxygen and the naturally wheat enzyme; ascorbic acid-oxidase, to stabilize the gluten network to 
ensure a more uniform dough. It is commonly added in both bread and flour. In bread or bun 
products emulsifiers are also commonly used as anti-stalling agent. Anti-stalling agents is 
prolonging the quality of fresh baked bread, as softness and elasticity throughout the shelf life of 
the product. Distilled monoglycerides (E471) also called (DMG) are used in bread productions to 
ensure a uniform quality throughout the shelf life period. The monoglycerides slow down 
retrogradation where crystalline amylose precipitates, by binding to the amylose fraction of wheat 
starch during high temperatures. This results in a slower retrogradation of the starch during cooling 
and storage (Gioia et al., 2017). Enzymes as fungal α-amylase are also often used in bread 
productions. They are not categorized as additives, and therefore do not need to be labeled on the 
product ingredient list or have an E-number. Still they are very important in the bread industry by 
degrading the damaged starch into smaller dextrins. The free mono- and disaccharides allow the 
yeast to continuously metabolize the sugars during fermentation, proofing and first stages of 
baking. This ensures a high loaf volume and even crumb texture (Damhus et al., 2008). If flour 
has a high FN, (see 3.1.1) α-amylase can also be added to the dough, to ensure better expansion 
during proofing.

3.2.2. Texture 
The texture of buns and breads varies from product to product and is a parameter that the 
consumers have knowledge about. While burger buns should be soft and airy, rye breads are heavy 
and dense, and a baguette should have a crispy crust and a firm crumb. To investigate the texture 
of a bread or bun a texture profile analysis can be carried out. The TPA is a tool used in food 
applications to determine textural properties of foods. The TPA compresses or cuts the food to 
investigate how foods act when they are handled and chewed. When TPA is performed on bread, 
buns or dough, parameters are given to describe how the dough and crumb act during two 
compressions and how the crust is acting during a cut though. The two compression TPA for crumb 
and dough analysis have two important expressions, upstroke and downstroke that is used 
conversely in the literature. In this thesis the upstroke energy is defined as the energy added when 
the probe is compressing the product, because the graph is increasing within the first distance 
which is indicated in area 3, see Figure 3.4. The downstroke energy is defined as the energy 
detected when the probe is withdrawing after the compression. The graph is decreasing, which is 
==PAGE:20==

indicated by area 4 see Figure 3.4. The two-compression graph is given with a y-axis with force, 
in gram or newton and a x-axis with time or distance see Figure 3.4. During the dough and crumb 
analysis, the parameter hardness is given by the force in Newton, that is added when the product 
is compressed the first time. On the TPA graph it would be located at the top of the first peak, see 
Figure 3.4. Resilience is a measure of the downstroke energy divided by the upstroke energy of 
the first compression, area 4 divided by area 3 see Figure 3.4. Resilience is dimensionless, but 
when multiplied by 100 the unit will be in %. The resilience describes how the product fights back 
the probe to gain its original height. The cohesiveness describes how a product withstands a second 
deformation compared to the first deformation. The cohesiveness is measured by dividing the area 
from the second compression with the area of the first compression, area 2 divided by area 1, see 
Figure 3.4. The value is dimensionless but when multiplied by 100 the unit will be in %. A dense 
bread like rye bread would have a higher cohesiveness compared to a hamburger bun because it 
can withstand tearing when cold butter is being spread. Springiness is the measure of how well the 
product springs back after it has been deformed by the first compression. It is measured by dividing 
the second compressions upstroke energy with the first compressions upstroke energy, distance 2 
divided by distance 1, see Figure 3.4. The value is dimensionless but when multiplied by 100 the 
unit will be in %. Bread usually have high springiness, because of the structure. But when dough 
is evaluated the springiness could be increased because dough can stick to the probe. This should 
be taken into account when dough is tested. Chewiness is a measure of the energy that is required 
to chew the product before it can be swallowed. Chewiness is used for solid products and is 
calculated by multiplying hardness cohesiveness and springiness. Chewiness is given in the unit 
newton (Texture Technologies, 2019). Two other parameters are given when a TPA analysis is 
carried out. Gumminess is hardness multiplied with cohesiveness, and is a measure used for semi 
solid products. Because buns are not semi solid, gumminess is neglected. Adhesiveness is another 
parameter describing the negative work under the curves between the two cycles (Texture 
Technologies, 2019). Dough could be subjected to this because of the sticking effect that could 
increase negative work when the probe is withdrawing, see Figure 3.5.

3.2.3. Proofing 
The most general characteristic that makes a bread or bun is the airiness, softness and elasticity. 
Without these characteristics the bread or bun would be more a biscuit or a cracker. Consumers 
expect these characteristics when they buy bread and buns. Because bread and buns are fermented 
with yeast they gain these important characteristics. During proofing the dough expands from flat 
and compact bread to a high volume loaf with the airy structure. A way to determine the expansion 
of bread or buns during proofing are by investigating the volume increase of the product. A 
measurement of volume increase can be investigated though various methods. The seed 
displacement methodology or rapeseed displacement method can be used to determine the volume 
of bread. It is still used and relies on the same concept as submersion method. Simard & Bouksaim 
1998 tested bread volumes by rapeseed displacement method for bread containing only baker’s 
yeast and bread containing baker’s yeast and brewer’s spent yeast 1:1. The volumes were 
respectivly 7.60 (cm3/g) and 7.40 (cm3/g) (Simard and Bouksaim, 1998). 
Another way to describe the proofing of bread is by measuring the production of CO2 in ml / min. 
This can be carried out by a Risograph (Rattin et al., 2009).  Methods like laser topography and 
3D scanning are also being used more often (Anderson et al., 2014).  When using 3D scanning the 
bread or bun expansion over time is measured by multiples mesh layers added to the surface of the 
product. These meshes are then added to the software for further calculation. The mesh layers are 
then connected to create a whole surface, where the volume is then retrieved from calculations by 
the software.  
3.3. Beer 
Beer is one of the most dominant alcoholic beverages and it is the fifth most consumed beverages 
in the world. In 2003 beer consumed in the U.S was 82 liters pr. adult, while Czech Republic as 
the top consumers with 161 liters pr. adult (Barth, 2013). In Denmark 277.2 million liters of beer 
was sold in 2018, where Pilsner made up 59 % of the sale (Danish Brewers' Association, 2018). 
Beer is produced during many steps and production can vary a lot from beer to beer. Three main 
steps are however always essential; mashing, boiling and fermenting. During these steps the malt 
is converted to wort during mashing. The wort is boiled, where hops usually are added, and cooled 
before transferred to the fermentation tank where yeast is pitched and the beer is made.

3.3.1. Beer Production 
Malt is the main ingredient for making beer as it is the source of the sugar that allows the yeast to 
ferment and produce alcohol.  Most malt used in brewing is based on barley grains, other grains 
or cereals are also used for specialty or regional style beers. Barley are suited for beer because of 
the fast enzyme synthesis and the high activity of α-amylase (Hansen, 2018). During the malting 
process the barley grains are treated through three main steps; steeping, germination and kilning. 
To make the grain germinate hydration must be implemented. This is done through steeping where 
the grains are submerged into water. After they have been hydrated they are retrieved and the 
germination is activated at controlled temperature and humidity. When the germination is achieved 
the grains are treated with heat during kilning to convert the malt into its finish storage form. When 
used in brewing the malt is partly germinated and contains starch, proteins and enzymes (Boulton, 
2013).  
The first step of brewing is the milling process where the malt is reduced in size to ensure the best 
yield of sugars during mashing. When the malt is milled it is termed grist. The grist is then 
transferred to the mashing process. This is carried out in a mash tank where the grist is mixed with 
water and heated during predetermined periods of time at controlled temperature or temperature 
intervals. The different temperature ensures the enzyme α and β amylase to degrade the starch into 
smaller fermentable sugars, see section 3.1.1. α-amylase is most effective at higher mash 
temperatures 68 – 72 °C, while β-amylase is most effective at lower temperatures 55 – 66 °C 
(Barth, 2013). After approximately one hour of mashing the liquid wort is transferred to a boiling 
tank. The leftover grist in the bottom of the tank is sparged with water to remove the residual 
sugars into the boiling tank. The grist is now called spent grain and is often sold to agriculture 
purposes. The wort is boiled as a heat treatment where unwanted microbes are killed, and off-
flavors are removed. Hops are usually added in the boiling step to provide bitterness and flavor to 
the wort. During boiling the hops forms iso-α-acids to provide the characteristic bitter flavor of 
beer. After approximately one hour of boiling, the hot wort is often transferred to a whirlpool 
where the hop residuals are removed by centrifugal force to gain a clearer beer. The wort is now 
cleared and need to cool down before it can be transferred to the fermentation tank. This is done 
by a heat exchanger where the liquid wort is cooled and transferred to the fermentation tank where 
yeast has been pitched. During fermentation the yeast convert the sugars in the wort into alcohol.

The time and temperature of the fermentation process varies from beer to beer. After fermentation 
the beer is filtrated, carbonated and bottled. Some beer requires maturing and some beers are not 
filtrated before they are packed, this depend on the beer style and type (Boulton, 2013). 
3.3.2. Yeast  
There are many yeast strains used in brewing and they differ from beer to beer. The most common 
strains used today are Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Saccharomyces pastorianus / uvarum. These 
strains are used in the two most popular types of beer; ales and lagers. Both strains have optimal 
properties like fast growth, high ethanol yield, and tolerance for environmental stress like high 
ethanol concentrations and low oxygen. This makes the strains very suitable for industrial brewing 
(Ferreira et al., 2010). Ales are used in traditionally brewing and often a part of beers that is 
distinctive for a special region. Ale is a top-fermented beer where Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the 
main yeast. S. cerevisiae ferments at higher temperature compared to lagers, around 15 – 23 °C. 
During fermentation the yeast produce a variety of esters together with other flavor and aroma 
compounds. Ales are often stronger beers with alcohol content around 6 % ABV (Ryan, 2014). 
Lagers are the most commonly consumed beer worldwide. It is a clear light beer often produced 
with the yeast Saccharomyces pastorianus. Lager is a bottom fermented beer that undergoes 
fermentation between 7 – 12 °C. The beer usually achieves alcohol content around 4.5 % ABV. 
Hereafter it goes through a longer secondary fermentation phase at 0 – 4 °C. During this phase the 
beer clears and the cold environment inhibits the production of aroma compounds providing a beer 
with the clean and light taste (Ryan, 2014).  
During the fermentation the yeasts primary task is to provide alcohol to the beer. Usually the 
fermenter is sealed with a one-way valve that allows gas to escape but non to enter. When the yeast 
has settled and beginning to multiply it will produce carbon dioxide and ethanol during anaerobic 
fermentation.  
C6H12O6 → 2 CO2 + 2 C2H5OH 
Besides from alcohol production yeast also contributes to aroma and flavor compounds that make 
a beer unique, in taste and odor. The compounds are depended on both yeast strain and 
fermentation temperature. During fermentation the beer release a great amount of heat and a 
cooling jacket is needed to ensure continually controlled temperature.

3.3.3. By-products in brewing   
In brewing there are two main by-products produced; spent grains and spent yeast. Spent grain is 
the major by-product that consists of approximately 85 % of the total by-products produced during 
brewing. On a scale of 100 liters of beer there will be produced 20 kg of spent grain 
(Chandrasekaran, 2012). Spent grains are often sold as animal feed for a low fee. The literature 
suggests some solutions on how value can be added to spent grains. Spent grains have been used 
in cultivation of mushrooms, compost, poultry and fish feed, or even building materials (Boulton, 
2013). Spent grains consists of 82 % moisture, 3.8 (% dm) ash, 52 (% dm) total organic carbon, 
4.3 (% dm) total nitrogen and 26.9 (% dm) total protein (Santos Mathias et al., 2015). The second 
major by-product from brewing is spent yeast. Spent yeast is often reused in breweries and during 
fermentation the yeast cell mass increases three to six-fold. The yeast is first discarded when being 
spoiled or unable to provide high enough yields of ethanol because of stress. Spent yeast in lager 
productions account for approximately 1.7 – 2.3 kg of spent yeast pr. m3 of final product. Spent 
yeast usually consist of 10 – 14 % of solids, including yeast, trub and beer solids and hops 
residuals, together with 1.5 – 2.5 % of the total beer production (Ferreira et al., 2010). It is often 
sold as inexpensive animal feed after inactivation by heat or discarded. When breweries sell spent 
yeast as feed the price of varies from 0 – 400 DKK / ton depending on the amount of dry matter. 
Usually spent yeast contains 8 – 12 % dry matter. By membrane filtration the dry matter can reach 
15 – 18 % and the price will increase to 200 – 700 DKK / tons (MBP Solutions Ltd, 2019). It is a 
source of proteins and nitrogen with a bitter taste and is generally recognized as safe (GRAS). 
Spent yeast consist of 86 % moisture, 5.9 (% dm) ash, 45.6 (% dm) total organic carbon, 9.1 (% 
dm) total nitrogen and 45.6 (% dm) total protein (Santos Mathias et al., 2015). The literature states 
different approaches where value can be added to spent yeast. Spent yeast contains both vitamins, 
and minerals, and is therefore investigated as food ingredients in multiple studies. Other areas of 
studies are fish feed applications of spent yeast and spent yeast as an extract for microbial 
substrates (Ferreira et al., 2010). The use of spent yeast is also investigated in the literature because 
of the content of β-glucan. β-glucan can be used to fortify food products to increase the fiber 
content.

3.3.4. Quality and control 
A beer is characteristic for its taste, bitterness, alcohol, foam, carbonate, color and haze. All these 
characteristics makes a beer, and it is important that the beer has a uniform quality, because it is 
expected from the consumer when they buy the beer (Ryan, 2014). Daily operations take place at 
breweries to ensure quality and uniformity every time the beer is produced. When working with 
beer and fermentation it is very important to have complete control to ensure the safety and quality 
of the beer. In beer brewing, control parameters such as alcohol content, original and final gravity, 
pH, and temperature are fixed parameters. All control parameters should be logged into a brew 
sheet during production and saved for verification. The control parameters are implemented to 
ensure that the final quality parameters are in place, these parameters include color, taste, foam 
and carbonation. During fermentation the yeast viability and control of beer spoilage are also 
important parameters to investigate. Boling of the wort is the only heat treatment step. It is very 
important that the equipment is sanitized from the boiling step to the beer is packed (Barth, 2013). 
Self-inspection programs and HACCP plans are often legal procedures that should be implemented 
to ensure quality and control. Every brewery must follow and comply to the local legislation to 
ensure consumer safety. 
3.3.5. Brewer’s spent yeast utilization as proofing agent 
To ensure consumer safety and quality from spent yeast utilization the handling of the product is 
essential. When spent yeast is discarded as animal feed the critical control point is the heat 
treatment received before formulation. Animal feed regulations differ from human consumption 
regulations and therefore the legal demands on storage, treatment and distribution of yeast as feed 
supplement are not comparable to human food demands. To ensure a controlled and legal handling 
of spent yeast for human consumption the quality and control should be extended from the 
fermentation of beer to the storage and distribution of the spent yeast. If spent yeast should be used 
as proofing agent for baked goods, a heat treatment would not be applied. Living cells needs other 
control steps to ensure quality and safety of the product. This implementation requires excess tanks 
with cooling jackets, controlled temperature, pH, oxygen and agitation together with a controlled 
feed of sugars to ensure transition from ethanol production to CO2 production. Hereafter the yeast 
should be packed and stored in a way that ensures uniform quality though out the shelf life period. 
Another parameter to investigate when using living cells as product is the environment they would

be stored and used in. Environmental factors as water activity, temperature, sugar and salt can 
affect the yeast performance. Low water activity and high temperatures would inhibit the yeast. 
Especially stress due to high salt content could be a problem for spent yeast used as proofing agent 
in baked goods. Lager strains are more sensitive to high salt concentrations compared to ale strains 
(Sanchez et al., 2012), therefore sensitivity of salt stress would be an area of investigation.  
Besides the general equipment, tests and control needed for production of spent yeast, the literature 
suggests further process steps that need to be evaluated to ensure the quality of the spent yeast. 
After retrieving yeast from fermentation, the yeast will consist of solids, like beer and trub solids, 
from other process steps. Trub solids are produced during boiling of wort and hops by coagulation 
of proteins and phenolic compounds. Both beer solids and trub solids are to be separated before 
spent yeast can be used as food ingredient (Ferreira et al., 2010). Another parameter that should 
be investigated is the removal of bitter taste from the yeast. Bitterness differs from beer to beer 
and therefore it is necessary to gain control of the bitterness when the yeast is used in food 
application. The bitter compounds can be removed by a surfactant or alkaline treatment to increase 
solubility of the bitter compounds before removal without affecting the viability (Simard and 
Bouksaim, 1998). Both treatments require expensive equipment as centrifuges or filters to ensure 
separation of solids or bitter compounds. If a brewery was to implement the equipment and control 
daily operations it would be very expensive and time consuming. An overview of possible profit 
is outlined below. Baker’s yeast is sold in grocery stores for 0.95 DKK pr. 50 grams with a dry

is 5.75 DKK / kg (Rosengreen, 2020). When estimating the profit, the sales price is estimated in 
relation to the liquid baker’s yeast because of the similar dry matter and the liquid form. If it is 
assumed that spent yeast will achieve the same quality and viability, the price is assumed to be 5 
DKK / kg with a dry matter content of 20 %. The price is lower than 5.75 DKK / kg to ensure 
market competition. If a brewery produces 3500 ton yeast slurry pr. year with a dry matter content 
of 10 % the sale price will have to be divided by 2 to make up for the lower dry matter. The profit 
will be 8,750,000 DKK / year.

at 70 min between 63 – 67 °C and 10 min at 75 °C. The brix was 17 and 14.9 before and after 
sparging with 230 L water. 3 different hops were added to the wort, cascade 400 grams, mosaic 
200 grams and magnum 200 grams, and boiled for 60 min at 100 °C. After boiling the wort was 
cooled and transferred to a fermenter with 125 grams of US-05 American ale yeast Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae, see data sheet Appendix C. The fermentation was carried out for 35 days with a 
temperature between 16 – 20 °C.  
5.1.1. Viability of spent yeast and baker’s yeast   
The viability test was performed with a methylene blue concentration to count the viable yeast 
cells in the solution (Sigma-Aldrich, 2019). The methylene blue solution was diluted 1 ml to 100 
ml of demineralized water and stored at 2 °C in maximum 2 weeks. 1 ml of spent yeast slurry was 
diluted in 100 ml of demineralized water. 1 gram of baker’s yeast was diluted in 100 ml of 
demineralized water. 1 ml of each solution was mixed with 1 ml of the methylene blue solution. 
After 2 min the solution was added to the hemocytometer and investigated trough microscope. The 
25 squares were photographed and transferred to a computer where minimum 200 cells were 
counted, see Figure 5.1. The %-viability is calculated by the number of live cells divided by the 
total number of cells multiplied with 100. The cell concentration is calculated by the number of 
total cells counted, multiplied with the ratio of total number of squares to squares counted, 
multiplied with the dilution factor and the constant volume of the chamber: 104 ml (White-Labs, 
2019). The results can be seen in section 6.2. When counting the cells, it is important to establish 
a method that is consistent through all counting procedures. The cells were counted as following; 
only single cells that lies within the whole square are counted, budded cells are not counted, cells 
touching the sides of the whole square are not counted. The shape of the cell should be round with 
a clear edge. If an edgy or unshaped item is observed, it is not counted. The red circles in Figure 
5.1, are examples of cells that are not counted.

5.1.2. Dry matter content of spent yeast and baker’s yeast  
The dry matter content was determined for respectively baker’s yeast and spent yeast, by adding 
0.5 grams of baker’s yeast into a weighed aluminum cup. The cup was placed 24 hours at 105 °C 
in a WTB Binder oven, before it was weighed again, and the dry matter content was calculated by 
subtracting the weight of the empty cup before drying from the weight after drying the sample. 1 
ml of spent yeast slurry was added to a weighed and dried 47 mm filter paper with a pore size at 
0.7 µm. The slurry was washed with 3 x 5 ml of demineralized water to remove excess beer and 
sugars from the slurry. The filter paper with the spent yeast sample was added to a weighed 
aluminum cup and placed in an oven for 24 hours at 105 °C. The dry matter content was calculated 
by subtracting the weight of the empty cup with the filter paper before drying from the weight after 
drying the sample.The mass ratio was determined from the dry matter values, and the water content 
needed for the spent yeast recipe was determined. For calculation method see Appendix B and for 
results see section 6.2.

6.2. Spent yeast performance 
During the recipe development both dry matter analysis and viability tests was conducted to 
continuously evaluate the spent yeast. The viability was tested during the thesis to investigate the 
cells and if the viability of the yeast could impact the proofing ability of the dough. The viability 
test was carried out with the methylene blue staining test, see section 5.1.1. The yeast was harvest 
from a fermenter during fermentation. When the yeast was harvest it was placed in the fridge at 5 
°C. The day of fermentation, viability, cell concentration and volume of proofed bun are shown in 
Table 6.9. The fermentation of the beer was carried out for 35 days, before the sedimented yeast 
was emptied from the fermenter and the beer was ready for further treatment. Throughout the 
fermentation the 125 grams of yeast pitched to the fermenter propagate to approximately 2 – 3 kg. 
The viability of the yeast increases from day 7 to day 13. This is expected because the alcohol in 
the fermenter is low and the sugars content is high. After 5 days in the fridge at 5 °C, the sample 
viability has increased with 11 %-point, which indicates the yeast had multiplied during storage. 
Because the buns (3) and (4) would not proof, the viability test was set on hold until a new batch 
of proofed buns with spent yeast was produced see Appendix A. After 22 days of fermentation the 
viability has decreased to 29 %. It was shown that a 24 hours maltose activation, where 20 grams 
of maltose pr. liter yeast slurry was stored at 25 °C water bath with stirring, could increase the

spent yeast tested at the last day of fermentation was at 9 %, no baking trial was performed with 
this yeast sample, but it is expected that it would have a lower proofing ability.

To ensure a complete and reliable usage of spent yeast the flow could be controlled to ensure same 
flow every time yeast was retrieved from the fermenter. Other methods as a centrifugation step 
would ensure a more reliable way to control the beer content before the dry matter analysis is 
performed. However, if this is done, all the spent yeast used in the buns should be centrifuged at

are analysed. The recipe development and results obtained through out the period of the thesis is 
outlined in Appendix A. The bun with ½ salt (12), had great potential and was therefore used as

6.3.2.1. 
Dough TPA results 
The dough data was subjected to one-way ANOVA and besides chewiness and resilience all the 
other parameters were significantly different from the control see Table 6.16. The height of the 
baker’s yeast and spent yeast dough was respectively 36.59 mm and 22.38 mm in average. It was 
clear to see the difference and the dough was proofing very differently. Where the spent yeast 
dough has a tendency to proof more outwards, the baker’s yeast dough proof more upwards. The 
hardness of the dough will also be affected by the 60 % compression. During compression the 
dough will become more compact and the force will increase. In the spent yeast dough the gas 
pockets are not developed as well as in the baker’s yeast dough, therefore the bun will also be 
subjected to greater force. Gas pockets decreases the applied force when the sample is compressed. 
The resilience is not significantly different from each other and are very low in both doughs, this 
is due to the fact that the ability to fight back the probe after the first 60 % compression is difficult 
for the dough, because most of the gas is pushed out of the dough. The baking paper between the 
probe and the dough should also be taken into account. It will affect the dough in pushing back the 
probe because the dough sticks to the baking paper and will have to push the paper back first. The 
cohesiveness data are significantly different, and it states that the baker’s yeast dough is better to 
withstand a second deformation compared to the spent yeast dough. It is easy to see how the force 
applied to push down the dough at the second deformation is decreased in the spent yeast dough 
compared to the baker’s yeast dough, see Figure 6.4. The 60 % compression occurs faster for the 
spent yeast dough, and the peaks are much more narrow compared to the baker’s yeast peak. This 
again is due to the fact that the baker’s yeast dough is containing more gas pockets and that the 
dough is less compact compared to the spent yeast dough and the fact that the baker’s yeast dough 
is higher than the spent yeast dough. The chewiness is not significantly different, and this is 
because generally dough is very soft, and it does not require much energy to break down. The 
springiness is also significantly different in comparison of the two doughs. The baker’s yeast 
dough is better at regaining its height after the first compression. Most of the gas in the dough is 
released during the first compression, but because the baker’s yeast dough is elastic it can regain

greater increase in volume compared to the spent yeast dough. The ability to proof is dependent

is able to proof to an acceptable size during 24 hours of proofing. The spent yeast is slowly 
converting the sugars from the dough into gas. Therefore, the main working hypothesis that states 
that, brewer’s spent yeast can be used as proofing agent in buns is accepted. However, 24 hours 
proofing is unfortunately often an unwanted and time consuming procedure. Some bakeries have 
bread with long proofing periods, but mainly the bread and buns sold have proofing periods around

damage during packing and transporting compared to the spent yeast buns. The chewiness is also 
increasing when spent yeast is added to buns, which indicates a higher amount of energy needed

soft and airy and an increase in chewiness would for some costumers be a decrease of quality.

Crust TPA results 
The crust data retrieved from the TPA analysis was subjected to one-way ANOVA and all the 
parameters besides the work at 5 mm was significantly different from the baker’s yeast control 
buns see Table 6.19. The first parameter evaluated is the height where it is clear from both the data 
but also from visual inspections that the crust height could not be equal, when the crumb height 
was equal. When the crust is higher more force is needed to cut through because of the excess 
crumb that builds up under the knife during the pressure. When the knife cuts through the baker’s 
yeast crust it does not cut the crust all the way through. This affected the force to increase within 
last seconds of the cut. Because the spent yeast crusts are lower in height the knife is able to cut 
through the crusts. This affected the max force by increasing the force needed to cut though the 
baker’s yeast crust compared to the spent yeast crust. When the total work is calculated it is 
multiplying height and work and because of the difference in height the values are not able to be 
compared. The work within 5 mm and 10 mm of the crust is however more comparable because 
the crusts are exposed to the force within the same distance. The 5 mm work applied to the baker’s 
yeast and spent yeast crust are not significantly different. However, the baker’s yeast crust has a 
high standard deviation within the first 5 mm. This I due to the variation in the crust because of 
the gas pockets. When the knife hits a pocket, it will need less pressure to cut though. Because of 
the variation in the distribution of gas pockets there is a high variation and uncertainty in the data. 
The 10 mm work applied to the baker’s yeast and spent yeast crusts are significantly different and 
the spent yeast crust has the highest value of work. At 10 mm the spent yeast crust is almost cut 
through and the crumb is building up like explained earlier. Therefore, an increase in force during 
the last seconds of the cut ensures a higher value of the spent yeast crust. The knife is still able to 
cut through the spent yeast crust because of the low height. When the data is compared it is easy 
to see that the compression graphs have the same trend, see Figure 6.6. The work at 5 mm for 
baker’s yeast and spent yeast crust are not that different from each other, and it would be expected 
that both total work and max force would be more comparable parameters in spent yeast and 
baker’s yeast if the height of the crust was the same. However, the standard deviation for baker’s 
yeast buns at 5 mm work is high, and to state anything from the mean values are difficult. To 
convert the work into joules for estimation of energy required to bite through the crust, standard 
deviation should be decreased. To ensure lower standard deviation and to obtain more comparable 
parameters, more buns should be baked to ensure both crust and crumb would be of equal height.

6.3.2.6. 
Spent yeast and baker’s yeast shelf life results  
After 4 days of storage, the crumb and crust were tested to investigate how the shelf life period 
effected the quality. During storage the buns are subjected to both retrogradation and loss of 
moisture. Both spent yeast and baker’s yeast buns were subjected to one-way ANOVA where 
hardness, cohesiveness and chewiness was significantly different in the crumb, see Table 6.21, and 
the max force was the only value not significantly different in the crust see Table 6.22. The height 
of the crumb was not significantly different which indicates that the buns were cut very precise in 
this experiment. This is due to the fact that the buns were harder and easier to handle. The crumb 
hardness is increasing very fast during the days of storage and the buns were very firm due to loss 
of moisture. The baker’s yeast buns did not increase in crumb hardness as much as the spent yeast 
buns, but both buns are increasing too much through the storage period which would be a very big 
problem if the buns were to be sold in grocery stores. The crumb resilience is not significantly 
different, but the mean values are lower compared to the fresh baked buns. The crumb 
cohesiveness is also is decreasing in the buns through the storage period. Since the moisture is 
decreasing the crumb are getting denser and would not be able to fight back or withstand a second 
deformation as good as a fresh baked bun because of the dry crumb. A decrease in resilience and 
cohesiveness is expected when the crumb is getting drier. The loss in moisture during storage is 
also the main factor for increase in both chewiness and springiness of the crumb. The bun gets 
denser and harder to chew. A high springiness value is usually consistent with light and airy 
qualities. After 4 days of storage the springiness is still high because of the loss of moisture and 
retrogradation that affect the buns by becoming denser and drier. Taken the force into account the 
high springiness is not a parameter in the crumb that would be appreciated by costumers.  
It was easy to see that the height of the crust, as discussed earlier was again significantly different. 
The max force of the crust however is now almost equal, this is due to the fact that during the 
storage period the buns become dry, dense and firm, which makes it easier for the knife to cut

ensure a decrease in force because the elasticity of the dough is very low after 4 days of storage, 
the crust is more dry and breaks easier. The crust TPA compression graphs after 4 days of storage 
vary a lot from what is observed from the fresh baked buns see Figure 6.8. At the top of the graph 
the baker’s yeast bun has a lot of variation in force applied. In the spent yeast curve, it is clear at

7. Discussion  
To investigate if brewers spent yeast can substitute baker’s yeast in bread, experiments were 
carried out to test different parameters of buns with spent yeast and buns with baker’s yeast. 
Baker’s yeast buns were used as control. The first period of the thesis was used to find a way to 
proof the dough when spent yeast was added. It was discovered that the dough showed an increase 
of proofing volume when the salt content was lowered in the recipe. A lowering of salt is affecting 
the taste of the bread, and many consumers would not appreciate bread containing less salt because 
it is one of the few dominant flavors of bread. The salt content was lowered, and the spent yeast 
buns were analysed and compared with the baker’s yeast buns. Most of the analysed parameters 
showed a difference between baker’s yeast buns and spent yeast buns because of the spent yeast’s 
weaker ability to proof. However, the lack of proofing, was not the only parameter affecting the 
results. During the analysis and tests performed, parameters and procedures could be further 
optimized for increased reproducibility and uniformity. The first test performed was proofing of 
the dough to measure the volume increase. The baker’s yeast control buns were proofing for 1 
hour and the volume was measured every 10 min. Because of the measuring every 10 min, some 
of the control buns were proofed at a temperature between, 21 – 23 °C in a separate room on the 
3D scanner plate. The rest of the baker’s yeast control buns in each batch were placed in a proofing 
oven at 80 % humidity and 25 °C, and visually it was easy to see they had a better proofing 
performance. The control buns in the 3D scanner room dried out on the surface and proofing was 
limited. Still the baker’s yeast control buns proofed at 21 – 23 °C in the separate room, had a 
greater volume after 1 hour of proofing compared with the spent yeast buns after 24 hours of 
proofing at 80 % humidity 25 °C. The volume of the 3D scanned baker’s yeast dough would have 
been even greater if the buns were proofed at 80 % humidity 25 °C. To ensure better volume data, 
the baker’s yeast control buns should only be tested at 0 and 1 hour instead of every 10 min and in 
the meantime stored in the proofing oven at 80 % humidity and 25 °C like the spent yeast buns. 
Another parameter that was hard to control was the TPA analysis of the dough. When the probe is 
compressing the dough, baking paper is used to ensure a controlled compression of the dough. 
Without baking paper between probe and dough, the dough would stick to the probe after the first 
deformation causing a stretch of the dough so the force applied would be greater during the second 
compression. If the force applied at the second compression is greater than the first, the TPA 
parameters cannot be calculated by the software. The baking paper did however cause a decrease 
==PAGE:62==
Line Lillelund Rasmussen  
 
Food Technology 
55 
 
in the dough’s ability to regain its height and spring back because the dough stuck to the baking 
paper. Another method with addition of oil added to the probe was tested, but the baking paper 
was chosen to ensure reproducible results. Since the dough is not for eating the parameters given 
in the TPA are hard to correlate to other products. However, the height of the dough is important, 
because it also provides an indication about how well the buns have proofed. But the parameters 
change very drastically when the dough is baked, and it is difficult to compare what is observed 
for the dough and for the baked buns. As mentioned in section 5.2.3, the TPA results of the crust 
had many uncertainties because of the difference in height. The cutting technique was optimized 
through the thesis where two new parameters were introduced to ensure that the force applied to 
the crusts were comparable at the 5 and 10 mm cutting depth. Still results showed that the force 
applied to the crust at both 5 and 10 mm had big variations because of the gas pockets produced 
during proofing.  
During the thesis statistics has been applied to the data to ensure that the data was evaluated for 
significance to determine a difference in the data. The one-way ANOVA provided the p-value, 
mean and standard deviation. When using statistics, it is important to understand that it only 
responds to the data provided. Data points are normally distributed and if the individual points are 
far away from each other p-values and means may be subject to some uncertainty. Also, one outlier 
can result in a very high standard deviation if the data are normally distributed within a small 
range. Both problems were seen when applying statistical analysis on the data. The standard 
deviation is of high importance when the mean values and p-values are evaluated. Some of the 
data sets have a standard deviation of ⅓ of the mean value or even higher, which makes it very 
difficult to state anything from the mean values. To obtain a lower standard deviation more sample 
are needed. But even with a lot of samples and a controlled environment the buns are proofed 
through fermentation by microorganisms with varying performance and it is therefore difficult to 
ensure complete uniformity and reproducibility.

8. Conclusion  
The results stated in the thesis indicates that it is possible to obtain a bun with acceptable 
characteristics and taste for consuming using spent yeast as proofing agent. Even though, they are 
eatable the only hypothesis that can be accepted is the main hypothesis stating that, brewers spent 
yeast can be used as proofing agent in bread because of the ability to proof the spent yeast dough 
at 24 hours, when the salt content was lowered. The second hypothesis stating that brewers spent 
yeast application does not change the texture of dough, must be rejected because of significant 
difference in height, hardness cohesiveness and springiness in the doughs. The third hypothesis 
stating that brewers spent yeast application does not change the texture of the crust and crumb in 
buns, must also be rejected because of significant difference in height, hardness, cohesiveness, 
chewiness and springiness of the crumbs and the significant difference in height, max force, total 
work and work at 10 mm of the crusts. Both the dough, crust and crumb hypothesis are rejected 
both because of the significant different parameters. The parameters are different because of the 
weaker proofing ability of spent yeast compared to baker’s yeast. The weaker proofing affects the 
dough, crust and crumb in becoming more compact, which led to the difference in the above states 
parameters. The fourth hypothesis stating that brewers spent yeast application does not change the 
crust color of the buns, must also be rejected because of the significant difference in the color 
values L, a and b of the crust colors. Visually the color of the crusts was much darker because 
when spent yeast was used ad proofing agent. The spent yeast had a dark color because of the dark 
roasted malt that was used in the brewing process. The fifth hypothesis stating that brewers spent 
yeast application does not change the texture of the crust and crumb in buns during the shelf life 
period, must also be rejected because of significant difference in hardness, cohesiveness and 
chewiness of the crumbs and the significant difference in height,  total work and work at 5 and 10 
mm of the crusts. After 4 days of storage the buns were more compact and dry, which were 
indicated by the above stated parameters but especially by the increased hardness of the crumb. 
By investigating other ingredients and adding enzymes and additives it is expected that the spent 
yeast buns can obtain similar quality as expected in buns or breads sold on the market and the shelf 
life can also be extended. Further investigation is needed to ensure that the salt content can be 
increased without affecting the spent yeast and inhibit proofing.

If spent yeast is retrieved from breweries with the purpose of being sold to bakeries and bread 
industries, there would be a high implementation cost to control and secure the quality and safety 
of the spent yeast. To ensure content control, spent yeast strains and beer types should not be 
mixed. Spent yeast should reach a desired viability, around 95 % without the presence of spoilage 
bacteria. A purification step should be evaluated in relation to the solids and residuals in spent 
yeast. If the hop content in the beer results in a bitter taste, a debittering step should also be 
evaluated. The spent yeast should be packaged in food approved containers, with labels according 
to the current legislation, and a description of storage, application and shelf life. Even though, it is 
possible to obtain a spent yeast bun with acceptable parameters, it is recommended to look at other 
ways to obtain a higher value for spent yeast because of the high implementation costs and low 
profit utilizing spent yeast as proofing agent.

9. Further investigations 
As stated in the discussion many problems occurred during the thesis and there are a lot of 
procedures to reevaluate in order to obtain better results with less uncertainty. However, the 
performance of spent yeast in bread is not the only subject to evaluate. After investigating the 
potential of spent yeast as a proofing agent, it was determined that both a lowering of salt content 
and an extension of proofing time to 24 hours was necessary. These two parameters are not very 
likely to be accepted by bakeries or baking industries. If the industries already have 24 hours 
proofing periods for special types of bread the spent yeast is could be a substitute for these recipes. 
The salt content however is still a problem.  
The recovery of good quality spent yeast from breweries requires both large capital investment 
and is costly to implement and operate compared to the possible profit. When higher value should 
be added to spent yeast there are other parameters of spent yeast which are interesting to 
investigate. In the literature the nutritional content in brewers spent yeast is investigated as a food 
ingredient, where the flavor is used or where the spent yeast adds higher nutritional value. Spent 
yeast can also be utilized as a cheaper yeast extract for growth media used in microbiological 
processes or analysis. All together they share the fact that the spent yeast does not have to be alive 
when used. In a production, where the spent yeast is heat treated the dry matter and composition 
would be of higher importance than the viability and cell concentration.
