Summary (English)
As smartphones become increasingly powerful and feature more sensors than
ever before, the ﬁeld of health monitoring is growing. In recent years much re-
search has focused on health behavior change using smartphones and monitoring
of health factors. This thesis seeks to investigate how a pedometer app can sup-
port behavior change in everyday physical activity levels. Through analysis of
state of the art in pedometer apps and related work a social pedometer app is
made. It is called StepMate. StepMate is implemented for the iOS platform
and takes advantage of iOS frameworks to extract physical activity data from
the phone’s sensors.
18 people participate in a test of StepMate over a two week period. A baseline
period is made out of data for each day of the year prior to the test and of one
week after the test period. In total 372 days of step counts are collected from
each participant. Data from the test indicate that daily step counts are inﬂu-
enced by many factors and that it takes a large dataset to have a representative
look at how much people walk.
The expected outcome were that participants would increase their number of
steps during the test and decrease them again once the test was over. This
pattern were observed in average step counts, but no statistically signiﬁcant
diﬀerence were found between the baseline and the test period.

Smartphones bliver mere og mere kraftfulde for hver år der går og bruger ﬂe-

ge undersøgelser der fokuserer på adfærdsændringer omkring sundhed ved brug

aktivitetsdata ud af telefonen.

per dag mellem perioderne, men der blev ikke fundet en statistisk signiﬁkant
forskel mellem perioderne.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Around the world obesity, chronic diseases and diabetes are on the rise.
In
previous times mankind had to be on the move to survive by catching food or
farming land, but as the world becomes more and more modernized, the need
for moving becomes less and less. In today’s modern society a vast majority
of people have jobs where they sit all day and the need for physical activity
is minimal. Obesity has become an epidemic linked to even worse side eﬀects
than smoking or drinking [1]. The cure is simple, good nutrition and regular
physical activity. Studies have shown that people can achieve health beneﬁts
simply by increasing the number of steps they take each day. Adults of all ages
beneﬁt greatly from just 30 minutes of brisk activity most days of the week [2].
The problem is, how do you get people to walk more and how do people know
if they have walked enough each day? This project will try to ﬁnd an answer in
the ﬁeld of self-logging using smartphones.
Smartphones and self-logging have been much researched in the last decade
or so. Smartphones are playing a bigger and bigger role in everyday life and
are spreading to a ever larger majority of the world’s population and thus, are
an obvious tool for logging everyday activity. Many studies have focused on
changing people’s behavior in regards to their physical activity level, with a
mobile phone as the medium. In previous years such studies were conducted
with old school pedometers and manual logging every day [3]. Smartphones are
changing how such studies can be made because people only need to have a

smartphone to participate and its sensors can do most of the logging for them.
Smartphones are becoming increasingly powerful, while at the same time more
aﬀordable and accessable to many people around the world. The accessibility of
these devices means that many people walk around with powerful computers in
their pockets. A ﬁeld that is already taking advantage of this is personal health.
Mobile devices have many sensors that monitor the device’s usage and thereby
also monitor the user. Companies are taking advantage of phone sensors to
make it possible to monitor personal health parameters. An iPhone for example
knows how much its user is moving during the day. Using its accelerometer
and gyroscope it is able to keep count of how many steps the user takes. By
collecting this data, a phone application is able to quantify a user’s daily or
weekly activity etc. One can imagine that people can use this self-quantiﬁed
data to improve their activity habits, e.g. by making sure they perform the
recommended 30 minutes of brisk activity per day.

Chapter 2
Analysis
This analysis is an exploration of the ﬁeld presented by the problem statement.
It seeks to present relevant theory and from it hypothesize a solution for the
problem statement. The starting point will be to seek a deﬁnition of motivation
and how to facilitate it.
2.1
Motivation
To be motivated is to have a desire to do something or behave in a certain
way. Without desire or inspiration to do something, a person is unmotivated.
Motivation does not only vary in the intensity with you feel it, but also in
what cause the motivation. The cause can spring from many reasons, desires
or needs, internally or externally. For example, you can be motivated to do a
task because you ﬁnd it inherently interesting. On the other hand, someone else
can be motivated to perform the same task because they identify the value of
completing it. A third person can ﬁnd the task uninteresting and fail to identify
the value of its outcome. When you are not motivated, you are amotivated.
Amotivation is the state of lacking a reason to act. Ryan and Deci (2000)[4]
describes the cause amotivation as not valuing a task, not feeling competent to
do it or not believing its outcome is valuable.
==PAGE:16==
2.1 Motivation
5
The intensity of motivation does not necessarily vary, but the nature of it does.
Ryan and Deci (2000) [4] introduce two types types of motivation: intrinsic and
extrinsic. They will be explained in the following two sections.
2.1.1
Intrinsic motivation
When you do an activity that you ﬁnd inherently interesting, you are intrinsi-
cally motivated to do that activity. For example, if a person riding mountain
bikes in the weekend is not motivated because of external factors, pressures or
rewards, but only rides because of the enjoyment it gives him, he is intrinsically
motivated.
What people get satisfaction doing is very subjective and so is intrinsic motiva-
tion. One person might be intrinsically motivated to do a task, while another
person ﬁnds it horribly uninteresting.
Ryan and Deci (2000) [4] argue that social structures like communication and
feedback that yield a feeling of competence will enhance the feeling of intrinsic
motivation because it will give the person satisfaction in himself. The catch is
that this perceived competence must come with a sense of autonomy, meaning
that the person experience their behavior to be self-deterministic.
Without
autonomy the activity will fell forced and feeling internal satisfaction from the
activity becomes diﬃcult.
Autonomy is an essential aspect in feeling intrinsic motivation. Anything that
hinders it, hinders a person’s feeling of intrinsic motivation. For example, tangi-
ble rewards, deadlines, directives and competition pressure diminishes intrinsic
motivation because 1) it removes the focus from internal satisfaction to external
outcomes and 2) it limits the feeling of being in control (autonomy). On the
other hand choice, opportunity and self-direction are good for intrinsic motiva-
tion because they give a greater sense of autonomy.
With all this in mind it is important to remember that intrinsic motivation will
still occur happen if a person is interested in the task that is to be performed.
[4]
2.1.2
Extrinsic motivation
Extrinsic motivation is when a person does an activity not because of the en-
joyment the activity entails, but because of an external, separable outcome. For
==PAGE:17==
2.1 Motivation
6
example, a student who does her work because she believes it will greatly inﬂu-
ence the outcome of her career is extrinsically motivated. It is the outcome of
the activity that motivates the student, not the activity itself.
Ryan and Deci [4] argue that to motivate people to perform tasks that are not
intrinsically interesting to them, they have to learn to value and self-regulate
these task. This process is described in the term internalization and integration
of values.
“Internalization is the process of taking in a value or regulation, and integration
is the process by which individuals more fully transform the regulation into their
own so that it will emanate from their sense of self.” [4]
To describe how diﬀerent forms of extrinsic motivation can promote or hinder
internalization and integration, Ryan and Deci (1985) [5] introduced a theory
called Organismic Integration Theory. It identiﬁes four states of extrinsic moti-
vation seen in ﬁgure 2.1. On the left is amotivation, on the far right is intrinsic
motivation and in between is the four states of extrinsic motivation. The state
closest to the left is the most similar to amotivation and the closest to the right
is the most similar to intrinsic motivation. It is important to note, that one does
not have to progress from amotivation through each state until you reach in-
trinsic motivation - the transition is not linear. One’s motivation for a behavior
can jump between states at any time.

2.1 Motivation
7
2.1.2.1
External regulation
External regulation is the classic deﬁnition of extrinsic motivation, the furthest
from intrinsic motivation. It is associated with rewards and punishments. Its
perceived locus of causality1 is external.
2.1.2.2
Introjection
Introjected regulation describes a type of internal regulation where the perceived
locus of causality is external because the activity feels controlled by external
factors, not oneself. Actions are performed in order to get approval from oneself
or from others in order to improve one’s sense of worth.
2.1.2.3
Identiﬁcation
It is a more autonomous form of extrinsic motivation where a person can iden-
tify with the outcome of a task to be personally important. The regulation
is therefore seen as the person’s own choice and not something that is being
forced upon him. Regulation through identiﬁcation is somewhat internal, but
not completely because the person does not value the task itself, but merely its
outcome.
2.1.2.4
Integration
Integrated regulation is the most autonomous form of extrinsic motivation, which
occur when the outcome of a task match with your values and needs and you
therefore identify with it. The outcome is important to you and you feel the
choice to perform the task is fully self-determined. Its perceived locus of causal-
ity is internal. While being very similar to intrinsic motivation in the level of
identiﬁcation with the task and being in control of the task, there still is an
important diﬀerence between the two. Behavior motivated by integrated regula-
tion is performed because of the value the outcome will give the person. If the
person had been intrinsically motivated, it would be performing the task that
was the motivator, not the outcome.
1 Perceived locus of causality relates to a person’s perception of the cause of success or
failure. If it is internal, the person sees the cause as a result of his own ability or eﬀort. External
means the result is caused by an external factor, e.g. luck, - something not controlled by the
person. [6]

2.1.2.5
Autonomy
While the progress from external regulation to integration does not have to
develop along a continuum, Ryan and Cornell (1989)[7] tested whether the states
of external motivation lies along a continuum of autonomy. The experiment
revolved around school children doing their homework. From it they argue that
when more autonomy is felt in a task, one will identify the task’s outcome more
to one’s self. Removing autonomy from doing homework reduced the childrens’
interest and eﬀort.
Introjected regulation was related to more eﬀort, but also more anxiety as the
task felt more important to the children. Identiﬁcation yielded more interest
and eﬀort, while intrinsic motivation was connected to enjoyment and feeling of
competence. [7]
From [7] and subsequent experiments, Deci and Ryan (2000) argue that more
autonomous extrinsic motivation yields greater engagement and better perfor-
mance.
The issue is how to promote autonomy and thus internalization in
extrinsically motivated tasks. [4]
2.1.2.6
Promoting internalization
Since extrinsically motivated behavior is not inherently interesting to a person,
it can be diﬃcult to promote internalization. Ryan and Deci (2000) argue that
the initial prompting to perform extrinsically motivated behavior must be that
signiﬁcant others value the behavior. If a person belongs to a group that values
the behavior, the person is more likely to do it because the person has a sense
of relatedness to it.
To promote internalization three factors must come into play: relatedness, feel-
ing of competence and autonomy. [4]
2.1.3
Intrinsic vs. extrinsic
There are diﬀerences and similarities between intrinsic- and extrinsic motivation,
especially when the perceived locus of causality becomes more and more internal.
In the case of exercising and walking it can be argued that if you ﬁnd such
activities to be intrinsically motivating, your health is not at risk because of
lack of exercise and you do not need an app to get you to do it more often. On
the other hand, people who are extrinsically motivated to improve their health,

identify with the outcome, but do not ﬁnd value in the task itself. They have a
discrepancy between their desired and actual lifestyle [8] and it is these kind of
people that can be helped to walk more.

2.2
Triggering a behavior
BJ Fogg [9] introduce a behavior model for persuasive design called Fogg’s Be-
havior Model (FBM). FBM describe that three factors are needed for a person
to perform a task: motivation, ability and trigger. Motivation were introduced
in the previous section. Ability and trigger will be explained in the following.
2.2.1
Ability
To perform a task people need knowledge about how to do it. If a person is asked
to do something that they know nothing about or is physically impossible to
them, they don’t have the ability to perform that task. Simplicity will enhance
ability. Simple tasks are easier to perform and more people will have the ability
to perform them.
Three pitfalls of simplicity:
• If a task requires time, a person that does not have time available will not
ﬁnd the task simple.
• If a task requires physical eﬀort, a person without the means to endure
the physical eﬀort will not ﬁnd the task simple.
• People ﬁnd tasks that are apart of their routine to be more simple, than
task that are not.
2.2.2
Trigger
A trigger is something that tells a person to perform a task now. It can also be
called: prompts, cues or calls to action. FBM introduces three kinds of triggers:
==PAGE:21==
2.3 State of the art
10
• Spark: when a person lacks motivation, but has ability a spark trigger can
be used as a motivational element.
• Facilitator: when a person lacks ability, but is motivated, a facilitator
trigger can be used. The goal is to ensure the person that the task is not
diﬃcult to do.
• Signal: when a person is motivated and has ability a signal trigger can be
used to trigger a task. This kind of trigger simply reminds the person to
perform the task.
BF Fogg argue that people will be most tolerant to triggers that are signals or
facilitators because the person is already motivated to do the task. Sparks may
annoy the recipient because it wants to motivate him to do something he did
not intend to do. It is important to that triggers are used at the correct time
or else the recipient will just ignore them. [9]

2.4.1
Social
In 2006 Consolvo et al [3] looked into how to encourage opportunistic physi-
cal activities, which means to incorporate physical activities into one’s normal,
everyday life. They conducted a study where participants wore a pedometer
on them all day and would then input they ﬁnal step count each night onto a
custom made application for Nokia 6600. They focused on how daily goals and
social interaction can induce behavior change, i.e. increase people’s physical
activity level. Three diﬀerent version of the application were made:
1. Baseline version
• Input step count
• View step count for the last seven days.
2. Personal version
• All features of baseline version.
• View daily goal and progress towards it.
• Receive appraisal for meeting the goal.
3. Sharing version
• All features of personal version.
• Send step count and comment to friends.
• See friends’ progress towards goal.
• Send messages to friends.
Participants were divided into three groups. The ﬁrst week all three groups
would use the baseline version to establish a baseline step count. In week two
and three, two of the groups used the sharing version and one group used the
personal version. Consolvo et al. (2006) found that participants in the sharing
groups were signiﬁcantly more likely to meet the goal, than participants in the
personal group [3]. Support from friends and family can increase one’s physical
level, but so can social pressure. Previous research have also shown that a social
setting can increase how much people walk [3][14][15].
2.4.2
Goals
The personal version of the application made in Consolvo et al. (2006) [3] did
not feature a social aspect, but instead focused on personal goals. Consolvo et
==PAGE:26==
2.4 Related work
15
al. (2006) found that daily step count goals should not be set automatically by
the system, but should be set by the user. If the user does not have any say, the
goal can feel forced upon the user. The goal will likely not ﬁt the user and be
either too low or too high. Too low a goal will be to easy and not be challenging
for the user. Too high and the user will be demotivated because the goal seems
unachievable. Consolvo et al. (2009) [8] argue that having a challenging goal is
essential and that progress should be reported to the user continuously during
the day, and not only once the goal has been achieved. Users should receive
positive reinforcement as they progress towards the goal and when they reach
it. If a user does not reach the goal, she should not be rewarded or punished,
but should be encouraged to keep going to keep her interest. [8]
Consolvo et al. (2006) [3] argue that while individually set goals are more likely
to ﬁt a user, if a social aspect is introduced an one-size-ﬁts-all goal is more
likely to spark competition between people as they are all striding towards the
same goal.
2.4.3
Abstract data representation
Consolvo et al. (2009) [8] introduced a similar experiment where participants
would use a mobile phone where the wallpaper on the screen was an abstract
representation of their physical activity level and progress towards the goal. The
wallpaper showed a garden that would blossom as physical activity level would
rise. Consolvo et al. (2009) argue that an abstract data representation will
allow people to create alternative explanations of the data and in their mind
create a story that will enable them to more easily reﬂect on the data.
Tollmar et al. (2012) [16] combined multiple streams of sensor data in a mobile
application and analyzed the data to ﬁnd patterns. They then presented the
user with feed of one sentence messages correlating data to each other. For
example:
“You walk 80% farther on weekends vs. weekdays (15000 vs. 8300).” [16]
Tollmar et al. (2012) found that presenting aggregated data gave users a quick
insight into their health and something that was easy to reﬂect on. One user
noticed that she gained more weight on Mondays and the reason was that she
had a big dinner at her Mom’s every Sunday. Graphs of raw data were also
available to the users, but they found it confusing and that it did not really tell
them anything about the physical activity level.

Tollmar et al. (2012) argue:
“While the text only allowed users to scratch the surface of the rich data that
our system collected, perhaps these simple one-sentence summaries are all that
is necessary for awareness of a particular wellbeing pattern.” [16]
2.4.3.1
Abstraction/aggregation vs. raw data
While people found the texts useful and the graphs less useful, there needs to
be a balance between the two. People would still like to be able to see raw data
about their latest progress. This could e.g. be done by using mini-visualizations
and by making the feedback more engaging and glance-able [16]. It seems that
when showing historical data, it should only be recent data or at least it should
be condensed. Showing a large dataset, seems to only confuse users.
This approach of analyzing data and showing abstractions on it is similar to
Lark, which were introduced in section 2.3.1.
2.4.4
Account for all activities
In Consolvo et al. (2006) and Consolvo et al. (2009) users complained that
they did not receive credit for all their physical activity.
In Consolvo et al.
(2006) riding a bike or performing other kinds of exercise did not count towards
their step count. Participants felt cheated because, for some, these activities
accounted for the majority of the physical activity.
It is argued that users
should receive credit for all physical behavior. [3][8]
2.4.5
Reminders
In Tollmar et al users had a pedometer and a scale they had to use everyday,
but found that users would often forget to use them. Tollmar et al. argue that
there was a need for reminders or notiﬁcation to ensure users would remember
to log data. Missing data resulted in false predictions and users had a lower
credibility in the data. [16]
Bentley et al investigated the use of mobile notiﬁcations to increase logging
behavior. They found that adding notiﬁcations to a manual food logging app
improved the logging frequency by over 5x compared to their pilot study. [17]

“These notiﬁcations do not get in the way of current activities but can be at-

Notiﬁcations can act like a trigger to encourage or remind people to do the activ-

Notiﬁcations should be:

2. Actionable
3. User conﬁgurable
One eﬀect is to remind users to use the app, another is to prime them to reﬂect on

2.4.6
Simplicity of use
Problem with many previous studies of behavior change with pedometer appli-
cations is that they required a standalone pedometer attached to the person. In
Tollmar et al. (2012) [16] and Consolvo et al. (2006) [3] participants complained
about the devices they had to use to participant in the study. In Tollmar et al.
(2012) participants complained about the application used drained the battery
on their phones. In Consolvo et al. (2006) people felt the devices they had to
use was big and clunky. It is a problem if participants ﬁnds using the required
devices unnatural or annoying as it will result in a diﬀerent behavior than nor-
mal or that they will stop using them. Consolvo et al. (2009) [8] goes as far as
to state that a design guideline is that the data collection has to unobtrusive.
With the advances in mobile devices in recent years, there is no longer a need
for anything else than a mobile phone to complete experiments like the ones
mentioned above. This will result in a unobtrusive experience and feel very
natural to the participants as they only need to carry their mobile phone as
they normally do.

Target group
People who enjoy to take walks in their free time (intrinsic motivation) or have
a job that requires them to walk a lot will not be asked to participate in this

2.5.1
Motivation
The target group will include people who are extrinsically motivated to walk
more. That is people who sees the reward of the outcome of walking more, but
does not see enjoyment in it.
2.5.2
Behavior stages
Consolvo et al. (2009) [8] introduce The Transtheoretical Model which brieﬂy
said describes the stages a person goes through to change their behavior.
• Pre-contemplation: no intention to change behavior.
• Contemplation: considering change, but no action has taken place yet.
• Preparation: wants to take action, but has not succeeded yet.
• Action: has taken action for less than six months.
• Maintenance: has taken action consistently for more than six months.
People in the pre-contemplation stage are not relevant as they do not want
to change, their extrinsic motivation might be regulated externally. People in
the action and maintenance stages are not relevant either as they already take
action and they are not likely to need motivation or to change behavior. Thus,
people in the contemplation and preparation stages are in the target group.

2.7
Testing behavior change
This section will look at related work to gauge how one can test an application’s
eﬀect on behavior change in people.
Klasnja et al (2011) [18] take up the task of determining how technologies that
facilitate health behavior change should be evaluated. A common practice is to
test the eﬃcacy of systems, e.g. comparing participants physical activity levels
before and after an experiment. Klasnja et al argue that this should not be the
main goal and that investigating how and why behavior change occur is of more
relevance to the ﬁeld. The problem is that it is often not a feasible approach
to take. Klasnja et al realize that such experiments require many participants
over a very long period of time. Research suggests that it can take years before
behavior change is fully adopted by a person. You need multi-year studies to
truly know if your system has worked. Such a experiment is often not possible
to conduct in early stages of development of a system. Klasnja et al goes on to
say that testing the eﬃcacy of system can be a good and feasible approach if
the outcome measures a speciﬁc to method of facilitating behavior change.
“To test a novel self-monitoring intervention, then, an evaluation should assess
whether during the period when participants are actively using the system, the
rates of the target behavior increase from their baseline levels prior to the in-
tervention. In addition, it should test whether after the intervention is stopped,
the rates of behavior begin to go down again.” [18]
This approach is adopted by some of the related work, namely by Consolvo et
al. (2006) [3] as explained in section 2.4.1.
This approach will give an indication if the system is working as intended in
early stages of development.

2.8
Tracking physical activity
In previous behavior change studies that focus on physical activity levels the
participants would usually have to wear an old school pedometer and another
device for recording the step count at the end of the day [3]. Today there are
many "smarter" options on the market for tracking physical activities. A few to
mention are FitBit, Jawbone Up and Nike FuelBand. These devices sit around
your wrist as an armband and track your activity throughout the day. The
problem is that on their own these devices only provide limited information
about daily activity. You need an app on your smartphone to unlock all the
potential of the armbands.
State of the art, introduced in section 2.3, presented a few apps that aggre-
gate physical activity data tracked on a iPhone or armband and show it in
a presentable way to its users. There are many pedometer, health apps and
self-logging apps for the iOS platform. Since the iPhone 5S, iPhone’s have had
a co-processor which runs separate from the phone’s main processor. The co-
processor collects data from the phone’s sensors and analyze it to determine e.g.
how many steps the user has taken. Both raw motion data and analyzed data
is available through the iOS SDK for developers to utilize in their apps. Its
problem is that people do not have their phone on them all the time, thus some
activities during the day will not be tracked.
The Apple Watch was released in 2015 and features a number of sensors, which
like the iPhone, enables it to detect motion data. The Apple Watch is even
more precise than its big brother the iPhone because it is closer to the user and
it is worn throughout the day. People can wear it when playing sports and even
when swimming, similarly to FitBit and other armbands. Thus, it can provide
even more precise motion data than the iPhone can.
While the Apple Watch, FitBit armband or similar would be most suitable for
this experiment, these products are still fairly new on the market and have not
been adopted by users on nearly the same scale as the iPhone have. In this
project, a solution that can work on most people’s existing devices is needed to
ensure a suﬃcient amount of test participants can be found.
Furthermore, to test behavior change, physical activity levels during the test
will be compared to baseline levels. The iOS platform provides methods for
accessing historical physical activity data from the date the person started using
the phone. This data is invaluable in determining baseline data. While it will
not be possible to track all physical activity with the iPhone, for now it will be
the platform of use in this project.

deemed a too wide approach. To test more than one element will simply make

that shows users’ step counts for the current day, can facilitate behavior change.
Looking at how related work have tested health behavior change, it is clear
that three stages are needed when tested future prototypes: 1) a baseline of
how much people usually walk, 2) step data during the test and 3) step data
from after people stop using the prototype. This way the three stages can be
compared and a potential change in walking behavior can be found.

Limitations on iOS prevents one from using the same approach as Consolvo et
al. The only way to present data on the homescreen on the phone is with the
app’s icon badge number. Since ranking is the important part of the concept,
the app’s icon badge number will display the user’s current ranking among her
friends. This way the user can see her rank even without opening the app.

5.2
Implementation
There are four major aspects to the implementation of the prototype. CoreMo-
tion and HealthKit to gather pedometer data, Parse.com to upload/fetch data
from and methods for updating data while the app is not running.
5.2.1
CoreMotion
CoreMotion is an iOS SDK framework made by Apple which lets developers
access data from the iPhone’s many sensors. Since the 5s, iPhones have had
a coprocessor which runs separate from the main processor. The coprocessor
collects information from the phone’s sensors and analyze it to determine pe-
dometer information, e.g. how many steps the user has walked. The coprocessor
store data for the last 7 days. StepMate use CoreMotion to retrieve the step
count for the current day that is displayed to the users.
5.2.2
HealthKit
HealthKit was introduced with iOS 8 and the Health app for iPhone. HealthKit
is a framework for retrieving health data the phone generates from its sensors,
like pedometer data, but it also lets app’s write data to it.
For example, a
food tracking app can add calories eaten and a sleep tracker app can save sleep
patterns. HealthKit can collect health data from multiple sources and show it
in the Health app, but developers can also access all this information to show
in their apps.
The prototype needs to collect a large bulk of pedometer information about the
user to use as a baseline.
There are two parts to retrieving pedometer data from HealthKit:
1. Getting authorization from the user to access the parameters. For security
reasons Apple have chosen not to let the developer know whether the user
authorize the use of health data. If a user decides not to enable access to step
count and distance in ﬁgure 5.2 no errors will be returned in the code.
2. Their are several methods for retrieving data from HealthKit. The one most
practical for this prototype is using HKStatisticsCollectionQuery. It enables you
to collect the type of data to query, how it should be formatted, an start date
==PAGE:44==
5.2 Implementation
33
Figure 5.2: Getting user authorization to access pedometer data
and the interval. For this prototype the start date is the current date subtracted
365 days. The interval is 1 day. This returns a step count for each of the last
365 days.
Step counts are collected this way, saved in the dictionary and uploaded to the
backend.
5.2.3
Parse
Parse is an online service that provide tools to help mobile developers add a
cloud database, handle logins, send push notiﬁcations and run code in the cloud.
StepMate utilize these features of Parse.
StepMate stores three types of user data in the cloud:
1. Current activity level

2. Activity archive
3. Usage
The current activity level object contains the current step count of the user.
When the app is opened it will retrieve the current activity level object for each
user in the group.
Activity archive contains a step count for each day going 365 days back in time.
This data is used as a baseline for how much the user normally walks.
Every time a user opens StepMate the current time and user uuid is saved. This
allows for analysis of how much and when the app is used.
5.2.4
Background execution
It is important that users’ step counts are frequently updated in the app. If
users’ step counts are only uploaded to the Parse backend whenever they open
the app, step counts will be updated infrequently. In this scenario, a user can
open the app and all her friends’ step counts have not been updated in the last
nine hours. Then the information is not relevant to the user. It is important
that user data is updated frequently - it needs to update even when the user is
not using the app.
On iOS there are various ways for an app to run in the background:
• Audio: apps can run in the background as long as their playing sound.
• Navigation: apps can run in the background to get updates on a user’s
location in real time.
• Location updates:
apps can subscribe to signiﬁcant location changes.
Then the app will be able to run in the background when the OS de-
tects a signiﬁcant location change.
• Background refresh: apps can be allowed to download small amounts of
data in the background to ensure new data is available as soon as the app
is opened by the user.
• Remote notiﬁcations: Apps can be allowed to run in the background for
a limited time when receiving a push notiﬁcation.
[19]
==PAGE:46==
5.2 Implementation
35
The optimal solution is one that requires as little background time as possible.
The longer the app runs in the background, the more battery it will use. Using
a lot of battery will be an annoyance to users and can cause them to delete the
app. With this in mind using audio and location updates are disregarded as
they will make the app run in the background all the time. The app only needs
to run in the background for short, but controlled amounts of time.
5.2.4.1
Signiﬁcant location changes
Subscribing to signiﬁcant location changes could be a possible solution, but it
cannot ensure the app will be able to run frequently. Most people probably
spend most of the day in a few locations, so the app will only update a few
times a day.
5.2.4.2
Background refresh
With background refresh the app will be allowed to run a limited number of
times per day and it cannot be controlled when. This solution is not viable as
controls are too limited.
5.2.4.3
Remote notiﬁcation
When sending a remote notiﬁcation you can indicate to the app that you want it
to run code in the background. A push notiﬁcation takes data in JSON format
where it is possible to specify the message displayed to the user, which number
to set the icon badge number to or any other parameter you wan to communicate
to your app. The "content-available" parameter can be used to tell the app that
there is new content available and lets the app run in the background for a brief
period of time to download the new content. This can for example be used
by a chat app, so when a user receives a notiﬁcation with a new message, the
app can run in the background and download the message, displaying it to the
user right away when she opens the app. There is no limit to how many push
notiﬁcations you can send or how often, so it is possible to get an app to run in
the background as often as you want. The only limitation is that the app has
to be in foreground (open), background or suspended state. If the app is not
running, because the user forced closed it or the phone was restarted, it will not
be allowed to run in the background.
==PAGE:47==
5.2 Implementation
36
Normally push notiﬁcations include a message for the user, but it is possible to
send push notiﬁcations to a device without it. This way a notiﬁcation will not be
displayed on screen, in fact, the user will not even realize her device has received
a notiﬁcation, but the app is still allowed to run in the background. This way,
it is possible to execute code in the background without annoying the user with
endless push notiﬁcations, but still be allowed to run in the background often.
Jobs
Parse provides an web interface and an API to send push notiﬁcations to users’
phones. Parse lets you run code on their servers and schedule jobs to determine
when code should be executed. Three diﬀerent background jobs are setup to
each run a script that uses Parse’s API to send a push notiﬁcation to the users.
The push notiﬁcations include information which tells StepMate what to do.
The list below shows intervals at which each job executes and the actions it
informs StepMate to perform:
1. Get step count and upload to backend. (Every 15 minutes)
2. Retrieve friends’ step counts, compare data and update app icon badge
number to show current rank. (Every 15 minutes)
3. Identical to number 2, but also shows a notiﬁcation on users’ devices. (At
2pm, 6pm and 10pm)
Job 1 ensures there is always current data in the app. Job 3 shows an actual
notiﬁcation message on user devices.
The background jobs were simple scripts written in JavaScript that Parse were
instructed to run at certain intervals. The scripts would call Parse’s API and
send out a notiﬁcation to each user.
Background time
The operations performed by the app to upload and download step data are
so quick, usually less than 1 second, that the app will only run for very short
amounts of time, but it will run quite often. It is estimated that this is approach
is the most customizable, yet battery eﬃcient available.

To test if the prototype of this iteration can provide a solution to the problem

Two versions of the app:

Furthermore, this can for some participants have created a mismatch between
step data the app showed and step data used when analyzing data after the
test.

5.4
Conclusion of Second iteration
A prototype app, StepMate, were designed, implemented and tested.
On a
functional StepMate worked as intended. The app were tested by 21 participants
over the period of three weeks, but only 18 participant’s step data were used in
data analysis. The app were well used by most participants in both groups, but
towards the end, it seems participants found the app less and less interesting,
as its usage dropped considerably.
The purpose of the test were to test if StepMate can serve as a solution to the
reﬁned problem statement. Looking at the Social Group the average step count
per day did change as expected, by increasing 12.4% from the winter period of
the baseline into the test period and decreasing by almost 10% into the post
test period. But it was not possible to ﬁnd a statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence
between the winter period of the baseline and the test period for the Social
Group.
The Individual Group had the unexpected outcome of seeing an increase in steps
taken after the test ended, but not enough that there is a statistically signiﬁ-
cant diﬀerence between the three test stages. Equally, there were no signiﬁcant
diﬀerence found between the two test groups’ stages, suggesting the data from
the two groups are equal.
At this point only the visually observable diﬀerence in average step count per
day between the three stages for the Social Group can indicate that the social
pedometer app, StepMate, facilitated an behavior change in regards to partici-
pants physical activity level.
==PAGE:69==
Chapter 6
Discussion
The following is a discussion of the test conducted in the Second Iteration and
of this project as a whole.
6.1
Bias
There are several potential biases that the participants might have been sub-
jected to. All the participants knew the author of this thesis personally, which
might have made participants feel an obligation to use the app or walk more.
Participating in a study or being subjected to new technology might have biased
the participants. Around half of the participants had never used a pedometer
app before, so trying something new might have felt interesting and gotten some
participants to walk more. With a test period of two weeks it is not possible to
see if user interest would have kept up or not, but the usage statistics presented
in section 5.3.5 suggest interest were declining. A longer study will possible
have to think about keeping participants interested over a long period of time.
==PAGE:70==
6.2 Variables
59
6.2
Variables
There are many variables that can aﬀect how much a person walks on a given
day. We have seen that there is a diﬀerence between how much people walk in
the four seasons of the year. It seems reasonable to say that people walk more
when the weather is nice and less when it is bad [3]. Many other factors also
comes into play, e.g. how the person is feeling, how busy she is or if she is on
vacation or at work [18]. One test participant went on vacation during the test
and saw a huge increase in steps per day compared to previous weeks. When
these variables and many others come into play, a test period of two weeks is
simply not enough.
6.3
Testing approach
Measuring behavior change is often not feasible in HCI research because you
need multi-year studies and it can take years for behavior change to truly stick
[18]. Klasnja et al recommend such multi-year studies to focus on why behavior
change occur and not just if it does occur. This project adopted its approach of
testing StepMate’s eﬃcacy at facilitating behavior change over a short period
of time because of time constraints.
There were simply not enough time to
conduct a multi-year study. But it can be argued that to conduct a multi-year
study you need to test your system in the early stages of development.
In an ideal scenario the focus of the testing would be on why people start to
walk more and the testing period would be much longer.
6.4
Data gathering
As described in section 5.3.6.4, step data were collected using CoreMotion and
HealthKit. In future work HealthKit should be used because it ensures there
are not mismatches between the data the participants see in the app and the
data that is analyzed. Participants have possibly been motivated by how well
they were during in their group or how much they had walked. If there is a
mismatch between what they saw and what was used in data analysis, it might
lead to false conclusions being drawn.
==PAGE:71==
6.5 Types of activity
60
6.5
Types of activity
The analysis states that ideally all types of activity should be counted in the
app, but because of technical limitations of the platform, this was not possible.
This lead some participants to feel cheated as they felt some of their physical
activity were left out. If a person’s main source of exercise is running, but the
person does not have her phone on her when running, the person will miss out
on tracking that activity. This might lead to mistrust of the app and feeling
demotivated because the app says you are doing worse than you actually are.
Future prototypes should seek to incorporate more types of activity than walking
and not only using the iPhone for tracking.
6.6
Historical data
After midnight all the step counts in StepMate would reset to 0 and there
were no way to view data from previous days. Observations indicate that some
participants found this annoying as they felt a good day’s worth activity were
wasted if they forgot to check the app or if other people had not noticed. State of
the art, presented in section 2.3, shows that most pedometer apps on the market
show historical data and related work specify that people’s improvement should
be made apparent to them [8]. Historical data were omitted from StepMate
to keep the test parameters simple, but in future iterations it seems to be an
element to keep in mind. One way to improve on this in future iterations could
be to include a weekly leaderboard like the FitBit apps does.
6.7
Missing data
The iPhone stores physical activity data from the day its owner started to use
the phone. As described in section 5.3.7.1, participants had varying amounts of
data because some had had their iPhone for longer than others. This creates a
problem when establishing baseline levels because the oldest data in the dataset
are based on fewer participants than the newest data. In future iterations this
should be taken into account when ﬁnding test participants to make sure all
participants have suﬃcient baseline data.
==PAGE:72==
6.8 Individual version
61
6.8
Individual version
Usage data indicates that the participants in the Individual Group did not ﬁnd
the app to be very interesting. Their use of it was limited, but to be fair, the
app for the Individual Group, was very simple. The goal of it were to simply
provide participants with their current step count to see if just having that made
them walk more. The version might have been too simple and discouraged the
participants to use it.
6.9
Tracking physical activity
Means of tracking physical activity were discussed in section 2.8. The iPhone
were chosen as the platform to use because of its wide adoption among con-
sumers, making it easy to ﬁnd test participants. As described there are alter-
natives to the iPhone that will ensure a higher quality of measurement such as
smartwatches and armbands. The problem is establishing a baseline is more
diﬃcult because it requires that test participants have had such a device for a
while. If this step is overcome or a baseline is not needed then these are strong
alternatives to the iPhone.
Smartwatches enable a diﬀerent and more subtle interaction than the iPhone
can.
E.g.
the Apple Watch is very much about providing notiﬁcations and
glanceable information to the user. The Apple Watch can provide tactic feed-
back that feels like a small touch on the user’s wrist. This subtle way of notifying
a user can be used well for priming a user to reﬂect on the current day’s physical
activity level. Furthermore, the watch’s UI makes it possible to display much of
the same data as StepMate currently does. The data will of course be presented
in a smaller and less rich way, but glanceable which makes it quick and easy to
look at. There are many other factors to consider before chosen a platform, but
in future iterations it could be interesting to look more into the Apple Watch
or other smartwatches or armbands.
6.10
Communication
Consolvo et al (2009) [8] argue that users should be in control of their data
because some people ﬁnd it uncomfortable to share activity levels on days they
have not been very active. Numbers do not tell the whole story. There can be
many reasons to why a person’s step count is what it is on a given day, but
==PAGE:73==
6.11 Motivation
62
StepMate did not oﬀer any explanation to the users or let the users commu-
nicate with each other. Communication between users in a social pedometer
app is important for users to feel they get the credit they deserve [3]. It seems
reasonable to think about this in future iterations to ensure participants feel
comfortable to share private data.
6.11
Motivation
In section 2.5 the target group of the project is said to be people who are
extrinsically motivated to walk more. Participants were not chosen because of
their motivations and were not asked about their motivations to walk more in
the questionnaire. People were asked to chose one among ﬁve questions about
their intentions to improve their ﬁtness level, which can be related to their
motivation, but no direct questions about motivation were asked.
In future
testing this should be of higher priority when selecting participants.
6.12
Autonomy
As described in the Analysis chapter, autonomy is a important key to creating
motivation in people, but it has not been in focus when designing StepMate.
The test participants were told to use StepMate as much or little as them wanted
to, so at no time were people forced or told to use the app. The only pressure
people might have felt is social pressure from their friends to walk more in order
for them not to lose and therefore also to use the app. Thus, while it have
not been investigated, it is argued that StepMate did not remove participants’
autonomy towards the app or walking more. Participants were encouraged and
nudged to use the app and walk more, but never in a forceful way.
==PAGE:74==
Chapter 7
Conclusion
The project started out looking into how people can be motivated to walk more
using a pedometer app. An analysis presented the ﬁeld of motivation, state of
the art of pedometer apps and how related work tackle the problem. A decision
to focus on measuring the eﬃcacy of behavior change facilitated by a social
pedometer app were taken.
Over two iterations the app, named StepMate,
were designed, implemented and ﬁnally tested on 21 participants, where 18
participants’ data were used in data analysis.
Data for 372 days for each participant were collected, making it possible to
get a good overview of participants’ normal walking behavior. Related work
had suggested that how much people walk on a given day can be inﬂuenced by
many factors, such as weather, sickness or busyness. Data show that there is a
correlation between the season of the year and how much people walk. People
walk the most in the summer and the least in the winter. Comparing the test
period’s data with the full baseline data would be unreasonable because the test
took place during the winter where people walk the least. Instead data from
the test period were compared to the winter period of the baseline.
No statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence were found between the winter period of
the baseline and the test period for either of the test groups. Neither were a sig-
niﬁcant diﬀerence found between the two test groups’ datasets, which indicates
that adding a social aspect had no more eﬀect than simply showing people how
==PAGE:75==
64
many steps they had taken.
However, looking at the average step counts per day for the Social Group, the
expected pattern of an increase in average step count from the winter baseline
into the test period and a decrease into the post test period is observable.
A similar pattern is seen for the Individual Group where average step count
increases from the winter baseline into the test period, but then keeps increasing
into the post test period. It seems that the highly subjective nature of step
counts makes it diﬃcult to conclude on it unless a very large data set is obtained.
Thus, with the test approach used, this project is not able to conclude that
the social pedometer app StepMate supported a behavior change in everyday
physical activity levels compared to baseline levels.
Finally, it is argued that to be able to conclude on diﬀerences between periods of
step counts, the periods have to be very large in size to make up for the natural
variance in how much people walk on a given day.
==PAGE:76==
Chapter 8
Future work
8.1
Test approach
As said in the Conclusion of this project, the natural variance of how much
people walk each day makes it diﬃcult to conclude on diﬀerence between time
periods unless those time periods are of a certain size. The two week test period
used in this project seems to have been too short. Related work presented in
the Analysis also suggest that getting behavior change to stick can take years.
In future work a longer test period should be used to make it easier to compare
the test period to the baseline period. The season of the year where the test
takes place should be taken into consideration as well. For example, a test of
three months in length could be from March to the end of May. This way, the
test period can be compared to the baseline data for the previous Spring season.
Furthermore, related work presented in the Analysis, suggest that the focus of
a behavior change study should not be the eﬃcacy of the system, but rather
why it succeeds or fails. With the test of the Second iteration as a preliminary
study that yielded many useful insights, perhaps this approach can be adopted
in the future.
