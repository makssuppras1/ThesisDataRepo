Abstract 
This thesis addresses the subjective perception of flutter echo and 
colouration in small ordinary rooms, such as offices, meeting rooms 
and classrooms. The goal was first of all to investigate the correlation 
between the subjective perception of flutter echo and a series of 
objective parameters, and then to develop a model for predicting the 
risk of flutter echo prevalence in small rooms, from these correlations. 
Secondly, the goal was to examine the degree of absorption needed to 
reduce or completely eliminate flutter echo in small rooms. From the 
findings it was clear that the perception of flutter echo and especially 
colouration proved to be a rather subjective matter, and four 
statistical analysis techniques were therefore used to express the 
subjective parameters in the subsequent correlation- and a linear 
regression analysis. From the correlation analysis the room volume 
was proved to have the best correlation with the subjective 
parameters, and was therefore also included as the main variable in 
the multiple linear regression analysis. From the linear regression 
analysis, one prediction model were computed for each of the four 
subjective parameters, and they proved to be quite sufficient in terms 
of predicting the prevalence of flutter echo in small rooms, especially 
compared to the sample size of 15 rooms. Finally, it was found to be 
quite difficult to establish the degree of absorption needed to 
completely eliminate flutter echo in small rooms. This was mainly due 
to the fact that the perception of flutter echo as mentioned, is very 
subjective.

Resumé 
Denne afhandling omhandler den subjektive opfattelse af flutter ekko 
og klangfarvning i små almindelige rum, såsom kontorer, møderum og 
undervisningsrum. Målet var dels at undersøge korrelationen mellem 
den subjektive opfattelse af flutter ekko og en række objektive 
parametre, for derefter at udvikle en model til at forudsige 
forekomsten af flutter ekko i små rum, ud fra disse korrelationer. 
Dernæst var målet at undersøge hvilken mængde af absorption, der er 
nødvendig, i forhold til at reducere eller helt fjerne flutter ekko i små 
rum. Ud fra resultaterne stod det klart, at opfattelsen af flutter ekko 
og især klangfarvning viste sig at være en ganske subjektiv sag, og 
derfor blev fire statistiske analyseteknikker anvendt til at udtrykke de 
subjektive parametre i de efterfølgende korrelations- og lineær 
regressionsanalyser. Fra korrelations analysen viste det sig, at rum 
volumen havde den bedste korrelation med de subjektive parametre, 
og indgik derfor også som den primære variable i den multiple lineære 
regressionsanalyse. Ud fra den lineære regressionsanalyse blev der 
udviklet en prædiktiv model for hver af de fire subjektive parametre, 
og de viste sig alle at være helt tilstrækkelige i forhold til at forudsige 
forekomsten af flutter ekko i små rum, især når der sammenlignes 
med stikprøvestørrelsen på 15 rum. Til sidst viste det sig at være ret 
vanskeligt at fastslå den nødvendige mængde af absorption der skal til 
for helt at fjerne flutter ekko i små rum. Dette skyldes primært, at 
opfattelsen af flutter ekko som nævnt, er meget subjektiv.

Introduction  
Humans spend up to 90 percent of their time indoor, which makes it 
very essential that the indoor environment is as good as it can possibly 
be.  Acoustics is an important part of the indoor environment, and can 
have a huge influence on the comfort of the people present in a room. 
When it comes to our daily life, the acoustic design in a room should 
support the human activities that takes place in them and the people 
performing them. Unfortunately, this is not always the case. Many 
buildings contain rooms that work against us, and influences our 
comfort by for example giving us headaches, making us drowsy and 
unable to concentrate. One of the most important activities that takes 
place in these rooms is communication, which is one of the most vital 
tools humans have. It connects us to our clients, our coworkers, our 
teachers, our students  and so on. One phenomena that can have a 
huge influence on the sound field within a room, and is an unwanted 
effect  in rooms for speech, is flutter echo and colouration, which will 
be addressed in this thesis. In the following, the motivation behind this 
thesis will be described together with the vision and different goals for 
the project. 
1.1  Motivation 
The inspiration to this thesis is derived from the fact that the more 
simple and ordinary rooms have a less straightforward sound 
propagation than first anticipated. The simplicity makes the smaller 
rooms very sensitive to flutter echo and colouration in particular, and 
==PAGE:13==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 2  
 
especially shoe-box rooms are vulnerable, which is mostly due to the 
parallel walls that occur in these rooms. In addition to having a 
significant influence on the sound perception in a room, flutter echo 
and colouration in particular can also be quite difficult to detect 
objectively. Especially the direction can be tricky to determine, even 
from a subjective point of view. 
Considering the logistically obvious advantages that shoe-box rooms 
provide, it is no wonder they are so common.  However, the design 
traditions has changed considerably throughout the recent years, 
which has led to more simple design solutions, and thereby the risk of 
flutter echo and colouration has increased. Especially widely used glass 
walls, stripped rigid walls and less and more stylish furniture has 
increased this risk. 
The fact that flutter echo and colouration is very common in the small 
simple rooms, which are the type of rooms people spend most of their 
time in, makes it quite important to develop a solution that can 
provide information on how to either minimize or completely remove 
the flutter echoes, or maybe even establish a model that can predict 
which objective factors make some rooms more sensitive to flutter 
echo and colouration. Of course absorbing and/or scattering on walls 
is a simple and relatively easy way to eliminate it, but since it can be 
difficult to establish which walls the problem is caused by, it would be 
necessary to treat half of the room with either absorbing or scattering 
materials , which is unnecessary expenses to use. Therefore, it could 
be beneficial to establish how much absorption that is needed in order 
to eliminate the flutter echo or at least minimize it to a level that is no 
longer audible. 
1.2  Vision and Goals 
The vision of this thesis is first of all to investigate the connection 
between subjective perception of flutter echo/colouration and 
objective measurements in small rooms. Second of all, it is desired to 
establish what measures are needed, in terms of absorption, to reduce 
or eliminate flutter echo. 
The first goal is partly to establish the degree of flutter 
echo/colouration within a series of rooms from listening tests with a 
test panel, and secondly to investigate if these subjective results can 
be supported by objective measurements. For this, a correlation 
analysis will be conducted where different subjective and objective 
parameters will be included. In relation to this, the different objective 
measures that can be obtained from a B-format impulse response, 
which contains one Omni-directional signal and three figure-of-eight 
signals in the X-, Y- and Z-direction, will also be included in the analysis. 
The advantage of including the data from the B-format impulse 
response in the analysis is that it will contribute with not only the 
magnitude and time arrival of a reflective sound wave but also 
directional information, which is not obtained from a regular Omni-
directional microphone.  
The second goal is to perform a linear regression analysis, and thereby 
compute a model that ideally can be used to predict the prevalence of 
flutter echo/colouration in small rooms from objective acoustic 
parameters.

The third goal is to establish the degree of absorption that is needed to 
reduce or remove flutter echo in small rooms. The concept is to treat

perception of flutter echo. In addition to this, it will be investigated if

1.2.1  Small Rooms 
Since people typically spend their time in smaller ordinary rooms like 
offices, meeting rooms and teaching rooms, these types of rooms are 
considered to be of great interest, and will therefore be addressed in 
this thesis. These three room types will at the same time ensure 
variety amongst the investigated rooms, since they typically have 
different needs when it comes to room size and furnishings, and

Theoretical Background 
In this chapter some of the knowledge that have been gained and 
utilized throughout the project will be presented. The chapter will go 
through the theory of flutter echo and colouration, spatial hearing, 
signal processing, and in the end describe the most essential parts of 
the IRIS system.  
 
2.1  Flutter Echoes and Colouration 
This section will give a brief summary of the definition and theory of 
flutter echoes from various researchers' point of view, including the 
work by Jens Bo Frederiksen, who investigated different aspects of 
audibility and propagation of flutter echoes. Even though flutter echo 
is a familiar issue today, especially in shoe-box rooms, it is still a topic 
that needs some attention.  
As described by Løvstad (2003), several contributions have been made 
to clarify different aspects of our perception and nuisance to echoes, 
over a period of numerous years. In his thesis, he found that  Dietsch 
and Kraak's echo criterion from 1986 did not reflect the subjective 
perception of echo retrieved from listening tests. Therefore, listening 
tests are considered to be very suitable in terms of verifying objective 
measures, in relation to the perception of flutter echoes.  
 
==PAGE:17==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 6  
 
According to Morfey (2001) flutter echo differs from the ordinary 
reverberation in a room and can be describes as: "a succession of 
echoes that arrive at equal or nearly equal spacing in time, when an 
acoustic space is exited by an impulsive sound source." Furthermore, 
he states that a flutter echo with a time delay of 10-20 ms between 
each individual reflection has a characteristic audible 'twang' - which 
could be perceived as colouration. 
Rindel (2016) describes that colouration can occur in small rooms, and 
is an unwanted effect for both speech and music. He stresses that, 
although colouration is quite easy to hear it is more difficult to 
measure objectively. He further describes that colouration can occur 
when a single sound reflection interferes with the direct sound or by 
multiple reflections between parallel walls, like a flutter echo. If the 
time delay of the reflection is Δt, the frequencies fn=n/Δt are amplified, 
while other frequencies in between are attenuated. This effect can be 
quite disturbing and is also called a comb filter. 
"While flutter echoes are audible in the time domain as a 
series of repeated reflections, a flutter echo with sufficiently 
short periodic time delay is heard in the frequency domain 
as a colouration of the sound" (Rindel, 2016). 
However, since the discrimination of coloration and flutter echo may 
be very tricky, this will not be addressed in this thesis. 
From Halmrast's (2015) point of view, the characteristic sound of a 
flutter echo is caused by a gradual filtering towards the "tail" of the 
flutter at 1-2 kHz. He further describes that the flutter echo's "tail" is 
not a result of the time between the repetitive reflections nor the 
room resonance, but instead of a gradual band-pass filtering of an 
impulsive signal. The filtering is a combination of two filtering effects. 
The lower frequencies are dampened due to the increased source 
distance and the diffraction, and the very high frequencies are 
dampened due to air absorption. When the lower frequencies are 
dampened, spherical waves are transformed to plane waves, and since 
the sound pressure level of a plane wave is only reduced by the 
absorption from the air and surfaces, the high frequencies will be more 
dominating and last longer. By comparison, a spherical wave is 
reduced by 6 dB per doubling of distance. 
2.1.1  Previous Work 
In 1985, Jens Bo Frederiksen wrote his thesis 'Flutter-Echoes', related 
to both physical/experimentally and psychoacoustic aspects. He 
performed a series of model experiments to find the importance of 
angled, diffuse, absorbent and curved walls, and furthermore he 
investigated the hearing threshold and annoyance of flutter echoes. 
He describes, that flutter echo often appears in rooms where at least 
two walls are parallel and plane, but also mentions that all situations 
where a sound wave passes the starting point with the same direction, 
after being reflected several times, has the potential for flutter. 
Furthermore, he uses three parameters to describe flutter: the 
reverberation time of the flutter (Tfl), which is a measure for the time it 
takes for the flutter intensity to drop 60 dB, the flutter's time period 
and the flutter's temporal structure within a time period. In his work, 
Jens defines flutter as an audible series of periodic repeated sound 
==PAGE:18==
Chapter 2 - Theoretical Background 
Page | 7 
 
reflections, and in psychoacoustic context he uses the term "flutter 
echo" for flutter with a time period above 50 ms.   
Regarding the parallel surfaces, his conclusions were that for distances 
between surfaces that was higher or equal to the surface size, the 
equation developed by Krauth, von E. and R. Büchlein could be used as 
a good estimation of Tfl's maximum value. However, for distances 
between surfaces that was lower than the surface size, Tfl was much 
higher than the equation anticipated.  
Additionally, he concluded that angled surfaces had a huge affect on 
Tfl, in fact a single degree angulation of the surface could result in half 
the reverberation time of the flutter.  Regarding the curved walls, he 
concluded that for convex surfaces, Tfl could be significantly reduced 
regardless of the distance between the walls. However, for the 
concave surfaces the distance between the walls should be more than 
the radius of the curvature, in order to reduce Tfl. Lastly, he concluded 
that diffuse walls had a great influence on Tfl, and at the same time it 
was proven that it is the smallest surface that determines Tfl. 
For the subjective listening tests he concluded that there were some 
significant standard deviations between the test persons, and so the 
sensitivity to flutter echo is very individual. However, it also turned out 
that the deviation for each person was significantly lower. 
Furthermore, he found that the flutter echo is not audible if the 
reverberation time of the room without flutter is higher than the 
reverberation time of the room with flutter, and if none of the flutter's 
single reflections are audible. 
In the end of his thesis, he concluded that there are great uncertainties 
connected with the psychoacoustic assessment of flutter echoes, and 
clarifies that more accurate knowledge could be drawn if flutter echo 
is studied where they naturally occur. Opposite his approach, where 
he generated a flutter echo to a recorded signal of a room.

2.2  Spatial Hearing 
This section will include some of the basic aspects regarding the 
perception of hearing, in order to be able to understand how sound is 
perceived for a potential listener. The section will describe the ear 
anatomy and the importance of binaural hearing related to localization 
of sounds and audio playback. 
2.2.1  Ear anatomy 
When a sound wave reaches a person's ear,  the outer part of the ear 
called the pinna causes filtering of the incoming sound, which helps to 
determine the location of the sound. The sound then enters the ear 
canal through the concha, as it can be seen on figure 2, before it 
reaches the eardrum. The ear canal is about 2.5 cm long and has 
resonant properties, which makes it act like a broadly tuned band-pass 
filter, and therefore humans are more sensitive to frequencies in the 
range between 1 kHz and 6 kHz. From the eardrum the sound passes 
to the middle ear and further on to the inner ear (Plack, 2005).  
Since humans are more sensitive to frequencies within 1 kHz to 6 kHz, 
it is understandable that we can clearly perceive flutter echoes, which 
typically end up around 1-2 kHz, as described previously in this 
chapter. 
2.2.2  Binaural Hearing 
Humans are able to localize one or more sound sources in a space due 
to binaural hearing. The term refers to hearing with two ears, 
compared to hearing with only one ear - monaural. A sound signal 
arrives at the two ears with differences in time and amplitude, which is 
referred to as interaural time difference (ITD) and interaural level 
difference (ILD), respectively. 
As described by Plack (2005), the time difference from one ear to the 
other depends on the path length, hence the distance the sound will 
have to travel as it bends around a person's head. For lower 
frequencies, the interaural time difference is the most important cue 
to sound location. On the other hand, level differences are not 
efficient when locating sound signals that consist of lower frequencies. 
This is due to the fact that the wavelength of the sound is long relative 
to the diameter of a person's head, and therefore the sound will  bend  
around  the head (diffract) and result  in a very  small  level  difference

Artificial Heads and Headphones 
The best way to introduce recorded binaural signal to a listener is by 
headphones. The reason for this is that the sound to each ear can be 
presented individually, opposite to loudspeakers where both ears will 
receive sound from each loudspeaker. However, binaural signal can be 
perceived as internal within the listeners head, instead of external, 
because the sound is not modified by the human head and torso. A 
way to prevent this is to use an artificial head simulator with two 
microphones at the ear canals, since it will contribute with a realistic 
amount of diffraction from torso, head and pinna, to the recordings 
(Plack, 2005). The head and shoulders will contribute to distortion in 
the mid-frequencies, while the pinna will contribute to distortion at 
higher frequencies. However, it should be noted that the diffraction is 
different from each individual person, which could be quite different 
from the one for an artificial head, and therefore the use of an artificial 
head may lead to some front-back confusion and uncertainties in 
localization in the median plane for some persons. Furthermore, the 
microphones should be positioned in the beginning of the ear canal of 
the artificial head, in order to avoid that the sound path between the 
headphones and the eardrum is included twice (Vorländer, 2008). 
2.3  Signal Processing 
This section will describe the signal processing technique used for this 
thesis, which consists of the post-processing of the recorded binaural 
signals. 
2.3.1  Root Mean Square Normalization 
In general, normalization of audio is a way to change the overall 
volume to reach a certain target value. There are many different types 
of normalization, which all have different applications. The benefits of 
using normalization is that it does not affect dynamics and ideally does 
not change the sound in any other way than simply changing its 
volume, which for example is the down side of Compression, where 
the volume changes over time in varying amounts.  
In this thesis, the Root Mean Square (rms) normalization is used 
because of its ability to create a more natural result across varying 
sound samples, due to its capability to simulate how the human ear 
works. A sound sample can contain both large peaks and softer 
sections, so when a rms normalization is applied it is the average of the 
sound sample that will be adjusted to a certain loudness level. This 
type of normalization is also called Average Loudness Normalization 
and can be used to obtain an equal average loudness level for multiple 
sound samples. The reason for normalizing the sound samples to the 
same average loudness level is to create a basis from which the sound 
samples will be evaluated equally, for example during a listening test, 
which is the case in this thesis (Learn Digital Audio). 
==PAGE:21==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 10  
 
Even though the rms normalization is one of the best methods in 
terms of depicting the human ear, there are still some challenges 
when using this type of normalization, especially when it comes to 
flutter echo and colouration. The problem is that the human hearing 
perceives different frequencies at different volumes, as it can be seen 
on figure 3.  Especially at higher frequencies, human hearing is very 
sensitive, as described previously, which means that we hear 
frequencies in the range between 1-6 kHz as louder (Learn Digital 
Audio). This is a problem because flutter echo and colouration typically 
end up around 1-2 kHz, which means that if there is a slight difference 
in the loudness it could be perceived considerably higher at these 
frequencies. Therefore, an A-weighting should be applied to the sound 
samples before the rms normalization is conducted. However, the final 
sound samples should not contain the A-weighting, because our hears 
will apply the A-weighting themselves and thereby the weighting 
would be applied twice, which is not preferred. Furthermore, when 
rms normalization is applied to a binaural impulse response, it is 
important that it is the average of the right and left channel that is set 
to a certain rms level for all binaural impulse responses, so that a 
possible level difference between the channels is preserved.  
Even though the A-weighting and rms normalization is applied to the 
impulse responses to equalize the loudness, some challenges may still 
occur. One of them could be that the length of the impulse response 
for each room differs a little, which could result in a slight difference in 
the loudness. The used Matlab script for the rms normalization and A-
weighting of the measured impulse responses can be seen in Appendix 
1.

2.4  The IRIS System 
This system is a new measuring system that provides information 
about the direction of reflected sound waves, in addition to the 
magnitude and time arrival.  The systems visualizes the 3D impulse 
response in the form of a "hedgehog pattern" as seen on figure 4, 
where the spikes or vectors represent the energy arriving at the 
receiver position. For each vector the length, angle and color indicates 
the level, direction and time of arrival for the incoming sound, 
respectively. The direct sound is by default assigned to a level of 0 dB, 
and all subsequent reflected energy is relative to this. The system uses 
some specific measuring equipment and signal processing which will 
be briefly explained in the following sub-sections.  
2.4.1  TetraMic 
The IRIS measuring system uses a Core Sound TetraMic, which is a 
relatively inexpensive microphone that can provide 3D room impulse 
responses. According to D. Protheroe (2013), who performed 
experiments with the system in an anechoic chamber, the accuracy of 
the directional estimation of the system is in the region of ± 7.5°. The 
microphone consists of four 12 mm capsules mounted 90 degrees 
apart, which defines the faces of a tetrahedron, as it can be seen on 
figure 5. The recorded signal from these four capsules are so-called A-
format, which is not usable as it is, but can be converted into a four 
channel B-format signal, by Core Sounds processing  software. The  
advantage of  using this processing from A-format to B-format is that 
even though the capsules are separated by about 3.3 cm, the signals 
are  phase-coherent,  as  if  they  occupied  the  same  point  in  space  
 
Figure 4: IRIS plot for speech, illustrating the incoming reflection in relation to the 
direct sound (red vector). 
(ProSoundWeb). So by using this method it is possible to achieve 
precisely coherent signals.  
As described by Craven et al. (1977), the input signals A, B, C and D, 
from the four microphone capsules have a maximum response of 
direction of left-back-down, left-front-up, right-front-down and right- 
back-down, respectively. These four signals are converted to four 
output signals E, F, G and H, where F is a zero order harmonic signal 
and thus an Omni-directional signal, which is equivalent to what would 
be produced by a microphone with a spherical directional response 
curve. The remaining three signals E, G and H are first order spherical 
harmonics, which correspond to signals that would be produced by 
microphones having a figure-of-eight directional response  curves.  For  
these three signals,  the direction of maximum sensitivity is front-back,

left-right and up-down. The spherical harmonics are illustrated in 
figure 6. In order to make sure that the relative frequency  and phase 
response is the same in all directions, to which the corresponding 
microphone responds, it is necessary to equalize the signals E, F, G and 
H. Craven explains that all spherical harmonics of the same order have 
the same frequency and phase response, and therefore one  
equalization characteristic is applied to the zero order signal F and 
another to the first order signals E, G and H. The signal processing

this thesis, instead the reader is referred to the paper by Craven et al.

exported into a wave file with four channels, of which the first one is 
the Omni-directional signal and the second, third and fourth are the 
figure-of-eight signals in the X- (front-back), Y- (left-right) and Z-
direction (up-down), respectively.

Methodology 
In this chapter the basic methodologies used in this thesis will be 
elaborated. The methods will be described in following order; 
measurements, parametric analysis, listening tests, statistical analysis 
and Cepstrum technique. In each section, the different considerations 
that has been addressed is elaborated and reasoned. 
 
3.1  Measurements 
Related to the subjective and objective detection of flutter echo, two 
different measuring approaches were used. First, the DIRAC system 
was used to record and de-convolve the excitation signal into a 
binaural room impulse response, which then could be used to evaluate 
the presence of flutter echo or colouration in a series of rooms, from a 
subjective point of view, hence from listening tests, which will be 
explained later in this chapter. Secondly, the IRIS system was used to 
measure a 3D impulse response which then could be used to identify 
the reflecting surfaces in the room, and perhaps make it possible to 
detect/predict flutter echoes objectively. In the following, the 
measuring procedures and equipment for the two systems will be 
described. 
3.1.1  Measuring Procedure and Equipment 
In general the two measuring systems contain different equipment and 
measuring procedures, however some details of the measurement

procedure are the same for both systems. For example, the same 
source and receiver positions were used, together with the same

DIRAC 
The Dirac system was used to measure and generate the binaural 
impulse response and e-sweep was chosen as the excitation signal, 
with a sweep length of 10.9 seconds and pre-average of 6-8. The 
reason for using such a long sweep length, was that at first, some 
distortion occurred in the last part of the impulse response, which 
then could be excluded by using a very long sweep length. The 
measurements were carried out in accordance with the descriptions in 
Acoustics Engineering (2007). The quality of the measured impulse 
response can to some extent be established by checking the Impulse 
response-to-Noise Ratio (IRN), which should be at least 35 dB for T20 
and 45 dB for T30, according to ISO 3382. On the other hand, it can be 
difficult to reach these IRN levels for the low octave bands, because of 
the loudspeakers' inefficiency at lower frequencies, and therefore it is 
also a good idea to listen to the impulse response, to check its quality. 
The used equipment included a Brüel & Kjær Head and Torso 
Simulator (HATS) type 4128 with pinnae, a Brüel & Kjær Nexus 
Microphone Conditioner type 2690, an amplifier, a computer with the 
DIRAC program and an Omni-directional dodecahedron loudspeaker. 
As described previously, the microphones should be placed in the 
beginning of the ear canal of the artificial head, in order to avoid that 
the sound path in the ear canal is included twice. The equipment setup 
can be seen in Appendix 5. 
IRIS 
The IRIS system was used to generate a 3D impulse response. The 
stimulus settings contain the Sweep Time and Impulse Response Time. 
This system also uses e-sweep as excitation signal, but only with one 
repetition. Therefore, the signal-to-noise ratio can only be improved 
by increasing the sweep time or the sound power of the source. For 
smaller rooms the system recommends a sweep time of 10 seconds. 
The impulse response time should be sufficiently longer than the 
rooms' decay time, in this case a time of 5 seconds was considered to 
be sufficient. In general, the recommendation described in the IRIS 
manual was followed carefully during the measurements. The quality 
of the measurements can be checked by inspecting the decay range in 
each octave band, which should be at least 45 dB. However, for this 
system it can also be difficult to meet this requirement, because of the 
loudspeakers' inefficiency at low frequencies. Therefore, it is also 
recommended to listen to the impulse response for these 
measurements. In addition, the onset time, which is the start time of 
the impulse response, also needs to be checked. Normally, the system 
can detect the onset time automatically, but if there is some excessive 
crosstalk in the measurement hardware the system could be tricked to 
choose an earlier time.

3.2  Parametric Analysis 
In order to examine how different degrees of vertical absorption will 
influence the perception/detection of flutter echo in small rooms, a 
parametric analysis was conducted for two of the investigated rooms 
that contained flutter echo. During this analysis, it was essential that 
the parametric changes that was conducted on the rooms were equal, 
in order to be able to compare the results. Svensson and Nilsson 
(2016) describes that the need for vertical absorbers is essential in 
terms of eliminating horizontal sound propagation and flutter echo. 
They discuss the effect of horizontal sound waves and refers to it as 
grazing waves. Grazing sound waves are not eliminated by ceiling 
absorbers since they hit the ceiling with a very grazing angle of 
incidence, and thereby a sound field where the sound waves 
predominately travels horizontally, is created. 
The absorption panels used throughout this analysis are so-called 
"reference absorbers", which consist of 100 mm glass wool Industry 
Modus from Ecophon, framed with wooded frames. The absorption 
coefficient, from a round robin test, for the used absorption panels can 
be seen in figure 91. The figure indicates that the absorption coefficient 
for the panels are quite good in the frequency range from 250 Hz to 
4000 Hz, which is considered to be very practical for reducing the 
flutter echo, which typically ends up around 1 kHz to 2 kHz, as 
explained previously. In figure 10, the practical sound absorption 
coefficients for the Ecophon Industry Modes System can be seen. It is

3.3  Listening Tests 
When conducting listening tests there are different considerations that 
needs to be dealt with, and therefore the guidelines described in 
HeadAcoustics and Nordtest were followed closely.  In this section, the 
purpose of the listening test will be described, together with the most 
important aspects of conducting a listening test, the chosen test types 
and the evaluation of the results.  
3.3.1  Purpose of Listening Tests 
In this thesis, the main purpose of the listening test was to investigate 
the subjective perception of flutter echo and colouration in a series of 
rooms from measured binaural impulse responses. The test was partly 
used to establish a foundation from which objective results could be 
evaluated against, but was also used to investigate how much 
absorption is needed to reduce and/or remove flutter echo and 
colouration in small rooms. 
3.3.2  Conducting Listening Tests 
One of the main challenges when conducting listening tests is to avoid 
bias, since it can have a significant influence on the result. However, 
careful planning of the test can minimize the risk of unwanted bias. For 
psychoacoustic listening tests unwanted bias is associated with non-
acoustic variance, such as visual or tactile cues. In this case, the 
listening tests were conducted in the Psychophysics lab booths at DTU, 
which ensures sufficient ventilation that can guarantee a comfortable 
temperature level and good air quality so that the test persons feel 
comfortable.  
Depending on the test type, various bias effects can occur, which will 
be elaborated for the chosen test types in Sub-sections 3.3.4. Some 
bias affects can be avoided by randomizing the sound samples, so that 
no test person is presented to the samples in the same order as 
another test person (Nordtest). Additionally, some or all of the sound 
samples should be presented more than once to establish the 
consistency of the test persons' answers. Ideally, all sound samples 
should be presented twice, but if this will conflict with the duration of 
the listening test, it would be useful to present at least some sound 
samples several times to make a so called rudimentary reliability check 
(Head Acoustics). 
Listening tests can be conducted for groups or individuals. In this case 
an individual listening test was chosen for several reasons. First of all, 
it makes it possible to present the sound samples in different orders 
for each test person, which can minimize bias, as described previously. 
Secondly, it makes it possible to use individual control method, which 
allows the test person to control the playback themselves, and thus 
control when the sound is played and how many times it is played. This 
is very beneficial for very short sound samples, like impulse responses, 
especially if the person is distracted at first. Thirdly, it prevents the 
influence and interference between test persons, which could 
influence the results significantly. Even though there are many benefits 
of using individual listening, it is more time consuming than a group 
test (Head Acoustics). 
The test persons' judgment can be written down on prepared forms, or 
it  can  be  entered  directly  into a computer  using  an  on-screen  user  
==PAGE:34==
Chapter 3 - Methodology 
 
Page | 23 
 
interface, of which the latter has the advantage of avoiding typing 
errors (Head Acoustics). In this case, an user interface was used to 
present the sound samples in a randomized order and to gather the 
results from the tests. The script for the user interface can be seen in 
Appendix 7. 
The sound samples can be played back by loudspeaker or headphones. 
In order to make sure that each test person hears the same calibrated 
signal, headphones are used in this case. Additionally, binaural 
recordings presented by headphones may give the best approximation 
to the live situation, because of the spatial sound impression obtained 
by using an artificial head recorder. Nevertheless, some inexperienced 
test persons may have difficulties when listening to recorded binaural 
impulse responses via headphones. This could occur if the 
characteristics of the recorded room differs significantly from the 
playback room, which  creates an inconsistency between the visual 
and acoustic impression. In such cases, the test leader could ask the 
test person to close their eyes and imagine the room the sound was 
recorded in (Head Acoustics). During playback of the sound samples 
the sound pressure level should be comfortable, especially in this case, 
because of the dynamics of the impulse responses. If the playback is 
either too loud or too quiet, the test persons could feel overexerted 
and therefore lose concentration. In this case a sound pressure level of 
around 56-57 dB was used for the playbacks, which was measured for 
Sennheiser HDA 200 headphones, using a IEC 60318 coupler. In 
relation to this, all sound samples had been normalized to a rms level 
of -50 dB in order to have approximately the same loudness level, as 
described previously. 
A total number of eight test persons was chosen to perform the 
listening test.  A majority of the chosen test persons were young, 
because they are more sensitive to high frequency sounds, which is 
relevant since flutter echo/colouration typically ends up around 1kHz-
2kHz. The ages range from 22 years to 46 years with a median 26, and 
an equal amount of men and women have participated. 
3.3.3  Test Procedure 
Before the listening test, the test persons must be instructed 
sufficiently so they know exactly what the listening test consists of. In 
this case, the test person were given a written description of the test, 
and in addition a short oral presentation of the most essential parts of 
the test were given before the start of the test. The written description 
contained information about the purpose of the test, what flutter echo 
is, what the test person would listen to and the two used test types. It 
is important that the instructions for the test includes all the necessary 
information and explanations there is to know, because it is essential 
that the test persons feel that they have fully understood the test in 
order to obtain reliable results (HeadAcoustics). The full description for 
the listening test can be seen in Appendix 8. The oral presentation was 
used to clarify what the test persons should focus on when listening to 
the measured impulse responses. For example, it was explained that 
the sound samples for the different impulse responses would sound 
quite different, depending on the room size and so on, which should 
not be judged in the test. On the contrary, the test persons were asked 
to focus on the very last part of the sound sample, and only judge this 
part in relation to the flutter echo/colouration. 
==PAGE:35==

Page | 24  
 
After the briefing of the listening test task, a training session was 
conducted, so that the test persons could get a better understanding 
of what flutter echo and colouration sounds like and get familiar with 
the test procedure. The training started by presenting the sound 
samples with most extreme characteristics, which in this case was 
chosen to be a room without flutter echo/colouration, a room with 
strong flutter echo, a room with strong colouration and a room with a 
long reverberation time. In that way, the test persons are made aware 
of what to expect during the test. All the examples were chosen 
amongst the measured impulse responses. Then the test person was 
given the opportunity to try out the test procedure, where the actual 
sound samples were presented. This will prepare the test persons for 
the actual test and again make them aware of what to expect. The 
training session should not be too long, in order to avoid a loss of 
concentration during the actual test (HeadAcoustics).  
In addition, the duration of the listening test should not be more than 
20 minutes per round, since it can be quite difficult for the listener to 
stay concentrated for a longer time than that. Furthermore, each 
round should be followed by a pause of approximately the same 
duration as the previous listening round. 
Pilot Study 
Before the actual listening test was conducted, a pilot study was 
carried out in order to optimize the test form and procedure. 
Additionally, the pilot study was used to test the quality of the 
measured impulse responses, test the significance of the orientation of 
the artificial head in relation to the perception of flutter 
echo/colouration, and sort the impulse responses in those cases where 
it was difficult to establish the position where the flutter 
echo/colouration was strongest within a room. 
After the training session in the pilot study, the test persons were 
asked to judge if the loudness difference between the sounds samples 
was significant, and if so, if it had an influence on the perception of the 
flutter echo/colouration. The reason for testing the loudness 
difference was to confirm whether the rms normalization was 
acceptable, or if other alternatives should be made to equalize the 
loudness level. 
During the pilot study the description for the actual listening test was 
introduced, together with a description of the pilot study which 
contained a number of questions. The pilot study was carried out in 
the same manner as the actual listening test would have been. The 
description of the pilot study and the questions asked during it can be 
seen in Appendix 9. Furthermore, a short review of the feedback and 
results from the pilot study can be seen in Section 4.1.  
After the pilot study, it was decided to include a counting system in the 
two parts of the listening test, so that it would be possible to see how 
many playbacks each sound sample would obtain. The number of 
playbacks would then be used to estimate if some sound samples were 
more difficult to judge than others.

There are many different ways to conduct listening tests, and all of

Category Judgment test and a Paired Comparison test were chosen 
because they were considered to be more compatible with the 
purposes of the listening test. 
Part One 
A Category Judgment test is a very useful tool when multiple sound 
samples need to be examined, especially compared to a Ranking test 
where the number of sound samples should be no more than six. In a 
Category Judgment test, the sound samples are presented one by one 
to the test person, who rates the respective sound on a scale with 
several categories according to a certain criterion, which in this case is 
flutter echo/colouration (HeadAcoustics). This test type is used to 
establish the subjective perception of flutter echo/colouration in a 
series of rooms, which then could be used as a comparative base for 
the objective measurements as described previously.  
Even though this test type is very advantageous in this case, some bias 
effects could occur. An example could be that the judgment of one 
sound may be influenced by the previous sound, which is called 
"context effect" (Head Acoustics). This bias effect is avoided by 
presenting the sound samples several times in a random order, and by 
recommend the test persons to separating the sound samples with at 
least a 10 seconds pause (Nordtest). Another bias effect is that the 
scale is used to a different extent by  different test persons. Some test 
persons often avoids the extreme categories and answers in the 
medium range instead, which is known as "central tendency of 
judgment". However, this bias affect can be avoided by introducing 
sufficient training before the actual test, where the most extreme 
characteristics are presented to the test person as described 
previously (Head Acoustics).

Part Two 
The other test type used in this thesis is a Paired Comparison test, 
where two sound samples are presented to the test person in 
succession. This test is used to evaluate how different combinations of 
absorption in the two rooms, as described in Section 3.2, can reduce or 
remove flutter echo/colouration. The test person judges these two 
sounds according to a certain criterion, hence flutter echo/colouration. 
This test type is very beneficial when small differences needs to be 
investigated for very similar sounds. However, there is also the risk 
that the possible loudness difference that could occur, as described in 
Sub-section 2.3.1, would be detectable when using this test type. 
Furthermore, this test type has the disadvantage that the duration of 
the test increases considerably with the number of sound samples, 
because of the many possible pair combinations (Head Acoustics). 
However, it is still desirable to check the test persons consistency, so 
therefore one sound sample will appear twice as often as the others 
for both rooms. Even though there is only one sound sample that 
appears more often than the others for each room, the many pair 
combinations with the "extra" sound samples will still give a good 
estimation of the consistency of the test persons. In order to minimize 
the number of combinations pairs, and thus the duration of the test, 
the absorption combinations for each room will only be paired with 
the combinations of that same room, and thus not between the 
rooms.  
Either two or three answers are possible when conducting this type of 
test. If a decision is to be enforced, the test person will only be given 
two possible answers, A>B or B>A, which is called "forced choice". This 
variant could however put the test person under pressure, because a 
decision is demanded even in cases where the person does not hear a 
difference, which will force them to choose an option that does not 
necessarily match their perception. To avoid this, the option of 
answering A=B could be given. However, this option could also result 
in some challenges. Because, some people will tend to avoid a decision 
and will therefore frequently answer A=B, which could make the 
evaluation of the results more difficult. Nevertheless, both downsides 
can be avoided with sufficient introduction and test instructions (Head 
Acoustics).

perception of flutter echo/colouration is believed to be very 
subjective, and therefore the test person should not be forced to 
choose between only two options, if it does not match their actual 
perception. However, it is then also very important to instruct the test 
person properly before the test begins. Additionally, two other

For the Category Judgment test, the objective was to establish a 
variety of subjective parameters that could be used in a correlation 
analysis with objective measuring  results. Therefore,  four different  
statistical data processing techniques were chosen - arithmetic mean, 
weighted mean, median and mode. The arithmetic mean is used to 
establish the central tendency of the data, but it has the disadvantage 
of being greatly influenced by extreme values in the data set. The 
weighted mean is similar to the arithmetic mean, except for the fact 
that some data points contribute more than others to the final 
average. In this case, the number of playback for each sound sample 
will be the factor of which each judgment will be weighed against. So if 
one sound sample requires many playbacks the judgment of that 
sound sample will be weighted less than one with few playbacks. But 
this technique also suffers from the fact that it is greatly influenced by 
extreme values in the data, unless these values have a low weighting in 
the final average. The median is not affected by extreme values in the 
data set, and therefore it might be more suitable for estimating the 
typical value. At last the mode is included, which returns the most 
frequently occurring or repetitive value and can be used to express 
information about a random variable. 
For the Paired Comparison test, the objective was to get a ranking of 
the treatments to see how the different degrees of absorption 
influences the flutter echo in the two rooms. For this purpose a rather 
simple approach can be used to analyze the results. This approach is 
described by Johansson , A. (2005, Paper A), and she recommends to 
base the ranking on the number of wins for each treatment. However, 
this approach can only be used if the paired comparison test has a 
==PAGE:39==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 28  
 
complete balanced design, i.e. all possible pairs are compared in the 
test, as opposed to incomplete balanced design where not all pairs are 
compared. When this approach is used, a win is awarded two points 
and a tie one point. The scores for the sound samples are given 
individually for each test person, before they are added together with 
the scores from the other test persons. The final scores for each sound 
sample will then indicate the ranking order. This ranking model is 
developed by Davidson as described by Johansson, and the system is 
consistent with the same ranking approach that is common in sports. 
However, this technique does not include the results for the answering 
possibility where one or both sound sample does not contain flutter 
echo or colouration. The objective of including this answering 
possibility was to estimate the audible limit of the flutter echo, in 
relation to the amount of absorption in the rooms. It was decided that 
the number of "No's" for each sound sample would give a good 
approximation of the subjective limit of the flutter echo. So each time 
a sound sample was judged to have "No Flutter Echo/Colouration" it 
would obtain one point. The final number of "No's"  for each sound 
sample might then give an indication of the limit between a weak 
flutter echo and a strong flutter echo, and perhaps also give an 
indication of when the flutter echo is removed. 
3.4  Statistical Analysis 
The results for the subjective and objective investigation were 
analyzed with different statistical methods. These methods will be 
described briefly in the following sub-sections, and will include 
Analysis of Variance, Correlation Coefficients and Linear Regression. 
3.4.1  Analysis of Variance 
For the results from the first part of the listening test, a one-way 
analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted in order to establish the 
significance of the results. The ANOVA test compares the means of 
two or more groups of data, and returns the p-value for the null 
hypothesis that the means of the groups are equal. The analysis is 
conducted to see if there is a significant variance between the 
subjective results for the different rooms, thus each room is equal to 
one group of data. Since the number of elements, in this case the 
number of judgments, is the same for each group, a balanced ANOVA 
test can be used. If the p-value is lower than five percent, the variance 
between the subjective judgments for the different rooms are 
considered to be significant.  
It is quite important to ensure a significant variance of the judgments 
between the different rooms, because it will ensure that the selected 
rooms provide a wider range of knowledge in terms of flutter echo and 
colouration perception. 
3.4.2  Correlation Coefficient 
A correlation analysis can be used to identify the connection between 
different parameters. In this case, the objective of this analysis is to 
==PAGE:40==
Chapter 3 - Methodology 
 
Page | 29 
 
find the correlation between the subjective results from the Category 
Judgment test and objective measurements. The correlation analysis is 
conducted in accordance with the method described by Brunskog and 
Gade et al. (2009). The subjective parameters include the arithmetic 
mean, the weighted mean, the median and the mode. These 
parameters were correlated with a series of objective parameters, of 
which most of them were obtained from the IRIS measurements. The 
objective parameters used in the correlation analysis will be further 
described in Section 5.2. 
The analysis generates a matrix of p-values together with a matrix of 
correlation coefficients R. Each p-value corresponds to an element of 
R. The p-value describes the hypothesis of no correlation, against the 
alternative that there is a non-zero correlation. So if the p-value for 
two parameters is small, say less than 0.05, then the correlation R 
between those parameters is significantly different from zero. 
3.4.3  Linear Regression 
The objective parameters which proved to have the best correlation 
with the subjective parameters can then be used to compute a model  
which ideally can predict the perception of flutter echo/colouration in 
small rooms. In relation to this, the linear regression will be conducted 
with both one and two variables to establish the most efficient 
prediction model.  
3.5  Cepstrum Technique 
This technique transforms a signal from the frequency domain to a 
new domain with the unit of seconds, or milliseconds, by the inverse 
Fourier transformation (IFT) of the logarithm of a spectrum. The word 
Cepstrum is derived from paraphrasing the word spectrum, and other 
terms relevant for the Cepstrum technique are derived in the same 
way. For example, the independent variable in the Cepstrum is 
referred to as quefrency (Rindel, 2016). 
The technique is able to detect periodic behavior in the spectrum, and 
can ideally be used to detect flutter echo and colouration in rooms. In 
a room with parallel walls the Cepstrum will indicate a peak at a 
frequency equal to the time it will take for the sound to propagate 
between the two walls. 
"...a single peak in the Cepstrum at quefrency Δt is an indication 
that the spectrum contains harmonics with frequency spacing  
1/ Δt." (Rindel, 2016). 
Rindel (2016) also describes that colouration due to single early 
reflections also can be seen as peaks in the Cepstrum. 
In this thesis, the technique will be used to investigated the effect of 
the previously mentioned absorption combinations from an objective 
point of view. According to Rindel (2016), increased absorption will 
reduce the Cepstrum peaks, and therefore the result from this 
technique will be evaluated in relation to the subjective ranking of the 
absorption combinations. The evaluation will be based on the 
connection between the subjective ranking of the different absorption 
==PAGE:41==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 30  
 
combinations and the magnitudes of the peaks in the Cepstrum. The 
Cepstrum is applied to the Omni-directional signal from the B-format 
impulse response, using the simulation program ODEON. In this 
program the Cepstrum is implemented as the real Cepstrum, and it can 
be done for both measured and simulated impulse responses. 
Several initiatives have been conducted in order to implement the 
Cepstrum technique in the correlation analysis. Ideally, a good 
correlation between the magnitude of the Cepstrum peaks and the 
subjective judgments would provide very valuable information on how 
to predict and check the risk of flutter echo in rooms that are not yet 
built. However, the scaling on the ordinate axis seems to be quite 
arbitrary, especially since the Cepstrum is normalized relative to the 
magnitude of the highest peak. Thus, it would be inappropriate to 
compare the Cepstrum peaks for the different rooms and therefore it 
was not considered to be useful in this case. 
On the other hand, this difficulty is not considered to be an obstacle 
for the investigations of the different absorption combinations in room 
9 and 13, since the comparison is not performed across rooms, but 
only within the same room.

In the following section the result from the pilot study will be 
presented. These results include the feedback on the questions 
handed out before the pilot study, where the test person were asked

4.1.2  Significance of Artificial Head Orientation 
From the pilot study it could not be fully confirmed if the orientation of 
the artificial head had any influence on the perception of flutter echo. 
During the pilot study, the Paired Comparison test was used to 
compare the measured impulse responses for the two orientations in 
the same measuring positions for a number of the investigated rooms. 
As it can be seen in Appendix 11, three fifths of the judgments 
indicated that the degree of flutter echo/colouration was the same for 
the two orientations, thus the orientation had no influence on the 
perception of flutter echo/colouration in these cases. One fifth of the 
==PAGE:44==

strongest for the situation where the artificial head was facing the 
sound source, and the last fifth indicated that the flutter

while facing the room. 
Thus it is not possible to conclude that a particular orientation of the

5.1  Significance of Subjective Results 
As mentioned in Sub-section 3.4.1, an analysis of variance test was 
conducted to test the significance of the variance for the subjective 
results for the Category Judgment test. A significant variance of the 
subjective judgments between the different rooms will ensure that the 
selected rooms provide a wide range of knowledge in terms of the 
flutter echo and colouration perception. The variance is considered to 
be significant if the p-value is lower than five percent.  
From the ANOVA test the significance of variance between the rooms 
was found to be 4.3e-20, which is a great deal smaller than five percent, 
and thus the rooms are considered to be sufficient in terms of  
providing a good data range. However, the p-value does not  provide 
any information about the spread of variance. Meaning, that if the mean 
of one room is significantly different from all the other rooms, that 
would also indicate a low significance of variance. However, as it can 
be seen in figure 27 the subjective judgments for the different rooms 
vary quite a lot, and thus the significance of variance proves to be 
adequate. 
Each box in figure 27 represents the data for one room. The center line 
marked with red is the median, and the edges of the boxes are the 25th 
and 75th percentiles. The end of the whiskers represent the most 
extreme data points which are not considered to be outliers, while the 
outliers are plotted individually and indicated by the crosses. For most 
of the rooms, it can be seen that there is a wide spread of the data. 
Only room 11 and 12 contain data which is centered around one 
judgment, and otherwise have only a few outliners.

5.2  Correlation of Parameters  
Several objective parameters, both physical and acoustical, were 
either measured or calculated for the 15 investigated rooms. As 
mentioned in Sub-section 3.3.5, the subjective parameters were 
calculated from different statistical data processing techniques. Most 
of the objective parameters were obtained from the IRIS 
measurements, either directly from the program or from the B-format 
impulse response, which can be exported from the program.  
The parameters which are obtained directly from the program include 
the average EDT and T20 from 63-8000 Hz and the EDT, T20 and T30 
for the 1kHz and 2kHz octave bands. The reason for including separate 
reverberation times for the 1 kHz and 2 kHz octave bands is due to the 
fact that the flutter echo typically ends up in this range, as described 
earlier.  
The other objective parameters obtained from the B-format impulse 
response included the average EDT, T20 and T30 from 63 Hz to 8000 
Hz, together with the EDT, T20 and T30, for the 1 kHz and 2 kHz octave 
band, for each of the four channels in the impulse response. 
Furthermore, the ratio between the maximum reverberation time in 
the X-, Y- and Z-direction and the average reverberation time in all 
three directions was investigated, because of the probability that there 
might occur a higher reverberation time in the direction of the flutter 
echo. These ratios were included for the average EDT, T20 and T30 
from 63 Hz to 8000 Hz, and for EDT, T20 and T30 in the 1 kHz and 2kHz 
octave band.  
Finally, the energy in the three directions was calculated, to see if the 
energy in one direction was significantly different than the other two 
directions, since this would be an important physical indication of 
flutter echo. The energy is calculated from the following equation.

Discussion 
Throughout this project, several different processes were used to 
analyze the flutter echo/colouration in small ordinary rooms. The 
purpose was to obtain a better understanding of the relation between 
the subjective perception of flutter echo and colouration, and 
objective parameters, which was obtained from a listening test with a 
test panel and a series of measurements, respectively. 
From the results of the listening test it could generally be confirmed 
that it is quite difficult to establish a basic understanding for flutter 
echo/colouration perception. The fact is that flutter echo and 
especially colouration perception is a very subjective matter, which 
very much depends on the individual person's understanding of the 
phenomenon. Furthermore, it is also clear that the perception of 
flutter echo and colouration is not necessarily absolute for the 
individual person. Thus, one person could judge a room to contain a 
weak degree of flutter echo in one situation, and then a medium  
degree in another situation. However, this could possibly be linked to 
the fact that the test persons generally indicated difficulties when 
distinguishing between the different answering possibilities in the 
category judgment test, especially in the case between weak- and 
medium flutter echo/colouration.  
Generally, the test persons also indicated difficulties in relation to 
identifying colouration. This relates to the fact that the periodic time 
delay between the reflections in a small room becomes sufficiently 
==PAGE:75==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 64  
 
small, and thus the flutter echo will be heard in the frequency domain 
instead of the time domain. Therefore, colouration is also more 
common in small rooms where the reflection density increases rapidly. 
This can be supported by the results that can be seen in figure 15 and 
17, where the rooms with the smallest volumes were generally rated 
to contain a weaker flutter echo/colouration. Furthermore, the 
subjective results from the listening test clearly indicated that the 
flutter echo was perceived stronger for rooms with larger volumes, 
which is consistent with the fact that the periodic time delay in these 
cases are significantly large, and thus it is easier to hear the periodic 
reflections of the plane sound waves in the room.  This connection 
between the flutter echo perception and the room volume was clearly 
supported by the results from the correlation analysis. In fact, the 
room volume was the objective parameter that proved to have the 
best correlation with the subjective judgments, with a correlation 
coefficients between 0.65 and 0.72, and a significance of less than 0.5 
and 1 percent. 
Several other objective parameters proved to have good correlations 
to the subjective results from the category judgments test. As 
mentioned previously, most of the objective parameters were 
obtained with the B-format impulse response, which contains an 
Omni-directional signal and three figure-of-eight signals in the X-, Y- 
and Z-direction. Some of the objective parameters that obtained the 
best correlations with the subjective parameters were derived as the 
ratio between the maximum reverberation time in the X-, Y and Z-
direction and the average reverberation time for all three directions. 
One of these parameters were T30max/T30avg in the 2 kHz octave band. 
However, this ratio based parameter proved to have a relatively 
important disadvantage. It will only indicate strong correlations in 
cases where the reverberation time is significantly longer in one 
direction, thus for situations where the flutter echo propagates in one 
direction. It will not however indicate strong correlations in cases 
where the flutter echo propagates equally or almost equally in two 
directions. 
Furthermore, some irregularities could be seen for the parameters 
T20max/T20avg and T30max/T30avg in the 1 kHz octave band. Here, room 
12 had a significantly higher ratio than room 11 and 13, as it can be 
seen on figure 21 and 22, even though they were all judged to contain 
approximately the same degree of flutter echo from the listening test, 
as it can be seen in figure 15. This is particularly unusual, since room 
11 and 13 have a longer room dimension and should thus contain a 
significantly longer reverberation time in one direction. However, no 
explanation was established to this irregularity. 
Finally, the energy in the X- and Y-direction also showed relatively 
strong correlations with the subjective judgments. It was especially 
noticeable that the energy might be used as an indicator for estimating 
the direction of the flutter echo and establishing whether the flutter 
echo propagates in one or two directions. Furthermore, the energy is 
well correlated with the room dimensions.

regression models were computed for each of the four subjective 
parameters, in order to predict flutter echo perception in small rooms 
from objective parameters only. It proved that the same objective

Overall, these prediction models are considered to be quite sufficient 
in terms of estimating the perception of flutter echo in rooms. 
However, as it was mentioned earlier it can be discussed whether or 
not these objective parameters, which proved to be the best fit for the 
prediction models, are easy to obtain. The room volume is probably 
among the most easy parameters to obtain in any circumstances, since 
it  only requires an electric distance meter. On the other hand, the 
parameters T20max/T20avg and T30max/T30avg for the 1 kHz octave band 
could be a little more difficult to obtain. Here, one will need a TetraMic 
and a signal processing software, which can convert the measured 
signal into a B-format impulse response. The use of the TetraMic is a 
relatively cheap solution, however it will require some specific 
software to convert the measured signal into usable figure-of-eight 
signals that can be used to calculate the ratios in the X-, Y- and Z-
directions. 
In relation to the prediction models, it can also be discussed which of 
the statistical subjective parameters that illustrate the actual 
perception of flutter echo best. Generally, the differences between the 
four parameters are not considered to be very significant, and thus it is 
assumed that they could all be used to predict the prevalence of flutter 
echo in small rooms. On the other hand, some of the parameters could 
turn out to be less suitable than others. For example, the weighted 
mean for one room could be influenced significantly if the sound 
sample for this room obtained considerably more playbacks than 
another room. However, a high number of playbacks does not 
necessarily mean that the final judgment for this room is more 
ambiguous than the judgment for a room which obtained fewer 
playbacks. Instead the judgment consistency would probably be more 
suitable to use as the weighting factor. Furthermore, the mode could 
also turn out to give slightly ambiguous results, since several modes 
could occur for the same room, which would make the 
implementation in the analysis more complicated.  
Finally, the results from the paired comparison test were used to rank 
the different absorption combinations, which contained different 
degrees of absorption panels, in two rooms. The ranking was based on 
a simple point score approach, where a win was awarded with two 
points and a tie was awarded with one point, and the results clearly 
indicated that the strength of flutter echo was reduced when more 
absorption was applied in the rooms. The final scores for each 
==PAGE:77==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 66  
 
absorption combination was also converted into a percentage 
distribution, in order to compare the score in relation to the lowest 
and highest possible score. Here, it was assumed that a combination 
which obtained the lowest possible score, equal to zero percent, could 
be confirmed to completely eliminate flutter echo, since all the test 
persons agreed that this combination did not contain any flutter echo. 
However, it proved that neither of the combinations in the two rooms 
were able to obtain the lowest possible score, and thus neither of 
them could be confirmed to completely eliminate the flutter echo. As 
it can be seen in table 8 and 9, the absorption combinations which 
contained the full number of absorption panels were 30 and 33 
percent from completely removing the flutter echo in room 9 and 13, 
respectively. However, it could be discussed if it is fair to compare the 
scores for the absorption combination with the lowest possible score, 
since it would require that all the test persons agreed that that this 
combination did not contain flutter echo. Especially considering the 
fact that the perception of flutter echo is very subjective, as discussed 
previously. Thus, it proved to be quite difficult to establish the flutter 
echo perception limit in relation to the degree of absorption in a room.  
The results from the subjective ranking of the absorption combinations 
were compared with the results from a Cepstrum analysis, in order to 
see if this technique could be used to support the subjective 
perception of flutter echo. In general, it was estimated that the results 
from the Cepstrum analysis were not reliable in terms of supporting 
the subjective ranking of the different absorption combinations in this 
project. This statement is based on the irregularities that has proved to 
arise during the analysis, where the magnitude of the Cepstrum peaks 
that correlated with the quefrency of the room dimensions in some 
cases increased when more absorption was applied to the rooms. 
Overall, it is estimated that the Cepstrum technique is still a quite 
uncertain technique for flutter echo and colouration detection. The 
Cepstrum is a potential tool in theory, but very hard to implement as a 
good and simple measure in practice, especially because of the fact 
that the results are quite difficult to analyze in terms of estimating a 
useful threshold.

Conclusion 
The vision of this thesis was first of all to investigate the connection 
between the subjective perception of flutter echo/colouration and 
objective measurements in small ordinary rooms. Second of all, it was 
desired to establish what measures are needed, in terms of 
absorption, to reduce or eliminate the flutter echo in small rooms. In 
the following, the main conclusions gathered from the numerous data 
obtained throughout the project will be presented. 
From the results of a listening test it was found that the perception of 
flutter echo and especially colouration is a rather subjective matter. 
Therefore, four subjective parameters were computed from different 
statistical data processing techniques - the arithmetic mean, the 
weighted mean, the median and the mode. From a correlation analysis 
between these subjective parameters and a number of objective 
parameters, it was possible to obtain quite good correlations. One of 
the objective parameters that proved to correlate best with the 
subjective perception of flutter echo and colouration was the room 
volume. Furthermore, it proved that several parameters that included 
directional information obtained with a B-format impulse response, 
which contains an Omni-directional signal and three figure-of-eight 
signals, also indicated good correlations with the subjective perception 
of flutter echo and colouration. These parameters were derived as the 
ratio between the maximum reverberation time in the X-, Y- and Z-
direction and the average reverberation time for all three directions. 
==PAGE:79==
Flutter Echo and Colouration in Small Rooms  
Page | 68  
 
For each of the four subjective parameters, several prediction models 
were computed from linear regression analyses. The best models for 
the four subjective parameters proved to be two variable models, 
which all had the room volume as one of the dependent variables. The 
second dependent variable was the ratio between the maximum T20 
in the X-, Y- and Z-direction and the average T20 for all three directions 
in the 1 kHz octave band, for the arithmetic mean, weighted mean and 
median, while it for the mode was the ratio between the maximum 
T30 in the X-, Y- and Z-direction and the average T30 for all three 
directions in the 1 kHz octave band. All the models proved to be quite 
good in terms of predicting the prevalence of flutter echo and 
colouration in small rooms, especially considering the sample size of 
15 rooms. 
Finally, it was clear that the strength of flutter echo was reduced when 
more absorption was applied in the two investigated rooms. However, 
it proved to be quite difficult to determine the degree of absorption 
that is needed to completely eliminate flutter echo in small rooms. 
Therefore, more absorption combinations and rooms are considered 
to be useful if conducting further studies. Additionally, the Cepstrum 
analysis did not prove to support the subjective results in this thesis, 
and thus this technique is considered to be a quite uncertain technique 
for flutter echo and colouration detection in this case.
