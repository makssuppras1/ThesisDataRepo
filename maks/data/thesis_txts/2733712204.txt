Abstract
Background Acute myocardial infarction (AMI), commonly known as a heart at-
tack, remains a significant global public health concern. However, with compre-
hension and treatment over the past years, it has evolved significantly, leading to
improved outcomes. Stress, influenced by traﬀic noise or traﬀic-related air pollu-
tion, is one of the risk factors associated with AMI. This study examines residential
proximity to major roads as a proxy for traﬀic-related pollution and investigates the
association between distance from major roads and the incidence of AMI.
Methods The study population consists of 3,872,462 Danish individuals aged 30
years or older with a 10-year residential history in Denmark, of which 71,993 expe-
rienced an incident AMI between 2012 and 2021. Two distinct methodologies were
used to assess the proximity to major roads: measuring the straight line distance to
the nearest road and counting the roads within a specified radius. The study uses
Poisson regression of incidence rate to examine the association between distance
exposure variables and incident AMI, categorizing major roads into large roads and
highways. Random Survival Forest (RSF) was used to determine the importance of
different distance exposure variables on incident AMI.
Results A notable increase in AMI cases was found for individuals living within
50 meters of a highway, showing a 22% heightened IRR. Additionally, a clear trend
was observed between the number of highways within a 50-meter radius and AMI,
revealing a 50% increase in IRR when residing near two highways. No significant
trend was found for large roads. RSF identified distance to large roads and high-
ways as the model’s two most critical exposure variables. Poisson regression and
RSF models underscore distance to highways as the most robust exposure variable
influencing AMI.
Conclusion The findings of the Poisson regression confirm previous findings on
the association between traﬀic-related exposure and risk of incident AMI. However,
RSF has not been as widely studied, especially in the context of this topic, so future
studies have to confirm the current results.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Acute myocardial infarction (AMI) has a significant impact on global health, it is
one of the leading causes of death in developed countries, resulting in an estimated 3
million deaths in 2023 [1]. While commonly recognised risk factors such as smoking,
physical inactivity, obesity, and an unhealthy diet are prevalent causes of AMI [2],
environmental factors such as traﬀic-related air pollution and noise pollution are
gaining increasing recognition as significant contributors [3, 4].
Numerous researchers have found positive associations between exposure to traﬀic-
related air pollution and disease mortality using air pollutants PM2.5 and NO2 as
key indicators in their investigations [5]. These pollutants can damage the heart
and blood vessels [6]. Research has also suggested a potential association between
noise pollution and Myocardial Infarction (MI), which can trigger oxidative damage,
elevate stress and blood pressure, disrupt sleep patterns and cause inflammation.
These detrimental effects can increase the risk of cardiovascular disease [7, 8, 9, 4].
One approach to simultaneously assess the impact of these two factors is to consider
residential distance to the nearest road as a proxy.
Previous research has demonstrated a positive association between consistently liv-
ing close to major roads and an increased risk of incident MI [10, 11]. However,
these studies have employed limited exposure metrics; Tonne et al. (2007) utilised
distance without considering a time-varying scale, while Hart et al. (2013) focused
on changes in residential distance to roads. These limitations highlight the need
1
==PAGE:14==
to explore more comprehensive exposure metrics, particularly those that incorpo-
rate time-varying distance to major roads, to understand better the true association
between residential proximity to traﬀic roads and incident AMI. The precise doc-
umentation and diagnostic precision of AMI in Danish national registers provide
a robust and reliable outcome for this study, strengthening the credibility of its
findings [12].
1.1
Study hypotheses and objectives
Road networks and traﬀic are a phenomenon that most individuals are exposed to
in their daily lives in one way or another. Existing studies from various corners
of the world have investigated the harmful aspects of traﬀic-related pollution. To
what extent residential proximity to major roads affects AMI occurrences in the
Danish population has yet to be systematically investigated. Finding appropriate
exposure to traﬀic is challenging since it is a complex topic with many aspects. Vedal
et al. (2009) published an article that delved into the complexities of estimating
traﬀic exposure [13]. The article explains the complex topic of estimating traﬀic
exposure. Traﬀic-related pollution is one aspect of exposure, but even that is hard to
estimate since no singular air pollutant or pollution component is a specific measure
of traﬀic pollution.
Traﬀic noise pollution can also be considered a measure of
traﬀic-associated pollution. Therefore, some researchers have deployed other forms
of exposure in contrast to measurements of air pollutants and noise levels, where
proximity to roads, traﬀic volumes and traﬀic emission concentrations based on
dispersion models are utilised [14, 8, 15, 11].
It is hypothesised that individuals with a history of long-term residence close to
major roads in Denmark have an increased risk of developing AMI when accounting
for relevant confounders. Consequently, the research questions are:
- Is there an association between long-term residential proximity to major roads
and incidence of AMI?
- What are the most informative ways to represent exposure of residential prox-
imity to major roads?
2
==PAGE:15==
- How important is residential proximity to major roads when analysing inci-
dence of AMI
The present study will answer these questions using two complementary method-
ologies: Poisson regression and Random Survival Forest (RSF) analysis. Initially,
Poisson regression is employed as a robust foundation for understanding the associ-
ation between incidence of AMI in the Danish population and residential proximity
to traﬀic roads. Subsequently, the study develops a RSF model for a dataset in lon-
gitudinal format. This model builds on the Poisson regression analysis and further
analyses the association between residential proximity to major roads. The main
aim of the RSF model is to examine the importance of different exposure variables.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter offers fundamental insights into AMI while investigating the envi-
ronmental factors contributing to its occurrence. It delves into existing literature
investigating the connection between residing in proximity to traﬀic roads and the
incidence of AMI. Section 2.1 provides an understanding of AMI, while section 2.2
gives details of the road network in Denmark. Sections 2.3 and 2.4 further explore
the impact of traﬀic-related air- and noise pollution. Section 2.5 examines the appli-
cation of machine learning models using health related data. Section 2.6 commences
with a summary of the literature review process and concludes with a review of pre-
vious literature on the association between residential proximity to major roads and
incident AMI.
2.1
Acute myocardial infarction
Acute- and chronic MI is commonly known as a heart attack. It occurs when blood
flow to a portion of the myocardium is reduced or blocked. The primary cause of
most MI cases is underlying coronary artery disease. If the coronary arteries become
blocked, the myocardium undergoes oxygen deprivation, causing damage or death
of the myocardial cells [16].
In Denmark, records indicate a total of 368,839 individuals experienced incident AMI
between 1987 and 2016, with 128,957 of these cases resulting in fatalities [1]. Over
the past 40 years, comprehension and treatment of AMI have significantly evolved,
4
==PAGE:17==
shifting from a fatal diagnosis at autopsy to a dynamically managed condition.
The understanding of its development has expanded, resulting in vastly improved
outcomes [17]. In Denmark, the myocardial infarction incidence rate per 100,000
individuals showed a consistent decrease from 2005 to 2021. The reduction in MI
incidence persisted across various age groups and genders into the current decade.
Notably, the most substantial declines were evident in the oldest age groups [18].
Symptoms of AMI can be chest pain or pressure that may extend to the neck,
jaw, shoulder, or arm, shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, and lightheadedness.
Women might encounter unusual symptoms like abdominal discomfort or dizziness
and may appear without experiencing chest pain [2, 16]. MI can present with uncon-
ventional symptoms or may even occur asymptomatically, only becoming evident
through electrocardiogram (ECG), elevated biomarkers, or cardiac imaging [19].
AMI has diverse risk factors, with some being modifiable and others non-modifiable.
Modifiable risk factors include smoking, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, obesity,
high blood pressure, and diabetes. Non-modifiable risk factors include age, family
history, and low socioeconomic status [20, 21]. Moreover, the risk of getting AMI
has been examined in relation to geographical inequalities, which shows a 40% in-
crease in the incidence rate of AMI when living in specific areas after adjusting for
sociodemographic factors compared to the country mean [22]. Furthermore, recent
epidemiological and clinical research advancements have highlighted the potential
adverse impact of environmental factors on the risk of AMI in the short and long
term. These factors include the potential effects of air pollution, weather condi-
tions, and climate change [6]. Therefore, exploring the impact of environmental risk
factors can contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of AMI.
2.2
Road network
A historical review of the Danish road network shows that large parts of the net-
work were built and developed based on demand at a particular time and the need
for connections between different urban areas within Denmark, therefore lacking an
overall plan for a nationwide road network. However, today, the Danish road net-
work has developed into an extensive network that services the entire country with
5
==PAGE:18==
an essential infrastructure connecting Denmark to its neighbouring countries and
providing better interconnectivity between parts of the country [23].
The Danish transport system comprises several infrastructures, including railways,
roads, seaports, and airports [24]. According to a statistics overview communicated
by Statistics Denmark on data acquired from the Danish Road Directorate, there
are 74,897 km of roads in Denmark, of which 1,355 km are highways [25]. A highway
is a road that has been specifically designed and built for the use of motor vehicles.
It must feature separate carriageways for traﬀic moving in two opposite directions,
remain unconnected to nearby properties, and refrain from having other roads, rails,
tram lines, or pedestrian pathways crossing it at the same level [26].
The Road Directorate of Denmark published an overview report on the conditions
and development of the Danish state road network. It states that, in general, traﬀic
on the road network has been steadily increasing over the years, with exceptions
in the years 2019 and 2020, where due to COVID-19, traﬀic drastically decreased.
A noteworthy aspect highlighted in the report is that sales of electric and hybrid
cars reached a new record in 2021. This positive development suggests that, despite
the overall increase in road network traﬀic, there is a gradual shift towards greener
transportation [27].
2.3
Traﬀic related air pollution
It is well-known that one of the primary causes of air pollution is emissions from
motor vehicles. Air pollution is a diverse and complex combination of gases, liquids,
and particulate matter. Ambient pollutants, including carbon monoxide (CO), ni-
trogen oxides (NOx), sulfur dioxide (SO2), ozone (O3), lead (Pb), and various sizes
of particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), can have notable effects on human health.
There are growing concerns about the association between these pollutants and cer-
tain diseases. Inhaling particulate matter can start a process of stress and inflamma-
tion in the lungs and throughout the body. This can affect human health in several
ways, including increased risk of blood clots forming, irregular heartbeats, sudden
narrowing of arteries, initiation of body-wide inflammation, promoting long-term
artery disease, and triggering various physical reactions, as outlined by the Ameri-
6
==PAGE:19==
can Heart Association [28]. Ultimately, these pollutants contribute to an elevated
likelihood of developing cardiovascular diseases, such as AMI [29].
In 2007, a thorough study combined data from over 30 research projects to ex-
plore how various pollutants decrease near roads, looking at areas with high and
low background pollution. The overall results showed that pollutant levels decrease
differently as distance to road increases, influenced by factors like background pollu-
tion, the nature of the pollutants, and local weather conditions such as wind speed.
A study focusing on elemental carbon (C) revealed a decrease in pollutant levels
ranging from 100 to 400 meters from the pollution source. For ultrafine particles
(UFP), the decrease occurred within 100 to 300 meters, while NO2 levels showed a
decline within 200 to 500 meters [30]. Moreover, the Health Effects Institute Panel
identified an exposure zone 300–500 meters from a highway or major road most
highly affected by traﬀic emissions [31]. Traﬀic-related air pollution is a significant
environmental concern in Denmark. However, compared to other countries, Den-
mark ranks 103 out of 131 countries when measuring levels of air pollution in 2022
[32].
2.4
Traﬀic related noise pollution
Road traﬀic not only generates air pollution but also contributes to issues related
to traﬀic noise. Noise levels are measured in yearly averages and are stated on Lden
terms, which is the European criterion for noise. Different measurements are used for
noise during the day and night since, generally, nighttime noise is considered more
irritating than daytime noise [33]. Denmark has implemented noise regulations to
address health and environmental concerns, especially in residential areas prioritising
the well-being of its residents. The permissible noise levels vary throughout the day,
with a maximum of 55 dB during daytime hours (7:00 - 22:00) and a reduced level
of 45 dB during evening and night hours (22:00 - 7:00) [34].
Noise is not a constant; it changes due to many different factors. Noise levels ex-
perienced by residential addresses in proximity to roads can vary depending on the
time of day, which vehicle is driving on the road, wind direction, and the specific
location of the residence, particularly in the presence of intervening structures. The
7
==PAGE:20==
proximity of a residence to a road, along with the vacant spaces in between, signif-
icantly impacts the level of noise experienced. With increasing distance from the
road, road noise diminishes. Specifically, the noise decreases by a minimum of 3 dB
each time the distance is doubled [35].
Approximately 40% of the population in European countries face exposure to road
traﬀic noise surpassing 55 dB, with 20% experiencing levels exceeding 65 dB during
the day and 30% encountering levels surpassing 55 dB at night [36]. The Road
Noise Strategy revised in 2010 indicates that 785,000 homes in Denmark are affected
by road noise above the recommended limits, which is almost one in every three
homes. Each year, traﬀic noise creates a heightened risk, leading to hospitalisation
for conditions such as raised blood pressure or heart disease in Denmark, affecting
between 800 and 2,200 individuals. Furthermore, 200-500 premature deaths each
year are linked to this added risk [37].
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), noise is a commonly under-
estimated threat that can lead to various short- and long-term health issues, like
sleep problems, heart-related effects, decreased performance at work and school,
and hearing diﬀiculties. In the WHO European Region, noise has become a major
environmental concern, particularly affecting vulnerable groups such as children,
individuals with lower income levels and individuals with chronic illnesses [36].
2.5
Application of machine learning in health sci-
ence
Machine learning models are a valuable tool in many sets of problem-solving tasks.
In particular, these models can be very beneficial when handling large data sets with
many independent variables. Several research papers have explored the application
of machine learning models to predict cardiovascular diseases [38, 39, 40].
A study by Ghosh et al. (2021) evaluated the performance of hybrid classifiers that
combine bagging and boosting methods for predicting heart diseases. The study
compared the accuracy of the Decision Tree Bagging Method, Random Forest Bag-
ging Method, K-Nearest Neighbors Bagging Method, AdaBoost Boosting Method,
8
==PAGE:21==
and Gradient Boosting Boosting Method. The study concluded that the Random
Forest Bagging Method, in combination with feature selection methods, achieved
the highest accuracy of 99.05% [38]. A study by Xing et al. (2007) developed pre-
diction models to predict the survival of individuals with Coronary Heart Disease.
This study compared three prediction models, a support vector machine, an artifi-
cial neural network, and decision trees, and then applied a 10-fold cross-validation
method to the results for each model. All three models delivered acceptable accu-
racy, with support vector machine as the best predictor with 92.1% accuracy [39].
A different study by Latha et al. (2019) used various classifiers to predict heart
disease risk, employing ensemble techniques to improve the performance of weaker
models. Random Forest (RF) as a bagging method significantly improved accu-
racy for all weak classifiers. Suggesting that RF is a particularly effective technique
for predicting cardiovascular diseases [40], supporting the findings of Ghosh et al.
(2021).
The RF algorithm is one of the prominent machine learning methodologies utilised
in developing predictive models. RF accommodates diverse response types, includ-
ing categorical or quantitative outcomes and survival times. RF effectively manages
predictors of varying scales or distributions, making it well-suited for applications in
high-dimensional settings where the number of predictors may exceed the number
of observations. Consequently, it is very suitable for analysing complex data. The
ability of tree-based methods to generate data-driven subgroups of samples holds
the potential for valuable applications in patient stratification. Including variable
importance measures further enhances the method’s utility by highlighting the sig-
nificance of each predictor in the model [41].
The Cox-proportional hazards (CPH) regression model is a common method when
analysing right-censored time-to-event data and is widely used by researchers. How-
ever, it has limitations, especially when dealing with non-linearity and high dimen-
sional covariates. Random Survival Forests (RSF) method is a method specifically
designed to extend the RF method to handle right-censored time-to-event data; its
strength is that it can easily handle high dimensional covariates [42]. Ishwaran et
al. (2008) compared the prediction accuracy of RF and CPH with RSF using dif-
9
==PAGE:22==
ferent sampling methods to assess performance. Experiments on eleven different
datasets were performed. RSF outperformed the RF and CPH in most experiments
or performed equally well for all the different sampling methods [43].
A study by Hsich et al. (2011) compared CPH regression to RSF in a cohort study
focused on the survival of individuals with systolic heart failure. Both models per-
formed equally well, but the researchers preferred RSF for its ability to identify risk
factors more robustly. They employed variable selection from the RSF to identify
the most important variables for predicting mortality. The minimal depth variable
was used to determine the most important variable, which most frequently splits the
branches near the tree trunks. The most important variables identified were similar
to those found in the CPH analysis but in a different relative order [44].
Zhang et al. (2019) utilised the importance of permutation to determine the most
significant factors associated with dyslipidemia in Chinese Han adults. By lever-
aging variable importance metrics, they could identify the most suitable features
for the RSF. The RSF, constructed with these features, outperformed a conven-
tional CPH, showcasing superior performance [45]. In another study, Hadanny et
al. (2022) examined machine learning methods and employed variable importance
analysis to determine the most critical factors influencing 1-year mortality in pa-
tients with acute coronary syndrome. The results indicated that RSF outperformed
other techniques in predicting mortality [46]. These findings highlight the versatility
of RSF in predicting diverse clinical outcomes using varying feature sets.
2.5.1
Machine learning models and longitudinal data
The examination of longitudinal data is essential in various scientific fields such as
medicine, life sciences, and economics [41]. Longitudinal studies analyse repeated
measurements of the same subject across time. Applying machine learning models to
longitudinal data can benefit medical researchers significantly. It provides an under-
standing of risk factors, enables early diagnosis, and helps identify at-risk individuals
[47]. Advancements in technology allow for comprehensive patient profiling across
genetic, molecular, demographic, and clinical dimensions. However, many of these
measurements are dynamic and evolve over time due to disease progression, treat-
10
==PAGE:23==
ment, comorbidities, and environmental factors. Therefore, repeated measurements
across time are essential to capture the longitudinal trajectory of these variables [41,
47].
In previous studies involving longitudinal data, various approaches, most commonly
statistical methods, have been used. Researchers have frequently utilised generalised
linear mixed models, demonstrating their ability to yield reasonable results when
modelling the association between small sets of variables. However, these models face
limitations when dealing with growing sets of variables and nonlinear relationships.
Machine learning models, however, are ideal for nonlinear relationships and handling
of complex and high-dimensional data [47].
The application of machine learning models on longitudinal data has its complexi-
ties. Longitudinal data has repeated measurements for the same subject, and those
observations tend to be correlated, breaking the ”independently and identically dis-
tributed” (”i.i.d”) assumption, which is a common assumption in machine learning
and can lead to biased results if not properly handled. Additionally, longitudinal
data can be highly complex and have significant variations between subjects, break-
ing the ”i.i.d” assumption. In longitudinal data sets, time intervals between obser-
vations are not always evenly distributed, and these data sets often have missing
observations, resulting in incomplete data sets. This excludes the possibility of util-
ising time-series algorithms that build on the assumptions of having complete data
sets. There can be highly different uncertainties between repeated measurements
that often are hard to estimate [47].
Summary features (SF), or aggregation, is the simplest way to handle longitudinal
data. A few standard approaches are mean/mode/median, count, variation, and
minimum/maximum value of available measurement for each subject. The issue with
this approach is the possible loss of information. Longitudinal features (LF) is an
alternative; it expands the features’ space by considering every variable’s observation
as a feature and then stacks them horizontally. Non-sequential LF are not time-
aware and require samples to have the same number of observations per interval
time and the same number of follow-ups. Sequential LF is time-aware and considers
the sequential nature of the features used in the model. Another way is to stack
11
==PAGE:24==
vertically (SV), discarding the correlation between repeated measures and building a
data set where each observation of a subject is its own instance, allowing for different
numbers of observations across subjects. This approach is good with incomplete data
sets with missing observations but does not account for correlation in the data set
and, therefore, violates the ”i.i.d” assumption. Multiple instance learning (MIL) is
the fourth approach; it uses a non-standard supervised learning method and labelled
bags, each with many observations. This approach is flexible for different numbers
of follow-ups but assumes that data observations inside a bag are ”i.i.d,” losing the
sequential nature of the data in the model. Finally, the multi-task learning (MTL)
approach suggests creating a separate model for each recorded follow-up time. The
models are trained together, each model provides a prediction, and the predictions
are aggregated by majority voting or other similar method. This approach is flexible
but requires many observations at each follow-up time to train the model properly.
The most commonly used approaches are the SF, followed by sequential LF and
then non-sequential LF [47].
In a recent study, researchers conducted a comprehensive investigation to assess
various extensions of the standard RF algorithm suitable for handling longitudi-
nal data in the context of classification and regression trees. In scenarios where
the data exhibits SF and lacks time effect, the study identified a critical approach
in subject-level bootstrapping techniques to address the SF effect effectively. For
situations where the time effect is essential, the study explored extensions derived
from generalised linear mixed models and R packages for RF that can be used on
longitudinal data. Notably, only a restricted set of methods demonstrated eﬀicacy
in analysing high-dimensional data, highlighting a gap in available techniques for
such datasets. Furthermore, the examination emphasised the usefulness of variable
importance measures, a distinctive and crucial feature inherent in the standard RF
for variable selection.
The study advocates for the ongoing development of this
measure within the context of longitudinal data analysis to enhance its applicability
and relevance in such scenarios [41].
Another recent study explored dynamic prediction, incorporating longitudinal in-
formation for time-to-event outcomes, focusing on using RSF within a landmarking
12
==PAGE:25==
framework.
Current dynamic prediction methods include joint modelling, which
involves defining models for both the longitudinal marker process and the survival
outcome, and landmarking, a technique for generating dynamic predictions over time
and typically relies on a proportional hazards model. In this study, RSF considers
the longitudinal nature of biomarker values, offering updated survival predictions
during follow-up. The study compared RSF landmarking with Cox landmarking and
joint modelling. RSF outperformed in scenarios with complex relationships between
survival and longitudinal markers. The variable importance was utilised and offered
valuable information for assessing which features had a greater impact on the model
over time [48].
In conclusion, many ways of working with longitudinal data in machine learning
involve some data aggregation, resulting in potential information loss. RSF stands
out for its ability to navigate the complexities of longitudinal datasets without ag-
gregation, offering a potential solution to minimise information loss and providing
valuable insights for improved research results.

2.6.3
Measure of traﬀic exposure
Traﬀic exposure varied among the nine studies. Six studies used distance to major
roads as an exposure [10, 11, 8, 50, 49, 52], and four studies focused on air pollu-
tion measures [14, 51, 15]. Two studies, Yankoty et al. (2021) and Roswall et al.
(2017) considered traﬀic-related noise and other exposure measures, air pollution
and distance to major roads, respectively [8, 14].
Among studies using distance as an exposure, methodologies differed. For instance,
a study by Tonne et al. (2007) employed cumulative traﬀic within 100-meter radius
and distance to the nearest road measured in kilometres but only had information
on participants’ current residence with no historical data [11]. Hart et al. (2013)
investigated changes in exposure, examining differences in incident MI and all-cause
mortality for individuals living close, moving closer, moving away, or constantly
living far from a road [10]. Yankoty et al. (2021) categorised distances into groups
[8]. Ward-Caviness et al. (2018) applied a logarithm transform to distances before
categorising the distances into groups, considering the primary residence location in
regions defined by traﬀic exposure zones and classifying regions according to traﬀic
type [50]. Kulick et al. (2018) similarly applied a logarithmic transformation to
distances before categorisation to better approximate decay in traﬀic-related air
pollution as distance to major roads increases [49].
Two studies used a five-year rolling average over pollutants. The first study em-
ployed dispersion calculations to obtain annual mean levels of air pollution metrics,
calculating a mean exposure for five-year time windows [15].
The second study
utilised a residential five-year running average exposure of four traﬀic-related pollu-
tants [51]. One study used a time-weighted average for periods of one, five and ten
years for traﬀic noise and air pollution for each residential address [14].

2.6.4
Main findings of existing literature
Several studies consistently demonstrated a positive association between AMI and
traﬀic-related environmental factors. A study by Roswall et al. (2017) demonstrated
a positive association with AMI, utilising exposure to both air pollution and traﬀic
noise, exhibiting a positive association with AMI. The strongest association was
observed in combined medium/high exposure [14]. Similarly, a study by Rosenlund
et al. (2009) showed an increased risk for first-time fatal myocardial infarction due
to long-term air pollution exposure [15].
A cohort study by Hart et al.
(2013)
consisting of US female nurses consistently living close to traﬀic faced a higher
risk of incident MI, emphasising the impact of long-term effects of traﬀic-related
pollutants [10]. Moreover, a study by Tonne et al. (2007) provided further evidence
supporting this association, revealing that increased cumulative traﬀic near homes
was associated with a 4% higher odds of AMI and living near a major roadway was
associated with a 5% increase per kilometre in AMI risk [11].
While several studies highlighted a positive association between AMI and proximity
to roads or environmental factors, a few studies did not find a significant connection
with AMI when considering these factors. For instance, a study by Ward-Caviness
et al. (2018) conducted in North Carolina, USA, between 2001 and 2011 reported
no association between MI and traﬀic exposure when examining distance metrics.
The study specifically focused on the distance between the primary residence and
the nearest major road. While no association with MI was found, the study did re-
veal associations with other cardiovascular diseases, such as peripheral disease and
hypertension. These findings emphasise the nuanced and varied nature of the re-
lationship between environmental factors and cardiovascular health outcomes [50].
Moreover, research conducted by Kulick et al. (2018) within the Northern Manhat-
tan Study (NOMAS) cohort found no clear association between proximity to major
roadways and incident AMI. However, they found that proximity to the major roads
was associated with a higher risk of ischemic stroke [49].
Adjustments were made in these studies to account for potential confounding vari-
ables and to isolate the specific impact of environmental exposures on incident AMI.
All the studies included age and sex, while most included education, income and oc-
18
==PAGE:31==
cupation as socioeconomic features. Moreover, several studies included risk factors
for cardiovascular diseases like smoking, alcohol use, physical activity, hypertension,
body mass index, and diabetes [49, 10, 50, 52, 15].
In the examined studies, diverse statistical models were employed. Logistic Regres-
sion was a commonly utilised method, evident in studies such as Rosenlund et al.
(2009), Yankoty et al. (2021), Kulick et al. (2018), Hoffmann et al. (2009) and
Tonne et al. (2007) [15, 8, 49, 52, 11]. This technique is valuable for binary out-
come variables, making it suitable for investigating incident AMI concerning traﬀic
exposure. CPH model, a survival analysis technique, was employed in Roswall et al.
(2017), Hart et al. (2013), and Ward-Caviness et al. (2018) [14, 10, 50]. This model
is particularly adept at handling time-to-event data, allowing for the exploration of
changes in risk over time. Additionally, a study by Poulsen et al. (2023) utilised
the Aalen additive hazards model, another survival analysis method, to evaluate the
impact of pollutants on incident AMI [51]. This method is beneficial for capturing
time-varying exposures and their influence on event occurrences.
2.6.5
Limitations of existing literature
In addressing the existing literature gap, it is noteworthy that most of the studies
using distance as an exposure metric did not incorporate long-term exposure or
residential changes. Instead, they treated distance as a fixed measure. Conversely,
the two studies employing long-term exposures by applying weighted averages did
not focus on distance measures. One study measured air pollution and noise levels
separately and used both as exposures in the models [14]. The other study only
focused on air pollution as the primary exposure, not accounting for the effects of
traﬀic noise [51]. This leaves a gap in exploring the effects of long-term exposure
distances to capture the evolving nature of residential proximity to major roads over
time. This addition can provide a deeper understanding of the combined effects of
traﬀic-related air pollution and noise.
Considering that only two out of the six studies using distance to road as an exposure
variable reported significant associations with AMI [10, 11]. Both studies include
distance to major roads as a measure of exposure, with an additional secondary
19
==PAGE:32==
measure of exposure to capture the effect of traﬀic air pollution by including either
NO2 levels or cumulative traﬀic within a 100-meter radius, a distance shown in
previous literature to be predictive of traﬀic pollutants PM2.5 and NO2. Tonne
et al. (2007) do include time-varying data, not taking into account how long the
individuals have lived close to a road but use cumulative values as proxies for long-
term residential exposure [11], and Hart et al. (2013) explore changes in residential
distance to roads and the association to AMI [10]. These two measures are quite
different and suggest room for improvement in study methodologies to capture the
effects of residential proximity by exploring a wider range of exposure metrics.
Studies that used distance to major roads as an exposure metric all used similar
assessment, distance to the nearest road from residential address displayed as a
categorical scale or as a straight line distance [8, 49, 11, 52, 10]. Two studies used
logarithm transform on distance measures to better approximate decay in traﬀic-
related air pollution as distance to major roads increases [50, 49]. Other means
of exposure metrics have yet to be explored, allowing for an investigation of other
possible exposure metrics that can capture the effects of having a residential address
in close proximity to major roads.
Interestingly, the nine studies applied either survival models (CPH or Aalen addic-
tive hazard) or Logistic regression models in their analysis. No one has applied more
complex machine learning models such as RF, support vector machines, or neural
networks. However, as seen in chapter 2.5, the application of many different machine
learning models in a variety of health science objectives has been shown to deliver
promising results. Inviting for an investigation of more complex machine learning
models in the analysis of the association between long-term residential exposure to
major roads and incidence of AMI.

3.1.1
Address and road data
The study acquired address and road data from a public geographical data source
administered by the Danish authorities, specifically The Danish Agency for Data

3.1.2
Danish national registers
Every individual with permanent residence in Denmark receives a unique personal
identification number, known as a CPR number [55]. This CPR number facilitates
long-term follow-up of individuals, linking information to an individual from multiple
registers and allowing for a wide range of research on the Danish population. This
section briefly outlines the Danish national registers utilised in this study [56].
The Danish Civil Registration System (CRS)
The CRS was established in 1968, where all persons alive and residing in Denmark
are registered for administrative purposes. The register generally has high cover-
age and quality and is the backbone of research. It includes personal identification
number, sex, date of birth, marital status, country of origin, and residential ad-
dress. It is updated yearly on December 31, with a range of information about the
population residing in Denmark, both as individuals and as part of families and
households. Additionally, the CRS register contains information about all immigra-
tions and emigrations from 1969 and onwards. It encompasses information about
the origins of individuals in three categories: native Danes, immigrants and descen-
dants. An individual is categorised as an immigrant if they were born abroad and
neither of their parents holds Danish citizenship nor were born in Denmark. If there
is no available information about either parent, and the individual is born abroad,
they are also classified as immigrants. An individual is categorised as a descendant
if they are born in Denmark and one or both of their parents are immigrants [55].
The Danish Register of Causes of Death (RCD)
The Danish National Board of Health has maintained a registry encompassing all
deaths in Denmark since 1875. Since 1970, all registrations have been electronic,
and the categorization of the causes of death has consistently followed the guidelines
set forth by WHO. It has utilised the 10th International Classification of Diseases
22
==PAGE:35==
(ICD-10) codes since 1994. The register contains information on all deaths among
persons residing in Denmark, regardless of whether the death occurred in Denmark
or abroad. The accuracy of data concerning the causes of death relies mainly on the
accuracy of physicians’ notifications and the coding process conducted by the Danish
National Board of Health. A reduced autopsy rate in Denmark could impact the
consistency and validity of cause-specific mortality data; however, this effect may
vary across different diagnoses [57].
The Danish National Patient Register (NPR)
The NPR was established in 1977 and is considered the most comprehensive national
hospital register internationally. It provides access to a wide range of administrative
information and clinical data concerning patients’ interactions with hospitals in Den-
mark. Within this register, individual profiles encompass various details, including
dates of referral, admission, and discharge at the hospital. The dataset originated
from reports submitted by public hospitals and was filtered on specific ICD codes to
use for AMI. Specifically, ICD-10 code I21 and ICD-8 code 410 serve as indicators for
AMI. These codes are markers for AMI incidents within hospital contact files, pro-
viding a standardised and reliable basis for identifying relevant cases. By isolating
records containing these codes, the dataset enables the identification of individuals
who have experienced incidence of AMI, with the date of admission representing the
date of AMI occurrence. Hospitals categorize the diagnoses into primary (A) and
additional (B) diagnoses. Individuals may have multiple additional diagnoses, but
the hospital considers the first contact associated with AMI, whether classified un-
der category A or B. AMI is always accompanied by health-related measurements,
such as ECG, biomarkers and cardiac imaging, ensuring a definitive identification
[58].

Registers from Statistics Denmark
The individual-level socioeconomic data used in this study is obtained from regis-
ters at Statistics Denmark, an organization that collects, compiles, and publishes
statistics about the Danish society.

The Danish Education Register (PER)
The PER has information about individuals’ highest completed education.
For
the period 1980-2007, the register includes information on the highest completed

The ISR goes back to 1970. It includes information on income for anyone in the

Data management
This section outlines the study design. Various distance calculations were employed

3.2.1
Study design
The Danish national registers, road data from SDFI, and registers from Statistics
Denmark provided extensive data for this population-based open cohort study. The
study period was selected to account for the newest available information and a ten-
year study period, resulting in a study period between 2012 and 2021. The open
cohort study design was chosen for its ability to allow enrollment into the study
at different points during the study period. Leveraging data availability and the
24
==PAGE:37==
longitudinal data structure, a historical assessment of each individual at the start
of the study allows for inclusion criteria based on individuals’ historical conditions
to ensure comparability among the entire study population.
The study design imposed the following four challenges that needed to be addressed:
1. Ensure that individuals included in the study have been exposed to the same
overall life conditions based on standards in Denmark.
2. Ensure that individuals have reached an age considered relevant regarding
incident AMI.
3. Incident AMI, before the study started, are not of interest, only first-time AMI
cases that occur within the study period.
4. Municipality codes changed in 2007 and therefore required an assessment of
whether that data was suﬀicient.
The study design was tailored to address the challenges mentioned here above. To
address the initial issue, a ten-year residency requirement was implemented to en-
sure that individuals included in the study had resided in Denmark for at least ten
consecutive years before enrollment. This was done because individuals who have
recently moved to Denmark might have experienced diverse living conditions and
different exposures compared to those residing in Denmark consistently. The ab-
sence of health data during the period individuals resided abroad poses a risk of
overlooking potential cases of AMI. This variation in living situations could intro-
duce confounding factors and impact the study’s accuracy. Due to the same reason,
individuals who emigrated during the study period were excluded on the day of
emigration and were not allowed to re-enter the study. Since they would need to
re-establish their residency in Denmark for another ten years, which is not feasible
within the study time frame of 2012 to 2021, this criterion effectively eliminated the
possibility of repeated participation.
The second issue was addressed by restricting the study population to individuals
aged 30 years or older, as AMI is relatively uncommon among younger age groups.
This meant that only individuals who had already reached 30 years of age at the
25
==PAGE:38==
start of the study or attained this age at any point during the study period were
included. This approach ensured that the study focused on individuals more likely
to experience AMI based on age, thus providing more reliable and generalisable
results. As participants can join the study at any time after turning 30, it prevents
the average age from rising during the study period. This is because individuals
who entered the study early on will continue to age, while new participants may
be younger than the average. This shift in age distribution is expected to reflect
the natural ageing of the Danish population and will not affect the validity of the
study’s findings.
Individuals with a history of AMI before the study were intentionally excluded from
the study population. This was done as the emphasis of the study is to analyse first-
time incident AMI. In 2007, municipality codes in Denmark changed. The dataset
with distance exposure was generated using the updated municipality codes. For the
study’s purpose and requirements mentioned above, distance data from 2007 and
onward was considered adequate. To provide a more thorough historical context,
the average distance for each individual was computed over a previous five-year
period. Consequently, the study period began in 2012, ensuring a five-year history of
exposure data available. The choice of a five-year average aligns with the assumption
that AMI, similar to other health conditions, take time to develop, enhancing the
study’s ability to capture meaningful trends and associations over time.
Figure 3.1 visually illustrates the study design. In conclusion, the present study
is an open cohort study including adults (age ≥30 years) with no prior history of
AMI and a ten-year consecutive residential history in Denmark. The study period
is from January 1st 2012 to December 31st 2021. Data dating back to 2002 was
used to derive a ten-year residential history, and data dating back to 2007 was used
to calculate the five-year average of exposure variables.

3.2.2
Distance calculation
The objective of this study is to focus on major roads, which is divided into highways
and large roads, consequently including roads characterised by heavy traﬀic while
omitting smaller roads with lower traﬀic volume. By applying these criteria, the
SDFI dataset was filtered to include records matching the specified status, traﬀic
type, and road category attributes, ensuring that it contained road data relevant
to the research and analysis. Two distinct methodologies assess the proximity to
roads: measuring the straight line distance to the nearest road and counting the
roads within a specified radius. Figure 3.2 visually represents the roads used in this
project, showing a colour split between large roads and highways for distinction.
The road network data and data on residential addresses in Denmark could identify
information on residential proximity to roads.
Initial analysis of the road data
showed complications regarding distance calculations. The road dataset included
information about road segments that together build up a specific road. Although
part of the same road, these road segments were classified differently regarding
traﬀic type and road category. It means some residential addresses are located near
large roads and highways with a shorter distance to the large road, resulting in
the program only recording the distance to the large road and vice versa. This
complication further supported the decision to examine highways and large roads
separately, capturing broader distance exposures and a higher level of detail in the
analysis.

The road and address data were loaded into QGIS, a Geographic Information Sys-
tem (GIS) software. The straight line distance from each address to the nearest
road was calculated, utilising joinbynearest function in QGIS. A maximum distance
of 5,000 meters was selected to limit the function’s runtime, as the function was
computationally heavy. Then, the count of roads within a 50 and 100-meter radius
of each address was calculated. The selection of radius sizes relied on prior papers
and research, suggesting that the influence of air pollution diminishes notably after
around 100 meters [61, 30], and the presumed reduction in noise levels is at least 3
dB each time the distance to the road is doubled [35]. This analysis was performed
in Python using the GeoPandas library, which extends the capabilities of the Pandas
library to handle GIS data. A 50 and 100-meter radius were generated around each
address, allowing for the count of roads within the radius zone. All addresses in
the datasets underwent distance calculations and road counts, and the procedure

expected to be significantly impacted by traﬀic-related pollution or noise levels.

Distance exposure
This study uses a representation of straight-line distances as an exposure, not util-
ising measures of air pollution, noise levels or traﬀic density to capture the overall
effects of residential proximity to roads. As mentioned in section 1.1, the study’s
objective is to explore two different exposure types: distance to roads in meters and
number of roads within a specific radius. The following section describes how these
exposure types were derived and represented in the final dataset.
As discussed in section 2.6.5, most previous studies did not incorporate long-term
exposure or changes in residential addresses in their research. It is believed that the
long-term effects of exposure are an essential factor to consider since AMI, like other
health conditions, takes time to develop, and the conditions of individuals change
over time. In an attempt to further explore eﬀicient ways to represent exposure
variables, the incorporation of long-term exposure was derived for the exposure
variables in this study and a five-year historical time window was created for the
two exposure types that could consider changes in residential addresses for each
individual between years in the study period.
The five-year average distance to the nearest major roads, large roads and highways
was categorised into groups to see that could be an informative exposure variable.
Results from previous studies exhibited slight variations in the distance groups asso-
ciated with various diseases [62, 63]. To analyse small distance intervals effectively
and integrate distances from prior studies into the grouped exposure variable, a
distance group for 50-meter intervals was implemented, forming 11 distance groups,
from 0 to 500+ meters.
The five-year average road count was calculated for both counts of roads within
a 50 and 100-meter radius from the residential addresses to see if this representa-
tion of distance exposure could be another informative exposure variable. These
calculations were again performed for major roads, highways, and large roads. The
five-year average road count was calculated for each individual. However, most indi-
viduals had few roads within a 50 and 100-meter radius of their residence; therefore,
these numbers were, in most cases, decimals. A round to the nearest integer was

Outcome variable
The outcome variable, incident AMI, was determined by identifying individuals

the Ndays_in_year would be one extra day. To account for individuals who joined and
left the study on the same day, an additional day was added to the numerator of
the risk time calculation. This ensures that these individuals receive a risk time of
one day.

4.1.1
Descriptive statistics
Examining descriptive statistics included the analysis of residential proximity to
roads, the exposure variable, and incident AMI, the outcome variable. The analysis
aimed to understand and compare different variables by utilising statistical variables
such as incidence proportion, incidence rates, and incidence rate ratio (IRR). Addi-

Incidence proportion, incidence rate and incidence rate ratio
Incidence is a measure that quantifies the number of cases of a disease for a particular
study population of individuals that are at risk during a specified time interval [64].
This study applies three incidence measures: incidence proportion, incidence rate
and IRR. Below is a summary and description of how they are derived.
The incidence rate is the total number of incident AMI over the study period, 2012
to 2021, divided by the total person-years at risk for the entire study population.
Therefore, it measures the ratio between the number of new cases and the total time
the study population is at risk. The general formula for incidence rate is expressed
by Formula 4.1 [65]. This study is an open cohort study, with varying follow-up times
for individuals within the study population due to differences in entry and exit times
for individuals. Some individuals do not qualify for the study population at the start
of the study. Consequently, they gain admission later when they reach a suﬀicient
age or have completed a ten-year residential history. Additionally, individuals exit
the study at varying times due to either death, emigration or incidence of AMI.
Each individual’s time at risk is derived to account for these varying follow-up
times. The person-time at risk, defined in person-years, has a value of 1, indicating
that individuals were in the study for the entire year. When individuals become
censored, the person-time at risk is the fraction of the censoring year. IRR is used
to compare rates between different groups. The Formula 4.2 presents how the IRR
is calculated.

4.1.3
Poisson regression of incidence rate
The study examines whether variations in the proximity of residential addresses
to major roads contribute to an elevated risk of AMI incidents, accounting for the
effect of relevant confounders. The examination assesses the IRR for incidents of
AMI using Poisson regression. The IRRs compare the incidence rate of AMI for
each exposure group with the reference group, which is estimated to have the lowest
exposure. This study includes three models with different exposure variables:
- Distance to major roads grouped by 50 meters
- Number of major roads within a 50-meter radius
- Number of major roads within a 100-meter radius
The analysis employed R version 2023.06.1 to conduct Poisson Regression.
The
model belongs to a family of generalised linear models [66]. The general form of the
Poisson regression equation is [67]:

4.2
Random Survival Forest analysis
RSF is an extension of Breiman’s RF method, specifically designed to handle right-
censored survival data. RSF follows the same principles as RF, employing boot-
strapping, random feature selection, deep tree growth, and averaging terminal node
statistics for predictions.
RF utilises randomness in two distinct forms, first by
drawing random bootstrap samples to grow individual trees and, secondly, by ran-
domly selecting a subset of variables as candidates for splitting in nodes of a tree. By
introducing additional randomness, RSF can capture complex relationships between
variables while preventing overfitting and enhancing performance [43].
The RSF algorithm follows a series of steps. Initially, it draws a predefined number
of random bootstrap samples from the entire dataset, excluding approximately 37%
in each sample, referred to as the Out-Of-Bag (OOB) sample. For each bootstrap
sample, the algorithm grows a survival tree. Growing the tree involves randomly
selecting k variables as candidates for splitting, where k is generally set to the
square root of the number of variables. The variable that maximises the survival
difference between the right and left daughter nodes is selected to split the tree.
This process repeats until a tree is grown to full size, ensuring that each terminal
45
==PAGE:58==
node includes a predefined number of unique events. Once a tree has reached full
size, the algorithm calculates each tree’s cumulative hazard function (CHF). The
CHF for the entire forest is then obtained by averaging the CHF over all the trees
in the forest. Subsequently, the OOB sample is utilised to calculate the prediction
error for the ensemble forest [43].
The best node split is found by searching over all variables x and their respective
split values c, choosing the x and c that maximise survival difference between the
two daughter nodes. Maximising the survival difference, the tree separates the nodes
into unlike groups of cases. As the tree grows, each node becomes homogeneous,
including cases with similar survival [70]. Splitting criteria when growing a tree
involves survival time and censoring information. In this study, the survival event
of interest is the incidence of AMI, and right censoring could happen if an individual
emigrates, dies, or the study ends before an AMI event occurs. To handle this, the
representation of time is adjusted.
Instead of using the observed time (T), the
minimum of the observed time (To) and censoring time (Co) is used, using the time
either individuals get censored or the actual event, AMI, occurs.
A censoring indicator (δ) notes whether the observed time is due to the event hap-
pening or because of censoring. If the event occurs δ equals 1, if censoring occurs δ
equals 0. The RSF data format is (T1, X1, δ1), . . . , (Tn, Xn, δn) for each observation,
where Xi is the feature vector for observation i [43].
The prediction error is expressed as 1−C, where C is Harrell’s concordance index.
The prediction error ranges from 0 to 1, with a lower value indicating better per-
formance. This metric evaluates how well the model predicts the order of survival
times. The OOB sample is used to estimate the prediction error by dropping an
OOB sample down the grown tree and averaging over the forest [71].
RSF model was employed due to its ability to handle survival data, enabling pre-
dictions of time-to-event outcomes using longitudinal data. RSF was implemented
using the randomForestSRC package in R [70].

4.2.1
Model tuning and implementation
The dataset utilised in this study was rather large, introducing computational chal-
lenges. The model needed tuning to handle the large dataset better. Optimal tuning
settings were found by utilising a stratified subset to reduce the size of the in-sample
data used to grow a tree, thereby reducing computational load and runtime. The
different tuning parameters are described in this chapter.
RSF can employ two different sampling methods to grow the tree, by adjusting
the models samptype. The process randomly selects a subject in the sampling with
replacement method (swr).
It returns it to the population before sampling the
next subject, allowing the possibility of choosing subjects multiple times. On the
other hand, in sampling without replacement (swor), the random sampling method
prohibits the return of selected subjects. As a result, sampled subjects can only be
chosen once [70].
Different splitting rules can be applied when tuning the RSF model, by adjusting the
models splitrule. The splitting must be considered with the censoring of individuals
in mind.
Therefore, the node is split into daughter nodes with unalike survival
behaviour to maximize the difference between the two.
The blocksize determines when the cumulative error is computed. Setting it equal
to the number of trees ensures that the error is calculated only once, accelerating
the model’s runtime.
When working with a large dataset a selection of a large
nodesize can speed up the model and provide better performance.
To find the
optimal value the tune.nodesize() function in R can be applied. By default the model
saves terminal node quantities to use when calculating prediction for the OOB data,
this can be adjusted by tuning the save.memory, and increase computational speed.
Finally, ntime can be tuned to decrease runtime of the model.
By adjusting to
a relatively small number the computational time provided for survival ensemble
calculations is limited, allowing for improvements in runtime [72].
4.2.2
Selection of exposure variables
Person correlation coeﬀicient (PCC) is a standard metric for measuring linear de-
pendency between variables. It can be derived for continuous and binary variables,
47
==PAGE:60==
making it a flexible metric. When PCC is derived, it takes a value between 0 and 1,
where a PCC value close to 0 indicates linear independence between variables. In
contrast, a value close to 1 indicates a linear dependency between variables. The
PCC values only give insights into linear dependencies, non-linear dependencies be-
tween variables remain unknown [73]. Spearman correlation coeﬀicient (SCC) is a
non-parametric version of the PCC. Instead of measuring a strictly linear relation-
ship as PCC, SCC measures the strength and direction of a relationship, providing
insights into monotonic relationships between two variables [74].
Utilising the SCC, the analysis examined the relationship between the exposure
variables. Machine learning models handle correlated variables differently, and, for
the most part, including two variables with high correlation as features in a machine
learning model is considered bad practice. However, RF and RSF are rather good
at handling highly correlated variables [42]. This was kept in mind when analysing
the results of SCC for the exposure variables and the final selection of exposure
variables for the RSF model.
4.2.3
Variable importance
Two examinations were employed to evaluate the significance of each feature within
the RSF model.
This analysis enabled a comparative study of various distance
exposure variables, revealing distinct impacts on outcomes in the survival model for
AMI.
Permutation importance
Permutation (Breiman-Cutler) importance was employed, representing a prediction-
based approach that utilises the prediction error from OOB cases. This technique
involves permuting a variable’s OOB data and comparing the resulting OOB predic-
tion error to the original OOB prediction error. The underlying motivation is that
a large positive value indicates a variable of high importance for predictions in the
model, while zero or negative values identify noise variables [71]. The general idea
is that the higher a split for a variable is in the tree the greater effect permutation
has on the terminal nodes. Meaning that the permuted variable is more likely to be
further away from its original terminal node and therefore has higher effect on the
prediction error [75].

4.3
Sensitivity analysis
To further evaluate the model results, three different sensitivity analyses took place.
In the first analysis, the Poisson regression model calculated the distance to roads
separately for highways and large roads. The second analysis explored the potential
improvement of results for the RSF model by narrowing the study population to
individuals with a residential address within 700 meters from roads. Finally, the
third analysis tested an alternative representation of the exposure variable in the
RSF model, transforming distance to a logarithm scale.
4.3.1
Split roads into highways and large roads
One research question for this study is analysing the optimal inclusion of proximity
to the road as an exposure variable. Road distances were calculated for the two sub-
types of roads, highways and large roads, to analyse potential differences between
road types. Highways are different from large roads; generally, the traﬀic speed is
faster and higher traﬀic volumes that subsequently lead to higher noise levels in
areas surrounding highways [79]. The sensitivity analysis compares results from the
Poisson regression model for distance calculations split by highways and large roads.
4.3.2
Excluding residential addresses far away
This study aims to analyse the association between residing near roads and incident
AMI. Previous studies have shown that traﬀic-related air pollutants decrease sig-
nificantly after 100-500 meters [31, 30]. In order to evaluate the robustness of the
RSF outcomes, an analysis was conducted to determine the impact of excluding res-
idential addresses that are an average of 700 meters or more away from a road. By
doing so, the focus of the analysis was solely on individuals living in close proximity
to major roads. This step was taken to evaluate if including individuals residing
further away from roads would negatively impact the model’s performance.
4.3.3
Logarithm transform on exposure variables
The exposure variables for average distances were transformed using the logarithm
(log()) to analyse the optimal inclusion of proximity to roads. This choice drew
motivation from studies conducted by Ward et al. (2018) and Kulick et al. (2018)
[50, 49], discussed in Section 2.6.3. These studies applied the logarithm to better

increases. The sensitivity analysis compares results from the RSF model for the
original road distance calculations and distances transformed to logarithm scale.

5.5
Random Survival Forest analysis
In this section, the study employs RSF analysis to answer the research question of
how important residential proximity to major roads is for analysing the incidence
of AMI. First, the exposure variables chosen for the model are introduced, followed
by a description of how the model was implemented and the parameters selected
to optimize performance and computational eﬀiciency. Next, an examination of the
most important variables in the model is presented. Results from sensitivity analyses
are presented to give insights into the robustness of the model. To conclude the
section, the dataset is evaluated for different subset sizes.
5.5.1
Variable selection
Before generating an RSF model, it was necessary to determine which exposure
variables should be included in the model.
Although RSF can handle multiple
correlated variables simultaneously, as mentioned in section 4.2.2, the aim was to
avoid including nearly identical highly correlated variables. A correlation matrix was
utilised to identify the correlation between variables and see which exposure variables
could be incorporated into the model simultaneously. It was decided to disregard
exposure variables for the groups of 50 meters since the RSF model can handle
continuous variables, which are considered more informative and accurate distance
measures. The exposure variables considered for the RSF model are presented in
the correlation plot in Figure 5.9.
69
==PAGE:82==
Figure 5.9: Correlation between the exposure variables, all indicating a five-year
average.
As indicated by the dark blue colours on the correlation plot, few variables are
highly correlated. Notably, the road count within a 50-meter radius for major and
large roads is entirely correlated, meaning that these exposure variables will not be
used simultaneously in the model. The same pattern can be seen for the road count
within a 100-meter radius for major and large roads. Additionally, a high correlation
exists between road count for 50 and 100-meter radius, and therefore, they will not
be used simultaneously in the model. For these reasons, it was decided to disregard
the road count within a 100-meter radius and instead include the road count for a
50-meter radius.
The model incorporates demographic and socioeconomic factors, including age, sex,
education, family income, cohabitation, ethnicity, and year. Four exposure variables
70
==PAGE:83==
were selected, all representing a five-year rolling average.
• Five-year average distance to the nearest highway
• Five-year average distance to the nearest large road
• Five-year average number of highways within a 50-meter radius
• Five-year average number of large roads within a 50-meter radius
The outcome variable in the model is defined as the survival time until the incident
AMI. The survival time is denoted as risk time, and the outcome variable is binary,
with 1 indicating that an individual experienced an incidence of AMI during the
study period, while 0 indicates that the incidence of AMI event did not occur during
the observed period.
5.5.2
Model implementation
This section explores the RSF model’s implementation, highlighting essential steps
in creating subsamples, model tuning, and interpretation. Two methods, variable
importance and partial effects, are then used to evaluate distance variables’ effects
in the models.
Creation of subsamples
Computational resources constrained the execution of the RSF model on the entire
dataset. Therefore, a decision was made to work with a subset of the data. Strati-
fication was applied based on AMI events to ensure that the outcome variable was
evenly represented. Stratifying based on the nearest road distance was also per-
formed to achieve a balanced representation of the exposure variable. To capture
diverse demographics, stratification by sex and age was carried out, acknowledging
potential variations in survival patterns across these variables.

out on 0.1% of the data. This involved testing various split rules to understand their
impact on the OOB performance error and computational eﬀiciency. Table 5.10

run times. The LogRank split rule was the most effective, it achieved the lowest

Chapter 6
Discussion
This chapter evaluates the most suitable distance-based exposure variables for as-
sessing incident AMI. It starts by summarizing the results of both Poisson regression
and RSF analyses and compares them to existing research. Potential biases affect-
ing the study population and exposure variables are then addressed, along with a
discussion of model selection and performance. Finally, improvements are proposed
in assessing exposure variables and ideas for future research are set forward.
6.1
Main findings
A comprehensive analysis of residential proximity to major roads and AMI incidents
revealed several key findings. The dataset included 2,228 major roads, categorised
into 2,098 large roads and 130 highways. On average, the distance to the nearest
highway was significantly higher (2,970 meters) than the distance to the nearest
large road (688 meters).
Consequently, only a few individuals had a large road
within a 50 or 100-meter radius, whereas even fewer had highways within the same
proximity. This suggests that the study population mainly consisted of individuals
with limited exposure to major roads, especially highways.
Poisson regression analysis of distance to major roads was inconclusive. However,
when divided into road types, the results for distance to the highway of 0-50 meters
showed a stronger association (IRR 1.22). Results for large roads were similar to
the major road results for 0-50 meters (IRR 1.03), which is unsurprising as large
80
==PAGE:93==
roads make up most of the major roads. Living within 50 meters of one highway
(IRR 1.26) or two highways (IRR 1.50) showed an increased association with AMI
incidents. Residing within 50 meters of large roads did not demonstrate as strong
an association with incident AMI. When using an extended radius of 100 meters,
the association became less significant for both highways and large roads.
This
reveals that living very close to a highway (around 0-100 meters) shows a possible
increased association with AMI while living further away from a road diminishes
the association.
The variable importance analysis from the RSF model revealed that age, sex, and
education were the most important predictors of incident AMI. These results align
with the baseline Table 5.1, where these three factors exhibited the most pronounced
differences in AMI incidence proportions. The consistency of these findings with the
variable importance analysis provides further support for the validity of the RSF
model in capturing the relationships between these variables and incident AMI. The
most important exposure variable was the distance to large roads. However, after
excluding individuals who lived further than 700 meters from roads, the distance to
the highway was slightly more important. Expectations were that the significance of
highways would become more pronounced after excluding greater distances; however,
the limited number of people in the dataset living very close to a highway made it
more challenging to assess the impact of residing in such proximity.
Therefore,
to further explore the effects of close distances, the partial effects were applied.
Revealing that for a distance of 50 meters from a highway, the overall survival rate
for the study population would be lower than for higher distances. Large roads also
show this trend, although less extreme.
6.2
Comparison to previous findings
The results of the present study show some consistency with previous findings from
studies conducted in Denmark. Although not incorporating distance as an exposure
variable, two previous studies in Denmark encompass time-averaged traﬀic-related
exposures. The results of these studies are compared to the findings of the present
study on incident AMI in Denmark to give insights into the topic within the Danish
81
==PAGE:94==
population.
A study conducted by Poulsen et al. (2023) in Denmark from 2005 to 2017 on a
cohort aged 50 years and older revealed an increased risk of MI associated with air
pollution. The study calculated a five-year average exposure to four different traﬀic
pollutants (PM2.5, UFP, EC, NO2) for each residence. Additionally, a weighted
sound pressure estimate combined as Lden was calculated for 2000, 2005, 2010 and
2015 and included as three-level categorical variables. Results showed that PM2.5
and UFP were most associated with incident AMI. The study clearly states that
individuals with lower socio-economic status are more affected by long-term expo-
sure to air pollution [51]. A study by Roswall et al. (2017) further supports this
association. The researchers modelled noise levels (Lden) and air pollution of NO2
for all residential addresses for a cohort aged between 50-64 years old between 1997
and 2011. Historical residential addresses were obtained from national registers, and
time-weighted averages for noise and air pollution levels were derived for periods of
one, five, and ten years, respectively. The findings revealed that both exposures
increased the risk of MI, with a stronger association with fatal MI [14].
Both studies used the same ICD codes to identify incident AMI, which were also
used in the current study. Like the current study, the first study had strict inclusion
criteria regarding residential history in Denmark. The present study found that
long-term residential proximity within 50 meters of a highway increases incidence
of AMI. These three studies include different exposures to road proximity, and the
two previous studies did not include distance to roads as a measure. However, these
studies show a likely trend in Denmark between residential road proximity and the
incidence of AMI.
6.2.1
Difference in exposure metrics
The association of exposure metrics investigated in the present study included the
straight line distance to the nearest major road, split into highways or large roads,
and major road count within 50 and 100-meter radius, also split into highways
and large roads. All exposure metrics were calculated over a five-year historical
average for all residential addresses in the study each year.
The incidence rate
82
==PAGE:95==
of AMI increased as the number of highways within a 50-meter radius increased.
Previous studies have yet to explore this representation of road exposure. However,
other exposure metrics have been utilised to examine the association of residential
proximity to roads with the incidence of AMI.
Two studies found no association between incidence of AMI and residential proxim-
ity to major roads [49, 50]. The two studies used residential proximity as a measure
of road exposure but did not utilise long-term effects by considering changes in resi-
dency. Kulick et al. (2018) examined residential proximity for distance of 100-meter
intervals on a logarithmic form to highways in the Northern Manhattan area in the
United States. This study was conducted between the years 1993 and 2001 and
included risk factors such as smoking, diabetes mellitus and hypertension. Kulick et
al. (2018) calculated the straight line distance to the nearest highway grouped into
100-meter categories. The study found no association between residential proximity
to highways and MI [49]. This study was limited to the within-city population of
the northern Manhattan area, which is very different to the nationwide population
cohort included in the current study, including a larger pool of residential addresses
with a broader range of exposure levels. Another study conducted by Ward-Caviness
et al. (2018) in North Carolina, USA, between 2001 and 2011 examined the distance
to major roads from residential addresses and association to MI. Distance groups
used in this study were similar to the current study, but the study showed no asso-
ciation between residential proximity to major roads and MI. The cohort included
in this study was relatively small and only included individuals with previous heart
conditions. The differences in the cohorts may explain the different results.
A logarithmic transform has been applied to the nearest distance to roads, a method
performed in previous studies in an attempt to better mimic the decay in air pollu-
tion as distance to roads increases [49, 50]. Kulick et al. (2018) and Ward-Caviness
et al. (2018) used the logarithmic transform to explore the association to AMI and
found no association. The logarithmic transform was applied to all three nearest
distance variables (major roads, highways and large roads) used in the current study
and showed no changes in the association between residential distances to roads and
incident AMI. Both Gilbert et al. (2016) and Pleijel et al. (2004) showed variations
83
==PAGE:96==
of NO2 concentrations with increasing distances from roads. Indicating that loga-
rithmic transform of distance can informatively mimic decay in NO2 concentrations
[62, 63]. However, these studies only examine concentrations of NO2, providing
no information about other traﬀic-related air pollutants, such as PM2.5 and UFP.
Logarithmic transform of distance to roads may provide an informative measure of
decay in NO2, but it is uncertain if this measure is representative of other pollutants.
Results from the current study are consistent with previous research from Tonne et
al. (2007) and Hart et al. (2013), who found an association between residential ad-
dresses located approximately 100 meters from a major road and incidence of AMI
[11, 10]. Tonne et al. (2007) used cumulative traﬀic near residential addresses and
the residential distance from the major roadways as a proxy for traﬀic-related air
pollution. The study was conducted in Massachusetts between 1995 and 2003. The
study found a significant association between exposure to traﬀic near residential
addresses and the occurrences of AMI. Specifically, the study found a stronger as-
sociation with cumulative traﬀic within 100 meter distances compared to distances
of 200 and 300 meters. In support, Hart et al. (2013) reported an association with
the incidence of AMI for those living consistently close to a road. That was defined
as either living within 50 meters from an A3 classified road (secondary road with
more than two lanes) or within 150 meters from A1 and A2 classified highways
(highways with and without limited access). An additional measure of exposure
was included in this study by measuring changes in NO2 between addresses. The
study was conducted between 1990 and 2018, including a population of US female
nurses aged 30 to 55 years old with 20 years of residential history. Varying risk
features were included, along with different health-related factors. These two stud-
ies have varying representations of exposure to roads. However, as in the current
study, a consistent trend is noticeable, where residential proximity under or around
100 meters is consistently associated with incidences of AMI. It must be mentioned
that these two studies are conducted in different countries, and therefore, the study
population may experience other exposures and effects from the environment and
society.

6.2.2
Trends in incidence rates of AMI
A study by Christensen et al. (2022) explored age and sex-specific trends in the
AMI population in Denmark. Results show that incidence of AMI in Denmark for
both males and females had decreased between the years 2005 and 2021. The results
showed a 44% decrease in incidence rate for women and a 28% decrease for males
[18].
This decreasing trend over the years is consistent with the findings in the
current study. The incidence rate decreases are higher than the ones calculated in
the present study for 2012 to 2021 (30% for women and 15% for males). Christensen
et al. (2022) included all individuals above 18 years of age and ICD-10:I21 and
ICD-8:410 were used to define the incidence of AMI. Both studies applied identical
identification methods for AMI events in the NPR. The differences in the incidence
rates in this study and the study by Christensen et al.
(2022) could be due to
differences in periods and different age limits of the study population.
6.2.3
Random Survival Forest results
No previous studies have been found that utilise RSF model to detect the most
relevant or important features in analysing the association between residential prox-
imity to roads and incident AMI. However, existing literature on RSF and different
measures of variable importance in various settings will be compared to the methods
chosen in this study.
Previous studies have compared CPH regression to RSF for predicting mortality in
individuals with heart failure [44] and dyslipidemia [45]. Both models have shown
comparable performance, but RSF was preferred due to its ability to identify risk
factors more robustly [44, 45].
Researchers have utilised permutation importance to evaluate the most crucial pre-
diction features, with RSF consistently demonstrating superior performance com-
pared to CPH [45] or other machine learning models [46]. The current study, em-
ploying a unique set of features compared to the previous two studies, utilises the
importance of RSF and permutation to assess the most critical predictive factors
with success.
While previous studies have compared errors and variable importances between RSF
85
==PAGE:98==
and other models, this study utilises Poisson regression to determine the IRR of the
exposure variable. Subsequently, RSF is employed to evaluate the ranking of these
exposure variables and assess their partial effects, confirming the association without
directly comparing the two methods.
6.3
Strengths and limitations
One of the main strengths of this study was the large sample size offered by the
nationwide register-based population data. The large sample size provides great
statistical power to the analyses and makes it possible to explore rare outcomes.
It also allows for a long and complete follow-up.
The completeness of the data
also minimises the effects of selection bias due to non-response and loss of follow-up.
Moreover, the long follow-up period makes it possible to examine the long-term effect
of living near major roads on AMI. Another strength is linking residential addresses
to the population data and then the chance to adjust for the potential confounders in
the whole population [80]. Limitations involved the lack of computational capacity
that highly limited the amount of analysis performed.
6.3.1
Selection Bias
Improper procedures in selecting a study population cause selection bias, and factors
influencing continued participation in the study can also contribute to this bias. The
issue escalates when there is a disparity in the association between exposure and
outcome for participants and non-participants, making non-participation a potential
problem [81]. The present study included the Danish population aged 30 years or
older with a ten-year residential history in Denmark before inclusion and no history
of AMI event before 2012. Consequently, the broad inclusion of study participants
minimises the risk of selection bias.
Individuals without a ten-year residential history in Denmark were excluded from
the study population. These individuals consisted of around 9% of the initial pop-
ulation. It was not optimal to exclude this significant part of the study population.
However, it was necessary to do so to take into consideration the long-term effect
of living near a road. Individuals who lived outside of Denmark for a year or longer
during the study period were excluded after emigration. This exclusion was based on
86
==PAGE:99==
the uncertainty surrounding their exposure while abroad. The proportion of these
individuals constituted only about 0.3% of the study population. It is not likely
that these individuals differed significantly from the broader population regarding
their exposure in Denmark and risk of developing AMI. Consequently, excluding in-
dividuals emigrating during the study period will unlikely introduce selection bias.
The family income data for the entire year of 2021 was missing. A decision was made
to substitute these missing values with the data from 2020 to maintain a consistent
study period of ten years. The year 2020 was chosen as placement for 2021 since
that year would best replicate. Therefore, the selection bias potentially introduced
was minimised.
6.3.2
Information bias
Information bias can happen when there is a systematic over- or underestimation of
an association, primarily due to inaccuracies in measuring or classifying key study
variables such as exposure, health outcomes, or confounders [81]. Information bias
in this study may have been introduced by misclassifying AMI cases, major roads
or residential addresses. However, since information on exposure for the study pop-
ulation was done independently of any other information, particularly the outcome
variable, bias is minimised. Misclassifying the exposure and outcome will often be
non-differential because it will probably be the same for all population groups and
will therefore tend to underestimate the true association [80].
Misclassification of AMI
One significant strength of this study is the accuracy and reliability of the AMI
diagnosis in the NPR register [58]. The AMI diagnosis in this study relies solely
on clinically recognized AMI events, resulting in hospital admissions that are doc-
umented in the register. Individuals undergo specific processes and measurements
during their hospital visit to obtain an AMI diagnosis. Consequently, some instances
of AMI that do not result in hospitalization may remain undetected. For example, if
an AMI event leading to death occurs at home or in a location other than a hospital.
Misclassification of AMI due to underreporting of AMI incidents would have been
non-differential since it would be the same for the overall population [80].
87
==PAGE:100==
Misclassification of geographical data
As mentioned in section 3.1.1, SDFI is responsible for public geographical data in
Denmark, mapping roads, buildings and other features in the environment. They
provide high-quality data with broad coverage, ensuring low concerns regarding the
validity of geographical data in Denmark [82]. Therefore, misclassifying exposure
due to a lack of positional accuracy is unlikely.
However, it is essential to highlight that the study based its road network data
on information from 2020. It introduces a potential challenge, acknowledging that
the study’s time frame, spanning from 2012 to 2021, may lead to the omission of
newly constructed roads and the absence of updates for existing ones. The study
focused on major roads in Denmark, excluding all other roads. This exclusion poses
a potential challenge in larger cities, as it may omit smaller roads with significant
traﬀic. Nonetheless, one can assume that larger cities generally experience higher
air and noise pollution levels. In this study, a feasibility test of incorporating larger
roads within cities that, despite their size, were not classified as major roads was
conducted. This inclusion resulted in the addition of multiple smaller roads that
were clearly beyond this project’s scope.
Consequently, a decision was made to
restrict the inclusion to only the previously defined major roads in Denmark.
Upon uploading calculated distances to the Statistics Denmark server, the data was
merged with an existing dataset on the server containing information on entrance
addresses, as described in section 3.2.2. For that reason, ensuring that all addresses
belonging to the same entrance had the exact coordinates was necessary. Three ad-
dresses had inaccurate entrance address coordinates, further examination revealed
duplication for these addresses in the raw data. Resulting in the inclusion of only
the newer observations. The data only provided coordinates for the entrance to
a residence, presenting the challenge of missing information regarding whether in-
dividuals reside on the bottom floor or higher up in the building. Roswall et al.
(2017) addressed this issue by incorporating the building’s height (floor) into the
analysis [14]. Furthermore, other findings indicate that specific particulate matter
concentrations vary with height, suggesting that air quality might improve at higher
elevations [83]. The lack of exact coordinates reveals misclassification in the address
88
==PAGE:101==
data, potentially making distance measures less accurate.
6.3.3
Exposure variables
Examining the overall impact of traﬀic-related exposure can be achieved through
distance measures. This method provides a straightforward way to include multiple
factors simultaneously, eliminating the need for extensive data access that might be
challenging. Studies have shown that distance from highways and traﬀic density are
significant predictors of outdoor measurement of some particulate matters [84, 85].
Furthermore, a study by Hochadel et al. (2005) demonstrated a strong correlation
between NO2 and PM2.5 levels and the proximity to roads and traﬀic volume [86].
It indicates that utilising the distance from major roads as a proxy for assessing
exposure to traﬀic-related pollution is prudent. Studies suggest that levels of air
pollutants linked to traﬀic decrease to background concentrations within a range
of 100 to 150 meters from the roadside, as demonstrated by Roordaknape et al.
(1998) [87, 30]. In another study by Tonne et al. (2007) a 100-meter radius from
a residence revealed an association with AMI [11].
These findings suggest that
monitoring traﬀic-related air pollution effectively may require a distance of at least
50 or 100 meters. To make the exposure variable even more informative, a five-year
rolling average was incorporated, making it possible to look at the long-term effects
of living within a certain distance from a road. Previous studies have utilised five-
year average showing an association between AMI and traﬀic-related environmental
exposure [15, 14, 51]; these studies, however, did not use distance as a direct exposure
but a measure of traﬀic-related air pollution and noise levels.
Some risks could be associated with using distance as exposure and not utilizing
measures of air pollution or noise levels, such as including impact from other sources
in the distance measures, thereby affecting and skewing the results and identifying
insightful exposure variables. This situation may arise when a residential address
is near industrial buildings or other means of transport, such as airports or railway
tracks [88].

6.3.4
Confounding
Considering confounding factors in research is essential, as it isolates the real impact
of the exposure on the outcome [89].
Adjusting for confounding factors enables
the accurate identification of the genuine causal relationship between residential
proximity to road and incidents of AMI.
This study incorporated several confounders, such as age, sex, cohabitation status,
ethnicity, income, and education, all known risk factors for AMI [90]. They also
influence where individuals reside and their proximity to major roads. Unevenly
distributing confounders across exposure groups is also necessary; Table 5.3 demon-
strates this distribution of confounders across the exposure groups. While some
variables exhibit a similar distribution pattern in the first ten exposure groups, the
last group shows significantly higher values for these variables.
Variables were either categorical or categorised into groups, requiring careful cat-
egorisation to address potential residual confounding. For instance, family income
was computed by dividing the total disposable income by a factor based on the
family size. Adjustments were made to income categories for retired individuals to
mitigate residual confounding arising from differences in income patterns among this
group. This adjustment aimed to minimise potential confounding effects resulting
from variations in income distribution among retired individuals.
A significant amount of educational data was missing because the data collection
period was limited to individuals who had completed their education before 1974
in Denmark. Consequently, individuals with missing educational information were
more likely to be older or immigrants. This situation could introduce bias into the
study, potentially misrepresenting an observed association between education and
AMI.
Age was divided into ten-year groups to better represent the varying risks across
different age cohorts and accurately reflect the relationship between each factor and
the risk of AMI.
While controlling for confounding factors is essential for Poisson regression [91],
RSF algorithms offer greater flexibility due to their non-parametric nature, implying
90
==PAGE:103==
that they treat variables without modifying or transforming them [70]. As a result,
incorporating more health data into the RSF analysis could have been beneficial for
a more precise estimation of the AMI incidence.

household income, and road density across all municipalities in Denmark.
The
study compared geographical maps of road density and household income with the

patterns between these variables. Geographical mapping in the current study utilises
x- and y-coordinates from the SDFI datasets for all residential addresses and road

A map of incidence rate of AMI incidents (Figure 5.7) showed high differences in

Clusters of mu-

comparing geographical maps for incident AMI and average disposable household

6.3.6
Model performance
The RSF model offers several advantages, including handling complex relationships
between variables without requiring prior knowledge of their underlying nature. It
also effectively prevents overfitting, handles high-dimensional data, and manages
imbalanced data effectively [93]. Moreover, the RSF model makes it straightforward
to see the interactions among variables by utilising variable importance measures,
as illustrated in Figure 5.10. In contrast to variable importance methods that rely
on cross-validation, such as Gini impurity importance, permutation importance uses
OOB data to asses the difference in prediction error. It compares the internal error
to the OOB error obtained in the model, which is good for computational eﬀiciency
and easy to implement.
Several factors may have contributed to the RSF model’s performance limitations.
The RSF model parameters underwent evaluation by testing various configurations
on a small dataset, and as a result, they were not fully optimized. Exhaustive cross-
validation methods could potentially improve parameter tuning, but computational
92
==PAGE:105==
constraints precluded their implementation. To ensure reproducibility, a random
seed was employed when running the RSF models. However, it is important to note
that variable importance measures may exhibit some variability due to the inherent
randomness involved in their calculation [94].
The extensive dataset used in this study necessitated using a subsample for RSF
model execution due to computational limitations. Table 5.13 illustrates that the
RSF model’s runtime increases considerably with larger datasets. Moreover, em-
ploying the model on larger datasets resulted in higher performance errors. When
designing the RSF model for this study, runtime was prioritised as the final goal was
to try to run the model on as large proportion of the dataset as possible, which was
desirable in an attempt to compare with the Poisson regression model outcomes.
This imposes a possible trade-off for prediction and variable importance accuracy.
Number of trees were set to 100, which was considered suﬀicient for 1% of the data.
It is likely that 100 trees are insuﬀicient for the larger subsets and respective num-
ber of observations, since OOB prediction error increased as the subsets grew larger.
Additionally, to few trees can effect the stability of the variable importance estima-
tor, potentially explaining the variations between subset sizes. This shows that the
tuning process and settings for the model, performed on a much smaller subset of
the data, is potentially not optimal for larger subsets.
The five most important variables were ranked in the same order for all subsets,
except for year, in the 3% subset, which outranked the exposure variables. There
is a lack of consistency for the less important variables, possibly explained by the
focus on runtime instead of prediction error when tuning the model. Partial plots
from RSF can eﬀiciently represent the partial effect of exposure variables of interest,
showing the comparison in survival rates between low and high exposures. Figures
5.11 and 5.13 show that living close to a highway results in lower survival rates
compared with living at a greater distance.
In conclusion, the study revealed that highways, particularly in close proximity,
were associated with incident AMI. Poisson regression and RSF identified distance
to the nearest highway as an important variable in their models. However, Poisson
regression also found a strong association between road count within a 50-meter
93
==PAGE:106==
radius and incidence of AMI. RSF, on the other hand, identified distance to large
roads as an important factor. These findings collectively suggest that proximity to
highways and road count within 50-meter radius may contribute to incident AMI,
with proximity to highways having a more substantial impact.
6.3.7
Model selection
Poisson regression of incidence rate is popular in epidemiological studies to investi-
gate the associations between exposure variables and health outcomes. It is useful
in various health-related studies [95, 96]. One of the main advantages of Poisson
regression is its ability to provide easily interpretable coeﬀicients, representing the
expected change in the event rate with increase in the exposure variable. Poisson
regression has limitations in handling complex relationships between variables. It
assumes a constant mean over the range of the exposure variable, which may only
sometimes be accurate.
RSF offers a promising alternative to Poisson regression for modelling complex re-
lationships. RSF automatically explores interactions between variables, eliminating
the need for manual specification, which regression-type approaches typically re-
quire [43]. Additionally, Poisson regression and RSF can handle longitudinal data
collected repeatedly over time for the same individuals, which makes them suitable
for the data used in this study.
Previous studies have successfully employed RSF models to predict clinical outcomes
[46, 45, 46]. However, these studies primarily utilised smaller datasets compared to
the present study. The current study used a substantially larger dataset, presenting
computational challenges in running the RSF model. Therefore, the RSF analysis
was utilised on a subsample of the data, maintaining the data’s integrity while
reducing the computational burden and completing the study within a reasonable
timeframe. Despite downsampling, the RSF model achieved promising results.

6.4
Future research
A key strength of this study was the access to extensive data from the Danish
national registers, which allowed for the computation of distance exposure over time
and the incorporation of confounding variables. However, this also made the RSF
model computationally heavy.
For future work, could involve exploring if other
survival models can handle more extensive datasets would be relevant. Another
option is to aggregate or transform the data to improve computational speed.
Finding a suitable exposure variable for measuring the effect of traﬀic-related pollu-
tion can be complicated. The results in the current study for counting the number of
highways within a 50-meter radius showed an increasing trend in AMI risk. However,
only a few individuals had more than one road within that radius, making the results
of the models less significant. For future work, instead of counting the number of
highways within a certain radius, it could be relevant to count the number of meters
of highways or retrieve information about traﬀic flow. Doing that can yield more in-
formation about individuals’ exposure. Additionally, exploring methods to address
geographical variations and inequalities between urban and sparsely populated areas
could be interesting. It could involve finding a good measure of exposure variables
based on residential addresses within cities versus in the countryside, different en-
vironments that potentially are affected differently by traﬀic-related exposures.
It could also be interesting to examine other measures of AMI, such as fatal AMI or
non-fatal AMI, separately. The distance to major roads may be differently associated
with these two stages in the disease process. Finally, the current study suggests
that potential future studies could explore other relevant disease outcomes, such
as respiratory or other cardiovascular diseases, to shed further light on whether
residential proximity to roads can increase the risk of other health-related problems.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This study explores the association between residing near major roads and AMI
incidence in Denmark. Major roads are categorised into large roads and highways.
Results show that living in very close proximity (0-100 meters) to highways increases
incidence of AMI, with a 22% heightened IRR of AMI when living within 50 meters
of a highway. A clear trend was observed between the number of roads within 50
meters of a highway and incidence of AMI, revealing a 50% increase in IRR when
residing near two highways within that distance. These results are not statistically
significant, likely due to the limited number of individuals living within 50 meters
of more than one highway. The results show a minimal association between large
roads and incidence of AMI regarding distance in meters and road count within
a radius. The Poisson regression model showed the strongest association between
distance to highways and the number of highways within 50-meters. However, the
RSF identified distance to large roads and distance to highways as the two most
important factors in the model. The consistent identification of a strong association
between distance to highways and incidence of AMI in both models leads to the
conclusion that distance to highways is the most robust exposure variable.
These findings of the Poisson regression confirm previous findings on the association
between traﬀic-related exposure and risk of incident AMI. However, RSF has not
been as widely studied, especially in the context of this topic, so future studies have
to confirm the current results.
