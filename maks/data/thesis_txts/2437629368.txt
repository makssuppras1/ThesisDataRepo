Abstract
Advancements in experimental techniques have made it possible, to probe and engineer sys-
tems which exhibit quantum behaviour. A deep understanding of such systems often requires
that the quantum nature of the environments are taken into account.
The environments
generally have a vast number of degrees of freedom which makes the total system a true
many-body problem. In the open system approach this is dealt with by ﬁnding an eﬀectively
evolution of the system. For environments with any memory the eﬀective evolution becomes
non-local in time and therefore hard to solve.
In this project two methods that captures these memory eﬀects are investigated. The
main system considered is a chain of 2 level systems coupled to a bosonic environment at
each end. Having diﬀerent temperatures for the two environments, pushes the chain into a
non-equilibrium steady-state.
The ﬁrst method investigated, that includes memory eﬀects, is an extension of quantum
ﬁeld theory that allows one to deal with non-equilibrium systems.
It is found that this
method’ main challenge is to deal with the 2 level system nature of the system. Making an
approximate mapping to a linear bosonic model, the steady-state observables can be found
exactly. Compared to the regular open system approach, the ﬁeld theory method makes it
abstractmuch simpler to extend the number of sites in the chain. For low temperature this
approach seems to capture the correct trends of the steady-state occupation but it is not as
good at capturing the steady-state current.
The second method is a numerical method based on tensor networks. This method was
recently developed for the purpose of investigating small systems connected to environments.
In this project we extend this method to systems where only a small part of the system
is directly connected to an environment. This is found to give a huge improvement on the
computational resources needed.
For the chain coupled to two environments this proves
challenging as even system consisting of only two sites is very computationally expensive.
This means that only a few cases can be solve numerically exact.
If the system has one
memoryless environment, then the implemented method provides a major reduction of the
computational resources. This method captures the current of the two-environment system
better than other investigated approaches but is not able to properly capture correct steady-
state occupations for stronger hopping between the sites.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The last decades have lead to an immense advancement of experimental techniques. This
has opened up for investigating and exploiting complex systems that lie within the quantum
regime. With these capabilities it becomes essential to ﬁnd theoretical methods that can shed
light on the dominating physical processes. The kind of complex systems considered in this
report is commonly known as open systems. These systems are characterized by having a
small system of interest, the system, which are interacting with large environments. Open
system frameworks become necessary when the environments also has to be modelled quantum
mechanically.
This need arises in a large class of physical systems from quantum optics [1] to semi-
conductor physics, like solid state quantum dots [2], and even in biological systems, such as
energy transfer in molecules [3]. Even though these examples a physically very diﬀerent, it
is found that the environment interaction can be described in the language of open systems.
The mentioned realisable systems have a range of interesting applications. A few examples
include engineering elements for building quantum computers [4, 5], understanding photo-
synthesis [6], improving solar cells [7] or creating computers based on molecular structures
[8]. Common for all of these examples is, that they can be extended by having several copies
interacting with each other. This allows for many-body phenomena to arise and for more
interesting real world applications.
One of the simplest approaches, which treats the environment quantum mechanically is
the Markovian master equation. Its starting point is a microscopic model and it assumes
that the environment has no memory of previous interactions between the system and the
environments. For this to be a valid assumption the environments’ and system’s dynamics
must be on diﬀerent time scales. When ﬁrst introduced in quantum optics this assumption was
well justiﬁed, as electromagnetic ﬁelds have short relaxation times compared to atoms. This
approximation is less justiﬁed in semiconductor and molecular settings. In these systems the
environment is not an electromagnetic environment but instead collective vibrations of ions.
Such vibrations, known as phonons, are oscillating much slower than electromagnetic ﬁelds
and thus also have a slower relaxation rate. It then proves important to take into account
that the environment eﬀectively stores information about the system’s previous state. Eﬀects
that arise as a consequence of this are commonly referred to as non-Markovian.
Treating non-Markovianity is by itself a big challenge and if one is further interested
in larger systems, many of the standard methods are not eﬃcient.
This report therefore
investigates alternative methods originating from condensed matter theory, where one mainly
deals with large systems, to see if they are applicable to the class of open systems in question.
To understand the constraints imposed on the methods by open systems, it is necessary to
start from a microscopic description of the entire system (system and environments), and see

how the diﬀerent concepts originate.
1.1
Memory eﬀects and non-equilibrium in open systems
Open quantum systems are commonly described in the setting of Hilbert spaces. In such
a description partitioning the quantum system into subsystems means the complete Hilbert
space (H) is a tensor product of all the subsystems’ Hilbert spaces
H = HS ⊗HE,
(1.1)
where HS(E) is the Hilbert space of the system (environments). Such a partitioning allows
for a transparent description of the open quantum system. Here a notation will be used,
where the system is denoted (S) and all the environments the system is interacting with, are
collectively written as (E). This means that the eﬀective environment can consist of several
diﬀerent environments.
The complete Hilbert space is huge, due to the many degrees of freedom (DOF) in E.
In many cases this makes direct calculations intractable, as the computations require an
impossible amount of data both to be stored and manipulated. Making these computations
tractable is the main challenge in most many-body quantum formalisms.
The fundamental simpliﬁcation introduced in open system frameworks is focusing only on
the behaviour of S. This simpliﬁcation is used to set up an eﬀective description of S, which
includes the eﬀects of E, without storing any information about the speciﬁc dynamics of E.
When only describing the DOF for S, many ambiguities arise when considering concepts
that are governing the complete system. For example even though the system and environment
is in an equilibrium state, it is not generally true that the system is itself in equilibrium.
This makes it necessary to describe the system in a manner that allows for non-equilibrium
and therefore has no constraints on the ﬁnal state of the system. Accommodating for non-
equilibrium greatly reduces the available condensed matter and quantum ﬁeld theory methods
available.
The usual starting point for an open system approach is the equation of motion (EOM), for
the density matrix of the joined state of the system and environment (χ) [9]. A density matrix
is an object which can both describe classical and quantum correlations. By integrating out
all parts of the EOM that belongs to E, one is left with an eﬀective evolution for a density
matrix of the system only. This is commonly denoted as the reduced density matrix. With
such a description E can eﬀectively introduce both classical and quantum correlations in S.
A consequence of integrating out E is that possible memory eﬀects manifest by the eﬀective
EOM being non-local in time. This is a result of the non-Markovian nature of the environ-
ment. Considering a realistic quantum system, it is very often the case that interactions with
some kind of environment is present. This makes developing new methods for open system a
very active ﬁeld of research. Many of the successful non-Markovian methods rely on a speciﬁc
kind of environments like the hierarchic equations of motion [10] and reaction coordinate [11]
methods. Both methods works well for environments which mostly interact with the system,
around a narrow frequency interval. More general methods like the Nakajima-Zwanzig equa-
tion [12, 13] establish the non-local master equation but do not give a general way of solving
it. Other methods rely on speciﬁc physical insights, to take advantage of transformations,
such that one can apply a Markovian master equation in a diﬀerent basis [14].
As previously mentioned, a common feature for many of these analytical methods is that
the diﬃculty increases signiﬁcantly, if the system dimension is increased. This is because many
of the methods use an extended space, which has a dimensionality equal to the square of the
system’s DOF. Increasing the system therefore results in an exponential increase of the DOF

in the extended space where the system evolution is solved. In this project we will investigate a
small, but extendible, system where the Markovian master equation description is challenged
but tractable. The same system will then be approached using a ﬁeld theoretical method that
is applicable for non-equilibrium systems. This method will scale better for larger system,
as the extended space is not necessary. As there will be a need for approximations in both
the Markovian master equation and the ﬁeld theoretical method, we are also investigating a
numerical method. This numerical approach was originally developed for small systems but
the structure of the method will be changed. This change is done to investigate how well
the method extends to larger systems. To construct such a numerical method a mixture of
methods from the non-equilibrium quantum ﬁeld theory, open system and matrix product
states, which is a tensor network method commonly used in condensed matter theory, will be
used.
1.2
Model and realisations
The focus is on dealing with the environments, so the system considered is a simple one
dimensional system consisting of Nc sites.
To make the investigation of all the methods
possible, within the time span of the project, we will not include any on-site interaction. The
sites are interacting with each other through a constant hopping. At each end of this chain
there is attached a large environment. The temperature of this environment will give rise to
a bias and therefore lead to a non-trivial steady-state.
Even though this model is simple, it is of relevance for the understanding of all the
realistic system mentioned previously. For quantum optics realisations, one can use a linear
array of coupled empty cavities [15]. In this case each site is bosonic in nature and so are the
environments which are the driving electromagnetic ﬁelds at the ends. Due to inﬁnite Hilbert
space of the bosons, these system can be challenging in a conventional open system approach.
Often it is necessary to truncate the Hilbert space or use a mean ﬁeld approximation.
The open system methods are simpler when considering sites with a ﬁnite Hilbert space.
This is the case for a large class of physical systems. As an example the two dimensional
Hilbert spaces occurs if low energy excitonic systems are considered. An exciton is a quasi-
particle consisting of an electron and a coulomb correlated hole.
In such a system, the
ground state has the electron in the valence band such that no exciton exists. For low energy
systems it is only necessary to include the ﬁrst exciton state. This means that each site is
eﬀectively described with only two states, empty or occupied. The algebra satisﬁed, for the
operators of a 2 level system (2LS), is similar to that of a spin-1/2 particle. Operators on the
same site anti-commute, which guarantees that only one excitation can be present at each
site, while operators on diﬀerent sites commute. This model is used for many systems for
example semiconductor quantum dots [16]. Due to the high precision fabrication techniques,
it is possible to engineer systems with coupled QDs. In this case the environments can be
described as the previously mentioned phonons, which couple to the exciton state [17].
Here each site will be a QD and thus modelled as a 2LS while the environment consists of
the normal modes of the large solid. The environments are then modelled as non-interacting
thermal bosonic systems.
A similar model is applicable for molecular systems [18]. Here the excitaions are also
of excitonic nature and hops between the diﬀerent sites in the molecule. Molecules relevant
for photosynthesis can be modelled as one dimensional chains of exciton sites where the
environments are the vibrational modes of the molecule.
Besides investigating photosynthesis and solar cells, the model can also be used to describe
a molecule which is coupled to harmonic environments. It therefore provides a testing ground

Chapter 2:
In this chapter the Markovian master equation will be described. This will highlight

small systems which this chapter derives an extension of which makes a speciﬁc class of
larger systems tractable.

In this chapter all three methods are applied to the small two-site system and their
results are compared and discussed.

Chapter 2
The Markovian master equation
In this chapter we will derive the Born-Markov master equation (BMME) and apply it to the
two-site system. The derivation will show why larger system are challenging in this approach
and how a general theory fails to describe the two-site system even in the Markovian case.

The BMME is second order in HI, has no memory and thus is time-local. The evolution
operator is given by [9]
ρ(t) = exp (Lt) ρ0,
(2.20)

(2.21)
This means that the evolution of the system can be described as a dynamical map with L as
the generator: V (t) = eLt. Due to the Markovian approximation the map satisﬁes the semi-
group property V (t1 +t2) = V (t1)V (t2). As the dissipator (2.19) is constructed by evaluating
commutators it is ensured to be trace preserving. The only remaining question is if the map
is mapping into a new physical state. This is guaranteed if the map is completely positive
(CP) [21].
To understand this postulate consider ﬁrst a positive matrix. This is an Hermitian matrix
which only has non-negative eigenvalues. Any physical density matrix is therefore an example
of a positive matrix. A positive map is characterised by the property that, when given a
positive matrix as argument, it outputs another positive matrix. This must obviously be
satisﬁed for the dynamical map for a quantum system, as it is equivalent to the statement
that the system can only evolve into a new physical state. CP maps are extensions of positive
maps which take entanglement into account. In the presence of entanglement, the composite
Hilbert space is described by a density matrix which does not factorise [20]. If one applies
an operation on a subsystem, then the total state of the system and the subsystems’ states
still have to be physical density matrices. A map which is positive when both acting on a
composite and a non-composite Hilbert space is said to be CP.
The BMME does not necessarily constitute a CP map [22], which means that it can give
rise to non-physical states. One way to test this is to attempt to write the dissipator in the
Lindblad form [23]. Here the main ideas will be explained. For a more careful derivation the
reader is referred to [9] and its references. The starting point is the time-local form of (2.15).

2.3.1
Completely positive master equations
To avoid the non-physical behaviour of the full BMME one can apply the secular approx-
imation, which puts the dissipator on a Lindblad form (2.28) and guarantees a completely
positive map. The eﬀect of using this approximation is that the diﬀerential equations for the
steady-state separates into one set of diﬀerential equations for the occupation of the system
energy eigenstates, and another set which governs the oﬀ-diagonal elements in the energy basis
[9]. The oﬀ-diagonal elements is found to decay [24] which means that steady-state resulting
from the secular approximation will be diagonal in the energy basis. For the system to have
a current there will generally have to be oﬀ-diagonal elements which means that the secular
approximation gives rise to a vanishing current through the system [32]. In this project it was
investigated numerically and found that, using the secular approximation, the steady-state
matrix in the occupation basis is always real and diagonal in the energy eigenbasis. As the
current will later be shown to equal the diﬀerence of hopping between the two sites (sec. 4.4),
the density matrix has to have imaginary elements in the oﬀ-diagonal to generate a current.
This is a direct consequence of the density matrix being Hermitian.
As the full BMME is not guaranteed to be a completely positive map and the secular
approximation does not give rise to a steady state current neither of these ME’s can give any
reliable information about the physical behaviour of the system. The main complication in

2.4
Chapter summary
The Born-Markov formalism has been derived and it has been found that for the speciﬁc
case it does not constitute a completely positive map. This means that it is not guaranteed
to give physical results. Enforcing the secular approximation the master equation comes on
a completely positive form, but it is argued that this form leads to a vanishing steady-state
current, even when a temperature bias is applied. The last resort is to derive a master equation
similar to the quantum optical master equation which is only valid for weak tunnelling between
the sites. This form also has the weakness that it does not thermalize the system correctly.
Besides the master equation being Markovian it is also only second order in the environment
interaction and its computational complexity increases exponentially with the size of the
system.

Chapter 3
Non-equilibrium quantum ﬁeld
theory
In this chapter the framework of non-equilibrium quantum ﬁeld theory (NEQFT) will be
presented. This theory will be an alternative method to the Markovian method presented in
the previous chapter. It is a qualitatively diﬀerent approach than master equation method
and allows us to go beyond the Born-Markov approximation and ﬁnd a fully non-Markovian
solution. The presentation will be centred around the simple model of single free bosonic
mode. A bosonic system is considered because this is found as the relevant physical system
for the speciﬁc calculations presented in the following chapters. Considering fermionic systems
instead does not drastically change the structure of the formalism [35]. The presented theory
will be the foundation on which all further NEQFT calculations will be based. This chapter
presents no original results, but aims to give a suﬃcient presentation of the theory to solve
the exciton chain model.
3.1
Evolving a non-equilibrium system
The main diﬀerence between a non-equilibrium and equilibrium theory is that, in the for-
mer case, one can not constrain the ﬁnal state of the system.
To deal with a truly out-
of-equilibrium system the only input is the initial state of the system and a Hamiltonian
describing its evolution. To make the theory valid for any quantum system it is necessary
to include both quantum and classical correlations. To this extent it is necessary to describe
the state of the system using a density matrix (2.1) This element can describe both pure and
mixed states and is therefore an optimal starting point for the NEQFT.
To determine the time evolution of this object one notices that it is a linear combination
of outer products of pure states. The evolution can then be found by evolving each pure state.
These evolve according to Schr¨odinger’ equation

3.3. CHAPTER SUMMARY
3.3
Chapter summary
In this chapter the non-equilibrium quantum ﬁeld theory for a free boson has been discussed
and derived. It has been shown how a non-equilibrium ﬁeld theory can be build by introducing
a closed time contour. This construction means that a non-equilibrium ﬁeld theory has twice
the number of ﬁelds compared to an equilibrium ﬁeld theory.
For a quadratic theory it
is shown that all correlation functions are accessible, if four diﬀerent propagators are found.
These objects describe how the system propagates between diﬀerent parts of the time contour.
By applying a clever rotation it was shown how a continuum theory that gave the same
predictions as the discrete representation could be constructed. The main object of interest
for the coming chapter is the structure of this continuum theory and the one-point correlation
function.

Chapter 4
The linear bosonic chain model
To go beyond the many restrictions found for the MME, the NEQFT will now be used to
solve the chain system.
In this chapter diﬀerent approaches to making the exciton chain
model (1.2) tractable for a ﬁeld theory will be discussed. The main diﬃculty is that the 2LS
operators are not behaving like bosons as the local Hilbert space is only two dimensional.
They do not behave as fermions either as diﬀerent sites commute opposed to anti-commute.
For the ﬁeld theoretic framework presented in chp. 3, one uses coherent states to turn
operators into numbers.
This proves useful because the free theory for both bosons and
fermions ends up being Gaussian functional integrals. These can be solved analytically and
one can include interactions either perturbatively or non-perturbativley [35]. For the case of
spins one can use spin coherent states [47], however their completeness relation is not covering
the entire real axis. The resulting integrals are therefore not Gaussian. The consequence is
that the integrals can not be solved analytically. The most often used approach is therefore
to map the spins to either bosons or fermions.

The Jordan-Wigner transformation is by far the most used method for mapping spin/2LS

body interactions is turned into four-body interactions, such that the theory is no longer
quadratic. It also necessary to constrain the Hilbert space to the one excitation subspace.
In the case of an equilibrium system this can be done by adding Lagrange multipliers to the
chemical potential, but is less clear in the case of non-equilibrium. Another approach is to map
to fermions and Majorana particles [50], which in this case leads to new questions regarding
working with Majorana particles in a ﬁeld theortical formalism. None of these methods seems
directly applicable without ﬁrst developing a deeper understanding of the methods. Given
the time constraints of the project a simpler approach is therefore chosen.

Here the retarded self-energies are the two identical terms in (4.20), where the the Kronecker-

propagator is known as a perturbed symmetric tridiagonal toeplitz (PSTT) matrix.
The
perturbation comes from the fact that the diagonal elements are not constant. This makes
the inversion more challenging compared to the regular symmetric tridiagonal Toeplitz matrix.
There still exists a closed form expression for the elements of the inverse of a PSTT matrix
which was found in [52]. The complication related to this closed form is that the solution
depends on the value of R. This is troublesome when the inverse Fourier transform has to
be done, as this requires integration over all values of E for which the spectral density is
non-vanishing. A way which could make this closed form useful is to transform to a rotating
frame. This makes it possible to force R into a speciﬁc regime such that the closed form
solution is unchanged throughout the ﬁnite support of the spectral density.
Getting the closed form solution to work is non-trivial but a more substantial diﬃculty
is related to ﬁnding the inverse Fourier transform of the closed form elements, as they are
described by multiple levels of hyperbolic functions.
As small chains are considered and the dimensionality of (GR)−1 is equal to the length of
the chain a more brute force approach will be used. If one is interested in longer chains, the
propagators elements can instead be found recursively [53].

4.5. CHAPTER SUMMARY
4.5
Chapter summary
In this chapter diﬀerent ﬁeld theoretical approaches to the chain of 2LS was considered. It
was shown that a Jordan-Wigner transformation is not suitable for the given problem and
instead a bosonic mapping was used. To simplify the derivations a linear bosonic mapping
was chosen. It was hypothesised that such a map should be valid in the low occupation regime
if the environments spectral density was peaked closely to the energies of the system. With
this map the theory was solved exactly and fully non-Markovian. It was found that solving
a longer chain only lead to inverting a PSTT matrix of the same dimension as the number
of sites. From this matrix inversion all the propagators of the theory could be found using
matrix multiplication. Lastly the steady-state occupation and current was derived in terms
of the propagators.

Chapter 5
A numerical tensor network method
The non-equlibrium ﬁeld theory approach allowed us to solve a model exactly without using
a perturbative approach nor a Markovian approximation. The drawback of this approach was
that including the correct statistics for the 2LS was non-trivial. To circumvent this diﬃculty
the 2LS chain was described as a number conserving bosonic system.
The hypothesis is that such a mapping is valid as long as the occupation of the sites are
small and the environment is close to the resonances of the chain. An intuitive hypothesis like
this must be supported by some sort of quantitative validation. For this purpose a numerical
tensor network method (NTNM) will be constructed. The main idea is based on a recent
method [55] which uses tensor network to include ﬁnite memory-time environments exactly.
In this report the method will be extended to better suit the one dimensional systems and
it will be derived using concepts from both the master equation derivation and the NEQFT
framework.

Finite memory time approximation and singular value decompositions
It was seen in chp. 2 that the environment relaxation time was described by the vanishing
value of the environment correlation functions (2.16). The environment will have no memory
of any earlier events. This can be used to prevent the tensor network from growing because
the autocorrelation function (5.19) goes to zero due to the ﬁnite memory of the environment.
As the autocorrelation function dies out the inﬂuence functional becomes an identity. These
identities can be contracted immediately, which means that the ADT’ length will not grow
further than kmax = Tmem/∆. Tmem is the memory time, after which the autocorrelation
function is eﬀectively zero.
Considering the chosen spectral density in (2.36) the autocorrelation function has to be
integrated numerically.
For the relevant parameters the autocorrelation and lineshape is
plotted in Fig. 5.5.a, b. The autocorrelation function goes to zero after approximately 5σ.

the two-site model and only appears for long time dynamics. The error introduced is an
absolute error. This makes it tricky to investigate observables with very low values. This
is exactly the regime of interest for this report. The investigated system and regime is thus
found to have several features that limits the possibilities with the present implementation of
the numerical method.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this project diﬀerent methods for solving open quantum systems have been constructed
and investigated. These systems interact with environments in such a way that one has to
include the quantum mechanical nature of the environments. The speciﬁc system which has
been used for the investigations, is an one-dimensional chain of Nc 2 level systems (2LS). Each
end of the chain is coupled to a bosonic environment of a speciﬁc temperature. With the two
environments at diﬀerent temperatures, a current ﬂows through the system. The steady-state
current and the site occupations have been the main observables for the project.
One of the widely used methods is the Born-Markov master equation (BMME). This
method is fully Markovian, which means that the environment is treated as completely sta-
tionary and always in its thermal state. This method is based upon a superoperator space,
which has the consequence that the space that one is eﬀectively solving is equal to 22Nc. This
makes it hard to solve longer chains analytically. Even for the two-site system (Nc = 2), it
was found that the full BMME gives rise to non-physical evolution as it is not guaranteed to
constitute a completely positive map. To guarantee a physical evolution, that still gave rise to
a steady-state current, it was necessary to assume weak hopping between the sites. This gave
rise to a Lindblad dissipator, which pushed each site towards thermal equilibrium with its
connected environment. It was found that this method, was reasonable at capturing very low
temperature currents even for intermediary hopping strengths. The occupation predictions
were not reliable as soon as the hopping or environment coupling was increased.
To go beyond the Markovian approximation and have a more eﬃcient way of describing
longer chains, a quantum ﬁeld theory approach have been investigated for the same system.
The main challenge with this method is treating 2 level systems with ﬁeld theory. They satisfy
an algebra similar to spin-1/2. A direct approach leads to inﬁnite dimensional non-Guassian
integrals, which can not be solved analytically. Instead one has to map the 2LS’s to either
fermions or bosons. The fermion mapping was found to lead to a non-local phase factor, which
was not normal ordered. This is a result of considering a ﬁnite chain and having environment
interactions that do not conserve the number of excitations in the chain.
Instead a linear bosonic mapping was used. With this model a fully non-Markovian theory
for the chains was derived. It was found that one has to do one matrix inversion to get access
to all propagators for the theory.
With a rotating wave approximation the dimension of
the matrix is equal to the length of the chain. This method therefore both includes non-
Markovian eﬀects exactly and scales linearly with the chain length, instead of exponentially
as the BMME.
Investigating the validity of this map in a very low temperature and weak hopping/coupling
regime the map was found to be in agreement with the Markovian master equation. Increasing
the temperature it was noticed that the predicted current becomes linear with temperature.
69
==PAGE:78==
7.1. OUTLOOK
This was a feature of the used bosonic map not having any on-site interaction to approximate
the 2LS anti-commutation relations.
Comparing with exact numerical results for intermediate hopping and weak environment-
system coupling, it was found that the trend of the occupation diﬀerences is captured better
with the NEQFT method than the MME whereas the current is not as well described.
The exact numerical method used for the comparison was constructed by using elements from
both open systems (superoperator space, reduced density matrix), non-equilibrium quantum
ﬁeld theory (path integral interpretation, derivation of inﬂuence functional) and tensor net-
works (matrix product states/operators). It was shown that if only a part of the reduced
system is interacting directly with the environment, then the computational resources can be
decreased signiﬁcantly. This simpliﬁcation is achieved by partitioning the Hilbert space into
a part connected to the environment and an isolated part. The isolated part only interacts
with the environment through the connected part. It was shown how one could construct
a tensor network that allowed for time-local evolution of an extended object, known as the
augmented density tensor. Using tensor network methods, this network can be truncated to
obtain a more eﬃcient representation including memory eﬀects.
For the two-site system it was found that the full computations was very resource intensive.
This meant that only a few data points could be generated for this project. To take advantage
of the partitioning of the Hilbert space, the cold environment was treated with the derived
Markovian dissipator. This allowed for calculations to be carried out. Comparing to the exact
non-Markovian data points it was found that the occupation was exhibiting similar wrong
features as the MME but the current was captured much better than the other two methods.
7.1
Outlook
The presented investigations leaves a lot of possibilities open. A ﬁrst step would be to ﬁnd a
way to improve the current numerical implementation, such that a larger parameter regime
can be accessed. One way of doing this would be to investigate the eﬀect of changing how the
network is contracted. More exact data points opens up for a more in-depth characterisation
of the validity regime of the two analytical approaches.
For the non-equilibrium quantum ﬁeld theory method there is two paths, which can be
taken to improve its accuracy. The ﬁrst step would be to add a on-site interaction and include
a ﬁnite repulsion between the bosons. This can be done using either perturbation theory or a
non-perturbative ﬁeld theory method like the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation. Another
option is to consider only the two-site system and use a fermionic mapping. In this case the
non-local factor becomes normal ordered and approximate approaches, like those mentioned
above, can be applied. The bosonic approach has the advantage that it can easily be extended
to longer chains, which is not the case for the fermion mapping. The advantage of the fermion
mapping is that it captures the anti-commutation properties of the sites exactly, and one can
therefore hope for a more accurate result. Lastly the NEQFT can be improved by not doing
the rotating wave approximation. This can be solved by using a Nambu space representation.

Appendix C

C.1
Original project plan
The goal of the project is to investigate energy transfer through molecular chain systems.
The molecular chain is modeled as a one-dimensional system of coupled sites, with the sites
only having the ﬁrst exciton state accessible. The sites are coupled by a number conserving
hopping interaction. At both ends, the chain is coupled to bosonic environments. The coupling
is such that the environments can add and remove excitations to the end sites of the chain.
The system is out of equilibrium because the two environments are at diﬀerent temperatures.
The temperature bias thus drives a thermal current through the chain. Transport phenomena
occurring in a one-dimensional system are a generic topic in condensed and mesoscopic physics,
and consequently the results obtained in this project are expected to shed light on the eﬀects
due to a non-Markovian coupling to the environments in general.
The studied system is interesting for a number of reasons.
The molecular chain can
be used as a model to describe the energy transport mechanism in photosynthesis, which
has experimentally been found to have mesoscopic features. Photosynthesis has a very high
eﬃciency in the energy transport process and a better understanding therefore could lead to
improvements of present day technology. Molecular chains are also very interesting systems
due to the advances in nano-fabrication which makes it possible to study and use these systems
experimentally. From a theoretical point of view the task of computing non-Markovian eﬀects

C.2. REVISED PROJECT PLAN
C.2
Revised project plan
The goal of the project is to investigate two non-Markovian methods for open systems. This is
interesting as the advancements in fabrication and experimental techniques have opened for a
number of systems in which the Markovian master equation breaks down. From a theoretical
point of view the task of going beyond the Markovian master equation is an active ﬁeld of
research.
This project aims to add value to this ﬁeld by approaching the chain problem
through two novel approaches. The ﬁrst method is a purely numerical method with is hoped
to be able to solve smaller systems exactly while the second method is based on quantum
ﬁeld theory. This method is investigated as it should allow for a more eﬃcient extension for
larger systems. The goal is to understand the limitations and advantages of both methods.
To understand how these method performs to the more often used master equation ap-
proach, a speciﬁc system is investigated. The system is an one-dimensional chain with each
site being a two level system. The two level system is chosen as this is a very typical system
for master equations. To generate a current the chain is coupled to bosonic environments at
both ends. The coupling is such that the environments can add and remove excitations to
the end sites of the chain. The system is out of equilibrium because the two environments
are at diﬀerent temperatures. The temperature bias thus drives a thermal current through
the chain.
A number of realisations can be described by this and closely related models. Examples
include coupled cavities in QED, semiconductor quantum dots, molecular chains and molec-
ular junctions. Each of these systems provide an interesting basis for improving modern day
technology. Quantum dots are for example interesting as indistinguishable photon sources,
which are important elements for the realisation of quantum computers. The molecular chain
is interesting as it can be used to model photosynthesis. In these molecules a very high trans-
port eﬃciency is found experimentally. Understanding this would allow for improvements of
the current photocell technology. As a last example molecular junction are relevant because
they are small and very stable structures. It is therefore very investigated if components for
computers can be build from molecules. This would allow one to build even smaller com-
puters and therefore circumvent the current size limitation approaching for semiconductor
components.
The numerical method
The numerical method tries to capture the memory eﬀect of the environment by creating
functions, which are the result of tracing away the environments. This gives rise to a non-local
time-evolution. To analyze the situation a new object will be constructed, which is similar to a
density matrix but also contains the memory eﬀect due to the coupling to environments. The
matrix representation of this new object will be much larger than that of the ordinary density
matrix, but can be evolved time-locally. To do computations with this huge matrix we will use
a Singular Value Decomposition. This approach will remove all unnecessary information in a
controlled manner. For a single two-site system this method is known as Time Evolved Matrix
Product Operators. The aim of this sub-project is to generalize this approach eﬃciently to
larger systems with the chain structure described above.
The analytical method
The analytical method which will be employed is a non-equilibrium ﬁeld theory method
commonly known as Keldysh ﬁeld theory. Our goal is to investigate how well this method
works for an archetypical open system model consisting of two level systems.
