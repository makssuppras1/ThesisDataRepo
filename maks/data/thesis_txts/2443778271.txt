Abstract 
Since the invention of plastic in the beginning of the 20th century and following the large scale production in 
the 1950s plastic saw an increased production in total of 7.8 billion ton from 1950 to 2015 (Geyer, et al., 
2017A). Plastic is a debate subject in the public. The trend is moving on minimizing plastic use and increas-
ing plastic recycling. This project will present a holistic description of plastic waste in Denmark  
The goal of this project was to to asses the quantity of plastic waste in Denmark across sectors in different 
lifecycle stages and the distribution spread out on polymer types. The goal was achieved using a material 
flow analysis (MFA) approach with the boundaries of plastic flows set to Denmark in 2016 due to the new-
est released national statistics. The focus of plastic overall and not only plastic waste was to establish any 
build up of plastic in the system and as a future tool in the transition to circular economy and the social 
movement of minimising plastic consumption. 
The method took a top down approach and collected data from governmental or national bodies, such as 
production, import and export from the European Commission’s statistical office Eurostat, Waste produc-
tion and treatment from the Waste data system (ADS) run by the Danish environmental protection agency 
(EPA) and waste import/export from the Statbank of Statistics Denmark. The statistical data was treated 
with plastic content estimations based on Danish studies. Polymer fractions in the estimated plastic 
amounts used market data from the European trade association of plastic manufactures PlasticsEurope and 
relevant studies both foreign and domestic for estimations. 
The material flow analysis of plastic waste in Denmark showed an import of plastic at 1.31 million ±50,000 
ton in 2016, which included raw materials for plastic production, imported plastic product and imported 
waste. The export of plastic was 691,000 ±25,000 ton in 2016, that consisted of exported plastic, exported 
products, exported waste and emissions to the environment. The difference represented a delta stock of 
620,000 ±50,000 ton in 2016. The build-up was in several processes in the plastic cycle. The consumption of 
plastic had an estimated global stock of 18.8 million ±3 million ton of plastic since 1966. The recycled plastic 
in Denmark sent to reprocessing within the geographical boundaries was 28,500 ±600 ton in 2016. The total 
plastic waste produced was 327,000 ±5,000 ton in 2016. 
The calculated recycling rates for plastic waste in Denmark was 9.9 ±0.3% as the end-of-life recycling rate 
(EOL RR). The calculated recycling rates contradicted the national reported 31 % recycled plastic in 2015  
and the goal of a European plastic recycling target of 20.5 % by 2022 (Nissen, et al., 2018). The plastic waste 
in residual waste sent for incineration was estimated to have a potential of achieving an EOL RR of 63 ±0.3% 
reaching the goal set by EU of a minimum plastic recycling of 50 % by 2030.

Sammenfatning 
Siden opfindelsen af plastik i begyndelsen af det 20. århundrede og efter den industrialiserede produktion i 
1950'erne har produktion af plastik steget til i alt 7,8 milliarder ton fra 1950 til 2015 (Geyer, et al., 2017A). 
Plast er et debatteret emne i offentligheden. Trenden i samfundet går på at minimere forbruget af plastik 
og øge genbrug af plast. Dette projekt vil præsentere en holistisk beskrivelse af plastaffald i Danmark 
Målet med dette projekt var at vurdere mængden af plastaffald i Danmark på tværs af sektorer i forskellige 
livscyklusfaser og fordelingen spredt ud på polymertyper. Målet blev opnået ved brug af en materiale-
strømsanalyse tilgang med grænserne sat for plastikstrømme i Danmark i 2016 på grund af den nyest of-
fentliggjorte nationale statistik. Fokusset på plastik generelt og ikke kun plastaffald var for at kortlægge en-
hver form for plast i systemet og som et fremtidigt redskab i overgangen til cirkulær økonomi og den soci-
ale bevægelse for at minimere plastforbruget. 
Metoden tog en tilgang startende oppefra og ned med indsamlede data fra statslige eller nationale orga-
ner, såsom produktion, import og eksport fra Europa Kommissionens statistiske kontor Eurostat, Affalds-
produktion og -behandling fra affaldsdatasystemet (ADS), der drives af Miljøstyrelsen og affald import/ek-
sport fra Danmarks Statistiks Statistikbanken. De statistiske data blev behandlet med estimater af plastind-
hold baseret på danske studier og senere for polymerfraktioner i de anslåede plast mængder, både ved an-
vendelse af markedsdata fra den europæiske forening af plastproducenter PlasticsEurope og relevante stu-
dier både udenlands og indenlands. 
Materialestrømsanalysen af plastaffald i Danmark viste en import af plast på 1,31 millioner ± 50,000 tons i 
2016, hvilket omfattede råmaterialer til plastproduktion, importeret plastprodukt og importeret affald. Ek-
sporten af plast var i 2016 691.000 ± 25.000 tons, der bestod af eksporteret plast, eksporterede produkter, 
eksporteret affald og udledninger til miljøet. Forskellen repræsenterede et difference lager på 620.000 ± 
50.000 tons i 2016. Lager differencen var tilstede i flere af de kortlagt processer i plastikcyklussen. Forbru-
get af plast havde et anslået samlet lager på 18,8 millioner ± 3 millioner tons plast regnet tilbage fra 1966. 
Den genanvendte plast i Danmark sendt til oparbejdning inden for de geografiske grænser var 28.500 ± 600 
tons i 2016. Den totale plast affalds produktion var i 2016 på 327,000 ±5,000 tons. 
Den beregnede genanvendelsesprocent for plastaffald i Danmark var 9,9 ± 0,3% som en sluttelig genbrugs-
rate. Den beregnede genanvendelsesprocent modsagde de nationale rapporterede 31% genanvendt plastik 
i 2015 og intentionen om et mål for genanvendelse af plastik på 20,5% inden 2022 (Nissen, et al., 2018). 
Plastaffaldet i restaffald sendt til forbrænding blev vurderet til at have et potentiale til at opnå en genbrugs-
rate på 63 ± 0,3% og derved opnå EU's mål om en minimumsgrænse for genbrug af plast på 50% inden 
2030.

1. Introduction 
To properly address a problem, one needs to understand the magnitude of said problem. 
This is the basis for this master thesis. Plastic waste is a heavily debated subject in the public. The societal 
trend regarding plastic minimization and recycling is growing, but little is known of the Danish condition 
regarding plastic waste. The need for information regarding national plastic consumption and waste treat-
ment are growing as EU is banning the use of some single-use plastic items and the Danish government 
plant to move Danish plastic consumption closer to a circular economy, where one initiative is to analyse 
the Danish plastic consumption and treatment (EC, 2018b; mfvm, 2018b). This project seeks to create a ho-
listic description of plastic waste in Denmark using material flow analysis (MFA) aimed at a growing attitude 
towards circular economy and recycling. There are several initiatives and plans for the future of plastic 
waste management but what is the present status of plastic waste on a national level? The MFA is a refer-
ence level to evaluate future recycling performances within plastic waste in Denmark. The MFA also func-
tioning as a marker to ensure reporting on national legislation and European directives, that are met. 
The previous studies found as inspiration for this project describes national waste systems or a certain 
waste type as e.g. residual household waste (Abbate, 2016). The previous MFAs of national plastic systems 
is based on up to 20 years old data and only for certain polymers and not overall plastic (Kuczenski & Geyer, 
2010; Patel, et al., 1998).  
1.1. 
Problem definition 
The problem definition for the project deals with the question of how big a problem is plastic and plastic 
waste in Denmark? And is this cause for alarm? The overall question is a broad subjective question open to 
interpretation. The following is a clarification with subsequent research questions.  
How much plastic is there in Denmark both as products and waste, are there any existing and future regula-
tions? How does plastic distributes across the consumption chain and does plastic pose a risk in terms of 
pollution? 
• 
What is the quantity of plastic waste in Denmark across sectors and how is the distribution spread 
out on polymer types?  
• 
What is the recycling potential of plastic waste in general and across sectors and polymers consid-
ering material deterioration and loss? 
• 
What are the regulations and legal requirements at a national, European and global level and what 
proposals are set in motion because of concerns about plastic waste?  
• 
In a circular economy, what is the consequences of closing the plastic loop, when considering waste 
purity and concentration of unwanted and harmful substances and how can concentration be 
avoided? 
1.2. 
Study objective 
The main objective of the project is to asses the quantity of plastic waste in Denmark across sectors in dif-
ferent lifecycle stages and the distribution spread out on polymer types. The study aims at mapping all the 
Danish plastic flows. The goal is to present an MFA model that consist of the plastic flows related to pro-
duction, use and waste treatment for a holistic understanding of plastic consumption and treatment flows 
in Denmark. The method used in the project rely on literary sources and data from collection companies, 
governmental institutions and waste handling companies in either Denmark or neighbouring countries.

2. Background 
The background section contains information about a plastic and polymer definition used in the project. 
The global plastic production both near present and historically is presented. Danish waste production from 
2016 is described. The present and future regulations and initiatives regarding plastic waste with studies 
indicating unintended consequences of insufficient recycling. MFA is described in general as MFA is the pre-
dominant method and tool in the project.  
2.1 
Definition of plastic 
The project focuses on plastic waste flows in Denmark. A definition of plastic for the project is then de-
scribed to ensure compliance and an overall understanding. Plastic is a product containing several different 
compounds. Plastic is primarily made of polymers.  
A polymer is a long chained molecular structure made up of a repeat unit called a monomer. A monomer is 
transformed into a polymer by polymerization. The different polymers are named after the monomer used 
e.g. the ethylene monomer through polymerization produces polyethylene. Polymerization occurs in nature 
e.g. plant fibres. 
There are two overall classifications of polymers. The first is thermoplastics or thermosets. Thermoplastics 
are individual polymer chains and thermosets contains bonds crosslinking between different polymer 
chains. Thermoplastics can be reheated and reused. Thermosets are stronger and more heat resistant but 
cannot be reheated and reused in the same way as thermoplastics (Cantor & Watts, 2011). The second clas-
sification of polymers are amorphous or semi-crystalline. Amorphous are a disorder of the individual poly-
mer chains. Semi-crystalline polymers have layers that align in a crystalline pattern. All polymers are amor-
phous as a liquid melted substance and no polymers are entirely crystalline (Cantor & Watts, 2011).  
Plastic contains other substances besides polymers such as additives and colorants. Plastic can have many 
shapes, sizes and properties. Plastic can be shaped by different techniques and be presented as different 
end products as films, fibres or moulded structures. 
There are certain types of polymers and plastics not fitting this description. These are inorganic polymers 
and plastics produced from renewable resources and labelled as bioplastic. The project does not contain 
further information about inorganic polymers and bioplastics.  
2.2 
Different polymers 
Plastic polymers are a mixed group of compounds spread out over functionality such as durability and 
strength. This section describes concrete examples of different plastic polymers and the use. Polymers con-
sists of but are not limited to Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS), Polyamide (PA), Polybutylene tereph-
thalate (PBT), Polycarbonate (PC), Polyethylene (PE), Polyethylene terephthalate (PET), Polypropylene (PP), 
Polystyrene (PS) and Polyvinyl chloride (PVS) (EC, 2012). 
The use of these polymers varies. ABS is used to manufacture e.g. LEGO® bricks, sports equipment and car 
parts (PlasticsEurope, 2018a). PA is also known as nylon and can be spun into fibres used for fabrics, fishing 
lines or buckles for backpacks. PBT is used in automotive parts, computer keys and the housing for irons. PC 
is an engineering polymer, which means it has e.g. higher heat resistance and strength compared to other 
polymers. The most commonly used PC contains Bisphenol A. PE can be a high density (HD) or a low density 
(LD) compound. PE is used as packaging, back sheets for diapers, high altitude balloons and kayaks. PET is 
often used blow moulded into bottles containing liquids for consuming, household purposes or chemicals. 
PP can be converted through different processes. PP is used as fibres in e.g. disposable medical garments, 
==PAGE:13==
Page 13 of 88 
 
as films is candy wrappers and moulded into toys and electronic housings. PS is found as packaging material 
as foam, lining of refrigerators and disposable cups. PVC is in flooring, toys and shower curtains. PVC needs 
to be managed well during manufacturing and disposal due to the stabilizing process and by-products of 
incineration (Peacock & Calhoun, 2006). This is not conclusive to represent all known polymers but shows 
the large diversity and implementation related to plastic polymers. 
2.3 
The world of plastic 
Plastic dates to the beginning of the 20th century with the invention of Bakelite (Baker, 2018). It was not 
until after World War II plastic entered a largescale production and use other than military purposes. Plastic 
production was expanding since the largescale introduction in the 1950s. The annual production has in-
creased from 2 million ton of plastic in 1950 to 380 million ton in 2015. The amount of plastic produced 
since 1950 until 2015 was 7.8 billion ton. 3.8 billion ton, half of the total amount, was produced within 13 
years from 2002 to 2015.  Plastic is a material consisting of different additives besides polymers. The con-
tent of plastic is found to be 93 % polymers and 7 % additives on a per mass basis. 75 % of additives con-
sists of plasticizers, fillers and flame retardants (Geyer, et al., 2017A). 
The most common polymers are PE, PP, PVC, PET, PUR, PS and PA. They make up 92 % of all plastic pro-
duced. 42 % of all plastic excluding fibres has been produced for packaging purposes. The second largest 
purpose for plastic is building and construction using 69 % of all PVC and 19 % of all plastic excluding fibres. 
It is estimated that 30 % or 2.5 billion ton of plastics are still in use. The durability and strength of plastic 
makes it difficult to be degraded in nature. Geyer, et al. (2017) states that without adequate waste man-
agement plan for plastic: “…humans are conducting a singular uncontrolled experiment on a global scale, in 
which billions of metric ton of material will accumulate across all major terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems 
on the planet.” 
2.4 
Generated waste 
Denmark generated 11.7 million ton of waste in 2016 excluding dirt (Nissen, et al., 2018). The generated 
waste was classified as primary waste or waste only reported from waste collectors or recipients directly 
from producers. The secondary waste was when e.g. waste collectors send waste to treatment. The waste 
could potentially be counted twice. The source separated waste fractions consisting of plastic represented 
0.9 % of the waste generated in 2016. The source separated waste fractions was plastic packaging, plastic 
and PVC  (Nissen, et al., 2018). The generated waste is equivalent to 2.05 ton per Danish capita or 19 kg of 
source separated plastic waste (DS, 2017) 
The Danish waste contained more than the 0.9 % source separated plastic waste, which was equivalent to 
110,000 ton. The 0.9 % plastic was only source separated plastic waste. The other waste fractions for classi-
fying Danish waste contained plastic. The electronic fraction was corresponding to the generic category 
Waste from Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE). A study found high grade WEEE to contain 26.5 % 
plastic (Bigum, et al., 2012). The same principle applied for the remaining fractions. The fraction fridges 
with freon 11.5 % (Larsen, et al., 2012) or Danish residual waste containing 14 % (Edjabou, et al., 2015)  
2.5 
Legislation 
The Danish government launched a Resource strategy in 2013 “Denmark without waste”. The strategy con-
tained several goals for a country without waste and waste as a secondary resource. The main initiatives 
were for Denmark to recycle more, incinerate less and instead send relevant waste to biogas production or 
recycling. The quality of recycled waste had to increas and improve utilization of nutrients in organic waste 
(EPA, 2014).

The tool for measuring and reporting the goal set regarding waste treatment and production is report pri-
marily from the Danish waste data system or Affaldsdatasystem (ADS). The purpose of ADS is to contain 
waste information and provide information on waste for authorities and for those reporting to ADS. The 
reporting part of ADS deals with companies that are responsible for treating waste (mfvm, 2018a). The data 
reported to ADS is made public available when releasing new Danish waste statistics. The latest available 
data is from 2016 (Nissen, et al., 2018). 
The government released a plastic action plan in December 2018, where the 2nd initiative out of 27 initia-
tives is the analysis of Denmark’s plastic consumption and treatment. The detailed plastic amount should 
be mapped across sectors and plastic types with inclusion of waste treatment. The Danish plastic waste 
production is approximately 340,000 ton annually, where 36 % is recycled and 63 % is sent to incineration. 
The mapping of plastic amounts is used to prioritise efforts of the national plastic centre. The national plas-
tic centre focuses on the move to circular plastic consumption (mfvm, 2018b). The action plan highlights 
other goals for a 2030 vision of plastic recycling from the European strategy concerning plastic in a circular 
economy, categorised as four central guideposts (EC, 2018a): 
- 
Increase recycling of plastic packaging to the level of paper, cardboard and glass recycling at 75-85 
%. However, 50 % of all plastic waste is sent for recycling as minimum. 
- 
A total stop of exported unsorted plastic waste, while the sorting and recycling capacity for Europe 
is increased fourfold compared to 2015. 
- 
Establishment of a market for recycled plastic based on industrial demands 
- 
Implementation of measures to counteract microplastic pollution 
Recycling of plastic poses a risk in relation to harmful substance in the treated waste. The risk is derived as 
plastic is a material made up of polymers with a variety of additives. Examples of harmful substances as ad-
ditives intentional or unintentional found in plastics are metals, phthalates and brominated flame retard-
ants (BFRs). Plastic can furthermore be contaminated during waste management if the harmful substances 
are not removed when recycled. The harmful substances will be recycled with the reprocessed plastic. This 
creates a need for quality data of plastic waste and reprocess plastic surrounding these harmful substances 
(Eriksen, et al., 2018b). Metal in plastic is found to be under the legal limit values. The difficulties arise with 
focus on plastic recycling and circular economy. Concentrations in reprocessed plastic when considering the 
increasing recycling rates, this could lead to higher metal concentrations (Eriksen, et al., 2018b). An exam-
ple is inefficiently sorted plastic from WEEE, which can introduce harmful substances into the reprocessed 
plastic such as BFRs and heavy metals (Turner, 2018). Close monitoring is recommended for plastics in-
tended for pollutant sensitive applications when the recycled plastic is substituting raw materials 
(Pivnenko, et al., 2016). 
2.6 
Generic MFA 
Material flow analysis (MFA) is an assessment of specific amount of relevant materials or goods in MFA ter-
minology for flows and processes in a defined system with geographical and temporal boundaries. The MFA 
is a process of connecting different data of the investigated material from different sources and intermedi-
ate products into a final state (Brunner & Rechberger, 2017). 
The foundation of MFA is the law of mass conservation, where input is equal to output plus stock. The pro-
cesses implemented in MFA are reconciled via mass balance for input, output and stocks for said processes. 
The techniques, method and software are easily and quickly implemented as mass balance and associated 
uncertainty calculations are the two dominant principles for MFA. The MFA model can be setup from the 
early start of a project with the use of the subSTance flow ANalysis software (STAN) and typically a form of 
==PAGE:15==
Page 15 of 88 
 
spreadsheet software. The processes are treated as a black box operation, where only the interaction with 
the surrounding flows and processes in the system are quantified (Cencic & Rechberger, 2008). 
The major advance of using a specific MFA software is overview. STAN balances and reconciles the system 
with mass flows and uncertainties. Furthermore, STAN can create layers in an existing MFA, making it possi-
ble to keep track of specific substances in the analysed material flows. The result of an MFA in STAN are di-
agrams of the system and holistic import, export and stock values for the defined system. The aim in to de-
scribe and analyse a defined system in a simple way for future decision-making (Cencic & Rechberger, 
2008).

3. Method 
The project aimed at mapping the Danish plastic flows. The goal was to present an MFA model that con-
sisted of the plastic flows related to production, use and waste treatment for a holistic understanding of 
the general plastic and plastic waste flows. 
3.1 
Material flow analysis 
The method used for the material flow analysis (MFA) in the project was the process presented by Brunner 
& Rechberger (2017) (Figure 1). The MFA method was a three-step process, where step 1 was the problem 
definition, step 2 was the system definition and step 3 was the determination of flows and stocks. All 
measures taken in the steps were described in the next sections. The MFA method was an iterative process, 
where previous work was revised further along the process to specify the problem, system and available 
data to the presented results in the end.  
The data collection and presentation were carried out in two programmes during the project. The software 
used was Excel for sorting and categorizing the collected data. The MFA software substance flow analysis 
(STAN) was used as data treatment and presented the collect result of the flows and processes.  
3.1.1 Problem definition 
Plastic waste is a debated subject in today’s world. The heightened focus on e.g. micro plastic in the oceans 
has fuelled a trend regarding plastic waste minimization. A Directive on a European level is proposed, for 
reducing certain plastic products and the impact from such. The proposal focuses on primarily single-use 
plastic products (EC, 2018b). The interest in plastic and plastic waste is addressed by assessing the magni-
tude of the flows of plastic waste in a national perspective. The project aimed at creating a holistic descrip-
tion of the plastic waste in Denmark using MFA.

3.1.4 Determination of processes 
The conservation of mass was the primary principle of the MFA. Flows not categorised as waste e.g. the 
production of plastic was included to ensure the mapping of all waste flows including possible mistreated 
and non-collected waste. The system dealt with a cradle to grave (CtG) or cradle to cradle (CtC) view on 
plastic flows. The distinction between CtG and CtC was whether there were plastic recycling activities in 
Denmark returning plastic to the production chain. Processes ranged from oil extraction to plastic 
==PAGE:18==
Page 18 of 88 
 
production to waste management were essential to give a holistic view on plastic waste in Denmark. The 
Danish plastic flows were derived from a generic model of the plastic lifecycle based on previous studies 
(Figure 2). This model was adapted to the Danish plastic system through the iterative process. The generic 
model had import, export processes on both sides to inform of possible import or exports through the en-
tire system. The processes “Consumption”, “Waste Collection” and “Waste Treatment” were made to com-
pensate for complicated unknown flows from production to consumption to waste production to waste 
treatment respectively. The relevant processes were identified by gathering information, that described the 
lifecycle of plastic. The first iteration reflected the reality and showed, that Denmark did not produce resin, 
additives and other chemicals related to primary plastic production. Denmark only imported primary plastic 
for product manufacturing (CCC, 2013) 
3.1.5 Determination of system boundaries 
 
The system boundaries consisted of two sets of boundaries: geographical and temporal. The geographical 
system boundary was Denmark. Denmark was a smaller system compared to other European countries. 
The geographical size of the system generated the assumption that import, and export were a substantial 
part of the flows. The aim was therefore to include information regarding import and export countries to-
gether with the assumed treatment of exported plastic waste. The notation of import, export country and 
treatment option made it suitable to model dynamic changes to the systems as possible legislative action 
and geopolitical changed.  
The temporal boundary was a present or near present timeframe. The realistic expectation is a temporal 
scope set to 2016. The temporal boundary was based on data availability primarily from the recent Danish 
waste statistic from 2016 published in 2018 (Nissen, et al., 2018). If it was not possible to find information 
relating to the temporal and geographical boundaries there were measures to completing the MFA.  For 
temporal modification there were the possibilities regarding changing the geographical search to Nordic,

Polymer fractions 
The polymer fractions were based of class descriptions from datasets, annual market demands for poly-
mers and studies. The polymers investigated were PE, PP, PVC, PS, PET and Other. Other was a group con-
taining polymers not mentioned earlier e.g. PA, PUR or ABS. The polymer fractions were assessed primarily 
from the same literary sources as was the case for untreated waste water, imported and exported waste, 
some product estimations and PVC in waste. The assessment of PVC content was possible for waste flows 
as PVC was reported as a separate waste fraction. The other group also contained undetermined quantities, 
so the sum of fractions equalled 1. The polymer fractions were multiplied by the relevant flows to get a 
mass flow for the investigated polymer layers in the collect MFA model with uncertainties derived from the 
Pedigree matrix’ CV. For the complete polymer content of flows, see Appendix F.

Uncertainties 
The primary data used derived from governmental sources with limited to no uncertainty. The plastic con-
tent and polymer fractions were mainly averaging numbers. The singular data sources created no data

intervals or confidence intervals for uncertainty estimations. Uncertainties were implemented using a PEDI-
GREE matrix from the procedure in Laner et al. (2016). The uncertainties were derived from a coefficient of 
variation (CV) establish from five data quality indicators: Reliability, completeness, temporal, geographical 
and other correlations. The quality indicators translated to individual CV for each indicator relating to the 
sensitivity of the flow. The flow sensitivity was grouped into Highly sensitive, Medium sensitive and Not 
sensitive. No actual sensitivity analysis was conducted. The sensitivity was based in relative contribution to 
the overall mass for flows and stocks not taken the global stock of consumption and product use into ac-
count. The distinction was if a flow, stock or delta stock was 10 % or higher of the sum of all flow, stock or 
delta stock it was scored as Highly sensitive. The contribution for Medium sensitive was below 10 % and 
above 1 %. The contribution for Not sensitive was below 1 %. The global stock for Consumption and prod-
uct use was set at a high sensitivity level because of the amount. The global stock for Consumption and 
product use had otherwise been left out of the total mass for flows and stocks, when rating the other sensi-
tivities, as it was an outlier with a quantity 5 times larger than the sum of all other flows and stocks. The 
flows were scored from 1 to 4 for all 5 indicators based on defined evaluation criteria and associated with 
CVs, see Appendix C and Table 2. The CVs for each quality indicator were used for a total CV. The calcula-
tion of the total CV was used for a standard deviation to the relevant flows, see Eq. 4 and Eq. 5. The stand-
ard deviations were transferred to the polymer fraction and the simplified MFA. A PEDIGREE matrix was not 
constructed for each polymer layer and its values. The uncertainty made data reconciliation possible in 
STAN.

Recycling indicators 
Recycling indicators were calculated from the method defining regional recycling indicators for metals 
(Espinoza & Soulier, 2018). The lettering had been changed to fit the flows of the Danish plastic MFA. The 
method used for various recycling indicators calculated Recycling input rate (RIR), Recycled content (RC), 
End-of-life recycling input rate (EoL RIR), End-of-life recycled content (EoL RC), End-of-life recycling rate 
(EoL RR), End-of-life collection rate (EoL CR), End-of-life processing rate (EoL PR), Overall processing rate 
(OPR) and Overall recycling efficiency rate (ORER), see Eq. 8 to Eq. 16 in Table 4. The recycling indicator Old 
Scrap ratio (OSR) was not calculated as the MFA model sould not distinguish between pre-consumer waste 
for recycling or End-of-life waste for recycling. It was modelled as one flow due to the data output (ADS, 
2016A; Espinoza & Soulier, 2018). RC, EoL RIR and EoL RC uses 3 parameters: α, β and γ. 
- 
α was a variable describing the share of recycled material in plastic import and export. α was as-
sumed 9 % as RIR global, 9 % of primary plastic waste have been recycled. The assumption was pos-
sibly an overestimation as only 10% of the recycled plastic had been recycled more than once 
(Geyer, et al., 2017A).  
- 
β was describing the secondary material from EoL in traded materials specified as products. The 
assumption was α = β as recycled material in products or material was counted as origination from 
EoL plastic.  
- 
γ was the content of EoL in traded secondary material. γ was assumed to be 100 % as it was waste 
flows, mentioned in the class description (Statistikbanken, 2016). Espinoza & Soulier (2018) distin-
guished between import or export for α, β and γ. The assumptions used on α, β and γ for the plastic 
MFA did not differentiate between import or export having different recycled content or origins, 
making α, β and γ 3 and not 6 variables.

Material flows and stocks 
The material flows and stocks amounted to a total of 22 million ton of plastic, with Consumption and prod-
uct use (CPU) constituted 84 % or 19 million ton of plastic. The 16 % at 3.6 million ton of plastic was spread 
out on the flows from raw material input to air emissions as mass conversion from incineration. The largest 
flows in the overall plastic layer for the detailed MFA was the Imported plastic pellets at 740,000 ±60,000

smallest flows for the overall plastic layer were Waste to special treatment with 8.95 ±0.21 ton, Water con-
taining plastic at 11 ±0.28 ton and Sludge to landfill at 31 ±0.8 ton. The numbers were rounded using the 
inbuild function in STAN for significant digits depending on the leading digit of uncertainty. The uncertainty 
was presented with 2 significant digits when 1 or 2 was the first digit to ensure limited percentage change 
from the reported uncertainty.  For the detailed Danish plastic MFA, see Figure 10 in Appendix H.

The polymer fractions were filled out for 95 % of the selected polymers and flows, see Table 12 in Appendix

4.3 
Uncertainties 
The uncertainties were quantified from a method to interpret subjective and qualitative data with fixed 
data quality indicators and associated CV calculated to standard deviations using the flow values. The total 
CV ranged from 2.3 % as minimum and a maximum of 20.9 %. The average was 6.9 % but the median was 
4.7 %.  The largest uncertainties based on this method was the 3 flows Impurities sent to incineration, Re-
processed plastic pellets and Exported reprocessed plastic pellets. The 3 flows scored a CV total of 20.9 % 
primarily due to the score of 3 in the data quality indicator: Reliability. This resulted in a reliability CV of 
20.6 %. The lowest CV was the 2.3 % and applied to several flows primarily the import and export waste 
flows. The import and export of waste were derived from a governmental institution and applied to the 
specific year. The import and export of waste flows were not sensitive due to the flow value and scored 1 in 
all 5 categories. 
The largest absolute standard deviations were 60,500 ton, 45,300 ton and 36,700 ton for flows Imported 
plastic pellets, Imported plastic products and Plastic products but the CV was 20 to 35 % above the average 
CVs.  
The method was finally implemented on the global stock for Consumption and product use set as highly 
sensitive, as the stock was 84 % of the sum of all flows and stocks.  
The uncertainties changed when the system was reconciled in STAN. The uncertainty change was to resolve 
any contradictions and reduce uncertainty. The reconciliation was relevant as the system was overdeter-
mined for certain processes. The only flow left unknown was the Untreated waste water and the losses. 
The implementation of uncertainties to the flows made the reconciliation possible and ensured a mass bal-
anced system.

5. Discussion 
The discussion section is structured like the method after the system definition and the results. The discus-
sion contains the material flows and stocks, polymer fractions reflections, deliberations surrounding uncer-
tainty implementation the simplified MFA and Recycling indicators. 
5.1 
Material flows and stocks 
The material flows of plastic in Denmark in 2016, started with the amount of imported plastic pellets used 
in Danish plastic manufacturing and ended with air emission from incineration or deposited as micro plastic 
in soil. The results are in some degree what would have been expected in relation to a smaller production 
activity in Denmark of plastic products in relation to the total imported and exported amounts and no pro-
duction of raw materials from crude oil. The plastic were build-up in several processes derived from the 
overall mass balance of the system. The overall import of the total MFA of the Danish plastic with produc-
tion, waste and recycling consisted of an 1.31 million ±50,000 ton, an export of 691,000 ±25,000 ton and an 
internal build up of 620,000 ±50,000 ton for the year 2016. Furthermore an accumulative plastic stock was 
estimated to be 19 million ±3million ton of plastic dating back to 1966. The government in its plastic action 
plan reported an annual plastic waste production of 340,000 (mfvm, 2018b). This project found similar re-
sults with a Danish plastic waste production of 327,000 ±5,000 ton in 2016.   
The MFA model in relation to domestic production, import and export had the potential to underestimate 
the actual plastic flows in 2016. Due to the method of only counting known or estimated plastic content 
with data retrieved. A precautionary principle is need for concluding on the project. Danish household plas-
tic accounts for 10 % of all plastic in household waste and 8 % of all plastic waste, yet we prioritize it as a 
source separated fraction for recycling mentioned above. The project then revealed a potential of roughly 
200,000 ton of plastic in the residual household waste, now making it a promising potential for recyclable 
plastic waste. 
5.2 
Polymer factions 
The polymer fractions in the flows previously assed was mapped to give the impression where larger quan-
tities of widely used polymers is likely to be. The safe and professional assessment of the use for the de-
tailed polymer layer of the MFA does not extend further. The judgement was based on the lacking data rec-
onciliation of the polymer layers in STAN. The system was reconciliated for all polymer layers when the sim-
plified model was constructed. This supports the use of the polymer layer used in overall polymer conclu-
sions. The differences in the polymer fractions were found to be relating to abundance and data availabil-
ity. The other fraction for polymer containing all other polymers not mentioned in the project or not re-
ported by the studies used for polymer fraction data. The compiled other fraction was the largest of the 
polymer layers. The other fraction accounted for an average of 41 ±4 % of the plastic amounts in the simpli-
fied MFA. The total system import for the other layer was at 583,000 ±29,000 ton out of the total imported 
plastic at 1.31 million ±50,000 ton in 2016, that was 45 % of the total plastic import in 2016 other polymers 
or polymers not mentioned in the used studies. The polymers that constituted the most out of the plastic 
MFA was in ranked order: Other, mentioned above, PE with 28 ±2 %, PP with 15 ±1 %, PET with 6 ±1 %, PS 
with 5 ±0.4% and PVC with 2 ±0.2%. All as averages of the layers in relation to the plastic flows in the sim-
plified MFA. The most abundant polymers was PE and PP, when reported on polymers included in the pro-
ject. The polymer fraction PVC in waste assumed to consist of the source separated waste fraction PVC. The 
polymer fraction estimation of PVC was the most confident under that premise of polymers in plastic 
waste. PVC was a small part of the Danish plastic waste production. PVC was 5,830 ±100 ton out of the 
327,000 ±5,000 ton plastic waste produced in 2016.

5.3 
Uncertainties 
The uncertainties were implemented using a PEDIGREE matrix. The uncertainties did not reflect the reality. 
The uncertainty estimations were a measure to display, that data could be inadequate. The data collection 
was based on governmental statistics centred on trade, production and waste. The reporting systems pre-
sented individual data sets for relevant flows without any uncertainty. The absence of reporting intervals or 
deviation was not an evidence of the data gathered was flawless and final. There was probably reporting 
errors e.g. underreporting or wrong classifications. The waste data from ADS was published just after the 
beginning of this project and was adjusted 3 months into the process. This was grounds for further precau-
tions as the produced and treated waste amounts rose. The implementation of uncertainties was to adjust 
for these reporting changes and to include the possible difference from literature data and the reality. All 
uncertainties reported stemmed from the PEDIGREE matrix used on the plastic content in flows and in-
cluded in all calculations and models forward. The uncertainties needed an update to reflect other than a 
possible reporting error interval but also in relation to the polymer fraction uncertainty. The further extent 
of the uncertainties not conducted is grounds for further studies. 
5.4 
Simplified MFA  
The simplified MFA presented the main findings of plastic flows in Denmark. The Simplified MFA derived 
from the original plastic MFA and subsequent flows had changes to the system when reconciled. The over-
all import was changed from 1.30 million ±80,000 ton in the detailed MFA to the import of 1.31 million 
±50,000 ton in the simplified MFA when reconciled. The changes affected the other flows in similar ways. 
The simplified MFA made it possible to reconcile the polymer layers; this had not been possible in the de-
tailed MFA. The difference made it hard to present possible conclusion for the individual polymer. The poly-
mer layers could be used to identify larger deposits of included polymers. An example is PVC going to incin-
eration. It was estimated that 2.62 ±0.06 ton of PVC was sent for incineration in 2016 out of the approxi-
mately 200,000 ton of plastic incinerated, see Figure 13 in Appendix H. 
5.5 
Recycling indicators 
The detailed MFA model made calculation of different recycling indicators possible. The recycling indicators 
(RI) were calculated for the researched polymers but the data were too inconsistent to establish RI for indi-
vidual polymers. When describing the uncertainties, it is also important to mention limitations of the study. 
The limitations are concerning estimated plastic content based on literary sources. The results about RI was 
limited to plastic recycling in source separated plastic waste, plastic in household residual waste and other 
plastic waste included in the fraction estimations, see section 3.2.4. In Denmark 9.9 ±0.3 % of plastic waste, 
considering processes within the national boundaries, was recycled. The recycling of plastic packaging re-
ported to be 31% in 2015 (Nissen, et al., 2018). There is a discrepancy, as this project reported a RR 3 times 
smaller than the 31%. The inconsistency stemmed from the EPA only considering source separated plastic. 
The solution to the lower RR has a solution regarding plastic in residual waste e.g. sent for incineration. 
Roughly, 200,000 ton of plastic is sent to incineration and 99 % of the plastic waste derives from residual 
waste. A study reported the contamination of plastic waste in contact with food at under 3 %, which was 
the required limit for organic content from the receiving facility. Smaller residues of food and fatty products 
did not constitute a problem in connection with further the sale of plastic packaging waste (Malmfren-
Hansen, et al., 2018). The change from residual plastic waste having been in contact with food e.g. makes it 
a challenge but the potential in regards to increasing the plastic EoL RR is over 500 % and will allow Den-
mark to achieve the goal of recycling 22.5 % of plastic packaging by 2022. The RR of the residual plastic 
waste sent for incineration would have to be 19 % to achieve the goal of recycling 22.5 % of plastic packag-
ing by 2022. The potential is larger than the waste related objectives set by EU (EC, 2018a).

5.6 
Circular economy, Import ban and microplastic 
The Danish government presented a vison for a circular plastic consumption, where the 2nd initiative was 
fitting for the project aim: Analysis of Denmark's plastic consumption and handling. The analysis was de-
scribed as a detailed mapping of plastic across sectors and types with inclusion of waste treatment.The 
Danish plastic waste production was estimated to approximately 340,000 ton annually (mfvm, 2018b) rela-
tively close to the 327,000 ±5,000 ton estimated in the results of the project. The result showed only 9.9 
±0.3 % of plastic waste, considering processes within the national boundaries, was recycled. The inclusion 
of plastic in a circular economy seemed farfetched when 18,600 ±1,900 ton was reentered to Danish pro-
duction out of a total import of 1.31 million ±50,000 ton of plastic. 
The consequences of plastic entering a circular thinking is still present. The limited amount of plastic sent 
for recycling and potentially substituting raw materials minimizes the risk of harmful substance in the 
treated waste introduce harmful substances into the reprocessed plastic. The risk is present but infor-
mation regarding imported raw material and imported products is need to assess the overall harmful sub-
stances recycled into the reprocessed plastic as a consequence of harmful substances not removed when 
recycled. 
The plastic MFA showed plastic ending up in soil as microplastic. The consequences of microplastic in soil 
was not estimated. News reported changed behavior of rainworms (Aagaard, 2017). The effects of the con-
tinues spread of sludge containing plastic was grounds for concern about diffusion into food production 
and finally the human organism. The plastic stems from various source and a the growing anerobic treat-
ment of organic waste is a possible source of microplastics accumulation in the environment (Afshar, 2018). 
China introduced an import ban of low quality waste in 2018. The MFA showed 3,800 ±90 ton of plastic 
waste exported to Hong Kong and further 26,600 ±800 ton of plastic waste was estimated exported to 
China as a second step after Denmark exported it to Germany. This was approximately 2/3 of the overall 
waste exported for 2016. Instead of recycling waste is exported as low quality plastic waste related to the 
Chinese import ban. Out of sight – out of mind. The country, that receives the exported plastic waste just 
notify, that it will be recycled. But it is not followed up. It is their problem now. It is estimated that on a 
global scale 111 million ton of plastic waste need to be displaced for a 10-year linear regression (Brooks, et 
al., 2018). The Danish recycling industry predicts that the impact on the Chinese import ban will bring other 
counties to offer services to facilitate the plastic waste export from Denmark (Larsen, 2018).

6. Conclusion 
The Danish situation regarding plastic production and recycling was found to consist of an overall input of 
1.3 million ±50,000 ton, an output of 691,000 ±25,000 ton and an internal build up of 620,000 ±50,000 ton 
for the year 2016. The accumulative plastic stock for Denmark was estimated to be 19 million ±3million ton 
of plastic since 1966. The plastic waste produced in Denmark in 2016 was 327,000 ±5,000 similar to govern-
mental estimations. The input, output and build up were mapped for the individual polymers PE, PP, PVC, 
PS, PET and other for polymers not described.  
The recycling rate for plastic in Denmark for 2016 was 9.9 ±0.3 % when plastic content in other waste frac-
tions were included and not only source separated plastic waste. The calculations indicated, that the na-
tional statistics based their recycling rate on source separated plastics in relation to the source separated 
plastics sent to recycling. The recycling rate for plastic waste in Denmark had a potential to benefit from 
waste export with credited recycling effort. The precise fate of the plastic waste and recovery efficiencies 
was not available and sent an out of sight out of mind option with a problem of who had responsible. The 
MFA with the polymer layers was subject to calculations regarding recycling, but the lack of reconciliation 
was a draw back for the further work. The level of detail in the MFA and the layer was an obstacle in differ-
entiating the various RI and polymers. 
The recycling requirements for plastic waste was set at 22.5 % of plastic packaging by 2022. The govern-
ment set a goal for recycling of plastic packaging at a minimum of 50 % by 2030.  The potential in residual 
plastic waste was found to be larger than the 2022 goal and adequate for the minimum demand of 2030, 
when the entire plastic waste amount was included. The increased recycling of plastic waste from the resid-
ual fraction was shown to be significant for meeting the government’s 2030 plan for plastic and plastic 
waste.  
In a circular economy, there was presented possible consequences of closing the plastic loop, when consid-
ering waste purity and concentration of unwanted and harmful substances. The MFA showed a fraction of 
plastic re-entering the production in 2016. The lack of recirculation of plastic material in Denmark and the 
uncertainty of treatment regarding exported plastic waste presented the plastic cycle in Denmark far from 
the idea of a circular economy and the accompanied consequences. The content of unwanted and harmful 
substances referred to the imported material and product and not the repeated recycling from Danish 
waste treatment activities. 
The system did not count plastic in other waste fractions than described. The conclusion dealing with waste 
was therefore based on waste fractions with plastic content estimations. The data used was from national 
or international governmental bodies. The data output used in the MFA could be adjusted to present day, 
as newer statistics is published and products change. The system was there for updated in under a day, 
when a newer version of the ADS report was retrieved.

7. Further research 
The further research deals with the subjects originated as independent new studies or further improve-
ments of the present plastic MFA. The grounds for further research would be a study in the effects of mi-
croplastic in agricultural soil as seen present in other studies. The effect in terms of degradation, build-up, 
uptake in products for consumption and the health effects for the human organism. 
An investigation into the potential and possibilities for Danish plastic recycling of lower quality waste then 
present day. Plastic waste in the residual waste sent for recycling has a potential to meet the role plastic 
have in the European and governmental goal of a minimum 50 % plastic recycling by 2030 
A search for the origins and composition of imported raw materials for plastic products in terms of recycled 
material and harmful substances. The composition of plastic in imported and exported products would also 
benefit the expanding degree of detail to the MFA. The End-of-life treatment of waste exported from Den-
mark is also important to possible map in relation to crediting of waste sent to recycling. This all serve to 
updating and maintaining the model with product estimations, publications of newer statistics and gather-
ing plastic and polymer content information to clarify the previous assumptions and working with the un-
certainties in another way than a PEDIGREE matrix’ coefficient variations. The uncertainties from the poly-
mer fractional estimations would be treated by firstly extending the PEDIGREE method to the polymer lay-
ers, so uncertainties derived from the system reflect the data quality of flow values and polymer fraction 
from other sources.
