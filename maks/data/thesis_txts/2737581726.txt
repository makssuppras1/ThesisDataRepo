ABSTRACT
Frost salt scaling (FSS) influences pathways and road infrastructure in many countries with freez-
ing conditions throughout the year. To prevent ice from building up on streets and pathways,
de-icing agents are used. Due to the de-icers being present, scaling occurs, and the concrete sur-
faces are damaged. This process has already been studied in several studies with two different
major theories proposed, the first being the glue-spall theory by Gulati and Hagy [1] considering
the stresses caused between the ice and the concrete due to their thermal expansion mismatch.
The second theory is the cryosuction theory, which considers the buildup of stresses inside the
capillary pores due to the suction effect of ice formed in the pores, which sucks non-frozen brine
solution into the cavities. Both theories account for most of the observed phenomena, but both
still have several factors left to be explained.
One factor that is not sure yet is how the different brine structures in the ice affect scaling ex-
actly. Previous studies have shown that fast freezing rates result in lower scaling, but the reason
why is not completely clear, and only the cryosuction theory has an explanation for this effect
for now. For now, simulations have not considered the brine structure in the ice and have only
assumed the ice to be a homogeneous material. In this study, the influence of the freezing rate is
investigated through experimental and numerical studies. The experimental studies were carried
out for two different freezing rates which, due to different minimum temperatures, could not be
compared, and due to the long curing time also not entirely to the data collected in a study prior
to this by Shpak. In the numerical simulations, the ice was simulated with different brine struc-
tures for the different freezing rates, which have then been analysed for their behaviour for the
temperature developments during temperature cycles obtained in the freeze/thaw experiments.
These results can not consider the cryosuction theory and can therefore only be discussed ac-
cording to the glue-spall theory. The results show a considerably higher stress caused by the fast
freezing rate, which is explained by less creep in the ice. Contrary to the fast frozen ice, which
behaves similar to ice as a homogeneous block, the slow frozen ice behaves as several smaller ice
islands, even if not separated. Due to this, slow frozen ice is expected to have more scaling due
to more locations being available in the ice to actually cause scaling, while fast frozen ice first
needs to break before causing stresses in the concrete.
Another factor, which is not entirely certain, is the effect that internal damage has on scaling.
For this, the strain and LU have been measured in the experiments, which have been analysed for
their correlation with the scaling amount. This has been done as well for the experiments carried
out by Shpak [2] and Asadi [3]. These measurements show a significant correlation between all
parameters, which suggests that they are somehow linked to each other. To investigate this in the
numerical simulation, cracks have been simulated under the ice for the fast and slow freezing rate
simulations. The simulations for the cracks show increased stress on the concrete surface when
cracks are present, and the plots show some kind of anchoring effect for cracks if the ice inside
them is connected to the ice above. This would suggest that, when considering the glue-spall
theory, increased internal damage would cause increased scaling as well due to higher stresses
being generated between the ice and the concrete.

CHAPTER
ONE
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
FSS is the deterioration of wet concrete surfaces as they undergo freeze/thaw cycles. It mainly
occurs on concrete, which is used on, or close to, pathways as they are subjected to de-icing
agents. This primarily has an influence on the aesthetics of the concrete surfaces as it becomes
a rough, deteriorated surface, but as it continues, it can have severe structural impacts as it can
reduce the concrete cross-section by a significant amount of time.
FSS occurs when water in concrete freezes and is weakened by additives like NaCl, urea, or
ethanol. If the ice and concrete are subjected to repeating freeze-thaw cycles, the concrete’s
surface will start to scale, which might lead to structural deterioration.
Scaling has been investigated for several years, but its process is not totally understood yet. Sev-
eral theories have been proposed to explain it, but several questions still exist. One promising
theory is the glue-spall theory by Gulati and Hagy [1] which originated from the interaction of
glass and epoxy when cooled. In the case of FSS, the materials are not homogeneous, like glass
and epoxy; they have different materials inside, such as the aggregate and cement paste in the
concrete, the frozen water, and the not-frozen brine in the ice. Another promising theory is the
cryosuction theory, which considers the water intake in the capillary pores and its freezing.
Several different tests have already been implemented to quantify the scaling resistance of con-
crete, but one thing they all differ in is the freezing rate. A study has shown that different
freezing rates also result in different ice-brine structures [4]. These different freezing rates do
cause different amounts of scaling, as shown in [5, 6] but the reason behind them is not com-
pletely understood.
Internal damage is almost always something that can be observed together with scaling tests,
as it is a process dependent on mostly the same parameters as scaling; it needs water, which
can freeze inside the concrete, and it needs freezing temperatures. But in what way the internal
damage has an influence on scaling, or if it even has an influence, is a parameter that has not
been thoroughly investigated.
1.2
Scope
The degradation of concrete when it is exposed to FSS poses a challenge for many applications
in the infrastructure and construction sectors. Here, ice with brine concentration forms on top
of the concrete and causes the concrete’s surface to scale. This master thesis aims to investigate

different ice and concrete parameters and how they influence scaling intensity. The analysis will
be performed using numerical methods, as well as previous and new laboratory experiments.
The analysis will be performed using numerical methods, as well as previous and new laboratory
experiments.
The influence of the following main parameters on scaling is being investigated:
• Different ice-brine structure with different freezing rates
• Liquid uptake during freeze-thaw cycles
• Internal damage of the concrete
The experimental studies aim to show the development of these different parameters with repeat-
ing freeze/thaw cycles, which can be used find differences between different freeze/thaw cycles
and possible correlations between parameters.
In this case, numerical simulations are used to take a closer look at the interactions between
the ice and its brine, as well as the concrete below with possible cracks. These simulations do
not aim to represent different amounts of scaling but rather show interactions between different
components to possibly explain the reasons for the results found in the laboratory experiments.
The results from the numerical and experimental analyses will be compared and analysed. Here,
the goal is to find any correlation between the freezing rate, liquid uptake, internal damage, and
the scaling intensity of the concrete. The experimental results will test any numerical results,
and the numerical simulations will in turn give deeper insights into the results of the experiments.
In conclusion, this master thesis is looking at the influence of freezing rate, liquid uptake, and
internal damage by using numerical methods and laboratory experiments and analyzing and
comparing their results.

2.1
Frost salt scaling
Currently, two different theories dominate the study of FSS. For one, the glue spall theory is
used to explain scaling, and another theory is the theory of cryogenic suction. Both of these are
described shortly in the following.
Glue spall theory
The glue spall theory was first brought up by Gulati and Hagy in [1] describing the interaction
of epoxy and glass in the manufacturing of ornamental glass. The stress, as seen in figure 2.1.1

expansion coefficient, which results in the epoxy contracting more than the glass and causing
high stresses at the edges of the epoxy islands. These stress peaks on the site are calculated by
using equations 2.1 to 2.3.

This theory has then later been adapted for FSS as seen in different studies such as [7] and [8].
The glue spall theory seems to be a promising theory as it accounts for the following effects
observed in experiments [9]:
• Progressive removal of flakes from the concrete surface, which shows similar behaviour as
in the glue spall theory with epoxy and glass.
• Scaling is most intense at a pessimum of 3% NaCl solution. According to the glue spall
theory, cracks need to be present in the ice to cause scaling. These do not occur in pure
ice, as this would be too strong. With higher concentrations, the ice gets too weak to cause
any stresses.
• Scaling only occurs if a solution is present on the concretes surface. Without an ice layer,
no stresses will be caused according to the glue spall theory.
• Above a temperature of -10°C, scaling does not occur as the 3% NaCl ice does not have
enough strength yet at this temperature.
• Air-entrained concretes show less scaling which is explained by the stronger surfaces of
the concrete, which prevent bleeding, and due to initial freezing in air voids, which causes
suction, which compresses the concrete skeleton.
• The salt concentration in the pore solution does not have an effect on scaling as only the
ice on the concrete surface causes scaling.
• Scaling and internal frost damage occur separately from each other. This is in favor of the
glue spall theory, as the mechanism does not relate to crystallization inside the concrete.
• With a weakened concrete surface, scaling increases with a strengthened one, scaling re-
duces. This is in accordance with the glue spall theory, as the ice does cause only a limited
amount of stress, which could cause scaling.
If the stress does not surpass the tensile
strength of the cement paste, no scaling will occur.
Cryogenic suction theory
Another promising theory to explain scaling is the cryogenic suction theory, which was first
introduced by Powers and Helmuth in 1953 in [10] in connection with the investigation of air
void protection during scaling, which has also been analyzed in [11]. Since then, the theory has
been developed further. A more thorough hypothesis for this theory is given by Lindmarks in
[12]. As described by him, scaling occurs due to osmotic ice body growth in the specimens and
pressures building up inside the pore spaces due to the provided reservoir of brine solution in the
ice, even below the freezing point, which is then sucked into the pore system of the specimens.
Due to this, the ice body inside the pores is able to keep growing, which ultimately results in
pressure on the pore walls, which can lead to their failure. This suction process will continue
4
==PAGE:13==

as long as no de-icer is transported into the pores to such an extent that an equilibrium is
established inside the pores, as represented in figure 2.1.2. This theory, as well as the glue spall
theory, accounts for most of the observed behaviours in scaling, as follows:
• FSS only in the presence of liquid on the testing surface is explained by the partial drainage
of the pores in the areas close to the surface, which then helps the pore solution establish
equilibrium, and therefore the damage caused in the surface parts of the specimens will
show less damage than the interior of the specimens, which does not have the option for
partial drainage as it is a closed system.
• In the presence of pure water, no scaling can be observed. This is suggested to be because
the ice layer on the test surface forms before ice can form inside the pore system. With
proceeded cooling, the pore solution will be drawn towards the outer ice, which therefore
protects the surface of the specimens. This process is similar to the process of air void
protection as described in [10] by Powers and Helmuth.
• With lower temperatures, increased scaling occurs. This is explained by an increase in
the driving force, which causes the ice body to grow and more of the pore solution that is
present inside the specimens to freeze, which ultimately results in a higher number of ice
bodies and therefore additional pressure inside the concrete.
• With rapid cooling, less scaling is expected as less time is available for moisture absorption.
It also places an intense heat flow rate on the concrete and freezing solution, which causes
a displacement of the balance between moisture flow rate and heat flow rate towards the
surface of the specimens, which causes the ice body growth zone to reduce.
• The reason for a 3% pessimum in the freezing solution to cause the most scaling is argued
to be because, with lower concentrations, the liquid will create a frozen layer earlier on
the surface, therefore, as said before, causing the pore solution to be drawn out of the
specimens. With higher concentrations, the specimens will penetrate more with the deicers
during the cycles above 0°C. The growing de-icer content then results in reduced ice body
growth and moisture uptake.
This is dependent on the time available for penetration,
the concentration of the de-icer, and the diffusion coefficient of the de-icer. Two contrary
arguments have been made by Müller in [7] as first, the diffusion process is a very slow
process that might not be sufficient to account for this effect, and second, liquid uptake
is also present when pure water is used, which likely happens during the thawing phase.
A solution for these concerns was proposed by Setzer in [13] who showed a model where
micro-ice lenses act as a valve for solution entering the pores. Pressure differences generated
after the triple-point shift will act as a kind of piston compression, which causes the gel
inside the pores to compress with cooling and expand again with heating. The micro-ice
lenses then act as a valve that traps the solution inside the pores during cooling, which
then cannot flow back out when heated.
• The air void protection effect for scaling is explained by the air inside the air voids con-
tracting, resulting in pore solution being sucked into the air voids. With increased moisture
uptake and lower temperatures, more solution will be drawn inside the air voids, there-
fore preventing the ice body’s inside the capillary pores from causing high stresses. When
thawing, the air inside the pores expands again and pushes out the accumulated moisture
inside the air voids. If the air inside the air voids finds a way out of the air voids, this
process will not function anymore, and it will result in a loss of this air void protection
effect.

2.2
Scaling test methods
Slab test (CEN/TS 12390-9) [14]
The slab test uses a temperature range of 20°C to -20°C with slabs of 50 mm thickness being
tested. In this test, the testing surface always consists of a sawn concrete surface. As a freezing
medium, a 3% NaCl solution is used in a 3 mm-deep layer on the testing surface.
The specimens are dried at 20±2 °C in a relative humidity of 65±5 % RH, with requirements for
a free water surface evaporation rate of 45±15 g/m2h. After drying the concrete is re-saturated
by applying a 3 mm layer of de-ionized water to the testing surface for ≈3 days until the test
begins, where the de-ionized water is changed out for the NaCl solution.
As a barrier for the NaCl solution a rubber sheet is glued around the slab with a thickness of
3±0.5 mm and requirements to keep its properties at low temperatures. To insulate the outer
surfaces of the concrete, a 20±1 mm thick Expanded Polystyrene cellular plastic layer is used.
To prevent the NaCl solution from evaporating, the specimens is covered by a PE sheet. The
test set-up can be seen in 2.2.1
Figure 2.2.1: Set-up for slab test freeze-thaw testing [14]
The temperature cycle used in the slab test and its upper and lower limits for the ice-concrete
interface are shown in figure 2.2.2. The temperature at the ice-concrete interface is required to
stay below 0°C for 7 to 9 hours.

2.3.2
Internal damage during freezing
Protection of air voids[10]
As shown by Powers in [10], air voids protect the concrete well from internal damage. If concrete
is non-air-entrained, the water in capillary cavities will freeze and cause hydraulic pressure in the
cavities. Additionally the gel water can start to diffuse into the cavities at lower temperatures
which causes the ice to grow and cause additional stresses. If air voids are present they prevent
the ice from causing high hydraulic pressure as the air voids will draw water from the concrete
paste around it. This will cause the paste to shrink. Eventually, the ice in the air voids will start
to draw excess water from the gel pores and the frozen cavities and they will cause the whole
concrete body to shrink. The expansion due to hydraulic pressure can be prevented if the air
voids are placed close together.
Sun and Scherer in [19] looked into the effect of air voids on FSS and on internal freezing. At low
temperatures it is observed that nucleation occurs in concrete specimens, and internal damage
starts for specimens not air-entrained. If the specimens are air-entrained, nucleation can still
be observed, but the concrete body starts to contract and no noticeable damage occurs. This
can be observed in the measurements shown in figure 2.3.13 which show continued contraction
of concrete for the 3 and 6% air-entrained specimens after nucleation and an expansion likely
due to internal damage in the 0% air-entrained specimens. Supported by calculations, fatigue
damage can still occur in air-entrained specimens after continued cycles due to local stresses
around mesopore crystals. In general, macroscopic strains show an overall contraction of the
concrete paste with air entrainment during the freeze-thaw cycles.

a nucleation of the samples at around -6°C [19]

In
the study, scaling, LU and strain were measured for the specimens and their correlation was
analyzed based on the concrete mixes and their curing conditions. The results show the lowest
scaling values for wrapped specimens. The scaling curves show different behaviour based on the
different curing conditions. Normally dried specimens show an almost linear scaling behaviour
whereas the more dried specimens show a less accelerated behaviour with continued cycles and
the wrapped specimens a more accelerated behaviour. In the strain measurements the more
dried specimens showed an increased dilation and the normally dried specimens showed the
lowest strain. The wrapped specimens showed the highest increase in dilation with cycles. Some
specimens showed increased leakage with the more dried specimens with earlier occurring leakage.
The correlation between the scaling, LU, and strain has been analysed by the R2 value separated
between the different curing conditions. The correlation between LU, and strain in this study
showed a significant correlation for the more dried and wrapped specimens but not as high for the
normally dried specimens. Between the LU, and scaling, significant correlation can be observed
between the results of the wrapped and normally dried specimens with lower correlation for the
more dried specimens. Between the strain and scaling, the normally dried specimens show a
slightly smaller correlation and the more dried specimens shows a lower correlation.

To measure the scaling intensity the scaled material after 7 ± 1 cycles is collected off the testing
surface. For this the remaining salt solution on the specimens surface is poured through a filter.
This separates all the scaled material from the solution. Material left on the surface is brushed
of with a brush and a squirt bottle. To get the dry mass of the scaled material, everything is put
into the oven at 110 °C until the debris is completely dried. The scaling intensity is quantified
by the scaled dry mass of the concrete specimens per m2.
3.1.6
Liquid uptake measurements
To measure the liquid uptake, the specimens is weighed at every data collection cycle and it is
quantified by the mass of liquid which has been absorbed by the concrete per m2. It is calculated
by using the weight increase of the concrete slab between the beginning or previous cycle and
the measured cycle.
To calculate the Liquid uptake following formula has been used [2]:
LU[kg/m2] := (mi −mi−1) ∗A + ms ∗((wsuc/wdry −1) ∗k + 1)
(3.1)
The sorption capacity of the scaled mass (wsuc/wdry) is provided by Andrei Shpak who previously
determined these for specimens originating from the same concrete mixture.
In the scaling experiments it is assumed that the liquid uptake of the scaled material is the same
as the suction porosity obtained in PF-test, which for the used concrete mixes is provided by
Andrei Shpak which has been used in [21]. For the air entrained concrete the suction porosity
obtained from the PF-test is 1.056 g/g and for the non-air entrained concrete it is 1.051 g/g.
The value k in this equation represents the correction coefficient of the cement paste volume
fraction in the scaled material. In experiments it is mostly observed that the scaled material
consists mostly of cement paste with just some aggregate in between occasionally. Therefore for
the calculations a paste content of 100% is assumed and and therefore a k value of 1.

3.2.1
Model development
The models aim to represent common experiment conditions. Therefore for each individual ice
island, a concrete block is chosen with a width of 50 mm and a height of 50 mm. The ice islands
have a width of 30 mm each and are placed in the middle of the concrete sections. A width of 30
mm was chosen to get results from the models were the ice island width does not affect the stress
caused by the ice any more and the maximum stress from the ice is applied to the concretes
surface. The minimum ice island width for this was found by Bahafid [20] to be beyond 15 mm
for an ice island of 2 mm thickness. To account for the thicker ice of 5 mm in these models, this
value has been doubled. To represent the carried out experiments and to get amplified results
with commonly 3 mm thickness used in models, a thickness of 5 mm of ice is chosen. In models
with more than one ice island, the ice islands have a spacing between each other of 20 mm to
ensure that the separate ice islands don’t affect each other in their results.
In practise FSS is a 3-dimensional problem with ice islands causing stresses in all directions on
the concretes testing surface and the brine in the ice will grow randomly in direction in the ice.
As it is suspected that the concrete will be damaged due to tensile stresses caused by flaws in
the ice or the concrete, this problem can be reduced to a 2-dimensional problem.

The brine is modelled according to the observations done by Maus et. al. [4]. He observed
different structures of brine with different freezing rates. With a fast freezing rate the brine

at this point very thin. The fast grown ice is simulated by adding brine spheres into the ice,
which do not connect over the cross-section. These spheres are sorted into columns as well and
between each column in the fast and slow frozen ice, a lamella spacing a0 is applied. This value
for ice with a NaCl content of 2.7% and fast frozen is about 0.4 mm, for slowly frozen ice it is
about twice as much with 0.8 mm. The column thickness in both case is assumed to be around
0.12 mm or thinner.

Calculations
The model materials are assumed to be linear elastic and isotropic. For the calculation the model
needs to be fixed. This would create additional stresses which interact with the stresses from the
ice, therefore the whole model has a rigid motion suppression while all boundaries are considered
free. The initial temperature in the whole model is set to 0°C as the water will only freeze below
this and the ice will not cause any scaling over this temperature. The temperature developments
shown in figure 3.2.3 and figure 3.2.4 are applied to the models. The temperature for the ice-
concrete interface is applied to all upper boundaries, this includes the ice outer boundaries and
the concrete boundaries which are open in these models. The concrete temperature is applied to
the left and right concrete boundaries as well as the bottom boundary.
3.2.2
Assumptions
Ice and brine properties
During freezing the ice will form slowly and it will enclose the remaining brine solution. With
continued freezing the water in the brine solution will freeze and less brine solution will be left.
The brine solution will be liquid and it will therefore not cause any stresses in the concrete. As
the salt is accumulated mostly in the brine solution, the ice in this model is considered to be pure
ice and the brine is considered to be a liquid and does therefore not have structural properties.
As the amount of ice also increases with freezing, the ice can’t cause any stresses in the beginning
as the ice construct will be to weak. As it is not feasible to simulate the growth of the ice in
this case, the ice and brine is assumed to be constant in amount as found in [4] by Maus et. al.
for saline ice at -20°C. Therefore it is assumed that the ice already has its full capacity from the
beginning and stresses might be overestimated in the calculations.
Perfect bond between ice and concrete
The bond between ice and concrete is not perfect and it will be very weak in the beginning
and it will strengthen as the freezing process continues. The ice and the concrete will probably
loose their bond in some places when the stresses at the ice concrete interface exceed the tensile
strength of the bond between the ice and the concrete. As there is no data available to how
strong the bond between these two materials is and due to the fact that the model would need to
be able to adjust to this and allow cracks when the tensile strength is reached, it is not feasible
to implement this characteristic to the model and therefore the bond between the ice and the
concrete is assumed to be perfect.
38
==PAGE:47==

Smooth surface of concrete
In practice the surface of concrete is rough and consists of paste or aggregate and it does have
bumps in the surface or even cracks with time or caused by curing. All these faults in the surface
strengthen the bond between the ice and the concretes surface and they are places were the ice
could cause the highest stresses as they would act as an anchor for the ice to pull at. In this
model on the other hand the surface is considered smooth as the bond between ice and concrete
is already assumed to be a perfect bond.
Concrete as uniform material
Concrete is not a uniform material and it consists of several different ones, like the paste and the
aggregate. All these materials have different properties which will affect the place and amount of
scaling as well as the formation of cracks. For this model the concrete is considered to be uniform
with the same strength all throughout the concrete. This would be a factor in practice which
causes high variations between samples as the placement of the different ingredients can not be
predicted, therefore trying to use the actual structure of the concrete would be a not feasible
option for this model as the goal is to show stresses caused between the ice and the concrete and
not all processes which occur during freezing inside the concrete.
No NaCl solution flow
In practice the concrete is not waterproof and the salt solution will slowly flow through the
specimens until it is saturated by the salt solution. This is an ongoing process and it might have
effects on scaling as well when processes like ice-lens formation are considered. To consider this,
a detailed structure of the concrete would be needed to be modelled containing its permeability
properties. This fluid flow problem would need to be considered over time and is also dependent
on the freezing and thawing process. To implement all this into the model is not feasible and
would need much further research in these topics to be considered properly.
This also makes the simulation a pure simulation of glue-spall stress which does not consider the
theory of cryosuction.
3.2.2.1
Differences in MATLAB simulations
Additionally to the previous mentioned assumptions, following are to be considered as well for
the models constructed in MATLAB:
Uniform temperature
For the models created in MATLAB, the temperature across the concrete and the ice is assumed
to be constant everywhere. Therefore this model does not consider thermal conductivity differ-
ences between the two materials. Additionally the temperature change is applied to the model
without a time factor, therefore factors like relaxation and creep can not be considered in the
stress calculation.
Ice parameters constant
As the temperature change does not include a time factor, the parameters from the ice and the
concrete can not be adjusted according to the temperature. In practice the parameters of the
materials will change according to temperature rises and falls.

3.2.2.2
Differences in COMSOL simulations
Additionally to the previous mentioned assumptions, following are to be considered as well for
the models constructed in COMSOL:
Strength development of ice
The saline ice will not be frozen all the way at 0°C as only pure water starts to freeze at this
point. After the starting freezing point the ice will only slowly develop its strength and the brine
spaces will become smaller and smaller. This is important as there can not be any scaling in
higher freezing temperatures close to 0°C as the ice does not have the tensile strength to cause
any high stresses in itself and the concrete. Unfortunately, this change in cross-section with con-
tinued freezing is not possible to be modelled here, that’s why it is assumed that the ice already
has its full strength after the heat release which was recorded by the temperature measurements
in the experiments in section 4.2.1.
Cracks are filled with ice
The cracks in these models are considered to be filled with ice and not with brine or air. Depen-
dent on the crack thickness the solution in the crack might freeze later than the solution on the
testing surface.

5.1
Uncertainties in testing
Adjustment in freeze-thaw cycle in fast freezing rate tests
During the first 21 cycles of the fast freezing rate, a freeze-thaw cycle was used, which did not
result in a low enough temperature to cause high enough stresses. The lowest temperature in
the freeze-thaw cycles at the ice-concrete interface was just above -7°C. In the thawing phase, a
highest temperature of -2.9°C was measured at the ice-concrete interface, which suggests that the
ice did not thaw in between the cycles. For the ice to cause scaling, it needs to thaw in between
the cycles as the cracks in the ice will not close and the ice will be in a state where no more
cracks can form in the ice as it has already adjusted to the stresses caused by the temperature
deformations. Because of this, the freeze-thaw cycle for the fast freezing rate had to be adjusted
so that the ice would freeze to a lower temperature and then thaw again in between the cycles.
After adjustment, the cycles showed a minimum temperature of just below -11°C and a maximum
temperature of just above -2°C. The maximum temperature is just around the thawing tempera-
ture of the ice, which gives uncertainty about whether the ice has actually thawed on the testing
surface. After the cycle ended, ice was observed to be floating in the salt solution. The temper-
ature at the concrete bottom was measured here to be 0°C and the concrete core at -1°C which
suggests that the concrete thawed a bit faster than the saline ice on top. Due to this, it can be
assumed that the ice has started to thaw at the ice-concrete interface due to the warmer concrete.
Uncertainties in dilatometry measurements
The dilatometer that was used during the experiments has not been entirely accurate through
all the experiments. In the beginning of the fast freezing rate experiments, the strains measured
showed high fluctuations between the measurements. As a result, measures were taken to ensure
greater accuracy. These measures included verifying the stability of the steel frame and taking
test measurements of the invar steel rod in between the specimens. During the last measurements
of the fast freezing rate, the dilatometer used became so inaccurate that no exact measurements
could be taken. Because of this, the dilatometer had to be exchanged for an HBM type WA/2mm
sensor.
Uncertainties in LU measurements
In the measurements, the LU seems to be negative for some of the first measurements and then
goes upwards again. This especially concerns the specimen dried at 20°C in the slow freezing
rate experiments and the NA-50°C in the fast freezing rate experiments. It is not sure why this
reduction has occurred. An error might have occurred in the initial weight measurements. This

is an option, but it is not sure why this effect is only observed in the 20°C dried specimen in
the slow freezing rate experiments. A more plausible explanation is that the suction capacity of

As the concrete blocks have been in the water bath for several years, the air voids have started

for water to enter the specimen and freeze in there. This would, for one, improve the conditions
for scaling as described in the cryosuction theory described in Section 2.1 as more ice bodies can
grow in the pore spaces and the suction of brine solution is improved through the cracks. On the
other hand, these cracks would also increase the stresses caused by the ice under the glue-spall
theory, as indicated in Section 5.3 as the ice in these cracks would act as a kind of anchor for
the ice islands.

In contrast to the slow freezing rate experiments, the specimens are less damaged if they are
NAE or if they are dried at higher temperatures. This suggests that the air void protection effect
does not work if the freezing rate is to fast. The air void protection effect works by drawing
water from the concrete paste due to a cryosuction effect [10]. This process is time dependent, as
the ice in the air voids freezes first and then draws water from the paste due to the temperature
difference. If the concrete is now freezing rapidly, the time between the water starting to freeze
in the air voids and the water in the capillary cavities becomes less, and therefore the cryosuction
process can’t keep going. This means the hydraulic pressure in the cavities will build up and
generate high stresses, despite the air voids in the concrete. Additionally to this, the ice can’t
move through the concrete cavities, as this also happens in a time dependent manner to reduce
stresses.[12]
The specimens in this experiment that have been dried at 50°C might show lower scaling results
because of this effect as well. These specimens have been damaged before in the concrete matrix
due to the high temperatures. This means that small microcracks have probably formed inside
the concrete, which would theoretically give the ice more and thicker ways to move and, therefore,
make it less restricted. This would probably reduce the stresses caused by the hydraulic pressure.

5.2.2
Internal damage
To analyse the internal damage of the specimen, two parameters can be used: the liquid uptake
and the strain of the specimen. The strain at a specific temperature (here at room temperature)
is a direct indication of cracks inside the concrete, as these cause a permanent dilation of the
concrete body, which can be measured. The LU is an indirect indication of internal damage, as
the cracks in the concrete will be filled with the salt solution. LU can also occur without cracks
being present if the specimen is not fully saturated before testing or if it is caused by the micro
ice-lens pumping effect described by Setzer in [13]. This way, LU itself is not a certain indication
of internal damage, but the increase in LU is a good indication of internal damage, as a steep
increase in LU indicates that cracks have formed in the concrete.
61
==PAGE:70==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
In the slow freezing rate experiments, the LU measurements (seen in figure 4.2.5) dropped in the
first measurement for the mildly dried specimen. These specimens then carry on to have increas-
ing LU between the 7th and 14th cycles and between the 21st and 28th cycles. The specimens
dried at 50°C don’t show a decrease in LU and increase at about the same rate as the specimens
dried at 20°C.
As it is not possible that the specimen has a negative liquid uptake in these experiments, the
failure should lie in the calculation of the LU. To calculate it, equation 3.1 is used. The variables
in the formula for the mass of the specimen mi, the dried scaled mass ms and the area of the
test surface A are values obtained by measurement, which means they cannot be changed in the
calculations and are very certain. The variables in the calculations that are uncertain are, for one,
the variable k and the suction capacity wsuc/wdry. The variable k is considered to be equal to 1
which is only dependent on the content of the cement paste in the scaled material. Observations
in the experiments showed that very little aggregate scaled together with the cement paste and
only in some measurements; therefore, the k value should not be adjusted. The only parameter
left that has an effect on the calculations is the suction capacity.
The suction capacity was
obtained by Shpak by using the PF test [2]. This suction capacity does not consider a degree of
hydration that is closer to 100%. According to [2] a degree of hydration of 0.6 to 0.9 is applicable
for the specimen. The specimens used now in the fast and slow freezing rate experiments have
been stored in water for several years. Due to this, the entrained air voids in the concrete have
been saturated, and therefore the degree of hydration will be much higher than for the specimen
used several years ago in the experiments by Shpak. For the specimen used in [2] a saturation
of the entrained air voids is assumed to be as seen in figure 5.2.13 a). After several years in the
water bath, the saturation of the entrained air voids has advanced to the state shown in figure
5.2.13 b). This saturation is dependent on the size of the air-voids and is shown in 5.1.1.

For the specimen dried at 50°C the suction capacity should not be adjusted due to this effect, as
all the water in the specimen, in the capillary voids and the entrained air voids, is evaporated
from the specimen, and therefore the air voids are not saturated anymore. The suction capacity
for the specimen dried at 20°C should be adjusted, as the water in the air void would not have
been able to evaporate in the seven days the specimen was dried. The water contained in the
air voids is therefore not considered in the suction capacity of the specimen, and it would need
to be adjusted. Even though it is known that this air void effect would not have an effect on
the specimen dried at 50°C, it should be considered that the suction capacity of the concrete
possibly changed due to the intense drying. Due to the drying, microcracks can have formed
in the concrete, which causes the concrete paste to be able to take up more water. This effect
should be even more intense on the outer surfaces of the specimen, which would be the testing
surface. Therefore, the suction capacity for these specimens is also assumed to be too low in the

after the 14th and 28th cycles. The specimen dried at 50°C showed a higher change in LU than
the specimen dried at 20°C. Between the series dried at 50°C or 20°C the differences between the
NAE and AE specimens are not in a big range. This suggests that the suction capacity would
need to be adjusted more for the NAE series than the AE series, as the AE series should show
lower LU results than the NAE results. The strain results (see figure 4.2.7) show an increase in
strain in the first seven cycles. Afterwards, there does not seem to be continued damage to the
specimen. The strain here does not seem to be affected by whether the specimens are AE or not.
Differences are observed only between the series based on the drying temperature. Specimens
with a higher drying temperature are shown to have a higher strain, with twice as much strain.
The measurement at 14 cycles for the NA-20°C series should not be considered in this as it
contains one outlier that probably originated from a measurement error (see figure B.1.9).
The LU results obtained in the fast freezing rate experiments (see figure 4.2.6) don’t show the
same behaviour as the series in the slow freezing rate experiments. The AE specimen series in
this experiment has shown a higher LU than the NAE specimen series. Between the two AE
specimen series, the mildly dried specimen shows less LU. On the contrary, between the NAE
specimen series, the mildly dried specimen shows a higher LU than the specimen dried at 50°C.
Due to the high fluctuations and uncertainties in the strain measurements as described in Section
5.1, the LU measurements are not possible to be compared to the strain measurements in the
fast freezing rate experiment. If the LU results are looked at from cycle 0 on and the previous
measurements before the adjustment of the freeze-thaw cycle are not considered, all series show
almost the same development of LU in cycles 0 to 28. This does not follow expectations, as it
would have been expected that the AE specimen would show less LU than the NAE specimen.
This observation suggests that these concrete specimens lost their air void protection effect. This
loss is present for the specimens dried at 20°C as well as the ones dried at 50°C which suggests
that liquid inside the entrained air voids does not have an influence here.
This behavior can be explained by the small duration’s the concrete has actually been thawed
between the freezing cycles. In this short period of time, the freezing solution does not have
enough time to be sucked into the newly formed cracks in the concrete. Also, due to the high
freezing rate, the suction effect described in the cryogenic suction theory does not have enough
time to be properly carried out. Due to this, differences between the AE and NAE specimens
and between their drying conditions have not yet manifested in the LU.
In the experiments done by Shpak, the AE and NAE specimens show about three times as high
LU as the specimens of the same concrete mixes in the slow and fast freezing rate experiments
(seen in figure 4.3.2). The LU of these specimen series starts to divert drastically after the first
14 cycles, so that the AE specimen has an LU of just above 1 kg/m² while the NAE specimen
have an LU of around 1.5 kg/m² after just 50 cycles. This suggests that a significant difference
should not be seen yet on the specimen in the slow and fast freezing rate experiments, as these
have only been carried out for 28 cycles.
The results by Asadi (seen in figure 4.4.2) show a steep increase in LU as well for the first
28 cycles. Afterwards, the LU increases less steeply for the rest of the cycles, with no abrupt

5.2.3
Correlation between scaling and internal damage
One important tool to determine if parameters are influenced by each other is their correlation.
This correlation has been analysed here using a correlation matrix for the fast and slow freezing
rate experiments and the experiments carried out by Shpak and Asadi. The correlation has been
analysed for every test series on its own, as these have different properties and will therefore also
have a different development of the different properties that would influence the correlation. The
correlation matrices for the slow freezing rate experiments are shown in Appendix B.1.5 and for
the fast freezing rate the matrices are found in Appendix B.2.5. The slow freezing rate shows a
very strong correlation between the LU and the scaling of the specimen, which has been dried
at 50°C. For the specimen dried at 20°C the results show only a strong correlation with a cor-
relation coefficient just above 0.6. As discussed previously, the suction capacity of the specimen
for this experiment would need to be adjusted. The correlation coefficient does not take this
into account and only determines if the parameters grow or fall with each other continuously
linearly. Because the suction capacity in this data is not adjusted correctly, the specimen dried
at 20°C had a drop in LU at first and continued with a rise in LU. In between, the LU falls
again slightly. This causes the correlation coefficient to fall. For the specimen dried at 50°C,
the LU does not have any steep declines in it, and therefore it does not influence the correlation
coefficient as much. If the data were adjusted, the specimen series would rise more linearly, and
the correlation coefficient would improve.
This is the same case for the fast freezing rate experiments, where the A-50°C specimen series
shows a very strong correlation, the NA-50°C and the A-20°C series show a strong correlation,
and the NA-20°C series shows a moderate correlation between the LU and scaling.
In the experiments carried out by Shpak (seen in appendix B.3.3), a very strong correlation
between LU and scaling can be seen for both the AE specimen and the NAE specimen. The
correlation values here are almost 1 and show, therefore, an almost perfect correlation. This also
shows that with an adjusted suction capacity for the fast and slow freezing rate experiments, the
correlation coefficients between the LU and scaling will improve immensely. Making sure that
this correlation does not depend on these specific concrete mixtures used in the experiments by
Shpak and in the slow and fast freezing rate experiments.
The vast experiments by Asadi are also analyzed for their correlation coefficients. These consist
of a total of eight specimen series found in Appendix B.4.0.4. All of these eight specimen series
show an almost perfect correlation with correlation coefficients between 0.95 and 1 with better
values usually shown by the wrapped specimen instead of the dried specimen. Between the spec-
imen series shown here from the experiments by Asadi, the dried series generally shows a more
linear correlation than the wrapped specimen. The wrapped specimen shows more variation be-
tween specimens in the LU for a low scaling value, which results in a steep incline if scaling starts
to increase. Therefore, the dried specimen series in this case has a higher correlation coefficient
by Pearson than the wrapped specimen.

Lower values can originate as well due to the fact that the ice island is basically separated into

are always in contact with the concrete surface. This is not always the case; the thickness and
size of the lamella vary, which would cause the lamella not always causing the ice to behave
like separate ice islands. This would also cause the stresses to increase at some points on the
ice-concrete interface. These stresses might surpass the tensile strength of the concrete as well.
This still confirms that the ice with brine, according to the slow freezing rate, does not need to
be separated by cracks to cause any stresses. The ice from the fast freezing rate would need to
separate and create a gap between the two ice islands to actually cause scaling. Therefore, a
slow freezing rate does give more places on the ice-concrete interface where scaling can occur,
which is limited by the stresses caused by these small ice islands. With the fast freezing rate,
fewer places are present for scaling to occur, and these are dependent on the behaviour of the
ice and when and where it will break.
The data obtained suggests that the stresses in the concrete will increase as cracks form in the
concrete. This is an effect observed for both the slow freezing rate and the fast freezing rate
simulations. In the simulations, it can be seen that the compressive stress on the concrete surface
does show increased values when cracks are present. This is shown at the time of maximum stress
in figures 5.3.5 and 5.3.6. The figures show a concentration of compressive stress close to the
cracks, which is much higher than the stress found under the ice with no cracks under it. This
suggests that these cracks, which are filled with liquid as well, can act as a kind of anchor for
the ice. This causes more stresses, which need relief at the edges of the ice island. This relief
occurs as soon under the ice island when the concrete sections are not confined any more with
cracks on the site and ice on top, as seen in figure 5.3.5. This suggests that with internal damage,
the stress on the concrete surface gets higher. The observations also suggest that the stresses
will get higher with continued internal damage, which gives high indications of internal damage
being a key factor in scaling amounts increasing with increased amounts of freeze-thaw cycles.
Well, AE concretes can, in this case, protect the concrete from intensive scaling as they are
mainly protected against internal damage. Freezing rates in the outside environment usually
come closer to the freezing rates of the slow freezing cycle, which gives lower stresses to the
concrete surface and therefore causes only minor to no damage. Only with the continued cycles
does internal damage occur and increases the stresses in the concrete, which then causes scaling
in the slow freezing rate. This needed internal damage does not occur with AE concrete, and
therefore stresses are usually not enough to cause intensive scaling in the specimen. With a fast
freezing rate, this effect should be minimized as the ice already creates high stresses without
cracks present. This would cause higher scaling already without cracks present. In previous
studies, as presented in Section 2.3, it was shown that faster freezing rates show lower scaling
values. As the stresses shown here would suggest higher scaling, other factors influence this as
well. As shown in the simulations, stresses with the fast freezing rate are only present on the
sides, so for scaling to occur, the ice would need to crack by itself due to inner stresses, which

6.1
Summary of Findings
The experiments carried out by Asadi and Shpak show a high correlation between scaling LU
and strain. This indicates that internal damage to concrete has a significant impact on scaling
as well as the specimen’s LU. The experiments carried out with a slow and fast freezing rate
show that the slow freezing rate causes higher scaling for specimens that have been previously
damaged due to the intense drying, and it is also observed that the AE specimen show less
scaling. Unfortunately, it is not possible to analyse the LU of both experiments with certainty
due to the suction capacity of the specimen differing from the suction capacity used, which has
been determined before in the experiments by Shpak. The strain measurements are not to be
used as well for the fast freezing rate experiments due to the fact that the dilatometer broke
during the experiments. The specimen show much reduced scaling in the 28 cycles carried out
compared to the previous experiments on the same concrete mixes by Shpak [2]. This reduction
is possibly due to the increased hardening of the concrete due to the long curing conditions in
water for the specimen.
The analysis of the numerical simulations in COMSOL shows increased stresses from the fast
frozen ice and lower stresses from the slow frozen ice. To accomplish this, different ice struc-
tures were simulated.
For one, the ice was simulated as pure ice in a form previously used
in studies. In another form, the ice was simulated with brine included in the structure. The
brine was here simulated according to the findings by Maus [4] for the fast and slow freezing rates.
The slow frozen ice does also show a much reduced stress when compared to the usually used form
of an ice-brine composite, which does not consider the actual brine structure and just considers
the weakening effect of the ice due to the brine. Showing this already implicates differences in the
resulting stresses with different freezing rates of the ice and, therefore, different scaling behaviour.
When investigating the influence of internal damage and its influence on the scaling process, the
internal damage was simulated by having vertical cracks in the concrete under the ice. When
comparing the simulations with and without cracks under the ice, it can be seen that, no matter
the freezing rate, the cracks increase the stresses caused by the ice in the concrete at the ice edges.
6.2
Implications for application
As shown in section 2.2 there are significant differences between existing tests that assess the
scaling protective properties of concrete. These differences do not only include differences in
the set-up of the test but also differences in the freezing rates of the freezing medium. This
71
==PAGE:80==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS
difference could have high differences in scaling results for the tests, which then do not represent
the actual scaling resistance of the concrete anymore. In the outside environment, very low freez-
ing rates are more common, which would suggest that tests that use a high freezing rate might
not assess the scaling resistance correctly for the purposes intended and show lower scaling values.
The scaling process and the internal damage process are mostly being evaluated with different
tests, and they are mostly considered to be two separate processes that occur in the same envi-
ronment with the same parameters causing them. While these two processes could certainly be
assessed separately, they should be combined to assess the internal damage of the specimen, as
well as scaling in tests.
As the presence of cracks can be observed to increase the glue spall stress in the simulations, it
can also be argued that the moisture uptake for the cryogenic suction effect can increase with
cracks in the concrete. This interaction between scaling and internal damage still corresponds
with both major theories, the glue spall theory and the cryosuction theory.
6.3
Recommendations for future research
This thesis only touches upon the topic of the influence of freezing rates and internal damage and
their influence on the scaling process. During this research, several different topics were found
that should be assessed more.
• Saturation of air voids with long water curing
Due to a long duration in water, the air voids inside the concrete will be saturated based
on their size with time.
After several years in water, most of the air voids have been
saturated. As a result, the suction capacity used to calculate the LU changes. This calls
for further investigation on how the suction capacity changes for the concrete with time
inside water. This increased saturation could also have implications for the scaling process
if the hydraulic pressure theory is considered, as this could have an effect on the air void
protection effect of the concrete.
This was already suggested and explained in [12] by
Lindmark, not proven yet in the context of FSS.
• Different freezing rates in experiments
The simulations show significant differences in the stresses caused by various freezing rates.
Unfortunately, the experiments carried out are not as intensive, so no certain conclusion
can be drawn from the results. For further research, the scaling, LU, and strain of different
concrete mixes should be investigated under different freezing rates.
• Simulations on internal damage
The simulations contained in this thesis only consist of no internal damage and one state
of cracks at a specific spacing. To further understand the influence of internal damage on
scaling, internal damage should be simulated in several different states to assess how the
effect changes with more damage. For this, several different crack structures should be
considered, as cracks do not just form in a vertical direction; they also form horizontally
and in other directions, and they might not show cracks on the testing surface.
• Differences in brine structure with different freezing rates
The measurements done by Maus [4] ] show 3D scans of brine at two different freezing rates.
It is not clear how the brine forms in between, and to further understand this, the tests
on different freezing rates should be accompanied by scans of the ice and brine structures.
The simulations done here show only a rudimentary representation of the brine contained
in the ice.

• Replacement of brine solution in experiments
The main aspect where the cryogenic suction theory and the glue spall theory converge
is the fact that the brine solution needs to be present. For the glue spall theory, it needs
to be present for the ice to be weakened so that it can crack. For the cryogenic suction
theory, the brine solution needs to be present for the ice in pores to be able to grow by
sucking in the brine solution. If it is possible to replace this brine with a non-liquid element
that acts as a weakening point in the ice and the ice consisting of just pure ice, no brine
solution would be present, but the weakening of the ice that needs to be present for the
glue spall theory is still present. For this, something would need to be found that behaves
with lowered temperatures like ice but also does not show a strong bond with the ice so
that the tensile forces of the ice are not transmitted into this object.
