English Abstract 
In recent years increasing energy demands, volatile oil prices, limited reserves of fossil fuels 
and concerns about pollution and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions have raised interest in 
exploring alternatives to fossil fuels utilization. Bioprocesses and biomass-based processes 
such as biofuels are alternatives that can help overcome these challenges. Although they 
represent an interesting alternative many concerns about the competition on the final use of 
the biomass as food or as energy source have arisen. Therefore, a sustainable, well-regulated 
and balanced biomass utilization is needed.  
Anaerobic digestion (AD) of agricultural waste provides an excellent opportunity to convert 
abundant and low-cost bioresources into renewable energy without being in competition with 
food needs. Moreover, biogas production from organic waste through AD is a multipurpose 
process that apart from obtaining saleable products such as biogas, fertilizers, heat and 
electricity can be used as a waste treatment technology. Nevertheless, the recalcitrant nature 
to degradation of lignocellulosic biomasses such as agricultural waste represents a big 
challenge for its utilization and economic viability. Therefore, an effective pretreatment step is 
required prior to AD to facilitate substrate’s degradation.  
Aqueous ammonia soaking (AAS) has proven to be a very efficient method for increasing the 
methane yield of lignocellulosic biomasses. One of its main advantages is the possibility of 
recovering and re-using the ammonia employed. Moreover, AAS has been investigated and 
optimized for manure fibers and wheat straw with very promising results but its efficiency may 
vary significantly depending on the biomass used and the pretreatment conditions applied.  
The main purpose of this project was to assess the efficiency of AAS pre-treatment in grass 
for methane (CH4) production. AAS was applied under different ammonia concentrations and 
treatment durations to pretreat garden grass. The pre-treated biomass was anaerobically 
digested with swine manure as inoculum in batch experiments and biochemical methane 
potential tests (BMP) were performed to assess the effect the pretreatment had on the 
methane yield. The results showed that after ~25 days of digestion the ultimate methane yield 
was enhanced 30% for the biomass pretreated at 18 %w/w ammonia concentration for 1 day 
and 20% for the biomass pretreated at 25 %w/w ammonia concentration for 6 days. The 
enhancement observed was not as big as the one reported by Lymperatou A for manure fibers 
and wheat straw of 244% and 43% after 17 days respectively1. The differences might be 
attributed to the biomass composition. 
==PAGE:8==
IV 
 
In order to allow a better comparison and understanding of the effects the pretreatment has, 
it is important to perform more experiments, test other pretreatment parameters and 
configurations, and carry out complementary studies such as biomass compositional analysis.  
It also has to be considered that while batch test can facilitate optimization goals in regards to 
pretreatment conditions, continuous mode experiments are more appropriate to evaluate real 
applications. Furthermore, to determine the viability of the pretreatment under the conditions 
tested and biomass used a technoeconomic analysis is needed.  
Equally important to achieve the economic viability on the pretreatment step is the possibility 
to recover and reuse the ammonia. Thus, another goal of this project was to assess in a pilot-
scale system the ability of the unit to remove ammonia from the biomass fibers and to recover 
the ammonia for its later reuse. Different experiments were performed varying the temperature 
and distillation duration and parameters such as ammonia recovery, system losses, ammonia 
concentration in the fibers and in the recovered vapor-ammonia solution were assessed. The 
results showed that all the parameters were more sensitive to temperature than to duration 
variations. In addition, under all conditions tested, the system sufficiently lowered the ammonia 
content in the fibers to allow its utilization in subsequent AD processes without the risk of 
ammonia inhibition.  
Finally, it was observed that there is a tradeoff between the ammonia that can be recovered, 
and the ammonia concentration of the solution recovered as higher temperatures and longer 
durations lead to higher ammonia recovery but lower ammonia concentration in the final 
solution.

Dansk Abstrakt 
Stigende energiforbrug, svingende oliepriser, begrænsede reserver af fossile brændsler og 
bekymringer vedrørende luftforurening og emissioner af drivhusgasser har i de seneste år 
drevet forskningen til at undersøge alternativer til fossile brændsler. Bioprocesser og 
processer baseret på biomasse, herunder biobrændsler, er eksempler på alternativer, der kan 
imødekomme disse udfordringer. Selvom dette udgør et interessant alternativ, har brugen af 
biomasse som ressource medført en stigende bekymring for konkurrencen mellem 
slutprodukterne fx mellem produktion af fødevare eller energi. Derfor er der brug et 
bæredygtigt, velreguleret og balanceret forbrug af bioressourcerne.  
Anaerob nedbrydning af landbrugsaffald udgør en glimrende mulighed for at omdanne de 
mest tilgængelige og billige bioressourcer til fornybar energi uden at konkurrere med 
fødevaresikkerheden. Endvidere er produktionen af biogas fra organisk affald ved hjælp af 
anaerob nedbrydning en multifunktionel proces, der, udover at producere kommercielle 
produkter som biogas, gødning, el og varme, kan bruges som teknologi for affaldshåndtering. 
En stor udfordring for nedbrydningsprocessen af landbrugsaffald, er den rigide struktur af 
lignocellulosiskbiomasse. Hvorved anaerob nedbrydning af lignocellulosiskebiomasser 
normalt omtales som ikke bæredygtig. Det er derfor nødvendigt at foretage en effektiv 
bearbejdning af biomassen inden den anaerobe nedbrydningsproces for at kunne nedbryde 
substratet.  
Aqueous ammonia soaking (AAS) har vist sig at være en effektiv metode til at øge metan 
produktiviteten af lignocellulosiskebiomasser. En af de primære fordele ved AAS er 
muligheden for at opsamle og genbruge ammoniakken. Herudover er AAS parametre blevet 
optimeret til brug på fibre fra gødning og hvedestrå, med meget lovende resultater. Men 
effektiviteten af produktivitet forøgelsen afhænger særdeles af biomassetypen og 
parametrene for AAS-behandlingen.   
Hovedformålet med dette projekt var at undersøge effektiviteten af AAS behandling af græs 
til at producere metan. AAS er blevet testet med forskellige ammoniakkoncentrationer og 
reaktionsvarighed for afklippet havegræs. Den AAS-behandlede biomasse var herefter udsat 
for anaerob nedbrydning sammen med svinegødning som inokulum i batch eksperimenter, 
hvor det biokemiske metan potentiale (BMP) blev målt for at vurdere effekten af AAS på metan 
udbyttet. Resultaterne viste, at efter ca. 25 dages nedbrydning, var der en forøgelse af det 
endelige methanudbytte på 30%, for biomasse AAS-behandlet ved 18 w/w% ammoniak i ét 
døgn. En forøgelse på 20 % blev observeret ved AAS-behandling med 25 w/w % ammoniak i 
6 dage. Forøgelsen var ikke i samme omfang som opnået af Lymperatou A hvor fibre fra

sammenligning og forståelse af effekten af ammoniakforarbejdelsen, er flere forsøg med 
forskellige parametre og indstillinger og biomasse typer nødvendige. Her er det også

andet fokus for dette projekt har derfor været at undersøge i hvor høj grad ammoniakken 
kunne blive fjernet og opsamlet fra biomassefibrene til senere genanvendelse. Dette er blevet 
undersøgt i pilot-skala. Flere forsøg var udført under forskellige temperaturer, destillations

følgende proces uden risiko for ammoniak-inhibering.   
Endeligt var der observeret, at ved højere temperaturer og længere varighed for processen 
var mængden af genvundet ammoniak øget mens ammoniakkoncentrationen i den endelige 
opløsning faldt. På den måde udgør procesbetingelserne et trade-off mellem den ammoniak 
der kan blive genvundet og koncentrationen af ammoniak i den genvundne opløsning.

1. Introduction 
In 2016 fossil fuels constituted 80% of the world’s primary energy consumption and only 10% 
was covered by biofuels and waste. Together the industry and transport sectors represented 
60% of the world’s energy consumption and within these sectors fossil fuels covered 61% and 
96% of their energy needs respectively2,3. In recent years increasing energy demands, volatile 
oil prices, limited reserves of fossil fuels and concerns about pollution and GHG emissions 
have increased interest in exploring alternatives to fossil fuels4. Focus has been given to 
sustainable energy sources such as solar, thermal, hydro and wind energy. However, these 
alternatives have high reliance on weather conditions and still have big challenges in areas 
such as energy storage. Moreover, since heat and electricity are the main outputs from these 
sources, its applicability in sectors such as transportation or heavy industry is quite limited.  
Biomass-based processes and bioprocesses have the advantage that a wider set of products 
ranging from heat, electricity and energy carriers (fuels) to chemicals and pharmaceuticals 
can be produced throughout these processes. Offering thus alternatives to processes and 
sectors believed to be exclusively based on fossil fuels and petroleum products. It is important 
of course to highlight that there are still many other challenges to overcome and many other 
processes to develop in order to fully replace fossil-fuel based processes but as technology 
evolves, right legislation is set in place and investment on research and development 
increases,  it is expected that this gap will be closed.  
Despite biomass representing an interesting substrate alternative many concerns about the 
competition on its final use as food or as energy source have arisen. In response to that special 
attention has been given to assess the viability of using wastes such as agricultural and 
farming residues, industrial waste, urban solid wastes and sludge from wastewater treatment 
plants. Anaerobic digestion of organic waste provides an excellent opportunity to convert 
abundant and low-cost bioresources into renewable energy without being in competition with 
food needs. Moreover, this process can be used as a waste treatment technology while 
saleable products are obtained helping to minimize waste and make the most of resources as 
circular economy proposes.  
With the introduction of sustainable energy sources, the reliance on fossil fuels and their 
environmental impacts will decrease, moreover a diversification of the energy mix will occur 
generating more stable energy markets improving the security of supply.

1.1. Anaerobic digestion 
Biogas production from organic waste through anaerobic digestion is of particular interest 
since it is a multipurpose process that apart from obtaining saleable products such as biogas, 
fertilizers, heat and electricity can be used as a waste treatment technology1,4.   
Biogas can be produced in different environments such as wastewater treatment plants, 
landfills and during anaerobic degradation of organic material in biowaste digesters.   
1.1.1. A brief history  
The beginnings of biogas production can be set around 3000 BC when anaerobic treatment 
of waste was practiced by the Sumerians and in ancient China where covered tanks were 
used to store wastewater. In Persia and Assyria during the 16th and 10th century BC, 
respectively, there is evidence that biogas was used to heat bath water1,4,5. 
In the 17th century, Jan Baptista van Helmont first determined that during the degradation of 
organic matter, flammable gases are produced. It was Alessandro Volta the first researcher to 
scientifically describe the formation of flammable gas in marshes and lake sediments. In 1776, 
Volta concluded that there was a direct correlation between the amount of flammable gas 
produced and the amount of organic matter degraded5. The fact that this flammable gas was 
methane was discovered around 1804-1808 by Dalton and Davy independently and it was 
Dalton who gave the correct chemical formula to the gas1,4.  
In the late 19th century, the first applications for treating wastewater took place with the 
development of a simple airtight chamber known as “septic tank”. In England, a few years later 
after the septic tank designed by Donald Cameron was implemented, the biogas produced in 
the chamber, was used to power a public electricity supply (1895-1896)4,6. During the same 
century, the first anaerobic digesters for producing energy were constructed in India (Bombay) 
and New Zealand (Otago). In France, Pasteur proposed the utilization of horse manure to 
produce gas in order to improve the street illumination of Paris, however his suggestion was 
not carried out4.  
At the beginning of the 20th century, significant experience on biogas production was gained 
at lab and pilot scale. Despite the efficient operation of the ambient temperature digesters 
(mesophilic), the first heated digesters (thermophilic) were introduced representing the 
beginning of industrial digestion at high temperatures. AD acquired special importance during 
the Second World War due to fuel scarcity. When the war finished, and fuel was available 
again, most anaerobic digestion facilities stopped working. However, with the energy crisis in 
1973 and during the 1980’s, AD regained importance. In recent years, some industrialized 
==PAGE:21==
3 | P a g e  
 
countries are proposing the development of industrial-scale anaerobic plants aiming to reduce 
GHG emissions and improve waste stabilization and management4.  
Higher energy prices, stricter environmental regulations and the necessity to rely on renewable 
energy resources in the near future have shifted the attention towards AD which due to its 
dual purpose and versatile characteristics is a relevant and interesting process regardless of 
times and circumstances1,4.    
1.1.2. Process description 
AD is a biological process that occurs naturally when multiple microorganisms break down 
organic matter in environments with little or no oxygen (O2). A flammable gas (biogas) is 
obtained when the carbon (C) content in the organic matter is reduced to methane and 
oxidized to carbon dioxide (CO2). Biogas is made up of around 60% CH4 and 40% CO2 with 
minor proportions of hydrogen sulfide (H2S), ammonia (NH3), nitrogen (N2), hydrogen (H2), O2 
and halogenated carbohydrates1,4,5,7  
The microbiology of AD is complex since a highly diverse group of microbes that act as an 
integrated metabolic unit are involved. Each group performs a separate task in the overall 
process where a series of sequential and concurrent reactions occur. The end-products of one 
group’s metabolism are used as substrate by the next group. Therefore, a variety of 
intermediates are formed during the degradation process of organic matter into methane. In 
general AD can be described through four main steps, namely hydrolysis, acidogenesis, 
acetogenesis and methanogenesis4,5,7. All these steps define the final efficiency of biogas 
production from the conversion of complex biomass and are briefly presented below and 
summarized in Figure 1. 
Hydrolysis 
Biomass used as feedstock for AD may present a soluble and easily degradable fraction which 
is available for further digestion.  In addition, complex particulate matter namely insoluble 
polymers such as polysaccharides (carbohydrates), proteins and lipids can be present in 
biomass. These polymers are too large to be transported across bacterial cell membranes and 
thus need to be disintegrated into shorter chain compounds. Different extracellular enzymes 
are synthesized within and secreted by the bacteria. These enzymes catalyze hydrolysis 
reactions that produce soluble monomers (easily degradable) by cleaving polymers and 
incorporating water, thus its name1,7. The anaerobic bacteria that produces the hydrolytic 
enzymes needed for hydrolysis include species of Bacteroides, Clostridium, Acetivibro and 
Fibrobacter5,7.  
==PAGE:22==
4 | P a g e  
 
Proteins are hydrolyzed by proteases to their constituent amino acids7. The extent of 
hydrolysis depends on their solubility and structure. Globular proteins are more soluble and 
therefore easier to hydrolyze than fibrous proteins1,5.   
Lipids are high molar mass polymers formed by fatty acids and glycerol. Lipids are hydrolyzed 
by lipases into long-chain fatty acids (LCFA) and glycerol. The glycerol and LCFA are further 
degraded in the acidogenesis and acetogenesis step respectively. The hydrolysis rate  of lipids 
depends mainly on their fatty acid chain length, surface tension and pH and it has been 
reported to be faster in comparison to that of carbohydrates and proteins1,7.  
Cellulose and hemicellulose are biomass carbohydrates. Cellulose is hydrolyzed to glucose 
by cellulase enzymes and hemicellulose is hydrolyzed to xylose, arabinose, mannose, 
glucose, galactose and sugar acids by different hemicellulase enzymes1,7.  Cellulose and 
hemicellulose are linked to lignin (complex aromatic polymer) which is recalcitrant to 
biodegradation. Its presence greatly affects the hydrolysis of structural carbohydrates 
therefore hydrolysis is considered to be the rate limiting step in the AD process when feedstock 
with high lignocellulosic content is used1.  
In summary, during hydrolysis, soluble and insoluble organic polymers present in the biomass 
are broken down into monomers by extracellular enzymes. As a result, the monomers become 
easily available for subsequent degradation steps.  
Acidogenesis 
During acidogenesis, also known as fermentation step, monosaccharides, glycerol and amino 
acids are further converted to short-chain acids – primarily acetate, propionate, butyrate and 
succinate –, alcohols, CO2 and H2 by fermentative bacteria1,4,7.  
Many of the bacteria that carry out the acidogenesis are the same as those involved in the 
hydrolysis step (Bacteroides or Clostridium). Other bacteria such as Lactobacillus and 
Anaerolineae are also involved in the fermentation step5,7.  
These organisms are facultative and obligative anaerobic bacteria which convert sugar 
monomers to pyruvate by central metabolic pathways, such as the Embden-Meyerhof 
pathway (EMP), also known as glycolysis, and the Enter-Douderoff (ED) or pentose phosphate 
pathway. Pyruvate is then converted through the Acetyl CoA to volatile fatty acids (VFA) and 
alcohols. 
Amino acids are fermented by proteolytic bacteria that often couple the reduction and oxidation 
of amino acids through the Stickland reaction1.  
==PAGE:23==
5 | P a g e  
 
During the fermentation reactions, NADH is oxidized to regenerate NAD+, while organic 
intermediates of the fermentation pathways are reduced7. 
Acetogenesis  
In this step production of acetic acid takes place. Acetic acid is an important substrate for the 
final conversion of intermediate products to CH4. Short-chain organic acids and alcohols 
produced in the fermentation step are converted by acetogens into acetic acid, CO2 and H2.  
As for the long chain fatty acids produced during the hydrolysis of lipids, they are further 
converted to acetate H2 and CO2 through β-oxidation. Due to their low water solubility, 
acetogenesis is the limiting step of the LCFA degradation1,7.   
Hydrogen producing acetogens, that oxidize organic acids, are reliant on the subsequent 
oxidation of H2 by methanogens (hydrogen consuming bacteria) to lower the H2 concentration 
and prevent end-product inhibition7. Therefore, a syntrophic relationship exists between 
acetogens and methanogens since the H2 – producing reaction by acetogens is only 
thermodynamically possible (ΔG°<0) when H2 partial pressures are low. Disruption of 
methanogenesis will cause high H2 partial pressures and thus accumulation of intermediate 
products such as fatty acids.  
Homoacetogens also constitute another important source of hydrogen regulation and acetate 
formation as they reduce CO2 by utilizing H2 through the acetyl-CoA pathway. Research has 
suggested that acetate can also be first converted into CO2 and H2 by fermentative bacteria 
and then to methane by methanogens though syntrophic acetate oxidation. Similar to organic 
acid oxidation, hydrogen concentrations must be very low for acetate oxidation to be 
energetically favorable. The main reactions involved in the acetogenesis step are presented

Methanogenesis 
In the final phase methane is produced by obligate anaerobic microbes known as 
methanogens. Methanogens belong to the archaea domain and are highly specialized as they 
use a small range of substrates to produce methane. Acetate, H2 and CO2 are the main 
substrates for methane formation. Through the acetoclastic pathway, acetate will be converted 
into methane while hydrogen and CO2 through the hydrogenotrophic pathway. Formate, 
methanol, methylamines and dimethylsulfide are other substrates that are converted to CH4 
to a lesser extent 1,7. 
The acetoclastic methanogens belong to the genera Methanosaeta and Methanosarcina. This 
pathway ferments acetic acid to methane and CO2 and is responsible for more than 2/3 of the 
methane produced biologically1,5,7. When acetate concentrations are low Methanosaeta spp. 
are favored and when acetate concentrations are high Methanosarcina spp. are favored7. The

When easily degradable substrates are used, methanogenesis is the rate-limiting step since 
the growth rate of methanogens is considerably lower when compared to the growth rate of 
the microbes involved in the other anaerobic digestion steps1.

In the overall process, methanogens are key players since they utilize acetate and thus 
remove a significant source of acidity. Moreover, they consume H2 allowing the continued 
growth of the syntrophic bacteria that convert propionate and butyrate to acetate in the 
acetogenesis step.  As discussed before, when syntrophic bacteria are inhibited (at high H2 
partial pressures) the short-chain organic acids will accumulate, decrease the pH and create 
an unfavorable environment for the methanogenic archaea. At this point the overall 
fermentation ceases7.

1.1.3. Process parameters 
As a complex process, AD is influenced by multiple parameters that will determine its success 
or failure. These range from environmental factors such as temperature, pH, nutrients and 
inhibitors to process loading and operational factors like hydraulic retention time (HRT), solids 
retention time (SRT), organic loading rate (OLR), mixing and feedstock properties. Some 
parameters are briefly discussed below. 
Temperature 
The influence of temperature is very pronounced, from both the kinetic and the thermodynamic 
point of view. Variations of even a few degrees affect almost any biological activity, including 
inhibition of anaerobic bacteria, especially methanogens. Therefore, it is very important to 
keep a stable operating temperature in the digester. 
Anaerobic digestion can occur under psychrophilic (10-20 °C), mesophilic (20-40 °C) and 
thermophilic (40-60 °C) conditions. Treatment is usually carried out in the mesophilic or 
thermophilic range achieving maximum methane production rates at temperatures between 
35-37 °C and ~55 °C for mesophilic and thermophilic systems respectively4,5,7.  
==PAGE:26==
8 | P a g e  
 
These two ranges are optimal for methanogens growth; nevertheless, methanogens can grow 
at lower temperatures when longer solid retention times are used to compensate for the low 
specific growth rates10.  
Operating temperature affects hydrolytic, acidogenic and methanogenic reactions. When 
readily organic matter is used as substrate, the effect of temperature on methanogenesis is 
the primary concern. However, when complex organic materials are used the effects of 
temperature on hydrolysis and acidogenesis will be the main concern5,10. 
In the thermophilic range, degradation rates, pathogen inactivation and biogas production are 
increased. Despite this, the methane yield is lower since more CO2 is produced. The latter can 
be advantageous since the biogas pressure increases and thus its delivery is facilitated and 
disadvantageous because the biogas produced has a lower heating value4,5,10. 
Other big disadvantages when operating at thermophilic range are that the free ammonia 
fraction, foam formation and odor potential increases. Furthermore, the process becomes 
more unstable, since the rate of acidogenesis is higher than the rate of methanogenesis 
causing increased VFA’s concentrations4,10.   
Nutrients 
Anaerobic processes are characterized by low nutrient requirements when compared to 
aerobic processes due to its low biomass production yield. However, in addition to organic 
matter, other organic and inorganic substances are needed for optimal growth of anaerobic 
bacteria and to avoid inhibitory effects. The main nutrients needed are nitrogen and 
phosphorus (macronutrients). In lower amounts minerals-containing elements (micronutrients) 
such as Fe, Ni, Mg, Ca, Na, Ba, Cu, Mo, Se or Co are also required4.  
pH and alkalinity 
Each group of microorganisms involved in the AD process has a different pH optimum range 
and sensitivity level. Their balanced growth is fundamental to maintain pH in the proper range 
as perturbations in the process may upset the microbial balance4,5,7.  
Methanogens have an optimum pH between 6.5 – 7.2 and are extremely sensitive to pH 
perturbations. Fermentative microorganisms are less sensitive to pH variations and can 
function in a wider pH range (4.0 – 8.5). Their activity is usually improved at pH values below 
6 however methanogens do not present an acceptable enzymatic activity below pH 6.2 1,4,7,10. 
This difference has given rise to the so-called two-step anaerobic digestion which will be 
further discussed in section 1.1.4.  
The fact that VFA are intermediates in the stabilization of organic matter, coupled with the high 
pH sensitivity methanogens have, can result in an unstable response of the system to a 
==PAGE:27==
9 | P a g e  
 
decrease in pH. When the pH drops VFA’s production rate rises and VFA can accumulate. If 
VFA’s production rate exceeds the capacity of methanogens to use acetic acid and H2, the pH 
will continue to drop, and the activity of methanogens will further decrease near cessation10.  
Alkalinity is essential for proper pH control as it serves as a buffer preventing rapid pH 
changes. Adverse pH conditions can be corrected by the addition of proper buffering 
substances. Commonly used substances include sodium bicarbonate, sodium carbonate, 
lime, sodium or potassium hydroxide. Sodium bicarbonate is preferred because its toxicity is 
potentially low and its impact is longer lasting5,7,10.  
Toxic substances 
The presence of some substances called toxicants at a given concentration can also inhibit 
the activity of the microorganisms. There is no general agreement on threshold limits for 
toxicants as their effect is difficult to predict since it depends on factors such as temperature, 
pH and concertation of other substances4. Methanogens are the microbial group that shows 
more sensitivity towards toxicants presence1,4.  
Many inhibitory effects have been reported in literature from a variety of compounds such as 
light metal cations, ammonia, sulfide, salts, VFA, lignin derivatives and heavy metals1,4,10. 
Ammonia is probably the most widely encounter inhibitor in AD process as is the result of 
nitrogen degradation which constitutes a basic element of organic matter. Nitrogen is essential 
for microbial growth nevertheless methanogens can be inhibited when feedstocks with high 
organic nitrogen content are digested. The toxic species is the ammonium non-dissociated 
molecule, free ammonia, because it can more easily diffuse to the inner parts of the cell. Its 
toxicity is dependent on pH mainly but temperature and solubility also have a large effect1,4,11.  
VFA are produced during organic matter degradation and are present in the system in variable 
amounts. In a similar way as ammonium, the toxic species are the non-dissociated acids and 
their toxicity and threshold value will be determined by the pH and alkalinity levels in the 
media4. 
Light metal cations may be present in the influent and released during the biomass breakdown 
or added for pH adjustment. While moderate concentrations stimulate microbial growth higher 
or excess concentrations can cause inhibition10.  
Redox potential 
Microorganisms obtain their energy through redox reactions which can take place in three 
different types of systems: anaerobic, anoxic and aerobic. In aerobic systems, oxygen is 
required, and the redox potential is positive. Anoxic systems have no oxygen presence in the 
medium however the redox potential can be positive, with values close to zero.  Lastly, a total 
==PAGE:28==
10 | P a g e  
 
absence of oxygen is required in anaerobic systems and the redox potential is negative. 
Methanogens, which are strict anaerobic bacteria require a redox potential between -300 and 
-350 mV although they can still show activity at -200 mV4.

Mixing 
By enhancing contact between substrate and microorganisms, it is believed that mixing 
promotes improved digester performance. However, the effects of mixing are not well 
documented. Opposingly it is believed that mixing destroys microbes or forces interspecies 
association decreasing the performance of the digester. Therefore, the effects of mixing will 
depend on multiple factors such as size and configuration of digester, particle size and 
composition of the feed and method of mixing among others4.  
1.1.4. Systems and reactor configurations 
A wide range of bioreactor configurations exist depending on the type of waste and treatment 
objectives. Some of these systems are briefly presented below. 
Batch reactors 
In this system the feed is treated with a suitable inoculum and the fermentation proceeds until 
gas production ceases. This is the simplest and most common type of system used worldwide.  
Due to changes in the bacterial population during the AD process, this system is relatively 
unstable and uncontrollable. These changes can lead to digester failure and variations in the 
composition and quantity of the biogas produced4.   
==PAGE:30==
12 | P a g e  
 
Continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) 
Consist of a closed digester that contains the biomass and has one inlet and two outlets, one 
for the produced biogas and one for the effluent. The solids and liquid retention times are 
identical since an equivalent volume of effluent is removed when feed is added4.  
Mixing is required and is provided by means of mechanical stirring, biogas recycling or liquid 
recycling. Active biomass grows in a suspended mixed system and remains inside the digester 
for the same amount of time as the liquid. Some major disadvantages of using CSTRs are that 
as the loading rate increases the retention time decreases and unreacted solids and microbes 
wash out. To prevent this the residence time should be extended which translates to larger 
reactor volumes. Moreover, mixing is difficult to achieve in large reactors which represents 
another limitation4.  
A variation of the simplest CSTR is a two-unit system that includes solids recycling known as 
continuously stirred tank reactor with solids recycle (CSTR/SR). It is integrated by a digester 
and a settler and a degasser is usually included between the two units to improve settleability.  
With the solids recycle, the retention of the microbes and unreacted solids is increased 
improving the kinetics when compared to the simple CSTR. These digesters are widely applied 
for the treatment of soluble industrial wastes and other feeds where solids recycle is needed 
to maintain a wide community of active bacteria4.  
Plug-Flow reactor 
This reactor has a rectangular shape and the influent is feed through one end while effluent is 
removed in the opposite side of the reactor. Hydrolysis and acid production happen initially in 
the digester (close to the inlet) while methane production increases towards the effluent end4.  
No mechanical mixing is applied allowing solids settling and achieving longer retention times 
for solids when compared to liquids. During gas production some vertical mixing takes place 
when the gas travels from the bulk to the headspace. However, longitudinal mixing is minimal 
promoting phase separation4.   
Upflow anaerobic sludge blanket (UASB) 
Soluble organic feeds move upward through an expanded dense blanket of solids consisting 
primarily on microorganisms where the gas production occurs.  
The environmental conditions created in the bioreactor allow the development of granules 
allowing higher residence time of the solids and microbes in the digester. For these systems 
the SRT is different (higher) to the HRT4,10.  
==PAGE:31==
13 | P a g e  
 
Mixing of the influent and granules is provided by the combined effects of the influent 
distribution system, designed to provide uniform flow across the bioreactor cross section, and 
the gas production.  
To prevent solids flotation and wash out when the influent exits the sludge zone, various types 
of gas-liquid-solids separators integrated in the bioreactor are used4,10.   
Attached film digester 
Biomass grows in a fixed support forming a biofilm preventing biomass washout and promoting 
stable inter-microbial associations. This constitutes another way to keep active biomass inside 
the digester translated into high SRT and low HRT4. 
The anaerobic filter (AF) is an example of an attached film digester and consist of a filter filled 
with inert support materials (gravel, rocks, plastic, charcoal). High rates of methane are 
achieved when soluble feeds are used however feeds with high suspended solids levels will 
clog the system making them unsuitable4.  
The anaerobic expanded bed (AEB) or fluidized bed (AFB) is one of the most successful 
attached film digesters. It contains inert particles in which the bacteria will be attached and 
thus be retained in the reactor. The particles are small enough to be hydraulically expanded 
or fluidized allowing their free movement and preventing clogging when feeds with high 
suspended solids are used. In the event of a transient presence of toxic compounds or sudden 
overloading, the stability of the reactor is increased given the high biomass concentrations 
that can be achieved. However, these systems are very complex to design and very energy 
intense which represent major disadvantages for implementation4.  
Two-phase digester 
As discussed previously, the microbes involved in AD have different nutrient requirements, 
sensitivity, growth characteristics and environmental optima. Therefore, in conventional 
digestion it is quite difficult to simultaneously optimize conditions for all microbial groups.  
Two-phase digesters carry out the AD process physically in two separate steps: non-
methanogenic and methanogenic phase. By doing so, several obstacles encountered in the 
conventional process are overcome and different benefits are achieved. Two step digesters 
permit the preservation of appropriate methane and acid-forming bacteria densities and 
maximization of product rates due to the independent control of operating variables 
(temperature, pH, biomass recycle and retention time) on each step. Moreover, the process 
instability, caused by fluctuations in pH, feedstock loading and toxic feed components, is 
reduced and methane content is enhanced in the final biogas composition4.  
==PAGE:32==
14 | P a g e  
 
Two-step hydrolysis is not recommended for biomasses with high lipid content, since 
hydrolysis and fermentation of lipids may not occur without the presence of methanogens7.  
1.1.5. Anaerobic digestion applications and main products 
As explained before, AD is a multipurpose process because it allows the recovery of energy 
from the organic matter, in the form of biogas, and simultaneously serves as a waste treatment 
technology for influents used in the process.  
The digested material (digestate) shows improved characteristics that make its final disposal 
safer. Its quality is related to the initial composition of the feedstock and the efficiency of the 
AD process.  Among others, the main effects AD has on waste are reduction of volume and 
odor and inactivation of pathogens and weed seeds1.  
During AD, the organic matter present in the waste is reduced and its organically bounded 
nutrients are liberated. Moreover, the potential for GHG emissions is reduced and nutrients 
such as N are present in forms that can directly be used by plants improving the properties of 
the digestates. Generally, the digestate is separated into a solid and liquid fraction to make 
more efficient the handling of the nutrient contents. Digested material is commonly used as 
soil improver and fertilizer however other applications within the biorefinery concept are raising 
interest. For example, the solid (fibrous) fraction can be combusted, incinerated, fermented or 
further digested to be energetically exploited to a greater extent, while the nutrient rich liquid 
fraction of the digestate can be used for algae cultivation1. 
On the other hand, the biogas obtained through AD can be used in relatively all the 
applications developed for natural gas (NG), provided that upgrading of the gas quality is 
carried out. These applications are mainly: heat and steam production, production of electricity 
through co-generation, vehicle fuel and production of chemicals12.  
1.1.6. Substrates 
Any biomass can be used as a feedstock for AD and its mainly composed by carbohydrates, 
proteins, fats, cellulose and hemicelluloses. The organic content, nutritional value, absence of 
pathogens, presence of inhibitors and composition of biogas and digestate obtained are 
factors that must be considered when selecting the right feedstock4.  
More often biomass wastes with high water content (>50%) are utilized. These wastes arise 
from municipal, industrial and agricultural sources. Carbohydrates, proteins, fats, cellulose 
and hemicelluloses are the main components present in biomass. Based on its origin a first 
classification of the waste used as substrate for AD is presented below4:

Primary sector: agricultural, farming and forest waste 
Biogas production from this source is of growing interest and importance because it offers 
environmental benefits and can be a source of income for farmers.  
A plentiful source of organic material for AD is animal manure. The methane potential of 
manure comes from the organic components (mainly carbohydrates, proteins and lipids) 
present in the feces and in the straw used as bedding material. The origin of the manure, 
which accounts for species, breed and growth stage of the animals will influence its methane 
potential. Other factors influencing the characteristics of the manure and thus its methane 
potential are the animal’s feed, amount and type of bedding and any degradation that may 
happen during manure’s storage4.  
Manure contains over 40-50% of biofibers which can only partially be degraded. Therefore, 
relatively low biogas yields are achieved through AD of manure alone. The utilization of other 
organic wastes as co-substrates can improve biogas production from manure4.   
Inedible residues from food crops like vegetable waste, energy crops and leaves have a great 
potential as feedstocks for AD. As by-products, they do not require additional land, energy or 
water for its production. Its cost is associated with the logistics, handling transportation and 
storage operations and its value will depend on the attainable biomass yield and its ability to 
produce high methane content biogas with respect to the efforts required for its cultivation4,13.  
Digestion of the cellulosic and hemicellulosic fractions of agricultural waste will vary the 
achievable rate and methane yield because the rate and yield of hydrolysis among these 
fractions is different7.  
Plant species which are rich in proteins and easily degradable carbohydrates and low in lignin 
and hemicellulose are more suitable for biogas production because they show high 
biodegradability. Other relevant aspect is the availability of the crop to allow its use 
continuously as feedstock for biogas production. The optimum harvest time and conditions for 
preservation and storage are factors that will influence its availability4.  
Secondary sector: industrial waste 
Industrial and commercial operations are a great source for waste materials that can be utilize 
in AD. Food and beverage, sugar, dairy and pharmaceutical industries have the potential to 
produce waste suitable for AD. Pulp and paper, slaughterhouses and rendering plants among 
others are also a good source of waste suitable for AD4.  
Annually a large amount of waste is produced from the food and beverage industry 
representing a serious disposal problem worldwide. These wastes are rich in sugars, vitamins 
and minerals which are easily assimilated by microorganisms4. 
==PAGE:34==
16 | P a g e  
 
In the pulp and paper industry there are different processes that result in the formation of 
sludge and solid wastes which if not treated properly cause significant environmental pollution. 
Wastes are mainly generated from pulping, de-inking and wastewater treatment unit 
operations. The raw materials, paper grade and process techniques used are going to define 
the solids waste composition and amount produced. In pulp and paper manufacturing, 
cellulose is separated from the non-cellulosic substances of the wood by chemical or 
mechanical processes. Therefore, the sludge contains more fine proteins and sugars making 
it suitable for AD. Recycled paper fibers, wood, chlorinated organic compounds, pathogens, 
ash and trace of heavy metals conform the organic compounds present in the solid waste4. 
Some major limitations of utilizing these wastes are that since the sludge normally contains 
heavy metals, further work needs to be done to integrate this resource with other feedstocks 
and that the inhibitory materials need to be removed from the sludge before its utilization13.  
Tertiary sector: urban solid wastes and sludge from wastewater treatment plants 
Mainly comprised by household waste and sewage sludge produced during wastewater 
treatment. Food waste is among household waste the most relevant and abundant one. It is a 
very good substrate for AD because it is biodegradable and has a high moisture content. 
However, its high biodegradability allows fermentative bacteria to produce large amounts of 
organic acids that can inhibit methanogenic activity. The characteristics of food waste will 
depend on the population’s culinary habits, location, lifestyle and recycling practices4. 
Wastewater treatment plants are used to eliminate the contaminants present in domestic and 
industrial liquid effluents before their discharge or reutilization. After treatment, some of the 
water’s contaminants are stored and concentrated in the sludge formed during the various 
treatment stages. The sludge can be considered a recyclable waste and must be eliminated 
to comply with regulations. Due to its complex composition, AD is the most suitable method to 
stabilize and treat sewage sludge4. 
1.2. Biogas 
1.2.1. Composition 
As explained before biogas is primarily composed of methane (55-65%) and CO2 (35-45%) 
and it can easily be recovered due to its low solubility in water. Moreover, the biogas 
composition and methane yield are influenced by the biomass source, the digestion system 
and the retention time selected4,7.  
The theoretical gas yield varies with the carbohydrate, protein and fat content of the feedstock. 
Carbohydrates and proteins present much faster conversion rates but lower gas yields when 
compared to fats. However, fats have poor bioavailability and longer retention times are

needed for its utilization. In general biomasses with high carbohydrate content will have lower 
methane and higher CO2 content when compared to biomasses with higher protein and lipid 
content4. 
Biogas will also contain different contaminants according to its source and the feedstock used. 
Sulphur gases, halogenated compounds, siloxanes, ammonia, dust and particles are 
contaminants often present in biogas. Such contaminants may need to be removed prior 
biogas utilization to ensure the quality of the gas is appropriate for its later application7,12. 
There is no single international standard that dictates the specifications biogas should have. 
There are, however, national standards, procedures and manufacturer’s recommendations in 
regards the quality of the biogas depending on its later use and purpose12.

1.3. Lignocellulosic feedstocks 
Even though many feedstocks have been proposed and studied for bioprocesses, their actual 
market viability is not yet proved. In addition, utilizing resources that have food value as raw 
materials for bioprocesses is very controversial since good arable land and water resources 
are being consumed and deployed for biofuel or biomaterials production instead of being used 
to cover food needs13,14. These practices are not sustainable and lead to socio-economic and 
environmental issues, starvation and loss of biodiversity.  
Common lignocellulosic feedstocks used in bioprocesses include agricultural, horticultural and 
forestry residues, municipal solid waste, livestock manure, perennial grasses, bioenergy 
crops, aquatic plants and paper and cotton wastes13.  
Excluding dedicated energy crops, lignocellulosic materials are a good alternative to 
overcome the food vs bioproducts controversy and aim for a sustainable way to obtain 
products other than from crude oil or fossil resources. Moreover, utilization of lignocellulosic 
==PAGE:38==
20 | P a g e  
 
materials offers the possibility to reuse and repurpose waste, given the fact that its production 
and handling is also of big concern nowadays.    
1.3.1. Composition 
Lignocellulosic biomass is composed of mainly three different types of polymers associated 
with each other. These polymers are cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin15,16. There is a 
significant variation of the lignin and hemicellulose content highly depending on the biomass 
source15. Cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin form structures called microfibrils, which are 
organized into macrofibrils that mediate structural stability in the plant cell wall16,17. In Figure 
3 a representation of the lignocellulose structure and its different components is presented. 
 
Figure 3. Structure of lignocellulose14. 
Cellulose is the most abundant polymer in nature and is formed in long linear chains of D-
glucose subunits linked by β-1,4-glycosidic bonds with a varying degree of polymerization 
(DP) up to ~10,00015,18.  
Cellulose tends to form inter and intramolecular hydrogen bonds through hydroxyl groups on 
its glucose units, which promote cellulose aggregation into the so-called cellulose fibrils. These 
fibrils lead to a supramolecular structure with a crystalline (organized) and an amorphous (not 
well-organized) domain15,17,19.  
Hemicellulose gives more rigidity to the whole cellulose-hemicellulose-lignin network because 
it serves as a connection between the cellulose fibers and lignin. Within the plant, 
==PAGE:39==
21 | P a g e  
 
hemicelluloses are fixed in the fiber structure because they are mostly connected to lignin by 
covalent links19. 
Different polymers like pentoses (xylose and arabinose), hexoses (mannose, glucose and 
galactose) and sugar acids form the complex carbohydrate structure of hemicellulose. 
Depending on the biomass selected, the dominant component of hemicellulose among these 
polymers will be determined. For example, xylan is the dominant component for hardwoods 
and agricultural plants like grasses and straw, while glucomannan is for softwoods17. 
The backbone chain frequently consists of pentoses or alternating units of mannose and 
glucose or galactose units. When compared to cellulose, hemicellulose has a lower molecular 
weight, and all possess branches with short lateral chains consisting of different easily 
hydrolysable sugars19.  
The solubility of the hemicellulose components starts around 150-180 °C and increases with 
increasing temperature19,20. In descending order, the solubility of the different compounds of 
the hemicellulose is: mannose, xylose, glucose, arabinose and galactose however, xylan 
appears to be the component that can be extracted the most easily17. A major portion of 
hemicellulose is soluble in water suggesting that this macromolecule plays an important role 
in water manipulation by the plant. For the isolation of the hemicellulose it is necessary to 
cleave the lignin polysaccharide linkages19. 
Lignin has an aromatic structure and is an amorphous heteropolymer consisting of three 
different phenylpropane units (p-coumaryl, coniferyl and sinapyl alcohol) with different degrees 
of methoxylation that are held together by different types of linkages15–17. Its main purpose is 
to give the plant impermeability, structural support and resistance to oxidative stress and 
microbial attack. Moreover, lignin is not soluble in water at low temperatures (<180 °C) and 
thus its degradation is very difficult to achieve15,17.   
1.3.2. Structural limitations 
Degradation of lignocellulose occurs naturally however its complex structure impedes the 
access of microorganisms and enzymes to carbohydrates for fast and efficient conversion 
thus limiting its usage on industrial applications or as a source for bioprocesses such as AD 
for biogas production.  
Several factors such as cellulose crystallinity, degree of polymerization, moisture content, 
lignin content and distribution, particle size and available surface area, limit the enzymatic 
hydrolysis of lignocellulose17.  
==PAGE:40==
22 | P a g e  
 
In the crystalline cellulose structure, the hydrogen bonds between different layers of the 
polysaccharides contribute to its resistance to degradation14. Hemicellulose in general has a 
shorter degree of polymerization when compared to cellulose making it easier to hydrolyze1. 
Given its purpose and function, the most recalcitrant component of lignocellulose is lignin 
which acts as a shield preventing hydrolysis17. Under anaerobic conditions it can be degraded 
to some extent and very slowly, therefore is often regarded as non-digestible. The covalent 
bonding between the lignin-carbohydrate complexes is an important factor that limits the 
microbial attack1   
The pore size of the substrate in relation to the size of the hydrolyzing enzymes also affects 
the lignocellulose hydrolysis. Hemicellulose removal increases the mean pore size thus 
increasing the probability of the cellulose to be hydrolyzed17.  
1.3.3. Logistic limitations 
Economical handling of lignocellulosic biomass such as forest, agricultural and municipal 
wastes requires improved biomass production and high-throughput capacity equipment, 
reduced dry matter losses at each process stage, efficient handling of high moisture content 
biomass, constant supply of biomass with desired quality and format and strategies to reduce 
environmental, social and infrastructural effects based on life cycle analyses13.   
Transport of the feedstock from the source to the final processing center highly contributes to 
the cost of the logistics. Moreover, in the current biofuel production scenario, biomass with 
high moisture content and low energy and physical density make their collection, handling, 
transport, storage and processing uneconomical. On-farm, processing technologies can be 
implemented to add value to the biomass by reducing its size and increasing its density13. 
The equipment currently used for harvest and transport of lignocellulosic feedstocks are 
expensive, inefficient and add no significant value to the raw materials. In addition, most of 
the technologies currently used run on fossil fuels which release GHG and air pollutants. 
Improvements in this area will help obtain higher efficiencies, cost reduction, less GHG 
emissions and reduce fossil fuel consumption. 
Soil erosion and deforestation are other factors that limit biomass utilization. Therefore, 
sustainable practices for harvesting and biomass production should be aimed for and utilized.   
In conclusion, the lack of a sustainable supply chain for lignocellulosic feedstocks is an 
obstacle to achieve economic feasibility of certain bioprocesses or to establish successful 
biorefineries.

1.3.4. Manure 
Depending on its origin, the composition of manure varies significantly. However, in general, 
manure has high water content (91-99%) and its organic matter corresponds to approximately 
60-85% of manure’s dry matter1.  As mentioned before the organic matter present in manure 
consist mostly of lipids, carbohydrates and proteins that were not degraded during digestion. 
It is important that manure is used as soon as it is produced since degradation of the organic 
matter will continue to happen during its collection, transport and storage. According to Søren 
et. al. up to 28-43%  of degradable volatile solids (VS) (organic matter) present in fresh manure 
from cattle and pigs, respectively,  can be lost through emissions during its management21. 
In addition to the organic matter, valuable degrading microbes are present in manure and 
manure offers a high buffer capacity. For these, manure is a good substrate for AD. However, 
utilization of manure alone for AD is a non-economically feasible process. Low biogas yields 
are achieved due to the characteristics of manure:  
 High water content which translates in low organic loading rates 
 Low biodegradability of lignocellulose that requires long HRT to allow its degradation 
 High ammonia content which has inhibitory effects on the bacteria 
Different approaches exist to overcome the challenges presented when using manure for 
biogas production.  
For example, as reviewed in section 1.1.4, different process configurations exist that can allow 
to achieve higher methane yields and productivity by varying factors such as homogenization 
of reactor content, suspension of microbes, cell concentration, the HRT and solids retention 
time. In practice, most of the AD systems used are CSTR-type while other configurations may 
present improved metrics, their implementation is difficult since investment in new 
infrastructure and more specialized control systems are needed4,10.  
To mitigate the inhibitory effect of the ammonia present in manure, factors like pH and 
temperature of the digester can be regulated. Different studies have shown that operating at 
mesophilic (37 °C) conditions rather than thermophilic (60 °C) can alleviate ammonia inhibition 
by reducing the share of free ammonia in the liquid11,22–24.  It has been suggested that the free 
form of ammonia is the actual toxic agent; therefore, an increase in pH results in increased 
toxicity since the free ammonia to ammonium ion ratio (NH3:NH4+) increases resulting in VFA 
accumulation11,25–27. A reduction in pH can lower VFA’s concentration and lead to its 
reutilization23.  
Ammonia can also be regulated by the addition of minerals and natural zeolites with ion 
exchange capacity to absorb NH4+ reducing the ammonia and ammonium concentrations11,28. 
==PAGE:42==
24 | P a g e  
 
Manure dilution is another method that can be used to avoid ammonia inhibition; however the 
resulting waste volume that needs to be handled raises the operational costs making this 
alternative unattractive11.   
It is possible to implement an ammonia removal unit to directly get rid of ammonia and avoid 
inhibition. Frequently used technologies are struvite precipitation, flash distillation, 
evaporation, reverse osmosis, ammonia stripping, electrodialysis and biological methods 
among others. The application of these technologies has mainly been used in digestates and 
not in raw manure prior to AD and scarce literature exists in regards the effects these 
technologies have on subsequent AD processing1.  
To overcome the high-water content, manure can be dried or send to an evaporating unit 
before AD takes place, however both alternatives are very energy intensive and thus are not 
very attractive methods. The addition of manure fibers to raw manure reduces the water 
content, however the level of N increases making the process more prone to inhibition1. As 
discussed in section 1.1.4, digesters with effluent recirculation units help increase the solids 
concentration in the digester and give more time to the recalcitrant part of the fibers to be 
digested4,10.    
Co-digestion of manure with other lignocellulosic biomasses can significantly improve the 
economic feasibility of manure-based AD processes. The carbon to nitrogen ratio of these 
materials is significantly higher when compared to manure which means that the nitrogen 
content is low enough to avoid inhibition and higher methane production can be achieved5,29–
31.  
As mentioned earlier, the use of lignocellulosic biomasses must be in a sustainable way and 
other structural and logistic limitations still need to be tackled however, as research is carried 
out to overcome the barrier, it is expected that this practice will be expanded in the near future.     
1.4. Pretreatment methods 
Pretreatment of biomass is one of the main bottle necks for the commercial use of 
lignocellulosic feedstocks for biofuel and biochemical production because it has significant 
effect on all subsequent processes therefore influencing the overall yield and cost of the 
process. In recent years research into this area has been gaining increased attention and 
many pretreatment processes have been developed and are being continuously improved. 
However, so far there is not a single best technology pathway applicable to all types of 
lignocellulosic material and end products13. 
In general, pretreatments are used for biomass deconstruction, to liberate or make accessible 
cellulose and hemicellulose from their complex with lignin and favor enzymatic hydrolysis 
==PAGE:43==
25 | P a g e  
 
(Figure 4)32.  Moreover, it can have different beneficial effects on the biomass such as the 
separation of hemicellulose, increased surface porosity, lignin alteration or removal, 
decrystallization of cellulose and hydrolysis of hemicellulose and cellulose32,33.  
 
Figure 4. Effect of pretreatment on lignocellulosic biomass34. 
Some of the properties and conditions an optimal pretreatment should fulfill to be industrially 
viable are13,35: 
 Simple and economical operation with low CAPEX and OPEX costs 
 Ability to change the structure of biomass achieving maximum recovery and minimizing 
the degradation of feedstock’s components 
 Good recovery of compounds from hemicellulosic fraction 
 Low production of toxic components (e.g. furfural) 
 Wide applicability among different feedstocks 
Different pretreatment methods exist depending to the characteristics of the feedstock and the 
desired end product. These methods can be generally grouped in four categories: mechanical, 
thermal, chemical and biological (Table 3). Combination of these also exist using synergistic 
effects to maximize their efficiency. A brief discussion of these categories is presented below. 
1.4.1. Mechanical 
This pretreatment aims to reduce the particle size and crystallinity of the feedstock. By 
reducing the particle size,  the available specific surface area increases and the DP decreases 
increasing the total hydrolysis yield5,17. Another advantage is that no inhibitors are produced, 
==PAGE:44==
26 | P a g e  
 
however mechanical pretreatments have a high energy requirement which raises the 
operational cost of the process17. 
Milling is a common mechanical pretreatment and in recent years, ultrasonic waves have been 
used to crush the cell walls of plants. Ultrasonic waves can increase the soluble chemical 
oxygen demand (COD) and allow microorganisms to easily access and rapidly consume the 
substrate’s nutrients enabling lower HRT in the digester. Although an effective process, the 
high cost of the ultrasonic pulse generator and the high energy consumption are major 
drawbacks of this pretreatment method5.  
1.4.2. Thermal 
Heating and pressurizing are common methods used to crush lignocellulosic feedstocks into 
simple compounds5. During thermal pretreatments, the lignocellulosic material is heated, 
commonly with steam or hot water, in order to solubilize its different components. In the 
process, hemicelluloses solubilize firstly and shortly after lignin starts to solubilize. 
Hemicellulose is partially hydrolyzed, and acids are produced from the hemicellulose 
branching groups. These acids are assumed to serve as a catalyst to further hydrolysis of the 
hemicellulose17. 
Some major disadvantages of these methods are that energy for heating is needed and 
operation at high pressure increases the cost of the overall process5,17. Moreover, when heat 
is applied, inhibitory compounds can be produced from the solubilization of hemicellulose and 
lignin. Therefore, depending on the following conversion process and the desired final product, 
an additional separation step to remove these inhibitors might be required17. Examples of 
thermal pretreatments are steam explosion and liquid hot water (LHW). 
1.4.3. Chemical  
Chemicals are used to increase the biodegradability of the lignocellulose components by 
breaking down the bonds of the polymers. These pretreatments are usually performed at 
ambient conditions avoiding additional costs related to high pressure and high temperature 
operating conditions5. The chemicals used for pretreatment are consumables that might be 
expensive and thus rise the OPEX. In addition, environmental concerns exist about their 
usage, but these disadvantages can be mitigated if the chemicals can be easily and 
economically recoverable for its reuse5.    
Oxidizing compounds, ionic liquids, supercritical fluids and concentrated or diluted acids and 
alkalis can be used as chemicals in this pretreatment method13. Depending on the chemical 
and conditions applied, delignification, hemicellulose solubilization and reduction of cellulose 
crystallinity and DP can occur1.  
==PAGE:45==
27 | P a g e  
 
Acid 
When acids are used, hydrolysis of hemicellulose is the main reaction that takes place, making 
cellulose more easily degradable. However, solubilization of lignin can also occur producing 
inhibiting compounds that must be removed before biochemical pretreatments. If the 
solubilized lignin condenses and precipitates, the digestibility of the lignocellulosic material 
decreases17.  High sugar release can be achieve when mineral acids are used but special 
equipment (corrosion free reactors), neutralization, detoxification and acid recovery are 
needed resulting in additional capital and operational costs5,13.  
Alkaline 
Cost effective method which enhances the biodegradability of lignocellulosic materials by 
decreasing the lignin content (delignification)5. Structural changes in lignin, cellulose and 
hemicellulose occur when bases are used since the bonds of the ester and glycosidic side 
chains of these polymers are cleaved13. The first reactions taking place are the solvation and 
saphonication, causing the biomass to swell increasing its biodegradability17.  
Cellulose is also altered into a denser and thermodynamically stable structure. When alkali is 
removed, modifications in the crystalline state of cellulose and solubilization, redistribution and 
condensation of the lignin structure can happen. These effects can lower or counteract the 
positive benefits achieved by cellulose swelling and delignification. Longer times are also 
needed when compared to acid pretreatment and there is a risk to lose some sugars in the 
pretreatment17. 
1.4.4. Biological 
In biological pretreatments, living organisms are used to destruct the lignocellulosic matrix of 
the biomass. These pretreatments have higher selectivity, higher yields and lower energy 
demand when compared to the thermo-chemical methods. In addition, no inhibitors are 
produced and since the use of chemicals is avoided, biological methods are considered more

microorganisms for their growth. Moreover, biological pretreatments require higher operation 
areas and are slower than thermochemical processes. All these drawbacks limit its application 
in industrial processes5,13.  
A good alternative to overcome the limitations of slow conversion and substrate consumption 
by microorganisms is the use of industrial enzymes. Minimum environmental problems are 
associated with industrial enzymes and mild processing conditions are normally applied 
avoiding high energy requirements. Further research and development in this area, will allow

1.4.5. Ammonia pretreatments  
More than 50 years ago, the first studies of ammonia treatment for lignocellulosic biomasses 
took place. Initially agricultural straws used as ruminant feed were treated with ammonia to 
improve their quality by increasing the nitrogen content and its digestibility. These 
pretreatments have also been tested in the pulping and biofuel industry, with extensive 
research more recently in the ethanol production process.  
==PAGE:47==
29 | P a g e  
 
Different ammonia pretreatment configurations have emerged as research is being developed, 
varying from operational parameters such pressure and temperature to the form of the 
ammonia used (anhydrous or aqueous, liquid or gaseous). 
One of the first configurations proposed was ammonia fiber expansion (AFEX), in which 
biomass is pretreated for a shot time (~ 5-30 min), at high pressure (~15-20 atm) and mild 
temperature (50 °C -120 °C) conditions with a concentrated aqueous NH3 solution (>70%) or 
an anhydrous ammonia reagent. Ammonia penetrates the lignocellulosic cell wall structure 
promoting partial decrystallization of cellulose, depolymerization of hemicellulose and cleaving 
of the lignin carbohydrate linkages avoiding inhibitor formation. The combining effect of 
pressure and ammonia causes the biomass to swell and disrupt the fibers physically while the 
lignin remains unaltered. The enzymatic access is improved through expansion of the biomass 
fibers caused by a sudden pressure release at the end of the pretreatment1,34.  
After the process no liquid fraction is left and the biomass is well preserved and stable since 
there is no degradation of cellulose and hemicellulose. Moreover, up to 97% of ammonia can 
be recovered and recycled. However, this pretreatment cannot be conducted on biomasses 
with high lignin content and high safety controls are needed due to the high pressure operating 
condition34.   
Another method used is the ammonia recycle percolation (ARP) in which aqueous ammonia 
is fed and recirculated through a packed-bed reactor at high temperature (150 °C – 210 °C) 
and pressure (~23 bar) conditions. Ammonia causes biomass swelling and high temperatures 
efficiently promote delignification. Significant hemicellulose solubilization and high lignin 
removal can be achieved with this method while the cellulosic component in the biomass is 
preserved34. 
AAS is probably the simplest pretreatment tested so far for biofuel production. Here, biomass 
is soaked in ammonia aqueous solution (1-32 %w/w) at low to mild temperature conditions 
(ambient – 90 °C) and at atmospheric pressure for a certain duration (few hours or days). An 
increase in pore size and surface area is observed and thus lignin can be removed efficiently 
by reducing its interaction with hemicellulose. This also allows hemicellulose retention in the 
biomass for further hydrolysis step. To avoid inhibition during the bioprocessing, ammonia is 
previously removed by washing the solids with water or by evaporation (distillation or drying). 
Since milder conditions are used, low energy input is required for AAS when compared to 
other pretreatments. Moreover, ammonia can also be easily recovered and reused. For these 
reasons it is anticipated that AAS is a more economically feasible pretreatment alternative1,34.  
In some processes it is desirable that the release of sugars happens fast (short pretreatment 
duration) which is hard to achieve using mild conditions. However, AD is a slow process thus 
==PAGE:48==
30 | P a g e  
 
milder pretreatment conditions are enough and relatively efficient to increase biomass 
degradability. 
To date, some studies exist in literature where AAS is used on different biomasses destinated 
for AD processing1,36–44. In some cases, the results showed that after pretreatment the 
methane yield increased significantly while in others the increase was insignificant. The 
differences can be attributed to factors such as the biomass used, the conditions of the 
pretreatment (temperature, duration and ammonia concentration), the inoculum used and the 
ammonia removal method after pretreatment. 
Mechanisms 
The use of ammonia is very attractive to the industry because it is considered to be highly 
effective on altering and removing the lignin fraction of biomass without significant 
carbohydrate loss. Moreover, given its properties and high volatility, it is very easy to recover 
for its reuse indirectly reducing the operating costs34. 
Generally, when alkaline pretreatments are used, the biomass initially swells due to the 
solvation of hydroxyl groups by hydroxyl ions. Depending on the temperature conditions 
applied in the pretreatment the following things can happen34: 
 
Dissolution of hemicellulose and lignin 
 
Peeling of the end-groups and formation of alkali stable groups 
 
Alkaline hydrolysis of glycosidic bonds and acetyl groups 
 
Degradation 
or 
decomposition 
of 
dissolved 
polysaccharides 
and 
peeled 
monosaccharides  
It is suggested that ammonia improves enzyme access to cellulose because it produces 
delignification by cleaving the ester bonds of lignin and the ether and ester bonds of lignin-
carbohydrate complexes. Delignification increases the pore size of the cell wall and as a result 
the non-productive binding adsorption of the enzyme is reduced.  In addition, ammonia also 
produces hemicellulose solubilization to different extents. As hemicellulose surrounds the 
cellulose fibrils, its efficient removal is believed to increase the pore volume and surface area 
thus exposing the cellulose fibrils and facilitating enzymatic access1,34,36.   
Cellulose content is less affected and nearly all the cellulose can be retained after 
pretreatment. Stacking interactions in cellulose Iβ fibers and intracrystalline hydrogen bonds 
make crystalline cellulose recalcitrant to enzymatic hydrolysis. However, reorientation and 
structure modification of the cellulose can occur due to the partial removal of lignin and 
hemicellulose during pretreatment. Cellulose crystallinity seems to be affected by the 
interaction of the ammonia molecules with the hydroxyl groups of the broken hydrogen bonds. 
==PAGE:49==
31 | P a g e  
 
A structural crossover from cellulose Iβ to another crystalline amorph form of cellulose named 
cellulose IIII is catalyzed when the fibers are in contact with liquid ammonia. As a 
consequence, rearrangement of the stacking interactions and the intracrystalline hydrogen 
bonds between the glucose rings occurs1,45.   
When ammonia is in contact with the biomass, a transient ammonia-cellulose I complex is 
formed. This complex is an intermediate state between cellulose Iβ and cellulose IIII, where 
the ammonia molecules are trapped in specific sites within the cellulose crystal and form 
hydrogen bonds with the neighboring cellulose chains. After the ammonia is removed, an 
alteration in the hydrogen bond network and a relatively small rearrangement of the packed 
cellulose chains is prompted resulting in cellulose IIII45.   
Cellulose IIII shows higher structural flexibility and hydration when compared to the initial 
cellulose Iβ. Since its structure is more similar to amorphous cellulose, it is believed that 
cellulose IIII is easier to hydrolyze1,45,46. Figure 5 depicts the mechanism of liquid NH3 on 
cellulose. 
 
Figure 5. Mechanistic model of the interaction between cellulose Iβ fibrils with liquid NH3. When 
naturally occurring cellulose Iβ fibrils of the biomass (A) are in contact with ammonia (B), an extended 
H-bond network between the cellulose surface chains and NH3 is rapidly formed (C and D). These 
bonds cause a relative shifting of the fibril layers leading to formation of channels (E) which will allow 
ammonia to penetrate the cellulose fibril45,46. 
Process considerations  
Different parameters influence the success of the ammonia pretreatment. The main 
parameters that can be adjusted in the AAS process are briefly discussed and presented 
below.  
==PAGE:50==
32 | P a g e  
 
The temperature at which the pretreatment takes place largely affects the configuration of the 
process and its cost. A strong correlation between lignin removal and heat application exists 
and lignin removal is desired since it efficiently improves the digestibility of the biomass43,47. 
Based on this, most of the studies reported in the literature have tested the AAS pretreatment 
efficiency with heat application. In the context of biogas plants which are often equipped with 
CHP installations, can use the residual heat to fulfill the energy requirements of the 
pretreatment. However, it is desirable to avoid heat application into the pretreatment because 
the waste heat can instead be used for hygienization of influent, distribution to neighboring 
houses or ammonia recovery.  
Despite the strong temperature-delignification correlation, an increased digestibility and 
methane yield have been achieved on biomasses pretreated with AAS at ambient or low 
temperatures36,39,41,44. This has raised interest into exploring pretreatment configurations 
working at ambient temperatures but the number of studies that exist is still limited.   
The concentration of ammonia in the reagent is also a relevant variable of the process and 
interestingly, only few studies have tested the effect that the ammonia concentration in the 
reagent has on pretreated biomass. In general, high NH3 concentrations are more difficult to 
handle because are more corrosive and costly and its efficient recycling is not possible.  
Another interesting parameter for process configuration is duration, which seems to have a 
strong interaction with NH3 concentration and temperature. For example, the energy and 
chemical input requirements can be reduced (to reduce the severity of the pretreatment) if the 
duration is increased. However, with extended pretreatment durations, more space is needed 
and thus the initial investment cost for the integration of the pretreatment step to the overall 
process will be higher.  
Finally, the biomass to reagent ratio (S:L) affects the configuration of the process and the 
contact of the ammonia reagent with the biomass. A high ratio is preferable for industrial 
applications because a higher mass of feedstock can be pretreated per volume unit of the 
reactor. This means that less reagent is needed, and smaller volumes need to be handled per 
kg of feedstock that is pretreated. Therefore, both the operational and investment cost will 
decrease. In this sense, high S:L ratios and the savings from the energy and chemical input 
can potentially compensate the initial investment cost needed for long duration AAS.  
In conclusion the operational parameters of AAS provide flexibility to the pretreatment and 
must be carefully selected because they play a crucial role in its effectiveness (improving 
biodegradability of biomass) and can determine its economic viability at industrial scale 
implementation.

2.2. Analytical methods 
2.2.1. Total and volatile solids determination  
Introduction and general principle 
A well-mixed sample is evaporated and dried to a constant weight in a weighted dish inside 
an oven at 105 °C. The TS correspond to the increase in weight over that of the empty dish. 
Afterwards, the residue in the dish is ignited to constant weight at 550 °C. The remaining solids 
represent the fixed TS while the weight lost on ignition corresponds to the VS. This offers a 
rough approximation of the amount of organic matter present in the solid fraction of the 
biomass.

2.2.3. Ammonia determination 
The colorimetric and the titration method for ammonia determination were used in different 
parts of this work. The colorimetric method, carried out with the Hach Lange kits LCK 305, 
was used to find the ammonia concentration left in the pretreated biomass after AAS 
pretreatment was applied. The colorimetric method was also used to corroborate the ammonia 
concentrations obtained via the titrimetric method on the experiments conducted in the pilot 
plant ammonia evaporating unit. The titrimetric method was only used in the sets of 
experiments carried out in the pilot plant system.  
Introduction and general principle 
A. Colorimetric (Indophenol Blue)53: The LCK cuvette tests for ammonium are based on a 
reaction to form indophenol. Ammonium ions react at pH 12.6 with salicylate and 
hypochlorite ions in the presence of sodium nitroprusside as a catalyst to form indophenol. 
The amount of ammonium nitrogen present in the sample is directly proportional to the 
amount of color formed. The color development is measured at 694 nm and the results 
are expressed as mg/L NH4+-N or NH4+.  
The LCK cuvette tests are calibrated by a ten point-calibration according to ISO 8466-1 at 
a defined temperature of 20 °C. Therefore, the reaction is temperature-sensitive, so the 
recommended sample and reagent temperature is 20 °C. After the sample has been taken 
the analysis should be carried out as soon as possible. 
Interferences: Primary amines cause high-bias results. All reducing agents interfere and 
cause low-bias results. The ions listed in Table 19 in the annex do not cause interference 
up to the given concentrations, however the cumulative effects and the influence of other 
ions are not determined.

2.3. Aqueous Ammonia Soaking pretreatment 
Introduction and general principle 
As discussed in section 1.4.5 AAS disrupts the lignocellulosic matrix of biomass thus, 
facilitating and speeding up the hydrolysis of cellulose and hemicellulose during subsequent 
steps. The biomass under investigation is soaked in ammonia reagent at a certain ammonia 
concentration in closed grass flasks to avoid ammonia evaporation. After a certain 
pretreatment duration, a distillation step under vacuum takes place in order to remove the 
ammonia and avoid inhibition in the subsequent biological conversion.

2.4. Biochemical Methane Potential (BMP) assays 
Introduction and general principle 
The BMP of an organic biomass is the maximum production of methane that can be achieved 
by anaerobic digestion in a batch experiment. This method is used to determine the methane 
production potential a biomass has when digested through AD. It is also used to assess the 
effect a pretreatment method has on the methane potential for a specific biomass.   
In principle, the selected biomass is mixed with inoculum (mixed anaerobic liquor from an 
anaerobic digester) containing microorganisms able to convert organic material into methane. 
The mixture of inoculum and biomass is incubated in sealed flasks under anaerobic conditions 
at a stable temperature and methane production is monitored in regular intervals. The AD 
process is considered complete when methane production has ceased.

2.5. Pilot-plant Ammonia Recovery System (ARS) 
Introduction and general principle 
Aqueous ammonia can be recovered through different methods but given that no azeotrope 
is formed with water and that it has lower boiling point when compared to water, aqueous 
ammonia can be easily recovered by steam distillation. Given these properties and due to the 
fact that the distillation occurs under vacuum conditions, the energy input is considered to be 
relatively low and so are the operational costs associated. Moreover, in the context of a 
biogas-based CHP facility, the energy inputs can be provided by the excess heat produced 
within the plant.     
These experiments had the purpose to assess at a pilot-scale, how efficient the ARS was in 
terms of its capability to remove ammonia from the biomass fibers and to concentrate the 
ammonia that was recovered (≥ initial ammonia reagent) thus allowing its reuse and recycling.

Process flow and unit description 
The pilot plant system for ammonia recovery (Figure 14) is composed by six subsystems or 
units: the steam supply unit (orange), the main distillation unit where biomass is soaked and 
steam is pass through to distill ammonia (purple), the absorption-recovery unit (green), the 
cooling unit (blue), the vacuum unit (yellow) and the draining unit (gray). The ammonia 
recovery system was constructed by the Department of Chemical and Biochemical 
Engineering (KT) of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). 
Wet biomass fibers are placed in the sieve in main container (T1) of the distillation unit and 
the aqueous ammonia solution (with known concentration) is fed through valve V3 into the 
main tank where the soaking and distillation takes place. After a certain time, when the soaking 
has ended, the ammonia reagent is drain out (3, V6, T2, 4, V8) from T1 and collected. The 
ammonia reagent that was left in the system and could not be drain is distillated under vacuum 
(130 mbar) at constant temperature. Vacuum is applied and regulated through the vacuum 
pump which is equipped with a pressure controller.

iii. Boric acid solution: The boric acid solution was prepared in the 10 L blue-cap bottle. An 
amount of 200 g of boric acid was weighted in the analytical scale and dissolved in 10 L

indicator (~2 mL) were added to the solution. The boric acid solution was used as an 
ammonia absorbent in the 4 L round-bottle flask and the 5 L blue-cap bottle. This solution 
was also used in the titration method used for ammonia determination (see section 2.2.3). 
iv. Wet biomass fibers: Using the analytical balance, approximately 250 g of wheat straw were 
added to the plastic bucket. Then 1 L of DI water was added to the fibers inside the bucket 
ensuring all the biomass was covered with water. The bucket was covered with a plastic 
bag and the fibers were left in water overnight. The next day before starting the distillation, 
the water and fibers were transferred to the pre-weighted removable sieve (that held and 
contained the biomass inside T1 during the distillation) and were drained until no water 
drops were dripping. Then the sieve containing the wet biomass was weighted in the bench 
scale and placed inside the main container of the ADS. After the experiment ended the 
sieve containing the biomass was again weighted. The weights were recorded. The 
biomass was reused throughout all the experiments.  
v. Preparation of the absorption-recovery and water wash flasks: In the pre-weighted 4 L 
round-bottom flask and 5 L blue-cap bottle, 3 and 4 L of boric acid solution were added 
respectively. For the water wash, 4 L of DI water were added in another pre-weighted 5 L 
blue-cap bottle. The flasks were weighted in the bench scale before and after each 
experiment and their weights were recorded.  
vi. Preparation of the distillation unit: Before and after each experiment, the distillation unit 
was thoroughly cleaned. The vapor outlet leading to the condenser (5), the round-bottom 
flask and the trap were removed and disconnected. The lid of the main container was 
removed, and the vapor outlet on top of the lid was flushed with water. The drain (V8) and 
feeding port (V3) valves were closed while the main container’s exit valve (V6) leading to 
the collector tank (T2) before the drain was open. Tap water was flushed into the main 
container (T1) until the collector tank (T2) was inundated and the water level reached ¼ 
of the main container (T1). The drain valve (V8) was then opened allowing all the water to 
flow out of the system. The lid of the main container was placed back at the beginning of

Operation of the distillation unit:  
i. 
First the chiller (C2) was turned on to allow the water to cool down. In the meantime, the 
ammonia reagent was titrated as described in section 2.2.3 and the pre-weighted distillate 
absorber and ammonia trap were attached to the condenser and vacuum pump. The sieve 
and flask containing the biomass and the ammonia solution respectively, were weighted 
and their weights were noted down.  
ii. All the valves of the unit were closed, the sieve was placed inside the main container (T1) 
and the container was covered with the lid. The lid was firmly secured to the container and 
the hose at the top of the lid (5) was connected to the top inlet of the vertical condenser 
(C1).  
iii. The vacuum pump was turned on and the blue-cap bottle containing the ammonia solution 
was connected to the feeding port. With the vacuum on, the valve (V3) of the feeding port 
was opened in order to feed the ammonia solution into the main container. The valve (V3) 
was closed, and the vacuum was turned off when all 4 L were fed. The stopwatch was 
then started.  
iv. After 30 minutes of soaking, the valves V6 and V8 leading to the collecting tank (T2) and 
the drain hose were opened, and the ammonia solution was carefully collected from the 
system drain outlet in the 5 L blue-cap bottle. The flask and the valves were closed. In the 
bench scale, the flask was weighted, and its weight was noted. A sample (1 mL) was 
withdrawn from the recovered ammonia reagent and was diluted 51 times in a 250 mL 
blue-cap bottle. A sample of that dilution was then titrated as described in section 2.2.3.  
v. The vacuum pump was turned on again and when the system reached the desired 
pressure 130 mbar, the main steam valve was opened followed by the subsequent 4 
valves before the inlet valve (V2) to the main container. The stopwatch was started when 
the V2 was opened, and steam was supplied into the main container (T1).  The steam flow 
was controlled using the valves to ensure constant temperature inside the main container.  
vi. After the desired duration of the distillation was reached, the steam supply stopped by 
closing the inlet valve (V2) leading to the main container. The rest of the vales of the steam 
supply system were closed in reversed stem flow direction. The stopwatch and the vacuum 
were stopped and the vapor that had condensed inside the main container was drain and 
collected in the plastic Jerry can (condensate). The condensate was weighted (recording 
its weight) and next the ammonia determination by titration was carried out as described 
in section 2.2.3.

2.6. Statistical Analysis 
Statistical analysis was performed on the experimental data obtained from the BMP 
experiments. The data points that were analyzed from all BMP batches were the ultimate 
methane potential yield and the methane yield at 21 days for the BMP 1,2 and 3 and 19 days 
for BMP 4.   
For the BMP 1 an unpaired t-test was performed for both the ultimate and the 21-day methane 
yield. An ordinary one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) along with a Dunett’s multiple 
comparisons test was used for analyzing the ultimate and the 21-day methane yield of BMP 2 
and 3. Finally, for the BMP 4, an ordinary one-way ANOVA along with a Dunett’s multiple 
comparisons test was used for the 19-day methane yield and an ordinary one-way ANOVA 
along with a Tukey’s multiple comparisons test ultimate methane potential yield.  
All the analyses were performed using GraphPad Prisim v8.1.2. using an alpha value of 0.05.

The BMP kinetic rate constant provides useful information in regards the degradation kinetics 
of materials to achieve optimal design and operation of anaerobic digesters. However, finding 
the correct value is difficult to achieve because the kinetic rate constant is more sensitive to 
the experimental conditions than the methane yield. Many kinetic models have been used to 
describe the methane production in BMP test but currently there is no standardized model to 
apply to all BMP results. The variability of the selection of the model is based on the substrates 
used62.  
Several studies have shown that the rate limiting step in AD is the hydrolysis of particulates to 
soluble substrates62–65. Based on this, in this work, hydrolysis was considered the rate limiting 
step on the BMP experiments. The first order model is commonly used for fast and abruptly 
stopping degradation profiles62 and to describe the progress of hydrolysis. The model 
describes substrate utilization rate as a function of substrate concentration only and many 
aspects such as microbial growth and decay or concentration are not considered.  
Another important parameter that can be calculated form the experimental data obtained in 
the BMP tests is the apparent degradability (yd) which can be use along with the hydrolysis 
constant to predict the VS destruction in a digester. The apparent degradability is defined as 
the fraction of COD that is degraded at infinite retention time66. 
The hydrolysis constant was calculated graphically from the experimental data obtained in 
each BMP test and for all the experiments (pretreated and non-pretreated). Using the ultimate 
methane potential (B0), the remaining methane potential was calculated (B0-B) and its 
logarithm was plotted against the days (t) when the methane measurements were done. A 
linear regression was performed, and the slope of the obtained regression corresponded to 
the hydrolysis constant. In Figure 22 the plots prepared using the first order rate model to 
obtain the hydrolysis constant are illustrated along with the linear regression equations and 
==PAGE:93==
75 | P a g e  
 
coefficients of determination (R2). The plots of the experiments were grouped within the BMP 
batch where each experiment was performed.  
A linear curve was produced when the equation of the first order kinetic model was applied 
(Figure 22). The coefficients of determination obtained had an acceptable value (>0.95) for 
almost all the experiments indicating in these cases that the model had a good fit to the 
experimental data.  For the pretreatment conditions of 8%-6 days and 25%-1 and 3 days the 
R2 values were below 0.95 which means that the model was not a good fit for the experimental 
data.

experimental conditions tested.  Concentrations are expressed in %w/w. 
Under the conditions tested, the results suggested that temperature changes had a greater 
impact in the performance of the system when compared to duration changes.  
It was observed that there is a tradeoff between the ammonia that can be recovered for its 
reuse (ammonia recovery %) and the ammonia concentration of the solution recovered. This 
is because higher temperatures and longer distillation durations lead to higher ammonia 
recovery, however more steam was needed to run the distillation under these conditions and 
as a result the ammonia concentration of the ammonia-vapor solution recovered dropped. 
Both the ammonia recovery and the ammonia concentration of the recovered solution are 
important parameters that need to be considered in the optimization of the distillation process 
because they are closely related to the operational and fix costs in the pretreatment and 
recovery step. As high recovery translates into less ammonia losses and higher ammonia 
==PAGE:101==
83 | P a g e  
 
concentration into an efficient reutilization of the ammonia solution because an additional 
concentration step is avoided. Higher concentrations also imply that smaller volumes need to 
be handled thus smaller collecting vessels can be used.    
Therefore, factors such as equipment, ammonia (consumables) and steam (utilities) costs 
have to be factored in and compared to help on the decision making. Moreover, alternative 
sources of heating such as waste heat, in particular for the case of a CHP facility, should also 
be analyzed. 
The results also showed that under all the conditions tested, the ammonia was successfully 
removed from the biomass fibers achieving low enough concentrations to allow its utilization 
in subsequent AD processes without the risk of ammonia inhibition. Moreover, since the 
biomass fibers were reused on every experiment, the results demonstrated that the time that 
the ammonia was in contact with the fibers was not a factor that affected the capacity of the 
system to remove the ammonia. 
As for the reliability of the system, experimental procedure and method, the results are 
inconclusive because in some experiments big ammonia losses were observed. In order to 
better asses the reliability of the system it is recommended that experimental duplicates are 
performed; e.g. using the same temperature, pressure and duration conditions and a fresh 
batch of biomass fibers and ammonia reagent each time. The reliability of the method can be 
tested by performing multiple titrations on each sample and analyzing if there are variations in 
the results. In addition, the results obtained by one method can be contrasted with the results 
obtained using other methods i.e. the colorimetric method.  
The system and experimental procedure also have room for improvement in the sense that a 
better feeding and collection mechanisms could be implemented to reduce the risk of potential 
ammonia losses. Another important recommendation for the current system is that a stirring 
or mixing mechanism should be incorporated to provide homogenic conditions during the 
soaking ensuring proper contact between the ammonia and the biomass fibers. This 
mechanism will potentially avoid the need to pre-wet the biomass. The system should also 
allow proper reading of the steam flow because even when it is a variable directly related to 
the temperature, an accurate monitoring will allow reproducibility during the experiments and 
accurate economic assessment.  
More experiments should be performed under different conditions and different biomasses to 
have a better understanding of what are the recommended and optimal operating conditions 
and if these conditions are applicable to other biomasses. As mentioned before so far, the 
==PAGE:102==
84 | P a g e  
 
results obtained suggest that the system is more sensitive to temperature changes than 
duration therefore this parameter should be further tested and optimized. Performing the 
distillation using temperature ramps instead of a fix temperature could also be interesting to 
assess.  
Finally, subsequent BMP tests should also be incorporated to assess the pretreatment method 
itself. The results should be compared to the results obtained from lab-scale pretreated 
biomass to ensure the scaling up of the pretreatment is successful and the pretreatment effect 
is reproducible.

4. Conclusions 
 
Biogas production from organic waste through anaerobic digestion is a multipurpose process 
because saleable products such as biogas, fertilizers, heat and electricity can be obtained 
while AD can be used as a waste treatment technology. 
Lignocellulosic biomass such as agricultural waste is an abundant and low-cost substrate that 
can be converted through anaerobic digestion into renewable energy without being in 
competition with food purpose needs.  
Pretreatment methods have the purpose to improve the digestibility of the biomass and 
facilitate its hydrolysis and further conversion into products with higher value. Different 
methods exist and among them ammonia pretreatments have shown promising results in 
different studies.  
The effect the ammonia has on the biomass will vary from biomass to biomass, but it is 
believed that the lignin fraction can be altered and removed without significant carbohydrate 
loss when ammonia is used. In addition, some studies have shown that hemicellulose 
solubilization to different extents can occur, exposing the cellulose fibrils to the ammonia. 
Molecular dynamics simulations from other studies have revealed that when liquid ammonia 
interacts with the cellulose fibrils a hydrogen bond network is formed that allows ammonia 
penetration into the fibril where a transient ammonia-cellulose complex is formed. After the 
ammonia is removed the crystalline cellulose has a relatively small rearrangement making it 
more similar to amorphous cellulose and thus easier to hydrolyze.  
The use of ammonia during the pretreatment step also has other practical advantages when 
compared to strong acids or alkalis that will influence in the investment and operational cost 
of the process. For example, is less corrosive and thus easier to handle while there is no need 
to use equipment fabricated with special materials. Moreover, given its properties it has a high 
volatility and therefore it is very easy to recover for its reuse.   
AAS was applied under different ammonia concentrations and treatment durations to garden 
grass. The pre-treated biomass was anaerobically digested with swine manure as inoculum 
in batch experiments and BMP tests were performed to assess the effect the pretreatment 
had on the methane yield.  
While BMP tests are useful to study the methane potential a biomass has, facilitate 
optimization goals in regards to pretreatment conditions and help determine at an early stage 
if the AD of that biomass is economically viable, experiments on continuous mode are more 
appropriate for evaluating real applications. Despite this, BMP tests can provide valuable 
==PAGE:104==
86 | P a g e  
 
information to develop mathematical models that will help predict or optimize the AD process 
in areas such as process technology and operating conditions.  
The results showed that the ultimate methane yield of the pretreated biomass improved 30% 
and 20% when the biomass was pretreated with 18 %w/w ammonia reagent for 1 day and 25 
%w/w ammonia reagent for 6 days respectively. However, the methane yield did not increase 
until after 25 days of anaerobic digestion. It is therefore necessary to perform a 
technoeconomic analysis to determine the economic viability of the pretreatment under the 
conditions tested and biomass used.  
Under the other pretreatment conditions tested the methane yield showed no significant 
difference when compared to the non-pretreated grass. Despite this, AAS has proven to be 
effective in other studies and with other biomasses therefore more experiments are 
encouraged either by testing other concentration and pretreatment durations, by varying other 
parameters (e.g. temperature or the biomass to ammonia ratio) or by using other biomasses.   
It is also recommended that other analysis methods commonly used such as SEM and 
structural and compositional analysis are performed in other to further understand how the 
pretreatment affects the biomass.  
In the experiments performed at the pilot-plant ammonia recovery system, it was concluded 
that under all the conditions tested, the system was capable to remove and reduce the 
ammonia concentration in the wheat straw fibers sufficiently to avoid ammonia inhibition if the 
biomass was further used as substrate for AD. Moreover, the time that the ammonia was in 
contact with the fibers was not a factor that affected the capacity of the system to remove the 
ammonia. 
From the two process variables tested, temperature variations had more effect in the 
parameters assessed than changes in the duration of the distillation. Thus, it is concluded that 
the system and the parameters assessed (ammonia recovery, ammonia concentration in the 
recovered solution, system losses and ammonia concentration in the fibers) are more sensitive 
to temperature perturbations.  
Within the parameters assessed, there is a tradeoff between the ammonia that can be 
recovered for its reuse (ammonia recovery %) and the ammonia concentration of the solution 
recovered. Higher temperatures and longer distillation durations allowed to achieve higher 
ammonia recovery but lower ammonia concentration in the ammonia-vapor solution 
recovered. Both parameters are closely related to the operational and fix costs in the 
pretreatment and recovery step and thus need to be considered for the distillation optimization. 
==PAGE:105==
87 | P a g e  
 
In some experiments big ammonia losses were observed thus the reliability of the system, 
experimental procedure and method needs to be further tested and some recommendations 
have been discussed and presented in section 3.5. 
It was concluded that more experiments should be performed under different conditions or 
using different biomasses in order to find optimal distillation conditions and evaluate if the 
same conditions are applicable to other biomasses. Subsequent BMP tests on the biomass 
pretreated in the ARS should be conducted and compared to results from lab-scale pretreated 
biomass to ensure the effects of the pretreatment are reproducible when the process is scaled 
up. 
Last, but not least an economic analysis and a life-cycle assessment (LCA) need to be 
conducted in order to fully understand the economic viability of implementing an AAS 
pretreatment and recovery step in existing and new biogas plants and to assess the 
environmental impacts associated to the process and more extensively with all the stages of 
a product's life.
