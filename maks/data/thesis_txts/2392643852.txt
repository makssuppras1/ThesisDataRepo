ABSTRACT 
 
The present study is in line with the recent entry of natural fibre reinforced composites in 
structural applications. In the context of several industry branches’ scepticism in using these 
new materials, which previous studies showed to be sensitive to surrounding humidity, it 
became necessary to develop empirical laws defining the behaviour of natural fibre 
reinforced composites exposed to moisture. It is well-known that this material adsorbs 
moisture until chemical equilibrium is reached by a slow diffusion process. Since this process 
represents a significant part of a component’s lifetime, i.e. several years, depending on the 
material thickness, this study focused on material properties change during moisture 
sorption. With this aim, an experimental study was led by monitoring weight gain, stiffness 
and dimensional changes of composite samples stored at different controlled relative 
humidity and temperature. The selected unidirectional flax/epoxy prepreg material from 
Lineo® was manufactured in autoclave. Porosity was minimized by adapting processing 
parameters. The obtained composite stiffness is approx. 35 GPa, with a density of 1.3.  
Experimentally corroborated laws of behaviour describing moisture diffusion and its 
Arrhenius temperature dependence, as well as variations in stiffness, dimensions and 
moisture content during diffusion, were implemented in a numerical model. Input data is 
component’s geometry and the surrounding relative humidity and temperature. As the 
model showed a good ability to predict changes in composite properties, it was used to 
forecast material properties changes when temperature and relative humidity are real 
meteorological data. Numerical simulations showed that the material properties can take 
several years to stabilize around significantly different values compared to the ones 
observed after manufacturing. These results can be used to compute natural fibre reinforced 
composite’s response to a mechanical load case, by taking moisture sensitivity into account 
when designing a component.

I. Introduction  
1. Context 
 
Current industrial application of plant fibres in non-structural composite components is 
supported by many publications on their mechanical properties (Lilholt, et al., 1997), 
(Zafeiropoulos, et al., 2002), (Oksman, 2000), (Plackett, et al., 2003).  
Thus, it is well established that randomly oriented plant fibre assemblies are suitable for 
reinforcement of composite materials with moderate mechanical properties. However, to 
use plant fibres in load-bearing composite components requires a fibre alignment and 
further it requires long-term data about the mechanical properties of the composite for a 
safe-life design. Indeed, previous studies have shown that the mechanical properties of 
aligned plant fibre composites are notably influenced by environmental conditions (Madsen, 
et al., 2007). In particular, the humidity is a critical factor since both fibres and matrix absorb 
several percent of their weight in moisture, depending on the surrounding humidity value. 
(Chilali, et al., 2016). This leads to a change in both dimensions (Madsen, et al., 2012) and 
mechanical properties (Madsen, et al., 2007) of the composite, and this should therefore be 
considered in the design phase of the structural part.  
At the moment, designing a structural component made of natural fibre reinforced 
composites is done by supposing the part is completely sealed, so no humidity comes in 
(Personal communication: Lingrove, USA, February 9th, 2017). However, this assumption may 
become invalid as soon as the life cycle includes longer exposure to humid environment.  
In the automotive and aircraft industry, bio composites recently passed the tests to be used 
as semi-structural components (Personal communication with R&D engineer from Lineo, 
France, April 16th, 2017): for instance, Lineo has developed a high-performance flax-epoxy 
prepreg composite and is about to start the production of Peugeot 3008’s luggage 
compartment’s floor using the developed prepreg material. The same material is also 
qualified to build air pipes in aircraft’s cabins.  
However, the synthetic fibre composite industry is still reluctant to this alternative. For 
instance, HEXCEL led its own experimental studies and indicated a critical sensitivity of 
natural fibre reinforced composites to fire and humidity. (Personal communication with a 
HEXCEL engineer, JEC, March 16th, 2017) 
Altogether, it is necessary to provide solid background to document the behaviour of Natural 
Fibre Reinforced Composites (NFRC) in humid conditions. 
 
2. Problem definition 
 
==PAGE:13==
11 
 
Previous studies have defined materials behaviour laws to predict the properties of NFRC at 
equilibrium states of moisture sorption (Madsen, et al., 2007), (Berges, et al., 2016). 
However, there is no existing model to forecast these properties’ evolution during the 
sorption process. It is critical because some studies highlighted a very slow moisture sorption 
process of several months (Davies, 2016). Due to constant changes in the surrounding 
relative humidity, NFRCs might never reach a constant moisture content across time.  
The problem is: How do physical properties of NFRCs vary during moisture sorption process?  
 
 
 
 
3. Objective of the Thesis 
 
The aim of this project is to provide good experimental data and develop related analytical 
models for describing the change in physical properties at transient state of the moisture 
sorption, e.g. before composite reaches equilibrium with its surrounding environment.  
Hence, by putting experimental data together with the models, the actual uncertainty for 
natural fibre composites in relation to moisture, will be documented. This will enable the 
industry to make decisions based on a solid background.  
 
4. Methodology 
 
Experiments will be done on a relevant material for wind turbines and other structural 
applications. 
These data will help finding the most appropriate model to simulate the moisture sorption 
process and its impact on physical properties of NFRC. 
 
See flowchart below, for entire process of simulating NFRC’s mechanical behaviour; A first 
model (in the white boxes) is used to determine how moisture sorption process occurred in 
the material, with surrounding relative humidity, temperature and material properties and 
component’s geometry as input parameters. This is what will be achieved in the present 
study. Then, these results are the input for a finite element analysis in which a mechanical 
load case is applied on the component, as described in the grey boxes. Altogether, these two 
simulations can give a forecast of an NFRC structural component’s behaviour when it is 
submitted to both an environmental and mechanical load case.  
 
==PAGE:14==

The experimental work in the present study starts with a series of manufacturing tests to 
define the optimal autoclave cycle to cure the flax/epoxy prepregs. Material quality check 
will be done by computing the laminate’s density and thus porosity content. Also, 
experimentally measured stiffness will be compared to the one from Lineo’s datasheet, to 
check obtained material’s quality. 
In order to study the material properties in humid conditions, several types of samples will 
be cut out from thin manufactured plates and stored in different climate chambers. Weight 
==PAGE:15==
13 
 
and dimensional measurements, as well as tensile tests will be made during moisture 
diffusion process, to see how these properties evolve.  
Laws of behaviour will be identified from these experiments, and will be implemented in a 
MATLAB model.  
The model will start by simulating moisture sorption with use of simple Fickian diffusion at 
constant surrounding humidity. Coherence of model will also be checked at this point. 
Once this work is achieved, more realistic simulation of atmospheric conditions will be 
implemented, including temperature and humidity changes over time. Also, experimentally 
stated models for stiffness and dimensional change, as a function of adsorbed humidity, will 
be included in the model.  
Thanks to that model, it can be verified for instance if typical environmental conditions can 
substantially affect global composite’s properties along the seasons, as it can be the case for 
wind blades. 
 
5. Scope and limitation 
 
Focus will be made on one material: Flax-epoxy prepregs by Lineo®, cured in autoclave. As 
mentioned above, this is one of the first NFRCs used for semi-structural industrial 
application. Therefore, it might be relevant to have a good understanding of its behaviour in 
humid environments, so it can be also used in applications where humidity is a critical factor.  
Limit tensile properties to elastic modulus in axial direction: In most applications, for 
instance wind blades, components are dimensioned by a stiffness-ruled design (Bak, et al., 
2013). Strength is not a critical factor. Also, previous studies have shown that moving from 
35 to 85% RH causes the stiffness to vary from 18.1 to 14.2 GPa, while strength remains 
constant (Madsen, et al., 2007). These two facts indicate that stiffness is a mechanical 
property that needs to be studied.   
Composite stiffness will be measured parallel to the fibre direction; This study is focused on 
fibres, and not on matrix, or fibre/matrix reaction to moisture. 
Process parameters, thickness of the laminate and unidirectional lay-up will remain 
constant, to focus on three experimental variables; length and width of the laminate, 
surrounding relative humidity, and temperature. 
Understanding on moisture sorption related changes will be studied on composite scale. 
Only a few considerations will be made at meso and micro-scale.

II. Background 
 
 
 
1. Material requirements for wind turbine blades 
 
To produce electricity at a competitive price, it is necessary to design wind turbines so their 
service life overpasses 20 years. The present study will focus on wind blades, as they are 
made of the studied material family, which is composites. 
First of all, aerodynamic forces and wind blades rotation induce stresses applied on the 
material they are made of. Aerodynamic considerations impose that wind blades’ deflection 
remains as small as possible; typically, material is strained of +/-0.5% (Bak, et al., 2013), 
which is quite low. Thus, blade’s design is called “strain-driven” design, as it takes a 
component’s deflection as design constraint, whereas a “stress-driven” design would take 
the applied load as design constraint. Therefore, for wind turbine application, material 
stiffness is a more important factor than its strength.  
Secondly, wind blades are implemented worldwide, which means they can be submitted to 
extreme environmental conditions, such as temperatures from -20 to +50°C (Mei, 2000). 
Also, surrounding relative humidity and rain interact with composite materials, and lead to 
macro and microscopical changes (Rocha, et al., 2017).  
Altogether, it means stiffness has to be carefully followed while composite is surrounded by 
air’s relative humidity.  
 
 
2. General presentation of composites 
 
Composite materials belong to one of the four material families, which are metals, polymers, 
ceramics and composites. 
Using the word “composite” implies the reunion of at least two distinct phases, made from 
materials that differ in a physical and/or chemical point of view. The aim of joint is to get 
new interesting properties.  In most of the cases, one constituent is under the form of a 
yarn, and is embedded in the second constituent. These two are respectively called fibres 
and matrix. 
 
==PAGE:17==
15 
 
One of the most commonly used composite material combines glass fibres with epoxy 
matrix, as shown on Figure 1. This composite is used in lots of industries, including wind 
turbine blades. As soon as the weight becomes a key factor, more advanced composites 
become necessary. For instance, better performances can be achieved by combining carbon 
fibres with epoxy resin. (Prabhakaran, 2014).  
Table 1 shows how composite materials compete against a traditional material such as steel. 
Although absolute values of stiffness of composites are lower than the ones of steel, taking 
the stiffness to density² ratio shows the advantage of composite over steel; The higher the 
ratio, the lighter a structure made of the considered material can be made, with constant 
deformation. Cellulose/polymer composite feature stiffness to density² ratio of 21, which 
makes an interesting lightweight material, but still far away from carbon/epoxy and its ratio 
of 74. Strength to density² of composites is about 10 times higher then the one of steel, so 
composite is definitely a better lightweight material than steel. Next step is to take a closer 
look at composites, and especially at cellulose/epoxy combination, which seems to be a

3. General theory about plant fibres  
 
The good mechanical properties of plant fibres can be explained by the plant fibre’s 
structure. A plant fibre is a long cell (e.g. its length is several thousand times bigger than its 
diameter) with a rigid cell wall (Lilholt, et al., 2000). 
 
 
  
 
 
Figure 3: Cell wall layers in a plant fibre. M is the middle specie 
connecting the fibres in the plant, P is the primary layer, S1, S2 
and S3 are the three secondary layers, and W is the cell 
membrane.  (Siau, 1995) 
 
 
 
 
On Figure 3, layers S1, S2 and S3 are made of cellulose microfibrils embedded in a 
hemicellulose and lignin matrix. In each of them, microfibrils are building a helicoidal shape 
together around the plant fibre’s axis. Each layer has a different helix’s twisting angle, and 
therefore a different microfibril orientation with respect to the main axis. In plant bast fibres 
for instance, layer S2 has a low twisting angle, below 10° in and represents 60 to 80% of the 
total cell wall. Similarly to the different type of fibre layers arrangement explained in 
previous section, low twisting angle allows the layer to bear a tensile mechanical load 
applied on the plant fibre. Thus, the layer S2 is responsible for most of the fibre’s stiffness 
and strength.  
Taking a closer look at a microfibril (Figure 4) shows that its cellulose ordinates in semi 
crystalline structure along the microfibril’s length (Siau, 1995). Unlike in crystalline regions, 
in amorphous regions cellulose polymer chains are not or weakly bounded together by 
hydrogen bonds, giving a random polymer arrangement.

4. Water sorption of plant fibres and natural fibre reinforced 
composites 
 
Another property of crystalline cellulose is that it has a very low moisture sorption capacity 
compared to amorphous cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin.  Understanding the cause 
requires to look at the molecules’ structure: the cellobiose molecule shown below (Figure 5) 
features several hydroxyl groups that can build hydrogen bonds whether with other 
cellulose chains to build crystalline cellulose, or with water, in case of amorphous cellulose. 
That is why amorphous cellulose has a high moisture sorption capacity.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
For the same reason, i.e. numerous unbounded hydroxyl groups, hemicellulose and lignin 
molecules are likely to adsorb consequent amounts of moisture (Madsen, 2004). Thus, plant 
fibres’ stability in humid conditions requires low contents of amorphous cellulose, 
hemicellulose and lignin. This requirement is compatible with the one to get good material 
properties, i.e. high content of crystalline cellulose.  
Hence, there is no compromise to do by choosing a plant fibre with good material properties 
and stability in humid conditions.

vapour pressure (pO). The ratio between the two defines what is called relative humidity (h):

8. Manufacturing and testing of composites 
 
Manufacturing of composites occurs in different steps. First step is fibre preparation. In this 
study, fibres are raw flax fibres. To get such a material, flax was grown in fields until it 
reaches maturity, then uprooted (not cut) to conserve the full fibres’ length. Flax stems lay in 
the field for weeks, so that micro-organisms can start separating fibres to other constituents 
of the stem, among which bark, pectin, and part of the hemicellulose and lignin. This step is 
called retting. Then comes the scotching, which refers to a mechanical process that gets the 
fibres out from the rest. Normally, these fibres are combed and drafted to get flax fibre 
yarns. Flaxtape® from Lineo (Figure 18) consists in unidirectional fibre mats obtained right 
after scotching. Skipping two manufacturing steps reduces both costs and defects in fibres, 
induced by these successive processes (Baley, 2002).  
 
 
Figure 18: Picture of Lineo 
Flaxtape®. Note the small thickness 
of layers and rather raw aspect of 
material. 
 
 
 
 
After natural fibre fabrics or mats are prepared, they should be impregnated by a matrix to 
form a composite. This step is decisive, as it has the most influence on porosity of the 
manufactured composite. Porosity is generally unwanted, as it reduces composite stiffness 
(Madsen, et al., 2003).  
Numerous methods are available for fibres impregnation; the one selected for the study is 
pre-impregnation; flax fibre mats are pressed against two thin epoxy resin films, to form 
what looks like a thin double-side adhesive tape, called prepregs.  
These tapes are stacked on top of each other on a half mould that gives the component’s 
form. Resin has to be cured (hardened), and this is done in an autoclave. Prepregs are 
bagged in a specific way, detailed in section II.412. Autoclave curing provides excellent 
material quality, as it allows to apply vacuum and pressure equally over the whole 
component’s surface. This helps compacting voids, to reduce porosity. Combined with the 
optimal impregnation of fibres thanks to pre-impregnation process, one can achieve very 
low porosity and optimal resin curing.  
==PAGE:40==
38 
 
Optimal resin curing leads to a more elastic than viscous behaviour of the matrix, and to a 
lowered moisture sorption capacity. It is obtained when glass transition temperature (Tg) of 
the resin is maximal, which is described by D.Benedetto relation (Prime, 1973). Tg can be 
measured with differential scanning calorimetry method (DSC). To fully cure an epoxy resin, 
i.e. complete reticulation of the thermoset, one has to reach a sufficiently high temperature 
for a sufficient amount of time.  
Figure 19 shows an example of curing cycle. Note the stage at 130°C, called curing stage. 
Stage at 80°C makes resin liquid again, or decreases its viscosity, so it can flow through the 
laminate, to suppress voids (Cherif, 2012).  
 
Figure 19: Example of a curing cycle for epoxy resin. From (Cherif, 2012). 
 
To measure the obtained laminate’s properties, specific norms for composites are defined to 
complete a measuring process. In the present study, stiffness measures will be conducted 
following the norm ISO 527-4. For instance, composite samples should be tabs of at least 
2mm thickness, 25mm width and 250mm long. Strain is measured with 2 extensometer 
gauges placed on each side of the sample, to compensate eventual bending of the sample. 
To compute stiffness, the slope of stress/ strain curve is computed between 0,001 and 
0,0025% strain. As the stress/strain curve of NFRC is highly nonlinear at these strain levels, 
stiffness will be measured earlier on, i.e. between 0,0001 and 0,001%, as proposed and 
experimented by (Madsen, et al., 2007).  
Porosity of composites can be measured with areal density method. It concretely consists in 
comparing a sample’s real thickness with the theoretical thickness it should have, knowing 
the areal weight of matrix and fibres it contains and their respective densities. See appendix 
2 for further details.  
Typically, porosity of NFRC is between 2 and 10%, (Madsen, et al., 2003), (Berges, et al., 
2016), (Espert, et al., 2004).  
==PAGE:41==
39 
 
The main measure conducted in this study is the weight gain of samples during moisture 
sorption process. The detailed protocol will be explained in section III.3.d. At this point, it is 
important to not the importance of humidity exposure method. Two methods can be used; 
storage of samples in climate chambers, or immersion of these samples directly in water. 
Similar to the difference between AFSP and moisture content of a fibre immersed in water, 
mentioned in section II.4, composites can adsorb additional moisture, under the form of 
liquid water, when directly immersed in it. This influences (i) the EMC, and (ii) the diffusion 
coefficients. For instance, at room temperature, NFRC stored in liquid water will adsorb at 
typical diffusion speeds of 5.10-7mm²/s (Espert, et al., 2004), whereas diffusion speeds in the 
range of 8.10-8mm²/s are measured with samples stored in humidity chambers.

1. Introduction    
 
The aim of this experimental study is to identify the behaviour of the studied composite as it 
adsorbs moisture, in order to have some inputs for the simulation described in section III.  
 
The requested data is:  
a. Checking the simple Fickian behaviour of the moisture diffusion in studied composite,  
b. Global moisture diffusion coefficient, as a function of temperature, 
c. Relationship between dimensional change and moisture content.   
d. Check the hypothesis of hygral strains independent from moisture distribution (see 
section II.6),  
e. Relation between moisture content of composite and its stiffness loss. 
f. Check the hypothesis of moisture distribution independent stiffness loss (C.f.  II.7) 
g. Sorption isotherm of the composite 
h. Sorption isotherm of the fibres, 
Requests c, d, e and f refer to the most innovative part of the project. Indeed, these latter

state.

e. Protocol Fibres 
 
Lineo graciously provided a roll of non-impregnated fibres which are the same as the ones in 
prepreg material. They will be used to build a sorption isotherm.  
To begin, it has to be defined how to make fibres adsorb and desorb moisture; in that 
purpose, fibres initially stored in ambient conditions, i.e. in the lab, are put in a vacuum oven 
at 80°C and weighed after longer and longer drying time, to assess the minimal drying time 
required to totally desorb moisture; this time is reached when fibres stop losing weight 
between two increasingly longer drying cycles. Same process is used to determine minimal 
adsorption time, thanks to one of the humidity chambers at 25°C. Minimal adsorption time 
is not dependent on humidity gradient.  
Drying, or desorption cycle has been stated to be at least 2 hours at 80°C, and adsorption 
cycle is minimum 3 hours in a humidity chamber at 25°C.  
The main experiments for fibre sorption isotherm start by “curing” the fibres for 2 hours at 
140°C in a normal oven, to age them in the same way as fibres in prepregs would be when 
curing in an autoclave. Indeed, curing cycle has an influence on moisture sorption capacity of 
plant fibres, like it has been discussed by the end of section II.4.

IV. Modelling study 
 
1. Introduction 
 
In this section, it will be explained how a MATLAB simulation of NFRC’s behaviour in humid 
conditions can be built. Material behaviour follows the equations and parameters measured 
in experimental study. Therefore, modelling consists in summarizing the observed behaviour 
in one tool, so material properties can be forecasted as a function of environment 
parameters.  
Starting from a first simple Fickian diffusion of moisture through a material’s thickness (1D), 
to assess the model’s coherence, numerous parameters and computation features will be 
added to the solver to move it into a helpful tool.

Simulated global moisture content curve appears to follow the trend of experimental data

It can be explained as follows; diffusion coefficient implemented in the model is the global 
diffusion coefficient (D) of a composite plate that sorbed moisture through its thickness, but 
also through its four lateral sides. If this plate was sealed on its lateral sides, then measured 
weight gains would have been lower and computed diffusion coefficient would have 
diminished. In other words, to be able to compute the same weight gain curve, it would have 
been necessary to diminish the plate’s thickness, enabling the sorption process to occur 
faster. What is simulated here is this latter situation; one compares moisture sorption of two 
composite plates of same thickness; the unsealed one follows experimental data, and the 
sealed one behaves like the simulated curve. To make the two plates’ weight gains 
correspond, it is necessary to decrease the plate’s thickness in the simulation. 
Since simulated changes in material properties are computed from simulated global 
moisture content, their curves also diverge from experimental data.

Computed changes in material properties align better with the corresponding experimental 
data. Strains in width direction are only diverging of a few tenth of percents; on day 18, 
experimental data indicates a strain of 0.02, and the model predicted a value of about 0.017. 
This represents a relative error of 15%, compared to experimental data. This divergence can 
be explained by the underestimation of global moisture content on this day; should the 
model have predicted an additional 0.7% in global moisture content, computed strain would 
have been 0.007x0.4=0.0028 higher, and new relative error would be lower than 1%.  
Strains in thickness direction diverge notably more; experimental datapoints such as the one

denote a big scatter in the measures, due to surface roughness of composite, or a 
hydrothermal relaxation of inner stresses, as it has been observed with samples stored at 
50°C in section III.4.g. Therefore, simulations at higher temperatures (above 30°C) may give 
non-realistic results.  
Stiffness forecasts are quite accurate; differences between experimental and model of about 
1.5GPa, on day 3, are observable; this represents a relative error of only 5% with respect to 
the experiences! On day 21, the divergence is higher, i.e. 3GPa, which corresponds to a 
relative error of 14%. This error is partly explained by a 0.7% underestimation of global 
moisture content by the model on this day, as shown on the “weight gain curves” graph. If 
global moisture content prediction had been exact on this day, forecasted stiffness would 
have been 0.7x2.5=1.75 GPa higher (see equation II.4.j.1). This would have decreased the 
stiffness divergence of the same amount, and lowered relative error to 6%.  
Thus, model errors in forecasting material properties remain in a rather small range, and in 
general, divergences from model to experimental data can be explained by errors in forecast 
of composite’s global moisture content.  
Therefore, a better understanding of the moisture diffusion process could help in making the 
model more accurate; as it has been detailed in section III.4, experiments were conducted 
until global moisture content equals to about half of the expected EMC had been reached. 
The second part of moisture adsorption curve is actually giving the confirmation of a Fickian 
behaviour, as it has been described thanks to Figure 14, in section III.5. So, moisture diffusion 
in the studied composite might slightly deviate from simple Fickian behaviour at high global 
moisture contents, as it seems to be observable in the previous simulation, when 
temperature is increased.  
Altogether, the model shows a good capability to simulate moisture sorption and already 
provides a good basis for simulation of NFRC in a given surrounding environment.

V. General conclusion and outlook 
 
1. General conclusion  
 
In a first experimental part, this study enabled to state laws of behaviour that predict weight 
gain, stiffness and strains of a natural fibre reinforced composite (NFRC), during moisture 
sorption process. 
Indeed, it was first hypothesized that, in a composite section submitted to moisture diffusion 
through its thickness only, stiffness and hygral strains, i.e. strains due to moisture sorption, 
would not depend on moisture distribution in the composite, that is not even during 
moisture process, but only dependent on global moisture content, i.e. the total amount of 
moisture over the dry mass of composite. Moisture diffusion was suspected to be Fickian, 
and dependent on temperature, following the Arrhenius law.  
To check these hypothesis, an experimental set-up was composed with two types of 
rectangular samples of constant thickness, stored in climate chambers at different relative 
humidity (RH) and temperature (T); first samples, the big plates, were large enough to study 
moisture diffusion through their thickness with a negligible effect of lateral diffusion; 
moisture content and hygral strains were measured on these samples during moisture 
sorption process. Second type of samples is called small plates, and it was used to measure 
the composite’s stiffness versus its moisture content. The idea was to get tensile specimens 
which underwent a moisture sorption through their thickness only, such that their moisture 
content and distribution would be the same as the one of a big plate stored in the same 
climate conditions. This can be achieved by using composite plates of sufficiently large 
dimensions, such that an area in their middle is not affected by lateral moisture diffusion. 
Then, tensile specimens are cut out from this area just before testing. To measure the 
stiffness at several moisture contents, several small plates were stored in each climate 
chamber with one big plate.  
To assess the impact of different moisture distributions along composite’s thickness, with 
same global moisture content, the sample’s groups were stored at 3 different RH. This also 
enabled to build a composite sorption isotherm, i.e. describe the relation between 
equilibrium moisture content of composite as a function of RH. Temperature effect on 
moisture diffusion speed was studied at 3 different temperatures.  
Hence, experimental investigations led to the statement of the following laws of behaviour;  
• Moisture sorption isotherm of composite and neat fibres can be 
modelled by a Zuritz equation fitted on experimental data. A 
moisture content dependence on temperature was also stated. 
• Moisture diffusion through composite’s thickness is pure Fickian. 
Diffusion coefficient at 25°C is 5.9E-8 mm²/s.

• Diffusion coefficient is influenced by temperature, following the 
Arrhenius Law. Diffusion constant D0 was measured to be 
0.813m²/s and energy activation Q=18kcal/mol. 
• No influence of moisture distribution was observed, so stiffness E

These laws of behaviour were incorporated in a numerical model on MATLAB, to simulate 
the moisture sorption process of the material. This model basically solves a Fick’s equation in 
one space dimension with following input parameters; (i)Temperature and RH of 
surroundings as a function of time, (ii) temperature dependent sorption isotherm of 
material, (iii) studied component’s thickness, (iv) temperature dependent moisture diffusion 
coefficient. Solver computes the global moisture content of the sample as a function of time. 
From this result, strains, stiffness and weight increase of the composite can be computed.  
Obtained model proved its ability to match with experimental data, even when surrounding 
relative humidity and temperature changes over time. After this model’s assessment, the 
program was used to forecast material’s behaviour in real meteorological conditions; With a 
thin material, (1.7mm), global moisture content of the sample increased over about 1 ½ 
year, until reaching a plateau. During this plateau, seasonal variations of surrounding RH and 
T caused non-negligible changes in material’s properties, i.e. its mass, stiffness and strains. 
The same simulation was run with a thicker sample (5mm). It showed the same plateau in 
moisture sorption, reached after a much longer time (15 years). However, this time seasons 
induced minor variations in material’s properties  
 
Hence, it was shown that designing a structural component made of NFRC requires taking 
environmental conditions into account. Indeed, surrounding humidity diffuses through the 
material and changes its properties. After some years, properties tend to stagnate around a 
value that is substantially different from the ones after manufacturing. In addition, seasonal 
changes of temperature and RH cause variations of the properties, that are non-negligible in 
the case of thin components.  
Altogether, the proposed simulation process for components made of NFRC, explained in the 
introduction, can be accomplished thanks to the present established model; Once material 
properties’ changes are computed with the present model, they can be associated to a 
mechanical load case on the component, as an input to a finite element mechanical solver.

2. Outlook 
 
Following suggestions for future experimental and modelling investigations on NFRC can be 
made:  
 
a. Future experimental work 
 
- 
Optimize the curing process used to manufacture the material:  
The curing process enabled to obtain very low porosity contents in composite, however 
it lasted almost 20 hours, which is not compatible with industrial requirements. 
Improvements can be made to obtain the same composite’s properties while reducing 
the processing time. 
- 
Get more experimental data for composite sorption isotherm and moisture diffusion:  
Indeed, present experiments provided limited experimental data, such that previously 
presented composite sorption isotherm was only a guess. It was also not checked if the 
studied material followed a pure fickian behaviour until the end of the moisture sorption 
process.  
- 
Understanding of natural fibres’ alteration at elevated temperatures: 
As observed in section III.4.a, sorption isotherm of processed fibres is different than the 
one before processing. Also, in (Madsen, et al., 2007), it was shown that processing 
temperature has an influence on tensile properties of the obtained composite. Next step 
would be to understand the cause of these properties’ change. 
- 
Model for width hygroexpansion during moisture sorption process:  
A first schematic of this model is shown on Figure 46. It should be verified if this model 
explains the lower hygroexpansion coefficient in width direction, at intermediate states 
of moisture sorption process.  
 
 
b. Future modelling work 
 
 
- 
Include the effect of a coating above the laminate in the present numerical model: 
In most applications, composite materials are coated to protect them from humidity and 
abrasion; It would be interesting to add this feature to the present model. This way, a 
more advanced feasibility analysis of the use of NFRC in outdoor conditions could be 
conducted.   
 
- 
Build a meso-scale mechanical model with differently orientated composite layers:  
==PAGE:100==
98 
 
Indeed, composite parts are rarely made of parallel stacked layers; Using the present 
model as a basis, it might be relevant to model moisture diffusion in a multi layers 
composite (meso-scale consideration), where each layer is unidirectional and has a given 
orientation versus the composite’s coordinate system. With increasing moisture content, 
each layer will tend to expand in thickness and width direction, as well as loose stiffness. 
As the layers are stacked on each other, their expansions are constrained, and inner 
stresses will appear. 
 
- 
Build a micro mechanical model that takes the inner stresses due to hygral strains due to 
moisture content into account for EMC computation. Similarly to the suggested meso-
scale model, micro-mechanical considerations can be relevant for more fundamental 
understanding of moisture sorption of NFRC. The model could consist in a cross-sectional 
view of natural fibres expanding in an expanding matrix, under the influence of moisture 
diffusion. Both components will restrict each other in their dimensional changes, and 
stresses will appear. These stresses can be used to (i) predict fibre/matrix debonding, as 
it has been done in (Rocha, et al., 2017), and (ii) compute the actual sorption isotherm of 
matrix and fibres, which is pressure dependent, according to (Willems, 2014). This way, 
moisture sorption and changes in material properties of NFRC could be better 
understood.

function [M,Mboundary,Dc] = 
solver(n_z,n_t,dz,dt,D0c,Q,M_ini,RHcase,TcaseReal,aac,bbc,K_EMCTemp) 
  
  
%----------------------------------------------------------------------- 
  
M= zeros ( n_z + 2, n_t) ; %Result matrix (Moisture content at each point of composite, at 
each timestep. 
Mboundary = zeros(1,n_t); 
  
  
%RH controlled Boundary conditions: Moisture content of boundary 
%layers is given by a Zuritz function that models the sorption 
%isotherm. The input is surrounding RH.  
  
  
  
  
==PAGE:111==
109 
 
for t=1:n_t 
     
    M_emc= (((log(1-RHcase(1,t))/aac)^(1/bbc))/100)-(K_EMCTemp*(TcaseReal(1,t)-25));  
                                                      % Zuritz isoth for composite 
                                                      % + Correction of EMC 
                                                      % versus temperature 
                                                       
    if RHcase(1,t)>0.99 
        M_emc=0.17;                                   % Avoids zuritz function to provide 
    end                                               % an infinite EMC value.  
     
    Mboundary(1,t)=M_emc;         % For plotting of boundary conditions.  
    M( 1,t )= M_emc; 
    M(n_z +2,t) = M_emc ;                  
  end 
  
  
  
%Initial inner humidity 
for i = 2 : n_z + 1 
M ( i,1 ) = M_ini ; 
end 
  
%----------------------------------------------------------------- 
  
  
%  S O L V I N G      Fick's equation 
  
  
for t = 1 : n_t-1 
     
    Dc = D0c * exp(-Q/(8.314*(TcaseReal(1,t)+273)));      % Varying Dc 
  
    
     
      
        %E x p r e s s i o n  of  a l p h a , b e t a 
        a = Dc*dt/(dz^2); 
        beta = 1 - 2 * a; 
        alpha = a; 
     
for i=2:n_z+1 
     
M(i,t+1)= alpha* (M(i+1,t) +M(i-1,t)) + beta*M(i,t);  
  
end 
end 
end 
  
  
  
function [W,Winf,epsY,epsZ,E,tEq,WtEq]=propertiesComp... 
    (M,RH,M_ini,kepsY,kepsZ,ke,Edry,aac,bbc,n_t,n_z,dt) 
  
W=zeros(1,n_t);             % Global moisture content 
EpsY = zeros(n_t,1);         % Hygroexpansion in Y direction, 
EpsZ = zeros(n_t,1);         % Hygroexpansion in Z direction, 
E = zeros(n_t,1);           % Array of stiffness vs time 
tEq=0;                      % Instant equilibrium is reached at 90% 
WtEq=0;                     % moisture content at this instant 
  
Winf = ((log(1-RH)/aac)^(1/bbc))/100;  % Zuritz : expected weight gain at equilibrium 
  
  
  
for t=1:n_t 
for e=2:n_z+1                 % Only for inner nodes :-)  
     
    W(1,t)= W(1,t)+ M(e,t);       % Total M is proportional to weight gain of each layer 
     
     
end  
  
W(1,t)=W(1,t)/(n_z);            % To make W independent from n_z, and absolute. 
  
epsY(t)=kepsY*(W(1,t)-M_ini); % Expansion proportional to total moisture content 
epsZ(t)=kepsZ*(W(1,t)-M_ini); 
E(t)= Edry -ke*W(1,t);   % Following linear stiffness loss versus total moisture content 
theory  
  
end 
  
for f=1:n_t                     % Search for instant at which 90% of equilibrium is reached. 
    if abs((W(1,f)-Winf)/(Winf-M_ini))<= 0.1 
        tEq=f*dt/(86400*365);        % tEq is time to reach equilibrium . Scala of graph 2 
        WtEq=W(1,f); 
        break 
    end 
end 
  
  
end
