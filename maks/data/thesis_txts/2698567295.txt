Abstract
In order to balance the supply and demand of energy in today’s society it is necessary to store
some of the generated energy. The use of Flywheel Energy Storage Systems (FESS) for this purpose is
becoming more relevant as the development of rotor materials advances. The use of Magnetic Bearings
for such systems are inevitable in order to avoid mechanical friction and in order to control the rotor
at the high speeds required for a FESS to run eﬃciently. A FESS test rig has been designed and
constructed at the Mechanical Department of the Technical University of Denmark. In the test rig a
rotor is levitated using a Permanent Magnetic Bearing, while two Active Magnetic Bearings (AMBs)
keep the rotor centred inside the housing of the system. The AMBs in the test rig can be controlled
using an already implemented Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller. However, the scope
of this project has been to design a new optimal controller for the test rig, using full state feedback.
For the full state feedback controller a simpliﬁed model of the system has been deﬁned. In order to
derive an accurate model of the system, the test rig has ﬁrst been thoroughly investigated and some
small changes have been made to the programme which controls the test rig. The ﬁnal model is based
on a set of assumptions about the system and all equations have been linearised around the point of
operation. The model has been validated using experimental results from the test rig with the PID
controller implemented. The new controller is designed as a Linear-Quadratic Regulator, for which the
gain matrices are designed using a set of weight matrices which describes the importance of each state
and each output in the model. The performance of the new optimal controller is assessed by deﬁning
a set of dynamical criteria which must be met. These criteria are deﬁned from the time response of
the system. By optimizing the entries in the weight matrices for the optimal controller, it is possible
to design the controller such that all criteria are met and the actuation eﬀort is minimized. The new
controller requires information about all the states in the model and therefore an observer has also
been designed. In order to account for noise in the measurements and uncertainties in the model,
the observer is designed as an optimal observer also known as a Kalman Filter. The gains of the
Kalman Filter are based on the noise detected in the system during the initial investigations. After
the new controller has been implemented on the test rig a number of experiments have been carried
out to asses the ﬁnal performance of the new control system. The experimental data has proven to
be in good agreement with the simulated response from the model, considering that some very rough
assumptions about the system have been made.

Introduction
Energy storage is of utmost importance in today’s society. The fundamental purpose of energy storage
is to balance out the energy supply and demand in the society, by storing energy during high produc-
tion periods and releasing energy during peak consumption periods. In Denmark the conversion to
greener energy solutions also implies the transition to more ﬂuctuating energy sources such as solar
or wind energy, and thus energy storage is becoming ever more important. The most well-known type
of energy storage system is probably the electro-chemical battery which can be found in almost any
electronic device from a smartphone to an electric vehicle. Rechargeable electro-chemical batteries
are often referred to as secondary batteries and are of most use in everyday life. The batteries consist
of one or more electro-chemical cells which generate electrical energy from chemical reactions. The
chemical reaction depends on the battery type.
Some well-known battery types are the lead-acid
battery and the lithium-ion battery. The lead-acid battery is for example used to provide energy for
the starter motors in diﬀerent types of vehicles, while the lithium-ion battery is the primary battery
for most of the new fully electric vehicles. Lately, a third very competitive solid-state battery is being
developed for use in electric vehicles as a substitute for lithium-ion batteries. The advantages of the
solid-state batteries is their high energy density, low charging time and resilience in regard to high
and low temperatures [20][3].
In this report a diﬀerent energy storage solution is investigated. A Flywheel Energy Storage System
(FESS) is a mechanical energy storage system, which works by accelerating a rotor with a high in-
ertia to a high speed. Flywheels are already being used to provide power during outages in diﬀerent
industries, and as reserve during grid frequency regulation when sudden changes between supply and
consumption occur in the electrical grid.
Usually, the acceleration process of the system is obtained using an electrical motor. The energy from
the electrical motor is then stored as rotational energy, and can be retrieved using a generator which
decelerates the rotor. The energy capacity of the FESS therefore depends on the moment of inertia of
the rotor and on the obtainable rotational speed. The approximated stored energy can be expressed
as seen in Equation (1) [6].
E = I ω2
2
(1)
Since the moment of inertia depends on both the mass and on how far away from the rotation axis
this mass is located, it is more convenient to increase the rotational speed in order to obtain high
energy density i.e. energy pr. mass. From Equation (1) it is evident that a change in the rotational
speed has an eﬀect which is quadratic compared to the eﬀect of the moment of inertia. However, the
rotational speed of the system is limited by the tensile strength of the rotor, since the rotor must
be able to withstand the high centripetal stresses which occur due to the high rotational speed. The
advancement in material sciences has enabled the development of carbon ﬁber composite rotors, which
can withstand higher rotational speeds and thus the energy density of these systems can be increased.
The advantages of the FESS are the low charging times along with low maintenance and long lifetime.
However, in order to reduce friction losses the system must be in a vacuum environment. Traditional
ball bearings do not perform well in vacuum, since the lubricant in the bearings might vaporize. Other
types of conventional bearings such as journal bearings are also not suitable for the given application,
since they are limited in their stability, damping and friction features. An appropriate solution to
support the rotor and at the same time ensure low friction, would therefore be magnetic bearings. In
general magnetic bearings are divided into two diﬀerent types; Permanent Magnetic Bearings (PMB)

The utilization of magnetic bearings for industrial purposes is not new. To name a few examples
magnetic bearings are often used in compressors, turbines and pumps. For most applications AMBs
are preferred because the bearing forces can be actively changed and controlled through the electro-
magnets. PMBs, on the other hand, generate bearing forces which cannot be changed and are acting
purely through permanent magnets. Furthermore, due to Earnshaw’s theorem which states that ”A
charged particle cannot be held in a stable equilibrium by electrostatic forces alone.”, any unstable
degrees of freedom (dof) must be stabilized through another acting force, which is also the case for
the electromagnet in an AMB. The combination of PMBs and AMBs is, therefore, often preferred in
FESSs.
Earnshaw’s theorem means that, as oppose to a regular mechanical spring system, the magnetic
bearing have a negative stiﬀness coeﬃcient which increases in magnitude as the air gap between
the stator and the rotor decreases or the current increases. Thus, the actual magnetic force is non-
linear.
Furthermore, once the air gap becomes very small, the magnetic ﬂux becomes saturated.
Nevertheless, it has been found that the magnetic bearing force can be linearised and approximated
using the expression in Equation (2).
fm = −ks x + ki i
(2)
The expression in Equation (2) is also derived in Section 3, in which also the coeﬃcients ks and ki are
described. These are also referred to as the force/displacement factor and the force/current factor in
that order [9].
As the name suggests the AMBs are actively controlled through a closed loop control system. The
closed loop system consists of a control object which in the case of the FESS is the rotor bearing
system, a set of actuators which are the electromagnets and a set of sensors to measure the response
of the system.
In a regular FESS the sensors usually measures the position of the rotor and the
current signal from the AMBs. For many applications the control system is simply realised through
a Proportional-Integral-Derivative controller (PID) on the position. This type of controller has the
advantage that it is fairly simple to implement, it is easy to tune in order to obtain a desired dynamic
behaviour and it does not require a lot of computational eﬀort to programme.
However, in the energy storage application it is of utmost importance that the control system itself
consume as little energy as possible. One way of meeting this requirement is by instead using an
Linear-Quadratic-regulator (LQR). The LQR is a special type of full state feedback controller. In the
full state feedback controller it is assumed that all states of the system are available and can be fed
Page 2 of 97
==PAGE:17==
Technical University of Denmark

back into the system. This allows for a faster and more precise control scheme. In other words the
full state feedback controller takes advantage of the fact that more information about the system is
readily available because of a predeﬁned model.
A more comprehensive description of the LQR controller is given in Section 7. For now it is simply
important for the reader to understand that in order to determine the gains of the LQR a set of
weighting matrices must be deﬁned. These matrices are used to describe how a deviation of one of the
states or one of the inputs in the system adds to the overall cost function for the LQR. In this report a
further optimization of these weight matrices will be suggested. The objective of this optimization task
is to minimize the overall actuation eﬀort of the control system. In general designing an optimization
task embodies deﬁning a set of design variables, deﬁning the objective of the optimization task and
deﬁning a set of constraints which the optimized solution for the design variables must meet.
In the article ’Multi-Objective Optimization Design of Nonlinear Magnetic Bearing Rotordynamic
System’ by W. Zhong, A. Palazzolo and X. Kang [16] an optimization algorithm based on Genetic
Algorithms (GAs) is suggested for optimizing the design and control scheme of a magnetic-bearing-
rotor system. In the statement of the optimization task, three objectives are deﬁned along with a
set of constraints and a set of design variables. The objectives and the constraints are based on the
dynamical response. The desired dynamical response is deﬁned using the ISO 14839 - ’Mechanical vi-
bration – Vibration of rotating machinery equipped with active magnetic bearings’. For multi-objective
optimization tasks the optimal solution is indeed a set of solutions often referred to as the Pareto-
optimal set. The article written by W. Zhong, A. Palazzolo and X. Kang investigates diﬀerent types
of algorithms which are applied to the same optimization problem and afterwards the optimal solution
of the diﬀerent algorithms are analyzed.
In this report a simple single objective optimization task will be deﬁned with the goal of improving the
control design in order for the system to obtain a desired dynamical response to a single step-input.
1.1
Motivation
A complete test rig consisting of a ﬂywheel situated in a gimbal frame and controlled through a
fully developed LabVIEW-programme has been the result of a collaboration between Maersk Drilling,
WattsUp Power and Aalborg university. The test rig is located at the Mechanical Department on the
Technical University of Denmark. The controller originally implemented on the test rig is a PID con-
troller, which was designed and tested by N. A. Dagnaes-Hansen as part of a PhD Project, see [5]. An
inner control loop for the current has also been realized through a Proportional-Integral (PI) controller.
The goal of the PhD Project was to asses the feasibility of an FESS on board an oﬀ-shore vessel.
Therefore, the frame of a gimbal mount has been connected to a set pneumatic valves, which can be
used to simulate the movement of the oﬀ-shore vessel. A control scheme for the pneumatic valves has
been developed and tested by A. Andersen in [1].
This project aims to ﬁrstly investigate all the components of the test rig in order to develop a satisfying
model to describe the most important system dynamics. Afterwards, the model must be validated
experimentally before a new controller can be designed for the test rig. The new controller is designed
using the theory of optimal controllers. The design of an optimal controller requires that all states
in the model are estimated, and thus an optimal observer must also be constructed. The intention
of the optimal controller is to obtain a better dynamic response for the system and at the same time
minimize the actuation eﬀort of the AMBs.

The Test Facility
The model and the experimental results presented in this report are based on a physical system
located in the laboratory of the Department of Mechanical Engineering at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU). The test rig consists of a mechanical system, electronics and a computer. A brief
description of the diﬀerent components of the test rig will be presented here.
2.1
The Mechanical System
The mechanical system consists of a frame, a gimbal mount, a housing, and a rotor. The frame is
made up of quadratic aluminium proﬁles, which can be moved using a pair of pneumatic cylinders.
The idea of the pneumatic system is to mimic the movement of a ship, and this part of the system has
been investigated through several projects, see [5] and [1]. A gimbal mount is installed in the frame,
and ﬁnally the housing with the rotor and magnetic bearings is installed in the gimbal mount. The
mathematical model presented later in this report will neglect the dynamics of the housing, the gimbal
mount and the outer frame, and thus only account for the rotor-bearing system inside the housing.
Figure 1: Sideview of the test rig with the foundation, gimbal mount, rotor housing, and pneumatic cylinder.
Two AMBs are installed inside the housing.
The AMBs consists of a number of coils, which are
wounded around some ferromagnetic cores situated inside the housing. Magnetic steel sheets have
been press-ﬁtted onto the shaft of the rotor.
The rotor shaft itself is made of steel and has two
aluminium hubs which are used for the position measurements. These measurements are obtained
through two pairs of eddy-current sensors. The eddy-current sensor is a type of proximity sensor
which detects a change in the impedance of the coils of the probes. The change in impedance is
caused by the eddy current generated on the target surface due to Faraday’s law. The change in
the distance between the eddy-current probe and the target surface can then be described using the
impedance. The ﬁrst pair of eddy-current sensors is located just below the bottom AMB and the
second pair of sensors is located just above the top AMB.

In the bottom of the housing a PMB is located. The PMB consists of two pairs of opposing ring
magnets placed in a closed loop conﬁguration. The ring magnets are essentially just a lot of smaller
cubic magnets which are ﬁxed in a circular groove on the inside of the housing. Likewise, a groove in
the bottom of the rotor is also ﬁlled with the small cubic magnets. There are two rows of magnets in
both the housing and the rotor, each row corresponding to one ring magnet. The inner row of magnets
in both the rotor and the stator has the south pole pointing outwards, while the outer row has the
north pole pointing outwards making the rotor and the housing repel each other.

thus it is possible to measure the rotation of the rotor, as the reﬂection changes according to which
part of the indicator tape is appearing in front of the IR sensor. The rotational speed is usually

(FPGA) VI. The Real Time VI contains the main code and runs from the associated computer, while
the FPGA VI is used to programme the FPGA module on the cRIO. The main reason for employing
the FPGA on the cRIO is to keep execution time and power consumption low, and the biggest draw-

3
Mathematical Model of the FESS
The controller to be implemented on the test rig is a model-based controller, which means that it
partly relies on a model to predict the dynamics of the system. Therefore, before designing an optimal
controller for the system, it is important to have a good model to predict the behaviour of the system.
For an optimal controller the model must be represented using a so-called state space formulation,
which means that all the equations which are included in the system are represented as matrix-vector
multiplications.
Before deﬁning the actual model, it is necessary to consider the importance of diﬀerent dynamics
Page 19 of 97
==PAGE:34==
Technical University of Denmark
2021
Master Thesis
inﬂuencing the system. To have a good estimate of the system behaviour all important dynamics
must be included in the model. However, the state space formulation is based on linear diﬀerential
equations and therefore it only makes sense to consider dynamics which, with good accuracy, can be
represented as linear diﬀerential equations. Furthermore, it is also desired to minimize the size of
the system in order to eﬃciently implement the controller in the LabVIEW-programme. A list of
assumptions is made in order to deﬁne the system dynamics which are neglected in the model.
 The elasticity of the rotor can be neglected meaning that the rotor can be described as a rigid
body with 6 dofs.
 Aerodynamical forces on the rotor are small and can, therefore, be neglected.
 The rotor housing is assumed to be stationary and forces can only be transferred between the
rotor and the AMBs i.e. the dynamics of the gimbal mount and the housing can be neglected.
 The pressurized air used to control the rotational speed of the rotor does not exert a radial force
on the rotor, but only controls the rotational speed to the system.
 The temperature rise in the electronical system is small and thus thermal eﬀects in the elec-
tronical system can be neglected. Furthermore, the heat generated in the AMBs is dissipated
through convection.

6
Tuning the mathematical Model
The results in Section 5 revealed some deviations between the simulated response from the model and
the actual response from the physical system.
Not all parameters in the model can be deﬁned with a high accuracy analytically. Parameters such as
ki, ks and ku described in Section 3.2 depends on several uncertain entities such as the initial air gap
s0 or the area Aa. Furthermore, the deﬁned model is based on several assumptions which means that
some of the dynamics in physical system is neglected all together in the model.
In order to account for the uncertainties of the parameters as well as some of the neglected dynamics in
the model, some of the model parameters are tuned to obtain a better agreement between experimental
and simulated results. The parameters are tuned using the experimental data obtained from the step
responses i.e. through time domain assessment. In general it is also possible to use frequency domain
data to optimize the model parameters, however, it is assumed that the most ampliﬁed dynamics will
be accounted for by tuning the against the time domain response. Nevertheless, by using the time
domain response, the tuning does not account as much for the high frequent dynamics, because it will
not be as apparent in the time domain response.
In Matlab the tuning routine can be established using the build-in function lsqnonlin. The lsqnonlin-
function is used to solve curve ﬁtting problems.
The function uses a Least-Square-Approach and
therefore minimizes the sum of squared residuals. The residuals here being the diﬀerence between the
observed values and the values predicted by the model. This approach can be represented using the
expression seen in Equation (81).
min

Where fn are the residual functions and x are the tuning parameters. For the given system the tuning
problem would be to minimize the error between the simulated response and the measured response.
This can be done for the response in each direction and in both the upper and lower part of the rotor,
resulting in a total of four residual functions.
The input to the lsqnonlin-function is thus a function deﬁning the error between the four simu-
lated responses and the four measured responses, as well as an initial guess on the tuning parameters
and an upper and lower boundary for the parameters.
The boundaries are deﬁned such that the
tuning parameters can only take on values within ±50% of their initial value, except for the param-
eters related to the initial state of the system, which are allowed to take on values between 0 and 10−3.

6.1
Frequency Response Functions
Besides from the response of the model in time domain, it is also relevant to look at the dynamics
in the frequency domain. This is usually done by establishing a relation between an input excitation
with a given frequency and the output response of the system. In order to establish this relationship,
the structure must be excited with a known signal. A typical signal used for this is the chirp signal. In
the chirp signal the frequency increases or decreases with time. Unlike the step signal which excites all
modes at the same time, the chirp signal excites diﬀerent frequency content at diﬀerent times. Since
high frequent modes usually have lower energy than low frequent modes, the chirp can be adjusted
such that a higher amplitude is imposed for the higher frequencies. For the experiments presented
in this section, a chirp signal is send through the AMBs in each direction independently. To obtain
a good average, 9 chirp signals are send over a period of 90 s. This is done three times for each
direction in each AMB, but for each of the three times the frequencies excited are adjusted as well as
the amplitude. Thus, the ﬁrst 9 chirp signals increase from 0-260 Hz, the next 9 chirp signals increase
from 240-500 Hz and the ﬁnal 9 chirp signals increase from 480-700 Hz. The overlap in frequencies
ensures a high coherence.

It is common practice to use block-based processing when transforming time domain signals into fre-
quency domain. This is done in order to avoid spectral leakages, which might occur if the signals are
not perfectly periodic or if the signal sampling cuts oﬀthe signal in the middle of one period. The
leakage occur because the signal is assumed to be completely periodic when the Fourier Transform is
performed.
Block-based processing divides the time based data into a number of blocks, using a so-called win-
dowing function. The simplest window function is a rectangular window, which has a value of 1 for a
ﬁnite number of data points and 0 for the rest. However, this type of window causes the the Fourier
Transform to have spectral leakage, which means that in addition to the peaks caused by the frequency
components of a signal, several smaller peaks will appear next to the true peaks. This can be solved by
using a non-square windowing function such as the Hamming-window which instead have a sinusoidal
shape. An example of a Hamming-window can be seen in Figure 42.

From the ﬁgures it is clear that some extra large peaks have appeared in the amplitude plots. These
peaks are due to the fact that the system will generate mechanical vibration at multiples of its running
speed. These types of vibrations are often referred to as synchronous vibrations. The synchronous
vibrations can be caused by several sources of vibrations such as mass imbalance, eccentric rotation
or misalignment in the system. The reason that the ampliﬁcations of the synchronous vibrations are
large compared to the rest of dynamics is due to the fact that the input force measured and used to
make the FRF is not the actual unbalanced force but instead simply the input current, and thus the
ratio between the input force and the synchronous vibrations is very small.
The ﬁgures reveal a change in the dynamics as the rotational speed changes. This change is expected
due to the gyroscopic eﬀects. From the ﬁgures it can be seen that the mode at 45 Hz begins to separate
at higher frequencies. This frequency is assumed to be related to a rigid mode of the rotor. As the
rotor begins to rotate the rigid modes changes from one-dimensional motions into two-dimensional
motions. When this happens the center of the shaft is goes from just moving up and down or from
side to side, to instead rotate around its own axis. When the rotation of the centre of the shaft has the
same direction as the rotation of the rotor itself it is referred to as forward whirl, and when rotation of
the centre of the shaft occur in the opposite direction of the rotor it is referred to as backward whirl.
The natural frequency of the forward mode and the backward mode will separate more as the angular
velocity of the shaft increases.

Design of Optimal Controller
The new control system consists of a Linear Quadrautic Regulator or LQR for short. The LQR is a
type of full state feedback controller. It is implemented in the exact same way as a pole-placement
regulator, and thus the only diﬀerence between the LQR and the pole-placement regulator is the way
the terms in the gain matrices are determined.
For pole-placement regulators the gain matrix is chosen based on the poles of the system, while the
gain matrix of the LQR controller is chosen based on speciﬁc system characteristics. Thus, the LQR is
often regarded as a more intuitive controller, since it is not always straight forward to chose a proper
set of poles or a system. This is especially true when the system is large and the dynamics of the
states are very diverse. The goal of the regulation is to force the states and the input of the system to
approach a certain stationary value. As an example it is often desired to keep the position at around 0.
As the name suggests any full state feedback controller requires all states to be readily readable.
Normally it is not possible to detect all states from an actual physical system, since the installation
of that many sensors would be both costly and practically hard to accomplish. Thus, it is necessary
to estimate some of the states in the model and this can be done through a so-called observer. The
observer combines the measured signals from the system with knowledge of the system components
to predict the system behaviour.

7.4
Implementation in LabVIEW
In order to implement the control design in the LabVIEW programme connected to the test rig, the
system matrices must be modiﬁed slightly. This is done in order to ensure that the model based
controller can be implemented in LabVIEW without the matrices loosing precision. The matrices are
ﬁrst discretized and in this process the run-time of the system is used. In LabVIEW the control action
signal is send to the system in a loop which takes 50 microseconds to execute. In Matlab the function
c2d is used for discretizing the system. The inputs to this function are the state space system and a
sample time. The 50 microseconds are fed to the function as the sample time.
The next step is to balance the system matrices. Using Matlabs build-in function balreal, a balanced
realization of the state space system is obtained. The coeﬃcients in the system matrices of this new
balanced realization are less ﬂuctuating, making the system easier to implement in LabVIEW, because
a smaller number of bits are needed to represent the matrices. After the balancing, a model reduc-
tion is also carried out. This is done using the build-in Matlab function Reduce. This function uses
the so-called Hankel singular values to determine which states have high energy and which have low.
The function then discards the low energy states [4]. The system is reduced from 36 states to 28 states.
Finally, in LabVIEW the matrices of the LQG controller are implemented using ﬁxed points. There-
fore, the values in the matrices are constrained in Matlab to have the same size and precision as
they will have when they are implemented in LabVIEW. In Matlab the function fi has been used
to transform the matrices into ﬁxed points. Another issue arises when the response from the model
with the controller is simulated in Matlab. In LabVIEW not only the matrices but also the states
and the input and output of the controller are deﬁned as ﬁxed points. The only way to simulate the
response in Matlab with states deﬁned as ﬁxed points is to construct a Simulink model where the
input and output from all matrices are converted into ﬁxed points. Such a Simulink model has been
build and can be seen in Appendix D. Since quite a lot of noise was observed in the results presented
in Section 5, noise is also added to the system in the Simulink model. This is done by generating
a random number between 0 and 1, and multiplying it by the standard deviations for the measured
signals. For this the build-in function rand is used to generate the random number in Matlab.
7.5
Optimization of weight matrices
The terms in the weight matrices R1 and R2, must be manually assigned according to which dynamics
of the system are considered most importance. In general some more speciﬁc dynamics of the system
might be sought. These could be speciﬁc time domain quantities such as settling time, overshoot or
stationary errors.
Therefore, in order to determine the weights in R1 and R2 an optimization problem is deﬁned. The
aim of the optimization task is to minimize the overall control current required by the controller, while
at the same time ensuring a good dynamical response of the system.

properties are evaluated using the response from a step input on ix,B.

solve the constrained optimisation problem. The function uses a direct method to ﬁnd the optimal
solution. This means that the function searches for a solution by using an initial guess of the solution.
therefore, a set of design variables must be given to the function initially. The algorithm iterates
over the quadratic problem stated in Equation (124). At each iteration step, the algorithm updates
the Hessian of the Lagrangian function ∇2L(DV ∗), solves the quadratic problem, and ﬁnds the d in

the previous point. This implies that the direction and the gradient of the function in the point must
have a negative dot product. When the direction has been found, the step size α must be determined.
This can be done by looking for a minimum point along the search direction d. A disadvantage of
using the sqp-algorithm is that it is a local method and therefore in order to ﬁnd a global solution the
initial guess must be close to the global solution [10].

8.5
Touch-down recovery
The overall goal of the control scheme for the rotor bearing system, is to avoid that the rotor ever
comes in contact with the backup bearing, since this would result in an impact where a lot of energy
is dissipated. However, sometimes external forces might cause the control system to fail and the rotor
to touch-down in the backup bearings for a short period of time.
In these cases the control system’s ability to recover the rotor from the backup-bearings is extremely
important. In order to investigate the control system’s ability to recover the rotor during a touch-down
the rotor is excited with a large impulse force over a short period of time. It can then be checked
whether the rotor returns to the centre of the housing and how fast it returns to the centre. On the
given system a large current signal is applied for 0.1 s and the rotor displacement is measured for both
the PID controller and the LQG controller. The results can be seen in Figures 81 and 82.

Discussion
9.1
Test facility
From some of the initial measurements it was found that one of the position sensors had more noise
than the three others. This position sensor has been added more recently due to a failure of one of the
older ones. The extra noise from this sensor was not accounted for when the observer was designed.
The reason for this was that by adding extra noise in the given direction the observer did not seem to
perform well. Therefore, a better performance of the system with the LQG controller could probably
be obtained by replacing this seemingly defect sensor with a sensor which has a lower sensitivity. The
sensitivity of the sensor determines the ratio between the output signal of the sensor and the entity it
measures.
The rotation of the rotor is determined by the pressure of the air which hits the turbine wheel at the
top of the rotor. This solution seem to be very eﬀective and simple in the case where some maximum
rotational speed is desired. However, the rotational speed is limited by the maximum obtainable air
pressure, and it is not possible to control the run-up or run-down of the rotor. Furthermore, it is
hard to obtain the same maximum speed when doing two or more executive experiments with the
rotor rotating. One way of dealing with these problems could be to make a control scheme for the
air supply by adding an electronically controlled proportional pressure regulator. Alternatively, an
electric motor could instead be used to spin the rotor. The installation of an electric motor would,
however, be quite expensive and would require a reconstruction of some of the frames for the test rig.
The maximum speed achieved with the current set up was approximately 10,000 RPM, however, in
earlier projects speeds of up to 31,000 RPM have been reached. Advanced FESS spin with up to
100,000 RPM [2].
In these systems the rotor is often made out of ﬁber glass or other composite
materials which have a high strength-to-weight ratio. Thus, if the system is to rotate a lot faster, the
rotor would have to be redesigned. In [5] it was, furthermore, found that the ﬁrst bending mode of the
rotor is at approximately 1300 Hz and this means that in order to achiever higher speeds i.e. above
78,000 RPM, the machine must be able to cross the critical speed. In general the gyroscopic eﬀect
will increase the natural frequency of the forward whirl of the ﬁrst bending mode as the rotor starts
to spin. This is usually considered the most troublesome vibration form to cross. The actual critical
speed would therefore have to be investigated for example by making a model which have enough dofs
to estimate the ﬁrst bending mode and then use this model to derive the Campbell diagram.
Finally, it was found that the rotor did indeed decelerate quite fast once the air supply was turned of.
In order to further develop the test rig as an actual FESS, it would be necessary to create a vacuum
around the rotor to decrease the power loss in the system.
9.2
Mathematical Model
The results found in this report have proved that the simple mathematical model does indeed capture
most of the important dynamics, which is profoundly important for the LQG controller to perform
well. However, the model did seem to have some limitations since, for example, it is not able to predict
any of the dynamics related to the frame structure of the test rig. In the experimentally obtained
FRFs it was seen that some extra dynamic did indeed appear at lower frequencies, and this dynamic
could very well be related to the frame of the gimbal mount or the housing itself. In fact in [8] a
very elaborate FE-Model of the system with housing and gimbal mount has been derived and the
model has been tested against experimental data. In the article it is found that a total of 7 diﬀerent
natural frequencies are present in the frequency range from 10.10 Hz to 68.87 Hz. The damping and
thus the eﬀect of these lower frequency dynamics is unknown, but from the results obtained in this
project, they seem to have some observable eﬀect as the amplitude of the FRFs had some peaks in
Page 94 of 97
==PAGE:109==

this frequency region .
As already mentioned the model of the rotor itself only has 6 dofs which is not enough to determine
any of the bending modes. It is, however, known that the ﬁrst bending mode is at 1300 Hz, which
means that it will not eﬀect the performance of the control design before a rotational speed at around
this frequency is reached.
The thermodynamic eﬀects have also been left out from the model presented in this project. Since air
is surrounding the rotor and the housing, any heat generated by the AMBs is eﬀectively transferred
away from the system through convection.
9.3
Optimal controller
In this project an optimal controller was successfully designed and implemented. The controller was
based on well-known control design theory and was combined with an optimal observer which led to a
satisfying control design. The ﬁnal control design seemed to have an overall better performance than
the already implemented PID controller both in terms of system dynamics but also in terms of the
actuation eﬀort required by the system. However, the design and implementation of an LQG controller
requires more time than the often used PID controller, due to the fact that an accurate model of the
system must ﬁrst be developed. Often when working with PID controllers it is possible to easily tune
the controller in order to obtain a satisfying dynamic response. Furthermore, the implementation of
the PID controller requires less memory than that of the optimal controller or any full state feedback
controller in general. Therefore, when deciding upon a controller for a speciﬁc control task, it is im-
portant to consider what the purpose of the controller is. If, for example, the system is very simple
and only needs to work around a small operation region, then the PID controller might be a better
option than the LQG controller. On the other hand the LQG controller has some obvious advantages,
such as the fact that it is possible to actively determine which states are of high importance and which
are not. This for example makes it possible to design a controller which is very fast, by allowing more
actuation eﬀort or vice versa.
The gains of the optimal controller designed in this project have been based on an optimization task,
for which the goal was to minimize the actuation eﬀort while the system was still meeting a certain
set of constraints. The constraints and the estimated actuation eﬀort are only based on a single step
input from one of the AMBs, and only while the rotor is not rotating. By simulating the response
of the system with a rotational speed, the gyroscopic eﬀects will be included in the response. The
design variables of the optimization task are chosen such that the weights in R1 were the same for
all four current signals as well as the four integral states. However, the weights for the displacement
and rotation of the CM for the rotor were diﬀerent in the x and the y direction. This resulted in a
an asymmetrical controller, which became evident from the modelled Campbell diagram and modes.
Thus, including the step response for the other three directions would lead to a more symmetrical
control scheme, which would probably also improve the overall performance of the system with the
LQG controller.

10
Conclusion
Throughout this project an optimal controller has successfully been designed, implemented and tested
on a FESS at DTU. In order to design the controller, a model of the rotor and AMBs was devel-
oped and tested. The model proved to be fairly accurate but some small adjustments of the model
parameters was made in order to account for some uncertainties regarding the model parameters, as
well as accounting for some of the dynamics that was otherwise neglected in the model. This was
only possible because a PID controller had already been implemented and could be used for making
some initial testing, which helped validate the model. The dynamics of the rotor itself was modelled
using only six dofs at the CM of the rotor and, thus, it was not possible to estimate any bending modes.
The tests carried out with the LQG controller showed an overall good agreement with the simulated
response in the time domain, but some deviations in the frequency domain. Regardless of the de-
viations between the model and the physical system, the LQG controller was able to keep the rotor
centred at 10,700 RPM, which was the limit of the pneumatic system. The results from the step
excitations, as well as the chirp experiments showed that the system performed better with LQG con-
troller than with the PID controller. For the step responses the amplitude was decreased signiﬁcantly
when the LQG controller was activated. The controller seemed to meet the constraints deﬁned in
optimization task and the overall control current was indeed minimized compared to that required
by the PID controller. The weight matrices used to deﬁne the gain matrices of the LQG controller,
were determined using an optimization task. The optimization task did not include the performance
at diﬀerent rotational speeds, which is otherwise important for the stability of the system. Also, the
optimization algorithm used did not ensure that the global minimum was found, but simply a local
minimum which was dependent on the initial values fed to the optimization function.
In general, a lot of time was put into implementing the controller in LabVIEW. When programming
the FPGA the programme must be recompiled every time a small change has been made. This was
found to be a very big downside of using the FPGA VI of LabVIEW rather than just implementing
the controller in the Real Time VI. Therefore, for future projects it might be worth considering how
fast the control system needs to be and whether or not it would be possible to simply build it in the
Real Time VI.
10.1
Future Aspects
The mathematical model of the system presented in this project only includes the rotor and the
AMBs. However, by including the dynamics of the housing and the gimbal frame it is believed that a
higher accuracy of the system dynamics can be achieved. This would improve the performance of the
controller further. This would, nevertheless, also require a larger control system to be implemented in
the LabVIEW-programme where memory is already a scarce resource. In general the feasibility of a
FESS depends on the power density, which is restricted by the speed of the rotor. One known failure
of FESSs can be assigned the tensile strength of the rotor itself, since the high rotational speed can
cause the rotor to burst. From earlier work it was found that lamination stack on the rotor would
separate from the rotor at around 34,000 RPM, therefore if the system is to ever spin faster the rotor
design must be revised.
The method used for optimizing the weighting matrices of the controller and thereby also the controller
gains, could be reﬁned by including also performance in the frequency domain and by testing out
diﬀerent optimization algorithms, but most importantly the step response in the y direction should
be included in the optimization routine in order to ensure a symmetrical controller. The optimization
method could also be tested for other systems where both performance and power consumption is of
importance when deﬁning a controller.
