Abstract
Hydrogen sulfide is a common issue in sewer systems that can lead to corrosion or
foul odours for nearby areas. To solve these issues, a multitude of solutions are used,
including chemical dosing.
This project is made in collaboration with Iwaki Nordic A/S and aims to develop a new
dosing system to counter hydrogen sulfide development. The goal is to create a system
that will be competitively strong on the market, by covering sewage utility needs not
currently being met.
The project covers analysis of the respective market, including currently available solu-
tions, the market sizing, trends and development, and the needs from different sewage
utilities. Based on the analysis concepts are generated and evaluated to end up with the
best possible product to introduce to the market. The final concept for a product is then
detailed and analysed to evaluate its feasibility.
The results from this project is a product design for a chemical dosing system intended
for placement in areas where it is problematic to connect to the power grid. It is mod-
ulized in order to provide flexibility for setups, dependent on case specific needs, and
optional features such as chemical saving and discretion. The unit is estimated to have
a production price ranging from 32.599DKK to 99.395DKK, depending on the con-
figuration. Based on evaluation, the resulting product is considered feasible, though
further work in the form of prototyping and testing should be conducted before a final
conclusion can be made.

Introduction
Hydrogen sulfide is a colourless, foul-smelling, and toxic gas. It is corrosive to metals,
and under certain circumstances its corrosive nature can effect other materials as well.
Hydrogen sulfide can commonly be found in areas where there is a high concentration of
sulphate and anaerobic conditions. One such area, that can commonly be found around
the world, is in the sewers. Here hydrogen sulfide is a growing issue, both in and near
the sewer. A common method that sewage utilities use for solving these types of issues,
is through the use of chemicals that can counteract or prevent hydrogen sulfide. This
method is considered reliable for dealing with the problems, but has its issues. The de-
velopment of hydrogen sulfide varies over time, but in order to ensure that problems are
covered at all times, sewage utilities will usually dose at a constant level to combat the
worst-case scenario. This leads to overdosing of chemicals in many parts of the sew-
ers, with many sewage utilities knowingly dosing several times the necessary amount of
chemicals.
The 12th UN sustainable development goal regards responsible consumption and pro-
duction. Specifically targets 12.2 and 12.4 state:
12.2: ”By 2030, achieve the sustainable management and efficient use of natural
resources”
12.4: ”By 2020, achieve the environmentally sound management of chemicals and all
wastes throughout their life cycle, in accordance with agreed international
frameworks, and significantly reduce their release to air, water and soil in order to
minimize their adverse impacts on human health and the environment”
Implementing a better dosing system would reduce the overdosing of chemicals. Thereby
helping to achieve the sustainable development goals.
This report regards the growing issue of hydrogen sulfide development in the Danish
sewer systems, the consequences thereof, and the development of a dosing system for
better administering chemicals to counteract it.
Firstly, there will be looked upon the circumstances for hydrogen sulfide development
in the sewers, and the problems arising from its presence. Secondly, the current state-of-
the-art methods used for dealing with hydrogen sulfide, will be explored and examined.
Next, an overview and analysis of the stakeholders relevant to the project will be con-
ducted, and a market analysis will be completed including interviews with the most
relevant stakeholders. The interviews will form the foundation for the current market
needs which again will form the basis of concept generation for a new chemical dosing
system for sewage utilities.
With the customer’s needs identified, a concept generation, selection and detailing phase

1.1
Project aim
The aim of this project is to design a dosing system to dose chemicals into sewer systems
in order to prevent or counteract the creation of hydrogen sulfide in the sewer systems.
The project will focus on dosing stations that will be placed at or near sewer pumping
stations or at the end of pressurised sewer pipes, as these are the areas with the largest
hydrogen sulfide formation. There will be a focus on pumping stations placed in rural
areas where the dosing stations would often have to be out in the open, which creates
more requirements for the dosing system housing.
1.2
Methods
The structure, analyses and practices used throughout this report will be based on the
methods and models from design engineering. Sources especially relied upon are the
structures and methods of Karl T. Ulrich and Steven D. Eppinger[1], as well as the
methods of Eskild Tjalve[2], and models from Marc H. Meyer and Alvin P. Lehnard[3].
The models and conclusions in this report rely mostly on empirical data in the form of
interviews with relevant stakeholders to the market segment or relevant fields of study
and observations made during field trips to facilities facing hydrogen sulfide-related
issues. The report will also include theoretical formulas concerning the formation or
reaction of hydrogen sulfide.

Introduction to the problem
The sewers and the wastewater within, contain many different chemicals, organic ma-
terials and organisms. With these high amounts of different substances, many different
processes can occur in the wastewater. One of the processes is the development of hy-
drogen sulfide (H2S).
On the sewer walls there is a layer of biological material, a so-called biofilm in which
there is a lot of bacterial activity. The bacteria in the biofilm get their energy through
breaking up the organic material in the wastewater and as part of this, the bacteria need
to use oxygen (O2). There are three main sources of oxygen in the sewer. In large parts
of the sewer, such as gravitational piping, there are aerobic conditions, meaning there
is free oxygen available for the bacteria to use. In other parts of the sewers, such as in
pressurised pipelines, there are anaerobic conditions, meaning there is no freely avail-
able oxygen. Under these conditions, the bacteria must obtain the oxygen through the
breakdown of another chemical compound. At first the bacteria will use nitrate (N03)
as its oxygen source, which can be called an anoxic zone, but once the nitrate in the
wastewater has been used the bacteria will instead have to obtain O2 through the de-
composition of sulphate (SO2−
4 ). The three sources of oxygen, when they are used, and
their relation to the formation of hydrogen sulfide can be seen in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Oxygen source for sewer bacteria and hydrogen sulfide formation[4]
Sulphate in the wastewater commonly originates from cleaning products, cosmetic prod-
ucts and other personal care products, but it also naturally occurs in limited amounts
from our drinking water and from infiltrating groundwater. When the bacteria use the
sulphate in the water, it will create a bi-product of sulfide (S2−). Sulfide will be bal-
anced between three forms in the wastewater depending on the pH level, it will occur
as pure sulfide (S2−), hydrosulfide (HS−), or hydrogen sulfide (H2S) as seen in figure
2.2. At a common pH value around 7, the sulfide will be in the form of hydrosulfide and
hydrogen sulfide.
3
==PAGE:14==
Figure 2.2: Sulfide form at different pH levels[5]
The amount of sulphate that will be converted into sulfide will vary based on several
factors. One of the most influential factors is the residence time for the wastewater in
pipes with anaerobic conditions. This is because the longer the wastewater is in anaero-
bic conditions, the more time the bacteria will have to convert the sulphate into sulfide.
Areas with longer pressurised pipelines or a slower flow rate through the pipes, will
therefore often see larger issues with hydrogen sulfide.
A second very influential factor is the size of the pressure pipe. Pipes with a larger
diameter will often have an increased development of hydrogen sulfide. This is due to
the fact that a larger diameter of the pipe leads to a larger surface area inside the pipe.
And this in turn will allow more bacteria to live on the pipe, thereby converting more
sulphate. A larger pipe diameter can also mean that the flow rate through the pipe will
be lower, thereby increasing the residence time.
Lastly, the temperature has an impact on bacterial activity. The rate of hydrogen sulfide
generation increases with 3-5% per degree Celsius the temperature increases[4]. This is
of course a general approximation that does not covers all ranges of temperature, but fits
for generally seen temperatures seen in sewage water over the year. Because develop-
ment of hydrogen sulfide depends on temperature, there is seen more problems during
summer than winter. In many areas the problems may only be significant for half of the
year.
The development of hydrogen sulfide is also a growing issue due to modern implemen-
tation of separate sewerage, where new pipes are implemented specifically for rainwa-
ter. This in turn leads to a lower flow rate in the already existing pipes for wastewater,
thereby further increasing residence time and hydrogen sulfide development.
4
==PAGE:15==
While the wastewater is inside a pressure pipe the sulfide developed within it will be
in a dissolved form in the water. Once the wastewater leaves the anaerobic conditions,
such as by leaving the pressure pipe, the hydrogen sulfide in the water can be released
into the air. At this point it can ventilate around in or out of the sewer system, or react
with new bacteria on the sewer walls and convert into sulfuric acid.
The entire process of hydrogen sulfide formation in the sewers is visualised in figure
2.3.
Figure 2.3: Left: Hydrogen sulfide formation process in anaerobic pipe. Right: Hydro-
gen sulfide release after pressure pipe
The development of hydrogen sulfide can create several different problems in the sewer
system or in the areas close to manholes. The three main problems found in or around
sewers caused by hydrogen sulfide are:
• Creates a smell nuisance for people living close to a sewer outlet.
• Creates a dangerous working environment for sewage workers.
• Corrosion of the concrete sewer piping.
Hydrogen sulfide is a gas with a highly intense smell of rotten eggs. At a concentration
of less than 0.1 ppm (parts per. million) it can be registered by the human sense of smell,
as seen in table 2.1. Because of this hydrogen sulphide can often become a nuisance for
people who live in the vicinity of a sewer well.
Another possibility is for the hydrogen sulfide to stay in the sewers. Hydrogen sulfide

State of the art
In order to begin the development of a competitive product for the market, it is neces-
sary to look at how the problems are being solved currently, what current solutions are
offered, and what the strength and weaknesses of these solutions are. For this reason,
some of the most common methods for dealing with hydrogen sulfide issues will be
presented in this section.
3.1
Chemical dosing
One of the most common and reliable methods for removing or preventing hydrogen
sulfide in sewer systems is through the use of chemicals that can solve hydrogen sulfide
issues.
The general concept of dosing chemicals into the sewers is that chemical reactions can
either prevent the creation of hydrogen sulfide in the system, remove hydrogen sulfide
from the system or immobilize it. Some of the most commonly used chemicals for
hydrogen sulfide issues, will be covered in chapter 3.1.1.
In Denmark chemical dosing is almost exclusively applied at the inlet of the pressure
pipe. Dosing at other points along the sewer system has only been done as tests. When
using chemicals that work as hydrogen sulfide preventives, dosing has to be done at
the inlet, as they have to be added before hydrogen sulfide has formed. For chemicals
that work by removing hydrogen sulfide, there is more flexibility when choosing the
dosing point, as it can be done both before and after hydrogen sulfide has formed. But
if hydrogen sulfide removing chemicals are added before hydrogen sulfide has formed,
it may react with other substances in the wastewater.
The main reason, why dosing systems are commonly placed at the pressure pipe inlet, is
due to convenience. At the pressure pipe inlet, there will be a setup for the sewer pump,
meaning that there is already space for a setup, run electricity to the area and in many
cases a shed has already been established. This simplifies the process of setting up a
chemical dosing system.
8
==PAGE:19==

and chemical tank on the right.
Chemical dosing systems come in varying sizes. Depending on the scale of hydrogen
sulfide issues, dosing can be seen in the range of 1L of chemicals a day to 170L a day.
Some systems are made to run only during the summer when the issues are the largest
while other systems are meant to run all year round. The scale and method of chemical
storage also varies. Most commonly used is either a replaceable IBC tank, holding 1m3,
or a permanently installed chemical tank varying in size, but usually in the range of 2m3

to 5m3. Different scales such as drums containing 200L or larger tanks containing 15m3
can also be found (Appendix A).
The dosing systems can also be set up with different rules for when to dose. The sim-
plest of systems run constantly, dosing a small quantity of chemical at all times. These
systems are usually not popular as they will concentrate a lot of chemical in stagnant
water before the wastewater is pumped and the chemical is mixed in. Slightly smarter
systems will only be activated when the sewage water is pumped into the pressure pipe.
The amount of chemical can then also be fixed or variable. Fixed dosing will pump at
the same flow at all times when the dosing pump is running. Variable dosing will be
dependent on variable factors. Most commonly the factor will be a time schedule that
lowers or increases the dosing to follow an expected pattern for hydrogen sulfide lev-
els throughout the day/week/month/year. Other factors may also be information such
as weather forecasts or wastewater temperature. Slightly more expensive systems may
also get variable information from sensors such as hydrogen sulfide detection sensors.
Such systems are not very common due to two factors. Firstly, they are expensive to set
up. Secondly, feedback time from sensors can be several hours, meaning the data will
not be reliable for correct dosing. This is because measurements must be taken at the
end of pressurised pipes, which can be several kilometers long, while dosing occurs at
the inlet to the pipe. Chemical dosing systems are generally regarded as highly reliable
systems. Depending on need, several different chemicals can be taken into use and dos-
ing systems can easily be scaled up or down to fit the size of the problem.
A negative side of using a chemical dosing system is that the chemicals used are not
environmentally friendly. On top of that, it is very difficult to precisely estimate the
needed amount of chemicals to solve hydrogen sulfide issues. This often leads to an
overdosing of chemicals, which is both environmentally unsafe, wasteful and costly.
Chemical dosing is also a solution with a permanent cost after implementation.
Chemical dosing systems can vary in cost depending on size, components, and func-
tionality. But prizing can also vary based on the how tailored the solution has to be for
the specific case, or the amount of consulting is included in the solution. Price ranges
and descriptions of services included at these prices can be categorised as:
• 14.000 DKK to 50.000 DKK - This will be the cost range for a wastewater utility
company wanting to obtain the needed components to build the system them-

cluding a pump or a control system. In the mid-range the pump may be included
and perhaps a PLC to calculate remaining chemicals and to create simple logic
pattern’s for the dosing system. In the high end, there may instead be used large

is not included in this price.

3.1.1
Chemicals
There are a lot of different chemicals that can be used to combat hydrogen sulfide in
sewer systems. The principle of how the chemicals work can generally be split into two
categories, preventives and correctives. Preventive chemicals work by stopping hydro-
gen sulfide from forming, while the corrective chemicals work by removing or limiting
the effects of the hydrogen sulfide after it has already formed.
The following sections will cover two preventive chemical solutions: ”Addition of ni-
trate” and ”Alkaline shock treatment”. And three corrective solutions:”Chemical oxi-
dants”, ”Iron salts” and ”pH elevation”.
Addition of nitrate
As sulphides are only formed due to bacteria in the sewer using sulfate for its oxygen
source, a solution to the problem is to prevent the bacteria from using the sulfate.
As explained in section 2, the sewer bacteria will only use sulfate as a source of oxygen
once there is no available oxygen or nitrate present. Therefore, by strategically adding
nitrate to the system in anaerobic areas, it is possible to prevent the bacteria from con-
verting sulfate into sulphide. A positive side effect of dosing nitrate into the sewer, is
the control of odor nuisances from sources other than hydrogen sulfide. This is because
nitrate limits the formation of sulfur compounds and fatty acids.[4]
A problem with nitrate is its ability to break down organic matter. As at the treatment
plant, there is a planned organic composition meant to help in the cleaning process of
the wastewater. If too much nitrate reaches the treatment plant it can disrupt the envi-
ronment in the treatment plant, causing problems. Overdosing of nitrate can therefore
be very problematic.
11
==PAGE:22==
Figure 3.2: Sewer bacteria’s oxygen source and hydrogen sulfide formation when nitrate
is dosed into the system[4]
Alkaline shock treatment
Another way to stop the formation of hydrogen sulfide is by reducing the amount of
sulfate-reducing bacteria in the sewer. One method for killing these bacteria is through
shock treatment.
Shock treatment works by utilizing a pulse of a strong alkaline solution, raising the pH
to around 12 for 1 hour, thereby killing the bacteria in the biofilm. This will temporarily
cease bacterial activity in the sewers for a varying time, often ranging from a couple of
days to 2 weeks [7]. A drawback of this method is that the treatment plant needs the
wastewater to be slightly acidic for the purification processes to work. This means that
the wastewater containing the alkaline solution has to be isolated at the plant and then
slowly added to the normal subsequent flow to dilute the alkaline solution and lower the
pH back to normal levels.
Chemical oxidants
A way of removing sulfide after it has already developed in the water is by adding strong
oxidants to the water. This could for example be hydrogen peroxide, hypochlorite,
ozone or pure oxygen. An oxidant such as pure oxygen would be quite expensive to
use, whereas hydrogen peroxide is less expensive.
The process works by oxidising the created sulfide in the water into either sulfur or
sulfate. The reaction when using hydrogen peroxide would be[8]:
HS−+H+ +H2O2 →S0 +2H2O
The process only works once the sulfide has been created in the water, so it has to be
dosed late in the process. At the same, time the process can take a long time depending
on the chemical used, hydrogen peroxide for example can take 15-30 minutes to react,
though it can be reduced by the addition of a catalyst[5].
Iron salts
Another way of removing existing sulfide in the water is through the use of chemical
precipitation. This is mainly done using iron salts specifically iron(II) or iron(III).
12
==PAGE:23==
While HS−or H2S is in the water phase it will bind to the iron salt and precipitate. This
will result in less or no hydrogen sulfide in gas form at the outlet of the pressure piping.
The method is simple to implement and effective at preventing the release of hydrogen
sulfide in its gas form[4].
A problem with this solution is that the efficiency of dosing iron salts decreases signif-
icantly once the pH of the water is under 7, as it only reacts with sulfide in the form of
HS−. At the same time, the iron salts are acidic when added and will therefore lower
the pH of the water when added. The pH of the water will therefore have to be balanced
with other types of chemicals to keep the iron salts efficient. Furthermore, the biproduct
of sludge from the precipitation will have to be removed or the sewer will clog up over
time, obstructing the flow and potentially damaging equipment such as pumps[4].
Figure 3.3: Sewer bacteria’s oxygen source and hydrogen sulfide formation when iron
salts are dosed into the system[4]
pH elevation
By adding bases and acids to the wastewater it is possible to manipulate the pH of the
wastewater. It is possible to control the form of sulfide developed in the water, prevent-
ing it from becoming hydrogen sulfide and being released into the air, as seen in figure
2.2. This will give the sulfide time to react with the available oxygen and oxidise back
to sulfate thus stopping it from turning into hydrogen sulfide.
The method requires a large amount of base to effectively work, but assuming the base
comes as a waste product from a nearby industry, this method can be worth it.
One problem with the method is that if the pH value lowers later downstream, then the
remaining sulfide will develop into hydrogen sulfide at this point thus creating problems
there. This area further down the stream might be more difficult to maintain than the
original point. One way to solve this is to strategically lower the pH in certain areas
where maintenance is easier. Thereby moving the corrosion issue to a less critical point,
but not solving the issue.
The handling of strong bases is also problematic when it comes to the work environ-
ment, as it can be quite dangerous[5].

3.1.2
Pumping
To safely handle reactive chemicals, it is necessary to have a pump that ensures no
leakage, can handle the strong chemicals, and ensures precise dosing of the chemical.
A typical method to ensure precise dosing is the usage of a metering pump. This is
a general term for pumps designed for precise dosing. Metering pumps comes in in
many forms. There will here be looked at diaphragm pumps using electromagnetic or
motor-driven pumping.
Membrane metering pumping The concept of metering pumping with a membrane is
that it is easy to determine the amount of dosed chemical based on the movement of
a membrane and thereby the displaced amount of liquid. How it general works is that
there will be a check valve at the inlet of the pump ensuring that liquid can only enter,
and another check valve at the outlet ensuring that liquid can only exit. A membrane is
then moved back and forth to force liquid in from the inlet and push it out of the outlet.
The principle can also be achieved through other means than the use of a membrane, but
membranes are often practical as they can be tightly connected to ensure no chemical is
leaked into other parts of the pump.
Motor-driven metering pumps Motor-driven pumps are pumps where the membrane
motion is controlled by the usage of a motor. This is the method that has been around
the longest and it is popular due to its simplicity. This type of pump often has simple
controls and can provide a very consistent dosing output.
However, the disadvantage of a motor driven-pump is the motor itself. Hazardous con-
ditions such as high moisture or strong chemical fumes can damage the motor, causing
failure.
Electromagnetic metering pumps Electromagnetic pumps use a solenoid to accurately
move the membrane back and forth. The electromagnetic pump system often has more
advanced controls than motor-driven pumps and is also able to deliver even more ac-
curate output. The rated capacity divided by the minimum capacity while maintaining
specified flow repeatedly can be found as 10:1, 100:1 or even 1000:1.
Electromagnetic pumps are often slightly more expensive than motor-driven pump types,
but they are also better at handling hazardous environments due to not having a motor.

3.1.3
Chemistry storage
The handling and storing of chemicals in Denmark are regulated by the Danish Envi-
ronmental Protection Agency and municipalities, and therefore requirements are not all
uniform. In general, several measures are required when chemicals are stored, mainly
to contain possible spillage from the surrounding environment.
Storing indoors, the flooring under the storage container must be sealed and resistant to
the substances. Additionally, a spill tray has to be installed with a minimum capacity
of the largest chemical container stored. Alternatively a double-walled container can be
used. The purpose being to capture spillage from entering drains, sewers or the general
environment.
Outdoors the same requirement still applies, but the chemical containers are also re-
quired to be covered and shielded from the weather. Access to the storage must also be
limited to authorized personnel only, and measures must be taken to secure the container
against collisions and general vandalism.
Lastly, chemicals must only be stored in their original container or an authorized con-

3.2
Removal of the biofilm
Hydrogen sulfide in the sewer systems is developed by bacteria in the biofilm on the
sewer walls. Therefore, removing the biofilm results in a diminishing of bacteria and
thereby a reduction in the production of hydrogen sulfide.
Removal of biofilm can be done in different ways. The most common method is pigging,
where a sewer pig is pushed through the pipe system by the water flow and used to clear
the pipe of residue and bacteria growing on the walls. This method is highly popular as
it is used for cleaning the pipes regardless of the necessity of reducing hydrogen sulfide.
The benefit of reducing hydrogen sulfide is therefore just a bonus to an already common
operation.
The sewer pig mechanism comes in many forms. The most basic mechanisms operate
by utilizing the compression of water around the pig to pressure wash the pipe, or by
simply pushing forward the deposits of waste in front of the unit. Other mechanisms
implement brushes, scrapes or special shapes to effectively clean certain residues or
pipe issues.
17
==PAGE:28==
Figure 3.6: Illustration of pig cleaning principle
In order to use a sewer pig, the piping must have an input point implemented at the start
of the piping stretch that should be cleaned. Once the cleaning pig is in the pipeline,
the processing time for cleaning depends on the length of pipe it has to clean, but it will
generally take several hours. The process of pig cleaning use a lot of workforce. First,
a worker will have to go out to send the pig through the system. Afterwards, the worker
will have to periodically check if the pig has come through the system as the traveling
time of the pig is not always consistent. All in all, a lot of work is used throughout
this cleaning method. Another disadvantage of the method is that the process has to
be repeated regularly to consistently combat hydrogen sulfide. As often as every week
in some cases. The method also risks clogging the system in case of a failure[4]. The
positive side of this method is that it does have to be completed nonetheless to clean
the pipe of other deposits, though not as regularly as when combating H2S. The initial
investment of an access point for the pig varies from 20.000 DKK to 200.000 DKK
depending on sewer size and whether it is being implemented while the piping system
is built or added later on. The cleaning pigs vary in price by type, but the can be seen
a general cost around 1800 DKK per pig. A pig will last for 1-5 uses depending on the
pipe condition and waste build-up (Appendix B.8).
3.3
Compressed air
As the development of hydrogen sulfide in wastewater occurs when wastewater is under
anaerobe conditions, one solution that reduces the development of hydrogen sulfide is
to introduce additional oxygen into these pipe stretches. This can be done using an
air compressor and simply injecting air into pressurized pipe systems along with the
wastewater. Injecting compressed air into the pipe system has two main benefits. Firstly
the added air will let the microorganisms consume oxygen instead of sulfur, and thus
produce less hydrogen sulfide, see figure 3.7.
18
==PAGE:29==
Figure 3.7: The effect on the hydrogen sulfide development then injecting air later into
the pipe system
The other added benefit is that the added pressure from the air will force the wastewater
to pass through the pressurised pipe much faster, thus reducing residence time in the
pipe and thereby the time in which hydrogen sulfide can be produced[4].
One of the downsides of adding air is that bubbles can form at elevated points in the
pipe system and inhibit the flow of wastewater. Whether this occurs highly depends
on the specific pipe systems, layout, and elevation, affected by e.g. the terrain. An-
other restricting factor is the amount of oxygen being dissolved into the wastewater and
therefore its effect, which depends on the current pressure, temperature and whether it
is atmospheric air or pure oxygen, that is being injected. [5]
A new method within the application of compressed air against hydrogen sulfide, is the
use of nanobubbles. With an average diameter of 80-120 nm, the bubbles obtain a nat-
ural buoyancy and can remain in the wastewater for several hours, following the water
flow without collecting at the surface. The small size dramatically increases the surface
area, which leads to a more effective transfer of oxygen to the water, making them much
more efficient for stopping hydrogen sulfide formation[10].
This method of utilizing air is new on the danish market, but is in use in other countries
around the world. The price for this new solution is approximated 175.000-250.000DKK
(Appendix B.9).
3.4
Filters
Once hydrogen sulfide has formed and released in gas form the preventive measures
mentioned earlier would no longer be effective. One way of solving or reducing the
hydrogen sulfide problems at this point would be to filter out the hydrogen sulfide gas.
There exists a lot of different types of filters that can help in removing hydrogen sulfide
from the air. The most common solutions on the Danish market are carbon filters/hybrid
19
==PAGE:30==
filters used for small-scale filtration, or so-called passive filters used for large-scale fil-
tration.
3.4.1
Carbon filters / Hybrid filters
Carbon filters or hybrid filters are small filters that do not take up too much space and
can be implemented at wells where there are complaints of foul smells caused by hy-
drogen sulfide. In order to get the best results possible, the setup will often have a fan
forcing the air and gas through the filter.
Filters use bio-filtration as the means of removing hydrogen sulfide. The design of a
filter usually consists of layers of carbon. On this carbon, there is living biomass con-
sisting of hydrogen sulfide converting bacteria. The method prevents hydrogen sulfide
odour issues, by having the microorganisms in the biomass convert the hydrogen sulfide
and thereby remove the gas. An advantage of using these carbon- or hybrid-filters, is
the low maintenance and the simplicity of the solution[11].
The disadvantage with filters is their limitations. When hydrogen sulfide is oxidised in
the filter it will produce sulfuric acid and sometimes sulfur. The acidity of sulfuric acid
lowers the efficiency of the microorganisms. Furthermore, the sulfuric acid can still
end up in the sewers and damage the pipelines[11]. The filters are also limited in how
much hydrogen sulfide they can filter out and therefore large scale problems may not be
possible to solve with such filters.
Hybrid filter installation costs around 35.000DKK. The filter itself costs roughly 3.000DKK.
Additional needs such as ventilation may be required. The lifetime of a filter depends
on the amount of hydrogen sulfide in the system. For a small-scale issue, the lifetime
can be 2-3 years, but for areas with higher hydrogen sulfide concentration the lifetime
may only be 6 months (Appendix B.10).

3.4.2
Passive filters
For more intense hydrogen sulfide problems it is possible in some cases to implement a
more extensive filtration solution, a so-called passive filter. Passive filters are large-scale
constructions that stand overground. Air and gas from the sewer are ventilated into the
filter over a long section of the sewer, using large ventilation fans over ground. This
ensures that all hydrogen sulfide has been released into gas form and can be ventilated
21
==PAGE:32==
into the filter. The large filter can be filled with different filter materials, this material
could be bark, carbon, dirt, seashells or some other material that the hydrogen sulfide
can bind to or react with. A popular material is seashells as it is a cheap component to
buy and reacts easily with the hydrogen sulfide due to the large surface area.
The advantage of this type of filter is the large scale of hydrogen sulfide it can handle.
As the ventilation setup often stretches over a longer section of the sewer it can remove
the hydrogen sulfide that releases further down the well. The running cost for the filter
is relatively low in relation to the effect.
The issue with this solution is the setup itself. The large-scale filtration takes up a lot
of room overground and can therefore not be implemented in many areas such as cities
where odor become the biggest issue. Passive filters are also quite expensive to set up
due to the large scale and need for ventilation. A passive filter installation costs around
150.000DKK to implement (Appendix: B.11).

3.5
Pipe encapsulation
Nowadays, when new sewer piping is being laid, the piping is made of plastics such
as PE in order to avoid corrosion. But digging up old piping and laying new PE pipes
would be an expensive and time-consuming project. To lengthen the lifetime of old
22
==PAGE:33==
pipes, they can instead be encapsulated and isolated from contact to hydrogen sulfide.
The encapsulation can be executed in different ways, firstly it can be done through the
method of sliplining, a method where the piping is covered by a membrane or a plastic
barrier, usually of GAP or PE. Another way is by coating the piping with materials that
are resistant to corrosion from sulfuric acid. The most common material used for this
is epoxy, but experiments have also been made with water glass. However, studies with
this type of coating have not shown significantly lifetime improving results[5].
Encapsulating the pipe is an efficient method for local protection of the pipe against cor-
rosion due to hydrogen sulfide. But in order for it to work, it has to be done thoroughly.
Even a small hole in the coating, membrane or barrier will let hydrogen sulfide come
through and the corrosion of the pipe will continue.
Encapsulating the pipes also does not deal with the hydrogen sulfide in the system. As
the hydrogen sulfide is not able to react with the pipes it will become more concentrated.
It will then either exit the sewers, creating odor issues for the nearby surroundings. Or it
can flow along in the sewer until it reaches a point where the piping is no longer encap-
sulated. The hydrogen sulfide in the system therefore still has to be dealt with by other
means.
The process of encapsulating the sewer piping can be very expensive, especially for
longer stretches. Prices vary by a lot of factors, one of the most important factors be-
ing the diameter of the piping. A rough estimate is that it will cost 5DKK per mm of
pipe diameter per meter of piping that has to be covered. A pipe with a diameter of
200mm thus costs 1000DKK/m and a pipe with a diameter of 1000mm costs roughly
5000DKK/m (Appendix: B.12). The procedure would also have additional costs de-
pending on additional needs. This could for example be cuts in the lining for inflows.
It is therefore not a procedure that is done without being certain that it will increase the
piping lifetime significantly.

3.6
Sacrificial piping
Another way to deal with hydrogen sulfide is through the opposite method to encapsu-
lating the piping. Instead of ensuring that the hydrogen sulfide cannot react with the
piping, it is instead encouraged to react with the pipe. To do this, a so-called sacrificial
pipe is laid as the first section after the anaerobic piping sections. When hydrogen sul-
fide then releases it reacts with the piping, corroding it, and thus the hydrogen sulfide is
removed from the sewer.
Sacrificial pipes are pipes designed specifically to be sacrificed to corrosion. The sac-
rificial pipes are made thicker than normal sewer pipes, in order to last longer from the
corrosion. At the same time, the piping is often made of concrete with an increased
content of calcium or marble[5]. This is to increase the reaction with hydrogen sulfide
and ensure as much of it has reacted as possible.
The sacrificial piping lives longer than normal piping would due to the increased wall
thickness, but it will still corrode and will need to be replaced after a number of years.
Because of the planned need to replace the pipe the piping is only used in areas where
there is easy access to replacing it. This means that this method is not implemented in
highly inhabited areas, such as cities or suburban housing areas. In these areas, it would
be too costly to periodically dig up and replace the pipe.
To implement a sacrificial pipe there needs to be a sewer well at both ends, preferably
in plastic as there will be released a lot of hydrogen sulfide. A plastic well costs roughly
20.000DKK. The sacrificial pipe’s price will vary in price based on size and length. The
most common sizes vary from a diameter of 300mm to 600mm and a pipe length of 2m-
2,25m pr. pipe. In these sizes, the pricing for the pipe will be in the range of 1.800DKK
to 5.900DKK. Commonly there will be a need for at least 50m of piping, but in some
cases the piping can be much longer. Based on the mentioned prices the cost of a 50m
pipe can be calculated to be between 40.000DKK and 130.000DKK (Appendix: B.13).
Besides there will also be the added costs of digging work, covering it afterward, and
similar necessary tasks.
3.7
Sensor technology for hydrogen sulfide
In order to properly counteract hydrogen sulfide, it is necessary to have a reliable indi-
cation of the concentration of hydrogen sulfide in the sewer. This can be done in many
ways such as looking at corrosion levels, doing pH testing, etc. But for a more auto-
mated setup it is possible to use sensory technology for measuring the hydrogen sulfide
levels. Sensors come with mainly two types of measurements. Either of hydrogen sul-
fide in gas form or of dissolved hydrogen sulfide in the sewage water.
24
==PAGE:35==
Gas measurement
The most common way of measuring hydrogen sulfide in the sewer is simply to measure
the concentration of hydrogen sulfide gas in the air. Gas sensors are simple to implement
and there are several good options on the market to choose from.
An issue with gas sensors is that the readings can vary based on the conditions in the
sewers. The ventilation in the sewers for example can change significantly over time
and can result in a change in the readings from the gas sensor, therefore making it hard
to ensure optimal readings and obtain reliable data[6].
An average price estimate for a gas sensor is 18.000DKK (Appendix: B.1). Generally
gas sensors will have to be calibrated twice a year.
Figure 3.11: Gaslogger hanging from sewer well
Water measurement
Measurements of the hydrogen sulfide in the sewers can also be done by applying sen-
sors in the wastewater. In this case, the sensors track the amount of dissolved hydrogen
sulfide specifically.
The concentration of dissolved hydrogen sulfide is often very uniform in the wastewater
as it has had time for mixing in the pipes. This means the measuring results are very
consistent and precise, leading to better data to determine proper dosing of chemicals to
counteract the hydrogen sulfide development.
In the wastewater dissolved sulfide is commonly found in the two forms, H2S or HS−.
Water sensors only measure the concentration of H2S. It is however possible to calculate
the concentration of HS−based on the pH value of the water. H2S is developed at acidic
25
==PAGE:36==
pH values and HS−is developed at alkaline pH values. This means that a water sensor
will only give useful data at pH values under 8 as otherwise, the H2S concentration will
be too small for the sensor to give proper estimations of the sulfide[6].
Water sensors are a unique product with a small number of suppliers. The only found
supplier of this sensor type is Sulfilogger. The cost of a Sulfilogger sensor varies de-
pending on needed specifications and additional equipment. The sensor itself will cost
35.000DKK. With a hanging setup and an online system, it costs 55.000DKK. The Sul-
filogger needs calibration every 3 months. Calibration equipment and calibration gas
cost an average of 1.850DKK/year. If the Sulfilogger is running on batteries the battery
life is also expected to be 3 months. Batteries cost roughly 2.160DKK/year (Appendix:
B.2).

5.1
Market description
Market size
In Denmark there are 98 sewage utility companies[13], which primarily overlook, oper-
ate and maintain the sewer infrastructure and treatment plants in their respective areas.
These companies are municipally owned, but run as individual companies independent
of each other. Their yearly budget is set by the municipalities which they cover and it is
regulated by these every year.
The market in focus for this project consists of these sewage utilities, specifically focus-
ing on the departments that work with overlooking and maintaining the piping systems.
In 2020, the total length of sewer pipes owned by Danish sewage utilities was esti-
mated to be 83.400km. Along this pipe system an estimated 21.553 pump stations are
currently running, together with an estimated 16.158 household pumps[13]. The num-
ber of household pumps is however questionable as two utilities located in Odense and
Ringsted, alone have a combined 3.700 household pumps, making the number 16.158
seem like a small number for the entire 98 utilities[14](Appendix A.3).
Currently, hydrogen sulfide development is not a problem at every pump station. How-
ever every pump station leaves potential for hydrogen sulfide problems to develop. For
household pumps it is usually not possible to set anything up at the pressure pipe inlets
as it is private land. At the outlet, where several pressure pipes from household pumps
may meet, there can be a need for a solution against hydrogen sulfide. Over time the
need for solutions is also likely to change due to separate sewerage, plastic piping and
other factors.
Market covered
The degree to which solutions have been implemented to combat hydrogen sulfide on
the market is highly varying between different sewage utilities. Hydrogen sulfide issues
are still a somewhat stigmatised subject among sewage utilities, and not everyone wants
31
==PAGE:42==
to speak up about it. This is part of the reason why solutions are not always imple-
mented. The hydrogen sulfide focus is also quite new for those utilities that are trying to
solve it. Many solutions are therefore still being tested and are only implemented when
problems become large enough.
Based on data from sewage utilities interviewed the estimated amount of pressure pipes
already installed with chemical dosing units are a little over 3%, as visualized in figure
5.1.

A.

solution. Many areas will also have other solutions in use, though solutions such as

installed.
Based on the data it can be seen that there is still plenty of areas in which solutions are
still desired and where there may be a need for a new solution.
Trends and development
In order to better understand the future needs, it is important to look into the direction
which the market is currently going.
From the perspective of methods and standards used in the sewage utility market, changes
can be seen. When new piping is laid for sewage there are two big changes that can be
observed. Firstly sewage is being separated between wastewater and rainwater. This can
lead to a lower flow in the wastewater pipes, this in turn leads to a higher development
of hydrogen sulfide. The market for hydrogen sulfide solutions is therefore expanding
32
==PAGE:43==
as sewage is separated over the country. The separation have been increasing over the
years as seen in figure 5.2.
Figure 5.2: Area, in %, of danish sewers that have separated wastewater and rainwater
over the years[13]
Another development when laying new piping, is that pipes are made out of plastics such
as PVC, PE or PP, instead of concrete which has been the common method previously.
Plastic pipes are unlikely to corrode from hydrogen sulfide, and focus on corrosive is-
sues therefore decrease in newer pipelines. In turn problems with odour can increase,
meaning that there will be a shift towards focusing on these issues. Smaller sewage
utilities are also being shut down and taken over by larger utilities that can run more
efficiently, and with higher quality. When utilities are centralised like this, new pump
stations are set up to pump sewage from the old utility to the new one. This makes new
long pressure pipe stretches in which hydrogen sulfide can form, and creates need for
more solutions to the problem.
From an economic perspective there can also be a continuing development for the
sewage utilities. Each year sewage utilities are given a lower budget for handling the
maintenance of the sewer system in their jurisdiction, as per Danish water sector law §6
pt 5[15]. The change is around 2% per year, and is implemented to enforce streamlining
among sewage utilities (Appendix A.2). The change in the operations costs for sewage
utilities over the years can be seen in figure 5.3. Because of this budget change, the
utilities have to find way to optimize their systems or cut something off in order to save
money every year. Processes with a running cost such as chemical dosing are therefore
areas in which optimization can lead to some of these savings.

From an image perspective there is in general a goal among many markets to be more
environmentally friendly, this is also a goal among utility companies. The overdosing
of chemicals is therefore an issue when it comes to achieving this image. Some utilities
have even taken a stand completely against the use of chemicals in the pursuit to be
more environmentally friendly.
In relation to the environment focus, a general trend in Denmark is saving water in
everyday use. This is for example done through reducing amounts of water used when
flushing, showering, cleaning and more. A consequence of this is further reduction in
sewage flow, and increase in hydrogen sulfide development.
Market economy
In 2020 the Danish sewage utilities spent a total of about 5.38 billion DKK on invest-
ment and renovations, and the operation costs were estimated at 2.93 billion DKK[13].
Part of operational costs are the chemical amounts used. The amount of chemicals dosed
varies highly between systems. But dosing amounts can be found around 360L per
month per dosing station(Appendix A). Assuming iron chloride is dosed and is bought
as IBC tanks this gives a monthly cost in chemicals per dosing station of 1.828DKK, or
a yearly cost of 21.946DKK. As more and more dosing stations are set up to counteract
hydrogen sulfide, the running cost can rapidly add up.
Legislation within the market
In section 3.1.3, it was covered what laws surround the subject of storing chemicals. But
there should also be covered the laws surrounding the handling and dosing of chemicals
into the sewer. This however is in fact a very short point as there is a lack of proper
legislation for the subject. Through contact with sewage utilities (Appendix A) and the
danish ministry of environment (Appendix B.15), it was noticed that there are no laws
about limits of hydrogen sulfide in the sewer or maximal allowed dosing amounts. The
only rule is that the safety data sheets for the chemical must be followed. This data sheet
covers the handling of chemicals, but not the amount allowed to be dosed in the sewers.
To further increase a need for more efficient dosing systems, regulations for the allowed
dosing amounts would be beneficial.

Kalundborg sewage utility (Appendix A.1)
Kalundborg utility has 6 sewage treatment plants and 284 pump stations under their

Kalundborg utility only uses iron chloride at their dosing sites. The main reason for this

where the chemicals are dosed at a constant level whenever the sewage pump is running.

at the other 3.
Kalundborg utility does not have any hydrogen sulfide sensors installed in their sys-
tems. In order to check for hydrogen sulfide issues they will listen to odour complaints
and check pipe stretches for signs of corrosion. Based on this the regulation of dosing

6.2.2
Concepts
Based on the solutions for the sub-problems determined earlier, concepts were generated
by combining solutions in different ways. Not all solutions were used as they were either
too detailing for the current stage, because certain solutions were unrealistic, or because
they were objectively worse than other solutions, without adding any other functionality.
Based on the mixing of solutions, 9 different concepts were generated.
General design for generated concepts
Certain sub-problems were solved in the same way in many solutions. As not to repeat
the reasoning for a certain choice several times, the following design choices are general
in each concept unless otherwise mentioned in the description.
• Solar cells[16] - Every concept utilizes solar cells as the source of energy. De-
pending on the components in the concept, and the expected seasons of operation,
the size of these solar cells will vary. All solar cell choices are made using an
assumption that the solar cell will be facing directly south with an angle of 25
degrees. At these specifications a 100W solar panel at 100% efficiency produces
425Wh in a day.
In the summer season, the lowest seen efficiency of a solar cell is 80%.
In the common season for highest H2S problems(Appendix A.3), Marts- October
henceforth described as spring-fall, the efficiency can drop to 25%.
In the winter the solar cell efficiency can fall as low as 10%.
• Battery[17] - To ensure operation all day and night, independent of current solar
power, and to ensure the system still runs on days with no sun at all, every con-
cepts also include batteries. The needed battery life is determined based on the
season in which the unit is designed to operate, and the expected days without
48
==PAGE:59==
solar power in these seasons.
In the summer season, it is expected that there can be up to two days without solar
power.
In the spring/fall, the expected number of days without solar power is 4.
In the winter, the expected time without solar power is 2 weeks.
• Sulfilogger - To better optimize the amount of chemicals dosed it has been cho-
sen to implement new sensor technology in all solutions unless otherwise men-
tioned. Specifically there is used a sulfilogger sensor to more precisely measure
the amount of H2S in the sewage and more accurately dose chemicals based on
that.
• Communication - For most concepts it has been decided that information on the
product should be send to the sewage utilities, such as pump running information
and remaining chemical amount. All concepts therefore have a communication
device for sending information but not receiving it.
• Various components - All concepts also contain various needed components such
as valves, pipes, electrical wires, solar charging regulators, power supplies and
likewise.
Concept 1 - The year-round dosing system
The first concept developed is intended for large scale problems, or problems that need
to be combated all year long. Because it is meant for substantial problems, it is assumed
that a large amount of chemicals will have to be dosed. Because of this, the chemical
tank installed in the unit is a 3.5m3 tank. The tank will be permanently installed and is
supposed to be refilled when more chemical is needed.
The solar cells and batteries in this concept are selected based on the needed specifica-
tions to run during winter. Not only does this mean that the batteries will need to be
large, but they will also need to be frost-proof with their own heating system.
For installation in the sewer, this concept is designed to ”close off” the well by extend-
ing the pressure pipe all the way to the gravitational pipe, with pressure fits in both
ends. On this extension there will be interfaces for installation of a sulfilogger sensor, a
pressure sensor and a hose connection for the dosing of chemicals. This closing of the
well serves to ensure the best possible data results from the sensors, good mixing of the
chemical with the sewage, and to limit contact with sewage during maintenance when
entering the well.
Concept 2 - The stylish and easy maintenance system
Concept 2 aims to simplify parts of the setup, configuration and maintenance of the
unit. Simultaneously the concepts have been given some features meant to improve the
appearance of the product, and thereby make it easier to place it in public space, as it is

more stylistic.
To make the design more stylistic the product has plastic panels with wooden styling on
the outside meant to give the product some depth, texture, and to cover the black colors
of the PE plastic.
To keep service easy during chemical refill, a fixed installation of a fillable 1m3 tank is
installed. There will be a filling point on the outside of the shed for refill from a tanker.
Refillable tanks are easier than replaceable ones as there is no need to open the shed and
work with the piping when replacing the chemical.
To simplify the configuration and maintenance it is intended to apply two-way com-
munication to the unit. Thereby allowing to check in and make simple configuration
adjustments to the unit online. To improve the setup off the unit there is implemented a
pipe extension unit for the well. The extension can be inserted into the pressure pipes
outlet using a pressure fit and can easily be inserted or removed. The extension will have
fittings for a sulfilogger and the dosing hose, allowing easier insertions of the sensor and
dosing hose.
Concept 3 - The general dosing system
Concept 3 is meant to be an average system to solve hydrogen sulfide problems. It has

The most commonly seen dosing setups from sewage utilities contain an IBC tank meant

is collected in one unit, and the self sufficient functionalities given by solar panels and

Concept 8 - The cheap flow measuring dosing system
Like with the previous concept, concept 8 also aims is to keep the product cheap, but for
this solution it is not wished to have constant dosing. To achieve this, the concept uses
a flow sensor in the form of a radar sensor hanging at the pressure pipe outlet, to detect
when there is flow. This sensor only measure the vertical distance meaning it can detect
the water level in the well. An increase in the water level would imply sewage flow, and
can therefore be used as an ”on” signal for the dosing.
The concept is intended to operate from the spring to fall, which both the battery pack
and solar cells are scaled for. And it utilises an IBC tank fitting for the problem sizes

already has implemented functions for simple sensor control. Without a communication
unit the system will not be able to send data on remaining chemical. The amount of
chemical in the unit will therefore have to be estimated by the sewage utility. This can
be done as the dosing pump is set to pump at a constant level, and only while there is
flow. There will only be flow when the sewage pump at the pump station is running, and
the sewage utilities already have data on this. Based on this, it should be possible for
the sewage utility to calculate how much chemical is used. The dosing hose is intended
to hang freely in the well.
Concept 9 - The cheap regulated dosing system
The last concept, concept 9, is intended to keep the costs low but at the same time retain
a sensor for measuring the H2S levels in the sewer. This is to remain capable of scaling
the dosing to the current H2S levels. To do this the choice of sensor is changed from a
Sulfilogger capable of measuring directly in the sewage to a gas-logger measuring the
released H2S in the air. Gas-loggers are cheaper compared to the Sulfilogger, but are not
expected to measure the H2S levels as precisely.
To ensure that the necessary amounts of chemicals are dosed, the gas sensor will only
be used to switch between two preset levels. A normal level and an increased dosing
level when the sensor detects significant increase in H2S. To ensure that the system only
activates when there is flow, there will also still be a flow sensor, in the form of a radar
sensor. The sensors and dosing hose are freely hanging in the well.

With 9 different concepts created to handle the issue of hydrogen sulfide generated in
the sewage, a screening process can now begin, aiming to identify the most promising
concept.
This is initially done utilizing a pugh matrix, where the concepts are rated by the criteria
from the product specification. subsequently, sewage utilities where drawn in, as experts
in the field, to make an assessment of the different concepts.
By comparing the findings from both the concept scoring and the expert assessment, the
best concept was determined, and improvements were made.
6.3.1
Concept scoring
As part of the concept selection process the different concepts are scored based on crite-
ria from the product specification. The process of scoring the concepts is done by using
a so called Pugh matrix. The Pugh matrix takes the criteria from the product specifica-
tion and gives them a weight based on their relative importance. Each concept is then
scored in every category in a given scoring range. The scores are multiplied with the
weight of the category in order to get a weighted score for the concept in each cate-
gory. When all the weighted scores for a concept are added together, the total weighted
score of the concept is found. These scores can then be compared between concepts
and should help in determining the best concept to continue with, or if certain concepts
should be added together.

Expert assessment
To better judge the concepts created, sewage utilities were contacted to get expert as-
sessments from them. Two utilities provided feedback on the different concepts, com-
menting with positives, issues with the designs, general comments, and which concept
they found best. With one of the utilities it was also possible to conduct a meeting to
further discuss the concepts in more detail. Some of the most relevant conclusions from

the assessments are summarised in this section.
In general there was a positive response to the possibility of system that collected,
chemical storage, pump and electrical components in one structure. The specific form
structure however was varying between concepts. The sewage utilities pointed out that
having access doors in opposite sites, for respectively the chemical container and the
electrical/pump departments, would be problematic. This is because they would like to
be able to place the system by a building and still have the chemical access door in the
front. A good solution should therefore have the access doors on perpendicular sides,
similarly to concept 7. There was also made a small note to increase the size of the unit
and the top hatches a little, in order to ensure easy and safe insertion of IBC tanks.
As for size choices and solution scale it was commented that for the large solutions,
concept 1 and 4, one utility found these systems too large compared to what they cur-
rently use, and the other would prefer their current solution of burying a 15m3 tank by
the pump station and dosing here. The large solutions would probably also need insula-
tion in the walls in order to run during winter.
For the small scale solution with a drum, it was noted by one of the utilities that they did
not have the needed equipment to lift out a drum, which may become an issue for them.
Other than this it was regarded as a great solution for small problems with hydrogen
sulfide.
In regards to buried solutions there were noted two very relevant factors. Firstly, when
the need for discretion is so high that a buried solution is needed, it would most likely
be in an residential areas, and electrical power would likely be easy to access. Solar
cells would therefore not be necessary.
In some situations, like in nature parks, there would still be problems with power, but
here solar cells would potentially be overshadowed by trees. Another thing with buried
solutions were safety risks. If chemicals leaked out in a buried solution it would collect
gasses in the room which would be a safety hazard when opening the unit. Especially
for concept 4, where one would have to climb down into the unit, it would be very prob-
lematic. In this case, a ventilation system would therefore be required.
When it comes to sensors and electrical components, the utilities were not enthusiastic
about the use of a sulfilogger sensor due to the price and maintenance level they have.
It was much preferred to have a flow sensor and just detect when there was flow. It was
also noted that 2-way communication with the unit was a must for both utilities.
The concepts that were stated as best by the utilities were concept 3, 5, 8 and 9. Con-
cepts 3, 8 and 9 can be regarded as very similar and concept 5 is quite different from
these.
Concept 5 was commented on as the best solution in case there was need for high dis-
cretion. The compact buried solution was a good size, and the access for pump and

maximum power consumption for every component was used.
For a needed battery life of 4 days, this lead to a minimum battery size of 24V and 84Ah.
A 24V 100Ah battery was therefore selected, giving a expected battery life of 4,7 days.
For the solar panels, as the intended seasons for use are spring to fall, the system would
require solar panels with a total peak performance of 474W, in order to generated the
needed amount of power per day. A safety factor of 1.25 was implemented to cover un-
expected circumstances and to ensure there is power left over for charging batteries. The
needed solar cells should therefore have a total peak performance of 600W. To achieve
this, three 200W panels are implemented.
Sealing
To ensure that the system is weather proof, the doors and the roof hatch are sealed with
rubber isolation. This should suffice in stopping water from entering the system, and
keeping the electronics safe.
IBC hose connection
To connect an IBC tank to the system, a standard S60 fitting is used. The fitting will
be immediately followed by a valve. This valve is to be closed while changing the IBC
tank, to ensure that any chemical leftover in the hose cannot spill out, thereby limiting
potential chemical contact and improving safety. A hose will then run from the valve,
to the piping at the pump.
Valve and piping
The valve and piping around the pump is designed with functionality, to ensure optimal
operation for the pump and for easy cleaning and maintenance of the system. Part of
this includes an outlet valve for draining the system. The piping also includes a multi
function valve, which can both work as a pressure retaining valve and for removing air
from the system.

Sensors
In regards to the sensors used in the system, choices are between the use of a radar sen-
sor, a gas sensor, and a Sulfilogger. The choice of sensor generally scales both the unit
pricing and the precision to which H2S levels can be measured.
The radar sensor works by measuring the distance, from the sensor to the surface level
of the wastewater. Based on the level of the wastewater in the well, it can be determined
if there is currently flow or not. The sensor will need to have a flood protection tube, to
ensure it does not come into contact with the wastewater. The sensor will be hanging
from the well top, but not too far away from the wastewater, as the sensor measures in a
cone and can detect the concrete, thereby giving a false reading.
If a gas sensor is added, the unit would have multiple dosing levels depending on the
measured H2S concentration. This method will still have some overdosing, as the gas
sensor is not reliable for precise measurements. The variation in dosing will for consist
of a baseline dosing level, applicable in most cases, and an increased function, for oc-
casional cases where extra high levels of H2S are measured. The gas sensor must hang
at the top of the well, to ensure that it never gets in contact with the wastewater.
If a Sulfilogger is desired, the sensor will be hanging from the well top all the way down
into the wastewater. In addition to the Sulfilogger itself, there will also be the need for a
pH sensor. This sensor as well will hang from the well top and down into the wastewa-
ter. The Sulfilogger will be able to measure the level of H2S in the wastewater, and with
the addition of a pH sensor, it is then possible to determine the amount of sulfide that is
not in the form of H2S yet.
PLC and GSM
For controlling pump operation based on input from sensors, the system utilizes a PLC.
The system only needs a small PLC unit for calculations, as not much data manipulation
65
==PAGE:76==
is required. The PLC has connection possibilities for a GSM module, and there is
included a PLC expansion to allow input from additional sensors.
The GSM uses a sim-card to allow for 2-way communication with the system. This
will provide the sewage utilities with information on remaining chemical, data from the
sensors, and the pumps running statistics. It also allows for the utility companies to
calibrate the system from afar when changes to the dosing is needed, for example when
it can be seen that the dosing level is too low or too high.

Self-sufficient vs connecting to power grid
A large part of how this product differentiates from competing chemical dosing systems,
is by being self-sufficient when it comes to power. It is therefore relevant to compare
solar power and laying new power cables, from both an economic and reliability per-
spective.
When looking at it from an economic focus, the relevant factor becomes the price of
solar cells, batteries, and charging regulators, compared to the price of connecting to
the power grid and laying cables. For this analysis, it will be assumed that the system
will need to be setup at an area where such a connection is not already made, as this is
obviously the targeted areas for this feature.
The needed power generating components for the final solutions are 3 solar panels, each
capable of producing 200W, a charging regulator for ensuring correct battery charging,
and a 24V 100Ah battery. In total these components cost 16.296DKK. In comparison,
the costs for a new connection to the power grid can be very high. Firstly, to connect to
the power grid the cost is 15.650DKK[18]. This allows a connection capable of running
up to 25A, and an increase in ampere costing around 1.200DKK/A. After this comes
the cost of laying cables. The initial price for starting the work, is around 8.500DKK
and it is needed to install a breaker panel, costing 5.500DKK. Making an immediate
sunk cost of 14.000DKK. The price for digging to lay cables is around 250DKK per
meter and an additional 48DKK for registration work and wire handling fees. Many
more costs exist depending on needs such as running under roads or having to remove
and lay asphalt(Appendix B.14).
As this product does not need more than 25A, there would not be an additional cost
for increased ampere needs. Based on this, it can be calculated that the components
required for self sufficiency, is cheaper than laying cables, assuming new connections
have to be made.
In relation to the reliability of the setup, it can of course generally be stated that solar
power is less reliable than a connection to the power grid. Solar power can be affected
by many factors, such as the weather. Especially factors such as snow or dirt that lays
on the panels can limit the power generated significantly. For increase reliability at all
times of the year, connecting to the power grid is therefore a good solution. It also has
to be considered that solar panels and batteries are additional components that will be in
need of maintenance in a significantly larger degree, than power cables running to the
unit.

The balance between reliability and the costs of connecting to the power grid, therefore
have to be considered. If a system only needs to run during summer, the reliability of a
solar-powered solution increases significantly, in terms of power generation.
Chemical saving
With the possibility of a dosing system now being off-grid and placed at a pressure pipe
outlet, a reduction of chemical used can be explored, utilizing a Sulfilogger.
Measuring and dosing at the same point, removes the need for a prediction model when
dosing based on sensor input. This is because there is no feedback loop created from
the residence time in the pressure pipe. Thereby making it easier to utilize a Sulfilogger
optimally.
Optimizing the use of chemical will not only have the economic benefit of lowering the
operational cost, but also have a positive environmental impact.
From a economical perspective, implementing a Sulfilogger to the developed dosing
system will have two effects. Firstly, the cost of the necessary components will be
50.500DKK, which covers a Sulfilogger and a pH sensor with its transmitter. Having
both will establish a full overview the total quantity of sulfide to further specify the
needed dosage. Secondly, a chemical saving should be observed, estimated to around
half of what is normally dosed(Appendix B.2). The actual savings in chemical will vary,
depending on how optimized the system is beforehand.

Risk evaluation
Naturally, a dosing system comes with the necessity of handling chemicals, which can
be a safety hazard if managed incorrectly. Similarly other safety and health hazards can
arise when, maintaining, setting up, or disassembling the system. In this regard, an oc-
cupational health and safety assessment have been performed, for the developed dosing
system in this project.
General working environment rules in connection with wastewater wells should be ad-
hered to. Examples hereof could be carrying a gaslogger against hydrogen sulfide, fall
protection in relation to installation and service of sensors in wells, etc. In addition,
complying with the safety data sheet for the used chemicals is also required.
For the risks found, an description of the relevant situations will be stated, the assessed
likelihood and possible consequences will then be explained, followed by the corrective
measure.

Conclusion
This project aimed to analyse the sewage utility market and develop a new chemical
dosing system, to combat hydrogen sulfide development in sewage systems.
There has been conducted analyses of the problem area and the market in which solu-
tions would have to be implemented. It was found that hydrogen sulfide development
varies based on many factors, while under anaerobic conditions. Some of the most im-
portant factors being, bacterial growth, wastewater composition, residence time, and
temperature.
The current solutions on the market were examined, in order to determine the strengths
and weaknesses of existing products. The market was investigated to determine its size,
what direction it is going, the current trends, and where there is a lack of solutions. As
part of this, interviews and field trips with sewage utilities were essential sources of in-
formation.
Based on the analysis completed, product specifications were made for a self-sufficient
dosing system that can be placed at the outlet of pressure pipes, where electricity is
often difficult to connect to. System function models were made as the basis for idea
generation, which lead to solutions to sub-problems, which could then be formed into
full concepts. In total 9 different concepts for dosing systems were created. These 9
concepts were evaluated based on criteria from the product specification and on expert
assessments from sewage utilities.
Grounded in the evaluation, the best concept was further developed upon, to form a final
concept. The detailing of the final product focused on modulization, functionalities, and
production. Based on this an analysis could be conducted where the unit price could be
estimated and compared to the market. This estimation showed that the unit price was
on competitive level with similar dosing systems currently in use.
In general, the final solution fulfilled the requirements of the product specification, and
showed good results in the criteria. A part of the solution, that was focused on, was the
possibility of using a sensor to better determine chemical dosing. Through evaluation
of concepts it was found that interest in utilizing this new sensor was low due to pricing
and maintenance. A relevant factor, that is currently missing, which could influence
sewage utilities into applying chemical-saving solutions, are governmental regulations
surrounding better control of chemicals dosed into the sewers.

Future work
The product design that has been developed in this project, could potentially be a com-
petitive product on the market. In order to better validate this, a prototype should be
developed and tested. This will both help in ensuring assembly of the unit is simple, if
the modularity is useful and if setup at the end of a pressure pipe is beneficial. Preferably
tests should be completed with different sensor setups to ensure all methods work, and
to validate if chemical savings are possible and how significant these would be. Based
on testing, it is very possible that some modifications to the design must be made, as
product development is an iterative process.
Surrounding electrical installation in the dosing system, there has been a lack of ex-
pertise during the project. Therefore, standards and regulations in the field should be
looked into, and an assessment of how this work will be conducted must be completed.
When entering the market, the different concepts for unit modules, described in the
report should be further developed, to cover the most common problems seen on the
market. As part of better covering the wider market, it can be beneficial to develop ad-
ditional modules for the unit, to cover the needs of more customers. To further spread
out there can be looked into other markets with similar needs and problems, such as the
fish farming industry, as described in the market analysis.

9 Koncepter for off-grid doseringsanlæg til 
bekæmpelse af svovlbrinte i kloakken 
 
Generelt: 
Placering: 
For at positionere sig på markedet er alle koncepter designet som off-grid løsninger, altså at være 
uafhængige af en tilslutning til elnettet. 
 
Doserings strategi: 
En generel besparelse af kemi er tiltænkt ind i de fleste af koncepterne. Det at være off-grid åbner 
her nye muligheder, da et feedback loop for sensor teknologi kan undgås. Med tydeligt den største 
mulige kemi besparelse er sulfilogger benyttet ved de flest af koncepterne, men koncepterne kan 
også kører med gas sensorer, flow eller tryg sensor, eller helt uden nogen sensor. 
 
Solceller: 
For solceller er der tiltænkt at de skal kunne dække enhedens drift ved laveste solenergi tilførsel 
under den tiltænkte driftsperiode. 
Ved 100% effektivitet i dele af sommeren producerer et 100W solpanel ca. 425Wh. 
Om sommeren er den laveste effektivitet på omkring 80% 
Om efteråret kan effektiviteten falde til omkring 25% 
Om vinteren falder effektiviteten helt ned på 10% 
 
Batteri: 
For batterier i løsningerne er der lavet tre niveauer. 
Sommerkørsel skal have en batteriforsyning som skal kunne køre enheden i 2 dage uden sol. 
Forår-sommer-efterårskørsel skal have en batteriforsyning som kan køre enheden i 4 dage uden 
sol. 
Helårskørsel skal have en batteriforsyning som skal kunne køre enheden i 2 uger uden sol. 
 
El komponenter: 
Hvis der ikke er nævnt andet i konceptets beskrivelse, så er det tiltænkt at løsningen skal have en 
lade regulator til batteriet, strømforsyninger til pumpe og til sensorer, en styringsenhed som fx: en 
PLC til at kontrollere pumpe ud fra sensorer, samt lave nødvendig kontrol og udregninger. Foruden 
et GSM-modul som kan sende information fra enheden til forsyningen.

De 9 koncepter: 
01 - Year around: 
Dette koncept er udviklet til steder med store doseringsbehov som forekommer året rund. 
Løsningen er designet med en 3,5 m^3 bulk tank, som skulle minimere opfyldningsbehov. 
Behovet for at dosere året rundet medfører nødvendighed for en stor og frostsikker batteripakke 
på 12V 400AH og solcellepakke på 2x325W solpaneller, for at sikre drift gennem vintermånederne.  
Til tilslutningen af sulfiloggeren og en tryksensor samt sikre god opblanding af kemi er her 
påtænkt at forlænge trykrøret så der er en direkte overgang til det videre gravitationsrør, samt 
isolere oppumpebrønden for at sikre et bedre arbejdsmiljø ved service. 
- 
Ingen grund til at isolere brønden. 
 
02 – Easy and facade design: 
Koncept 2 er tiltænkt at gøre nogle dele af vedligeholdelsen og opsætningen nemmere. Samtidig 
er der tilføjet en facade på konstruktionen for at gøre den stilmæssigt flottere, så den kan placeres 
i mere offentlige rum. Konceptet er tiltænkt at kører i forår, sommer og efterår. 
Konceptet indeholder en fast installeret 1m^3 tank, og bør derfor opfyldes oftere end andre 
permanente opsætninger. 
Løsningen har et batterilager på 12V 100Ah og anvendt 2x175W solpaneller. 
I dette system er det tænkt at der skal være mulighed for kommunikation til enheden, og at man 
derfor kan styre/programmere pumpe på afstand. 
Det er tiltænkt at lave en forlængelse af trykrøret hvor der kan måles og doseres, dog forlænges 
der ikke hele vejen til gravitationsrøret. Der indsættes sulfilogger og doserings slange på denne 
forlængelse. 
- 
Hvorfor ikke her, eller hvorfor i den forrige?  
Hvis der er brug for tryksensor i den forrige, så er der vel også her, eller? 
Ulempe at palle tanken ikke kan skiftes. 
 
03 - Average: 
Koncept 3 er tiltænkt at være en gennemsnitlig løsning som hverken tilføje flere funktioner eller 
fjerner funktioner for at bespare. Konceptet er tiltænkt at kører forår, sommer og efterår. 
Konceptet har en 1m^3 IBC-tank som kan udskiftes, et batterilager på 12V 100Ah og 2x175 
solceller. 
Det er tiltænkt at løsningen skal have frithængende slange og sulfilogger i brønden hvor der 
drypdoseres. 
- 
Er der frihøjde nok til at en lastbil med palle gafler kan udskifte palle tanken? 
Normalt kan toppen tippes ved kemikasser til palle tanke. 
 
04 - Discreet - buried: 
Dette koncept er, ligesom koncept 1, tiltænkt som en løsning til større svovlbrinte problemer og 
med mulighed for at køre året rundt. Men for at løsningen er mere diskret da denne begravet 
under jorden. 
Dette koncept indeholder en fast installeret 3,5m^3 tank og bruge en sulfilogger til at bestemme

==PAGE:160==
doseringen hvorved doseringen holdes til et minimum. 
Løsningen har et batterilager på 12V 400Ah med indbygget varme for at køre om vinteren.  
Da løsningen er under jorden, bliver der brugt et solcelletårn med 2x325W solceller hvilket kan 
drive elforbruget selv under vinterperioder. 
Da løsningen allerede nedgraves, tiltænkes det at den nedgraves langs trykrøret, gerne nær 
afslutningen, og at sensorer samt dosering tilkobles direkte på trykrøret. 
- 
Vil kræve der laves en brønd over trykrøret, hvor målere installeres i, så man kan komme 
til dem. 
 
05 - Discreet - buried compact: 
Dette koncept er ligesom 4 tiltænkt at blive nedgravet, men er her holdt mindre og bør køre i 
forår-sommer-efterår, men ikke vinter. 
Konceptet indeholder en fast installeret 1m^3 tank, og bør derfor opfyldes oftere end andre

Løsningen har et batterilager på 12V 100Ah, og har et soltårn med 2x175W solpaneller. 
Det er tiltænkt at løsningen skal have frithængende slange og sulfilogger i brønden hvor der 
drypdoseres. 
Da det er tiltænkt at tanken placeres under pumpen er der implementeret en større pumpe med 
bedre løftehøjde end andre concepter. 
- 
Det er vel behovet for mængden af kemi der bestemmer størrelsen på pumpe på alle tyder 
af installationerne, og ikke kun løftehøjde? 
Det er snare modtryk ind på pumpeledningerne der kan få indvirkning på pumpe størrelse. 
 
06 - Discreet - small: 
Koncept 6 er designet ud fra at holde sig diskret ved at gøres mindre i sine ydre dimensioner. 
Konceptet er også kun tiltænk at skulle køre i sommerperioden, og hovedsageligt i områder med 
mindre problemer som kun kommer frem i disse sommerperioder. 
Konceptet har en mindre beholder end andre koncepter. Her er der anvendt en 200L tønde som 
kan udskiftes. 
Da der om sommeren ikke er store problemer med solenergi er der implementeret en 
batteripakke på 12V 50Ah og anvendt 4x20W solpaneller. 
Det er tiltænkt at løsningen skal have frithængende slange og sulfilogger i brønden hvor der 
drypdoseres. 
- 
Hvordan er det tænkt at håndtere en fyldt / tung tønde? Enten bør tværstiver i åbning 
kunne demonteres, og der skal være plads nok til en tøndeløfter, eller der skal være højde 
nok til at den kan stå på en palle, som kan tages med lastbil.   
- 
Hvis solpaneler kun kan vende på en bestemt måde, kan det blive en udfordring, da 
åbningen til beholder altid skal vende mod kørevej. Så er det ikke sikkert at det passer 
med bedste retning i forhold til sol. Gælder alle enheder med faste solpaneler og 
udskiftelige beholdere. 
 
07 - Cheap - no measurement: 
Koncept 7 er designet som den billige off-grid løsning, med konstant dosering som køre fra forår til

Der er her ingen kontrol eller kommunikations enheder, hvilket betyder der ingen meddelelse er 
ved lavt kemi beholdning. Estimering af kemibeholdningen skal foregå ved forsyninger ud fra at

I koncept 9 er det tiltænkt at bespare på enhedens omkostninger, men stadig have et system der

Enheden kører i forår, sommer og efterår. 
Konceptet kører med en 1m^3 IBC-tank som kan udskiftes. 
Enheden har en batteripakke på 12V 100Ah og 2x175 solpaneller. 
Det er tiltænkt at løsningen skal have frithængende slange og gaslogger i brønden hvor der 
drypdoseres. Gasloggeren skal hænge helt i toppen af brønden.
