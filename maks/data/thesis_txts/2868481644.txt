Abstract
This thesis starts with an analysis of a spatial prey-predator model with diffusion. First,it
shows what parameters will make the Turing pattern appear. Next, we study the numerical
solution of the partial differential equations with high-order accuracy. To evaluate the
quality of the numerical solution, we compute the H2 norm and the mean value.
In the second part, we extend the model by incorporating spatiotemporal noise and solve
the resulting stochastic partial differential equations via finite difference methods. Our
focus is on analyzing how noise influences the Turing pattern when the diffusion coefficient
d is near its critical value and the system’s eigenvalues are close to the Turing bifurcation.
To investigate the impact of noise in this near-critical state, we use eigenvalue analysis,
numerical simulations, fast Fourier transforms, and Lyapunov equation.
Through numerical examples, we illustrate that when d is at its critical value, noise signif-
icantly amplifies small perturbations near the Turing bifurcation, yet does not always lead
to the formation of a stable pattern. Conversely, when d significantly exceeds the critical
point, noise-induced perturbations can also give a clear Turing pattern.

1
Introduction
Patterns are everywhere in nature, from the gathering of species in communities to the
complex patterns on animals, such as the spotted fur of a cheetah and the fingerprints
of humans. These patterns often arise from one or more fixed mechanisms. The main
driver of such patterns is the reaction-diffusion mechanism, and the resulting structures
are often called Turing patterns.
In ecological systems, environmental uncertainty can be regarded as a form of ”spa-
tial noise” that affects species coexisting within the same environment. This noise also
changes over time. The main objective of this thesis is to investigate how spatiotemporal
noise influences the dynamics between two species in a predator-prey relationship. Using
pattern formation as the foundational framework and building on Sirohi’s work (Sirohi et
al., 2015), this thesis employs tools such as fast Fourier transforms, eigenvalue analysis,
numerical method of partial differential equations, and trace(P) of Lyapunov equation.
These methods are used to explore the impact of noise on species pattern formation.
This chapter begins by introducing the research background of the study, the next is a
detailed explanation of the model employed in the thesis. Finally, an overview of the
thesis structure is shown.
1.1
Background
In the paper (Turing, 1990), Alan Turing first proposed that patterns can emerge from the
spatial organization of an initially uniform system due to instability. Specifically, he showed
how chemical morphogens generate patterns by reacting and diffusing in tissues. These
spatial structures appear because the uniform equilibrium state becomes unstable under
small random disturbances. Turing’s pioneering work provided the first mathematical and
physical explanation for the formation of complex natural patterns (e.g. animal coat mark-
ings, leaf arrangements), sparking a wave of theoretical research on pattern formation.
Its biological significance, however, was not revisited until the 1970s (Bard and Lauder,
1974).
By the 1980s, the Turing model was validated both numerically and experimentally in ar-
tificial systems (Harris et al., 1984; Aguado et al., 1989). Later, (Castets et al., 1990)
observed wave and spot structures akin to Turing patterns in Belousov–Zhabotinsky re-
actions, while (Koch and Meinhardt, 1994) extended Turing’s ideas to explain biological
morphogenesis, such as animal fur stripes and fish markings. Since then, researchers
have repeatedly confirmed and expanded upon Turing’s theory in a variety of systems:
for example, (Lee et al., 1994) observed self-replicating dot patterns in the ferrocyanide–
iodate–sulfite reaction, showing how a dot structure can form new dots via splitting and
ultimately evolve into complex spatiotemporal patterns. (Mecke, 1996) applied morpho-
logical metrics (e.g., Minkowski functionals) to classify Turing pattern transitions in a two-
dimensional open gel reactor, finding that these transitions can be treated analogously to
thermodynamic phase transitions. In addition, (Miyazaki, 2013) reviewed Turing patterns
in Belousov-Zhabotinsky reactions, clarifying their fundamental formation and dynamics.
More recently, (Van Gorder, 2021) analyzed how spatial inhomogeneities affect pattern
formation by extending Turing instability analysis. For Turing pattern in ecological mathe-
matics, (Carfora et al., 2025) employed a multiscale approach to show how Turing struc-
tures shape interactions in food-chain predator–prey systems, and (Ghorai et al., 2024)

core mechanism behind Turing instabilities. Meanwhile, (Bhargava et al., 2025) investi-
gated the impact of prey behavior and fear on the generation of Turing patterns in predator–
prey networks, and (Saha et al., 2024) addressed the role of non-local intraspecific com-

these studies highlight the variety of mechanisms-ranging from spatial inhomogeneity and
behavioral traits to noise, non-local competition, and time delays, which will generate or
change Turing-type patterns in ecological domains.
Stochastic partial differential equations (SPDEs) combine stochastic processes and par-
tial differential equations (PDEs) to describe the interaction between randomness and
dynamic systems. The origins of SPDEs can be traced back to the combination of prob-
ability theory and PDEs, particularly the connection between Brownian motion and diffu-
sion equations. Adolf Fokker and Max Planck independently proposed the Fokker-Planck
equation to describe particle diffusion (PLANCK, 1901; Fokker, 1914).
Now, SPDEs
have been applied in a wide range of fields, such as climate modeling (Nozhkin et al.,
2019), fluid mechanics (Hofmanová, 2016; Sapsis, 2011; Goodair et al., 2024), biostatis-
tics (Ditlevsen and Samson, 2013; Lelievre and Stoltz, 2016; Donnet and Samson, 2013),
and machine learning (Beck et al., 2019).
Over the past few decades, significant progress has been made in developing numerical
methods for solving SPDEs, including finite difference methods (Allen et al., 1998; Duffy,
2013), finite element methods (Gunzburger et al., 2014; Deb et al., 2001), and spectral
methods (Nouy, 2009, 2007). Despite these advances, challenges remain in efficiently
solving high-dimensional, nonlinear SPDEs, particularly in ensuring numerical stability
and accuracy (Han et al., 2017).
Stochastic partial differential equations (SPDEs) have become a crucial tool in the study of
pattern formation, providing a robust framework for understanding how noise and diffusion
interact to shape complex spatial structures. Classical SPDE-based models, such as the
stochastic Ginzburg-Landau equation (Bressloff and Kilpatrick, 2008) and the stochastic
Cahn-Hilliard equation (Blömker et al., 2005), have been widely applied in both physical
and biological systems to investigate how stochastic fluctuations influence the generation
and stability of patterns.
Unlike classical physical systems, ecological models often exhibit additional complexity
due to species interactions, external environmental fluctuations, and intrinsic stochastic-
ity. SPDEs have therefore been particularly useful in studying predator-prey interactions,
where diffusion, stochasticity, and nonlinear feedback mechanisms collectively shape
population patterns. For instance, Hu et al. (Hu et al., 2015) explored how Hopf bifur-
cation and Turing instability arise in predator-prey systems with diffusion, demonstrating
that stochasticity can modify the onset and stability of ecological patterns. Similarly, Lai
et al. (Lai et al., 2024) applied the Furutsu-Novikov theorem to a stochastic predator-prey
system with dichotomous noise, revealing that memory effects and noise intensity are
key regulatory factors for Turing patterns. Noise-induced pattern formation has also been
widely investigated in broader biological and physical contexts. Sagués et al. (Sagués
et al., 2007) systematically examined how noise interacts with nonlinear dynamics to ei-

ther enhance or disrupt spatiotemporal order, leading to Turing patterns, noise-induced
transitions, and coherence resonance in extended systems.
In predator-prey models, multiple studies have demonstrated how diffusion, external pe-
riodic forcing, and stochastic noise interact to generate complex spatiotemporal patterns.
Rao et al. (Rao and Wang, 2012; Rao et al., 2009), Malchow et al. (Malchow et al., 2004),
Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2011), Sun et al. (Sun et al., 2010), and Yasin et al. (Yasin et
al., 2023) collectively showed that different noise types (e.g., colored noise, dichotomous
noise) and external forces can induce spatiotemporal chaos, frequency locking, symmetry
breaking, and noise-driven oscillations. Notably, Malchow et al. (Malchow et al., 2004)
and Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2011) analyzed how noise alters ecological pattern stability,
while Rao et al. (Rao and Wang, 2012) and Sun et al. (Sun et al., 2010) demonstrated
how periodic forcing and stochastic perturbations drive transitions between oscillatory and
chaotic states.
The theoretical foundations of SPDEs in pattern formation have been systematically de-
veloped by García-Ojalvo et al. (García-Ojalvo and Sancho, 2012), who established a
rigorous framework for incorporating different noise types into SPDEs and analyzing their
impact on system stability and pattern evolution. Their work demonstrated that noise can
drive transitions from regular Turing structures to spatiotemporal chaos, with significant
implications for understanding predator-prey dynamics in heterogeneous environments.
Taken together, these studies emphasize the importance of incorporating stochasticity
into pattern formation research and highlight the need for continued investigation into
how noise and deterministic dynamics interact to shape complex ecological and physical
systems. Future advancements in numerical methods for solving SPDEs and analytical
techniques for understanding noise-driven bifurcations will be crucial for deepening our
understanding of these intricate processes.

This thesis is divided into two main parts. In Chapter 2, we first introduce Turing pat-

Turing Bifurcation
In this section, we examine how various parameters influence Turing patterns, specifically
focusing on the relationship between the system’s eigenvalues and Turing patterns un-
der different parameter settings. In (Turing, 1990), Turing proposed a reaction–diffusion
model to explain the possible mechanisms of morphogenesis in organisms. Subsequent
studies (Gierer and Meinhardt, 1972; Murray and J., 2003) have shown that a homo-
geneous steady state, which remains stable in the absence of diffusion or under uni-
form perturbations, can become unstable when subjected to perturbations with non-zero
wavenumbers. At this time, this change in the system is called ”pattern formation” or
”Turing mechanism”. This mechanism arises from the interaction between two (or more)
substances or populations, leading a homogeneous solution to evolve into a stable, spa-
tially inhomogeneous solution, often called a “Turing pattern” or “Turing instability.” For
instance, for system (2.1), if we fix y, only observe x and t, and choose α = 1.95, β = 1,
γ = 0.6, δ = 0.1, d = 10, we obtain the spatiotemporal pattern shown in Figure 2.3:
From Figure 2.3, we observe that, at smaller t values, the distribution along the x-direction
remains nearly uniform, indicating that no pronounced spatial differences have emerged
yet. As time progresses, however, the distribution gradually exhibits noticeable fluctu-
ations or stratification in the x-direction, suggesting the onset of spatial inhomogeneity.
Finally, at larger t values (on the right side of the figure), distinct bands or stripes form
and remain relatively fixed in the x-direction, implying that the system settles into a stable
spatial pattern. Beyond this point, no further significant morphological changes occur.

diffusion system,

The Numerical Method
In this section, we aim to apply different numerical methods to solve model (2.1). The
method we mainly use is the Strang operator splitting method (Strang, 1968), which is
particularly suitable for complex partial differential equations (PDEs) with multiple oper-
ators and is frequently employed in multi-physics simulations. Since system (2.1) is a
coupled system involving reaction and diffusion, we can naturally split it into these two
sub-problems via Strang splitting. Additionally, because Strang method achieves second-
order accuracy in time, the separate solvers for the reaction and diffusion parts must also
preserve second-order accuracy in time and space.
2.2.1
Stang Splitting Method & Nested Loops for Diffusion Term

step ∆t is, the numerical solution can remain stable and will not produce numerical di-
vergence due to the step size problem. Therefore, under the implicit Euler method, we
can choose a larger time step, thereby improving computational efficiency without sacri-
ficing stability. This unconditional stability is particularly important for diffusion equations,
because diffusion phenomena often require a smaller spatial step h to accurately char-

h.
Meanwhile, from (2.14), the error order is O((∆x)2 + (∆y)2). The second-order finite
difference scheme has second-order accuracy in space and can better balance the com-
putational accuracy and computational cost. Although the high-order difference scheme
can further improve accuracy, it comes at the cost of increasing computational complex-
ity and the risk of error propagation, especially on larger grids (high resolution), which is
not beneficial to to the stability of the diffusion equation. At the same time, since we use
the Heun method in the reaction term discretization, the Heun method has second-order
time accuracy. Therefore, in order to ensure that the numerical solution maintains con-
sistent accuracy in time and space, it is necessary to choose a finite difference scheme
with second-order space and time accuracy. This makes the entire solution framework
have second-order accuracy in both space and time, thereby improving the overall accu-
racy of the numerical solution. However, the implicit Euler method can not guarantee the
second order accuracy in time, so choosing a method with the second order accuracy in
time is important. According to the (Crank and Nicolson, 1947), Crank Nicolson method
is a method with second-order space and time accuracy. We will introduce this method
in next section.
In this step of the Strang splitting method, the diffusion term is initially discretized over the
full time step ∆t. However, we can refine this process further. Despite the second-order
spatial accuracy of our method, employing a large time step directly under certain pa-
rameter settings—such as large diffusion coefficients or fine grid resolutions—may easily
lead to numerical instability. Therefore, we subdivide the diffusion step into n sub-steps
(∆tD = ∆t/n) and perform iterative updates in each sub-step, thereby significantly en-
hancing numerical stability.
This subdivision helps prevent divergence and other numerical instabilities under large
diffusion coefficients or fine grids. While most PDE solvers remain stable under suitably
chosen step sizes, our additional sub-steps bring the numerical solution for the entire dif-
fusion phase closer to the ideal outcome. Notably, at the coding level, only a few extra
iterations or sub-step loops are added during the “diffusion” stage, thus avoiding a sub-
stantial increase in implementation complexity. Although this procedure does introduce a
moderate increase in runtime, it remains acceptable for most practical scenarios.
In the following subsections, we compare different methods of sub-step subdivision and
demonstrate that our proposed approach is indeed both effective and efficient.
After completing the calculation of the ”full-step diffusion”, we only need to calculate the
last half-step reaction again according to the methods of (2.12) and (2.13) to complete
the entire Strang splitting method.

First, consider the H2 norm. The H2 takes into account the function values themselves,
their gradients, and their curvature (Laplacian). Numerically, if a method’s error is large
or the solution exhibits significant fluctuations, the H2 norm will increase or show anoma-
lies. Under small time steps, if each method produces H2 norm values similar to that of
the Crank–Nicolson (CN) method, it indicates they are largely consistent in terms of the
solution’s overall smoothness and oscillation level. Conversely, if a method’s H2 norm de-
viates markedly from the CN result, it suggests that the method induces extra oscillations
(spatial or temporal) or fails to preserve certain features of the true solution.
Next, look at the mean values. The mean value reflects the general “energy level” or
“overall trend” across the domain. If the mean value from Method A is substantially higher
than that of CN, it may indicate accumulated bias during the simulation (for example, due
to unbalanced numerical dissipation or dispersion). On the other hand, if several methods
have nearly the same mean value as CN, it indicates they preserve the overall magnitude
of the solution.

Discussion
This thesis investigates the formation mechanism of Turing patterns in deterministic and
stochastic reaction-diffusion systems, employing numerical simulation, eigenvalue anal-
ysis, the Lyapunov equation, and FFT for analysis. In deterministic systems, when the
diffusion coefficient d exceeds the critical value, the uniform steady state becomes unsta-
ble, leading to the formation of Turing patterns. Different numerical methods are applied,
and nested loops refine the diffusion time step, improving the quality of the numerical
solution. H2 norm and mean value analysis further validate these findings.
To explore the system’s behavior in greater detail, Chapter 2 focuses on the deterministic
model, where we employ eigenvalue analysis to examine stability conditions. The results
indicate that when d > dc (dc ≈7.2267), the system evolves from a uniform state to a spa-
tially structured pattern. Conversely, when d < dc, all disturbances decay, and the system
remains spatially homogeneous. In the numerical solution, the Crank–Nicolson method
provides high accuracy and stability, the explicit method is computationally efficient but
sensitive to step size, and the ADI method is computationally slower but applicable in spe-
cific scenarios. To enhance numerical stability, nested loops are introduced to refine the
diffusion time step, improving the quality of the numerical solution. The results demon-
strate that smaller time steps ensure stable solutions across all methods, while larger
steps may introduce numerical errors or even instability. In addition, H2 norm and mean
value analysis confirm that combining the Strang splitting method with the Crank–Nicolson
method and nested loops enhances solution quality.
In stochastic systems, noise does not fundamentally change the pattern formation mech-
anism but plays a significant role in modulating perturbations near the critical diffusion
coefficient. Unlike deterministic systems, where patterns emerge solely due to diffusion-
driven instability, noise can amplify fluctuations, influencing pattern evolution and, in some
cases, affecting local pattern morphology and transient behavior.
To further investigate the role of noise, Chapter 3 focuses on the stochastic model, where
we employ Lyapunov equation analysis to quantify noise-driven perturbations and FFT
transform to examine their spatial characteristics. The results indicate that near the crit-
ical diffusion coefficient dc, noise significantly enhances small perturbations, but these
perturbations do not always lead to stable patterns. In the supercritical state (d > dc),
noise has a limited impact on pattern formation, with Turing patterns predominantly de-
termined by deterministic mechanisms.
The result of FFT transform of u −u∗further supports these findings, demonstrating that
noise induces a ring-shaped energy distribution near the critical wave number k ≈0.3,
confirming that this mode is most susceptible to noise-driven amplification. When d ≈dc,
noise effects are particularly pronounced, leading to localized instabilities in the pattern. In
contrast, for d ≫dc, patterns become more structured as noise effects diminish, ultimately
resulting in a more stable and regular Turing pattern.
Numerical simulations validate these observations, showing that at low noise levels, the
overall structure of the pattern remains intact, but localized distortions may occur. At
higher noise levels, however, pattern regularity is significantly disrupted, sometimes lead-
ing to a transition from structured patterns to a more disordered state.
These results highlight the dual role of noise in stochastic reaction-diffusion systems.

While moderate noise can enhance pattern formation, excessive noise may destabilize
structures, leading to disorder. The findings suggest that noise is not merely a source
of randomness but an active participant in shaping spatial structures, influencing both
pattern stability and morphology in reaction-diffusion systems.
Comparing the results from Chapter 2 (deterministic system) and Chapter 3 (stochastic
system), we draw the following conclusions:
• Pattern formation mechanism: In deterministic systems, Turing pattern formation
is entirely governed by the diffusion coefficient d exceeding the critical value dc.
In stochastic systems, noise amplifies disturbances, influences pattern evolution
near the critical point, and, in some cases, affecting local pattern morphology and
transient behavior.
• Impact of numerical methods: The Crank–Nicolson method provides the highest
accuracy and stability, while nested loops refine diffusion calculations, ensuring sta-
bility even for larger step sizes.
• Effect of noise: the result of FFT transform of u −u∗and Lyapunov equation cal-
culations consistently verify the influence range and critical wave number k ≈0.3,
further confirming the key role of noise in Turing pattern formation.
While our study provides insights into stochastic Turing pattern formation, several chal-
lenges and limitations remain. First, the numerical methods used, although effective, are
limited in order of accuracy and efficiency. Second, the noise model adopted in this study
is relatively simple, and more complex stochastic influences remain unexplored. Finally,
parameter uncertainties are not fully considered, whereas real-world systems often exhibit
variability across space and time.
These limitations suggest potential directions for future research:
1. Higher-Order Methods. Owing to project time constraints and the complexity of
implementation, we used an explicit method to handle the noise term (Brownian
sheet), which allows for very fast computation but yields a maximum weak order of
only 1. Even when combined with Strang splitting, this approach remains insufficient
to achieve higher-order accuracy. Therefore, developing higher-order schemes for
solving such SPDEs is an important direction.
Achieving higher-order accuracy
would enable a more precise identification of the internal boundaries within the Tur-
ing patterns, facilitating deeper analysis of their numerical properties.
2. Generalizing the Noise Term. Our model employs a relatively simple multiplica-
tive noise. A natural extension is to consider more complex noise terms such as
σ1(u, v) ˙W1(x, y, t) and σ2(u, v) ˙W2(x, y, t), where σ1(u, v) and σ2(u, v) are nonlin-
ear functions of u and v. Whether these more general noise structures would alter
the solutions or our main conclusions is an open question worthy of further study.
The other way to generalize the noise term is using Lévy noise. For 1-dimensional
noise, we have Itô-Lévy decomposition, then we can also introduce the correspond-
ing Lévy–Itô type representation in high dimensions, constructing the random field
by integrating over a jump measure (Poisson random measure or the more general
Lévy measure). But, if constructing this random field, we need more theoretical sup-
port. Also, if we want to solve the SPDE with more complex noise term, maybe we
also need more stable methods, like Polynomial Chaos Expansion method(PCE).
3. Generalizing the Parameters. In reality, measured parameters—such as preda-
tion efficiency and mortality rate—are often noisy.

schemes, incorporating more general noise models, and accounting for parameter un-

reaction-diffusion systems under stochastic influences.

Turing patterns and suggests directions for future research on complex noise structures,
stochastic parameter systems, and high-order numerical methods.
