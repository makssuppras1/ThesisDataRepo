Abstract 
 
 
 
Being integral part of the cities, public spaces play an important role in the public life contributing to 
the wellbeing of the citizens. The design of highly performing public spaces is a fundamental goal 
towards the sustainable development of the cities. Hence, the determination of the parameters that 
influence the occupancy of the public spaces can significantly favour the urban planning and design. 
The current study investigates the relation between the thermal environment and the use of public 
spaces. On-site measurements of the climatic conditions in four public spaces of Copenhagen and 
observations related to the occupancy of those places constitute the basis of the analysis undertaken 
herein. The relation between the outdoor thermal environment and the occupancy is illustrated by 
graphs that show the presence of people conducting particular activities under specific climatic 
conditions. The thermal index UTCI and the provided assessment scale is used to quantify the level of 
thermal comfort related to the measured climatic data. Four public places in Copenhagen including 
Kongens Have, Nyhavn, Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv were surveyed, while the relevant data was 
measured in two different periods, i.e., winter 2020-2021 and spring-summer 2018. Differences on the 
conditions during the data collection entail the separate analysis of the Data set A (winter 2020-2021) 
and Data set B (spring summer 2018). The outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic and the implied 
restrictions for preventing the spread of the virus during the winter of 2020-2021 affected the public 
life and accordingly the presence of people in public spaces.  
 
The analysis of the measured data revealed differences among the graphs that describe the influence of 
climatic conditions on the occupancy of public spaces with regard to various activities. Therefore, 
several case-specific findings can be derived that refer to particular parameters and their influence on 
the measurements. Nevertheless, considering specific exceptions, generic trends can also be drawn. In 
particular, the analysis of Data set A indicates that the change of the environmental conditions from no 
thermal stress to cold stress is not expected to reduce abruptly the presence of people in the public 
places. For slight cold stress conditions (e.g., 4oC≤UTCI≤9oC), the total occupancy of the places is quite 
similar to the one that the places experience for UTCI values corresponding to the no heat stress zone 
(9oC≤UTCI≤26oC). However, under cold conditions, the decrease of the thermal comfort level has a 
varying influence on the decrease of people’s presence in public spaces performing a specific activity. 
Additionally, the change of the climatic conditions from no thermal stress to heat stress conditions 
(Data set A) has a varying influence both on the performance of the different activities in the examined 
public spaces and on the total occupancy of the public areas. Therefore, the specific function of a public 
space and the type of the activities should be considered to properly investigate people’s response to 
climatic conditions.

1. Introduction 
 
 
 
 Background and motivation 
 
Nowadays, the proportion of the world’s population that lives in urban areas is estimated to be 55%. 
Projections published by the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs (UNDESA) 
show that 2.5 billion more people will be added to the urban areas’ population by 2050 and hence, the 
proportion of urban population will reach 65% [1]. The rapid growth of the urban population from 751 
million by 1950 to 4.2 billion by 2018 [1] is accompanied by an increase in the number of challenges 
related to city life and the use of public of spaces. Hence, it is highly relevant for the societal welfare 
and prosperity to deal with and eventually, address the environmental, social and economic challenges 
that arise from the urban population growth. 
 
It is, though, of paramount importance to ensure that the future generations will be able to meet their 
needs without being compromised by the way the current societal needs and challenges are addressed. 
The latter reflects the general rationale of the sustainable development as it is thoroughly defined in the 
report of the World Commission on Environment and Development “Our Common Future”  [2]. The 
set of universal goals, which have been adopted by all United Nations Member States by 2015 in order 
to meet worldwide the urgent environmental, political and economic challenges, is known as the 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) [3]. The 17 SDGs and the associated 169 targets are related to 
various aspects of people’s life covering a broad range of sustainable development issues. 
 
The SDG 11, stated as “Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable”, 
is associated to the sustainability in the city level. The specific SDG aims to secure long-term prosperity 
for the cities and the population that lives in them [4]. The growing interest that the SDG 11 is receiving 
can be attributed to the fact that the cities are widely recognized as pivotal elements of the society’s 
development and have dominant role in the growth of national economies [5]. For example, the World 
Bank currently prioritizes significant efforts and relevant resources in order to contribute to the 
successful implementation of SDG 11 towards sustainable cities and communities [6]. Additionally, the 
need to design liveable communities and resilient cities lies top in the agenda of the World Economic 
Forum [7]. 
 
The public spaces, which constitute integral part of modern and sustainable cities, contribute 
significantly to the livability of the cities. For instance, the outdoor public spaces are commonly used 
to accommodate the pedestrian traffic and plenty of additional activities that the citizens normally 
undertake for leisure and/or social life purposes [8]. Hence, encouraging the use of public spaces has 
physical, economic, social and environmental benefits for the cities [8]. In this regard, high-quality 
urban spaces have become a key component of the regeneration and the sustainable development 
schemes worldwide [9]. In other words, the need for well-designed and highly performing public spaces 
attains currently an increasing significance by considering the benefits that modern communities can 
experiences due to high-quality public spaces [9].

Introduction

In order to substantiate the discussion made above, health is one of a fundamental aspect of people’ s 
live that has been already proven to be advanced by the existence and use of public spaces. People’s 
productivity and health are promoted by outdoor public spaces [10], since they facilitate the needs of 
the population for a more active lifestyle [11]. Hence, the development of adequate and well-designed 
public spaces is currently receiving increased importance since it constitutes the remedy to create 
modern cities with infrastructures that support the good health and wellbeing for all city dwellers at all 
ages. The latter is incorporated into SDG 3 agreed by the world leaders in 2015. The relevance of the 
public spaces to support health and wellbeing is further stressed nowadays due to the outbreak of the 
coronavirus disease pandemic (COVID-19). 
 
Especially, in December 2019, the disease COVID-19 caused by Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome 
Coronavirus 2 (SARS-COV 2) was first identified in China and quite soon became an overwhelming 
factor that provoked, among others, significant changes in human lives worldwide. The outbreak of the 
pandemic has had destructive effect on the global healthcare systems and significant socioeconomic 
implications [12]. The national and/or local lockdowns as well as the recommendations for social 
distancing constitute the prevailing restrictions that are still imposed in order to prevent the spread of 
the coronavirus disease. Under these extraordinary conditions, the importance of available and safe to 
function open urban spaces in the cities became more evident. Given that 90% of COVID-19 cases have 
been recorded in urban areas [10], the impact of urban planning in public health is further underlined. 
The COVID-19 pandemic period made clear that better urban planning is strongly related to improved 
public health [10]; hence, an almost inevitable readjustment of the national urban plans was prompted. 
According to statistics provided by the United Nations, a significant number of governments, which 
had developed national urban plans up to May 2020, modified considerably their strategies in order to 
accommodate the challenges imposed by COVID-19 pandemic [10].  
 
Following the discussion made above, it is evident the use of public or else urban spaces is of vital 
importance for the modern cities and societies. Therefore, over the last decades, remarkable effort has 
been dedicated to support and advance urban planning towards the design of “successful” urban spaces 
[13]. Numerous published studies have already defined parameters that determine the quality of public 
spaces and propose criteria, which should be considered in order to enhance urban livability [14]. The 
microclimate is one of the parameters that influences the quality and the performance of outdoor spaces 
while increased research works have lately shown the strong relationship between the microclimatic 
conditions and the use of outdoor areas [15]. In particular, the microclimate has been found as being 
the most important influential factor for people’s decision regarding the visit of an open space [16]. 
Hence, it is gaining ground the argument that climate sensitive analyses should be considered at the 
decision-making process for improving urban planning [17]. 
 
 In this regard, the modern trends in urban design and planning require a microclimate analysis aiming 
to define the optimal design of public spaces in terms of outdoor thermal comfort. Commonly, 
assessment scales of thermal comfort indices are used to define the bounds, which are used at this kind 
of analysis in order to define the criteria for acceptable design with respect to thermal comfort. The 
thermal comfort indices constitute the link between the climatic conditions and the human thermal 
sensation [8]. Therefore, through the current study, the flexibility level of the thermal comfort 
boundaries considered on microclimate analyses is under investigation since the range of the indices 
values that define the thermal comfort might not have the pivotal role that is assumed. Hence, a thorough 
examination is pursued herein focusing on the relation between the thermal comfort and the human 
presence in the public spaces of a major Nordic city like Copenhagen. 
 
==PAGE:14==
Introduction 
 
14
 Main goal  
 
The main objective of this study is to investigate the relation between the thermal environment and the 
use of public spaces. To address this objective, people’s presence and performance at four public spaces 
in Copenhagen was thoroughly observed in relation to the local climate conditions. The thermal comfort 
index UTCI is used herein to account quantitatively for the thermal environment. The UTCI is 
calculated from the climate data recorded during on-site visits at the four public spaces including the 
Kongens Have, Nyhavn, Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv. In more details, the use level of the urban spaces 
is estimated through unobtrusive observations of people’s presence at the examined public spaces while, 
at the same time, the climate conditions are measured. The relation between outdoor thermal comfort 
and outdoor activity is illustrated by figures. The latter show the presence of people conducting 
particular activities under specific climate conditions, which correspond to specific UTCI value. The 
climate response figures are produced for plenty of the activities, i.e., which can take place in each one 
of the four examined urban spaces. 
 
The derived relation between outdoor thermal comfort and outdoor activity can provide valuable 
information for the use level of the outdoor spaces under various climatic conditions. Input regarding 
the public spaces’ success can be also attained by the current study since, as mentioned by relevant 
literature, “successful public places are characterized by the presence of people” [9]. In other words, 
this study aims to elucidate the extent that the city life and the use of public spaces are affected by 
environmental conditions. The better understanding of this relation constitutes a useful tool for urban 
planning and facilitates the assessment of the outdoor thermal comfort criteria used at microclimate 
analyses.  
 
Following the central objective of the study described above, three research questions are considered 
of high relevance to navigate the investigation of the relation between the thermal environment and 
human behaviour: 
 
1. Which is the influence of the thermal environment on particular activities that take place in 
public spaces? Does the change of climatic conditions have identical effect on all activities? 
2. Which is the extent that the urban life at a public space is affected by change in the climatic 
conditions?  
3. Are the cold and heat stress conditions, quantified by the UTCI, strongly related to abrupt 
decrease of the activity/traffic of an urban space?  
 
 Structure of the study 
 
The background, the motivation as well as the central objective and the main research questions are 
intrinsic parts of the current study and therefore, are described by the introductory chapter (§1). The 
second component (§2) of this study is dedicated to the literature review. The latter is essential to be 
undertaken in order to identify both existing advances and potential limitations/deficiencies for the area 
of urban planning and especially about the relation between climate conditions and use of public spaces. 
The literature review precedes the detailed description of the methodology (§3), which is followed by 
the current study in order, eventually, to address the aforementioned research questions. Especially, the 
third chapter describes the chosen urban places, the collection of the field data as well as the method 
adopted to quantify the climate conditions and to process the collected data. A detailed time plan of the

2. Literature review 
 
 
 
Numerous studies have been conducted worldwide investigating the parameters that describe the 
“ideal” public space and the characteristics of the urban environment that positively affect people’s 
satisfaction. The diversity both at the researchers’ background and at the studies’ focus area, leads to 
the thorough analysis of different features that affect the attractiveness of an outdoor public space and 
respectively the usage level of the place. The interaction between the people and the particular 
parameters are examined under different perspectives. The findings of the various studies contribute to 
the analysis of the multidimensional challenge of designing “successful” public spaces.

 Cities  
 
As the world experiences the effect of the rapid urbanization the concept of sustainability comes to the 
forefront of politics and decision making [18]. More than half of the world population is living in urban 
areas nowadays and it is expected that this fraction will increase even more the next decades [1] . Hence, 
sustainable urban development seems to be the mean to address the emerged challenges without 
jeopardizing next generations’ future.  
 
Cities are the complex systems that accommodate people and their activities and require great number 
of resources in order to function and evolve [19]. Therefore, one of the main goals within architecture 
and urban planning is the design of ‘comfortable’ living environment for the people in the cities [20]. 
In addition, studies have found evidence of positive correlation between access to urban green spaces 
and human wellbeing [21]. Hence the design of high-quality urban spaces that can be utilized by the 
people who live in the city is strongly related to the resident’s quality of life.  
 
The methods used to evaluate urban environment are divided in two main categories: the objective and 
the subjective [22]. The objective methods consider mainly the way that the quality of urban 
environment is affected by the economy while the subjective methods focus on the resident’s 
satisfaction with the urban livability [22].  
 
Although up to date there is no unified definition and measurement of “livability”, however the 
individual wellbeing and the quality of life as it concerns the urban environment are two terms that are 
often used in the literature in order to describe “livability” [22]. Livability’s multi-dimensional nature 
has led to the use of the term in different ways by various groups [14] and accordingly no consensus 
has been reached on the evaluation criteria used for the measurement of the urban livability. 
Nevertheless, all different definitions indicate that livability is associated to the physical and 
sociocultural environment in urban areas, while the numerous indices developed for assessing the level 
of urban livability have many elements in common and minor differences [22]. Hence, the factors that 
affect resident’s contentment constitute the determinants for planning and design attractive and livable 
urban spaces. 
 
 Public space  
 
The outdoor public spaces are areas that citizens have free access and range in type from civic set pieces 
such as parks and squares to streets and informal open spaces in the city [23] [9]. At the public spaces, 
a great variety of recreational, commercial, and outdoor activities are accommodated [9], hence they 
play a significant role in daily urban life while affecting the citizen’s outdoor activity and social 
interaction [23].

To this context, the public spaces add value to the experience of urban areas and respectively to the 
quality of the city since significant economic, social and environmental benefits are related to successful 
urban spaces [9].  As a result, the quality of citizens’ life is influenced by the public spaces [23], while 
the use of a public space constitutes one of the most important criteria for assessing the success of the

 Criteria that define the quality of a public space 
 
People’s perception on the quality and the use of the public spaces is influenced by several different 
factors [20]. The function of the space together with physiological and psychological parameters 
influence the human response to the physical environment and the decision making regarding the visit 
at a place [20]. The need for the defining the parameters that urban planners should consider in order to 
make inviting and attractive public spaces that people will broadly use has been detected many years 
ago. At the literature there is a significant number of guidelines addressing characteristics that high-
quality public spaces should have in order to be attractive. People’s satisfaction with a place is the main 
requirement and several surveys and studies have been conducted in order to be answered the question 
“What makes a place to be pleasant and thus used?” [25]. The answer seems to be quite objective. The 
latter is the reason for the existence of a great variety of approaches trying to define the framework 
under which the assessment of public spaces’ quality should be conducted. Part of the compilations and 
the empirical studies has focused on assembling knowledge related to the nature and the use of public 
spaces while others have focused on analysing people’s assessment of public spaces’ design and 
functionality [26] as well as their behaviour regarding the use of the outdoor public spaces.  
 
A significant number of different factors related to the quality of a public space has been described by 
studies conducted worldwide based on data collected with either theoretical or empirical methods. The 
variance of the factors provided in the literature is strongly related to the circumstances under which 
the data are collected and the type of analysis used for the data processing. A schematic representation 
of some of the criteria used often to assess the public spaces’ quality is shown at Figure 2. Accessibility, 
activities, comfort and safety are some of the parameters that are often found in the literature as being 
the pillars of successful design encompassing many other important factors within the framework that 
they define.

 Microclimate analysis 
 
One of the important features involved at the design of urban spaces is the urban climatology, which is 
strongly related to the climate that prevails at the micro-scale level [30]. The small-scale pattern of the 
climate within a particular area is defined by the term “microclimate” [31]. The deviations in urban 
areas’ climate experienced at different places within a few kilometres in the same region result primarily 
from the variations at the form and the materials of buildings and hardscape [32]. Especially, the 
topography, the soil structure, the ground cover and the urban forms are the main parameters that 
influence the microclimate [31]. Therefore, the combination of the local climate with the geographical 
location and the human interventions that shape the built environment of a place affects the thermal 
comfort of the pedestrians and accordingly the use of the space [33]. Recently microclimate analysis 
tools, that predict the effect that various parameters have at the microclimate, gain architects and urban 
planners’ appreciation since they enable the assessment of different design solutions [32]. 
 
The conduction of microclimate analyses facilitates the design of thermally comfort public spaces, since 
this type of analysis enables specifying the correlation between urban spaces’ characteristics and the 
climate conditions [32]. The microclimate simulation software are the main tools used at the 
microclimate analyses for modelling the surface-plant-air interaction in urban environments [32] and 
plotting the relevant meteorological variables. One of the most important parameters that affect the 
findings of the microclimate analysis refers to the consideration of the boundary conditions. The human 
perception regarding the outdoor thermal comfort determines the acceptable limits that are used in order 
to evaluate the design solutions [32]. 
 
Indices that provide information about the people’s assessment of climate conditions, are utilized at 
microclimate analyses for defining the thermal comfort acceptability criteria that meet the design goals 
for urban spaces. In this regard, the microclimate analyses constitute a comprehensive tool for 
considering the thermal comfort at urban planning and design. The combination of climate data with 
information about human behaviour can lead to design solutions that fulfil planning requirements for 
high usage of urban spaces. Hence, particular importance should be given at the consideration of the 
means used to assess bioclimatic conditions for humans and respectively at the defined boundaries that 
indicate the limits of the optimal experience. 
 
 Thermal indices 
 
The intensity that human activities take place in public spaces is significantly influenced by the 
satisfaction or dissatisfaction that people experience in the outdoor areas [31]. The climatic conditions 
as well as aesthetic and psychological aspects are some of the main factors involved at the decision 
making for the use of the urban spaces [20]. Although, the prioritization of the factors has a subjective 
nature, it has been proven that the thermal comfort has significant part at the satisfaction related to the 
==PAGE:21==
Literature review 
 
21
use of outdoor space [31]. Hence, understanding the thermal comfort conditions in the urban context 
and consider them at urban planning could significantly facilitate the design and use of urban spaces 
and respectively the cities’ development [34]. 
 
In particular, thermal comfort is defined as “the condition of mind in which satisfaction is expressed 
with the thermal environment” [23]. The thermal comfort is related to the physical, physiological and 
psychological responses of the people to the environmental variables [35]. Both physical (e.g. ambient 
temperature, air velocity, relative humidity and mean radiant temperature) and external parameters (e.g. 
activity level, clothing) [31] are considered for determining the thermal comfort sensation. One of the 
main tools used for assessing the interaction between the human body and the surrounding environment 
are the comfort indices that are developed within the research scope of human biometeorology [20]. 
 
The assessment of the outdoor thermal environment in a valid and practical way is important for several 
science fields that are related to various aspects of human life (e.g., public health, urban planning, 
climate impact research, tourism, recreation and public weather services) [36]. Therefore, the need for 
the development of models that facilitate procedures of thermal comfort assessment was created over 
the last decades [37]. The models that simulate the human thermal perception are called thermal indices 
and they are used as indicators of the strain caused by climatic conditions [38]. The wide use of the 
thermal indices in various areas for different purposes has led to the development of a great number of 
indices (more than 100) the past century [39].  
 
The thermal indices can be categorized in three main groups. The “direct indices” that are based on 
direct measurements of environmental variables, the “empirical indices” based on subjective and 
objective strain and the “rational indices” based on human body heat balance equation [39]. Although 
direct indices are more user friendly since they are based on the monitoring of environmental conditions, 
the indices of the other two groups have advantages that make them suitable for several 
implementations. The indices’ applicability is strongly affected by their individual characteristics and 
the limitations related to the parameters considered for their calculation. Research over the last years 
has revealed that simple indices exhibit important shortcomings compared to heat budget models [40], 
while indices derived from human heat-balance models provide more accurate representation of the 
impact that environmental conditions have to humans [37]. The growing awareness of the value from 
the consideration of the interaction between the human body and the thermal environment on the 
estimation of the thermal sensation has increased the use of the thermo-physiological indices [38].  
 
The term “thermal environment” refers both to the atmospheric heat exchanges with the body (stress) 
and the physiological response of the body (strain) [40]. Therefore, in order to describe the thermal 
environment a complete heat budget model that considers the heat exchange mechanisms has to be 
applied. The heat exchanges are influenced by the environmental parameters (air temperature, water 
vapour pressure, wind velocity and the mean radiant temperature), the metabolic rate and the clothing 
insulation of the human [40]. Consequently, there is an essential requirement that for the various index-
values there should be a corresponding thermo-physiological state (strain intensity), irrespectively of 
the combination of climate parameters [40] . The latter cannot be provided by simple indices. The 
thermal climate models can be utilized to various biometeorological applications and a wide range of 
climate zones, seasons and regions only when considering all the parameters involved at the human 
heat budget model [40]. Therefore, in the research for the thermal comfort in the outdoor environment 
where the climate conditions are changing, the thermo-physiological indices are appropriate to be 
considered [38].

The majority of the thermal sensation indices are based on steady state models, assuming that the 
people’s exposure to climatic environment over time results to the thermal equilibrium [8]. These 
models provide numerical elaboration of the energy balance without counting the dynamic aspects of 
the human thermal adaptation [8]. Some of the indices that belong at this category are the PMV 
(Predicted Mean Vote Index), the PET (Physiological Equivalent Temperature), the ITS (Index of 
Thermal Stress), the OUT-SET and the COMFA [8]. The PMV and the PET are two indices that have 
been widely used at the outdoor thermal comfort studies the last decades. The PMV predicts the mean 
thermal response of a large group of people on the 7-point scale of thermal sensation. Whilst the PET 
is a temperature dimension index used in order the changes in human thermal comfort to be assessed 
[41]. Especially PET provides the equivalent temperature of a reference environment that would cause 
the same heat balance in the subject as the conditions that are assessed [39]. 
  
Studies that investigated the relation between the calculated thermal indices and the perceived thermal 
sensation of people that experience specific environmental conditions, showed that the generic 
applicability of steady state models such us PMV is in doubt. The indices used until the beginning the 
21st century could not meet the need for a unique representation of the thermo-physiological effect by 
the individual index values, irrespectively of the combination of the input values (e.g., meteorological 
parameters) [42]. In principle, the heat budget models, considered by the indices, were developed in 
order to be used for the assessment of several types of thermal environment [40]. However probably 
due to the weaknesses related to thermo-physiology and heat exchange theory, none of these indices 
were accepted as a fundamental standard [40]. The rational expression of bioclimatic conditions only 
under specific climate conditions was found to be valid for several of the indices used until the 
beginning of 2000 [39]. Therefore, raised the requirement for the use of a universal index, that could 
apply in all different outdoor conditions. The ability of an index to represent bioclimatic conditions that 
apply to human strain under climatic conditions ranging from extreme cold to extreme heat [39] led to 
the development of the Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI).  
 
 UTCI 
 
Purpose 
The need for using an international thermal index, based on the recent scientific progress in thermo-
physiology and in the heat exchange theory was the reason for the development of the Universal 
Thermal Index (UTCI) [41]. The “universal” feature of the thermal index refers to the applicability of 
the index in all the outdoor thermal conditions at applications in the area of “public weather service, 
public health system, precautionary planning and climate impact research in health sector” [40]. 
According to the executive summary of the UTCI description [42], the specific index was developed in 
order to meet the following requirements: 
 
Thermo-physiologically responsive to the whole range of heat exchange between humans and 
environment, of existing thermal conditions 
 
Appropriate for being used in various spatial, climates, seasons and time scales 
 
Suitable for key applications in human biometeorology. 
 
The development of the UTCI aimed at integrating the existing knowledge into a thermal index that 
could be used for the assessment of the outdoor thermal environment [42]. The COST (European 
Cooperation in Science and Technology) Action 730 contributed to the development of the UTCI, by 
==PAGE:23==

23
providing the framework for the collaboration of European scientists and scientists from Canada, Israel, 
New Zealand and Australia [40]. 
 
Definition 
The Universal Thermal Climate Index was developed as an equivalent temperature expressed in oC [36] 
and is defined as “the equivalent ambient temperature (oC) of a reference environment providing the 
same physiological response of a refence person as the actual environment” [39]. The characteristics 
of the reference environment to which all the other climatic conditions are compared, have the following 
attributes: i) 50% relative humidity (with vapor pressure less than 20hPa), ii) calm air observed at 10m 
above ground (0,5m/s) and iii) radiant temperature equal to air temperature (ambient temperature) 
≤29oC [36]. Considering the latter, the UTCI index provides “the air temperature of the reference 
environment which would elicit the same physiological reaction as predicted by the dynamic response 
of the physiological model” [36].  
 
Background 
The basis for the development of UTCI is the multi-node ‘Fiala’ model of human thermoregulation 
augmented with a state-of-the-art clothing model [36]. The ‘Fiala’ model is a mathematical model that 
predicts the human regulatory and the thermal responses in a wide range of environmental conditions 
(e.g., cold, cool, neutral and warm) [43]. The active system of the model simulates i) the heat transport 
mechanisms within the body and ii) the heat exchange between the 12 body elements ( [39]) and the 
surrounding environment [43]. The active system simulates “the thermoregulatory reactions of 
shivering, sweating, and peripheral vasomotion of unacclimatised subjects” [43]. 
 
The people’s clothing adaptation is strongly affected by their perception of the outdoor environment 
[39]. Therefore, at the clothing model the following were taken into account: i) the urban population’s 
clothing behaviour (i.e. the insulation they put on) in accordance to actual air temperature, ii) the 
clothing of different body parts that provides local insulation values for the various parts of the model 
and iii) the decrease at the evaporative and the thermal clothing resistance, related to the wind and the 
movement of the people. The walking activity of 4km/h that corresponds to 135W/m2 metabolic rate is 
considered at the model [36]. The clothing adaptation to the air temperature is assumed of being non-
linear, ranging between Icl=0.4 to 3.0clo [42]. 
 
The coupling of the thermoregulation and the clothing model described above resulted in the 
development of the UTCI. For any combination of humidity, radiation, air temperature and wind, the 
calculated UTCI value refers to the air temperature of the reference environment that produces the same 
strain index [42]. The single dimensional strain index is the result of the integration of the seven 
variables (rectal and mean skin temperature, face temperature, sweat production, skin wettedness, skin 
blood flow, and shivering) produced by a cluster analysis. The cluster analysis (principal component 
analysis) conducted over a wide range of data in order to be feasible that the multidimensional 
physiological response is considered without being required to compare multidimensional physiological 
responses between the reference and the real environment. Besides, a single value is used for translating 
any condition into the relevant reference condition [44]. The whole concept of the UTCI calculation is 
described in Figure 4.

The abovementioned four meteorological parameters constitute the input at the six-order polynomial

FORTRAN and ii) the online executable program (http://www.utci.org/utcineu/utcineu.php) have

Mean radiant temperature: 
The mean radiant temperature (Tmrt) is defined as “the uniform temperature of an imaginary enclosure 
in which the radiant heat transfer from the human body is equal to the radiant heat transfer in the actual 
non-uniform enclosure” [46]. The particular environmental parameter is used for expressing how is the 
radiation experienced by the humans in their adjacent environment [47]. Based on the energy transfer 
consideration, the mean radiant temperature (Tmrt) is an indicator of the sun radiation’s effect on our 
heat sensation without being related to the personalized perception [13]. The estimation of the mean 
radiant temperature (Tmrt) outdoors is rather challenging since it is related to the temperature of the 
surrounding buildings, the ground, the sky and the vegetation [47]. Hence the calculation of the Tmrt is 
accomplished by using an equation that combines the interaction of air temperature, wind speed and 
globe temperature Tmrt = f (Ta, va, Tg). 
 
UTCI assessment scale 
The various UTCI values resulting from the operational procedure of UTCI calculation, are categorized 
in terms of thermal stress in ten categories (Figure 4). The physiological responses to the conditions of 
the reference environment are used for the deduction of the load caused by the physiological and 
thermoregulatory responses of the human body to the conditions of the actual environment [37]. Hence, 
the assessment scale refers to a “temperature based physiological strain” [39] with values categorized 
at levels that range from the “extreme cold stress” to the “extreme heat stress”, defining also a “no 
thermal stress” category.

Overall evaluation  
An extensive research on the UTCI applicability, resulted that UTCI is able to provide good assessment 
of the human physiological response at a wide range of climatic conditions [36] and consequently is 
suitable for applications in various areas (e.g. urban planning, environmental epidemiology, daily 
forecasts) [36]. However, the UTCI has the following shortcomings: 
 
only one activity level (walking with fixed metabolic rate of 2.3MET) is considered at the 
calculation [48],  
 
the static clothing insulation is adjusted to the air temperature and no further customization is 
possible [48] and  
 
wind speed values lower than 0.5m/s cannot be considered at the calculation, excluding null 
values that may occur around buildings [32]. 
 
Despite the limitations, the UTCI has features that prove the eligibility of the specific index in research 
studies. According to the literature UTCI: 
 
provides valid assessment of the thermal comfort at a significant variance of climates and 
locations [39] 
 
has high sensitivity at the surrounding stimuli (e.g., wind speed, air temperature, humidity and 
radiation) reflecting even slight changes [39] 
 
has been developed based on the latest scientific progress in thermo-physiology [40], heat 
exchange theory and biophysical modelling [36] 
 
is readily determined by utilizing simply accessible measurements of four climatic parameters 
[48]  
 
The aforementioned characteristics amplify the argument that UTCI is “a state-of-the-art-work in 
human thermal sensation and comfort studies” and a useful tool that can be used in various studies 
within the area of urban planning and design [48] .

 Outdoor thermal comfort and outdoor activities 
 
As it concerns the microclimate, at the past decades several research projects focused on the 
investigation of the outdoor thermal comfort at different climates worldwide. The studies were 
conducted either under a thermophysiological perspective with regard to the modelling and the 
assessment methods or they were conducted in order to identify the climatic parameters that influence 
the humans’ thermal comfort [8]. The relation between the people’s behaviour at the public spaces 
regarding the climatic conditions was first studied by Jan Gehl in 1971 [8]. At the influential work “Life 
between buildings” it was investigated the impact of the climatic conditions at people’s choice to 
conduct various activities at public spaces [8]. Since then, a variety of studies with different research 
methods conducted in order to be investigated the relation between the use of the outdoor public spaces 
and the thermal comfort [8].

The differences at the research perspective, are associated with the considered levels of the assessment 
framework utilized for the description of the link between the climatic conditions and the human 
behaviour [8]. The physical level refer to the measurements of the climatic conditions (e.g., temperature, 
wind speed, humidity, radiation etc.) and the information about the landscape design (e.g., building 
form). The physiological level concerns the thermoregulation models regarding the energy balance of 
the human body [8]. The psychological aspect of the assessment refers to people’s expectations and the 
past experiences related to the use of an outdoor space as they result from the participants answers at 
structured interviews. The social/behavioural aspect considers the activities that can be conducted due 
to the function of the public space and the people’s preference on conducting particular activities.  
 
The type of the data required to fulfil the research objectives entails the use of a proper method for the 
collection of the data. The main methods used at are: i) the measurements, ii) the observations, iii) the 
interviews and iv) the questionnaires. The systematic recording of measurements and observations are 
used for the depiction of the occurred conditions while the interviews and questionnaires introduce the 
subjective opinion of the participants providing a valuable input on the people’s perception about the 
topic of the survey. For several categories of data, more than one method or a combination of methods 
are used. The latter applies to the data related both to the climatic conditions and to people’s behaviour.  
 
The climatic conditions are considered either directly as they are measured by the relevant equipment 
or through the thermal comfort rationale. The assessment of the thermal comfort can be done through 
the calculation of thermal indices or through the declared perception of people (interviews, 
questionnaires). In this regard, the human satisfaction or dissatisfaction with the weather conditions is 
taken into account and it is used as the basis for the interpretation of the findings. At the various studies 
the people’s behaviour is considered mainly through the counting of their presence. Generic 
observations or assumptions about people’s intention to use a place are also considered.  
 
The analysis of the collected data at the reviewed studies is accomplished mainly with two methods. 
The frequency distribution method and the regression models are used to produce diagrams describing 
the findings derived from the data analysis. These two analysis methods are scientifically reliable and 
in many cases both of them are used to depict different aspects of the gathered information. The time 
period, the place and the climatic conditions occurred during the research are parameters that differ in 
various studies. Table 1, summarizes the information about the aforementioned characteristics at the 
reviewed studies.

All the studies at Table 1 have as main research focus the investigation of people’s behaviour at the 
public spaces in relation to the climatic conditions. At Nikolopoulou et al. (2001) [16], it was 
investigated the relation between the predicted thermal comfort as defined by the thermal index PMV 
and the thermal comfort declared by the people who participated at the study’s interviews. The field 
studies took place in Cambridge during winter, spring, and summer 1997 and the main outcome was 
that there is a significant discrepancy between the predicted thermal comfort (PMV) and the 
interviewees’ thermal sensation at the specific case study.  
 
The effect of climatic conditions at the usage level of seven urban open spaces in Montreal (Canada) 
was investigated at the study conducted by Zaharias et al. [50]. At this study the measurements of the 
air temperature and the wind speed at sunny and shaded areas are combined with the counting of 
people’s presence that sit, stand, and smoke at the examined areas. Multiple regression analyses 
provided quantitative information about the effect of climatic parameters to the presence of people. The 
latter revealed preferences for particular local climatic conditions and a strong relationship between the 
air temperature and the sitting activity [8]. 
 
The study conducted by Thorsson et al. (2004) [51] reveals the effect of the thermal environment on 
the use of an urban park in Göteborg (Sweden). The study conducted between July and October 2002 
and the answers from questionnaires were compared with the predicted mean vote (PMV) index and 
the mean radiant temperature (Tmrt) [51]. Regression analysis and frequency distribution method were 
both used to elaborate on the collected data. The calculated discrepancy between the thermal sensation 
assessed by PMV and the relevant sensation experienced by the interviewees, led to the conclusion that 
maybe the PMV index is not appropriate for the assessment of short-term thermal comfort [51]. 
Moreover, it was indicated that psychological aspects (e.g., thermal expectations and time of exposure) 
may have a significant effect on the subjective assessment. 
 
Meteorological measurements and observations of people’s presence are the main tools used at the 
Ktzschner’s study (2006) [52]. The main goal of the study is to investigate the effect of the outdoor 
thermal conditions on people’s behaviour [52]. The data were collected on the 20th of June 2005 and 
the study areas are two open spaces near a bistro in Kassel (Germany). The observations on people’s 
behaviour were combined with the Physiological Equivalent Temperature (PET) index that was used to 
consider thermal comfort. The findings are in line with the Thorsson et al. (2004) study [51], showing 
that apart from the outdoor thermal conditions, the individual expectations of the weather and the 
activities that can be accommodated at public spaces have significant influence at people’s behaviour. 
 
The usage levels in two different outdoor public spaces in a satellite city of Tokyo (Japan) with regard 
to the perceived thermal comfort is the main objective of the Thorsson et al. (2007) study [53]. The 
investigation focuses on the influence of thermal environment’s spatial and temporal variations on 
people’s subjective perception and on space’s usage [53]. Structured interviews, measurements of 
micrometeorological parameters and observations were used for the collection of data. The field surveys 
took place on the following periods: i)12-24 March 2004 and ii) 16-26 May 2005. The subjective 
thermal sensation was considered through 1192 answers of the provided questionnaires while PET was 
used for quantifying the objective thermal comfort. One of the main outcomes of the current study is 
that the influence of the microclimate at the park’s usage level is more notable than the corresponding 
influence on the square’s usage, assumed at being attributed to the different functions of the two places 
[53]. Of equal importance is the observation that for the particular case studies the total attendance at 
the public spaces shows low relation to the thermal environment.

Another study that conducted at two places with different characteristics is the Nikolopoulou et al. 
(2007) [15]. Microclimatic monitoring, observations and interviews of 1503 individuals that provide 
information about the people’s perception on the environment are the main tools used at the specific 
study. The field surveys conducted at a neighbourhood square and at a seashore area in Athens (Greece) 
from July 1001 until March 2002 [15]. The regression analysis of the counted number of people with 
regard to the variance of different meteorological parameters, revealed the predominant influence of air 
temperature and solar radiation at the use of the public spaces. Moreover, the consideration of people’s 
social characteristics (e.g., age, sex etc.) at the study led to conclusions regarding the sensitivity of 
various age groups at the microclimatic conditions and respectively at the use of the particular spaces 
in a Mediterranean city. 
 
Eliasson et al. [20] studied the effect of the microclimate on the people’s perception in urban outdoor 
environment focusing on the Nordic city of Gothenburg (Sweden). Interviews and observations of 
human activity at four open public spaces were conducted at the same time with measurements of 
meteorological variables at all places. The field studies conducted in October 2003, January2004, 
April2004 and June 2004 while the study period at each month was five days. Hence, in total 20 days 
were occupied for the data collection. Multiple regression analyses were performed in order to examine 
the way that the three meteorological parameters (air temperature Ta, wind speed w and clearness index 
CI) affect the people’s assessment of the weather and their behavioural and perceptual estimations for 
the four case studies [20]. The significant influence of the three meteorological parameters on people’s 
evaluations was disclosed, while the study’s findings confirmed the importance of climate-sensitive 
planning in future urban planning and design [20]. 
 
In contrast to the aforementioned studies that conducted in areas with temperate climate, Lin Tzu-Ping 
(2009) [54], conducted a relevant study in the hot and humid region of Taichung City in Taiwan. A 
public square at the National Taiwan Museum of Fine Arts is the site that considered in the study. 
Measurements of climatic conditions, observations of attendance in the square and a questionnaire 
survey took place at a period of 21 days, between the 15th of April 2005 and the 2nd of February 2008. 
The investigation of the thermal comfort and the possible correlations between the occupancy of the 
square and the thermal environment constitute the main objectives of the study.  The attendance of the 
people in the square was counted by utilizing high-resolution photographs (taken at 10min intervals) 
while the considered thermal index in this study is PET. One of the main outcomes of the study [54] is 
the observed discrepancy between the people’s acceptable thermal comfort range provided by similar 
studies in Central/western Europe and the acceptable range of the respondents in Taiwan. The analysis 
of the data also suggested a positive correlation between the people’s satisfaction with the thermal 
environment and the level of autonomy related to their choice to visit the place [54]. 
 
At the studies described above, different approaches were applied aiming to describe the relationship 
between the outdoor thermal comfort and the outdoor activity. The findings of the studies have both 
similarities and differences that are related to each case’s particular characteristics. However, the main 
mutual outcome derived from all the studies is that the microclimate significantly influences the use of 
the public spaces. Hence, the consideration of the knowledge about the relationship between the 
microclimate and people’s behaviour at urban planning and design can be vitally important for the 
identification of optimal solutions that will enhance outdoor spaces’ livability. Considering the 
differences resulted from studies conducted in different countries with diverse climate conditions and 
cultural background, the current study aims to identify the variation level of the outdoor spaces 
occupancy in the Nordic city with regard to the change of the thermal comfort conditions, as they are 
described by the thermal index UTCI.

3. Methodology 
 
 
Detailed monitoring of the climatic conditions and simultaneous observations of the people’s presence 
at four public spaces are conducted within the framework of the current study. Video recordings are 
gathered during the field study in order to be captured the people’s response under specific weather 
conditions. The video recordings are assembled into 2-3 minutes video documentaries that can be used 
for further analysis. 
 
The observations of the utilization of public spaces from people that execute various activities under 
recorded weather conditions, are used for producing the climate response graphs that depict the people’s 
response to the microclimate at the various public spaces. The extent of a place’s occupancy is 
demonstrated by the “Utilization/Capacity” ratio, while the microclimate is considered through the 
thermal index UTCI. In this regard, general tendencies for the relation between the use of urban spaces 
and the thermal comfort are illustrated.  
 
The dependency of the UTCI from the four weather parameters considered at the calculation of the 
specific index, is examined through the sensitivity analysis. The effect that each one of the four 
parameters has at the UTCI calculation, is an indication about the extent that the thermal comfort 
(represented by the UTCI) is influenced by the change of the intensity of a specific climatic parameter. 
The latter can facilitate the design of the urban spaces through the implementation of engineering 
interventions, that provide protection from a specific weather parameter (e.g., wind) that negatively 
influences the experienced thermal sensation. 
 
 Field data collection 
 
The collection of the data was carried out at four public spaces in the city center of Copenhagen in two 
time periods. The student that conducts the current master project collected the data for the period 
December 2020 - March 2021 (Data set A). The data for the period April - June 2018 (Data set B) is 
collected from the students that participated at a Special Course within the framework of their master 
studies at DTU (Technical University of Denmark). The data is organised in two different groups due 
to reasons that are related to the analysis. Detailed information about the particular reasons is provided 
at the §4. Nevertheless, the core structure of the current (Data set A) and the former measurements 
(Data set B) is the same as it concerns the field areas, the equipment used for the monitoring and the 
investigated activities. Hence, the process about the field collection described herein refers to both data

Utilization/Capacity ratio 
The utilization of the public spaces for particular activities with respect to the place’s capacity, provide 
information about the occupancy level of the examined areas. In this context, the Utilization/Capacity 
(U/C) ratio is used as an indicator about the public spaces’ occupancy. The utilization (U) refers to the 
people conducting a specific activity at the public space, while the capacity (C) refers to maximum 
public space’s capability to accommodate people performing the same activity. The assessment of the 
level of the public spaces’ occupancy conducted both during the field surveys and after revisiting the 
video documentaries created for the particular measurements. The latter applies mainly at the 
assessment of the utilization/capacity ratio that refer to the observations of the Data set B (2018), since 
they were not conducted by the same person that collected the data of the Data set A. Hence, the 
beholder of the Data set A, reassessed the U/C of the Data set B though the video documentaries, in 
order to reduce the conflicts that may occur due to the objective perception at the observations.  
 
 The process  
 
The analysis of the collected data (measurements and observations) aims to the production of the 
climate response graphs that provide information about the people’s behaviour regarding the climate 
conditions as this is expressed by their presence at the public spaces conducting various activities. The 
graphs are produced for each one of the provided activities at the four places that the measurements are 
conducted. Therefore, these graphs depict the people’s decision to conduct a particular activity under 
specific weather conditions at a particular day and the time.  
 
The effect of the non-climatic parameters on the occupancy of the places may bias the findings that 
refer to the relation between the microclimate and the people’s presence. The day and the time that the 
data is collected constitutes one significant factor that affects the visits of a place, since it has been 
observed that on the weekends more people are visiting the public spaces. Therefore, if data recorded 
at various days and the time periods is plotted in the same graph, the influence of the climatic conditions 
to the occupancy of the place may be overshadowed by the influence that the day of the recording has 
at the U/C ratio. In this regard, the determination of a common basis for all the processed data constitutes 
an important requirement for extracting reasonable conclusions.  
 
The common basis regarding the day and the time, is achieved by adjusting the data collected at various 
days and times to the corresponding data that could have been measured at a specific day and time. The 
scaling of the collected data to refer at the day and the time that the public spaces exhibit the highest 
occupancy, could constitute the basis for prognosis. The prediction of what is “likely” to happen is 
described by the term “plausible” when referring to the categorization of the future actions [62]. 
Therefore, the plausible occupancy of a place under specific weather conditions can be defined from 
the analysis of the scaled data. Detailed information about the particular parts of the process is provided 
below. 
 
 
 Calculation of the UTCI

 The U/C ratio 
  
The observations during the field studies and the video documentaries created for the particular 
measurements were utilized, for assessing the Utilization/Capacity ratio of the public spaces for various 
activities. At the current study the U/C ratio is used as measure for the intensity of the use of a place. 
Therefore, counting of the exact number of the people would not add significant value to the findings. 
The exact number of people that describes 100% occupancy at an outdoor public space may varies if 
considered different criteria for the acceptable proximity limits among place’s users. In addition, the 
counting of people that execute dynamic activities (e.g. walking, brisk) at a public space with big area 
(e.g. Kongens Have, Nyhavn) without special technical equipment (e.g. drone) might not be feasible 
from one beholder.  
 
On the contrary, the quantification of general observations through a readily used scale that translates 
individuals’ assessments of the utilization of a public space into specific values fulfils appropriately the 
==PAGE:42==
Methodology 
 
42
study’s objectives. The utilization/capacity ratio is assessed by the beholder and the assignment to the 
relevant value considered at the study is done in accordance with the scale described at Figure 15. 
 
 
Figure 15: Assessment scale for the Utilization/Capacity ratio. 
At the background of the U/C assessment for the static activities (e.g., sitting) the interpersonal zones 
prescribed by “proxemics” are considered. The term “proxemics” refers to the study of the social 
spacing in humans [65] considering the way that people use the space in their interactions [66]. The 
studies regarding the effects that the proximity has on the social behaviour of people constitute the basis 
for the description of interpersonal zones that refer to the various ranges of physical distance between 
people that interact. Broad research on proxemics have showed that the distances defined by the 
interpersonal zones may differ cross-culturally [67]. According to [67], the relevant zones that apply to 
the Western culture are defined considering the following distances between people: 
 
Figure 16: Graphical depiction of the interpersonal distances of man as defined by Edward T.Hall 
The intimate distance delineates the space around humans that involve direct contact, while the personal 
zone is the area where interactions among people can take place allowing the physical contact [66].The 
social distance is considered the distance among people that allows social interaction and a bit further 
is defined the public distance where the physical contact is not possible, and the eye contact is limited. 
 
At the assessment of the U/C ratio for the activities “Sitting on a bench” and “Sitting on a 
café/restaurant” the social distance is considered. Hence, a street restaurant that has five tables with 
four chairs at each table, is considered as fully occupied if at least one chair at all the tables is occupied. 
It is assumed that a new customer will not have the opportunity to take a seat if the social distance 
constitutes a criterion for sitting at a place with acceptable proximity to other people.  
 
Similar rational applies to the estimation of U/C ratio for the activity “Sitting on a bench”. For example, 
if there are two people sitting on a bench the U/C is not assessed as moderate (U/C=0,50) but rather as 
“almost filled” since it is considered that the bench will be filled if one or maximum two more people 
will seat there. For the situations that criteria of social distancing are not met and more than 4 people 
==PAGE:43==
Methodology 
 
43
sit on a bench, the characterization of “crowded” (U/C=1.1) and “overcrowded” (U/C=1.2) could be 
used for describing the occupancy of the benches. The same values of the assessment scale (U/C=1.1 
or U/C=1.2) are used for describing the utilization of a public space that unregulated activities occur as 
an extension to regulated activities. Therefore, if all the benches at a place are occupied and people are 
sitting on structures not indented for sitting then the place’s U/C ratio for the specific activity is 
characterized as overcrowded or crammed. 
 
For the Data set A, that observations were conducted under a period with recommendations for social 
distancing, the full occupancy of the bench is considered when only two people are sitting at the bench. 
The social distancing recommendations deter the occupancy of the bench from people that do not belong 
to the same household. Therefore, the occupancy level for this activity is slightly different compared to 
the corresponding of the Data set B. 
 
 
 Scaling data 
 
The U/C ratios, collected through on-site observations, provide information about the presence of the 
people in the public place during the measurement. However, for further analysis of the findings, it is 
required the establishment of a common basis regarding the day and the time, when the measurements 
were conducted. This common basis refers to the day and the time that the public spaces exhibit the 
highest occupancy since the latest satisfies the research objectives of the current study. 
 
In order to be identified the time during a week that the public spaces accommodate the maximum 
number of visitors both personal observations and data provided by Google, were utilized. The initial 
plan was to collect data for the popularity of the places from people who work at restaurants/café located 
close to the four public spaces. However, due to the lockdown this option was not possible to occur, 
and the relevant data was collected from the conductor of the current study. The daily visits during a 
consecutive period of two weeks at the four public spaces showed that there is a significant variance at 
public spaces occupancy between weekends and weekdays, with the highest number of people being 
witnessed during weekend. The latter was combined with the information provided by the “popular 
time graphs” developed by Google, that depict relevant and more detailed information by using a 
significant amount of data.  
 
Especially, the “popular times graphs” are the histograms provided by Google, that show how busy is 
a location typically during various times at different days [68]. Aggregated and anonymized data from 
users that have activated the “Google Location History” [68] are collected at a few months period before 
the production of the graphs. At the “popular time graphs”, the popularity of a place/business at any 
hour and day is depicted relative to the peak popularity of the place for a week [68] . Hence, the values 
of the histograms refer to the ratio of the utilization of a place at a specific time and day (UgmaxDay), over 
the max utilization that could have been observed at the specific place during the week (Ugmax). A typical 
“popular times graph” is shown at Figure 17. At the graph it is depicted the hourly change of Kongens 
Have occupancy in relation to the max occupancy that has been observed at this place. Therefore, the 
values at “popular times graphs” are already standardized and this makes them a useful tool for 
discerning general trends [69].

Based on the observations of the current study and the general knowledge about the intensity of the

mobile phone data that is considered to be a massive source of feeding. The information related to 
people’s presence provided through phone tracking has a significant level of spatial and temporal 
accuracy, since the collection of this type of data is done in high frequency and with high spatial 
resolution [71].

Plotting lines that connect the successive points provides valuable information about the local changes 
that occur from point to point. However, the use of trendlines is considered as a more suitable tool to 
show the general trend indicated by the data. The trendlines are the lines that best fit at the points of the 
graphs aiming to capture the prevailing direction of the values. Moreover, the trendlines can establish 
the basis for performance comparisons and they can be used in forecasting [72]. The variety of the 
trendlines is significant and the type of the trendline that is appropriate for a particular analysis depends 
both on the type of data and the information that is required to be obtained. Two of the main categories 
used for the classification of the trendlines based on the method used for their production are: i) free-
hand curves and ii) curves resulted from regression analysis.  
 
The trendlines produced with the free-hand method are curves drawn though the plotted points of a 
scatter diagram so that the majority of the points lie close to the curve [73] without being required that 
the points precisely fall on the curve. The freehand curves are scarified by individuals, who provide to 
the curves the smoothness that efficiently describes the data based on their personal perception [73]. 
Although, this method is simple and can be easily used, the reliability of the method is limited due to 
the personal bias [74]. The individuals may draw curves that may vary in slope and intercept [73] 
providing different information for the same data.  
 
Another category of trendlines is produced through regression analysis, which is a set of statistical 
processes used in order to estimate the relationship between variables [75]. The fitted lines describe the 
upward or downward trend of the points of the scatter plot while the coefficient of determination R2 
that shows how well trendline fits the data, can be readily calculated with the use of the relevant 
software. In the current study, trendlines resulting from regression analyses are mainly used to illustrate 
the relationship between the UTCI and the Utilization/Capacity ratio of the public spaces. The 
description of the relationship between the examined variables with a model based on a widely used 
statistical technique attach importance to the findings since no personal bias occurs from the person 
handling the data.  
 
The identical calculation method of the trendlines that result from regression analysis, enables the 
comparison of the trendlines and the extraction of coherent conclusions. Therefore, the specific method 
is applied to describe the tendencies that reveal from the process of the data. However, the regression 
trendlines cannot always efficiently describe the available data. The limited amount of processed data 
and the significant scattering might be related to the poor fitting of the regression trendlines with the 
data. In these cases, free-hand lines are used in order to incorporate information related to the particular 
==PAGE:47==
Methodology 
 
47
measurements and illustrate more accurately the real trends. At the produced climate response graphs 
both the regression trendlines and the free hand trendlines (when required) are displayed.  
 
As it concerns the regression trendlines, the fluctuation of data points of the current study is better 
described using polynomial trendlines. The low order polynomial curves provide the level of accuracy 
required for mapping the information provided by the available data. The value of the R2 data constitutes 
a useful tool for identifying the trendline that better describes the data at particular cases. The main 
requirement is to be captured the general trend revealed by the whole range of data. Hence, the use of 
high order polynomials trendlines that consider the individual data is not proper for the objectives of 
the current study. As a result, although at some cases the R2 value is not high (i.e., close to 1.0 indicating 
the best fit of the line with data), the trendlines are used to provide an indication about the tendency.

Holger H.Koss. A wide range of the values of the four climatic parameters, is considered through the

4. Analysis  
 
 
 
The analysis conducted within the framework of the current study contains two parts. At the first part, 
the data of the Data set A (2021) and Data set B (2018) is processed. The monitored climatic conditions, 
and the assessment of the utilization level of the public spaces are analysed in order to describe the 
observed human response to the climatic conditions. The produced climate response graphs that 
illustrate this response, provide the relevant information for particular activities at the examined public 
spaces for both data sets (Data set A and Data set B). The sensitivity analysis that shows the change 
rate of the calculated UTCI value with regard to the variation of the climatic conditions (involved at the 
UTCI calculation) constitutes the second part of the analysis. The produced graphs demonstrate the 
influence that the different climatic parameters have at the calculation of the UTCI and provide 
quantified information about the extent of their influence.  
 
The methodology described at the §3 is followed for the most part in the data processing of both data 
sets (A and B). However, due to limitations related to the collection period of the two data sets the 
processing cannot be identical. The main difference at the analysis of the two data sets is related to the 
scaling of the data with respect to the occupancy levels described at the §3.3.3. In particular, the 
adjustment of the observed Utilization-Capacity ratio of Data set B at the day and the time that public 
spaces exhibit the highest occupancy is not applied. Due to lack of available information about the 
occupancy levels at the various days and time periods during the spring-summer on 2018, the 
conversion of the data cannot be performed. The relevant input for the occupancy levels is utilized from 
the google “popular times graphs” that are available only at the present time. Hence information about 
previous years (2018) is not available.  
 
In addition, there is a limitation that refers to the collection period of the Data set A. The background 
of the analysis that utilizes observations regarding the presence of people in public spaces is affected 
by the outbreak of coronavirus disease (COVID-19). Restrictions in order to prevent the spread of the 
virus were adopted by the governments all around the world. The abrupt influence of the implied 
restrictions to the public life, differentiates the interpretation of the observations. Due to COVID-19 
pandemic, lockdown conditions occurred in Denmark during the period that the Data set B was collected 
(December 2020-March 2021). The cafes, restaurants, and shopping centers were closed during the 
whole period of interest while small retail shops first opened on the 1st of March. Moreover, 
recommendations for physical and social distancing have been issued disrupting people’s private and 
public life. The ambiguous influence of the pandemic at the people’s opportunities to visit a public 
place requires the separate analysis of recordings conducted at different periods [2018 (before 
pandemic) and 2021(during pandemic)]. Since, a combination of the relevant data could have biased 
the extracted conclusions about the people’s behaviour. Hence, the analysis is conducted separately for 
the two data sets in order to avoid reducing the comprehensiveness of the results attained from the 
combination of the different inputs.

effect on the data collected during weekends provides reliable information that can be used in order to 
do prognosis for the highest expected occupancy.

The comparison between the collected and the scaled data at §4.1.2 for all public spaces revealed that 
for the current study the removal of the effect that the day and the time have at the measurements is 
accomplished through low scaling. The scaled U/C values describe the plausible occupancy that can 
occur in the studied areas without changing the general trend described by the collected data. The strong 
scaling that may incorporate significant uncertainties, is avoided due to the time period that the data 
was collected. Hence, the scaled data are utilized for producing the climate response graphs that indicate 
the occupancy level that could occur at a public space under particular climatic conditions (described 
by the UTCI). For the thoroughness of the study the relevant diagrams from the collected data are 
included into the Appendix.

5. Discussion 
 
 
 
The climate response graphs presented at §4.1.3, §4.1.4, §4.2.3 and §4.2.3 illustrate the people’s 
response to the thermal environment in four public spaces in Copenhagen. The detailed description of 
the processed data and its graphical representation, provided by the relevant sections of the Chapter 4, 
constitute the basis for the elaboration that is required to address the central objective of the study and 
the related research questions (see §1.2). The collection of the data under significantly different 
circumstances entails the separate elaboration of the Data set A (winter 2020-2021) and Data set B 
(spring summer 2018). 
 
 No thermal stress to cold stress (Data set A) 
 
The findings, which are related to the occupancy of public spaces under cold and no thermal stress 
conditions, are derived by processing Data set A that has been collected under exceptional 
circumstances due to COVID-19 pandemic. The collection of this data set only during the weekends 
and the adjustment of the occupancy observations to the day and time, assumed as being the busiest for 
all public spaces, aim to reduce significantly the effect of the day and the time on the observed 
occupancy. However, the effect of the lockdown restrictions occurred during the on-site measurements 
could not be removed. Therefore, the observations are interpreted within the context of the pandemic. 
 
The climate response graphs reveal a general pattern. Especially, for the majority of the cases shown 
herein, the decrease in the UTCI values was found to reduce the number of people that undertake various 
outdoor activities. Nevertheless, the change rate of the public spaces’ occupancy related to a specific 
activity in various places may differ due to the function of the place and the type of the activity. For 
example, differences are observed between the change rate of the occupancy related to the “Sitting on 
the ground” activity with respect to the change of the climatic conditions in both Kongens Have and 
Nyhavn. The type of the existing sitting facilities in Kongens Have significantly influences the presence 
of people during the cold period. Especially, sitting on the green grass areas in Kongens Have requires 
higher comfort conditions to become significantly attractive for the citizens. In other words, the wetness 
and moisture of the grass deter the citizens to sit unless high enough UTCI values are observed.  Hence, 
only a minor change in the U/C ratio can be seen as the thermal conditions were changing in the area 
of no thermal stress. On the other hand, the wooden edge of the quay wall in Nyhavn can become more 
attractive even with small improvement in the thermal comfort conditions. Therefore, the latter can 
contribute to the increase in the occupancy with the change of the experienced thermal comfort is more 
rapid in Nyhavn.  
 
Moreover, for the activities “Brisk” and “Walking” at Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv, no influence is 
observed from the thermal environment on the number of people executing the specific activities. This 
observation may be attributed to the type of these activities with respect to the function of the places. 
More specifically, the observations regarding “Brisk” and “Walking” in these two squares refer mainly 
to people who pass through. Hence, the reason that causes the occurrence of these activities may have 
==PAGE:99==
Discussion

greater influence than the microclimate of the place, which is not designed to exclusively accommodate 
the particular activities. 
 
The influence of the function of a public space in the climate response graphs can be also observed for 
the activity “Sitting on a bench”. The different change rate of the climate response graphs for the 
specific activity in the various public spaces, indicates that the climatic conditions do not affect with 
the same way this outdoor activity for the different urban places monitored herein. At the central park 
(Kongens Have), which constitutes a highly preferred destination for the “Sitting on a bench” activity, 
the change rate of the related occupancy with respect to the climate conditions is moderate. On the other 
hand, the climate response curve, drawn based on data from the square of Axeltorv, shows only a weak 
variation of the U/C ratio with the change of the climate conditions. Therefore, both the place and the 
type of the activity should be considered when associating the thermal environment with the activity-
related occupancy of the public spaces. 
 
Despite the observed variance in the climate response graphs for various activities and the four public 
spaces, it is nearly a catholic observation that the total occupancy of the urban places does not 
significantly change when the UTCI values change from no thermal stress to slight cold stress. 
Excluding the activity “Sitting on a bench” that refers to the occupancy of the sitting facilities of a place 
and not directly to the occupancy of the place per se, it is observed that the presence of people in 
Kongens Have, Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv slightly increases for UTCI values ranging between 4oC 
and 16oC. Similarly, low increase in the utilization of Nyhavn is observed for UTCI values higher than 
4oC and lower than 12oC. In other words, for climatic conditions that refer to UTCI values close to the 
lower limit (UTCI=9oC) of the no thermal stress area, the change rate of the occupancy in the park, the 
two squares and the waterfront area of Nyhavn is insignificant when the weather gets slightly colder. 
The danish culture, which does not consider cold as a factor to deter the use of outdoor areas, may be 
one of the reasons that could explain the low decrease of the overall use of the places when there is a 
change on the experienced thermal comfort. However, the lockdown and the lack of opportunities for 
performing indoors activities during the Data set A measurements may also affect the findings. The 
impact of the different factors can be quantified more precisely with further analysis since the limited 
amount of the current data cannot provide sufficient input to scrutinize the influential factors. 
 
Finally, for climatic conditions corresponding to UTCI > 12oC, it is interesting to note that the highest 
increase in the presence of people is observed in Nyhavn. At the specific place, the U/C ratio for all 
activities rise notably when UTCI becomes higher than 12oC. In the current measurements (Data set 
A), the UTCI values higher than 12oC are mainly attributed to the presence of sun on cold winter days. 
Hence, on the sunny winter days, the increase in the occupancy at the waterfront area of Nyhavn is 
observed to be higher than the increase of the occupancy in the park and the two squares. Under the 
lockdown conditions, the effect of the neighbouring amenities on the occupancy of the examined places 
is eliminated since the majority of the adjacent facilities are closed. Therefore, it can be assumed that 
the high increase of the occupancy in Nyhavn is related to the landscape of the specific place indicating 
the preference of people to visit the specific seaside place on a sunny winter day.

The analysis of the data set shows that the various activities were influenced in different ways from the 
change of the climatic conditions. The climate response graphs for the static activities including “Laying

Additionally, the activity “Sitting on bench” shows a positive relation with the increased heat stress. 
On the other hand, for the majority of the places, the number of people walking decreases with the

was found to be associated with different tendencies (increasing, decreasing, stable) as the climate 
conditions are changing. Therefore, the significant variability in the observed trends indicates that the 
influence of the warm climatic conditions on a particular activity cannot sufficiently be assessed by 
accounting for only one characteristic of the activity (e.g., the metabolic rate).  
 
Furthermore, the change of the U/C ratio under climate conditions ranging from no heat stress to heat 
stress, shares similarities for a particular activity undertaken in various urban places. There are only 
marginal differences in both the shape and the change rate of the corresponding climate response graphs, 
the latter estimated for the four places considered herein. This common observation does not apply to 
the “Brisk” activity, since different trends between the related occupancy the UTCI values are observed 
for the examined public spaces. In addition, a differentiation of the climate response functions for 
“Standing” and “Walking” is observed for Axeltorv. In more details, a significant part of the occupancy 
in Axeltorv is normally related to the neighbouring facilities (e.g., Tivoli, theatres etc.) since plenty of 
people just pass through this place in order to visit an adjacent place of interest. On the other hand, the 
number of people visiting by intention Axeltorv in order to execute any activity is low. Hence, the 
climatic conditions have, de facto, almost zero influence on the occurrence of specific activities in this 
square. Nevertheless, considering the few exceptions mentioned above, it can be stated that activities 
will experience similar type of influence from warm conditions and this observation seems to be 
independent on the public space under investigation.  
 
According to the findings from analysing Data set B, the total occupancy at the park and the two squares 
were found to be slightly affected by the variety and the extent of the influence that the climatic 
conditions have on the people’ response. More specifically, the change of the total occupancy of both 
Kongens Have and Gråbrødretorv is limited for climate conditions, which vary from neutral to heat 
stress (9oC<UTCI<32oC) according to the UTCI assessment scale. It is only for UTCI>32oC that the 
total occupancy starts to decrease in Kongens Have and in Gråbrødretorv. Whilst nearly no climatic 
influence is observed for the occupancy of Axeltorv. The latter is, though, valid by excluding the “Siting 
on the bench” activity. The almost zero change in the occupancy of Axeltorv may be related to the 
function of the specific public space since, as mentioned above, the public life observed in the specific 
place is strongly related to activities taking place in the adjacent areas. On the other hand, for Nyhavn, 
which constitutes a destination for performing various activities, the decrease in the total occupancy is 
more notable with the change of the climate conditions from no thermal stress to heat stress 
(UTCI>26oC).

 Improve thermal comfort 
The analysis of the data collected both under cold and warm conditions indicates that the public life in 
the examined urban spaces of a Nordic city like Copenhagen, is not affected significantly when the 
environmental conditions change from the no thermal stress zone towards either slightly cold or slightly 
warm conditions respectively. For UTCI values from 4oC to 9oC (slight cold stress) and from 26oC to 
32oC (moderate heat stress), the use levels of public spaces are similar to the ones observed for the no 
thermal stress zone (26oC<UTCI<32oC). Therefore, when the design of an urban space is of interest, 
the urban planners can expect to see similar occupancies of the public spaces in a Nordic city for a quite 
long range of climate conditions, i.e., 4oC<UTCI<32oC. 
 
The current study favors also the investigation of the environmental parameters that mostly influence 
the levels of thermal comfort. The UTCI-related sensitivity analysis, presented by §4.3, shows the 
significant influence that the wind speed has on UTCI values for low and moderate air temperature. 
Especially, the wind speed reduces the thermal comfort for low and moderate air temperature, while the 
thermal radiation is found to be positively correlated with the UTCI values. According to 
meteorological data recorded in Copenhagen by the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI), the annual 
average mean wind speed is approximately equal to 4m/s while the mean air temperature ranges 
between 4oC and 20oC. The latter is valid for several months of a year. Therefore, the combination of 
the meteorological data and the sensitivity analysis findings can enable one to conclude that the UTCI 
values calculated for the studied areas are influenced by both the wind speed and the thermal radiation.  
 
To exemplify this conclusion, a winter day is considered with the air temperature being close to 5oC. 
For this case, the sensitivity analysis reveals that the decrease of wind speed by 1 m/s leads to UTCI 
values increased by 2oC. Therefore, the design of wind-protected public spaces may remarkably 
increase the thermal comfort and hence, favour people’s presence in those areas. Such an increase in 
the UTCI values is highly beneficial for the cold stress conditions, which were found to be associated 
with significantly low occupancy levels. Especially, for the “Sitting on a bench” activity that shows 
rapid increase in the people’s presence with the increase of UTCI values, the high thermal comfort due 
to lower wind speed is expected to lead to high usage levels. Practical ideas to establish wind-protected 
public spaces can include, among others, the use of wind barriers close to the sitting equipment in order 
to minimize the wind speed and hence, increase the number of visitors and extent the use period for the 
urban spaces.  
 
The design of outdoor urban spaces should also account for the effect of the thermal radiation on the 
thermal comfort. According to the observations made during the measuring campaign of the current 
study, the direct sunlight in the public spaces increases their usage levels over the winter period. Hence, 
the existence of sunlight at outdoor urban areas for a long period is expected to increase the presence 
of people especially in a Nordic city, where the population positively responds to the exposure in the 
sun regardless of the air temperature values.

 Limitations of the current study and future work 
 
 Limitations 
 
The current study combines on-site measurements of meteorological parameters and concurrent 
observations regarding the occupancy of four public places. Limitations are presented below, which are 
related to the conducted research and exogenous parameters that influenced the study. The 
consideration of the research constraints for the interpretation of the data enables the assessment of their 
impact on the analysis and the derived findings. Whilst for the parameters that their influence could not 
be considered, specific references are included at the description of the analysis, elaborating on the 
conditions that may affected the outcomes. 
 
COVID-19 
The measurements related to the Data set A were conducted during the 2020-2021winter period under 
the outbreak of COVID-19 pandemic. Hence, this data set has been affected by the occurrence of 
extraordinary conditions related, among others, to the public life. The restrictions for preventing the 
spread of COVID-19 were continuously changing during the conduction of the current study affecting 
the use of public spaces that was investigated. Hence, the initial time schedule of the on-site visits had 
to change during the research, while changes occurred also at the structure of the research plan since 
initially defined investigation areas and activities (e.g., “Sitting on a café” activity) could not be 
considered anymore due to the lockdown conditions.  
 
Moreover, the influence of the applied restrictions at the findings cannot be readily quantified. 
Although, it is clear that due to the lockdown restrictions, the incentives related to the pedestrian life in 
particular places were significantly reduced. However, the effect of the lockdown conditions on the 
presence of people in public spaces is still ambiguous. The lack of opportunities to perform social 
activities indoors (e.g., visit a museum, café, restaurant etc.) might have favoured people’s decision to 
visit an outdoor public place. Nevertheless, people that do not live at the adjacent area of the examined 
places might have no stimulus to travel and visit the specific places. Therefore, the limited reasons for 
commuting might have reduced the observed use of an urban place. In addition, the recommendations 
for social distancing might also deter the use of public spaces that are frequently considered to be 
crowded (e.g., Nyhavn). Hence, the findings of the current study have been interpreted within the 
context of the existing COVID-19 affected conditions while further investigation is required to identify 
to which extent the outcome of the study has a generic use. 
 
In addition, the extraordinary conditions of the winter 2020-2021 required the distinct analysis of the 
corresponding data set (Data set A) from the data collected in 2018 (Data set B). Therefore, the 
interpretation of the two data sets is done separately. However, it should be noted that the collection of 
data in a continuous period of one year without significant interferences in the public life could provide 
a better overview of the relation between the use of public spaces and the climate conditions. 
 
Day and time of measurements 
The simultaneous measurements at all the study areas at a specific day and time were not possible for 
the data collected during winter 2020-2021 since the measurements were conducted by a single person. 
Considering the significant effect that the day and the time of the measurement has on the data, the 
observations were deliberately scaled in order to remove the influence of this factor. The scaling of the 
data was done by utilizing the “popularity graphs” provided by Google. The graphs were used as an 
==PAGE:103==
Discussion 
 
103
indicator regarding the time and day that public spaces show the highest occupancy levels. However, 
limited information is provided about the data collection method used by Google. Moreover, for the 
two out of four places, i.e., Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv, the relevant graphs were not available by 
Google; hence, assumptions were made for these cases. The scaling was undertaken by the application 
of a simple analogical method that utilizes an upper threshold. The latter was defined by considering 
general knowledge about the occupancy level of public spaces. Additionally, the reduction of the 
uncertainties imposed due to the scaling was achieved by limiting the period, during which the Data set 
A was collected. However, since data was eventually subjected to scaling, its influence cannot be 
eliminated. 
 
The measurements conducted by the 2018 summer period were organised under a different framework. 
Hence, different rationale and methodology have been applied for Data set A and B respectively. In 
particular, the Data set B was collected within a wide range of days and periods. Due to the lack of 
available information (e.g., Google “popularity graphs”) that could be used to homogenize the collected 
data, empirical judgement was used to analyse and process the data. However, it needs to be noted that 
the collection of data at specific days and periods of interest constitutes the most efficient way to reduce 
the undesired influence of scaling methods and waive the need for assumptions. 
 
Size of data sample 
Different reasons restrained the size of the two data sets. The changing restrictions of COVID-19, the 
conduction of the measurements during only weekends as well as the unstable weather conditions of 
the wintertime in Copenhagen (i.e., plenty of days with precipitation) are the main reasons that limited 
the sample size of the Data set A. On the other hand, the rather short time window, which is usually 
associated with a special course, restrained eventually the amount of data collected in 2018 (Data set 
B) by the students. A greater sample of data could refine the reliability of the input and hence, strengthen 
the outcome of the current analysis. 
 
 
 Future work 
 
Despite the limitations, the current study can be seen as the steppingstone for additional and wider 
investigation of the relation of microclimate to the planning and design of highly performing urban 
spaces. Along these lines, the utilization of Big Data related to the climatic conditions and the presence 
of people at public spaces may lead to the development of tools that can facilitate the sustainable urban 
design. The big amount of the data requires different collection methods compared to the conventional 
on-site measurements. For example, the use of data provided by mobile phones can advance the 
collection process, providing access to spatial or temporal details of the population presence [77]. The 
continuous, extensive and unobtrusive “character” of data harvested with the use of mobile phones can 
increase both the abundance and reliability of the input data and render it a highly valuable tool in 
research.  
 
Additionally, the collection of data in several places during a wide range of days and periods can provide 
valuable information to enhance the analysis about the influence of the thermal environment on the use 
of the public spaces. The Big Data techniques can reveal parameters affecting the people’s presence in 
public places that cannot be identified by on-site measurements over a limited period. The identification 
of the influence that several parameters may have on the public spaces occupancy will allow the 
refinement of the data, while the use of less biased data will advance the final findings.

6. Conclusions 
 
 
 
The current study sheds light on the influence that the change of the thermal environments exerts on 
the use of public spaces in a Nordic city. The basis of the analysis undertaken herein consists of 
observations regarding the presence of people performing different activities in four central public 
spaces of Copenhagen including the Kongens Have, Nyhavn, Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv. The analysis 
presented by this document is further supported by on-site measurements of the climatic conditions 
recorded in the aforementioned public spaces during two different time periods, i.e., December 2020-
March 2021 and April-June 2018. The effect of several factors on the occupancy of those public spaces 
was found to differentiate from place-to-place and from period-to-period the observations, the recorded 
trends and eventually, the outcome from this analysis. Therefore, several case specific conclusions can 
be derived about the way that the different public spaces are used in connection to the climatic 
conditions. On the other hand, the current study and the associated analysis indicate that some common 
trends in the use of public spaces with regard to the thermal environment can be observed. Hence, these 
revealed trends can be used as milestones to trigger deeper investigation on the area of urban planning 
and design focusing, mainly, on the increase in the use of public spaces by improving the thermal 
comfort. 
 
As mentioned above, the current study takes advantage of data collected by both measuring the climatic 
conditions in public spaces and monitoring the response of humans and their activities in the public 
spaces. Especially, two data sets were used, i.e., the Data set A and the Data set B. The first data set, 
collected under exceptional circumstances due to the COVID-19 pandemic, was analysed to identify 
how the transition from thermal comfort to cold conditions may affect the use of public spaces and the 
related activities. For the majority of the activities, the analysis of Data set A shows that the people’s 
presence decreases with the decrease of the level of the experienced thermal comfort. This trend is seen 
to be valid for the four public spaces monitored via this study. On the other hand, the change rate of the 
people’s presence that perform a specific activity is found to vary among the investigated places when 
the weather is getting colder. This variation in the change rate of the people’ presence can be attributed 
to both the type (i.e., function) of each public space and the nature of each activity (e.g., Walking or 
Siting on a bench). Hence, the relation between the thermal comfort and a specific activity should be 
examined in the context of a particular public space. 
 
Regarding the overall utilization of the public spaces, a rather weak relation is identified between the 
occupancy of the four public spaces and the change of the environmental conditions from thermal 
comfort to slightly cold. Despite the differences at the use levels of the public places, the change of the 
occupancy due to weather is similar to all the study cases. In more details, the analysis revealed that 
people’s behaviour and activity, expressed with their presence in the public spaces, do not dramatically 
decrease when the weather is getting colder. The decrease in people’s activities starts to be more evident 
for UTCI values lower than 4oC. Hence, according to the analysis, it can be expected that the occupancy 
of the public spaces will not rapidly decrease when the lower boundary of the no thermal stress 
conditions prescribed by the UTCI will change from 9oC to 4oC. However, it should be noted that the 
collected data (i.e., Data set A) refers to the COVID-19 period, during which public spaces constitute 
one of the limited options for performing social activities. The restrictions due to this pandemic reduced

the social activities, which cannot be performed at open public places (e.g., visit indoor facilities). 
Therefore, the use of outdoor areas, even under cold climate conditions, may be increased. On the other 
hand, the recommendations for social distancing and the lack of stimulus for commuting may have a 
negative influence at the use of public places. Hence, it is hard to reach a rigid conclusion about the 
way the pandemic influences the use of the public spaces. Further investigation can identify the type of 
this influence at the observed trends. Nevertheless, this data set includes valuable information about 
such an extraordinary period; therefore, unique insights can be drawn about the use of public spaces 
that cannot be easily found in the majority of existing data sets and relevant studies. 
 
The current study enables also the investigation of environmental parameters that influence the levels 
of thermal comfort. To this regard, the wind speed and the thermal radiation were found to affect the 
UTCI values and hence, the presence of people in the urban spaces. Especially, the sensitivity analysis 
shows a clear trend that describes the increase of the wind speed and the consequent reduction of the 
thermal comfort. The latter was primarily found for low and moderate air temperature conditions, which 
are dominant for the entire winter period in Copenhagen. Hence, the design of wind-repellent urban 
places by introducing, for example, of wind barriers close to sitting infrastructure, may significantly 
augment the thermal comfort and hence, favour people’s presence in those areas. The design of highly 
performing urban places can also include considerations about the impinging sunlight. The observations 
made during this study shows that the existence of sunlight at outdoor urban areas for a long period can 
inflate the presence of people especially in a Nordic city, where the population positively responds to 
the exposure in the sun regardless of the air temperature. 
 
Apart from Data set A, a second data set was recorded in the aforementioned four public spaces over 
the 2018 spring-summer period. The Data set B favours the analysis of the variation in the occupancy 
level of the public places with respect to the change of the climatic conditions from no thermal stress 
to heat stress. The figures presented at §4 show a varying influence of the thermal comfort conditions 
on the different activities that can be undertaken by people in the public spaces. However, it is seen that 
the way a specific activity (e.g., “Walking”) is influenced by the thermal comfort conditions follows 
quite similar trends for all the public spaces investigated by this study (i.e., Kongens Have, Nyhavn, 
Axeltorv and Gråbrødretorv). “Brisk” constitutes an exception to this observation since different trends 
between the specific activity and the thermal comfort conditions were observed for the four public 
spaces. 
 
It is also interesting to comment on the total occupancy of the public spaces that is composed by the 
occupancies corresponding to the different activities. Based on the observations made over the spring-
summer of 2018 for both Kongens Have and Gråbrødretorv, a rather weak dependency was identified 
between their total occupancy and the microclimatic conditions when the UTCI is varying from 12oC 
to 32oC. Marginal influence of the no thermal stress conditions (12oC<UTCI<26oC) on the total 
occupancy was also observed for Nyhavn. On the other hand, the people’s response to the microclimatic 
conditions is more intense for higher UTCI values, i.e., UTCI>32oC, since the decrease at the occupancy 
levels for these microclimate conditions is notable. Additionally, no change in the total occupancy of 
Axeltorv can be seen when the microclimatic conditions move from the no thermal stress zone (<26 
oC) to heat stress zone (>26 oC). Therefore, it can be concluded that the total occupancy of the public 
spaces, is expected to decrease mostly for UTCI> 32oC. However, the catholic consideration of the 
particular conclusion in microclimatic analyses for a variety of study cases, should be supported by 
results from additional studies. 
 
==PAGE:107==
Conclusions 
 
107
The aforementioned conclusions are based on the analysis of data (Data set A and B) collected under 
specific conditions with the use of the available tools. These conditions (e.g., COVID-19 pandemic) are 
thoroughly explained by the study and their impact on the analysis outcome should be seriously 
considered when assessing the general applicability of the results derived herein. In order to examine 
the influence of thermal comfort in the use of the public spaces, the current study highlights the 
importance of removing the influence that various parameters (e.g., day and time of measurement) may 
have on the collected data. Additionally, instead of sporadic observations, the systematic collection of 
an extensive amount of data can provide the essential foundation in order to scrutinize the effect of 
various parameters on the measurements. Big data constitute an advantageous tool to favour the precise 
portrayal of existing trends while they can be highly beneficial to investigate multidimensional 
challenges. However, the value and quality of a study are not only related to the amount of the processed 
data but also to the suitable interpretation of the data. Hence, the combination of the knowledge acquired 
though on-site studies with the utilization of big data techniques could facilitate predictive analysis and 
future decisions in urban planning towards well designed public spaces.
