Abstract
Fault detection of photovoltaic cells (PV) is an important tool that can be used for au­
tomating the process of maintaining a solar farm. This thesis investigates the application
of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) for the task of fault detection and classification
in PV cells. This thesis presents four different CNN architectures with varying depths and
complexity. Training and testing of the networks are carried out in two separate experi­
ments, where one has the goal of binary classification, and the other aims to achieve multi
class classification. In the project we were able to create several models which could ef­
fectively detect faults on solar cells with a fault detection accuracy of 73.8%, while only
misclassifying 2.6% of healthy cells. Multi class classification models were created, how­
ever they were not fully completed and require further work. Lastly, several suggestions
were made in order to improve the overall models, including further investigation into dif­
ferent hyperparameters.

Fejldetektion af fotovoltaiske celler er et vigtigt værktøj, der kan anvendes til at automa­

fotovolaiske celler. Projektet undersøger fire forskellige CNN­arkitekturer med varierende
dybde og kompleksitet. Træning og test af netværkene udføres i to separate eksperi­
menter, hvor det ene har målet på binær klassifikation, og det andet sigter mod at opnå

blev der fremsat flere forslag til at forbedre de overordnede modeller, herunder yderligere
undersøgelse af forskellige hyperparametre.

Introduction
Sustainability and green energy are topics that have seen a significant growth in the past
years and are expected to keep growing in the future. The need for clean energy is
becoming more and more dire in the world today as the climate crisis becomes more
dangerous and the temperature of the world is rising. One way to decrease the need for
fossil fuels is through the usage of solar energy. A way that energy from the sun can
be utilised is with the use of photovoltaic (PV) cells. The use of PV cells has seen a
significant increase in recent years [1], as the world shifts towards clean and renewable
energy sources. In Denmark the government has come to an agreement that by 2030
Denmark will increase its usage of solar and wind power by a factor of 4 [2].
With the growing number of solar farms in the world, there are a number of challenges
that are posed in terms of the maintenance and inspection of said solar farms. Generally
solar panels do not need a lot of attention apart from regular maintenance, however the
current state of the maintenance is not as efficient as it could be. When the solar farms are
being manually inspected it can take days or even weeks for employees to walk through
and examine acres of solar panels, an example of the size a solar farm that is located
in Denmark is shown in Figure 1.1. This means that maintenance issues often are not
discovered in a timely or efficient fashion. Even if inspections are regularly performed,
they tend to be expensive exercises that drive up labor costs and erode profits [3]. When
the maintenance of the solar farms requires a lengthy time frame it delays the time before
any faults on the solar panels are discovered.

PV cells are made up of semiconductor material, and when sunlight strikes the cell, some
of the photons from the light are absorbed by the cell in order to generate electricity.
The amount of energy produced by these cells, however, is not only dependant on the
weather or the amount of sunlight that they might be exposed to, but also by the state of
the cells and their surface. The performance of the cells is often impacted by faults, which
can lead to a decrease in energy efficiency. In the article ”Rapid testing on the effect of
cracks on solar cells output power performance and thermal operation” they investigated
the impacts of cracks and fracture defects in solar cells and their cause for output power
losses [5]. In their investigation they found that large cracks lead to drastic decreases in
the output power with numbers close to 60%.
In order to operate a solar farm that is more efficient, it would be beneficial to be able to
catch faults in solar panels quickly as well as introduce automation in the inspection pro­
cess. Doing so would not only make the process more efficient but also make inspection
cheaper, which could in turn result in the inspection being done more frequently. Due to
this, this project will be focused on exploring a part of a possible solution of this automa­
tion. More specifically, we will analyze electroluminescence images of photovoltaic panels
captured by drones in order to automatically detect the defects that the solar panels might
have.
1.1
Related Work
During preliminary research other’s approaches were investigated. One article proposes
the usage of U­Net_12, U­Net_25, PSPNet, and DeepLabV3+. They found that the im­
plementation of the DeepLab V3+ using class weights resulted in the best recall value
of 86% [6]. A different article investigated the approach of implementing a simple Con­
volution Neural Network (CNN) [7] as well as performing feature extraction with Support
Vector Machines (SVM). Another approach was the implementation of a more complex
CNN which was designed by the author [8]. This CNN was able to obtain an accuracy of
96.17%.
1.2
Purpose of This Study
The aim of this study is to investigate the application of deep learning networks for the
task of fault detection in solar cells and inherently decrease the amount of manual labour
required when detecting faults in solar cells. The project includes the following sub­goals
• Design and implement different CNN architectures
• Perform data augmentation that is suitable for solar cells
• Train and evaluate the implemented CNN architectures
• Create an optimal model that can autonomously detect faults in solar cells
• Create a model that can classify different faults that a solar cell might have.

2.2
Supervised Learning
Supervised learning is a type of machine learning in which an algorithm learns to map
input data to output labels by being trained on a labeled dataset. In other words, the
algorithm is given a set of examples where both the input data and corresponding output
labels are available to the algorithm, and it learns to predict an output label for new input
data [11].
In supervised learning, the algorithm is provided with a training dataset that consists of
input data and their corresponding output labels. The algorithm uses this dataset to learn
the mapping between the input data and the output labels by minimizing a loss function
that measures the difference between the predicted output and the true output. This
process is also known as optimization or training.

2.3
Convolutional Neural Network
A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a type of artificial neural network that is com­
monly used for image recognition. CNNs are designed to take in raw pixels from images
and use them to learn features such as lines, edges, textures and other features that are
important for classification of the images.
CNN’s are constructed using many different layers. The input layer will contain the images
that are processed in the architecture. The image is then passed through a number of hid­
den layers that consist of of convolutional layers, pooling layers, and batch normalization
layers. Each of the layers are described in detail below.

2.3.3
Pooling Layer
Another important building block of a CNN are the pooling layers. The pooling layer is
a layer that performs a downsampling operation. The purpose of the pooling layer is to
reduce the dimensions of the hidden layers by combining the outputs of neuron clusters
at the previous layers into a single neuron in the next layer [13]. Different types of pooling
exist such as max pooling, average pooling, and L2­pooling, however for this project only
max pooling was used. An example of what max pooling looks like is shown in Figure 2.6
Figure 2.6: Example of max pooling with strides of 2,2
Max pooling is computed by taking each feature map output from the convolutional layer
and then preparing a condensed feature map where the maximum value is chosen. Max
pooling is a way for the network to check if a given feature is found anywhere in the region
of the image and it can then approximate the exact location of the feature. The idea is
that once the feature has been identified the exact location is not as important as its rough
location and so it is better to reduce the number of parameters needed in the later layers
[14]. By reducing the number of parameters, max pooling can also help prevent overfitting
of the model.

As mentioned earlier the weights in the convolution layers are not predefined and are
learned through the training of the network. This also means that no pre trained weights
were used in this project and all weights were learned from scratch. The aim of the training
is to solve the optimization problem of minimizing the loss as much as possible. To train
the network is also to map the relationship between the training data and the outputs such
as in equation 2.5, where ˆy is the correct output and x is the input data.
ˆy = σ(W T x + b)
(2.5)
The CNN will update its weights, w, and biases, b, to satisfy the equation above. In each
training the input data will be lead into the neural network (NN) in a process called forward
propagation. From this an output will be produced, this output is then compared against
the real output in a process called back propagation, the back propagation is the update
of the network parameters (weight and bias). From this the parameters are adjusted in
order to try and get an output that is closer to the targeted results [15].

2.4.2
Backpropagation
Backpropagation is the method used in order to calculate the error contribution through­
out the network. Backpropagation allows the network to learn from the calculated error.
It starts by comparing the predicted outputs to the desired outputs (the ground truth). The
algorithm then works backwards through the network, calculating gradients that indicate
how much each weight and bias contributed to the overall error. These gradients repre­
sent the partial derivatives of the loss function with respect to each weight and bias. Once
the gradients are calculated, the gradient descent algorithm is used. Gradient descent is
an optimization algorithm which is used to update the weights and biases of the neural
network. It leverages the gradients calculated during backpropagation. The idea is to
adjust the weights and biases in a way that minimizes the loss function. The gradient
descent works by updating the weights and biases in small steps proportional to the neg­
ative gradient. The negative gradient indicates the direction of the steepest descent in
the loss function. By taking steps in the opposite direction of the gradient, the algorithm
gradually moves towards the optimal values that minimize the loss.
2.4.3
Epochs and Learning Rate
Two parameters that are used in training a neural network are epochs and learning rates.
Epochs are used to control the number of iterations the training algorithm goes through the
entire dataset. By going through multiple epochs, the network has the opportunity to learn
and refine its parameters over time. During each epoch, the network sees the training
dataset in its entirety, providing an opportunity to capture the patterns and relationships
in the data. As the network goes through multiple epochs, it can further adjust its weights
and biases based on the accumulated knowledge from previous iterations [16].
The learning rate is a value used in optimizer’s for the networks. For this project the
Adaptive Moment Estimation also known as the Adam optimizer, was used as the op­
timizer. The learning rate determines the step size at which the optimizer updates the
weights and biases of the network. It controls the magnitude of the adjustments made to
the parameters during each iteration of the optimization process. A larger learning rate
allows for more significant updates, but it may risk overshooting the optimal values. Con­
versely, a smaller learning rate may converge more slowly but could provide more precise
adjustments.

Architecture
The following sections provide a theoretical overview of four different architectures that
are used in this report: ResNet34, ResNet152, AlexNet, and Xception. The main goal is
to describe the underlying principles of these architectures and how they differ from each
other.
2.5.1
AlexNet
AlexNet is a convolutional neural network that was first introduced by Alex Krizhevsky
in 2012. The architectures consist of 8 layers, five convolutional layers, and three fully
connected layers [17]. A depiction of the original AlexNet is shown in Figure 2.7, however,
this shows all the layers divided into two to train them on separate GPUs. Since we had
access to an HPC at DTU this was not necessary and the implementation was therefore
not split in two, this was also done in order to simplify the implementation.
Figure 2.7: The AlexNet architecture [17]
The AlexNet works by inputting an image, and the first layer will then extract local features
of the images as explained earlier in Chapter 2. Once the features are extracted a batch
normalization is applied and then a maximum pooling layer is applied to further decrease
the dimensions. This can also be seen on the first 3 block in Figure 2.8, illustrating the
different ’layers’. The layers shown are the ones in yellow and dark blue, these are the
convolutional layers and the dense layers, making the AlexNet 8 layers deep.
The second layer and fifth layer of the AlexNet are pooling layers, meaning that they
perform sub­sampling to reduce the spatial dimensions and therefore decrease the com­
putational complexity and make the network more robust to spatial translations. The next
2 layers, layer 3 and 4, perform a convolution followed by a batch normalization. The last
convolutional layer is similar to layer 1 and 2 as it performs a convolution followed by a
batch normalization and then a maxpooling.
The last 3 layers are the fully connected layers. These layers use traditional artificial intel­
ligence neural network structures. However, before these dense layers are implemented,
the output from the convolutional layer 5 is flattened. Flattening is used to convert all the
resulting 2­dimensional arrays from pooled feature maps into a single long continuous
linear vector which is then fed as input to the fully connected layer to classify the image.
After this two dense layers with the ReLu function are applied with a dropout (value of
0.5), and the last dense layer with the size of the number of classes that need to clas­
sified is then applied with a softmax function. The overall implemented AlexNet can be
seen in Figure 2.8. One thing to note that is not shown in the figure is the activation func­

first 2 dense layers) the ReLu function is applied, whereas the last dense layer uses the
softmax function.

2.5.2
ResNet34 and ResNet152
The ResNet34 and ResNet152 architectures were first introduced in order to ease the
training of networks that are substantially deeper than those used previously. They are a
type of deep learning model architecture that is often used for image recognition tasks. It
was introduced in 2015 and it builds on the success of other convolutional neural network
(CNN) models, such as AlexNet and Visual Geometry Group (VGG) architectures [18].
ResNet is short for Residual Neural Network, and the key innovation in the network was
the introduction of residual blocks. An example of the residual block can be seen in Figure
2.9. The main problem that the residual block is trying to fix is the vanishing gradient
issue. This problem occurs when trying to train neural networks and specifically deep
networks. The main issue is that the neural network is unable to propagate useful gradient
information from the output end of the model back to the layers near the input end of the
model and the gradient used for updating the networks’ parameters tend to diminish as
they propagate backwards through the layers [19]. When the residual block is used it
introduces a shortcut that allows the information to bypass one or more layers in the
network and the information can flow through the network more easily and prevent the
gradient from vanishing.
Figure 2.9: Residual Block that is used in the ResNet networks [18]
An overview of the ResNet34 can be seen in Figure 2.10. The figure shows the 34 convo­
lutional layers that the architecture has, as well as the skip connections that are between
the different layers. An important thing to know when looking at the addition operation in
the residual block, is that ResNet employs a technique called bottleneck. This involves
using a 1x1 convolutional layer to project the input to the same dimensions as the output
before performing the element­wise addition.

2.5.3
Xception
The Xception architecture was first introduced by Francois Chollet in collaboration with
Google, and was introduced as a novel deep convolutional neural network architecture
inspired by Inception, where Inception modules have been replaced with depthwise sep­
arable convolutions [20]. The key difference between depthwise separable convolution
and a standard convolutional layer is that depthwise convolution applies the convolution
separately for each channel and then mixes the output of each channel. When a stan­
dard convolution filters information both spatially and across channels at the same time,
the depthwise separable convolution factors the process in two steps. Using depthwise
separable convolution will therefore cut down the amount of computations. With less com­
putations, the network is able to process more in a shorter amount of time. The Xception
architecture is shown in Figure 2.12.

Class Weight
The next thing that was tested was the usage of class weights when training the models.
Since we have an unbalanced dataset, in order to add more balance to the training, a
class weight can be implemented when training the model. The class weight is calculated
using equation 4.1.
Weight =
Number of samples
number of class’s · class samples
(4.1)
When the class weights are implemented, the model is punished higher for wrongly clas­
sifying the faulty cells than it is for wrongly classifying the healthy cells since the weight
of the faulty class will be higher than the weight for the healthy cells, therefore it should
make the models more willing to classify the cells as faulty. When the class weight was
implemented it was tested against the results that were collected in the previous test.
First the AlexNet was investigated. All the previous TP’s and FP’s from the initial test
were kept and compared to the TP’s and FP’s from the model when class weight was
implemented in the training process. The comparison can be seen in Figure 4.3. From
the plot we can see that when the model is trained with class weights implemented then
the model is more likely to classify a cell as faulty, however, we can also see that there are
some cases where the model classifies all the cells as faulty, but overall we see that when
the class weight is implemented the model is better at detecting faults. It was therefore

Augmentation in Test and Validation Set
The next test that was performed was to see what the effect of having augmentation in
the validation dataset would have on the results. In order to perform this test, augmen­
tation was only performed on the images in the training dataset. Adding augmentation to
a dataset should add some robustness to it since it now does not only contain ’perfect’
images, and the model will therefore be able to perform on the new dataset that which
it might not have in the original dataset. This is why the addition of brightness augmen­
tation, rotation, and contrast are added. The question then comes to whether or not the
augmentation should be added in the validation and test dataset. One argument would
be that augmentation should only be added to the training set in order to make the model
robust, but the validation and test dataset should only contain the original data since this
is what the models will encounter once implemented in the real world. Another argument
would be that augmentation should be included in the validation and test dataset in order
to analyze the models as this is what they have been trained on, as well as the fact that
the data that the models may encounter in the real world will not always be perfect images
and testing the models on images that have variation may be an evaluation of the models.

dataset. Figure 4.26 shows the comparison for the AlexNet models. From the figure it

Future Works
The current project on automatic detection and classification of defects on photovoltaic
panels has yielded promising results using deep learning techniques. However, there are
several avenues for further exploration and improvement to enhance the accuracy and
generalization of the models. This chapter will cover some suggestions that might result
in improved models and results.
The first thing to consider is the parameters that were used during the project. To optimize
the performance of the deep learning models, it may be a good idea to delve deeper into
the hyperparameters that were previously tuned. Specifically, further investigation of the
optimal number of epochs could be conducted to determine if extending training time
leads to enhanced accuracy. Additionally, experimenting with alternative image sizes and
batch sizes for training may reveal new insights into the impact of these parameters on
the models’ performance. Furthermore, evaluating different optimization functions could
potentially lead to improved results in defect detection and classification. Since there was
not enough time to spend in the multi class classification, doing a deeper investigation
into those models would also be beneficial with increasing the epochs as well as doing
further investigation into the reduced dataset in order to have more consistent labeling for
the faults and remove the false positives that are in the dataset.
Apart from exploring the parameters that are already set and explored, incorporating new
techniques may also lead to improvements in the models. A technique that was not used
in the project was the usage of principle components analysis (PCA). PCA can be used
to extract more meaningful features of the images prior to training which then has the
potential to enhance the models’ ability to capture relevant information and improve clas­
sification accuracy. Another technique that can be used in order to adjust for overfitting is
label smoothing. Label smoothing can prevent the models from becoming overly reliant
on the training data and improve their generalization capabilities.
While the current research utilized a specific dataset for testing, it would be beneficial to
acquire a separate dataset to validate the models’ performance. This additional validation
would provide a more robust evaluation of the models’ accuracy and generalizability. Fur­
thermore, exploring the models’ applicability across different photovoltaic panel types or
manufacturers would contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of the models’
effectiveness in real­world scenarios.
Lastly, gathering more data and especially images of faulty cells for the dataset would be
advantageous, as the models would then have more information when training as well as
during the validation and test. An expanded dataset would facilitate the development of
more robust models with increased accuracy and reliability in defect detection and clas­
sification. A deeper dive could also be done into the cells which are being misclassified,
either as healthy or as faulty, and see if there is a overlap between the different models
and then manually investigate those cells.

6
Conclusion
Throughout the project, several experiments were conducted in order to find the best mod­
els for binary classification, as well as for multi class classification. For the binary classifi­
cation 4 different architectures were used, AlexNet, ResNet34, ResNet152, and Xception.
These architectures were then trained with and without class weight in order to determine
the effect that class weight would have. We saw that the implementation of class weight
resulted in more faults being detected in the AlexNet, ResNet34, and Xception models.
The architectures were then tested for a range of epochs in order to determine the best
model for each architecture. The models chosen were then trained and tested on the ex­
clusion of cells which contained more than one type of fault. It was found that excluding
these cells resulted in worse results for all of the different architecture models. A new
reduced dataset was then introduced which had been reduced by removing images that
were too small to be classified as cells, and the labeling had been reevaluated in order to
get rid of the false positives. It was found that when using the reduced dataset the mod­
els were more able to differentiate between the healthy and faulty cells, resulting in better
models. Then different types of augmentations were investigated. First, augmentation in
the validation and test dataset was removed and tested on the models. It was found that
not having augmentation in the test and augmentation dataset, results in a lower true pos­
itive rate for the models. Second, amount of augmentation in the dataset was tested, here
3 different scenarios were looked at; no augmentation, 3 types of augmentation, where
only flips of original images were included, and 7 types of augmentation. Here it was
found that the more augmentation added to the model the better they performed. Lastly,
the best performing models from all of the previous tests were found and tested on a test
dataset giving us the ability to conclude on a certain type of robustness for the models.
We were able to find 11 different models that can be used for binary classification. Look­
ing at the two models with the highest average precision, with a value of 0.93, one was
able to detect 73.8% of the faulty cells while only misclassifying 2.6% of the healthy cells,
and another model was able to detect 79.0% of the faulty cells and misclassified 12.4%.
For the multi class classification 3 different tests were performed. Effects of class weight
was tested and it was found that the architectures generally performed better without class
weight being implemented. Exclusion of cells which contained more than 1 type of fault
was tested and here we found that the inclusion of them did not have a significant effect,
however it was chosen to continue to exclude them in the last test. The last test was
to see how the amount of augmentation affected the results. Here it was found that the
more augmentation the better the models performed. Overall we were able to create a
model for each architecture with an F1 score of 0.76. These final results however were
on the validation dataset which included augmentation rather than a test dataset that only
included the original cells. The multi class classification models therefore require more
work before having a proper working model.
Overall several models were created for binary classification with a range of true positive
rates from 58.3% to 79.0% and a false positive rate ranging from 1.3% to 22.6%. A couple
of multi class classification models were created, however not fully tested. It was possible
to create successful deep learning models, however more work can be done in order to
further improve the model and increase the overall accuracy.
