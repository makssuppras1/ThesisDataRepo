ABSTRACT
Increasing the share of wind- and solar-based renewable energy sources in electricity systems
is key to improving the sustainability of energy production worldwide and mitigating the
eﬀects of climate change. However, it requires that electricity production, consumption,
transmission and storage can accommodate these variable, uncertain sources of power.
While this calls for investments in ﬂexibility resources, it may be more economic to
exploit potentials for ﬂexible operation in existing infrastructures, for example at the
interface of electricity, heat and gas sectors. In deregulated electricity sectors, market-
based mechanisms are expected to utilise resources eﬃciently, but limited options for
representing the physical and economic properties of ﬂexible assets in electricity markets
can hinder these potentials.
This thesis proposes a generalised bid format that allows market participants to represent
arbitrary linear physical and economic characteristics in electricity markets. It is referred
to as a price-region bid, and can include state variables which allow complex, though linear
characteristics to be represented. A market-clearing mechanism based on price-region bids
shows that, even if bidding prices are associated with state variables, an eﬃcient market
equilibrium based on locational marginal prices satisﬁes properties of feasibility, incentive
compatibility, socio-economic eﬃciency and budget balance for the market operator.
Price-region bids are applied both to a short-term market for electrical energy, and to a
long-term market for ﬁnancial products indexed on future, short-term electricity prices.
The new bid format generalises, and is compatible with markets where competitive actors
participate using speciﬁc linear bids, such as price-quantity bids, and where system assets
are represented by linear models of operation. A case study considers the use of price-
region bids by a district heating utility. Under no constraints of bid size, it shows that a
price-region bid for a short-term energy market can straightforwardly be derived from a
linear operations model. Analytical and numerical results also show that, with a simple
bidding strategy for long-term ﬁnancial products, a ﬂexible asset operator can improve
the lower bound to the uncertain economic surplus it will derive from future short-term
market prices.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Context and motivations
In the past decades, many countries have deregulated their electricity sectors, and
substituted vertically-integrated utilities with competitive markets. These include spot
markets for the short-term dispatch of energy, as well as markets for electricity derivatives.
An electricity spot market is typically organised as an auction where power producers,
consumers and retailers submit oﬀers and bids for energy. Depending on local market
design speciﬁcities, spot markets may allow their participants to submit diﬀerent types
of oﬀers/bids. Based on the information gathered from market participants, the market
operator issues prescriptions for each participant’s injection of withdrawal of electrical
energy, as well as spot prices at which these transactions will be settled.
The spot
market operator is expected to ﬁnd an economic market equilibrium, i.e.
a set of
injection/withdrawal quantities and spot prices which corresponds to a socio-economically
optimal dispatch, while satisfying all participants’ willingness to pay or to be paid for
energy. As electricity demand and available means of power production are variable over
space and time, and the transport and storage of electricity rely on costly infrastructures
with limited capacity, spot market prices tend to be volatile. Spot prices are diﬃcult to
predict with certainty, especially on a long term, which can be challenging for actors whose
ﬁnances depend largely on the economic surplus they derive from spot markets.
Electricity derivatives are long-term ﬁnancial contracts with entitle their owner to a
payment based on future spot market prices. Derivatives can be traded ahead of the spot
market in dedicated auctions, and are notably used as instruments to reduce exposure
to spot price uncertainty. For some actors, these can be key to guaranteeing recovery
of investments, increasing bankability, or reducing the risk of bankruptcy. Derivatives
markets moreover provide signals for capacity planning and investments.
The past decades have also been characterised by growing concern about the consequences
of climate change, largely driven by greenhouse gas emissions. The mitigation of these
emissions calls for a transformation of the electricity sector in many countries, notably
through the deployment of large shares of renewable sources of energy such as wind and
solar power. However, these means of producing power feature substantially diﬀerent
characteristics than conventional fuel-based power plants, in particular variability and
uncertainty of power production. To properly accomodate a large penetration of these
technologies, new sources of ﬂexibility need to be deployed in the power system, so as to
complement and eventually substitute some of the ﬂexibility provided by conventional
power plants.

1. Introduction
While ﬂexibility requirements have typically been fulﬁlled by fuel-based power plants,
emerging technologies such as battery storage systems, including in distributed resources
such as electric vehicles, appear as interesting new candidates [1–4]. Moreover, substantial
ﬂexibility potentials can be found in existing systems and infrastructures, including
conventional electricity end-users [5, 6] as well as district heating infrastructures [7–9] and
gas infrastructures [4, 10, 11], whose networks are often strongly coupled with electricity
networks. While new technologies, e.g. power-to-gas, can further enhance the ﬂexibility
these infrastructures can provide to electricity systems, current assets can already hold
un-tapped, economically eﬃcient potentials. For example, in the presence of adequate
market signals, large district heating systems can act ﬂexibly and (1) choose between
diﬀerent alternatives for generating heat (combined heat and power plants, gas boilers,
heat pumps...), (2) store energy in hot water storages, (3) activate ﬂexible demand and (4)
adjust temperatures and mass ﬂows in hot water pipelines.
As electricity markets and regulatory frameworks were initially conceived for systems
with large shares of fuel-based power plants, unconventional sources of ﬂexibility can face
diﬃculties in participating eﬃciently in electricity markets. In particular, inadequate
bidding formats may prevent these resources from being truly valued in the markets.
This can constitute a barrier to the utilisation of cost-eﬃcient sources of ﬂexibility, as
well as foster revenue uncertainty for owners of ﬂexible assets and potential investors.
Consequences can be particularly signiﬁcant for capital-intensive technologies such as
heat pumps or battery storage systems, which require strong price signals to motivate
investments. This thesis aims at improving the eﬃcient utilisation of a broad range of
ﬂexibility resources using market-based solutions, so as to minimise the costs of deploying
renewable energy sources on a large scale in deregulated electricity sectors.
1.2
Objective and scope of the thesis
In the context of this work, ﬂexibility is deﬁned as the possibility, for an asset in a power
system, to adjust its injection or withdrawal of power at given nodes and times, in response
to signals such as the local marginal cost of electricity production. This thesis focuses on
the use of ﬂexibility resources to increase economic eﬃciency of the short-term dispatch
of electrical energy production, consumption, transmission and storage, as well as their
ability to jointly participate in long-term derivatives markets. Following the motivations
described in Section 1.1, the thesis work is driven by the following research question:
How to improve the representation of diverse ﬂexibility resources
in electricity spot and derivatives markets?
Focus is put on linear market mechanisms, thus increasing compatibility with existing
markets which are often based on linear dispatch models. In order to ease the clarity of
analytical results, it is further assumed that the physical and economic characteristics
of assets taking part in the short-term energy dispatch are well represented using linear
models. The markets are also assumed to be perfectly competitive, in the sense that the
market participants do not believe that their bidding strategies can meaningfully alter the
market prices, i.e. the market participants are price-takers. These assumptions are recalled

1.3
State of the art
1.3.1
Standard framework
In countries with deregulated electricity sectors, a typical market for the short-term
dispatch of electrical energy (hereafter referred to as “spot market”) includes the following
actors:
• competitive bidders, such as power producers and consumers, which place bids in the
market;1
• a spot market operator, which dispatches energy accross the power system based on
the participants’ bids, as well as the operational constraints of system assets such as
transmission systems;
• system asset operators, such as transmission system operators (TSOs), which make
their infrastructure available for market operations (note that, in some regions, the
market operator and the TSO are a single actor, then referred to as system operator).
Bid-based spot markets are typically cleared using linear bids such as price-quantity bids,
which are deﬁned independently for diﬀerent time periods and price areas, or block bids,
which enforce withdrawal/injection quantities for multiple time periods to follow a given
proﬁle. Diﬀerent spot markets may use diﬀerent temporal resolutions and clear bids
over diﬀerent horizon. A day-ahead market clears for example bids for the following day,
typically divided in hourly time periods. Spot markets also diﬀer with regard to their
spatial resolution: while US markets are typically modelling the whole transmission network
and derive diﬀerent prices for each electrical node in the network, European markets are
typically cleared using large, aggregate price areas [12].
In some regions, cost-based spot markets are preferred to bid-based spot markets, generally
with the intention to limit market power [13]. Market participants in cost-based markets
communicate their operation constraints and costs to the market operator, which includes an
operational model of the market participants’ assets in its dispatch problem. Truthfulness
of the market participants’ information is a condition and is veriﬁed through audit.
Spot markets are often complemented with markets for electricity derivatives, either
traded on exchanges or over the counter [14]. Common derivative products include energy
forwards2 and ﬁnancial rights. Energy forwards are typically bought and sold by actors

1.3.2
Market-based integration of ﬂexibility resources
Simple bid formats such as price-quantity bids do not allow the formulation of bids with
temporally- or spatially coupled constraints, and thus make it challenging for some actors
to participate eﬃciently in spot markets. Some authors have investigated the participation
of ﬂexible assets on spot markets with simple bidding formats under price uncertainty.
For example, [23] optimises the participation of a district heating utility on a day-ahead
market, while [24, 25] consider the case of a battery storage system, though also include
multiple markets. Other works have explored the beneﬁts of combining ﬂexible assets
with inﬂexible, uncertain assets such as wind turbines. For example, [26] considers a
day-ahead market bidding strategy for a wind power producer jointly operating a ﬂexible
load, while [27] considers the combination of a wind farm and a pumped-storage facility.
These approaches require either joint ownership of assets, or some form of agreement
regarding the coordination of assets with diﬀerent owners. The combination of a set of
similar ﬂexibility resources is generally referred to as aggregation. When joint ownership of
the aggregated assets is not a feasible or a favorable option, this approach may involve a
middleman referred to as aggregator. Aggregation of resources may be required when the
assets are too small to justify individual participation in wholesale markets, but may also
help the formulation of simple market participation strategies for assets with complex and
possibly uncertain operating constraints. For example, [28, 29] consider the participation of
a demand response aggregator in a day-ahead market, while [30] proposes the aggregation
of combined heat and power plants with thermal storages. Alternatively, [31, 32] suggest
to represent an aggregate collection of ﬂexible loads as a storage system. This approach
allows methods for market participation of storage assets to be applied to aggregated
ﬂexible loads.

1. Introduction
The authors in [33–35] propose to create an additional market where physical storage rights
are traded. These products would allow storage owners, or actors whose ﬂexibility can
be represented as a storage system, to sell physically binding rights for their charging,
discharging, and reservoir capacities. Right buyers may include, but are not limited to,
actors who seek to optimise their bidding strategy on a spot market. By selling physical
storage rights, owners of ﬂexibility resources can commercialize their capacity over diﬀerent
applications through a single platform.3 When auctions for physical storage rights are
held a long time ahead of the period of application of the rights, these products can also
act as risk-hedging products: owners of ﬂexibility resources receive an upfront payment
for their capacity, while the right buyers are guaranteed capacity for their speciﬁc needs –
which may include hedging against price volatility. Other types of bilateral contracts for
trading ﬂexibility capacity have been proposed by [36, 37] for the case of aggregated loads.
Markets for physical storage rights are particularly relevant for ﬂexible asset owners willing
to monetise their resource through multiple applications. However, this thesis focuses
on application of ﬂexibility resources to improve short-term energy dispatch eﬃciency,
and prioritises methods which integrate these resources in already existing markets for
short-term energy dispatch.
Recognising that simple spot market bid formats such as price-quantity bids or block
bids fail to truly valuate certain types of ﬂexible assets, some authors have proposed bid
formats designed to better represent speciﬁc types of ﬂexibility resources. For example, [38]
proposes a multi-time-period demand bid which allows for ﬂexible time of consumption, by
including bounds for the consumption at each time period as well as for the total energy
consumption. The authors in [39] propose additional bid types targetted to deferrable,
adjustable as well as storage-type loads, and show that a spot market which clears these
bids retains eﬃciency and incentive-compatibility properties. These bid formats are linear.
In contrast, the authors in [40] propose a discrete bid format in the form of asymmetric
block oﬀers and discusses its application to aggregate collections of thermostatic loads.
Other authors have suggested to treat storage assets as system assets, alike the way
transmission systems are traditionally treated in spot markets [21, 41, 42]. In that case, the
operational constraints of storage assets would be directly incorporated in the spot market
operator’s market-clearing program. Analogously to the transmission case, ﬁnancial storage
rights (FSRs) are deﬁned, and serve purposes similar to their transmission counterparts:
right buyers can hedge against intertemporal price risk during a future period, while
storage owners obtain upfront payments for their ﬂexibility capacity. Power- and energy
capacity rights are proposed in [42], in analogy with the transmission case’s ﬂowgate rights.
Proﬁle-based FSRs are proposed in [21], in analogy with point-to-point FTRs. Following

1.4
Thesis contributions and outline
Diﬀerent propositions were made to better integrate unconventional ﬂexibility resources in
spot markets, either through complex bid formats [38–40], or through the treatment of
ﬂexibility resources as system assets [21, 41, 42]. These propositions were however derived
for speciﬁc types of ﬂexibility resources, and thus do not straightforwardly apply to diverse
forms of ﬂexibility resources. For example, infrastructures such as district heating systems
or water supply systems feature complex operational constraints and link multiple power
system nodes. These infrastructures hold substantial ﬂexibility potentials that can be
underestimated from the power-system perspective if unadequate bidding formats are
available to these actors.
In order to better integrate a broad range of ﬂexibility resources in spot market operations,
this thesis proposes a novel bid format which allows market participants to represent
their feasible region of operation as a custom set of linear constraints. This bid format
is referred to as price-region bid. Alike [21] and [42] who propose a consistent joint spot
and derivatives framework for the treatment of storage as a system asset, this thesis
proposes that actors who participate in a spot market using price-region bids can also
participate in a derivatives market with a similar representation of their assets. For this
purpose, a broad deﬁnition of energy forwards is provided, which generalises point-to-point
FTRs and proﬁle-based FSRs. Price-region bidding in spot and derivatives markets is
shown to generalise the market participation of both competitive bidders and system asset
operators to any ﬂexible asset whose physical and economic properties can be described by
a linear model. As linear bids, price-region bids are compatible with the standard spot and
derivatives market framework, and can co-exist with market mechanisms such as those
proposed in [21, 38, 39, 42].

based on this type of bid. Desirable market properties for feasibility, eﬃciency, incentive-

PRICE-REGION BIDS
This chapter formulates the price-region bid format and shows how a market based on
price-region bids can satisfy important economic properties. Section 2.1 deﬁnes the price-
region bid format. Section 2.2 provides illustrative examples. Section 2.3 formulates a
market-clearing method based on price-region bids and deﬁne properties of feasibility,
eﬃciency and incentive compatibility for the market equilibrium, as well as budget balance
for the market operator. Section 2.4 demonstrates that the properties hold. Section 2.5
concludes the chapter.
2.1
Price-region bid format
Let s denote a bid, as well as the actor placing the bid (“the bidder”). Let qsnk ∈R denote
a transaction quantity associated with a bid s ∈{1...S}, a price area n ∈{1...N} and
a time period k ∈{1...K}. In this thesis, price-region bids are applied both to a spot
market where contracts for electricity delivery are traded, and to a derivatives market
where ﬁnancial contracts are traded:
• In the spot market case, the quantity qsnk denotes a withdrawal of electricity by
bidder s in price area n in period k. Note that qsnk can be negative, and would thus
represent an injection of electricity into the network.
• In the derivatives market case, the quantity qsnk corresponds to a ﬁnancial contract
indexed on future spot market prices.
Chapter 3 discusses the application of price-region bids to electricity spot and derivatives
markets in more detail.
Quantities are diﬀerentiated between diﬀerent price areas and time periods because
electricity as a commodity is not homogenous over space and time, i.e. it can be valued
diﬀerently in diﬀerent areas and time periods. Electricity markets are typically divided
into price areas, which can include one or several power system nodes depending on
the market resolution [12]. Let qs ∈RNK be a vertical vector containing the variables
qsnk, n = 1...N, k = 1...K, so that qs = [qs11 ... qs1K ... qsN1 ... qsNK]⊤. The vector qs
characterises a transaction bidder s may be willing to enter.
Let xsl be a state variable l ∈{1...Ls} associated with bid s ∈{1...S}. Let xs ∈RLs be a
vertical vector containing the variables xsl, l = 1...Ls. Let As be a matrix of real numbers,
for s ∈{1...S}. Let Is and Js be the number of columns and lines in As, respectively. Let
Bs be a horizontal vector of (NK + Ls) real numbers, for s ∈{1...S}.

Theorem 1. Any actor whose physical and economic properties can be truly represented
by a linear model – i.e. a set of linear constraints which couples transaction variables and
state variables, and an economic value which linearly combines transaction variables and
state variables – can bid its true parameters using a price-region bid.
The implication of Theorem 1 is key to this thesis: some actors are unable to bid their
true parameters in existing markets due to limitations in available bid formats, but, under
the right economic incentives, could do so using price-region bids. Theorem 1 is proven by
construction of the price-region bid format, i.e. a linear model for an actor’s physical and
economic properties can be directly mapped to a price-region bid (As, Bs).
The condition that a given actor’s physical and economic properties can be truly represented
by a linear model is strong. Thus, a looser, though more broadly applicable generalisation
of Theorem 1 is formulated in Corollary 1 below.
Corollary 1. Any actor whose physical and economic properties are well represented by a
linear model in the sense of Theorem 1 can formulate a price-region bid representative of
its true parameters.

2.3
Market-clearing based on price-region bids
The rest of this chapter formulates a general, price-region-based market-clearing problem
and shows that important economic properties hold. By “general”, it is meant that the
market-clearing problem is not formulated speciﬁcally for a spot market or a derivatives
market, but has applications in both contexts. Furthermore, the problem is formulated
for a market based on price-region bids only. The formulation neither includes traditional
bid formats such as price-quantity bids, or operational models of system assets such
as transmission lines. Chapter 3 will more speciﬁcally discuss the application of this
market-clearing problem to an electricity spot market and an electricity derivatives market,
respectively. They will show that common linear bid formats and the operation of system
assets based on linear models are special cases of a price-region bid formulation. Based on
this result, they will argue that existing markets could include price-region bids.
Section 2.3.1 describes four important economic properties for a market and their
implications. Section 2.3.2 formulates a market-clearing problem which satisﬁes these
properties. Section 2.4 provides a demonstration that the properties hold.
2.3.1
Problem description and economic properties
Let a set of actors s = 1...S (the market participants) place price-region bids in a centralised
auction. A market operator is in charge of clearing the auction with an eﬃcient market
equilibrium [22, 43]. An eﬃcient market equilibrium is a written as a pair (q⋆, λ⋆), with
q⋆∈RSNK the vector of transaction quantities prescribed by the market operator and
λ⋆∈RNK the vector of market prices. Let q⋆
s denote bidder s’ transaction quantities in
the eﬃcient market equilibrium, such that q⋆is a concatenation of all vectors q⋆
s, s = 1...S.
The transactions are priced using the price vector λ⋆. If, for a given bidder s, the amount
λ⋆⊤q⋆
s is positive, it means the bidder should give money to the market operator. If λ⋆⊤q⋆
s
is negative, it means bidder s receives a compensation from the market operator.
An eﬃcient market equilibrium must satisfy the following three properties [22, 43]:
Property 1 (Feasibility). The quantities q⋆

bids’ feasible regions, and PS

In the case of a spot market, Property 1 implies physical feasibility of the dispatch. In
the case of a derivatives market, it implies ﬁnancial feasibility, i.e. the entity in charge of
settling the ﬁnancial contracts is budget balanced (see Chapter 3 for more details).
Property 2 (Incentive compatibility). Competitive market participants are incenti-
vised to bid their true parameters.
Property 2 implies that, as far as the available bid formats allow, the market participants
have no reason to behave strategically by announcing, e.g., a diﬀerent willingness to pay
than their true willingness to pay.
Property 3 (Eﬃciency). Social welfare is maximised.
Property 3 implies that, if there exists multiple market equilibria satisfying Properties 1
and 2, only those that maximise the social welfare are eﬃcient market equilibria.

Moreover, in order to guarantee neutrality and revenue adequacy of the market operator,
the following property is required:
Property 4 (Budget balance). The market operator is budget balanced regardless of
the market-clearing outcome.
Property 4 implies revenue adequacy, as the market operator can pay all market participants
according to the eﬃcient market equilibrium without requiring external funding. This
property is also important for the neutrality of the market operator’s decisions as, given
that the market operator’s budget is independent from the market-clearing outcome, the
market operator has no incentives to chose an ineﬃcient market equilibrium.
Section 2.3.2 formulates a market-clearing problem for price-region bids that satisﬁes these
properties.

Note that only Property 2 requires the assumption of perfect competition. This is a
classical result: markets based on marginal pricing are only incentive compatible under
the assumption of perfect competition [22, 43]. While perfect competition is an ideal
that is never reached in practice, electricity markets are generally regulated to avoid the
enforcement of market power, e.g. by preventing any single ﬁrm to have too large a market
share, or by issuing ﬁnes when strategic behavior is detected. In markets particularly
subject to market power, cost-based markets are sometimes preferred though remain
disputed [13].
Assuming linear characteristics is required to prove a strong property of eﬃciency, i.e. that
the market maximises the true social welfare. However, using Corollary 1, if an actor’s
characteristics is relatively well modelled by a linear model, the market-clearing problem
(2.18a)-(2.18c) maximises a relatively good representation of the true social welfare, and a
weaker, yet more practical version of Lemma 1 holds.

2.4.1
Property 1 (Feasibility)
Property 1 is equivalent to the constraints (2.18b)-(2.18c). Property 1 is thus satisﬁed
by construction of the market-clearing problem, but requires that a feasible solution to
(2.18b)-(2.18c) can be found.
Feasibility of the market-clearing problem is dependent on the bids that are submitted. It
can however be guaranteed, for example, by requiring all bids s ∈{1...S} to let qs = 0 be
a feasible solution, i.e. to allow the market operator to prescribe empty transactions. In
that case, at least the solution qs = 0 ∀s ∈{1...S} is feasible to (2.18b)-(2.18c).
Note that (2.18a)-(2.18c) is a linear problem. Computational method for ﬁnding feasible
solutions to linear problems are well established and only present challenges for very large
problems. The computational eﬀort required to ﬁnd feasible solutions depends on the
problem’s size, i.e. the number of variables and constraints. This depends on the number
of bids and their “size”, which is characterised by:
• the number of price areas and time periods the bid is placed for (resp. N and K),
• the number of state variables included in the bid (Ls),
• the number of constraints included in the bid (Js, the number of lines in matrix As).
This thesis assumes that the size and number of bids is not large enough to compromise
the problem’s tractability, and thus a market equilibrium which satisﬁes Property 1 can be
found. In Chapter 3’s case study, eﬃcient market equilibria are computed in negligible
time. However, for larger problems, investigating the relevance of restricting the size of
bids, as well as developing methods for bidding under bid size constraints, remain the topic
of future work.

3.1
Market products and auctions
An electricity spot market is an exchange marketplace where short-term contracts for the
delivery of electrical energy are traded. Typical spot markets are taking place a day ahead
of delivery, or within the delivery day. In some regions, the majority of transactions for
electrical energy are made through a spot market.
Based on information gathered from competitive bidders and system assets, the spot
market operator is in charge of ﬁnding an eﬃcient market equilibrium. The eﬃcient market
equilibrium consists of an economic dispatch, i.e. a set of withdrawal/injection proﬁles
prescribed to all participating actors, and a set of market-clearing prices referred to as
spot prices. Spot prices are typically derived from locational marginal prices, i.e. from the
optimal Lagrange multipliers of the constraints imposing that the power system is locally
balanced at all times [22, 43, 44].
The delivery horizon of a spot market may vary from a region to another. A day-ahead
market typically optimises the dispatch over a day-long horizon discretised in, e.g., 24
hourly periods. Multiple price areas may be included, with a spatial resolution which can
vary from power system nodes to entire regions. The deﬁnitions provided in the rest of
this chapter are formulated independently of any speciﬁc spatio-temporal resolution.

3. Applications
Let an electricity derivatives market denote an exchange marketplace where ﬁnancial
electricity forwards, including ﬁnancial rights, are traded through an auction. Auctions
for derivatives are typically held a month-ahead, a year-ahead, or several years ahead of
the periods for which they apply [17]. These allow actors with long-term perspectives to
reduce their exposure to uncertainty in future spot prices.
An auction for derivatives may use a coarser time resolution than the corresponding spot
market, e.g. only two diﬀerent periods in a day [14, 17]. Derivatives traded a long time
ahead of their maturity time typically apply to multiple spot market horizons: for example,
the period T of a point-to-point FTR bought in a year-ahead auction may include the
oﬀ-peak hours of all days in a month, while being indexed on the prices of a spot auction
held daily with a 24-hour horizon [17]. For simplicity, this thesis however assumes that a
spot auction and a derivatives auction have horizons of the same length, and are divided
in the same amount of time periods, K.

of bids and system asset characteristics. It is assumed in this thesis that the derivatives
market participants are a subset of the spot market participants, i.e. Ωforw ⊂{1...S}

3.2.1
Competitive bidders
Electricity market actors such as wholesale electricity producers and consumers, aggregators
of distributed resources and retailers, as well as merchant storage operators, participate in
spot and derivatives markets using competitive bids.
In a spot market, these actors typically formulate bids so as to maximise the economic
surplus they expect to derive from the market, for any feasible withdrawal/injection proﬁle
they can sustain. However, in a perfectly competitive market, actors are incentivised to bid
their true parameters, or an approximate representation of their true parameters if limited
bid formats are available. Typical bid formats in spot markets include price-quantity bids
and block bids, while a range of more complex, application-speciﬁc bid formats are found
in some markets or are proposed in the literature, e.g. [38–40, 45].
Some of those bid formats are linear, and are thus special cases of linear price-region bids
(see Deﬁnition 1 in Chapter 2) in that they include:
• a feasible region for the withdrawal/injection proﬁle, which can be described as a set
of linear constraints,
• a set of bidding prices, representative of the bidder’s willingness to pay for feasible
withdrawal/injection proﬁles.
For example, a price-quantity bid for withdrawal of energy in a given price area and time

3.2.2
System asset operators
In spot markets, system assets such as transmission and storage assets support the feasibility
and eﬃciency of the energy dispatch by providing opportunities for moving energy over
space and time. In some regions, they also participate in derivatives market where they
provide ﬁnancial rights. System assets traditionally only include transmission system
operators as they are natural monopolies. Some authors however propose to consider
storage assets as system assets so as to utilise these resources more eﬃciently [21, 41, 42].
Transmission and storage infrastructures can allow energy injected in some price areas and
time periods to be withdrawn in other areas and/or time periods. For a infrastructure
s, let a vector qs ∈RNK describe the contribution of the infrastructure to the local
energy balance in each price area and time period (qsnk > 0 means the infrastructure is
withdrawing power from price area n and time period k, qsnk < 0 means the infrastructure
is injection power). The feasible region of qs is denoted by Fs, and is communicated to
the market operator so these assets can be eﬃciently utilised when the market operator
computes an eﬃcient dispatch. It is assumed in this thesis that the feasible region Fs is
well represented by a system of linear constraints. These assets have negligible variable
operation costs, so only their physical constraints are considered by the market operator.

congestion rents. Congestion rents are derived from the spot market operator’s budget

3.3
Participation of complex ﬂexible infrastructures
The market mechanisms described in Section 3.2 are well suited to actors who can
communicate their true parameters (or good approximations thereof), i.e. whose operation
constraints and willingness to pay can be well represented by these mechanisms. Some
ﬂexibility resources however feature more complex characteristics that are not well caught
by these speciﬁc mechanisms. District heating or gas infrastructures, for example, may
have constraints coupling their electricity consumption or production in multiple price areas
and time periods, in ways that may combine diﬀerent sources of ﬂexibility such as energy
storage and transport capacity, demand-side ﬂexibility, as well as multiple alternatives for
energy conversion.

Characteristics and strategies of other market participants
This section describes the other actors and their participation in the derivatives and spot

The transmission line is a system asset, modelled by the market operators based on physical

lossless transmission line, with a limited transmission capacity of 5 MW.
The wind power producer has a variable electricity supply capacity in a range between 0
and 25 MWh/h. The available power is assumed to be well-known at the time the spot
market clears (a day ahead of delivery), but largely uncertain at the time the auction for

The load-serving entity has a variable electricity demand in a range between 5 and 15

The peaking power plant has a ﬂexible power output. Its variable cost of operation is

This actor oﬀers energy in the spot market by placing price-quantity bids.

3.5
Conclusion of Chapter 3
This chapter applied price-region bids to electricity spot and derivatives markets. It argued
that price-region bids can improve the representation of ﬂexibility resources such as district
heating infrastructures in a spot market. Moreover, Lemma 3 showed that ﬂexible spot
market participants can obtain economic surplus guarantees ahead of time by participating
in a derivatives market using price-region bids equivalent to their spot market bids. A case
study showed how price-region bids can straightforwardly be derived from a linear model of
operation. It also showed that the derivatives market bidding strategy proposed in Lemma
3 improves the worst-case total economic surplus of a ﬂexibility resource operator, while
increasing liquidity in the market.
This chapter has shown that, in standard electricity spot and derivatives markets, linear
bids such as price-quantity bids and linear block bids as well as the treatment of linear
system assets are special cases of price-region bids. This means that the market-clearing
procedure from Chap 2 can be used to clear these markets. Thus, price-region bids are
compatible with standard electricity spot and derivatives markets in the sense that:
• price-region bids can be added to these markets without aﬀecting the pricing method,
i.e. traditional market participants would be treated under the same terms as before;
• if these markets include price-region bids, Lemma 1 is veriﬁed, i.e, under the
assumptions of perfect competition and that participating actors are able to express
their true parameters, the market operators can issue feasible, incentive-compatible,
eﬃcient market equilibria which guarantee their budget balance.

CONCLUSION
This thesis has formulated a price-region bidding format. It is meant to allow market
participants to represent arbitrary linear physical and economic characteristics in electricity
markets, and thus to support the eﬃcient utilisation of ﬂexibility resources in energy
systems with large shares of renewable energy.
The parameters of a price-region bid characterise a feasible operating region as a set of linear
constraints, and represents the bidder’s willingness to pay with bidding prices. Bidding
prices can be associated both to market transaction quantities (e.g. energy withdrawal
in a short-term energy market) and to state variables. The thesis has formulated a price-
region-based market-clearing mechanism with applications to short-term energy markets
(spot markets) and to long-term markets for electricity derivatives. It was shown that
an eﬃcient market equilibrium based on locational marginal prices satisﬁes properties of
feasibility, incentive compatibility, socio-economic eﬃciency and budget balance for the
market operator. This result holds even if price-region bids include non-zero bidding prices
associated with the bid’s state variables.
Examples have illustrated how a ﬂexible infrastructure operator such as a district heating
utility can use price-region bids to represent its physical and economic properties in a
short-term energy market. It was shown, both analytically and through numerical examples,
that participating in a long-term market for electricity derivatives with the same bid as in
a future spot market provides ﬂexible actors with a lower bound to the uncertain economic
surplus it will derive from future short-term market prices. This strategy requires prior
knowledge of the ﬂexible asset’s characteristics, but does not require prior knowledge of
the withdrawal/injection proﬁle that will be prescribed by the spot market operator.
The price-region bid format generalises both speciﬁc linear bid formats used by competitive
market participants in existing spot and derivatives markets.
It also generalises the
treatment of system assets based on linear models of operation in both markets, including
the provision of ﬁnancial rights.
In particular, the price-region bidding strategy for
derivatives proposed in this thesis generalises the simultaneous feasibility test used in
ﬁnancial rights auctions, extending it to ﬂexible assets with variable costs of operation.
Price-region bids were shown to be compatible with markets where competitive actors
participate using speciﬁc linear bids, such as price-quantity bids, and where system assets
are represented by linear models of operation. This minimises regulatory and acceptance
barriers for the integration of price-region bids in existing markets.

4. Conclusion
4.1
Broader perspectives
A generalised bid format was proposed so as to target a wide range of ﬂexibility resource
types, including assets which combine multiple ﬂexibility characteristics such as district
heating infrastructures. Speciﬁc markets may however feature a range of speciﬁc bid
types which already provide good opportunities for characterising the locally-available
ﬂexibility resources. This thesis chose not to focus on speciﬁc, local market conditions,
but instead reach out to a broader range of applications. While some markets may beneﬁt
from integrating price-region bids, some other markets may already be eﬃcient enough
given the mechanisms and resources in place.
The choice to focus on the utilisation of ﬂexibility resources in short-term electrical
energy markets justiﬁes by the fact that these markets concentrate a large share of energy
transactions in deregulated electricity sectors, and that these markets are already used
for the eﬃcient dispatch of ﬂexible assets. However, markets for ﬂexibility capacity such
as reserve markets are commonly found in practice, and markets for physical rights are
discussed in the literature [33–35]. Complex ﬂexibility resources can thus be utilised in
other markets than short-term energy markets, or by combining revenue streams from
multiple markets. For some types of ﬂexible assets, the range of existing options may be
suﬃcient to utilise the resource eﬃciently, though participating in multiple markets can
be complex and pose regulatory issues. Market for physical rights are interesting in that
sense, as they concentrate revenue streams from multiple application in a single platform.
In line with ambitions for a stronger coupling of diﬀerent energy sectors, some authors
consider the possibility of fully integrating cross-sector markets, e.g. for power and gas
[50]. Integrating markets from diﬀerent sectors increases the cost-eﬃcient utilisation of
resources from both sectors as both sectors’ objectives are jointly optimised. Acknowledging
the gap with most common conﬁgurations where market operators for diﬀerent energy
carriers are independent entities, a body of literature has put focus on cooperation methods
between independent market operators based on distribution methods. For example, the
authors in [51, 52] propose an iterative algorithm for co-optimising the energy dispatch
over a power and gas network, while the authors in [53] consider a power and water
network. In such market settings, price-region bids could remain valuable market-based
coordination instruments between the market participants and the respective market
operators. Alternatively, embracing the decentralisation and digitalisation of the power
sectors, a growing body of literature is investigating fully decentralised energy markets,
which are cleared in an iterative process involving all market participants [54–56]. In these
conﬁgurations, each actor takes part in the iterative process with its own local optimisation
problem. If this type of market develops widely, the capability of the market to account
for speciﬁc physical and economic properties of market participants would not longer rely
on available bid formats, as the communication between the actors may solely rely on
iterative estimates for transaction quantities and prices.
4.2
Further research directions
The results presented in this thesis regarding the applicability of price-region bids relied on a
set of assumptions. Firstly, it has been assumed that physical and economic characteristics
47
==PAGE:56==
4. Conclusion
of ﬂexibility resources are well represented by linear models. In practice, some systems
may have non-linear features that can only be approximated by linear models. While
the thesis has shown that price-region bids can straightforwardly be derived from linear
operation models, it is of interest to consider price-region bidding strategies for non-linear
ﬂexibility resources, for example using inner approximation. Secondly, the statement that
price-region bids can truly represent linear characteristics relies on the assumption that the
size of price-region bids is not limited. In order to guarantee computational tractability,
constraints on bid sizes may be imposed by a market operator receiving price-region bids.
These limits may concern the number of constraints that are deﬁned, the number of state
variables that are required, or the number of price areas and time periods that are covered
by the bid. Methods for bid size reduction could be of interest, both in non-destructive
ways (e.g. keeping only umbrella constraints [57]) or in ways that lead to reasonable
approximations of the full linear model.
The price-region bid format proposed in this thesis is a generalised linear bid. Extensions to
mixed-integer linear, or convex non-linear bid formats could be of interest. The formulation
of mixed-integer linear price-region bids may not need to diﬀer greatly from this thesis’
formulation. Allowing the use of discrete state variables would readily enable price-region
bids to generalise mixed-integer common bid formats such as discrete block bids (such
those in [40, 45]). However, non-convexity of a mixed-integer market-clearing problem
poses challenges as marginal pricing cannot straightforwardly be applied [58–60].
The case study from Chapter 3 describes the price-region bidding strategy of a district
heating utility with perfect foresight over its conditions of operation (such as the heat
demand, the pipeline mass ﬂow rates, or the heat pump’s coeﬃcient of performance). Some
of these parameters may not be known with certainty a day ahead of operations, and it is
thus of interest to consider price-region bidding strategies under uncertainty. For example,
a robust feasible region of electricity withdrawal could be derived from a set of scenarios
for the conditions of operation. Lemma 3 argues that acquiring forward contracts with
a price-region bid identical to a future spot market bid provides a lower bound for the
uncertain economic surplus derived from spot prices. A spot market bid may however not
be known with certainty a year ahead. It is thus of interest to consider an extension of
Lemma 3 that could provide conditions for economic surplus guarantees that do not rely
on prior knowledge of future spot market bids.
Chapter 2 shows that eﬃcient market equilibria can be derived by clearing a price-region-
based auction. However, it relies on the assumption that the markets of interest are
perfectly competitive. Real markets are sensitive to market power, and the literature on
forward markets has paid attention to the implications of successive auctions on strategic
market participation. For example, [61] looks into the eﬃciency of successive forward
markets under cournot competition, [62] investigates the eﬀect that the allocation of
ﬁnancial transmission rights can have the behavior of actors with market power, and [42]
shows that the allocation of capacity-based ﬁnancial storage rights aﬀects the gaming
behaviour of strategic generators and loads. Allowing price-region bidding in energy and
derivatives markets may also have implications for the exercise of market power which need
to be investigated. Note that in some regions, the exercise of market power is tracked and
regulated with ﬁnes. Price-region bid format may make this procedure more challenging
48
==PAGE:57==
4. Conclusion
due to larger amounts of information to process.
It is also of interest to consider whether augmenting spot and derivatives markets with
price-region bids would provide good market signals on the long term, and incentivise
eﬃcient investments in ﬂexibility capacity. For the case of storage, [63] identiﬁes that a
strong penetration of competitive storage asset owners in a short-term energy market can
lead to price signals that prevent the maximising of welfare gains from those resources.
The author suggests that welfare gains are maximised when diﬀerent ownership structures
of ﬂexible assets are combined.
In Chapter 3, price-region bids were applied to a long-term auction where forward contracts
as well as point-to-point ﬁnancial transmission rights and proﬁle-based ﬁnancial storage
rights can be traded. Extending the application of price-region bids to an auction which
also clears capacity-based ﬁnancial rights (as in [15, 16]) is of interest.
The main contributions of this thesis are theoretical. Assessing the actual opportunity cost
of bid format limitations in existing markets would be of interest, and would help targetting
countries or regions where the under-utilisation of ﬂexibility resources and the potential
added value of price-region bids are most signiﬁcant. In order to apply case-speciﬁc insights
to other contexts, it would be interesting to examine how sensitive the under-utilisation
of ﬂexibility resource is to: (1) the ﬂexibility characteristics (see e.g. [64] for the case of
storage assets with diﬀerent energy capacities in the PJM market); (2) the local market
price dynamics (e.g. volatility, uncertainty). It may also appear that, in some regions,
the eﬃcient utilisation of ﬂexibility potentials is more signiﬁcantly hindered by regulatory
barriers (see, e.g., [65, 66]).
