CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Goals
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate techniques for reducing the complexity of
mathematical models used to predict real-world systems, such as fluid flow, acoustics,
heat transfer or quantum mechanics. Advances in science lead to increasing complex
models for describing these dynamical systems in the form of ordinary differential
equations (ODEs) or partial differential equations (PDEs). In many cases analytical
solutions does not exist and we have to resort to numerical methods for approximating
solutions.
As complexity of models increases, so does the computation times for
approximating their solutions.
Model reduction can be done in many ways. One way to handle the challenge of
increasing complexity is, if possible, to simplify the governing equations. As an exam-
ple, the Navier-Stokes equations, describing viscous fluid motion, can be simplified
by assuming advective inertial forces are small compared with viscous forces, which
lead to the simplified Stokes equations. Another way is to follow a brute-force like
approach by increasing the computational capacity, e.g. by using massively parallel
high performance computers (HPCs). The field of HPC has been subject to extensive
research the past years, both in computers and algorithms, and has lead to great de-
velopments. Yet another approach is to construct reduced-order systems that provide
accurate approximations to the original full-order systems. In this thesis we will focus
on the latter approach.
The field of reduced-order models (ROMs) can be described as computationally
inexpensive mathematical representations that provide fast approximations to the
full-order system. Discretizing a given model by a Galerkin method using standard
basis functions, e.g. piecewise linear basis functions, Fourier basis functions or poly-
nomial basis functions typically results in large discrete systems when high accuracy
is needed. These standard basis functions are problem independent and are designed
to capture local phenomena and hence do not contain the global characteristics of
the system. In this thesis we investigate methods for constructing problem dependent
basis functions containing the specific dynamics of the system. Provided the problem
dependent basis functions span lower dimensional subspaces than the standard ba-
sis functions, reduced-order systems using these problem dependent basis functions
can be obtained by projecting the full-order system onto low dimensional subspaces.
Each of these subspaces is often determined by a set of reduced basis functions with
global support and are constructed from high dimensional full-order solutions and
hence are problem dependent. Fine scale detail is embedded in these global basis

tem with few basis functions. The methodologies considered here are motivated by
the real-time context, e.g. parameter estimation or control, and by the many-query
context, e.g. design, optimization or multimodel/scale simulation. Both contexts are
crucial to computational science and engineering and to more widespread application
of numerical methods for ODEs and PDEs.
This thesis is undertaken to upon up a new research area at DTU Compute and
should be read in this context. Reduced-order methods have gained increasing atten-
tion the past years and have promising potential. Hence, it is a research area that
DTU Compute wish to expand into and hopefully be able to contribute to. The over-
all goal is to gain enough insight into state-of-the-art methods and the mathematics
behind them and to be able to apply them on a set of test problems. Another aim of
this written work is to provide enough information, examples and code to allow the
reader to understand and reproduce the results obtained.
The systems considered in this thesis are obtained from the discontinuous Galerkin
finite element method (DG-FEM) discretized systems of linear and nonlinear time
dependent partial differential equations. Common for all of these time dependent
systems are the need to solve the spatial approximation rapidly at each time step.
The choice of the DG-FEM approach for obtaining full-order systems is partly due
a personal wish to gain more knowledge about this method but it was also chosen
because, to my knowledge, it has not been combined with the reduced-order methods
considered here.
Since some of the PDEs considered in this thesis are nonlinear, projection based
reduced-order systems have computational complexity in the order of the full-order
system. Techniques for remedying this complexity dependence due to nonlinearities
are also considered.

CHAPTER 2
Full Order Method
Before taking on the task of constructing reduced order models we inevitably will
need a method for obtaining full order solutions.
For solving partial differential
equations computationally, an array of different methods exists for doing so. Among
these are the widely used finite difference, finite element and finite volume methods,
which are all methods for approximating spatial derivative operators. For advancing
the equations in time, there likewise exists many methods for approximating the
integration of systems of ordinary differential equations.
In this thesis the Discontinuous Galerkin (DG) finite element method is chosen
for the spatial approximations. The reason for this is due to its high-order accuracy
and hp-adaptivity, i.e. refinement of the approximation can be done by increasing
the number of elements (h-adaptivity) and by increasing the polynomial order (p-
adaptivity). For solving the ordinary differential equations we will resort to an explicit
high-order Runge-Kutta method.
2.1
Spectral Approximation
Spectral methods owe their origins to series solutions of partial differential equations
(PDEs). The fundamental idea behind spectral methods is to approximate solutions of
PDEs by finite series of orthogonal functions such as complex exponentials, Chebyshev
or Legendre polynomials. There are essentially three choices that needs to be made
to derive a spectral method:
• What expansion functions should be used
• How to represent the approximate solution
• The procedure by which the solution unknowns are determined
Throughout the remainder of this chapter we will cover the mathematics behind these
choices with specific focus on the Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element Method
(DG-FEM).

2.3.1
Polynomial Basis Functions
For polynomial spectral methods we need an efficient method for constructing or-
thogonal polynomial basis functions. The reason for choosing polynomials as basis
functions is because they are easy to evaluate, integrate and differentiate.
One convenient way to generate these basis functions is to use the Sturm-Liouville
theorem, which concerns the eigenfunctions of the eigenvalue problem known as the
Sturm-Liouville problem. For brevity we will not present the Sturm-Liouville problem
here but instead refer to the detailed coverage in [A K09].

Decomposition (POD)
The Proper Orthogonal Decomposition is a mathematical technique used for data
compression and low-order approximation. It is widely used in the context of fluid
dynamics and also goes under the names Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), in
linear algebra, Principal Component Analysis (PCA), in statistical analysis, Empirical
Orthogonal Functions (EOF), in meteorology and geophysics, and Kahunen-Loeve
transform (KLT), in signal processing and random processes.
The method provides an optimally ordered, orthogonal basis in the least-squares
sense given a set of snapshot data. Reduced order models are then obtained by trun-
cating this optimal basis. POD falls in the general category of projections methods
where the high-dimensional dynamical system is projected onto a subspace of the
original space. Since the POD basis functions are constructed specifically for the
dynamical system in consideration, they contain the dynamics of the original sys-
tem even for nonlinear systems. Hence, the nonlinear dynamics are preserved as the
low-dimensional model typically also will be nonlinear.
POD is widely used for providing reduced-order systems such as for compress-
ible flows, computational fluid dynamics (CFD), aerodynamics and optimal control
[Cha09]. Since POD provides optimal approximations in the least-squares sense, it
is usually gives better approximations than other reduced basis approaches with the
same dimension. For this reason it is chosen as the method for obtaining a reduced
basis and subsequently constructing reduced-order systems in this thesis.
In the following Section 3.1, we present the POD algorithm in a discrete setting
in the standard ℓ2 space. The relationship between POD and SVD is explored and
two methods for computing the POD basis is presented. Section 3.2 covers POD in
a general setting, where the continuous formulation of the POD is analysed. The
treatment of POD in Section 3.1 follows [Vol05; Vol11] and the general POD analysis
in Section 3.2 follows [CB03]. The Galerkin projection of full-order time dependent
systems onto reduced-order systems based on POD is introduced in Section 3.4 and
the last three sections 3.5, 3.6 and 3.7 are dedicated to applying POD and Galerkin
projection on three test cases for the one dimensional linear diffusion equation, the

4.1
Methods for Treating Nonlinearities
4.1.1
Missing Point Estimation (MPE)
Missing Point Estimation (MPE) was developed by Astrid et al. and was first in-
troduced in [Ast+08] as a method for improving the efficiency of the POD-Galerkin
projected reduced-order model of a nonlinear Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD)
model for a glass melting feeder. The approach is inspired by the Gappy POD ap-
proach proposed by Everson and Sirovich [ES95] for recovering missing data from a
static image. The basic idea behind MPE is to calculate the time dependent general-
ized Fourier coefficients a(t) based on partial observations by selecting a finite subset
of distinct spatial points and apply Galerkin projection on the subset of governing
equations corresponding to the these points. In particular, a restricted POD basis is
formed by extracting the rows of the standard POD basis vectors corresponding to the
selected spatial points. Subsequently, the subset of governing equations are projected
onto the subspace spanned by these restricted POD basis vectors. The selection of
sample points is based on limiting the aliasing error of the MPE approximation. Two
==PAGE:83==
4.2 Discrete Empirical Interpolation Method (DEIM)
69
selection criterions are presented in [Ast+08] based on minimizing the alias sensitiv-
ity of an alias operator representing the alias error. Since the minimization of the
alias sensitivity is a combinatorial optimization problem over all possible choices of
the sample points, suboptimal solutions are sought by limiting the condition number
growth of the restricted POD basis matrix.
4.1.2
Empirical Interpolation Method (EIM) and Best Point
Interpolation Method (BPIM)
An alternative approach for approximating the nonlinear term is to represent it based
on an interpolating scheme by a linear combination of its orthogonal basis functions
for which the generalized Fourier coefficients are determined using a small set of inter-
polation points. The Empirical Interpolation Method (EIM), proposed by Barrault,
Maday, Nguyen and Patera in [Bar+04], and the Best Point Interpolation Method
(BPIM), proposed by Nguyen, Patera and Peraire in [NPP08], follows this approach.
The set of basis functions can be determined using the same method as for the state
basis, e.g. using POD or, as originally suggested in [Bar+04] for the EIM, through
a greedy Reduced Basis (RB) approach. By using a small number of interpolation
points, the nonlinear term only needs to be evaluated over a subset of spatial points,
recovering the efficiency of the reduced-order system. EIM and BPIM differs in the
way the interpolation points are selected. In EIM, a heuristic greedy selection proce-
dure is employed to iteratively build the set of interpolation indices such that the nth
interpolation point is selected at the spatial location where the approximation error
of the nth basis function using the first n −1 interpolation points and basis functions
is the greatest. In contrast, BPIM selects the interpolation indices by solving an n-
dimensional optimization problem to minimize the least squares error between each
of the collected nonlinear snapshots and their approximation using n interpolation
points. The optimal selection of interpolation points are the reason for the naming
’best’ point interpolation method. The need for solving a constrained optimization
problem for obtaining the interpolation points makes BPIM computationally much
more expensive than EIM and in [NPP08] it is reported that BPIM only gives a
relative small improvement in approximation error.
4.2

jection with interpolation and can be considered a discrete variant of the Empirical
Interpolation Method (EIM) proposed in [Bar+04]. Since the DEIM is applied in a
semi-discrete setting, it is more easily implemented than its continuous counterpart,
the EIM and for this reason it is chosen method in this thesis.
DEIM is explained following Chaturantabut and Sorensen who introduced the
method in [CS10]. DEIM is an effective procedure for approximating the nonlinear

DEIM is a discrete variant of EIM proposed in [Bar+04]. As opposed to EIM,
DEIM threats the continuous domain Ωas a finite set of discrete points in Ω. DEIM se-

4.6
Numerical Test: 1D Nonlinear Shallow Water Equations
(NSWE)
The Nonlinear Shallow Water Equations (NSWE), also called Saint Venant equa-
tions in its one-dimensional form, are a set of hyperbolic partial differential equations
describing the flow below a pressure surface in a fluid. Here we will use them for
modelling the so-called dam-break problem which is a model of a dam containing two
uniform levels of water, both at rest, separated by a wall. At time t = 0, the wall
separating the two uniform levels of water collapses leading to two dominant features
emerging in the form of waves. A right-facing discontinuous shock wave travels into
==PAGE:123==

wave travels into the deep water region reducing the free-surface water height.
