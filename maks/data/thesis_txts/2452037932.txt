Executive Summary  
In today’s globalised world, supply chains are highly fragmented, complex and interwoven 
networks, as companies outsource production processes on different suppliers around the globe 
driven by economic incentives. This leads to increasing risks, such as safety incidents, human 
rights violations. geopolitical conflicts or environmental disasters, and an organisation must 
consider social and environmental factors in their supply chain in order to achieve a long-term 
success. Thus, sustainable supply chain management becomes increasingly critical, which 
describes the management of material, information and capital flow as well as cooperation 
among companies along the supply chain with consideration of all three sustainability elements, 
namely, economic, ecological and social.  
The scope of the thesis is to identify strategy and implementation approaches of selected 
German OEMs including the critical factors, the gap between literature and real-life situation, 
the main drivers and benefits as well as the challenges within a successful implementation of 
sustainability practices in supply chains. Therefore, a literature review is executed by, firstly, 
investigating the topics of sustainability and supply chain management individually and link 
them together in a final step. Thereafter, a qualitative study is conducted, which is based on six 
semi-structured expert interviews with general experts and representatives of the selected 
companies.  
Major findings of the study include the necessity of the promotion of industry-wide 
collaborations, the establishment of a department fully responsible for sustainable supply chain 
management, the implementation of a sustainability rating in the supplier selection process, the 
establishment of close supplier relationships as well as the creation of transparency mainly 
through technology.  
 
Keywords: Sustainable supply chain, Sustainability, Supply chain management, Supplier 
relationships, Industry collaboration, Transparency in supply chains

1. Introduction  
In today’s global business environment, companies are not only facing challenges in terms of 
economic aspects, but also in terms of social and environmentally friendly practices within their 
own business processes and supply chain in order to achieve a long-term success (Leppelt, 
2013). Multinational organisations as well as mid-sized companies are ramping up their 
international outsourcing activities. Thus, the significance of internationalised supply chains is 
nowadays visible in almost every industry. However, the international supply chains in the 
globalised world should not be entirely focused on the economic perspective but should also 
consider ecological and social factors. Ecological and social elements such as green supplier 
selection, circular economy, reverse logistics as well as fair labour conditions and protecting 
human rights must be taken into consideration (Golinska, 2014). Therefore, sustainable supply 
chain management (SSCM) is becoming increasingly critical due to changing customer 
behaviour, legislation and competitiveness. SSCM is defined as the intra- and inter-company 
configuration and optimisation of business processes with supplier and partners among the 
supply chain under consideration of economic, ecological and social aspects (Seuring & 
Mueller, 2008; Carter & Rogers, 2008) 
 
Focusing on selected original equipment manufacturers (OEM) in the automotive industry such 
as Porsche AG, Daimler AG and BMW Group, the present master thesis aims to analyse the 
strategy and implementation approaches of the selected companies as well as identify factors 
for the successful implementation of sustainable practices in their supply chain management 
(SCM).  
 
The first chapter investigates and clarifies the scope of the problem, thesis objective, the 
deviated research questions and the authors’ motivation to pursue the research topic. The 
second chapter introduces the executed methodology, which includes the research approach and 
design, whilst illustrating the data sources, where these include a distinction of secondary and 
primary data. Chapter three provides an extensive literature review, which is broken into two 
segments. Firstly, both concepts of sustainability and SCM are observed individually. 
Secondly, the linkage between both points according to the current state of SCM research is 
demonstrated. In chapter four, the procedure of the executed qualitative research is described 
and the findings of the analyses of general expert interviews and the analysis of the selected

1.2 Thesis Objective and Research Problem  
As previously stated, sustainable supply chain management is inevitable caused by government 
pressures in form of laws and regulations as well as increasing customer awareness. To meet 
these increasing regulations, whilst maintaining brand reputation, OEMs are willing to 
implement and include SSCM into their corporate strategy (BMW Group, 2019; Daimler AG, 
2019; Porsche AG, 2019). However, currently a generally accepted and clear strategy and 
implementation guide including critical factors OEMs have to consider is missing. 
 
On this basis, the research problem can be defined as follows:  
Which strategy and implementation approaches should OEMs consider in order to 
successfully implement sustainability practices in their supply chains?  
 
According to Malhotra, Birks, & Wills (2012) defining a precise research problem is the most 
important step in a research project. Therefore, the formulated research problem should be 
further broken down into several components in order to focus on key aspects providing clear 
guidance on how to proceed further.  
 
The research problem can be subdivided into the following main components:  
1. Explore important and critical factors an OEM must consider in order to successfully 
implement sustainability in their supply chains. 
2. Identify the gap between literature and the OEMs in terms of implementation 
approaches of SSCM. 
3. Show main drivers and benefits of SSCM for OEMs. 
4. Identify challenges OEMs are currently facing and develop corrective actions to 
overcome these challenges.

complex logistics in global supply chains may result in higher costs (Christopher, 2011).

a fifth trend called ‘yearly updated’ as the four mentioned disruptive technologies will lead to 
an increase in the rate of innovation and consequently faster model cycles which are updated 
annually (PwC, 2018).

rethinking their traditional business models and restructuring in terms of timescale, volume and 
complexity. The traditional revenues from vehicle sales and aftermarket will be extended with

technology affine and open-minded generations which have a pragmatic view of cars as a 
transportation resource, whilst ownership becomes less important as a status symbol (PwC, 
2018). The four key trends also show a clear vision towards a more sustainable future of the 
automotive industry considering environmental and social sustainability dimensions. 
Additionally, this transformation is causing changes to existing and developing supply chains. 
Thus, the implementation of SSCM has to be considered from the beginning on in this 
transformation.

2. Methodology  
This section explains the researchers’ methodological approach that has been chosen for 
conducting the present master thesis. The first subsection describes the ‘Research Approach’, 
the second subsection, the ‘Research Design’ and the final subsection, the ‘Data Collection’ 
and sources.  
 
2.1 Research Approach  
The research approach refers to the approach towards the development of theory. Saunders, 
Lewis, & Thornhill (2009) elaborate on three different research approaches namely deduction, 
induction and a mixture of both known as abduction. On one hand, deduction describes an 
approach where the researcher develops a theory or hypothesis and designs a research strategy 
to test the developed hypothesis. The objective of this approach is to establish new measurable 
perceptions as an outcome of a clear theory-guided research (Malhotra, Birks, & Wills, 2012). 
On the other hand, induction describe the procedure of collection of data and development of a 
theory based on the data analysis. Therefore, generalisable conclusions are drawn from repeated 
observations or events (Malhotra, Birks, & Wills, 2012). In general, the deductive approach 
tests a theory, whereas induction builds upon a theory (Saunders, Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009). 
The major difference between the two is that a deductive approach emphasises scientific 
principles and moves from theory to data, whereas an inductive approach focuses more on 
gaining an understanding of the meanings which humans attached to events. Thus, deduction 
concentrates more on the collection of quantitative data while induction mostly collects the data 
in a qualitative manner (Saunders, Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009).  
Subsequently, abduction, a combination of both aforementioned approaches, is possible and in 
a practical context often advantageous. Instead of only moving from theory to data or the other 
way around, the abductive approach moves back and forth between both approaches (Saunders, 
Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009). This approach often is used when an unexpected insight is 
discovered during the research, which redevelops the original theory. These theories will be 
tested by existing and new data and if necessary, revised.  
In this thesis, the researcher will follow an abductive approach, consequently the combination 
of the deductive and inductive approach. Firstly, an overview regarding sustainability in SCM 
will include a definition, starting points and steps for an implementation of SSCM and strategy 
approaches, drivers as well as identified challenges by gathering information through reading 
scientific articles, sustainability reports, annual reports and an extensive literature review. 
Furthermore, interviews with general experts and representatives of the investigated companies 
==PAGE:17==
     
 
 
 
10 
are conducted to acquire an insight of the real practical situation to evaluate how organisations 
approach and tackle the implementation of SSCM. To follow a deductive approach the 
hypothesis of factors for the strategic and practical implementation defined in the literature 
review are compared with the real-life situation and an analysis on differences and 
commonalities takes place. To go back to an inductive approach, a theory based on the lessons 
learned of the interviews is developed to state a lesson learned and practical SSCM 
implementation approaches for companies.  
 
2.2 Research Design 
Kothari (2004) defines the research design in his book Research Methodology – Methods and 
Techniques “[…] as the arrangement of conditions for collection and analysis of data in a 
manner that aims to combines relevance of the research purpose with economy in procedure” 
(Kothari, 2004:31). In fact, the research design is the conceptual framework and contains the 
scheme for the collection, measurement and analysis of data (Kothari, 2004). A well-developed 
research design ensures the efficiency and effectiveness within the making of the research as it 
provides a clear blueprint how the research is conducted and the research questions are 
answered (Malhotra, Birks, & Wills, 2012; Kumar, 2009). In literature, three different research 
designs are categorised for different types of research studies, namely exploratory, descriptive/ 
diagnostic and hypothesis-testing or also called explanatory studies (Kothari, 2004; Saunders, 
Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009).  
Firstly, the purpose of exploratory research studies is to formulate a problem suitable for more 
investigations or to develop the working hypotheses from an operational problem point of view. 
Thus, the research design for such studies has to be flexible and adaptable to changes, as the 
research process is usually unstructured and discover new insight and ideas (Saunders, Lewis, 
& Thornhill, 2009). Moreover, the exploratory research design has to provide the opportunity 
for considering different aspects of the problem and the sample size is often small. Information 
is often collected qualitatively from individuals with a high level of know-how, which is 
considered more important than quantity (Malhotra, Birks, & Wills, 2012).  
Secondly, descriptive research studies are concerned with describing the characteristics of an 
individual or of a group, whereas the diagnostic studies focus on the frequency with which 
something occurs (Kothari, 2004). From a research design point of view, descriptive and 
diagnostic studies have common requirements as the researcher has to define clearly what to 
measure and find adequate methods for this measurement. In contrast to the exploratory 
research design, the research design of descriptive and diagnostic research study has to be 
==PAGE:18==
     
 
 
 
11 
carefully planned and rigid to make enough provision for protection against tendencies in order 
to maximise the reliability.  
Lastly, within hypothesis-testing research studies or also known as explanatory experimental 
studies researchers are testing hypothesis of causal relationships between variables. Besides 
procedures reducing bias and increasing reliability, conclusion about causality has to be drawn. 
In fact, experiments usually meet this requirement and the research design of hypothesis-testing 
research studies could be described as the design of experiments (Kothari, 2004).  
 
In this present master thesis, the exploratory research design is used as the formulated problem 
area of sustainability in SCM is investigated further from an operational and practical point of 
view. The overall goal is to identify new insights in strategy and implementation approaches of 
OEMs regarding SSCM. To gather relevant data, a small sample size of experts was interviewed 
to gather qualitative data and acquire new findings and ideas within the area of SSCM. 
Therefore, the focus lies on qualitative rather than quantitative data. The new aspects, 
discovered through the interviews with individuals of a high-level of know-how, are taken into 
consideration and therefore a flexible research design is applied. Through the comparison of 
different OEMs, the opportunity of considering the identified new elements and aspects is 
clearly used.

3.1 Sustainability  
Sustainability and sustainable development3 have become common phrases in today’s business 
environment and society. Almost all companies including large-scale and mid-size 
organisations, governments and non-profit organisation focus on sustainable development and 
include it as a main component in its corporate strategy. Additionally, most organisations 
publish a sustainability report once a year in order to demonstrate their commitment to internal 
and external stakeholders (Kanning, 2013). 
The origins of the sustainability discussion can be traced back to the 18th century. However, the 
debate about linking economic, ecological and social development did not find international 
resonance until the early 1970s. A milestone in the sustainability discussion was the world 
commission on environment development (WCED) in 1982, also known as Brundtland 
commission, where countries united together to pursue a sustainable development. This was 
followed by the UN conference on environment and development in Rio de Janeiro in 1992 
where they developed and concluded on the Agenda 21. The Agenda 21 is an action plan 
developed by 172 nations, which includes guidelines for a sustainable development (UN Global 
Compact, United Nation Global Compact, 2019). 
The signed documents as well as the initiated activities led to the international awareness of 
sustainable development and to the establishment of sustainability as a central issue in business, 
science and society (Grunwald & Kopfmueller, 2006).  
Currently, the world’s largest sustainability initiative is the UN Global Compact (UNGC) with 
around 13 000 participants in over 170 countries. UNGC has defined ten universally accepted 
principles in different areas of sustainability, such was human rights, labour, environment and 
anti-corruption. In 2015, they published the report ‘Transforming our world: The 2030 agenda

for sustainable development’ which includes seventeen specific sustainable development goals 
(SDGs) where companies often align their sustainability strategy (UN Global Compact, United

3.1.1 Definition 
Originally, the term “Sustainability” descends from agriculture, forestry and fisheries and dealt 
with maximal sustainable yield, specifically regarding the quantities at which are not exceed 
the ability of regeneration in order to ensure a permanent availability of resources (Michelsen 
& Adomßent, 2014). Questions such as how many trees can be harvested and still have forest 
growth were understood as a sustainable mindset. The first major discussion was held at the 
WCED, headed by the Gro Brundtlandt prime minister of Norway. The outcome was a report, 
named “Our Common Future”, published by the Brundtland commission in 1987, which defines 
the term sustainability as the “development that meets the needs of the present without 
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” (United Nations 
General Assembly, 1987:46).   
This most famous definition is based on two perspectives (United Nations General Assembly, 
1987):  
q Intergenerational perspective, which takes the responsibility and needs of future 
generations.  
q Intragenerational perspective, which takes the responsibility of the present generation 
and claims equality between nations. 
Based on the definition and the two included perspectives the following essential findings are 
derivable (Kanning, 2013):  
1. Addressing human needs is the key for designing sustainable development processes.  
2. In terms of to the present as well as future generations.  
3. Equalisation between developed and developing countries is necessary  
==PAGE:24==
     
 
 
 
17 
4. Linking with the view that economic, ecological and social development should be seen 
as a single entity.  
 
Sustainable development consists of three main pillars, namely economic, ecological and social 
which are interconnected and have intertwining relationships (Kanning, 2013). Gladwin, 
Kennely, & Krause (1995) stated that a single-minded focus on the realisation of economic 
aspect alone will not be sufficient to achieve a long-term sustainable success as all three 
dimensions must be satisfied concurrently. Therefore, sustainability is about realising the 
potentials of each pillar or dimension simultaneously, managing trade-offs and synergies 
(Gladwin, Kennely, & Krause, 1995).  
Furthermore, sustainability is about integration and cannot be achieved through isolated actions, 
but rather by the result of interaction between organisations, individuals, societies and states in 
a global context (Gray, 2010). Finally, sustainable development is seen as a process of 
continuous development and social change (Grunwald & Kopfmueller, 2006).

3.1.3 Reasons and Value-adding Factors of a Sustainable Management Approach 
A sustainable management approach can deliver multiple value-adding factors and advantages 
to a business. The following section presents reasons why managers address sustainability and 
companies include it in their corporate strategies. The reasons and value adding factors range 
from a pure business-oriented approach to macro-orientated political and ethical reasons 
(Schaltegger & Burrit, 2005). 
 
Legal compliance  
Increasing legal aspects and regulations, which have to be considered by companies, force them 
to include environmental and social aspects in their business activities. Sustainability tries to 
qualify public and governmental concerns about climate change, industrial pollution as well as 
natural resource protection (Lubin & Esty, 2010). 
 
Managing business risk  
Business risks in todays globalised world such as child labour, discrimination, inhumane 
working conditions are reasons for implementing a sustainable management approach. With a 
sustainable management approach, the aforementioned risks and disruptions within a company 
and its supply chain can be minimised or even eliminated. Besides the stated business risks 
related to the social dimension of sustainability, risks may relate to the two other dimensions.

3.2.3 Trends and Development of SCM  
In today’s context, the main objective of a supply chain manager is to control and reduce costs 
while mitigating potential risk to maintain strong service levels (CSX, 2017). However, 
companies are facing different challenges caused by supply chain trends, which they must 
consider to stay competitive. The following common most important key supply chain trends 
are identified from several research papers: 
 
New Technology and digitalisation 
Titze, Searle, & Klappich (2018) identified eight main technology trends in SCM in their report 
pubslihed by the research and advisory firm Gartner. Artificial intelligence (AI) assist 
companies to automate decision making and to enhance the customer experience. Furthermore, 
AI solutions can identify patterns and predict future scenarios. In order to mitigate risk and 
disruptions in the supply chain advanced big data analytics enable companies to proactively 
take adanvatage of future opportunities. Thus, it helps to improve decision making in various 
areas, such as supply chain planning, sourcing, procurement and transportation (Titze, Searle, 
& Klappich, 2018). Furthermore, these tools develop capabilities of intelligent connection of 
data, which take over processes that previously were managed by human judgement (Handfield, 
Straube, Pfohl, & Wieland, 2013).  
In addtion, internet of things (IoT) has become a key method to collect valuable machine-
generated data to drive improvements in several supply chain domains. IoT provides detailed 
insights into performance and quality control and assist companies to install predictive 
maintenance, to better understand customer needs as well as to shape customer demand 
(Frolova, Eichstaedt, & Shestova, 2019; Titze, Searle, & Klappich, 2018). Additionally, 
intelligent things and robtotic process automation such as autonomous guided vehicles (AGV) 
improve intra logistics in controlled environments, such as warehouses. This gives companies 
the opportunity to redeploy their human workers to more value-adding activities, increase 
effienciency as well as cut costs. Virtual personal assistants, chatbots and other conversational 
systems are taking customer interaction to the next level and increase customer service without 
any human agent envolvement (Titze, Searle, & Klappich, 2018). Moreover, immersive 
technologies, namely, virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) enhance employee and 
customer digitial experiences. Therefore, repair and maintenance training instructions in

Sustainability  
As mentioned previously, companies are exposed to sustainability pressure caused by customer 
expectation, stricter governmental rules and laws as well as increasing societal awareness 
regarding sustainability (Grunwald & Kopfmueller, 2006). However, there is still high 
uncertainty around the implementation of sustainability practices in the supply chain and its 
critical factors affecting the performance. Due to this uncertainty, this research study aims to 
focus on these areas.  
 
Cost pressure  
Competitive cost pressure continues to drive companies to optimise their supply chain and 
logistics network. Simple cost savings methods such as moving supply and production to 
emerging countries to save labour costs are no longer enough and require more sophisticated 
cost saving methods (Handfield, Straube, Pfohl, & Wieland, 2013). As mentioned previously, 
additional cost savings can be achieved with the appropriate technology and digitalisation, 
which may need large antecedent investments. 
 
Globalisation  
Nowadays, companies outsource more and more activities globally to benefit from cost 
advantages. Therefore, a noticeable trend is the increased complexity of global networks which 
occur in different forms such as more suppliers, more production facilities, increasing product 
variations and stock keeping units (SKUs). However, this overwhelming complexity faces 
increasing risks and disruptions in the highly fragmented supply chain where many 
organisations struggle to maintain (Handfield, Straube, Pfohl, & Wieland, 2013). As minimum 
wages in typical outsourcing countries such as China, Thailand, Vietnam or Malaysia for 
mainly manufacturing are increasing many companies have invested in supply chain sources in 
Africa (McKinsey & Company, 2010).

Increasing customer expectation and customer relationship management  
As stated in the previous chapter, SCM’s main objective is to satisfy a customer need and 
provide the right product in right quantity and quality in the right time at the right place. In fact, 
customers have become more critical and demanding the perfect order and high-quality delivery 
reliability (University of Tennesse, 2013). Besides that, they expect highly customised and 
complex products and specialised logistics solutions. Globalisation and consequently an 
increasing global customer base create a new set of challenges for companies to serve all 
customer in a standardised way (Handfield, Straube, Pfohl, & Wieland, 2013).  
 
Increasing collaboration and networked economy  
In the past, companies typically considered themselves as individual player in the market and 
manage the supplier and customer relationships themselves (Handfield, Straube, Pfohl, & 
Wieland, 2013). However, in order to stay competitive and survive in today’s highly globalised 
and complex supply chain environment organisations have to develop collaborative 
relationships and strategic partnerships (University of Tennesse, 2013). Thus, in a networked 
economy, organisations are expected to be flexible and be able to quickly adapt to changes. For 
example, nowadays, customers are served through an increasing number of channels in direct, 
indirect and omnichannel distribution. In fact, products are not only sold directly to the end 
customer but also through middle entities such as distributors, wholesalers and retailers or 
omni-channel through a combination of indirect and direct approaches (Mangan, Lalwani, & 
Lalwani, 2016). To be able to provide such services, companies must outsource some processes 
and activities such as technology design, inventory management and planning executions to 
other supply chain partners and focus on its core competencies (Mangan, Lalwani, & Lalwani, 
2016). 
 
3.3 Sustainable Supply Chain Management  
Due to immense cost pressure and competition in the market, organisations often outsource 
value-adding activities in countries with lower price-levels and are therefore confronted with a 
higher number of suppliers as well as an increasing diversity and various supplier countries 
(Schaltegger & Harms, 2010). To meet political and socio-economic requirements companies 
have to focus on the implementation of sustainability in the global and complex supply chains. 
This chapter introduces the concept of sustainable supply chain management including a 
definition, the linkage between sustainability and SCM, starting points, as well as drivers and 
challenges organisations are facing.

3.3.1 Definition and Goals of SSCM 
Seuring & Mueller (2008) define sustainable supply chain management “as the management of 
material, information and capital flow as well as cooperation among companies along the 
supply chain while taking goals from all three dimension of sustainable development, i.e. 
economic, environmental and social, into account which are derived from customer and 
stakeholder requirements” (Seuring & Mueller, 2008:1700).  
 SSCM is increasingly recognised by organisations and is given increasing focus on the 
management of economic, environmental and social impacts of supply chains whilst 
maintaining competitiveness through meeting economic goals and customer needs (UN Global 
Compact, 2010; Seuring & Mueller, 2008). In fact, externalities, such as raw material shortages, 
climate change including changing weather patterns or geopolitical conflicts cannot be ignored 
and must be considered by companies within the SCM. This is necessary to improve their local 
impacts on workforce, society and environment in all places where they source, manufacture 
and transport their goods and services (EY & UNGC, 2016). Thus, SSCM aims to reduce social 
and ecological risk within the supply chain and realise opportunities product and process 
innovations through a cooperative development (Schaltegger & Harms, 2010). The overall goal 
is an entrepreneurial development towards a sustainable and long-term business success (Pagel 
& Wu, 2009). Moreover, SSCM plays a key role for organisations to maintain brand integrity, 
ensure sustainable business continuity and manage operational costs. A guideline of aspects 
included in SSCM show aforementioned ten principles of the UN Global Compact, covering 
all sustainability dimensions by considering factors such as human rights, labour, environment 
and anti-corruption (UN Global Compact, 2010).  
In the report ‘the state of sustainable supply chains’, 2016, Ernst & Young (EY) and UNGC 
identify two main characteristics of a sustainable supply chain, namely resiliency and 
responsibility. On one hand, resiliency describes the necessity of adaption to external factors 
and challenges such as weather impact, cybersecurity, natural disasters, resource shortages and 
climate change, which influence the ability of producing and delivering a product or service. 
On the other hand, responsibility addresses the mitigation of economic, social and 
environmental risks impact of a product or service during its lifecycle by adapting to changing 
conditions rapidly and being innovative (EY & UNGC, 2016).

UN Global Compact Principles  
The ten principles of UNGC are derived from several declarations, such as the universal 
declaration of human rights, ILO declaration on fundamental principles and rights at work, Rio 
declaration on environment and the united nations convention against corruption. The ten 
principles provide a guideline for a company’s value system including a principle-based 
approach, which covers major areas of sustainability, namely, human rights, labour, 
environment and anti-corruption. By incorporating these principles into strategies and policies, 
companies are establishing a culture of integrity and uphold their responsibilities for planet and 
people as well as achieving long-term success of their business (UN Global Compact, United 
Nation Global Compact, 2019). Further details about the UNGC principles can be found in the 
Appendix Section A3. 
 
SA8000 
The SA8000 standard was established by the Social Accountability International (SAI) and is 
a leading social certification for companies across the globe. The standard is contained within 
the universal declaration of human rights and ILO conventions and provide companies a 
framework to demonstrate the dedication to fair worker treatment. Thus, it measures the social 
performance in eight areas, which are important for social accountability at workplaces. 
Furthermore, SAI offers to assist organisations to continuously improve their social 
performance in areas of capacity management, stakeholder engagement and buyer-supplier 
collaboration (SAI, 2019).  
 
ISO 14001 
ISO 14001 is an environmental management norm from the international organisation for 
standardisation (ISO) and support companies in the specification process of requirements for 
the environmental management system. The intended outcomes of a successful implementation 
of the ISO 14001 norm in an organisation’s environmental policy are the enhancement of the 
environmental performance, the fulfilment of compliance obligations and the achievement of 
environmental objectives. Moreover, the ISO 14001 norm is applicable to any company, 
regardless the size, industry, type or nature (ISO, 2019).  
 
Global Reporting Principles (GRI) 
The GRI reporting principles offer standard disclosures and an implementation manual for 
organisations to prepare sustainability reports regarding the most critical impacts on

environment, society and the economy. The principles and guidelines are designed to be 
universally applicable to any organisation regardless their size, sector or location and to assist 
them to generate reliable, relevant and standardised information including an assessment of 
opportunities and risks. This enables companies make informed decision making within their 
business and their stakeholders (GRI, 2019).  
 
OECD Guidelines for multinational enterprises  
The OECD guidelines for multinational enterprises include recommendations addressed by 
governments to globalised companies operating from or in adhering countries. Thus, OECD 
provides non-binding principles which are consistent with international applicable laws and 
standards to form a responsible business conduct. (OECD, 2011). The guidelines express shared 
value of governments of countries which originates large direct international investments and 
are the home country of multinational enterprises. Moreover, the OECD covers economic, 
environmental and social topics such as human rights, employment and industrial relations, 
environment and science and technology (OECD, 2011). 
 
AA1000  
The AA1000 is a series of principle-based standards which provide organisations with a 
framework to demonstrate leadership and performance in accountability, responsibility and 
sustainability. Therefore, it assists companies to identify, prioritise and respond to sustainability 
challenges to improve their long-term performance and success. 
(Accountability, 2019) 
 
Code of conduct  
A code of conduct includes sustainability standards, expectations and guidelines for a 
company’s suppliers to ensure safe working condition, respectful employee treatment and 
environmentally friendly production processes. It usually consists of different components 
namely labour and human rights and standards, environmental policy, ethics as well as review 
and documentation policy (Ecovadis, 2019). Thus, it refers to both general standards and 
international norms such as the ones previously described.

3.3.4 Starting Points and Steps towards SSCM 
In 2010, the UN Global Compact published a paper including a practical guide for the 
implementation of supply chain sustainability, which establishes seven steps which a company 
has to consider and traverse (UN Global Compact, 2010):  
 
1. Understanding external landscape and establishing a vision for sustainable supply chain 
management. Additionally, a company has to identify the expectations of stakeholders 
including local governments, employees, customers, advocacy and activist organisation, 
non-governmental organisations (NGOs) as well as supplier themselves in a materiality 
analysis. Engaging stakeholders in an early stage of relevant standards and approaches 
of SSCM can be determined by identifying environmental, social and economic issues.  
2. Establishing sustainability expectations for the supply chain. Organisations have to 
translate and formulate their expectations identified in the materiality analysis in 
understandable guidelines which provide suppliers, and internal employees with clear 
directions. These guidelines should be summarised in a code of conduct which meets 
the specific concern regarding sustainability of a company. Supply chain management 
professionals must get familiar with the code and communicate it to all existing and new 
suppliers. There are multiple ways to successfully communicate the code to suppliers 
of an organisation, namely, in a special and on-time communication, inclusion in the 
first contact with suppliers, the integration of the code in supplier contracts as well as 
in a regularly scheduled business meeting where the code of conduct is reviewed. 
3. Determine the scope and goal of SSCM instead of just starting out aimless. Nowadays, 
supply chains are globalised, complex and spread out and it is unrealistic to focus on 
the whole supply chain at once. Thus, it is necessary to focus on strategic key suppliers 
which a company directly source from and which are critical to production. 
Furthermore, the focus should be set on industry-specific risks and problems regarding 
sustainability such as raw material extraction or labour issues.  
4. Engaging with suppliers to be aligned in terms of sustainability and to develop a shared 
mindset. This can happen through existing customer-supplier communication channels 
where sustainability expectations and dialogue can be regularly interchanged and 
monitored. Furthermore, sustainability should be added to the agenda for supply chain 
forums and meetings with key suppliers which enable discussions to share priorities and 
expectations. Besides the dialogues and discussions, monitoring and evaluating 
practices have to take place to check if suppliers are complying with the previously 
==PAGE:42==
     
 
 
 
35 
defined codes regarding sustainability. This can be executed by supplier self-
assessments and audits of the sustainability performance based on international 
standards and frameworks.  
5.  Determine roles and responsibilities to ensure an internal alignment of sustainable 
supply chain strategies with the overall business strategy. The executive leadership must 
commit to SSCM by defining specific milestones and metrics. The SCM professionals 
of a company are responsible for the implementation, however, are required to pursue 
cross functional coordination among business managers to create common alignment 
and understanding. Different functions impact supply chain sustainability e.g. product 
design, marketing & sales, logistics, business development. Therefore, it is empirical 
that clear roles and responsibilities are defined to meet the milestones and vision set by 
executives.  
6. Industry collaboration and multi-stakeholder partnerships are necessary to solve 
complex SSCM together. Typically, there are industry-wide problems which can be 
tackled by sharing best practices, joint standards and implementation. Nevertheless, 
there are several risks and opportunities in this phase of the SSCM implementation. 
Risks which occur mainly around the identification of the right partners in the industry 
which are at similar stages of supply chain sustainability, the draining of resources 
without appropriate results and the unwilling change of approaches to align with each 
other companies. However, besides the risks there are various opportunities in the 
collaboration. On one hand, it is easier to put pressure on suppliers as other companies 
which use the same suppliers have similar expectations in terms of sustainability. 
Furthermore, it increases the credibility of a company to its stakeholders by 
demonstrating awareness of supply chain issues within the industry. Finally, the sharing 
of resources in an industry collaborative approach helps smaller companies to realise an 
optimised implementation of SSCM in terms of time and expenses.  
7. Establishing goals and tracking as well as communicating performance. Setting 
appropriate goals is a collaborative process and have to be aligned with the company’s 
overall business strategy and objectives. In order to evaluate the performance against 
the defined goals, data collection and performance tracking are necessary. Finally, 
communicating and reporting the progress assists companies to create transparency to 
external and internal stakeholders. Reporting is a tool to demonstrate the management 
of environmental and social impacts in the supply chain with the assistance of the GRI 
framework.

Similar to the UNGC implementation guide, the Federal Ministry of Environment, Nature 
Conversation, Building and Nuclear Safety (2017) in Germany has launched a step-by-step 
guide to sustainable supply chain management that is defined by seven steps, which is slightly 
different and more practical oriented when compared to the UN Global Compact procedure 
(Federal Ministry for the Environment, Nature Conv, 2017):  
 
1. Mapping of the supply chain and clarifying key question regarding the upstream steps, 
the key suppliers, the activities in the supply chain as well as locations in the form of a 
materiality analysis. Based on the obtained data from internal (colleagues) and external 
(suppliers) information sources three activities take place: the most important product 
and service streams can be illustrated; the activities of direct suppliers and sub-suppliers 
can be compiled and direct and sub-suppliers’ sites can be categorised.  
2. Identifying sustainability impacts, assessing risk and determining action areas. In this 
step, a rough assessment of sustainability impact of direct- and sub-suppliers is first 
executed. Thereby, data sources may be internal discussion, direct exchange with 
suppliers, document research or dialogues with NGOs or industry associations. Based 
on the analysis, a prioritisation of the identified negative impacts and risks take place. 
Finally, specific action areas are defined, which are relevant to optimise and develop 
sustainability in the supply chain. 
3. Analysing gaps and deriving measures. Based on the analysis of step two, the 
organisation has to record objectives, measures and processes which are adaptable 
within the framework of SSCM. Therefore, the actual situation has to be defined by 
reviewing written documents such as company guidelines, process diagrams and target 
definitions as well as contact persons from various business units. Subsequently, 
appropriate measures, both internal and external measures, must be derived and 
prioritised. On one hand, internal measures are for monitoring NGO and media reports 
on sustainability topic or conducting trainings for employees. On the other hand, 
external measures may be formulating code of conducts, checking validity of supplier 
information and implementing auditing processes. 
4. Adapting internal structures and processes. New and existing processes within the 
organisation are implemented or adjusted based on the previously executed steps. 
Therefore, resources are needed to be integrated in existing structures and processes in 
==PAGE:44==
     
 
 
 
37 
form of expert-led teams, interdisciplinary overarching teams and concrete operational 
responsibilities and powers, to anchor SSCM in the company.  
5. Formulating supplier requirements and making them binding. During this step, a code 
of conduct is developed to communicate requirements and expectations to direct 
suppliers which is also integrated into the supplier contract. The core content of a code 
of conduct is based on international standards and frameworks, which are described in 
the previous chapter 3.3.3. 
6. Evaluating the sustainability performance of suppliers and building competences. The 
evaluation is usually performed through the supplier’s self-assessments and audits 
which could be differentiated among internal, supplier and third-party audits. If it 
reveals potential for improvement or failures within the evaluation the organisation 
should support the supplier with corrective action plans and therefore build long-term 
supplier competencies. This is only applicable if the supplier is willing to participate. 
Moreover, the existing supplier selection process must be supplemented with 
sustainability criteria.  
7.  The final step is the reporting where companies build transparency by disclosing 
information about the sustainability in SCM. It can be relied on different sources and 
reporting standards such as the previous described GRI.  
 
Based on the above described step-by-step guides for the implementation of SSCM by UNGC 
and the German federal Ministry of Environment, Nature Conversation, Building and Nuclear 
Safety, it could be summarised that a successful implementation, based on literature, includes 
the following steps: Firstly, the external landscape must be understood and a mapping of the 
supply chain is necessary. After that, significant sustainability impacts and risks must be 
identified in order to determine a clear scope. Consequently, the findings have to be translated 
in supplier expectations and requirements in the form of a code of conduct and anchored into 
the contract to ensure binding. In addition, the alignment and adaption of internal structures and 
processes are indispensable including defining clear roles and responsibilities. A permanent 
evaluation of the suppliers’ sustainability performance in the form of self-assessments and 
auditing is necessary to ensure the implementation of the requirements and expectations. 
Industry collaborations and partnerships are advantageous to demand common standards and 
requirements for suppliers. Finally, the last step is to report the progress and disclose 
information about SSCM with the GRI framework, to create transparency to all internal and 
external stakeholders

3.3.6 Challenges of SSCM  
Possibly the biggest challenge for companies that are implementing successful SSCM is to 
define a clear scope and to align the sustainable supply chain strategy to overall business 
strategy (UN Global Compact, 2010). Companies struggle to apply a strategic and holistic 
approach including breaking down economic, ecological and social objectives and balancing 
these (Cetinkaya, et al., 2011). In fact, the determination of sustainability impacts and risks are 
demanding and a focus on specific aspects, objectives, countries or sites is necessary (Federal 
Ministry for the Environment, Nature Conv, 2017). In addition, due to the global and complex 
supply chains, companies have to begin with the implementation pragmatically and to provide 
a step-by step as SSCM implementation takes time (Federal Ministry for the Environment, 
Nature Conv, 2017). SSCM goes beyond the direct suppliers of an organisation and 
sustainability impacts and aspects have to be investigated on a sub-supplier level (Federal 
Ministry for the Environment, Nature Conv, 2017). Thus, another challenge and issue for 
organisation is the missing transparency which consequently brings difficulties in engaging 
with their sub-tier suppliers (EY & UNGC, 2016).  
Furthermore, a lack of government enforcement, legislation and regulatory drivers in certain 
countries and regions, mainly developing countries, constitute a challenge for the 
implementation of sustainability in supply chains. In line with that, inconsistency of legislation, 
a multitude of jurisdictions and conflicting standards are a growing challenge for companies 
(EY & UNGC, 2016). Moreover, promoting sustainability with suppliers might be 
accompanied by obstacles especially if the possibility of influencing the supplier is relatively 
low caused by small contract volumes as well as missing direct contractual relationship (Federal 
Ministry for the Environment, Nature Conv, 2017). A challenge for specifically smaller 
organisations and companies may include the high costs for information search, communication 
as well as monitoring and screening of the development. Additionally, these companies fear the 
so-called spillover effects where competitors take advantage of the taken actions involving 
suppliers without investing themselves. Therefore, inter-organisational and industry-wide 
approaches and collaboration can be beneficial (Schaltegger & Harms, 2010).   
Finally, SSCM must be integrated into various existing internal processes to be effective. 
Besides sustainability effects, classic procurement criteria such as price, delivery time and 
quality must be considered in order to be effective and to maintain economically health. 
However, with a successful implementation of SSCM the sustainability aspect can bring a long-
term value, which must be communicated to external and internal stakeholders (Federal 
Ministry for the Environment, Nature Conv, 2017).

4. Analysis  
The analysis of the present master thesis consists of three main components, namely the 
‘Qualitative Data Analysis’, ‘Findings of General Expert Interviews’ and ‘Findings of Analysis 
of Selected OEMs’. Firstly, the procedure of the executed qualitative data analysis is explained. 
The findings of the interviews with general experts are then stated to get an initial impression 
about important topics, practices and implementation projections regarding SSCM. Finally, the 
analysis of the OEMs, namely, Porsche AG, Daimler AG and BMW Group is performed in 
order to identify different specific strategy and implementation approaches.   
 
4.1 Qualitative Data Analysis  
To investigate the primary data, gathered from semi-structured interviews with general experts 
and company representatives, a qualitative data analysis is executed. The process is performed 
by the researcher in three sub-steps, namely, transcribing, summarising as well as displaying 
and categorising which are further described below.  
 
Transcribing the data  
The conducted interviews are audio-recorded and subsequently transcribed as a first step in the 
data analysis. Transcribing means that the audio data is “[…] reproduced as a written (word-
processed) account using the actual words” (Saunders, Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009:485). The 
transcribing of an audio-recorded interview is extremely time-consuming, since not everything 
can be always understood immediately whilst listening to the recordings (Malhotra, Birks, & 
Wills, 2012). Within the transcribing process of the present master thesis, verbal utterances 
such as ‘hmm’ or ‘ehm’ as well as coughs and sighs are not included. All interview transcripts 
can be found in the Appendix section A13-A18. 
 
Summarising the data  
After transcribing the audio-recorded interviews, the most important key points of the 
interviews are summarised and proofed through quotations of the respective interviewee. In this 
process step the meaning of large amount of text are condensed into fewer words (Saunders, 
Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009). However, it is important to keep the main sense of what has been 
said to not falsify the gathered data (Kvale, 1996). As the interviews are conducted in German 
language, the most important quotations are verbatim translated accordingly.

4.2.1 Important and Critical Factors for the Implementation of Sustainability in Supply 
Chains 
The most critical factor, from a VDA point of view, is transparency in the supply chain in terms 
of sustainability where “[…] the focus should be laying on tracking not only the first-tier 
supplier but rather the whole supply chain” (P2)4. 
Tools such as self-assessment questionnaires (SAQs) can create transparency as well as help 
companies forward the requirements and expectations to suppliers and sub suppliers (P2). In 
fact, it is existing an automotive standard SAQ, which was developed in collaboration with the 
Drive Sustainability Initiative5 facilitated by CSR Europe6 (P2; P4; P5; P6; Drive Sustainability, 
2015). This standardised SAQ covers the areas of social and environmental sustainability, 
business conduct and compliance and supplier management and aims to identify if suppliers 
meet the minimum sustainability requirements (Volkswagen AG, 2019; Drive Sustainability, 
2015). The completed SAQs can be voluntarily shared by the supplier on the platform of the 
external service provider NQC, which reduce the burden on suppliers to fill in many 
questionnaires from different OEMs (Volkswagen AG, 2019). P2 clearly exemplifies that “[…] 
IT-systems in the form of a platform which connects OEMs with suppliers might be the key for 
creating transparency in supply chains” (P2). Thus, the VDA is currently working on a platform 
where findings or ratings of on-site supplier audits, not only result in SAQs, based on industry-
wide standards that are published and all cooperating OEMs can access. How exactly the 
outcomes are illustrated is not clarified yet. However, it is curtained that the audited company 
is deciding with which specific OEM they want to share their results, due to data privacy 
reasons.  
Another supplier-buyer platform is provided by EcoVadis, a company where P3 is working at.

four parts namely environment, labour and human rights, ethics as well as sustainable 
procurement” (P3). The SAQ is predicated on international standards such as the ILO 
guidelines, the Global Compact principles and the ISO26000, where the fulfilment must be 
proved with an extensive documentation by the rated supplier. Furthermore, the SAQ, which

4.2.2 Supplier Regulations and Compliance  
Based on the professional experience of P1, International acting companies attempt to 
implement sustainability in supply chains with a management system where ecological and 
social standards are considered (P1). “[…] The supplier selection process should be not fully 
focused on economic reasons but rather considered social and ecological standards in the 
assessment potential of suppliers” (P1). At the beginning suppliers were confused because they 
feared higher costs. “[…] However, these standards were implemented and surprisingly the 
bottom line was that costs have not increased significantly, with just a small difference of 2-
3%” (P1).  
 
All three experts agreed that the use of code of conducts, where expectations and requirements 
are clearly defined and communicated to the supplier, is mandatory. International standards and

the code of conducts is ensured with audits which can be differentiated in so-called desktop

must be proofed with extensive documentations (P2, P3).

4.2.3 Responsibilities within a Company for the Implementation of SSCM  
According to P1 the development of sustainability strategies and objectives are developed by 
cross-functional teams with representatives from various departments such as procurement, 
finance, production, quality control, logistics and SCM as well as public affairs (P1). The 
decided strategy and goals are presented in front of the board of management and discussed, 
adapted and approved. The tracking of the approved goals is in the responsibility of the cross-
functional team, which is regularly meeting with the board of management to report current 
progress (P1). In addition, the public affair department discuss the goals with public 
organisation such as World Economic Forum (WEF) about feasibility and rationality (P1).  
In line with that P2 states “[…] in my experience it is an absolute must that the top management 
of a company is involved and give their commitment in terms of sustainability especially in 
supply chains” (P2). Furthermore, P2 is recognising a trend that OEMs are implementing a 
department which is specifically responsible for sustainable supply chain management (P2).  
Similar to P1, P3 states that “[…] organisations establish a cross-functional commodity team 
with one person in charge” (P3). Especially in smaller or mid-sized companies the 
responsibility is often laying only at the sustainability department, which often does not have 
the manpower to approach other departments and leads to inconsequent implementation of 
SSCM (P3). Therefore, the commitment and involvement of the top-management is 
indispensable to sensitise all departments to set focus on sustainability in supply chains (P1, 
P2, P3). P3 complements “[…] it is very clear that sustainability practices in supply chain 
management must come from the executive level to be implemented successfully” (P3).

According to P1, in a short-term view “licence to operate” is the main driver. This describes 
the acceptance of the society and customers including a positive brand reputation (EY &

supply chains a challenge and problem may be the lack of understanding of suppliers. The 
problem is solved relatively fast for bigger organisation as the large quantity of orders is an 
important lever (P1). In addition, the explanation of requirements, both intra-company wide 
and to suppliers, is a challenge for organisation to achieve full commitment to SSCM (P1, P3).

To ensure compliance with SSCM requirements Porsche, Daimler and BMW have a code of 
conduct where sustainability standards are anchored and communicated to the supplier. The 
examination takes place through the industry wide SAQ, which all investigated OEMs use 
similarly, as well as external audits and internal assessments. Moreover, Porsche implemented 
a so-called S-rating to ensure a strict focus on sustainability in the supplier selection and 
contract awarding process. Thus, sustainability is as important as other criteria such as quality, 
cost and punctual logistics.  
 
When it comes to roles and responsibilities, BMW is the only OEM which has its own 
department fully responsible for all topics regarding SSCM. However, this department is 
integrated in the overall procurement process and works closely together with other functional 
departments. In general, in a BMW perspective it is important that all tools, measures and 
actions regarding SSCM are actively implemented in the procurement process to be successful. 
In contrast, Daimler and Porsche both see the main responsibility for SSCM in the procurement 
department in collaboration with a cross-functional team with members of all affect 
departments such as purchasing, finance, logistics, R&D and public affairs.  
 
All three OEMs identify pressure from legal institutions and NGOs in terms of laws, regulations 
and standards as a key driver. Additionally, the investigated OEMs recognise risk management 
and prevention as a valuable benefit and a corresponding driver. With social and environmental 
standards, which are implemented through SSCM, risks are perceived earlier or do not even 
occur. This leads to a higher delivery reliability and less shortages in supply. Furthermore, 
==PAGE:76==
     
 
 
 
69 
Porsche sees a trend that customers actively request sustainability within the product itself as 
well as all operations of the core business. In line with that the implementation of sustainability 
in supply chains help an OEM to keep the competitive advantage and thus SSCM is necessary 
to fulfil customers’ needs. For Daimler, the ongoing transformation of the automotive industry 
towards electrification is mentioned as an additional key driver as especially in battery supply 
chains occurring social and environmental issues. Whereas, BMW adds anchor shareholders as 
well as stakeholders, which are increasingly pushing sustainability in supply chains as a driver.  
 
The main challenge for all investigated OEMs while implementing SSCM is the creation of 
transparency in supply chains. Approaches towards transparent supply chains are currently 
focusing on close supplier collaboration and partnerships as well as the cooperation with NGOs 
especially for critical raw materials. However, the use of IT system will bring a great benefit in 
terms of transparency in supply chains whereas, blockchain is often mentioned as a key 
technology. Thus, Daimler is already implementing a blockchain prototype whereas Porsche 
and BMW are still in the initial stages. Moreover, expandable or missing standards and 
regulations especially in terms of auditing are another challenge, leading to overwhelming 
suppliers. Finally, Porsche is facing the company internal challenge of creating an appropriate 
business to justify investments and get full internal commitment.  
 
All in all, it can be stated that all OEMs follow a similar approach in the implementation of 
SSCM with focusing primarily on first-tier supplier and the 18 critical raw materials. There are 
several initiatives and associations, such as the Drive Sustainability initiative or VDA where 
they already work together to create industry-wide standards.

5. Recommendation 
This chapter answers in particular the following research questions:  
RQ 1(partly): Which factors are important for OEMs to consider within the implementation of 
SSCM?   
RQ4: Which corrective actions can be suggested in order to overcome the identified 
challenges? 
Based on the qualitative analysis performed in chapter 4, five major recommendations are 
deviated which should be considered to successfully implement sustainability in an 
organisations’ supply chain.  
5.1 Promoting Industry-wide Collaboration  
All three company representatives (P4, P5, P6) and the contact person from VDA (P2) indicate 
industry-wide collaboration as a key success factor for the implementation of sustainability in 
supply chains. To ensure the industry-wide collaboration, the participation and involvement in 
various sustainability initiatives and industry associations is necessary in order to create 
common standards. The first step towards industry-wide standards is already executed with a 
common SAQ from the Drive Sustainability initiative, which is used by all investigated OEMs. 
However, several platforms are used to share the results such as NQC, which is used by VW 
Group, BMW and Daimler among others and Achilles used by other members of Drive 
Sustainability, for example, Jaguar and Land Rover (Drive Sustainability, 2015). A common 
industry-wide standard is desirable for both, the OEMs and suppliers, for an easier and 
frictionless implementation of SSCM and reduction of distress for suppliers. Furthermore, this 
common platform should be connected with the new auditing platform, which is currently 
developed by VDA and the OEMs. This connection ensures a transparent, structured and well-
organised way of sharing sustainability information and data of supply chains across the whole 
automotive industry. To further relieve suppliers and the corresponding easier implementation 
of SSCM, industry-wide code of conducts and audit procedures are necessary to ensure a 
common approach. However, it is also important that the OEMs must be careful industry-wide 
collaboration to be compatible with antitrust legislation as well as competition laws and 
policies. OECD states in the conference paper ‘Competition Law and Responsible Business 
Conduct’ four prohibition areas through the competition laws and policies (OECD, 2015):  
1. Hard core cartels / naked restrictions  
==PAGE:78==
     
 
 
 
71 
2. Other anti-competitive agreements 
3. Anti-competitive unilateral conduct that exploits or extends market dominance and 
or market power 
4. Anti-competitive mergers and acquisitions  
Usually collaborative activities between competitors are investigated under 1. and 2. Thus, it is 
important to avoid cooperation of independent decisions on prices, volumes or where to or 
whom to sell.  
In the distant future, standards across industries and countries are advantageous to ensure the 
implementation of SSCM at the same extent everywhere on the world. To achieve this inter-
industry standards, as a basis the introduced platform by EcoVadis (P3) may be beneficial 
which already displays the results of supplier ratings based on SAQs from different industries 
on one common platform.  
 
5.2 Establishing a Department responsible for SSCM 
BMW is presumed as a pioneer in terms of sustainability in supply chain. Consequently, it is 
recommended to establish a department which is purely responsible for SSCM. It is important, 
that this department is integrated in the procurement process and is not acting independently. 
The SSCM department should be the central office which regulates and coordinates all SSCM 
topics in strong collaboration with affected departments such as purchasing, sourcing, quality, 
legal affairs and sales. Furthermore, the SSCM department should own all process and methods, 
which are applied to successfully achieve implementation. This assists the company to achieve 
full commitment as there is one department, which is clearly responsible for the implementation 
instead of several contact persons from different departments.  
 
5.3 Implementing a Sustainability Rating in the Supplier Selection Process  
In the automotive industry, traditional supplier selection criteria are usually price, quality, lead 
time, flexibility in supply, distribution frequency, collaborative design capabilities, exchange 
rates and tariffs among others (Chopra & Meindl, 2007). In addition, Weber, Current, & Benton 
(1991) identified that the ability to establish long-term paternships as an important supplier 
selection cirtierion. Along with that, factors such as performance evaluations, manufacturing 
capabilities’ assessment and quality system assessment should be considered (Barbarosoglu & 
Yazgac, 1997).  
Following the example of Porsche’s S-rating, to achieve a successful implementation of SSCM 
it is necessary to expand the traditional supplier selection criteria with a sustainability 
==PAGE:79==
     
 
 
 
72 
assessment of suppliers. Thus, suppliers with a poor sustainable performance in the supply 
chain will be identified from beginning and a collaboration can be avoided. In case of passing 
the sustainability rating, weaknesses and focus areas of the supplier are identified which leads 
to a relief of the supplier. In fact, the supplier is not overwhelmed by the multitude of 
sustainability requirements as the OEM can clearly show him where to focus in order to meet 
them. Currently, OEMs use a sustainability rating in form of SAQs only to assess the 
performance of suppliers, which are already awarded, but not in the supplier selection process.  
This will set standards in the strategic importance of sustainability and the issue of sustainability 
will be elevated in the procurement process to the same level as traditional criteria of price, 
quality lead time and flexibility in supply. A positive sustainability rating is mandatory for an 
order award to suppliers. As a result, potential social and environmental risks in the supply 
chain are mitigated and reduced at an early stage. 
 
5.4 Establishing close Supplier Relationships  
Supplier relationships encompass multiple points of interaction between various representatives 
of the organisation and its key suppliers. A common alignment including a clear governance 
structure, standardised performance measurement systems and corporate supplier strategies are 
indispensable. (Deloitte, 2015). The general benefits of supplier relationship management 
(SRM) are the following (Deloitte, 2015):  
q Reduce costs through increase of efficiency.  
q Increasing transparency and visibility on key relationships. 
q Driving supplier performance in a more transparent and sustainable manner.  
q Establishing continuous improvement for a more efficient and effective supply chain.  
In the context of SSCM implementation the advantages are mainly the increased transparency 
within the supply chain as well as the control regarding the supplier’s performance which 
enables an early risk assessment also in terms of social and environmental issues. SRM is a 
continuous process and includes usually four key steps, namely supplier segmentation, 
governance, performance management and supplier development (Deloitte, 2015; PwC, 2013). 
The first step is the segmentation and prioritisation of suppliers and the reflection of the 
strategic importance of the suppliers. Within SRM for the implementation of SSCM, the focus 
should be set on first-tier key suppliers. After that, in the governance process clear ownerships 
of supplier relationships are defined (PwC, 2013; Deloitte, 2015). In case of the successful 
establishment of an SSCM department, the ownership should be with them. As a third process 
step, the performance management takes place which includes the continuous tracking of 
==PAGE:80==
     
 
 
 
73 
operational measures (Deloitte, 2015). In terms of a SSCM implementation, the standardised 
SAQs, which a supplier has to fill out on a yearly basis, including documentation for stated 
information are desirable to manage a suppliers SSCM performance. Finally, the supplier 
development process takes place which consists of establishing additional value creation 
activities with the supplier. In the SSCM implementation procedure this step contains 
continuous support in form of workshops and trainings for supplier in terms of SSCM.  
 
5.5 Creating Transparency in Supply Chains  
A major challenge and issue during the implementation of SSCM is the missing transparency 
in supply chains (P2, P3, P4, P5, P6). As described in chapter 3, sustainable supply chains 
require two main characteristics namely, responsibility and resiliency. In fact, transparency is 
a critical issue to create responsible and resilient supply chains (EY, 2018). However, it has to 
be carefully evaluated in a company-specific context how much transparency is even needed 
for the implementation of SSCM (P6). The increasing transparency can also lead to the risk of 
losing knowledge and critical know-how of the organisation.  
EY suggests in their report ‘Towards transparent supply chains’ a five-step approach for the 
creation of transparency in supply chains (EY, 2018):  
1. Hot spot analysis: A good understanding of risks and impacts in the supply chain is 
indispensable. Thus, a list of all suppliers, including risk assessments per supplier is 
required to prioritise areas of focus. To get the full picture of the supply chains, sub-tier 
suppliers should be included in the analysis.  
2. Quantifying risks and opportunities: The current supply chain operation model is 
assessed with indicators such as governance, processes, people and technology. 
Therefore, risks and opportunities are quantified.  
3. Develop an issue specific program: An issue specific program must be developed to 
manage the identified risk and opportunities accordingly. Training and workshops for 
critical suppliers and stakeholders should take place as they play a fundamental part in 
identifying environmental, social and economic issues in the supply chain.  
4. Implementation program design: The program design has to be implemented through 
trainings and accurate communication. In fact, it is important to integrate the program 
design in existing processes to ensure compatibility. In addition, KPI frameworks are 
desirable to follow strategic objectives and as a guidance for the employees’ 
contribution.  
==PAGE:81==
     
 
 
 
74 
5. Reporting and continuous improvement: This phase includes measuring and 
communicating the results to internal and external stakeholders. Suppliers should be 
turning risks into assets through strategic partnerships including a two-way information 
sharing.  
 
Besides the five-step approach various papers identify the blockchain technology as a key for 
creating transparency in supply chains (EY, 2018; Deloitte, 2019; Capgemini Research 
Institute, 2018; Francisco & Swanson, 2018; DHL & Accenture, 2019). Blockchain is a 
distributed ledger technology that enables authenticated data communication between different 
participants of the supply chain without “the intermediation of a trusted central organisation” 
(Deloitte, 2019:4; Capgemini Research Institute, 2018). A blockchain consists of a ‘chain’ 
where each block stores the data of a transaction or executed event and is shared across all 
participating parties. Each block or transaction is time-stamped and “veriefied by a consensus 
of a majority of partcipants of the system” (Deloitte, 2019:4). Thus, the technology facilitates 
authenticity by proving that the data matches with all other existing data of the blockchain. An 
example of an blockchain transaction process can be found in the Appendix section A12 for 
better illustration.  
The primary potential benefit of blockchain is the increase of transparency and traceability of 
material supply chain and enables end-to-end tracking of products. Thus, it can be checked if 
corporate standards and requirements are met throughout the entire supply chain (Capgemini 
Research Institute, 2018). Moreover, blockchain achieve cost savings by lowering the losses 
from grey market trading and enable easier management of outsourced contract manufacturing 
though improved visibility and compliance. Finally, in times of increasing digitalisation 
blockchain reduces paperwork and therefore cuts administrative costs (Deloitte, 2019).  
However, the implementation of blockchain is a huge step and has to be carefully evaluated. 
There are some potential risks and hurdles in terms of lack of clear ROI, regulatory challenges, 
security of transactions and lack of complementary IT system of supply chain partners 
(Capgemini Research Institute, 2018).

6. Discussion  
6.1 Answering the Research Problem   
In this chapter the research problem is answered through evaluating each main component 
individually. 
 
Research Problem 1: Explore important and critical factors an OEM must consider in order 
to successfully implement sustainability in their supply chains. 
Through the analysis of secondary and primary data the following important and critical factors 
are identified:  
q Creating transparency in supply chain to ensure SSCM implementation beyond the first-
tier supplier (P2, P4, P5, P6). 
q IT Systems which connect OEMs and suppliers (P2, P3, P4).  
q Commitment of the top management (P1, P2, P3, P4, P5, P6).  
q Focus on critical materials such as cobalt and mica which are increasingly used through 
the transformation towards electrification especially in terms of human rights, anti-
corruption and antitrust laws (P4, P5, P6). 
q Promoting national and international sustainability initiative and industry associations 
to create industry-wide standards (P3, P4, P5, P6). 
q Stakeholder dialogue and close collaboration with supplier to support the 
implementation of SSCM through workshops, trainings and E-learning modules (P5, 
P6).  
q CO2 emissions as a critical aspect when focusing environmental factors (P1, P6).  
 
The given recommendations in chapter 5 are supporting the implementation of the listed factors. 
In fact, all aspects as previously mentioned should be considered to successfully implement 
sustainability in supply chains. 
 
Research Problem 2: Identify the gap between literature and the OEMs in terms of 
implementation approaches of SSCM 
To answer this research problem a comparison between the implementation approaches 
described in chapter 3.3.4 and the real-life situation based on the executed analysis is drawn.  
All OEMs performed a materiality analysis in order to understand the external landscape as 
well as stakeholders’ expectations and requirements. However, the materiality analysis is 
executed in terms of general sustainability aspects not directly for SSCM approaches.  
==PAGE:83==

The expectations and requirements for suppliers are recorded in OEM-specific code of conducts 
which is distributed to all existing and potential suppliers and included in supplier contracts to 
make them binding. The requirements formulated within the code of conduct of the OEMs is 
based on international frameworks and standards described in chapter 3.3.3, where the 
interviewees often mentioned the ILO standards, UN Global Compact principles, the OECD 
Guidelines and ISO 14001.  
For the determination of scope and goals of SSCM, the OEMs start with the direct first-tier 
suppliers and require them to implement sustainability also in its own supply chains. In 
addition, 18 critical raw materials were identified by the Drive Sustainability initiative, where 
all three investigated OEMs participate. For these materials, additional actions beyond the first-
tier supplier are executed such as supply chain mapping, supplier auditing and assessments are 
executed. Thus, OEMs operate in accordance with literature which recommends focussing on 
industry-specific risk and problems, in this case raw material extraction (UN Global Compact, 
2010)  
The OEMs engage with suppliers in various ways. Where Porsche is mainly focusing on the 
SAQ, BMW and Daimler additionally offer various supplier trainings, workshops and E-
learning opportunities and thus stay in constant dialogue with the supplier as suggest from UN 
Global Compact (2010).  
In terms of roles and responsibilities, the OEMs choose different approaches which ranges from 
lying in the procurement department (Porsche, Daimler) through having an own department 
which coordinates among all affected departments (BMW). In literature, a cross-functional 
team, with a clear head which is leading and coordinating the team, is suggested. Thus, the 
approach of BMW with a central SSCM department which coordinates and leads the cross-
functional representatives is identified as the best approach.  
To ensure industry collaboration and multi-stakeholder partnerships, all analysed OEMs are 
participating in various sustainability initiatives and industry associations. Porsche is focusing 
on national associations due to the fact that the suppliers are mainly located in Germany or 
greater Europe, whereas Daimler and BMW also participating in international associations such 
as AIAG. However, there is still room for the creation of more standards, such as an industry-
wide auditing system, where the VDA and OEMs are currently working on, and industry-wide 
code of conducts.

Research Problem 3: Show main drivers and benefits of sustainable supply chain management 
for OEMs. 
Through the executed qualitative study in this master thesis several drivers and benefits  are 
identified.  
First of all, the increasing legislation and political formalities as well as pressure from NGOs 
are identified as important main drivers for the implementation of SSCM (P2, P3, P4, P5). In 
addition, increasing expectations and requirements of stakeholder as well as shareholder, 
including investors force OEMs towards SSCM (P2, P3, P6). A driver, specifically in the 
context of the automotive industry is the transformation towards the electrification and the 
corresponding increasing usage of critical materials such as cobalt and mica. In fact, human 
rights violation and child labour is increasingly occurring in the supply chains of these materials 
needed mainly for the battery production (P4, P5, P6). Besides that, customers are actively 
requesting sustainable products, including sustainable management of all business operations 
especially in the supply chain (P3, P5). In line with that, through the fact of increasing 
awareness and importance of sustainability in today’s world, it is an important aspect of OEMs 
to keep the competitive advantage (P4). Finally, organisations are ensuring the ‘licence to 
operate’ through the implementation of SSCM, which describes the acceptance of society and 
customers and leads to a positive brand reputation (P1).  
 
As one of the main benefits for OEMs, risk elimination and prevention of potential risks is 
identified. In fact, through SSCM supply chain risk are identified in an early stage, are 
minimised or do not even occur (P3, P4, P5, P6). Moreover, through to closer supplier-buyer 
relationships, initiated by SSCM, an improving delivery reliability and security of supply are 
recognised. Further to that, the product quality is increasing through more qualified and trustful 
suppliers (P2, P3). Finally, in accordance the drivers as previously mentioned, the compliancy 
of governmental regulations, laws and formalities as well as the fulfilment of customer needs 
is warranted.  
 
Research Problem 4: Identify challenges OEMs are currently facing and develop corrective 
actions to overcome these challenges.  
The following major challenges are identified throughout the analysis of both general expert 
interviews and selected OEMs: 
q Missing transparency in supply chains (P2, P3, P4, P5, P6).  
q Missing or/and expandable industry-wide standards and regulations (P2, P3, P4, P5). 
==PAGE:85==
     
 
 
 
78 
q Missing internal commitment and business case to justify investments (P1, P3, P4). 
q Excessive and diverse demands of requirements and lack of understanding as well as 
overwhelmed of supplier (P1, P2, P4, P5). 
The given recommendations in chapter 5 address the listed challenges and give instructions in 
more detail how to overcome them.  
First of all, the promotion of industry-wide collaboration including the participation in national 
and international sustainability initiatives and industry associations is desirable. On one hand, 
this helps OEMs to create an industry-wide standard, which relieve suppliers and bundle the 
excessive and diverse demands of requirements together to one common approach. On the other 
hand, the collaboration is creating transparency when results of SAQs, auditing or assessments 
are shared on one common platform which connect the OEMs with its suppliers.  
Secondly, the establishment of a department fully responsible for SSCM, which owns all 
process and methods for the implementation, is favourable. This department should act as a 
central office which regulates and coordinates all SSCM topics in close collaboration with the 
affected departments. This ensures a clear definition of roles and responsibilities and thus helps 
the OEM achieve full internal commitment.   
Thirdly, the involvement of a sustainability rating in the supplier selection process helps OEMs 
to implement SSCM from beginning on. Therefore, suppliers with a poor sustainability 
performance are identified in an early stage and collaboration can be avoided. The sustainability 
rating of suppliers should be added and complement the traditional supplier selection criteria 
such as price, quality, lead time and flexibility. If the supplier is passing the sustainability rating, 
weaknesses and areas of improvement are investigated and the supplier knows exactly where 
to start and on what to focus. This gives the supplier the necessary support for the 
implementation of SSCM and decreases the complexity of requirements.  
Fourthly, another corrective action is the establishment of close supplier relationships through 
supplier relationship management, which provides increasing transparency and visibility in 
supply chains and supports the supplier within the implementation of SSCM. This can be 
ensured by tracking the performance of the first-tier suppliers with the SAQ and supplier 
development through various workshops, trainings and E-learning opportunities.  
Lastly, the creation of transparency and the corresponding achievement of responsible and 
resilient supply chains is recommended. Besides the suggested five step approach in achieving 
transparency, IT technologies such as blockchain and the participation of sustainability 
initiatives and NGOs can further help OEMs.

6.2 Limitations of the Recommendations 
First of all, the recommendations are limited to the acquired primary data of small sample size 
of six interviewees. In addition, several limitations for each specific recommendation should 
be taken into consideration, which are described in the following section. 
As previously indicated, companies must be careful in terms of industry-wide collaboration 
mainly for two reasons. Firstly, cooperation of independent decisions on prices, volumes, where 
or whom to sell have to be avoided, in order to stay compatible antitrust and competition laws 
(OECD, 2015). Secondly, organisations must evaluate carefully, which data and information 
they share with its competitors, for reasons of the protection of know-how and sensitive data 
(P4; P6).  
The establishment of an own department fully responsible for SSCM implicate a fundamental 
change in the organisational structure of the respective company. Risks of job redundancies and 
resource inefficacies may occur. Thus, clear roles and responsibilities as well as strict boarders 
to other departments involved in SSCM such as procurement and purchasing must be drawn. 
Moreover, this step has to be carefully planned and the implementation may take a certain 
amount of time, as the organisational change has to be approved by several instances and 
management stages especially of large companies.  
Within the implementation of a sustainability rating in the supplier selection process, companies 
may face extraordinary higher costs. An equal evaluation and assessment of all criteria is 
indispensable, to select the best supplier which is in accordance with both the sustainability 
requirements and the economic profitability (P4).  
The establishment of close supplier relationships is a key factor within the implementation of a 
successful implementation of SSCM. However, the degree of closeness to the supplier must be 
carefully decided to ensure know-how protection as well as the avoidance of showing supplier 
dependence (PwC, 2013). 
In terms of the creation of transparency in supply chains, technology is definitely a key 
differentiator. Nevertheless, the creation of a complicated IT infrastructure, such as blockchain, 
are implicating huge investments, a time-consuming process which a company is usually not 
able to manage on their own and need assistance of experienced consultancies within these 
areas (Capgemini Research Institute, 2018; DHL & Accenture, 2019). Furthermore, due to a 
spill over effect, other companies will benefit from the IT investments of the focal company. 
Thus, organisations should identify supplier or other players in the supply chain which will 
experience advantages and try to collaborate in order to split costs and implementation 
resources (Schaltegger & Harms, 2010).

6.3 Limitations of this Study  
For empirically testing the research problem a fully qualitative approach was selected. Even 
though, the researcher of this mater thesis paid close attention to guarantee validity, replicability 
and reliability of the qualitative study, several weaknesses and limitations are identified.  
To acquire primary data for both, general practical insights in the area of SSCM as well as 
understanding how the selected OEMs currently attempt to implement SSCM, expert interviews 
were conducted. Nonetheless, the results have to be viewed with caution, since the data is 
stemming from in-depth interviews whereas it “[…] remains the possibility of the bias of 
interviewer as well as respondent” (Kothari, 2004:97). In fact, misinterpretations, biases and 
practical errors, such as the confirmaiton bias or cognitive erros can occur and falsify the data 
(Malhotra, Birks, & Wills, 2012). In addition, it is hard to approach to interview important 
officials and members of higher management which may have given additonal valuable insights 
and data which is not aquirable by lower executives. Therefore, the quality of the data results 
depend strongly on the interviewee’s skills, expertise and willingness to share practical insights. 
Even though the experts were carefully evaluated and have severals year of experience in the 
area of SSCM, this might be a limitation of the study.   
 
Moreover, the focus of this study is lying on German OEMs due to the fact of the researchers 
origin and personal network. However, to gather data from OEMs of different countries would 
be desriable to get objective data and compare differenty approaches beyond country boarders.  
In line with that, a comaparison of the implemenation and strategy approaches of lower rated 
OEMs of the initial sustainability rating of chapter 4.3 Findings of analysis of selected OEMs 
would be interesting to identify a connection score and implementation practices in order to 
justify the rating. 
 
In total six expert interviews were conducted, namely three general expert interviews, and one 
interview with a representative of each selected OEM. Due to the fact that the interview method 
is relatively time-consuming, interviewing additional experts to validate the data with different 
opinions would have excelled the scope of this master thesis and the given timeframe (Kothari, 
2004).  
 
6.4 Future Research  
Taking the above-mentioned limitations into account, follow-up analyses and future research 
are needed to get further insights and practical implications in strategy and implementation 
==PAGE:88==
     
 
 
 
81 
approaches of OEMs related to SSCM. Since in total only six experts were interviewed the 
knowledge is highly limited by their experience and proficiency. Thus, to increase variety and 
validity of the results, more industry experts also from other OEMs should be interviewed to 
get greater understanding of different strategy and implementation approaches. Further to that, 
it is desirable to extend the scope of the German car manufactures to other industries and 
countries to identify best practices which might be applied simultaneously in German OEMs.  
As previously mentioned, one major limitation of qualitative research study by conducting 
interviews, it the difficulty of approaching members of higher management to acquire more 
detailed insights especially in strategy related topics. Therefore, to validate the data the 
subsequent study should target to conduct interviews with member of higher management to

In addition, the identified recommendations such as industry-wide collaboration and close

opportunities in a specific SSCM context. 
Furthermore, the blockchain technology is identified as a key technology in the creation of 
transparency in supply chain. In the recommendation section the general concept, advantage 
and requirements for companies for the implementation are shortly introduced. However, future 
research especially in the area of blockchain for supply chains should be conducted and further 
investigated.  
 
Lastly, the present master thesis focuses on strategic and general management approach of 
supply chains considering selection, coordination and planning of suppliers. Subsequent studies 
should investigate the different action areas of supply chain in dependence of the SCOR model 
to identify sustainability implementation approaches in operational processes design, make, 
deliver and return (Christopher, 2011).

7. Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to identify which strategy and implementation approaches should 
OEMs consider in order to successfully implement sustainability practices in their supply 
chains. By capturing primary data through expert interviews and analysing secondary data in 
form of sustainability reports, company websites and supplier portals, the researcher of this 
master thesis can draw the following main conclusions on the basis of the investigated research 
questions. 
 
First, several critical and important factors for a successful implementation of SSCM are 
identified, namely transparency in supply chains, IT systems which connect OEMs and 
supplier, Commitment of the top management, focus on critical raw materials, promotion of 
sustainability initiatives and industry association as well as stakeholder dialogue.    
 
Second, several similarities of OEMs are discovered such as the focus on first-tier suppliers and 
on 18 critical raw materials, where additional activities are executed such as supply chain 
mapping and auditing. Additionally, all three participate in various sustainability initiatives and 
associations where an industry-wide SAQ is developed to track the performance of suppliers. 
Minor differences are detected within roles and responsibilities where BMW established an 
own department responsible for SSCM whereas at Porsche and Daimler the procurement 
department accountable.  
 
Third, key drivers are the increasing legislation and political formalities, expectations and 
requirements of stake- and shareholders, customer request and the licence to operate more 
specifically the acceptance of society and brand reputation. In terms of benefits, risk 
management and prevention, improved delivery reliability, security of supply and quality as 
well as the compliance with governmental regulations are noticeable.  
 
Fourth, the key challenges of missing transparency, expandable industry-wide standards and 
regulations, internal commitment as well as overwhelmed supplier are identified. 
Recommendations in form of corrective actions are developed, namely promotion of industry-
wide collaboration, establishment of SSCM department, implementation of sustainability rating 
in the supplier selection process, establishment of close supplier relationships and creation of 
transparency in supply chains through a five-step approach and technologies such as 
blockchain.

Finally, this master thesis contributes to existing literature by identifying important factors, 
drivers and benefits and challenges in a practical context. Therewith, the study assists global 
acting companies within the implementation of SSCM through providing them best practices 
of OEMs and critical factors.

A13: Interview Freelance Consultant (P1) 
 
Critical factors of sustainability in supply chain  
Was sind die wichtigsten /kritischen Faktoren für international agierende Unternehmen 
bei der Thematik Nachhaltigkeit in der Lieferkette – auf was sollte bei der 
Implementierung ein besonderer Fokus gelegt werden?  
Hauptfaktor war in beiden Unternehmen CO2 Emission, mit der Nachhaltigkeitsfrage was sind 
die CO2-Mengen, welche die Lieferkette verursacht hat, Hauptsache Transport. Wir haben mal 
eine Studie gemacht mit der TU Berlin, die eine Reihe von Faktoren, hauptsächlich 
Umweltfaktoren. Interessant war da, dass der Hauptfaktor beim Transport immer wieder die 
CO2 Emission ist und dann haben Strategiesachen eine Rolle gespielt wie kurzen 
Lieferdistanzen vom Depot in die einzelnen Märkte oder Verkaufsstellen ein, und natürlich 
Elektrofahrzeuge. Ansonsten war der Hauptfokus, einen relativ schnellen Ersatz in den LKW- 
LKW-Flotten zu haben um von verbesserten Diesel-Fahrzeugen zu profitieren und die haben 
im Schnitt wenn man es mit einem LKW von vor 15 Jahren vergleich und einen von vor 5 Jahren 
vergleich haben wir da schnelle über 30% CO2 Emissionen gespart. Inklusive Fahrer-Assistenz 
Systeme, das sind umfangreiche Dinge, die mit MAN und Mercedes damals gemacht wurden, 
die nach wir vorlaufen, dass die Flotten relativ schnell ausgetauscht werden. Dies führte auch 
zu einem neuen Flottenkonzept, da hat man früher vorwiegend die Fahrzeuge gekauft, wobei 
es heute mehr Leasing-Systeme sind. Und dadurch sag ich mal einen relativ vernünftigen Weg 
gefunden hat die Fahrzeuge auszutauschen, typischerweise nach 7-8 Jahren.  
 
Sie sprechen hauptsächlich die Umwelt bzw. Ökologie Dimension der Nachhaltigkeit aus, 
haben Sie auch Fokus auf die zwei anderen Dimensionen der Ökonomie und Soziales der 
Nachhaltigkeit gesetzt?  
Ja klar, alle drei Säulen wurden verankert. Ökonomie wie vorhin schon erwähnt mit dem 
Leasing-System mit den Herstellern, weil zumindest bei Coca-Cola war es so, dass wir eine 
relativ große Flotte hatten und somit ein großes Momentum bei den Automobilherstellern wie 
MAN und Mercedes hatten und wir dadurch du Mengen Angebote profitieren konnten. Von der 
sozialen Seite war es bei Coke so, dass die Fahrer keine Dienstleister sogar Mitarbeiter des 
Unternehmens sind, das gilt in ganz Europa. Das heißt diese gehören ganz normal in die 
Tarifverhandlungen rein und deshalb hatten wir im Vergleich zu anderen Transporteuren 
immer den Vorteil gehabt, dass die Fahrer unter normalen Tarif und Freizeit Regeln sind. Das 
ist bei Coca-Cola ein Spezialfall. Wobei es bei dem Punkt auch die Discounter gibt, die ihre 
==PAGE:110==
     
 
 
 
103 
Ware selber abholen und dabei ökonomisch, ökologisch und sozial unter deren Regime laufen 
und Coke kein Einfluss hat.  
 
Welche Rolle haben ihrer Erfahrung nach IT Systeme gespielt, gerade zur Schaffung von 
Transparenz in der Lieferkette?  
IT-Systeme spielen in zwei Bereichen eine große Rolle. Zum einen in der Produktionsplanung 
und in der Belieferung der Haupt- und Zwischenläger. Dabei macht die IT die gesamte 
Routenplanung, d.h. wenn für den LKW eine Route festgelegt wird, dann wird über IT 
Programme komplett die Route festgelegt und die Ware entsprechend dazu berechnet. Im 
Prinzip läuft es ja darauf hinaus, dass verschiedene Aufträge in das Computer Programm 
eingegeben und das Programm berechnet dann die optimale Route. Das spart im Bezug auf 
Nachhaltigkeit natürlich CO2 Emissionen ein und kosten natürlich im ökonomischen Sinne.  
 
Supplier regulations and responsibilites 
Wie 
versuchen 
international 
agierende 
Unternehmen 
Nachhaltigkeit 
in 
der 
ganzheitlichen Lieferkette zu verfolgen? Liegt der Fokus auf Schlüssel Lieferanten, first-
tier Lieferanten sprich direkte Lieferanten oder werden auch die Lieferanten der 
Lieferanten überprüft?  
Dort gibt es ein weltweit einheitliches Lieferantemanagement System, wo die Lieferanten aus 
Sozial- und Umweltstandards eingeschworen werden. D.h. der Einkauf entscheidet bei der 
Lieferantenwahl nicht nur aus rein ökonomischen Gründen, sondern zieht Sozial- und 
Umweltaspekte mit ein. Da sind vor ca. 8-9 Jahren gewisse Kriterien erarbeitet worden. Da 
geht es zum Beispiel um die ganzen Sozialstandards, typischerweise bei Lieferketten weltweit 
keine Kinderarbeit, fair Arbeitszeiten, Mindestlöhne. Das hat am Anfang gewisse Probleme 
erzeugt, weil die Lieferanten gesagt haben, dass es natürlich teurer wird was wir euch liefern. 
Diese Kriterien wurden trotzdem mit den Lieferanten umgesetzt, wobei einige dann über Bord 
gegangen sind und andere Lieferanten sind neu dazugekommen, weil sie sich entsprechend 
qualifiziert haben. Unter dem Strich sind die Kosten nicht entscheidend höher geworden und 
hat nur einen kleinen Unterschied von 2-3% gemacht.    
 
Wie werden Richtlinien und Anforderungen für Lieferanten definiert? Arbeiten 
Unternehmen mit Code of Conducts wo diese beschrieben werden?  
Dies wird sichergestellt über Audits. Coca-Cola hatte zum Teil eigene Auditoren und hat zum 
Teil auf groß Auditgesellschaften und schicken dann auf Basis von Standards und Katalogen 
==PAGE:111==
     
 
 
 
104 
die Leute raus und überprüfen diese sozialen Standards. Es gibt dabei internationale 
Standards, z.B ILO, und solche wurden dann eingefordert. Coke ist dabei hingegangen und hat 
versucht internationale Standards zu nutzen und die in die Auditkataloge einzupacken. 
Ähnliches gilt dann auch im Thema Nachhaltigkeit, da bei Coke relative viele Produkte z.B 
Orangensaft, Zuckeranbau wo es internationale Standards, wie man dann z.B. mit der 
Herstellung von Agrarprodukten umgeht, ob das mit oder ohne gerodeten Wald stattfindet oder 
Düngemittel. Wobei auch viele andere Unternehmen in der Branche sich auch den 
internationalen Standards anschließen. Dabei werden die Lieferanten dazu verpflichtet diese 
Standards bei sich und ihren Unter-Lieferanten einzuhalten.  
 
Wenn ja, wie wird sichergestellt, dass diese Richtlinien und Forderungen eingehalten 
werden? Gibt es standardisierte Audits seitens für bestimmte Branchen? Gibt es 
Nachhaltigkeitszertifizierungen für die Lieferanten erlangen können? 
Dies wird hauptsächlich durch NGOs sichergestellt. Da gibt es gerade in der Getränkeindustrie 
z.B. Water Alliance, die bis hin zum Bauern die Standards untersucht und denen hilft diese 
einzuhalten.  
 
Responsibilites 
Wie sollten/waren Ihrer Erfahrung nach, die Verantwortlichkeiten innerhalb der Firmen 
bei der Implementierung von Nachhaltigkeit aufgeteilt werden? Ein Team das zuständig 
ist oder jeder Fachbereich eine Teilverantwortlichkeit die konkreten Meilensteine haben, 
die die zuständigen Abteilungen erreichen/abarbeiten muss? Ist Top-Management in dem 
Themengebiet involviert und pusht die Implementierung?  
Strategieentwicklung und Zielsetzung ist von Cross-funktionalen Teams gemacht worden. Da 
waren Leute aus dem Einkauf, Finanzen, Produktion, Qualitätssicherung, Logistik und nicht zu 
vergessen Public Affairs. Dort wurden von jedem Fachbereich Ziele entwickelt und

Critical factors of sustainability in supply chain 
Was sind die wichtigsten /kritischen Faktoren für OEMs bei der Implementierung der 
Nachhaltigkeit in der Lieferkette – auf was sollte ein besonderer Fokus gelegt werden?  
Der wichtigste Faktor aus Sicht des VDA und auch der Unternehmen ist die Transparenz in der 
Lieferkette in Bezug auf Nachhaltigkeit. Darauf sollte meines Erachtens und auch aus der Sicht 
des VDA ein großer Fokus gelegt werden, um die Verfolgung der Nachhaltigkeit nicht nur beim 
first-tier Lieferanten sicher zu stellen, sondern eben auch die Anforderungen weitergeben 
entlang der Lieferkette. Dies sollte bis hin zu den Rohstoffen erfolgen, um eine ganzheitliche 
Nachverfolgung zu gewährleisten. Das ist natürlich eine große Schwierigkeit und 
Herausforderung. Aus eigenen Quellen und Erfahrungen kann ich schon sagen das eine

Konfliktmineralien z.B. Excel Fragebögen. Da gibt’s gespaltene Meinungen darüber, jedoch 
ist es ein Verfahren, um die gesagte Transparenz herzustellen und auch die Anforderungen 
weiterzugeben an die Lieferanten und die Lieferanten der Lieferanten. Und da sollte auch in 
Zukunft Fokus daraufgelegt werden.

Es gibt spezielle Programme und jedes Programm hat verschiedene Komponenten und

Entwicklung der direkten Lieferanten, also diejenigen mit denen wir direkt Verträge haben. Bei
