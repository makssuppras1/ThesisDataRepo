Abstract
In an attempt to create new methods to dynamically analyse the power system, [1] devel-
oped a method to assess a generators’ aperiodic small signal rotor angle stability (ASSRAS)
in real-time, using analytical methods. This thesis applies the ASSRAS methodology to
assess the aperiodic rotor angle stability for two cases of voltage collapse. The search for
ASSRA unstable cases was narrowed down using the TESCA contingency analysis.
Building upon the previously developed SOSPO SW-platform [2], real-time PMU measure-
ments were emulated using dynamic simulations in PSS/E. The ASSRAS margins were
calculated from the measurements and published back to the platform, where assessment
and visualization methods could be accessed agent-wise.
Using Thevenin-equivalent circuit theory, generator-wise distances to instability with re-
gards to active power injection were derived and visualized within the impedance plane.
In addition to this the active power margins and over excitation limiter statuses of the gen-
erators were also visualized. Ultimately, this could inform system operators of operation
close to ASSRA instability in real-time.
A remedial action against ASSRA instability was developed using optimal power flow
(OPF). The OPF is convexified using semi-definite programming (SDP) to locate the
global optimum. The SDP-OPF is used in combination with an ASSRAS constraint to
compute the optimal re-dispatch, to ensure ASSRA stability of the system. The SDP-OPF
developed was validated against known implementations in Matlab and verified using load
flow calculations in both PSS/E and Matlab.
The results obtained throughout the thesis serve as a proof of concept for a stability con-
strained optimal power flow (SCOPF). The project successfully redistributed the generator
set-points, ultimately resulting in a steady-state operation of a power system.

1
Introduction
The share of renewable generation is increasing on a worldwide basis, adding more stochas-
tic elements to the planned power grid operation. The fluctuating nature of wind and solar
energy will decrease the robustness of current power systems, which will be further lowered
as larger inertial plants are phased out.
In the past, offline diagnosis has ensured a stable and reliable operation of the power
grid, but the uncertainty that comes with renewable energy will continuously increase
the computational efforts needed to perform contingency analysis and state estimations,
respectively. Therefore, the importance of defining methods for quickly and accurately
assessing the power grid stability has increased, because previously used methods are
simply not fast enough. These new assessment methods should aid system operators in
real-time monitoring of the power grid and in implementing preventive control, ensuring
the grid stability.
The control center’s responsibility can be boiled down to three main tasks: Monitoring,
analysis and control.
Traditionally, monitoring and analysis is performed using state
estimation and contingency assessment via steady state power flow and local measurements.
The protection and control for a previously undefined contingency is based on the power
system operators’ speed of reaction [3]. In some cases, by the time it takes them to process
the situation, come up with a possible solution, and implement it, it is already too late.
Previously, the lack of computational power and insuﬀicient power system measurement
data has made automatic real-time control of power systems an infeasible solution. How-
ever, with the large-scale implementation of Phasor Measurement Units (PMUs) and de-
velopments in fast assessment methods, real-time wide-area monitoring and control is
becoming a real opportunity [4], [3]. Combining the traditional methods with dynamic
look-ahead simulations as well as automatic reaction to contingencies would fortify the
power system against the ever changing nature of renewable energy sources.
1.1
Background
In an attempt to create new methods to dynamically analyse the power system, [1] devel-
oped a method to assess a generators’ aperiodic small signal rotor angle stability (ASSRAS)
in real time, using analytical methods. This particular stability mechanism is thought to
have been one of the links in the chain of events leading to the 2003 blackout in Den-
mark and southern Sweden [4]. There, the heavily loaded system experienced a number
of contingencies, pushing its generators to their limits, and eventually past them, causing
a system wide voltage collapse and causing millions of people to be without electricity.
Simulations of this event show that the voltage collapse could have been detected via
dynamic simulation and even avoided by redistributing only around 7.6 MW [4].
The ASSRAS method was included in both the SOSPO and SARP initiative, which aimed
to address problems related to future power system operation as well as furthering along
the technology needed for said challenges. One of the results of these projects was the
SOSPO Software Platform (SW-Platform), intended for development and testing of new
methods. The SW-Platform offers the option of using any preferred programming lan-
guage and the ability to upload test systems, along with supporting software in the loop
functionality. Furthermore, multiple agents can subscribe and interact on the platform
[2].

1.2
Problem Statement
Building upon the previous work of [1] and the SOSPO and SARP projects [2], the aim
of this thesis is to implement a real-time stability assessment on the transmission level of
a power system, along with designing a corrective and stabilizing controlling action. The
assessment method used, Wide-Area Assessment of ASSRAS in Real-Time [5], provides
the ability of early warning and early prevention of a system voltage collapse caused by
aperiodic rotor angle instabilities.
Using the Nordic32 power system model, several contingencies leading to a system voltage
collapse will be modelled in PSS/E and simulated dynamically. Using these simulations,
Phasor Measurement Unit (PMU) data are synthesized, simulating real-time measure-
ments. Once the PMU data have been obtained, the wide-area software platform is used
to create a stream of PMU snapshots, emulating real-time power system measurement data.
On this same platform, several applications will be created in Python. This includes an
implementation of the assessment method and applications to visualize and analyze the
ASSRA stability of the power system. Finally, a remedial action incorporating optimal
power flow (OPF) will be designed and tested.

2.1
Power System Stability
Ever since the formation of the first electricity grids, power system stability has been, and
continues to be, an immensely important and relevant topic within the field of electrical
engineering. Despite the extent of the subject, the authors of [9] manage to define it rather
neatly with only a few sentences:
”Power system stability is the ability of an electric power system, for a given
initial operating condition, to regain a state of operating equilibrium after
being subjected to a physical disturbance, with most system variables bounded
so that practically the entire system remains intact.” [9, p. 1388]
It is important to note that this definition applies to the system as a whole. A single
generator might lose synchronism without risking the stability of the rest of the power
system [9].
Figure 2.1: Power system stability as classified in [9] and [10].
These physical disturbances referenced manifest as for example line or generator outages,
and a single outage can cause a cascading effect in a power system. As a consequence,
multiple instability mechanisms may arise endangering the system. In order to ease and
coordinate analysis of power system stability, the authors of [9] and [10] have classified the
three main instability mechanisms as seen in Figure 2.1. The principal focus of this thesis
will be on aperiodic rotor angle stability, which falls under the category of angle stability
in Figure 2.1, further discussed in section 2.2. Of course, fully de-coupling the instability
mechanisms described in Figure 2.1 is easier said than done, and system frequency is
directly correlated to the rotor angle speed of each generator. However, in order to keep
the thesis concise, frequency stability as it is defined in Figure 2.1 is considered to be
outside of the scope of the thesis.

2.3
The ASSRAS Method
In [1], the author presented a method which can assess a generator’s stability with regards
to ASSRAS by using the generator’s injection impedance Zinj as well as the Thevenin
impedance ZTH of the system, as seen from the node of injection. The method has since
been further tested and developed in multiple papers, with the end goal of incorporating it
into real-time observation and control of future power systems ([5], [7], [6] and [11]). The
ASSRAS method requires that a high number of PMUs be present in the monitored power
system, which most TSOs are already implementing. The ASSRAS assessment method
is valid when the observed system is considered to be in QSS. Therefore, the following
assumptions must hold in order to obtain accurate results [5]:
• The node of active power injection has a constant voltage magnitude.
– This node can either be at the machine terminal (automatic excitation) or be-
hind the saturated direct axis reactance Xd (manual excitation/over excitation
limter has been activated).
• Loads are modelled as constant impedance.

– The instantaneous impedance values seen from the generators are used, since
the method is evaluating the instantaneous operating conditions.
– For constant impedance loads, the active and reactive power vary with the
square of the voltage magnitude [12].
– Long term dynamics for the load are omitted.

3.1
Choosing Test Systems
In order to analyze ASSRA unstable cases, a well-known system prone to rotor angle
instabilities was needed.
For the project, the Nordic32 system was chosen, which has
a total of 46 buses, 52 transmission lines and 22 transformers, amongst other elements.
Compared to the eﬀiciency of the ASSRAS assessment, this system has been shown to
be appropriate for close-to-real-time computation ( ≈3 ms ), which allows for real-time
monitoring of ASSRAS margins. Furthermore, the Nordic32 system is well-known within
stability studies [13], which makes it ideal for analysing ASSRAS instabilities.
Since the verification processes of optimal power flow can be highly complex in larger
networks, the project needed a smaller system to verify OPF results and methodology. This

The Nordic32 System
The power system model chosen to perform ASSRAS analyses is a variation of the Nordic32
system, initially described by [13]. It was originally designed to test transient stability as
well as long term dynamics and has been widely used for conducting voltage stability tests
(see [5], [7], [15] and [16]). At the time of creation the dynamics of the system were meant
to emulate the Swedish/Nordic power grid, which was characterised by a long grid with
large generation in the north and a large load in the central area. The grid is especially
prone to voltage and rotor angle instabilities, making it an interesting system on which to
conduct an ASSRAS analysis [13].
An approximate geographical representation of the system can be seen in Figure 3.2,
where the North area has a lot of hydro generation, the Central area has large loads, and
although the Southwest area has some thermal generation, its only connection to the rest
of the system are two transmission lines going to the Central area. All of the synchronous
machines are equipped with AVRs and PSSs as well as over excitation limiters, except
for the machine connected to bus 2032, which is manually excited. A single line diagram
of the system can be seen in Figure 3.3 for 130kV, 220kV and 400 kV. Not included in
the Figure 3.3, are the 15 fixed shunt elements. Their respective buses along with MVAR
contribution can be found in Table B.5. The nominal frequency of the system is at 50 Hz.
As is listed in [15], two types of contingencies are likely to cause voltage instability for the
Nordic32 system:
• Tripping of a transmission line from the North to the Central area.
• A generator outage in the Central area
The voltage instability stems from the over excitation limiters of the synchronous machines
activating due to reactive power limitation and instability usually ensues 1-2 minutes after
the initial contingency [15].
In recent years the authors of [5], [7] and [6] have extensively used slightly modified versions
of the Nordic32 system to test the ASSRAS assessment. The most notable modification is
the addition of four load buses 141-145 where ULTC transformers connect the load buses
to buses 1041-1045.
The base case system used in this thesis is stable, and using the TESCA method described
in section 4.2, as well as the contingencies described [13], two scenarios leading to aperiodic
rotor angle instabilities are identified and simulated, see chapter 4.

4
Case Selection
To establish a baseline for the real-time stability assessment using ASSRAS there is a need
for identifying critical ASSRA scenarios. Conventionally, offline contingency analysis tools
can be utilized to identify ASSRAS instability scenarios. These methods commonly rely
on fixed boundaries for voltage, active- and reactive power margins, from which a solution
can be obtained for the classical power flow problem either by AC- or DC power flow.
In a context where the system is operated close to the planned operating point, these
methods may be suﬀicient to assess system stability. However, it has been proven that
dynamic limits interfere with the convergence properties of the power flow methods [18],
which causes a requirement for a dynamic stability assessment method.
The necessity of a dynamic assessment method is further supported by the increased
integration of renewable technologies within the power system. The stochastic nature of
renewable production influences the operating characteristics of the power system. This
is where conventional methods fall short in assessing the system stability dynamically
because of the vast computational burden.
Unlike conventional power flow solvers, the TESCA method is capable of evaluating sev-
eral contingencies in real-time. By approximating the the power system with its Thevenin
equivalents as seen from the generators’ point of view, the power flow equations can be
solved to derive stability margins. For the Nordic32 system, the TESCA (Thevenin Equiv-
alent Static Contingency Assessment) method will be used to identify relevant operating
scenarios that lead to ASSRAS instability.
4.1
Case Criteria
A viable ASSRA instability case needs to have similar characteristics as the cases described
in for example [5] and [7], where a contingency causes OELs to trip, and the lack of
steady-state synchronizing torque results in a voltage collapse and a loss of synchronism.
As discussed in chapter 2, it can often be hard to tell the difference between a voltage
collapse due to voltage instability versus one caused by rotor angle instability.
Furthermore, for a case to be viable for testing, the time to collapse must be long enough
for the margins to be calculated whilst also leaving some time for automatic dispatching
of a predetermined remedial action or emergency control. With this in mind, the TESCA
method is used to narrow down the search for contingencies.
4.2
TESCA methodology
Essentially, the TESCA method is a contingency analysis tool which detects possible
ASSRA instabilities, but in stead of using conventional methods like Newton-Raphson
and the Gauss-Seidel power flow, the TESCA method is based on Thevenin equivalent
circuit theory. This allows for a simplification of the system impedances, and a reduction
of the computational burden. Since the TESCA method is based on the same principles
as the ASSRAS method (described in more detail in chapter 6), where the power system
is also reduced to its Thevenin equivalent as seen from the node of injection, the same
assumptions apply for the TESCA method as is discussed in section 2.3.
In [18], an emphasis was put on addressing individual generators’ Pδ-curves to determine
whether an equilibrium exist between the generators’ capabilities and the power flow solu-
tion. It is fundamental to the TESCA method that a post-contingency steady-state exists

in order to derive a justifiable stability assessment. The method has been proven to be
able to detect ASSRAS instability for both single and multi-machine assessments, but the
distance to instability was only valid for single-machines. Thus, a differentiation between
single- and multi-machines must be taken into account, when designing remedial actions
to sustain system stability. Further details about the TESCA method can be found in
[18].

The SOSPO Environment
5.1
Reasoning behind environment
For the project to reflect a physical implementation of Wide-Area applications, a com-
munications framework called the SOSPO Software Platform was established to emulate
real-time applications. The framework was originally constructed to support and demon-
strate the development of individual Wide-Area applications, but it can also be extended
to perform demonstrations with closed-loop functionality according to [19].
The closed-loop functionality allows for simulating and verifying results with RTDS (Real-
time Digital Simulation), reducing the time for possible commercialization of the proposed
WA technology. Additionally, the framework allows for offline dynamic simulations with
Siemens’ PSS/E (Power System Simulator), which can supply the fundamental data for
simulating system measurements.
The SW-platform was developed as a part of the SOSPO (Secure Operation of Sustainable
Power Systems) project, which received public funding to address problems arising in
power systems with a high percentage of renewable generation in regards to stability and
security [20].
As previously mentioned, incorporating high shares of renewables adds
stochastic elements to the system’s production, consequently causing heavily fluctuating
operating points. Thus, assessing the system stability in real-time is essential to identify
proper controlling actions for maintaining secure and stable operation of the power grid.
The SOSPO SW-platform can utilize synchronized PMU measurements as the enabling
technology for real-time stability assessment as well as available RTU (Remote Terminal
Units) measurements. It can be accessed from various programming platforms, enabling
development of WA-applications in many environments.
5.2
WA-Control Framework
The Wide Area Control framework within the SOSPO-Platform enables data accessibil-
ity from various platforms, which can be used for supervision and control, or as a data
foundation for more complex assessment methods. The framework’s structure has been
visualized in Figure 5.1.

5.2.1
Phasor Measurement Units
Wide-Area applications depend to a large extent on the quality of data as well as the
assessability of the data. The data stream from PMU measurements is gathered close to
real-time, which makes them suitable for real-time observability.
Furthermore, PMUs enable monitoring of voltage and current phasors, where samples
from different PMUs are synchronized through GPS time-stamps. This allows for a precise
comparison between multiple measurements, all originating from the same moment of time,
and opens up the possibility of system state identification using real-time wide-area data.
The sampling of PMU measurements is given by the monitored frequency, implying that
a PMU measurement are available every 20 ms and 16.6 ms for 50 Hz and 60 Hz systems,
respectively [1]. The accuracy of the PMUs is considered to be very good, with Total
Vector Error under 1%. In worst cases, the deviation adds to ±0.01 rad equivalent to
±0.57°, which results in a maximum time error of 31.8 µs [1].
To reduce the scope of the project, the concentrated data is assumed to be non-corrupted
by communication errors, representing the actual system states. Moreover, it is assumed
that PMUs are located at each node within the system, allowing full observability of the
power systems states. Needless to say, bad data detection policies should be addressed
in future implementations, since a distorted state identification could lead to misinformed
decision making or unwanted automatic control. For future implementation, state esti-
mation techniques could be addressed to validate the representativity of the data in a
real-time applicable fashion. Further details on the subject can be found in [19].

7
Optimizing ASSRAS
To address the problem of generators operating too closely to the ASSRAS instability
boundary, it is advantageous to find a new stable operating point, which would ideally
decrease the output from highly loaded generators and thus distribute the burden across
the remaining capacity.
The optimal distribution of the burden is a diﬀicult subject because many stakeholders
within the power system are affected by a potential redistribution. Ultimately, the re-
distribution could affect the electricity market and thereby, affect the free market power,
which is unfavourable.
To prevent redistribution from interfering with free market forces, it would be beneficial
to minimally affect the market clearing set-points. This would render the power system
more robust against system contingencies whilst ensuring minimal biasing of stakeholders
within the market.
However, in emergency conditions, where a system blackout is imminent, the only objective
for the redistribution should be to maintain system operation at any cost, as a system
blackout is considered to be the ultimate price. The duality in this redistribution calls for
some different approaches to maintain system stability.
In [23], emphasis has been put on addressing redistribution of active power in emergency
conditions, where the electrically closest generators active power reserves’ are successfully
used to avoid an ASSRA induced instability. This method outperforms time-consuming
offline computations, which makes it suitable for real-time remedial action that can prevent
instabilities.
Combining the two, this projects aims to develop a redistribution, where emphasis is put
on robustifying the power system against ASSRAS instabilities and correcting the active
power set-points to avoid the unstable scenarios. To solve this problem, an Optimal Power
Flow (OPF) is performed. An OPF allows the specification of constraints that may prevent
further instabilities, whilst also ensuring that the deviation from the original set-points is
minimized, resulting in a more robust system state.

7.4
Stability Constrained Optimal Power Flow (SCOPF)
The SDP-OPF can be modified to incorporate system security and stability factors. Once
a system threat has been detected using real-time measurements, an SDP-OPF can be
used to redispatch the active powers to avoid unfavorable system conditions. The SDP-
OPF is favourable in emergency conditions, because unlike convex solvers it is able to
overcome local optima.
Emphasis has been put on including ASSRAS margins in SDP-OPF, whilst complying
with generic system constraints. The work done was inspired by some of the work done in
[8], where the author successfully re-dispatched generation to ensure rotor angle stability
for an 11-bus system. To visualize the concept of ASSRA induced re-dispatch, Figure 7.2
has been constructed.
Figure 7.2: Utilization factor U and the trigger margin
From Figure 7.2, five generators’ operating points are visualized on the ASSRAS-impedance
plane. The trajectory of G1 illustrates that a system disturbance has occurred, forcing
G1’s operating point to move within a pre-defined boundary. This boundary is designed
as a trigger margin (similar to the one described in [7]) for redistributing active power
to avoid ASSRA instabilities.
In an optimal case, the exact trigger margin should be
examined system-wise but for [7] it was found to be as low as 0.5%. The authors of this
thesis have found that the SDP-OPF computation time and system trajectories are rele-
vant for remedial action, but determining a suitable stability margin is considered outside
the project scope.

7.5

Traditionally, the SDP-OPF mainly focuses on a variant of the cost objective, which was
used in (7.27). However, the optimal objective of a stability constrained OPF may deviate
from a cost-objective. For example, more emphasis on keeping the same voltage profile
or reactive power output might be desirable. For this project, the aim was to minimize
the effects of the remedial action, whilst still ensuring system stability.
Therefore, an
objective that minimizes the deviation from generators’ original set-points seemed to be a
good starting line. The set-points are often set by market clearings mechanisms, which in
non-critical stability cases should ensure a minimal deviation from market clearings and

Case Studies
For the two objective functions used in the optimization, the penalty weight ω0 was varied

might deviate from one another, but this should only affect reactive power and objective

discussion on the rank 1 condition can be found in section 8.8, as some of the optimizations
did not produce rank 1 results using the eigenvalue analysis.
During the sensitivity analyses, it became apparent that the MOSEK solver sometimes
fails at what appears to be random penalties. To solve this issue, the penalty only needed
to be varied slightly, allowing the solver to converge again. Within the testing phase of the
project, the reactive power limits were scaled up according to [32] to force convergence of
the algorithm. Additionally, the branch ratings were also varied within the testing phase,
as they may not be representative for the actual system. The final results are obtained
with non-scaled constraining limits.

8.5
Constructing the Ybus in MATLAB
In an attempt to locate the source of the error, the PSS/E raw data file was uploaded to
MATLAB, where the function PSSE2MPC was used to construct a MATPOWER case struct.
From there, the makeYbus function was used to construct the admittance matrix as well
as the branch admittances of the steady state case described in subsection 8.3.1. Seeing
as the shunt was not incorporated in the admittance matrix currently in use, the main dif-
ference between the two matrixes was expected to be on the diagonal. The MATPOWER
matrix includes the shunt admittances, whereas it was excluded from the Real Time Grid
Model’s Ybus. This was discussed in subsection 5.4.4, where shunts were approximated by
subtracting it from the load demand during the optimization process.
By using MATPOWER to construct the admittance matrix, the hope was to remove any
possible man-made sources of error, since a lot of small changes had been made to the
Ybus during the optimization process. A comparison was thus made between the most
recent version of the Ybus and the MATPOWER one. In Figure 8.7 the difference between
the diagonal elements of the two matrices is shown for susceptance, with the shunt buses
colored green. The shunts contribute a considerable amount of susceptance to the diagonal
line, along with what is presumably the self-admittances of the tap-changing transformers.
The self admittances of the transformers were included in the construction of the Real
Time Grid Model admittance matrix, but seem to have been omitted by accident from the
most recent admittance matrix (see further elaboration on this in chapter 11).
Comparing the off-diagonals, very slight errors were seen for both conductance and sus-
ceptance. These errors are most likely due to numerical approximations and the inclusion
of the small resistance of R = 10·−4 to the system branches.
These were not further
investigated.
With limited time, the authors chose to use MATLAB’s bus admittance matrix for further
investigations. Implementing the admittance matrix in the Real Time Grid Model so that
it is suitable for SDP-OPF was left for future work due to time constraints.

8.6
Verifying SDP-OPF Results in Matlab and PSS/E
Since the load flow in PSS/E seemed to be converging towards low-voltage solutions, the
OPF results were also verified using MATPOWERS load flow solver. The MATPOWER li-
brary [28] includes a standard power flow solver which ueses the standard Newton Method.
In PSS/E the load flow used the Full Newton-Raphson Method. Furthermore, the initial
guesses for PQ-busses in PSS/E were changed to V = 1 and δ = 0° to ensure conver-
gence towards higher voltage solutions (above the locus described in Figure 2.6). Load
flow analysis was conducted using the admittance matrix constructed with MATPOWER.
Presumably, both solvers should convergence in very few iterations with a minimal shift
in generator operating points.

Discussion
The results of the report are considered to be adequate as a sort of proof of concept.
Nonetheless, the concept should be further developed to ensure reliable and favourable
system conditions after redistribution.
The SCOPF could potentially be used within
actual system operation but the following matters are essential for success.
The admittance matrix proved to be a much more time-consuming part of the project
than first thought, and multiple versions and iterations of it were created during the
process of implementing the OPF on the Nordic32 system.
Ultimately, it would have
been preferable to start from the MATPOWER matrix, using it to compare and verify the
implementation in the RTGridModel. That way the phase-shifting transformer problem as
well as the missing shunts might have been discovered quicker. In future implementation,
the OPF would utilize either the database or the RTGridModel matrix to represent the
system’s characteristics, depending on the system’s state. The current implementation is
thus disqualified for real-time use, as tap-changing transformers, variations in load, and
FACT devices might change.
Figure 9.1: Voltage Dynamics affecting the bus admittance matrix, Ybus
Using Figure 9.1, four periods are illustrated to determine the representativeness of the
bus admittance matrix, Ybus, using PMU measurements in different scenarios. For the first
one, the voltages remain around unity voltage. In this period, the database admittance
and measured admittance matrix should be identical, where the bus admittance Ybus
is representative for the actual system state. On the contrary, if a system disturbance
occurs, the PMU-measurements lead to inaccurate admittance estimation, where they
are not representative of the system state.
In this period, the database admittances
might be representative, but since the ASSRAS methodology assumes bus-voltages to
be in QSS, the ASSRAS constraint in this period would not be valid, thus making the
optimization irrelevant. In the third period, the voltages have settled, which again implies
that measurements and database admittances reflect the system state accurately. Lastly,
the forth period emphasises that automatic tap-changes regulate bus voltages close to unity.
In this period, the database admittance would be inaccurate, because the transformers are

==PAGE:70==
in off-nominal positions. In this case, the PMU measurements provide an accurate measure
to estimate transformer admittances in off-nominal positions.
In addition to the above, the OPF results revealed to cause rather large deviations in
generator set-points, which was not the intention of the the original objective functions.
In an automated process, the redistribution should only minimally shift generator set-
points to securely avoid ASSRA instabilities.
However, new set-points were obtained
for all generators within the Nordic32-system, which the authors believe would cause
instability if applied in a real power system. For future implementations of the SCOPF as a
redistribution measure. The authors suspect that the objective function indirectly causes a
minimization of active power losses, which drives voltages towards maximums. This further
drives generators located electrically closest to loads towards their maximum capacities.
As a countermeasure, it is proposed to enforce soft or hard constraints, whereby, a change
in generator set-points are minimized to a certain extent. Lastly, the incorporation of
OEL margins as a constraint should be implemented to ensure a stable saddling point for
future systems.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis has been to develop tools for assessing aperiodic rotor angle sta-
bility in real-time using PMU measurements, and for visually informing system operators
about the system state and potential instabilities. Furthermore, the thesis aims to de-
fine a corrective control method, which can divert the system against impending ASSRA
instabilities and robustify the system operation.
First and foremost, the aperiodic rotor angle phenomena has been described and related
to other stability studies within power systems. Using Thevenin-equivalent circuit theory,
the ASSRAS assessment successfully provides an active power margin to instability, which
can be projected upon an impedance plane, ultimately visualizing multiple generators’
active power margins. Using the ASSRAS methodology, the active power margins were
computed and visualized using Python and the SOSPO-platform.
Building upon the ASSRAS methodology, the TESCA methodology has proven effective
as a search mechanism that narrowed-down potential contingencies leading to ASSRA
unstable scenarios. In summary, a few adjustments were made that enabled the project to
define two scenarios, where aperiodic rotor angle instability leads to voltage collapse. The
dynamic simulations were generated in PSS/E and were uploaded onto the SOSPO-server,
to emulate the time-series of real-time bus-wise PMU measurements.
Since the developments made in the project extend from the existing SOSPO-platform,
the main modules and methods have been outlined. Additionally, the framework of the
SOSPO-environment has been addressed to outline the functionalities within the environ-
ment and changes made to these methods. Using the modules, methods, and functionali-
ties of the environment, the project has successfully implemented modular python-scripts,
which can be utilized in an agent-wise fashion. This allows for user-specific subscriptions
to different assessment data and visualization tools. The project successfully implemented
assessment and visualization of the ASSRAS methodology in real-time. Furthermore, the
project successfully developed a tool to visualize generator-wise active power- and OEL-
trigger margins in real-time. Lastly, the ASSRA unstable cases were analyzed using these
methods.
The corrective control was implemented by using SDP-OPF to rapidly compute global
optima. Utilizing the static database from the SOSPO-server, the project manages to
initialize the physical bounds of the optimization. Furthermore, the project succeeded in
defining a generator-wise upper boundary for maximum active power injection utilizing
the dynamic ASSRAS assessment. Ultimately, defining stability constrained optimization.
In addition, the project was able to verify the optimization technique by comparing results
for the IEEE 9-bus network. The methodology was further scaled to the Nordic32-system,
where optimization results were verified using load flow calculations from both MATLAB
and PSS/E. The rank condition of the SDP-OPF solutions could not be verified using the
eigenvalue ratio but was instead verified using active and reactive mismatch on PQ-busses.
Lastly, the ASSRAS constraint was successfully implemented, scaling back the active
power injection of a generator, for a steady-state scenario, which should fortify the system
against aperiodic rotor angle instabilities. Despite ensuring aperiodic rotor angle stabil-
ity, the project did not manage to incorporate an OEL constraint into the optimization.
As a result of this, the dynamic simulations revealed OELs triggering after redispatch,

shifting the operating points of generators. No further collapses or contingencies were
observed. Unfortunately, the project did not succeed in verifying the method on the AS-
SRAS unstable scenarios found nor in fully incorporating the SCOPF on the SOSPO-SW
Platform.
All in all, the project manages to inform system operators of the margin to aperiodic rotor
angle stabilities using PMU measurements and additionally, provides a solid foundation
for remedial action using SDP-OPF, which in future applications could potentially be used
to fortify the system against voltage collapses caused by rotor angle instabilities.

Future Work
Throughout the project development, the authors have developed methods that could be
further improved upon. As the scope of the project has spanned far, improvements and
ideas will now be briefly summed up.
The OEL-margins visualization could be further improved upon by normalizing the over
excitation limits. This would make the boundaries for different generators comparable,
similar to how the generator’s maximum active power injection is represented by the same
boundary. Furthermore, a tracing of the generators’ previous operating points could be
added to the ASSRAS visualization to give better insights into to how quickly or slowly
they stabilize after a system disturbance.
For the optimization, it is considered essential to incorporate a constraint that avoids
activating the over-excitation limiters of the generators. This would hopefully produce
a more stable system operation when it comes to the dynamical solution. Additionally,
some further investigation should be done on the choice of objective function. For this
project, the objective functions tested seemed to drive the operating points far from the
original ones, which is likely unfavorable from a system operator’s point of view.
Moreover, the construction of the bus admittance matrix, Ybus, could be automated using
the RTGridModel, where system measurements make the basis for deriving a representa-
tive Ybus for the system state. Ideally, the SCOPF could then be fully integrated into the
SOSPO SW-Platform to compute remedial action with regards to ASSRAS.
