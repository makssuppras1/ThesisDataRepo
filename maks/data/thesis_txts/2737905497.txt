ABSTRACT
This thesis contributes to the SOLUTIONSplus project, which seeks to introduce innovative
electric mobility alternatives to enhance local life quality and promote sustainability. More
precisely, it investigates the optimization of electric vehicle (EV) deployment within
Kathmandu’s public transport system by adding e-buses, e-waste collectors and e-shuttle
vans to the already existing vehicle fleet. A global framework is proposed to achieve the
project’s goals.
The thesis starts with a comprehensive Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA), using the
Simple Multi-Attribute Rating Technique (SMART). This first step allows the identification
of 34 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs), which cover all essential focused areas without
overlapping one another.
This is known as the Mutually Exclusive and Collectively
Exhaustive (MECE) principle. Each KPI is then weighted according to the preference of
decision-makers’ (DMs), reflecting their importance. Moreover, the KPIs are normalized
on a 1-star to 5-star scale, using a value function, allowing comparison across KPIs with
different units and purposes. This process helps the stakeholders agree on a common goal
when optimizing the number of EVs to be introduced in Kathmandu: maximizing the
overall star ratings of the KPIs while respecting the project’s available budget, which is
€2,000,000.
The study continues with the use of the Future Mobility Calculator (FMC) to assess the
environmental impact of the SOLUTIONSplus project in Kathmandu, especially GHG,
NOx, and PM2.5 emissions. For this, two scenarios are defined. The first one is the
baseline scenario, which describes how the public transport system in Kathmandu develops
from the base year (2024) to the target year (2050), without considering the investment in
additional EVs through the SOLUTIONSplus project, while the second one is the scaled-up
scenario, which shows how the transport system would develop after investing in the base
year 2024 in an optimal EV fleet to introduce given a specific budget. Consequently, the
difference in values between both scenarios gives the savings that can be reached. The
environmental impact is first calculated for the baseline scenario since the score of KPIs
related to emissions also depends on the total emissions in the baseline scenario, but
the impact for the scaled-up scenario is computed after optimization of the EVs fleet to
introduce.
After the FMC, the optimization of the EVs to introduce is addressed using metaheuristic
techniques. Three different metaheuristics are explored: the Simulated Annealing (SA)
algorithm, the Evolutionary Algorithm (EA), and the Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO). The
evaluation of these three algorithms shows that the GWO outperforms the two other
metaheuristics, both in terms of effectiveness (the average objective value found is higher)
and efficiency (the average runtime of the algorithm is lower). Consequently, the optimal
configuration of EVs to introduce, respecting the upper and lower bound constraints on
the vehicle modes and the budget constraint, is found using the GWO: 25 buses, 20 waste
collectors, and 30 shuttle vans. The environmental impact of the scaled-up scenario is then

calculated, with the optimized number of EVs to be introduced in the base year 2024. It is
seen that the scaled-up scenario can save up to 10,177 tons of CO2, 80 tons of NOx, and
28 tons of PM2.5 in 2050, compared to the baseline scenario.
The study ends with the robustness assessment of the optimization framework.
Consequently, the constraints of the project are modified to expand the solution space
and create more feasible solutions. The first case is an increase in budget by a factor
of 50, while the second case is an increase in the number of vehicle modes available (up
to seven). These two evaluations show that the GWO is still able to find the optimal
solution in a reasonable amount of time (around 3 minutes) with a good percentage rate
(33% of the time). Moreover, the first case highlights that a larger available budget in the
project would lead to a more significant environmental impact through greater emissions
reductions.
To conclude, this master’s thesis offers an overall framework which combines MCDA
(SMART) and mathematical optimization (metaheuristic algorithms) to determine the
optimal quantity of EVs to introduce in the base year 2024, in regards with the KPIs
determined within the scope of the SOLUTIONSplus project.

1.2
Methodology
As mentioned, the SOLUTIONSplus project in Kathmandu aims to reduce emissions by
introducing EVs into public transportation. The project focuses on three vehicle modes:
buses, waste collectors, and shuttle vans, each evaluated using Key Performance Indicators
(KPIs) covering financial, environmental, and social areas.
KPIs are determined using the Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) method,
specifically the Simple Multi-Attribute Rating Technique (SMART). To maximize
the overall KPI ratings, metaheuristic algorithms such as Simulated Annealing (SA),
Evolutionary Algorithm (EA), and Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO) are tested and compared,
considering the project constraints. This leads to an optimized number of EVs to be
introduced into public transportation in the base year 2024 for each vehicle mode.
Consequently, the project’s environmental impact is assessed by defining two scenarios:
baseline and scaled-up. For each scenario, CO2, NOx, and PM2.5 emissions are evaluated
over the period from 2024 to 2050, also taking into account assumptions agreed upon with
the stakeholders. The potential emissions reduction due to the optimized EV fleet is then
demonstrated and further discussed in the scaled-up scenario.

BACKGROUND CONTEXT
This chapter provides an overview of the most important concepts and topics, and thus
describes the basis for the following chapters. It examines both the concepts and methods
of MCDA and mathematical programming, and provides insights into their theoretical and
practical application. This framework helps the reader to better understand the complexity
and benefits of these analytical techniques as the thesis progresses.
2.1
Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis
In today’s world, it is crucial to make well-informed decisions, especially in public
projects that impact the economic and social sectors, given the limited resources and
complex environment. Financial analysis aims to maximize the efficient use of limited
financial resources while ensuring the fair distribution of costs and benefits among various
stakeholders such as governments, consumers, and the environment. Cost-Benefit Analysis
(CBA) has been widely used as a fundamental economic assessment tool in several parts of
the world. This approach ensures that major projects with significant economic returns
are implemented and promotes a clear decision-making process at both administrative
and political levels. However, to incorporate non-financial factors such as political and
environmental impacts of infrastructure projects, CBA needs further development to enable
more comprehensive assessment methods for complex organizations [Barfod and Leleur,
2024].
MCDA has become an important method as it is able to consider a broader range of
criteria than economic aspects. It marks a shift towards a more detailed evaluation process
in which various factors are not only recognized but also subjectively weighted according
to their importance to the Decision-Maker (DM). MCDA is known for its adaptability
and ability to handle complex decision scenarios and is particularly suited to projects that
require careful consideration and prioritization of different impacts [Tsamboulas, 2007].
MCDA is characterized by a systematic decision support structure that breaks down
decisions into a series of manageable elements: the potential options, their characteristics,
and the preferences of DMs through the weighting of criteria. This approach promotes a
clear and structured assessment that enables DMs to thoroughly evaluate each alternative
and make decisions that are in line with strategic objectives and desired socio-economic
outcomes [Barfod and Leleur, 2024].
2.2
Mathematical programming
Mathematical modeling is a logical technique for simulating reality to predict and optimize
real-world scenarios. It uses a range of components, operations, and computations to
convert situations and issues into mathematical expressions that explain interactions and

rules inside a certain system. This kind of modeling is essential for formulating optimization
problems in mathematical programming in order to identify the most effective solutions
within predetermined bounds. Decision activities and constraints are the terms used
to describe interactions and activities in this context, which are presented as variables
and equations. Alongside constraints, the role of the objective function is crucial as it
defines the focus of the optimization [Sánchez and Manuel, 2021]. Overall, several different
branches of mathematical programming are explained in the following sections.
2.2.1
Linear programming
The first and most basic type is Linear Programming (LP). Its main goal is to optimize a
linear objective function while taking into account a set of linear restrictions. In LP, a
mathematical model is constructed to represent the fundamentals of a problem in which
constraints and decision variables show linear relationships.
The main components of an LP include:
• Decision variables: These variables represent the choices available in the problem.
In LP, the relationships among variables are linear, and the variables themselves can
be continuous or integer.
• Objective function: This linear function defines the goal of the model, such as
minimizing costs or maximizing profits. It is expressed as a linear combination of
the decision variables.
• Constraints: These linear equations specify the limitations under which the problem
must be solved. They define feasible solutions by setting boundaries on the values of
decision variables [Sánchez and Manuel, 2021].
2.2.2
Single and Multi-Objective Optimization
In mathematical programming, two types exist: Single- and Multi-Objective Optimization.
Depending on the context and complexity of the problem, both techniques can be used
[Savic, 2002].
In a given scenario, Single-Objective Optimization aims to maximize efficiency or minimize
costs by optimizing just one objective function. This kind of optimization is simple since
it has a single, distinct objective. The purpose is to determine the optimal value of the
objective function, which measures the quality of any solution and is defined by constraints
that identify feasible solutions.
In comparison, Multi-Objective Optimization involves simultaneously optimizing multiple
conflicting objectives. As there is no single best solution, this kind of optimization is more
complex. Instead, it seeks to find a set of solutions, known as Pareto optimal solutions
or Pareto front, each of which cannot be improved in any objective without worsening
at least one of the other objectives. This approach is essential for managing trade-offs
between many desirable outcomes since it offers a range of options for solutions based on
the priorities of the objectives [Deb, 2004].

2.2.3
Non-linear optimization
Non-linear optimization can be described as the optimization of decision variables included
in non-linear functions. This type of optimization is crucial when the relationships within
the system being modeled are complex and involve quadratic, exponential, logarithmic,
or any other non-linear forms. In non-linear programming, the objective function or the
constraints contain non-linear equations and, therefore, deal with a landscape on which
optimal solutions may be found in more complex areas, such as along curves or in the
interior points of the feasible region. In contrast, LP is usually solved at the peaks of the
feasible region [Sánchez and Manuel, 2021].
2.2.4
Metaheuristics
Metaheuristics are defined as advanced computational algorithms that intelligently explore
the solution space through mechanisms that balance exploration (investigating new areas)
and exploitation (deepening the search around promising areas). This dual approach
enables to efficiently navigate through complex problem landscapes to identify solutions
which are close to the global optimum. Metaheuristic algorithms are particularly useful
when traditional optimization techniques fail to find an optimal solution within a reasonable
time frame, or when non-linearity constraints are introduced in the model [Affenzeller
et al., 2009].

3.3.1
Optimization of vehicle fleet size
For exploring the possibilities of using optimization in the context of vehicle fleet sizing
seven articles have been reviewed. The first category includes application-driven fleet sizing.
This category focuses on the practical applications of fleet sizing theories, showcasing
how optimization methods are adapted to the unique demands of different industries and
operational needs. The first three articles in this category, by Beaujon and Turnquist
(1991), Monteiro et al. (2021), and Sawik et al. (2017), focus on the application of Integer
Programming (IP) for optimizing fleet size, followed by the second category with two
articles from Sayarshad and Ghoseiri (2009) and Liu et al. (2009) discussing the use of
metaheuristic methods in fleet sizing. The third category includes two papers by Askari
et al. (2016) and Yaghini et al. (2012) that explore dynamic and hybrid optimization
techniques for determining the optimal number of vehicles.
Integer programming
In their article, Beaujon and Turnquist (1991) deal with the task of determining the size
of the vehicle fleet and allocation through the use of advanced mathematical programming
techniques. In this paper, a comprehensive model is developed to find the ideal structure
and functional procedure of vehicle fleets to effectively fulfill the needs while limiting
operational costs [Beaujon and Turnquist, 1991]. The methodology focuses on LP and IP
techniques. LP forms the basis of the optimization process and provides a strategy for
minimizing costs under continuous assumptions. This method addresses various constraints
related to cost, vehicle capacity and demand requirements.
This ensures that the most cost-effective fleet configuration is determined. IP is then
used to refine these solutions by taking into account the discrete nature of fleet decisions,
such as the number of vehicles of a particular type. To ensure that the solutions are
practically feasible, usable in real-world situations, and theoretically ideal, this approach is
crucial. Beaujon and Turnquist (1991) are able to develop a strong decision-making tool

Metaheuristics
A new technique for allocating and sizing rail cars within a rail network is presented by
Sayarshad and Ghoseiri (2009). This work focuses on the creation of a novel formulation and
solution process that tries to meet vehicle demand till the end of the planning horizon, while
taking deterministic trip durations and vehicle demand into account. An effective method
for navigating and optimizing inside a complicated solution space is the SA technique,
which is used to handle the optimization problem. Because of its high computational
complexity, this technique is essential for solving medium- to large-scale problems that
are intractable using just accurate approaches. Using probabilistic transitions to inferior
solutions, the SA algorithm avoids local minima and finds near-optimal combinations to
produce a workable solution. SA was selected because fleet sizing decisions are frequently
non-linear and discrete, and the dynamic environment is marked by fluctuating demand

Dynamic and hybrid optimization techniques
The article by Askari et al. (2016) presents a hybrid model for optimizing vehicle-sharing
systems. This model aims to minimize total system costs by optimizing the locations,
number of stations, routes, vehicles and transportation modes while considering station
capacities and time intervals between stations [Askari et al., 2016].
The authors use a hybrid technique that combines Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
and a GA in order to handle the complexity of the model and the shortcomings of existing
software in solving it quickly. Comprehensive sensitivity analysis demonstrates the better
efficiency and applicability of this hybrid strategy when compared against classical methods.
Using the GA, the non-linear and discrete nature of the problem is efficiently handled
while choosing the best station sites and transport modes. In contrast, PSO shows how
flexible the approach is in complex, multimodal transport environments by fine-tuning
operational elements like fleet size and allocation to satisfy changing demand conditions.
The study represents an important development in the field of transport planning and
technology since it offers a reliable tool for planning in the face of fluctuating demand and
operational restrictions [Askari et al., 2016].
Yaghini and Khandaghabad (2012) introduce an approach to optimize the fleet size for
rail freight cars. The developed model is dynamic and takes into account the complexity

effectively addresses the non-linear nature of the problem and its dynamic variables over
multiple periods.
Since it employs SA to effectively distribute these cars to fulfill demand and GA to calculate

approach is especially helpful for solving the fleet sizing problem. By using this approach,
the model’s capacity to adjust dynamically to changing demand and operational limitations
is enhanced, all the while maximizing fleet utilization and reducing expenses. Several test

3.3.2
Integrating MCDA and optimization methods
This section examines the integration of MCDA with mathematical optimization techniques
in various studies.
The focus is on the methodology and methods described in nine
articles that deal with decision problems involving multiple, often conflicting, criteria.
These methods improve decision-making processes, especially in environments that require
optimization with multiple objectives. In the following, the various articles are analyzed
individually in more detail with regard to their application and methods.
A New Scheme for Interactive Multi-Criteria Decision Making
Streichert and Tanaka-Yamawaki (2006) present an MCDA scheme that integrates a
priori, interactive and a posteriori decision-making approaches into a unified framework.
This integration attempts to utilize the strengths of each approach and eliminate their
weaknesses through the use of EA and Multi-Objective Evolutionary Algorithms. These
algorithms enable dynamic reformulation of the problem through de novo programming and
were selected due to their adaptability to various sorts of objectives and restrictions. This

helps in decreasing the target space and improving the problem descriptions repeatedly for
the DM [Streichert and Tanaka-Yamawaki, 2006].
Using an iterative optimization process, the DM begins with a first problem definition and
refines iteratively by incorporating constraints, changing objectives into constraints, and
aggregating objectives after analyzing the potential solutions. This framework makes use of
a number of decision-making techniques, including the Reference Point approach and the
Geoffrion-Dyer-Feinberg method. An example of this methodology is demonstrated through
its application to a three-objective portfolio selection problem, showing how the DM could
interactively engage with the optimization process to refine preferences and objectives based
on emerging solution sets. The proposed scheme offers significant advantages in terms
of flexibility for MCDA and can handle various optimization problems, allowing detailed
investigation of solutions. However, its complexity can be challenging for those new to EA
as it requires significant computational time and resources, especially when approaching
the Pareto front. Its effectiveness also depends on the initial problem definitions and the
ability of the DM to engage with the interactive elements of the framework [Streichert and
Tanaka-Yamawaki, 2006].
Progressive Multi-Objective Optimization
Sörensen and Springael (2013) follow the approach to integrate MCDA directly into the
multi-criteria optimization process, aiming to improve decision-making by taking user
preferences into account at an earlier stage, rather than after a set of non-dominated
solutions has been generated. The methodology created by Sörensen and Springael (2013)
employs the PROMETHEE method to continuously evaluate and include the preferences of
DMs and update a reference archive of non-dominant solutions throughout the optimization
process. A metaheuristic for local search, in particular tabu search, is used to effectively
navigate and exploit the search space, using the multi-criteria evaluations performed by
PROMETHEE as a guide. This approach maintains a dynamic reference archive that stores
high-quality solutions that are regularly updated to reflect new findings and remove less
optimal solutions, enabling continuous improvement of solutions [Sörensen and Springael,
2013].
Handling Preferences in Evolutionary Multi-Objective Optimization: A Survey
Coello (2000) addresses the integration of DMs preferences in evolutionary multi-objective
optimization. The paper categorizes existing methods for handling preferences into three
main approaches: a priori, where preferences are defined before optimization; interactive,
where the dynamic articulation of preferences is enabled during the optimization process;
and a posteriori, where preferences are applied after a Pareto front has been created
to support the selection of the most appropriate solutions. The article continues on to
describe other approaches within these groups, including employing utility functions to
rank solutions, utilizing predefined weights to aggregate objectives, and evaluating solutions
using outranking techniques like PROMETHEE after optimization. DMs can evolve and
learn during the optimization process by adjusting their preferences in response to ongoing
solution assessments using interactive approaches like the STEP method [Coello, 2000].

for the HUMANT algorithm. It improves decision-making in complicated optimization
scenarios by incorporating the DMs a priori preference, which are established before the
start of the optimization process. This is not the same as interactive approaches, where the
DM and the optimization process are in continual communication, or a posteriori methods,
which provide a Pareto front of solutions from which the DM chooses. The HUMANT
algorithm avoids these methods in a unique way by applying metaheuristic principles to
present only near-optimal solutions for multi-criteria evaluation, thus streamlining the
decision-making process in environments with potentially unlimited or extensive alternatives.
The HUMANT algorithm is a strong tool for optimizing complex problems with multiple
conflicting criteria. However, the approach may not work effectively in situations where
preferences are unknown or fluctuate over time, which is a drawback of its reliance on
clearly specified a priori preferences [Mladineo et al., 2015].

optimization

(2021) focuses on a framework to evaluate
the creditworthiness of Italian Small and Medium-sized Enterprises (SMEs) using the
Multicriteria Ranking Method (MURAME). This new methodology uses PSO, a nature-
inspired metaheuristic, to effectively navigate the complex solution space. The study
uses historical financial data from a large sample of Italian SMEs, incorporating the
DM’s preferences into the parameter setting of the MURAME model. The study is also
innovative, in that it introduces a new unrestricted reformulation of the problem that
balances solution quality and computational efficiency. The main strength of this method
lies in the sophisticated integration of the disaggregation of preferences, which allows
dynamic adjustment of the MURAME parameters to better reflect the DM’s preferences
and real data [Corazza et al., 2021].

the method combines a mix of hill-climbing, SA, and PSO algorithms. The method
described uses the Characteristic Object Method (COMET), where dimensions and fuzzy
numbers are assigned to each criterion. Then, characteristic objects are created using
these values to enable a structured comparison of different decision-making options. With
stochastic optimization supporting the COMET technique, this structured methodology
makes it easier to objectively identify preferences while addressing the inherent uncertainties
and difficulty of decision scenarios. By combining a stochastic approach with COMET,

populations and increased iterations. The decision techniques produced slightly different

preference-based decision-making into complex optimization issues. It successfully identifies
solutions that align with the DM’s expressed preferences [Sekizaki et al., 2019].

Conclusion
The researched literature highlights the approaches and advancements in various studies,
providing a thorough analysis of the integration of MCDA with mathematical optimization
techniques. Each presented contribution offers a different viewpoint on how MCDA may
be used to solve difficult decision problems efficiently alongside with different optimization
techniques.
The integration of DMs’ preferences is a common element across the publications analyzed.
This is evident in the works of Streichert and Tanaka-Yamawaki (2006), Mladineo et al.
(2015), and Coello (2000), who highlight strategies that prioritize the incorporation of
these preferences in various stages of the optimization process. This is essential to creating
solutions that satisfy the requirements and expectations of consumers. Additionally, a lot
of research uses EA, including studies by Streichert and Tanaka-Yamawaki (2006) and
Sekizaki et al. (2019).
The differences emphasized in the articles include the methodological approaches and the
application contexts. While Streichert and Tanaka-Yamawaki (2006) propose a framework
that combines different phases of decision-making, Sörensen and Springael (2013) integrate
decision-making early in the optimisation process using PROMETHEE and focus on the
continuous adaptation and improvement of solutions. In contrast, Kizielewicz and Sałabun
(2020) use stochastic optimization to deal with the inherent uncertainties of decision
scenarios and point to a variety of strategies depending on the specific requirements of
the problem. The applications of these methods vary widely, demonstrating the versatility
of MCDA techniques -from managing power distribution networks to optimizing building

of the methods vary, with some approaches requiring significant computational resources
and iterative interactions that can be more demanding.
3.4
Discussion
3.4.1
Optimization of vehicle fleet size
Across the reviewed literature, a clear trend is the shift from traditional methods of fleet
sizing using IP towards more advanced, hybrid optimization techniques that combine
multiple metaheuristic algorithms. For example, Beaujon et al, Monteiro et al. (2021) and
Yaghini and Khandaghabad (2012) present advanced models that extend the possibilities of
IP and MIP significantly by incorporating real-time data and simulations into fleet sizing
systems. The trend towards hybrid models, as discussed by Askari et al. (2016), reflects a
broader movement towards systems that can dynamically adapt to changing conditions
and complex problem spaces, often involving multiple modes of transport and fluctuating
demand.
By analyzing these articles, it becomes clear that there remains a gap in integrating
environmental and sustainability aspects into fleet optimization models. While Sawik et
al. (2017) address this by including measures of environmental impact, the majority of
studies continue to focus primarily on cost and efficiency metrics. There is a need for more
comprehensive models that also consider environmental impacts and social factors and
provide a more holistic approach to fleet sizing.
3.4.2
Integrating MCDA and optimization
The integration of MCDA with optimization techniques has evolved significantly, moving
from single objective optimization to more complex multi-criteria frameworks. A trend
across the examined studies is the use of EAs and other heuristic methods such as PSO and
ACO. These methods are preferred for their adaptability and efficiency in handling diverse
and dynamic optimization scenarios, as they allow flexible adjustments to constraints and
objectives in real-time.
The transition from a priori to interactive approaches to decision-making within the
optimization process represents a significant trend. Interactive methods, such as those
described by Streichert and Tanaka-Yamawaki (2006) and Coello (2000), allow DMs to
iteratively refine their preferences in response to upcoming solutions, increasing decision
accuracy and satisfaction.
Despite these developments, there are notable gaps. The complexity and computational
cost of these advanced algorithms, such as EAs, reveal a significant gap in accessibility
and usability for non-expert users. The complexity barrier can hinder wider application
in practical, everyday decision-making scenarios outside of academic or highly technical
environments.
Furthermore, there is a lack of comprehensive tools that seamlessly integrate different
phases of decision-making —from data collection to initial preference setting to final
decision execution. While some articles, such as those by Coello (2000) and Sekizaki et al.
17
==PAGE:30==
3. Literature review
(2019), discuss the integration of preferences throughout the optimization process, there
is still a need for more holistic systems that make this integration more transparent and
efficient.
The debate between prioritizing algorithm efficiency and user interaction accuracy is very
strong. Some researchers prioritize the development of algorithms that can quickly generate
optimal solutions, as seen in the work of Weerasuriya et al. (2021), while others, such as
those discussed by Coello (2000), place a higher value on the adaptability of solutions to
changing user preferences. This reflects a fundamental conflict in this area: the question
of whether optimization should focus on computational efficiency or on adapting to user
expectations and changing preferences.
Another point is the balance between a priori and a posteriori decision-making approaches.
Some researchers argue in favour of setting preferences before the optimisation process, as
in the HUMANT algorithm, while others suggest refining preferences interactively, which
can lead to more tailored and potentially more satisfactory results.
3.5
Conclusion
The articles reviewed for vehicle fleet optimization provide a wide range of approaches to
fleet sizing, each of which contributes in different ways to the understanding and capabilities
in this area. As fleet sizing continues to evolve, particularly in the context of smart cities
and increased environmental awareness, future research needs to address the existing gaps
by developing more integrated models. These models, if successfully developed, could
not only optimize operating costs and efficiency but also prioritize sustainability and
adaptability to real-time data. This could lead to a more efficient and sustainable fleet
sizing system, capable of responding not only to the economic aspects of transport but
also to its environmental and social impacts, thereby contributing to a greener and more
efficient future.
After reviewing various methodologies in the field of integrating MCDA with optimization
techniques, numerous innovative approaches and methods have been identified. However,
challenges remain regarding complexity management, user-friendliness, and the balance
between algorithmic efficiency and decision accuracy. Addressing these gaps and debates
will be interesting for developing more effective and widely applicable decision-support
tools in the future. This analysis highlights the current state of the art and points to
potential areas for further research and development in this area.

This chapter introduces the SOLUTIONSplus project, the city on focus, and the different

4.1
SOLUTIONSplus project overview
The SOLUTIONSplus project aims to enable transformational change towards sustainable
urban mobility through innovative and integrated electric mobility solutions. This project,
in line with the Paris Agreement on climate change, the Sustainable Development Goals,
and the New Urban Agenda, fosters the implementation of e-mobility transportation
modes by boosting the availability of EVs, supporting their integration in urban areas,
and addressing user needs over four different continents: Europe, Asia, Africa, and Latin
America [SOLUTIONSplus, 2024a].
This ambitious project, in partnership with the United Nations Environment Programme
(UNEP) and the IEA, is implemented in multiple cities around the world to act as city-level
demonstrations to test different types of innovative and integrated e-mobility solutions.
Among these cities are Hanoi in Vietnam, Pasig in the Philippines, Kathmandu in Nepal,
Kigali in Rwanda, Dar es Saleem in Tanzania, Quito in Ecuador, Montevideo in Uruguay,
Madrid in Spain, Hamburg in Germany, and Nanjing in China. The learning experiences
gathered through these city-level demonstrations will shape a global platform for sharing,
public, and commercial e-mobility solutions, which can be the origin of a kick-start towards
low-carbon urban mobility transition in cities all over the world [SOLUTIONSplus, 2024a].
This master’s thesis covers the implementation of the SOLUTIONSplus project in
Kathmandu, Nepal.
4.2
Kathmandu presentation and SOLUTIONSplus project
4.2.1
Geography and urban context
Kathmandu Valley, the largest urban center in Nepal, includes eighteen municipalities
and, among them, the capital city of Kathmandu. It is a growing metropolitan area
with a combined population exceeding 2,5 million inhabitants. In 2022, Nepal’s GDP
per capita was $1,337 [IBRD, 2024]. Moreover, it is worth noticing that the country
contributed to a value of 0.06% of the global CO2 emissions in 2010-2011 [Ministry of
Forests and Environment, 2021]. This low value can be explained by the fact that most of
Nepal’s electricity is generated from hydropower. To take advantage of the green electricity
produced in Nepal and to reduce fossil fuel dependency, Nepal aims to significantly increase
the sale of EVs [GGGI, 2018].

Geographically, Kathmandu Valley is bowl-shaped, surrounded by mountain ranges, and

Transports overview
Urban transport in Kathmandu is dominated by road transport, which accounts for over

4.2.3
Purpose of SOLUTIONSplus project
The SOLUTIONSplus project in Kathmandu follows the effort of stepping towards pollution
reduction by creating an ecosystem for electric mobility to enhance public transport. More
precisely, different modes of vehicles are considered to be introduced in Kathmandu: e-
buses, e-waste collectors, and e-shuttle vans. Each of these vehicle modes is valued with a
performance score based on multiple KPIs, such as financial viability, emission savings, or
comfort of the vehicle mode. The main idea of this project is to determine the optimal
quantity of vehicles to introduce, distributed among the three vehicle modes, to maximize
the overall rating of the KPIs which is defined in the SOLUTIONSplus project. The main
constraint to respect is the budget of €2,000,000 available for this project.
Once the optimal number of vehicles is determined, the next step is to assess the
environmental impact of this optimization, particularly on pollutant emissions; two scenarios
are defined. The first one corresponds to the baseline scenario, which describes how the
public transport system in Kathmandu develops from the base year (2024) to the target year
(2050), without considering the investment in additional EVs through the SOLUTIONSplus
project. The second one corresponds to the scaled-up scenario, which shows how the public
transport system would develop in Kathmandu up to the target year (2050) after investing
in the base year (2024) in an optimal EV fleet given a specific budget. The emission of
pollutants over the year can then be compared between the baseline scenario and the
scaled-up scenario.

or mode-1 vehicle, the waste collector as pick-up truck or mode-2 vehicle, and the shuttle
van as micro-bus or mode-3 vehicle.

4.3.1
Selection of KPIs
Techniques used
Even though the literature discussed various MCDA methods, it was decided, in consultation
with the project’s stakeholders, to use SMART in the context of this project. SMART
is a method that simplifies the evaluation process by using a straightforward approach
to rate and compare different alternatives based on multiple criteria. This approach is
especially helpful when DMs must make complicated decisions involving various variables
or attributes. It involves the following steps: [Barfod and Leleur, 2024]
1. Identify criteria: Determine the relevant criteria that will influence the decision.
2. Assign weights: Assign weights to each criterion based on its importance. These
weights reflect the priority of each criterion in the overall decision.
3. Score alternatives: Each alternative is scored on each criterion.
4. Calculate weighted scores: Multiply the scores for each criterion by their respective
weights to get weighted scores for each alternative.
5. Sum weighted scores: Add the weighted scores across all criteria for each alternative
to derive an overall score.
The choice of using SMART is diverse. Firstly, it is easily understandable by stakeholders
with limited exposure to project assessment methods. Secondly, its structure is similar
to the CBA, allowing an easy combination of both methods. Thirdly, it is suitable for
problems with many criteria, which is the case for the SOLUTIONSplus project [Panagakos
et al., 2024].
The selection of the KPIs is crucial because the optimization criteria and the assessment
of the different demonstration activities in the project cities around the world are
based on these KPIs. That is why they should facilitate a common impact assessment
approach, enabling cross-cutting evaluations. Moreover, this selection should be built
on solid theoretical foundations, which can lead to bankable projects at the end of the
SOLUTIONSplus project [Panagakos et al., 2024].

1-star and the one which is equivalent to 5-star)
2. Finding the KPI value which corresponds to 3-star. To help the stakeholders to make
this decision, it is useful to consider the midpoint between the KPI value which is
equivalent to 1-star and the one which is equivalent to 5-star, and ask to the DM
which of the two halves is the most valuable. The considered point can then be moved
towards the most preferred half and the question is repeated until the midpoint is
identified.
3. Determining the KPI value for the 2-star rating and the one for the 4-star rating. To
do so, the same method is used as in step 2, by considering the two end-points (KPI
values for the 1-star and 5-star ratings), and the point determined in the previous
step (KPI value for the 3-star rating).

4.3.4
Weight of KPIs
Technique used
To emphasize the relative importance of the KPIs in the SOLUTIONSplus project, a weight
is attributed to each of them. The weighting technique used is called swing weighting
28
==PAGE:41==
4. Project presentation
and is considered the most solid theoretically. Swing weights are obtained by asking the
DMs to compare the change from the least-preferred (1-star) to the most-preferred (5-star)
value on a KPI with the same change in another KPI [Goodwin and Wright, 2014]. This
technique follows three steps:
1. The DM is asked to visualize all KPIs within a specified family varying from their
minimum to maximum potential values (rated from 1-star to 5-star). From this
assessment, the most impactful KPI is identified as the primary one, and a weight of
100 is assigned to it.
2. Other KPIs within the same family are assessed: each of them is given a weight
between 0 and 100, relative to the primary KPI, by evaluating the perceived impact
of their value swing from 1-star to 5-star. In this step, the primary attribute, whose
weight value is 100, serves as the benchmark for the other ones of the same family.
3. It is necessary to normalize the weights of all KPIs such that their total sum is equal
to 100.
Assessment of KPI weights
The weight assigned to each KPI is indicated in Figure 4.1 (written in red). It is important
to remind that some attributes are excluded from the analysis in the project applied in
the city of Kathmandu, as explained in Section 4.3.1. Therefore, the weights of the KPIs
that remain relevant to this thesis are normalized so that their total sum equals 100. This
adjustment ensures that the focus stays appropriately distributed among the selected
attributes.

4.3.6
Limitations for KPIs
Regarding the KPIs, several uncertainties must be addressed before implementing the
proposed framework. Firstly, it is crucial to ensure that the KPIs are clearly defined, as
this determines the scores calculated in subsequent steps. An inaccurate definition can
lead to miscalculations and misrepresent the actual performance, affecting the subsequent
star value assessment.
Secondly, DMs need to confirm comprehensive coverage of all relevant areas of interest.
Neglecting critical areas such as social, economic, and environmental aspects could result
in suboptimal overall star values, failing to deliver maximum benefits to stakeholders
and cities. The defined KPIs in the project can be considered as Mutually Exclusive,
Collectively Exhaustive (MECE). This concept guarantees that each category is divided
into different segments that do not overlap (mutually exclusive) and together cover all
possible options (collectively exhaustive).
Lastly, accurately defining the value functions is essential for converting scores into
30
==PAGE:43==
4. Project presentation
meaningful star values. For example, if the ranges for value functions are not precisely
defined, all alternatives, such as different vehicle modes, could receive similar star values
for a KPI, making it ineffective in influencing optimal vehicle distribution. Addressing
this issue might involve simulating feasible solutions to create a solution pool that helps
define problem ranges. While this approach does not cover all possible solutions, as the
potentially large number of solutions makes the calculation infeasible, it does provide DMs
with a clearer range of expected scores during the optimization process.

ELECTRIC MOBILITY CALCULATOR
As urban centers continue to grow, so will the share of global emissions they produce
[Kothari et al., 2021a]. The adoption of EVs offers a valuable opportunity to lower emissions
and operational costs. However, this shift necessitates a predefined strategy for vehicle
adoption and the development of a charging infrastructure network, and it is also essential
to account for the rise in electricity demand due to EVs, for efficient resource management.
To help in facilitating the planning phase, electric mobility tools are developed to support
the transition towards more sustainable urban transportation systems. They are designed
to evaluate the potential impacts of integrating EVs into the current transport ecosystem,
addressing key areas such as energy consumption, and greenhouse gas and pollutant
emissions reductions. These tools are typically used by city planners, policymakers, and
stakeholders in the context of urban development and environmental policy making.
By providing scenario-based analyses, they help in strategizing and implementing electric
mobility solutions to achieve cleaner, more efficient, and sustainable urban environments.
5.1
eMob and Future Mobility Calculator
Two tools are identified as an aid for the implementation of electric mobility solutions:
eMob Calculator and FMC.
The eMob calculator, developed by the UNEP, serves as an Excel-based tool in the
advancement of electric mobility initiatives worldwide. eMob offers a platform for assessing
the potential impacts and benefits of transitioning to electric mobility solutions. The
tool incorporates a range of functionalities, including scenario analysis, cost-benefit
evaluations, and emissions modeling, to facilitate informed decision-making processes.
Furthermore, eMob provides users with access to a diverse array of data sets, enabling
thorough assessments of various aspects such as vehicle electrification rates, infrastructure
requirements, and environmental implications.
With its user-friendly interface and
interactive features, eMob empowers users to explore different electrification scenarios,
visualize potential outcomes, and devise strategies for sustainable transport development.
It also features methodologies for conducting CBA and calculating the Total Cost of
Ownership, crucial for assessing the long-term financial implications, and supplementary
sections provide necessary conversions and definitions, ensuring clarity and uniformity in
the calculations. Ultimately, the outputs are displayed through graphs based on individual
requirements and preferences [UNEP, 2018].
On the other side, the FMC is a collaborative effort between the World Resources Institute,
Siemens, and the Coalition for Urban Transitions, aimed at facilitating urban infrastructure
planning for the adoption of EVs and charging stations. Developed as an open-sourced
Excel-based tool, the FMC relies on city-specific inputs including general city data, mobility
32
==PAGE:45==
5. Electric mobility calculator
metrics, charging infrastructure details, and cost data to project the quantity, cost, and
social benefits associated with necessary infrastructure investments up to 2050. This tool,
while offering transparency through its interface allowing users to view and customize inputs
and calculations, also incorporates over 500 default data points sourced from institutions
such as the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), World Bank, and C40
Cities Climate Leadership Group (C40), among others, to supplement in case of missing
user data. Users are encouraged to provide as much city-specific information as possible
to enhance the accuracy of the outputs, ensuring a tailored analysis for each city. The
FMC is designed for fully battery EVs, excluding non-motorized vehicles, plug-in hybrid

infrastructure that integrates mobility characteristics and electricity consumption and

5.2
Selection of tool
For this master’s thesis, the decision is made to utilize the FMC instead of the initially
proposed eMob tool for two primary reasons. Firstly, while the eMob calculator is widely
recognized for its contribution to e-mobility development, it lacks comprehensive guidance
on the calculation methodologies, making it challenging to interpret critical data points.
Additionally, as of the writing of this thesis, eMob remains in its Beta version, missing an
official and completely tested release. In contrast, the FMC offers a self-explanatory guide,
underlying the concepts of the tool with well-documented procedures. Moreover, the FMC
provides flexibility for customization, allowing users to adjust key parameters and input
data according to specific requirements, thereby enhancing its completeness and usability
for informed decision-making in urban mobility planning.
Moreover, it is crucial to note that the FMC tool is tailored for the scope of the master’s
thesis, which predominantly focuses on environmental emissions and emissions reduction.
However, it also offers interesting financial and social considerations, which are not evaluated
within this purpose.

5.4
Limitations
Although the analysis provided can be considered accurate and in line with the initial
expectations, it faces some limitations:
• The data have been in various meetings discussed and agreed with the stakeholders,
but, in case of non-accuracy, it could significantly impact the outputs. For instance,
as seen in Chapter 5.2.3, a small change in the amount of vehicles would impact
considerably the final total emissions, or emissions reduction.
• Regarding the Kathmandu region, the term “EVs” refers to fully EVs, excluding other
potential technologies including plug-in hybrid EVs (battery and petrol), and fuel
cell vehicles (hydrogen fuel). As a consequence, a fully EV would not have impact
in terms of emissions, as the emission factors are zero, but a plug-in hybrid vehicle,
even if in reduced quantity in comparison to ICE vehicles, would have an impact
in terms of air pollution. However, due to the data provided from the stakeholders,
Kathmandu region is substituting ICE vehicles with fully-EVs, so the considerations
made are in line with the data provided.
• The revised-FMC tool implemented by the team uses as a baseline year 2024, and
short/medium-term influences, as it could have been COVID-19 in 2020 are not
taken into account for future projections. Indeed, a world-stop of manufacturing
processes would influence negatively the production of EVs, thus a slower transition
to electrification of public transportation.
• The FMC focuses on a limited number of transport modes including mini-buses,
micro-buses and pick-up trucks, as per stakeholders’ request and data provided,
limiting the possibilities of exploring other means of transportation that are used in
the public.
• The tool does not consider how new technologies such as autonomous vehicles would
impact the electrification process.

VEHICLE FLEET SIZE OPTIMIZATION
This chapter outlines a methodology designed to determine the optimal number of vehicles
to be deployed in Kathmandu, with the purpose of maximizing the overall star rating of
the KPIs. Moreover, the proposed framework is intended to be adaptable for application
in other cities facing similar challenges.
6.1
Introduction
As seen in the literature review (Chapter 3), various methodologies are available to calculate
the optimal size of the vehicle fleet. Some approaches, such as mathematical modeling
and Excel-based analysis, yield precisely the optimal solution. In contrast, other methods,
including simulations and heuristic techniques, provide solutions that closely approximate
the optimal solution. Several of these techniques are explored in this chapter.
It is reminded that in the subsequent sections, mode-1 vehicle corresponds to bus (equivalent
to mini-bus), mode-2 vehicle to waste collector (equivalent to pick-up truck), and mode-3
vehicle to shuttle van (equivalent to micro-bus).

6.2.2
Problem encountered
A mathematical model can be processed and solved using a computer-based optimizer
considering the linearity of the objective function and all associated constraints. However,
particular models can also be solved, even if all the constraints are not linear, and these
exceptions include, for example, constraints that can be written as convex functions.
With regards to the mathematical model of the SOLUTIONSplus project, the constraints,
employed to express the scoring value of the KPIs within the DSR category, introduce
non-linearity and can not be written as convex constraints. This complexity arises from the
use of the weighted average of vehicles, defined as the ratio of the decision variables. As a
consequence, it is decided to use an alternative technique —metaheuristic algorithms— to
solve this model. Metaheuristics aim to solve the model by finding a feasible solution that
approximate the optimal one.

6.3
Metaheuristics: Different algorithms
Metaheuristics are defined as advanced computational algorithms that operate by
intelligently exploring the solution space through mechanisms that balance exploration
(investigating new areas) and exploitation (deepening the search around promising areas).
This dual approach enables to efficiently navigate through complex problem landscapes to
identify solutions which are close to the global optimum. Metaheuristic algorithms are
particularly useful when traditional optimization techniques fail to find an optimal solution
within a reasonable time frame, or when non-linearity constraints are introduced in the
model.[Affenzeller et al., 2009]
Metaheuristic algorithms are divided into three main categories based on their underlying
principles and sources of inspiration. The first category, trajectory-based metaheuristics,
involves iteratively exploring the solution space by making local modifications to a single
solution, progressively advancing towards improved solutions. The second category includes
evolutionary-based metaheuristics, which draw on the principles of natural evolution and
genetics. These algorithms maintain a population of potential solutions, applying genetic
operators like mutation, crossover, and selection to evolve this population towards better
outcomes. The third category, nature-based metaheuristics, is inspired by various natural
phenomena, including animal behaviors and physical and chemical processes observed in
nature, to solve optimization problems [Affenzeller et al., 2009]. An overview of these
categories, with examples of algorithms, is given in Figure 6.1, along with their respective
year of publication.
Figure 6.1. Categorization of metaheuristics
In order to cover the three categories, one metaheuristic is chosen from each of them. Among
the algorithms selected, there are the SA and the EA, which are respectively categorized in
the trajectory-based and the evolutionary-based groups. These two algorithms stand out

in the literature review and are part of the most well-known metaheuristics in optimization.
For the nature-inspired category, the PSO is considered to be used since it is cited multiple

6.3.1
Simulated annealing
Inspiration of algorithm
SA is inspired by the annealing process in metallurgy. Annealing is a physical process used
to alter the properties of metals: the material is heated to a high temperature and then
allowed to cool down slowly. The slow cooling process is crucial as it lets the atoms within
the material rearrange themselves into a state of minimum energy, leading to a more stable
and orderly structure. This process helps reduce defects, increase ductility, and relieve
internal stresses. The inspiration for SA as an optimization technique comes from this
physical process where the idea is to find a low-energy state of a system that corresponds
to an optimal or near-optimal solution to a given problem. In the context of optimization
problems, the energy of the system is analogous to the objective function that needs to be
optimized, and the state of the system represents a potential solution [Kirkpatrick et al.,
1982].
Steps in the algorithm
The algorithm starts by initializing with a random feasible solution to the problem, which is
then iteratively improved upon. At each step, the temperature of the system decreases, and
the SA heuristic considers moving from the current solution to a neighbor one. The choice
to move to a neighbor solution is taken based on the comparison of the objective values: if
the adjacent solution is better than the current one, this becomes the new arrangement,
which is then explored in the next iterations. However, by only accepting the solutions
with an improved objective value, the algorithm could lead to a local optimum. To avoid
this behaviour, an acceptance criterion is also defined based on the current temperature of
the system. Indeed, early in the algorithm, when the temperature is high, there is a higher
likelihood of accepting worse solutions, allowing the algorithm to explore the solution
space more freely and potentially escape local optimum. As the temperature decreases, the
algorithm becomes more conservative, preferring only moves that improve the solution or
that represent slight deteriorations, thereby focusing the search on regions of the solution
space where a near-optimal solution is likely to be found [Kirkpatrick et al., 1982].
Generation of a neighbor solution
A neighbor solution is derived from a current feasible solution by modifying one of its
component (vehicle mode). This modification involves randomly selecting a vehicle mode
and then randomly adding and subtracting a number between 1 and 10 to the current
count of that vehicle mode. This approach produces two potential neighbor solutions,
allowing the exploration of the solution space in two directions. If both new solutions

for each mode), the one with the superior objective value is selected. If only one of them
is feasible, this one is chosen as the neighbor solution. If neither of them is feasible, the
process is repeated to generate two new potential neighbor solutions from the original
feasible solution.
Acceptance probability of worst solutions
The probability of accepting a less favorable neighbor solution in comparison to the current
solution depends on the difference in their objective values and the system’s temperature.
This criterion is inspired by the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, which is derived from
statistical mechanics [Kirkpatrick et al., 1982]. This probability is calculated using the

6.3.2
Evolutionary algorithm
Inspiration of algorithm
The EA is based on the principles of Darwinian natural selection and genetic mechanisms
observed in biological evolution. The core concept of this algorithm is to mimic the
evolutionary process of natural selection where the fittest individuals are more likely to
survive and reproduce. This process leads to the evolution of a population of individuals over
generations towards an increasing adaptation to their environment. The main biological
inspirations behind this algorithm are natural selection (strong species can pass their genes
to future generations whereas weak ones phase out through natural selection), genetics
and inheritance (biological organisms inherit traits from their parents through genes),
and mutations of the children’s genes (variation of the genes, represented as random
changes, enabling the exploration of the solution space and the possibility of discovering
new solutions). These changes may provide additional advantages, which are carried onto
the next generations, and they often help to ensure that the solution does not get stuck on
48
==PAGE:61==
6. Vehicle fleet size optimization
a local extreme point. Over time, fitter solutions will dominate the population, giving the
possibility to identify a feasible solution close enough to the optimal one [Holland, 1992].
Steps in the algorithm
The algorithm starts by establishing an initial population, ensuring a diverse gene pool.
Following this initialization, two individuals of the population are chosen to act as parents
for the next generation. The subsequent phase involves the production of two offspring
through the crossover of parental genes. To maintain genetic diversity within the population,
mutations are introduced to the offspring’s genes. Then, to integrate the new generation
into the existing population, two current individuals are selected for replacement with the
new offspring. These processes are repeated until the algorithm reaches the designated
maximum number of iterations [Holland, 1992].
Selection of the two parents
The selection of two individuals from the population to serve as parents is conducted using
a roulette wheel selection method associated with a tournament selection. The former
method assumes that each individual is associated with their objective value. Then, the
probability of an individual to be selected is determined by dividing its objective value
by the total sum of all objective values within the population. This approach ensures
that individuals with higher objective values have a greater likelihood of being chosen.
Four individuals are selected from the population with the roulette wheel selection, and
then compared using the latter method. Indeed, in the tournament selection, the selected
individuals are paired for comparison in terms of objective value. The first individual
chosen is compared with the fourth, and the second with the third. The superior individual
from each pair is selected as the parents for the new generation.
Offspring and genes’ mutation
Offspring are produced through the crossover of genes from the selected parents: half of
the components (vehicle modes) are randomly selected, and their corresponding component
values are swapped in the offspring’s genes. A visual representation of this process is
illustrated in Figure 6.2, which depicts the gene crossover mechanism. If the resulting
offspring fail to adhere to the budget constraint and the bound constraints for each vehicle
mode, the algorithm goes back to the selection of the two parents without advancing in
the iteration count.

Genes mutation is subsequently performed by generating a neighbor solution for each of
the two offspring. This process follows the principles explained in Section 6.3.1.
Selection of individuals leaving the population
The selection of the two individuals from the population to be replaced by the offspring is

process described for selecting the two parents in Section 6.3.2. However, in this case, the
selection probability for the roulette wheel method is inversely related to the objective
values: individuals with lower objective values have a higher probability of being selected

6.3.3
Grey wolf optimizer
Inspiration of algorithm
The GWO is an optimization algorithm inspired by the social hierarchy and hunting
techniques of grey wolves in nature. These animals exhibit a structured social order and
adopt cooperative strategies for hunting. The hierarchical structure within a wolf pack is
divided into four ranks: alpha, beta, delta, and omega. The alpha wolf leads the pack,
followed by the beta as the second in command, and the delta as the third. The omega
wolves, at the bottom of the hierarchy, comply with the directives of higher-ranking wolves.
The hunting method of grey wolves involves initially locating prey, undertaken by the
alpha, beta, and delta wolves. The pack then encircles the prey before launching an attack
[Mirjalili et al., 2014].
Grey wolf optimizer: A variant of the particle swarm optimization
It is important to note that the GWO is a variant of the well-known metaheuristic called
PSO [Kennedy and Eberhart, 1995]. The main difference lies in the interaction among
the particles, wherein the classical PSO, the swarm’s position is updated based on the
51
==PAGE:64==
6. Vehicle fleet size optimization
best-performing swarm, while in the GWO, the position of the wolf population is updated
based on the three top-performing wolves. Additional modifications are also carried out in
the GWO, compared to the PSO, particularly in the formulas used to update the position
of the wolves, where the range of certain random values is different. The advantages of the
GWO are the ease of understanding its mechanisms by all the stakeholders involved in the
project and its suitable structure to the SOLUTIONSplus model.
Steps in the algorithm
The algorithm initiates by forming a pool of feasible solutions, each representing the
position of a wolf within the pack. The objective value for each solution is calculated
to identify the most optimal solutions: the best is designated as the alpha wolf, the
second-best as the beta wolf, and the third-best as the delta wolf. This stage simulates
the search for prey. Once the high-ranking wolves are identified, the omega wolves begin
to converge around them, symbolizing the strategy of prey encirclement. As the omega
wolves move around their leaders, they may discover new, potentially superior solutions.
In that case, the hierarchical structure of the wolves is adjusted accordingly: new alpha,
beta, and delta wolves are designated. This iterative process continues until the algorithm
reaches the predetermined maximum number of iterations. At the end of the algorithm,
all the wolves converge on the same position, symbolizing the attack of the prey [Mirjalili
et al., 2014].

6.4
Metaheuristics: Parameter tuning
The implementation of metaheuristics requires setting initial parameters to ensure that
the algorithm runs correctly. These parameters must be carefully tuned to guarantee
the algorithm’s efficiency and effectiveness because they control the behavior of heuristic
mechanisms in the algorithm.
Various methods for tuning parameters are available,
including simple, iterative, and high-level generate-evaluate techniques [Huang et al.,
2020]. For the SOLUTIONSplus project, a generate-evaluate approach is applied using the
brute-force method. This technique is chosen due to its ease of comprehension, making it
accessible to all stakeholders involved in the project. Moreover, it is a well-known method
in the metaheuristic field.
6.4.1
Brute-force method for parameter tuning
The brute-force method for tuning parameters involves systematically testing every possible
combination of parameters to identify the most effective one, for each metaheuristic [Huang
et al., 2020]. This approach requires several steps for a proper implementation. First, it
is essential to identify the parameters used in each metaheuristic. Then, it is necessary
to define the ranges of these parameters where promising solutions are likely to be found.
Finally, it is important to establish different metrics to evaluate the various parameter
combinations.
Firstly, for each metaheuristic defined in Section 6.3, the pseudo-code of the algorithms
already highlights the parameters used. Secondly, to determine the parameter ranges, a
preliminary evaluation is conducted by running each metaheuristic with random parameter
values, which helped in identifying promising ranges. Thirdly, for a given metaheuristic,
each combination of parameters is run over 30 repetitions, and the best set of parameters
is assessed based on the average objective value obtained across these repetitions. If there
54

6. Vehicle fleet size optimization
is a tie on this value, the parameter combination with the shortest runtime is preferred.
Two other metrics, the highest objective value achieved and the standard deviation of the
objective value across the 30 repetitions, are provided for informational purposes only.

6.7
Metaheuristics: Limitations
The GWO presents several limitations. Firstly, as a metaheuristic algorithm, it does
not guarantee that the solution found is the optimal solution to the problem. Secondly,
while the GWO demonstrates robust performance for the SOLUTIONSplus project with
the parameters established during the tuning phase, adjustments to the parameters are
required in case of changes in the budget or the number of vehicle modes. This phase is
essential because an expanded solution space may require a larger wolf population to ensure
adequate coverage at the initialization of the algorithm, or more iterations to reach the
best solution possible. These two issues, the potential failure to find the optimal solution
and the need to redo the parameter tuning in case of changes in the problem scale, are
typical challenges associated with the use of metaheuristic algorithms.
Another limitation of the GWO relates to the structural applicability of the algorithm.
Considering again the SOLUTIONSplus project, the algorithm aligns with the objective
of determining the optimal number of vehicles per mode to introduce, while maximizing
the overall star rating of the KPIs, which can be symbolized by finding the position of a
prey in an n-dimensional space for the wolves. However, if the project scope expands to
include additional requirements such as routing or time window constraints, the current
structure of the GWO may become unsuitable. Under such circumstances, an alternative
metaheuristic algorithm would need to be used to address these new complexities.

SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS
A sensitivity analysis is a technique used to study how different sources of uncertainty in
the inputs of a model can affect its outputs. In the following sections, a sensitivity analysis
is carried out for the GWO in regard to the SOLUTIONSplus project.
It was previously noted that the SOLUTIONSplus project is of a relatively small scale,
particularly due to the limited number of vehicle modes and low budget available. Under
these conditions, identifying the optimal solution by brute-force method is possible in a
short amount of time. This method consists of enumerating all the feasible solutions and
evaluating them based on their overall star rating to find the optimal solution. However,
as mentioned in Chapter 6, this master’s thesis seeks to establish an applicable framework
that can determine the optimal number of vehicles to introduce in a city or a country,
regardless of the budget size, or the number of vehicle modes. That is the reason why the
GWO is used to determine the optimal solution for the SOLUTIONSplus project. While
the analysis demonstrates that the GWO performs effectively in the project context, it is
now crucial to assess whether this metaheuristic can also deliver strong performance in
larger-scale projects.
In the following sections, two scenarios are examined to test the robustness of the
optimization framework. The first scenario maintains the same number of vehicle modes
as the SOLUTIONSplus project but increases the budget by a factor of 50. By doing
so, the optimal solution found is different, and therefore, a new analysis of the emissions
of CO2, NOx, and PM2.5 is done to compare it with the baseline scenario. The second
scenario keeps the project’s original budget but expand the number of vehicle modes to
seven. These scenarios will help demonstrate the framework’s adaptability to varying
scales and complexities.

OVERALL FRAMEWORK
For clarity, the following chapter outlines the overall framework developed for this thesis
in the context of the scaled-up project in Kathmandu. Figure 8.1 shows the framework
and highlights the different concepts. This overview summarises the methods used in the
different chapters and provides a comprehensive understanding to simplify the application
of these concepts within other projects related to the introduction of sustainable urban
transportation in cities beyond the scaled initiative in Kathmandu.
Figure 8.1. Overall framework
77
==PAGE:90==
8. Overall framework
The methodology is divided into different concepts as well as different phases. The various
concepts and their application are split into the following points:
1. Key Performance Indicators:
It starts with the selection and the performance measurement of the KPIs, which
include the phases Criteria Identification, Criteria Scoring and Criteria Weighting.
The KPIs serve as important metrics for defining criteria that can be evaluated based
on the different alternatives in the context of the project, in this case, the number
of EVs introduced to the vehicle fleet. To ensure the comparability of KPIs across
different performance metrics and units, a star value system is integrated into the
framework. Converting all scores of each KPI to a star value with the help of the
value functions enables their utilization within the mathematical optimization model.
2. Future Mobility Calculator:
Additionally, in the Criteria Scoring phase, the FMC is used to provide DMs with
a better understanding of the sustainability impact of urban transport in relation
to emissions and to create a baseline scenario that serves as input for the KPIs. In
addition, the baseline scenario serves as a point of comparison for later decisions and
their consequences.
3. Optimization:
After converting the scoring values to star values, they can be combined with the
weights of the KPIs to form the objective function for the mathematical model. Due
to the non-linearity of the constraints, the metaheuristic GWO is utilized. This
advanced optimization technique is used to maximize the various star values of the
KPIs, enhancing the overall performance of the urban transport system. To ensure
the efficacy of the GWO, parameter tuning is conducted initially to identify the
optimal parameters. This process increases the likelihood of finding high-quality
solutions within the solution space, aligning with the project’s objectives.
4. Scaled-up emission calculation:
Following the optimization phase, the results are evaluated to assess the impact of the
implemented solutions. By comparing the outcomes before and after optimization,
stakeholders and DMs can measure the effectiveness of the selected interventions and
refine strategies accordingly.
By incorporating these elements, including a mathematical modeling approach, into
the framework not only enhances the decision-making process but also promotes the
introduction of green mobility solutions within the urban transport context.
This
comprehensive approach addresses the complex challenges of urban mobility by balancing
social, economic, and environmental considerations, and also demonstrates the significant
impact it can have in promoting sustainability in cities worldwide.

CONCLUSION
The analysis presented in this thesis on the optimization of electric mobility for sustainable
urban transport in Kathmandu delivers, on the one hand, a framework that combines
MCDA through SMART and metaheuristic algorithms to determine the optimal quantity
of EVs to introduce in the base year (2024); on the other hand, the calculations of the
emissions in the baseline scenario and the resulting emissions reduction in the scaled-up
scenario.
9.1
Results
The project provides a comprehensive understanding of how EVs deployment can
significantly contribute to enhancing urban life quality by promoting environmental
sustainability. It begins with the creation of a decision-making framework using SMART,
an MCDA method.
The SMART methodology facilitated the identification of KPIs
across financial, environmental, and social dimensions, adhering to the Mutually Exclusive
and Collectively Exhaustive (MECE) principle.
These KPIs were agreed upon with
the stakeholders and meticulously weighted to reflect the importance assigned by DMs.
Afterward, they are scored and then normalized with a 5-star scale with the help of
value functions to ensure comparability across diverse metrics. This thorough evaluation
provided a robust foundation for optimizing public transportation in Kathmandu, aligning
the project goals with the broader objectives of the SOLUTIONSplus initiative.
The subsequent analysis involved a detailed environmental impact assessment using the
FMC, employed to quantify CO2, NOx, and PM2.5 emissions projected to be released
into the environment. This was achieved by defining a baseline scenario, illustrating the
progression of Kathmandu’s urban fleet composition from 2024 to 2050, without additional
investments in EVs. It resulted in a cumulative 20,241,843 tons of CO2, 175,685 tons
of NOx, and 92,321 tons of PM2.5 to be released into the environment by 2050. These
alarming figures outline the need for changes in the public fleet in Kathmandu, where even
small reductions in emissions could make a huge impact on future generations.
The optimization phase employed advanced metaheuristic algorithms to identify the optimal
fleet composition to introduce in Kathmandu’s public transportation system within the
given budget constraints. Three algorithms were evaluated: SA, EA, and GWO. Among
these, the GWO algorithm emerged as the most effective, consistently delivering optimal
solutions (96% of the cases) with greater efficiency than SA and EA (GWO performed 0.04s,
compared to SA 0.25s and EA 1.59s, for one repetition). The GWO’s performance in both
effectiveness and runtime efficiency enabled it to identify the optimal configuration of EVs,
recommended to be of 25 electric buses, 20 electric waste collectors, and 30 electric shuttle
vans; achieved by balancing the constraints on vehicle modes and financial limitations
79
==PAGE:92==
9. Conclusion
while maximizing the overall star ratings of the KPIs.
Moreover, it was essential to assess the environmental benefits of introducing EVs into
Kathmandu’s public transport system within a budget constraint of €2,000,000, in the
base year 2024, revealing reductions in emissions. The optimized scenario (which resulted
in the introduction of 25 electric buses, 20 electric waste collectors, and 30 electric shuttle
vans, as stated above) projected savings of up to 10,177 tons of CO2, 80 tons of NOx,
and 28 tons of PM2.5 by 2050. Even if the findings appear almost negligible, they are
expected values, due to the low budget invested in additional EVs in 2024. However, they
underscore the potential of strategic EV integration to mitigate air pollution and enhance
urban air quality, reinforcing the principle of investment in sustainable transport solutions.
A critical aspect of the study involved conducting a robustness assessment through a
sensitivity analysis, which explored the impact of varying project constraints on the
optimization outcomes. Two primary scenarios were analyzed: an increase in the available
budget by a factor of 50 (scenario 1) and the inclusion of additional vehicle modes up to
seven (scenario 2). This analysis demonstrated that the GWO algorithm maintained its
computational efficiency and ability to identify optimal solutions even as the solution space
expanded. In scenario 1, reductions of 371,320 tons of CO2, 2,390 tons of NOx, and 847
tons of PM2.5 by 2050 are projected compared to the baseline scenario. This expanded
investment allowed for the introduction of a larger e-fleet (an additional 500 mini-buses,
500 pick-up trucks, and 1,500 micro-buses to the number of EVs in the baseline scenario
in the base year 2024), further enhancing the project’s capacity to reduce emissions and
improve urban air quality. These results highlight the critical role of financial investment
in maximizing the environmental benefits of electric mobility initiatives, demonstrating
that increased funding can significantly amplify the positive outcomes of EV deployment.
In conclusion, the integration of MCDA with metaheuristic optimization and environmental
modeling through the FMC offers a robust and adaptable framework for urban transport
planning. This framework effectively addresses Kathmandu’s current needs and provides
a replicable model for other cities pursuing sustainable transport solutions. The study
demonstrates the feasibility and benefits of a data-driven, optimized approach to EV
deployment, advancing the field by showing how comprehensive decision-making frameworks
and advanced optimization techniques can support the transition to low-carbon urban
mobility.
9.2
Overall limitations
In examining the limitations, several constraints and challenges emerge in the context of
optimizing electric mobility in Kathmandu.
Firstly, the KPIs, while comprehensive, are subject to challenges related to their selection
and weighting processes.
Their choice, influenced by available data and stakeholder
priorities, may not capture all relevant dimensions of sustainability, potentially overlooking
important factors such as social equity or long-term economic impacts. Moreover, the
subjective weighting of KPIs, essential for reflecting stakeholder preferences, can introduce
biases that affect the comparability and overall assessment. The normalization of KPIs,
intended to facilitate cross-comparison, can also be problematic, especially when dealing
80
==PAGE:93==
9. Conclusion
with diverse metrics that do not naturally align on a common scale. This can lead to
difficulties interpreting the results and ensuring that the aggregated KPI scores accurately
represent the underlying performance. Also, the focus of the KPIs on specific, measurable
outcomes may miss broader qualitative aspects of electric mobility, such as community
acceptance or behavioral changes, which are crucial for the understanding of the impacts
of EV deployment.
Secondly, regarding the FMC, it reveals limitations related to data accuracy, scenario
assumptions, and model precision. The reliability of FMC’s outputs is constrained by the
accuracy and completeness of the input data, and incomplete or outdated data can lead to
inaccurate estimates of emissions and environmental impacts. Furthermore, the scenario
modeled in FMC rely on several assumptions regarding technological advancements, policy
changes, and market adoption rates, which introduce uncertainty into the predictions. These
assumptions, while necessary for modeling, could diverge from real-world developments,
potentially affecting the projected outcomes. Additionally, the FMC’s capacity to model
complex interactions between different transport modes and urban factors is limited,
potentially oversimplifying the challenges of urban transport systems.
Lastly, limitations are also related to the mathematical modeling and optimization
techniques employed to determine the optimal fleet size and composition. The metaheuristic
algorithms used, SA, EA, and GWO can encounter difficulties in escaping local optima,
particularly in highly complex and non-linear problem spaces, which may prevent the
identification of the global optimal solution. The computational complexity and runtime
can also be significant, especially for large-scale problems or when incorporating multiple
objectives and constraints.
Parameter tuning, essential for the effectiveness of these
algorithms, can be challenging and time-consuming, as it requires extensive experimentation
to identify the optimal settings for different scenarios. Lastly, the model’s reliance on
a predefined set of vehicle modes and budget constraints may limit the exploration of
alternative or innovative solutions that could emerge under different policy or market
conditions.
9.3
Future work and recommendations
Future work on optimizing electric mobility for sustainable urban transport in Kathmandu
should focus on addressing the limitations identified in this thesis and enhancing the
framework’s adaptability and precision.
Firstly, integrating real-time data sources into the FMC would significantly enhance the
accuracy of environmental impact assessments by providing current emissions data, vehicle
performance metrics, and transport usage patterns. This real-time integration would
also facilitate dynamic modeling that better captures the complexities of urban transport
systems, such as fluctuating traffic conditions and seasonal variations. Additionally, it
would be beneficial to employ the FMC tool for comprehensive cost-benefit evaluations,
including infrastructure costs, electricity costs, and vehicle investment costs, as well as
assessing social impacts. Achieving this could involve collecting data from stakeholders,
thus providing a complete overview of the implications of introducing additional EVs into
the public urban transport system. Afterward, conducting sensitivity analyses with varying
81
==PAGE:94==
9. Conclusion
socio-economic scenarios and policy changes would help in understanding the robustness
of the proposed solutions and their applicability under different conditions.
Also, the current scope of this thesis covers only the usage phase of EVs and ICE vehicles,
where emissions and emissions reduction are calculated based on the days the vehicles
are in operation. However, for a more comprehensive overview of the environmental
impact of EVs, a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is necessary. An LCA would consider the
impact of EVs and ICE vehicles from the extraction of raw materials to final disposal,
including the production and disposal of batteries for EVs. In this context, the use phase
(analyzed in this thesis, as stated above) would be just one part of a broader process
that also includes the extraction of raw materials, transportation to the production site,
production, distribution to the end user, and final disposal. Consequently, comparing
the total emissions generated by the two types of vehicles (EVs and ICE vehicles) would
provide a better understanding of the overall environmental impacts and the potential
lifecycle emissions reduction achieved by introducing new EVs.
Another aspect that should be taken into consideration is expanding the scope of the
optimization framework to include a broader range of vehicle types and alternative
transport modes, which could provide more comprehensive solutions that align with
evolving technological and policy landscapes. Enhancing the metaheuristic algorithms
used by incorporating hybrid techniques or machine learning approaches could improve
their ability to escape local optima and reduce computational demands, leading to more
efficient and effective optimization processes. For instance, in the GWO, a machine learning
algorithm could serve as an aid to initialize an evenly distributed population of wolves in
space, reducing the current randomness.
Furthermore, it would be highly advantageous to develop a template that combines
metaheuristic algorithms with Excel-based tools, designed to be adaptable for any country
or city. This template should be highly customizable, allowing for the selection of KPIs
and the choice of vehicle modes and ensuring the accuracy of input data. Moreover, it is
crucial to involve DM iteratively throughout the development of optimization algorithms
rather than solely in the initial (a priori) or final stages (a posteriori) of the decision-
making process. This continuous engagement with stakeholders would ensure that the
evolving preferences and constraints of the community are integrated, thereby enhancing
the relevance and acceptance of the proposed EV deployment strategies.
Finally, it is crucial and highly beneficial for Kathmandu to increase its budget for
a more significant impact on emission reduction. As demonstrated in the sensitivity
analysis, introducing more EVs to the fleet will substantially reduce total emissions,
thereby enhancing both the environmental and living conditions for city residents. The

KPI DEFINITIONS
B.1
Financial indicators
B.1.1
Financial viability
Financial viability can be assessed through several indicators depending on the type of
project examined (profit maximizing or cost minimizing operation) and the intended use.
Profit maximising projects
Commercial applications undertaken by private operators are usually profit maximizing
projects. In these cases, both revenues and out-of-pocket costs need to be estimated for the
entire life duration of the project. The indicators used for such cases are the Net Present
Value (NPV), Internal Rate of Return (IRR) and the Payback Period. The first two are
considered more formal and are usually required by the financing institutions. Payback
period is the most popular one among non-economists, as it is the easiest indicator to
comprehend.
NPV (Net Present Value)
Reflecting the present worth of an investment, NPV is defined as the sum of all future
cash flows discounted at a periodic rate of return to account for the time value of money.
A positive NPV indicates that the projected earnings generated by the project exceeds the
anticipated costs and the project can be accepted. The NPV of the up-scaled project will
be calculated via a specialized software, including the UNEP e-MOB, which offers this
possibility. A value function will be needed to transform the NPV (expressed in monetary
terms) into a star value as required by the evaluation framework.
IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
IRR denotes the rate of return that sets the net present value of the future cash flows of
a project equal to zero. An IRR higher than the opportunity cost of the project owner
indicates a profitability that exceeds the expected one from other activities and suggests
the undertaking of the project. The higher a project’s IRR is, the more desirable its
undertaking becomes. The IRR of the up-scaled project will be calculated via a specialized
software. A value function will be needed to transform the IRR (expressed in %) into a
star value as required by the evaluation framework.
Payback period
It denotes the time (in years) required to recover the funds expended in an investment
or to reach the break-even point. It does not consider the time value of money, a fact
that makes it easy to apply and understand. The lower a project’s payback period is, the
more desirable its undertaking becomes. The payback period of the up-scaled project will
be calculated via a specialized software. A value function will be needed to transform
90
==PAGE:103==
B. KPI definitions
the payback period (expressed in years) into a star value as required by the evaluation
framework.
Cost minimising projects
There are projects, mainly in the public sector, where revenues either do not exist or are
very difficult to monetize. The Cost Effectiveness Ratio (CER) is the appropriate indicator
for such cases.
CER (Cost Effectiveness Ratio)
CER relates the costs of a project to its key outcomes. The method identifies the costs of
the project and ascribes monetary values to them. It then identifies the primary outcome
of the project and quantifies it in terms of ‘units of effectiveness’ (e.g., number of lives
saved, volume of waste collected, etc.). CER is obtained by dividing total costs by the
units of effectiveness. The lower a project’s CER is, the more desirable its undertaking
becomes. A value function will be needed to transform the CER (expressed as a percentage
difference from the CER of the baseline solution) into a star value as required by the
evaluation framework.

B.5.5
Effect on charging safety incidents
Ensuring charging safety is a key element in the pursuit of e-mobility solutions.
Consideration towards the type of batteries and their charging technology/infrastructure
to be utilised must be noted when assessing risks associated with battery operation and
charging (i.e. conductive, inductive, battery swapping), as well as whether communication
and charging coordination are featured in the system. The assessment should also take
into consideration the mitigation measures and good practices that have already been
103
==PAGE:116==
B. KPI definitions
embedded to address the risks. The KPI on charging safety is hinged on the assessment of
the risks (and essentially, the project’s risk performance) relating to the following categories
of hazards:
• Electrical shock to users and personnel: Charging facilities can cause electrical
hazards, which can include potential electrical shock to customers (if applicable to
the design of the project), as well as electrical shock and arc flash hazards to workers.
Here are some examples of instances, which can lead to electrical shock: potential
failure of ground fault circuit-interrupting breaker, potential failure of charging circuit-
interrupting devices due to environmental factors or due to vandalism activities like
copper theft. Electric shock hazards greatly depend on the characteristics of the
charger. Protection against electric shock can be achieved through basic protection
(e.g. preventing persons from being in contact with the energized components or
parts), and fault protection (protection in the event of failure of the basic insulation
via disconnection of the supply). The reliability of the charging components with
electrical safety protection features should be monitored and assessed through periodic
safety inspections.
• Fire hazards: Fire hazards caused by charging of EVs may also affect personnel safety,
as well as result in damage to property. Lithium-based batteries, for example, can
self-ignite due to manufacturing errors, short-circuiting, exposure to extreme heat, or
damage to the battery cell. The pursuit of fast charging (and discharging) combined
with the high driving performance of EVs is also documented to have a negative
effect on fire risk. Fires due to charging may result from instances related to the
following: overcharging, short circuiting, overheating of the charging environment,
ignition of flammable materials, cable overload, faulty or insecure charging stations
and cables, improper installation, improper charging practices, failure of the onboard
charging equipment, and failure of the charging system in general. Protection against
external forces that may result in fires should also be taken into consideration (e.g.
arson, burning in the vicinity, among others).
• Power grid instability: The potential impacts of the high penetration of uncontrolled
charging can result in negative impacts to the power system due to potentially
significant increases in peak demand; voltage deviation from acceptable limits; phase
unbalance due to single-phase chargers; harmonics distortion; overloading of power
system equipment; increase of power losses. The main key variables are: penetration
level (i.e. the amount of EVs to be introduced into the system); the EV battery
charger (i.e. fast chargers expected to increase peak demand than slow chargers);
time of charging (i.e. EVs charging at the same time; interference with the peak
demand time); location; battery capacity (i.e. high capacity batteries will draw larger
amounts of energy); battery state-of-charge; state of the distribution system (e.g.
structure, equipment loading conditions, voltage level, and profile, load profile, etc).
Procedure: The assessment of this KPI requires that the analyst scores the three categories
in terms of severity and probability of occurrence. Only experts with good technical
knowledge are involved in the assessment.

Effect on well-being due to active traveling
The basis for this KPI is the number of active kilometres associated with a specific up-scaled
scenario. The active kilometres associated with the corresponding baseline solution are used
for benchmarking. Since there exist different modes of active traveling, a homogenization
process is required. The number of calories burned per kilometre of each transport mode is
used for transforming active traveling distances into walking-equivalent kilometres, which
serve as the homogenized unit. The conversion is based on the arithmetic mean of the
calories burnt per kilometre by a 60kg 1,65m female and a 75kg 1,75m male person, as
provided by the Activity Based Calorie Burn Calculator:
• Walking: 50.0 calories/km (based on 5km/h walking pace, 0% inclination)
• Cycling: 22.0 calories/km (based on 18km/h cycling pace)
• Driving scooter/motorcycle: 4.5 calories/km (based on 35km/h average speed)
• Driving car: 3.0 calories/km (based on 50km/h average speed)
The formula, then, for calculating active traveling activity (in walking-equivalent km) is:
Active kilometres = kilometres walking + 22/50 * kilometres cycling + 4.5/50 *
kilometres scooter/motorcycle + 3/50* kilometres car
Procedure:
1. Define the ‘typical route’ or the boundaries of the analysis
2. Define the transport solution that would be used under the baseline scenario for the
same transport defined in Step 1 (it can be the dominant alternative or a mix of
alternatives as explained in Section B.5.2)
3. Determine the number of kilometres per active transport mode for the baseline
scenario
4. Calculate the total number of walking-equivalent kilometres for the baseline scenario
using the formula provided above
5. Based on information collected during the demonstration actions, determine the
number of kilometres per active transport associated with the up-scaled scenario
6. Calculate the total number of walking-equivalent kilometres for the up-scaled scenario
using the formula provided above
7. Calculate the difference in walking-equivalent kilometres between the up-scaled and
the baseline scenarios.
A value function will be needed to transform the active traveling activity calculated as
described above into the 5-point scale of the evaluation framework.

B.6.1
Effect on national/local budget
In public transport (e.g., buses) costs are often borne by the government. Therefore, any
costs (capital and operational) higher than current expenditures put an additional burden
on the government finances. To the contrary, a positive impact on budget is expected in
the case of lower than current expenditures on public transport. Public investments are
also needed for the provision of charging infrastructures, and these can put an additional
burden on public finances.
Proposed unit: Percentage change in the relevant public (national/local) budget due to
the up-scaled project
Procedure:
1. Define the baseline scenario to be used for benchmarking purposes
2. Calculate the annual public budget flows (expenditures and revenues) associated
with the up-scaled project over its life. The e-MOB model or another specialized
software can be used for this purpose.
3. Calculate the annual public budget flows (expenditures and revenues) associated
with the baseline scenario over the same period.
4. Calculate the annual differences in budget flows and the average net annual flow. For
cities that can use the UNEP e-MOB calculator, this figure can be obtained as the
difference in the annual total cost of ownership between the up-scaled and baseline
scenarios
5. Express the net annual flow as a percentage of the average public (national/local)
budget calculated over the last three years (2019-2021).
The assessment should be performed by experts using information on capital expenditures
and operating expenses over the project period. The results should be validated by local
teams/stakeholders.
A value function will be needed to transform the percentage change in public budget as
calculated above into the 5-point scale of the evaluation framework.

B.6.3
Effect on employment
Job creation
This KPI is defined as the absolute number of net additional jobs (NNET ) expected to be
generated by the assessed new e-mobility solution in comparison to the baseline scenario.
NNET is calculated as the difference between the jobs expected to be added (NADD) due
to the new solution over the assessment period (2019 to 2030) and those expected to be
lost (NLOST ) during the same period (NNET = NADD˘NLOST ). It is expected that the
calculation will be based on the number of EVs entering the market and the estimated
effects on the labour market as experienced through past projects in the demo city or
elsewhere in the world. A value function will be needed to transform the number of
additional jobs into a star value as required by the evaluation framework.
