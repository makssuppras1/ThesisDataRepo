2 Abstract 
Introduction. In 2009, snakebite envenoming was for the first time recognized by the World Health 
Organization as a neglected tropical disease. The estimated global burden of snakebites ranges from a 
minimum of 421,000 envenomings and 20,000 deaths up to as high as 2.5 million envenoming cases and 
over 100,000 deaths each year.  
With its fast movement, short-tempered behaviour and highly potent venom Dendroapis polylepis (the 
Black mamba) is feared and categorized as a dangerous snake by the World Health Organization in Africa. 
Its venom is mainly composed of highly potent neurotoxins and the medically most relevant toxins are the 
three finger toxins short neurotoxin-I and α-elapitoxin Dpp2c and the dendrotoxin, dendrotoxin-I, these 
were the basis for this study.  
D. polylepis caused envenoming is today treated with antivenom prepared from polyvalent snake venom 
immunized animal antiserum, and is shown to be effective in the neutralization of lethality. Due to the 
animal based production, the antivenom suffers from drawbacks of high risk of side effects and low efficacy 
against D. polylepis medically relevant toxins. An analysis specifying the ideal antivenom treatment against 
D. polylepis caused envenoming points towards that affinity maturated variable heavy chain antibody (VHH) 
fragments and peptides could constitute relevant molecule types to be used in future antivenoms.  
 
Method. Phage display using six different M13 phage displaying libraries, one VHH phage library and five 
peptide libraries, against medically relevant toxins was used as a screening technique. ELISA was used to 
determine specific binding selected binders against toxins. Monoclonal phages were isolated and their DNA 
sent to sequencing. 
 
Results. Three binders against the fraction 7, know to contain α-elapitoxin Dpp2c and dendrotoxin-I, were 
identified, two within the VHH phage library and one within the 16- and 20-mer peptide library. The ability 
of the two VHH binders to cross-react with the three fingers toxins, short neurotoxin-I and α-cobratoxin, 
revealed no significantly greater binding compared to the binding to α–elapitoxin Dpp2c and dendrotoxin-I. 
Only the DNA sequence of the peptide binder was obtained. Further characterization of the binders should 
include in vitro and in vivo studies and characterization of the interaction with the toxin. Last optimization 
of the binders is needed in order to convert them into antivenom drugs.  
Last, a bioinformatics analysis of the medically relevant toxins from D. polylepis venom revealed that 
conserved sequences are present within the dendrotoxins and 3FTxs, and might be targets for cross-
neutralizing antivenom.

3 Resumé 
 
Introduktion. Forgiftning efter slangebid blev i 2009 for første gang anerkendt af World Health 
Organization som en forsømt tropisk sygdom. På verdensplan er effekten af slangebid estimeret fra et 
minimum på 412.000 forgiftninger og 20.000 dødsfald til et maksimum på 2,5 millioner forgiftninger og 
over 100.000 dødsfald om året.  Med dens hurtige bevægelser, aggressive adfærd og yderst kraftfulde gift, 
er Dendroaspis polylepis (den Sorte mamba) en frygtet og karakteriseret som en farlig slange i Afrika af 
World Health Organisation.  Dens gift består hovedsag af kraftige neurotoksiner og de mest medicinsk 
relevante toksiner er Tre finger toksinerne, kort neurotoxin-I og α-elapitoxin Dpp2c og dendrotoksinet, 
dendrotoksin-I, disse har dannes basis for dette projekt.  
Forgiftning forårsaget af D. polylepis er i dag behandlet med modgift, fremstillet af polyvalent slangegift 
immuniseret dyre antiserum, og disse er effektive til at neutralisere dødelighed. Der har desværre vist sig at 
være en del ulemper ved at producere modgiften i dyr. Blandt ulemper kan nævnes en høj risiko for 
bivirkninger og lav virkeevne mod medicinsk relevante D. polylepis toksiner. I en analyse af den ideelle 
modgift mod D. polylepis blev det konkluderet, at mulige molekyletyper til en fremtidig modgift kunne 
være affinitets modnede variable tung-kæde dele af antistoffer og peptider.  
 
Metode. Teknikken fagdisplay er blevet brugt, hvor seks forskellige M13-fag biblioteker, et med variable 
tung-kæde-bibliotek og fem med peptidbiblioteker, blevet screenet mod medicinsk relevante toksiner. Til 
at bestemme specifik binding til toksinerne blev der benyttet ELISA. Enkeltkloner blev isoleret og deres DNA 
fældet og sendt til sekvensering.   
 
Resultater. Der blev fundet tre binder mod fraktion 7, som indeholder toksinerne α-elapitoxin Dpp2c og 
dendrotoksin-I. To af binderne stammer fra det variable tung-kæde-bibliotek og en fra 16- og 20-mer 
peptidbiblioteket. De to variable tung-kæde bindere blev testet for deres evne til at krydsreagere med and 
tre finger toksiner, kort neurotoksin-I og α-kobratoksin. ELISA resultatet viste ingen signifikant bedre 
binding til de to toksiner sammenlignet med α–elapitoxin Dpp2c og dendrotoxin-I. Det lykkedes kun at få 
sekvenseret DNA fra peptid binderen.  
Yderligere karakterisering af binderne bør inkludere både in vitro og in vivo studier og karakterisering af 
dens interaktion med toksinet. Før binderne kan bruges i modgift, skal de ved forskellige metoder 
optimeres til funktionelle og stabile medikamenter.  
Der blev også lavet en bioinformatisk analyse af de medicinsk relevante toksiner isoleret fra D. polylepis 
gift. Analysen viste at der findes konserveret sekvenser både i dendrotoksin og tre finger toksin familien, 
hvilket tyder på at en krydsreagerende modgift kan udvikles.

5 Introduction  
5.1 Snakebite envenoming – a neglected tropical disease 
In 2009, snakebite envenoming was for the first time recognized by the World Health Organization (WHO) 
as a neglected tropical disease [2]. Most snakebites occur in areas of Africa, Asia, Latin America and New 
Guinea, and most victims are groups of agricultural workers and children [2]. Due to a poor access to fast 
administration of antivenom and treatment at the local hospitals, the mortality and disability rates are high 
[2, 3]. WHO has estimated the global burden to range from a minimum of 421,000 envenomings and 
20,000 deaths up to as high as 2.5 million envenoming cases and over 100,000 deaths each year. An exact 
estimate is impossible to obtain, since the local hospitals rarely report envenomings or mortality caused by 
snakebites [2].  
Snakes are classified into six families and several subfamilies [1], with the world’s most venomous snakes 
belonging to the families of Atractaspididae, Elapidae, and Viperidae. In Africa, members of Elapidae, 
including the genera Dendroaspis and Naja, are of the highest medical importance. By identifying the snake 
families causing the greatest public health burden, the development of specific antivenom could be focused 
on specific snake species, thereby leading to optimized treatment for the victims [2].  
5.2 Dendroaspis polylepis 
Dendroaspis polylepis (the Black mamba) is the most feared snake in southern, western and eastern Africa 
(Figure 1b) due to its length, prevalence, ferocity of attack, and rapid victim death due to its bite [4]. D. 
polylepis belongs to the four-membered genus Dendroaspis, which includes Dendroaspis angusiceps 
(Eastern green mamba), Dendroaspis viridis (Western green mamba), and Dendroaspis jamesoni (Jameson’s 
mamba), all are found in different areas in Africa (Figure 1b). 
 
D. polylepis lives in open bush country, often found in trees [4], and has an olive-brownish, dark grey 
colour, a light belly, and a characteristic black mouth (Figure 1a). The maximum recorded length of D. 
polylepis is 4.3 m, and it is capable of lifting 40% of its body off the ground [5]. The fangs are 3-6 mm in

length and a bite can release 1 mL of venom enough to kill a human adult [4]. The venom is mainly 
composed of highly potent neurotoxins [6] and will be further discussed in section 5.6.   
D. polylepis often bites its victim several times [4]. Early symptoms include systemic, autonomic, and 
circulatory symptoms, such as flaccid paralysis and direct cardiotoxicity [4, 5] followed by palpitation, 
hypertension and propulsive sweating, dyspnoea, nausea, difficulties in speaking and swallowing, cough,

muscle fasciculation [5], and progressive paralysis in respiratory muscles lead to cardiopulmonary collapse 
and death if no treatment is applied [4, 5]. With its fast movement, short-tempered behaviour and highly 
potent venom D. polylepis is categorized as a dangerous snake by WHO [2, 5].  
 
5.3 Antivenoms today 
The only effective treatment against snakebite envenoming is antivenom. All antivenoms are antisera 
isolated from an animal, typically a horse, immunized with a selection of venoms from different snakes [7, 
8]. The process of antivenom production is outlined in Figure 2a.

efficiency of antivenom available today, and these are listed in Table 1.

Of the drawbacks listed in Table 1, the presence of many redundant IgGs and high risk of side effects 
following administration of antivenom are the main problems of today’s antivenom [2].  
The animal-dependent production method is both responsible for the reduced amount of therapeutically 
effective IgGs and the increased risk of immune system activation in patients, possibly leading to side 
effects such as anaphylactic shock and serum sickness [8]. The antivenom processed today is known to 
treat envenoming caused by snakebite [10], and manufactures are aware of the above listed drawbacks. 
But due to low budgets and funding, many improvements are yet to see the light of day [8]. 
5.4 Efforts today  
To increase the survival rate of snakebite victims, fast-acting, cheap, safe, and efficient antivenom is 
needed. Today, different efforts on improving antivenom development and production have been 
suggested (Table 2).  
A major effort being undertaken in order to improve antivenom efficacy is by using venomics and 
antivenomics to characterize snake venoms. This provides knowledge of the immunological profiles, 
sequence and structure of each toxin to enabling the possibility of designing new immunising mixtures, 
which can be used to raise potent antisera with wider therapeutic ranges [6, 8, 14, 16]. Furthermore, the 
toxin sequence could be used for sequence analysis to identify sequences shared by several toxin and snake 
families in order to develop a broad ranged antivenom capable of cross-neutralizing toxins by binding to 
the shared sequences [8]. By identifying the toxins associated with the damaging and lethal effects of 
snakebite envenomings, neutralization could be achieved by using a range of different components, such as 
small molecules, peptides, or antibody (fragments) [17].

5.4.1 Antibodies and antibody fragments 
Antivenom used today either contains IgGs or IgG fragment F(ab)2  or Fab (Figure 3), they all have the 
drawbacks listed in Table 1, but certainly also have several benefits. IgGs, F(ab)2 and Fabs have different 
molecular weights, but exert no difference in inhibiting local damage or systemic toxicity. IgGs have a slow 
inhibitory onset and a low volume of distribution when administrated. Furthermores, IgGs are efficient 
antivenom components with a long circulatory half-life of 20 days in humans [10]. F(ab)2 and Fabs have a 
smaller molecular weight with a larger volume of distribution, but they unfortunately low serum half-life of 
1-2 days [10, 26].  
In studies of improving antibody based drugs in treating autoimmunity, humanization of antibody 
fragments has been investigated with success [11]. These efforts have inspired antivenom researchers to 
investigate the potentials of coupling antibody fragments to human Fc-regions in order to limit antivenom 
side effects [27]. These efforts are discussed in section 9.4.1.

Another type of antibody fragment with promising potential for being used in future antivenoms is camelid 
variable heavy chain (VHH) antibodies (nanobodies) (Figure 3) [28]. Nanobodies have been shown to be as

efficacious as IgGs [8] and have several benefits, including high stability against both heat, extreme pH, and 
various chemicals [18-21]. The main drawback of nanobodies is their low serum half-life which limits their

Nanobodies with high affinity against the α-neurotoxin, α-cobratoxin (α-Cbt) from Naja kouthia, have been

death induced by α-Cbt [29]. Camelid nanobodies have been shown to be less immunogenic and therefore 
less likely to elicit adverse effects in patients compared to horse antivenom antibodies [29]. 
5.4.2 Proteins and peptides.  
Some animals, including snakes, have evolved resistance to some snake venom components as part of their 
natural defence against envenoming. In particular, neutralizing proteins from animal blood have been 
shown to inhibit metalloproteinases and phospholipase A2s (PLA2s) [10, 24]. These inhibitors are of interest 
for clinical use in snake antivenom due to their potency compared to other inhibitors described so far, 
especially against phospholipase A2s, which are responsible for major pathological effects of many snake 
venoms. Such antitoxins have also been isolated from mammals possibly evolved through evolutionary 
specialisation, exemplified by the California ground squirrel. The serum from this squirrel is able to reduce 
necrosis and haemorrhages induced by rattlesnake venom [31]. The sequences from animal derived 
proteins that are responsible for inhibition of snake toxins have in certain cases been as few as 10 amino 
acid residues [10]. 
5.4.3 Small molecules 
To mitigate the risks of adverse reactions associated with administration of animal-derived antivenoms and 
to obtain different and more optimal bio-distribution of snakebite therapies, attempts have been made to 
discover small molecule inhibitors against snake toxins [9]. Small molecules have been isolated from plants 
and microorganisms and could be synthetically prepared [32]. Most of the studied small molecules have 
been shown to inhibit PLA2s and metalloproteinases from different snake subfamilies. Furthermore, small 
molecules are capable of inhibiting local tissue damage by exerting antihemorrhagic, antidermanecrotic, 
and/or antimyotoxic activity [9, 10]. Despite the fact that some small molecules exert inhibitory effect 
against snake toxins and crude venom activities, the inhibitory effect is enhanced when the small molecules 
are co-administered with well-known animal-derived antivenom [33, 34]. An example includes the 
polysulfonated polyanion small molecule, Suramin. When Suramin was administrated together with 
antibothropic serum, an enhanced inhibitory effect of the in vivo thrombus formation induced by Bothrops 
jararaca venom was observed, suggesting that this combination could be used as a broad-spectrum 
inhibitor of myotoxic effects induced by PLA2s [33].

5.6 Toxins in Dendroaspis polylepis 
The venom of D. polylepis is a complex mixture of pharmacologically active proteins and polypeptides 
(Figure 4a). Many of the most abundant toxins can be divided into a low number of predominant protein 
families with a high number of homologous components within each family [6]. A proteomic 
characterization of D. polylepis venom has identified the most abundant, lethal, and pharmacological 
relevant components of the venom to be: 
 
short neurotoxin-1 
 
α-elapitoxin Dpp2c  
 
dendrotoxin-Iα 
 
dendrotoxin-B 
In addition, it is also likely that muscarinic toxin-α and a toxin similar to dendrotoxin-δ from D. angusticeps 
(P00982) are relevant [6]. In terms of abundance and toxicity, two families are represented: Kunitz-type 
proteinase inhibitors (KUN), including dendrotoxins (Figure 4b), and Three-Finger toxins (3Ftx), including 
short neurotoxin-1, α-elapitoxin Dpp2c, and muscarinic toxin-α (Figure 4c).

5.6.1 Three-Finger Toxin family 
3FTxs belong to a non-enzymatic protein family exhibiting a distinct protein fold of β-stranded loops 
extending from a hydrophobic core and stabilized by minimum four conserved disulfide bridges (Figure 4c), 
indicating that the structure is highly conserved. The 3FTx family is represented in many venomous animals 
[35, 36] and exhibit different pharmacological functions, such as neurotoxicity, cardiotoxicity, cytotoxicity, 
anticoagulant, hypotensive effect, antimicrobial activity, and platelet aggregation [37, 38]. These effects 
arise from the different targets of 3FTxs, including the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR) and the G-
protein coupled receptors, and muscarinic AChR [39]. 
5.6.1.1 Short neurotoxin-I 
The 3FTx, short neurotoxin-I (SNT-I), is a 60 amino acid long toxin, which belongs to the subfamily of α-
neurotoxins [35, 40].  Its X-ray crystal structure has been solved (PDB ID: 1NTX) (Figure 4c). SNT-I binds with 
high affinity to different muscular and neural nAChRs [41, 42] and prevents acetylcholine from binding to 
these receptors resulting in reduced neuromuscular transmission [35]. Physiologically, this results in flaccid 
paralysis and death within 10-30 minutes in mice. SNT-I is responsible for most of the toxicity of D. polylepis 
venom and has a LD50 of 0.09 μg [6]. 
5.6.1.2 α-elapitoxin Dpp2c 
The 3FTx, α-elapitoxin-Dpp2c (Dpp2c), is a long-chain α-neurotoxin consisting of 72 amino acids with a 
molecular weight of 7.9 kDa. Dpp2c blocks the choline-evoked Ca2+ response in neuroblastoma cells 
expressing subtypes or muscle-types of nAChRs.  Its X-ray crystal structure has been solved (PDB ID: 4LFT, 
Figure 7d) and the toxins has high sequence and structural similarity to other neurotoxins targeting the 
nAChR [43]. In solution, Dpp2c is present both in mono- and dimeric form [43].

5.6.1.3 Muscarinic toxin-α 
Muscarinic toxin-α (MT-α) is a toxin composed of 65 amino acids with four disulfide bridges [44, 45]. It 
exhibits a non-selective ligand binding to five muscarinic AChR subtypes [44-46]. 
 
MT-α binds to muscular AChR in a two-step mechanism, first a receptor-toxin complex is formed followed 
by a slow process of isomerization. The isomerization delays the effect of MT-α, but increase their potency 
compared to other toxins [46, 47]. Binding of MT-α to muscarinic AChR affects physiological processes, such 
as the heart rate, contraction of smooth muscles, and release of neurotransmitters [45]. 
5.6.2 Dendrotoxins 
Dendrotoxins are unique for the genus Dendroaspis and are venom components of high abundance [48, 49] 
[6, 50]. Dendrotoxins belong to the KUN family and have a conserved structure composed of an α-helix, a 
twisted β-hairpin, an antiparallel β-sheet, and a 310-helix [51, 52], stabilized by three conserved disulfide 
bridges [49], which are essential for the structure and activity of the toxins (Figure 4b). 
Dendrotoxins enhance [53] the evoked [54] release of neurotransmitter by reducing subtypes of voltage-
dependent potassium channels in nerve membranes. Thereby, they affect the presynaptic activity [53-57] 
and induce a potent epileptic activity in the prey [54, 57, 58]. 
5.6.2.1 Dendrotoxin-I 
Dendrotoxin-I (Dtx-I) is a 60 amino acid long toxin that reduces the current in mammalian brain potassium 
channel subtypes [51, 59, 60], thereby reducing the release of neurotransmitters at the neuromuscular 
junction. This leads to convulsive activity [61] and paroxysmal seizures [62]. The in solution nuclear 
magnetic resonance structure has been determined (PBD ID: 1DEM, Figure 4b), and this is homologous to 
other dendrotoxins [51, 61, 63]. 
5.6.2.2 Dendrotoxin-B 
Dendrotoxin B is a 57 amino acid long toxin, which acts as a weak trypsin inhibitor. Very little has been 
described about Dendrotoxin B, but the sequence is available and revealed two recognition sites; one 
recognizes the protease and the other is for other proteins [64].  
5.6.2.3 Dendrotoxin-δ 
Dendrotoxin-δ is a 57 amino acid long peptide with a molecular weight of 6.5 kDa isolated from D. 
angusticeps [40, 65]. Dtx-δ irreversibly inhibits certain subtypes of the potassium channel [60, 66] by partly 
inhibiting the current [65].  
5.7 Treatment against D. polylepis envenoming 
The venom of D. polylepis is dominated by neurotoxins of the 3FTx family and dendrotoxins of the KUN 
family that both act systemically in prey [6]. D. polylepis caused envenoming is today treated with 
antivenom prepared from polyvalent snake venom antiserum, shown to be effective in the neutralization of 
lethality (Table 3). 
The polyvalent antivenom is prepared from a mixture of different snake venoms, resulting in an antivenom 
mixture composed of antibodies against wide variety of toxins. The venom composition among even 
related snakes is diverse, and the most medically relevant toxins vary among snake genera.  
It has been shown that antibodies often are raised against larger more immunogenic toxins in the venom, 
such as metalloproteinases rather than smaller 3FTxs [67]. This is unfavorable in the case of D. polylepis 
envenoming treatment, since the medically relevant toxins are small neurotoxins and dendrotoxins [6].

To improve D. polylepis antivenom the efficacy towards the medically relevant toxins should be increased, 
this could potentially decrease the antibody titer and the necessary dose to neutralize the venom. A lower 
dose could reduce the risk of side effects.   
From the antivenom component comparison in Table 3, two components were selected for further 
investigation, nanobodies and peptides. Nanobodies have the potential of having high efficacy and low 
immunogenicity, while peptides are cheap to produce if a high affinity binder is identified.  
 
To discover high affinity binders, including peptides or nanobodies, selected against D. polylepis medically 
relevant toxins, the most efficient method is phage display. 
5.8 Phage display 
Phage display screening is a rapid, easy-to-use technique for discovery of antibody, nanobody, or peptide-
based drugs or drug scaffolds. By expressing billions of variants of peptides or antibody fragments fused to 
the surface of filamentous phages, these phages can be used to screen against a specific target in order to 
select high affinity binders [68, 69]. The expressed antibody, nanobody, or peptide is encoded in the phage 
DNA, and the sequence of potential binders can easily be determined via DNA sequencing [68]. The 
principles of this techniques were developed by George P. Smith in 1985 [70].  
5.8.1 Technical details 
5.8.1.1 M13 filamentous phages 
The M13 filamentous phage is one of the most typically used phage types in phage display screening. 
Phages are viruses that infect Gram-negative bacteria, such as E. coli TG1 [68]. M13 has a flexible rod-
shaped structure composed of major capsid protein g8p encapsulating the phage circular genome [69] 
(Figure 5a). One end of the phage is covered by 3 to 5 copies of gene III(g3)-encoded adsorption protein 
(g3p) called the minor coat protein. The library of peptides or antibodies is displayed by fusing the peptides 
or antibodies to the N-terminal of g3p [68]. M13 infects E. coli and uses the cells replicon for reproduction. 
Furthermore, the phage induces a state in which the infected bacteria continuously secrete progeny 
phages, thereby avoiding lysis [68, 71, 72].

There are several advantages of using filamentous phages. (1) Their genome tolerates insertions of non-
essential regions without disrupting phage packaging, and their size is not constrained by the additional 
DNA [73]. (2) Their genome can be isolated as single stranded DNA (ssDNA) from the phages or doubled 
stranded DNA (dsDNA) from the infected E. coli and used for cloning and library construction [69]. (3) 
Another essential advantage is the ability of the coat proteins to be modified to display foreign entities as 
fusion protein without loss of phage infectivity [69, 70]. (4) Finally, filamentous phages are pH and 
temperature stable and can accumulate in high concentrations in the infected bacteria due to non-lytic 
propagation [69]. 
5.8.1.2 The phage display screening procedure 
When performing a phage display screening round, snake toxins are first immobilized on plate wells, 
impermeable plastic beads, nitrocellulose membranes, paramagnetic beads, or permeable beaded agarose 
gels [71] (Figure 5b). The phage library is (bio-)panned onto the coated toxin, and phages displaying 
peptides with affinity towards the toxin will bind to this toxin, while unbound phages are washed away 
(Figure 5b) [68]. The bound phages are eluted by changing the peptide interaction properties. This can be 
done by changing the pH. Eluted phages are amplified in E. coli and used as input for another round of 
panning [71] (Figure 5b). Phage display screening is thus an iterative process of selecting high affinity 
binders typically through 3-5 rounds of panning.    
5.8.2 Phage libraries used for panning 
Several different phage libraries have been constructed and include phages displaying peptides, antibody 
epitopes, antibodies, and VHH (nanobodies) of antibodies. In this study, D. polylepis toxins were used as 
targets in phage display using libraries of random peptide and nanobodies.  
5.8.2.1 Peptide libraries 
Random peptide libraries are displayed on M13 phages as peptide-capsid fused proteins to either g3p or 
g8p. The randomization is obtained by cloning synthetic oligonucleotides with a constant length, but 
unspecific codons into the phage. The unspecific codons are randomized through site-directed mutagenesis 
of all degenerated oligodeoxynucleotides [68], excluding cystein. Throughout the different rounds of 
panning, mutations are introduced into the phage population, which expand the size of the libraries. 
Furthermore, increased peptide length leads to an increase in the number of possible combinations of 
amino acids, and thus the number of possible randomized peptides. Therefore, when using peptide 
libraries, several rounds of panning and experiments might be need to identify the best binders.  
Crucial to the success of phage display screening is the complexity of the peptide libraries. A high 
abundance of peptide sequences in the library increases the chances of finding a binder to the target but 
the complexity is limited by the volume requirements and solubility of the M13 phages [74]. 
5.8.2.1.1 Peptide library composition and construction 
In this study, random peptide libraries of various lengths ranging from 7 to 20 amino acids were used. 
These were constructed by the same procedure. The peptides are encoded by randomized condons 
represented as diversified ssDNA. The ssDNA is created into dsDNA and digested together with cloning 
vector M13KE DNA. M13KE and dsDNA were ligated to g3p and transformed into electroporated E.coli [74, 
==PAGE:18==
[Skriv tekst] 
 
18 
 
75]. Within the E. coli, the library was amplified and harvested in order to extract the final phage display 
peptide library [74]. This construction results in five copies of peptide-g3p fusion on the phage surface [74]. 
All peptides are linked to the g3p through a short linker sequence of Gly-Gly-Gly-Ser [76]. 
5.8.2.1.2 7-, c7c-, 12-mer library 
The first library used for screening for D. polylepis toxin binders was a pre-made library consisting of linear 
heptapeptides (7-mer peptides) and dodecapeptides (12-mer peptides) and loop-constrained 
heptapeptides (c7c-mer peptides). The c7c-mer peptides are flanked by a pair of cysteine residues forming 
a disulphide bridge during oxidized phage assembly, resulting in a loop structure [76]. Constrained peptides 
might improve the free binding energy by having less unfavourable binding entropy. However, the 
constraints might also exclude certain binding possibilities by restraining the flexible binding structure. 
Therefore, the constrained c7c-mer library is screened together with the linear 7-mer library to find all 
potential binders for the selected target [74].  
5.8.2.1.3 16- and 20-mer library 
The 16- and 20-mer library is a random peptide library, where the cloning vector carries a LacZα gene 
(coding for β-galactosidase). Therefore, when grown on plates containing 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-β-D-
galactopyranoside (X-gal) and Isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG), these phages will yield blue 
plaques, since β-galactosidase will hydrolyse X-gal, leading to colour formation. On the other hand, 
environmental filamentous phages will only yield colourless plaques, making it easier to isolate the plaques 
containing the specific toxin binders [76]. 
The quality of the library is determined by a titer test, to estimate the size of the library, and PCR using 
M13-96 primer, Extension primer, and random sequencing to test percentage of positive clones containing 
randomized inserts. The amino acid composition is tested to reveal if equal distribution of peptides are 
available in the libraries [75]. 
5.8.2.2 Nanobodies  
There are two different approaches for using nanobody expressing phage libraries. 1) Using naïve libraries, 
or 2) using affinity matures libraries. By displaying naïve nanobodies on the surface of the filamentous 
phages, bio-panning is used for mimicking immune selection of the variable antibody domain [77]. In this 
study, the use of an affinity matured llama VHH phage library (Figure 3) has been used. 
The VHH phage library used for this study was kindly provided from by Richard et. al [29]. Her group 
constructed the library by immunizing a llama with whole N. kaouthia venom, where after leukocytes from 
the llama blood were isolated and RNA extracted. The VHH encoding regions in the RNA was amplified, 
cloned into a pMED1 phagemid vector, and transformed into E. coli TG1 to create the library [29]. The 
quality of llama VHH phage library was estimated based on library size, by titer test, insert ratio, and 
diversity of the VHH sequence by sequencing. The library was estimated to be 5.0 x 109 individual 
transformants with 84 % containing a VHH coding sequences, and were used to isolate high affinity binders 
against α-Cbt  [29].  
Phagemid vectors are hybrids of phage and plasmid vectors carrying an origin of replication for both M13 
phage and E. coli, g3p, appropriate multiple cloning sites, and an antibiotic-resistance gene, but lack all 
other structural and non-structural gene products to create a complete phage due to lack of space in their 
genome [68, 71]. Thus, this special nanobody expressing M13 phage needs helper phages to assist in 
expression of standard phage features needed for a successful infection of E. coil. A few limitations, such as

[Skriv tekst]

gene deletion and plasmid instability, present themselves when using helper phages [68]. Here, the KM13 
helper phage was used.  
In this study, the SNT-I and other 3FTxs were used as targets for selecting binders from the llama VHH phage 
library constructed against whole N. kaouthia venom known to contain 3FTx. One, is the α-Cbt which has a 
highly conserved structure similar to the structure of SNT-I I from D. polylepis venom (Figure 6a). 
Additionally, sequence alignment using Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) resulted in an alignment

6 Cross-reactivity, a bioinformatics analysis  
An interesting approach to improve the antivenoms is to find cross-reactive components that can neutralize 
several different types of toxins from either, the same species, genus or even family. As mentioned in 
section 5.6, the six most important toxins in D. polylepis venom to be neutralized were identified [6]. The 
toxins are listed in Table 4. To obtain cross-reactivity of the antivenom, the toxins in venom must have 
conserved sequences. Using bioinformatics tools, an assessment of the feasibility of creating cross-reactive 
toxin inhibitors against these D. polylepis toxins was investigated. First, an alignment of the six toxins 
sequences was performed using BLAST. Then, conserved sequences, consisting of five to eleven amino 
acids (from discussion with supervisors), were identified. The conserved sequences were BLASTed against 
all known toxins from snakes. Last, the conserved sequences were BLASTed against the human proteome to 
investigate the chance of a toxin inhibitor targeting the given conserved sequences also binding to a native 
human protein, which could lead to adverse effects.

6.1 Cross-reactivity between toxin families 
To extend cross-reactivity of a potential antitoxin against the medically relevant toxins, the conserved 
sequences were BLASTed to investigate their conservation within and outside their toxin families, the 
results are summarized in Figure 7f and c. A Clustal W2 BLAST, based on the identified conserved sequences 
within the KUN family toxins, with a 90% sequence identity cut-off was performed (Figure 7c). A cut off at 
90% was used since no sequences were identified at a cut-off at 100%, properly due to the length of the 
sequnce. According to the Clustal W2 algorithm, the green and orange conserved sequence were too short 
to BLAST. The blue conserved sequence was conserved in five toxins, which all belong to the KUN family. 4 
out of 5 these were dendrotoxins, including Dendrotoxin-K and α-Dendrotoxin from the venom of D. 
polylepis and D. angusticeps, respectively (Appendix table 2). Dendrotoxins are unique components only 
found in the genus Dendroaspis and are very abundant in Dendroaspis venoms [6, 48]. Thus, the blue 
conserved sequence could potentially be an interesting target sequence to identify high affinity binders 
against in order to find one single inhibitor that could cross-neutralize all dendrotoxins.  
A Clustal W2 BLAST based on the conserved sequences identified in the 3FTx family toxins was performed 
with a 100% sequence identity cut-off. The BLAST identified 18 proteins that have the cyan sequence 
conserved (Figure 7f). All these proteins originate from the Elapidae family, and 50% of them from the 
Dendroaspis genus including all its four members. In more detail, 67% of the conserved sequences were 
found in long neurotoxins belonging to the 3FTx family (Figure 7f). Long neurotoxin-I from N. nivea (Cape 
cobra) displayed the highest alignment score of 34.6 (Appendix table 1). N. nivea lives on the African 
Savanna like D. polylepis [1, 4]. Venom from the genera Naja and Dendroaspis include several toxins, which 
belong to the 3FTx family [6, 78]. Although the toxins belong to the same family, diversity in the toxins exist 
[78]. Thus, makes this sequence an interesting target sequence for toxin inhibitors due to the potential 
possibility of targeting lethal toxins from snakes from different genera. 
21 proteins have the yellow sequence conserved. 95% of these proteins were toxins from the Elapidae 
family and 33% from the Dendroaspis genus (Figure 7f). One 3FTx that includes the conserved sequence 
came from the venom of S. catenatus (Appendix table 2). S. catenatus belongs to the Vipertidae family and 
lives in North America [1]. It is interesting that the sequence is conserved in another snake family [78]. 
Another encouraging outcome of the BLAST was that the yellow sequence is represented in different types 
of 3FTx, including long and short neurotoxin, muscarinic, and some uncharacterized proteins. This indicates 
a relevance of yellow sequence as a pan-specific sequence that could be targeted by novel toxin inhibitors.  
 
6.2 Cross-reactivity within human protein repertoire 
As a last investigation, the conserved sequences were BLASTed against the human proteome. This was 
done to ensure that the sequences were not conserved in any human protein and would not be targeted by 
a potential toxin inhibitor, which could lead to adverse reactions. The results of the BLASTs are in Appendix 
B. 
The conserved sequences of the 3FTx family (cyan and yellow) were present in some human proteins with a 
sequence similarity below 88%. The cyan sequence was conserved in proteins belonging to the zinc finger 
protein family. Zinc finger proteins function in intracellular DNA regulation [79], and even though the 
sequence identity is high this would most likely not be affected by an antibody, which cannot by itself cross 
the membrane to the intracellular environment. On the contrary, the yellow sequence is conserved in 
human extracellular secreted phospholipase A2 inhibitors [80] and transmembrane bone morphogenetic 
protein receptors. These types of proteins might thus be affected by sequence-specific toxin inhibitors, but 
==PAGE:23==
[Skriv tekst] 
 
23 
 
further investigation of the effectors antitoxin binding to the human protein is needed. For now and based 
on these finding, the yellow sequence may not be as relevant as the cyan sequence for target by antitoxin. 
The blue sequence present in the dendrotoxins was also found in some human proteins with sequence 
similarities below 88%. The sequence was mainly conserved in the two proteins, Collagen alpha-1 (VII) 
chain protein and Coiled-coil domain-containing protein 125. Collagen alpha-1 (VII) chain protein is a 
building block in collagen, which is a major part of the extracellular matrix [81]. Since, Collagen is very 
abundant in human due to its function in the extracellular matrix it might not be significantly affected by a 
potential cross-reacting antitoxin. Based on these finding, further investigation of the effect is needed in 
order to evaluate the potentials of the blue sequence.  
 
To investigate the possibilities of developing an antivenom capable of cross-neutralising toxins from 
different snakes genera and families, different bioinformatics tool were used. Based on the analyses, the 
cyan sequence is the only conserved epitope from the 3FTx family that did not cross react with extracellular 
human proteins. Furthermore, the sequence was conserved in 18 different proteins from D. polylepis and 
other, far-related Elapidae snakes, making it a potentially interesting sequence to target for toxin inhibitors.

7.1.1.2 Coating 
Wells on a MaxiSorp plate were washed 3 times with 0.05 % Tween® 20 in PBS, where after 1 µg toxin in 
100 µL PBS was added to each well. Control wells were coated with 100 µL PBS. The coated wells were 
incubated for 1 hour at room temperature (RT) on a plate shaker followed by overnight incubation at 4oC. 
7.1.1.3 Superinfection with helper phages 
Prior to panning, the VHH-phage library was amplified and precipitated as described in the following. The 
library was grown in 200 mL of 2x yeast extract tryptone (2xYT) medium supplemented with 100 µg/mL 
ampicillin and 1% glucose, and incubated at 37oC, while shaking, until an OD600 of 0.4 to 0.6 was reached. 
The culture was superinfected with 1010 helper phage KM13 (1.2x1011 plaques forming units (pfu)/10 mL) at 
30oC for 30 min, without shaking. The E. coli TG1s were harvest by centrifugation for 10 min at 10,000 rpm 
(Sorvall® EvolutionTMRC Superspeed Centrifuge) to leave the E. coli TG1 in the pellet. The pellet was re-
suspended in 100 mL 2xYT medium containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin, 50 µg/mL kanamycin, and 0.1% 
glucose, and incubated overnight at 30oC, while shaking.  The next day, the phages were harvest as 
described in 7.1.1.5. 
7.1.1.4 Panning 
Coated wells and control wells (7.1.1.2) were blocked with 100 µL blocking buffer for 2 hours at RT on a 
plate shaker. In experiments with 7-mer, C7C-mer, 12-mer, and VHH libraries, the wells were blocked with 
4% skim milk in PBS or 4% BSA in PBS. In experiments with 16-mer and 20-mer libraries the wells were 
blocked with blocking buffer containing: 0.1 M NaHCO3 pH 8.6, 0.02% NaN3, 0.1 μg/mL streptavidin, and 5 
mg/mL BSA. 
For panning round 1, 10 µL stock solution of the phage libraries 7-mer, C7C-mer, 12-mer, 16-mer, or 20-mer 
was mixed with 100 µL blocking buffer. For the VHH phage library, 50 µL of the precipitated phages from 
7.1.1.3 were mixed with blocking buffer. For panning rounds 2-5, the added phages were the precipitated 
phages from the previous panning round. 
To remove plastic binders, 100 µL of these phage library solutions were transferred to non-coated wells in a 
MaxiSorp plate and incubated for 1 hour at RT on a plate shaker.  
After blocking, the wells were washed ten times with 200 µL 0.05 % Tween® 20 in PBS. Then, 100 µL of 
plastic-pre-incubated phages were transferred to the coated wells and incubated for 1 hour at RT on a plate 
shaker. Next, unbound phages were removed by washing 10 times with 0.05 % Tween® 20 in PBS. The 
bound phages were eluted by addition of 200 µL 0.1 M HCl-glycine for 10 min and neutralized with 200 µL 
1M Tris-Base. The eluted phages were transferred to 500 µL exponentially growing E. coli TG1 in 2xYT 
medium and incubated for 20 min at 37oC. For phages from peptide libraries (not the VHH library), this 
solution was then added to 10-100 mL 2xYT medium and incubated at 37oC overnight. The phages present 
in this solution are referred to as Eluted phages. 
 
The eluted VHH phages were added to 500 µL exponentially growing E. coli TG1 in 2xYT medium and 
incubated for 20 min at 37oC. Then, they were spread on a LB agar plate containing 100 μg/mL of ampicillin 
and 0.1% glucose and incubated overnight at 37OC. The next day, monoclonal plaques were scraped of the 
plate and re-suspended in 100 mL 2xYT medium supplemented with 100 μg/mL ampicillin + 1% glucose and 
incubated at 37oC, until an OD600 at 0.4-0.6 was reached. Then, the phages were superinfected as described 
in 7.1.1.3.

9 Discussion  
9.1 Identified toxin binders  
In the present study, three binders against the fraction 7, know to contain Dpp2c and Dtx-I, were identified 
using phage display screening. Two binders were found in a VHH phage library and one in a 16- and 20-mer 
peptide library (Figure 9). 
Prior to this study, venomics studies were performed. These identified the medically most relevant toxins in 
D. polylepis [6], which were the basis for this study. Furthermore, an analysis specifying the ideal antivenom 
treatment against D. polylepis caused envenoming points towards that affinity maturated VHH antibody 
fragments and peptides could constitute relevant molecule types to be used in future antivenoms (section 
5.5). Last, a bioinformatics analysis of the medically relevant toxins from D. polylepis revealed that 
conserved sequences are present within the dendrotoxins and 3FTx, and might be targets for cross-
neutralizing antivenom. All three binders were sent for DNA sequencing, but no DNA sequences could be 
obtained. If the sequences were obtained, an alignment would have been performed in order to identify 
similarities both between the two VHH binders and the peptide. Furthermore it would have been 
interesting to align med VHH binders against the VHH binders selected against α-Cbt, by Richard et al, since 
she used the same library to select binders against α-Cbt. 
9.2 Cross-reactivity 
A cross-reactivity study of Nanobody-7 and -8 was performed and a significantly greater binding was 
observed against the fraction containing Dpp2c and Dtx-I than against SNT-I, and α-Cbt (Figure 11). This 
indicates that the two VHH binders do not cross-react with the coated toxins, although these also all belong 
to the 3FTx family.  
The VHH phage library was constructed by immunizing a llama with crude N. kaouthia venom followed by 
isolation of its blood to isolate VHH antibody fragments. The library was composed of 5.0 x 109 clones with 
85% containing a VHH coding sequence, from which high affinity α-Cbt binders were detected [82]. Even 
though, α-Cbt and Dpp2c are both related 3FTxs, neither binding of Nanobody-7 nor -8 to α-Cbt were 
detected.  The two VHH might instead be more reactive to other 3FTxs in the N. kaouthia venom, which also 
includes other 3FTx, such as muscarinic toxins, and α-elapitoxins [78]. The cross-reactivity test indicates 
that it is difficult to find cross-reactivity binders even against quite similar toxins belonging to the same 
toxin family. This is supported by the bioinformatics analysis of the medically relevant toxins belonging to 
3FTxs (section 6.1). The 3FTxs isolated from D. polylepis, all have a conserved structural motif, but a 
sequence identity of only 32%.  
The lack of cross-reactivity indicates that high diversity within just a few toxin families might be a general 
theme in many snake families [86]. This has also been observed between venom of the genera Naja  and 
Dendroaspis, which both belong to the family of Elapidae [6, 78]. The variation in venom toxins at the 
genus, subfamily, population and individual level is an evolutionary advantage for the snake. This enables 
the snake to adapt to different ecological niches in order to find food [86] and makes it harder for prey 
evolve resistance to some toxins.  
Due to the toxin variation, the importance of identifying antivenom components with cross-neutralizing 
properties is of huge interest. In order to obtain cross-reactivity, similar binding motifs need to be present 
among venom toxins. Despite, the low sequence identity between the medically relevant toxins (section 
6.1), short conserved sequences were identified both within the 3FTxs and dendrotoxins. The sequences 
were conserved not only within D. polylepis, but also within the Dendroaspis genus and Elapidae family. 
==PAGE:33==
[Skriv tekst]

Potentially, it might be possible to find antitoxin components with cross-neutralizing effect across toxins 
from different snake genera.  
The manufacturers of conventional antivenom try to account for the diversification by immunizing the 
horse with snake venom from different geographical areas, age groups, genera, and subfamilies [86], as 
already discussed in section 5.5.  
Nanobody-7 and -8 have been selected against a fraction containing both Dpp2c and Dtx-I. Even though, 
Dtx-I was present during panning, it would be unexpected that the two binders are selected against Dtx-I. 
Dendrotoxins are uniquely found in the Dendroaspis genus and are thus not present in N. kaouthia venom. 
The bioinformatics analysis (section 6), revealed that dendrotoxins have a conserved structure and a 
sequence identity of 50%. The short conserved sequences (green, orange and blue) identified in the 
dendrotoxins are conserved among very few toxins, further explaining why VHH binders could not be 
selected against Dtx-I (Figure 7).  
Dendrotoxins are structurally homologous to KUN proteins present in many different animals [53], most 
likely also llamas. By being present in llamas means that no antibodies or fragments bind to KUN 
homologous, since this would result in autoimmune diseases.  
9.3 Next step, characterizing the binders 
More binders could potentially be identified if more monoclonal peptide and VHH phage binders are 
isolated both against Dpp2c, Dtx-I and SNT-I, as mentioned in section 8.  Characterization of the three 
obtained binders will for a start include (in order of priority): 
1. In vitro inhibitory effect 
2. Characterization of interaction  
3. Binder optimization  
4. In vivo inhibitory effect 
In vitro inhibitory characterization of the three binders will be performed in order to investigate toxin 
affinity, selectivity, and their capability of inhibiting the effect of the toxin. One setup for measuring in vitro 
potency could be an assay with skeletal muscular cells expressing nAChR, which will compete with the 
isolated binders in interacting with the toxin. Another method is to detect the inhibitory effect using two-
electrode voltage clamp technique of Xenopus oocytes, which measures the difference in membrane 
potential of Xenopus oocytes expressing the nAChR, to detect changes in membrane transporter protein 
activity and thereby the inhibitory effect of the binders [87]. 
 
Characterization of the interaction between the toxin and the identified binders would be performed to 
obtain knowledge of the inhibitory mechanism. For all three binders, the interaction could be studied using 
surface plasmon resonance by BIAcore as a comprehensive, label-free analysis, to characterize 
biomolecular interactions in real-time, determine structure function and binding kinetics. Furthermore, in 
order to characterize the toxin-inhibitor complex, H/D-exchange mass spectrometry could be applied. The 
method predicts the solvent accessibility of the inhibitor, and tertiary structure of the protein complex and 
binding interfaces.  
For the Peptide-8, the investigation of amino acids responsible for the interaction should be localized by 
constructing truncated versions of the peptide and testing their binding affinity. The knowledge of the 
responsible amino acids could be used to affinity maturate the complementary determining region (CDR) in

[Skriv tekst]

antibodies to increase their affinity towards the toxin, e.g. by grafting the core binding sequence to the 
antibody CDR. 
 
Last, the three binders might be tested for their ability to neutralize whole venom and relevant venom 
components in vivo. This could be performed by injecting a mixture of a fixed amount of venom/toxin and 
different concentrations of binder into mice, to observe the effect. In the cases where the binders show 
ability to neutralize D. polylepis venom/toxin, the Median Effective Dose (ED50, in milligram venom 
neutralized per mL antivenom) of the antivenom could be obtained. 
It would be interesting to compare the ED50 of the binders and conventional antivenom against D. polylepis, 
in order to see the effect of neutralizing the most medically relevant venom components. Furthermore this 
would also provide information of the binders’ ability to eliminated specific symptoms of the D. polyelpis 
caused envenoming.  The elimination of specific symptoms could also be studied in an ELISA, by measuring 
the binders’ and antivenom’s response to all D. polylepis venom component. The comparison would reveal 
which toxins are responsible for the specific symptoms.  
 
 
9.4 Converting the binders into drugs 
The selected binders have potential of becoming antivenom components if in vitro and in vivo tests are 
positive. To convert these into good drug candidates, improvements of pharmacokinetic properties must be 
considered. Treatment of a medical disorder is a balance of matching the target (e.g. toxin or receptor), 
antibody/peptide and the patient [11]. Suggestions on improvement of the VHH and peptide selected 
binders are discussed below.  
9.4.1 VHH stabilization  
As discussed in section 5.4, VHH fragments have very low serum half-life, and using VHHs as the only reagent 
in an antivenom preparation would likely be limited by their rapid clearance from the body before the 
venom toxins reach circulation [29, 30].  
To prolong the effect of VHH based antivenom, two approached are suggested, fusion of the VHH to a Fc-
region from antibody IgG or administration of VHH together with IgG.  
One theory, not yet tested, is to develop an antivenom mixture of low (VHH) and high (IgG) molecular 
weight antibody reagents. This will ensure high efficacy of the antivenom by having high concentration of 
IgG and their fragments, lower risk of adverse effects due to lower loading volume of IgG, and prolonged 
effect after VHH clearance from the blood mediated by IgG [82]. 
Another approach is to fuse an Fc-region to the VHH binder to increase half-life. In IgGs, the Fc-region is a 
region of the heavy chain that couples to the variable fragments, Fab (Figure 3). While the Fab fragments 
mediate specific binding to antigen, the Fc-region is responsible for the varying effector mechanism, 
including activation of immune responding cells and complement activation [11]. The effector mechanism is 
mediated by the Fc-region binding to neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn). The FcRn interaction with Fc-region plays 
a key role in IgG homeostasis and is largely responsible for its prolonged half-life by rescuing IgG from being 
eliminated [25]. The fusion of proteins and other antibody fragments to Fc-regions has been shown to 
improve half-life of antibody fragments, while maintaining binding affinity and low immunogenicity [11]. 
This was performed by Richard et al. [82] with specific α-Cbt VHH binders isolated from the VHH phage 
library used in this study. A promising binder against α-Cbt was fused to human IgG Fc-region, and this VHH-
==PAGE:35==
[Skriv tekst] 
 
35 
 
Fc antibody had high affinity towards α-Cbt and was equally capable of neutralising α-Cbt comparable to 
the VHH binder alone.  
Coupling of Fc-region to antibody fragments is a promising approach in order to stabilize the serum half-life 
of the VHH selected binders, Nanobody-7 and -8. The prolonged half-life would also contribute to a reduced 
dosage of antivenom, thereby reducing the chance of side effects. Additionally, by fusing the binders to 
chimeric or humanized versions of rodent antibodies, the potential immunogenicity could be reduced [11]. 
9.4.2  Peptide grafting or direct injection  
If the binder, Peptide-8, exerts inhibitory effect, several possibilities exist to develop the peptide into an 
antivenom component. If Peptide-8 has high biostability, the peptide can be used as a direct antivenom 
component. If the biostability is low, peptidomimetics can be designed using peptide-8 as template, e.g. by 
applying amine alkylations chain substitutions within the amino acid backbone in order to stabilize the 
peptide biostability in the blood circulation, while still maintaining the inhibitory effect [88].  
Another approach is to graft the peptide onto the CDR of a humanized antibody. CDR grafting is a 
commonly used method and would increase the efficacy of the antibody, while keeping the risk of side 
effects in the patient low [89].  
 
9.5 False positive  
Throughout these studies, unspecific binding was observed for the toxin selected phage libraries. One 
example is in the polyclonal phage library from panning round 3, in the selection of fraction 7 VHH 
displaying phage binders (Figure 9a). The phenomenon has been observed throughout all experiments 
performed, and different attempts were tried in order to understand and reduce these false positive results 
(Table 10 and Appendix I).  
Through this study, the procedure for panning and ELISA were modified on the basis of the experiments 
listed in Table 6. For panning and ELISA, MaxiSorp plates were used. As blocker, skim milk in PBS was used 
for panning and BSA in PBS was used for ELISA, pre-plastic incubation was applied to panning rounds and 
ELISA to reduce the risk of isolating plastic binders during the panning rounds. As the reason for the high 
unspecific binding might be a result of overloading of phage library binders. This might result in saturation 
of the wells and no difference between the Test library and Control library can be observed. As a 
consequence, prior to the last ELISA performed during this study, a dilution series of phage panning library 
was performed in order to determine a suitable loading concentration.  
Future approaches to improve the Phage display and ELISA experiments are listed below: 
 
“Blocker swapping”, change blocking buffer between each round of panning, to reduce the risk of 
selecting blocking buffer binders.  
 
Try immobilizing toxin on beads. To further avoid the risk of isolating plastic binders [76]. 
 
Until now during panning, phages are eluted with either an acidic or basic solution. Since, nothing is 
known about the properties the interacting amino acids of the peptides or VHH fragments, there 
might be a possibility that not all binders are eluted by either acidic or basic solution. It could be 
interesting elute the selected phages first the acidic solution then the basic solution, to evaluate if 
the outcome is higher.

10 Conclusion  
With its fast movement, short-tempered behaviour and highly potent venom D. polylepis is feared and 
categorized as a dangerous snake by WHO in Africa. Its venom is mainly composed of highly potent 
neurotoxins, with the medically most relevant toxins being the 3FTxs, SNT-I and Dpp2c and the 
dendrotoxin, Dtx-I 
D. polylepis caused envenoming is today treated with antivenom prepared from polyvalent snake venom 
immunized animal antiserum, shown to be effective in the neutralization of lethality. Due to the animal-
based production method, the antivenoms suffer from several drawbacks including low efficacy against 
medically relevant toxins in the venom, high immunogenicity and batch-to batch variation. An analysis was 
performed, which specified that the ideal antivenom, treatment against D. polylepis caused envenoming, 
==PAGE:37==
[Skriv tekst] 
 
37 
 
points towards of using affinity maturated VHH antibody fragments and peptides as relevant molecule types 
for future antivenoms. Based on the medically relevant toxins and the ideal antivenom analysis, this project 
has contributed to improve D. polylepis antivenoms, by targeting snake venom using DNA encoded 
libraries.  
Phage display using six different M13 phage display libraries, one VHH phage library and five peptide 
libraries, against medically relevant toxins was used as a screening technique in order to identify binders.  
Three binders against the fraction 7, know to contain Dpp2c and Dtx-I, were identified, two within the VHH 
phage library and one within the 16- and 20-mer peptide library. The ability of the two VHH binders to 
cross-react with the 3FTxs, SNT-I and α-Cbt, revealed no significantly greater binding compared to the 
binding to Dpp2c and Dtx-I. No sequences were obtained from the DNA sequencing.  
Further characterization of the binders can include in vitro and in vivo studies and characterization of the 
interaction with the toxin. Last optimization of the binders is needed in order to convert them into 
antivenom drugs.  
Last, a bioinformatics analysis of the medically relevant toxins from D. polylepis venom revealed that 
conserved sequences are present within the dendrotoxins and 3FTxs, and might be targets for cross-
neutralizing antivenom.
