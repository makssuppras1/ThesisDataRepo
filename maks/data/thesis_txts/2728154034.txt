Abstract 
The surge in global meat production has generated substantial amounts of slaughterhouse 
wastewater, necessitating innovative and sustainable treatment approaches. Traditional methods 
have limitations in dealing with the complex composition and potential pathogens present in this 
wastewater. In response, microalgae-based treatment systems have emerged as promising 
alternatives, offering the potential to tackle environmental pollution while yielding valuable biomass. 
Chlorella sorokiniana, recognized for its robust growth and versatile nature, holds promise for 
wastewater treatment due to its efficient nutrient uptake capabilities. 
However, a crucial aspect that remains understudied is the role of specific nutrient deficiencies, 
especially phosphorus, in influencing microalgal growth within different wastewater compositions. 
This study seeks to fill this knowledge gap by investigating how phosphorus deficiency affects the 
growth of C. sorokiniana in post-belt slaughterhouse wastewater.  
We investigated the suboptimal growth of C. sorokiniana in wastewater, attributing it to phosphorus 
deficiency. Experimental batches involved two main conditions: (i) Cultivation without supplemental 
phosphorus, and (ii) Cultivation with added phosphorus (sodium phosphate dibasic) in autoclaved 
wastewater. A control group provided a baseline for comparison in the second condition. 
Furthermore, we looked into microscopic analysis to observe the impact of other microorganisms on 
the microalgal growth. 
Our study reveals that despite the increase in phosphorus content due to supplemental addition, 
there were no notable enhancements in Chlorella sorokiniana's growth or nutrient removal 
performance. Intriguingly, filtration processes may inadvertently strip away essential nutrients, 
disrupting the symbiotic harmony between beneficial bacteria and Chlorella sorokiniana. This 
disruption potentially explains the limited microalgal biomass growth observed in filtered batches, 
underscoring the pivotal role of microorganism interactions in cultivating optimal growth conditions

1 Introduction 
Growing global demand for meat has resulted in a significant increase in meat production, resulting 
in large volumes of slaughterhouse wastewater. This wastewater, with its complex composition and 
pathogenic risks, necessitates a long-term treatment strategy. Traditional methods fall short of 
meeting the challenges posed by slaughterhouse wastewater. Innovative approaches, such as 
microalgae-based treatment systems, provide a long-term solution by efficiently removing pollutants 
and mitigating environmental impacts, protecting public health and promoting a more resource-
efficient meat industry. (Danaee et al., 2019) 
Traditional wastewater treatment uses aerobic/anaerobic processes to convert contaminants into 
inert compounds, making the water safe for reuse or disposal. However, these processes consume a 
significant amount of energy, cause nutrient loss, and contribute to negative environmental impacts 
via greenhouse gas emissions. (Speece, 1983)  Microalgae-based wastewater treatment has gained 
significant attention in recent years due to its potential to simultaneously address environmental 
pollution and biomass production. Microalgae are capable of assimilating nutrients, including 
nitrogen and phosphorus, from wastewater and converting them into valuable biomass, thereby 
aiding in nutrient recovery and pollutant removal. (Cai et al., 2013) This symbiotic relationship 
between microalgae and wastewater treatment holds promise for sustainable and eco-friendly 
solutions. Among various microalgae species, Chlorella sorokiniana stands out as a potential 
candidate for wastewater treatment due to its robust growth characteristics and ability to thrive in 
diverse environments (Eladel et al., 2019). This unicellular green alga possesses a high capacity for 
nutrient uptake, making it a suitable candidate for nutrient removal from wastewater streams (S.S & 
Girisha, 2019).The utilization of C. sorokiniana in treating wastewater aligns with the broader goal of 
harnessing nature's inherent mechanisms to mitigate anthropogenic pollution. 
While the potential of microalgae, particularly C. sorokiniana, in wastewater treatment is recognized, 
there exists a notable research gap concerning the influence of specific nutrient deficiencies on 
microalgal growth in different wastewater types (Guldhe et al., 2017a).  Notably, phosphorus, a key 
nutrient, plays a critical role in microalgal metabolism and growth (Yaakob et al., 2021a). Microalgal 
cells require phosphorus for vital functions like creating cell components such as phospholipids, DNA, 
RNA, and ATP. These are important for processes like energy transfer and making nucleic acids (Atiku 
et al., 2016). Microalgae can absorb phosphorus as polyphosphate or orthophosphate, which boosts 
their growth and the nutritional value within their cells (Yaakob et al., 2021). 
Slaughterhouse wastewater (SWW) is known for having elevated levels of phosphorus (P) and is 
recognized as a primary industrial pollutant leading to eutrophication (Sun et al., 2021). However, in 
this study, the water sample has undergone belt filtration, which might be a contributing factor to a 
decrease in total phosphorus content. This deficiency could potentially limit the growth of 
microalgae, thereby impacting their efficiency in wastewater treatment. 
In this study, we address this research gap by investigating the growth of C. sorokiniana in post-belt 
slaughterhouse wastewater, characterized by low phosphorus levels. We hypothesize that the 
observed suboptimal growth of C. sorokiniana in such wastewater could be attributed to phosphorus 
deficiency. To test this hypothesis, we conducted a series of batch cultivations under two main 
experimental conditions: (i) Batch cultivation of C. sorokiniana in autoclaved wastewater without a 
supplemental phosphorus source, and (ii) Batch cultivation of C. sorokiniana in autoclaved 
wastewater with a supplemented phosphorus source using sodium phosphate dibasic. A control 
group was maintained to provide a baseline for comparison for the second experimental condition.  
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
To elucidate the impact of phosphorus availability on microalgal growth, we monitored various 
growth parameters, such as cell density and nutrient uptake, throughout the cultivation period. By 
contrasting the growth of C. sorokiniana under these distinct conditions, we aimed to provide 
insights into the relationship between phosphorus availability and microalgal performance in 
wastewater treatment scenarios. By comprehensively assessing the potential of C. sorokiniana in 
nutrient removal and microalgal growth, under varying phosphorus conditions, we contribute to the 
optimization of microalgae-based wastewater treatment strategies. Finally, we also monitored the 
presence and impact of bacteria in the batches to understand the impact of bacteria- microalgal 
symbiosis in the microalgal growth.  
In summary, this research endeavors to bridge the existing knowledge gap regarding the influence of 
phosphorus deficiency on C. sorokiniana growth in post-belt slaughterhouse wastewater. Through 
systematic experimentation and analysis, we seek to enhance our understanding of microalgal 
behavior in nutrient-limiting conditions and its implications for sustainable wastewater treatment. 
The outcomes of this study hold significance for both environmental remediation and biomass 
production, aligning with the growing imperative to develop innovative and effective solutions for 
wastewater management. 
2 Literature Review: Chlorella sorokiniana in industry 
2.1 Role of Microalgae in Wastewater Treatment 
In the world of wastewater treatment, an exciting innovation has taken center stage: the Microalgae-
based wastewater treatment (MBWT) process. This approach is gaining significant attention due to 
its potential to significantly improve both advanced treatment and nutrient recovery from 
wastewater. Researchers have been studying microalgae, particularly types like Chlorella and 
Scenedesmus, to see how they can enhance the last stages of wastewater treatment. Their success 
has demonstrated that these tiny organisms are highly effective in rapidly removing nutrients during 
the advanced treatment of different types of wastewater, including from cities, farms, and industries. 
Numerous studies by researchers ((K. Li et al., 2019)Cai et al., 2013, Craggs et al., 2003, De Godos et 
al., 2009, Gikonyo, 2013, Johansen, 2012) have collectively highlighted the remarkable impact of 
microalgae in this crucial field. 
This exploration of microalgae's potential started back in the 1950s and has led to a fascinating 
journey. Initially limited to industrial and commercial applications, its scope quickly expanded to 
encompass wastewater treatment. A pivotal discovery by Oswald et al. in 1957 unveiled a unique 
partnership between microalgae and bacteria. This relationship not only protected microalgae from 
harmful compounds present in wastewater but also boosted the removal of dangerous pollutants. 
This collaboration saw microalgae using sunlight to create oxygen, which the bacteria used to break 
down carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus. The byproducts of the bacterial process, including CO2, 
inorganic nitrogen, and phosphorus, became nourishment for microalgae's continued growth 
(Oswald et al. 1957). 
Originally, microalgae were introduced to wastewater treatment to reduce excess nutrients and 
prevent water bodies from becoming overly rich in nutrients (eutrophication), as observed by Oswald 
and Golueke in 1960. However, this story took an unexpected turn. Researchers realized that 
cultivating microalgae in wastewater offered multiple benefits. This biomass became a valuable 
resource, serving as a versatile feedstock for various applications within biorefinery systems and 
other innovative uses. Researchers highlighted this new dimension, presenting microalgae as not 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
only agents of purification but also catalysts for efficient resource utilization. (Chinnasamy et al., 
2010a, Wang et al., 2010b, Zhou et al., 2011, Zhou et al., 2012c) 
This pioneering journey ushers in a new era where microalgae thrive within wastewater, 
transcending traditional boundaries. This dynamic approach offers several advantages that extend 
beyond conventional methods. Firstly, microalgae's efficient pollutant assimilation reduces 
wastewater treatment costs. Secondly, their exceptional nutrient removal capabilities exceed even 
stringent regulatory standards. Thirdly, an exciting potential emerges: microalgae could become a 
source of carbon-neutral fuel, using inorganic carbon from fossil fuel byproducts or wastewater 
treatment plants. Additionally, microalgae's resilience allows them to thrive with minimal extra water 
or nutrients, potentially reducing production costs and the environmental impact of traditional 
fertilizers. Lastly, harvested microalgae, once considered waste, can be transformed into valuable 
products such as biogas, biofuels, fertilizers, and animal feed, showcasing the remarkable versatility 
of microalgae as nature's engineers. (Raheem et al., 2015, Xin et al., 2010, Zamalloa et al., 2011, Zhu 
et al., 2013) 
In summary, the story of microalgae's involvement in wastewater treatment unfolds like an intricate 
artwork, merging innovation, collaboration, and efficient resource use. Starting from its modest 
beginnings as an undiscovered possibility, microalgae have now evolved into a powerful catalyst for 
change. They offer a route towards environmentally friendly and effective strategies for wastewater 
treatment. As this narrative continues to evolve, it prompts us to imagine a future where wastewater 
isn't just a problem but a potential source of positive outcomes, driven by the impressive abilities of 
microalgae. 
However, there are several obstacles that must be addressed to ensure the economic and 
environmental viability of this approach. Such as:  The specific challenges differ based on the 
characteristics of the wastewater and the type of microalgae being used. These issues need to be 
handled on a case-by-case basis. There are a few ways to overcome the challenges based on its 
requirement. One approach is to add more nutrients to increase productivity. Another method 
involves mixing highly concentrated wastewater with less harmful water or low-nutrient wastewater 
to make it less toxic. Additionally, an initial treatment can be used to reduce the presence of 
unwanted microbes or larger organisms in the wastewater. Lastly, selecting particular strains of 
microalgae can also help tackle these issues (Guldhe et al., 2017b). 
2.2 Impact of Microalgae and Bacteria Symbiotic Consertium 
The synergy between algae and bacteria reveals a mutualistic bond, where algae function as primary 
producers, generating organic compounds from CO2. In this cooperative relationship, heterotrophic 
bacteria act as secondary consumers, breaking down the organic compounds produced by algae. This 
dynamic interplay underpins the potential of these microorganisms in wastewater treatment. (Khoo 
et al., 2021) 
Within the context of wastewater treatment, attached microalgal-bacterial consortia emerge as an 
economically viable and technologically superior option compared to suspended counterparts. This 
approach entails careful consideration of factors such as suitable carriers, bioreactor types, 
operational modes, and key operational factors. The engineering of attached microalgal-bacterial 
consortia holds promise for optimizing their performance in various applications. (Khoo et al., 2021) 
Bacteria play a multifaceted role in this collaboration. They not only foster microalgal growth but also 
contribute to processes such as bio-flocculation and cell wall disruption. These interactions enhance 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
the potential of microalgal biofuel production, showcasing the significance of understanding and 
harnessing algal-bacterial synergy for sustainable resource utilization. (Zhang et al., 2020) 
While the microalgal-bacterial coculture system offers advantages like increased biomass production 
and enhanced nutrient adaptation from wastewater, challenges persist. The intricate web of 
interactions and coexistence within aquatic environments requires further clarification. (Sial et al., 
2021) This intricate interdependence holds immense potential for advancing wastewater treatment 
and resource utilization strategies, while uncovering the intricacies of microalgal-bacterial 
interactions continues to be a compelling avenue for scientific exploration. (Fallahi et al., 2021) 
2.2 Introduction to Chlorella sorokiniana and Wastewater Treatment 
Municipal wastewater originates from various sources within a city, including households. This 
wastewater contains primary contaminants such as organic matter (measured as COD or BOD), 
nitrogen, and phosphorus. Commonly, conventional biological processes are employed for treating 
municipal wastewater. Conversely, over recent years, numerous microalgae have undergone testing 
to assess their viability for thriving in municipal wastewater and their potential to effectively 
eliminate major pollutants (Acién et al., 2016). Among these microalgae, Chlorella sorokiniana (2–4.5 
µm diameter) has displayed a distinctive ability to flourish in heavily polluted environments. 
 
Fig 1. Chlorella sorokiniana strain observed under optic microscope (G x 40) (Source: Chader et al, 
2010) 
 
Chlorella sorokiniana belongs to the Eukaryota domain, specifically categorized within the order 
Chrorallales and the Chlorophyceae family. While predominantly inhabiting freshwater and soil, 
Chlorella species are also found, albeit less frequently, in marine environments. Their adaptability 
extends to a broad temperature range spanning from 15°C to 40°C (WEHR, 2007). Notably, recent 
studies have focused on Chlorella sorokiniana, alongside other microalgae species, in the context of 
wastewater treatment (X. Chen et al., 2018; Gupta et al., 2016; Ramsundar et al., 2017), 
demonstrating their potential for this critical application. 
Chlorella sorokiniana has demonstrated robust proliferation across diverse wastewater samples, 
encompassing sewage treatment plants (STPs), effluent treatment plants (ETPs), fishery water, 
untreated sewage, diluted sewage, swine wastewater, and sludge (C. Y. Chen et al., 2020a; S.S & 
Girisha, 2019b).  
In the context of wastewater treatment and biomass generation, Chlorella sorokiniana displays 
encouraging potential, particularly comparing to synthetic wastewater. It thrives notably well in raw 
domestic wastewater, showcasing robust growth and cost-efficiency during 24-hour light/dark cycles. 
Continuous light exposure improves biomass production and the retrieval of value-added products, 
indicating Chlorella sorokiniana's inclination for autotrophic growth. These findings support Chlorella 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
sorokiniana's economic viability for large-scale cultivation through increased pigment, protein, and 
carbohydrate accumulation. (Rani & Ojha, 2021) 
For the optimal growth of Chlorella sorokiniana in wastewater, two factors: COD and TP were found 
to be significant. C. sorokiniana exhibited accelerated growth in the range of 20–60 mg/L TP 
concentration, while its growth was hindered under TP concentrations below 20 mg/L. Growth rates 
and final OD540 values notably improved with higher COD levels (300 or 500 mg/L), indicating a 
positive correlation between organic matter and Chlorella growth. However, excessive COD (1000 
mg/L) hindered growth due to turbidity and light transmission issues. (Zhang & Chen, 2015a) 
2.3 Nutrient Removal by Chlorella sorokiniana 
The nutrient removal capacity has been studies extensively and it has shown promising results. 
Researches have been conducted on C. sorokiniana in different conditions such as: heterotrophic, 
autotrophic and mixotrophic. Under heterotrophic conditions, Chlorella sorokiniana exhibited growth 
and nitrogen/phosphorus removal rates exceeding twice those of autotrophic conditions, while no 
significant differences were observed under mixotrophic conditions, attributed to reduced light 
availability and limited organic carbon sources. This highlights the potential of heterotrophic 
Chlorella sorokiniana for efficient organic substrate and nutrient removal, beneficial for advanced 
wastewater treatment, potentially resulting in reduced operational costs compared to autotrophic 
counterparts dependent on light. (Kim et al., 2013a)  
Chlorella sorokiniana appears to be a promising candidate for wastewater treatment, as it 
demonstrated robust growth across diverse conditions and exhibited a heightened ability to 
efficiently remove ammonium and phosphate from synthetic wastewater. Gao et al., 2021 discovered 
that Chlorella sorokiniana exhibited removal rates ranging from 38.9% to 60.4% for total nitrogen, 
49.4% to 94.9% for total phosphorus, and 80.0% to 86.6% for chemical oxygen demand when applied 
to frigon wastewater.Alongside the proliferation of microalgal cells, the precipitation of phosphate as 
calcium phosphate also played a role in phosphate removal. However, practical confirmation of this 
system's efficacy in real-world wastewater scenarios necessitates further research. (Liu et al., 2012) 
 
2.4 Heavy Metal and Contaminant Uptake 
The ability of Chlorella sorokiniana to effectively capture heavy metals and harmful substances from 
wastewater highlights its strong performance in treating wastewater. It achieves this through 
processes like adsorption, ion exchange, and accumulating these substances within its cells, showing 
promise in reducing heavy metal pollution. The way Chlorella sorokiniana and metal ions interact is 
influenced by factors such as pH, temperature, the specific form of the metal, and the presence of 
other ions. 
Research findings indicated that Chlorella sorokiniana cells, when immobilized in a calcium-alginate 
matrix, exhibit efficacy as biosorbents for removing Cu2+, Ni2+, and Cd2+ ions, albeit with potentially 
lower efficiency for Ni2+. The biosorption process was notably influenced by the initial 
concentrations of metal ions and the initial pH conditions. Optimal removal rates were observed at 
97%, 51%, and 74% for Cu2+, Ni2+, and Cd2+ ions, respectively. (Petrovič & Simonič, 2016) 
Research also emphasizes Chlorella sorokiniana's resilience in withstanding elevated Cu, Cd, and As 
concentrations, showing minimal growth inhibition. The microalgae responded to metal stress by 
enhancing antioxidant enzyme and amino acid synthesis, including precursors for phytochelatins. 
This metabolic adaptation enables Chlorella sorokiniana to effectively manage high heavy metal 
levels. Notably, its substantial Cd accumulation capacity, removal capability, and robust Cd tolerance 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
position it as a promising candidate for potential heavy metal bioremediation (León-Vaz et al., 2021). 
In a separate investigation, the utilization of C. sorokiniana for Pb(II) mitigation was explored, 
revealing a notable 92% reduction of Pb(II) levels over a 14-day cultivation period (Jaiswal et al., 
2022).Another study proposed a comprehensive approach, combining algae cultivation with 
wastewater treatment, heavy metal remediation, and biocrude oil/hydrochar production. This 
integrated method demonstrates potential for vital metal recovery, waterborne heavy metal 
detoxification, and the simultaneous generation of value-added bioproducts in synergy (H. Li et al., 
2020) 
 
2.5 Biomass Production and Growth Kinetics 
 
Exploring the biomass productivity and growth kinetics of C. sorokiniana, a noteworthy study 
revealed its exceptional adaptability to swine wastewater. Particularly, strain AK-1 excelled, achieving 
the highest biomass concentration (5.45 g/L) and protein productivity (0.27 g/L/d) when cultivated in 
50% strength swine wastewater. Moreover, the application of cell immobilization using sponge as a 
solid carrier proved to be an effective strategy, resulting in further advancements with impressive 
maximal biomass concentration and protein productivity, reaching 8.08 g/L and 0.272 g/L/d, 
respectively (C. Y. Chen et al., 2020b). 
In another study, C. sorokiniana cultivated in dairy wastewater accumulated 31% and 35% lipid in 
preliminary and secondary treated effluent. The outcomes of the GC-MS examination indicated that 
the identified fatty acids are well-suited for the production of premium biodiesel, notably excelling in 
suitability for regions characterized by colder climates. (Asadi et al., 2020) 
The microalgae displayed growth rates of 0.24 d(-1), 0.53 d(-1), and 0.44 d(-1) in autotrophic, 
heterotrophic, and mixotrophic environments, respectively. Significantly elevated growth rates were 
observed for C. sorokiniana when subjected to heterotrophic cultivation (Kim et al., 2013b). In a 
different experimental setup conducted with adequate lighting, the microalgae thrived in 
wastewater, showcasing a maximum specific growth rate (μmax) within the range of 1.37 to 1.46 d−1 
(Fan et al., 2019). 
To sum up, the research on C. sorokiniana's biomass production, growth kinetics, and lipid 
accumulation highlights its promise in efficient wastewater treatment, adaptable biofuel generation, 
and robust growth across diverse environments. 
 
2.6 Economic and Environmental Viability 
To enhance the economic and environmental viability of Chlorella sorokiniana in wastewater 
treatment, it's imperative to address challenges linked to utilizing cost-effective organic substrates 
and preventing culture contamination in industrial settings. Resolving these issues will pave the way 
for scaling up mixotrophic culturing of Chlorella. By integrating microalgal biomass production with 
wastewater treatment, the commercial cultivation of Chlorella species becomes economically more 
feasible. Given the complex and diverse composition of wastewater, it is advisable to rigorously 
screen novel mixotrophic Chlorella species capable of effectively assimilating a wide array of organic 
compounds from waste streams, accumulating valuable molecules (e.g., lipids), and simultaneously 
enduring other contaminants (Dragone, 2022).  This approach holds significant potential for further 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
advancing the practical implementation and environmental soundness of Chlorella-based wastewater 
treatment strategies.  
2.7 Future Directions and Research Gaps 
The trajectory of Chlorella sorokiniana's application in wastewater treatment holds promise in 
several key directions. Optimization of growth conditions is crucial to enhance its nutrient removal 
and pollutant uptake efficiency. Strain selection and genetic enhancement could unveil strains with 
improved capabilities, while integrating Chlorella sorokiniana into existing treatment systems may 
lead to synergistic effects. Scaling up cultivation methods for industrial use and conducting long-term 
stability studies are necessary for large-scale application. 
Significant gaps in our understanding persist with regard to harnessing the full potential of Chlorella 
sorokiniana. Elucidating the precise mechanisms underlying its interactions with various pollutants 
holds the key to optimizing its performance effectively. Moreover, comprehending the complicated 
interplay between the composition of wastewater and Chlorella sorokiniana's efficacy is of 
paramount importance. A comprehensive exploration encompassing economic viability, ecological 
implications, biological dynamics, pollutant toxicity, and alignment with regulatory standards 
constitutes essential stepping stones towards fully realizing Chlorella sorokiniana's prowess in 
industrial wastewater treatment. Filling these gaps in our understanding is crucial for smoothly 
incorporating Chlorella sorokiniana into the structure of sustainable methods for managing industrial 
wastewater treatment. 
3 Materials and methods 
3.1 Algae and Wastewater collection 
The wastewater samples used in this study were collected from Danish Crown, a prominent meat 
processing company specializing in pork and beef production. 
 In this research, the post-belt water sample is slaughterhouse wastewater that has been treated 
with a belt filter press, which is a mechanical dewatering device that efficiently separates water from 
sludge. The efficient separation process within the slot is facilitated by the presence of a polymer, 
which is deposited onto the belt. This polymer enables water to pass through the belt while retaining 
the solids. Prior to reaching the belt, various chemicals, including NaOH for pH adjustment and Fe2Cl, 
are introduced into the water stream. Toward the end of the process, polymer is added to further 
enhance filtration. The filtration is carried out using Filterdugen (filter cloth) with a dimension of 
1000mm and perforations of 400-500 micrometers, ensuring effective removal of particulate matter 
from the wastewater. This integrated approach enables the successful and controlled separation of 
solids and liquids within the filtration system. 
Microalgae and wastewater were selected based on the screening conducted in a previous project 
course (Appendix VII). Three microalgae strains (Haematoccus pluvialis, Chlorella sorokiniana and 
Scenedesmus obliquus) were grown in different wastewater with different dilutions and synthetic 
media (MWC, recipe in appendix I) in 2ml culture plates.  
The Chlorella sorokiniana strain (CCAP 211/8K) was collected from Culture Collection of Algae & 
Protozoa (CCAP). It was grown on MWC media (Guillard and Lorenzen, 1972). The cultures were 
grown in a light intensity of 16000 LUX (285 mol/m2/s) provided by a 4000K color temperature LED 
light. A conversion factor of 14.3 was used to convert the photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD) 
in mol/m2/s. At temperatures ranging from 20 to 24 degrees Celsius, the cultivation cycles consisted 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
of alternating periods of 12 hours of light and 12 hours of darkness. Prior to beginning the 
experiments, these conditions were maintained.

3.2 Batch reactor experiment without additional phosphorus source 
To evaluate the nutrient removal efficiency, microalgal growth and competition between bacteria 
and Chlorella sorokiniana strains in undiluted wastewater, the experiment was scaled up from 2ml 
culture plates to 1L glass bottles. The setup involved the use of 1L Schott Duran Original GL45 
Borosilicate Glass Laboratory Bottles equipped with a multi-port system consisting of a screw cap 
(GLS 80) with four ports (Figure 2). The bottles, screw caps, and tubes were autoclaved at 120°C for 
15 minutes before the experiment. Each glass bottle contained a mixture of 5% (50 ml) Chlorella 
sorokiniana inoculation and 95% (950 ml) undiluted autoclaved post-belt water. Continuous stirring 
was maintained at 460 rpm using magnetic stirrers. Aeration was achieved through one of the ports 
connected to an air pump and a diffuser. Light was provided from the side using an 18W fluorescent 
lamp (GroLux, Silvana) with alternating cycles of 12 hours light and 12 hours dark, at an intensity of 
13000 LUX (299 μmol/m2/s). 
 
Fig 2: Experimental setup for batch reactor experiments 
Bi-daily sampling was conducted from the glass bottles to monitor the growth and conditions of the 
cultures. Measurements included in vivo autofluorescence (IVF), optical density (OD), total 
suspended solids (TSS), dissolved oxygen (O2), and pH levels. Microscopy analysis was conducted to 
monitor bacterial growth and analyze the competition between bacteria and Chlorella sorokiniana 
strains. The experiment consisted of three growth curves in series, with each subsequent batch 
inoculated with microalgae from the previous batch. The first batch lasted for 6 days, while the 
second and third batches were run for 12 days each. The microalgae was reinoculated from the 
previous batch in the last two batches.  
3.3 Batch reactor experiment with additional phosphorus source 
Following three consecutive batches and subsequent analysis of total nitrogen (TN), total phosphorus 
(TP), and skalar, indications of potential phosphorus deficiency in the culture medium were 
observed. To further investigate this hypothesis, an additional batch experiment was conducted, 
wherein an external phosphorus supplement (sodium phosphate dibasic) was introduced at a

To ensure optimal conditions for the microalgae, the wastewater underwent centrifugation (2500 
rpm for 5 minutes) to remove any debris, followed by filtration through Econfltr Nyln 13mm 0.3 μm

performance in wastewater treatment. The remaining conditions for this batch remained consistent 
with those of the preceding three batches, and identical analytical procedures were applied as 
before. 
 
3.4 Analytical Procedures 
The soluble phase of the wastewater was analyzed using HACH LCK cuvette test kits to determine 
various parameters including Soluble COD, N-NH4+, N-NO3-, N-NO2-, and P-PO43-. Samples were 
filtered using a 0.45 μm pore membrane (Whatman glass microfiber filters, Grade GF/A). To measure 
Total Suspended Solids (TSS) in the project, Econfltr Nyln 13mm 0.3 μm filters from Agilent 
Technologies were used. These filters were pre-dried and pre-weighed. A 10ml portion of each water 
sample was filtered and then dried in an oven at 105°C for over an hour. After complete drying, the 
filter and solids were re-weighed. The TSS was calculated using the formula: TSS = ((Final weight of 
dried solids in grams - initial weight of filter in grams) / (Volume of sample in ml)) * 1000000. 
Volatile Suspended Solids (VSS) represents the fraction of TSS that can be burned off by ignition at a 
high temperature of 550°C. It indicates the amount of organic matter present in the sample and 
serves as an indicator of its biodegradability. The formula used to calculate VSS is: VSS = ((Final 
weight of dried solids after ignition in grams - initial weight of filter in grams) / (Volume of sample in 
ml)) * 1000000. 
The measurements of optical density (OD) at 670 nm and in vivo fluorescence (IVF) with excitation at 
440 nm and emission at 690 nm were performed in triplicate using a Synergy™ Mx Monochromator-
Based Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (BioTek Instruments, Inc., USA) on a 24-well Co-star Multiple 
Well Plate (Corning, USA). The pH and temperature measurements were obtained using a Hach 
HQ40D Portable Multimeter and a PHC10101 probe, respectively. TSS  filtrates were re-filtered using 
Agilent nylon 0.2 μm pore filters (USA) and stored frozen at -20 °C until analysis. Segmented flow 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
analysis was conducted on a San++ Automated Wet Chemistry Analyzer with RØZ series 1052 
autosampler (Skalar Analytical B.V, Netherlands) to determine the nutrient levels. 
Microalgae were imaged using an inverted microscope (Zeiss Axioskop, Carl Zeiss microscopy, 
Oberkochen, Germany) equipped with a Leica DFC320 camera. The measurements of microalgae 
were conducted using Image-Pro Plus version 7.0.1.658 software by Media Cybermetrics (Rockville, 
MD, USA).  
Growth rates, OD and IVF are the average of the duplicates or triplicates and have been illustrated in 
the appendix for the first three batches. The growth rate (µ) was calculated according to 
Khoshnevisan et al, 2020. 
µ= d ln (IVF750)/ dt 
where µ is the specific growth rate during exponential phase and IVF750 is the measured in vivo 
fluorescence at a wavelength of 750 nm. It was determined numerically using linear regression in 
Microsoft Excel.   
 
4 Results and discussion 
4.1 Microalgae growth and nutrient removal in batch reactors  
Initially the investigation focused on evaluating the exponential growth rates of distinct Chlorella 
sorokiniana strains across three consecutive batches conducted without the addition of 
supplemental phosphorus sources. To impart a comprehensive perspective, each batch featured a 
distinct duration: the first batch spanned 6 days, while the subsequent two batches were extended 
to 12 days each. The growth rates for these batches are as follows: For the initial 6-day batch, μmax 
was recorded at 0.0192 day-1; within the extended 12-day batches, growth rates were measured at 
0.0144 ± 0.0024 day-1 and 0.0156 ± 0.0012 day-1, respectively. 
The initial batch, with an observed umax of 0.0192 day-1, indicates a vigorous growth phase in the 
shorter duration. In contrast, the subsequent 12-day batches exhibited slightly lower growth rates of 
0.0144 ± 0.0024 day-1 and 0.0156 ± 0.0012 day-1. This phenomenon could stem from complex factors 
such as adaptation to the wastewater environment, fluctuations in nutrient availability, and potential 
interactions with other microorganisms during the extended cultivation periods.

Upon closer examination of nutrient dynamics in the three successive batches, a noteworthy trend 
emerged. The PO4 levels exhibited a gradual decline throughout each batch, registering values 
consistently below the 1 mg P/L threshold. Simultaneously, NH3 concentrations underwent a 
discernible reduction, transitioning from an initial 61.7 mg N/L to 54.1 mg N/L by the culmination of 
each batch. 
This trend of nutrient modulation further extended to the domain of total phosphorus and total 
nitrogen. Specifically, total phosphorus levels experienced a decline from 1.29 mg/L to 0.145, while 
total nitrogen content underwent a transformation from an initial 111 mg/L to a concluding 54.2 
mg/L. This equated to approximate removal rates of 88.75% for phosphorus and 51.15% for nitrogen. 
Furthermore, the soluble COD exhibited a noteworthy reduction, deescalating from an initial 
concentration of 480 mg/L to a notably lower 143 mg/L, signifying a substantial removal rate of 
70.62% in the course of the treatment. Zhang & Chen, 2015b indicated that the concentration of 
total nitrogen (TN) in wastewater exhibited limited influence on algae growth, whereas the levels of 
chemical oxygen demand (COD) and total phosphorus (TP) exerted a substantial impact on growth 
dynamics. 
In the initial batch, the Total Suspended Solids (TSS) exhibited a gradual decline from an initial mean 
of 262.5 mg/L ± 46.73 to 118 mg/L ± 46.73 by the end of day 6. Subsequently, the second batch 
commenced at 135 mg/L, achieving a peak of 255 mg/L on day 3, and then gradually decreasing to 
90 mg/L by day 12. In the third batch, TSS began at 76 mg/L, reached its zenith of 122 mg/L on day 8, 
and concluded at 66 mg/L by day 12. 
Remarkably, the TSS concentrations in the latter two batches were consistently lower than that of 
the initial batch. This correlation aligns with the growth rates observed, indicating that the initial 
batch exhibited a superior growth rate of Chlorella sorokiniana compared to the subsequent two 
batches.  
These findings collectively suggest the potential presence of a nutrient deficiency that may be 
impeding the optimal growth of this microalgae species. Considering these conclusions alongside our 
experimental outcomes, it becomes evident that the hindrance observed in the growth of the last 
two batches was not primarily attributable to COD and TN concentrations. Instead, it becomes 
apparent that the pivotal factor responsible for the observed hindered growth was the concentration 
of TP. 
Consequently, an additional batch experiment was undertaken, introducing a supplementary 
phosphorus source. The final batch encompassed two distinct cultures: a control culture (A), 
mirroring the composition of the preceding three batches, and an experimental culture (B), enriched 
with an additional phosphorus source. Initially, Culture A exhibited a Total Suspended Solids (TSS) 
concentration of 120 mg/L, which peaked at 244 mg/L on day 4, before gradually declining to 128 
mg/L by the end of day 6. Concurrently, Culture B initiated with a TSS of 140 mg/L, achieved a similar 
zenith on day 4 at 272 mg/L, and subsequently decreased to 164 mg/L by the conclusion of the ninth 
day. Notably, despite a slightly higher TSS in Culture B, the variance between the two cultures lacked 
statistical significance.

indicated their existence (Fig 6). Additionally, these batches displayed discernible debris within the

On the other hand, a notable contrast emerged in the final batch, which received an additional 
supply of phosphorus. Through the strategic employment of centrifugation and filtration, any 
remnants of debris that were visibly present in the initial three batches were effectively eradicated. 
Notably, upon microscopic examination of this advanced batch, the exclusive presence of Chlorella 
sorokiniana became evident, with negligible signs of extraneous contaminants. The refined 
purification techniques applied to the final batch played a pivotal role in eliminating debris, thereby 
creating an environment less conducive to the proliferation of other microorganisms. 
Microalgae and bacteria engage in various interactions, including motility, chemotaxis, type IV 
secretion systems, quorum sensing systems, and the production of growth promoters. These 
interactions have the potential to enhance algal biomass (Fuentes et al., 2016). Bacterial 
communities contribute growth-promoting factors like indole-3-acetic acid and essential vitamins 
such as B12, fostering improved microbial growth (Yao et al., 2019). Additionally, mixed strains may 
be more resilient and stable in long-term cultivation compared to pure cultures based on stable 
chlorophyll content (Watanabe et al., 2005). This correlation aligns with our observations of growth 
rates, indicating that the initial three batches exhibited higher exponential growth compared to the 
batch that underwent water filtration. 
Filtration may inadvertently remove vital nutrients required for the coexistence of beneficial bacteria 
and Chlorella sorokiniana in a symbiotic relationship. This absence of a harmonious environment

could account for the comparatively restrained microalgal biomass growth observed in the filtered 
batch, supporting the significance of the interplay between microorganisms in promoting optimal 
growth conditions. 
Additional Observations: 
Interestingly, visual observations revealed distinct differences in the appearance of the two cultures. 
(figure 8) The culture B exhibited a greener coloration, indicating a higher chlorophyll content, 
compared to the control culture A. Additionally, the in-vivo fluorescence (IVF) measurements further 
supported this observation, showing a higher IVF reading in the culture with the supplemental 
phosphorus compared to the control. The greener appearance and higher IVF measurement in the 
phosphorus-supplemented culture suggest a potential enhancement in photosynthetic activity, 
possibly contributing to the increased biomass productivity.

within the chloroplast. This build-up typically coincides with a decline in the chlorophyll content of

5 Conclusion 
Our initial hypothesis suggesting phosphorus deficiency as a limiting factor for optimal C. sorokiniana 
growth in the examined wastewater has been disproven by our experimental results. Despite the 
elevation of phosphorus content through supplemental addition, discernible improvements in 
growth or nutrient removal capabilities were not achieved in Chlorella sorokiniana cultures. This 
finding emphasises the complex and multifaceted nature of nutrient interactions, emphasising the 
need for a better understanding of the intricate relationships that govern microalgal growth in 
wastewater treatment systems. 
Furthermore, the inadvertent removal of essential nutrients through filtration processes raises 
intriguing questions about the potential impact on the symbiotic relationship between beneficial 
bacteria and Chlorella sorokiniana. This disruption of a harmonious coexistence may have 
contributed to the observed restrained microalgal biomass growth in filtered batches, highlighting 
the critical role of microbial interactions in fostering optimal growth conditions. 
Future research on Chlorella sorokiniana in wastewater treatment should focus on the complex 
interactions between microorganisms and nutrients, defining the underlying mechanisms that 
influence growth dynamics. Exploring alternative nutrient management strategies and optimising 
conditions to maximise microorganism synergy could pave the way for increased microalgal biomass 
production for valorisation in different industries (eg. Biofuels, food and feed etc.) and more efficient 
nutrient removal.

Special Project Course:  
Abstract 
The rising demand for meat worldwide has led to a corresponding increase in the amount of 
slaughterhouse wastewater (SWW) produced. SWW is complex and hazardous due to its composition 
and pathogenic risk. The conventional treatment processes for SWW have high energy consumption 
and adverse environmental impact. Microalgae-based treatment processes are a promising 
alternative, as they are cost-effective and environmentally friendly. Three microalgae strains: 
Haematococcus pluvialis, Chlorella sorokinana and Scenedesmus obliquus were tested against SWW 
collected from the industry before and after treatment. Results show that H. pluvialis and C. 
sorokiniana are more suitable for cultivation in both Horsens and Post-Belt water compared to S. 
obliquus. However, during upscaling, C. sorokiniana projected more growth than the other two 
strains. It also demonstrated that autoclaved wastewater samples can promote better growth of 
microalgae, possibly due to the elimination of microbial competition and reduction in inhibitory 
compounds. This study provides insights into the potential of microalgae for sustainable SWW 
treatment and could contribute to the development of effective, low-cost, and environmentally 
friendly wastewater treatment methods. 
1 Introduction 
Due to the constant population growth and industrialization, wastewater treatment has become one 
of the major environmental challenges in the recent times. This necessitates the process to shift into 
a more sustainable approach and thus it is critical to have a high treatment efficiency. In this report, 
the focus has been on treating the wastewater that is produced in slaughterhouses. 
In most of the countries around the world, meat is an important part of the diet and its demand has 
been increased immensely in the recent years. The production of meat has increased by three times 
in the last fifty years. In 2018, the production was around 340 million tons (Khandaker et al., 2021) 
With the increase of meat production, large amount of slaughterhouse wastewater is generated. The 
meat industry has a significant impact on the global water balance, releasing 1.5 to 18 m3 of 
wastewater per ton of meat produced. (UNESCO Publishing, 2018) Slaughtering cattle and pigs 
produce 1.6 to 9 m3 of wastewater per ton of meat (European IPPC Bureau, 2005). SWW is regarded 
as hazardous throughout the world due to its complex composition of fats, proteins, and fibers, as 
well as the pathogenic risk posed by fecal bacteria and potentially infectious carcasses (Bustillo-
Lecompte et al., 2015). Thus, the SWW requires a sustainable treatment plan.  
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
The conventional wastewater treatment is depended on sequential aerobic/ anaerobic processes 
where the contaminants present in the WW is converted to inert compounds, making the water safe 
for reuse or disposal. Even though these conventional processes remove carbon, nitrogen and 
phosphorus at a satisfactory level, however, it requires consumption of high energy and also results 
in nutrient loss. It also results in an adverse environmental impact due to the emission of greenhouse 
gases (Acién et al., 2016). An alternative to such processes is the treatment of wastewater using 
microalgae which is both environment friendly and cost effective. Over the years, microalgae have 
shown promising results in not only removing the organic contaminants from wastewater, but also 
provides a source of useful biomass by-products (Renuka et. Al, 2015). 
In this project, we have collected slaughterhouse WW both directly from industries before and after 
treatment as well as sludge. However, we have run the experiments using Horsens and Post- belt 
water only. We investigated which microalgae strain exhibits the best outcomes against our WW in 
terms of growth, nutrient removal and biomass production.  
2 Materials & Methods 
2.1 Wastewater source 
The wastewater was collected from the company Danish Crown which is a Danish meat processing 
company that specializes in the production of pork and beef products. It is one of the world's largest 
pork exporters and is also a significant player in the European beef market. We have collected water 
both before and after treatment. In this report, Horsens water sample is without any for of 
treatment, pre- belt water has gone through preliminary treatment steps that are taken before the 
wastewater is subjected to its primary treatment. The purpose of pre- treatment is to remove or 
reduce the number of pollutants, solids, and other contaminants in the wastewater before it enters 
the main treatment process. This helps to improve the efficiency of the overall wastewater treatment 
process and prevent damage or blockages in the equipment. The post- belt water sample refers to 
the wastewater that has gone through belt filter press treatment. It is a type of mechanical 
dewatering equipment that removes water from sludge or wastewater using a series of rollers and a 
porous belt. The belt filter press can effectively remove solids from slaughterhouse wastewater and 
separate them from the liquid fraction of the wastewater. The following steps are typically included 
in the treatment process: 
• 
Screening: To remove large debris such as bones, hair, and other solids, the wastewater is 
first screened. 
• 
Coagulation and flocculation: Chemicals are added to the wastewater to cause suspended 
solids to coagulate and form larger particles known as flocs. 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
• 
Belt pressing: The wastewater is then passed through a series of rollers on a porous belt 
filter. 
• 
Disposal: The dewatered cake is typically disposed of in a landfill, while the clarified water is 
further treated before being discharged or reused. 
(Priyantha et al., 2013) 
2.2 Slaughterhouse Wastewater characterization 
The wastewater was collected and stored at -10° C until its use. In the soluble phase, wastewater was 
characterized by using kits HACH LCK cuvette test kits to determine Total COD, Soluble COD, N-NH4+, 
N-NO3-, N-NO2- and P-PO43. A 0.45 μm pore membrane (Whatman glass microfiber filters, Grade 
GF/A) was used to filter the samples. TS, VS, TSS and VSS were determined following APHA standard 
methods.  
To measure Total Suspended Solids (TSS) in this project, Econfltr Nyln 13mm 0.45 μm filters from 
Agilent Technologies were pre- dried and pre- weighed. 10ml of each water sample was filtered 
through the sample and dried in an over at 105°C for more than an hour. It was weighed again after it 
was completely dried. The formula used to determine TSS is: 
TSS= ((Final weight of dried solids in grams – initial weight of filter in grams)/ (Volume of sample in 
ml )) * 1000000. 
VSS is the fraction of TSS that is volatile and can be burned off by ignition at a high temperature of 
550° C. VSS is a measure of the amount of organic matter present in the sample, and it is often used 
as an indicator of the biodegradability of the sample. The formula used: 
VSS= ((Final weight of dried solids after ignition in grams – initial weight of filter in grams)/ (Volume 
of sample in ml)) * 1000000.

2.3 Analytical Procedures 
Three microalgae strains: Haematococcus pluvialis, Chlorella sorokiniana and Scenedesmus obliquus 
were selected for this project and were collected from cultures of The Culture Collection of Algae at 
the University of Göttingen, Germany (SAG) and Culture Collection of Algae & Protozoa (CCAP): 
Haematococcus pluvialis (SAG 192.80), Chlorella sorokiniana (CCAP 211/8K), Scenedesmus obliquus 
(SAG 2327). The entire project was carried out in three different steps. Initially, for the screening of 
microalgae, microplating of the microalgae cultures were performed in three cycles. Later, a 
comparative analysis was conducted to investigate how autoclaved and non- autoclaved wastewater 
can affect the growth of microalgae strains. Finally, the strains were inolculated in 1L glass bottles 
using undiluted Post- belt water. All the referred autoclaved water samples used in this project were 
autoclaved at 120 °C for 20 minutes prior to each experiment.  
 
2.3.1 Screening of microalgae 
To evaluate which SWW provided the best results against the three microalgae strains, 24- well cell 
culture plates (Cellstar ®) were used for the purpose of microplate inoculation. Each microalgae 
strain sourced from SAG or CCAP was transferred into a 2ml Eppendorf tube and centrifuged slightly 
at 6000 rpm for 5 minutes. The supernatant was discarded and the pellet was resuspended in WC 
media (Recipe in Appendix I).  
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
 
Fig 22. Schematic diagram of micro- plating of microalgae cultures 
Each microalga was grown in the SWW with different dilution conditions containing undiluted, 5%, 
10%, 15%, 20% original wastewater (Horsens and Post- Belt) and Wright Chu (WC) medium. Each well 
had 2ml of culture which contained 0.1ml of algae inoculation and 1.9ml of water samples of 
different concentrations or nutrient media. Cultures were grown at light intensity of 16000 LUX (285 
μmol/m2/s), provided by a LED 4000K light (PPFD in μmol/m2/s :conversion factor= 14.3) and at 
cycles of alternated light (12 hours light-12 hours dark).  Cultures were continuously shaken at 160 
rpm on a microplate shaker. As estimating algal biomass is required for monitoring microalgae 
growth, the emphasis is on optical density (OD) and in vivo autofluorescence (IVF) of chlorophyll, 
both of which have proven to be excellent candidates for monitoring. The OD and IVF of the cultures 
were measured on a bi- daily basis. OD at 750 nm and IVF at (excitation: 440 nm; emission: 690 nm) 
were monitored using BioTek™ Synergy™ MX Microplate Reader. As the IVF measurements of the 
cultures provided the best results, all the conclusions of this project were thus estimated solely 
based on the obtained IVF measurements. To estimate the rate of change of the specific growth rate 
μ with respect to (t2 - t1) in a biomass system, linear regression was performed on the derivative 
obtained using the quotient rule of differentiation. 
2.3.2 Comparison between autoclaved and non- autoclaved SSW 
In the project, we have also analysed microscopic images of cultures using autoclaved and non- 
autoclaved SWW. In all the other experiments in the project, starting from microplates to upscaled 1L 
bottles, autoclaved WW samples were used. However, to monitor if there are any contaminations in 
the cultures with any other microorganisms, we ran four microplate algae cultures with three 
different strains against autoclaved Horsens and post- belt water versus non- autoclaved Horsens and 
Post- belt water samples. The experimental design for this particular test followed the same 
conditions as the screening of microalgae using 24- well cell culture plates and the same dilution 
conditions. The IVF measurements were taken bi-daily for this experiment and after the end of 7 
days, microscopic images were captured for analysis using Motic AE31 microscope at 40x 
magnification.  
==PAGE:41==

2.3.3 Upscaling 
In order to monitor nutrient removal and biomass quality of the three microalgae strains against the 
most suitable SWW, the project was further upscaled to 1L glass bottles from 2ml culture plates. For 
this, the post- belt water was used in undiluted form. 1L Schott Duran Original GL45 Borosilicate 
Glass Laboratory Bottles were used in this experiment with a multi-port system consisting of a screw 
cap (GLS 80) with four ports (Figure 2). Through suitable tubing, one port had one tube connected 
with an air pump at one end and a diffuser at the other. Another tube was attached for collecting 
samples for monitoring. For proper aeration, one of the screw caps were slightly opened. The 
cultures were continuously stirred at 460 rpm with the help of magnetic stirrers. The glass bottles, 
screw caps and the tubes were autoclaved at 120°C for 15 minutes before the experiment. In each 
glass bottle, 5% (50 ml) of each microalgae inoculation and 95% (950 ml) of undiluted autoclaved 
post- belt water was present. Light was supplied from the side from one 18W fluorescent lamp 
(GroLux, Silvana) at cycles of alternated light (12 hours light- 12 hours dark) at an intensity of 13000 
LUX (299 μmol/m2/s).  
Samples were taken daily from the bottles for monitoring the growths and conditions by looking into 
IVF measurements, TSS, and VSS. O2 and pH were measured using WTW™ MultiLine™ 3430 Portable 
Digital Multiparameter. On day 5, pH was lowered by adding drops of 3M HCl in all the cultures.

samples compared to non-autoclaved ones. Non-autoclaved post-belt water had negligible growth. 
Microscopic image analysis revealed that, despite some contamination with bacteria, the culture 
species were mostly dominating in the culture plates. 
Studies have shown that autoclaving wastewater can have a positive effect on the growth of 
microalgae. One study published in the journal Bioresource Technology found that autoclaving 
domestic wastewater resulted in a higher biomass yield for the microalgae Chlorella sorokiniana 
compared to non-autoclaved wastewater (Diniz et al. 2017). Another study published in the journal 
Water Science and Technology found that autoclaving wastewater from a pulp and paper mill led to 
increased growth rates of several microalgae strains, including Chlorella vulgaris, Scenedesmus 
quadricauda, and Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (Jain et al. 2012).

On the other hand, non-autoclaved wastewater can contain contaminants that can inhibit microalgae 
growth. A study published in the journal Bioresource Technology found that non-autoclaved 
wastewater from a slaughterhouse inhibited the growth of the microalgae Scenedesmus sp. and 
Chlorella vulgaris due to the presence of high levels of ammonium and free fatty acids (Hernández-
Zamora et al., 2015). Chinnasamy et al. 2016 refers that autoclaved wastewater samples supported 
higher growth rates of Chlorella vulgaris and Scenedesmus obliquus compared to non-autoclaved 
wastewater samples. The authors suggested that the presence of microbial contaminants in non-
autoclaved samples may have hindered microalgae growth. 
Overall, these studies suggest that autoclaved wastewater samples can promote better growth of 
microalgae, possibly due to the elimination of microbial competition and reduction in inhibitory 
compounds. However, further research is needed to fully understand the mechanisms underlying 
these effects and their implications for large-scale microalgae cultivation in wastewater treatment. 
In conclusion, the use of autoclaved SWW samples is recommended for the growth of microalgae 
strains in large scale cultures. Additionally, microscopic image analysis is a useful tool to monitor the 
presence of contaminations in the cultures with other microorganisms. 
3.3 Comparative Analysis of Growth and Productivity of Microalgae Species in Upscaled Cultures 
Even though our findings suggest that 5x and 10x diluted Horsens WW seems more fitted for the 
growth of the microalgae, however, for the upscaling part of our experiment, we have used undiluted 
post- belt water as the nutrient media for microalgae growth  
There are scientific papers that have investigated the use of undiluted wastewater for microalgae 
growth. (Jiang et al., 2022) evaluated the use of undiluted municipal wastewater for the cultivation of 
microalgae. The study found that the microalgae grew well in the undiluted wastewater and that the 
wastewater had a positive effect on microalgae growth. 
(Oliveira et al., 2022) investigated the use of undiluted textile wastewater for the cultivation of 
microalgae. The study found that undiluted textile wastewater was an effective nutrient source for 
microalgae growth, and that the microalgae could remove organic matter, nitrogen, and phosphorus 
from the wastewater. 
Overall, the use of undiluted wastewater for microalgae growth can be beneficial, but it is important 
to carefully consider the potential risks and benefits of this approach. Thus, we have chosen 
undiluted water for our upscaling project.

Our results suggest that C. sorokiniana may be better suited for growth in autoclaved undiluted post-

4 Conclusion 
The findings presented in the research paper demonstrate the promising potential of microalgae-
based treatment processes for the sustainable treatment of slaughterhouse wastewater (SWW). The 
findings suggest that H. pluvialis and C. sorokiniana are more suitable for cultivation in both Horsens 
and Post-Belt water, and C. sorokiniana is projected to exhibit more growth than the other two 
strains in upscaling. Additionally, autoclaved wastewater samples have shown to promote better 
growth of microalgae, indicating the potential for further optimization of the process. 
The outcomes of this research have significant future implications. For instance, these findings could 
pave the way for the development of cost-effective and environmentally friendly wastewater 
treatment technologies, which could help address the economic and environmental challenges 
posed by conventional treatment methods. Moreover, the results could encourage the use of 
microalgae as a sustainable source for bioenergy and high-value products, such as pigments and 
proteins, as well as the use of SWW as a nutrient source for microalgae cultivation. 
Overall, this study highlights the potential of microalgae-based treatment processes as an innovative 
approach to SWW treatment, which could lead to more sustainable, efficient, and economically 
viable solutions for wastewater treatment in the future.
