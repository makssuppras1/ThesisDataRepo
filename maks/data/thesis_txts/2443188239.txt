Abstract
The continuous provision of the steadily increasing world wide energy consumption with
the combustion of fossil fuels imposes serious dangers on health and environment. A
realignment of the energy supply system, at least in the long run, is inevitable. Largest
potential in green energy production is usually ascribed to solar and wind power, but their
unpredictability and intermittency cause problems for grid stability and security of supply
as their penetration of the market proceeds. In addition, the transportation and the heating
sector have to be coupled to the green energy sources as well. A solution to both problems
is the electrolysis of water into hydrogen – driven by renewable energy – which can either
be directly stored as fuel or be processed further into higher value chemicals.
Even though it is the cheapest and most mature technology and there is no need of any
noble catalyst, Alkaline Electrolysis (AEL) is often ascribed only low potential. Its major
drawback: The low current densities and consequently the large stacks. However, recent
research at DTU Energy has shown that AEL with Nickel foam and Nickel ﬁbre electrodes
may achieve signiﬁcantly increased performance and further improvements are expected
by optimization of their microstructure.
In order to support the research at DTU Energy, this project has developed a three dimen-
sional, steady state model of the electrochemical processes in the anode. The model is
based on fundamental physical principles rather than empiric relationships. Unlike many
other models in literature, this model avoids effective transport parameters for the porous
electrode but the transport processes are modelled by applying the bulk coefﬁcients in de-
tailed morphologies. Due to its rather fundamental nature, the model can, in principal, be
applied to all microstructures where the particles are assumed to be fully dissolved in the
electrolyte and do not occur in the gas phase.
During the course of this project, two kinds of microstructures were created which resem-
ble the ﬁbres and foams already experimentally investigated at DTU Energy and also a
nanostructure which represents the catalyst coating of the microstructures. These struc-
tures were, then, analysed by applying the electrochemical model and it was solved with
a ﬁnite element approach in COMSOL R⃝. In the nanostructure, overpotentials other than

Introduction
Even though almost half a century ago, the dilemma described by Gregory (1973) usually
termed the world energy crisis has lost nothing of its currency nor actuality. A glimpse
in the annually published World Energy Outlook (IEA 2016) reveals that the world wide
primary energy consumption has steadily grown during the last decades. This trend has
recently slowed down in Europe, but considering all parts of our planet, the curve has
linearly risen from about 9000 Mtoe in 1990 ending up at 14000 Mtoe in 2016 and it is
not expected to stop in near future. In the medium run, the actual problem in this regard
is not the scarcity of fossil fuels: The outlook shows as well that at current consumption
the readily exploitable reserves will last 175 years for coal, 50 years for oil and 62 years
for natural gas1. The main danger relates to the release of harmful substances when the
fuels are combusted – ﬁrst and foremost the pollution of CO2 and the resulting change in
climate.
Renewable and non polluting energy technologies are the core of the transformation from
fossil fuel based economies into sustainable ones – this transformation is hardly imagin-
able without hydropower, biomass, wind turbines and solar collectors. Hydropower and
biomass plants have been well established technologies since the beginning of the last
century whereas solar power, usually ascribed the largest potential among the renewable
energy sources, and wind power, which may at least play a crucial role in some parts of
the world including Denmark, were immature and too expensive (Moriarty & Honnery
2012). The cost for energy generation could simply not compete with the combustion of
fossil fuels. This situation has changed substantially in recent years. Figure 1.1 shows a
dramatic decrease of the average lifetime cost of energy from solar in particular, but also
wind power. These power sources have in fact become able to economically compete with
conventional combustion technologies. Due to their large potential and their declined cost,
it is likely that future energy systems are to be built mainly on solar and wind energy and,
therefore, the unpredictability and intermittency of these energy resources leaves only one

1.1
Hydrogen or Methanol?
In 2016, 29.6 % of the electricity in the European Union was produced in a renewable way
(EU 2018a); a cheering trend which is expected to continue as the European Union has
recently adjusted their targets from 27 % to 32 % renewable energy in 2030 (EU 2018b).
Nevertheless, the ﬂuctuating nature of solar and wind have prevented conventional power
plants from being closed down in order to ensure grid stability and security of supply.
Furthermore, the task of phasing out fossil fuels becomes even more challenging for the
transportation sector, requiring mobile energy sources, and the heating sector, having sig-
niﬁcant annual ﬂuctuations in demand. Remedy is found by converting electricity in times
of renewable overproduction into a form that can be stored and used for transportation or
during the heating period.

Chapter 1. Introduction
3
The role of energy storage and the integration of the energy sector have been greatly dis-
cussed in literature for example by Lund et al. (2016) or Møller et al. (2017). However,
not all types of storage are suitable: Mechanical and thermal storage technologies can-
not be used for transportation and electrochemical storages are too expensive to scale up.
Only storing energy in chemical substances is in principle possible at every scale, even for
mobile applications. Further, suitable substances can be converted readily into any other
form of energy, e.g. electricity or heat, rendering chemical storage technologies as the
most plausible alternative for energy systems based on renewable sources.
Hydrogen, being an effectively unlimited element, has already been suggested in the 1960s
as future energy carrier. The so called Hydrogen Economy was considered more efﬁcient
primarily due to two reasons: Firstly, as early supporters state, the utilisation of hydro-
gen in fuel cells is not subject to the Carnot efﬁciency, which represents the maximum
efﬁciency to every combustion engine (Bockris & Srinivasan 1969) and secondly, hydro-
gen transmits energy more efﬁciently in pipelines than electricity does in power lines,
connecting remote energy sources to cities and industries (Gregory 1973). Only recently,
possessing the highest gravimetric energy density among all known substances and due
to its clean combustion, hydrogen has been considered of great signiﬁcance for green de-
velopment. Having recognised this role, many countries have adopted their policies (by
e.g. hydrogen road maps) leading to the expectation of a remarkably increasing market
for hydrogen in the future (Dou et al. 2017).
Although acknowledging the importance of chemical storage, the Hydrogen Economy has
been subject to criticism. Due to difﬁculties in storage, transport of hydrogen, safety con-
cerns and the enormous costs of new infrastructure, Olah et al. (2009) have introduced
the Methanol Economy as an alternative. Methanol is a liquid fuel with all its advan-
tages regarding handling and storing. The authors suggest that methanol and its derivated
hydrocarbons could replace fossil fuels in our everyday life with only minor changes in
infrastructure, rendering the transformation of the economy easier and more cost efﬁcient.
Methanol can be reformed from hydrogen and CO2 using any possible energy source and
eventually recycling the carbon in the atmosphere. In fact, not only synthetically produced
fuels as methanol, but also methane and ammonia, have been greatly discussed in litera-
ture and are often regarded as the most suitable energy storage (Graves et al. 2011).
The globally working energy systems are complex and their future cannot be predicted
easily. Indeed, nobody can anticipate which energy carrier is likely to play the major role
in upcoming economies. Nevertheless, the argumentation above suggests that hydrogen
is likely to take part in it, either for the distribution of energy, as precursor of hydrocar-

1.2
Electrolysis Cells
In principle, electrolysis cells work right the opposite way as fuel cells – instead of hy-
drogen being burnt to power, power is used to generate hydrogen. This is managed by
separating two suitable electrodes by a suitable electrolyte, applying sufﬁcient voltage and
supplying some water. In this case, the water will be broken up into hydrogen and oxy-
gen. There are different types of electrolyses cells which are distinguished according to
the electrolyte applied; worth mentioning are alkaline electrolysis, proton exchange mem-
brane electrolysis and solid oxide electrolysis. The detailed reactions at the electrodes are
different for each type, but the overall reaction is valid for all of them:
H2O →H2 + 1
2O2
(1.1)
This reaction does not occur spontaneously, it needs to be driven and the bulk of the
reaction enthalpy has to be provided electrically. At 25◦C and atmospheric pressure the
Gibbs free energy with respect to the higher heating value is 237.2 kJ/mol corresponding
to a reversible voltage of 1.23 V. Unfortunately, the real process always includes some
irreversible effects, so even for very small currents the required voltage is always higher
than the value stated above. Further, the necessary energy is not only represented by the
direct power consumption of the cell, but the balance of plant has to be included as well.
The system efﬁciency for electrolysis is, therefore, usually deﬁned as3:

The Contribution of this Project
Even though it is the cheapest and most mature technology and there is no need of any
noble catalyst, AEL is often ascribed only low potential. Its major drawback: The low
current densities and consequently the large stacks. However, recent research, for instance
conducted by Schalenbach et al. (2018), has shown that AEL can well provide similar
voltage-current characteristics as state-of-the-art PEMEL. Also, DTU Energy has devel-
oped concepts for AEL with Nickel foam and Nickel ﬁbre electrodes achieving similar
performance. In order to achieve good performance of the cell, the importance of the spe-
ciﬁc electrode structure and material can hardly be overestimated.
Research in this ﬁeld is traditionally characterized by experimental work and has only
started to be supplemented with modelling effort. A recently published review paper of
Olivier et al. (2017) stresses the importance of the modelling effort in the ﬁeld of electro-
lysis in general. It also reveals that there are only very few existing models for AEL which
are not based on an empirical approach that limits the validity of the model to only one
speciﬁc cell. Furthermore, many authors have developed only one dimensional mathemat-
ical models using effective properties for the transport parameters in the porous electrode
and neglecting their microscopic structure.
The performance losses in the cells are usually ascribed mostly to the slow reactions at
the electrodes. Since the reaction rates are proportional to the surface area, the structure
of the cell has crucial inﬂuence on the reaction kinetics. Finer structures lead to higher
surface area and accordingly, to better performance. On the other hand, if the pores are

The Electrochemistry of an Alka-
line Electrolysis Cell
Electrochemical phenomena emerge, if electrons or ions leave their bonds with a substance
in one phase for forming new ones with other substances in another phase. The chemi-
cal reaction of these charged particles comprises an electric current and the so formed
electric potential has immediate inﬂuence on the reactants and therefore on the reaction
itself. Since chemical and electric driving forces cannot be distinguished unambiguously
anymore, the nature of the process is rather electrochemical (Vetter 1967).
The electrochemical processes in electrolysis cells are characterized by interweaving dif-
ferent phenomena – namely: thermodynanimcs, chemical kinetics, mass transport and
electronics – rendering its description rather difﬁcult. In fact, fuel cell pioneer Bock-
ris (1972) went even as far as blaming the lack of knowledge in electrochemistry in the
beginning of the 20th century – especially the lack of understanding electrochemical over-
potentials – for the rise of the combustion engine with its low efﬁciency.
This chapter aims at providing the necessary knowledge to understand the processes oc-
curing within an AEL cell. Emphasis is put on the discussion of the different kinds of
overpotential occurring in the anode and phenomenological equations to their description
are given.

of oxygen takes part at the anode, where the electrons are returned to the electric circuit.
As the K+ ions do not take part in any electrode reaction, a stable concentration gradient
is formed along the electrolyte due to the applied potential. The hydroxyl ions are the
only charge carriers in the electrolyte and have to be transported from the cathode to the
anode. The formed gases i.e. H2 and O2, then, are still solved in the solution and they
diffuse out of the electrolyte where they are gathered and often further pressurized. If
their concentrations become too high, gaseous bubbles may form within the electrolyte.
A diaphragm – being nonpermeable for H2 and O2 but permeable for OH−and H2O – is
needed between the electrodes to prevent the gases from diffusing through the electrolyte
and recombining. A basic sketch showing the main components of an AEL cell is shown
in Figure 2.1, where the redly dashed rectangle indicates the parts which are analysed in
this thesis.

2.3
The Overpotential
Electrolysis cells are usually described with means of the polarisation curve relating the
voltage between the electrodes to the current in the cell. The equilibrium state corresponds
to zero net current and is characterized by Vrev. Then, if the net current is different from
zero, the potential difference will assume values different from the reversible potential and
this deviation is called overpotential or overvoltage and denoted η. Overvoltages occur
due to various reasons, but the deﬁnition given here applies disregarding of the cause for
the deviation.
The overpotentials occurring in an AEL cell are due to the anodic reaction (ηan), the
cathodic reaction (ηca), mass transport of the ions in the electrolyte (ηl), mass transport of
the ions through the diaphragm (ηd), bubble formation of oxygen and hydrogen (ηbub,O2
and ηbub,H2) and the ohmic resistances in the electrode (ηs). The performance of the
electrolysis cell is then fully described by the reversible Voltage Vrev and the mentioned
overpotentials, which are function of the current density i:
Vcell(i) = Vrev + ηan(i) + ηca(i) + ηl(i) + ηd(i) + ηbub,O2(i) + ηbub,H2(i) + ηs(i)
(2.9)
A typical shape of the polarization curve for an AEL cell is calculated in appendix
A and shown in Figure 2.2. Higher overpotentials translate into lower efﬁciencies and,
therefore, their reduction is the inherent target of research in electrolysis. The scope of
this thesis only comprises ηan, ηl, ηbub and ηs. This section discusses these contributions
and aims at providing the knowledge necessary for setting up the mathematical model in
the succeeding chapters.
2.3.1
Electrode Kinetics
Applying voltage to the cell inﬂuences the ions in the electrolyte. The positively charged
K+ ions are attracted to the negative cathode and the negatively charged OH−ions to the
positive anode. The so-called double Layer is formed, positioning the ions in ordered
layers at the electrode surface (Inner Helmholtz Layer) and ions of the opposite charge in
rather diffuse layers next to them (Outer Helmholtz Layer). The total interfacial region is
electrically neutral but an electric potential gradient is established across the layer. The
thickness of this double Layer is usually only of a few nm, so macroscopically the electric
potential drop can be treated as sharp. Still, its effects have to be taken into account by the
equations used (Newman & Thomas-Alyea 2004).

Overpotential due to Bubble Formation
As shown in Figure 2.1, O2 is formed at the anode and dissolved in the electrolyte in the
ﬁrst place. The solubility of O2 in aqueous KOH-solutions is not very high though. A
values of 5.86 10−6 mol/dm3 was found at standard conditions (cf. Secion 4.2.2); this
limit is already reached at very low current densities. As the electrolyte degases, hydrogen
bubbles are formed having effects on the cell performance due to their high resistance
for the transport of ions. In simple words: the gas bubbles block the reaction sites at the
electrode surface.

from buoyancy effects and surface tension. If a ﬂow exists, convective forces have to be
taken into account as well. The buoyancy force is strongly correlated with the growing

2.4
Enhanced Temperature and Pressure
The preceding sections have shown that the parameters for the reaction kinetics as well as
the diffusion coefﬁcients are a function of temperature. Furthermore, the concentration of
the oxygen in the electrolyte is a function of the operational pressure of the cell i.e. the
pressure of the oxygen in the vessel where it is gathered. Consequently, the overall cell
performance depends on the operation conditions as well. As a general rule, increased
temperature may be associated with increased kinetics and lower overpotentials whereas
higher pressures may drive the reaction into the unwanted direction. Additionally, as a
matter of fact, the reaction enthalpy and the Gibb’s free energy of the reaction depend on
the operational conditions as well. In the search for the best conditions, all these effects
have to be outweighed against each other.
The effects of pressure and temperature on enthalpy and Gibb’s free energy of the reaction
are discussed in common fuel cell literature as by O-Hayre et al. (2016) for instance. A
problem is that usually assumptions, as the ideal behaviour of gases, are made, rendering
the calculations inaccurate. Remedy is found in a speciﬁc study conducted by LeRoy
et al. (1980) providing numerical expressions for the thermodynamic entities on demand.
Empirical data is displayed in the ﬁgures 2.4 and 2.5.
The ﬁgures reveal that the reaction enthalpy is roughly constant with respect to changes
in temperature and pressure whereas the Gibb’s free energy shows a signiﬁcant depen-
dence on both. As discussed above, the degrease in Gibb’s free energy associated with
high temperatures may enable the reuse of the intrinsically created heat i.e. the energy
lost due to the overpotentials, or may even allow the use of an external heat source. The
dependence of the Gibb’s free energy on the pressure shows approximately logarithmic
dependency. This is no coincidence as logarithmic correlation between work and pressure
level is expected for ideal gases. In fact, Larminie & Dickens (2003) showed that if water
vapour is supplied to the cell and all gases are treated ideally, there will be no difference
in work of pressurized electrolysis and atmospheric electrolysis with subsequent pressur-
ization. Onda et al. (2004) even pointed out that if the pressurisation of the liquid water
supply requires less work than the pressurization of the output gases and pressurised elec-
trolysis will even use 5% less power.
To wrap it up: Commercially available AEL cells are usually operated at 60 −90◦and
10 −30 bar (cf. Table 1.1). As discussed, enhancing the operative temperature may im-
prove both, the thermodynamics and the kinetics of the reaction whereas increasing pres-
sure may simplify the plant by rendering further pressurisation of the hydrogen obsolete as
well as suppress bubble formation and consequently, high temperatures and pressures are

The Microstructure
In principal, an AEL-cell works by immersing the two electrodes into the KOH solution
and applying the required voltage as illustrated in Figure 2.1 and cells built in this man-
ner could in fact be used to produce hydrogen. However, real cells have been optimized
with regard to various aspects. Two main approaches are discussed in literature, for in-
stance by Marini et al. (2012): zero-gap conﬁgurations and gas diffusion electrodes. In
zero-gap electrolyzers, the distance between the electrodes and the diaphragm has been
minimized to reduce the overvoltage in the electrolyte. Another effect is that bubbles are
prevented from entering the space between the electrodes so their effect on its ion conduc-
tivity is minimized. In gas diffusion electrodes the electrolyte is located in the hydrophilic
diaphragm and in hydrophilic agglomerates where it is immobilized by capillary forces.
The reactions happen in the agglomerates and the gases escape through the pores in be-
tween them. The electrodes investigated in this project belong to the former type. In both
cases, electrodes are often rendered porous on two levels: Pores with sizes in the order of
some 100 µm – the Microstructure – facilitate the escape of gas bubbles and increase the
reactive surface area (Zhang & Zeng 2016) and pores with sizes in the order of only some
100 nm – the Nanostructure – remarkably magnify the surface area to allow for outstand-
ing performances.
Obviously, transport processes in porous electrodes take place somewhat different to the
processes in the bulk material: The transport of electrons in the electrode and the transport
of ions in the pores ﬁlled with electrolyte occur both in series and in parallel complicating
their mathematical description. In porous electrode theory as introduced by Newman &
Tiedemann (1975), the porous electrode is treated macroscopically as the superposition
of the electrolyte and the electrode and is geometrically described only by the volume
fraction of the electrode phase – the Porosity. The transport processes are described by
effective transport properties accounting for the essential effects of the pores while dis-
regarding any geometric details. Such correction could for example be the Bruggeman
factor:

The Model
In order to obtain the performance of the electrode performance, the overvoltages across
the structures presented in Chapter 3 had to be computed. This was done by Finite-
Element-Method calculations which were implemented in COMSOL Multiphysics R⃝5.3a.
This chapter discusses the mathematical model including the governing equations and the
used parameters. In addition, the equations are solved analytically for a very simple geom-
etry and the solution is compared to the simulated results as a means of model validation.
4.1
Governing Equations and Boundary Conditions
In COMSOL R⃝, there are three different physics modules to simulate the current distribu-
tion in electrochemical cells. The Primary Current Distribution module only accounts for
losses due to electric resistance, taking no other overvoltages into account. This model can
be applied if the reaction kinetics are very fast and can be neglected, but as we expect the
largest overpotentials due to the kinetics, this module is not suitable for our purpose. The
Secondary Current Distribution module includes the electrode kinetics, but still does not
account for effects of different concentrations of the electrolyte and, therefore, neglects
hindrances of mass transport in the electrolyte. The Tertiary Current Distribution module
is the only one, considering all described phenomena and is, consequently, the only suit-
able physics module for the description of the current.

the electrostatic potential in the electrolyte Φl, the electrostatic potential in the electrode
Φs and the concentrations of the ions cOH and cK. As stated in chapter 2.3, the variables

principle of electroneutrality (equation (2.21)) and by the Ohmic law (equation (2.28)).

5.6
Final Discussion
Various curves have been shown in the sections above representing the performance of the
microstructures. Some results were as expected, while others deviated from expectations.
But why? How do we expect porous electrodes to perform and what exactly happens
in them? Newman & Tiedemann (1975) explain: Even if the electrolyte is uniform in
composition the ”reaction distribution is generally nonuniform within the electrode. To
minimize the ohmic potential drop, the current should be divided between the solution
and the matrix in proportion to their effective conductivities. However, this requires high
reaction rates” at the beginning of the electrode and close to the current collector. ”Slow
electrode reaction kinetics force the reaction to be more uniformly distributed in order to
reduce the transfer current density. These competing effects of ohmic potential drop and
slow reaction kinetics determine the resulting distribution.”
What does this mean for this project? Since the conductivity in the electrode is several
orders of magnitude higher than in the electrolyte, we would expect the reaction to be
shifted away from the current collector. Further, the electrode potential will hardly change
and the variations of the surface overpotential along the electrode are only caused by
the gradual increase of the the electrolyte potential. The largest surface overvoltage is
expected at the edge of the electrode that points towards the diaphragm and that is where
the largest reaction rates are expected to be located. This is, however, not the case.
Figure 5.12 shows the distribution of the local current density at the electrode surface,
which is proportional to the local reaction rate, for the foam with 1000 µm thickness (cf.
table 5.6). For the total current density1 i = 100 mA/cm2 we notice that the reaction
rates are concentrated close to the current collector, the total opposite of our expectations
(picture (a)). Increasing the total current density creates another hot spot for the reaction
rates at the opposite end of the electrode (picture b). This second pole gains relative
importance by increasing the total current density further, until most of the reaction rates
are concentrated there and the initially expected distribution appears (picture c). A similar
course for the distribution of the reaction rates was observed for the other structures as
well, although the shift occurred at different total current densities.
1Here, it is distinguished between the local current density which is related to the electrode surface and
the total current density which is related to the section of the model.

The preceding discussion allows for insight into the behavior within the electrode by ex-
plaining the distribution of the reaction rates in the model. We did see that without consid-
ering the cathodic term in equation (5.1) the reactions would mostly happen at distance to
the current collector. However, under certain conditions, depending on geometry and total
current density the oxygen concentration drives the reactions towards the other end of the
electrode. Another interesting fact is implicitly discovered as well: The cathodic term in
the Butler-Volmer equation is crucial in understanding the electrochemistry of the electro-
lysis cell and should not be neglected. As it is discussed in the subsequent paragraphs, the
performance curves shown in the previous sections cannot be explained when this term is
omitted.
In section 5.2, the overvoltages were shown for several ﬁbre structures. It was found that
increasing the surface area would not lead to an endless decrease in overvoltage but opti-
mum structures exist. This behavior is explained as follows: At the start of the curves the
reactions happen mostly close to the current collector. If the pores are too ﬁne the mass
transport of the OH−species through the electrode is hindered causing higher overvolt-
ages. Increasing the total current density leads to the reactions being shifted to the other
direction where they do hardly starve OH−ions any more. At this point, the performance
is lowered due to the high oxygen concentrations driving the backwards reaction. The
pores are too ﬁne to allow the oxygen to escape and, accordingly, the process is hindered

Section 5.4 revealed the, at ﬁrst unintuitive, property that thin electrodes perform better
than thick ones. Of course, a thick electrode causes stronger hindrance of mass transport
than a thin one. On the other hand, the pores’ geometry does not change and at least
the ones directed towards the diaphragm, where most of the reactions were expected to
happen, should be equally supplied with OH−even for different electrode sizes. This

electrode is indicated in the ﬁgure. Note that the curves have different lengths due to the
different thicknesses of the electrodes. We obtain that the concentrations follow mostly
the same course. Especially the concentrations in the 400 µm and the 500 µm electrode
show hardly any difference and the locations, where most of the reactions where expected
to happen, are almost equally supplied with OH−. Again, only the oxygen concentration
explains the observations. As above, if the reactions happen close to the current collector
the overvoltage is caused due to mass transport of OH−and if the reactions happen at the
other border it is caused due to mass transport of O2. As the hindrance of mass transport
is smaller in thin electrodes they perform better. It was shown that this is only true to until
a certain point size when the reaction sites become too little. Figure 5.16 gives evidence
that the O2 concentration in the different electrodes differs greatly.
In this section, the importance of the cathodic contribution to the reaction was strongly
stressed and the outcome of the model could not be explained without it. Albeit, it should
be emphasized as well that the O2 concentrations predicted by the model will hardly occur
in reality due to bubble formation. Unfortunately, this strongly reduces the conclusions
one may draw from the model. Suggestions for its further development are given in the
next chapter.

Conclusions and Prospects
During the course of this thesis a transient model was developed based on physical prin-
ciples rather than on empirical expressions. The intention was to investigate different mi-
crostructures so effective transport parameters were avoided. This model was then applied
to a 2 µm thick structure with pore sizes in the range of 0.1 µm which represented the
nanoporous coating on the electrode surface. The results showed that ohmic losses and
losses due to mass transport can be completely neglected and the resulting total current
density of the structure simply represented the increase in surface area within the coating.
Even at the highest current density i = 1500 mA/cm2 the oxygen concentrations within
the coating does not exceed about 180 times the solubility limit and is, therefore, hardly
expected to cause bubble formation. Afterwards, two kinds of microstructures were anal-
ysed: ﬁbres and foams. The performance of these structures was analysed by comparing
the polarisation curves showing the overvoltage with respect to the current density. It was
already argued in the introduction that structures should exist, where the overvoltages are
at optimum and a change of the structure will either worsen overvoltage due to reaction
kinetics or the one due to mass transport. Evidence for this statement was given by the
results for the ﬁbres. The optimum ﬁbre length were found to be 30 −50 µm and their
thickness around 30 µm. The foam structures were analysed for pore sizes down to 10 µm
but a corresponding optimum could not be found. The best explanation is that the analysed
structures were still too rough and an optimum will only be reached at even ﬁner pores.
Both structures were analysed with respect to the electrode thickness as well. Interestingly,
optimum sizes were found to be around 200 −300 µm for the ﬁbres and 100 −200 µm
for the foams. These results can only be explained if the backwards reaction induced by
high oxygen concentration expressed by the cathodic term in the Butler-Volmer equation
is taken into account. It was shown as well that the model shows the expected response to
a change in operational temperatur and pressure.
The aim of this project, as formulated in the introduction, was to investigate the behav-
ior of different microstructures with regards to the occurring overpotentials and serve as
reference for future experiments. As no model can ever perfectly mirror reality, assump-
==PAGE:82==
62
tions had to be made for the sake of simpliﬁcation. Most of them were already explicitly
mentioned and discussed when the physical model was set up whereas others came rather
implicitly. Though, the output of a model can not be interpreted correctly and wrong con-
clusions can be drawn if one is unaware of the assumptions. This is why the so far implicit
assumptions are listed here, followed by a brief discussion. Also some suggestions for the
further development of the model are provided.
Perhaps the most crucial assumption concerns the formation of bubbles. In the developed
model it was assumed that all particles are completely dissolved in the electrolyte and do
not enter the gas phase. As explained though, bubbles form within the pores and block
reaction sites and mass transport having considerable inﬂuence on the performance. This
is of crucial importance for the developed model in two aspects: First, at the blocked
sites equation (5.1) does not give any contribution. Chen et al. (2017) state that, in the
electrode tested, bubble formation arises from current densities of around 30 mA/cm2 and
that they cover nearly the entire electrode above 70 mA/cm2 at 25◦C. Abdin et al. (2017)
has investigated the effect for several temperatures and has come to the conclusion that
the coverage ratio at 80◦C is only around Θ ≈0.2 −0.4. To give an idea of ηbub: Using
equation (2.27) gives an additional overvoltage of ηbub ≈100 mV for a coverage ration of
Θ = 0.9 and ηbub ≈15 mV for Θ = 0.3. Although these values may differ from electrode
to electrode, they suggest that bubble formation is an issue which cannot be neglected.
Second, bubbles form if and only if a certain O2 concentration is exceeded. This implies,
that this concentration threshold cannot be exceeded in the electrolyte. According to expe-
riences from experimental work at DTU Energy, this threshold value is around 250−300
times the solubility cO2,bound which is exceeded by two orders of magnitude in the results
of the simulations. If the a value of 300cO2,bound cannot be exceeded and the conclusions
that can be drawn from the results are crucially limited.
What could be done to adjust the model? The surface coverage ratio Θ(i) as introduced
in section 2.3.3 is a macroscopic quantity which has to be determined experimentally. A
model, as the one developed here, that puts emphasize on microscopic structure and care-
fully distinguishes between the processes in different parts of the electrode can hardly be
improved by a macroscopic quantity that is not locally deﬁned. An idea could be to de-
termine the surface coverage ratio as function of the oxygen concentration Θ = Θ(cO2).
As a ﬁrst approach, this could be a monotonous function which shows a steep increase at
cO2 = 300 cO2,bound and sufﬁces the following conditions:

and

Although this deﬁnition is a bit vague and further investigation about the shape of the
function is required, the quantity is locally deﬁned and sufﬁces the condition that bubble
formation starts around the threshold value and increasingly blocks the reaction sites. It
should be mentioned here that Θ(cO2) cannot become unity if equation (2.27) should be
applied. This is indeed only a ﬁrst approach as the diffusion of bubbles will follow dif-
ferent laws than the diffusion of dissolved oxygen. Further elaboration could involve the
Bubbly Flow module of COMSOL R⃝into the model.
The diaphragm serves as separator from the solved O2 at the anode and the H2 at the cath-
ode. In the model it is assumed that this diaphragm is impermeable for both species so no
crossover occurs. This is usually not the case in real cells and the overpotential is under-
estimated by the model accordingly. If the properties of the diaphragm are given it should
be no problem to implement the loss of a certain amount of O2 particles in the boundary
condition of the model by considering the diffusive ﬂuxes of the corresponding species.
In zero-gap electrolyzers, the electrodes are tightly connected to the diaphragm with no
bulk electrolyte in between. This conﬁguration is not represented by the model as a layer
of dl = 100 µm bulk electrolyte is added to the model. Mass transport from a diaphragm
to the electrode will happen slower than from the pure electrolyte. Also the modelling of
the diaphragm which partly covers the boundary should not be any problem.
Any convection of the electrolyte is omitted in the equations. This assumption contradicts
real electrolysers which often circulate the electrolyte in order to remove bubbles and keep
control of the temperature. Also the maintenance of the electrolyte is simpliﬁed. How-
ever, as the direction of convective ﬂow can only be perpendicular to the direction of the
current in the cell, as a ﬁrst approximation the movement of the particles described here
may be treated independently of the convective ﬂow. A closer investigation cannot neglect
the ﬂow any more as the electrolyte will inevitably be stirred up due to surface friction.
This could be modelled with ﬂuid mechanics models that exist in COMSOL R⃝.
It was assumed that the temperature is constant throughout the electrode and the elec-
trolyte. The problem is that the energy lost due to the overpotentials dissipates into heat
increasing the temperature of the system locally. Nevertheless, in real electrolysers the
temperature is usually controlled through the circulating electrolyte so although tempera-
ture gradients exist they will be kept within a certain range.
==PAGE:84==
64
The concentration of the water is treated as constant. On the system level for the entire
cell this requires that exactly the amount of water is added to the system as it is used. On
a local level it means that changes in electrolyte concentration due to water production are
negligible compared to changes due to ion consumption. As a 35wt% solution contains
approximately six times more water molecules than hydroxyl ions and the water produc-
tion is also only half as fast as the consumption of the ions, this assumption may well be
justiﬁed.
What is the ﬁnal achievement of this project? A model was developed based on physical
principles rather than on empirical expressions. The model can be solved in three dimen-
sions so the effects of the microscopic structure of the electrode are represented in the
output. Due to its physical foundation, the model can be applied to every kind of structure
where the phase in the pores is assumed to be fully ﬂuid. Further, the model can easily
be adopted to changes of the operational conditions as for instance temperature, pressure,
strength of solution or catalyst material by changing the concerning parameters. For the
reaction, not a simpliﬁed discription by a Tafel equation is used but a concentration depen-
dent Butler-Volmer equation which takes into account both, the depletion of the hydroxyl
species throughout the electrode and the formation oxygen driving the backwards reaction.
A comparable approach was not found in literature.
The microstructures analysed were rather simple, yet they may well represent the signiﬁ-
cant properties of real electrodes. The most crucial result may be that hard evidence was
given for the existence of optimum geometries. The tendency is that reaction kinetics
dominate the overvoltage for most analysed parameters and mass trasport starts only to
become an issue at scales which are signiﬁcantly smaller than the structures tested exper-
imentally. Since the blocked surface area due to bubble formation adds, in the operational
range of interest, an almost constant term to the overpotential1 the obtained results are still
qualitatively valid. This is of course only true if smaller pores do not prevent the bubbles
from escaping and in that way impose void spaces impassable for ions. Also the observa-
tion that the reaction rates are mostly concentrated at the edges of the electrodes leading
to the fact that thin electrodes perform signiﬁcantly better than thick ones, is an interesting
result. It may even be supported under the assumptions that bubbles escape better from
thin electrodes.

The Inﬂuence of the Mesh Size
The choice of the best mesh is not obvious at all. Coarse meshes may be constituted
of elements which are too large for representing the ﬁeld variables adequately and they
may lead to results which do not represent real behavior. On the other hand ﬁne meshes
are constituted of a large number of elements and may lead to unjustiﬁed and unbearable
computational effort. Hence, the aim of the mesh size optimization is ﬁnding the coarsest
mesh which still achieves physical results. Since the structures used differ strongly in
scale and also by the way their meshes are generated, the inﬂuence of the mesh size was
tested for three different structures: the catalyst layer, the ﬁbres and the foams.
The structure for the catalyst layer was created as voxel data and then converted into a
mesh which could be processed by COMSOL R⃝. For the investigation of the mesh, both,
the solution of the voxel data and the coarseness settings in Simpleware R⃝were varied.

conclude that even the smallest mesh sufﬁciently represents the coating layer.
