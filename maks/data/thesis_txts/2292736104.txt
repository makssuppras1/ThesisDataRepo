by Fotios Sioutas
Tests of a prototype Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge Flap (CRTEF) are performed
in the VELUX wind tunnel and the rotating rig facility at Risø campus of DTU. The
purpose of the project is to analyse the experimental results in order to investigate the
aerodynamic performance of the ﬂap prototype systems. The objectives are presented,
along with a description of the test cases. The post-processing of data is discussed and
results for steady and unsteady response are presented. The performance and the validity
of the aerodynamic analysis are veriﬁed by comparison with 2D airfoil aerodynamic tools.
This study includes the post-processing of wind tunnel and rotating rig test data, the
2D and rotor aerodynamics modelling of active ﬂaps.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Several research projects have emphasizes the potential beneﬁts of a new generation
of a turbine blade which can reduce the cost per kWh produced. Reducing the cost
of wind turbine blades, the cost per kWh will be reduced but only in a small amount
of the total[2]. However, technological improvements can reduce the load and increase
the aerodynamic performance by including active aerodynamic devices distributed along
the blades[6]. The reduction of the cost in this case will be higher as rotor loads aﬀect
the loading of other components, such as tower, drive train, required materials, main-
tenance costs and system reliability[2]. Mainly, there are two methods for control and
reduce the rotor loads which are the passive and the active control. To achieve passive
load control the ﬂuctuation in wind speed is overcome through the passively adapting
aeroelastic response of the rotor blades[2]. Though, with active control, the loads are
reduced by adjusting the aerodynamic properties of the blades based on appropriate
sensor inputs[2]. However, such systems are not necessarily easy to maintain[2]. The
active control oﬀers more ﬂexibility especially when has to do with unsteady changes of
the ﬂow state[2] and should be capable to be operated in each blade at any azimuthal
position and any span-wise point by aerodynamic control devices. This kind of control
is often referred as ’smart rotor control’. Active aerodynamic mechanism such as active
ﬂaps are able to change the aerodynamic forces acting on the the blade length locally by
modifying the shape of the airfoil section[6].Thus, with these devices the aerodynamic
loading varies through the blade span and the reduction of blade fatigue loads is in a
more detailed way than the modern blade pitch control[2]. The ﬂuctuations of the aero-
dynamic loads arise from the variations of the wind. Adaptive trailing edge ﬂap (ATEF)
devices modiﬁes the shape of the airfoil by deﬂecting the trailing edge of the airfoil. This
deﬂection leads to a better aerodynamic performance which corresponds to an increase
in the output power of the wind turbine and lower the cost per kWh. In addition, the
fatigue damage is decreased, thus lower structural requirements are needed. As a result,

the cost of energy produced by the wind turbine is reduced[6]. The potential beneﬁts of
such advanced controls for wind turbines are considered necessary to be analyzed.
A ﬂap actuation system, the Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge Flap (CRTEF), for dis-
tributed load control on a wind turbine blade has been developed in the period from
2006 to 2013 at DTU. A ﬁrst prototype has been tested in the Velux wind tunnel in
2009. A second prototype has been tested in the rotating rig facility in 2014. The pur-
pose of the project is to analyze existing experiment results in order to investigate the
aerodynamic performance of the ﬂap prototype systems. Modelling with 2D unsteady
airfoil aerodynamic tools will also be pursued for comparison and veriﬁcation purposes.
This project includes the post-processing of wind tunnel and rotating rig test data of
active ﬂaps, the 2D and rotor aerodynamics modeling of active ﬂaps.
The ﬁrst chapter presents the setup of the VELUX wind tunnel test, the processing of
the experimental data and the aerodynamic analysis. The eﬀectiveness of the CRTEF
in the steady state is presented, which is the main objective. The results from the ex-
periment are compared with simulated results.
The second chapter presents the unsteady aerodynamic analysis of the experimental data
from the VELUX wind tunnel test. The airfoil section with the adaptive trailing edge
ﬂap is deﬂected following a smooth deformation shape. These unsteady ﬂap steps are
compared with the ATEFlap aerodynamic model, an engineering dynamic stall model
including the eﬀects of trailing edge ﬂap deﬂection[6]. Finally, there is a comparison
between the step results from the VELUX wind tunnel test and the step results from
the ATEFlap model.
In the last chapter, the setup of the rotating test rig is described and the calibration
procedure of the data is explained. The objectives are presented, along with the eﬀec-
tiveness of the CRTEF system under realistic inﬂow conditions. The steady and the ﬂap
step cases are illustrated, a comparison between the rotating test rig and the VELUX
wind tunnel test was made and the results from the test are the proof of concept of the
ﬂap system in atmospheric conditions.

2.1
Introduction
A development work started at Risø DTU in 2006 and the main objective was to develop
a robust and eﬃcient ﬂap system for implementation on MW turbines[10]. This work
started due to the fact that there is lack of technology solutions for ﬂap control for
wind turbines[10]. The design of the CRTEF relates to a ﬂap made of elastic material,
like rubber or plastic, with suitable reinforced voids that can be controlled by means
of compressed air or a liquid and in this way give the desired deﬂection to the ﬂap[10].
In addition, the basic design is considered to be simple without any moving mechanical
parts, and this makes the maintenance easier if the ﬂap implemented in a MW wind
turbine[10]. A number of prototypes with a chord of 150mm have been manufactured and
tested. The ﬂaps that was used for the VELUX wind tunnel test were manufactured in
silicone rubber with size of chord 15cm and spanwise length 30cm suited for a NACA0015
airfoil section. The total chord of the airfoil is 1m.
2.2
The test in the VELUX tunnel
The prototypes that tested in the VELUX tunnel in December 2009 have an active
ﬂap of 150mm, a spanwise length of 30cm suited for a NACA0015 airfoil section with a
total chord of 1m resulting in a ﬂap relative percentage of 15%. The airfoil NACA0015
that was used is symmetrical airfoil. The NACA0015 is considered to be thin airfoil,
and the thin airfoil theory is applicable. The prototype that was tested in the VELUX

2.3
Correction of the experimental data
As mentioned previously, the steady-state pressure distributions near the trailing edge
are missing.
Two methods were used for ﬁnding the missing pressure distributions,
the extrapolation method and the oﬀset method. In mathematics, extrapolation is the
process of estimating beyond the original observation range the value of a variable on the
basis of its relationship with another variable. The extrapolation has high uncertainty
and risk of producing meaningless results. The polynomial extrapolation is considered
to be more reliable in this problem. A polynomial curve can be created through the
entire known data or just near the end. For this problem, only data near to the end
is used, so the results to be more trustable. The resulting curve can then be extended

The oﬀset method that is used to predict the missing pressure distributions is considered

XFOIL is an airfoil design and analysis tool. The user may input an airfoil shape from
a ﬁle and XFOIL will build the appropriate coordinate ﬁle. Also, the user can make
changes to inviscid/viscous properties such as Mach number and Reynolds number (Re).

lift coeﬃcient (Cl), drag coeﬃcient (Cd), and moment coeﬃcient (Cm) in the form of a
saved polar ﬁle[9]. XFOIL was chosen for this project because it gives results much more
quickly than more advanced CFD programs and still provides results accurate enough
to be a good design tool, and because it allows the user to simulate the eﬀects of adding
ﬂaps to an airfoil[9]. The limitations of XFOIL are that it works for two dimensions only,

2.5
VELUX wind tunnel boundary corrections
The VELUX tunnel is open jet type, and is not built especially for 2D airfoil testing[8].
Therefore, boundary corrections should be applied for the wind tunnel ﬂow conditions.
The imposition of the tunnel setup, causes the ﬂow around the airfoil to diﬀer from
what it would have been like in an unlimited free ﬂow[8]. Because of this situation,
VELUX tunnel boundary corrections should be applied for downwash and streamline
curvature[1]. Solid walls do not bound the ﬂow, which is then free to diverge down-
stream of the airfoil section. The curvature of the ﬂow induces drag and inﬂuences the
geometrical angle of attack over the airfoil[1]. In the case of the VELUX tunnel, the

3.1
Introduction
This chapter presents the unsteady aerodynamic analysis of the experimental data from
the VELUX wind tunnel test and is compared with simulated results. The airfoil sec-
tion NACA0015 with the adaptive trailing edge ﬂap is deﬂected following a smooth
deformation shape. These unsteady ﬂap steps are compared with the ATEFlap aero-
dynamic model, an engineering dynamic stall model including the eﬀects of trailing
edge ﬂap deﬂection[6].
The model computes steady and dynamic response for a 2D
airfoil undergoing arbitrary motion and ﬂap deﬂection[6]. In the attached ﬂow, the un-
steady dynamics are based on Gaunaa’s work while the dynamic stall model is based on
Bedddoes-Leishmann-type of model[6]. In this dynamic stall model the total lift force is
analyzed as the weighted sum of the fully attached and fully seperated ﬂow component[6].
These two ﬂow components, are derived from the steady state lift characteristics of the
airfoil with the trailing edge ﬂap[6]. A use of a preprocessor is necessary and a manually
correction of the data is taking place, so that any possible discontinuities that can be
occur with no physical meaning to be corrected[6]. The algorithms that are used by
the preprocessor and the ATEFlap engineering model are described. Finally, there is a
comparison between the step results from the VELUX wind tunnel test and the step
results from the ATEFlap model.

3.2
Preprocessor for ATEFlap model
The input that is imported in the peprocessor is two sets of data. The ﬁrst set is the clean
airfoil data which contains the steady state lift, drag and moment coeﬃcient at various
angles of attack without ﬂap angle. The second set is named ﬂap delta-steady data and
contains the steady state variation induced in the lift, drag and moment coeﬃcient at
various angles of attack and ﬂap deﬂections[6]. The Bedddoes-Leishmann dynamic stall
model split the steady state lift coeﬃcient Clst into the fully attached component Clatt,
and the fully seperated component Clfs. The fully attached lift corresponds to the case
where the viscous eﬀects of the ﬂow seperation are neglected[6]. The inﬂuence of each
component depends on the steady seperation function fst, as it can be seen in equation
3.1.

3.3
ATEFlap aerodynamic model
The ATEFlap model computes the lift, moment and drag coeﬃcient in an airfoil that
undergoes arbitrary motion and tailing edge ﬂap deﬂection[6]. The model analyzes the
aerodynamic response both steady and dynamic. The dynamic eﬀects that the model
captures can be spit into:
• Added mass eﬀects(potential ﬂow): describe the forces that comes from the acceler-
ation of the ﬂuid due to motion of the airfoil and ﬂap(no memory eﬀects exist in this
term)
• Wake dynamic eﬀect(potential ﬂow): describe the memory eﬀects of the vorticity shed
into the wake(e.g. change of aoa or ﬂap angle)
• Dynamic stall eﬀects: describe the dynamic of the forces on an airfoil that undergoes
ﬂow seperation

4.1
Introduction
Tests of a prototype Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge Flap (CRTEF) are performed
on the rotating test rig at the Risø campus of DTU[3]. The objectives and the general
description of the test are presented in this chapter. The setup of the test is described
and the post-processing of the data is discussed. Furthermore, results of steady and
ﬂap step cases are presented.
The performance of the smart blade technology is an
important section of the project. The VELUX test of an airfoil with ﬂap system was
presented in the previous chapter and proved that the actuation concept works in a wind
tunnel. However, there is big change between a wind tunnel testing on a stationary blade
section to full scale turbine application[3]. The main objective of the test is to capture
the behavior of the controllable rubber trailing edge ﬂap (CRTEF) system under realistic
inﬂow conditions. Thus, the rotating test rig has been developed and the aerodynamic
analysis of the CRTEF is compared between the VELUX test and the rotating rig test.
Finally, the characteristics of the ﬂap tests are the proof of principle of the ﬂap system
in atmospheric conditions and the aerodynamic performance of the CRTEF in free wind
is an important supplement to VELUX wind tunnel measurements.

The rotating test rig setup
The setup of the test is close to the rotating environment on a real turbine and have
the same unsteady inﬂow conditions and the size of the blade section is close to a full

to the VELUX wind tunnel test but high enough in order to make a noticeable change
in the lift coeﬃcient and in the aerodynamic performance of the blade.

5.1
Conclusion
A development work started at Risø DTU in 2006 and eﬃcient ﬂap system for implemen-
tation on MW turbines was developed. A number of prototypes have been manufactured
and tested in the VELUX wind tunnel and in the rotating rig facility. The present re-
search work has focused on the eﬀectiveness of the Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge
Flaps (CRTEF) on multi-MW wind turbines. The ﬂap eﬀectiveness has an average of
0.4526 in the VELUX wind tunnel in the steady state, close to the simulated results.
Additionally, the unsteady aerodynamic analysis has good agreement with the ATEFlap
aerodynamic model. In the rotating test rig, the eﬀectiveness of the CRTEF system un-
der realistic inﬂow conditions was presented. The CRTEF system has a ﬂap eﬀectiveness
close to 0.21 for both steady and ﬂap step cases. All in all, it can be said that the CRTEF
leads to a better aerodynamic performance which can increase the output power and to
lower the cost per kWh. Furthermore, possible reductions of the loads is practicable,
thus lower structural requirements are needed.
5.2

As a further work, the aeroelastic data has to be processed, along with aeroelastic
simulations. The CRTEF has now been developed to a stage where the functioning

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
