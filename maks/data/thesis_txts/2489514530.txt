Abstract
In this thesis, a method of designing membrane based micro resonators using
density based topology optimization is presented. The basic concepts of micro
resonators actuated by radiation pressure forces are introduced with a focus on
features relevant for design using topology optimization. The frequency response,
mode shapes and natural frequencies of the structures are calculated using the ﬁ-
nite element method, which employ a two step linear approach to take into account
prestressing of the membranes. The density based topology optimization method
used, uses a frequency averaged objective function which has gained popularity in
photonic cavity design and has been modiﬁed for membrane design. The sensi-
tivities of the objectives were derived using the adjoint method for cases with and
without prestress. The topology optimization method was implemented in a Mat-
lab code which uses a 20 degree of freedom Mindlin shell ﬁnite element formulation
and is able to design a resonator for a number of input parameters, such as desired
resonance frequency, material parameters, prestress, dimensions, boundary con-
ditions and damping parameters. Diﬀerent design examples using the developed
Matlab code are presented and discussed. More thorough work needs to be con-
ducted on using the method at a ﬁner discretization, accurate evaluation of the
designs performance using commercial software and or experiments.
Keywords:
Micro resonator · Topology optimization · Finite element analysis ·
MEMS · NEMS · Optomechanics · Prestressed membranes · Quality
factor · Matlab

Part I
Introduction
The ﬁeld of micro and nanomechanical resonators actuated by the radiation pressure
force exerted by light is a relatively new technology, which is found in the intersection of
engineering and fundamental science. As coupling of light to mechanical motion on the
micro-scale is only possible for systems with very small losses, an engineering challenge
arise in the design of these vibrating structures. In this thesis, we will investigate how
to design micro resonators using the density based topology optimization method.
Topology optimization has previously been used with success in the design of eﬃcient
resonating devices including optical [14, 17, 40] and microwave cavities [1], and is
believed also to be applicable in the design of micro resonators.
1
Project Overview
Initially, the goal was to ﬁnd a method for generating eﬃcient micro resonator de-
signs using a prototype code in Matlab and then move on to implementing the found
procedure in the TopOpt-Group’s PETSc-based large scale code [2] for high resolution
designs. However challenges, especially when modelling the prestress present in the
membrane resonators forced the author to cancel this part of the project. Whilst not
coming as a complete chock, topology optimization of dynamic problems, especially
maximizing the response at resonances is a highly sensitive problem, and thus require
special attention to help stabilize the convergence of the optimization algorithm. The
items below serve to give an overview of the diﬀerent aspects and, to some degree, the
chronology of the project.
• Created a ﬁnite element code-basis in Matlab for performing density based topol-
ogy optimization using plate, shell and 3D elements on ﬂat structured meshes.
• Implemented a continuum based linear shell element enhanced with Mixed In-
terpolation of Tensorial Components, Enhanced Assumed Strains and Assumed
Natural Strains.
• Investigated static topology optimization using a Mindlin ﬁnite element including
a proposal to inhibit the formation of hinge-like joints in the optimized designs
for clamped boundary conditions.
• Implemented a classical 20 degree of freedom Mindlin shell element.
• Implemented a two-step linear approach to account for stiﬀening due to prestress
using a stress stiﬀness matrix.
• Implemented diﬀerent objective functions including static and a frequency av-
eraged power based dynamic compliance based on the formulation for optical
cavity design in [17].

1.1
Structure of the thesis
The thesis has been composed as follows. In Part I, the design problem along with basic
background information on micro resonators sets the stage for the work conducted on
ﬁnite element modeling and topology optimization as a design tool for micro resonators.
In Part II, the necessary ﬁnite element modeling for simulating the response of a micro

code. In the last part, we conclude on the work conducted and suggest future work.

Introduction to Micro Resonators and Optomechanics
This section presents relevant concepts within micro- and nanomechanical resonators.
The text is not meant to be a comprehensive text on applications of micro resonators,
but a brief introduction from a mechanical engineering point of view, as the overall
goal of this work is developing a design methodology using topology optimization and
not details of applications and experiments.
2.1
Micro- and nanomechanical resonators
The search for eﬃcient designs of micro resonators is driven by their applications
in fundamental research and metrology, especially in force sensing applications. An
exciting application of micro resonators is within non-contact atomic force microscopy.
Here a probe is excited at a resonance frequency whilst being scanned across a specimen
at a small distance (0 −10 nm). The specimen could be a soft sample such as biological
tissue or an organic ﬁlm. The van der Walls forces present near the surface decrease the
resonant frequency of the probe, which is then used to sense the surface using a feedback
loop. This technique has been used to image single chain polymers which reside in
the nano-scale. Other sensing applications include that of force phenomena such as
gyroscopes and accelerometers [25] which utilize the same method of force interactions
aﬀecting the resonant properties of the sensor. Resonators in the micro- and nano-scale
can have many diﬀerent designs spanning from stings, beams, membranes and bulk
resonators, a review of diﬀerent designs can be found in [4]. In this work, we will design
membrane-based resonators which are to be manufactured by micromachining using
selective etching and deposition of a crystalline silicon based material. A schematic of
a basic membrane resonator can be seen in Figure 1, where the design domain for the
membrane part of the resonator, attached to a relatively thick substrate is shown. The
laser beam, which is used to excite the mechanical modes of the structure, is aligned
with the center of the resonator which explains the term optomechanics, coupling
optics to mechanics.

2.2
Optical actuation
While one can easily imagine the excitation of a micromechancial resonator by an
acoustic pressure, mechanical or electrodynamic force, the resonators designed in this
project are to be excited by a radiation pressure force. The force exerted by light on
a surface is referred to as a radiation pressure force and results from the momentum
carried by the photons. This behavior was ﬁrst observed by Kepler, who in the 17th
century noticed that dust trails of comets point away from the sun.
The assertion
however, that electromagnetic radiation carries a momentum and thus can exert a
pressure, was ﬁrst to be published in 1862 by Maxwell [4]. The momentum carried by
a photon is calculated p  h/λ, where h is Planck’s constant and λ is the wavelength.
Except for large scale problems in astronomy, these properties of light are usually too
small to be usefulin mechanicalsystems [8]. Recently severalsuccessful optomechanical
resonators have been designed, which convert the momentum carried by a laser beam to
mechanical motion if the loss associated with the vibration is suﬃciently small. In order
to tune the frequency of the laser light used to actuate the optomechanical resonator,
an acousto-optic modulator3 can be used to create pulsed laser light. By adjusting the
frequency of the pulsed light, it is therefore possible to excite the mechanical modes of
the resonator. Observation of the mechanical motion of a micro resonator is typically
achieved via laser interferometry.

energy lost in a cycle of oscillation, alternatively the ratio of the resonance frequency
to the bandwidth at half the peak value [25, chapter 1].

Clamping loss
One of the hardest losses to quantify, is the loss associated with the
clamping or boundary conditions of the resonator. There exist some empirically and
analytically based expressions for evaluating the loss associated with clamping for both
beam and membrane resonators which take into account the boundary, in this case
substrate material, and geometry [25]. However for more intricate shapes, accurate
estimation of the clamping loss can only be found using experiments. A very eﬃcient
way of removing losses associated with the clamping condition is a design by which the
vibrations are suppressed close to the boundary. This can be achieved by locating the
resonant mode inside a phononic bandgap structure, thus inhibiting the propagation
of elastic waves to the boundary [36, 35].
Intrinsic loss
The loss intrinsic to the material can be divided into two categories,
frictional loss and fundamental loss.
In this work, only frictional loss will be con-
sidered, as this is typically what can be modelled in simulations of micro resonators
[18, 35]. Frictional loss intrinsic to the material typically results from imperfections in
the crystalline grain structure. The fundamental loss covers more advanced physical
phenomena present within an ideal material on the atomic lattice level. Crucially, the
fundamental losses typically become signiﬁcant when the frequency of the vibrations
enter the GHz-regime [25]. Implementation of intrinsic loss in the ﬁnite element model
of a resonator is discussed in Section 3.5.

Performance indicators
When looking into the attributes characterising a well per-
forming resonator, there exist a few important features. Naturally, the quality factor
is a crucial and popular indicator of performance as this characterize the degree to
which the loss aﬀects the resonator. There exist some guidelines for the Q-factor and
quality frequency Q f -factor which must be exceeded in order to achieve so called room
temperature quantum domain optomechanics. Also more practically oriented aspects
are important, such as a manufacturable structure in which stresses resulting from
the prestress does not exceed the yield stress [35, 18]. It is also desirable to have a
highly localised mode in a non-dense frequency spectrum, this is in particular why
the phononic crystal based resonator has achieved such high popularity, as it creates a
resonator with a very high natural frequency of the low order localized modes in the
central defect.

the equilibrium equations, makes the total potential energy stationary with respect to
small admissible variations of displacement. The total potential energy Π is the sum of
strain energy stored in a deformed body Ωand the potential of applied forces W. For

Element Types for Topology Optimization of Plates and
Membranes
Structures where one dimension is much smaller than the others can often modelled
using shell elements where the 3D continuum is represented by mid-surface nodes
instead of surface nodes. The displacement of the structure is then represented by
ﬁve degrees of freedom, three displacements and two rotations at each node. These
simpliﬁcations imply that lines along the thickness direction remains straight but can
rotate with respect to the shell mid-surface, which is consistent with Mindlin plate
theory assumptions. The shell elements assume that strain normal to the surface of the
structure is negligible. As a recent DTU master project [34], had success using a con-
tinuum based shell element for compliance minimization of large shell structures, the
continuum based hexahedral shell element described in [15] was also implemented.
Eventually it was decided to use the Mindlin Shell based quadrilateral element de-
scribed in [23, chapter 16] which has a computational advantage over the continuum
based element having 20 degrees of freedom as opposed to 24 and ease of the stress
stiﬀness implementation. Advantages of more advanced shell elements such as the
continuum based or MITC shell elements [5, chapter 5.4], could have been further ex-
ploited, however for structured ﬂat meshes, it is not anticipated to experience large
gains in accuracy, and unfortunately time didn’t allow for further investigations of el-
ement types. Classically, choice of elements for topology optimization are based on
the the main characteristic, that ﬁnite elements should be as computationally eﬃcient
as possible whilst capturing essential properties of the physical problem. This means
that many cheap elements are used to get suﬃcient accuracy, while providing a ﬁne
representation of the resulting design without large computational cost per element.
This means that topology optimization often use 1st order rectangular or brick-type
elements. This however is slightly challenged by problems in dynamics, which can be
highly design sensitive and thus small inaccuracies with certain elements can dramati-
cally alter the response of the system in post-processing using commercial software or
experiments.

Brick elements and continuum based shell elements
While the Mindlin shell can generally be referred to as a degenerated formulation
of a continuum element, it can be interesting to compare the performance to that of
a brick element and also a more advanced continuum shell element with diﬀerent
enhancements. Thus two other element types were implemented
• The 8-node continuum based shell element described in [15] which uses three
diﬀerent enhancements to improve the properties of the element to eliminate
locking phenomena:
1. Enhanced Assumed Strain (EAS), which introduces additional degrees of
freedom locally in each element which serve to inhibit shear locking behavior
of the element. Either 5, 8 or 11 additional degrees of freedom are added
to the strain ﬁeld.
Including EAS complicates the process of calculating
the strains and thus stresses a bit, as the displacements associated with the
incompatible strains needs to be evaluated.
2. Assumed Natural Strain (ANS), which serves to avoid locking in the out of
plane normal strain by alternative interpolation functions that interpolate
the strain found at four mid-plane tying points.
3. Mixed Interpolation of Tensorial Components (MITC), which employs an
alternative interpolation of the transverse shear strains using tying points
and resulting alternative interpolation functions.
• The classical 8-node brick element enhanced with incompatible modes (EAS)
according to the description in [23, chapter 6.5-6.7].

Part III
Topology Optimization
This marks the beginning of the interesting part of the thesis, where the density based
topology optimization method used is introduced. Density based topology optimiza-
tion was ﬁrst conceived in the seminal papers [6, 42] and is used to turn a 0 (void) or 1
(solid) material distribution problem on a domain into a continuous problem relating
material properties such as stiﬀness and mass to a continuous density variable ρe, for
each element in a ﬁnite element discretization of the design domain. The goal of the
method is naturally to form a design where material either exist or does not exist at
every point in the design domain, also known as a 0-1 design. This is achieved by
using a penalization on material properties of elements having intermediate densities,
making it infeasible in the optimization objective to attain designs with intermediate
densities10. Further measures to counter intermediate densities includes thresholding
or projections of density values or other properties of the ﬁnite element model. The con-
cepts presented in the following sections have been implemented in a Matlab program
which in large is based on the ideas introduced in [26, 3] with regard to implementation
of the optimization routine and assembly of ﬁnite element equations.
One of the ﬁrst applications of topology optimization for structural problems outside
of compliance minimization, was in the optimization of natural frequencies.
This
problem is interesting in the design of machinery and structures under periodic loading
conditions, where it is of interest to distance natural frequencies of the structure from
sources of periodic loading. In this case, the aim is to design an eﬃcient resonator,
meaning that the objective is to maximize the dynamic response at a certain frequency.

8.4
Filtering of design variables
To avoid the formation of artiﬁcial stiﬀness phenomena by checkerboarding [7], a re-
striction is imposed on the design by ﬁltering the design variables based on neighboring
values. This means, that the stiﬀness in one point depends on the material distribution
in a neighborhood deﬁned around this point. Only using the density ﬁltering, naturally
results in a transition region between void and solid material depending on the ﬁlter
radius. For many problems such as compliance design, this transition region can be
removed in post-processing whilst maintaining a similar performance. For more sensi-
tive problems such as those involving prestress and dynamics, simple post-processing
such as identifying holes and other features using a iso-density curve [27] may alter
the performance signiﬁcantly to an extent where they cannot be considered a solution
to the posed problem. Another challenge found by the author is the nature of the dy-
namic problems to promote grey areas. To mitigate this, projection methods or density
thresholding is used to end up with designs having a 0-1 material distribution.

8.5
Optimization algorithm: The Method of Moving Asymptotes
There exist many diﬀerent methods for solving the non-linear and often highly non-
convex optimization problem in Equation 8.1 both gradient and non-gradient based.
One of the most popular gradient based algorithms in topology optimization is The
Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA)13 developed by Krister Svanberg [31] which
is an iterative non-linear programming method.
The algorithm is capable of solve
optimization problems with a large number of variables and multiple constraints.
In basic terms, MMA works by replacing the optimization problem by an approx-
imate convex subproblem where objective and constraint functions are replaced by
locally approximate explicit convex functions. The moving asymptotes refers to asymp-
totes which bound the approximation and thus control the step size of the optimization.
Because MMA is gradient based, one needs to calculate the sensitivities with respect to
each design variable at each iteration, which is done for the considered objective and
constraint functions in the following sections.

Topology Optimization of Micro Resonators
Now that the fundamentals needed for the analysis and topology optimization of
prestressed structures have been introduced, the attention is directed towards utilizing
these concepts for optimization of the dynamic response of a structure. The goal in
designing optomechanic micro resonators is to achieve the design which oscillates
with the smallest possible loss at a given frequency. A straight forward way of using
topology optimization to design an eﬃcient resonator is to maximize the Q-factor which
has been done in [10] for the fundamental mode, resulting in an optimized trampoline
type resonator similar to that found in [18].
Another intuitive approach would be
to maximize the dynamic compliance which can be formulated in several ways [13].
This however, results in a highly non-convex design sensitive problem unsuitable for
gradient based optimization as shown in Figure 9. The dynamic compliance approach
has been used with success for problems in optics using an energy objective function
with additional artiﬁcial convexifying damping [14]. In this work, a similar approach
is followed using a frequency averaged objective function. The beneﬁt of using an
objective function based on properties of the forced response is, that it does not limit
the optimization scope to a speciﬁc mode of the resulting structure, hopefully achieving
structures which have not been previously seen using the optimization of eigenvalues
done in [10, 20, 19] and many others. One of the challenges in performing maximization
of the frequency response of a structure, is the tendency of the design to attain non-
physical designs, where there is no unbroken load path unless a two material model is
used as in [16], so this must be dealt with as well.

Part IV
Results
In this part, we present and discuss the results obtained using the topology optimization
method introduced in the previous part and append a few extra features. It is important
to stress, that the optimization problem, even with the convexifying artiﬁcial damping,
is very sensitive. The extra features were implemented due to issues encountered when
using the method. Firstly, adding the volume constraint function to the objective serves
to get rid of disconnected islands of material in the designs. Secondly, as the simple
projected design variables have a tendency to contain very small features only a few
elements in size, the robust topology optimization formulation was used to obtain
structures with length scales. This part of the thesis will in some respects feel quite
lengthy as many ﬁgures have been included which could have been removed for brevity
or organized in diﬀerent ways. However as we are dealing with dynamic studies, there
are a lot of diﬀerent aspects to the performance of the designs. This is why mode-shapes
and frequency responses have been included in most cases, because this qualitative
behavior and not just resonance frequencies and quality factor are important.

Design Domain and Material Parameters
There exist an abundance of possible dimensions and material parameters for micro
membrane resonators. These are based on the diﬀerent applications and especially
operational frequencies of the device. The choice of the design domain size is not
completely straight forward, however many membrane resonators have side-lengths in
the 1 mm range and thickness in the 50 nm range and are exposed to 1 GPa prestress.
Thus the chosen design domain is (a × b × c)  (1 × 1 × 50 · 10−6)mm3, in accordance
with Figure 1. Clamped boundary conditions are imposed on all edges of the resonator
design domain. The material properties resemble that of a silicon nitride ﬁlm (Si3N4),
which is often used to fabricate micro resonators [35, 18], having a modulus of elasticity
E0  250 GPa, density ς0  3100 kg/m3 and a Poisson ratio ν  0.23. As experiments
have revealed, that it is highly eﬃcient in the objective to attain disconnected structures,
diﬀerent methods of enforcing passive areas to help promote connected designs are
sketched in Figure 10. Both the density ﬁlter and threshold projection with threshold
η  0.5 is used to get rid of intermediate densities which tend to form.
To reduce computational cost, only one quarter of the design domain is modelled
and symmetry boundary conditions are imposed. Another reason to reduce the model
in this fashion, is due to the point excitation in the center of the design domain will
only be able to excite modes having anti-nodes at this location. This means that more
modes than the ones found in analysis of the reduced system are present, but are not
taken into consideration due to the location of the load point.

Optimizing for higher frequencies
A typical frequency for optomechanics is above
100 kHz [4], corresponding to a very high order mode for the non-prestressed square
membrane case. Increasing the design frequency into the optomechanics range and
using the passive cross gives the designs shown in Figure 14. It is interesting to note
the rings forming in the design and the formation of a very isolated mode at the design
frequency when inspecting the frequency response. This is likely attributed to vibration
suppressing features of the concentric ring-like geometric features. Ring-like features
like those in Figure 14, are often seen in topology optimized optical cavities like those
designed in [17] also known as a Bragg-mirror, which reﬂects waves having a speciﬁc
wave-length.
Inspecting the mode shapes reveal, that in most cases we see a highly localized
mode at the loading point, thus giving similar characteristics to that of a phononic
crystal based membrane resonator in [36]. This suggests, that in fact damping will be
smaller in practical experiments, as damping due to the clamping condition is likely
very small. These interesting designs could possibly allow for the creation of eﬃcient
optomechanical resonators without using prestress to increase resonance frequencies

Recapitulation of Results
Generally, it has been shown that topology optimization in fact has potential to be
used in the design of membrane micro resonators. For the non-prestressed case, very
interesting designs are formed which seem to have eﬃcient wave-inhibiting properties
allowing for a very localized mode, similar to that seen in micro resonators using
techniques from phononic engineering.
For the prestressed case, the optimization
problem is a lot harder, and not nearly as interesting structures are formed. Primarily
the optimizer seems to take advantage of the imposed passive cross, and use it to form
a trampoline-like resonator embedded in a suﬃciently stiﬀgrounding structure to fulﬁl
the constraint on static compliance. If no static constraint or passive areas are imposed,
concentric rings are also formed for the prestressed case, however this structure is not
connected and thus not a practical design. The robust topology optimization method
works to promote length scales in the designs, this is especially useful for the non-
prestressed resonator which had a tendency to end up having very small feature sizes.

Part V
Conclusion
This project set out to use topology optimization to design micro resonators. The work
relies on shell ﬁnite element analysis of the membranes which was based on a two-step
linear modelling of the prestressed membranes. Three ﬁnite element types were imple-
mented and their performance in comparison to a benchmark problem was evaluated,
resulting in the choice of the 20-d.o.f. Mindlin shell element. After verifying that the
implementation was able to model the physics needed within acceptable tolerances, the
next step was to deﬁne the optimization problem, objective and constraint functions
allowing for density based topology optimization to be used for resonator design. A
method was proposed using a frequency averaged objective function inspired by suc-
cessful applications in photonic cavity design. Several tweaks were needed in order
to use this objective function, as there exist several weaknesses in the optimization
formulation and ﬁnite element modelling, which were exploited by the optimization
algorithm. A large part of the work was spend tweaking the method to enforce connec-
tivity of the designs. This involved experimenting with the constraints, passive areas in
the design domain and appending the volume constraint function to the objective. For
the non-prestressed cases, the method was able to produce some interesting designs
with band-gap like characteristics. For the prestressed cases, the diﬀerent restrictions
imposed in large part dictated the designs, resulting in trampoline like resonators em-
bedded in a supporting structure. A possible way to counter this, would be to do a
start the optimization using the passive cross and at some point reintroduce the passive
design variables. Unfortunately, time and the work on the other parts of the project
didn’t allow for such a study.
Generally, the performance of the designed prestressed resonators were not com-
petitive with trampoline resonators and phononic crystal based resonators, but are
to some extent comparable with the simple membranes. However this is somewhat
hand-waving, as the value of the loss factor when modelling intrinsic damping is likely
design dependent [35, 37]. Reﬁning the method using robust topology optimization
showed some promising designs with better deﬁned length scales. The really inter-
esting designs using the robust method were at a higher resolution where the current
Matlab problem cannot solve the eigenvalue problem, and thus we cannot evaluate their
performance. But it is expected that they perform similar to the non-robust designs.
To summarize, the method introduced for the most part works and is able to produce
interesting resonator designs targeting a speciﬁc design frequency, which to the authors
knowledge has not previously been seen. This is one of the most important features
of the topology optimization method in general; it’s ability to generate new designs
without prejudice. A major drawback of the proposed method however, is the tendency
for non-physical designs to be very eﬃcient in the optimization and thus very careful
consideration must be made to counter this.
Future work
This work has demonstrated the possibility of designing membrane
based micro resonators using an objective function based on a frequency response

• Thorough investigation of important aspects such as studies using higher reso-

material is needed at zero bending moment points.
