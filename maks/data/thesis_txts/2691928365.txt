Abstract
Circumaural open headphones, also known as open-air headphones, tend to have an open
acoustic design with the back of the cup left open where the loudspeaker driver radiates into
the environment, making the airflow in and out of the cup. Another major design signifi-
cance is that the acoustic load between the front and back of the driver is shorted to have
a lighter ear pressure when coupled to pinna to make a comfortable and prolonged listening
experience compared to closed headphones. Such headphones can produce a wide frequency
response across the audible frequency range from 20 Hz to 20kHz. This study attempts
to describe the important acoustic design considerations for designing open headphones to
cater an extended frequency response at a suﬀicient sound pressure level. The scope of the
project is to investigate the behavior of open headphone in low frequency region where the
whole system approximated as a lumped element when compared to the wavelength where
its spatial properties can be reduced to a single point.This limits the interested frequency
range up to a few kHz.
The project starts with analyzing an existing open headphone present in the market.
Initially, a sketch of the existing headphone model has been developed to understand the
physical structure of the open headphone. Then a measurement setup is developed to under-
stand the acoustic properties of each of the design parameters that influenced the frequency
response on the existing open headphone. In continuation to the investigation, a lumped pa-
rameter model of the open headphone is developed in an electric circuit simulator simulated
and compared with measured results to get a detailed understanding of each parameter’s
influence within the frequency range of interest.
The study from simulations and measurements on existing open headphone reveals that
the short circuit holes design has a major influence on the overall frequency response, partic-
ularly at low frequency region. The center hole and vent holes behind the driver reveals the
importance of rear volume behind the diaphragm region. The acoustic tuning paper revealed
a significant contribution in improving low-frequency region. It is recommended to estimate
a lower front volume as it influences the shift in resonance frequency of the driver. The leaks
from cushion revealed the importance of low frequency roll-off at a very low frequency region.
Further investigations on Thiele & Small parameters measured in free air revealed that the
mechanical properties of the driver also includes the acoustical properties of vent holes and
tuning paper present behind the driver. Hence, the vaccum pot measurement setup is used
for an accurate measurement of mechanical properties of the driver.
Finally, a working prototype has been attempted to implement on one of Jabra’s closed
headphones with certain design modifications based on the study and findings from the
investigations on existing open headphones. Initially, the model was simulated with some

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The invention of headphones revolutionized the consumer audio segment. Over the years,
the design improvements in headphones took various shape and form factors based on per-
sonal needs, which led to better designs. Current trends in audio technology involved in
the personalized audio listening experience and the boom in virtual and augmented reality
have pushed the consumer audio market, especially in the headphone segment. Based on
the wearing style and comfort, headphones are categorized as circumaural or over the ear,
supra-aural or on-ear, concha earphones, and in earphones. Further, the circumaural and
supra-aural headphones are broadly classified based on their acoustic designs as open and
closed headphones.
Open headphones in general, have open cup designs where the back of the driver is
exposed to radiate into the environment. In contrast, in closed headphones, the cups are
entirely sealed to isolate sound from the environment. Each has its advantages and disad-
vantages on the environment where they are used. Unlike loudspeakers, headphones and
earphones were not mainstream research focus in terms of electroacoustical design concerns,
especially for open headphones. With advancements in signal processing, various research
works were carried in further developments of closed headphone designs, which led to the
development in active noise cancellation (ANC) and wireless headsets. Though closed head-
phones isolate external noise environments, they usually have a higher acoustic impedance
effect at low frequency. This leads to an increased sound pressure level at low frequencies
and in turn to an unnatural perception of an individual’s voice also known as the occlusion
effect [1].
Though very little literature touches the acoustic designs on open headphones, they
significantly lack component measurements when modeling such designs. Nevertheless, the
research works from Carl Poldy [2] [3] [4] have paved the path on investigating key design
considerations in the electroacoustic aspects of open headphones.

This project focuses on understanding the low frequency behaviour of open headphones
through lumped parameter modelling. This can be achieved by equivalent electrical circuit
modelling through SPICE simulations. Similarly, such approaches were carried by [3] [5] [6]
[8]. The fundamental concepts of electrical mechanical and acoustical elements and their
relation in lumped-parameter modelling is explained in the following sections.
2.1
Electrical impedance of passive elements
The electrical equivalent circuit model can be constructed using three passive electrical
elements, namely resistors (R), inductors (I) and capacitors (C). With these three basic
elements the acoustical behaviour in the low frequency regions of device can be modelled.
The acoustical analogies are further described in section 2.3. The intention is to make sure
the user is familiar with electrical networks, the electrical impedance behaviour of each
passive elements when connected in series and parallel when driven with voltage source is
the key priority of this section. Impedance is the opposition to flow of electric current. It
differs for inductors, capacitors and resistors. Ideally, the electrical impedance of resistor
is constant across the entire frequency spectrum. For inductor, the electrical impedance
is minimum at low frequency region and increases with frequency in linear fashion.
It’s
the complete opposite for capacitors, where the electrical impedance is maximum at low
frequencies and decreases with increasing frequency. So far, the impedance behaviour of
each passive electrical components has been described. More fun begins in understanding
the impedance behaviour when two or more passive elements are combined in series or in
parallel, which is briefly explained with illustrations in the following section.

Lumped Parameter Model
Lumped parameter modelling is an approach where complex structural elements can be mod-
elled into simplified ones. The lumped parameter approach is more appropriate to physical
system whose dimensions are comparatively small compared to the wavelength of sound.
The term lumped comes from electrical circuit theory. Almost any work involved in physical
system comprises of mechanical and acoustical systems. In our case, the electro-mechano-
acoustical behaviour of vibrating systems involving mass, spring, damper systems. Confined
acoustical systems where the dimensions are small compared to wavelength are vibrations
in single dimension. Therefore any mechanical or acoustical systems can be modelled to an
equivalent electrical circuit using the basic electric elements [12]. Various literature works on
lumped parameter modelling on headphones and loudspeakers are presented and discussed
using PSPICE a well known electric circuit simulator developed by Analog devices, a semi-
conductor manufacturing company. An overview of SPICE simulation is provided with an
illustration and how it aids to explore open headphone design has been explained in detail
in chapter 7.
Based on the works from Olson who first attempted the electroacoustical modelling
through lumped parameter approach. One of his works titled Dynamic analogies [12] ex-
plained the analogies of electrical mechanical and acoustical systems through networks of
passive electrical elements.
Later, this works were extended by Beranek [8] in modeling
loudspeaker along with their behaviour in different enclosure designs. W.Marshall Leach
used SPICE simulations for loudspeaker modelling [11]. Leach used a strategy to do con-
version between domains by implementing controlled sources. A Current Controlled voltage
Source (CCVS) is used to convert electrical current i to mechanical force f. Then to model
the conversion from mechanical velocity to acoustical volume velocity, a Current Controlled
Current Source (CCCS) is used. Similarly for the generated back pressure, a Voltage Con-
trolled voltage Source (VCVS) is used [11]. The present simulations were inspired from the
works of Leach [11] and Carl Poldy [4] that helped in understanding the acoustic behaviour
of open headphones.

3.2.1
Moving mass behaviour
The mechanical system of the loudspeaker driver comprises of the spider, outer suspen-
sion and the diaphragm. Consider the mechanical system of the loudspeaker driver unit as
mass spring damper system where the moving mass comprises of the diaphragm, the spider
functions as a damper and the suspension acts as a spring. When such a system is set in
motion, they tend to get displaced and released performing a simple harmonic motion. At
one particular frequency the excursion will be maximum and eventually gets died away due
to dampness in the system.
It is important to understand the behavior of such mechanical system when excited by
pure sinusoidal signals at constant amplitude levels at various frequencies. Below the reso-
nance frequency, the excitation is low and little force needed to displace the mass. The force
applied to deflect the spring stiffness is greater and hence this region is said to be stiffness
controlled. As said, the amplitude is maintained constant and so the velocity increases with
6 dB per octave up to the resonant frequency. Above the resonance frequency, the inertia
f the mass is greater than the stiffness of the spring and the system is is now said to be in
mass controlled region. From newton second law, For a constant force, a constant accelera-
tion is expected but as frequency rises, there is less time for acceleration to act for such a
force applied. Due to this, the velocity response drops of at -6 dB per octave beyond the
resonance frequency.
Finally, at resonance frequency, the system is dampness controlled
which is taken care by the mechanical damping present in the spider and outer suspension
of the driver.

driver tends to operate in the linear region. This situation does not prevail at high amplitude
levels where the driver tends to behave non-linearly causing extra signal components which
are not present in the input signal. The non-linearity behavior of driver is of least concerned
and ignored as the project is focused on investigating the linear operation of driver at low

4.2
Outer Ear functions
Before diving into the acoustic behaviour of headphones, it is recommended to get brief
overview of outer ear. The idea is not to get deep into the physiology of the outer ear but a
short understating on its functionality with respect to headphones performance.The human
ear is divided into three sections namely the outer ear, middle ear and the inner ear. The
outer ear consists of auricle also known as the pinna, the long narrow tube called the ear
canal and the tympanic membrane which is also referred as the ear drum. The pinna plays
a primary role in source localization and concha acts as an acoustic resonant cavity. The
pinna along with concha are responsible for collection and transmission of incoming sound
waves and redirects it to the ear canal. The ear canal is a short irregular tube like structure
measures approximately from 2.6 to 3.1 cm in length.
The ear canal has no effects in localization techniques rather it acts as a transmission
line from pinna to tympanic membrane. As a result, most of the low frequency behaviour is
of a compliant membrane up to 800 Hz. At above 2 kHz, quarter wave resonance starts to
occur and the sound waves are no more a plane as the wavelength his smaller than length
of ear canal. The locations of these resonance frequencies depends on the effective length
of ear canal. In addition to this, the shape of the pinna also has its effect on the pressure
response at ear drum. As sound travels through the tube with one end closed, maximum
pressure is felt at the end of the tube. Hence the ear drum is called as pressure detector
where the peaks in pressure during the quarter wave resonances are detected at ear drum.
The surface of ear drum not rigid and it has its impedance behaviour which tends to shape
the frequency response by damping the ear canal resonances.
In addition to this, extensive research has been carried in modelling the ear canal and ear
drum acoustic behaviours such as ear simulators, ear couplers for acoustic measurement that
are extensively used in hearing aids industry and consumer audio segments where a replica
of human ears are made to understand the ear drum impedance and occlusion effects [7] [13].
One such model of ear canal along with ear drum impedance is the IEC 711 couplers which
is used in recent times.
A lumped parameter modelling approach of IEC 711 coupler is
implemented in this project which are discussed in section 5.1

Acoustical behaviour of headphones when
compared to loudspeakers
The working principle of headphones when coupled to the ear canal is of pressure chamber
type where the performance of the driver is stiffness controlled whereas to the loudspeaker,
they are mass controlled beyond resonance frequency. Ideally, it is expected that the driver
displacement to be constant irrespective of frequency which leads to a constant sound pres-
sure level. Due to its coupling to ear canal resonances, the response is almost flat in the
low frequency upto 3 kHz where the wavelength of sound is large compared to dimensions
of the ear canal cavity. In loudspeakers, constant displacement is achieved below resonance
due to compliance dominance but the sound pressure level drops by 12 dB/octave due to
acceleration drop as discussed in subsection 3.2.1.
When comes to perceived listening experiences, sound from loudspeakers in rooms creates
an auralisation due to the combined response of both direct and early reflection sound that
reaches the listener. Additionally, the listener’s ears along with head and torso helps in source
localization which is prominent when listening to stereo systems due to the precedence or
Haas effect which is missing when listening with headphones. This was mentioned only for
a general understanding on perceptual differences and are irrelevant to the scope of this
project.

computing the transfer function of the measured response.

In this chapter, the simulation model of HD 559 is designed using SPICE circuit simula-
tor with an objective to understanding the behaviour of each design parameters and their
influence to overall frequency response in the low frequency region. The following sections
illustrates the approach carried in simulating the HD 559 open headphone along with illus-
trative results.
7.1
Simulation Design Methodology
The entire simulation is inspired from the extensive literature works of Carl Poldy’s Head-
phone chapter from the book [4]. Initially, the sketched model of Figure 5.2 is analysed
to understand how the components are interlinked in electrical, mechanical and acoustical
domains. The impedance analogy has been followed throughout the simulation where the
pressure and volume velocity are analogous to voltage and current in electric circuit. One
better strategy of Leach [11] is implemented where the entire circuit model is segmented into
electrical mechanical and acoustical domains and are interlinked using controlled sources as
mentioned in section 2.4. The final schematic of HD 559 model is shown in Figure 7.9.
7.1.1
Electrical and Mechanical domain
The electrical domain consists of electrical DC resistance in series with an inductor. One
end is connected to the input AC voltage source and the other end to the current controlled
voltage source (CCVS) denoted by H in SPICE components. This is to model the Bl factor
which is the transduction unit that converts from electric current to mechanical force. The
mechanical domain consists of mechanical mass of the diaphragm Mmd, mechanical com-
pliance Cms and mechanical resistance Rms of the suspension, all connected in series with
one end to back electromotive force (EMF) generated from the mechanical velocity of the
coil displacement (Bli) with current controlled voltage source (CCVS). The other end is
connected to a current controlled current source (CCVS) (denoted as F in SPICE compo-
nent section) and the same in acoustical domain with surface area of the diaphragm Sd as
coupling coeﬀicient. One can notice an additional voltage source (V3 and V2) as seen in the
schematic (Figure 7.9). They act as dummy generators to sense currents across these two

7.1.2
Acoustical domain
The acoustical components are identified from the sketch of HD 559. Careful placement of
the components in forming the circuit is the crucial part of the simulation. Any wrong place-
ment results in ambiguity and false positive results become problematic. To overcome this
dilemma, it is encouraged to think in volume velocity point of view. For instance, consider
the back holes in rear part of the driver. All 4 holes share a common plane and to think
in acoustic perspective, the volume velocity travels through all 4 rear holes at a time with
least effort. This can be modelled with all 4 holes branched in parallel combination. To sum
up, when components are placed one after other they are connected in series. If they share
common plane then they are placed in parallel. In the end, it is the overall structure that is
more important than the component values.

Design process
The prototype of an open headphone is developed based on the findings from the simula-
tions and measurements of HD 559 model. The model is developed on Jabra Evolve 2 85
headphone. The Elite 2 85 is a wireless closed headphone design that features Active Noise
Cancellation (ANC) intended for oﬀice use. The initial phase of design starts by sketching
the structure of the headphone by modifying the exciting design of evolve based on the
findings of design aspects from HD 559 model to design an open headphone prototype. The
sketched model is transformed to analogous electric circuit model in SPICE simulator whose
parameters are modified according to the physical dimensions of the headphone. The sim-
ulation is performed on two different Jabra owned drivers, a 32 ohm driver which is the
original driver present in evolve headphone model and an alternative 16 om driver. A brief

Discussion
The findings from the simulations and measurements on HD 559 open headphone design
has opened gates in understanding each design parameters along with the frequency range
where they get mostly influenced. Apart from the aforementioned list of key design con-
siderations, there has been one more important acoustic design factor that has been least
bothered or touched upon in this investigations. It is the rear volume behind the membrane
which plays a crucial role in driver displacement. The driver of HD 559 and the driver of
Jabra’s 32 Ωhad it’s own design significance in venting out the air volume from the cavity
behind the membrane leading to uniform pressure distribution in the cavity. If these centre
holes or air gaps had not been present in the design, then it would have become the most
important parameters in design consideration. This leads to be a considerable factor for a
driver manufacturer but when it comes to be a headphone designer then you are restricted
to making design modifications surrounding the driver assuming that the vendor had taken
all these parameters into consideration to make the driver.
Irrespective of the Thiele & Small parameters of the driver, it is the acoustical proper-
ties that governs the overall frequency response of the headphone. This is clearly evident
from the investigations on measured responses on existing open headphone and from the
modifications done on prototype which was shown in Figure 10.6. By tuning the acoustic
properties, the frequency response can be modified to achieve a target curve modelled for
that particular headphone.
Modelling an existing physical headphone and including the parameters by making an
analogous equivalent circuit model and later fit the simulated results with the measured
response provides a good understanding before getting the details of it. But in the end, the
lumped parameter modelling is a mere approximation to an actual physical model. The
physical dimensions are transformed to its analogous component values and then we tweak
certain parameter values to fit with measured response. By the time we tweak an initial
value, we have already changed its corresponding physical dimensions which shouldn’t be the
case. This reminds the operational limits of lumped parameter modelling approach. Even-
tually, in simulations modifying an existing circuit has it’s own restrictions and limitations
to replicate the scenarios in physical world. One evidence in supporting this argument is
the 4 vent holes along with centre hole modelling in HD 559 equivalent circuit model (refer
Figure 7.6b).
Finally, the accuracy of lumped parameter modelling is ambiguous at high frequencies.
Lumped parameter models hold true for frequencies were wavelength is larger than the phys-
ical dimensions. As the wavelength becomes smaller, modal resonances across the dimension
occurs and with these variation increase complex pressure distributions within the bound-
==PAGE:88==
76
11 Discussion
aries of the physical system. All these are not handled by lumped parameter model where
precision and boundary value problems needs to get solved. Hence a more refined model
needs to get replaced to start investigating the complex behaviour at high frequencies. These
investigations can be considered as research scopes for future researchers.
11.1
Future Research Focus
The present study on investigating the open headphone design i low frequency region has
channeled several research focus in each of the following areas
11.1.1
High frequency behavior using numerical methods
Understanding the limitations of lumped-parameter modelling has opened doors for numeri-
cal analysis to study the high frequency behavior for accurate analysis. Numerical solutions
such as Finite Element Methods(FEM) can be incorporated to model the acoustic behav-
ior of open headphone designs using well known simulations software such as COMSOL
multi-physics serves this purpose.
11.1.2
Research on perceptual sound quality evaluation
There has been a huge debate on understanding the difference in perceived sound on head-
phones when compared to loudspeakers listening in a room. Several attempts were made
to obtain diffuse field equalization by obtaining an averaged measured response with a mi-
crophone on a dummy head placed in a diffuse filed environment. The averaged response
can be used to adjust the measured headphone response by placing it on a dummy head
(HATS). With this the end user could perceive the sound as perceived from loudspeaker in
a room [16].
11.1.3
Research on estimation of Occlusion effect on open
headphones
The study on open headphones design considerations especially on the investigating short
circuit hole behavior and open back area had raised one significant question on headphone
openness. These together play a significant role in regulating the acoustic impedance in-
curred in the ear canal region. One particular study suggest that the occlusion effect is
observed less in open headphones compared to closed headphones [1]. The occlusion effect
can be estimated with a single value called occlusion index (OI) which is mean deviation of
the acoustic impedance relative to the acoustic impedance of the open artificial ear that is
used for the measurement [1].
==PAGE:89==
11.1 Future Research Focus
77
11.1.4
Research on transducer engineering
Present investigation is made on drivers using electrodynamic transducer principle. Nev-
ertheless being the most eﬀicient transducers there are design modifications attempted to
overcome the convectional design challenges. One particular attempt were made by Reson-
ado labs [17] called the flat core speaker (FCS) design where the conventional cylindrical
design has been replaced with planar voice coil placed between bar magnets. Apart from
electrodynmaic transducers principles, there were equally competing transducer designs such
as planar magnetic transducers, electrostatic transducers and recent trends on piezoelectric,
balanced armature transducers that work as an alternatives.

Conclusion
The project intended to identify the key acoustic design considerations for an open head-
phone design. A detailed study on an existing open headphone was attempted by modifying
each parameter’s existing design structure and measured its frequency response to observing
the modified behavior. Further, a simulation of the existing headphone design was designed
through SPICE circuit simulator to get a closer understanding of each parameter’s influence
on the frequency region. Based on the findings, It has been understood that the short circuit
model had an overall dominance in the low-frequency region. In addition, the importance
of Leaks from cushion is understood through simulation and their dominance to very low
frequency roll-off factor.
The influence of the mesh grille at the open back region influenced the low frequency
behavior. It indicated that the size of the perforated openings matters irrespective of the
open back area. Importance of front volume between the cushion area had been understood
that varying front volume leads to shifting the driver’s resonance frequency region indicating
the volume to be as low as possible. Further, the importance of vacuum pot measurement
provided an alternate measurement method to extract mechanical parameters of the head-
phone driver, neglecting its acoustical properties with high accuracy than the existing free
air measurement.
The importance of volume compliance behind the diaphragm was analyzed. Their in-
fluence in the low frequency region has been studied through modifications done at the
driver’s center hole and vent holes. Further, a prototype was designed and built based on
the investigations and findings from the existing open headphone. For this, two different
driver designs were analyzed. Later, it was realized that the acoustic properties determine
the overall frequency response of the headphone than its electrical and mechanical properties.
Finally, the limitations of lumped parameter model at the high frequency region were
mentioned, and the need for numerical modeling was explained. Finally, a future research
perspective in four significant areas in continuation with present findings on open headphones
was presented.
