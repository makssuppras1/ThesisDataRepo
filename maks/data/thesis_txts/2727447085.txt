Summary
Intrusion Detection Systems are computer systems capable of recognizing attacks and
intrusions. In the last twenty years their use has increased dramatically, especially in
contexts where defending resources and assets is extremely important. This has led as
a natural consequence to the increase of the numerous challenges related to IDS and
intrusion detection in general.
The main category of detection systems that will be addressed in the following thesis
work is Network Intrusion Detection Systems and in particular those that make use
of datasets for the training of their intrusion detection models. These datasets, to be
considered valid for use, must reflect certain qualities and possess specific properties.
Since it is not always possible to have these datasets publicly available and reflecting the
desired properties, it has been necessary to create tools and frameworks that generate
datasets starting from a background traffic into which a previously modeled synthetic
attack is injected.
However, the use of these tools and frameworks is a process that is prone to the
introduction of artifacts and defects in the datasets. Therefore, they must also comply
with certain requirements and properties to ensure the quality of the datasets. One of
the most sought-after qualities of forged synthetic datasets is realism and to achieve this,
the following thesis work focuses on modeling specific Denial of Service attacks using
OMNeT++ and the INET model, and incorporating a template generation process into
the ID2T framework, which is then used to forge synthetic datasets.

Introduction
1
Intrusion Detection Systems are computer systems, software or hardware, capable of
recognizing attacks and intrusions. The historical roots of Intrusion Detection Systems,
also known as IDS, date back to the 1980s, when there were created the first rudimen-
tary set of tools for identifying unwanted or malicious access to systems. At that time,
the foundations for modern intrusion detection systems were being laid and in the last
twenty years their use has increased dramatically, especially in contexts where defending
resources and assets is of the utmost importance. This has led as a natural consequence
to the increase of the numerous challenges related to IDS and intrusion detection in
general.
The main category of detection systems that will be addressed in the following the-
sis work is Network Intrusion Detection Systems, namely those sentinel systems that
are installed in strategic positions of a network, and in particular those that make use
of datasets for the training of their intrusion detection models. These datasets, to be
considered valid for use, must reflect certain qualities and possess specific properties.
Since it is not always possible to have these datasets publicly available and reflecting
the desired properties, it has been necessary to create tools and frameworks that generate
datasets starting from a background traffic into which a previously modeled synthetic
attack is injected.
However, the use of these tools and frameworks is a process that is prone to the
introduction of artifacts and defects in the datasets. Therefore, they must also comply
with certain requirements and properties to ensure the quality of the datasets.
Among others, one of the most sought-after qualities of synthetic datasets is realism.
1.1
Research questions and objectives
During the thesis work, we extensively used the Intrusion Detection Dataset Toolkit
framework, also known as ID2T [1]. This framework was first presented in 2015 as a
project initiated within the Telecooperation Lab of TU Darmstadt. Our main goal is
to create several models, to embed in ID2T, for generating different Denial of Service
attacks that are as realistic as possible.
==PAGE:12==
2
1 Introduction
Although we have to introduce certain assumptions and deal with several limitations
due to the complexity of the problem, they do not stop us from pursuing the challenges
and objectives that we have set out to achieve, and from answering our research ques-
tions.
The research questions that we asked ourselves during the project planning phase
were the following:
• Is there a way to model the interactions between hosts involved in a resource
exhaustion attack, and specifically Denial of Service, at the packet level and in a
realistic way?
• Is it possible to embed such modeling within the ID2T framework in a highly
customizable, efficient and transparent way for the end user?
• Is it also possible to propose a solution approach to modeling the interaction be-
tween framework-provided attack and user-provided background traffic?
• Once we can generate synthetic datasets, is it possible to evaluate them using
existing intrusion detection systems?
Starting from the previous research questions, we set ourselves the following objectives:
• Understand challenges and problems associated with creating realistic NIDS datasets.
• Explore methods and find an efficient way to generate different Denial of Service
attacks based on given parameters.
• Implement and embed the process of attack generation within ID2T, transpar-
ently to the end user.
• Evaluate and analyze the impact of generated datasets through the use of NIDS.
This thesis aims to explore the generation of synthetic attacks and their injection into
appropriately captured background traffic, to answer the previously presented research
questions. To achieve this goal, we have initially attempted a type of mathematical
approach based on research in the literature concerning the modeling of resource ex-
haustion attacks, in particular of the Denial of Service type. However, the lack of pub-
lications that tackled the problem comprehensively and inclusively, independent of the
specific type of attack, has made this approach ineffective in achieving the intended
results.
Therefore, it has been decided to pursue a more pragmatic and heuristic approach,
based on the use of network events simulation software such as OMNeT++. Together
with the INET framework, they have been embedded in the process of creating and forg-
ing a new dataset in the ID2T framework [1]–[3]. The process and approach modality
will be explored in more detail in the following chapters.

The purpose of this chapter is to familiarize the reader with the terminology and con-

2.1
Background
Modern Intrusion Detection Systems, from now on IDS, can be divided according to
the detection method or the main source from which the data is taken [4].
The two main types of IDS that can be differentiated based on the input data source
are Host-based IDS (or HIDS) and Network IDS (or NIDS). HIDS are typically in-
stalled and placed on a host which is part of a network. The main activity of HIDS
is to analyze the operating system, directories and files of the machine on which it is
installed. NIDS, on the other hand, are installed in strategic points of the network and
have the task of analyzing the network traffic itself, in search of intrusions and anoma-
lous behavior [4].
According to [4], another type of differentiation is possible based on the detection
mode. There are two other categories: Signature-based IDS and Anomaly-based IDS.
Signature-based IDS are detection systems that identify intrusions based on attack sig-
natures. An attack is identified if, once its signature has been calculated or in any case
generated, there is a match with at least one of the attacks present in a database that
contains all the signatures of already known attacks. These IDS are generally very fast
and accurate, however, they are not capable of recognizing zero-day attacks, i.e. attacks
for which there is not yet an available signature. On the other hand, anomaly-based IDS
aim to identify behavioral anomalies, whether network or host-level. They are therefore
also able to identify new types of attacks not previously seen but may be more prone
to give false positives. They are also more time and energy-demanding than signature-
based IDS, as they often need to be trained on datasets, and this can be a computationally
demanding process [4].
As mentioned, Network Intrusion Detection Systems (from now on NIDS) are sen-
tinel software and hardware capable of monitoring and analyzing network traffic. Traffic
==PAGE:16==
6

is mainly monitored via packet capture and most of the time they are used along with
other detection systems, such as firewalls and host-based IDS. Their main purpose is
to stop an attack at the very beginning of its development and therefore to block an
external threat before it can cause damage to the system to be defended [4].
Following, we present the key attack classes that NIDS must be able to identify and
detect, according to [4], [5]:
• Denial of Service (DoS): this type of attack falls under the broader category of
resource exhaustion. Attacks residing into this category involve one or more at-
tackers, such as botnets, interrupting one or more services by exhausting resources
like CPU, memory, or bandwidth. When the attack involves multiple sources, it
is commonly known as a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack.
• Probing: attacks falling under this category involve an attacker scanning the ports
of a victim, to gather information about the target. This is typically part of the
reconnaissance phase that an attacker carries out before launching an attack on a
system.
• Remote-to-Local (R2L): this scenario generally involves an attacker who gains
unauthorized access to a local system by leveraging a vulnerability in a remote
system (e.g., a web server).
• User-to-Root (U2R): in this case, the attacker obtains initial normal access to a
victim's system, and through a privilege escalation manages to obtain full control
over the system. Typically, the attacker will silently maintain his presence in the
system and control it remotely (e.g., C2, command and control).
Resource exhaustion attacks aim to render devices and services unavailable (affecting the
CIA triad Availability) by depleting their resources. Denial of Service attacks, specifi-
cally, have various purposes and results. Firstly, we distinguish between simple attacks
and distributed attacks based on the number of attackers involved. Distributed attacks
are typically orchestrated by a master, within the context of a botnet [6]. Attacks can be
further categorized based on their target and method. They can be Volumetric Attacks,
Protocol Exploitation Attacks or Application Layer Attacks [7], [8].
• A volumetric attack involves the flooding of a victim host or an entire system
by one or more attackers. This kind of attack primarily aims to deplete network
resources, particularly bandwidth. The result of this type of attack is network
congestion and the unavailability of services for legitimate users. A volumetric
attack can be further classified into two types: flooding and amplification. In
flooding attacks, an attacker directly targets a victim, and all the generated traffic
originates from the attacker. On the other hand, in amplification attacks, an
==PAGE:17==

7
attacker, potentially acting alone, exploits a weakness in a protocol (for example,
the DNS resolving process) to create a large amount of traffic that is then directed
toward the victim by generating only a few initial messages. Both amplification
and flooding techniques heavily rely on IP spoofing [7], [9].
• An attack that exploits weaknesses in a protocol is called a Protocol Exploitation
Attack. For example, the SYN Flood falls into this category of attack. It consists
of exploiting the TCP handshake process, which is the basis of the majority of
internet connections. In particular, a normal flow between the client and server
requires the client to send a SYN packet, to which the server will reply with a
SYN+ACK. Upon completion, the client will confirm the connection with an ACK.
However, when a client stops responding to the SYN+ACK, i.e. by not sending
the final ACK, the connection will remain half-open on the server side. Typically,
an attacker will use IP spoofing to insert the IP of a non-existent host into the
connection opening packet (the SYN). The server will then send the SYN+ACK to the
spoofed IP, which will not be followed by any ACK. The server's memory will be
completely saturated with all half-open connections for which no response has
been obtained [7], [10].
• An attack that exploits application layer vulnerabilities falls into the category of
Application Layer Attacks. This type of attack aims to exploit the highest level of
the Internet stack, which is the application layer. It does not require the generation
of large amounts of data but rather a few packets with a specific purpose. These
attacks are often difficult to recognize as the malicious traffic is camouflaged with
legitimate traffic. Examples of attacks include Slowloris and R.U.D.Y., which
aim to exhaust resources through badly formed HTTP requests (GET in the case of
Slowloris and POST in the case of R.U.D.Y.). These attacks specifically fall into
the subcategory of low and slow attacks, because they generate a minimal amount
of traffic and are executed over an extended period [7], [10]–[13].
2.2
Related work. Datasets and synthetic attack generation tools
The fundamental unit of NIDS are datasets, that are massive containers of captured
network traffic. They contain both benign network traffic (so-called background traffic)
and properly recorded malicious traffic. Thus, the datasets can be used in two ways: for
the training of the NIDS itself, in case the NIDS is of the anomaly-based type, and for
the evaluation of its performance and ability to recognize attacks, even in the case in
which the NIDS is of the signature-based type [14].
To be considered valid, these datasets must reflect certain characteristics, possess
specific properties and follow those requirements well defined in the literature.
Datasets must be based on real and recently captured traffic, have a large time span,
==PAGE:18==
8
2 Background and related work
be appropriately labeled, and have a recognized format. For detection or training pur-
poses, malicious traffic in a dataset may or may not be labeled. A label is mainly used to
differentiate malicious traffic and its characteristic (timestamps, hosts, ports, services in
use or targeted) from background traffic, and can be generated by the IDS itself or set
manually. It has an important value in the case of NIDS supervised training; however,
the traffic labeling process is usually time-consuming and may not be conducted accu-
rately, especially in the presence of large datasets, thus jeopardizing the effectiveness of
the NIDS [1], [14], [15].
The format of a dataset, on the other hand, can typically be divided into three cate-
gories [14]:
• Packet-based network traffic: it contains the whole traffic content, with all the
packets and their payloads. It is typically stored in PCAP files, which are large in
size but provide the fullest details.
• Flow-based network traffic: it contains only metadata about network connections.
It is a leaner format but doesn't provide the same level of granularity and details
of the packet-based network traffic.
• Hybrid network traffic: a mix of the previous two types, mainly flow-based traces
with packet-based data.
These, however, are not the only properties a dataset must possess. Indeed, there are
requirements that a dataset must comply with and which can be divided into functional
and non-functional [1].
2.2.1
Datasets functional and non-functional requirements
To meet the functional requirements, a dataset should include payloads in the data sec-
tion of the packets. In this way, it is possible to use the dataset to test different NIDS,
such as those that perform deep packet inspection. This requirement makes also possible
to test the ability of a NIDS to recognize an attack on the highest layer of the stack, the
application one. A dataset should also have labeled attacks and, ideally, a label for each
packet involved in a recognized attack. It should possess ground truth. This constraint
involves dataset accuracy, in other words, only malicious packets are marked as such by
the labels. Another functional requirement is the property of being renewable. In such
a manner, new types of attacks and scenarios can be included in an existing dataset. In
the end, it should also be flexible, which happens when the dataset includes attacks of
different classes, with different scenarios and scopes [1].
Along with the previous list of functional requirements, [1] suggest some non-functional
requirements, which are: the possibility of autonomously replicating the dataset or its

availability to the public; having a recognized standard format, such as PCAP, so that
other tools and frameworks can interact with it; the absence of artifacts or defects.
2.2.2
Tools for synthetic attacks injection and datasets generation
To compensate for the lack of datasets that reflect the requirements listed above, it has
become necessary to create tools that could generate them starting from acquired back-
ground traffic into which to inject suitably labeled synthetic attacks. The invention of
these tools has also made it possible to create datasets that are always different, replicable
and flexible, allowing the evaluation of the performance of the most diverse NIDS [1].
This has been of particular benefit to those realities which, like the academic one, do
not have updated datasets or datasets that include recently discovered attacks. In fact,
unlike the academic reality, the world of the industry allows for much more heteroge-
neous internal datasets to be obtained, which unfortunately cannot be made public to
the community.
Below, we present a subset of tools used for creating datasets or related products.
FLAME is a tool developed in 2008 that operates on a flow level and offers three
primary methods for modifying network flows [16]. The first method involves injecting
network flows without altering the background traffic, known as the additive mode. The
second method, called subtractive mode, involves removing flows from the traffic. The
last mode is interactive and allows both addition and removal of flows [1], [16].
INSECS-DCS is another tool developed in 2018 and its purpose is to extract packet
features from background traffic [17]. However, this tool is limited to outputting fea-
tures and cannot generate traffic. Therefore, attacks and labels must be written manually
[1], [17].
The last tool we propose is called Intrusion Detection Dataset Toolkit, or more sim-
ply ID2T [1]. It allows, starting from a background traffic PCAP file, the generation of
statistics, injection of modeled synthetic attacks, as well as an interface to model attacks
and include them in the set of injectable attacks. The framework and the rest of the
toolset will be presented in the next chapter.
2.2.3
Datasets defects and tools requirements
From the literature, we learn that the generated datasets may contain defects. These
can be classified into two main types: artifacts, which are defects introduced during
the generation of synthetic attacks, and deficiencies, which result from the incorrect
management of network packets [1].
These two types can be further divided into two macro-categories: Invalid Network
Traffic and Inconsistent Network Traffic, respectively. Invalid Network Traffic refers to
defects that make a certain section of traffic invalid, such as a TCP header option that is
incompatible with the current traffic. Inconsistent Network Traffic, on the other hand,
==PAGE:20==
10
2 Background and related work
refers to defects that make a section of traffic inconsistent with the rest of the flow, such
as a host that maintains the same throughput despite being attacked by a Denial of
Service attack [1].
However, these defects also depend on the context in which they are generated and
discovered. Defects can be differentiated into two types: those related to the network
context, known as the Network Specific Context, and those that do not depend on it,
known as the Network Agnostic Context [1].
Regardless of their nature, datasets should ideally be free from any type of artifacts
and closely resemble real-world traffic. Achieving this requires tools and frameworks
that meet specific functional and non-functional requirements [1].
The functional requirements for synthetic attacks include modeling attacks at the
packet level and the ability to inject them into arbitrary PCAP files, while also ensuring
that the statistical properties of the background traffic are respected. Synthetic attacks
should be generated with minimal user interaction, and the injection process should
be deterministic and replicable. Lastly, tools need to include packet-based or payload-
based attacks [1].
Non-functional requirements include scalability, which means that the tool should
be able to efficiently inject attacks into both small and massive PCAP files; extensibility,
which refers to the ability to easily extend the set of modeled attacks to support new
attacks; usability, which requires that the tool doesn't need specialized hardware or high
computational capabilities; and finally, public availability, which means that the tool
should be made freely available for the community to use [1].

In this chapter, we will explore the tools that have been used in the thesis work. Essen-
tially, there are two frameworks and one model. ID2T is the framework used for gen-

3.1
Intrusion Detection Dataset Toolkit
Intrusion Detection Dataset Toolkit, from now on ID2T, is a framework written in
Python and C++ with a modular architecture [1]. Its main feature and goal are to inject
a previously modeled attack into a PCAP file. More broadly, the toolkit is capable of
accepting a PCAP input, which will be used as a background traffic starting point, and
various attack parameters, such as "attack duration" or "number of attackers".
When the tool encounters a PCAP file for the first time, it calculates various statistics
and saves them in a relational database. These statistics are later used to create attacks
that will not have the artifacts and deficiencies discussed in the previous chapter. If
the tool is given a PCAP that it has seen before, it will retrieve the statistics from the
internal database rather than recalculating them. The calculation and use of statistics
are crucial for injection purposes because they enable the injected traffic to maintain the
characteristics of the background traffic [1].
The entered parameters and the supplied PCAP are managed by the Coordinator
module, which serves a dual purpose of providing information to the Statistics module
and the Merger module, which is responsible for creating the final dataset based on
the initial PCAP and an intermediate one generated in the process. As part of the
mandatory parameters, the user needs to provide the type of attack, which must be one
of the attacks implemented in the framework. The list of available attacks can be viewed
using the "./id2t -l" command.
The Attack Controller module is responsible for managing the attacks and is ad-
justed by the Statistics module, while another module called TIDED uses the provided

3.1.1
TIDED module
The TIDED module, whose acronym stands for Testing Intrusion DEtection Datasets,
deserves special mention as it is responsible for performing background traffic tests to
identify possible artifacts or deficiencies, also known as "reliability tests". The purpose
of these tests is to validate the compliance of the background traffic with specific prop-
erties and provide the Attack Controller module with the capability to replicate these
properties in the injected attacks [1].
The reliability tests are divided into three main categories: Availability, Validity, and
Diversity.
• Availability tests. Tests that fall under this category check for specific proper-
ties or attributes in network traffic. This category includes "Payload Availability",
which verifies the presence of payloads in packets, tracks the payloads' entropy,
and reports the number of encrypted ones. Another test, "IP Classes Availabil-
ity", identifies the IP classes present in the capture and describes the distribution
and ratio of public and private addresses [1].
• Validity tests. The tests falling under this category check the consistency of spe-
cific fields in captured packets. For example, the "TCP Checksum Validity" test
reports the number of TCP checksum errors found in the packets, while the "Port
==PAGE:23==

Range Validity" test tracks the distribution of all ports used in the analyzed net-
work traffic, both as source and destination [1].
• Diversity tests. This test class verifies the accuracy of observed TCP header values.
It includes "IP Distribution", which checks whether the distribution of source
and destination IPs conforms to certain normality patterns; "TTL Distribution",
which measures the distribution of Time To Live values in TCP headers and de-
tects any unexpected anomalies; "MSS Distribution", which monitors and records
the variation of Maximum Segment Size within captures; "Window Size Distri-
bution", which tracks changes in Window Sizes; and "ToS Distribution", which
traces the Type of Service value in TCP headers and verifies if it conforms to the
specifications of modern protocols [1].

3.1.2
Currently supported attacks
Although researchers and end users are provided with APIs to independently develop
other attacks, the ID2T already includes various attacks belonging to different categories
[1]:
• Probing and Surveillance. Attacks that fall into this category include port scan-
ning and the use of auxiliary scanners, such as those found in Metasploit. Typi-
cally, a threat actor launches such an attack to obtain information about a victim
and then uses this information to their advantage in creating more complex and
specific attacks. Examples of available attacks in this category are Port Scan, SMB
Scan and MS17 Scan [1].
• Resource Exhaustion. Attacks that fall into this category are those that involve
a threat actor significantly reducing the resources available to a victim, such as
CPU, memory or network bandwidth. This category also includes Denial of Ser-
vice attacks, which in the case of multiple actors are called Distributed Denial

3.2
OMNeT++ and INET framework
OMNeT++ is a library and framework written in C++ mainly used for creating network
simulators, currently at version 6.0.1. Developed under an academic license, it allows
its users to describe, create and configure their models. These can then be run, under
specific configurations, and the results can be available for analysis. Although it can be
run in headless mode via the command line, graphically it is presented as a variant of
the Eclipse IDE [2], [18].
Models consist of modules as their fundamental units, which are connected via gates
and combined to form compound modules. Each module can send and receive messages
through gates with other modules. These modules can be nested indefinitely or exist as
simple modules, while messages can be customized by developers [2], [18].
There are mainly three tools and features available to the user for creating modules
and models: NED files, INI files and C++ development.
3.2.1

NED files are used to describe the majority of entities within the OMNeT++ environ-

the form of text files and are based on their language and syntax. When used to de-

well as the names and values of any parameters. It may also include ad hoc defined types,
submodules, and connections.

This chapter aims to present and describe the models of specific Distributed Denial of
Service (DDoS) attacks.
It is divided as follows: first, there will be a presentation of common features and
properties that all the attacks share, along with the topology analogies, IP address assign-
ment process, PCAP recording, victims' queueing system, and channel specifications.
Subsequently, we will present the types of attacks taken into consideration.
For all of the attacks, we will mention specific settings in the INI configurations,
and additionally, we will present the C++ applications that have enabled the effective
execution of the attacks, as well as the necessary modifications for the INET model.
Finally, we will discuss the modifications required for the INET TCP and UDP imple-
mentations to support the maximum number of acceptable incoming connections.
The attacks presented and modeled are abstractions of the attacks found in real-
world scenarios. However, they strive to encompass as many characteristics, properties,
and peculiarities as possible from various attacks.
The DDoS attacks examined, which have been abstracted in the OMNeT++ lan-
guage, are three: the UDP Flood, the DNS amplification, and a Low and Slow attack,
which in the specific case is Slowloris. We have decided to include the mentioned at-
tacks to provide ID2T with a diversified set of attacks that are currently of interest [23].
In particular, DNS Amplification and UDP Flood both make use of UDP, which has
been found to be preferred by threat actors as it does not require establishing a con-
nection, like TCP does. On the other hand, there has also been a notable increase
in Application Layer attacks. This is the reason why we have decided to implement a
low and slow attack from that category, attacks that are notoriously more stealthy and
difficult to identify [23].

4.1.1
Network topology
The network topology has the same structure, in the form of NED files, for all at-
tacks. The environment in which the attack takes place is presented as a network with
a parametric number, fixed at runtime, of attackers and victims. Specifically, there are
"attackersCount" attackers and "victimsCount" victims, which by default are at least
equal to 1. This is due to practical reasons, meaning that there is always at least one
attacker and one victim present. Next, we introduce the channel, named C, which is
defined as a type that extends the "DatarateChannel". The latter is an ideal channel pro-
vided by OMNeT++, allowing for the direct configuration of certain properties in the
INI file. For a detailed explanation of the parameters, we refer to Subsection 4.1.2.
The submodules present are:
• Attackers. They are of type StandardHost, following the type provided by INET
[24]. It represents an IP host with an entire network stack, including applications
running on the top layer.
• Victims. Similar to Attackers, Victims are also of type StandardHost.
• Router. It is defined by the type Router, serving as a demarcation line between
the attacking sphere and the victim sphere. It consists of a StandardHost with
enabled IP forwarding.
• NIDS. Defined by the type EthernetSwitch. It is important to note that this
device serves as the main point of view for the entire simulation execution. Its
purpose is to capture network traffic and by design it will capture all traffic destined
for victims, aiming to emulate a real-life scenario.
• Configurator. Defined by the type Ipv4NetworkConfigurator, it is responsible,
among other things, for retrieving IP to interfaces assignments from external
XML files (Refer to Subsection 4.1.3).

network has the following connections: one between the Ethernet interfaces of the
attacker and the router, via channel C, for each attacker; a single connection between
the router and the NIDS; a connection between the Ethernet interfaces of the NIDS

The channel present between the hosts of the abstract network is a subtype of the primi-

• Delay. Represents the propagation delay, expressed in seconds. By default, it is

4.2
UDP Flood Attack
The UDP Flood attack (Technique: Direct Network Flood, ID: T1498.001)1 is a volu-
metric DDoS attack in which one or typically multiple attackers flood a victim or target
system with UDP datagrams. A receiving host can typically respond in two ways: if
there is a service available behind a specific port, it will accept the packet and process a
possible reply; if there is no service bound to the port, it will reply with an ICMP Type 3
Destination Unreachable message. However, the victim, bombarded by a continuous
burst of UDP packets, with often substantial payloads, becomes overwhelmed by the
number of connections it has to handle, resulting in a denial of service for legitimate
users [26].
4.2.1
Assumptions and limitations
Typically, a UDP flood attack involves attackers spoofing their IP addresses into UDP
datagrams. As a result, the ICMP reply packets will be lost on the internet and will
never reach the original senders (the attackers). In our model, we have considered the
attacking hosts as "spoofed hosts", meaning that the IPs appearing as the source of the
attack are the spoofed IPs of the attackers. This assumption does not affect the generality
of the problem. In fact, an ICMP packet or a legitimate reply from the victim will not
arouse any response from the attacker, who will continue with the UDP burst.
4.2.2
INI configuration file
The configuration file, in addition to presenting the properties described in Section 4.1,
includes the following fields:
• Attacker:
– Application typename. This indicates the name of the application already
present in INET or the name of the main C++ class if it is instead present

– messageLength. This field specifies the size of the UDP payload that each
attacker sends. By default, it is set to a minimum of 8 bytes, the lower bound

– sendInterval. The parameter indicates the frequency at which datagrams

4.3
DNS Amplification Attack
The DNS Amplification attack (Technique: Reflection Amplification, ID: T1498.002)2
is a volumetric DDoS in which one or more attackers manage to generate a large amount
of traffic from a small number of packets. In particular, this is achieved by exploiting
a weakness of the DNS resolving process. An attacker sends DNS queries, typically
of the ANY type, to multiple open DNS resolvers using the victim's spoofed IP address.
Unlike well-configured resolvers, these open resolvers are freely accessible from the in-
ternet and are configured to accept recursive queries from any IP address [29]. These
DNS resolvers will then send back DNS responses to the victim, with a size that will be
orders of magnitude higher than the original query. As a result, the victim will find itself
flooded with traffic coming from DNS resolvers, leading to a denial of service similar
to that of a UDP Flood attack [30], [31].
4.3.1
Assumptions and limitations
As with other attacks, there are certain assumptions and limitations to this modeling.
The first assumption is that the attackers present on the network, visible via the NED
file, are indeed the open DNS resolvers. Therefore, the packets present in the regis-
tered PCAP and addressed to the victims have the IP addresses of the DNS resolvers
themselves as the source address. This simplification has been adopted because it is not
2https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1498/002/
==PAGE:37==
4.3 DNS Amplification Attack
27
possible to see the traffic generated by the attackers toward the resolvers from the vic-
tim's point of view, which includes the NIDS. Thus, from the victim's perspective, it
is only possible to see the large packets coming from the resolvers, which contain the
DNS responses.
Another assumption is the use of DNS over UDP. Typically, DNS responses larger
than 512 bytes require and use TCP as the transport protocol instead of UDP. TCP,
however, is a connection-oriented protocol and therefore requires a correct TCP hand-
shake between the resolver and the victim. This handshake cannot occur in the case of
a DNS Amplification attack, as the attacker, not the victim, requests DNS information
on the victim's behalf. Therefore, for the sake of simplicity (and not due to OMNeT++
limitations), DNS responses will have a fixed size of less than 512 bytes.
4.3.2
INI configuration file
The INI configuration file contains the same information as the UDP Flood. However,
it has been necessary to include a new parameter called "dnsResponse", set to true. This
way, the Flooder application, also used in the DNS resolvers, will send forged DNS
responses instead of random payloads.
A special mention should be made for the chooseDestAddrMode parameter. A DNS
resolver, within the INI configuration, cannot have more than one application bind-
ing on port 53 or 5353, due to OMNeT++ limitations. As a result, each DNS re-
solver can only send responses to one victim at a time in a single burst. Unlike the
UDP Flood attack, where each attacker has n = victimsCount applications to flood
the victimsCount victims, in DNS Amplification, it has been necessary to set the
chooseDestAddrMode parameter to perSend. This allows the DNS resolver to randomly
choose the destination address at the beginning of each DNS response, enabling flood-
ing of more than one victim within the time window of a single burst.

4.4
Low and Slow Attack: Slowloris
The Slowloris attack (Technique: Service Exhaustion Flood, ID: T1499.002)3 is an
application layer DDoS in which one or more attackers overwhelm a target host by send-
ing malformed HTTP requests. Unlike typical HTTP GET requests that quickly start
and end with a reasonable packet exchange, Slowloris GET requests aim to keep the
HTTP connection open for as long as possible by sending fragmented HTTP packets.
The model is based on Slowloris implementation of [33].
A Slowloris-generated HTTP GET request is divided into multiple small sections.
The first section includes the actual GET request for a random resource on the webserver.
The GET packet is then followed by another packet that includes the selection of the
user agent, through the User-Agent header. After that, an indefinite list of headers is
appended to each packet.
The server mistakenly assumes that the attacker has a slow connection and keeps it
open, waiting for the attacker to finish, even though it never will. This process creates a
new thread for every piece of the GET request, leading to a complete consumption of
the server's physical resources [12], [33].
In Figure 4.2, we can see what a TCP stream looks like during a Slowloris attack.
The initial GET will be the first packet to be sent, followed by the transmission of
information that enables the attacker to maintain the connection open: a user agent,
and subsequently, a meaningless header every few seconds (X-a header).

4.5
UDP and TCP buffers extension
One of the peculiarities of a Denial of Service is the unavailability of a host, service, or
particular resource once attackers successfully overwhelm the victims. To introduce this
concept, since INET does not allow any type of behavior that is unnatural or illegitimate,
we included a buffer counter within the INET Tcp and Udp classes. Specifically, for
both Udp and Tcp, it is possible to set a maximum amount of incoming TCP/UDP
packets within the INI file. When this threshold is reached, the host's resources are
considered depleted, and the host in a state of non-availability. This concept differs from
that of the queue, as the DropTailQueue can slowly dispose of packets if it becomes full,
while the introduced buffer acts as an upper bound and tipping point for the victim host.
In the case of UDP Flood and DNS Amplification, the concept is conceptually
equivalent to the number of incoming packets after which the victim will no longer be
able to respond or process them. For Slowloris, which operates on TCP, it also includes
any packets without payload, such as those involved in the TCP handshake.

Implementation
5
Currently, ID2T presents a simple and modular process for injecting an attack. First,
we proceed to enter the parameters. One of the most important parameters is the back-
ground PCAP, which is analyzed to evaluate the statistics necessary for the injection
process. Afterward, the user can decide whether to inject the attack directly into the
background traffic or create a PCAP containing exclusively the attack.
Once the type of attack to be injected has been entered as a parameter, in our case
DDoS, it is possible to specify other parameters that are strictly dependent on the type
of attack. Nevertheless, there are some generic parameters, such as ip.dst or port.dst
(respectively, destination IP and destination port) or attack duration, which are also
common to other attack classes.
One last parameter is the destination file in which to write the result in the form of
a PCAP.
5.1
DDoS attack injection process
Once all the necessary information, such as the statistics, is passed to the Attack Con-
troller, it is tasked with choosing the attack and injecting it. Specifically, the execution
flow will be as follows:
1. Initialization and update of the attack parameters. This phase provides for the
initialization of the attack parameters with default values. If the user wants to
override certain values (e.g., attack duration or number of attackers), the parame-
ters will be updated accordingly.
2. Generation of attack packets. This phase is the most crucial part of injecting
an attack, involving the use of statistics and parameters to supply the attack logic
and subsequently generate an intermediate PCAP. It can essentially be divided
into two sub-phases:
a) Retrieving statistics and parameters usage. This part of the execution is
responsible for retrieving all the parameters set either by default or by the
==PAGE:44==
34
5 Implementation
user (typically using the get_param_value() function) and utilizing auxiliary
variables.
b) ForginganintermediatePCAP. It represents the core of the attack's writing
process. Here the logic of the attack itself will be written, either in the form
of a flow with the use of Scapy or through the use of an external template
that will be adjusted [36]. At the end of this process, the attack packets will
be added one after the other in an intermediate and temporary PCAP file.
3. Generation of final PCAP starting from background traffic and intermediate
PCAP. In this phase, the intermediate PCAP will be sorted chronologically, if
necessary. Then, based on the user's preference, it will either be merged with the
background traffic or exported directly without it.

we considered a scenario in which each attacker sends 2000 packets within its session,

and victims must be at least 1, and by default, it has been set to this value.

port.src includes randomly generated ephemeral ports unless it is a DNS Ampli-
fication attack where the ports can exclusively be 53 or 5353.
• ip.src, mac.src, ip.dst, and mac.dst, which refer to the source and destination

5.2.5
OMNeT++ template PCAP inclusion and attack termination
After generating the PCAP template, placed in results/template.pcap, it will be parsed
by the Scapy utility called PcapReader. Right after, a dictionary structure will be cre-
ated, which contains the unique assignment of IP and MAC addresses for victims and
attackers. At this point, the PCAP template needs to be traversed completely for two
reasons: first, to correct the timestamps based on the offset of the first proper attack
packet (usually the first packet with a valid inet.IP layer, according to Scapy); second,
to modify the MAC address of each host for each corresponding IP.
If the attack is of the type "Low and Slow" (Slowloris), the values of TSVal and
TSecr will be modified in such a way that they start with an offset equal to the injection
time of the first attack packet. This is done to compensate for the lack of functionality in
OMNeT++ to set a starting TSVal based on a clock different from the simulation one.
As a result of this modification, the checksum of each packet needs to be recalculated.
Then, for each modified package, it will be added to a temporary list of new pack-
ages. Once this process has finished, which has O(n) complexity with n being the num-
ber of packets inside the PCAP template, the packets will finally be merged by the
generate_attack_pcap() function. As mentioned earlier, this function is responsible
for creating the final PCAP, whether it includes background or not. In the worst-case
scenario, this final process has a complexity of O(n+m), with n template packets and m
background traffic packets.

6.3
Rules writing
The present section aims to explore the process of writing rules for the recognition and
identification of attacks in the previously generated PCAP files through the use of a
signature-based NIDS, which, in our case, is Suricata [42].
Suricata is an open-source software used as an intrusion detection system and intru-
sion prevention system, offering multiple features such as, among others, alerting and
offline analysis of PCAP files. For attack recognition, it is possible to utilize community
rules or rules that are freely accessible after registration, such as the Cisco Talos rules1.
However, it has not been possible to find suitable rules for our use cases, either because
the content match and the rule itself are too specific, or because the rule does not exist
at all. Therefore, we have decided to create our rules based on the fundamental and
theoretical concepts of the attack, which are presented below.
To begin writing a rule, we need to present its constituent elements [43]. Below, we
present the one used in our rules:
1https://www.snort.org/talos
==PAGE:62==
52
6 Results
• action: it represents the action that Suricata must take the moment the rule
matches. It is typically an "alert", but it could also be a packet "drop" with a
consequent alert, a "reject", or a "pass". For a complete list of valid actions, we
recommend to refer to the manual.
• protocol: it indicates the protocol that Suricata needs to consider. The basic
protocols are "tcp", "udp", "icmp" and "ip". The latter is equivalent to "any", as
it matches all the other protocols. For a complete list of possible protocols, we
recommend to refer to the manual.
• $HOME_NET, $EXTERNAL_NET: they indicate two variables (typically set in the de-
fault configuration file /etc/suricata/suricata.yaml) that contain IP addresses
to be considered part of the HOME network, to be defended, and EXTERNAL
network, from which a potential attack can arrive.
• ports: they indicate specific ports to watch. They can be single (e.g., 53), multiple
(e.g., [53, 5353]), or negation of a single value or interval (e.g., ![53, 5353]).
• direction: it indicates the direction of the flow. Typically represented as $EXTERNAL_NET
-> $HOME_NET, it can also be bidirectional like $EXTERNAL_NET <> $HOME_NET.
• msg, classtype, sid, rev: they indicate the metadata of the rule, particularly
"sid", which expresses the unique identifier of the rule; "rev", the revision number;
"classtype", the class of the potentially recognized attack. As for "msg", it stands
for the message to be printed out when the rule matches.
• threshold, track, count, seconds: they form the core of the rule [44], [45]
– threshold: it controls the rule’s alert frequency and its value can be threshold,
the minimum threshold for a rule before it takes action, limit, the maximum
number of actions that will be performed, or both, which is a mix of the pre-
vious two approaches.
– track: it indicates where to track the rule matches and it typically can have
three values: by_src (by source), by_dst (by destination) or both.
– count: it denotes the number of rule hits before the action is taken.
– seconds: it indicates the time frame within which the previous parameter,
count, needs to be reached.
• content, offset, depth: they denote a specific match with the content of the
packet payload, the offset from which to match, and the depth of the matched
content, all measured in bytes.
==PAGE:63==
6.3 Rules writing
53
• flowbits: a flowbit consists of two parts: an action, typically "set" or "isset" (to
bind or check if the binding of the flowbit is present), and the name that identi-
fies the flowbit. It is used in the wider context of flow and on multiple packets
that match different rules. Its practical use will become clearer with the rules for
Slowloris [46].
• flow: it is a keyword used to match the direction of the flow itself, from/to the
client, or from/to the server [46].
For all the rules, $HOME_NET has been set as the set of all ID2T's ip.dsts, retrieved from
the XML labels after the execution of ID2T itself. $EXTERNAL_NET, instead, has been set
to !$HOME_NET.
6.3.1
UDP Flood signature
The signature used to match the UDP Flood attack provides an alert triggered by UDP
datagrams arriving from the external network, any port except 53 or 5353 (as these
ports are likely indicative of a DNS Amplification attack) toward any port of the home
network. It is tracked by source, with a count of 100 packets received in 1 second. The
threshold is of type "both", meaning the alert will only be generated once if there have
been 100 or more packets received within 1 second. The classtype is "attempted-dos",
and it has been assigned a previously unused sid. In Listing 6.8, we find the complete
rule.
1 alert udp $EXTERNAL_NET ![53, 5353] -> $HOME_NET any (msg:"UDP flooding attack"
; threshold: type both , track by_src , count 100, seconds 1; classtype:
attempted -dos; sid :202307042; rev :1;)
Listing 6.8: UDP Flood Suricata rule.
For the UDP Flood case, the rule has been written specifically as it does not exist in
either the community rules or the Talos rules.

only be generated once if there have been 100 or more packets received within 1 second.

6.3.3
Low and Slow: Slowloris
The Slowloris signature is made up of three distinct but connected rules. The three
rules utilize the concept of flow and expect that a GET HTTP request occurs at least
once within 60 seconds after the connection has been established between the attacker,
from the external network, and the server, from the home network (flow: to_server,
established). In this case, the 'slowloris' flowbit will be set. Afterward, it will similarly
wait for a generic HTTP User-Agent, to be received at least once in 60 seconds, through
a second rule that checks if the 'slowloris' flowbit is set. In the third and final part,
Suricata will have to check if an HTTP header (in our case, 'X-a') is present within a
packet at least once every 60 seconds, as before, and the flowbit 'slowloris' is set.

Conclusion
7
At the beginning of this thesis work, we pondered the feasibility of realistically modeling
specific Denial of Service attacks and injecting them into pre-existing background traffic.
Additionally, we questioned whether we could create a process capable of generating a
realistic dataset that seamlessly integrates into a well-known framework used by the
academic and industrial community, such as ID2T, while maintaining transparency for
the end user. Having successfully implemented and incorporated the attack generation
process, our next inquiry centered around evaluating its effectiveness using a Network
Intrusion Detection System.
Through this thesis work, we propose a new methodology to address the problem of
generating realistic datasets for the training and evaluation of existing and future NIDS.
Our approach combines the ID2T framework for injecting malicious traffic with the
accuracy and reliability of the INET model. We believe that this type of approach can be
highly interesting and useful, both for academics and industry, to gain a comprehensive
understanding of selected DoS attacks and assess one's security posture.
The process has not been linear, and there have been many challenges along the way.
The main challenges we faced initially involved defining the specific problem, especially
in terms of how to effectively model certain types of Denial of Service attacks. Initially,
we had set out to address a broader and more complex problem but ultimately decided
to narrow our focus exclusively to the Denial of Service attack class. Another challenge
we encountered was understanding how to model and determine the appropriate ap-
proach to follow since there is limited literature on modeling specific Denial of Service
attacks. Other challenges we encountered along the way included learning three dif-
ferent frameworks and libraries: ID2T, OMNeT++, and INET. Last but not least, we
faced the challenge of using the latter two frameworks to abstract and model the attacks
and implement the generation process in a lean and efficient manner. Nevertheless, we
succeeded in proposing a new method for injecting different types of DoS attacks and
also in proposing the necessary tools for modeling other DoS attacks, as well as other
types of cyber attacks, including recently discovered ones.
Certainly, like any project, there is always room for improvement and a need for
future work. When it comes to our project, there are a few areas we can focus on for
==PAGE:72==
62
7 Conclusion
further development. First and foremost, we can extend the set of modeled and imple-
mented Denial of Service attacks. This aligns with the purpose of ID2T, which aims to
have an increasing variety of attacks available. Additionally, we can optimize the com-
putational complexity of the intermediate PCAP cleaning and injecting process. Lastly,
we can explore a more effective integration of malicious traffic into the background traf-
fic. One approach could involve subtracting sessions or connections originating from
the victims, starting from the moment they become overwhelmed.
