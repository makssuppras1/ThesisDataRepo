Abstract
This thesis aims to develop a support tool that will aid in accelerating the green transition
of the built sector. By accelerating this transition, the tool aims at supporting the over-
all circularity in the built sector, and hopefully contribute to the reduction of the overall
emissions and environmental impacts that the built sector currently accounts for. The re-
search has been carried out using the Design Research Methodology as a type 5 research
project, and initially defined key factors have guided the focus of the study throughout all
phases. Three overall research questions have been defined and answered.
• Research question 1: What types of RL/take-back systems exist and what makes
it difficult to implement in the built sector? was answered by carrying out a SLR
of 414 articles found in the database Scopus, and by interviewing 9 carefully se-
lected companies and organisations within the built environment. Thematic findings
(Contextual and Influencing factors) were identified and analysed, and 3 key factors
were expanded. Lastly a design specification was formulated aimed at decreasing
the difficulty of reverse logistic system implementation.
• Research question 2: How might a support tool be developed to aid organisations in
implementing RL/takeback systems in the built sector? was answered by generating
ideas and conceptualising on the basis of the design specification. A workshop with
the case company, Milva, was carried out to prioritise and define functions for the
support tool and morphology was used for combining ideas. The method of the
Weighted Objectives Matrix was used to choose the right concept idea, based on
the criteria from the design specification. The developed support tool constitutes
of a workshop of a duration of 2x2.5 hours. The workshop material consists of 7
steps, that guides the manufacturing companies through the process of generating
3 concepts of RL systems. Finally all workshop materials are gathered in an output
poster to provide overview and comparability of the designed RL concepts.
• Research question 3: How can the developed tool be assessed and the viability,
feasibility and desirability increased? was answered by carrying out 3 test sessions
with 3 different test subjects. Due to limitations of the project and time frame, the test
plan was formulated in two versions; an ideal test plan and an actual test plan. As
result, a list of suggestions for improvement was formulated and should be further
processed in further work.
A discussion of the analysis, the solution and the test is provided with the purpose of
reflecting upon the process and results of the project.
The thesis has been carried out in collaboration with the case company Milva, whose main
services consist of a digital mapping tool for environmental and resource screenings for
buildings up for demolition or renovation along with a range of other advising services.
By developing the support tool and aiding manufacturers in designing take-back systems,
Milva can support these companies in designing the systems of taking back building prod-
ucts and thereby further developing their re-circulation plan as a part of their mapping tool
services.

Research Methodology
As this project aims to develop a support tool for design processes, and aid in the contri-
bution to the wider design research field, the general methodology of the process will be
founded in the Design Research Methodology (DRM) (Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2008)
and the work will follow the four phases of: Research Clarification, Descriptive Study 1,
Prescriptive Study and Descriptive Study 2.
Research Clarification aims to understand the problem initially, and set goal for the overall
Research project. Descriptive Study 1 aims to define the typology of takeback systems,
understand why takeback systems are difficult to implement in the built sector and what
capabilities that are necessary to acquire to adopt take-back systems. Prescriptive Study
aims to select and consolidate the gathered knowledge into a support tool. Descriptive
Study 2 aims to test and evaluate the consolidated tool according to viability, feasibility
and desirability.
The research project is categorised as a Type 5, as the phase of Research Clarification
will be conducted review based, the phases of Descriptive Study 1 and Prescriptive Study
will be comprehensive (since they cannot be sufficiently conducted only on the basis of
the review), and Descriptive Study 2 will be non-comprehensive.
1.1
Research clarification
The aim of the Research Clarification (RC) phase is to formulate the research goal. The ar-
eas of selective demolition and reuse of building products are initially investigated through
a screening of white papers, fieldwork and academic literature. Along with this, literature
on transition design is also reviewed. The relevant actors related to building projects and
demolition sites are identified, and using several systems focused methods (situational
mapping, reference modelling, root cause analysis) an actor-network is established. The
research is conducted as a mix between literature reviews, interviews, fieldwork and re-
views of white papers. The outcome of the phase is a set of research questions that
defines the goal of the research project, along with an initial problem statement and re-
search focus (key factors).
1.2
Descriptive Study 1
The aim of the Descriptive Study 1 (DS1) phase is to gather data to sufficiently answer
Research Question 1: ”What types of RL/take-back systems exist and what makes it dif-
ficult to implement in the built sector? ”
This involves investigating typologies of takeback systems which will be investigated by
performing a systematic literature review. Furthermore, an understanding of why take-
back systems are difficult to implement in the built sector, and what capabilities that are
necessary for the companies to acquire will be investigated by conducting literature re-
views and performing expert interviews. The findings are collected in a design specifica-
tion.
1.3
Prescriptive Study
The aim of the Prescriptive Study (PS) phase is to consolidate the knowledge gathered in
a support tool. The phase will be guided by Research Question 2: ”How might a support
tool be developed to aid organisations in implementing RL/takeback systems in the built
sector?”.

The support tool will be developed by synthesising knowledge from the DS1 phase through
an iterative design approach using systematic methods.
1.4
Descriptive Study 2
The aim of the Descriptive Study 2 (DS2) phase is to test and evaluate the consolidated
tool. The evaluation will be guided by Research Question 3: ”How can the developed tool
be assessed and the viability, feasibility and desirability increased?”.
The method for evaluation will consist of test sessions with experts and relevant manu-
facturing companies.
1.5
Research questions
The following research and -research questions have been formulated for the study:
RQ1: What types of RL/take-back systems exist and what makes it difficult to implement
in the built sector?
RQ 1.1: What influencing factors for adoption of take-back systems in the built sector
exist?
RQ 1.2: What types of RL/take-back systems exist?
RQ 1.3: What capabilities are necessary to acquire to adopt take-back systems in the
built sector?
RQ2: How might a support tool be developed to aid organisations in implementing RL/-
takeback systems in the built sector?
RQ 2.1: How can RL systems be designed specifically for the built sector?
RQ 2.2: How might structural couplings between actor worlds in RL systems in the built
sector be systematically designed?
RQ 3: How can the developed tool be assessed and the viability, feasibility and desirability
increased?
RQ 3.1: How can the viability, feasibility and desirability of the developed support tool be
assessed?
RQ 3.2: How can the viability, feasibility and desirability of the developed support tool be
increased?

Introduction
2.1
Overarching problem
The construction sector is one of the largest sectors in the world – accounting for 13 %
of global domestic product (GDP) and set to expand due to expected rises in popula-
tion.(Foundation and Arup, 2022). This expansion will have large environmental conse-
quences. For example, the built sector accounts for 37 % of total CO2 emissions globally
– it is the largest contributing sector to the climate crisis (WorldGreenBuildingCouncil,
2022). The continuation of this trend without intervention in the built sector will have dev-
astating consequences. The built environment must as such be a focus for sustainable
development, which is defined by on Environment and Development, 1987: ”development
that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations
to meet their own needs”.
The problem of the built environment is two-fold: Energy and material consumption. The
energy required to maintain temperatures at a comfortable level and to provide electric-
ity for appliances, presents a large problem. Along with this, the materials required to
construct the buildings are quite intensive on the environment. Great measures have
already been taken to make buildings more energy efficient in Denmark, but more fo-
cus is required on the building components (Tozan et al., 2021). According to a study
comparing life cycle analysis’ (LCA’s) of 60 of buildings in Denmark over a lifetime of 50
years, building components accounts for approximately 70 % of total CO2 emissions over
the buildings lifetime (Tozan et al., 2021). This is naturally not the only impact the build
environment poses. In Denmark, the built environment also accounts for 42 % of all pro-
duced waste, and has been increasing over the past few years (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023).
This waste is generally recycled, especially steel, but most of the waste is downcycled as
roadfill (Economy, 2024). This needs to change.
The circular economy is highlighted as key strategy for sustainable development (C. E. An-
dersen et al., 2020,EllenMacArthurFoundation, 2022). A circular economy entails a sys-
tem where resources are never wasted, but are regenerative by design, through e.g. re-
pair, maintenance, remanufacturing and reuse of products (Foundation and Arup, 2022).
C. E. Andersen et al., 2020 highlights that circularity as a strategy in the built sector even
poses a significant pathway to stay within the environmental planetary boundaries.
Recent political developments in Denmark are also set into place to further the transi-
tion towards circularity. From July 2024, accounting for materials and measuring impact
through a resource mapping (a bill of materials and products in a building created prior to
demolition/dismantling (“Cirkulær Nedrivning Vejledning”, 2020)) of buildings over 250 m2
will be a requirement before demolitions can take place. (Konstruktørforeningen, 2024).
Along with this, from January 2024, all reused building components will be considered as
CO2 neutral (DanskIndustri, 2023). Thus, demolition practices are pressured to change.
The new practice ”selective demolition” is highlighted as the new convention within the de-
molition sector, where instead of demolishing, the aim is to recover as many products as
possible through careful dismantling processes (“Cirkulær Nedrivning Vejledning”, 2020).
A lot of demolishers are prepared for this change. A big problem is however the uncer-
tainty and risk preciding in the selectively demolished products. This greatly diminishes
their incorporation into projects (Risiko Som Barriere for Bæredygtige Materialer, 2023).
These uncertainties come from different perspectives, but the lack of trust in the products

that must be dealt with (Den Gode Dokumentationsproces, 2023).
One might pose that developing new products with an ingrained circularity would be the
best pathway forward, but due to the long life-span of building products, strategies imple-
mented today will not have a meaningful environmental impact until the far in the future,
which may be too late (50+ years for many products) (Risiko Som Barriere for Bæredygtige
Materialer, 2023). It is therefore important to address the problem we face today by recov-
ering the building products and components at a high level with as much environmental
value retained as possible. To do this, new systems for recovery needs to be established,
and new capabilities within organisations to be able to recover used products are needed
(Den Gode Dokumentationsproces, 2023).
2.2
Milva
The thesis is carried out in collaboration with the company Milva. Milva aims to be an
actor to aid in the transition towards a circular built future.
Milva is an IT company providing digital tools and services for carrying out the needed
preparations related to demolition and renovation of buildings, to ensure appropriate han-
dling and/or disposal of building materials and components. Their core services include
tool development and advising related to environmental mappings and resource map-
pings. Both types of mappings are a legal requirement when demolishing or renovating
buildings over 250 m2. In Appendix RC2 notes from the first meeting with Milva is shown,
together with a User Activity Cycle (Hellek et al., 2013), carried out in collaboration with
Tim Tolman (Senior Advisor at Milva). The User Activity Cycle shows the work that Milva
does before, during and after a screening or mapping is carried out. The screening or
mapping itself is carried out through Milva’s digital platform, where the mapper is guided
step by step through the process, along with collection of samples for testing. The work
conducted after the mapping or screening consists of documentation, both internally and
externally, and by collecting feedback from client for further development of tools and
services.
As a part of their resource mapping services, they wish to incorporate the possibility of
making a ”re-circulation plan” of the mapped materials, by incorporating new elements
in their tool that eases the initiation of contact between building owner/demolisher and a
taker/buyer of the products. To do so, they need more actors to take back used building
products and products at a larger scale. The problem for Milva consists of determining
who these actors should be and how to best support their development of recovery sys-
tems. This thesis aims to aid Milva in this endeavour.
Throughout the project, Tim Tolman provided valuable insights, feedback and contacts
that have contributed greatly to the project and its form.

The Point of Transition

tions of the building sector towards circularity, the perspective of whole systems transition
theory can aid greatly. The general theory was put forth by Geels and Schot, 2007, and
has since been developed further into looking at consumption-production systems (CPS)
in more detail (Geels et al., 2023).

innovative means. These go in a lot of directions, and most do not make it to the
second phase.
2. Stabilisation - A general trend within the niche level is created through consolidated
learning processes at multiple levels (e.g. knowledge, technical specifications, mar-
ket dynamics, etc.). Niches develop in the same innovation direction, and solution
offered become more easily available (often cheaper).
3. Disruption - transitioning from the second to this phase, visible struggles between
the current regime and the niches occur. During this phase, the radical new innova-
tions question the current regimes cultural, political and business oriented method
of operation. At this stage, it is not assured that the niches will break through.
4. Institutionalisation - Should the niches break through the disruption phase and be-
come diffused within the regime, a new socio-technical system will occur, reconfig-
ured by the means of the niche.

3.3
Reverse Logistics Systems
Reverse logistics (RL) is described as a cornerstone of the circular economy (Alkahtani
et al., 2021, Mallick et al., 2023, Singh and Ordoñez, 2016, Ding et al., 2023), and is
defined as:
”... the effective and efficient management of the series of activities required to retrieve
a product from a customer in order to either dispose it or recover value” (Mallick et al.,
2023).
RL entails the of collection end-of-life or end-of-use products with the purpose of recover-
ing and reselling them, and the term covers all activities from acquisition to recovery of a
product to establishing sales channels for the recovered products (de Brito and Dekker,
2004). Mallick et al., 2023 and de Brito and Dekker, 2004 defines several general activities
are necessary a logistics system, which are depicted on figure 3.6.
These activities may not happen in the specified order, however RL systems are typically

Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
==PAGE:23==
Figure 3.6: Activities from Mallick et al., 2023 and de Brito and Dekker, 2004
initiated through a collection/acquisition of a product and ends with distribution and/or sale
of recovered products.
As de Brito and Dekker, 2004 describes, reverse logistics does not only cover End-of-Life
products, but all products, components, or materials that have to be returned for vari-
ous reasons and with various recovery options. de Brito and Dekker, 2004 distinguishes
between 5 general initiating points of a reverse logistics system:
1. Initial return within some money-back-guarantee time (or similar)
2. Warranty and service (repair or replacement free of charge under conditions)
3. Repair and maintenance (without warranty, with additional charge)
4. End-of-Use (A return opportunity at a certain life stage of the product, where the
product is deemed to not be applicable by the user any longer. Could be through
leasing or to second hand markets)
5. End-of-Life (end of economic, emotional or physical lifespan)
While all these reasons are probable and valid, the focus will however lie on End-of-Use
(EoU) and End-of-Life (EoL) in this thesis.
Considering the recovery activity, Dekker et al., 2000 poses that the recovery strategy
defines much of the network structure and necessary activities. One of the most recent
contributions to recovery strategies was by Blomsma et al., 2019, who defines and array
of circular strategies to consider when designing circular economy products and services
(as depicted on figure 3.7).
Combining these strategies defined by Blomsma et al., 2019 with the defining network
layout typologies found in Dekker et al., 2000, 4 general types of RL systems can be
defined:
1. Bulk recycling systems - includes recycle, cascade and recover as strategies. Here,
as noted by Blomsma et al., 2019, recovery is focused on material value.
2. Remanufacturing/repurposing systems (of both products ands parts) - includes up-
grade, refurbish, remanufacture, repurpose (with added processeing steps). Here,
value is created through processes to either meet old requirements and criteria, or
new ones.
3. Direct reuse systems - includes reuse as its core strategy, and repurposing without
added process steps. Here, value is simply maintained, and requires the product to
be in a condition that meets new requirements/demands.
4. Ladder systems - a mixture of the strategies above. This process is more flexible
to different input qualities, and may result in more total recovered products/compo-
nents/materials.

3.4
Whole system theory - coupling theory
As the proposed system in figure 3.5 builds on developing new couplings between sys-
tems, A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023 provides insight for how to do so. Couplings
describe physical and meta-physical artefacts that aid in the flow of resources from one
system to another. A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023 explains that transitions are facilitated
by the coupling of systems that were not previously coupled. This could for example be
the coupling between the mobility system and the electrical grid, between which a cou-
pling is necessary if we want to be able to drive electrical vehicles. Not only does the
systems need to become integrated, but changes within each system to become aligned
is necessary.
To explain the emergent dynamics and interactions between the systems, A. D. Andersen
and Geels, 2023 proposes two types of couplings that defines the relationships between
systems.
Figure 3.9: Inter relatedness of functional and structural couplings, adapted from A. D.
Andersen and Geels, 2023
1. Functional Couplings. The flow of resources from one system to another. It is
this new ”high-level” coupling between socio-technical systems, that is the goal of a
socio-technical systems transition. An example of a functional coupling is the flow
of electricity between the electrical grid and mobility system to enable the use of
electrical vehicles. The functional couplings is facilitated by structural couplings.
2. Structural Couplings These are elements that exist (or are created) between sys-
tems on three dimensions (technology, actors, and institutions). A structural cou-
pling may be any type of boundary object/artefact that facilitates a functional cou-
pling between systems. An example of a structural coupling is the plug for electric
cars, which facilitates the flow of electricity from electric grid to electric vehicle.
16
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
==PAGE:27==
When new functional couplings are created between systems, tension will often occur, as
the necessary structural couplings to facilitate this transition are often not present when
the systems are first established (A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023). The creation of
structural and functional couplings is a difficult task, often due to the interrelated nature
complex socio-technical systems (Tromp and Hekkert, 2019, Isaksson et al., 2021). It is
incredibly difficult to predict how systems will interact once combined (Heino et al., 2021),
and new systems usually do not have a common ground on which to build upon, es-
pecially during the early phases of a transition (Bakker et al., 2015, Maier et al., 2023).
This common ground is what is necessary for creating socio-technical systems, and un-
derstanding the values, conventions, challenges and perspectives of the other systems
is necessary. To speed up the process of transitions, this common ground needs to be
facilitated and supported. This involves understanding at a deep level what structural cou-
plings are needed in/between which systems (Geels et al., 2023). Thus, the time it takes
to identify the structural couplings and necessary boundary objects between the systems,
along with the ability to create the couplings are what may slow or speed up progress
between systems transitions (A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023).
Creating these new functional and structural couplings creates new interactions between
the businesses, manufacturers and actors within the socio-technical system. A. D. An-
dersen and Geels, 2023 defines 6 multi-system interactions:
1. Technology related interactions - The use of new technologies may require further
couplings of systems unforeseen prior to implementation. These necessary cou-
plings may create bottlenecks or unforeseen consequences.
2. Complimentary technologies - When attempting to diffuse a technology into a sys-
tem, other complimentary technologies may be required. Thus, what might have
seemed like a single-artefact change, was a portfolio of changes.
3. Businesses diversifying into new fields - Some firms may see an opportunity to di-
versify their portfolio and build cross-system actor couplings on their own or through
collaboration. Thus, businesses with capabilities that were needed in one system,
may find their capabilities to fit into new systems. Otherwise, new capabilities must
be created internally.
4. Learning processes - When users begin learning processes in one system, this be-
havioural change may lead to enhanced capabilities in another similar system, mak-
ing adoption much easier. (for better or worse)
5. Institutional tensions - Across systems, tensions may occur due to the new functional
couplings, especially if the structural couplings are not created. These tensions can
greatly diminish the transition speed.
6. Cultural conventions - New meta rules facilitate the couplings. This is derived from
convention theory, where the rules of the game may need to be put into question. An
example may be seen in the change in the behaviour concerning electrical usage in
households. A change A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023 finds is conventions going
from ”supply meet demand” towards ”demand meets supply”.
To facilitate a successful and faster transition, these interactions and the structural cou-
plings should be systematically designed for, but no current support exists. Thus, to accel-
erate the rate of transition, identifying and enabling and supporting the design of couplings
and the interactions between systems is a key factor.

3.5
Organisational Transitions
Transitions do not occur only between systems and organisations, but internally in the or-
ganisations as well. This requires in large part new capabilities. Pigosso and McAloone,
2021 unfolds the different aspects of necessary capabilities needed in an organisation
or company to be ready to transition towards Circular Economy. They detail how per-
forming a gap analysis between current and needed capabilities (defined as a readiness
assessment) before investing heavily in transitioning towards Circular Economy leads to
higher chances of success of implementation. Thus, bridging the gap between current
and needed capabilities creates a larger level of readiness.
Pigosso and McAloone, 2021 defines 8 Circular Economy Readiness dimensions (con-
taining different capabilities) in which an organisation attempting to transition to a circular
business model should develop capabilities within (see figure 3.10). The readiness di-
mensions frames the future development of the businesses to be able to create a circular
transition.
Figure 3.10: Adapted from Pigosso and McAloone, 2021
Pigosso and McAloone, 2021 argues that clarity about which capabilities are required
is determining of the success of the implementation of circular business models in gen-
eral. Capability clarification will be necessary when seeking to design and implement a
reverse logistics system, and therefore a necessary aspect to consider when seeking to
develop RL systems. A key factor is thus clarifying what capabilities are needed for RL
implementation in the built sector for the organisations that Milva will be supporting.

4.3.1
Systematic Literature Review
The SLR had the purpose of defining what types of RL systems that exist in the literature
(RQ1.2). The goal of answering this research question was to expand the understanding
of what commonalities within RL systems are prevalent, and expand the initial problem of
structural coupling identification time and level of capabilities necessary to develop.
Originally this inquiry was limited for the built sector, but attempted search-strings provided
limited results. This issue has been voiced by other literature reviews (e.g. Ding et al.,
2023, Wijewickrama et al., 2021). Thus, the SLR was expanded into all domains with
appropriately described RL systems. Findings from these other domains will need to be
compared to findings in the built sector (empirical study), as they may be too domain-
specific and therefore non-applicable within the built sector.
To ensure that the protocol created for this inquiry is replicable, the template and process
proposed by Biolchini et al., 2005 was followed (See appendix DS1.20 for filled out tem-
plate). To limit and ensure that the most applicable insights are found, only recent articles
were included (the past 10 years). Along with this, articles could include both journal arti-
cles and reviews, but conference papers and book chapters were excluded. Only English
papers were accepted. The database Scopus was chosen due to its focus on engineering
design. The following search string was used:
TITLE-ABS-KEY((”Take back” OR ”reverse logistic*” OR ”reverse supply chain” OR
”close* loop supply chain” ) AND ( archetyp* OR categor* OR design* OR framework OR
typ* ) AND ( ”end of life” OR ”end of use” OR waste ) AND ( product OR component OR
part ) ANDNOT ( ”life cycle assessment” OR ”life cycle costing” OR algorithm))
The search string was designed to include a description of the type of RL system to be
designed, and only for end-of-life/use. The search string also excluded evaluations and
algorithmic optimisation models for RL systems, as the focus was on RL system design
and not optimisation. The inclusion and exclusion criteria may be viewed in figure 4.2,
along with an overview of the process.

The open looped business model, meaning that of third party manufacturing companies
that recover other manufacturers products, but does not have any original manufactur-
ing, can have certain advantages compared to closed looped manufacturing companies
(Kalverkamp and Young, 2019). An open looped business model model have better op-

current production (Kalverkamp and Young, 2019, Veleva and Bodkin, 2018). Original
manufacturers (OM) with closed looped RL systems will however have better knowledge
about the requirements for the products and be able to integrate recovered and new prod-

and capabilities for scaling systems to industrial levels rapidly (Veleva and Bodkin, 2018).
However, a challenge is complexity management when integrating new systems with old
ones. According to Veleva and Bodkin, 2018 this need for low complexity of business op-
erations may be a reason for lower a lower level of innovative RL systems and recovery
strategies.
As legislative initiatives are now expected to create a market pull for recovered products,
OM’s must consider offering their products in a recovered version. Even though the RL
systems might not be a profitable business case at first glance, some OM’s may thus
choose to establish RL systems to increase and/or protect their market share (Veleva and
Bodkin, 2018). This protection of market share can drive the transition, as exemplified by

party company will, and their market share will decrease (See appendix DS1.8). Other
motivations were also highlighted, but protection of market share seems to be a key driver.

Along with understanding the problem space of the couplings, the contextual factors points
to two aspects of each coupling that needs to be addressed, which expands upon the pure
technical view of A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023:
• Technical structural couplings (physical boundary objects that facilitates the flow
of resources). This type of coupling is derived from A. D. Andersen and Geels,
2023, where boundary objects aids to facilitate the flow of physical objects. Exam-
ples from literature include Lewis et al., 2017 who looks into optimising the use of
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector

returned products as raw material through software, Ding et al., 2023 who highlights
the lack of facilitation from boundary objects across systemic boundaries, Kongar
et al., 2015 who highlights technology as mediators of information linkages, and
Kumar, 2017 and Wilson and Goffnett, 2022 who highlights the need for physical
boundaries/locations for the RL system to facilitate returns.
• Informational structural couplings (Formalised information that enables the flow
of resources through building trust, awareness and sharing characteristics of com-
ponents). This perspective adds to A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023, and is inspired
from Wijewickrama et al., 2021, Ding et al., 2023, Shi et al., 2019 and Conti and

facilitate the physical flow of products. Shi et al., 2019 highlights the lack of tech-
nical standards that enables trust for the users/procurers, and Wijewickrama et al.,
2021, Conti and Orcioni, 2019 and Ding et al., 2023 stresses that missing informa-
tional couplings between life-stages of buildings affects the RL system negatively. If
an informational coupling is missing between the newly coupled systems, tensions
within the systems will greatly increase and inhibit the transition speed due to dis-
trust (Wijewickrama et al., 2021). Considering couplings that support specifically
informational flows needs to be supported.
When designing couplings between systems, there is thus a need to both include infor-
mational and technical couplings. These couplings are dependent on the available infor-
mation, biases and perspectives the designers have (Wijewickrama et al., 2021). This
is not only in what couplings should be created, but also how they should be designed.
Enabling designers to have a domain specific foundation from which to design and ex-
plore the problem space of couplings is a key factor (Ding et al., 2023, Wijewickrama
et al., 2021). Supporting this aspect of coupling design may decrease the difficulty of
adopting/designing RL systems in the built sector.

As stated in the research clarification, Pigosso and McAloone, 2021 found that clarifying
capabilities internally in a company and comparing with the necessary capabilities for
circular economy increases the chances of implementing a circular economy successfully.
However, as RL systems design is only a corner of the wider circular economy research
field, a prioritisation of what capabilities to focus on may be necessary.
From the empirical study, results verify that establishing a RL system will require ca-
pabilities that are not currently within the organisation capabilities (IF RS1, RS4, RS-

ployees are needed (as underlined by A:Gain (appendix DS1.4) and J. Jensen Genbrug
(appendix DS1.6) who hired new employees for RL specific activities), and identifying
what processes should be mastered internally (as indicated by E&P (appendix DS1.5)).
These necessary production and process capabilities should be in focus.

a clarification of where these links between current production and taken back products
may be beneficial for identifying changes to facilitate this synergy. Uriarte-Miranda et al.,
2018 highlights the system-dependency of infrastructure, especially for recycling systems
at scale and understanding what the underlying necessary production changes are is an
integral part of the RL design. Kongar et al., 2015 also highlights the need for specific
information gathering processes in their case of reselling medicine. Understanding what
information is necessary within the value chain to test for internally is important, as the
internal information gathering needs to match the needs further up the value chain.

• ”Takeback and end-of-life” since capabilities are needed to ensure maximised value

• ”Product & Service Innovation” since technical capabilities within the area of devel-
oping new products from returned products are needed

systems and partnerships

Design Process
A main capability necessary to implement Take-back for the manufacturing companies is
to be able to design RL systems (as underlined by Mallick et al., 2022, Mallick et al., 2023,
Wilson and Goffnett, 2022, Ding et al., 2023, Uriarte-Miranda et al., 2018, Veleva and
Bodkin, 2018, Mascle, 2018 and Kumar, 2017). The empirical study found that none of
the companies had used any frameworks, tools or methods to develop their RL systems
to design their RL systems (See appendices DS1.3 through DS1.11). An evolutionary
approach was favoured, due to the lack of support systems. This was highlighted as a
slow process, which is echoed by Geels et al., 2023. Several frameworks and methods
for RL design do exist (See appendix DS1.1 for frameworks), but specific support tools for
RL system development in the built sector is lacking. This point is shared by McAloone
and Hauschild, 2023 who looks at the general design for sustainability domain:
”Design for sustainability is a well-established discipline, with many potentially useful
methods and tools to enable sustainable engineering systems, but there is limited
material on actual process support to aid the design of sustainable engineering
systems.” (McAloone and Hauschild, 2023)
Thus, increasing the companies capabilities through understanding, guiding and provid-
ing an overview of the design process of RL systems may decrease the difficulty of RL
adoption, and increase the transition speed. The question is therefore what in the design
process needs to be supported?
From the literature analysis and empirical study there was no significant difference be-
tween the factors for different network typology design. This original hypothesis regarding
RQ 1.2 ”What types of RL systems exist?” was that there would be a significant difference
between RL typologies in the design process. In the contrary, there were a lot of simi-
larities between the 4 typologies. The only true difference between the network typology
design was a shift in focus on processes. For reuse, the inspection/sorting/testing pro-
cess was increasingly more important than for recycling and remanufacturing, where the
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
43
==PAGE:54==
recovery process was more in focus (see appendix DS1.15 for a detailed analysis).
Looking at the more generalised findings, the design process of RL systems in the liter-
ature is often based on new lines of products (A. L. Andersen et al., 2023, Veleva and
Bodkin, 2018, Mallick et al., 2022). Creating new products with increased take-back sup-
port may be possible to realise an environmental impact within a shorter time frame in
certain domains like electronic equipment (Conti and Orcioni, 2019, Kumar, 2017), the
bio-medical industry (Mallick et al., 2022) or the textile industry (Lewis et al., 2017), but
within the built environment, product lifespans are too long for this approach, as high-
lighted by Ding et al., 2023. Thus, an increased amount of control over the operation/use
stage is not possible, and many of the tools and frameworks are not directly applicable
(in Wilson and Goffnett, 2022, Mallick et al., 2022, and Kumar, 2017).
While this may paint a dark image of RL systems design focusing on existing products
in use, the existing frameworks may yet provide useful insights for the further design of
RL systems in the built sector. A. L. Andersen et al., 2023, Veleva and Bodkin, 2018
and Lewis et al., 2017 highlights how the current design is crucial for the disassembly
process when considering remanufacturing as a recovery strategy. A. L. Andersen et
al., 2023 highlights that the recovery option is dependent on the complexity of the re-
covery processes. While it may seem contradictive, considering destructive methods for
disassembly may be an option. Wilson and Goffnett, 2022 and Uriarte-Miranda et al.,
2018 highlights that along with complexity, profitability needs to be realised in innovative
manners. In certain cases, excessive reprocessing in an activity may require new per-
spectives/frames. This perspective adds to the findings from Wilson and Goffnett, 2022,
Mascle, 2018 and Conti and Orcioni, 2019, who underlines that considering all network ty-
pologies is quite necessary, as possibilities for successful RL systems exists within every
typology. Thus, expanding the solution space of the recovery and its activities is important
to challenge the complexities of the RL system.
As such, the design of recovery strategy needs to be supported to manage complexity.
Cases in the SLR concerning a ladder strategy found very implicit rules for when a prod-
uct would be reused, remanufactured or recycled. Kumar, 2017 found the recovery of
phones in India to be based on experience without any formal training. Conti and Orcioni,
2019 and Wilson and Goffnett, 2022 highlighted that information could be deciding what
recovery option was most appropriate, where Conti and Orcioni, 2019 expands this by
underlining that requirements for the choice of recovery option is very much necessary.
Much of the complexity is an emergent property of the former use (Conti and Orcioni,
2019, Kumar, 2017, Wilson and Goffnett, 2022). Thus, it is important to consider where,
when and how the product has been installed and operated - as Conti and Orcioni, 2019
and Kumar, 2017 highlights, even products characteristics once thought to be good can
change, as hazardous materials (which has changing regulations over time and geogra-
phy) can inhibit certain recovery options.
From the 8 frameworks in the SLR, the following commonalities for RL design process
was found:
• Providing an overview of the entire system is necessary when designing RL sys-
tems, and using a holistic approach for the design of the RL systems is key. This
means considering all activities in the value chain (Mallick et al., 2022,Mallick et al.,
2023, Ding et al., 2023, Wilson and Goffnett, 2022, Uriarte-Miranda et al., 2018,
Mascle, 2018), and designing the synergies between the current forward loop of
new products to work with the RL system (Ding et al., 2023, Mallick et al., 2022).
To ensure that a holistic design is achieved, de Brito and Dekker, 2004 and Mallick

Design Uncertainty Management
Design uncertainty management was found in the SLR and empirical study to be of high
importance as a capability within the organisations. In this project, design uncertainty
management refers to uncertainty internally in the design process of the RL system, and
external sources of uncertainty affecting the design choices and strategies. The exter-
nal uncertainties were found to be especially concerning the amounts and quality of the
returned products.
Internal Design Uncertainty Management
The case-company Milva indicated that assumptions often drives the perceived problems
of creating RL systems (See appendix DS1.16). Along with this, Veleva and Bodkin, 2018
46
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
==PAGE:57==
underlines that assumptions should be challenged to create the best possible designs. In-
terviewees indicated that initial assumptions had to be overcome in order for the design
to be successful (See appendix DS1.17 for full list of assumptions found in the prioritised
IF’s). Most assumptions they found were also ”untrue ”when challenged (i.e. they were
only a perceived barrier and not an actual one). Through challenging the assumptions,
their RL systems could be implemented and designed more freely. Examples of this
includes E&P (appendix DS1.5) mentioning that assumptions drove demolition choices
(i.e. they found that a toilet is easier and faster to de-install rather than destroy, yet the
common assumption was that is was not), J. Jensen Genbrug (appendix DS1.6) defying
the assumptions of Ifö, who assumed that a toilet could not be cleaned properly at EoU,
A:Gain (appendix DS1.4) defying assumptions of DEKO by handling tolerances and incor-
porating recovered insulated glass units in partition walls, and Gentræ (appendix DS1.11)
challenging the assumption that there is only low risk appetite out there, finding that a
private market was okay with a higher uncertainty of product characteristics.
As such, assumptions need to be challenged, as the design will otherwise be hindered by
internally perceived issues.
External Design Uncertainty Management - quality and quantity
The SLR found three main sources of external uncertainty that affected the RL design:
• Quality of returned products. A. L. Andersen et al., 2023 postulates that differing
qualities of returned products will require a more flexible remanufacturing process,
as variations within the returned products will high. Along with this, sorting product
types may be a difficult process. They also reflect that too many variations in the
returned products may infer that only the material value remains.
• Quantity of returned products. A. L. Andersen et al., 2023 highlights that the
amount of returned products is influenced by obscure return flows, fluctuating re-
turns, diverse product types and perceived conditions. Veleva and Bodkin, 2018
highlights that the demand for recovered products can be quite uncertain as well.
• Product return timing. Tripathy et al., 2023 highlights that forecasting of returns in
the electronics industry can aid to lower the uncertainty of timing. Ding et al., 2023
juxtaposes this within the built sector, where products have much longer lifespans,
and returns are near impossible to predict. However, they highlight that including
buffer times in the RL system can be an appropriate solution (through e.g. extended
storage).
Interviewees agreed on these perspectives as well. The perceptions of uncertainty re-
garding quality and quantity would create requirements for acquisition that would greatly
limit the amount of collected products (Gamle Mursten interview (appendix DS1.3), Troldtekt
interview (appendix DS1.8), E&P interview (appendix DS1.5), Gentræ interview (appendix
DS1.11)). There would also be a chance of assumed available products differing be-
tween screenings (Teknologisk institut (appendix DS1.10), J. Jensen Genbrug (appendix
DS1.6)).
Thus, being able to manage and circumvent these uncertainty sources in the design of
the RL system is a key factor. Perspectivating to external literature apart from the SLR
articles, Oehmen and Kwakkel, 2023 defines an appropriate level of uncertainty to design
systems from as ”scenario uncertainty”. This level describes a finite amount of possibili-
ties/variations that can occur within a product-system. Being able to guide the uncertainty
level towards a scenario description may facilitate the management of the uncertainties.

Prescriptive Study
5.1
Research Questions tackled in this section
The aim of the Prescriptive Study phase is to consolidate the knowledge gathered in
a support tool that aids closed loop manufacturers to create an initial RL system. The
following main research questions was created to support the development process:
RQ 2: How might a support tool be developed to aid organisations in imple-
menting RL/takeback systems in the built sector?
The following two sub-questions were defined to further aid the prescriptive study process:
RQ 2.1: How can RL systems be designed specifically for the built sector?

As both A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023 and Wijewickrama et al., 2021 highlights, the
considerations of the couplings needs to be context specific, as every RL system provides
unique system interactions. As such, there is no perfect perspective or frame that can be
universally applied to all RL systems design contexts. Thus, to enhance the tool for criteria

questions) based on the IF’s and CF’s uncovered in the empirical study and SLR (for list

problem and solution space of each coupling by considering each frame one at a time.
The frame is discussed and facilitates new problem/solution space perspectives. The
participants thus explore potential solutions, which are explored and documented on the
template.

plings. This is inspired by A. D. Andersen and Geels, 2023, who (as detailed in section

2023. Thus, considering the roots of the interactions may aid in highlighting potential ten-
sions within the couplings. To explore the root causes of the tensions, the participants are

6.3
Testing setup
As the tool seeks to aid the development of the RL systems to aid in the transition of the
built sector towards a circular economy, several potential test methodologies may be use-
ful. However, as the tool needs to function properly in the development process, a frame-
work that provides user experience feedback may be most useful at the current stage.
A powerful perspective of orientating the needs that require fulfilment and understanding
when seeking to solve a design problem are the ”Three Lenses of Human-Centered De-
sign” (T. Brown, 2009). The three lenses consists of viability, feasibility and desirability,
and a slight adjustment of their definitions lead to the following use of the terms:
Feasibility refers to whether the solution can technically possible to facilitate the change.
To ensure that the entirety of the developed support tool is feasible, the feasibility must be
assessed in relation to the aspects of capability clarification, coupling design and usability
for the company.
Viability refers to whether the solution works successfully (i.e. how well it facilitates the
workshop with for the OM’s). Viability must also be assessed in relation to the aspects of
capabilities, couplings and usability for the company.
Desirability refers to how much people want the solution and whether it meets their needs
and desires. Desirability must be assessed in the aspects of Way of working and usability.
To answer the research questions tackled in this phase, a test setup was defined to ensure
that both viability, feasibility and desirability was tested. Since the time frame of the project
was limited, two versions of the test setup were formulated; an ideal test setup and the
actual test setup.

Reflection, Discussion and Limitations
7.1
Reflection of the Process
7.1.1
Research Clarification
The project started out from a very wide perspective in the RC, looking at the broad ques-
tion ”how to accelerate the green transition of the built sector”. The potential challenges
and problems within this scope was extensive, which resulted in a lot of explorative search.
This could have been avoided by choosing a more narrow scope to begin with, but may
have provided in a poor choice of problem.
The research questions and key factors guided the process in part, but due to the ab-
ductive nature of the process it was difficult to follow them precisely. They did provide
guidance to the process, but the RQ’s were refined over time as the authors gained in-
sights into the specific domains. As such, we could have been more systematic in our use
of the RQ’s and set them up more robustly from the outset. For example, RQ 1 contained
in part the hypothesis that network typologies were a defining factor of RL systems design.
This RQ was formulated too narrowly, as it turned out that the types of RL systems design
was less important than first assumed. The authors had to pivot after the first investigation
into this, where the expansion of the key factors became more important. In hindsight,
decoupling the hypothesis from the RQ would have been beneficial, to avoid fixation on
a RQ that was too narrow.
7.1.2
Descriptive Study 1
Systematic Literature Review
The SLR builds strongly upon the results gathered by using a specific search string to
search for articles at the research database, Scopus. This way, the entirety of the study
was very dependant of the specific search string and the inclusion/exclusion criteria. The
search string was composed of key words that build upon the material and information
gathered from the RC phase. Before making the final decision of the formulation of the
search string, a few iterations of the search string was tested in Scopus, and the results
were briefly investigated, mainly by reading the titles of the hits. Since one of the con-
clusions from the literature search was, that a very limited amount of material existed on
design of take-back systems and take-back systems of building components in general,
and since the search string was very much influenced of the gathered information from
the RC phase, searching with another search string and other inclusion/exclusion criteria,
might have changed the results.
When analysing the found articles in the SLR for their key take aways, the extraction of
such information will always be biased of nature, and the reader will naturally search for
take aways within a specific focus, being more or less conscious about it. The extracted
key take aways from the literature was thus to some degree biased by the focus areas
already established in the RC phase.
The initial analysis of the extracted key take aways were done by separating the key take
aways into 4 groups depending on their category of network typology. In these groups the
take aways were clustered in themes to form contextual factors. later on in the project, it
was realised that the factors influencing the success of a reverse logistics system were
less dependant on the network typology than initially expected. This lead to the decision
of choosing not to target the development process of the support tool to be dependant
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector

==PAGE:100==
of the different network typologies. If the contextual factors were formed from clusters of
the key take aways from all articles, instead of clusters only within the specific network
typologies, the contextual factors may have looked different and the analysis of them may
have shown a slightly different result.
Empirical Study
The empirical study consisted of interviews with relevant actors within the built environ-
ment and manufacturing companies of building products that already have implemented
RL systems. Only a very limited amount of manufacturers in the built sector with RL sys-
tems were identified, and most worked with an open looped RL system. Furthermore,
during interviews they often recommended each other as well. This may be derived from
the support networks they are part of. Companies trying to make a change seek each
other and meet in different discussion forums. Interestingly, only one of the interviewed
companies, Rockwool, was a manufacturing company with an implemented closed looped
RL system, and their recovery strategy was recycling. Rockwool was however not part of
the support networks. A natural question to ask may be whether it makes sense to inter-
view companies with open looped RL systems, when developing support for companies
with a focus on closed looped strategies. The two models of RL systems are both differ-
ent and share similarities. Open looped systems are in general less complex and have
wider possibilities in terms of options when designing the activities, as the RL systems
does not need integration into a current production, and the recovered products does not
necessarily need to match already existing products. Closed looped system, on the other
hand need to integrate their processes to fit their current business processes, which adds
complexity. With that being said, the process and prompts needed when designing the
specific activities related to collection, inspection, recovery, storage and resale, should
not differ significantly, since most of the necessary considerations are similar for the two
types of systems, with the integration aspects for the closed looped systems as the main
difference. The definition of the receiver between OM’s and third party manufacturers
may not have as large of a consequence as originally thought. However, designing for
the most complex actor was beneficial, as the tool can be translated to an open loop con-
text now. If the user of the tool is an open-loop manufacturer, retailer or distributor, they
skip part of steps 1 and 6 of the tool. It will also require more pre-work, as the original
manufacturer was assumed to have extensive knowledge of requirements, characteris-
tics, functionality and more of the product. That said, the output may be very different
between the two types of users as described in section 4.4.1. While open loop manufac-
turers can more easily create the RL system and offer a deeper transition, to meet the
speed of the transition at a global level closed loop RL systems may be more necessary.
While the interviews conducted had the main focus of mapping out a value chain, the
interviews could have emphasised the company’s process of establishing their system
more. As described, all interviewees from companies declared that no systematic pro-
cess or tools had been used to develop their take-back system, but to deep dive in this
unstructured process of establishing a system could have provided useful insights. Seek-
ing out the specific considerations the companies had made during their design processes
in a more exhaustive manner may have provided interesting considerations for the tool
development and problem definition.

Requirements Generation
The specification underwent several iterations before the final version in this report. The
process of creating and managing requirements and criteria is naturally an iterative pro-
cess, as described by Zimmermann and de Weck, 2023. Requirements may see changes
due to reductions in ambiguity, general volatility or increasing/decreasing completeness of
the requirements. The general process of the specification development was to increase
completeness and reduce ambiguity, which may have increased the amount of volatility in
the project. When requirements changes to become more complete or less ambiguous,
the scope of the project would slightly be narrowed further, which aided the end-result,
but created increased need for active reflection during the project. The feasibility of the
specification was also in focus, as the set requirements encompassed a larger problem
initially, including vision definition and evaluation of the designed RL concepts. Given the
time and resources available, including these parts into a potential design may have ”wa-
tered down” the solution, and the support would not have been developed satisfactorily.
That said, the defined requirements are still encompassing a large problem. In hindsight,
narrowing the problem space even further would have allowed for more time developing
the tool to become more specific. Doing this may have reduced the completeness of the
specification though, and the support tool may have lacked in key parts, as Zimmermann
and de Weck, 2023 highlights.
Furthermore, the case company Milva also affected the specification significantly. The
specification (including vision and evaluation aspects) was used initially rather systemati-
cally for the ideation, but as recommended by Cross, 2008, functions were defined for the
ideation instead. The defined functions used for ideation were derived from a feedback
session with Milva (see appendix PS2). Using the functions for solution space exploration
aided in defining the problem space as well, as they aided in prioritising the requirements.

the higher prioritised requirements would require more time and resources to meet satis-

Prescriptive Study
The main focus of the tool development was placed within the value-chain design and
coupling design aspects, as indicated by the weights in the WOM (see Appendix PS5).
This prioritisation of design aspects was assessed due to the analysis in chapter DS1,
where the focus of a lot of the findings was on the value chain design and coupling de-
sign. That said, other aspects could be more important than designed for in the current
iteration. For example, readiness is a precursor for initiating circular projects/programs/-
portfolios (Pigosso and McAloone, 2021). Thus, there could have been a larger focus on
the necessary changes and the change-management process for the companies. That
said, knowing what you are getting ready for is maybe just as important and a precursor
for change management.
As such, the focus was mainly on the value chain design and the coupling design, but
providing an overview of changes in capabilities was still part of the design (6th step, pro-
duction mapping). Adding this focus of capability changes was done to meet the key factor
3.1, where having the overview of necessary changes within the three highlighted dimen-
sions weas highlighted. A question may thus be, was it satisfactory to simply provide
an overview of current and future capabilities? The approach in Pigosso and McAloone,
2021 defines 8 dimensions to consider in the readiness assessment, along with a prioriti-
sation of the dimensions based on the gaps the companies have. In our work, we found
that 3 of the 8 dimensions were especially important for RL design. These dimensions
were integrated into the tool implicitly (manufacturing and value chain capabilities in the
value-chain design and coupling design templates, product and service innovation in the
first step, and take-back and end-of-life strategies in the value-chain design). This focus
on the three aspects could be questioned, and certainly the other dimensions will become
key aspects in the following development of the RL system (as underlined by McAloone
and Hauschild, 2023). However for an initial conceptual design, focusing on a less com-
plex problem space may be beneficial. The overview of changes is thus primed largely
by the tool, but it could also be more explicit through prompts aimed at these dimensions.

7.2
Discussion of Support Tool
The overall aim of the support tool was, as stated in the design brief, to provide a solid
foundation from which manufacturing companies may design RL systems in a systematic
manner. This was done to decrease the time necessary to define the initial problem space
for the companies. The question was thus if we have provided an appropriate level of
experience and knowledge to build upon, and if it was done in an appropriately systematic
manner.
As for the foundation, the tool incorporates over 300 insights from 6 cases, 2 knowledge
institutions and 1 contractor who all are involved with RL systems in the danish context,
along with 80 insights from the SLR 22 cases. While this amount of insights seems large,
it still does not represent the entire field of the RL systems design. It was however still
possible to find thematic similarities within the problem space across the different cases
(as visualised in appendix DS1.13). All problem perspectives could however not be in-
cluded, as the problem area for RL design in the built sector was quite large, and were thus
prioritised. As the prioritisation effort laid the foundation for the problem/solution space
exploration, doing a second or third prioritisation may have been helpful. The prioritisation
was however done through several iterations, through both the interviews, expert testing
and internal discussions between the authors.
The tool functions by speeds up the process of exploring the problem space for the man-
ufacturing companies by punctuating main factors into the tool as requirements and cou-
pling questions. However, as problem spaces are unique to every case and needs to be
contextualised for the specific cases (Cross, 2008), priming systems design towards spe-
cific problems could provide poor results. This is especially true for the requirements in the
tool, which may either be constricting the solution space poorly, or even wrongly in some
cases. Thus, while the requirements in the tool provide a solid foundation from which
to design, they may constrict the solution space poorly. Further exploration of potential
requirements and delivery methods needs to be explored (as the test at Lendager group
noted, requirements could be areas that needs to be explored, see appendix DS2.9).
The IF questions in the coupling design template goes in another direction than the re-
quirements in the value chain, as the design of couplings is potentially more complex
than internal processes due to the nature of complex systems (Geels et al., 2023). Here,
the foundation to design from is conceptualised as frames, from which the manufacturing
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
93
==PAGE:104==
companies can consider the coupling from different perspectives. The choice of design is
much more dependent on the context of the coupling, and setting pre-made requirements
and criteria for the coupling design was not considered appropriate in this context. The
systematic process used in the value chain design tool could also be used here. How-
ever, clear cut logic for choices is difficult to apply, which is also why we veered away
from the idea of a configurator for the design. While the process of the coupling design
could be more systematic, the foundation is deemed to be appropriate, but needs to be
further explored.
As for the systematic manner of the tool, much inspiration was derived from Cross, 2008
concerning the systematic generation of ideas and morphological approach for idea com-
bination. This was especially used for the value chain template. Along with this, consid-
ering what specific stage each part of the workshop is in (divergence/convergence) aids
to systematise the approaches. The need for the systematic approach was seen in in
several articles (Shi et al., 2019, Veleva and Bodkin, 2018, Kongar et al., 2015, Mallick
et al., 2022, Mallick et al., 2023, and Mallick et al., 2024), but with slightly different per-
spectives. For example, Veleva and Bodkin, 2018 focuses mainly of the business model
end with a focus on managerial issues, Kongar et al., 2015 focuses mainly on the infor-
mation gathering aspect, while Mallick et al., 2023 broadens out the scope to a holistic
approach. While specific activities may require more attention depending on the network
typology, we side with the more holistic approach in Mallick et al., 2023. This attends to
one of the core barriers for developing RL systems, as stated previously, of having the
overview of all the activities necessary to consider. It could be argued that not all parts
of the RL system needs to be considered systematically with alternatives (e.g. transport
can be optimised through mathematical modelling as indicated by Dekker et al., 2000),
but in the initial design stage it may be beneficial to consider different alternatives, so the
design may be as effective as possible, and can subsequently become efficient.
Support of overall transition
As the tool aims to speed up the process of transitions, a key question is whether the
tool does so on the account of neglecting the depth of the transition. This paradox is
highlighted by A. D. Andersen et al., 2023 and Geels et al., 2023. A deep transition is
a fundamental shift between systems, and will more often than not take a longer period
of time to take root (Geels et al., 2023). Geels et al., 2023 argues that rapid changes in
systems may not be changing enough to meaningfully impact the system transition goals,
while deep transitions may not be able to be implemented in time to have the sustain-
able impact necessary at the societal level. As the depth of transitions are increased, the
amount of necessary structural couplings increase, and the tensions inside systems are
often increased as well (A. D. Andersen et al., 2023). To take this into account, the support
tool attempts to systematically address each coupling and explore the roots of potential
tensions. The tool thus implies that knowing what couplings are necessary and clarifying
what potential tensions may occur between actors will aid the transition speed. This is dif-
ficult to test for within the scope of the thesis, and thus builds largely on the whole system
transition theory. The focus has been on the OM’s and what they can design, but they
should preferably not be the only actors pushing for the transition. There is also a need for
regulators to be part of the change, and to work together with the manufacturers (a per-
spective shared by Shi et al., 2019). Providing cross-sector support collaboration could
be a potential avenue for future design. That said, building upon as much experience
and knowledge as possible may aid in both accelerating and deepening the transition.
As indicated by A. D. Andersen et al., 2023, designing from experience for more radical
change is necessary, which was sought to be part of every aspect of the tool.

Another part of the transition path question is that of multiple interfaces, as the focus of
this thesis is on single-product systems in isolation. When/if multiple manufacturers im-
plements RL systems, what will happen? As systems may become developed at a faster
rate with tools like the one in this thesis, multiple RL systems within the same product
arena with unique coupling designs may arise. While the emergence of several potential
systems may seem positive for the overall transition, they can prove to be detrimental
to the overall scaling and impact of the transition. This is highlighted by Papachristos,
2017 and Bakker et al., 2015, who looks into the standardisation of systems to accelerate
transitions. When multiple systems create their own standards for structural couplings,
non-harmonized systems are created, where the actors within the different systems are
faced with multiple platforms and local standards to tap into (Papachristos, 2017). This
can be intrusive through local path dependencies, which may block global standards to
be formulated (Bakker et al., 2015). As Papachristos, 2017 finds through their systems
dynamics modelling, having multiple platforms as local lock-ins will greatly diminish the
transition speed at a global scale. Thus, while our tool will aid the local organisations
in transitioning, the more global transition needs to be incorporated into the tool so the
design of new RL systems in the built sector does not inhibit the overall transition to-
wards circular built future. How to do so is however quite unclear, as the literature on the
multi-systems interactions is rather new. As noted by Bakker et al., 2015, identifying and
designing couplings to be harmonised across industry may be a potential way forward -
how to do so is also unclear. Geels et al., 2023, Papachristos, 2017 and A. D. Andersen
et al., 2023 highlights that an evolutionary approach is the current best approach to ensure
a deep transition, but this may, as previously argued, be too time-consuming. Maier et al.,
2023 poses this global scale coupling harmonisation in the chapter ”Engineering Systems
Design: A Look to the Future” from the ”Handbook of engineering systems design” as
”mastering connectability” at a societal level, and underlines that this lacks greatly. Thus,
developing an approach for developing harmonised couplings across different competing
systems would be an important future research endeavour.
Another aspect of the transition is that of complexity. The support tool was designed for
single-product system, and without considerations for ladder typologies (which may in-
crease the overall amount of recovered products). As highlighted by Veleva and Bodkin,
2018, the complexity of RL systems is a great barrier to adoption of RL systems. When
a company has a large portfolio of products, the complexity increases. The built sec-
tor is naturally an ”easier” industry in that regard, since most products are usually quite
simplistic (Risiko Som Barriere for Bæredygtige Materialer, 2023). However, creating
recovery systems for multiple products and product-families may require them to share
similar product modules, which is not easy to backtrack (A. L. Andersen et al., 2023).
This problem may be more prevalent in other industries as the built sector has been using
many of the same product parts reconfigured in different manners (Risiko Som Barriere
for Bæredygtige Materialer, 2023). Thus, exploring more of the complexity management
for multiple products may be a worthwhile effort.
Impact
The transitioning of the built sector towards a circular economy builds on the fact that it cre-
ates a meaningfully sustainable change. Reducing Greenhouse gas emissions has been
at the forefront of the interviewed companies, and a key driver for initiation of projects.
There is as such an assumption that generally recovering products will meaningfully im-
pact the high emissions by the built sector. This notion is shared by the danish govern-
ment in their new regulation (DanskIndustri, 2023). The potential of circularity is indeed
rather high, with sources claiming large reductions of emissions (Foundation and Arup,
2022). At the product level, especially for products that are emission intensive at the raw-
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
95
==PAGE:106==
material and production life-cycle phase, this is true. As most products in the built sector
can be considered passive products (i.e. non-energy consuming products), the potential
is therefore high (Tozan et al., 2021). That said, there are limitations to the potential.
Recovery processes may exceed the emissions of new products, and rebound effects
(systemic responses to sustainability efforts that offset the anticipated effects) (Guzzo et
al., 2023) may also hinder the impact of the circular products. For example, Kalverkamp
and Young, 2019 highlights that new markets could be created, and the substituted prod-
ucts are not the original source. This ”danger” is especially true for repurposed products,
where the recovered products substitutes products in new contexts (thus substituting the
wrong product). Recovered products may also deliver function using more space/weight
than new ones due to worse characteristics, and the impact therefore needs to be consid-
ered at a whole-building scale. That said, as C. E. Andersen et al., 2020 and McAloone
and Hauschild, 2023 highlights, circularity does pose as a significant strategy to meet
planetary boundaries.
While the potential may be true for the individual product, meeting the needs of the growing
housing demand may be a challenge. It touches upon the paradox introduced by Maier et
al., 2023: ”A high standard of living for everyone without paying the environmental price”.
As Maier et al., 2023 poses, the change that is necessary is great, as 25% of the worlds
population account for 75% of the worlds resources. Thus, conventions and rules for
these 25% needs to change at a fundamental level. As criticised by A. D. Andersen and
Geels, 2023, the circular economy has not yet changed the conventions of the systems
they are part of. If circularity should have its highest possible impact, A. D. Andersen
and Geels, 2023 poses that the rules of the economic system should change from from
”supply meets demand” to ”demand meets supply”. To make matters worse, living spaces
per person has increased over the past 25 years (“Boligstørrelse”, n.d.), as has the amount
of people (“Statistics Denmark, Population figures”, n.d.). Thus, meeting new demands
using a product stock that met the needs 25+ years ago seems impossible. That said, it
can create part of the change, and the transition towards planetary boundaries will need
multiple approaches (C. E. Andersen et al., 2020, Maier et al., 2023). While our tool can
help organisations to create potentially more sustainable products, the overall system-
wide impact will not be reached until consumption also decreases (Foundation and Arup,
2022). The support tool does not directly help to facilitate this change. The hope of using
the tool to change business models might be to change the underlying conventions, but
more direct approaches for this would be needed.
7.2.1
Limitations of the tool
The tool has certain limitations. To begin with the the tool is limited by the input of informa-
tion from the empirical study and SLR, and suffers the same limitations as the analysis.
In the tool specifically, the requirements and criteria in the value chain may have been
very different if we had interviewed different companies or people within the companies,
or if we had read different articles. Therefore, the requirements and criteria used in the
value chain design to make it more domain specific are not perfect. The tool may be too
inflexible at its current stage to reflect this, and the degree of constraint for the ideation
may need to be lowered. As the requirements and criteria are attempting to encapsulate
problems found for RL design in the built sector, they do offer a good starting point, but
users of the tool must be made aware that the findings are not universal to every situation.
For the coupling design, the use of frames aids to limit the amount of constraint. By using
frames the participants of the workshop can choose which frames are most applicable to
their context. That said, using requirements to ”force” considerations for problems they
might not have foreseen may still be appropriate. A limitation within the coupling design is

however that of the system change perspective. It was included due to its importance, but
its integration in the tool could have been better. In the current version of the tool there are
no support mechanisms to aid the companies in developing tension-relieving strategies.
Several exist in the literature (See appendix RC4 for a non-exhaustive overview), but due
to time constraints, they were not integrated.
Secondly, the tool is developed to match the point of transition that was presented in
the introduction. If the point of transition had been found to be at an earlier stage, pilot
project support may have been more appropriate, and if the transition was at later stages,
management guidelines and complexity reduction may have been in focus. This does
however mean that the development of the tool is coupled to the transition point analysis,
and is only applicable within a ”short” time frame, before the whole system has transitioned
further. Support for later stages of the transition will be necessary once companies have
begun the design of their initial RL systems.
Thirdly, the tool was made with a danish context in mind. This was done to decrease the
level of complexity in the project. However, considering that many manufacturing com-
panies are international, the tool should preferably aid them in an international context.
Therefore, the findings using the tool may not be applicable within other countries where
other requirements and criteria for activities are needed, or other frames for coupling de-
sign more appropriate. The foundation for building a good initial RL design is thus weaker
in these contexts, but the tool could still be used for the initial exploration.
Fourthly, the tool also assumes several preconditions, and may be limited in use if this is
not further supported. The tool assumes that there already exists motivation and a vision
within the companies using the tool. While the motivation might be existing within the
companies (indicated by Gate21, 2023), supporting the vision definition might be neces-
sary as it will define a lot of the decisions of the design. This lack of support might be
viewed using the definitions from Wilson and Goffnett, 2022 looking at RL systems from
a strategic, tactical and operational perspective. Wilson and Goffnett, 2022 argues that
all perspectives needs to be included in the RL design process, but that the actual design
will happen at a tactical level. The developed support tool is thus a tactical tool, and at its
current stage easily translatable to operational level, as specific steps are easily extracted
and communicated. However, the strategic communication and collaboration part of the
tool is lacking. This could however include setting KPI’s and goals, and defining what a
successful project will entail for the RL design internally within the companies (Wilson and
Goffnett, 2022). This was also touched upon in the DS2 findings with Milva, and further
work to support this aspect is necessary.
Lastly, certain exclusions from the tool limits its applicability, specifically vision definition
and evaluation support. The evaluation of the RL designs would at the very least need to
consider economic viability, uncertainty and sustainability aspects, which was found to be
important in the empirical study (see appendix DS1.13). The uncertainty and economic
aspects were highlighted in both the literature and empirical study, and the sustainability
aspect is an underlying driver for initiation of the RL systems. Evaluation methods was
considered as part of the ideation, and was initially part of the design specification, but
ultimately due to uncertainty limiting its applicability, time constraints, and complexity of
the support, it was excluded. That said, a deeper evaluation of the concepts to support
the further development of the OM’s RL systems design could be important further work.

Conclusion
This thesis aims to develop a support tool that will aid in accelerating the green transition
of the built sector.
The research have been carried out using the Design Research Methodology as a type
5 research project. The project consisted of four phases: Research clarification in which
the overall problem scope have been defined and initial key factors have been formulated,
Descriptive study 1, where the problem area was unfolded and the key factors have been
expanded, before being translated into requirements and criteria for the tool, a prescriptive
study in which a support tool was developed based on the requirements and the developed
support tool have been evaluated based on the criteria, and lastly a Descriptive Study 2
phase where the developed support tool has been tested based on the requirements and
criteria, see Figure 8.1.
3 overall research questions were defined and answered.
• Research question 1: What types of RL/take-back systems exist and what makes it
difficult to implement in the built sector?
By carrying out a SLR of 414 articles found in the database Scopus, along with
an empirical study consisting of 9 carefully selected companies and organisations
within the built environment, it was determined that typologies for RL design was
less impactful than hypothesised. Focusing the support development towards orig-
inal manufacturers was found to be a key factor. Along with this, the design of
couplings defines much difficulty in establishing and designing RL systems. Here,
three main couplings were found to be important to design for, and couplings need
to be explored from both a technical and informational perspective. The clarification
and support of capabilities was also found to be a key factor. Here, aiding the design
process, managing design uncertainty and providing an overview of current and fu-
ture capabilities with 3 capability dimensions were found to decrease the difficulty
of RL system implementation in the built sector.
• Research question 2: How might a support tool be developed to aid organisations
in implementing RL/takeback systems in the built sector?
By generating ideas and conceptualising on the basis of the design specification.
A workshop with the company, Milva, have been carried out to prioritise and define
functions for the support tool and morphology have been used for combining ideas.
The method of the Weighted Objectives Matrix have been used to choose the right
concept idea, based on the criteria from the design specification. The developed
support tool is material that constitutes a workshop of a duration of 2x2.5 hours. The
material consists of 7 steps. Step 1 is performed before the workshop and consists
of a mapping of the company’s current production, a definition of which product the
take-back system should be designed for, and some preparing questions related to
design, sale, construction and operation. Step 2 consists of a definition of 3 scenar-
ios for quantity and quality of the chosen product, that will be the starting point form
which the take-back system will be designed. In step 3 a value chain is designed on
the basis of each of the defined scenarios from step 2. In step 4 underlying assump-
tions is challenged in a assumption game. In step 5 the network of the take-back
Accelerating the green transition of the built sector
101
==PAGE:112==
system is formed for each concept by designing the couplings from the manufactur-
ers to the demolition world, to the construction world and the coupling that targets
awareness. In step 6 the activities of the designed RL systems are analysed for
integration opportunities with the current production. In step 7 the developed RL
system concepts are evaluated and compared by identifying pros and cons, and all
materials is gathered at an output poster to provide overview and comparability of
the designed RL concepts.
• Research question 3: How can the developed tool be assessed and the viability,
feasibility and desirability increased?
By carrying out 3 test sessions with 3 different test subjects, viability, feasibility and
desirability of the support tool was assessed. Due to limitations of the project and
time frame, the test plan was formulated in two versions; an ideal test plan and
an actual test plan. As result, a list of suggestions for improvement have been
formulated and should be further processed in further work.
A discussion of the analysis, the solution and the test have been provided with the purpose
of reflecting upon the process and results of the project.
By developing the support tool and aiding OM’s in designing take-back systems, Milva
can support these companies in designing the systems of taking back building products
and thereby further developing their re-circulation plan as a part of their mapping tool
services. Furthermore, the project aims at accelerating the green transition of the built
sector by aiding the movement from the end of the stabilisation phase and towards the
next transition phase of disruption, as described by Geels et al., 2023. By accelerating
this transition, the tool aims at supporting the overall circularity in the built sector, and
hopefully contribute to a reduction of the overall emissions and environmental impacts
that the built sector currently accounts for.

Further work
The further work can be divided into the following two parts:
• Further iterations of tool
• Expansion of support
8.1.1
Further iterations of tool
The tool needs to be further tested, and several full alpha tests of the current tool needs to
be made following the test plan indicated in appendices DS2.1-2.3. The initial indications
are that the requirements and criteria in the value chain design may need several itera-
tions. As described in Zimmermann and de Weck, 2023, the degree of design freedom
could be tuned up and down. Furthermore, the IF questions may need more iterations as
well. The findings from the tests should preferably also be verified in other tests. Main
findings including combining the value chain and coupling design into a single overview,
and if data provided by Milva could inform the scenario creation might be worth to explore
further. By combining the overviews together, there may be further findings the compa-
nies could extract, and decreasing the level of uncertainty would greatly benefit the design
support to match the real context.
An interesting finding during testing was a potential integration into the realm of software.
By integrating into e.g. Milvas software it would be possible to facilitate more companies
towards RL systems. Different versions of the tool might thus be explored, where a full

version could be facilitated by in-person facilitators, and a ”light” version online. A gen-
eralization of the tool and methods would in this regard also be interesting to explore, as
this might allow designers internally in the companies to apply them themselves.
Lastly, expanding the tools findings to an international context would also broaden the
potential companies to use the tool, and the applicability of the tool for the companies
(as most companies also sell products internationally). To test this, the findings of the
analysis could be tested with European companies/knowledge institutions to begin with,
and further markets after. More focus on regulations may also be necessary to do this.
8.1.2
Expansion of support
As the design choose to narrow in the scope, further support may be necessary to facil-
itate the transition successfully. This would include evaluation support, vision definition
support, pilot-project implementation and RL system operation support.
The evaluation should at the very least include cost of interventions, uncertainty analy-
sis and sustainability evaluation (including rebound effects). Evaluation based on these
parameters should preferably be done in accordance with a vision definition. By defin-
ing or matching the design with KPI’s, the design would be more easily communicated
at a strategic level as well. This strategic level translation is a large lack of the tool, but
including evaluation parameters matched with well defined KPI’s would greatly improve
this.
Expanding the support tool to go beyond initial concept development would also benefit
the companies using the tool. Translation to pilot projects would be a potentially impactful
avenue, as the support for developing pilot projects and increasing actionable learning
from them is rather lacking. In the SLR, no support for pilot projects was found - at most
it was mentioned as an afterthought. The pilot project translation was also highlighted
by Milva and Troldtekt, stating that being able to find pilot projects was a barrier as well.
Thus, further research into decreasing the entry barrier and increasing actionable insights
would be greatly beneficial for RL design.
Research going into multi-system interactions would also benefit the RL system creation
and maintenance. As discussed, RL systems will not exist on its own, but as a system of
systems that interacts with other similar systems. Supporting the collaboration between
the system stakeholders to define standards of system interfaces (e.g. similar acquisition
tools) would be a potential future endeavour.
