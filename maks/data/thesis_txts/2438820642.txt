Abstract
A water cooled grate element from B & W Vølund, is subjected to a series of experi-
ments and simulations, to better understand the failures that is observed on the elements
that have been in service in incinerator plants.
The experiments have been conducted with the following focus areas
Solid Mechanics
How the element reacts to mechanical loads.
Fluid Dynamics
How the water in the cooling channel inside the element behaves.
Heat Transfer
How the heat is transported from the hot surface to the cooling water, and how it aects
the stresses.
Every experiment is also simulated in a FEM-model, and the simulations have been vali-
dated by the experiments. In the end, a simulation is created, coupling all three areas. And
this simulation has proved that the elements are subjected to high thermal stresses, that
are likely the source of many of the observed failures. It is advised to lower the heatload
on the elements, if possible, and increase the ow velocity of the cooling water, that should
have a maximum inlet temperature of 71 ◦C.
Some of the observed failures, originate from external metal welding itself to the surface
of the elements and causing small air contraptions, that initiate fractures driven by loads
from the dierence in the thermal expansion coecients. It has not been possible to recreate
these condition in experiments in this project, but possible solutions to the problem are
discussed.

Introduction
In the solutions for incinerator plants, delivered by Babcock & Wilcox Vølund, Dyna-
grate is a vital part. In general terms it is the oor inside the combustion chamber, that
can wiggle like a caterpillar, and push the trash forward through the combustion.
Dynagrate is made by rows of grate bars, consisting of series of grate elements. And
each row rotate slowly 60◦, to make the motion that moves the trash. The speed of the
rotation is adjusted so the trash is fully burned when it reaches the end of the combustion
chamber.
In the big incinerator plants, the grate elements are cooled with a water channel in-
ternally, to lower the temperature. But these water cooled elements have problems with
cracks and surface failures, that make the elements leak cooling water. Small cracks are
not a problem, as the ow from the water pump is just increased, to ensure sucient water
ow in the last element in the row. However, at some point the cracks grow so big, that the
pump can not maintain the pressure, and the entire incinerator plant has to be closed down
for maintenance. If this can not be timed with the scheduled time slots for maintenance,
every 6 or 12 months, it is very costly.
Naturally it is of high importance for Vølund to avoid these failures, and this project
is the third in a row of projects investigating these grate elements.

Analysis of Earlier Works
The projects made prior to this project, are rst of all read carefully and analysed, so
this project in the best way possible can be an extension of the results obtained in the
prior reports. The rst project was conducted on bachelor level by the two students Emil
Hjorth Espensen and Anders Elsøe in the spring 2017[EE17], and the second project as a

Observations from Emil & Anders' Project
This project looked at used grate element with failures, and made a test rig with com-
pressed air in the cooling channel and soap on the surface, to localise the cracks in the
surface of the grate.
In the casting simulation software MAGMA, the casting process of the grates were simu-
lated, and showed up to 90 % porosities in the edge of the grate, and between 1 % and 4
% micro porosity in an area on the surface of the grate. See gure 2. All of the areas with
porosities corresponds to the areas with cracks, from the experiment with compressed air
and soap.
They also found that the inlet in the casting, does not function as intended and they pre-
dict that gas bubbles might be trapped inside the nished grate.

porosity of mainly the edge

that at multiple locations in the cooling channel inside the grate, the ow was not optimal,
and at some locations there were almost no ow in the water. This shows that there might
be areas of the grate with poor cooling. This was conrmed by an analysis made in the
nite element software Abaqus. See gure 3 and 4.

Observations from Jon Ottens Project
In this project the grate with fractures and cracks, was cut into smaller pieces to have a
closer look at them. And in the progress of cutting the grate, a trapped gas bubble showed
up, just as it was predicted bin the project by Emil and Anders. The bubble was circular
with a diameter of 2.5 mm.
On the surface of the grate, there are spots of residue that has welded itself to the grate.
In a microscope it is possible to see, that in this residue there iare small bubbles of trapped
air, and that from these bubbles, cracks have been initiated, and then grown down into
the grate. See gure 5
The residues were further investigated by Scanning Electron Microscopy, which showed
that 92.2 Wt% of the investigated residue was iron, suggesting that the temperature of the
material that settled on the surface of the grate was at least 1538 ◦C.
As the residues on the surface of the grate has a thermal expansion coecient, that diers
from the one of the grate, there will be a dierence in the strain of the surface when the
grate change temperature, causing tension.
Furthermore, the project notice that with the alloy chosen for the grate, chromium carbides
might form at the operational temperature, which can result in intergranular corrosion.
However, it was not possible to investigate if any chromium carbides were precipitated in
the grate.
One of the cracks on the edge of the tip of the grate, was investigated in regard to the
changing temperatures from ame burst that occur at the tip of crates when they rotate.
Many material constants were not known, but estimated from similar steels. It is found
that the cracks are likely to reach a critical length during the 6 to 12 months of operation,
and if not, the grate is likely to fail during the cool down prior to maintenance.

Ideas for this Project
Based on these two projects, the following subjects are found interesting to investigate
further.
 Simulate if the water ow in the elements have been improved, with the new design
introduced after the project from Emil & Anders
 Get more precise data on the temperature of the grate during operation
 Verify the material parameters at the operation temperature
 Experimentally determine the heat transfer coecients
 Test if the water vaporize during operation
 Test if there could be ways to prevent molten metal to weld itself to the surface
Regarding the last item, with molten metal welded to the surface, and the general corrosive
wear that is inevitable with the various materials in the trash, table 9.2 in [LP86] states
possible actions against this. Of course the rst suggested action is to remove the corrosives
or lower the temperature, but as this is not possible in a incinerator plant, the next action
is to change the surface material. And for the grate element with the high temperatures,
the best choice for the surface is ceramics. This could both be a ceramic layer on top of
the element, or to make the entire element out of ceramics.
One of the best suitable ceramics for this use, is carbon-carbon composite coated with
e.g. ZrB2-SiC-ZrC, that can withstand 2000 K [YZ18].
A fully ceramic element like this, would have the advantage that it would not require
internal cooling, lowering the processing cost greatly. On the downside the material price
for coated carbon-carbon composite is very high.
A cheaper solution could be to use ordinary high temperature steel coated with ceramics as
Nitrided, that is suited for elevated temperatures [LP86]. Or the two ceramics NiCr20AlTi
and CoCr29W that according to [Fis91] have a very low wear at high temperatures.
However, it has not been possible to obtain any ceramics in this project. Instead focus
will be on simulating the cooling ow and its heat transfer, and experimentally investigate
the grate element under conditions as close to a combustion chamber as possible.
Project Statement
Based on the above mentioned ideas, this project will consist of experiments and nite ele-
ment calculations, to determine the mechanical and thermal stresses in the grate elements,
under conditions close to those in the incinerator plant.
The mechanical load on the grate element comes from the hydraulics, driving the rota-
tion of the elements, in the case where something blocks the rotation.
As water is running in channels inside the elements, to cool them down, thermal stresses
arise due to the resulting temperature gradients.

Sensors
In the rst experiment, the strain and deection is measured during a load case. The
deection is measured with a dial indicator, mounted with a magnet. To avoid that the
deection of the frame is added to the measured deection, the dial indicator is attached
to the lower part of the shaft that is assumed to stay parallel to the bottom of the grate
element during the entire experiment.
To measure the strain, two strain gauges are glued to the surface of the grate. One to
do the actual measurements, and one as a reference, to correct for the thermal expansion.
In regard to the placement of the strain gauges, there are multiple factors to take into
account:
 The surface of the element should be very smooth and polished, which is not easy on
hardened cast iron. So the selected areas should be accessible by heavy tools.
 The positions should not interfere with either the shaft or the straps used with the
crane to handle the element.
 As it is hoped the strain gauges can be used for the later experiments where the
element is heated, the gauges should be placed where the temperatures are not to
high, to avoid that they are burned.

Loading
There are two options when loading the grate element; either pushing it down in one end,
or pulling it up in the other. It is deemed most secure, to pull it up, by adding weight in
the bottom of the frame, and then lifting the nose of the element with a crane. If there
should then be a failure somewhere, the weight will only fall a few mm.
The element and frame will rest on the opposite corner of the load, so to calculate the load
at the crane, the precise center of gravity of the weight should be known. This is hard to
determine, as the weight is various pieces of metal.
So instead a scale is added to the crane, to measure the load while the experiment is
conducted.

in the frame.

Linear regression is made on all of the results, to nd the average displacement as a function

The measured strain is showed on gure 11, plotted in the same manner as the dis-

FEM Simulation
The grate element is now modelled in a Finite Element software, to make simulations of
how the grate element behave when loaded with the moment from the shafts the elements
are mounted on.
In this project the commercial software COMSOL Multiphysics with the structural me-
chanics add-on has been used. Even as there are several other FEA software solutions
that are specialised in structural mechanics, COMSOL is chosen, as the structural analysis
needed in this project is relatively simple and easily covered by COMSOLs features. And
then the big advantage of COMSOL is, that it easily makes it possible to couple multiple
physics, which is highly relevant in this project.

and internal work, be related by the virtual work principle (VWP). In a simplied form

Renement of the CAD-model
The CAD-model of the grate element received from Vølund, is very detailed, with thousands
of faces smaller than 1 mm2.
And many of these details are not relevant for neither
structural nor uid dynamic calculations, and they give problems with bad elements in the
mesh. Hence, quite a lot of eort is used to rene the geometry, by joining edges laying
closer than 1 mm, deleting tiny faces, and remodelling faces to avoid very sharp corners.
An example of this is shown on gure 13.
The CAD model shows the grate element as it looks after the casting, before any
machining. So to make the correct boundary conditions, the CAD-model is processed in
order to get the same holes and planes as the nished grate element.
Mesh size

But

Discussion
Even though there is a good match on the strain in the experiment and simulation, the other
results obtained with the simulation, are still not fully trustworthy. Further experiments
should be conducted, with both higher load, and dierent load cases, in order to completely
verify that the simulation match the reality.
Should the experiment in this project be repeated, it is advised to have a longer steel
tube, so that the strap does not squeeze against the element, and all force is transferred
from the steel tube, that preferably should be modelled in the simulation together with
the element, in order to get the eect of the bending tube on the loading. This is not done
in this simulation, as it would not bring any value, when there is still an unknown force
from the strap squeezing the sides.
If the same frame is used, there is no room for a longer tube. Instead two rigid arms could
be welded to the tube, that do not interfere with the element.
A better solution for the dial indicator, would also be preferable for future experiments,
as it is better to evaluate the simulations on more than one parameter. Perhaps the shaft
could be processed in a way that makes it possible for the dial indicator to be attached
directly to the same plane that the element rest on.
Some better data on the material properties, would also be preferable for future exper-
iments. The estimated values for E and ν, seems to be good, but it might just be a lucky
case where an error in the experiment cancel out an error in the material properties.
The resulting Von Mises stresses found in the cases with the maximum load, are not
high enough to initiate plastic ow, or other immediate failures. But they do however
add up to the total stress in the element together with the thermal stresses and expansion
from the water pressure. All together (and separately) these stresses cause a higher risk
of fatigue. To maximize the lifetime of the grate element, the total stress is one of the
important factors to minimize. One way to do this is by increasing the thickness of the
internal walls, creating a bigger volume to absorb the forces. This does of course aect the
water cooling, which could the lead to higher thermal stresses.
Mechanical engineering is a ne balance between many pitfalls. This is why FEA is an
important tool in the design process, as long as it is used correctly.

When laminar ow is modelled, these two equations are the backbone of the solver. But
when solving turbulent ows, some additions to the equations are needed. Equation 15 is
time-averaged and the ow variables are seperated into a mean and uctuating component,
thus becoming the Reynolds-Averaged Navier Stokes (RANS) equation, and then dierent
turbulence models are added to model the turbulence eects. Some of them are described
here4.
k −ε turbulence model
is a very common model that consist of two transport partial
dierential equations (PDE). The rst solves for the turbulent kinetic energy (k), and the
other the rate of dissipation of turbulent kinetic energy (ε). This model has got a relatively
good conversion rate, and low memory requirement.
But as it uses a wall function in the buer regions between the walls and the free stream,
it does not model strong curvature very well, and is best used for external ows.
k −ω turbulence model
is very similar to k −ε, but solves for the specic rate of
dissipation of kinetic energy (ω), instead of ε. This makes the model good to model strong
curvatures and separated ows, and is mainly used for internal ows
But the model is more nonlinear, and thereby more dicult to converge and more
dependent on good initial conditions.
SST turbulence model
is a combination of k −ε and k −ω, that uses k −ε in the free
stream, and k −ω near the walls. This makes the models one of the best to simulate what
is seen in experiments, and is more stable than the two previous models. However, it is
also dicult to get to converge, and demands quite a lot of memory.
This is the main turbulence model used in this project, as it describes the ow near
the walls well, and this is important in this project where the cooling abilities of the water
should be simulated. This is conrmed after a discussion with Erik Gotfredsen that is
studying a Ph.D. in uid dynamics
When the model is prepared in COMSOL, a 'no slip' condition is used, as the high friction
of cast steel, will prevent the ow from sliding along the walls.

The solver is made in two steps, with a wall function as step one that calculates the dis-

To simulate the strain measured in the experiment, a third step is added with structural

chapter, except the load, that in this case will come from the pressure of the ow. The
coupling between the ow and the structure, is handled by COMSOL's 'Fluid-Structure
interaction' tool.
Mesh Size
When working with CFD, the mesh size and quality of the elements are very important.
Even if the mesh consist of millions of elements, a single element in poor quality can cause
the solver to never converge.
To ensure the best conditions for the meshing tool, the
geometry is rened even more, so that there is a minimum of lines and points to constrain
the mesh. The only lines left in the geometry are those between plane and curving planes,
and a few to avoid too large curved areas.
As there are almost no regions in the geometry where turbulence or a quick variation of
velocity, is not expected, everything is meshed with tetrahedra elements, that is the best
element for a situation like this. An alternative as the brick element could decrease the
number of elements a lot, but it would require signicant user interaction. And then it
would probably get in trouble when the ow is varying quickly, and might keep the solver
from converging.
Load Ramping
When the computation is initiated, the solver starts with the initial conditions, that as
a standard are zero pressure and velocity in the geometry. But the boundary conditions
state that the mass ow at the inlet is 1.177 kg
s , while the initial conditions state that the
ow right next to inlet is 0. This is quite a big step for the solver to overcome. If the
problem investigated is in a simple geometry with relatively low velocities, the solver is
able to converge anyway and nd a correct result. But as the grate element is a complex
geometry with multiple contractions and a separation, the solver never converges.
There are multiple ways around this, and the most accessible is to use load ramping.
The problem is rst solved with 10% of the inlet mass ow, as 10% is a lot closer to the
initial guess, the solver can easier converge. When it does, the inlet mass ow is increased
to 30%, and the problem is solved once again, but with the solution obtained with 10%
used as the initial condition. This is then repeated until 100% of the mass ow is reached,
and a nal solution is obtained.

number, making the ow less turbulent. This is done by multiplying the viscosity with a

Discussion
It is hard to evaluate if the preliminary results from the simulations correspond to the
results from the experiments. Even as there are made precise measurements on the strain
from the water ow and pressure, they are inuenced by the piping to the element, and if
there is just a small temperature dierence between the water and the element, thermal
strain occurs, and disturbs the measurements. A third uncertainty in the experiment, is
the mass ow though the element. Now the mass ow is based on the settings on the
pump, but as it does not measure the mass ow directly, but estimate it from the speed
of the engine and the pressure, the actual ow might dier from the one displayed on the
pump. And lastly it has not been possible to measure the pressure at a location that can
be included in the simulation.
In order to better evaluate if the simulations t the experiments, some better pressure
sensors should be mounted inside the element, as the current manometers are inaccurate,
dicult to read and placed outside the element. And then a precise ow sensor would be
a big advantage, to ensure that the ow actually is at the desired level. The uncertainty
of the strain measurements, could be improved, by placing the strain gauges at a location
that is not inuenced by the external piping, and then ensuring that the water and the
element is the same temperature.
The CFD simulation itself could probably be greatly improved as well. One way could

Discussion
The two experiments did not give any results, that make it is possible to conclude anything
from. However, it might be possible to make them again, with a larger pile of Magnesium,
preferably as a powder, to give a higher temperature on the surface, and damages large
enough to measure and compare.
Also the experiments with molten metal, could give some good results, if just molten
steel at a temperature around 1600 ◦C. This is the temperature that in the project [Ott17],
was predicted as the temperature of the residue on the surface of used grate elements, in
the moment they welded themselves to the surface.
If the Therma 253 MA steel should still be evaluated and compared to the present
steel in the elements, some simulations can be done. But as the plate steel is not good for
casting, it would not make sense to update the current model to this steel. Instead some
new designs are proposed.
Ideas for a design without casting
As the design with a internal water cooling in a cast, gives a lot of challenges with thermal
stress and casting problems. Some simulations are made to see if it is possible to make a
new design of the element, that is either forged or just cut out of plate, and made of the
Therma 253 MA, with just a simple linear water cooling channel. The dierent ideas is
showed her, together with the resulting thermal stress and max temperature. The designs
are tested with the same nominal heatload and water mass ow, as in the current grate
elements.
The design in table 4 is the easiest to produce, but got some very high thermal stresses
and steam in cooling ribs, that would make the element fail. Hence, this design is not
good.
The design in table 5, show some quite good results in the simulations, as the max-

a good margin below the maximum temperature of 1150 ◦C, that the steel mill states as

It would not be necessary to cast it, but it is however still challenging to produce it.

Conclusion
Both the mechanical and thermal stresses have been found from simulations and veried
with results from experiments. From this it has been discovered that water temperatures
above 85 ◦C at atmospheric pressure, should be avoided at the inlet to the elements, as
the cooling abilities of the water decline at temperatures above this, due to steam forming
inside the channel.
Today the temperature of the cooling water is up to 125 ◦C, which is not necessarily
a problem, as most of the elements have pressurized cooling channels, which increase the
boiling point of the water. But as the pressure declines down to 0 barg, at the last elements,
that also receive the hottest water, there will very likely be steam forming inside the last
elements. Consequently the last elements in the series will experience poor cooling.
As the water temperature only has a small impact on the thermal stresses, it is advised
to lower the temperature of the cooling water at the rst element in a row, to ensure
that the temperature at the last element is maximally 85 ◦C. Is this not possible, the
outlet pressure could be raised, to ensure that the temperature of the water in none of the
elements will get above the boiling point of the water.
The simulations show that changing the pressure or temperature of the water, either
has none or very little impact on the resulting thermal stresses. However, it is possible to
lower the stresses by increasing the ow velocity.
It has also been established, that the thermal stresses are much higher than the me-
chanical stress. Hence, focus should be on lowering the thermal gradients in the grate
elements, in the future progress of avoiding the failures in the elements.
Vølund has stated that the elements will be exposed to a nominal heatload of 21 kW
m2 ,
during operation. But they are designed to withstand a heatload of up to 100 kW
m2 . Based
on the results of this project, it is not deemed to be plausible that the elements would
survive 100 kW
m2 , for more than a short period of time.
During operation with a nominal heatload of 21 kW
m2 , the thermal stresses, in combination
with potential mechanical loading, are not high enough to result in any immediate failures.
But as the temperature, and thus the heatload, is not constant in the incinerator plant, the
element is vulnerable to failures from fatigue. In addition, the smaller mechanical loads
are added, and as they oscillate due to the rotating rows, they are per denition fatigue
inducing.
In experiments performed by G.P. Tilly in 1971, it is concluded that thermal cycles
result in larger cracks than similar purely mechanical cycles. Thus it is important to min-
imize the magnitude of the thermal stresses, as it is hard to change the amplitude of the
thermal cycles in a furnace burning trash with varying heating value.
Lastly a proposal for a new design of the elements has been presented. It removes the
need for a cast process, and the problems induced by it. Of course new problems will be
introduced, but it is something to consider.
