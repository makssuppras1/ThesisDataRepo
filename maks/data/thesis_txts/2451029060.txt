Preface
This M. Sc. thesis is the product of a ﬁve-month study within the ﬁeld of indoor climate of exist-
ing buildings. The purpose of the thesis is the analysis of the improvement of the indoor envi-
ronmental quality of an existing oﬃce building based on green building certiﬁcation schemes
and consequently its energy optimization.
The current thesis was conducted between 28-01-2019 and 28-06-2019, a period which cor-
responds to 30 ECTS points. It was carried out under the supervision of Associate Professor
Pawel Wargocki.
The project is submitted in partial fulﬁllment of the requirements regarding the M.Sc. degree in
Sustainable Energy/Energy Savings at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and consists
of ﬁve chapters and brief appendices.
Lyngby, June 28, 2019

Abstract
Abstract
The current thesis describes a methodology towards the improvement of the Indoor Environ-
mental Quality (IEQ) of an existing oﬃce building and demonstrates the energy compromise
for that. The motivation behind this project derives from the fact that the existing renovation
strategies focus more on the building’s energy use, rather than on its indoor climate, often re-
sulting in the deterioration of the latter.
An existing oﬃce building in Copenhagen was used as the reference building. In order to pro-
pose the actions needed to improve the IEQ, two green building certiﬁcation schemes were
used, WELL Building Standard and LEED, and only the parameters aﬀecting IEQ were reviewed
and modelled, to the extent possible. Diﬀerent scenarios were created within each scheme,
representing two certiﬁcation levels (Certiﬁed and Premium) followed by sub-scenarios, cre-
ated in order to investigate either cases with diﬀerent number of credits or the eﬀect of various
actions on the building’s performance, both energy and IEQ. The key performance indicators
used to evaluate the results included thermal comfort, indoor air quality and ﬁnally, energy
use.
The scenario with the highest IEQ, which also resulted in the highest energy use, formed the
optimal scenario and was used as the basis for the energy optimization. This included the
implementation of typical renovation actions, such as adding an extra insulation layer in the
building envelope, upgrading the glazing units and improving the window opening and the ven-
tilation control. The objective was not only to improve the building’s energy performance, but
to maintain the high IEQ level as well.
The results indicated that, ultimately, no energy compromise was needed in order to deliver a
building with a superior IEQ. The thermal comfort indices of the optimal scenario fulﬁlled all
requirements, while the CO2 concentration did not exceed the value of 600 ppm. Finally, the
energy optimization procedure mitigated the total delivered energy from 62.6 kWh/m2/year to
42.1 kWh/m2/year, resulting in a decrease of almost 34%.
Apart from the great potential there is to improve a building’s IEQ and maintain a low energy
performance, this project demonstrated the huge amount of limitations building designers face
when modelling parameters that aﬀect IEQ. This means that potential eﬀects on both IEQ and
energy use cannot be predicted and thus, designers become hesitant regarding their imple-
mentation. This sets an obstruction in the process of developing new renovation strategies
that have IEQ as a primary objective and subsequently, address the issue of the building’s en-
ergy performance.

Resumé
Resumé
Denne afhandling beskriver en metode til forbedring af IEQ (Indoor Enviromental Quality) som
er en parameter for indeklimakvaliteten, af eksisterende kontorbygninger og demonstrerer en-
ergikompromiset for det. Motivationen bag projektet stammer fra, at de eksisterende renover-
ingsstrategier fokuserer mere på bygningens energiforbrug end på indeklimaet, hvilket ofte
medfører forringelse af sidstnævnte.
En eksisterende kontorbygning i København blev brugt som referencebygning. For at foreslå de
foranstaltninger, der var nødvendige for at forbedre IEQ, blev der anvendt to grønne byggecer-
tiﬁceringsordninger, WELL Building Standard og LEED, og kun de parametre, der påvirker IEQ,
blev gennemgået og modelleret i videst muligt omfang. Der blev oprettet forskellige scenar-
ier inden for hver ordning, der repræsenterer to certiﬁceringsniveauer (Certiﬁed and Premium)
efterfulgt af subscenarier, der blev oprettet for at undersøge enten sager med forskellige antal
point eller virkningen af forskellige handlinger på bygningens præstationer, både energi og IEQ.
Nøgleindikatorerne til evaluering af resultaterne omfattede termisk komfort, indendørs luftk-
valitet og endelig energiforbrug.
Scenariet med den højeste IEQ, som også resulterede i det højeste energiforbrug, dannede det
optimale scenarie og blev anvendt som grundlag for energioptimering. Dette omfattede imple-
menteringen af typiske renoveringshandlinger, som f.eks. tilføjelse af et ekstra isoleringslag
i klimaskærm, opgradering af vinduer og forbedring af vinduesåbning og ventilationskontrol.
Målet var ikke alene at forbedre bygningens energimæssige ydeevne, men også at opretholde
det høje IEQ-niveau.
Resultaterne viste, at der i sidste ende ikke var noget energikompromis for at kunne levere
en bygning med en overlegen IEQ. De termiske komfortindikatorer for det optimale scenarie
opfyldte alle krav, mens CO2-koncentrationen ikke oversteg værdien på 600 ppm. Endelig re-
ducerede energieﬀektiviseringsproceduren den samlede leverede energi fra 62.6 kWh/m2/år
til 42.1 kWh/m2/år, hvilket resulterede i et fald på næsten 34%.
Foruden det store potentiale er der at forbedre en bygningens IEQ og opretholde en lav energi
ydeevne, viste dette projekt de mange begrænsninger, som bygningsdesignere står over for,
når de modellerer parametre, der påvirker IEQ. Det betyder, at potentielle virkninger på både
IEQ og energiforbrug ikke kan forudsiges, og derfor bliver designere tøvende med hensyn til
deres implementering. Dette er en hindring i processen med at udvikle nye renoveringsstrate-
gier, der har IEQ som et primært mål og derefter løse problemet med bygningens energimæs-
sige ydeevne.

Introduction
1.1
Sustainability and buildings
A community’s growth and development has a large impact on the natural environment. As
urbanization increases on a high pace, so does the demand for resources such as energy, ma-
terials, fuels and water. Thus, it is not surprising that over the past decades, the concept of
sustainability has become of primary importance. Sustainable development is deﬁned as the
development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future
generations to meet their own needs, and it results from the balance between three pillars:
social, environmental and economic [1]. Meanwhile, global warming as a result of greenhouse
gas emissions has forced the European Union to impose policies and regulations on climate
and energy. In this way, energy eﬃciency has been put in the center of attention, setting one
of the most cost-eﬀective ways to enhance the security of the energy supply and decrease
the emissions of greenhouse gases and other pollutants [2]. In this direction, European Com-
mission has issued several Directives, such as the Energy Eﬃciency (EED) and the Energy Per-
formance of Buildings (EPBD), which promote the importance of setting a clear vision for the
decarbonisation of the building stock by 2050, as well as integrate national long-term building
renovation strategies.
The concepts of sustainability and energy eﬃciency have focused on the building sector due
to the fact that it is responsible for 40% of the total ﬁnal energy consumption and over 30%
of the CO2 emissions within EU [3], while at the same time the great majority of buildings was
constructed during periods with no, or minimal, energy-related building codes. Taking into
account that 75% of the existing building stock is expected to still be in use by 2050 due to
their long lifetime, it represents the greatest potential for energy savings. At the same time,
the manufacturing, design, construction and operation of buildings are all responsible for the
consumption of a huge amount of natural resources. Speciﬁcally, the building sector alone,
globally consumes 25% of water and 40% of resources [4], and thus it sets a great opportu-
nity to implement sustainability practices.
The aforementioned parameters reﬂect the importance of creating green and sustainable con-
structions and setting measures to reduce their impact on the environment. According to U.S.
Environmental Protection Agency (U.S. EPA) [5], green buildings are designed to reduce the
overall impact of the built environment on human health and natural environment. This reduc-
tion can be achieved by eﬃciently using energy, water and other resources, protecting occu-
pant health, improving employee productivity, and ﬁnally by reducing waste, pollution and en-
vironmental degradation. Green buildings are also known as sustainable or high performance
buildings, where intelligent approaches to energy are implemented, water resources are safe-
guarded, waste is minimized and health, well-being and urban environment preservation are
promoted [6]. These properties of green buildings concern their total life-cycle: from their de-
sign to the construction, operation, maintenance, renovation and lastly, their deconstruction.

1.2
Green building certiﬁcation schemes
In the process of implementing the concepts of sustainability and energy eﬃciency in the build-
ing stock, several green building certiﬁcation schemes have been created as rating systems
awarding buildings designed in a more environmentally conscious way. They set voluntary
tools to achieve more sustainable buildings and, contrarily to national building codes, are es-
tablished to propose exceptional rather than minimum requirements [7]. Practically, they aim
at mitigating the impact a building has on the environment through a sustainable design [8].
Nowadays, they acquire even a larger market share, not only because they constitute a valu-
able marketing tool, but also due to the fact that they claim to improve a building’s energy
performance even by 50%. At the same time, they are supposed to improve the indoor envi-
ronmental quality, and thus promote occupant health, comfort and as a consequence, produc-
tivity.
According to U.S. Green Building Council (USGBC) [9], Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) is
aﬀected mainly by air quality, lighting, thermal comfort and acoustics. Research has proved
that IEQ is very closely related to the building occupants’ health and comfort, which has been
correlated with the self-estimated job performance of the workers and may be expected to af-
fect the overall productivity [10]. Moreover, according to World Health Organization (WHO)
[11], poor indoor environmental quality can cause several health eﬀects such as irritation,
respiratory infection and Sick Building Syndrome (SBS), among others. This is the reason why
it is necessary to maintain a high indoor air quality (IAQ) in all spaces. Nevertheless, IEQ is
rather disregarded within the diﬀerent certiﬁcations compared to other concepts. Actually, it
has been demonstrated that among 55 green building certiﬁcation schemes, the average con-
tribution of IAQ is not more than 7.5% [12]. This is because green building rating schemes
dedicate most available credits to the energy conservation, which is actually the purpose for
which they were initially built. According to Altomonte et al (2019) [13], this acts as their weak
point; the fact that they focus more on the quantity and not the quality, often resulting in an
improved energy performance with no beneﬁts for IEQ.
Some of the most common rating schemes are Leadership in Energy and Environmental De-
sign (LEED) (United States, 1998), BRE Environmental Assessment Method (BREEAM) (United
Kingdom, 1990), DGNB (Germany, 2007), WELL (United States, 2014) and Green Mark (Sin-
gapore, 2005). Each one has a diﬀerent structure, and consequently it is diﬃcult to directly
compare them. Nevertheless, their characteristics can be clustered according to the three dif-
ferent concepts of sustainability (economic, environmental and social), as depicted in Figure
1.1 [1]. Energy, water and resources in general, are part of the environmental aspect, while IEQ
is correlated with the occupants’ well-being and thus belongs to the social aspect.

1.3
State of the art
Besides the fact that the Danish government aims at a green energy future, where by 2050,
100% of the energy will be coming from renewables, in the Energy Agreement of 2012 [3],
an overall strategy for the existing buildings’ energy renovation was planned, setting out the
initiatives to be taken to ensure that energy consumption in buildings is reduced. Renova-
tion actions, in general, lead to both an added economic value for the construction [14] and
an overall improved performance. The term performance may refer to either comfort of oc-
cupants or energy. Actions that aim towards the latter, set the so-called energy renovation,
which is the current state of the art. Positive impacts of a buildings’ energy renovation can
be categorized into direct beneﬁts, including higher energy eﬃciency, lower carbon emissions
and less life cycle costs, and complementary beneﬁts, such as higher thermal comfort, lower
operation and maintenance costs, improved building aesthetics, better indoor air quality and
increased building value [15].
The existing renovation strategies focus mainly on upgrading the building envelope, the glaz-
ing units and the mechanical installations, by potentially installing LED lighting or night cooling
[16]. This is also supported by a study conducted by the State Building Research Institute [17],
that has assessed the potential heat savings in existing residential buildings and reported that

the major heat losses in buildings built within the time period 1960–2004 are attributed to the
exterior walls, ventilation system and windows. In order to improve the thermal performance
of the building envelope, actions such as adding an extra layer of insulation externally or in-
ternally, designing a double skin facade or installing a curtain wall [18], all depending on the
construction of the facade, can be pursued. The external re-insulation could be characterized
as the best solution, since in this way the construction would be more eﬃcient, while at the
same time the occupiable ﬂoor area would not be aﬀected. In addition to an improved thermal
performance, additional external thermal insulation improves the thermal indoor environment
and decreases the risk for mould growth in the corners because of the higher surface tem-
perature. Additionally, it protects the building envelope against climate loads and improves
aesthetics [19]. Of course, it is of great importance to investigate whether the windows are
re-positioned according to the new external surface. An important aspect of this strategy is,
also, the presence of thermal bridges. The share of thermal bridges in the total transmission
heat losses depends on the climate and the construction. According to Hannoudi et al (2015)
[19], in warm climates the inﬂuence of thermal bridges is between 5 and 38%, while in cold
climates it is no less than 20% for prefabricated concrete sandwich walls. Placing an extra
insulation layer on the external surface of the walls that will include the intermediate ﬂoors,
mitigates substantially the linear heat losses through the connection, as presented in DS/EN
ISO 14683:2017 [20]. On the contrary, adding an insulation layer internally, results in a rather
small decrease of the linear heat losses of the assembly.
Green building certiﬁcations are not only used for new constructions, but in the renovation
process of existing buildings as well. Implementing energy renovation actions based on the
guidelines of such schemes often leads to the assumption that occupants’ perception, satis-
faction and productivity is improved. Though, this is not always the case [7]. According to
Awada and Srour (2018) [21], actually, occupant satisfaction is often disregarded when inves-
tigating potential renovation actions, due to the fact that the focus is, mostly, put on the direct
costs and beneﬁts of the project, such as the energy savings, rather than on attempts to ex-
press the value of improved user health and productivity in monetary terms.
According to Földváry et al. (2016) [22], energy saving measures in apartment buildings, in-
deed led to a reduced energy use, but at the same time the acceptability of air quality and the
exchange rates were lower, while the CO2 concentration higher. Altomonte et al (2018) [13],
indicated that a superior certiﬁcation level does not necessarily lead to improved occupant
satisfaction. An interesting fact is that, according to research results [23], it is possible for oc-
cupants to be positively biased regarding IEQ satisfaction after moving to a new or renovated
oﬃce. This is attributed to the fact that they are excited about the new workplace and the fact
that it promotes the concept of sustainability. As a result, analysts should be cautious when
interpreting the results of post-occupancy surveys. Altomonte et al. (2017) [24] performed
an occupant satisfaction survey in oﬃce buildings certiﬁed with BREEAM and compared the
results with non-certiﬁed ones. The conclusion of the study was that BREEAM certiﬁcation did
not seem to substantially inﬂuence occupant satisfaction. In fact, occupants of BREEAM cer-
tiﬁed oﬃces tended to be less satisﬁed with air quality than users of non-certiﬁed buildings.
On the other hand, a similar study by Lee et al (2018) [25] not only suggested that occupants
of Green Mark certiﬁed oﬃce buildings express higher satisfaction with IEQ parameters, but
also proved through measurements that these buildings indeed delivered better IEQ compared
to non-certiﬁed ones. Finally, according to Da Silva (2015) [7], measurements in Green Mark
certiﬁed buildings suggested that their performance in terms of IEQ was not entirely improved
compared to this of non-certiﬁed buildings, even though the occupants’ satisfaction and per-

All this knowledge leads to inconsistent conclusions regarding the eﬀect of sustainability rat-
ing schemes on the actual IEQ and occupant satisfaction. As a result, when such certiﬁcations
are implemented with energy savings as objective, the eﬀects on the ﬁnal IEQ of the building
cannot be predicted.
1.4
Objective of the present study
The objective of this master thesis project was to investigate the potential of delivering a build-
ing with a superior indoor environmental quality (IEQ) and a low energy performance at the
same time. To achieve this goal, a diﬀerent renovation strategy was proposed: the sequence
of actions was reversed and the driving force was a superior IEQ level, which, when achieved,
was followed by an energy optimization, which set the secondary objective. In order to do so,
two diﬀerent sustainability rating schemes were implemented and typical energy renovation
actions were applied. In order to meet the aforementioned goals, the following steps were
followed:
- Review of two green building rating schemes
- Modelling of parameters focusing on the improvement of IEQ
- Energy optimization of the modelled case with the best IEQ
- Determination of whether the high IEQ level can be maintained when the energy perfor-
mance is optimized
- Determination of whether it is possible to achieve a ﬁnal energy use similar to the one of
the reference case
In the present study, two schemes of diﬀerent rationale were used: LEED and WELL Building
Standard. The former provides a framework to create healthy, highly eﬃcient and cost-saving
green buildings, focusing on various aspects, such as IEQ, water eﬃciency, energy, etc [26],
while the latter focuses exclusively on the interactions between the built environment and the
various systems of the human body, setting a version of social sustainability [13]. Therefore,
it could be said that LEED focuses more on energy, while WELL is occupant-focused. They,
thus, provide diﬀerent guidelines for achieving a building with high IEQ. A modelling tool was
selected to model their requirements, so that dynamic simulations could be performed. The
main hypothesis of this project was that the more the credits earned within the context of each
certiﬁcation, the better the IEQ, without necessarily leading to a higher energy use. This was
to be accepted or rejected by the project’s results.

2. Methods
The modelling tool was selected to be IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) version 4.8,
developed by EQUA Simulation AB [29]. This was because IDA ICE can simulate both indoor cli-
mate and energy, while it accurately models the building’s systems and controllers. Moreover,

2.1.6
Ventilation system
The reference building was mechanically ventilated. In order to determine the required outdoor
airﬂow rate, both the occupants and the building components were taken into consideration,
as prescribed in DS/EN 15251 [37]. Then, the total ventilation rate was calculated based on
Equation 2.1 .
qtot = n · qp + A · qB
(2.1)
where:
qtot is the total ventilation rate in a room, l/s
n is the design value for the number of people in the room
qp is the ventilation rate for occupancy per person, l/s/person
A is the ﬂoor area, m2
qB is the ventilation rate for the emissions from the building, l/s/m2
The building was initially considered to be a low-polluting one of Category III, as it this is deﬁned
in DS/EN 15251 [37], so the corresponding ventilation rates were used for the calculation. This
was an assumption based on the fact that the building was relatively new, so it would not make
sense to consist of polluting materials, but at the same time a very-low polluting case would
form a best case scenario. As a result, the selected category was on the safe side. Moreover,
since in the building’s EPC no implemented renovation actions were mentioned, it was consid-
ered of Category III in order to model the worst case scenario. The Speciﬁc Fan Factor (SFP) of
each fan was input based on the provided product speciﬁcation, while the rated ﬂow was based
on the total ﬂow for all rooms. This, of course, may have led to a slight underestimation of
the energy useLast but not least, the fans were set to operate only between 7.00 and 18.00 on
Page 12

weekdays, in order to prevent any unnecessary energy use during hours that the building was
not occupied.
At ﬁrst, the ventilation system was set to operate with Constant Air Volume (CAV), with a ﬂow
rate of 0.4 l/s/m2, based on the requirement of BR95 [33] for the minimum required ventila-

levels to most of the zones, the system was changed to one with Variable Air Volume (VAV) with
CO2 control. The minimum and maximum CO2 concentration setpoints were 900 and 1200
ppm, respectively, also corresponding to Category III. The supply temperature was assumed to
be 17 °C, meaning that the ventilation system, besides maintaining a good indoor air quality,
provided an amount of cooling as well.

2.1.9
Heating and cooling devices
In order to determine the heating and cooling loads of each room, ideal heaters and coolers
were used, with the corresponding setpoints to be 20 °C and 27 °C. Ideal heaters and coolers
are ﬁctional units, being able to provide all the power needed to maintain the operative tem-
perature within the desired range. They are used in the ﬁrst phase of a building’s modelling
procedure, in order to determine the heating and cooling loads of the rooms and based on
these, deﬁne the capacity of the actual heating and cooling units. This was done after simula-
tions of the heating and cooling loads were performed. Afterwards, water radiators and ceiling
cooling devices were installed to provide heating and cooling respectively, with PI controllers
according to the operative temperature. The new setpoints were adjusted according to the
minimum and maximum operative temperatures detected in each room. Due to the fact that
in certain rooms, the cooling setpoint of 27 °C led to overheating, it was set to 26 °C, while the
heating setpoint was set to 20 °C.

2.2
Scenario description
Before the diﬀerent modelling scenarios were developed, WELL and LEED rating schemes were
reviewed and the parameters that refer to IEQ were identiﬁed. Both certiﬁcations present a
large number of parameters referring to the IEQ, not all of which could be modelled. This was
due to limitations on the inputs of the selected modelling tool and on the modelling proce-
dure in general. This matter is more thoroughly discussed in Chapter 4. Speciﬁcally, in WELL,
among the 30 available credits within the concepts of Air and Thermal Comfort, only 7 could
be modelled, while in LEED the maximum number of credits was 3 out of the 16 available. All
parameters of both certiﬁcations that could be modelled are presented in the following sub-
sections. Firstly, they are described, then they are grouped based on the diﬀerent scenarios
and ﬁnally, the actions taken in order to implement them in the models are explained.
In general, WELL and LEED certiﬁcations provide diﬀerent rating levels based on the number
of credits: Certiﬁed is achieved when only the prerequisites are implemented, while Platinum
when 80% of maximum number of credits is earned. In the present project, it was attempted
to form two levels of certiﬁcation based on a diﬀerent number of credits. The ﬁrst one was
the basic certiﬁcation level, achieved when the prerequisites were fulﬁlled and is referred to as
”Certiﬁed”. The second one, called Premium, was formed by pursuing credits on the optional
requirements, with the objective to earn as many credits as possible, always referring to IEQ.
As far as LEED is concerned, there are diﬀerent versions for every type of building project.
For the present study, LEED for Building Design and Construction was used [28]. This was be-
cause, apart from new constructions, this version also refers to buildings undergoing major
renovation. This exists when the total cost of renovation related to the building envelope or
the technical building systems is higher than 25% of the value of the building (excluding value
of the land) or when more than 25% of the surface of the building undergoes renovation [42].
Before the diﬀerent scenarios were deﬁned, the credits of each certiﬁcation that could be mod-
elled were reviewed and are presented in Subsections 2.2.1 to 2.2.4.

Since the windows of the long facade were openable and the ventilation was controlled based
on the indoor CO2 concentration, the system should not be operating while the windows were
open. This is because, in this case, there is natural air circulation, suﬃcient to maintain the
CO2 concentration levels within the desired range, so the use of the ventilation is unnecessary.
Consequently, the control of the ventilation system had to be modiﬁed. Moreover, a more ad-
vanced window control strategy was pursued, which would take into consideration not only the
indoor temperature, but the diﬀerence between the indoor and outdoor temperatures, as well.
Both strategies are described in detail below.
The window opening was controlled by three parameters that needed to be fulﬁlled simulta-
neously in order to give an opening signal. The ﬁrst parameter referred to the occupancy
schedule, as, in general, it is not necessary to open the windows when there are no people
present. At the same time, the windows should not be open during winter, as this will cause a
steep decrease in the operative temperature, leading to excessive energy use for heating. As
a result, the schedule contained the oﬃce occupancy during summer. The second parameter
concerned the outdoor air temperature and more speciﬁcally the temperature diﬀerence be-
tween indoors and outdoors. This was named as ”cooling potential”, meaning that when the
outdoor temperature was more than 2 °C lower than the indoor one, then there would be a
lot of potential for free cooling. Then it was reasonable to open the windows, which otherwise
should remain close. Finally, the free cooling should be used until the operative temperature
was 1 °C lower than the cooling setpoint, as after this point it was considered that free cool-
ing was not suﬃcient and the cooling units should be used. Following these rules, the custom
made control was developed, as depicted in Figure 2.9.

4
Discussion
In the context of the current study, the requirements of two green building rating schemes
within the concept of IEQ were implemented to the extend this was feasible by modelling limi-
tations, in an existing oﬃce building. The objective was to improve the building’s IEQ and then
investigate whether it was possible to further decrease the energy use, through typical energy
renovation actions. According to the results presented in Chapter 3, it was shown that, indeed,
the applied actions resulted in superior thermal comfort and CO2 concentration levels, which
were maintained even after the energy renovation that led to a decrease in the building’s en-
ergy performance from 62.6 kWh/m2/year to 42.1 kWh/m2/year. The renovation actions did
not have only a positive impact on the indoor conditions, but also a negative one as the day-
light conditions got worse. The project confronted a number of limitations, which along with
the main outcomes are discussed in this chapter. Moreover, motivation for future research is
reviewed.
A general limitation observed while reviewing both certiﬁcations, was that there were several
mutually exclusive parameters. This means that pursuing one parameter precluded the im-
plementation of another one. This happened in WELL certiﬁcation with credits for Enhanced
Ventilation (A06) and Radiant Thermal Comfort (T05). In this case, when installing radiant
systems, it is mandatory to also have a ventilation system able to maintain relative humidity
within a certain range. In the modelling procedure, this corresponded to a ventilation control
based on relative humidity, which meant that Option 2 of Enhanced Ventilation, regarding a
demand-controlled ventilation system based on the CO2 concentration level could not be pur-
sued, resulting in losing one credit.
4.1
Modelling limitations
First of all, the reference building was associated with several limitations. This was due to the
fact that, besides some of its components, no data were provided regarding the operation of
the building systems, such as the ventilation system and the room units. This meant that the
building was, arbitrarily, designed as one of Category III, adjusting all the setpoints to meet Cat-
egory III requirements. One could argue that the building was designed with a very poor IEQ, in
order to show that this was improved by implementing the rating schemes. Indeed, the building
was intentionally designed like this, so that it formed the worst case scenario. In this way, it
was possible to point out the magnitude of potential there was for IEQ, when implementing the
green building certiﬁcations. Consequently, there was an evolution from the worst-case to the
best-case scenario.
Moreover, the existing building envelope was interlinked to limitations that had as a conse-
quence the fact that certain certiﬁcation credits could not be pursued. For instance, LEED
prerequisite for Minimum Energy Performance could not be fulﬁlled due to the fact that the
mandatory provisions of ANSI/ASHRAE/IENSA Standard 90.1-2010, Appendix G [47] could not
be achieved. The reason for that was that the vertical glazing units are not supposed to consti-
tute more than 40% of the gross wall area, while the corresponding value in the existing build-
==PAGE:71==
4. Discussion
ing was 46%. This is a representative example of the way the existing building construction
may lead to credit loss. Since the implementation of the green building certiﬁcation schemes
in the reference building was the ﬁrst step of the current methodology, followed by the energy
renovation, altering in any way the existing building envelope would lead to a diﬀerent refer-
ence case and consequently, would constitute a diﬀerent research approach. This was why
actions like these were not pursued.
Another constraint was detected in the energy retroﬁt procedure, meaning that the potential
renovation actions were conﬁned. This was due to the fact that their objective was to demon-
strate the potential of decreasing the building’s energy use after the improvement of IEQ, by
following a typical renovation strategy. Extensive and extremely invasive renovation proposals
were not within the scope of the current study, meaning that, for example, it was not suggested
to modify the glazing area or completely replace the existing building insulation. Even though
such actions could further decrease the building’s energy performance, it was decided to con-
sider most of the building components, such as the wall composition, the windows’ position
and size, etc., constant during the renovation process.
Furthermore, in the context of the present study, the IEQ conditions developed only in the reg-
ularly occupied spaces were analyzed. This derived from the fact that the thermal comfort
requirements prescribed by WELL Building Standard referred only to these rooms. It goes with-
out saying, though, that the conditions in the other rooms are equally important and could
have a substantial eﬀect on the ﬁnal energy performance of the building.
The most important limitation of the study lied within the fact that it was not possible to predict
the eﬀect that certain parameters of both certiﬁcations had on the IEQ and consequently, on
the energy use. This was because, as mentioned earlier, the schemes propose actions that
could not be modelled neither generally nor speciﬁcally in IDA ICE. Such cases were, for exam-
ple, requirements for individual thermal control, acoustic performance, environmental tobacco
smoke control and microbe and mold control. Even though these parameters were anticipated
to inﬂuence the IEQ in a positive way, their actual impact remained unknown. This is a general
limitation of the modelling tools and derives form the fact that they are developed from an en-
ergy and not from an indoor climate point of view. This means that, even though they are able
to model indoor climate conditions, their inputs are limited to parameters that directly aﬀect
the building’s energy performance. Therefore, more advance models are needed to carry out
a realistic simulation that will be able to model IEQ in a holistic way and predict the impact
diﬀerent actions have on this.
4.2
Modelling parameters
In light of the results presented in Chapter 3, there are a few key performance indicators that
need to be discussed.
The ﬁrst one is relative humidity. Relative humidity levels are mainly important in the case
where there are radiant heating and cooling systems. This is because high relative humidity
may lead to condensation on the surfaces where radiant systems are installed. This could
be investigated by deﬁning the dew point of the air and comparing that with the ﬂoor surface
temperature. Due to the fact that the the present study focuses on IEQ and no condensation
warnings were presented during IDA ICE simulations, this was not considered necessary. On
the other hand, very low relative humidity levels may cause discomfort and promote the trans-
mission of infectious diseases. This can be prevented by using humidiﬁers or by reducing the
Page 59
==PAGE:72==
4. Discussion
operative temperature. Humidiﬁers are not commonly used in Denmark for two reasons: ﬁrstly,
they set a compromise in terms of energy and secondly, studies have indicated [48] that they
pose a threat to indoor climate through the development of fungi or mold. Meanwhile, reducing
the operative temperature may lead to thermal discomfort and thus, to loss of credits or to an
extensive use of space heating.
Apparently, the relative humidity requirements of WELL Building Standard in the current study
could not be fulﬁlled without the use of humidiﬁers and dehumidiﬁers. The former, apart from
increased in relative humidity, could possibly also lead to increased air temperature and could,
thus result in aﬀecting other credit parameters. Nevertheless, the current modelling sug-
gested that even without the use of such equipment, thermal comfort was achieved without
any condensation warnings. Consequently, with the climate conditions dictated by the Danish
Reference Year, the condensation management requirements of WELL may not be necessary.
Moreover, several input data of the ventilation system, such as the supply temperature, were
based on assumptions, which in general aﬀect the operation of other room units, like the heat-
ing and cooling systems. Normally, the ventilation system should have been designed from
scratch based on whether its objective was to provide cooling or simply to maintain the indoor
air quality at a high level. Then, the supply temperature and the SFP would have been accu-
rately determined based on the rated ﬂow and the required energy use would have been closer
to the realistic one.
4.3
Future work
In this section, several initiatives for further research can be proposed.
Firstly, as mentioned earlier, there are quite restricted opportunities to predict the impact of
several actions on the building’s IEQ, due to the numerous modelling limitations. This means
that the only way to determine this eﬀect is through measurements. This fact may, on the one
hand, make building designers hesitant to use green building certiﬁcation schemes with an IEQ
approach, as they cannot somehow verify their decisions. On the other hand, this can possibly
result in the false assumption that such actions will bring the expected results, which is not ac-
curate. For these reasons, it is considered important to create modelling tools that will be able
to verify the actions taken to improve IEQ and examine their impact on the building’s energy
use.
Secondly, it is recommended to investigate aspects that were not included in the present study,
such as lighting and acoustics. This could be done by using speciﬁc software for daylight and
lighting or by specifying the noise levels in each space. Especially the subject of acoustic con-
ditions in oﬃces has gained a lot of attention, since most work spaces have been transformed
to open plan oﬃces and noise discomfort is among the most common issues for occupants.
Even though software regarding the evaluation of daylight exist, they do not combine such an
analysis with indoor climate conditions and energy, forcing users to integrate software with
diﬀerent modelling objectives in order to obtain a holistic analysis.
A few additional actions could also be taken in order to supplement the existing work. First of
all, it would be beneﬁcial to determine how much human behavior can inﬂuence the results of
such a study. It is widely known that the presence of occupants is modelled through the oc-
cupancy schedules. Nevertheless, human perception can be a decisive factor of the building
performance. This is because occupants may have a thermal perception that does not neces-
sarily comply with the design heating and cooling setpoints, leading to a more extensive use of
Page 60
==PAGE:73==
4. Discussion
the heating and cooling units, or to a diﬀerent window opening pattern. In order to specify how
big this eﬀect can be, new simulations can be performed, where all parameters that occupants
can alter will be investigated and will receive values within a deﬁned range. In this way, there
will be two extreme scenarios, quantifying the inﬂuence occupants may have on the building’s
IEQ and energy use.
Last but not least, what would be a great extension to the study is a life cycle cost analysis
of the construction. In this way, it could be determined whether the proposed energy savings
measures indeed lead to a reduced ﬁnancial cost in the lifetime of the building, compared to
the conventional case. This will take into consideration the cost of renovation, as well as the
expression of the indoor environmental quality improvement in monetary terms.

Conclusions
In this section, the main conclusions in regards to the present study are summarized.
First and foremost, the major conclusion is that, indeed, there is the potential to deliver a build-
ing with both superior IEQ and low energy performance. Green building rating schemes can set
the requirements for the former, but not all of them at the same level. WELL, which has oc-
cupants’ wellness as a driving force, resulted in much better IEQ results than LEED, where the
IEQ parameters that could be modelled were very limited. This points out the importance of
using certiﬁcations with the appropriate objective. Implementing some of the most common
renovation actions led to a substantial decrease in the building’s total delivered energy, even
by 34%, while the IEQ was maintained in a very high level. In fact, the renovated building was
almost within the current Danish energy frame for new buildings. This indicates that improving
a building’s IEQ can result in absolutely no energy compromise, providing building designers
with an alternative to the typical renovation strategy dictating that compromises on IEQ will
have to be made in order to achieve a low energy use. This, of course, is limited by the mod-
elling tools’ conﬁguration, meaning that only few of the IEQ parameters proposed by the rating
schemes can currently be modelled. For the great majority of them, the impact on both IEQ
and energy cannot be predicted. The current approach sets the ﬁrst step towards establishing
a new framework for the renovation of the existing building stock, which will focus, primarily,
on the indoor quality and, subsequently, on the energy eﬃciency.
Secondly, the basic research hypothesis dictating that as the number of credits increase, so
does the IEQ level, is only partially validated. In the case of WELL certiﬁcation, it was shown
that a higher number of credits did not necessarily lead to a better IEQ. For instance, WELL
Premium Scenario A2 with 6 credits had a much lower CO2 concentration level than WELL Pre-
mium Scenario B1, which obtained 7 credits, while the thermal comfort indicators were more
or less the same. In LEED, on the other hand, the scenarios that were modelled satisﬁed this
anticipation, but it should be mentioned that they were distinguished only between two cer-
tiﬁcation levels: certiﬁed and premium. As it was not possible to model cases with diﬀerent
number of credits, it cannot be concluded whether LEED validates the research hypothesis.
Moreover, it was demonstrated that the building’s energy use does not, inevitably, increase as
the earned credits increase. This was again the case in WELL, as WELL Premium Scenario A1
resulted in a lower energy use than WELL Certiﬁed Scenario. This points out the fact that the
energy use is more dependent on the actions themselves, rather than on the credits they of-
fer. For example, providing operable windows may oﬀer only one credit, but has a considerable
eﬀect on the energy use, as it oﬀers free cooling. Again in the case of WELL Premium, a 20%
increase in the credits, lead to a 40% increase in the energy use, but it cannot be said whether
all scenarios follow the same pattern. Meanwhile, LEED Scenarios with the same number of
credits resulted in diﬀerent total delivered energy values. Consequently, it can be concluded
that the points one action can provide do not reﬂect the impact on the IEQ, nor on the energy,
meaning that many credits do not necessarily result in high IEQ and energy use.
Finally, through LEED Scenarios, it was possible to determine the eﬀect of the ventilation con-
trol strategy on the IEQ. The ventilation control dictates how much the ventilation system op-
erates and taking into account that this provides the room with clean air, it can substantially
==PAGE:75==
5. Conclusions
aﬀect the indoor air quality parameters. More speciﬁcally, the temperature control led to CO2
concentration levels slightly higher than those derived from the CO2 control, but still within the
acceptable limits. At the same time, the total delivered energy was lower, as the cooling units’
operation was mitigated. Consequently, a temperature control within this certiﬁcation proved
to be more eﬃcient.
All things considered, the main conclusions can be summarized into:
- In the process of implementing green building certiﬁcation schemes, building designers
are confronted with several modelling limitations, meaning that they are not able to know
the eﬀect of several actions on the IEQ and consequently, on the energy use. This makes
them hesitant in using such rating schemes with IEQ as a primary objective.
- The reversed renovation strategy proposed, which has as a driving force a superior IEQ
followed by an energy optimization proved to have a huge potential.
- Pursuing the requirements of green building certiﬁcation schemes within the concept of
IEQ can result in great improvement of the IEQ levels of an existing building.
- Implementing energy renovation actions in parallel can lead to absolute no energy com-
promise, while maintaining a superior IEQ level. In fact, the building could also be within
the current Danish energy frame for new buildings.
- The research hypothesis is partially validated, as pursuing more credits does not neces-
sarily lead to a better IEQ. This is more dependant on the actions themselves, rather than
on the credits they oﬀer.
- Pursuing more credits does not necessarily lead to a higher energy performance. This is
because actions with very few credits may have a substantial impact on the energy use.
- The ventilation control strategy can play an important role on the energy use. Temper-
ature control was demonstrated to be more energy eﬃcient, while fulﬁlling the IEQ re-
quirements.
