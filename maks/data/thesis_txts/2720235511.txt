Abstract
This work has examined an Al­0.54Mg­0.38Si (at%) conductor alloy AA6101 subjected to
a thermomechanical processing (TMP) route. The effects of solution treatment at 530◦C
for 30 minutes, 30% deformation by cold rolling and aging treatment at 260◦C for 15 min
and 240 min were investigated. The solution treatment was compared with the storage
at ambient temperature. A significant increase in conductivity was observed for the rolled
samples compared to the of undeformed ones in particular after aging for 15 min. After
240 min of aging the conductivities were observed to be at a level comparable to that
of the undeformed samples. Hardness of the samples was observed to increase as a
result of the strain­hardening caused by the cold rolling and additionally, for the stored
condition, the hardness was further more increased due to natural aging. An estimation
of yield strength was done based on a microstructural investigation of grain size by light
optical microscopy, dislocations and precipitates by transmission electron microscopy.
The estimation overestimate the yield strength when compared to the measured yield
strength from tensile testing. The findings and the discussions presented are important
for future alloy development of Al­Mg­Si conductor materials.

Introduction
We encounter terms like ”renewable energy,” ”climate change,” or ”energy efficiency”
every day. They are among many words that have become a part of our everyday life,
whether in newspapers, commercials, or the news. Thinking about the environment and
changing our habits for more sustainable options has become a part of our lives. Sorting
our waste, choosing to spend vacation locally, or switching a diesel car for an electric
version are examples of this change. Consumers demand green alternatives, which puts
pressure on the industry to supply green options. Zero­emission transportation, pack­
aging made of recycled plastic, and restoring forests are some selling points companies
benefit from, to differentiate themselves from competitors in the market. This forces com­
panies to be innovative, creative, and green in their production. Especially the aforemen­
tioned electric cars have been a growing market for the last ten years. Many car brands
today can deliver an electric version or a hybrid. However, electric vehicles (EVs) still
need to be improved to outperform conventional fuel cars in driving range and amount of
time to charge fully. This development requires the optimization of factors such as the
weight and size of batteries, materials, and alloys. The latter is in focus in this thesis.
Figure 1.1: The wiring harness of a Porsche Taycan. The Orange cables are high­current
cable network
Copper is the dominating material choice for the wiring harness in EVs. The wiring
harness of a Porsche Taycan can be seen in figure 1.1. The copper wires are usually
made of strands continuously cast and bundled together to form cables. The feedstock
of the cast is electrolytically refined copper with a purity of at least 99.9% [1]. Copper
has high electrical conductivity, σ, and is widely applied as conductor material. Therefore
copper, more specifically annealed copper, is used as a standard reference for electrical
conductivity. The conductivity of other materials is expressed in % of the international
annealed copper standard (IACS) at 58 MS/m. Electrical conductivity is vital in conductor
applications to minimize energy loss during power transmission.
Another material of choice for conductor components in EVs is aluminium. Aluminium
is also used in offshore power cables and overhead lines. The solid busbars, seen on
1
==PAGE:10==
figure 1.2, are essential components, as they transmit high currents in EVs’ power net­
work. Even though aluminium is not as good a conductor as copper, it offers low density,
a high conductivity­to­weight ratio, good corrosion resistance, and good formability. The
1xxx series aluminium is in particular a good conductor with a conductivity of 59­65%
IACS [2]. Still, its use in EVs is limited due to its low mechanical strength, even in a work­
hardened state. Moderate mechanical strength is desired for automatic assembly of the
wiring harness in EVs.
Figure 1.2: Different designs of solid aluminium busbars for EVs
The heat­treatable 6xxx (Al­Si­Mg) series Al alloy is an appealing candidate material
for the high­current components in EVs if processed and optimized correctly. Several
studies have already examined the 6xxx series Al alloys thoroughly, reaching conductivi­
ties of 57.4% IACS [3], 58.9% IACS [4], and 59.3% IACS [5]. The challenge in improving
Al­Si­Mg alloys for EV conductor applications is to optimize electrical conductivity while
maintaining good mechanical strength, as these properties are counteracting.
Electricity is conducted by electrons that travel through the crystal lattice when an ex­
ternal electric potential is applied. The longer the conducting electrons can travel without
being scattered, the better the material’s conductivity. Scattering of conducting electrons
causes electrical resistivity, ρ, which is given by the reciprocal relationship ρ = 1
σ. Defects
like dislocations in the crystal lattice lead to the scattering of electrons, while dislocations
also contribute to mechanical strength. The strengthening in the 6xxx alloy is caused by
dislocations (work­hardening), nano­sized precipitates (precipitation­hardening), and so­
lutes from alloying (solid solution strengthening). An optimal thermomechanical process­
ing (TMP) route must be implemented to simultaneously optimize electrical conductivity
and mechanical strength.
The thesis has examined the development of electrical conductivity and mechanical
strength of the 6101 Al alloy by a TMP route with three processing parameters; solution
treatment, cold rolling, and aging treatment. From an industrial perspective, the project’s
overall goal is to find an optimized relationship between conductivity and mechanical
strength. As a contribution to this, my thesis aims to relate the observed conductivity
and mechanical strength to the microstructure.

Experiments and methods
2.1
Samples
Aluminium samples of the AA6101 with the composition Al­0.54Mg­0.38Si at%, were
extruded at Hydro facilities in Tønder on the 11th of October 2021 and stored at ambient
temperature until they were sent to DTU in late August 2022. The samples were received
as strips of about 30 cm in length, 2.5 cm in width, and 3.55 mm in thickness as seen on
figure 2.2a.
After extrusion at the Hydro facilities, half of the samples were subjected to solution
treatment at DTU, while the other half was kept in the stored condition. The reason for
this was to compare the effect of newly solutionized samples and samples that had been
stored for an extensive amount of time prior to cold rolling. This difference is interesting
to investigate from an industrial perspective to see if the time from extrusion to cold rolling
would have an effect, since this could simulate a delay in the production or some additional
transportation time. The storage duration from extrusion to cold rolling of the samples in
this project was 326 days. After that all samples were subsequently cold rolled and aged
treatment at DTU facilities. Solution treatment was carried out on the 1st of September.
All samples were from this date on stored in a freezer to prevent any recovery or natural
aging. Cold rolling was done on the 2nd of September.
An illustration of the TMP route can be seen on figure 2.1a. Figure 2.1b relates to
figure 2.1a and gives an overview of the sample conditions based on which processes
the samples were subjected to.
Figure 2.1: (a) The TMP route investigated. (b) A branching model showing in detail the
process parameters and the total number of conditions.

2.1.1
Solution treatment
The solution treatment was done in a box furnace at 530◦C for 30 minutes. The furnace
was preheated 30 minutes before the solution treatment in order for the temperature to
stabilize. The samples were not placed in the furnace as a stack, but individually placed
in the furnace with one end on the furnace floor and the other end leaning on a ceramic
brick to ensure air flow and thereby equal heating of all surfaces of the samples. After 30
minutes the samples were taken out of the furnace and immediately water quenched.

2.1.2
Cold rolling
A rolling mill at DTU Risø was used to roll the samples. Before the rolling was per­
formed a calculation of the deformation step size and number of rolling steps was calcu­
lated with respect to the l/h ratio. Initially and between each rolling step, the samples were
lubricated with WD­40 lubricant and the thickness of each sample was measured with a
digital micrometer screw gauge. An arrow indicating the initial rolling direction was drawn
on each sample, see figure 2.3a. After the first rolling step, the samples were flipped so
the surface with the arrow was now pointing down, but the same rolling direction main­
tained. The samples were flipped between each deformation step. This procedure was
performed until the level of deformation was achieved to in an attempt to counteract cur­
vature of the samples during rolling as they should be as straight as possible.

2.2
Conductivity measurements
To investigate the conductivity, the 4­point probe technique was used and from that
conductivity was calculated. The principle of the 4­point probe technique, procedure and
samples preparation will be explained in the following sections.
2.2.1
Principle
Figure 2.5 and 2.6 show the experimental setup and the schematic principle of the
4­point probe technique, respectively. The idea of the technique is to have a circuit with a
current source which injects the current into the sample from the two outermost probes.
The current flows through the sample and the two innermost probes measure the voltage
drop over the distance l. The idea of using 4 probes with two individual circuits instead
of just two probes with and one circuit, is that the voltage measurements become much
more precise. With two probes there is a higher uncertainty from the increased resistance
of the connection between probes and the sample and additionally the resistance from the
cables. The used setup was lowered into a temperature controlled oil bath from which the
temperature was recorded to later correct for any temperature difference (see figure 2.5).
Figure 2.5: The 4­point probe setup within a temperature monitored oil bath, used to
determine conductivity.
When a voltage drop was measured over the spacing of the probes, the resistance
was calculated from the following equation:
R = ∆V
I
(2.1)
Where R is the resistance, V is the measured voltage drop and I is the current. With
6
==PAGE:15==
the calculated resistance, the resistivity is calculated using the following relation:
ρ = R · A
l
(2.2)
Where ρ is the resistivity, A is the cross section of the sample and l is the distance
between the voltage probes on the sample. Conductivity, σ, can now be calculated from
the following relation:
σ = 1
ρ
(2.3)
While the 12 samples were measured with the setup, the recorded temperature in­
creased from 20.96◦C to 21.17◦from the first measurement to the last measurement.
Increased temperatures are known to affect the resistivity, and thereby the conductivity,
as electrons gets excited. This is why the resistivity must be ”back calculated” to 20◦C
for an accurate determination of the conductivity. The following linear relation between
resistivity and temperature was used:
ρ1 = ρ2(1 + α(T1 −T2))
(2.4)
Where ρ1 and ρ2 is resistivity, α is the temperature coefficient of resistance at 20◦C of
0.00395 K−1 and T1 and T2 is temperature in Kelvin.
Figure 2.6: Schematic principle of the 4­point probe technique.
A second method of determining conductivity was also tried. The equipment used
for this was a hand­held Foerster sigmatester. The sigma tester is a quick and easy to
use instrument which reads out the conductivity directly when the probe is placed on the
sample surface. Initially the instrument requires calibration by probing standard samples
of well known conductivity. The sigmatester is based on the Eddy current principle, which
is a circulating electromagnetic field which induces Eddy currents in the surface of the
sample. The conductivity is measured from the difference of the transmitted field and the
created magnetic field in the sample. It is though not considered to be as accurate as the
7
==PAGE:16==
4­point probe technique. The results from these sigmatest measurements are then only
reported in appendix A.1 and not discussed further here.
2.2.2
Procedure
As the samples were stored in a freezer, when not used for experiments, taking the
samples out and making sure that the samples were at ambient temperature before mea­
suring was of importance. The samples were one at a time placed on the equipment be­
fore tightening a screw in each end ensuring contact between the sample and the probes.
The probes had contact with the sample across the whole width. The setup would then be
lowered into the oil while a propeller circulated the oil in the bath. After approximately 1
minute the equipment would read out a value of resistance directly and the temperature of
the bath was noted down. After measuring all 12 samples one time each, the dimensions
of each sample were measured using a MarVision MM320 measuring microscope (see
figure2.7).
Figure 2.7: Measuring microscope used to measure dimensions of conductivity samples.

2.3
Hardness measurements
To get an impression of strength development during aging, Vickers hardness testing
was performed using a Struers Durascan hardness measuring machine in accordance
with ISO 6507­1 standard for Vickers hardness test [6].
2.3.1
Principle
The principle of the Vickers hardness measurement is to examine the materials ability
to resist deformation from a pyramidal indenter made of diamond. The test is performed
by assigning a force and a dwell for the test and afterwards the length of the diagonals
of the square indentation (see figure 2.9), left by the indent, are measured. The program
would attempt to measure the indents by automatically positioning lines on the diagonals
and measure the spacing, but this automatic positioning was not always accurate, so all
indent measurements were reviewed and lines were placed manually if necessary. From
the diagonal lengths and the force, the program calculates the hardness. The equations
for manually calculating the hardness can be found in the standard for Vickers hardness
test [6].
Figure 2.9: Picture of a hardness indentation. The green lines on the diagonal of the
indentation is placed automatically by the program. The length measured between the
lines are reported in the lower left corner along with the hardness.
9
==PAGE:18==
2.3.2
Procedure
To simplify the test and reduce operator errors, a well defined pattern of indentations
were pre­programmed. The pattern accounted for a minimum distance between adjacent
indentations and the sample edge (see figure 2.10). The indent pattern consisted of 24
indentations, which also mean that the average hardness values reported is based of
24 measurements for each sample condition. The test was performed using 1 kg load
for a dwell time of 10 seconds. The test requires samples to be plane­parallel with the
stone platform of the Durascan machine. As none of the samples were embedded, the
placement of samples on the testing platform was carefully done to ensure the samples
was placed as stable and flat as possible.
Figure 2.10: Minimum distances between adjacent indentations and the sample edges.
The recorded distances for light metals was used [6].

2.4
Tensile test
Tensile testing was done in order to determine the yield strength of the samples. The
tensile test was performed on an electro­mechanical Instron machine with a capacity of
100 kN.
2.4.1
Principle
Tensile testing by a uniaxial force is a fundamental engineering test where a controlled
force is applied to a sample until failure. By applying force to the sample it deforms elas­
tically according to Young’s modulus. At some point the force is so high that the sample
begins to deform plastically until it breaks. From this test properties like strength at break,
ultimate tensile strength and strain can be directly measured. From the direct measure­
ments other properties like Young’s modulus and yield strength can be determined. When
plastic deformation is happening during tensile testing, it is due to dislocations moving in
the material. Dislocations mainly move along the slip systems as these acts as ”highways”
for dislocations. A slip system is defined by a the combination of a slip plane and a slip
10
==PAGE:19==
direction. Aluminium is known to be face centered cubic and have 12 slip systems. The
slip planes are {111} and the slip directions are <1 ­1 0>
2.4.2
Procedure
Before testing could begin, an extensometer was calibrated using a calibrator device.
The samples were one by one placed in the tensile jaw grips equipped with serrated
wedge shaped grips. The opening of the jaws were marked with a pen to ensure the
same placement of the samples in the wedge grips, which was in the center of the grips.
The extensometer was attached to the sample by rubber bands (see figure 2.11) .
Figure 2.11: The setup for tensile testing. The sample is positioned in the jaw grips with
the extensometer attached.
After the test were performed tensile curves with engineering strain (measured by the
extensometer) on the x­axis and engineering stress on the y­axis were made (also called
stress­strain curves). From these curves the Young’s modulus can found from the slope
of the elastic region of the curves. The slope of the elastic region is also used to determine
the yield strength of the samples by the off­set method. The slope of the elastic region of
the curve is off­set by 0.2% and extended until it intersects the curve. The intersect with
the curve is reported as the yield strength of the sample.
2.4.3
Sample preparation
The preparation of samples required the aid of a technician to manufacture dog bone
samples by milling to the specified dimensions seen in figure 2.12. The aluminium plates
were initially cut to the desired total length of the dog bone samples using a shear cutter
before machining, so only the central narrowing was machined by milling. Temperature
was controlled by water cooling during milling of the dog bone samples. A total of 14 dog
bone samples were manufactured which gives 3 test for each of the four tested conditions
and additionally 2 dog bone samples for testing the setup of the equipment.

2.5
Light optical microscopy
2.5.1
Principle
Optical microscopy is the primary means for metallurgists to examine metallic mate­
rials. The optical principles starts with the image formation illustrated in figure 2.13. The
object of interest is placed between one and two focal lengths from the objective lens.
Light rays from a lighting source illuminates the object and the light rays are converged
by the objective lens and focused to form a magnified inverted image (1st image). The
light rays are then further converged by the projector lens to form the final image. In this
case the real image is formed on a screen, but normally objects are examined with our
eyes, so the light rays goes through an eyepiece instead of the projector lens to form a
virtual image on the eye retina. This is of course the basics of optical microscopy, since
modern microscopes today are very advanced and complicated, but the working principle
is the same.
Figure 2.13: Principle of magnification
Light optical microscopy is a quick and easy method for examining metallic samples,
but it requires some sample preparation. The limitation to the technique is magnification,
which is limited by resolution. Resolution is the minimum distance between two Airy disks
at which they can still be distinguished as two.
The samples investigated by LOM had prior to the optical examination been anodized.
Anodizing is the electrolytic passivation process of artificially increasing the thickness of
the naturally occurring oxide layer of the aluminium substrate. This is done by immersing
the aluminium sample into an electrolyte and sending a direct current through the medium.
The sample acts as an anode in the process. Upon examining the anodized samples
in an optical microscope using a polarized light filter (lambda filter) many colors appear
12
==PAGE:21==
on the polycrystalline surface. The colors of the oxide from each grain is related to the
independent oxide thickness resulting in light being reflected with different wavelengths
and thereby different colors. This is why this technique is very convenient for investigating
grain sizes and shapes.
2.5.2
Procedure
The micrographs used for analyzing the grains of the samples were taken by manu­
ally setting the polarized filter to where the best color contrast between the grains were
achieved. For each sample four to five micrographs were taken from one edge of the sam­
ple to the other along the normal direction, so that the whole thickness of the samples was
investigated. On each micrograph one line were drawn randomly along the rolling direc­
tion and another along the normal direction. The size of grains were measured along
these line for each micrograph of all conditions.
2.5.3
Sample preparation
To investigate grains samples were cut using a shear cutter. The cutting of samples
and the examined surface can be seen on figure 2.14. Each sample were hand ground
using a clamp to ensure a plane grind of the surface to be examined. Samples were
ground for on 500 grid paper until plane then for 1 minute on 1000 grid paper until fi­
nally for 45 seconds on 4000 grid paper. After grinding the samples were electropolished
using a Lectropol­5 using Struers preprogrammed standard method for aluminium. The
procedure uses an electrolyte called A2 of ethanol, 2n­Butoxyethanol, perchloric acid and
destilled water. The Lectropol­5 runs for 35 seconds at 48 Volts. The anodizing was done
also with Lectropol­5 using Barkers reagent (flouroboric acid) as electrolyte and running
for 40s at 25V.

2.6
Scanning electron microscopy
2.6.1
Principle
Scanning electron microscopy creates images by scanning the surface of a sample
using an electron beam in high vacuum. The samples were examined using secondary
electrons (SE). SE’s are emitted by atoms excited by the electron beam scanning the
surface of the sample and they are detected by a SE detector. When the electron beam
scans the surface, the incident electrons knocks out electrons from the sample surface.
13
==PAGE:22==
The emitted electrons are secondary electrons (SEs can also be generated by escaping
backscattered electrons), which are low in energy. The low energy SEs have a small
escaping depth from the sample, smaller than the depth of the interaction volume, which
means that SEs can provide detailed topographic information. The interaction volume of
the SEs is both dependent on the acceleration voltage used and the atomic number Z.
Another method of sample examination used with SEM is electron backscatter diffrac­
tion (EBSD). The technique is performed by tilting the sample to 70◦for increased signal.
When the primary electrons of the incident beam is focused on the sample inelastic scat­
tering occurs. Electrons scatter in all directions from this interaction. From all the electrons
there will be an electron beam of scattering angle (θ) from the interaction which satisfies
the constructive diffraction condition called Bragg’s law:
nλ = 2dsin(θ)
(2.5)
where n is an integer, λ is the wavelength, d is the interplanar spacing and θ is the
scattering angle. The constructively diffracted electron beams forms paired cones which
light up a phosphor screen which is then recorded by a camera. The cones formed by
diffracted electrons are seen as Kikuchi bands. Many Kikuchi bands form a Kikuchi pattern
which hold information about e.g. local orientation of the lattice, interplanar spacing and
angle between planes.
Energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) was another technique used with SEM. EDS
is a method that enables chemical characterization of materials. It works by scanning the
electron beam over the selected surface and the incident electrons will excite the sample
and dissipates some of the absorbed energy by sending out a core­shell electron. A higher
energy outer shell electron then takes its place and by doing so it releases some energy
in the form of a characteristic x­ray. The x­rays are detected and mapped and elements
can be identified.
2.6.2
Procedure
The Zeiss Sigma SEM was operated using 15 kV for SE investigation.
2.6.3
Sample preparation
Samples for SEM were hand ground using a clamp to hold the samples and ensure a
plane surface. The same procedure for grinding of SEM samples were done as for LOM
samples. Both hand polishing and electropolishing were performed, but electropolishing
showed the best results as the hand polishing caused particles to get stuck in the surface
which would create comet tails. This was not observed when prepared with electropolish­
ing. The samples was managed to be prepared with hand polishing, but this method was
very time consuming and required a heavy amount of consumables, so electropolishing
was the method used for polishing of samples. The procedure uses an electrolyte called
A2 of ethanol, 2n­Butoxyethanol, perchloric acid and destilled water. The Lectropol­5 runs
for 35 seconds at 48 Volts. Samples were put in a plastic box covered with scratch free
paper on the inside and stored in the freezer when not used.
2.7

2.7.1
Principle
In some ways a TEM is similar to SEM, it also uses electrons for microscope illumi­
nation, but with TEM the image is formed by electrons transmitted through the sample.
To acheive high resolution, the TEM is commonly operated at an acceleration voltage

==PAGE:23==

primarily from diffraction contrast. The local scattering of electrons causes fewer of the
transmitted electrons to reach the image plane and thereby appear dark in the bright­field
mode(low intensity). A bright­field image is created in the diffraction mode by projecting
the diffraction pattern, formed on the back­focal plane, onto a flourescent screen as seen
in figure 2.15.

4
Analysis of relations between
electrical/mechanical properties and
microstructure
There are several factors that contribute to mechanical strength in AA6101.
The
mechanical strength of interest here is the yield strength, which involves a stress large
enough to initiate slip by the movement of dislocations. There is a gradual transition be­
tween the elastic and plastic region. A contribution to mechanical strength in this sense
would be a factor that would hinder the movement of dislocations. Factors contributing to
mechanical strength, besides the strength of the aluminium matrix, are considered to be
grain boundaries, dislocations, precipitates and solutes in solution. These factors are also
contributing to the resistivity, and thereby negatively affecting the electrical conductivity.
Pure aluminium exhibit high conductivity and just a small addition of solute elements are
known to reduce the conductivity.
Grain boundaries contribute to yield strength by acting as insurmountable obstacles
for moving dislocations which leads to pile up. These pile up of dislocations build up
stress in the neighbouring grain until a sufficient stress causes deformation and thereby
new dislocations in the adjacent grain. The contribution of grain boundaries on resistivity
is small because the size of the grains are generally much larger than the mean free path
of the conducting electrons which is 14 nm in Al at room temperature [5] compared to the
reported average grain sizes of 40­70 µm.
Dislocations can hinder the movement of other dislocations and thereby contribute to
increased yield strength. This involves introducing new dislocations to the material by
deforming it, also called work hardening. This would cause a complex and entangled
dislocation network. The increased dislocation density would hinder the movement of
dislocations and require a additional force on the material for dislocations to move. Dislo­
cations are also known to contribute to resistivity. Unless the material has been severely
deformed this contribution is thought be small. Dislocation lines can have an indirect con­
tribution to electrical conductivity by making diffusion of solute atoms easier, as suggested
by [5].
A main contribution to increased yield strength is from precipitates. Precipitates grow
as the solid solubility changes with temperature during aging treatment of the super satu­
rated solid solution in the present material. The strain field around the precipitates impede
the movement of dislocations as an additional force is required to overcome this strain
field. It can be difficult to accurately determine the contribution from precipitates as other
complex factors are contributing in the precipitate/dislocation interaction. Factors like co­
herency and precipitate size are affecting the contribution, but simplifying assumptions
can be made, but precipitates will hinder precipitation glide. Regarding the contribution
to resistivity it is thought to be small as the interparticle spacing is much larger than the
mean free path of the conducting electrons.
Solutes dispersed in the aluminium matrix contribute to strength by imposing a strain
field on the surrounding atoms from the size misfit of the solute atom. This lattice strain
exerts a force on the dislocations that must be overcome before plastic deformation can
occur. Although the contribution of solid solution strengthening to mechanical strength is
35
==PAGE:44==
low, the AA6101 is considered a lean alloy in terms of additions, which makes strength­
ening from solutes significant. The contribution of solute atoms to resistivity is thought
to be substantial. The size and dispersion of solute atoms in the matrix results in more
frequent scattering of conducting electrons and thereby contribute to resistivity.

5
Conclusion
In this work, the development of electrical conductivity and mechanical strength of the
AA6101 was investigated. A TMP route with three processing parameters, being solution
treatment, cold rolling and aging treatment, was studied to relate the observed conductivity
and mechanical strength to the microstructure.
It was shown that by cold rolling the samples to 30% deformation before the aging
treatment the development of electrical conductivity, after just 15 min at 260◦C of aging,
was significantly improved. The deformed samples showed an increase in conductiv­
ity from 51.6% to 55.9% IACS for the stored conditions and 50.4% to 53.8% IACS for
the solutionized conditions. All conditions, except SO30, have similar conductivity above
58%IACS after aging at 260◦C for 240 min.
The hardness was shown to increase as a result of the cold rolling, becasue of strain­
hardening. The rolling increases the hardness of the non­aged SO30 from 40 HV to 65 HV
and for ST30 from 46 HV to 73 HV. Upon aging for 15 min, the hardness of all conditions
increases except for the ST30. The reason for why the hardness of ST30 decreases
upon this aging is expected to be due to natural aging that has occurred during storage
of the sample. Extensive aging for 240 min will leave most of the samples over­aged and
thereby softer than in the non­aged state.
A number of microscope techniques have been used in order to relate microstruc­
tural details to the electrical and mechanical properties. The microstructural investiga­
tion showed that there are many factors contributing to yield strength as well as resistiv­
ity. Grain boundaries, precipitates, solutes and dislocations are all contributing factors,
among which the most important may be precipitates for the present set of samples. This
was also supported by an estimation of yield strength. The parameter contributing most to
resistivity is shown to be the solutes. Increased dislocation density is showed to improve
the electrical conductivity by enhanced precipitation and thereby depleting the matrix of
solutes.
This investigation has shown the vast possibilities of TMP as tool to achieve desired
electrical and mechanical properties and has demonstrated that microstructural inves­
tigation, if performed carefully may be used to predict and interpret the electrical and
mechanical results.

6
Outlook
Future work would involve more careful characterization of precipitates from TEM mi­
crographs to get a better estimation of their contribution to yield strength since the current
work is based on counting of precipitates in one of the three alignments along the (100)Al
directions. Along with this it would be advantageous to know the thickness of the foils from
the specific micrographs to more precisely determine the precipitation density, from which
volume fraction of precipitates can be obtained, and thereby their strength contribution.
Another method is to perform atom probe tomography to more accurately measure the
number of solutes left in solid solution.
What would also have been interesting to investigate are different aging temperatures
at lower temperatures than the present. This would be of interest because temperature is
accelerating the diffusion processes exponentially and from the present work it was shown
that the development of electrical and mechanical properties is very rapid in the early aging
stage. So to slow down this development and more carefully map the evolution of the two
properties would be interesting to do. in addition to this it would also be interesting to
extend the sample matrix to include lower rolling reductions.
Future work would also include more measurements of conductivity with the used
4­point probe equipment to investigate the low uncertainty of the technique. More tensile
tests would be of interest to get a better understanding of the difference between the
estimated and the measured yield strength. In addition to the estimated yield strength it
would be interesting to do an estimation of the conductivity based on the microstructure,
as done by Jonas k. Sunde et al. [5].
