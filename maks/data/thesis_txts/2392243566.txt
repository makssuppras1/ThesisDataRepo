Abstract
A low-delity source-doublet panel method is presented in order to rapidly assess the aerodynamic
ow around 2D airfoil in the early design phase.
This allows to perform design modications
in a fast and robust manner at a reasonable accuracy. The content of this thesis is conned to
low-speed aerodynamics where both inviscid and viscous ow theory is presented. The objective
is to investigate the necessary aspects and challenges that arise when applying the panel method
procedure in the shape optimization framework.
The presented shape optimization process is gradient-based for which an ecient adjoint-based
sensitivity analysis is elaborated. Since the ow domain is based on a singularity method, the panel
inductions inuence the entire ow domain which makes the evaluation of the partial derivative of
the aerodynamic residual a cumbersome process. Furthermore, dierent parametrization strategies
are presented which include a non-parametric nodal approach, a B-spline parametrization and a
free-form deformation technique which have all been implemented in MATLAB. Especially, the
nodal approach has gained attention due to its great exibility. Therefore, dierent strategies to
perform the nodal movements are presented in order to avoid nodal clustering; a phenomena that
is well-known in shape optimization. Depending on the problem formulation, the optimizer may
nd numerical deciencies in the model that require some kind of regularization of the nodes.
Therefore, two lters are investigated to smooth the nodal coordinates; the one is a "density" lter
that lters a certain number of nodes along the surface by assigning each node geometric weight
factor. The other is an adjacent nodal lter that is capable of smoothing each node by ltering a
subset of neighbouring nodes multiple times. To investigate the properties of the adjacent node
lter, it has been applied to a shape optimization program for continuum structures.
Three ow problems are considered which include the classical inverse design problem, a max-
imum lift problem and a drag minimization problem. For the inverse design problem, both the
nodal approach with and without regularization and the B-spline curve have been successfully em-
ployed. The pressure distributions for airfoils at dierent angles of attack are investigated which
showed that the trailing edge is very prone to folding over if for instance too large movements of
the nodes are performed. The maximum lift problem uses the nodal approach with the adjacent
nodal lter to obtain a maximum lift coecient that is subject to an area and a maximum pressure
constraint. The results resemble a realistic design but issues were detected with respect to the
convergence rate of the lift coecient. Lastly, the drag minimization problem is conducted by
using a viscous ow model. The attempt to implement a two-way coupled boundary layer was
not accomplished in the timeframe of the project which is why the open-source program XFOIL
has been used for this purpose. By only allowing vertical movement of the nodes and require the
original area to be preserved, the airfoil drag were reduced by more than 20 % for two dierent
Reynolds numbers.

Introduction
For centuries, shape optimization of 2D airfoils and 3D wing congurations has been an ever-
advancing eld due to continuous quest for a more ecient design. The early wing congurations
and airfoil designs took a wide variety of shapes ranging from prototypes of birds to legendary
ying animals. Only gradually, the soaring wings of birds became the dominating type. At the
time of the rst powered ight by the Wright Brothers (1903), very thin and highly cambered
airfoil sections were utilized, see Figure 1.1. The erroneous belief that ecient airfoils took such
shape meant that the rst aircrafts became biplanes. Eventually, based on trial and error, the
more successful Clark Y airfoils were developed in the 1920's [5]. Later, in 1939, the rst laminar
ow airfoil by Eastman Jacobs was tested which produced high lift and extremely low drag [6].
Despite these properties, modern airfoils take a large variety of shapes because the ow conditions
and design goals change for each application that may occur, not only in the aerospace industry,
but also, for instance, in the automotive and racing car industry [1].
By the advancement of computational resources, high-delity methods have been increasingly
employed for the modelling of aerodynamic ow. However, since conventional optimization tech-
niques usually require a large number of simulations, the current high-delity methods are still too
expensive and thereby not feasible for shape optimization of full 3D wing congurations. Therefore,
the faster low delity methods have gained renewed attention as they are capable of giving a rea-
sonable representation of the physical ow. By only considering low-speed aerodynamic problems,
the ow can be considered frictionless which means that the eect of viscosity can be neglected.
Also, the ow is considered to be incompressible and irrotational which form the basic assumptions
for the low-delity approach. These assumptions greatly simplies the ow which can be eciently
modelled by using the so-called panel methods. The approach of using panel methods are then
more suitable for generic models than to obtain detailed information about a given design.
The main content of this thesis concerns the modelling and optimization of 2D airfoil. The
practical applicability of 2D wing sections is limited to some extent as they does not account for
crossow eects. For aircrafts with a low aspect ratio ÆR(wing span2/planar wing area<2, [7]), this
greatly impacts the airfoil characteristics which is why the obtained results only applies to airfoil
sections of large aspect ratio wings such as gliders (or in theory, only to innitely long wings).

1.4
Introduction to Panel Methods
During the late 1950s, the rst panel methods were developed for analysing incompressible po-
tential ow problems around arbitrary bodies. The panel method approach serves as numerical
==PAGE:20==
1.5.
Introduction to Shape Optimization
5
tool for solving surface integral equations of linear partial dierential equations in a robust and
ecient manner. The body surface is discretized by a set of panels where each panels is assigned
a distribution of singularities with an unknown singularity strength. By requiring no penetrating
ux through the panel mid-point, a linear system of equations is generated which allows to solve
directly for the singularity strengths. Once known, all information about the entire ow eld is
provided. By surface pressure integration, the aerodynamic forces and moments can be computed.
Since panel methods only treat the boundaries of the objects in the uid domain, the dimen-
sionality of the ow problem is reduced by one. Hence, the eld solution of a 3D ow problem can
be obtained by studying only the surface of the submerged object. Therefore, the panel method
approach is referred to as a boundary element method (BEM). In contrast to more sophisticated
methods such as the nite element method (FEM) or nite volume method (FVM), these meth-
ods oer higher delity at the expense of increased computational costs since their dimensionality
matches the one of the physical space. In addition, these methods require the entire ow domain
to be discretized which usually is a time-consuming process whereas panel methods only requires
a less demanding surface discretization.
The representation of the ow eld using singularity sheets (of sources, doublets and vortices)
is advantageous for several other reasons [3]:

The thin viscous layers for aerodynamic ow elds only introduce a little loss of accuracy
when the vorticity is lumped into the vortex sheets along the airfoil and wake.

Only the geometry and wake shedding needs to be modelled.

The sheet representation requires about 100 fewer unknowns than grid-based approaches for
obtaining the same level of accuracy.

Once the velocity or potential jumps across the panels are known, the strengths of the ow
singularity can be evaluated immediately.
The assumptions that governs the potential ow greatly simplies the real physics in the
aerodynamic ow.
In many cases, it is reasonable to neglect viscosity in the bulk of the ow
region (the inviscid outer ow). By neglecting the viscosity term in the momentum equations,
viscous surface drag is lost too which poses the main disadvantage of the panel method procedure.
However, in the vicinity of body separate models can be applied to cope with the more complex
aspects of the ow such as compressibility and viscous eects.
The main drawback with panel methods is the stringent requirement for the wake discretization.
It is necessary to include a potential jump across the wake meaning that the wake surface mesh
must somehow be body conforming. This is, however, often ignored in 2D.
Despite the availability of more exact methods, the speed and exibility of panel methods make
them an ideal tool for conceptual design or rapid design iterations of large-scale models in the early
design phase; this advantage is paramount in optimization cycles where multiple attempts are
typically required for testing dierent design parameters. Once the nal conguration is obtained,
it is reasonable to apply high-delity CFD methods for design renements.
1.5
Introduction to Shape Optimization
The aerodynamic performance of a body can be improved by using the techniques in structural
optimization. The purpose of structural optimization is to nd the optimal shape conguration or
distribution of material in a design space which provides the best system performance with respect
to a specic quantity and given constraints. Structural optimization can be divided into three main
categories which are sizing, shape and topology optimization. The three optimization techniques
==PAGE:21==
6
Introduction
are illustrated in Figure 1.4 where the objectives are the same but the optimal congurations are
very dierent [12].
In sizing optimization, the general shape of the design model is known a priori and is retained
during the optimization process. Typically, a structural dimension constitutes the design variables
which are systematically modied in order to minimize (or maximize) a physical quantity while
satisfying the equilibrium and constraints on the state and design variables. Since the domain of
the design model is xed, the methodology limits the geometric exibility meaning that holes for
instance cannot occur which is one of the the main limitations. Nevertheless, sizing optimization
has become a powerful tool in large scale applications with multiphysics phenomena.
In shape optimization (SO), the system performance is improved by modifying the shape of
the boundary curves which are controlled by a set of design variables. In comparison to topology
optimization, the mathematics in SO are based on completely dierent techniques [13]. There are
two main limitations of SO. Firstly, large adjustments of the boundaries may cause problems by
a distorted discretization of the design. This issue can be alleviated by remeshing the domain
during the optimization process which in turn increases the computational costs. This, however,
is a minor problem when using panel methods. Secondly, the original connectivity of the structure
remains unchanged and new boundaries cannot be formed. This prevents new internal structural
modications to occur such as holes. Therefore, the initial design must resemble the nal cong-
uration to some extent, thus, requiring that the engineer has some knowledge about the optimal
design [13].
Topology optimization (TO) is the most general class of structural optimization. Unlike sizing
and SO, the shape conguration is not predened and does not require a qualied initial guess, see
Figure 1.4. The design can attain any form within the design space with only a few limitations.
Therefore, TO is highly applicable when the engineer has only limited knowledge about optimal
design.
In this thesis, the optimization of airfoil proles are considered for which the general shape is
known a priori. The content is conned to single element airfoils, thus, excluding multi-element
proles and the spacing between the airfoil control mechanisms at the ailerons, aps and leading
edge slats. Under these conditions, the framework of SO is ideal. Dierent issues regarding the
parametrization, regularization and so on will be addressed in Chapter 4.

Chapter 3
Two-Dimensional Panel Method
A constant strength source-doublet panel method is presented which will form the basis of the
optimization of the airfoil performance in Chapter 5. Both an inviscid and a viscous formulation
is described which are validated against analytic results and experimental data.
3.1
Preliminary Considerations
The following summarizes the most important considerations regarding the numerical panel code.
3.1.1
Choice of Singularity Elements
In principle, the ow eld can be represented by innitely many combinations of source, doublet
and vortex singularity distributions, however, only a few combinations are suitable for a numerically
stable model. According to [30], a good model constitutes a ow eld that is benign and related
to the boundary at the inner region of the airfoil.
This means that when passing through a
boundary, the jump from the internal ow to the external ow should be small, thus, requiring
minimum perturbations of the singularities. A bad model allows large internal ows which must
be compensated by using high panel densities for acceptable results.
In section 3.2, a combined source/doublet panel method with piecewise constant singularity
surface distributions is presented due to the following considerations.

Singularity Elements: 2D panel codes are usually represented by vortex panels due to their
ability to model lifting as well as non-lifting ows better than doublet panels. In addition,
most panel codes are also constituted by a representation of source singularities in order to
account for model extensions. The singularity representation of the presented 2D panel code
is considered to form the basis of a 3D model and therefore the expected diculties in 3D
are addressed. Despite the attractive properties of the vortices to represent aerodynamic
ow elds in 2D, the 3D vortex sheet strength becomes a vector whose components are not
entirely independent, see [3].
The complications arises from the surface gradients of the
vortex strength which are to ensure zero surface-divergence such that the ow resembles the
velocity vectors of the divergence-free 2D incompressible ow. Therefore, doublet sheets are
favoured over vortex sheets in all 3D panel methods as the doublet strengths remain scalar
quantities. Similarly, the source ow also keeps it's scalar properties. Therefore, the 2D
model is based on a combination of doublet and source ow types.

Panel Order: For subsonic ow, both low and high order panels can be applied. According
to [24], high order panel methods provides better accuracy in the modelling of the ow eld
but this is at the expense of increased code complexity and computation time. Low order
panels, however, are more simple and provides comparable accuracy at lower computational
costs.
They do not enforce continuity of the singularity distributions from one panel to
another which makes the assembly procedure of panel contributions more straightforward.
A continuous representation of the surface singularity distribution is required in high order
formulations when studying supersonic ows or evaluating ow elds between multi-element
airfoils. High order panels are very sensitive to gaps between panels (which mostly occurs
in 3D) whereas low order panels are rather insensitive and, therefore, more favourable in
17
==PAGE:33==

terms of stability and exibility [24] [18]. Thus, a low panel order is more practical to apply
to non-linear problems for iterative solutions like wake relaxation, viscous/inviscid coupled
boundary layers and time-stepping for unsteady ow problems. Since the presented panel
code is used for shape optimization, a simple formulation is ideal when deriving the nodal
sensitivities, see section 4.3. Therefore, low order panels of constant strength are chosen.1

Panel Geometry: In most panel codes, a at panel geometry is applied since this allows the
integrations to be performed analytically at low computational costs. The usage of curved
panels (often paraboloidal in shape) are far more accurate in regions of high curvature [31].
However in general when using at panels, this problem can be alleviated in the discretization
process. Since the presented code is used for shape optimization, only at panels are applied.

Model Extensions: For general applications, the presented method must contain source sin-
gularities in order to model inlet ows, jets, unsteady conditions and boundary layer eects.
Consequently, the advantages in terms of computational costs by using the doublet-only
formulation disappears [30].
3.1.2
Boundary Conditions
According to [32], the inow at the airfoil side edges are normally constrained by Neumann BCs
whereas Dirichlet conditions are often applied to the leading edge. For simplicity, the presented
method only utilizes one set of BCs. The Dirichlet BCs generally require fewer numerical manip-
ulations since the surface velocity potential is calculated as a scalar quantity. The Neumann BCs
need both the normal and in-plane ow components but also require additional transformations
between the panel and wing reference frames. Since ∇Φ = v, the potential formulation appears
like it is one order higher than the velocity formulation for a given distribuiton of singularities, [33].
This is a drawback since the evaluation of surface velocities must be conducted though numerical
dierentiation [20]. However, by using a low order panel formulation in conjunction with constant
perturbation potential requirement at the interior of the airfoil, the solution becomes less sensitive
to changes in the airfoil geometry. Therefore, the Dirichlet BCs are chosen for this panel code.
3.1.3
Wake Model
The most convenient choice is to apply a doublet sheet as wake due to the preliminary model
choices. The shape of the wake segment is usually ignored in 2D since it has zero vorticity in
steady ow conditions. Therefore, only the location of the trailing edge needs be stated in order
to enforce the Kutta condition.

3.3.2
Coupling of Potential Flow Model and BL Equations
The two most common ways to couple the inviscid ow solver and the BL equations are:

One-way Coupling: This approach rst solves for the inviscid potential ow where the edge
velocity is forwarded to BL solution method to obtain the viscous variables.
Since the
viscous displacement eect is ignored, the loss of lift cannot be accurately predicted.
If
separation occurs, the boundary layer solution will fail at that streamwise location since no
solution exists when the wall shear stress is zero. Also in this weak interaction approach,
the BL equations cannot be integrated into the wake. The correlation method of Thwaites
utilizes this approach where separation is expected either when it's closure relations becomes
undened or when a transition criteria is met (e.g. Michel's). After this point, Head's method
is usually applied to model the turbulent reattach ow which is a rather rough way to handle
separation [34].

Two-way Coupling: An alternative approach is the viscous-inviscid coupled method where
the displacement eect is incorporated into the potential ow solver. This is known as the
strong interaction method where the pressure and edge velocities are not known in advance
like the one-way coupled system. The BL equations and the ow solver are instead two-
way coupled meaning that they must be solved together. This is conducted by either using
a displacement body model or a wall transpiration model.
The rst model modies the
airfoil geometry by moving the solid surface a distance corresponding to the displacement
thickness. Hence, the edge velocity equals the tangential velocity of the potential ow solver.
The second method utilizes source sheets to simulate the displacement eect in low speed
ows. A ctitious mass ux is generated at the wall (ρiui)w which means that the ow is
no longer tangential to the airfoil. According to [3], it is suggested that the equations are
solved simultaneously by a Newton procedure to avoid solution failures.
The two-way coupled formulation is superior to the one-way coupling approach as it can be
integrated beyond the point of ow separation. Since the transpiration model only require the
inuence coecients to be calculated once, this method has been considered next instead of the
displacement body model.

4.2
Parametrization
The choice of parametrization strategy is crucial in order to preserve the space of optimal solutions.
The geometry should be neither under- nor over-parametrized in order to avoid ill-posed parametric
formulations. In addition, the following criteria are considered [41]:

Conciseness: The parametrization model must consist of a compact set of design variables
with as few variables as possible. This narrows the design search space which reduces the
computational costs of the optimization solver.

Robustness: The parametric geometry should be capable of producing shapes that are phys-
ically reasonable. Relocation of nodal coordinates may critically distort the original dis-
cretization providing unacceptable designs. To alleviate this problem, dierent constraints
may be imposed upon the coordinate movements which harms the exibility.

Flexibility: The range of shapes that the parametric geometry is capable of generating must
be as wide as possible.

4.2.1
Literature Review
Dierent strategies applied for aero-structural shape optimization are listed below:

The Nodal Approach: performs design modications directly on the boundary coordinates
where each has assigned at least one design variable. Hence, the geometric changes are only
limited by the number of design variables which provides a strong local control. Another
advantage occurs as the parametrization can be applied to already existing discretizations.
Due to the high exibility, the geometry is dicult to maintain smooth as it is neither concise
nor robust meaning that some kind of regularization should be employed in many cases.

The Spline Approach: is used extensively in shape optimization as it provides a high de-
gree of continuity and hence a smooth geometry such that the state variables are continous
which is crucial for problems in uid mechanics, see Figure 4.1a. The representation of the
shape requires only a few control points and design variables. Additionally, the derivatives
can be evaluated eectively and accurately. Often Bezier-splines are used to control the
design globally which have been applied in [41] for inverse design problems and [34] for drag
minimization problems. For relatively complex geometries, locally controlled B-splines (ag-
gregations of low degree Bezier curves) are favourable which are applied in [43]. For both the
Bezier and B-spline representations, the derivatives of the spline curves (the basis functions)
stay constant during the optimization, see Section 4.3. An extended form of the B-spline are
the non-uniform rational B-spline (NURBS) where each control point is assigned a weight
factor which allows more geometric freedom. The NURBS representation has been applied
in [2] and [44].

Free Form Deformation (FFD): is a image morphing technique that utilizes Bezier or B-spline
surfaces or volumes to manipulate the embedded aerodynamic mesh for shape deformations.
The FFD mapping R2 →R2 is shown in Figure 4.1b-4.1c for the 2D case. This approach
resembles the properties of B-splines, thus, providing smooth and dierentiable geometry
modications at low computational cost. The physical model is deformed as a rubber-like
object by simple operations such as twist, bending etc.
[45].
In [46], this method was
successfully applied for the optimization of 3D wing structure by sequentially adjusting the
chord, span, thickness-to-span followed by the twist, dihedral and sweep properties. The
result of this parametrization is depicted in Figure 1.2. Further explanation on the approach
is given in Appendix A.6.1.
Due to the favourable properties of spline curves, they have been used extensively in literature.
The nodal approach has received less attention which is why the following investigates the capability
of the method and some actions to be implemented for improving its robustness. The spline method
is also elaborated and has been implemented for comparison of results.

4.3
Sensitivity Analysis
The sensitivity analysis concerns how the output of the model responds to changes of the input
variables. Since the optimization scheme is gradient-based, it is essential to accurately compute
the derivatives of the objective function and constraints with respect to the design variables. The
derivatives indicate how the design eld should be modied such that the objective function is
minimized under the given constraints. Some popular techniques for obtaining the derivatives are
described below [50],[51]:

Finite Dierence (FD): The FD approach is derived from a rst order Taylor series expansion
which yields a truncation error proportional to step size h, O(h). The method is widely used
due to it's simplicity as it does not require information about the computational model. The
main drawback occurs as the computational cost is proportional to the number of design
variables. Secondly, if the step size is too small, the perturbation value matches the reference
value yielding incorrect derivatives.

Complex-Step Finite Dierence (C-SFD): This approach distinguishes from the FD approach
as it utilizes a pure imaginary perturbation, ih. This is tremendous advantageous since the
estimate is not subjected to cancellation errors at very small step sizes. Therefore, the steps
are typically at the order of 10−15. The only source of error is the truncation error which is
an O(h2) estimation, see [52], [53].

Discrete Adjoint Method: The discrete adjoint method is used extensively in many opti-
mization disciplines. This is primarily due to the fact that the cost of computing the total
derivatives is independent of the number of design variables and is proportional to the quan-
tities of interest. This method is elaborated further below.

Algorithmic Dierentiation (AD): The AD approach utilizes that every program consists
of sequences of arithmetic operations and elementary functions. By using the chain rule
repeatedly, the derivatives can be computed accurately to machine precision. Depending on
==PAGE:53==

the application, this method might require more computational time than the FD approach
if high coupling between software modules is introduced. But, if used for coupling adjoint it
is typically faster.

problems. Instead, the adjoint method serves as a more appropriate choice which has already been
sucesfully employed in aerostructural applications by Kennedy (2014).

4.5.1
Optimization Solver
Optimization problems are normally non-convex which make them dicult to solve. Therefore,
they are typically transformed into smaller subproblems or dual problems which generally are
simpler to solve.3
Several optimization algorithms have been developed for solving non-linear optimization prob-
lems such as the Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) method, Convex Linearization (CON-
LIN) [58], the BroydenFletcherGoldfarbShanno algorithm (BFGS) 4, etc. The CONLIN method
has shown excellent convergence properties for structural optimization problems. However, in some
cases the convex approximations may either be too conservative causing slow convergence or not
suciently conservative which causes the solution to oscillate. To remedy this problem, Svanberg
(1987) proposed the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) where the conservativeness of the
approximations is adjusted by introducing lower and upper asymptotes for each subproblem [60].
This stabilizes the convergence of the solution which is one of the reasons that the MMA algorithm
is used for all optimization problems that utilizes the source-doublet method. More information
about the MMA algorithm is given in Appendix A.9.1.
When the design bounds are updated, the MMA-solver is not restarted. Only the updated
design variables and bounds are changed such that the nodal positions remains the same after the
update. Therefore, the history of the previous design variables is preserved.
The drag minimization problem is treated by using a black box solver meaning that the exact
derivatives wrt. the design variables cannot be calculated. Therefore, the derivatives are found by

4.5.2
Existence and Uniqueness of an Optimal Solution
Since most optimization problems are non-linear and non-convex in nature, it is dicult to ensure
that an optimal solution is global. Non-convex problems have a tendency to settle into local optima
meaning that solutions must be assessed by starting the optimization from dierent starting points.
If the problem converges towards the same optimum from dierent starting points, the solution is
reasonably assumed to be global (or a strong local minima).

Chapter 6
Conclusion
A derivation of the source-doublet boundary integral equations is presented which are derived on
basis of the Laplace equation. By using a combination of sources and doublets, a mathematically
concise and numerical stable 2D panel method was obtained that is well-suited for shape optimiza-
tion. This allows to perform rapid assessments of the aerodynamics ow which is ideal in the early
design phase. The basic source-doublet code is validated against the Van de Vooren airfoil which
showed excellent agreements.
The setup of the shape optimization routine has been conducted on basis of the developed
source-doublet method. Dierent parametrization strategies have been addressed which includes a
non-parametric nodal approach, a B-spline approach and a free-form deformation (FFD) approach
where the latter is a geometric technique used in computer graphics. All of these method were
implemented in MATLAB where the rst two methods are used in the shape optimization routine
whereas the FFD method is used to gain insight into the modelling technique. For the nodal
approach, dierent strategies have been discussed in order to avoid nodal clustering; a problem
that typically occur when the model is insuciently constrained. By utilizing an approach where
free vertical movements are allowed except at the trailing edge and where the horizontal node
locations are determined by scaling the movements of the trailing edge, a suciently constrained
model was obtained. The same approach was employed to the control vertices of the parametric
B-spline parametrization.
Utilizing the B-spline, the ability of replicating modern airfoils and
discretization of panels along the spline was investigated.
In order to perform gradient based optimization, a sensitivity analysis was conducted on basis
of the discrete adjoint method. This includes the determination of the partial derivatives of the
discretized aerodynamic state equations which is a slightly cumbersome task. Unlike grid-based
methods, where elements typically can be evaluated individually, the singularity methods connects
all points in the domain.
Hence, each panel contribute to the partial derivatives of the state
equations wrt. nodal coordinates where the inuence depends on the location and connectivity of
the panels.
In many cases when considering the nodal approach, the free-moving nodes in the vertical
direction cannot hinder that the optimizer nds numerical aws in the model. Hence, some sort
of regularization must be imposed which is why a density lter approach and a nodal average
approach is presented to overcome this issue; the rst is based on geometric distances of the initial
airfoil whereas the latter is based on a weighted average of neighbouring nodes. Both method were
tested in the optimization routine.
Also, a two-way coupled boundary layer model has been elaborated and validated to experi-
mental data. Since it was not managed to develop the boundary layer model within the time frame
of this project, the optimization was performed by using XFOIL for the ow analysis.
The following aerodynamic problems are treated:
The Inverse Design Problem: concerns the replication of a prescribed target pressure prole
for which the airfoil geometry is sought. This was successfully conducted when using the nodal
approach with and without regularization as well as B-spline representation.
Since the target
pressure coecients are assigned to the panel discretization, a notch was formed at the front edge
when targeting NACA airfoils at 8 deg. AoA using the nodal approach. Therefore, in order to
preserve physical validity of the model, the regularization techniques were employed. Both the
density and the average nodal lter successfully smoothed the geometry at the front edge. The


Use the Prandlt-Glauert equations (2.6) and (2.7) to optimize the performance of 2D (and/or

This approach, however, is not valid for fully 3D ows which may occur at the wing root and
wing tip regions. Therefore, a fully 3D boundary method is suggested which is presented in

The span updates are shown in Figure A.9 for a symmetric and cambered airfoil where only the
bounds within the airfoil are updated wrt. to the camber line. The advantage of this approach
occurs when the proles have narrowed in to have the minimum allowable thickness. Then, they

bounds. Therefore, a counter is introduced that ensures that a minimum optimization cycles are
performed before updating.
The second strategy is shown in Figure A.10 where both sides of the span are updated to have
equal lengths. Unlike, the previous approach a counter is not strictly necessary since the design

airfoils at the minimum thickness since the spans may have almost zero length and, thus, requires
many iterations to generate the desired thickness.

A.9.1
The Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA)
The MMA algorithm is based on the rst-order Taylor series expansion of the objective and con-
straint functions. By this method, the optimization problem is divided into simpler explicit convex
subproblems which have independent solutions. By generating these subproblems, the implicit non-
linear optimization problem can be approximated locally, thus, allowing a dual approach to solve
it. This provides a lower bound to the solution of the minimization problem. The design variables
are surrounded by lower and upper asymptotes which are updated in every iteration of optimiza-
tion routine. If the process tends to oscillate, the algorithm is stabilized by moving the asymptotes
towards the iteration point x. Conversely, if the process is slow, it can be relaxed by moving the
asymptotes away from the current point of iteration. This ensure a robust stability performance
but also provides the disadvantage that the exact minimum of the optimization problem cannot
be reached. By approaching the exact minimum, the step size gets smaller which means that
the solutions of the sub-problems must be non-exact. The solution process is interrupted when a
certain convergence criteria is met. A more in-depth explanation of the MMA routine is given in
Svanberg (2007) and Thierauf (1988).
The MMA optimizer has become popular for several reasons:

It is capable of handling all kinds of constraints where the only requirement is the derivatives
of the constraint function with respect to the design variables. These can be calculated either
numerically or analytically.

The method is exible and capable of handling general non-linear optimization problems.

It is generally very stable and capable of providing sequences of improved feasible solutions.

It provides excellent convergence rates for advanced problems with multiple constraints.

B.2.3
Wake Requirements
In order to satisfy the Kutta condition, a wake sheet must be imposed to create zero vorticity along
the trailing edge. The wake either consist of vortex or doublet sheets which are shed downstream
from the trailing edge in a certain formation.
The geometry of the wake is determined such that it does not support uid loads which requires
wake boundary elements to be parallel to the local streamlines.
Using the Kutta-Joukowsky
theorem, the lifting force is proportional to the circulation (f = ρv×γ) and therefore the following
condition must apply for the wake panels:
v × γW = 0
(B.18)
v × ∇µW = 0
(B.19)
This is typically obtained by using iterative methods.
