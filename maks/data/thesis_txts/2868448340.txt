Abstract
The terahertz (THz) absorption spectrum of water is predicted using properties from molecular
dynamics (MD) simulations to study the vibrational dynamics of hydrogen bonded networks. Com-
puted spectral intensities relate to the autocorrelation of the system dipole moment in the absence
of external perturbation. Two methods of calculating the autocorrelation are compared. The first
method directly interprets the autocorrelation as an ensemble average and finds this over segments
of a single trajectory. The second utilizes the Weiner-Khinchin theorem to perform the calculation
in the frequency domain. The second method leads to a considerable decrease in computation time
and improves the signal-to-noise ratio of the spectra. Vibrational intensities depend on the Fourier
transform of the autocorrelation. This is computed using a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) with a
Hann window function to reduce spectral leakage.
The THz spectrum of water features a broad peak at 600 cm−1 related to librational motion and
a peak at 200 cm−1 related to vibrations along hydrogen bonds. Previous studies using forcefield-
based MD simulations have proven it a challenge to correctly predict the intensity of the peak at 200
cm−1. More recent methods of MD simulations, such as ab initio MD (AIMD) and the AMOEBA
polarizable model, have yielded results that are in good agreement with experimental spectra. The
dipole moments are usually computed as part of the simulation process, whereas in this project, the
system dipole moments are calculated for each frame post-simulation. These calculations are based
on trajectories from MD simulations that use non-polarizable flexible forcefield models.
Three models for calculating the system dipole moments are compared for a pure water sys-
tem. One is a simple point charge model, and the second and third are polarizable models. The
polarizable models decompose the correlation function into permanent dipole, induced dipole, and
cross-correlation terms. The simple point-charge model fails to predict the peak at 200 cm−1, which
has also been the case in previous studies. Using a polarizable model with standard parameters
for point charges and polarizabilities yields a negligible increase in the intensities of the 200 cm−1
peak. Including second-order atomic multipoles in the polarizable description leads to a considerable
improvement of the relative intensities in the THz region but does so at an increased computational
cost. In all three cases, the peak at 200 cm−1 is underestimated compared to experimental spec-
tra. These results are qualitatively similar to studies in which the spectra are predicted using a
polarizable MD simulation. This indicates that the frequencies associated with vibrations in the
hydrogen bond network are present, even in a non-polarizable MD simulation, and can be found
post-simulation using a polarizable description of the dipole moments.
The lower region of the THz absorption spectrum of water is sensitive to the presence of solutes.
Previous studies have shown a linear decrease in intensity of the sub 200 cm−1 region with glucose
concentrations in a dissolved sample. To explore this trend, spectra from pure water and solvated
glucose systems are compared. Initial results show a slight decrease in intensity, but due to the level
of spectral noise, further study is needed to conclude the effects of solutes on the spectra.

Introduction
The study of solvents as active participants in chemical processes is referred to as solvation science [2].
Water is a fascinating solvent due to its affinity for forming extensive hydrogen bond networks with
itself and other molecules. Hydrogen bond networks in water are thought to affect the structure,
stability, and dynamics of solvated proteins [3–6], and likewise of other biomolecules [6–8]. This
project focuses on predicting vibrational spectra from molecular dynamics (MD) simulation to un-
ravel the dynamics of the hydrogen bond networks in water. Studying these dynamics is an essential
step towards a deeper understanding of biochemistry and solvent processes in general.
Water has been the subject of countless experimental studies, including infrared (IR) spec-
troscopy [9,10], Raman spectroscopy [10], and neutron scattering [11]. IR absorption spectroscopy
is a widely used method for experimentally studying the vibrational dynamics of molecules. The ab-
sorption of electromagnetic radiation at IR frequencies is linked to excitations into higher vibrational
states. However, this occurs only for vibrations that affect the molecular dipole moment [12]. There
are three IR-active normal mode vibrations in the water molecule: the asymmetric and symmetric
stretching modes along the O-H bond, with frequencies around 3400 cm−1 and the bending mode
around 1650 cm−1 [6].
Hydrogen bonded water networks exhibit additional vibrational resonances in the far IR (FIR)
range of 30 to 200 cm−1 which corresponds to approximately 1 to 6 terahertz (THz). THz ab-
sorption spectroscopy can measure fluctuations in molecular dipoles at frequencies in this terahertz
gap, which correspond to processes on the picosecond (ps) timescale. For water, these processes
include hydrogen bond rearrangement as well as translational and rotational relaxation within the
hydrogen bond network [2]. This makes THz absorption spectroscopy an excellent tool for probing
the dynamics of the hydrogen bond network.
The vibrational spectrum of pure water has been measured experimentally by Hans R. Zels-
mann in search of the refractive index n(ω) and the absorption index k(ω) [9]. These results are
recreated from tabulated data at different temperatures T and shown in Figure 1. Figure 1a shows
the refractive index, which is the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to that in the specific
medium [13]. Figure 1b shows the absorption cross-section α(ω), which is the ratio of energy ab-
sorbed by a system to the energy produced by an electromagnetic field. As α(ω) depends on the
refractive index, it is common practice to represent the THz spectrum by α(ω)n(ω), such that n(ω)
is canceled out.

The THz spectrum of water features two maxima at around 200 cm−1 and 600 cm−1, respectively.
The peak at 600 cm−1 is broad and related to librational motion, that is, hindered rotational motion.
The peak at 200 cm−1 is related to hindered translational motion in the hydrogen bond network,
corresponding to vibrations along a hydrogen bond [14]. Multiple studies have shown that the in-
tensity of this peak and the region below depends heavily on the presence of solutes [15–17]. One
such study has found a near-linear decay in the absorption cross-section at 0.5 THz with increasing
blood-glucose concentrations [17].
Theoretical prediction of vibrational spectra can generally be divided into static and dynamic
approaches. Static methods involve quantum mechanical calculations of the potential energy in a
geometry-optimized system. Compared to dynamic approaches, the main challenge of static ap-
proaches is the fast scaling of computational costs with system size. [18]. Prediction of the THz
spectrum of water has previously been attempted using density functional theory (DFT) calcula-
tions in a static system.
Their results improved, with larger cluster sizes, but were all in poor
agreement with experimental spectra [19].
This project focuses on the dynamic approach to predict the vibrational spectrum of water
in the THz region from classical forcefield-based Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations. Classi-
cal molecular dynamics scales more favorably with system size than quantum-mechanical methods,
allowing the study of much larger systems. Previous forcefield-based MD simulation studies have

moments of each frame post-simulation. To investigate this, the system dipole moments of a pure
water system are calculated using three different models of increasing complexity. Additionally, the
spectrum of a solvated glucose system is predicted and compared to that of pure water to illustrate
the effect of solutes in the terahertz gap of the vibrational spectrum.

electromagnetic field. The probability of the system absorbing a photon with a frequency ω is highest

3
Molecular Dynamics
The MD simulations in this project are primarily based on previous work in the TCBC group [15].
This chapter briefly summarizes the utilized forcefield models and the simulation procedure.
3.1
Forcefield Models
Two forcefield models from the single point charge (SPC) family are used for simulating water
molecules: the extended (SPCE) and flexible water (SPC/FW) models. The SPC models feature
single point charges centered at each atom with qO = −0.82 e and qH = −qO/2 = 0.41 e. In SPCE,
these have been reparametrized to qO = −0.8476 e and qH = 0.4238 e to better represent the dipole
moment in bulk water.
In the SPC/FW model, harmonic potentials account for the stretching
and bending of intermolecular bonds [15]. The SPCE model is rigid per default but has also been
reparametrized for flexible O-H bonds, meaning the two models are similar but are parametrized
differently.
Simulations of glucose in water utilize the GROMOS-56A-Carbo forcefield. This model is for-
mulated to account for both α-D-glucopyranose and β-D-glucopyranose. Both anomers are present in
solvated glucose, and since they differ by the torsion angle of the first carbon hydroxyl group, the dis-
tinction is essential to represent hydrogen bonding with surrounding water molecules accurately [15].
3.2
Simulation Procedure
MD simulations used in this project were performed using GROMACS 2023.1, and the process is
briefly summarized in this section. A simulation box is created either empty or with glucose. The
box dimensions are defined, and water molecules are distributed using the gmx solvate function.
The energy in the system is minimized using the steepest descent integrator. The first equilibration
step is initialized with velocities at 298.15 K in the NVT ensemble and runs for 100 ps utilizing a
Nose-Hoover thermostat to preserve the temperature. In the NPT ensemble, the system is further
equilibrated again for 100 ps utilizing the Nose-Hoover thermostat and a C-rescale barostat to
preserve the pressure.
The final equilibration is run in the NVT ensemble and uses the same
parameters as the first [15]. All simulations are run with a refresh interval of 0.5 femtoseconds (fs).
A total of four MD simulations have been utilized in this project, out of which the second and third
were run specifically for this project. The simulations will be referred to using their abbreviations
in the remainder of the thesis.
• MD1 is a simulation of pure water using the SPC/FW water model. The system contains 510
molecules in a quadratic simulation box with a side length of 46.604 Bohr. The production
run uses a sampling interval of 4 fs for 500000 frames, corresponding to 250 ps.

both to the combined length of the two minus one. This ensures that the resulting function decays
to zero at both limits. The array of the system dipole moment autocorrelation function thus has a
length of 2M −1, where M is the length of the trajectory.
4.5
Window Functions
The continuous Fourier transform features an integral from −∞to +∞. In the discrete case, the
sum is calculated within a defined window. A rectangular window is defined by an upper and a
lower limit, where the window function has a value of one within the limits and zero outside [44].
To find the exact wave frequencies and amplitudes in a DFT, one must choose a sampling window
within which all signal frequencies are present with an integer number of periods. Frequencies with
a non-integer number of periods have a discontinuity between the start and end of the windowed
signal. This leads to frequencies that spill into other frequency bins, a phenomenon known as spec-
tral leakage. Since it is implausible for any physical signal to have a region with only whole-integer
constituents, spectral leakage is unavoidable.
Spectral leakage can be reduced by applying a non-rectangular window function before trans-
forming. Most non-rectangular window functions are characterized by a smooth convergence towards
zero or near-zero values at each window limit, which reduces the discontinuities at the boundary and,
thereby, the spectral leakage [45]. The dipole autocorrelation and lineshape functions are calculated
and shown using several well-known window functions in Figure 3. Figure 3a shows the normalized
autocorrelation of the dipole moment CMM(t) for a segment length of 5000 corresponding to 20 ps.
In addition, it shows several window functions and the point-wise multiplication products of the
autocorrelation with each of these windows.
In the rectangular window, the IR spectrum suffers from spectral leakage, as seen in Figure 3b.
The spectral leakage becomes dominant at higher frequencies of the lineshape due to the squared
dependence on ω. The Hamming window differs from the rest by converging at a non-zero value,
and in this case, it results in no noticeable reduction of the spectral leakage. The Barlett window

rotation of the system dipole moment, which is considerably slower than the rotation of individual
molecules, as it has to propagate through the system. The time-averaged dipole moment of the
system will thus cancel out over time, as expected for a system with no net charge.

6
Conclusion and Outlook
In summary, this thesis covers the recreation of THz spectra from MD simulations of water systems.
The MD trajectories are used to calculate the system dipole moments frame by frame, from which
the autocorrelation is found. The total dipole moment autocorrelation is calculated directly and
from a decomposition of the permanent, induced, and cross-correlation contributions. Additionally,
a method using the dot-product over segments of a single trajectory is compared to one based on
the Wiener-Kinchin theorem. The second method is faster and yields an autocorrelation function
that is even across time, which leads to a real-valued spectrum after the Fourier transform. The
amplitudes in the even spectrum are reduced less by applying a non-rectangular window function,
and the resulting spectrum has higher intensities, leading to a better signal-to-noise ratio.
The system dipole moments are calculated with three different models: a simple point-charge
model, a standard parameter polarizable model using only the zeroth-order electric moments, and a
polarizable model expanded to second-order multipoles. The point-charge model fails to recreate the
peak at 200 cm−1, corresponding to hindered translation in the hydrogen bond network of water.
The standard parameter polarizable model shows a small peak around 200 cm−1 from the induced
dipole autocorrelation but at intensities that are too small to affect the spectrum for the total dipole
moment. Including a second-order multipole expansion leads to a more pronounced peak, which
compares well to the results of previous studies using polarizable forcefield MD. Thus, although the
polarization was not included in the MD, the vibrational frequencies associated with the fluctuations
in the hydrogen bond network are present and can be extracted post-simulation using a model of
sufficient complexity. Initial results for the solvated glucose system indicated that a longer trajectory
than 40 ps is needed to improve the signal-to-noise ratio.
More advanced MD simulations, such as the AMOEBA polarizable model or AIMD, predict
the peak at 200 cm−1 to better agreement with experiments. These advanced methods may be
advantageous to the methods presented in this project. However, the multipole expansion method
described in this project is a fitting alternative if only nonpolarizable MD simulations are available.
Compared to static approach methods, these methods also have favorable scaling with the system
size, making them more suitable when a large system is desired.
The method of computing the permanent and induced dipole moment from multipole expansion
parameters relies heavily on the quality of these parameters, as also seen in the quality difference
between the two polarizable models. In this project, the multipole expansion parameters are found
for each water molecule in vacuum, meaning only the molecular geometries affect the parameters.
An approach to potentially improve the parameterization is to use molecular fractionation with
conjugate caps (MFCC), where each molecule is extracted alongside nearby molecules within a
defined cut-off to calculate the multipole expansion parameters in the presence of these molecules.
This may lead to a more accurate description of the electrostatic potential and, thereby, more
pronounced peaks in the spectrum.
Inclusion of MFCC will increase the computational cost of the parametrization, which is already

the most time-consuming step of this workflow. Since the geometries of all water molecules are
relatively similar, and for this project, over 30 million water molecules have been parametrized, it

In conclusion, the multipole expansion polarizable model for calculating the system dipole

that the vibrations of the hydrogen bonds are present, even in a non-polarizable MD simulation.
Results from studies using the AMOEBA model show better agreement with experimental results
than found in this project, indicating that calculating the dipole moments in each step of the MD
leads to an improvement in the description of the dynamics.
