Abstract 
Biopharmaceuticals like insulin or vaccines are unsuitable for oral delivery due to stomach 
degradation or first-pass metabolism. Transdermal delivery bypasses these barriers but 
struggles with limited skin penetration. Subcutaneous injections remain the main solution but 
are invasive and cause pain and poor adherence. Microneedles offer a minimally invasive 
alternative, with hydrogel microneedles being biocompatible and tunable for drug release and 
penetration. However, achieving both sufficient strength and controlled release with hydrogels 
remains challenging.  
This study investigates the development of hydrogel microneedles based on GelMA combined 
with polyacrylamide, aiming to enhance swelling capacity and drug release without 
compromising mechanical integrity. A series of hydrogel formulations with GelMA 
concentrations at 20%, 30% and 40% w/v, was prepared and tested for swelling behavior, 
mechanical strength, and drug release performance. Results showed that incorporating 
polyacrylamide increased the swelling capacity compared to pure GelMA with up to 20%. 
However, the sample releasing the most drug within a timeframe of 8 hours was pure 40% 
GelMA, releasing 67,2% of the total amount of drug. The component with the lowest drug 
release was a 20% GelMA with 2:1 (polyacrylamide:GelMA) which only released 36,8% of 
the drug. Higher swelling did therefore not translate into improved drug release. In all the cases 
drug release decreased, potentially due to tighter polymer network structures or pore closure 
during swelling. The microneedle molds were fabricated with a needle height of 800 µm, a 
width of 300 µm and a density of 225 needle / cm2, combining 3D-printed positive molds, 
PDMS casting and CO₂ laser ablation. While initial vacuum casting methods and 3D printing 
proved unsuccessful for needle creation, centrifugal filling combined with CO₂ laser-fabricated 
needles enabled the production of sharp, intact microneedle arrays with needle tips with a 
diameter of 25 µm. Penetration testing in 2.65% w/v agarose gel demonstrated that the 
microneedles with a GelMA content of 20% w/v could withstand a force of 32N without any 
visible deformation or failure. However, agarose was found to inadequately mimic the 
mechanical resistance of human skin, necessitating alternative testing methods. In conclusion, 
GelMA/polyacrylamide hydrogels show promise for microneedle fabrication, offering 
enhanced swelling and sufficient mechanical strength. Nevertheless, further optimization is 
required to improve drug release characteristics and validate performance in biologically 
relevant skin models.

2 Introduction 
Biopharmaceuticals such as insulin or vaccines are generally not suitable for oral drug delivery 
due to degradation in the stomach or first-pass metabolism1,2. However, efficient drug delivery 
systems are crucial for ensuring therapeutic effectiveness while minimizing side effects and 
patient discomfort. Traditional methods such as subcutaneous injections are very effective in 
administering such therapeutics. However, these injections are invasive and often associated 
with pain and discomfort during management of conditions like diabetes, which limits patient 
adherence3,4. Other transdermal approaches, such as creams, gels, and liquid sprays, offer non-
invasive and more comfortable alternatives 5. Nevertheless, these are limited by the skin barrier, 
which restricts the delivery of many medicaments, including biopharmaceuticals such as 
insulin. Microneedles have emerged as a promising solution to crossing this barrier, enabling 
the delivery of a broader range of drugs with minimal discomfort in a non-invasive way6. 
Various types of microneedles have been developed, each with advantages and limitations 
related to mechanical strength, drug loading, and fabrication complexity6. Some microneedle 
types exhibit low drug loading efficiency while others are not either biocompatible, produce 
sharp waste or may cause tissue trauma6. However, hydrogel-based microneedles represent an 
attractive alternative to the other needle types due to their great biocompatibility, high drug 
loading capacity and tunable characteristics6. Gelatin Methacryloyl (GelMA), a semi-synthetic 
polymer derived from natural gelatine7, has been widely studied for microneedle applications 
due to its very good biocompatibility, its swelling properties and its tunable mechanical 
characteristics for both drug delivery and interstitial fluid sensing purposes7. However, 
balancing its properties to achieve either increased mechanical strength for skin penetration or 
allow adequate drug release for drug delivery poses a challenge, as drug diffusion is limited 
when its strength is increased8. Polyacrylamide (pAAm) based hydrogels, however, are known 
for their high mechanical strength and significant swelling capacity in biofabrication contexts9, 
though they have not yet been explored for microneedle fabrication. Combining GelMA with 
pAAm may offer a way to achieve microneedles that are both mechanically robust and capable 
of effective drug release.

This thesis is part of a larger research project investigating a multifunctional microneedle 
device for application in glucose monitoring and drug delivery in diabetes management. While 
the overarching project includes both sensing and therapeutic functionalities, this thesis focuses 
specifically on developing hydrogel microneedles for drug delivery purposes. 
Based on this context, the research question for this thesis is: 
“How can GelMA/pAAm microneedles be designed and fabricated for transdermal drug 
delivery applications?” 
To address this question, the aims of this thesis are to: 
1. Develop hydrogel formulations based on GelMA and polyacrylamide suitable for 
microneedle fabrication. 
2. Design and fabricate microneedle molds using CAD and high-resolution 3D printing. 
3. Produce GelMA/pAAm microneedle arrays and evaluate their mechanical strength for 
skin penetration. 
4. Characterize the drug release performance of the GelMA/pAAm microneedles to assess 
their potential for transdermal delivery. 
And furthermore, it is hypothesized that:  
“By adding pAAm to GelMA, the hydrogel is expected to show increased swelling and drug 
release, while maintaining mechanical strength needed for skin penetration.”. 
 
3 Theory 
3.1 Transdermal Drug Delivery 
Drug delivery refers to the methods and technologies used to transport pharmaceutical 
compounds to their site of action in the body10. Effective delivery systems are crucial for 
ensuring therapeutic efficacy, reducing side effects, and improving patient compliance11. 
The skin consists mainly of three layers. The outermost layer is the stratum corneum, which is 
approximately 10–20 µm thick and composed of densely packed dead skin cells (keratinocytes) 
and can withstand a piercing pressure for up to 3,18MPa12. This layer functions as a strong 
barrier, protecting the body from environmental factors on the outside13.  Beneath the stratum

corneum lie the living layers of the epidermis, which have a thickness of 50–200 µm, depending 
on the location on the body. The stratum corneum and the underlying viable layers are 
collectively referred to as the epidermis13. Below the epidermis is the dermis, which is 0.6–3 
mm thick, depending on the anatomical site. The dermis contains blood vessels, interstitial 
fluid and networks of nerves13,14, making it a suitable destination for drug delivery.

In contrast, subcutaneous injections involve needles penetrating through the dermis into the 
underlying subcutaneous tissue 3. While this method is effective for delivering larger molecules 
and achieving systemic effects, it is invasive and often associated with pain or discomfort3. 
Furthermore, the subcutaneous tissue contains fewer capillaries compared to the dermis 15, 
potentially reducing the efficiency of drug delivery. In addition, the depot effect associated 
with subcutaneous injections, as seen with insulin, can contribute to variability in drug 
absorption 16.  
Traditional topical applications such as creams, gels or liquid sprays, however, are limited to 
small, lipophilic molecules with low molecular weights5,17. These drugs can passively diffuse 
through the stratum corneum due to their low size and chemical compositions. To expand the 
potential of transdermal drug delivery, various strategies have been developed, including 
chemical enhancers, iontophoresis, and laser-assisted techniques5. While these methods offer 
certain advantages, including increased delivery of small molecules, they have generally shown 
little clinical impact on the delivery of macromolecules due to safety concerns and limited skin 
permeability5.

Over the past decades, microneedle (MN) systems have emerged as a promising technology 
for minimally invasive drug delivery. They work by creating tiny channels in the stratum 
corneum, the outermost layer of the skin, in a way that causes minimal discomfort. These 
microchannels enable hydrophilic and macromolecular substances, such as insulin, peptides, 
and vaccines to penetrate into the viable epidermis or dermis, effectively bypassing the skin’s 
primary barrier and allowing for controlled delivery with limited pain or irritation6,18. 
3.2 Microneedles 
MNs are typically divided into five major categories based on structure and mechanism of drug 
delivery. Each class offers unique features in terms of drug release, mechanical strength, 
fabrication complexity, and biocompatibility6,18.  
Solid MNs are mainly composed of metals, silicon, and polymers19. They function by piercing 
microholes in the stratum corneum, and after creating these micropores, drugs are applied 
topically to facilitate diffusion through the channels20. They offer advantages such as 
mechanical robustness and precise skin penetration. However, they can cause tissue trauma, 
produce sharp solid waste, and result in uncontrollable drug doses21.  
Coated MNs are solid MNs whose surfaces are coated with small drugs or large biomolecules 
applied as solutions or dispersions22. Once inserted into the skin, the coating dissolves and 
enabling rapid drug release, after which the bare microneedles are removed22. Their advantage 
lies in the capacity to deliver various therapeutic agents efficiently23. However, the limited 
surface area available for coating restricts the amount of drug that can be loaded, with the exact 
capacity depending on factors such as the coating method, microneedle size, and thickness of 
the coating layer24.

Dissolving MNs are made from biocompatible, water-soluble materials like sugars and 
polymers. They dissolve in the skin after insertion and releases their drugs from the needle 
matrix. Advantages include no sharp waste, simple use, broad drug types, and low cost. 
However, they often have lower mechanical strength than solid microneedles, and penetration, 
dissolution, and drug loading depend on materials and fabrication methods25. 
Hollow MNs are tiny, hollow needles that can inject fluids through the skin painlessly. They’re 
usually made from metals, silicon, glass, ceramics, or polymers, chosen for strength and 
biocompatibility. They offer precise, minimally invasive drug or vaccine delivery and can 
handle larger doses than solid microneedles. However, they have drawbacks: the needle bores 
can clog with skin tissue, production is complex and costly, they require pumps or syringes for 
fluid flow, and fragile materials like glass or ceramics can break easily26. 
Hydrogel MNs (HMNs) are made from swellable polymers like GelMA, Polyvinyl alcohol 
(PVA) and Poly(ethylene glycol) diacrylate (PEGDA). They are biocompatible, biodegradable, 
and with tunable mechanical properties. However, out of all the polymers mentioned above, 
GelMA comes with the best biocompatibility and mechanical tunability27. Upon insertion, the 
HMN swell by absorbing fluid, enabling controlled drug delivery or passive extraction of ISF 
for diagnostics27. And compared to the other microneedle types, HMNs offer advantages such 
as enhanced drug loading capacity, tunable drug release, and the unique ability to extract ISF 
for sensing purposes and controlled drug release27,28. However, challenges remain, including

3.3 GelMA Hydrogel Microneedles  
Gelatin Methacryloyl (GelMA) is a semi-synthetic biomaterial derived from gelatin, a product 
of collagen. It has become a prominent candidate in biomedical engineering, particularly for 
microneedle applications due to its biocompatibility and tunable mechanical properties7.  
GelMA can be crosslinked using ultraviolet (UV) light in the presence of a photoinitiator to 
form a covalently bonded hydrogel network29. The material contains methacryloyl functional 
groups, introduced during synthesis, that crosslinks and enable the formation of stable three-
dimensional networks upon light exposure30.  
The synthesis of GelMA typically begins by dissolving either type A or type B gelatin30,31 in 
phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) at pH 7.4 and approximately 50 °C. Methacrylic anhydride 
(MAA) is then added to react with the primary amine groups of gelatin via methacrylation. The 
reaction is subsequently stopped by dilution with PBS, after which the solution is dialyzed to 
remove unreacted reagents and lyophilized to yield dry GelMA30. For hydrogel formation, the 
lyophilized GelMA is redissolved in an aqueous solution at a concentration ranging between 
10-25% w/v to ensure sufficient strength for skin penetration27. Upon addition of a 
photoinitiator and exposure to UV light (typically 365 nm or 405 nm31), GelMA undergoes 
covalent crosslinking to form a solid 3D hydrogel matrix30.

gelatin in a PBS buffered solution to form GelMA, which is then purified and dried through dialysis and lyophilization (not in 
the figure). Then it is dissolved again with water or PBS, and then a photoinitiator is added to the solution. Then GelMA 
monomers are crosslinked into 3D polymer networks through UV exposure. The right-most squares illustrate polymer

The main parameters contributing to GelMA’s mechanical properties are the degree of 
crosslinking and the polymer concentration30. A higher crosslinking degree and increased 
polymer concentration results in a stiffer and mechanically stronger hydrogel due to the 
formation of a denser crosslinked network 30, but may lead to lower swelling27,32. One key 
factor controlling the crosslinking density is GelMA’s degree of substitution. The degree of 
substitution refers to the extent of methacrylation along the gelatin backbone, i.e., the density 
of methacryloyl side groups attached to each GelMA molecule30, and depends on the amount 
of methacrylic anhydride added during GelMA synthesis30. A higher degree of substitution 
means more methacryloyl side groups are available for crosslinking, which leads to a denser 
and mechanically stronger 3D hydrogel network30. But for the crosslinking to happen, a 
photoinitiator together with UV exposure is needed, as mentioned earlier.  
 
3.3.1 LAP 
Photoinitiators are molecules capable of generating reactive free radicals upon light exposure, 
enabling covalent crosslinking of methacryloyl groups in photoresponsive hydrogels such as 
GelMA. These free radicals initiate polymerisation reactions that convert the liquid precursor 
into a stable three-dimensional hydrogel network30. 
The two most widely used photoinitiators in GelMA-based systems are 2-hydroxy-1-[4-(2-
hydroxyethoxy)phenyl]-2-methyl-1-propanone (Irgacure 2959) and lithium phenyl-2,4,6-
trimethylbenzoylphosphinate (LAP)33,34. Irgacure 2959 is activated by UV light at 365 nm, but 
exhibits lower water solubility, faster GelMA degradation and relatively high cytotoxicity 
compared to LAP which is more water soluble, lesser cytotoxic at higher concentrations and 
initiates gelation faster34–36.  
In typical applications, LAP is used at concentrations between 0.05% and 0.5% (w/v) for 
hydrogels 37–39. Photocrosslinking is achieved by exposing the prepolymer solution to UV light 
at 365 nm or visible light at 405 nm, where one study used light doses between approximately 
80 and 850 mJ/cm² to crosslink the hydrogel. Here, the higher UV dosis maximized the strength 
of GelMA37. In general, LAP seems to be less cytotoxic if unreacted free radicals are limited, 
meaning that an excess of LAP might be toxic. One study showed that there was a threshold of 
0,5% w/v LAP for 10% w/v GelMA to prevent cytotoxicity40. Also, the concentration of LAP 
seems to play a role in the mechanical properties of hydrogels. Usually, it has been said that an

increasing amount of photoinitiator, (in this case Irgacure 2959) would create a stronger and 
more stiff hydrogel30 , however, according to a new study from Duymaz et al. 202541, this is 
not necessarily the case: A LAP concentration of 0,02% w/v seemed to create a stronger 
network than all superior concentrations until 0,5%. However, at 0,5% the hydrogel maximized 
strength. This applied for both 5- and 10% GelMA w/v concentrations. Their explanation for 
this was that rapid free radical formation initially led to the crosslinking of shorter polymer 
chains, resulting in a stiffer network. However, when crosslinking occurred too rapidly, the 
polymer network became heterogeneous due to premature termination, leading to the formation 
of “dangling ends”, where they refer to polymer chains only connected at one end without 
contributing to the mechanical integrity of the network. This could indicate that LAP has an 
optimum zone at a relatively low concentration where it, relative to its concentration performs 
at its best. However, it should be taken into consideration that the DS of the GelMA might also 
be a relevant factor in optimal LAP concentration determination30.  
However, even though increasing the crosslinking density of GelMA leads to a stronger and 
denser 3D network, this comes at a cost. As GelMA becomes mechanically stronger, its 
capacity to swell decreases27, and decreased swelling is correlated to a lower diffusion of drugs 
from the hydrogel matrix42. Therefore, while higher mechanical strength improves GelMA’s 
ability to penetrate the skin, it simultaneously reduces its effectiveness in delivering drugs, due 
to limited swelling which is considered to be a significant disadvantage for drug delivery 
applications. 
3.3.2 Acrylamide 
In literature, a combination of GelMA and acrylamide for microneedle fabrication purposes 
has been hard to find during this study. However, for biofabrication purposes, these two 
materials are often combined to facilitate swelling, mechanical strength, thermic stability (at 
37°C), and controlled degradation and -drug release32,43–45.   Acrylamide monomers are highly 
toxic and potentially carcinogenic compounds, that through free radical polymerization, such 
as photopolymerization46,47, forms extended polymer chains, which are subsequently 
crosslinked using N,N′-methylenebisacrylamide48, which is also a highly toxic component49. 
This crosslinking yields a stable, covalently bonded three-dimensional hydrogel network. Once 
fully polymerized, the material is considered non-toxic, chemically stable, and insoluble in 
water48. The resulting polyacrylamide-based hydrogel exhibits a highly interconnected and 
==PAGE:17==

porous internal architecture, which enables exceptionally high swelling capacities, reported to 
exceed 800% in water47. 
The component is in literature also combined with other hydrogels such as PEGDA46 to form 
double-network hydrogels with increased strength. Furthermore, Lisa et al. (2024) claims that 
GelMA can itself serve as a functional crosslinker when combined with acrylamide, 
eliminating the need for traditional crosslinkers such as N,N′-methylenebisacrylamide (MBA). 
By exploiting the methacrylate groups on GelMA, covalent crosslinking occurs during 
polymerization resulting in a stable, three-dimensional network9.  
To sum up, the mechanical properties of GelMA-based hydrogels depend on formulation 
parameters such as degree of methacrylation, polymer concentration, photoinitiator content, 
UV-exposure, and the use of co-monomers like acrylamide. In microneedle applications, high 
stiffness is required for skin penetration, while pore size and swelling determine drug loading 
capacity and diffusion rate. Tightly crosslinked networks provide higher stiffness and enable 
sustained release of small molecules. Loosely crosslinked, swollen networks allow bulk release 
of larger or hydrophilic drugs50. Material formulation must balance mechanical strength and 
diffusion properties to match the intended drug profile50,51.  
3.4 Needle design 
The design of hydrogel microneedles (HMNs) is essential to ensure sufficient mechanical 
strength for effective skin penetration and to ensure minimal discomfort for the user52,53. One 
important parameter is the needle length which should be long enough to reach the dermis, but 
also because longer needles increase the risk of pain:  According to one study, increasing the 
microneedle length from 480 μm to 1450 μm led to a sevenfold increase in reported pain, rising 
from 5% to 37% of the pain associated with a conventional hypodermic needle53. Reported 
lengths for HMNs commonly range from 500 μm to 800 μm54–57. 
The base diameter of the needles is also important, since thinner needles are more likely to 
break, while a wider base provides better mechanical strength. However, increasing the base 
diameter has not been found to increase pain during insertion 53. Reported base diameters 
generally range between 150 μm and 300 μm28,58,59 . 
The tip diameter affects how easily the needles can penetrate the skin. A smaller tip requires 
less force to pierce the skin. Römgens et al. reported that a tip radius of 5 μm required a force

3.5 Fabrication techniques  
After defining the microneedle geometry, the next step is the fabrication of the needles. For 
hydrogel microneedles made from GelMA, this process mainly involves creating molds with

the desired shape and filling them with the hydrogel prepolymer. Mold fabrication enables 
precise control of dimensions such as height and tip sharpness. This section describes the steps 
involved in mold fabrication, mold filling and demolding of the GelMA microneedles. 
 
3.5.1 Mold fabrication 
Several microfabrication and micromachining techniques are used to produce such molds, 
including lithography, micro-milling, electroforming, hot embossing, PDMS casting, additive 
manufacturing, and laser ablation6,62–64, each offering specific advantages and limitations 
depending on the desired geometry, resolution, material compatibility, production speed and -
costs.  
In many fabrication workflows, the process begins with the creation of a master structure, 
which defines the microneedle geometry to be replicated in the mold65–67. Photolithography is 
frequently employed to produce high-resolution master structures by patterning photosensitive 
materials on substrates such as silicon wafers, followed by etching processes68. This method 
offers excellent precision and fine feature definition, with needle tips reaching diameters as 
low as 500nm69, making it suitable for intricate microneedle geometries68. However, it requires 
cleanroom facilities and specialized equipment, leading to higher costs and longer production 
times, which may limit its use for rapid prototyping or low-volume production70. 
Micro-milling utilizes micro-scale cutting tools to machine molds into materials like metals or 
polymers62,71. It provides flexibility and is relatively cost-effective for custom geometries72. 
However, micro-milling typically achieves resolutions down to 10µm73 and may struggle with 
very sharp tips or highly detailed microneedle features as for lithography.  
Electroforming allows the production of durable metal molds by depositing metal onto a 
patterned master structure74. Electroformed molds offer excellent dimensional accuracy and 
surface quality with a precision of ±0,23 μm74, which is advantageous for hydrogel applications 
where smooth mold surfaces help prevent damage during demolding. The downside is that 
electroforming involves multiple processing steps and specialized equipment, making it time-
consuming and therefore expensive75. Furthermore, the molds lack flexibility, which may result 
in challenges during the demolding process of the microneedles.

Hot embossing transfers micro-scale patterns from a master into thermoplastic mold materials 
under heat and pressure76. It is suitable for producing molds with fine details and is efficient 
for larger production volumes76. However, it is limited to thermoplastic materials and requires 
a high-quality thermoplastic master for optimal replication76, making it less attractive in lab 
settings, when fabricating prototypes. 
PDMS casting is another widely used technique for fabricating microneedle molds, especially 
for applications involving hydrogels27,77. PDMS (polydimethylsiloxane) is poured over a 
master structure and cured for 70 °C for 2 hours to form an elastomeric negative mold that 
captures fine details with high fidelity27. Its flexibility and low surface energy potentially 
facilitate easy demolding of delicate hydrogel microneedles and reducing the risk of structural 
damage during demolding 78,79. Moreover, PDMS molds are biocompatible and transparent78, 
which can be advantageous during UV crosslinking and to ensure no contamination of the 
needles. However, due to the low surface energy of PDMS, it may be challenging to fully fill 
the mold cavities with hydrophilic hydrogels, as poor wetting on the hydrophobic PDMS 
surface can prevent complete filling in the needle tips79. Still, this method seem very sufficient 
for microneedle prototyping.  
Among newer approaches, additive manufacturing methods, such as stereolithography (SLA) 
3D printing, have become increasingly popular for fabricating microneedle molds for hydrogel 
applications80. Leading companies in this field include Formlabs, whose SLA printers build 
mold structures layer by layer, with a minimum layer thickness of 25 µm and a laser spot size 
ranging from 85 to 140 µm81. The process cures liquid photopolymer resin with a focused light 
source, enabling the creation of complex and precise geometries and offering rapid prototyping 
and design flexibility82. However, the laser resolution is limited by its spot size and layer 
thickness, which may lead to surface roughness and larger needle tip diameters83. Krieger et al. 
printed microneedles using a Formlabs Form 2 SLA 3D printer with a layer thickness of 25 µm 
and a laser spot size of 140 µm, achieving tip radii between 20 and 40 µm82, suggesting that 
the newer Formlabs Form 3 series, with a smaller laser spot size of 85 µm81, may potentially 
enable the fabrication of microneedles with lower tip radii than 20 and 40 µm, which makes it 
very suitable for microneedle fabrication and prototyping. 
CO₂ laser ablation, operating at a wavelength of 10.6 μm, enables the direct fabrication of 
microneedle molds in polymers like PDMS by locally vaporizing the material through focused 
thermal energy, allowing rapid and versatile prototyping without the need for traditional 
==PAGE:21==

lithographic processes84. It enables precise patterning in various materials and offers fast 
fabrication times, and the process can be tuned by adjusting parameters such as laser power, 
scanning speed, and focus position84,85. Higher laser power or lower scanning speed results in 
deeper ablation and thus taller microneedles85, and a lower focus position (with the focal point 
deeper inside the material) results in thinner and more V-shaped needle cavities, while a higher 
focus position (with the focal point closer to the surface) produces broader, more U-shaped 
cavities84. For this method, needletip diameters of 16-20 µm can be achieved84. However, 
potential thermal effects on surrounding material86,87  can be a limitation. But for prototyping, 
and to achieve fine needle tips, this method seems promising.   
To sum up, the choice of mold fabrication technique is strongly influenced by the specific 
requirements of hydrogel microneedle applications, including the need for high fidelity in 
microneedle geometry, smooth surface finishes to facilitate demolding, material compatibility 
with hydrogels, and production scalability, and lab accessibility. For this project, both micro-
milling, PDMS molding, 3D printing and laser ablation seem to be very effective fabrication 
methods for lab settings during prototyping as these methods have the potential to fabricate 
needles with tip-sizes below 20 µm in a flexible small-scaled lab setting. Lithography, hot 
embossing and electroforming might create even better results, but they seem to fit better at a 
larger scale after the prototyping steps.   
3.5.2 Mold Filling and Demolding 
Efficient mold filling and demolding are essential for producing hydrogel microneedles with 
precise geometries. Incomplete filling may result in defects such as truncated tips, voids, or 
surface irregularities, compromising microneedle performance88. Standard methods for 
hydrogel mold filling include vacuum-assisted filling and centrifugal filling65,88.   
Vacuum-assisted filling works by reducing air entrapment in micro-cavities, enabling the 
hydrogel solution to penetrate fine structures. This minimizes air bubbles, which are 
problematic in microneedle molds89. However, very viscous formulations or solutions with 
high surface tension may still hinder complete filling of the microcavities, potentially leading 
to air bubbles or incomplete microneedle formation90. 
Centrifugation uses radial forces to drive hydrogel solution into mold cavities, promoting rapid 
and uniform filling27,91. This technique can effectively “force” viscous polymer solutions into 
microcavities, overcoming surface tension and ensuring complete filling of intricate 
==PAGE:22==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
16 
 
microneedle molds89,92. Nevertheless, excessive centrifugal forces can induce shear-related 
defects potentially compromising microneedle geometry and mechanical integrity93. 
Hydrogel viscosity and polymerization rates of the prepolymer solution are critical for 
successful mold filling94,95. These parameters can be tuned via concentration, degree of 
methacrylation, light exposure, and temperature to facilitate the filling process96; however, a 
trade-off exists, as such adjustments also affect the final microneedle performance.  
As mentioned in 3.5.1 Native PDMS molds are hydrophobic and can hinder GelMA wetting, 
leading to incomplete filling of microneedle tips79. Oxygen plasma treatment introduces 
hydrophilic functional groups, temporarily improving wetting and cavity infiltration97. 
However, hydrophilicity diminishes over time due to surface reorganization, so it must be 
coated again from time to time97. 
To sum up the filling part, both methods might work well, depending on the material 
characteristics. However, vacuum filling might still be the best option for prototyping as it does 
not require advanced mold designs fitting a centrifuge, and multiple needles can be made 
simultaneously in the same vacuum chamber. After sufficient filling and polymerization of the 
hydrogel, demolding the microneedles might possess a challenge if the mold is solid98. Flexible 
molds such as PDMS seem as a better alternative, as they can be gently deformed to release 
the microneedles without applying excessive force27. The low surface energy of PDMS further 
facilitates demolding79.  
 
3.6 Needle testing 
Following the fabrication of HMNs, a range of characterization methods is required to ensure 
that the system meets the necessary requirements for drug delivery applications. For GelMA-
based HMNs, the most common analyses include testing of mechanical strength, swelling 
behavior, skin penetration ability, and drug release profiles8,99. 
Mechanical strength is critical to ensure that HMNs can penetrate the skin without deformation 
or fracture. One of the most frequently employed methods is compression testing, often with a 
texture analyzer, in which the entire microneedle array is pressed against a flat surface while 
measuring the force required to induce deformation or failure8,99. In addition to this, bulk 
compression tests are often performed on hydrogel materials to determine their overall

mechanical properties, such as elastic modulus and compressive strength, which highlights the 
performance of the microneedle materials100,101. The minimum force required for penetration 
through human stratum corneum is generally around 3,18 MPa12,102, which GelMA-based 
microneedles can typically achieve depending on formulation parameters such as stiffness, 
degree of substitution, and GelMA concentration: A study conducted by Zhu et al. (2020) 
reported that GelMA microneedles fabricated from 20% w/v GelMA with a degree of 
substitution (DS) of 0.71 and UV crosslinked using 1% w/v Irgacure 2959 achieved a 
compressive modulus of 5.8 MPa103.

Drug release testing is essential for evaluating how well the hydrogel performs regarding drug 
delivery. A common method for analyzing drug release is UV-Vis spectrophotometry, which 
measures how much light a substance absorbs at specific wavelengths111. The technique is well-
suited for drug detection due to its ability to measure light absorbance in clear solutions. 
However, it becomes challenging when the medium is turbid, for example due to aggregation 
or undissolved substances, as scattered light or reduced light transmission can affect 
measurement accuracy112.  Automated systems such as the MicroDISS Profiler are based on 
UV-Vis principles and enable real-time monitoring of drug concentration without manual 
sampling. This is particularly useful for studying drug release profiles113. 
 
3.7 Model drug 
The release profile of drugs from hydrogel-based microneedle systems depends not only on the 
surrounding medium and the composition of the hydrogel, but also on how the drug interacts 
with the hydrogel matrix. Macromolecules often experience difficulties diffusing out of 
densely crosslinked hydrogels, as they can become physically trapped within the network 
structure. In contrast, small molecules are generally able to diffuse more readily, even through 
tighter meshes, due to their lower molecular size114,115. However, the size of the drug is not the 
only factor controlling the drug release, because there is also a correlation between the drugs 
hydrofilicity and its release from a hydrogel. Qiao et al. tested four different drugs with varying 
hydrophilicity and found that the more hydrophilic a drug is, the faster and more linearly it’s 
released from the hydrogel, so the least soluble drug had the slowest release, and the most 
soluble drug had the highest release116. Another important feature to consider is the drug 
loading capacity of the hydrogel. An et al. found that regular GelMA is too hydrophilic to load 
hydrophobic drugs effectively, but by introducing hydrophobic side groups, they significantly 
increased the drug loading capacity for hydrophobic compounds like doxorubicin and 
rifampicin117. This suggests that matching the hydrophilicity of the drug and the hydrogel may 
be important for achieving high drug loading. 
As an example, insulin is a hydrophilic macromolecule. Its relatively large molecular size can 
hinder diffusion out of tightly crosslinked hydrogel networks, potentially resulting in sustained 
or limited release profiles. Significant swelling of the hydrogel matrix, however, can help 
mitigate this limitation by increasing mesh size and facilitating drug transport. Due to its 
==PAGE:25==

hydrophilic nature, insulin tends to migrate toward aqueous environments118, and its loading 
capacity in hydrogels generally improves as the hydrogel becomes more hydrophilic. Since 
acrylamide is more hydrophilic than GelMA, incorporating acrylamide into the hydrogel 
formulation may potentially enhance drug loading for hydrophilic compounds like insulin. 
 
3.8 Summary and Hypothesis 
The mechanical properties of GelMA hydrogels depend on formulation parameters such as the 
degree of methacrylation, polymer concentration, photoinitiator content, and UV exposure. For 
transdermal drug delivery, high mechanical strength of the hydrogel is required for skin 
penetration. However, as high mechanical strength correlates with lower swelling and drug 
release, pAAm is considered a good candidate to compensate for this, as it is more hydrophilic 
than GelMA, which could potentially counteract these negative effects by improving swelling 
and thus potentially enhancing drug delivery, while still maintaining sufficient strength for skin 
penetration. Therefore, it is hypothesized that:  
By adding polyacrylamide to GelMA, the hydrogel is expected to show increased swelling and 
drug release, while maintaining mechanical strength needed for skin penetration. 
And to address this hypothesis, GelMA and pAAm will be made in different combinations at 
20%, 30% and 40% w/v respectively, as these high concentrations might clearly clarify pAAms 
contributions to the hydrogels performance. However, to get an even better picture, pAAm will 
also be varied in different combinations. The photoinitiator concentration and DS will for this 
thesis be held constant to limit variables. The LAP concentration will for all samples be 1% 
w/v, as the concentrations of polymer in these hydrogels will exceed the 10% w/v GelMA 
mentioned in section 3.3.1. A suitable microneedle design for skin penetration has a needle 
height of 500–800 μm, a tip diameter of approximately 15 μm, a base width of 150–300 μm, 
and a density of 220–800 needles per cm² will be fabricated. This should ensure skin 
penetration with the force of a thumb without causing any pain. For the molding fabrication, 
micro-milling, PDMS molding, 3D printing, and laser ablation appear to be very effective 
methods for laboratory-scale prototyping, as these techniques have the potential to produce 
needle tips below 20 µm in a flexible, small-scale setting. However, 3D printing, potentially 
combined with PDMS casting will be the main approach for this project, as it allows very

4.1 Synthesis of Gelatin Methacrylate (GelMA) 
GelMA was synthesized via standard methacrylation procedures 30,119. For each batch of 
GelMA, 100g type A porcine skin gelatine from Sigma-Aldrich was dissolved in 1000mL 
milli-Q (MQ) water containing 2 tablets of dissolved phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) from 
gibco, at a pH=7,6 at 50 °C to a final concentration of 10% (w/v) gelatine under constant 
stirring for 1 hour at 400rpm.  
Then, 80mL (0.8 mL/g of gelatin) of MAA from Sigma-Aldrich was added dropwise with a 
pipette, 8x10mL, while stirring continuously.  The reaction proceeded for two hours at 60 °C, 
while the beaker was wrapped in aluminium foil to ensure homogenious heating and prevent 
the liquid from becoming too viscous for stirring. 
Then, 6 tablets of PBS were dissolved in 3L of MQ water that was preheated to 60 °C, and the 
GelMA:MAA solution was then diluted in this solution and mixed for 1 hour until the solution 
was considered well-mixed.  
The resulting mixture was dialyzed using Spectra/Por 12–14 kDa MWCO Standard RC Tubing 
for 5 days in stirred MQ water at 50 °C, with daily water changes to get rid of any unreacted 
MAA. In the dialysis tubes, 10% v/v air were trapped inside before closing them to enable 
floating and motion of the tubes while stirring.  The purified solution was then frozen at –20 °C 
for 24h and lyophilized for 7 days to obtain completely dry GelMA.

For hydrogel casting, both vacuum and centrifugation-based methods were tested. In the 
vacuum-assisted filling process, liquid GelMA prepolymer was pipetted into PDMS molds and 
placed in a vacuum chamber for 15 minutes121. In some later cases, pipette tips and molds were 
pre-warmed to 37 °C to reduce solution viscosity and promote better cavity infiltration. While 
this improved needle formation slightly, the vacuum method failed to reliably fill all 
microneedle cavities. 
To overcome these limitations, a centrifugation-based filling method was implemented. Molds 
were placed inside 25 mL Falcon tubes and elevated to the opening using custom 3D-printed 
spacers, as the ones illustrated in Figure 17. Both molds and pipette tips were pre-heated at 
37 °C for approximately 24 hours before use. A volume of 500 µL hydrogel was pipetted into 
each mold, more than required, to avoid underfilling and ensure consistent cavity filling across 
different formulations and viscosities. Excess hydrogel settled at the bottom of the Falcon tube 
during centrifugation. 
==PAGE:38==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
32 
 
The molds were centrifuged at 3500 rpm for 5 minutes at room temperature122. This method 
allowed the hydrogel to fully infiltrate the microneedle cavities, regardless of formulation 
viscosity, with minimal bubble formation. 
Immediately after centrifugation, Falcon tubes were placed upright in vertical holders, their 
caps removed, and the entire setup was transferred to a UV crosslinking chamber. Samples 
were irradiated for 10 minutes at 365 nm, receiving a total dose of 5000 × 100 µJ/cm². 
Following UV curing, each mold was cryo-frozen to –80 °C for 10 minutes123. This step was 
intended to reduce mechanical damage during demolding by increasing the strength of the 
hydrogel and minimizing adhesion to the PDMS. However, later trials showed that freezing 
was not necessary.

PDMS microneedle molds placed on top of the extenders inside the tubes. (C) Falcon tubes (without lids), with molds and 
hydrogels inserted, positioned vertically in a rack inside a UV chamber for crosslinking. 
Demolding was performed immediately after freezing. Each mold was handled individually to 
ensure consistency in timing, exposure, and post-processing. After demolding, the samples 
were transferred to a 4 °C refrigerator, allowing for slow thawing and reduced structural 
disruption within the hydrogel network. And again, for later trials these steps were skipped, 
and the needles were demolded gently, directly after UV crosslinking.  
 
4.3 Penetration test 
To evaluate the mechanical integrity and penetration capability of the fabricated microneedles, 
penetration tests were performed using synthetic agarose gel to simulate soft tissue-like 
resistance.  
==PAGE:39==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
33 
 
A 2.65% (w/v) agarose gel solution was prepared by dissolving 2,65g agarose powder in 
100mL MQ water at approximately 85°C under continuous stirring for 20min124. Microneedle 
types A3, B3, and C3 were selected for penetration testing based on results from prior swelling 
and compression studies. The hot agarose solution was poured into 3x 90 mm petri dishes and 
allowed to cool and solidify for 1 hour at room temperature, followed by 24 hours at 4 °C prior 
to testing. On the underside of each petri dish, four equally sized quadrants were marked using 
a permanent marker to divide the agarose gel. Each section was labeled with the specific 
microneedle patch assigned to that quadrant Figure 18. In total, nine patches were meant to be 
tested: three of each variant (A3, B3, and C3). Unfortunately, one of the C3s was lost during 
the process.   
Prior to the penetration test, each microneedle patch was imaged using a light microscope at 
10× magnification, capturing side views of the needle geometry. The texture analyzer was 
configured to move at a speed of 0.5 mm/s until a force of 32 N was reached125. The trigger 
force was set to 0.049 N. The same probe as used in previous compression tests was employed. 
Under these conditions, the microneedle patch was pressed entirely through the agarose gel 
and into contact with the bottom surface of the petri dish. In the finalized test protocol, the 
texture analyzer was operated in displacement control mode with a fixed penetration depth of 
1.8 mm. The trigger force was set to 0.049 N, and the probe speed was 0.5 mm/s. The same 
probe as used in previous tests was applied. The 1.8 mm depth was selected following 
preliminary tests with penetration depths of 1.0 mm, 1.5 mm, and 2.0 mm. All tests were 
conducted in a temperature-controlled chamber set to 37 °C. For each measurement, the 
microneedle patch was carefully positioned over the designated quadrant of the agarose gel. 
The probe was manually lowered to just above the patch without making contact. The test 
sequence was then initiated, and this procedure was repeated for all nine patches.

4.4 Drug release testing  
Drug release testing of the microneedle matrices was performed using a MicroDISS Profiler 
(Pion). FD4 (FITC-Dextran 4) was initially used as a model compound in early microneedle 
formulations. However, due to insufficient efficacy, it was excluded from further experiments.

Triplicates of A3, B3, C3, and A0 were prepared, each containing 1 mg ciprofloxacin (2 
mg/mL), for release into 10 mL of PBS dissolution medium within the MicroDISS vials.

for 8 hours, resulting in the system only capturing release data for the first 8 minutes. Although 
the system was promptly activated after 8 hours to record final concentrations, all intermediate

6 Discussion 
6.1 Freezedrying 
The freeze-drying process for GelMA often required up to seven days to achieve complete 
drying, which appears partly linked to pouring the solution into containers with a liquid height 
of 3–4 cm. Larger volumes seemed to increase the risk of leaving partially frozen, undried 
GelMA in the center, necessitating additional drying cycles. Such incomplete drying could 
potentially introduce measurement uncertainties, as small pockets of moisture or ice might go 
undetected during weighing. It is assumed that drying begins from the outer layers and 
gradually progresses inward. This suggests that a fully dried outer layer might insulate the 
interior and prolong the process. An alternative approach could involve using wider containers 
with a lower liquid height for the same total volume, potentially reducing the diffusion distance 
for water and shortening the drying time. However, this remains hypothetical and was not 
tested in the present work. 
==PAGE:58==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
52 
 
An incident occurred in which the vent valve on the freeze-dryer was left partly open, resulting 
in damage to the vacuum pump. Consequently, further drying had to be carried out on another 
freeze-dryer in a different building, which had a maximum capacity of only 0.6 L. This limited 
capacity added considerable challenges, especially because several users depended on the same 
equipment. Plans to analyze pore sizes in the freeze-dried hydrogels were ultimately abandoned 
after the prepared samples were damaged during overnight thawing. Such data could have been 
valuable for assessing the hydrogel’s internal structure and its potential influence on drug 
release.  
6.2 Degree of Substitution 
The DS of GelMA was not directly determined in this project. GelMA was synthesized 
according to an established laboratory protocol, as described in the Methods section. However, 
DS can vary and significantly impacts the extent of crosslinking achievable by GelMA. In 
future projects, it could be valuable to characterize and control the DS. For instance, it has been 
reported that gelatin A, 300 Bloom, 10% w/v, reacted with 0.74 mL MAA in PBS yields a DS 
of approximately 82% ± 5% was achieved [link]. Which corresponds very well to the protocol 
for this project, although 0.06 mL more methacrylic anhydride was added in this project. So 
it’s approximately the same, or perhaps slightly more. 
 
6.3 UV Exposure and Dosage Settings 
There was some uncertainty regarding the UV exposure parameters during hydrogel 
crosslinking. The equipment (UVP CL-1000 Ultraviolet Crosslinker) used displayed a unit of 
× 100 µJ/cm², which appears to indicate a total dose rather than intensity, since no time unit 
was specified. In the literature, UV exposure is typically described as power density (W/cm²) 
rather than energy dose (J/cm²), creating some confusion when interpreting these settings. It 
should be made clear that the device constantly measures the UV energy being delivered and 
automatically adjusts the exposure time to reach the total UV dose set127. While this might 
seem obvious to some users, it wasn’t clear at first and caused confusion when trying to control 
the crosslinking precisely. For example, setting the energy to 5000 × 100 µJ/cm² and then also 
entering a time of 10 minutes doesn’t mean the device will deliver that energy dose in that time. 
Instead, choosing the time mode cancels out the energy setting, so the device simply exposes 
the material for 10 minutes at the lamp’s fixed intensity, say 20 mW/cm²127. That would result 
in a total UV dose of 20 mW/cm² × 10 min × 60 s/min = 12,000 mJ/cm². Understanding this 
==PAGE:59==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
53 
 
difference might be important to avoid similar mistakes, and it is therefore considered as the 
best option to use the energy setting alone to achieve an accurate dose. Using only the time 
setting can result in an unknown UV dose, especially if it is considered that lamp aging might 
reduce output. This means that settings that work in one UV chamber might not deliver the 
same dose in another chamber, depending on how much the lamps have worn out. 
 
6.4 PDMS 
During fabrication of the PDMS mold for the swelling and compression tests, incomplete 
curing was observed after 24 hours at 40 °C. The underside of the molds, which had been in 
contact with the casting surface, remained sticky, suggesting that the curing process was 
insufficient at this lower temperature. However, the literature suggests that curing is more 
reliably achieved at 70 °C for two hours27.  Therefore, it is recommended to heat PDMS molds 
to higher temperatures in the future as incomplete curing may pose a health risk if the partially 
cured PDMS is handled without gloves, or PDMS residues may both contaminate the samples, 
and complicate demolding.  
The primary reason for fabricating a PDMS mold was that GelMA hydrogels adhered strongly 
to the surfaces of cell culture plate lids, often causing damage during demolding. In contrast, 
PDMS molds proved highly effective due to their non-stick properties. 
This outcome did not extend to other molds stored continuously in the oven to maintain 
readiness for polymer casting. Constant heating led to complete curing of the PDMS (PDMS 
curing literature), which limited the flexibility and usability of the molds in subsequent 
experiments. 
 
6.5 Swelling Tests 
Although swelling studies are sometimes performed at 37 °C to simulate physiological 
conditions128,129 , swelling experiments in this study were conducted at room temperature to 
ensure reproducibility and consistent access to laboratory equipment. Since the primary 
objective was to compare swelling behavior across different formulations, this was deemed 
sufficient for relative analysis in a fume hood. The experiments were conducted using single 
samples without replicates, as preparing triplicates would have required excessive resources. 
This limitation may have introduced measurement uncertainty regarding the true swelling 
==PAGE:60==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
54 
 
profiles of the material combinations. However, the swelling tests were primarily designed as 
a screening tool to eliminate less suitable material compositions and to identify trends in how 
GelMA and pAAm concentrations influence swelling behavior. 
Samples demonstrating favorable swelling profiles were assumed to have potential for 
comparable drug delivery properties, based on the anticipated relationship between swelling 
behavior and drug release performance128. These candidates were further evaluated in 
mechanical testing to identify formulations suitable for microneedle applications, where both 
mechanical strength and controlled drug release are critical. The target was a sustained release 
profile. 
Notably, swelling tests were conducted on bulk hydrogels rather than actual microneedles. 
Although microneedles might exhibit different swelling profiles due to their larger surface 
area-to-volume ratio, bulk measurements were considered sufficient for comparing materials 
relatively. Given the large number of samples, and the considerable effort required to produce 
microneedles for each formulation, it was deemed impractical to fabricate needles solely for 
swelling comparisons. The method for microneedle fabrication were not fully developed at that 
time either.  
The lower experimental temperature may have affected swelling, potentially reducing it 
relative to measurements performed at 37 °C. Conversely, lower temperatures might have 
reduced GelMA degradation compared to 37 °C, as 37 °C is closer to GelMAs Gel-Sol 
transition temperature30. Lower degradation could provide a more accurate representation of 
the intrinsic swelling capacity, as degradation-driven mass loss would reduce measured 
swelling and introduce uncertainty. Acrylamide, being a water insoluble hydrogel48, may have 
contributed to stabilizing GelMA, possibly compensating for any degradation effects. Future 
studies could investigate degradation analysis of the microneedles themselves, as GelMA may 
still dissolve to some degree, even if acrylamide remains insoluble. 
During sample handling, it became evident that preheating both the solutions and the 
equipment was essential. Without heating, GelMA solutions became highly viscous and 
difficult to transfer with pipettes. Higher GelMA purity increased viscosity, causing material 
to adhere to pipette tips and dry on the surfaces. Wrapping samples in foil and pre-warming 
them, as well as storing pipette tips in the oven, greatly improved handling and reduced 
measurement variability. Therefore, this approach is recommended for future applications.  
==PAGE:61==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
55 
 
Several formulations—specifically B5, C4, and C5—could not be fully dissolved, indicating 
polymer saturation limits. These samples were therefore excluded. Although A5 was prepared, 
it displayed visible undissolved acrylamide aggregates, evident in in the swelling pictures in 
appendix, and was excluded from microneedle fabrication considerations due to concerns about 
inhomogeneity and potential mold-filling issues. B5, C4, and C5 contained higher GelMA 
concentrations than A5, further increasing viscosity and reducing acrylamide solubility. Thus, 
higher GelMA content appeared to limit the amount of acrylamide that could be incorporated. 
Consequently, compositions such as B5, C4, and C5 were deemed unsuitable for microneedle 
production due to exceeding physical processing limits. A4 and B4 were excluded for the 
mechanical testing because acrylamide did not fully dissolve, indicating insufficient potential 
for further microneedle testing. However, for the swelling tests they still contributed to getting 
a better understanding of how GelMA and acrylamide concentrations influence swelling 
behavior. 
Swelling profiles revealed that pure GelMA at 20% w/v (A samples) tripled in size within the 
first two hours, rapidly approaching swelling equilibrium. In contrast, formulations containing 
acrylamide swelled more slowly but eventually absorbed more water overall, achieving a 
higher swelling equilibrium. Thus, increasing acrylamide content led to more sustained 
swelling behavior, while pure GelMA exhibited burst swelling. Given the presumed 
relationship between swelling and drug release128, and between mechanical strength and 
sustained release32, this trend aligns with expectations that higher mechanical strength may 
reduce burst effects. However, the effect may also be influenced by increased total polymer 
content.  
Sample A5, for example, showed an initial burst swelling phase, reaching near-equilibrium for 
almost a day before undergoing a second burst. This could reflect incomplete acrylamide 
dissolution, where GelMA dominates the initial swelling, followed by delayed swelling driven 
by the acrylamide. In formulations without GelMA, it is conceivable that the slower, steady 
swelling observed between 2000 and 3000 minutes could be extrapolated back to the origin, 
potentially indicating consistent swelling over two days in the absence of GelMA’s initial burst 
effect. However, this remains speculative. 
Comparing A and B groups indicates that swelling  equilibrium decreases across all samples 
as polymer concentration increases. Hence, maximum swelling does not necessarily correlate 
directly with higher polymer content. Examination of the C group, particularly pure GelMA at 
==PAGE:62==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen

40% w/v, shows a pronounced effect of GelMA concentration on swelling. Nevertheless, 
acrylamide significantly increased swelling relative to pure GelMA, confirming that higher 
GelMA concentrations produce a denser network, impeding water penetration32. Acrylamide 
counteracts this by enhancing water uptake, leading to higher swelling levels despite increased 
GelMA content. These results also suggest a slower and more controlled swelling profile over 
the first 24 hours, potentially indicating improved sustained drug release. 
 
6.6 Compression testing 
Compression tests were performed on bulk hydrogel samples rather than on microneedles 
themselves. This choice was primarily driven by the large number of formulations under 
investigation and the fact that the microneedle fabrication method was still under development 
at the time (the tests were conducted in parallel to save time). Evaluating the bulk samples’ 
ability to withstand compressive forces and comparing results among different formulations 
was deemed sufficient for the purpose of narrowing down candidate materials. 
As mentioned in the swelling section and discussed further, some formulations—specifically 
C5, C4, B5, B4, A5, and A4—were unable to fully dissolve acrylamide. Additionally, 
formulations C0, B0, and A0 were too viscous to handle effectively, resulting in their exclusion 
from further testing. A0, B0, and C0 were also deemed unsuitable for mechanical testing due 
to the presence of air bubbles and deformations. Excluding these formulations inevitably limits 
the overall understanding of the system, as the contribution of acrylamide to the mechanical 
strength of the hydrogels could not be fully evaluated. 
Examining the compression curves shown in Figure 24 reveals that the C-series samples 
appeared uniformly strong, as they all compressed similarly under equivalent forces. This 
suggests that acrylamide may not contribute substantially to the hydrogel’s mechanical 
strength. Conversely, the lower-concentration A and B series showed that acrylamide slightly 
increased material strength, enabling these hydrogels to withstand greater loads before failure. 
This trend was observed in both series. 
A clear pattern emerged: higher GelMA concentrations resulted in less compression under load, 
indicating increased stiffness and mechanical robustness. Photographs in Figure 23 depicting 
indentation marks in A3, B3, and C3, support this trend, showing progressively smaller 
deformation with higher GelMA content. This supports the notion that increased GelMA 
concentration reinforces the hydrogel network structure30. 
==PAGE:63==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
57 
 
Samples able to endure compressive forces up to 500 N without fracturing are estimated to 
withstand at least that level of stress, corresponding to a minimum pressure of approximately 
6.37 MPa. This pressure is above the force required to breach the stratum corneum, which can 
has shown to break at 3.18MPa12. However, it must be emphasized that bulk compression data 
cannot directly predict microneedle performance. Microneedle geometry, tip sharpness, and 
the elastic properties of GelMA significantly influence penetration behavior. Direct strength 
tests on individual microneedles or penetration experiments will be required to confirm 
suitability for clinical applications. However, microneedle patches typically require forces of 
around 32 N to penetrate skin61, which is way below 500 N. Nevertheless, stress concentration 
at the needle tips may still be way higher than 32 N due to the small contact area. 
In summary, samples with higher GelMA content demonstrated superior mechanical 
properties. However, the goal of fabricating microneedles for transdermal drug delivery 
requires balancing mechanical robustness with drug release capabilities. Since samples with 
lower GelMA content exhibited higher swelling, materials A3, B3, and C3 were selected as the 
most promising candidates for continued development. This does not imply that samples like 
C1 and C2 lack sufficient strength; rather, the selection focused on optimizing swelling 
behavior and narrowing the experimental scope for further testing. 
 
6.7 Mold Fabrication and Filling 
Initially, the project plan included learning CAD software to design microneedle molds with 
varying parameters, enabling testing of different needle geometries and material compositions. 
However, this approach proved unfeasible due to the large number of required samples and the 
unexpectedly complex design and production process that came with fabrication of the molds. 
6.7.1 
Transparent 3D printed negative mold 
As shown in Figure 25, the first mold represented a first-try prototype. It was printed using a 
transparent material intended to permit UV light penetration during crosslinking, thereby 
enabling hydrogel casting and curing directly within the mold. This one-step process was 
envisioned as an efficient means for prototyping and rapid testing of various designs. However, 
the material providing the best print resolution was grey. Consequently, mold fabrication 
shifted to creating positive molds, which were then cast with PDMS to produce transparent, 
flexible negative molds. PDMS were widely used in the literature, and it offered significant 
==PAGE:64==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
58 
 
advantages: hydrogels did not adhere to it, it remained durable over multiple uses, and it was 
flexible enough to facilitate easy demolding without fracture, see section 3.5.1. 
6.7.2 Grey 4x Microneedlepatch Positive Mold 
Design revisions for Mold 2 included increased spacing between microneedle plates to 
accommodate material overflow and prevent cross-contamination. However, pouring PDMS 
into these molds introduced new challenges, including bubble formation even under vacuum 
and insufficient curing of some printed regions, leading to structural defects in the molds. 
Ultimately, only one of the four plates in Mold 2 was usable, prompting the development of 
Mold 3: a more compact design accommodating twelve microneedle patches.  
6.7.3 Grey 12x Microneedlepatch Positive Mold 
Mold 3 was optimized to fit twelve microneedle patches within a limited area, allowing room 
for defective units and facilitating larger-scale production. Small walls were incorporated to 
separate individual patches and minimize cross-contamination. Given doubts about the 
effectiveness of vacuum-assisted mold filling, a custom holder was designed for a 25 mL 
Falcon tube, enabling tests of both vacuum and centrifugal filling methods. Vacuum was 
preferred for its potential to fill multiple molds simultaneously. However, longer residence 
times during vacuum processes risked premature hydrogel crosslinking and helix formation30, 
potentially causing inhomogeneous structures. In contrast, centrifugation offered shorter 
residence times, reducing the risk of unwanted crosslinking. 
6.7.4 First Vacuum Test and Mold-Filling Challenges 
Initial vacuum tests revealed significant problems. Few microneedles were formed, and those 
produced had extremely large tip diameters—approximately 100 μm. Such wide tips would 
require excessive force to penetrate skin and might cause discomfort, contradicting theoretical 
design criteria for microneedles, see section 3.4. Moreover, microscopic analysis raised 
questions about whether poor mold filling or needle breakage, or both, were responsible for the 
defective needles. 
Repeated vacuum casting with higher GelMA concentrations yielded no successful needle 
formation. Under the microscope, circular cavities were observed where needles were intended 
to form. Many cavities contained an inner white ring, interpreted as reflections from the 
microscope light bouncing off hemispherical surfaces deep within the gel. In some areas, 
irregular patterns or color changes suggested partial needle formation or breakage. 
==PAGE:65==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
59 
 
It was ultimately concluded that the inner white rings likely resulted from incomplete mold 
filling, leading to semi-spherical voids rather than broken needles. The PDMS molds’ inherent 
hydrophobicity, combined with the hydrogel’s increased hydrophilicity from acrylamide 
additions, likely exacerbated this problem. 
6.8 CO₂ Laser Mold Fabrication 
In response to these challenges, a new mold design strategy was adopted, focusing solely on 
centrifugation and achieving finer microneedle tips. The new approach required: 
1. Compatibility with Falcon tubes for efficient handling. 
2. Ability to produce microneedles meeting target design specifications, such as 800 μm 
length, 300 μm base width, and tip diameters within ranges reported in literature (see 
section 3.4). 
Another technique involving CO₂ laser ablation was then considered, as reports of laser-based 
microneedle fabrication seemed a promising, precise alternative to 3D printing, see section 
3.5.1. Multiple iterations were conducted to refine needle dimensions in PDMS molds. 
Initially, the laser process posed difficulties. Accurate positioning was critical to ensure laser 
pulses drilled vertically into mold cavities without damaging surrounding walls. A preliminary 
step involved patterning a PDMS sheet in the laser bed to map out precise coordinates. This 
facilitated subsequent alignment of molds atop the patterned sheet, allowing manual laser 
focusing directly over intended sites. This method proved more reliable than attempting to 
position features solely through digital CAD files, which often required meticulous manual 
measurements to align molds physically. However, manual laser focus adjustment can 
potentially lead to significant variability. Even minor focus errors might drastically alter needle 
dimensions. Furthermore, achieving uniform needle geometry across an entire mold may 
depend on maintaining perfectly level PDMS surfaces, as deviations introduced during PDMS 
casting could potentially cause uneven surfaces and influence laser focus depth. In addition, 
the mechanical limits of the laser’s manual focus adjustment, which operates in fixed 
increments of approximately 1 mm per step, could further complicate efforts to ensure 
consistent structuring across the mold. 
Despite these limitations, the absolute precision of needle length may be less critical in early-
stage research. Variations between 500 and 800 μm might be acceptable, provided needles 
remain sharp and mechanically robust enough to penetrate the stratum corneum and reach the 
==PAGE:66==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
60 
 
dermis. Thus, the CO₂ laser method proved to be a practical and highly effective tool for rapid 
prototyping, surpassing the resolution achievable via 3D printing. The laser consistently 
produced needle tips with radii between 20 and 25 μm, representing significant progress toward 
the goal of fabricating microneedles for transdermal drug delivery. 
 
6.9 Centrifugational Filling 
When fabricating multiple microneedle patches simultaneously prior to centrifugation, it 
proved efficient to preheat all solutions and molds, allowing nearly simultaneous casting of the 
hydrogels. This approach helped ensure uniform conditions across all samples during the 
molding process, and is recommended for future work.  
After centrifugation, due to prior challenges, including potential needle breakage from the 
vacuum demolding, the sticky PDMS mold observed during swelling and mechanical tests, and 
an overall urgency to produce a complete microneedle patch, samples were subjected to freeze-
drying at −80 °C before demolding. This cryogenic approach, together with centrifugational 
filling, significantly improved demolding success and led to a key milestone: successfully 
fabricating an entire microneedle patch with sharp, intact needles. However, there is concern 
that cryogenic processing may alter the matrix structure of the GelMA hydrogel due to ice 
crystal formation123. To address this, subsequent tests confirmed that demolding was also 
possible without cryo-freezing, further validating the process and reducing potential impacts 
on material integrity.  
 
6.10 Penetration Testing 
An initial penetration test used a 2.65% agarose gel as a skin surrogate, applying a force of 
32 N, consistent with forces reported in the literature for microneedle insertion tests61. 
However, during testing, an A1 sample was pushed entirely through the agarose and into the 
bottom of the Petri dish. Although literature identifies agarose gels as suitable skin models for 
microneedle penetration studies110, this outcome suggested that agarose might not fully 
replicate the mechanical resistance of human skin. In practice, pressing one’s thumb into 
human skin with 32 N would not breach it, whereas this force easily penetrated the agarose 
model. The needles alone would have been acceptable, but whole patch penetration raises 
concerns about the validity of using agarose alone to predict microneedle performance against 
==PAGE:67==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
61 
 
the stratum corneum barrier. Importantly, the A1 microneedle subjected to the full 32 N force 
and pressed into the rigid Petri dish remained completely undamaged. This suggests that even 
microneedles with lower GelMA content (20% w/v) can withstand forces necessary for skin 
penetration, demonstrating promising mechanical resilience. Therefore, the test parameters 
were changed to the fixed 1,8mm displacement depth instead. Although agarose is cited for its 
similarity to skin’s elasticity, it may inadequately represent the actual toughness of skin, 
particularly the stratum corneum. Notably, penetration tests showed that as little as 4–5 N was 
sufficient to penetrate the agarose gel, far below the forces required to breach human skin. It is 
possible that agarose better models the tissue layers beneath the stratum corneum, once initial 
skin penetration has occurred. Therefore, while the test provided valuable mechanical data, it 
does not conclusively predict microneedle performance against the primary skin barrier, 
stratum corneum. Additional studies, potentially involving ex vivo porcine skin or human 
cadaver skin, will be required to fully validate penetration capabilities. 
During subsequent examination of agarose samples under a microscope, one of the C-series 
samples (C32) was unfortunately damaged during extraction from the gel. Despite multiple 
attempts to salvage usable areas for analysis, this sample could not be recovered. This left only 
one C-series sample available for evaluation, potentially reducing the robustness of conclusions 
regarding this formulation. Nonetheless, having at least one remaining sample preserved data. 
Future experiments should avoid removing the tested region directly from agarose. Instead, 
surrounding gel should be carefully excavated to free the sample. Using a removable substrate 
beneath the agarose might also facilitate safer extraction, as agarose strongly adheres to the 
Petri dish, increasing the risk of damaging samples and losing data. 
Furthermore, the interval between penetration testing and subsequent microscopic analysis 
may have permitted partial closure of the perforations, as the agarose samples were left exposed 
for a considerable time due to the physical distance between the experimental setup and the 
microscope, as well as the time required for testing. Consequently, distinct needle-shaped 
channels were not consistently observed; instead, only fissures or superficial marks were 
visible, which are presumed to result from needle impact rather than full penetration. For 
instance, in sample A33, although clear surface marks were evident when viewed from above, 
microscopic examination revealed no deep perforations, and the central mark appeared 
inclined, suggesting possible misalignment of the microneedle array. In contrast, sample A32 
exhibited well-defined perforations reaching depths of approximately 400 μm, a depth 
considered sufficient to target the dermis layer. Similar findings were observed across other 
==PAGE:68==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
62 
 
samples, although considerable variability in perforation depth suggested that factors such as 
needle bending, insufficient insertion force, or deviations from the designed needle length may 
have contributed to inconsistent penetration outcomes. 
Efforts to enhance visibility of perforations using Rhodamine B dye were unsuccessful. When 
applied, the dye rapidly spread across the agarose surface rather than penetrating into the needle 
tracks, negating its intended purpose as a tracer. Consequently, Rhodamine B was excluded 
from further testing. 
When considering the needles before, and after penetration, some needles appeared pre-
deformed even before testing. Microscopical images of needles such as B32, B33, and B31 
revealed spiral distortions within the needle structure, akin to a twisted cone shape. These 
defects are considered likely to result from the CO₂ laser fabrication process, where the 
combination of circular movement and progressive downward motion during ablation may 
have introduced such features. Although such deformation is thought to potentially affect 
uniformity across an entire patch, the CO₂ laser is nevertheless regarded as a significant 
improvement over 3D printing methods, as it appears capable of producing sharper tips with 
radii consistently in the 20–25 μm range. 
Overall, the penetration tests demonstrated that microneedles were capable of creating 
perforations of adequate depth to reach the dermis. However, further work is needed to confirm 
their ability to reliably penetrate the stratum corneum. Tests on more biologically relevant 
tissues, such as porcine skin, are recommended to bridge this gap. Nonetheless, the successful 
endurance of a 32 N force, equivalent to thumb pressure used in microneedle applications, 
without damage to the needles, strongly suggests a promising potential for practical 
transdermal use.  
 
6.11 Drug Release Testing 
Drug release studies initially aimed to use FITC-Dextran 4 (FD4) as a model compound due to 
its relatively high molecular weight (~4 kDa), which could simulate the release profile of larger 
therapeutic molecules, such as insulin (~5.8 kDa). However, FD4 raised concerns about 
potential photobleaching during UV curing130,131. Experiments using the MicroDISS system 
yielded unreliable results within FD4’s absorbance spectrum (490–520 nm). Consequently, 
FD4 was excluded from further experiments due to potential photobleaching. Ciprofloxacin 
==PAGE:69==
Fabrication of Hydrogel Forming Microneedles for Transdermal Drug Delivery                                Martin Nyvang Pedersen 
 
63 
 
was selected as an alternative model drug. Ciprofloxacin, a small-molecule compound with a 
molecular weight of 0.331 kDa, is substantially smaller than insulin or FD4. While useful for 
characterizing drug release behavior, ciprofloxacin’s low molecular weight inherently 
facilitates diffusion, imposing limitations on how well these results can predict release profiles 
for larger therapeutic agents. 
Analysis of release curves revealed that the hydrogel with the highest GelMA concentration 
and lowest acrylamide content (C0) released the most drug (67,2%), followed by B0 (63,0%). 
This was unexpected, as it was initially hypothesized that higher swelling would correlate with 
increased pore size, greater water transport, and thus enhanced drug release. Contrary to this 
hypothesis, the hydrogel that swelled the least exhibited the greatest drug release—and 
displayed a pronounced burst effect. While a burst release is consistent with pure GelMA, this 
effect became even more pronounced at lower swelling levels. 
Introducing acrylamide increased the swelling capacity of the hydrogels but paradoxically 
appeared to reduce drug release. One possible explanation is that network swelling occurs 
isotropically, tightening the hydrogel matrix rather than creating larger pores, thereby 
restricting drug diffusion. Whether pore constriction alone can fully account for the observed 
suppression of drug release, particularly for small molecules like ciprofloxacin, remains 
uncertain. Another possibility is that acrylamide is not fully crosslinked into the GelMA 
network and instead remains as physically entrapped polymer chains within the hydrogel. This 
could explain the eventual closure of pores during swelling.  
Another inconsistency arises from the observation that hydrogels with higher GelMA 
concentrations released more drug than those with lower concentrations, despite expectations 
that denser networks should impede diffusion. This suggests that acrylamide’s highly 
hydrophilic character might be retaining drug molecules within the matrix. However, if 
acrylamide alone were responsible for reduced release, one would expect identical release 
profiles across samples A3, B3, and C3, given that these share identical pAAm-to-GelMA 
weight ratios. Notably, C3 contains a higher total polymer content due to increased GelMA, 
and still demonstrates higher drug release, suggesting that increased GelMA concentration 
might counteract acrylamide’s restrictive effects. 
Another possibility is that higher polymer concentrations could reduce crosslinking efficiency, 
leaving portions of the network insufficiently interconnected. This might theoretically promote 
==PAGE:70==

dissolution of the hydrogel and increase drug release, though this hypothesis is speculative and 
would require targeted experiments to validate. 
An alternative hypothesis relates to network dynamics during swelling. If network expansion 
occurs within pore spaces, it could constrict effective pore diameter rather than enlarging it. 
Consequently, the formulation with lower GelMA content (e.g., A3) might swell more 
extensively and rapidly, leading to pore closure and reduced permeability, a hypothesis 
supported by the burst effect observed in A3’s swelling curve. Whether this mechanism 
significantly impacts drug release remains an open question and warrants further study.  
Regardless of mechanism, current data suggest that acrylamide does not positively influence 
drug release characteristics and may in fact hinder release. This trend may hold particularly 
true for small-molecule drugs like ciprofloxacin, which can otherwise diffuse through 
relatively tight networks. It is less certain whether the same limitations would apply to larger 
molecules, as these may be even more restricted by dense polymer networks.

7 Conclusion 
This project explored how GelMA/pAAm microneedles could be designed and fabricated for 
transdermal drug delivery applications. The hypothesis was that incorporating polyacrylamide 
into GelMA hydrogels would improve swelling and drug release while maintaining sufficient 
mechanical strength for skin penetration. 
A range of hydrogel formulations was developed and tested. The results showed that combining 
GelMA with polyacrylamide indeed increased the swelling capacity compared to pure GelMA. 
However, the relationship between swelling and drug release was more complex than initially 
expected. While higher swelling was achieved, drug release from hydrogels containing 
polyacrylamide was consistently reduced rather than enhanced, even for a small-molecule drug 
like ciprofloxacin. In fact, the sample expected to achieve the lowest drug release, due to its 
low swelling, was the one that showed the highest drug release, namely, the pure GelMA 
sample with the highest GelMA concentration. This suggests that swelling does not necessarily 
translate into improved drug diffusion, possibly due to changes in pore structure or increased 
tightness of the polymer networks during swelling. 
Mechanical testing on bulk hydrogels indicated that higher GelMA concentrations improved 
stiffness and compressive strength, which is promising for microneedle applications requiring 
sufficient force to penetrate the skin. Nonetheless, direct microneedle fabrication posed 
significant challenges. Initial attempts with 3D-printed molds and vacuum filling were 
unsuccessful due to poor mold filling and needles with excessively large tips due to insufficient 
printing resolution. Switching to a combination of 3D printing, PDMS molding and CO₂ laser 
needle fabrication combined with centrifugal filling enabled the production of whole 
microneedle patches with all needles having sharp tips and acceptable geometries. An initial 
agarose test showed that one microneedle patch withstood 32 N without damage, but this force 
pushed the whole patch through the gel, revealing agarose’s limitations as a skin model.  
Overall, the hypothesis was partially confirmed. The GelMA/polyacrylamide hydrogels 
offered improved swelling and adequate mechanical strength. However, they did not enhance 
drug release. Nevertheless, the successful production of mechanically robust microneedles 
demonstrates that the combination of GelMA and polyacrylamide is a promising basis for 
further development of transdermal drug delivery systems.

8 Future perspectives 
One important direction is to investigate the hydrogel’s internal pore structure in more detail, 
as pore size and distribution influence both drug release and mechanical properties. Future 
studies could therefore include pore analysis. Another aspect is the DS in GelMA. It is 
important not only to measure DS directly, for example using H-NMR spectroscopy, but also 
to explore how DS can be varied by changing the amount of MAA used during synthesis, the 
concentration of LAP, and the UV dose and exposure time. Systematically varying these 
parameters could help establish a complete tunability profile for the hydrogel network. The UV 
exposure step should also be examined further, including how a varying UV dose and time 
influences crosslinking and the properties of the hydrogels. This is especially relevant because 
UV light might damage sensitive drugs like insulin. To address this, future work could involve 
testing protective carriers for insulin, which could help shield the drug from UV exposure and 
improve its long-term stability and commercial shelf-life.  
Future research should also look into drug release studies in tissue-like media, such as agarose 
gels or other systems where only the microneedle tips penetrate and release the drug. This could 
help clarify whether microneedle dimensions or the size of the entire patch need to be adjusted 
to deliver the desired amount of drug. Mechanical testing should also be extended to the 
microneedles themselves, rather than only on bulk hydrogel samples, to evaluate the 
mechanical strength of the actual structures and how well they withstand forces during skin 
penetration. Future studies should use better skin models, since agarose gels might not fully 
simulate the barrier properties of real skin. Testing on ex vivo porcine skin could provide more 
relevant data for evaluating microneedle penetration performance. 
Safety considerations are important, particularly the risk of residual acrylamide monomers, 
which are toxic. It will be crucial to check for residual monomers using methods like H-NMR 
spectroscopy to confirm full polymerization. Biocompatibility studies will also be necessary to 
examine cytotoxicity and potential effects of degradation products. It would also be interesting 
to compare the maximum drug-loading capacity of each hydrogel component and conduct 
degradation studies to understand how the materials behave over time.  
Beyond drug delivery, these microneedles could have potential in sensing applications. pAAm 
increases swelling, allowing the microneedles to absorb more interstitial fluid, and also adds 
an extra layer of tunability to the hydrogel network. This could be relevant for applications 
such as glucose monitoring, and it might also be interesting to consider whether the technology

could be used for other parts of the larger research project beyond just drug delivery. For 
example, the swelling properties of pAAm might be useful for extracting interstitial fluid in 
sensing applications.
