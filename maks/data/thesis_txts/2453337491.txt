Abstract
Precision is crucial in radiotherapy (RT), to ensure correct treatment and to diminish se-
vere side eects. Imaging is used in RT for treatment planning, during treatment delivery,
and for monitoring and assessment of the outcome. The newly developed MR-linac (MRL)
system combines a magnetic resonance (MR) scanner with a linear accelerator (linac) and
enables precise position verication of the tumor right before and during RT. Magnetic
resonance imaging (MRI) is generally of great potential in RT due to its superior soft
tissue contrast and good spatial resolution. However, MRI is also known for geometrical
distortions due to mainly three sources: patient-induced eects, fundamental physical ef-
fects, and hardware imperfections. It is crucial to know the magnitude of the distortions
to assess the inuence on the precision in RT.
In this project, distortions due to hardware imperfections have been investigated to assess
their impact on the geometrical accuracy in the MRL at Odense University Hospital (OUH).
Distortions due to main eld inhomogeneity, gradient non-linearity, inuence from the linac,
and eddy currents have been assessed, as well as a general characteristic of the hardware
components in the MRL system.
The characteristic of the MRL and its sources of distortion was performed by following
a newly published MRL specic quality assurance (QA) protocol [1], and comparing the
results obtained on the MRL at OUH in this project with benchmark results published
along with the protocol. The main eld inhomogeneity was assessed based on the dual-
echo method [2]. The distortion due to gradient non-linearities were determined by using
the gradient-reversal method [3]. The inuence from the linac was investigated by assessing
the inuence from gantry position on the main eld inhomogeneity and the inuence of
irradiating while imaging. Eddy currents were not characterized in the QA protocol, but
were included in this project to assess if they would have impact in diusion-weighted
imaging (DWI), which can be used for assessment of the tumors response to RT. The eddy
currents were characterized based on gradient impulse response functions (GIRFs) provided
by University Medical Center Utrecht (UMCU) from their similar MRL system.
The geometrical accuracy in the center of the scanner was high (sub-mm distortions were
seen), and thus expected not to be signicant for the RT. As the eld of view (FOV) in-
creases several sources of distortions become more signicant, e.g. gradient non-linearities
and concomitant elds. The main eld showed low inhomogeneity in the transverse plane,
but inhomogeneity that could be an issue in the sagittal and coronal planes. A small inu-
ence of the gantry position was seen, but would result in sub-mm distortions (depending
on the applied gradients). Noise sources were found when testing the radio frequency (RF)
cage seal, and when assessing the temporal stability of an echo planar imaging (EPI) se-

Chapter 1
Introduction
In Denmark every third citizen will be diagnosed with cancer in their lifetime according to
statistics from The Danish Cancer Society [4], and the search and development of eective
treatment methods is important to improve the recovery and survival rate.
Radiotherapy (RT) is an eective way of treating many cancer types, and it is estimated
that a little more than half of all cancer patients receive RT during the course of the disease
and treatment [5, p. 1][6]. RT is used for both curative treatment of cancers, as well as
palliative treatment. Linear accelerators (linacs) are the most widely used treatment source
in external beam radiotherapy [7, p. 123], where high-energy X-rays are produced and
used for irradiating the tumor. An important part of RT is the planning of the treatment,
where medical imaging is necessary to create a treatment plan. Images are important to
obtain precise structural (and functional) images of the tumor and surroundings.
The
images are used to dene treatment volumes (e.g. tumor) and organs at risk (OAR); the
process of dening volumes is called delineation, and the delineations are used to create
the treatment plans. Njeh [8] states in a commentary concerning delineation of tumors
"Ultimately, image-guided radiotherapy (IGRT) is only as good as the accuracy with which
the target is known" [8, p. 136]; the accuracy in the images used for the delineation is of
high importance to ensure high quality delineation of the target, and thereby high quality
treatment.
Imaging is used in several aspects of RT besides treatment planning, it is
also used in the diagnosis, during treatment delivery and for outcome assessment after
treatment.
Besides treatment planning, image guidance is also used in the treatment delivery process
for position verication; this is called image-guided radiotherapy (IGRT). Good visualiza-
tion and geometrical accuracy ensures correct positioning of the patient, which is crucial
when RT is performed, as the treatment elds are aimed to be as accurate as possible to
avoid damage to healthy tissue and surrounding OAR. Damaging of important anatomical
structures will result in severe side eects. Life-long side eects can include damaging of
the salivary glands, or infertility after treatment of pelvic tumors, or even paralysis and
death if important structures in the brain have been hit. On the other hand, all of the
tumor has to be irradiated and receive the prescribed dose for maximal benet of the treat-
ment; missing a part of the tumor can result in recurrence of the cancer. The positioning
verication process is thus of high importance in RT.

1.1
Image-guided radiotherapy
Today computed tomography (CT) is used in the process of RT-planning, since CT is
known as the gold standard for geometrical accuracy among the imaging modalities. CT
is also fast, easily available in most RT departments, and gives critical information i.e. CT
give information regarding the electron density of the tissue about to be irradiated which
is essential for dose calculations [9].
A low quality form of CT, cone-beam CT, is typically present in the linacs, and is used to
verify the tumor position right before RT treatment. However as the soft tissue contrast
in CT is poor, the position verication is often based on the visualization of either bony
anatomy close to the organ or surgically implanted ducial marks (of e.g. gold) [10]. The
positioning is therefore often based on landmarks close to the tumor, rather than the tumor
itself. As CT is based on X-rays, each image acquired will expose the patient to extra dose,
and continuous position verication with CT is therefore not used.
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is known as the gold standard in medical imaging when
it comes to soft tissue contrast, and is thereby superior when it comes to delineation of tu-
mors during RT-planning and position verication right before treatment when compared
to CT [11]; the superior soft tissue contrast results in less inter-observer dierences in the
delineation [12] and also removes uncertainty from positioning based on nearby landmarks
rather than the tumor. MRI has the ability of determining the chemical composition of
tissues, and provide functional information like diusion, level of hypoxia and chemical
exchange of tumors [9]. The tumor's response to and thereby the outcome of RT can thus
also be assessed with MRI by analyzing other parameters than merely gross morphological
changes which is the information seen in CT.
Organ movement during treatment delivery is also an issue, that MRI could take into
account. E.g. the prostate will move due to lling of the bladder and rectum, bowel move-
ment, respiration and positioning of the patient (up to 10.2 mm, [13]). This is problematic
as dose coverage can become insucient, which can inuence the result of the treatment
if not corrected for during treatment delivery; today this is taken into account by adding
margins to the gross tumor volume, so that the nal planning target volume is larger [7,
p. 221]. In the combination of MRI and linac, the treatment plan can be adapted to the
"anatomy-of-the-day" and continuous imaging (i.e. real time monitoring) can be done.
The treatment can then, in principle, be gated based on organ movement to minimize
irradiation of healthy tissue and to ensure adequate dose coverage of the tumor itself. If
the organ position was based on nearby landmarks as bony anatomy, the organ movement
would not have been seen.
1.2
MR-Linac
To exploit the benet of using MRI for position verication, adaption of treatment plans,
and outcome monitoring in RT, the combination of MRI and RT has been implemented in

the MR-linac (MRL) system; a linac is placed in a ring (with zero magnetic eld) around the
MRI scanner, which will be elaborated upon later in this report, see 2.1 Linear accelerators.
Two CE-marked cylindrical bore setups exist: a 0.35 T setup (ViewRay MRIdian, [14])
with a 6 MV linac and a 1.5 T setup (Elekta Unity, [15]) with a 7 MV linac. The rst
patient treated in an Elekta Unity MR-linac (MRL) setup was in 2017 by Raaymakers
et al. [16] at University Medical Center Utrecht (UMCU). The system has since then been
installed at other sites, including Odense University Hospital (OUH). The rst patients at
OUH were treated in October 2018.
There are some known issues when using MRI in RT. MRI lacks information on the electron
density of the tissues about to be irradiated, which is a necessity for treatment planning,
since it is used to estimate the attenuation property of the tissue [17]. This needs to be
taken into account if an MRI-only workow is used, and can be corrected for by creating e.g.
substitute-CT images created from assigning electron densities to MRI [17]. In the MRL
workow at OUH and UMCU, a pre-treatment CT scan is obtained to get the attenuation
property of the tissues [16]; CT is however removed in the position verication step during
treatment delivery, where MRI is used instead.
Another signicant problem with using MRI in RT is the fact that MRI is known for
geometrical distortions [18].
Geometrical distortions in MRI can be divided into three
origins: patient induced, hardware imperfections and fundamental physical eects [19].
The patient induced distortions arise from the susceptibility eects of the patient that will
distort the main eld of the scanner. Hardware imperfections are inevitable in MRI design:
e.g. if a fast switching gradient is desired, a gradient with few turns would be optimal,
however, fewer turns will aect the linearity of the gradient eld generated badly which can
lead to distortions [20]. Fundamental physical eects include that turning a gradient on in
an magnetic resonance (MR) scanner will not only create a magnetic eld change in the
direction of the gradient, but due to the Maxwell equations, smaller elds, called Maxwell
terms, will inevitably also be generated in other directions [19, p. 292]. The distortions
and their sources will be elaborated upon in 2.2.2 Distortions in MRI.
It is of high importance that the magnitude and relevance of the distortions is known
before the RT is planned, adapted, and monitored based on the MR images. Distortions
can be present even after corrections techniques are applied [18], and it is therefore also
of relevance to examine distortions in corrected images. Due to the very dierent origins
of distortions, they can be dicult to correct for - i.e. susceptibility eects are dependent
on the individual patient and will need dierent correction for each patient, others are
sequence specic (i.e. eddy currents), and lastly some are system specic (i.e. gradient
non-linearities). Distortions tend to scale with the distance to the isocenter of the scanner,
and distortions at the edge of the eld of view (FOV) can reach high levels [16]. This
becomes an issue in RT since it often is not possible to position the tumor in the center of
the scanner. The geometric accuracy thus has to be good in a large FOV to enable precise
RT treatment of both supercial and deep-lying tumors in the patient.
As Weygand et al. [9] mentions, based on a review of 11 studies regarding spatial precision
in MRI-guided RT, most system-related geometric distortions are well understood and

1.3
Scope of the project
The following research questions have been dened for the project:
1. What is the magnitude of the distortions present in the MRL system at OUH, and
what is the relevance of measuring the distortions?

However, MRI requires a large, homogeneous main magnetic eld to obtain good quality

the design of an MR scanner, issues can arise. This regards both the impact of the linac

functionality of the linac. In the following section, an introduction to the components of a

of the two systems. Thereafter distortions in MRI is elaborated upon.
2.1
Linear accelerators
The RT treatment in the MRL system is based on an X-ray beam of high energy. The
energetic photons will interact with the electrons in the body, and deposit energy that
damages the irradiated area.
To create the treatment beam consisting of high-energy
photons, particle accelerators (linacs) can be used.
A linac must meet two basic conditions to work: the particle to be accelerated must be
charged and an electric eld must be provided in the direction of the particle acceleration
[7, p. 133]. The linac accelerates electrons to an energy in the range of 4-25 MeV by cyclic
acceleration, i.e. the nal kinetic energy of the electron is obtained by submitting the
charged particle to the same, relatively small, potential dierence a large number of times,
each cycle adding a small amount of energy to the kinetic energy of the particle [7, p. 133].

Eddy current eects can be reduced in mainly four ways: shielded

2.2.2.2
Gradient non-linearities
The purpose of the imaging gradients is to vary the magnetic eld linearly across a di-
ameter spherical volume (DSV) and the associated FOV [31] to enable spatial encoding
as previously described [9]. However, in gradient design trade-os have to be made which
results in gradient non-linearities (GNL). In imaging today, there is a need for strong gradi-
ents, that are able to switch rapidly (especially in imaging techniques as diusion-weighted
(DW)-EPI [19, p. 274]), as well as a large imaging volume but also a need for spatial and
geometrical accuracy. The trade-o is therefore often found in one of these parameters.
For example, a short rise time of the gradient (and thereby a high slew rate) restricts the
number of turns in the coil, as well as the length of the coil which will result in poorer
linearity of the gradient [9][20].
The non-linearity of gradients lead to distortions, as the reconstruction of the images
typically relies on an assumption that the gradient elds are linear. Linear gradients will
lead to linear sampling in k-space, and the reconstruction of the image is thereby done by
a linear FT [32]. The assumption of linear sampling is however not true when a non-linear
gradient eld is present, and the spatial encoding becomes incorrect, and this leads to
spatial distortions in the images [32].

Reduction methods
The concomitant elds (or the concomitant eld phase error) can
be corrected by phase correction during image reconstruction, hardware compensation
and alterations of the gradient waveforms in the pulse sequence. The alteration of the
waveforms include waveform symmetrization (or waveform reshaping), waveform de-rating,
and gradient separation.
By applying symmetric waveforms surrounding a refocusing pulse, the concomitant-eld
phase error (Eq. (2.24)) is nulled. Gradient de-rating decreases the strength of the applied
gradient, and thereby the concomitant elds. With gradient separation, the cross-term
phase errors will be eliminated/reduced, when minimizing the temporal overlap of the
playing gradients.

extracted from both data sets and used for processing; the method developed in this
project does thus not assess the distortion in the z-direction.
 Coordinate estimation: The coordinates of the markers were found by using the
build-in Matlab function regionprops, that determined the weighted centroid of
each marker. The weighted centroids were found on the data sets for both readout
directions. The nal coordinate set for each marker was found as the mean of the
two coordinate sets for each marker.
 Distortion map creation: The distortion map was created by calculating the eu-

Investigation of eddy currents
After having performed the QA of the MRL system, an investigation of the inuence of
eddy currents was conducted to answer the third research question. The rationale for con-
ducting this investigation lies in the potential for using diusion-weighted imaging (DWI)
(which will be introduced below) for RT response assessment at OUH (and other sites). As
described further in the following paragraph, DWI sequences utilize high-amplitude gradi-
ents, which is strongly associated with eddy currents. A characterization of eddy currents
is thus considered relevant in the MRL setup, and as the QA protocol did not explicitly
investigate this the second part of this project is allocated for such investigation. Further-
more, the special design, with a split gradient coil of the MRL, has been hypothesized to
be more prone to eddy current generation than MRI systems with conventional design [33].
DWI sequences consist of the diusion sensitizing part and a read-out part, where EPI
is typically used [38]. The diusion-weighting (DW) is obtained by applying two strong
diusion gradients, that create sensitivity towards motion of water molecules. To lower
the eect of 'bulk' motion of the patient and mainly be sensitive to the Brownian mo-
tion characterizing diusion, the diusion-weighting (DW) gradients are followed by a fast
single-shot sequence as EPI, which also results in high temporal resolution [19, p. 830].
In DWI it is important to minimize TE to reduce signal loss [19, p. 851]. To preserve the
specic area of the diusion gradient, minimizing the gradient duration to achieve a short
TE implies increasing the gradient strength. As described in 2.2.2.1 Eddy currents, eddy
currents are dependent on the gradient strength, and characterized by a time constant, τ,
why they can interfere with the following part of the sequence, the EPI read-out if they
are long-lived.
B0 and linear eddy current elds will be produced with the diusion gradient (and all other
gradients as well) [19, p. 852]. The B0 eddy current eld will give rise to image shifts in
the phase-encoding direction of the EPI image, whereas the linear eddy current eld with
characteristics of readout and phase-encoding gradients will cause shearing and scaling (see
2.2.2.1 Eddy currents) [19, p. 852]. The linear eddy current eld with characteristics of
the slice selection gradient can lead to a loss in signal intensity, as the linear eddy current
eld will result in a non-matching re-focusing lobe and thereby imperfect refocusing.
The eddy currents in DW-EPI can thereby lead to errors in the apparent diusion coecient
(ADC) maps [19, p. 852], that can be used as biomarkers in the response assessment of the
RT [38].

4.2.3
Discussion
As seen from Figure B.4, the tting applied to the GIRFs is not optimal as it does not con-
verge towards zero when more Lorentzians are tted. This suggests that a local minimum
has been found, which is not optimal. In the x- and y-direction, it is evident that one of
the Lorentzians tted has the maximally allowed width of 20 kHz; several larger maximum
widths were tried, and each time one of the Lorentzians tted would match this maximum
width. If this is due to a weakness in the tting algorithm or there actually are some wide
Lorentzians that describe the GIRF well is still unknown. The maximum width and local
minimum was not investigated further. On the other hand, when looking at the sum of
the tted Lorentzians in Figure 4.6 - Figure 4.8, the ts describe the shape of the GIRFs
rather well. Therefore, it is assumed that the resulting time constants are valid.
The estimated time constants from the GIRFs are all very short; this indicates that the
eddy current corrections applied on the MRL system are successful in compensating for the
longer-living eddy currents. Brodsky et al. [39] states that "in practice, this compensation
(of the eddy currents, ed.) is typically limited to time constants greater than 1 ms as shorter
time constants do not eect the Cartesian sequences used clinically. Therefore phase error
may still be signicant during and shortly after periods of time-varying gradient (which is
the source of the eddy currents, ed.)" [39, p. 509].
The short lived eddy currents will, as described in Eq. (2.14), create a delay of the gradient.
In conventional Cartesian-sampling of an EPI-sequence, several lines of the center of k-space
==PAGE:81==
4.3 Partial conclusion
67
are often read-out to use for correction; here the shift in k-space that the delay will cause,
can be corrected for, as the read-out is in the same direction (or reversed) every time, which
creates robustness of the sequence and minimizes the eect of the short-lived eddy current
elds. As Brodsky et al. [39] also mentions, in Cartesian sampling where sampling starts
at the "edge" of k-space, the short-lived eddy currents are not of very great importance
since they will die out before the center of k-space is sampled.
For non-Cartesian sampling schemes, as e.g. radial or spiral sampling, the short-lived eddy
currents can have a much larger eect. The read-out occur while gradients are slewing
(and thereby while the short-lived eddy currents are "living" and still generated), and TE
is often very short if the sampling start in the center of k-space; this means that the eect of
the eddy currents will be seen in the center of k-space, which can lead to signicant impact
of the image quality [39]. It can therefore be argued that for the case of non-Cartesian
trajectories, the eddy currents in the MRL system could have a signicant impact.
Only the 1st order GIRFs (and direct linear terms) are used for this investigation of the
eddy currents. In the case of DW-EPI, it is known that both the B0 and linear eddy current
elds from the read-out and phase-encoding gradients can contribute to the geometrical
distortions, if the time constants are suciently long [19, p. 852]. The time constants of the
B0 eddy current elds should therefore also be considered. To complete the analysis, the
cross-terms of the linear eddy currents elds (see Eq. (2.10)) should also be investigated, but
are not included in the acquisition of these GIRFs [33]. The next step, as seen in Figure 4.1
would be to simulate a DW-EPI sequence, which can be done in the MRI simulation tool
JEMRIS to assess if the eddy currents generated from the diusion gradients have any
impact, or if the short time constants are insignicant as expected from the discussion
above.
4.3
Partial conclusion
The linear eddy currents in the MRL system at UMCU were characterized by estimating the
time constants and amplitudes. From the characterization of the eddy currents, remaining
after automatic system corrections, it can be concluded that they are short-lived and thus
not expected to have a large impact on sequences using Cartesian trajectories, as DW-EPI
often does. The eddy current elds could have a signicant inuence on sequences utilizing
k-space trajectories originating in the center of k-space, i.e. sequences with an ultra short
TE.

Chapter 5
Perspectival discussion and
future work
In this project, the eects of mainly hardware related distortions have been investigated. As
mentioned in 1 Introduction, the sources of distortions can be divided into primarily three
categories: patient-induced eects, hardware imperfections, and/or fundamental physical
eects.
Fundamental physical eects, i.e. the concomitant elds described in 2.2.2.4 Concomitant
eld and Maxwell terms, can be corrected for and are not dependent on the specic system,
like e.g. gradient non-linearities and eddy currents that were assessed in this project.
Patient related distortions are completely dierent as they vary and depend on the indi-
vidual patient and even on their positioning in the main eld, which also poses a challenge
when it comes to correction methods. For a complete investigation of the total distor-
tions present in treatment situations in RT, the susceptibility aects should of course be
included.
In a study by Wang et al. [40], on a 3 T scanner, B0 eld maps were created for 19 patients;
the largest distortion found due to susceptibility eects from patients were reported as 4
mm, which was found at the air/tissue boundary in the sagittal sinus. However, 97.4% of
the volume investigated in the study (avg. volume was 3290 cc) was displaced less than 1
mm. Overall the distortions are small, but does become problematic at tissue boundaries,
especially when air is present, and these situations should be taken into consideration when
using MRI in RT, and adjusting treatment plans based on the MR images.
For correction of patient related distortions, some of the same methods as presented in this
report, can be used, e.g. B0 mapping. Susceptibility eects from patients will distort the
main eld, which can be assessed from B0 mapping, and corrected for by using shimming
optimized for the specic B0 map [40].
However, as B0 mapping and the subsequent
optimization of shimming based on the maps is not a product functionality in the MRL
at OUH, it becomes dicult to make this correction for each patient. As discussed by
Crijns et al. [41], it is also quite time consuming to calibrate the system and optimize the
shimming based on the B0 map. Another approach, which is also described by Crijns et al.
[41], is to correct the images instead, still based on the information from the B0 maps.
Crijns et al. [41] have developed a method that utilizes real-time correction of main eld
inhomogeneities, including patient-induced eects, which is based on correcting the pixels

This project has investigated the geometrical accuracy of MRI, since it is crucial for the

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Geometrical accuracy is essential in radiotherapy (RT), to ensure the best treatment of
cancer. MRI-guided RT, with the MR-linac (MRL) system, enables precise position veri-
cation, monitoring during treatment, and response assessment of the RT. However, MRI is
also known for geometrical distortions due to hardware imperfections, fundamental physical
eects, and patient-induced eects. The distortions can be present even after correction
techniques are applied. The magnitude of the dierent distortions should be known to
evaluate the inuence on the RT precision.
The MRL system at OUH was characterized by using a newly published QA protocol
[1]. All reported results for distortions due to B0 inhomogeneity, gradient non-linearities,
gantry dependency, and radiation inuence show that the overall distortion level is low for
the MRL system in the center of the scanner. However, the distortions often depend on the
FOV and the applied gradient strengths. In the edge of the scanner the distortions increase,
and can become problematic since they exceed sub-millimeter precision. Additional results
for e.g. receiver coil performance and general QA tests (periodic image quality test (PIQT))
test show that the gradients complies with the dened criteria. Some ndings were made,
e.g. unknown noise components in the RF cage seal test and in the Fourier-analysis of
the EPI sequence in the functional biomedical informatics research network (FBIRN) test,
which have to be investigated further.
The linear eddy currents generated in the MRL were investigated, to assess their inuence
in diusion-weighted (DW) EPI. It was seen that the eddy currents found in the MRL
system probably not will aect Cartesian-trajectory sequences as EPI, which is often used
in DWI, due to very low time constants (all < 84 µs).
They could however inuence
sequences with very short TE, e.g. non-Cartesian trajectories originating in the center of
k-space. The hypothesis should be tested further by e.g. simulations, to see if they support
or not that these eddy currents have no impact in Cartesian-sampledes DW-EPI. The rst
order GIRFs were used for the investigation, and thereby the linear eddy currents have
been assessed; the B0 eddy current elds should also be investigated to assess the inuence
of these.
It is assessed that the distortions have low signicance in the center of the scanner, but
that the signicance increases as the distance to the center of the scanner increases. For
the eddy currents generated, the assessed lack of inuence in DW-EPI should be tested
with simulations.

Conclusion
In this project, the distortions from mainly hardware imperfections (gradient non-linearities
and eddy currents, along with possible inuence from the linac) has been explored. How-
ever, patient-induced distortions are also a critical and important source that also should
be investigated to explore the inuence on the geometrical accuracy.
