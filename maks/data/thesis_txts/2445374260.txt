Abstract
Smart technologies cover almost every aspect of daily life and thus many of them
have been used in urban development projects. Smart technologies have affected so-
cial relationships and forms of communication, it is therefore useful to investigate
whether this also applies when it comes to urban smart technologies. Six projects
located in the Greater Copenhagen have been used as case studies. Four of them
focus on lighting and two of them on energy. Interviews were conducted on project
leaders and planners to understand the history and objectives of the projects. An
indicator framework was developed to analyze how the projects affect or potentially
affect social sustainability. The indicator framework for urban social sustainability
assessment was inspired from already existing ones, but establish an alternative rela-
tionship between its elements. From here, the case studies are compared to identify
the key factors for promoting or hindering social sustainability when implementing
smart city technologies.
Keywords: Urban Social Sustainability, Urban Sustainable Development, Smart
Technologies, Smart City, Greater Copenhagen, Indicator Framework.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This project is an attempt to investigate the interaction between smart city projects
and urban social sustainability. It will investigate whether, and in which extent, smart
solutions in an urban scale can contribute to social sustainability and in general how
a smart city can be livable and socially sustainable. The role of social sustainability
in urban and city scale projects will be underlined, as well as the importance of
smart technologies for the future of the cities, the inhabitants and the environment.
Afterwards, an appropriate indicator framework for social sustainability will be set.
An assessment of a smart neighbourhood and/or a smart technology, will be conducted
in order to export useful conclusions, relevant to the research topic.
1.1
Background
Rapid urbanization is an ongoing phenomenon, which started occurring the last
decades. This fact creates the need for an alternative approach in the way we create,
use and develop the cities nowadays and in the future. United Nations have set sus-
tainable development goals, which also concern directions towards the development
of sustainable and livable cities, by establishing the sustainable development goal
number 11. A way to obtain urban sustainability is to implement smart solutions
through the collection and usage of data, to solve some already existing issues and
also prevent any potential future ones.
Social sustainability is one of the three pillars of sustainability that consider quality of
life. Although sometimes disregarded, social sustainability concerns important values
that improve the societal function. Participation, knowledge sharing, personal and
collective welfare are among social sustainability aspects. Consequently, it is also
considered necessary to include social sustainability in the design and planning of
cities and urban environment.
1.2
Problem statement and research question
According to the latest information by the United Nations, it is stated that 50 % of
the world’s population currently lives in cities, while this percentage is expected to
increase in the upcoming decades. (UN, 2018). Therefore, it is crucial to ensure that

cities will be able to support a proper well-being of their increased population. The
concept of a smart city has been progressively gaining ground, as a mean to address
environmental and sustainability issues. According to this concept, smart technolo-
gies and data are used in order to optimize processes, reduce energy and material
consumption and improve the quality of life. More and more, nowadays, city master
plans and urban renewal projects put efforts to integrate smart solutions into the
cities, which makes the smart city concept the city of future. So far, experience has
shown that a city needs balance between environmental, economic and social aspects,
in order to function properly. However, during the smart city planning, environmen-
tal sustainability is prioritized and so the majority of the smart solutions aim to solve
environmental issues. As a result, the desired equilibrium tends to be disrupted, as
social sustainability and economic sustainability, usually plays a secondary role in the
smart city. Regarding social sustainability, community thinking, social cohesion and
cultural characteristics of the citizens are crucial in the success of any urban project
and therefore their absence can cause important problems in the city’s operations
and goals achievement. The relation between social sustainability and smart cities
is not widely investigated, yet. Therefore the present paper aims to contribute by
answering the following questions:
To what extent does smart city project affect / improve social sustainability, and how
can social sustainability be better considered in the design of smart city projects?
1.3
Objectives and goals
The main objective of this paper is to contribute to the researches of social sustain-
ability and to relate it with the new trend of smart cities.
Technologically driven cities are to become the future cities and the risk of social
isolation is around the corner. People are the core of the city and the importance of
a city to be people oriented, livable and promote values has to be considered through
the planning and development phase. Despite the fact that some technological inno-
vations, such as ”internet of things” and ”big data”, can contribute towards sustain-
ability, it must not be forgotten that technology is the way and not the goal in itself.
Additionally, it can be researched how technology can actually provide assistance in
social sustainability instead of considering it as a barrier to social interaction.
Below, the goals of the project are summarized:
• Develop a social sustainability indicator framework that integrates the smart
city aspects into urban social sustainability and also takes into account the
United Nations sustainable development goals.
• Identify whether and in which way social sustainability has been ensured in the
smart urban project plans.

2.1
Urban Social Sustainability
First described by the Brundtland report [4] in 1987 sustainability is a concept ex-
pected to assist in solving many worldwide problems, in many matters. Sustainable
development is the process by which the state of sustainability is succeeded. There-
fore, sustainable development is a term that is widely used in the latest years, aiming
towards the optimization of processes and savings in materials and energy, in order
to deliver the world to the future generations in the same or even better condition. [7]
Due to the latest global environmental issues, sustainability is considered by many
people only with its environmental aspect. In reality, sustainability has three pillars;
environmental, economic and social.[34] However, while environmental and economic
sustainability is relatively easy to be defined, social sustainability still remains a vague
concept.[3] In this thesis there will be an extent research regarding social sustainabil-
ity and therefore it is important to define the term.
Social sustainability is, usually, inextricably linked to the urban context, as cities
are the place where people live and interact. There are certain features that affect
social sustainability and most of them are related to the urban environment. Social
sustainability reflects the influence of people in the sustainability concept. It is a
concept that has to ensure values and ethics regarding people’s quality of life, their
interaction and their involvement to community processes. There are many parame-
ters that can contribute to enhance social sustainability and they have been defined
in various literature sources. Some of these physical and non-physical factors can be
seen in Table 2.1.[7] Therefore, the aforementioned facts can sum up the following
definition of urban social sustainability:
The term urban social sustainability describes the influence of the urban context in
the social sustainability concept. It is the spaces between buildings that makes the
urban space livable and these spaces have to be made to promote values such as
cultural traditions, equity, social interaction, community thinking and participatory
governance.

2.2
The importance of social sustainability
Rapid urbanization has brought cities in the focus of sustainability. With the urban
population constantly increasing, new areas are being built, sometimes creating new
communities from scratch. It is important to keep in mind that the successful function
of these communities is based on people and not on buildings. In many cases, private
companies that are involved in the planning prioritize profit and financial concerns
and as a result, apartments and housing for sale is considered more important than
public local facilities. [33] The struggle of balancing profitability and quality of a
neighbourhood, sometimes leads to the opposite results than the desired-ones. New
residents in newly built areas fail to feel connected to the place and therefore basic
community aspects are unsatisfied. As a result, the area holds bad reputation and
apartments remain empty and unsold. Therefore, there is a risk of failure even in
terms of profitability. [34]
Social sustainability has usually been neglected or not taken into account in the de-
gree that it was needed in city development projects. Several city projects have been
unsuccessful because the aspect of social sustainability was disregarded. In the case
==PAGE:21==

of Ørestad, for example, besides the fact that the buildings consist of numerous ar-
chitectural masterpieces, it is concluded that too much attention has been given to
the buildings rather than the public spaces between them. According to Jan Gehl’s
famous quote: F̈irst life, then spaces, then buildings̈, this sequence should not be
interrupted if people are still considered to be the core of a community. [10] The
consequences of these choices appeared after the development, when less people than
expected moved to the newly built area. This fact of course can be due to a com-
bination of factors, but without doubt, public spaces play an important role to an
area’s identity. The empty public spaces support the statement that calls Ørestad
as a ”ghost city” and makes the claim that the project was unsuccessful, stronger. [30]
Therefore, evidence proves that the lack of consideration of social sustainability into
urban projects sometimes leads to the failure of a project. Apart from that, the well-
being of the inhabitants is partly reflected upon social sustainability and hence the
inclusion of it in the planning and design phase must is imperative.

2.4
Why smart cities
2.4.1
The definition of smart cities
Smart city is a concept that has gained popularity the latest years. It concerns the use
of information and smart technologies in a city scale in order to achieve effectiveness
in processes, energy consumption, material use etc. It is considered an innovative ap-
proach of city building and management that leads to sustainable cities. Furthermore,
it is believed that smart cities will help in solving major problems in the urban scale.
Main reasons that lead to serious environmental issues are going to be addressed by
using technology and information, such as material and energy waste, congestion etc.
Furthermore, economic benefits are achieved by optimizing and automating processes
through information and communication technology.[21] But what is a smart city
exactly and why is it an important approach for future cities?
Various definitions have been given to the smart city concept, with the vast majority
of them connecting it with technology and more specifically with Information and
communication technology (ICT). [1]. However, in order to define what a smart city
is, it is needed to consider if the approach is technology-oriented or strategy-oriented.
For a example, can a city that uses efficiently smart technologies, but lacks of livabil-
ity, be considered as smart? On the other hand, can a livable and sustainable city be
called smart, if it does not use smart technologies? The truth can possibly be found
somewhere in between and a holistic approach is needed in order to characterize a
city smart.
The latest report of EU, regarding smart cities, approaches the topic from various an-
gles, including socio-economic and multi-stakeholder analysis. According to European
Parliament, the smart cities can be described by the following 6 subcategories:
==PAGE:23==

• smart economy
• smart living
• smart mobility
• smart environment
• smart people
• smart governance
Furthermore, it is stated that a smart city’s strategy should aim, at least, one of the
aformentioned characteristics. [31] Therefore, the definition of the smart city includes
the strategic goals that should be aligned with the smart characteristics and the use
of smart technologies to reach these goals. Consequently, the part of technology in
smart cities should be in assisting functions to facilitate sustainable development and
not to be technologically driven as a purpose. [1]
By summarizing the gathered information, a smart city definition is briefly formed
as it is stated below:
A smart city is a city, which forms strategies to establish sustainable development by
the use of information and modern technologies and includes the active participation
of citizens and various stakeholder in the processes.
2.4.2
Smart city and people
Considering the fact that cities are made for people, to serve their needs and improve
their life quality, their role in a smart city should be taken into account. People’s
contribution to the function of a smart city can be beneficial for both increase inclu-
siveness and belonging in the city and optimize processes and services. [15] However,
there a tendency to forget the effect oh people’s behaviour in these procedures. Any
project that involves people can be turn from success to failure if the human actor
was not taken into account at the planning phase. Moreover, participation leads to
more successful smart cities by sharing knowledge, which is an important value in
smart cities. [21]
Smart technologies in general, play an important role in the everyday life of peo-
ple. People’s habits and routines have changed due to technology and things are
constantly changing over the years. Smart technologies are all around us, visible or
invisible and have a large impact on our reality. The way people work, communicate,
travel is changing and it is important, urban development to follow the trends. Given
that, it is of great importance to try to integrate technology into the city, in order
to achieve sustainability. People are constantly becoming smarter and smarter and

2.5
Indicator framework
Many cities worldwide set sustainability goals and plan actions in order to achieve a
level of sustainability in their region. After the planning and implementation phase,
appear the need to evaluate the results and check whether these actions improve
sustainability conditions. The latest methodology, that has been used to measure
sustainability in a city is through indicators. Miscellaneous sustainability indicator
frameworks are used, depending on the special characteristics of each city.
In the sections below, a general methodology for forming an indicator framework has
been described, as well as various urban social sustainability frameworks and a smart
city framework.
2.5.1
Decide the appropriate indicator framework
According to McLaren (1996) there are some steps, that can be followed in order to
set the right indicator framework for the examined case each time. These steps are
presented in Figure 2.2 and described in this section.
Define sustainability goals: Sustainability goals are either already defined in the
planning phase or needed to be defined for the specific evaluation. In any case, in or-
der to set the sustainability goals, a multi-stakeholder analysis should be considered,
in order to get an overview of the local status and set the desirable sustainable future
of the city.
Scoping: The spatial and temporal border of the indicator framework, the number
of indicators, the objective of the assessment and the target audience are defined.
First the target audience is determined, in order to identify the number of indicators.
The characteristics of the audience, for example if it is scientists or the public, affect
the indicator’s number and presentation. Finally, the time frame and geographical
boundaries are chosen.
Choose indicator framework: There are six generic frameworks that can be used
to identify indicators:
• domain-based framework, which identifies indicators for each domain of
sustainability (environment, economy, society)
• goal-based framework, in which indicators reflect the community goals

and help then feel integrated as soon as possible. Furthermore, social and cultural life
has to be supported by the presence of shared spaces, collective activities and social

4.1
Define Social Sustainability Goals
It is crucial for a neighbourhood or a city to be social sustainable in order to transfer
sustainable values and way of living to the present and next generations. Besides the
environmental and economic sustainability, social sustainability has to be taken into
account, during the planning and development phases, because societal behaviour is
equally important, in order to characterize an area fully sustainable. The values that
are predefined form the strategy of an area towards sustainability and therefore af-
fects the urban design and development decisions. The other way around, the urban
environment also shapes the values that the area aims to promote. Therefore, this
circle of reciprocal value framing needs to agree and not fall into contrasts. The most
important step in achieving that is to determine the social sustainability goals, that
will compose the character of the area. So what are the values that are crucial in
order to develop a social sustainable community?
4.1.1
Welfare
First of all the residents’ quality of life is one of the most crucial goals, in order
for an area to be described as social sustainable. The level of the well-being has
to be prioritized and form the way of planning and design of the area. Well being
constitutes a part in the PLEEC smart city guideline.
4.1.2
Safety
Safety as a state and the feeling of safety is an important factor in order to have a
social sustainable city. Residents need to feel comfortable to use their area in order to
develop social relationships and to feel connected with their neighbourhood. Safety

Culture
It is wise to preserve cultural heritage and diversity through the urban context. His-
tory, traditions and values need of the cultures of the residents needs to be promoted
by the urbanity of the area. In an city like Copenhagen, where multicultural context
takes place, it is important to grow people with respect to other cultures and create a

Identity of a place is the presence of unique architecture, symbols or activities, that

It is important for a place to possess and preserve this characteristic, in order its
residents to feel connected and proud for their area.

Equity is a fundamental value of a sustainable society.
Social equity exists when

Social cohesion and inclusion
Nowadays, people from different cultural backgrounds need to live together. Refugees
and immigrants search for a better place to live, which leads to raise of diversity in
many large urban areas. Therefore, a goal of a sustainable community should be
to avoid marginalization and the creation of ghettos and to focus on social cohesion
reinforcement. Inclusive public spaces is in target 11.7 of SDG 11.

4.3
Choose indicator framework
The chosen framework is an alternative framework based on the described ones in
the literature review. The framework of social sustainability is divided into three
parts: Amenities and Infrastructure, Social and Cultural Life and Voice and Influ-
ence. The difference of the suggested framework is the connection between the three
categories. Instead of a circular relationship, it is considered that each category is a
prerequisite for the next one, starting from amenities and infrastructure, continuing
to social and cultural life and finishing with voice and influence. Similar to the think-
ing of Maslow’s hierarchy of needs [20], it assumed that the presence of amenities
and infrastructure it is needed in an area, in order people to be able to form healthy
and productive social and cultural life[24]. Decent housing, adequate transportation
system and well-shaped public spaces form an base to build social interaction. A lack
of these basic amenities and infrastructure incommodes people’s lives and their inter-
action with their neighbours. For example, a bad transportation system can cause
delays and long travels and hence reduce people’s free time. Therefore, people do
not have enough time to rest and socialize and so the social relationships are being
affected. This is even more intense in developed countries where there is a lack of
roads, sanitary infrastructure or water facilities. At the next level, the willingness
and ability to participate in the community decisions can not be formed if previously
relationships between neighbours are not shaped and developed and if there is no
attachment with the area. Therefore, there is a hierarchy of the categories of social
sustainability.

5.3.1
Motivation for the project
The project is located in Roskilde and it is a part of Lighting Metropolis projects,

activities. Fostering a healthy lifestyle and a sustainable way of transportation are
ways to affect positively in social sustainability of an area.
5.3.2
The description of the project
The path to Musicon is a path that connects Roskilde train station with the newly
developed cultural district of Musicon. The municipality of Roskilde wanted to create
a path both for bikes and pedestrians, that is inviting and inspiring. The implemen-
tation of smart and interactive lighting throughout the park was an initiative that
supports the functionality of the path as well as creates identity to the area. The
purpose of the project is to promote healthy outdoor activities to the residents of
Roskilde. Furthermore, the path endorses the sustainable image of Musicon, as it
serves as an alternative way to approach the area.
The path is designed to have seven spots with different activities along its length, as
it can be seen in Figure 5.11. The lighting played the most important role in order
to achieve the goals of the project. Smart and interactive lighting technologies were
used to make the path feel shorter, indicate the way to people that do not use the
path in a daily basis and make the route interesting for the daily users.
The main design idea was inspired by the water flow. The colours of the lights change
as they detect motion in a way that resembles water waves. Moreover, the light colour
changes depending on the amount of rain.
There are three layers of lighting that aim different purposes. The functional layer
provides lighting along the path in order to achieve the adequate visibility to the
users. The spatial layer provides additional luminance to the the seven spots, which
are turning spots to increase awareness and divide the path to make it feel shorter. In
addition, different small projects are designed in the seven spots and therefore extra
brightness, makes people feel safe and want to explore the area. Finally, the effect
layer is also positioning at the seven spots and provides dynamic and interactive light-
ing. It makes the path interesting for the daily users and orientates rare users. At
all of the seven spots the light changes colour according to the amount of rain that
falls, while at spots 3, 4, 5 and 6 lighting is also interactive. At square 4 the place

5.5.2

Figure 5.15: Copenhagen International School in Nordhavn.
Copenhagen international school’s Nordhavn campus is located in the new developing

Discussion
Any important points and notes regarding the findings in results and analysis are
further discussed in this chapter.
7.1
Smart solutions in the lighting projects
The lighting projects share some similarities beyond the fact that their focus is light-
ing. Among the goals of each project, it is intended to extent the usage hours of public
spaces. The use of public spaces is a factor which partly indicates the condition of
social sustainability in the area. As it can be seen in the results, the use of public
spaces was increased right after the implementation of the projects.
Smart technologies are used in the favor of social sustainability in the described
lighting projects. Those case studies have shown that the integration of innovative
technologies in the urban context contributes in social sustainability. However, in
order to generalize the statement, it is important to conduct more extended and pe-
riodic researches, since the concept is still relatively new. Curiosity might be the
driving force that makes people want to explore the new features, and therefore, it is
important to investigate whether new technologies continue to keep people using the
space.
Regarding the smart solutions, all of them are related to lighting and they are try-
ing to catch the residents’ attention and make the user’s experience more interesting.
Sensors and innovative technologies are integrated to make the space unique. It is
unclear whether the lighting itself, the interaction with it or both of them have the
most important effect on people. Lighting projects offer visible and direct results
and it is easy for people to understand the concept while sensors make the experi-
ence more interactive and involving. Interactive lighting can serve as a new way to
develop distinctive architecture. In the projects, different styles of lighting were used
to make the place unique, instead of building a new construction. To strengthen this
statement, it could be useful to analyse if these new implementations had any effect
on the number of visitors from other neighborhoods that these places attract.
There is a clear difference in the way children and adults understand and interact
with the smart features.
In the case of Roskilde, which is not made entirely for
==PAGE:84==
70
7 Discussion
children, people do not seem to realize the smart features at all. In the other two
projects, where kids are involved, since they are recreational spaces for the youth, the
smart features are explored from the very first moment the project was released to
the public. Unlike children, adults usually do not have the time or the imagination
to explore their surroundings and most of the time just pass by a place. Therefore,
it is important to value the children’s imagination in the design and their openness
to new experiences. Including children in the design can also make adults to interact
with each other, for example as parents in a playground and hence enhance social
sustainability.
In Albertslund, the attempt to involve the local citizens in the design failed and this
shows a lack of commitment in the area. On the other hand, in Herstedlund School,
where it was already involved in another lighting metropolis project, it was easier to
get teachers and people participate. Consequently, commitment is a value that is
gradually developed. Actions towards this should be taken step by step to increase
the level of commitment.
Overall, urban innovative elements contribute to some extent to social sustainability,
when the associated project goal is related to social sustainability core values. Chil-
dren, as users, seem to be more interested in the smart features than adults, as new
ways of playing are presented and consequently it is advantageous to involve them in
the projects. The fact that the projects use light make the results direct and visible
and also extent the usage hours, which is a very important fact in Denmark. Already
existing infrastructure can be renewed by adding different effects, using technology
and sensors, without changing the landscape and also making the place distinctive.
New opportunities are open in architecture by the use of lighting and smart tech-
nologies. However, the use of smart technologies in the favor of social sustainability
should not be limited in the lighting projects, since the opportunities in this area
are much wider. Smart city initiatives should be encouraged to use technologies to
strengthen social sustainability, instead of focusing only on energy and environment.
7.2
Smart grid and social sustainability
Kalvebod and Nordhavn aspire to become smart cities by following two different ap-
proaches. In Nordhavn, with a top-down approach, first the smart grid was planned
and implemented. After that, attempts to engage people in participatory manage-
ment are tried. Considering that the development is new and there were no people
living in the area before, it was difficult to approach the case differently. Workshops,
exhibitions, and involvement in the smart grid management are ways used to inform
and empower inhabitants. The Copenhagen International School aspires to serve as a
multidisciplinary element in the area. Its vision includes to serve as a school, provide
energy, offer its facilities to the public and be a community hub. However, the con-

7.2 Smart grid and social sustainability

tribution of the school is limited, at the moment. Present regulations prevent energy
grid to use more local energy and the energy community is trying to change that. A
potential high participation of the public in the energy community could assist in
that regulation to change.
Kalvebod possesses a high level of commitment. Collective thinking was the only way
to achieve their goals to become legal, and later on green and sustainable, since they
had to fight for their requests. The planning of the smart grid was their own initiative
by following a bottom-up planning approach. Community thinking preexisted and
it is an important factor that led to these results. Therefore, Kalvebod is already a
social sustainable community which plans and designs towards a green and smart city
concept. However, it is worth mentioning that in Kalvebod, the regulations were not
considered and therefore the procedures are both faster and have limited constrains.
Smart technologies in Kalvebod are not implemented yet in the area, but the planning
phase is the one that so far has assisted in this level of commitment. A new research
must be conducted after the completion of the smart grid installations, in order to
evaluate whether the smart grid continues to assist in community development. So
far, it can be claimed that there are promising signs.
In Nordhavn, unlike Kalvebod, the smart technologies are already there as they were
planned through a top-down approach. However, the residents’ participation and in-
terest about the technologies and their benefits are not as developed as in Kalvebod.
However, an energy community has been built and it is slowly evolving. The smart
technologies inside the school building promote sustainable thinking and it is obvious
from the methods the students are informed about the features.
Smart city initiatives in Nordhavn and Kalvebod brought subjects for discussion to
surface and a reason for people to participate. After all, a city cannot be considered
smart without high participatory levels, because the smart city is much more than
technology and data.
Participation is in the high level of the pyramid and it is considered the most advanced
level to reach in a community. The developed sustainability framework is confirmed
by the cases of Nordhavn and Kalvebod. In Nordhavn they try to increase participa-
tion without having formed social and cultural life and that might be a problematic
approach. In Kalvebod, the sequence was kept and therefore they reach high levels
of participation. Thus, it is crucial to take actions to develop social and cultural life,
before aiming participation.

Limitations of the research
The concept of smart technologies in the urban environment is relatively new and
therefore limited appropriate projects in the area were found. Considering also the
limited time of a thesis and the author’s limited knowledge in the danish language,
the number of projects that could be subject to research was reduced. A study that
will include a more broaden selection of projects in the area would offer a more holis-
tic view of the subject.
Moreover, the projects with smart technologies in Greater Copenhagen are new and
therefore are not yet evaluated, which made the research have some gaps, but it is
easy to use the methodology when the evaluations are available and extract more
solid conclusions.
Due to the limited findings regarding the evaluation results, in some projects empir-
ical methods and assumptions, as well as authors predictions, were used and hence
the results might be subjective.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The predictions that the percentage of the world’s population that lives in cities is
going to be increased, raises the need of alternative city management.
More and
more smart technologies are used in the urban development processes, as the concept
of smart city is gaining ground. Studies are made for the contribution of the afore-
mentioned concept in environmental and economic sustainability, but the knowledge
regarding social sustainability is still limited. Therefore, the purpose of this research
was to examine the influence of smart city elements on social sustainability.
Urban social sustainability values were determined, such as welfare, safety, culture,
identity, equity, social cohesion and inclusion, sense of community, participation and
lifelong learning. These values were chosen from the literature review and integrated
both smart city aspects and targets of the SDG 11.
Several indicators to reflect
these values where selected and divided in three major categories; Amenities and
Infrastructure, Social and Cultural Life and Voice and Influence. The relationships
between these categories formed a pyramid, indicating each level of the pyramid is a
prerequisite for the next one, starting from the bottom to the top.
The preliminary research of the case studies showed the areas of effect. The lighting
projects used smart features to achieve their goals which are related to social sustain-
ability. Smart elements were expected to affect distinctive architecture, recreational
spaces, relationships between neighbors and use of public spaces. Furthermore, two
projects associated with smart grid were expected to affect less the social sustainabil-
ity.
By using the indicator framework, the projects were evaluated towards their effect
on social sustainability.
The use of smart technologies in the urban environment
contributes to increase the levels of social sustainability. Mainly, the smart features
increased the use of public spaces and offer a different way to increase the identity
of the area. The importance of the interaction between children with the design was
identified, since adults did not observe the new features. Furthermore, it was con-
cluded that the level of commitment needs time and effort to be developed.
The smart grid projects revealed that the smart grid initiatives caused the creation
of energy communities and actions to increase the level of commitment. Top-down
==PAGE:90==
76
8 Conclusion
and bottom up approach both work depending on the case’s background, with new
developments needed regarding the top down methods. In cases where the level of
commitment is high, a bottom up approach could be more beneficial. The engagement
of people in Kalvebod helped them gain matters even beyond the energy regulations,
something that can be an inspiration for the Nordhavn Energy Lab. If more people
participate in the decisions making phase, there is a bigger chance to achieve benefi-
cial changes in the formal energy regulations.
Furthermore, it is important to follow the sequence of the framework, as it is difficult
to develop voice and influence without the previous categories settled.
However, it is important to note that smart cities need to take actions for social
sustainability and gradually develop its aspects in order to form successful projects.
Participation is a crucial smart city aspect, but it needs time to be reached. Smart
technologies can be introduced in the urban design focusing on social sustainability,
especially for playgrounds and recreational spaces meant for kids.
Overall, smart city developments is a promising concept, that can enhance social
sustainability. The combination of technology and smart principles can bring new
insights to the urban planning and development.

Future Work
The study could be supplemented with additional information in order to be more
accurate and completed. Firstly, the limitations of the study could be eliminated by
adding the evaluation information in the results. Afterwards a number of practises

Evaluation Results of path to Musicon in Roskilde
The evaluation was conducted by Albertslund’s Municipality in October 2018. The
translation from danish to english was made by the author of the thesis. The relevent
to this thesis results are presented in this part of the appendix.
B.1.1
Aim of the evaluation
The evaluation should clarify whether the path system - and new lighting - has affected
the number of pedestrians and cyclists.
In addition, the evaluation should show
whether there are changes in the citizen’s general impression of the path and show
any effects on the pattern of use. Finally, a number of questions are asked to describe
perception and effects of the new lighting.
B.1.2
Overall conclusion of the evaluation
A. How has the pathway affected the number of pedestrians and cyclists?
Cyclists in autumn 2017 and in late summer 2018 show a growth of 13.9 %. Pedes-
trian traffic has increased by 11.1 % from 2017 to 2018, before and after construction
of path and lighting. The qualitative part of the evaluation is based on two interview
rounds. The two groups of interviewed, in 2017 and 2018, can be considered as rea-
sonably similar in terms of age and gender.
B: What are the citizens’ general impression of the path, and what effect
do the changes and new constructions have the path connection had for
the usage pattern?
The evaluation shows that the new installations have influenced the citizens to change
behavior and / or attitude to the area. Supplemented by several very positive qual-
itative statements, it is therefore reasonable to assume that the project has had a
positive effect on the general impression of the path.
C: What is the citizens’ perception of the effect of the lighting that is new
to the northern stretch?
The new plant has not changed the overall impression of the lighting. However, a
number can be determined significant effects on the perception of the lighting.
