Abstract
This thesis explores the improvement of the search strategies in Multi­Agent Path Find­
ing problems by introducing some novelty­based algorithms. Some of the classical ap­
proaches such as BFS and DFS are analyzed in this work, as well as IW, SIW and BFWS.
Furthermore, the thesis presents several original contributions, including IW­H (IW with
Heuristic Comparison), RPS (Reintroducing Pruned States) and OCNA (Ordering by the
Count of New Atoms). IW­H algorithm differs from BFWS by modifying the way heuris­
tic comparison between similar states is made. RPS focuses on reinserting previously
pruned states that may still contribute to promising solutions, while OCNA prioritizes those
states with a greater amount of new information into the search. Derelaxation techniques
are also explored to initially simplify complex domains by focusing on specific properties
or objects, solving these simpler subproblems, and subsequently using their solutions to
guide the overall planning process. Additionally, several heuristics that use novelty to
guide and improve the search process are presented.
The results demonstrate that integrating novelty information with basic heuristic guidance
can substantially improve search performance. More specifically, in problems where both
BFWS and IW­H can lead to a solution, IW­H does so faster than BFWS, and both BFWS
and IW­H faster than any other algorithm. Techniques such as controlled expansion help
reduce solution times and make it feasible to control complex environments. Moreover,
methods like RPS achieve solutions with lower final width, and OCNA achieved to solve
the maximum number of problems. Additionally, this work highlights the effectiveness of
complementary techniques to address complex MAPF problems.

Introduction
Automated planning and pathfinding play a central role in artifial intelligence, robotics and
specially logistics. Among these, MAPF has gained a particular attention due to its direct
applications in different multi­robot systems scenarios. MAPF problems involve comput­
ing collision­free paths for multiple agents operating in shared environments, where each
agent must move from its initial position to a designated goal while avoiding conflicts with
other agents and obstacles. The complexity of these problems grows exponentially with
the number of agents and goals, making the efficient search strategies essential.
Traditional search algorithms such as BFS and DFS provide exploration on the state space
but often face complex challenges due to the large branching factors these problems have.
While these methods guarantee optimal solutions or reach deeply into the search space,
they lack of guidance to prioritize efficient paths and often explore irrelevant states.
To address this limitation, novelty­based search algorithms have emerged as an alterna­
tive, introducing the concept of novelty in states to guide the exploration. The algorithm
IW, for example, focuses on expanding states that introduce previously unseen combina­
tions of atoms or state features. SIW, on the other hand, builds upon this idea of decom­
posing complex problems into subproblems which are solved sequentially, allowing more
efficient search in domains with multiple subgoals. BFWS faces the search process by
integrating heuristic guidance into the novelty definition, prioritizing states based on both
novelty and heuristic values. IW+ and IW++ modifies IW by introducing more parameters.
Apart from analyzing these existing methods, this thesis introduces several original con­
tributions aimed to improve performance of search algorithms:
• IW­H: A variation of BFWS that adjusts pruning criteria by comparing heuristic val­
ues in states containing same atoms, pruning those with higher or equal heuristic
value.
• RPS: A technique designed to reinsert previously pruned states that may still con­
tribute to optimal solutions.
• OCNA: An approach that prioritizes the exploration of states contributing with the
highest number of new atoms.

Fundamental Concepts
This chapter presents a formal mathematical formulation for MAPF problems. All the
essential components of the problem are defined, including formal definitions, states and
goal specifications, the hospital domain and movement constraints. These definitions
provide the necessary information for the analysis of later search algorithms.
2.1
Problem Definition
Let Σ = (S, s0, Sg, A, f) (see [1]) be the classical model of planning, where S is a set of
states, s0 ∈S the initial state, Sg ⊆S a set containing goal states, A a set of actions
(where A(s) are all the actions applicable in the state s), and f is a function where
f : S × A →S
f(s, a) = s′
being a ∈A(s) and s, s′ ∈S. Note that s′ is the result of applying the action a in state s.
Let s0 ∈S and sn ∈Sg be the initial state and the goal state, respectively. We denote
π = a0, a1, . . . , an a solution to the problem, where that sequence of actions generates
s0, s1, . . . , sn a sequence of states. Note that, ∀i ∈{0, . . . , n −1}, f(si, ai) = si+1, ai ∈
A(si), si, si+1 ∈S.
Let S be the state space, and let fo : N →S be a function representing the order in which
states are explored by a given search strategy, where fo(i) is the i­th state explored.
Then, for any state s, the index of s is defined as
idx(s) = min{i ∈N | fo(i) = s}
Definition 2.1.1. We define the set of states explored before s as
Ss = {s′ ∈S | s′ = fo(j), j < idx(s)}
This definition 2.1.1 is adapted for the purpose of this thesis and it is not standard literature.

Let the branching factor of the state s be the number of all the possible actions that are
applicable in that state. The branching factor of s is denoted by |A(s)|.
Another important concept in search algorithms is the frontier. A frontier Fr is the set of
all searched states that have been generated, but not yet explored. This frontier is crucial
to maintain and update efficiently the search. Commonly, this frontier consist of a queue,
where algorithms can add states, pop them, or empty the queue.
Directed graphs are widely used to represent states and actions in these types of problem.
MAPF problems can also be defined as a pair M = (G, Ag), where G is a graph G =
(V, E), and Ag is a set of fixed agents. For each agent agi ∈Ag, we can also define agi
as agi = (si, gi), where si is the starting position and gi is the goal location, with si, gi ∈V .
Note that, in the graph G, the vertices V are states in S, where the initial state s0 ∈S is a
root vertex in V , and edges in E are actions a ∈A. The number of edges emerging from
the vertex is the branching factor of that state. The depth of a state s is defined as the
number of states on the path from s0 to s following a single branch in the graph G.

Let a MAPF problem be represented as P = {F, I, O, G}, being F a set containing
boolean variables (called atoms or propositions), I, G ⊆F sets of atoms representing
the initial and goal state respectively, and O being the actions.
An atom contains essential information about a state. These atoms are Boolean (meaning
that their value can be either True or False), and a set of True atoms represents a state
s ∈S.
All actions a ∈O are represented in the STRIPS language as a set of preconditions and
effects. The preconditions indicate which atoms need to be True in order to be able to
apply that action, and the effects are the result of applying the action and can change the
value of different atoms to True or False.
More formally, let s ∈S be a state. Then s = {p1, p2, . . . , pn}, pi ∈F is a set containing

f(s, a) = s′ = {s ∪Add(a) \ Del(a)}

Hospital Domain
Throughout this work, all the different algorithms and methods explained are going to be
applied in a type of MAPF problems called Hospital Domain problems (see [2]). These
problems were introduced in order to solve agent box problems, where the main purpose
is to satisfy certain position­based goals.

2.3.1
The levels
The levels of these problems are going to consist of a grid­cell environment, where each
cell can be occupied by either an agent, a box, a wall, or not be occupied. Agents are
typically indicated with numbers from 0 to 9, while boxes are indicated with letters from A
to Z. On each cell, there can also be a subgoal (typically colored as yellow), indicating
that the agent/box needs to be at that cell in order for that subgoal to be satisfied. The
goal cell has a number/letter, indicating which object needs to satisfy that subgoal.
Both agents and boxes have different colors C = {c1, c2, . . . }. The primary color is the
color blue. The agents can only move boxes of their own color, no other colors.
All states have an initial state and a goal state, and it is assumed that there is a solution
to that level. A solution is a sequence of actions­states, where the final state is the goal
state.
The atoms (or propositions pi introduced in Section 2.2) that each level must have are:
• agent(i), box(b) ­ indicating that i is an agent and b is a box
• color(i,c), color(b,c') ­ indicating that agent i has color c and box b has color c′
• wall(x,y) ­ indicating that there is a wall in position (x, y)
Additionally, the initial state must have the following properties:
• agentAt(i, (x,y)), boxAt(b, (x,y)) ∀i∧∀b ­ indicating the position of the agents
i and boxes b at the beginning
• free(x,y) ∀(x, y) ∈Grid | ∄agentAt(i, (x,y))∧∄boxAt(b, (x,y)) ­ indicating
all the free cells in the initial state
The goal state consists of atoms of the form
• agentAt(i, (x,y)), boxAt(i, (x,y)), for some i and b ­ indicating the positions
at which the agents and boxes need to be in order to achieve the solution.

2.3.3
Conflicts
When solving MAPF problems, one consideration to take into account is that that no
conflicts are happening between the different objects (agents and boxes) in the level.
A conflict occurs when two or more agents attempt to occupy incompatible cells at the
same timestep. In this section, these possible conflicts are explained. Note that red
arrows indicate movement actions, whereas green arrows indicate Push/Pull actions. The
possible conflicts are:
1. Exchanging positions
In this conflict, atoms agentAt(a, (x, y))) and agentAt(a′, (x′, y′))) convert into
agentAt(a′, (x, y))) and agentAt(a, (x′, y′))) in 1 step.
2. 2 agents occupying the same position
In this conflict, both agents agentAt(a, (x, y))) and agentAt(a′, (x′, y′))) convert
into agentAt(a, (x′′, y′′))) and agentAt(a′, (x′′, y′′))), leading to an impossible state.

Algorithms
This chapter presents the main algorithms employed in this work to address MAPF prob­
lems. Each algorithm is built upon a different strategy for exploring the state space, and
they differ in several key dimensions (such as optimality, complexity,...). In the context of
MAPF, selecting an appropriate algorithm depends on the specific context of the problem
and on the specific constraints and goals, such as whether optimal solutions are required
or whether it is preferred suboptimal but faster solutions. The algorithms involved in this
section serve as building blocks for more advanced methods.
The non­original algorithms discussed in this work are presented from Section 3.1 to Sec­
tion 3.5. Specifically, BFS and DFS are introduced in Section 3.1, followed by IW and
SIW in Sections 3.2 and 3.3, respectively. The algorithms IW+ and IW++, proposed by
[1], are described in Section 3.4. Finally, algorithm BFWS is presented in Section 3.5.
The original contributions of this work are presented from Section 3.6 to Section 3.9. In
particular, Section 3.6 introduces IW­H algorithm, followed by RPS algorithm in Section
3.7. Section 3.8 presents the OCNA algorithm, and finally, the derelaxation algorithms
are described in Section 3.9.
3.1
BFS and DFS
Two of the most elemental search algorithms in this type of problems are called BFS and
DFS.
3.1.1
BFS
Breadth­First Search (BFS) is an uninformed search algorithm, which explores the state
space level by level. It begins at the initial state (or root node of the graph), and expands
and visits all nodes at depth d before proceeding to depth d + 1.
It uses a FIFO (First In First Out) queue to manage its frontier. At each step, the algorithm
removes the first node from the queue, expands it (generating the possible successor
states), and appends these successors to the end of the queue. In other words, the
frontier of the algorithm BFS satisfies
F = [s1, s2, . . . , sn],
F[i] = si, F[j] = sj

i ≤j ⇐⇒depth(si) ≤depth(sj)

3.1.2
DFS
On the other hand, Depth­First Search (DFS) is an algorithm that explores the state space
as fast as possible along a branch before backtracking. The queue follows a LIFO struc­
ture (Last In First Out). Starting from the initial state, it iteratively dives down a single path
until it encounters a terminal state or a dead end. At this point, the algorithms backtracks
to the previous state, and continues through alternative paths unexplored.
3.2
IW
Iterative Width (IW) is a novel search algorithm based on the concept of novelty (see
[3]). Rather than exploring all nodes based on their depth, it explores states based on the
uniqueness of the information they contain, looking at the atoms of each state.
The novelty of a state s is defined by the smallest number k such that exists a tuple of size
k of atoms in s that has not yet been encountered in any previous states. More formally:
Definition 3.2.1. Given a state s, the novelty of s is defined as
novelty(s) = min{k ∈N | ∃t = {p1, . . . , pk} ∈s, t /∈SAs}
where p1, . . . , pk are atoms of s.
IW(k) only expands states whose novelty is less or equal than k, pruning the other states

depth based on the novelty.

3.6.1
Ordering in the frontier
Once states are identified as novel, it’s important to prioritize them effectively in the frontier
to ensure the search proceed efficiently. One way to order the states is by the values
given some heuristics. The states can enter the frontier, and then be introduced in their
respective position following an order.
More formally, a frontier F is ordered by some heuristics h ⇐⇒
∀s, s′ ∈F , F[s] < F[s′] ⇒h(s) < h(s′)
Note that F[s] indicates the position of the state s in the frontier F. The heuristics h can
either be a composite function, defined as a weighted sum of multiple heuristics (more
formally h(s) = α1 · h1(s) + · · · + αn · hn(s)), or a tuple heuristic values, where states are
compared lexicographically (i.e., first by the first component, then by the second, and so
on, until a difference it’s found).
Some of the heuristics that can be used are found in Section 5. Making sure that an
effective order is made is essential to find an optimal solution in terms of time and length.

3.9
Derelaxation algorithms
This section introduces a fundamentally different strategy, which it is named derelaxation.
The core idea is to simplify (or relax) the original problem by temporarely modifying the
preconditions, effects or atoms of certain actions or states, solve the relaxed problem more
efficiently, and then use the resulting plans to guide the search in the original problem.
Concretely, a relaxation changes the domain description, so that the resulting level is
easier to solve. Although a solution to the relaxed problem may be infeasible in the original
domain (e.g., due to collision or additional constraints not seen in the relaxed problem), it
provides useful structure or heuristics that accelerate search.
For example, consider the action MoveNorth
• Precondition: agentAt(0,(x,y)) & free(x,y-1)
• Action: Move(0,N)
• Effect: agentAt(0,(x,y-1)) & free(x,y) & ¬free(0,(x,y-1))
A simple relaxation would remove the requirement free(x,y-1) from the precondition,
allowing the agent to move through occupied cells. Although this produces an invalid
plan in the real level, a solution can be found much more quickly and then used to inform
the full search.

3.9.3
OBA (Obstruction Based Attention)
OBA combines classical search with a relaxation strategy that focuses only on the objects
essential to reaching the goal. It was introduced by [1].
The algorithm works as follows:
1. From the goal description, it collects every object (agents a or boxes b) that appears
in a goal state of the form agentAt(a,(x,y)) or boxAt(b,(x,y)) into the set of
minimum relevant object m(g)
2. Starting from the initial state s0, the algorithm constructs a relaxed state sr0 by re­
moving all object expect those in m(g). This relaxed state contains only objects
potentially necessary to achieving the goal.
3. OBA will apply the search algorithm (BFS, DFS, IW,...) to the relaxed level, gener­
ating a sequence of relaxed states {sr0, . . . , sri}.
4. At each step i, let sri be the relaxed state and si be the corresponding full state (with
the original objects). If an action can be applied in sri but not in si, this indicates
that some object not in m(g) (that is, ∃agent a | a /∈m(g) ∪∃box b | b /∈m(g))
is blocking the search. As a consequence, that object will be added to m(g), and
also inserted it into sri. Resume the relaxed state search from this new updated
configuration.
5. Once a path has reach to a goal in the relaxed states, it can replay the same actions
in the full state to obtain a valid solution
By continually expanding m(g) only when absolutely necessary, and using an effective
enough search algorithm with a good heuristic, OBA search concentrates on objects that
truly matter for reaching the goal, improving efficiency by ignoring irrelevant objects.
In the figure 3.5, we can observe an example of OBA algorithm. The left column represent
the full state, whereas the right column the relaxed level. Note that, after B is encountered,
it remains in the relaxed level.

Additional features
While the core of this thesis focuses on novelty based search strategies, the techniques
discussed here address complementary challenges that arises in complex environments.
This chapter presents a set of additional techniques designed to improved the efficiency
of the search process. This efficiency can be obtained through different techniques, al­
though we are going to focus on the branching factor, the atoms, and methods to compute
distance.
4.1
Controlling expansion
In multi­agent environments, the branching factor can grow exponentially with the number
of agents and available actions. Typically, each agent can perform four directional move­
ments (north, south, east and west), but many domains include more complex actions
such as push and pull boxes. The combined actions space per state is computed as
|Actions||Agents|
For instance, with 4 agents and 4 actions per agent, the branching factor is 44 = 256. In
more complex levels involving 9 agents and 9 actions per agent (e.g., 4 moves, 4 push/pull
actions, and 1 NoOp), the branching factor grows to 99 = 387,420,489. Exploring all these
joint actions per state is computationally expensive.
To address this issue, we implement 3 pruning strategies to control expansion: one based
on randomness, the other guided by heuristics, and the last one based on individual ac­
tions.
4.1.1

This method attempts to reduce expansion through sampling of joint actions. The proce­
dure is as follows:
1. Generate the complete set of possible joint actions (i.e., all combinations of
individual actions)

To mitigate the shortcomings of the random method, this approach introduces a heuristic­
based ordering. Instead of shuffling randomly the list of joint actions in step 2, the list is
sorted using a heuristic function h : S →N ∪{0}. This ensures that the most promising
joint actions are considered first. The algorithm is then pruning less useful combinations,
and only taking into account the most efficient ones.
4.1.3

4.2
Precomputed Distance Heuristic
In many search algorithms, the Manhattan distance 5.2 (or any other distance heuristic)
is a widely used heuristic due to its simplicity. However, to avoid repeatedly computing
the distance between two point during the search every time is needed, we precomputed
all pairwise distance in advanced. This is done by generating a distance matrix where
each entry stores the actual shortest path length between two reachable cells, taking into
account walls but no obstacles (due to the fact that walls cannot be moved, but obstacles
can).
The matrix is built using a flood fill algorithm. For each cell in the level, we explore its
reachable neighbors layer by layer: immediate neighbors (distance 1), their neighbors

==PAGE:45==
Sergio Monzón
Master thesis

(distance 2), and so on. Each reachable cell’s distance is recorded in the matrix. Once
computed, this matrix allows us to retrieve the real distance between any 2 points in
constant time O(1) complexity. By doing this preprocessing step once at the beginning, it
is eliminated the need to recompute distances during the search, resulting in a significant
performance gain, especially in large or complex maps.
4.3
Renew atoms every m steps
In certain environments, agents or boxes may need to pass through the same cells mul­
tiple times during the solution. This encounters a challenge for novelty based algorithms,
because these algorithms only consider a state novel the first time a particular configura­
tion of atoms is encountered. The following visits to the same cell by the same object are
typically pruned.
To address this issue, we introduce a mechanism to renew atoms periodically. At search
depths d, with d being multiples of a given parameter m, atoms are refreshed so that the
previously seen configurations can be re­evaluated as novel. This method allows to revisit
key positions, without being pruned, as needed.
More formally, in a given state s at depth d, the atoms describing the position of agents a
and boxes a are redefined as:
• agentAt(a, (x, y)) →agentAt(a, (x, y), ⌊d/m⌋)
• boxAt(b, (x, y)) →boxAt(b, (x, y), ⌊d/m⌋)
By inserting the value ⌊d/m⌋to the atom, we reset the novelty evaluation every m steps,
allowing repeated use of key cells during deeper levels of the search.

We begin by analyzing the execution time. The results show that the color­based re­
laxation algorithm outperformed the others, followed by the box­based relaxation. This
suggests that, in domains with multiple colors, relaxing the problem by color leads to bet­
ter performance than relaxing by individual boxes. When examining other metrics such as
stat space size and solution length, the difference among the algorithms are minimal, indi­
cating that all three approaches found similarly optimal solutions, although, as mentioned,
color­based achieved these solutions more quickly.
These results suggest that performing relaxation based on color could lead to better per­
formance, as relaxing based on individual boxes can lead to multiple conflicts between
individual solutions.

Conclusion
This thesis has examined how novelty­based search algorithms can significantly improve
the efficiency of solving MAPF problems. Starting form the limitations of classical meth­
ods such as BFS and DFS, the research explored how incorporating state novelty with
heuristic information can lead to more informed and effective search strategies.
Experiments confirm that, the original contributions such as IW­H, RPS and OCNA, reduce
the search times and solve more challenging levels. These algorithms outperform clas­
sical approaches by selectively expanding states that introduce new information,focusing
the search on promising areas,. . . Specifically, we have observed that, in levels where
both BFWS and IW­H could find a solution, in general IW­H did it faster than BFWS. Ad­
ditionally, RPS could find solutions with the lowest final width, in comparison to the other
algorithms. On the other hand, OCNA was able to find solutions to the maximum number
of problems.
Despite these advances, there are certain limitations. The effectiveness of methods incor­
porating heuristics, such as BFWS and IW­H, is influenced by the quality of the heuristic
used. It is of great importance to select and design an optimal heuristic function. Ad­
ditionally, derelaxation techniques depend of appropriate decomposition of the problem,
which may not be straightforward for all domains. Managing the trade­off of the pruning
aggressiveness also remains a challenge, as insufficient pruning can lead to increased
computational cost, as seen with IW+ and IW++.
In summary, this thesis demonstrates that novelty­based algorithms, complemented with
heuristic guidance provide a powerful framework for addressing the complexity of MAPF
problems. By balancing exploration, heuristic evaluation, and problem decomposition,
these approaches enable efficient planning in large, multi­agent domains, contributing
valuable insights to the field of automated planning and multi­agent systems.

Future work
Some of the algorithms introduced in this work would benefit from further research. Pos­
sible directions for improvements include:
• Heuristic study: Since both BFWS and IW­H, two of the most promising algorithms
in this work, rely on heuristic functions, it would be of interest to investigate which
heuristic functions can improve effectiveness of these algorithms. Potential can­
didates may include goal­count, manhattan distance, bn,qn&qb (Section 5.3, Fast
Forward heuristic (see [6]), Fast Downward heuristic (see [7]), . . .
• Parameter m in RenewAtoms algorithm: In this work, the value of m = 50 was
chosen uniformly for all levels in the RenewAtoms every m steps algorithm (see Sec­
tion 4.3). Further research could analyze how to select the optimal m dynamically,
potentially relating it to the complexity or characteristics of the levels.
• RPS criteria: Currently, in RPS algorithm, states are reincorporated into the frontier
only if their parent state was not initially pruned. Future work could explore alterna­
tive reinsertion strategies that might use information from atoms or heuristic values
to make the reinsertion process more effective.
