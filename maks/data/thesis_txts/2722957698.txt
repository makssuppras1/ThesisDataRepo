Abstract
The behaviours of Building Integrated Photovoltaics need to be investigated as new meth-
ods in module mounting are utilised along with the addition of using coloured panels.
These variables could have a large impact on the thermal properties of a system and with
increasing popularity of BIPVs they need to be understood for the technologies growth.
Temperature modelling is an important part in designing and utilising photovoltaic sys-
tems for it directly relates to the performance of the system. There are multiple variables
that affect the temperature of the system and with a change in environment around the
mounting construction the thermal properties can experience different behaviours.
The temperature models from Sandia National Laboratories were used and an analysis
was done on the dependencies used in calculations and the modelled temperatures were
investigated in depth with comparison to measured values as well as the inputs used for
the model. An experiment was carried out that utilized six single cell glass/glass coupons
with measurements of the cell and back-of-module temperature. The coupons used had
slightly different construction to analyse the affect of having varying number of layers
within them and the affect of using coloured coupons was analysed as well. Two different
studies were conducted where the mounting structure was altered to analyse the differ-
ence in having rear ventilation for the coupons and no rear ventilation, with insulation
placed behind them.
The experiment took place during autumn and winter in 2022 where the coupons were
mounted vertically on a South facing plywood wall with a datalogger for measurement
collection. Clear sky periods were used in the analysis to achieve a near steady state
temperature within the coupons, limiting inconsistencies in the data acquired.
Sandias Photovoltaic Array Performance models had a tendency to underestimate the
back-of-module temperature but a difference in the coupons construction was identified.
The addition of colour changed the thermal behaviour of the coupon skewing the temper-
ature assessment. Calculations aimed to calibrate the model to the module construction
and mounting configuration gave an indication that the coloured coupons needed an al-
tered model but lack of data collected deemed the results unreliable. The Sandia tem-
perature models had the largest errors in estimating the non ventilated temperatures with
the largest offset of 0.8K in cell temperature estimation and over 10K in back-of-module
estimation.

Due to its economical attraction solar PV has seen an accelerated forecast increase and
with that expectation different methods are emerging with varying use cases. A decen-
tralised method of rooftop solar brings the power production closer to the user and the
method of Building Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV) has taken that concept with the added
feature of an aesthetically pleasing design for buildings. For a new method such as BIPVs
an investigation is needed to understand the implications of integrating the solar panels
into the structure of the building.
1.1
Motivation
The behaviour of BIPVs differ with the modules type and mounting structure used. In
a vertically mounted construction the modules can have a limited cooling due to wind
obstructions and/or limited ventilation around the modules back leading to an increase in
temperature.
The thermal behaviour of the vertically mounted BIPV is crucial to understand for the
continued growth of the technology for better modelling and analysing tools for use in
designing a BIPV system. The affects of using an altered PV panel, with the additions of
colours, can impact the absorption of the module and change the thermal properties of
the system.
A look into the performance of a PV temperature model on BIPV installments will shine a
light on the differences and a conclusion on where the differences lie can be drawn.
1.2
Report structure
The report provides an analysis of a temperature model commonly used in photovoltaics
on vertically mounted BIPVs in two different mounting configurations with differently con-
structed panels.
In chapter 2 the theory behind the topic at hand is explained and described with a focus
on how PV modules are constructed and the thermal behaviour of the technology. In
chapter 3 the experimental set up used in measurements is described as well as the data
acquisition and handling with methods and assumptions made throughout explained. In
chapter 4 the results were presented and analysed where the temperature model used
was analysed from different perspectives to get an in depth view of how it behaved when
comparing to measured values. Chapter 5 is the conclusion of the experiment where the
main takeaway is presented and discussed to give a bigger picture of the results.

Theory
Theory introduces the concepts used in the analysis. With an introduction and description

2.1
Solar Irradiance
The earth receives an irradiance outside the atmosphere, also known as power density,
from the sun of 1367W/m2 called the solar constant [4]. Solar irradiance on a horizontal
surface is often measured in Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) which is the sum of Angle
of Incidence (AOI) corrected Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI), the incoming energy that
has a direct path from the sun to a surface, and Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI), the
incoming energy that is scattered in the atmosphere or reflected before hitting the surface
in question, as illustrated in figure 2.1. Clouds both absorb and scatter solar irradiance
depending on wavelength and cloud type leading to a large uncertainty when it comes to
the effect a cloud cover has on irradiance received on the surface [5].
Figure 2.1: Illustration of Solar Irradiance’s path to a specific surface [6]
With a tilted surface a measurement of Irradiance in Plane of Array (POA) is used and
ground reflected irradiance becomes a factor in DHI. The Angle of Incidence for a tilted
surface is affected by the tilt angle (θ in figure 2.1) along with the zenith and azimuth angle,
as opposed to solely being affected by the zenith angle for GHI due to 0° tilt and symmetry
in the azimuth angle.
The magnitude of the Solar Irradiance hitting a surface is influenced by the Air Mass
(AM), a variable comparing the distance the light has travelled inside the atmosphere to
the shortest distance possible, when the sun is directly overhead. When the light travels
a longer distance inside the atmosphere more of the photons get absorbed and scattered
by particles and therefore the portion of DNI decreases and GHI increases [4]. As the
absorption lessens the intensity of the light and the scattering diffuses the light, less energy
reaches the surface compared to direct sunlight.

2.2
Photovoltaic Technologies
The first silicon photovoltaic (PV) solar cell was developed in 1954 by Daryl Chapin, Calvin
Fuller, and Gerald Pearson in Bell Laboratories based on the pn-junction in semiconduc-
tors [4]. The pn-junction is a boundary between a p- and n-doped semiconductor, that
are semiconductors that have been altered by introducing an impurity to add holes and
electrons in the crystal. Following the initial silicon solar cell development has been made
where the solar energy to electricity conversion efficiency has increased, averaging ap-
proximately 20% in current mono-crystalline solar cells [7], while the price has decreased
vastly [8]. This has opened up different use cases for solar cells as different types of solar
modules are introduced.
2.2.1
Semiconductor Physics
Semiconductors are used in Photovoltaic cells and a vast majority of the semiconduc-
tors used is silicon [9]. Semiconductors have the property of being both conducting and
insulating depending on the circumstance. As seen in figure 2.2 they have a band gap
between that of insulators and metals.
Figure 2.2: Band gap diagram for insulators, semiconductors, and metals [10]
Semiconductors have the desired property of being photosensitive meaning that the ma-
terial has a response to being exposed to light. If an incoming photon, with energy ex-
ceeding the band gap, is absorbed in the crystal its energy excites an electron to rise
from the valance band to the conduction band, creating a temporary electron-hole pair
before recombining. Using a pn-junction the recombination of the electron-hole pair can
be circumvented by the electric field formed in the space charge region, as seen in figure
2.3.

2.2.2
Relevance of Cell Temperature Assessment
Like other material that absorbs sunlight, solar cells get warmer with incoming solar irradi-
ance due to the excess energy being converted to thermal energy. The incoming energy
is converted into electricity and thermal energy and with efficiencies of 20% the remain-
ing 80% of the incoming energy gets converted into thermal energy that dissipates in the
module and surrounding environment.
The performance of a photovoltaic system has a dependency on operating cell temper-
ature where the short circuit current of the cell increases slightly with rising temperature
but the open circuit voltage decreases. This dependency results in a decreased power
output with higher operating cell temperature, as seen in figure 2.4, making temperature
assessment in PV systems crucial during the design process. The temperature coefficient
in products on the market ranges from just under 0.3% up to 0.5% power loss for each
degree the cell reaches above 25°C, the Standard Test Conditions (STC) temperature,
commonly expressed in units of %/◦C [11, 12].
Figure 2.4: A power-voltage curve for different cell temperatures [13]
2.2.3
Building Integrated Photovoltaics
Building Integrated Photovoltaics or BIPVs is a concept of a PV mounting configuration
to integrate the solar module into the infrastructure of the building, replacing a construc-
tion product. A PV installation is therefore considered to be BIPV if it provides weather
protection, structural integrity, or thermal insulation among others [14]. They can come in
different configurations, used as curtain walls, rooftops, roof shades, and more.
Depending on the configuration of the BIPVs they can have more limited back ventilation

In fabricating PV modules a construction to protect the fragile solar cell is needed. A

PV module but there are varying types of materials used. As an example a back sheet

The materials have specific roles in the PV module as the glass and encapsulant have an
additive for UV light protection, as it degrades the encapsulant. The frame and glass/back-
sheet serve to protect against moisture as well as provide a mechanical stability [18].
A tempered, low iron glass is usually used in PV modules due to its high transparency

The predominantly used encapsulant is Ethylene Vinyl Acetate (EVA) although in recent

because it induces degradation and corrosion in modules, thus decreasing its lifetime.
POE does not form acetic acid and has the additional property of being semi transparent
in the UV region giving it an advantage over the typically used EVA [21].
The module is constructed with these materials layered and placed into a laminator. The
laminator uses heat and vacuum to adhere the layers together, enabling cross-linking
in the encapsulant to make a mechanically and thermally stable compound [22]. The

Convection happens at an intersection between a solid material and flowing gas or fluid

vertical boundary layer, for example a wall, shows this effect as air in close proximity to

accurately use the energy balance the system in question needs to be defined as well

• Heat generation originates in the solar cell
• One dimensional heat transfer away from the cell
• Constant properties of the materials
• The system is in a steady state condition

wind gradient where the wind speed increases logarithmically with height as it experiences
less surface friction [29].

equipment was used to measure the necessary parameters.
Irradiance sensors
Pyranometers, illustrated in figure 2.10(a), are used to measure solar irradiance with a
180° field of view by utilizing a central black absorber inside two domes of glass. The
glass domes serve to protect the sensor and provide a cosine response as the reflection
losses are equal for all angles. The irradiance is measured as the temperature difference
between the hot- and cold junction of a thermopile placed beneath the black absorber. A
thermopile is a series of thermocouples connected to give a voltage output proportional
to the temperature difference where the hot junction is connected to the base of the black
absorber and the cold junction is connected to a heat sink towards the bottom of the
device [31]. The pyranometer has a flat spectral sensitivity and covers the largest part of
the solar spectrum received on the ground, in the 285-3000nm range.

2.8
PV Temperature Models
Models are widely used in engineering to assess feasibility of projects on location. Ana-
lytical and Empirical models are two types of models that can be utilized, where analytical
models use physical properties and laws of physics to predict a behaviour of a system and
empirical models use experimental discoveries and observations to derive a formula to
estimate a particular behaviour. These models in solar PV use physical characteristics of
systems to estimate energy output of systems with weather data from the location. To be
able to calculate the output of a system temperature estimations are needed to account
for power loss. As BIPVs experience different environments, with less ventilation and
possibly more wind obstruction as compared to open solar farms investigation is needed
for validating and if needed fit existing models to its characteristics.
2.8.1

electrical, thermal and optical characteristics empirical model, published in 2004, that was

describes the model for estimating the back-of-module temperature, often referred to as

4.3.3
Summary
As a limited amount of data was collected during the experiment the results from calculat-
ing the coefficients for calibration purposes were not conclusive due to lack of confidence
and large uncertainties. The results gave an indication that coefficients for all BIPVs with
the same module and mounting configuration would not suffice as a large discrepancy
was detected with difference in colour. This difference was considered to be caused by
differences in optical properties of the coloured interlayers with considerable differences
in reflection.
As future work this experiment could be replicated with the same set up over a longer
period with an anemometer attached above it to limit local wind errors in a and b calcula-
tions. A comparison to interlayer reflection could be done with investigation into fits that
could improve model precision.
Similarly a scale up version with full modules could be used to analyse if a significant
difference could be detected on a vertical axis in the modules due to thermal stratification
on the backside of the modules.

5
Conclusion
The main objective of the thesis was to compare and determine how present Photovoltaic
temperature models could be used in vertically mounted Building Integrated Photovoltaic
(BIPV) installments with varying interlayers. With an increasing popularity of BIPV and its
versatility with options of coloured PV panels the affect of the unique mounting structure
and the addition of colour needed to be investigated.
The experimental set up used in the analysis consisted of six single cell coupons that
consisted of different interlayers. A comparison was made with one clear, two black and
three differently coloured coupons, red, beige, and terracotta coloured. All coupons were
fitted with a thermocouple inside the laminate to monitor the cell temperature as well as a
thermocouple on the backside of the coupon. Mounted on a plywood wall the coupon set
up was placed in a vertical orientation facing South. Environmental measurements were
acquired from facilities in the vicinity of the coupon set up, around DTU Risø Campus.
The measurements took place from the 14th of September to the 31st of December 2022
on DTU Risø Campus near Roskilde, Denmark. Clear sky periods were used with data
points of 5 minute averages split into measurements with and without rear ventilation.
A total of 10.5 hours of ventilated clear sky periods were used and 24.25 hours of non
ventilated clear sky periods.
The temperature measurements showed the affect of the thermal time constant of the
coupons with a varying response time compared to the irradiance in plane of array. A
noticeable difference was observed between the differently coloured coupons with the
lightest coloured interlayer, beige, showing the slowest response and lowest measured
back-of-module temperature and the darker colours, red and terracotta, a faster response
and considerably higher temperature.
To analyse the temperature models a comparison to the empirically determined coeffi-
cients in the Sandia Array Photovoltaic Performance Model was done that reflected simi-
lar results in the coloured coupons in that a large difference was found between the beige
and the red and terracotta coupons.
The addition of interlayers in the laminate of the coupons did not show a significant differ-
ence in the results but a difference in absorption was detected between colours. During
ventilated measurements the clear and beige coupon resulted in the lowest temperature of
the six whereas the black and the two darker coloured coupons, red and terracotta, gave
results higher than the given coefficient in a similar mounting configuration. Meanwhile
for non ventilated measurements all coupons gave results higher than the determined
coefficient, that assumed a uniform temperature in the coupon. A conclusion of a higher
temperature difference between the back-of-module and cell was needed in estimating
the cell temperature for the mounting structure used however the variation between the
colouration would need to be accounted for as well.
The ventilated results from analysing modelled cell temperature showed that coupon #2-
Black had the largest error where the cell temperature model underestimated the tem-
perature by a maximum of 0.8K and the largest model overestimation was for coupon
#5-Beige with a maximum offset of 0.5K. The most accurate cell temperature modelling
was for coupon #6-Terracotta which had a calculated MAE of 0.097K and MBE of −0.04K.

The non ventilated results showed that from the modelled cell temperature an underesti-
mation was made for all coupons used. The results showed that coupon #4-Red had the
largest error with an underestimation with a maximum offset of 0.8K and the most accurate
was calculated for #3-Black which had a calculated MAE of 0.199K and MBE of −0.049K.
In calculating the empirically defined coefficients used in modelling the back-of-module
temperature the lack of data had a large impact in that the coefficient of determination (r2)
for a linear regression was in the range of [0.041-0.784] for the ventilated data and [0.300-
0.499] for the non ventilated data. A discrepancy was found in whether measurements
from the morning or afternoon were used that indicated a slower cooling effect in the
coupons than the warming effect. As a lack of measurements were in the experiments
the results of these coefficient calculation proofed to be unreliable and inconclusive.
The back-of-module modelled temperature analysis resulted in an error for both venti-
lated and non ventilated measurements, exceeding Sandias given tolerance of ±5°C. For
coupons with rear ventilation the highest MAE across coupons was 3.18K and for the non
ventilated measurement 5.25K. Although there was a lack of measurements the results
showed a larger error than that given by Sandia National Laboratories indicating an addi-
tional consideration was needed for an accurate back-of-module temperature model for
BIPVs.
The discrepancies found in the coloured coupons were analysed and a cause in the dif-
ferences was given as the coloured layers had differences in reflection. The reflection of
the interlayers corresponded to the temperature results where beige reflected more and
terracotta the least. These differences indicated that a calibration of the coefficients used
in the back-of-module temperature model would not suffice. Additional considerations
were needed other than that of construction and mounting configuration in the model to
increase accuracy with coloured BIPV in particular. As absorption values of the coupons
vary with coloured layer making the temperature dependent on the reflection/absorption
of the added interlayer an added component calibrating the model to reflection/absorption
could prove to improve accuracy.
