Abstract
This project focuses on the co-optimization problem which aims to maximize the annual
energy production (AEP) of a wind farm while minimising the possibility of the distance
misplacement of the wind turbines. Most of the previous studies which aimed to address the
problem tend to assume that the boundaries of a given wind farm are fixed. In this study, an
attempt to solve this issue is presented, by modifying the shape and orientation (S & O) of a
wind farm within a fixed area and around a fixed point. A two-stage algorithm that minimises
the potential distance violation between wind turbines and increases the energy production
was formulated. The shape of a wind farm can then take countless different forms, yet, for
simplicity reasons, this project is limited by parallelograms while taking into consideration
certain constraints to keep the solutions as close to reality as possible. A three-case study
is presented around the Horns Rev 1 offshore wind farm, it illustrates the significance of the
aforementioned parameters and concludes with the evaluation of the presented optimization
approach.

Introduction
For the past two decades, wind power played a significant role in the reduction of CO2
emissions leading to cleaner and more sustainable solutions in electricity markets although
energy demand worldwide has shown a significant increase. Moving towards the future, off-
shore wind farms tend to replace the on-shore ones, since they have a higher power coefficient
and more importantly, as opposed to the on-shore ones, they do not occupy land areas and
can be used for a variety of other purposes, such as in agriculture or housing. Planning
and development of offshore wind farms is a procedure that requires multiple factors to be
taken into account in order to reach the desired result, which is no other than higher Annual
Energy Production (AEP) with the lower Financial and Environmental cost. This project
was conducted to investigate how the wind farm boundaries and layout affect the AEP of
offshore wind farms and how the AEP can be optimized by developing an algorithm that
analysed the data and performed calculations to reach the optimum result for each case.
2.1
Background Theory
This project is based on previous work conducted by Ju Feng and Wen Zhong Shen [3]
at Denmark’s Technical University. In the aforementioned study, the focus was to improve
the shape and the layout of a wind farm at the same time while keeping the total area fixed.
To achieve such a goal, a two-stage random algorithm was developed. Different shapes of
wind farms have been randomly acquired in the first stage, while different layouts have been
randomly acquired in the second one.
In the end, the outcome AEP was compared with the reference AEP. The study has taken
into consideration four constraints which are presented more thoroughly in 3.1.5 including
the total length of the internal cable network, the latest has been neglected in this project.
The study has shown that the shape and layout of a wind farm can have a significant impact
on its AEP and therefore a follow-up study was decided to be conducted in order to include a
second optimization method which was the minimisation of the distance constraint between
a wind turbine and all its neighbouring ones.

2.2.3
Annual Energy Production Calculations
The AEP calculation of a Wind Farm Site came from the simulations which have been
conducted using an open-source library in Python called Pywake [4] which has been de-
veloped over the years in Denmark’s Technical University. In the beginning, a wind farm
model combined with a site and a Wind Turbine model has to be initialised, then the simu-
lation result is based on the following information: wind turbine number, ambient reference
wind direction, ambient reference wind speed position, hub height of wind turbines as well
as data variables like local free-stream wind direction, local free-stream wind speed, local
free-stream turbulence intensity, probability of flow case (wind direction and wind speed),
local wind speed, local turbulence intensity, power production, thrust coefficient, and yaw
misalignment. The simulation takes into account all the above information for each wind
turbine individually and by using the sum method calculates the Wind farm site AEP taking
into consideration the wake effect which has been explained in detail in sections 2.2.1 2.2.2.
2.3
Horns Rev 1 Wind Farm
This study was done taking into account the data provided by pywake about the Horns
Rev 1 wind farm. This wind farm was built in 2002 by a danish energy company named Elsam
(now Ørsted) and it was the first large-scale wind farm in the world. It was established in
the North sea and it contained 80 x Vestas V 80 wind turbines. The Vestas V 80 wind turbine
model has a rated power of 2.0MW, and a rated wind speed of 15m/s with a diameter of
80m. The maximum water depth in the area that the wind farm occupies varies from 6m to
14m and the distance from the shore of the closest wind turbine is 6km.

Methodology
3.1
Problem formulation
The goal of this project was to take as a reference the Wind farm site Horns Rev
1 and maximize its AEP by optimising the parameters of shape, layout, and orientation
while minimizing the distance violation by running an initial simulation before the AEP
maximization occurs. To complete this task, a python script had to be created in order to
perform all the necessary calculations.
The project was divided into two main stages: the first stage was set to gain a new shape
and orientation (S & O) while minimizing the violation simulation. When the sum of the
distance of all violations reached 0, then this S & O was considered a feasible solution and
therefore the algorithm proceeded into the second stage of the calculations which was the
maximization of AEP. If the total AEP of the new wind farm was greater than the original
one without violating any of the constraints3.1.5 then it was considered successful and that
newly shaped wind farm with a new layout proceeded into further optimization.
3.1.1
Shape and Orientation
Denmark is one of the countries that have predefined areas where a wind farm can be
developed. Figure 5 represents the predefined shapes and layouts of three major wind farms
in Denmark where their shape and layout can be observed.
This study focuses exclusively on examining the factors of shape, orientation and layout,
therefore, parameters such as sail routes, flyways of migrating birds, water depth, or areas
with high preservation interest are neglected.

In the beginning of the algorithm, the only information that could be acquired from the
pywake library and the data provided was the coordinates XiandY i which represented the

this stage, the AEP of the original Horn Rev 1 wind farm was calculated using the pywake
library and that value was used as reference in order to compare the potential improvements
from the next stages of the algorithm.

3.2.3
Custom Functions
For the completion of the calculations, several formulas have been used multiple times
and in different stages of the algorithm. That approach was useful in order to create a
program that can debunk and read as well as possible the formulas that were created and
called via custom functions. The customs functions which have been created and used for
the completion of this project were:
• Translate coordinates function
• Reverse translate coordinates function
• Random move function
• Shape and Orientation function
• Violation Optimisation function
The first two functions as explained in 3.1.3 were used to normalize the wind farm
boundaries and layout, and vice versa, in order to perform certain tasks with precision.
One of the aforementioned tasks was to implement the random algorithm by using the
random move function 3.1.4 and allow the randomly chosen wind turbine with (XiandY i, i =
1, 2, ..., Nwt) coordinates to move freely within the defined boundaries of the wind farm, but
at the same time preventing it from fleeing away and violate the first constraint as it was
defined in section 3.1.5. The S & O functions were used in the first stage of the algorithm in
order to redefine the boundaries of the Horns Rev 1 wind farm while keeping the area fixed.
The violation optimization function was called afterwards in order to identify if there was
a distance violation among the redistributed wind turbines which formed the new layout in
the reshaped farm.

3.2.5
Second stage of the algorithm
The second stage of the algorithm was evolved in order to maximize the outcome AEP of
the reformed Horns Rev 1 wind farm. During this stage of the process, three custom functions
were used and those are: the translate coordinates function which again normalized the wind
farm boundaries in order to make the second custom function, the random move function be
able to be applied and then the reverse translate coordinates function was used in order to
translate newest version of the wind farm layout which came out of the second function into
real coordinates and dimensions. For this stage of the algorithm, the third constraint which
is related to the shape of the wind farm was not taken into consideration since no changes
were applied to the L1 and θ parameters which could affect the feasibility of the wind farm
shape. Instead, it was found necessary to check the minimum distance violation constraint
as described in 3.1.5 before the process was concluded with the calculation of the AEP. That
was tested by simply check every single wind turbine of the wind farm against all others.
Every wind turbine had to be at least at dmin meters away from all neighboring turbines.
The second change of the algorithm was concluded with the calculation of the AEP of the
reformed wind farm and the comparison of the new value of the AEP with the reference one.

4.4
Discussion
Based on Figures 14 17, it is clear that most wind turbines tend to occupy positions
close to the edges of the wind farm leaving a significant amount of space towards the middle
of the wind farm uncovered. This pattern can be justified by the fact that the wind which
blows from all directions towards the wind farm has no wake effect on the first line of wind
turbines. The wake effect also fades as the wind travels throughout the wind farms and
meets the wind farms from the opposite side.
During the final computational runs, an error seemed to have occurred with the code
leading to the minimum distance violation of the wind turbines. This error happens in the
final run of the final stage since the algorithm always minimises the V total value before it
moves into the next stage as Figures 16 18 indicate.
The values of AEPnew and V total as the algorithm progresses through iterations (j),(i)
are not expected to change with the correction of the minimum distance violation. The
pattern of the increase of the value of AEPnew though is expected to be smoother if the
number of repetitions increases.
From the final comparison, it can be said that the AEPopt had the lowest increase for
case 1, it improved further for case 2, and it was expected to increase its value by around
4 - 5 % in cases 3a and 3b based on the previous study [3] which included the total cable
length as an additional limitation factor.

5
Conclusions
In conclusion, the AEP performance is significantly influenced by the shape, orientation,
and layout of a given wind farm as this study proved.
Most of the studies around the
improvement of the AEP take the shape, orientation, and layout of a wind farm as granted.
In this project, an algorithm with two different objective functions was proposed, the first
one was the minimisation of the distance violations among the wind turbines and the second
one was the maximisation of the AEP. Results showed a very optimistic increase in the
AEP which comes close to 9% of the original Horns Rev 1.The amount of increase expected
from this procedure was around 4 −5%. Although in reality that number will be decreased
with the implementation of further constraints, the study indicates that the importance of
the examined parameters remains solid. In addition, executing the algorithm with a higher
amount of repetitions could lead to an even higher increase of the AEP and a smoother
transition throughout the history of the iterations will be observed.

6
Limitations and Recommendations
A limitation of this project is considered the large amount of time required for the sim-
ulations due to lack of computer power, and therefore, time. This project showed promising
results in improving the AEP of a wind farm which could inspire future work around the
subject while taking into consideration different parameters. The first parameter that can
be included is a more flexible S & O with polygons or other more complex shapes. However,
this study was limited to parallelograms only for simplicity reasons. Other parameters could
also be used as constraints to provide more realistic results such as flyways of migration
routes, Natura 2000 areas, and other limiting factors.
