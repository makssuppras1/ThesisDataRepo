Abstract
NewAthena is a future X-ray observatory by the European Space Agency. DTU Space
is developing X-ray reflective mirror coatings that will be used for the mission. In this
report, the surface roughness of representative mirrors for NewAthena is investigated sys-
tematically. The surface roughness plays a crucial role in the performance of an X-ray
telescope such as NewAthena, as the point spread function gets broader if there is too
much scatter contributing to a poorer angular resolution.
The surface roughness is investigated on three different types of mirrors, i.e. uncoated mir-
ror substrates, iridium-coated mirror substrates, and platinum-coated mirror substrates.
Iridium has until now been part of the baseline coating for NewAthena, but platinum is
currently being investigated as a new candidate.
The mirror surfaces are characterized with X-ray reflectivity and scatter measurements,
atomic force microscopy, and optical microscopy. These methods probe the samples in
different ways and therefore yield complementary information about the surfaces. Atomic
force microscropy and scatter measurements in particular can probe lateral roughness,
which is not accessible through the reflectivity measurements. To analyze the measure-
ments, different software programs are needed. The reflectivity and scatter measurements
in particular are fitted using Bede REFS and GenX. These two software programs are
compared.
GenX and Bede REFS were able to produce similar fits of the specular measurements, but
it was demonstrated that for non-specular (scatter) measurements, the underlying models
in the two software must be different. It was found that GenX is the best choice for future
use, since it is more flexible and allows the user to implement their own models.
The effect of the working gas pressure during the coating process was investigated for the
platinum coatings as well. The general trend indicated that the roughness and thickness
of the coatings increase with pressure.
The specular reflectivity measurements showed that the best platinum coatings had only
slightly higher rms roughness than the iridium coating on the witness samples. However,
the lateral surface roughness/structure of iridium and platinum turned out to be very
different.
This was discovered through the analysis of the non-specular measurements
and the atomic force microscopy measurements.
The platinum coatings showed wavy
surface structures, whereas the iridium coatings were much more flat. Further investigation
is needed to understand the underlying cause of this difference. However, the analysis
that has been made may help establish a new methodology for quality assurance of the
NewAthena mirrors.

Introduction
Although the universe has been studied with modern technology for decades, more ques-
tions arise as more knowledge is acquired. There are many unanswered questions that cur-
rent technologies cannot answer. Therefore, it is of great importance that new technologies
with better performance are developed to enhance our understanding of our universe. One
of such missions is NewAthena.
The New Advanced Telescope for High-ENergy Astrophysics (NewAthena) is one of ESA’s
flagship L-class missions. The mission is still in the study phase and will be approved for
mission adoption by early 2027. After this, construction will take place and launch is
expected to occur in 2037 [1]. The mission theme of NewAthena is to put a ”Sharp focus
on the X-ray Universe”. NewAthena will be able to study some of the most energetic and
hot events in the universe. These include, but are not limited to, the nature of accreting
black holes and the evolution and formation of large-scale hot gas structures in galaxy
clusters [1].
NewAthena is an X-ray telescope that will be operating at energies in the low keV range
of approximately 0.1 to 15 keV. It will have two different instruments, the X-ray Integral
Field Unit (X-IFU), which is an imaging spectrometer, and the Wide Field Imager (WFI),
which is a large field-of-view spectral imaging camera [2]. One instrument at a time can
be placed in the focal plane of the telescope. The telescope has a focal length of 12 m and
a large collecting area more than 1 m2 at 1 keV and an angular resolution of 9” [2].
It is important for such a telescope to have as large an effective area as possible across the
relevant energies, which can be achieved through thin film coatings on the mirrors. And
since NewAthena is a focusing telescope, a sharp focus is also of great importance to obtain
the required resolution in the observations. To achieve this, the mirrors must be very flat
and precisely assembled with tolerances in the scale of µm to mm. The mirrors and thin
films must additionally be exceptionally smooth in terms of microroughness (referred to
as roughness in this report), which is in the scale of nm to µm. The short wavelengths of
X-rays make them very sensitive to small variations in the mirrors caused by roughness.
This can make the resulting image blurry, which is not desired. Therefore, it is of great
importance to study the roughness of the mirrors that will be used in NewAthena.
This report therefore investigates the surfaces of different mirrors that are representative
of the NewAthena mission. This includes substrates without coating and substrates with
single layers of iridium or platinum. Iridium has been part of the baseline coating for
NewAthena for a long time and has gone through many tests and characterizations over
the years [3][4][5].
The use of platinum coatings for NewAthena specifically is in the
very early stages of investigation, and the platinum-coated samples characterized in this
report are some of the first of their kind that are produced by ESA’s coating machine
at cosine Science and Computing B.V..
A transition from iridium to platinum-based
coatings will require extensive investigations of platinum coatings, e.g. the impact of the
coating process parameters. This report examines the effect of the working gas pressure on
platinum coatings and the general structure of platinum coatings compared to uncoated
and iridium-coated samples, providing insights that could shape future developments for
the NewAthena mission.
The mirrors will be characterized using X-ray reflectometry (XRR), atomic force mi-
croscopy (AFM), and optical microscopy. The X-ray reflectometry measures include both

2.1
X-rays and their interaction at interfaces
Electromagnetic radiation comes in many variants, determined by their wavelength. One
type of radiation is X-rays, which have a very short wavelength, λ, in the range of about
0.01 to 10 nm [6]. Electromagnetic radiation consists of an electric field, E, and a magnetic
field, H, which are both perpendicular to the propagation direction, z. In addition to the
wavelength, other measures can be used to characterize the radiation. This includes the
wavevector, k = 2π/λ and the so-called radiation energy. The energy, E, of a specific
wavelength can be determined through the following relation [7]:
E = hc
λ
(2.1)
where h is the Planck constant and c is the speed of light. Through this it can be deduced
that X-rays have energies in ranges of roughly 100 eV to 100 keV.
For simplicity, only the electric field is considered. The amplitude of the electric field can
be described by a sine wave, and it is assumed that it is plane waves. Therefore the electric
field can be expressed as [7]:
E(r, t) = ˆεE0ei(k·r−ωt)
(2.2)
where ˆε is a unit vector that describes polarization, k is the wave vector that describes
propagation direction (|k| = k), E0 is the wave amplitude, r is the position vector, t is
time and ω is the cyclic frequency [7].
2.1.1
Refraction and reflection
The interaction between X-rays and matter occurs through scattering, absorption, and
transmission. Scattering can be elastic or inelastic, depending on whether energy is trans-
ferred to the material during the interaction. In an elastic scattering, no energy is ex-
changed, and the photon simply changes direction, while in an inelastic scattering (such
as the Compton effect), part of the energy is transferred to an electron, resulting in a
change in the photon’s energy. Absorption occurs when a photon is fully absorbed by
an atom, transferring its energy to an electron, which is then expelled from the atom.
Transmission refers to the process where the photon passes through the material, often
with some scattering or absorption along the way, but without being fully absorbed. These
processes can be described through reflection and refraction at interfaces between different
media [7].
Reflections and refractions occur when the wave travels across an interface between two
different media. The interface is assumed to be sharp and both media are assumed to be
homogeneous. Each medium will have a unique refractive index that determines the phase
velocity, v, as the refractive index can be defined as n = c
v. The interface also gives rise to
a direction change of the wave, which depends on the refractive index and the incidence
angle. For X-rays the refractive index is often defined as [7]:
n = 1 −δ + iβ
(2.3)
δ is the real part of the refractive index, while β is the imaginary part. These are also
referred to as the optical constants. The refractive index is highly dependent on both the

Snell’s law and the critical angle
The incident grazing angle of the X-rays determines, together with the refractive indices of
the two media, the interaction that will occur at the interface. The relation that describes
this is Snell’s law.
n1 cos(θ1) = n2 cos(θ2)
(2.6)

incidence angles. At some point the critical angle is reached, which is when the refracted
rays are parallel with the interface. At grazing incidence angles below the critical angle,
the X-rays undergo total external reflection, which is illustrated in fig. 2.1 [7]. This can
be very beneficial for many applications. In the context of this report it can be utilized

In an X-ray telescope, thin film coatings are often utilized to increase the critical angle.
The choice of material depends on the application, but it is common to use iridium, gold,
or platinum, since these have relatively high densities. In many cases a single layer of a
high-Z material (high-density) can enhance the performance at higher energies enough by
itself, but it can also be beneficial to use bilayers or multilayers to account for losses in the
lower energies due to absorption edges of the high-Z material. In a bilayer, a low-Z (low-
density) material is coated on top of a high-Z material. A multilayer is then a repetition
of the bilayer [8].
2.1.2
Bragg diffraction
Bragg diffraction is a phenomenon that occurs in crystalline materials due to their periodic
structure. This means that at a certain angle of incidence θ, the periodic structure of the
material will give rise to constructive interference of the X-rays. The relation is stated as
[7]:
mλ = 2d sin θ
(2.8)
where m is the diffraction order, λ is the wavelength, and d is the spacing between the
lattice planes (in a crystal). The geometry behind the formula is shown in fig. 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of the principle behind a Bragg diffraction. The figure is reproduced from [9].
The principle of Bragg diffraction can be utilized for many things. In the context of X-ray
telescopes, Bragg diffraction can be utilized to increase the reflectivity above the critical
angle. This is done using thin film coatings arranged in multilayers. Under the right
circumstances, the multilayers act as periodic crystal-like structures. At certain incident
angles and energies the reflectance will be boosted by the interference. Multilayers can be
designed in different ways to optimize reflectance using Bragg reflections [8].

2.2
Rough surfaces
When the incident angle and the exit angle of a reflection are the same, the reflection is
specular. This is not always the case, since most surfaces are not ideal and therefore are
rough to some extent. In these cases, non-specular reflections from the interface can also
be of interest. This is the reflection that can be measured at angles that are different from
the specular angle, that is, when the incidence angle and the exit angles are not equal
[8]. These reflections happen because the roughness changes the surface normal locally
in the nanometer scale. This is the same scale as the wavelength of X-rays, which means
that the X-rays are very sensitive to the roughness. So, while roughness causes a loss in
the specular reflection, the non-specular reflections actually maps the scatter distribution
and thus the lateral structure of the surface. Through this information, additional surface
parameters, other than the rms roughness σ described in section 2.1.3, can be inferred
[14]. These additional parameters are the so-called correlation length ξ and the Hurst
exponent h.
The Hurst exponent is a measure of jaggedness. The value ranges between 0 and 1. A
small value indicates a very jagged surface, meaning that the surface height vary a lot on
a small scale. With a high value of h, the surface will be smoother with hills and valleys
[15]. This effect is illustrated in fig. 2.5a.
The lateral correlation length refers to the distance at which similar patterns or features
in the surface roughness start to repeat or influence each other. It basically describes
how far apart areas on the surface can be while still showing a similar structure. A long
correlation length indicates that the features on the surface have a larger extent than if
the correlation length is short. This effect is illustrated in fig. 2.5b.
These parameters characterizing the surface can be inferred from the non-specular reflec-
tions. These reflections can be measured in X-ray scatter measurements. This will be
described in section 4.2.1. Another way to probe these parameters can be by measuring
the surface topography using AFM. In this type of measurement, a more direct image of
the surface is made, and the parameters can be extracted using different techniques. This
is elaborated in section 4.3.

2.3
Distorted Wave Born Approximation
To model the non-specular reflections different models can be utilized. One of them is
the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA). DWBA is an extension of the Born
Approximation (BA). Both are used to model the intensity of diffuse scattering from the
interfaces [16]. The main difference between the two are the assumptions. In BA it is
assumed that the incident electric field does not interact with the scattered field or that
the interactions are so weak that they can be neglected. This is often not the case for
grazing angles near the critical angle. The scattered rays can also interact with each other
or with the incident field. This is especially the case if multiple interfaces are present,
since scatterings happen at all the interfaces, which is bound to cause other scatterings.
Therefore, the assumption of BA breaks down [14][16]. DWBA extends BA so that more
of the scattering effects can be taken into account in the model. The DWBA can, in
principle, include as many terms as one would like to include more scatterings. But the
cost is, of course, computation time and at some point the higher-order terms become
unlikely and do not contribute much to the measured reflectance [16][17] . DWBA is also
able to handle incident waves that are not plane waves.
Since the DWBA equations are quite lengthy and complex, only a brief description of
the main components according to [16] is mentioned here. The scattering cross-section,
S(q), where q is the scattering vector, closely related to the angle θ, by q = 4π
λ sin(θ), is
the probability that a specific scattering will occur. This probability depends on many
factors, see [16]. But one of the key inputs is the autocovariance function. This function
contains information about the interface morphology and the morphology between layers.
The autocovariance function can take different forms depending on the application and
the assumptions of the interfaces. One adaption was introduced by [15]:

Mission overview
NewAthena is an X-ray telescope that is currently under development and will be approved

The mission is a reformulation of the Athena
mission, which was selected as one of the ESAs large-scale missions in 2014 to explore

was approved by SPC [1][18].
The theme has also been reformulated to ”Sharp focus on the X-ray Universe”. NewA-

in galaxy clusters, but also other science cases such as gamma-ray bursts, supernova ex-

auroras, comets in our Solar System and the interstellar medium [2]. Thus, NewAthena
will be able to bring a lot of new knowledge about the universe in combination with
observations from other telescopes.
NewAthena is an X-ray telescope that uses Wolter-type optics, i.e.
two reflections at
grazing angles that focus the rays into the focal plane. The optic of NewAthena will have

The angular resolution of an X-ray telescope such as NewAthena should be as small as
possible to achieve high-quality measurements. The aim is to get it below 9”. The angular
resolution, sometimes called the half-energy width (HEW), is closely related to the PSF.
The PSF describes the distribution of the focused X-ray intensity around the focal point.
The HEW is defined as twice the median value of the PSF [21]. The microroughness of
the mirrors directly affects the PSF by causing non-specular scattering. These scatterings
lead to a broader PSF, which results in a larger HEW and degrades the angular resolution
of the telescope. This effect becomes more pronounced at higher X-ray energies, as shorter
wavelengths are more sensitive to surface irregularities [21][22].

4.1
Thin film deposition
4.1.1
DC magnetron sputtering
The coating process is crucial for the performance of mirrors in a telescope. The process
needs to be very stable so that the coatings can be reproduced over time with almost
identical characteristics and properties.
The coatings on the mirrors for NewAthena are all made using a deposition process called
direct current (DC) magnetron sputtering. This process takes place in a vacuum chamber,
where the substrate, the target material (coating material), and a process gas (typically
argon) are all present.
Figure 4.1: Illustration of the DC magnetron sputtering system. The figure is reproduced from [31].
The process is illustrated in fig. 4.1. The first step is to evacuate the chamber until it
is in vacuum. In this way, potential contamination is minimized. Then, a process gas is
injected into the chamber and forms an atmosphere. The pressure of this gas is defined
as the working gas pressure. A plasma environment is created by having an electric field
and a static magnetic field in which both free electrons, argon atoms, and argon ions are
present. The target material influences the geometry of the electric field, which means
that it also affects the plasma environment. The ionized argon is accelerated towards the
target and bombards the material. Some of the target atoms are released and sputtered.
As the sputtered atoms travel towards the substrate, they can interact with the atoms
that are present in the chamber. This can affect their direction and kinetic energy [8][31].
As they arrive at the substrate, they form the thin film.

4.2.2
Differential evolution algorithm
The differential evolution (DE) algorithm is a genetic algorithm used by both GenX and
Bede REFS to fit the X-ray reflectivity data. Differential evolution exceeds compared to
other algorithms as it is better at converging even with a large parameter space. Some
algorithms require very good initial estimates, while others are ineﬀicient in exploring the
parameter space. These are not a problem in DE [36].
18
Surface roughness characterization and analysis of mirror plates for NewAthena
==PAGE:31==
The general principle is that a number of individuals are created, which are called parame-
ter vectors p = [p1, p2, ..., pn]. Each parameter vector contains the n number of parameters
that are fitted. p0 is the guess made by the user, and the rest of the parameter vectors are
randomly chosen within each of their allowed ranges. The parameter vector population of
m parameter vectors, P = [p0, p1, p2, ...pm−1], is then updated with respect to a scheme
that makes the individuals to fit the data better than the previous population. Each of
these steps is called generation [36][37].
The scheme that updates the values includes mutations, recombination, and selections of
parameter vectors. The first step in the algorithm is to evaluate the error function of each
parameter vector in the population, P. The best-fit vector is saved as b = [b1, b2, ...bn]
and is used to check whether the new parameter vectors can yield better fits with regard
to the error function. If better solutions are found, the vector is updated [36].
The new population members are found by selecting two random vectors from the current
population, pa and pb. The difference between those is used to mutate the best-so-far
vector b as follows [36]:
b′ = b + km(pa −pb)
(4.1)
where km is the mutation constant. This value can be chosen by the user and should be
between 0.5 and 1.5 [38]. The mutation makes slight changes to the parameter vectors in
the population.
The next step is the recombination process, which is the process of creating new parameter
vectors by combining b′ and p0. A trial vector t = [t1, t2, ..., tn] is created that will compete
with the vector p0. The trial vector is constructed by randomly choosing one value at a
time from b′ or p0. The choice is made by generating a random number from a uniform
distribution between 0 and 1. The random number is then compared to a user-selected
recombination constant (cross-over), kr. If the random number is less than or equal to
kr, the value of b′ is loaded into the trial vector. If kr is larger, the value is loaded from
p0. This is done for n −1 of the parameters in the trial vector. The last parameter in
the trial vector will always get the value of b′ to ensure that there is at least one new
parameter different from the parameters of p0. If any of the parameters in the trial vector
falls outside the constraints, it is updated to a random value between the boundary values.
The trial vector is adopted as the new vector p0 if the error function using the parameters
in t is less than or equal to the error function using the parameters from p0. If that is
not the case, p0 is propagated to the next generation without changes. This procedure
is performed for all parameter vectors in the population P with a new b′ vector every
time. This selection process ensures that the best parameter values from the parameter
vectors are propagated to new generations. At some point, after many iterations, the error
function for the best-fit vector, b, stops decreasing in each generation. This is when the fit
has converged to the global minimum and the vector b in the latest generation is selected
as the best-fit parameters [36].
Thus, the DE algorithm both make sure that new parameter spaces are explored using the
mutation, while keeping information from previous generations through the recombination.
The use of a whole population of parameter vectors ensures that a larger area of the
parameter space is explored and increases the probability of finding the global minimum.
One thing to note is that the best-fit depends very much on the chosen error function [36].

Atomic for microscopy
Atomic force microscopy is a surface characterization technique that can measure surface
topography down to nanometer scales. An AFM measurement consists of an image in the
XY plane with height variations in the Z plane. The method can be destructive if the
instrument is used incorrectly.
4.3.1

the probe, the scanner, the controller, and the software. The probe consists of a tip, a

specifically, either the PeakForce Tapping mode or the Tapping mode is typically used.

4.3.2
Power Spectral Density
From an AFM image, a power spectral density (PSD) function can be derived. It captures
variations in roughness across different length scales by looking at the spatial correlation
between surface heights rather than looking at the surface ”pointwise”. Thus, the PSD
describe the structure and thus the roughness of a surface in a more complete way [44]
[45].
The PSD decomposes the surface profile from the image into spatial frequencies, where low
frequencies correspond to large-scale features, and high frequencies to fine-scale roughness.
When power is plotted as a function of spatial frequency, the shape of the PSD provides
insight into the characteristics of the surface at different scales.

Gwyddion analysis software
Gwyddion is a program that can process and has analysis tools for scanning probe mi-
croscopy (SPM) data. This is fx AFM data. The SPM data are only an approximation of
the surface since it is sampled in different spots/pixels. In most cases some post-processing
is needed in order for the data to provide useful information [47].
Often times, the analysis of SPM data needs to be done using the software provided by the
instrument manufacturer, which is often built into the PC that operates the instrument.
This also means that the processing algorithms are unknown to the user [47]. Gwyddion is,
on the other hand, open-source and free of charge. Gwyddion has a lot of features, but in
this context it is mainly used for loading the SPM files from the AFM measurements and
then processing each file with some alignments and corrections. Then the rms roughness
is extracted as well as the 1D PSD (horizontal). Lastly, the processed image is saved as a
text file so that it is easier to plot using Python.

The parameters effect in non-specular simulations in
REFS
At the beginning of learning how to use REFS, a brief investigation was conducted to
examine the impact of various parameters on non-specular simulations. One of the reasons
for this was that REFS can not fit the non-specular measurements, and thus it has to be
done manually. Therefore, an understanding of the effects of each parameter was crucial.
The effects were investigated by changing one variable at a time and making simulations
with different values of the individual parameters.
The parameters that were studied
included the roughness of the substrate, σSiO2, the roughness of the coating, σIr (iridium
was assumed), the Hurst exponent, and the correlation length. The simulations assume a

reflections. In fig. 6.1 four plots are shown where each of the four parameters σIr, σSiO2,
Hurst exponent h, and correlation length ξ are varied one at a time. Here, it is evident that
the roughness of the substrate does not have much influence compared to the roughness of
the iridium layer. This is expected since the measurement is made below the critical angle
where the total external reflection is occurring. Therefore, we mainly measure the top
layer. An increased roughness of the iridium layer increases the amount of non-specular
reflections. The correlation length and the Hurst exponent seem to affect the shape of the
curve, where the roughness increases the reflectance over the whole measurement (except

became very apparent as there were discrepancies between the fits. This section compares

6.4
The equipment function in REFS and offsets
During the initial data analysis of the specular measurement of SPO 1945-06, there seemed
to be an issue causing the fit to be poor both above and below the critical angle. This
was corrected by introducing an angular offset of −0.009° such that the data was shifted.
As discussed in section 6.3, a shift in the data can also be introduced by changing the
density of the material. It was decided to use offsets to have fewer variables in future fits
and also because it was uncertain if the offsets were actually caused by the density. Other
measurements made on FCXR by other people (during the same period) also showed offsets
of the same order of magnitude, which could indicate that it came from the instrument.
Another issue was that the fit below the critical showed slightly higher reflectance than
the data. This was caused by the fact that the equipment function was turned off by
mistake due to a typo. Turning it on meant that the parameters of the instrument used
for the individual measurement were now included, see appendix A for the measurement

Investigation of the specular peak width in non-specular

After some initial simulations in REFS of the non-specular measurements of SPO 1219-10,
it was found that the simulated specular peak was generally not wide enough compared
to the data. Therefore, it was decided to try to determine which parameters could affect
the width of the specular peak. This was only done for non-specular measurement of SPO
1219-10 at a specular angle of 0.4°. The simulations made are compared in fig. 6.20.
It was found that it is mainly the instrument settings that affected the width of the
specular peak. The initial simulation, the blue curve, does not capture the width of the
peak. Two other simulations are also present (although they lie on top of each other). In
the yellow curve, the slit width of the beam shaping slit is increased from 0.12 to 0.8 mm.
This only made the peak wider and did not seem to change anything else. To correct for
the height of the peak, a normalization of 3 was used. A similar result was also made using
again a slit width of 0.8 mm but excluding the equipment function, instead of normalizing
the data.
Of course, none of these simulations are physically correct, since the slit width of the beam
shaping slit is known from the measurement that was performed. A normalization would
be nice to avoid since other parameters can also affect the height of the peak. Excluding
the equipment function also does not make sense since the settings of FCXR are known.

6.7.1
The broad specular peak in scatter scans
The very first scatter scan of cs0319 showed a very broad specular peak. This was not
really expected. The second sample that was measured with a scatter scan was cs0313 and
it showed the same broad specular peak. Therefore, the measurement was stopped early
and two tests were made in order to see if the setup had an influence on the broadness.
The resulting measurements are shown in fig. 6.29. The original measurement is shown
in orange. The first test was where the width of the ROI was narrowed down from the
original size, that is, from 12 pixels to 4 pixels. This measurement is shown in green in
fig. 6.29. The peak width was not significantly affected by this, which means that the
detector did not detect much more additional scattering using the wider ROI. The main
difference is that the reflectance is slightly lower using the narrow ROI, which is expected
since there will be fewer counts. The other test is shown in fig. 6.29 as red data points.
This measurement is made with the a ROI width of 12 pixels, but the chosen specular
angle is above the critical angle. Above the critical angle, the reflectance will decrease
quite quickly. Therefore, the angle is chosen so that it corresponds to the angle of the first
kiessig fringe, that is, 0.85°. This measurement shows the same tendency as the others
with a very broad specular peak.

6.7.4
Non-specular fits and simulations with GenX and REFS
The non-specular measurements of the platinum-coated witness samples showed a very
broad specular peak, which was not expected at all.
None of the other non-specular
measurements (uncoated and iridium) had shown such broad peaks. Therefore, it was also
very diﬀicult to determine which parameters could cause the specular peaks to become so
broad. From the short study described in section 6.7.1 it did not seem to come from the
instrument. The next thing to test was whether GenX and REFS could fit the data to see
if it is a result of surface roughness. Since all non-specular measurements of the platinum-
coated samples are nearly identical, see fig. 6.30, the focus was mainly on trying to fit the
measurement of cs0304 with different fitting approaches. The rest of the samples, cs0307,
cs0310, cs0313, cs0316, and cs0319, were also fitted using GenX, but more fits/simulations
were made on cs0304 using both GenX and REFS.

7
Discussion
7.1
General results
The specular measurements for all of the samples showed realistic roughness and thickness
estimates compared to what was expected. The roughness of the three uncoated samples
was a little large around ∼0.7 nm, but not completely off. The iridium-coated SPO and
WS seemed to have an rms roughness of ∼0.45 nm and ∼0.3 nm, respectively. The
roughness of the platinum WS varied, but the least rough samples were around ∼0.35
nm. This is slightly higher than the iridium-coated WS, but not by much.
The non-specular measurements caused a bit of trouble for some of the measurements.
Fitting these measurements in GenX yielded some unconstrained parameters with large
errors, and it was necessary to fix certain parameters in almost all of the fits to get
better and somewhat more constrained solutions. However, it seems like there is a lot
of correlation between the roughness parameters, which means that fixing one parameter
will make another parameter compensate for it. One of the measurements of SPO 1219-10
were not able to be fitted to a satisfactory degree, probably due to the fact that it was
made above the critical angle and showed very different behavior with fringes in the non-
specular part of the measurement, which GenX could not capture. The platinum samples
showed very similar non-specular fits, which was expected because of the almost identical
measurements. However, these fits will need further investigation since the rms roughness
values generally were very large for both the substrate and the platinum layer. Fixing
both roughness values did not yield a realistic fit, either, so it has to be something else
that causes the large roughness.
REFS were unable to perform actual fits of the non-specular data, they had to be done by
hand, which makes the results very subjective. The REFS simulations also had problems
simulating the width of the specular peaks in almost all cases without making corrections.
These corrections do not really make physical sense since the instrument settings are
known from when the measurement was made. So, unless there is a bug or maybe an
over-sighted parameter, it seems that REFS may not be the best choice for non-specular
fits. REFS is very much like a ”black box” and it is diﬀicult to fully understand how the
model works, and the manual does not really go into detail either.
The AFM images proved to be really valuable because they show the surface topography
directly. In general, there appeared to be no major outliers with regard to the estimated
rms roughness derived directly from these images with Gwyddion. Of course, some of
the measurements that were of poor quality yielded rms roughness values that deviated,
but this can be easily explained. However, the mounting issue that caused this should be
resolved to be able to make measurements of SPOs in the future. This might just be the
use of another adhesive than the stickers available in DTU Nanolab.
The extraction of PSDs from the AFM images did yield information on the distribution
of spatial frequencies. Generally, the 1 × 1 µm2 scans for each sample were similar to each
other, indicating that the roughness is consistent in this scale. The PSDs from the larger
scans generally appeared to extend the trends observed in the smaller scans, maintaining
a consistent shape and overlapping across different scan sizes. The only slight deviation
from this is the PSDs of cs00095 in fig. 6.28.

7.2
Comparison between methods
The specular and non-specular measurements did not yield the same roughness parameters
in either REFS or GenX for all three types of samples. The differences between the two
methods were greatest for the platinum-coated WS and lowest for the uncoated samples.
The AFM measurements and the PSDs also showed different results in terms of both rms
roughness, but also in terms of correlation length and Hurst exponents compared to the
other methods.
In general, it is expected to see differences in the estimated parameters. The different
methods probe the samples in completely different ways and are limited by different things.
The lateral resolution is different in the X-ray reflectivity measurement compared to the
AFM measurements. The resolution of X-ray reflectivity measurements is of the size of its
wavelength, i.e. ∼0.15 nm for 8.048 keV, while the resolution of the AFM is limited by
the tip (∼1 nm) and the scan settings [50]. There is also a difference between looking at a
very small local area, as in AFM, and averaging over a large area, as in X-ray reflectivity
measurements.
The roughness could be very different depending on how much of the
surface is probed. This size difference was described in section 6.2.
7.3
Effect of working gas pressure
An important investigation of this report was the effect of the working gas pressure on
the platinum coating. The differences were most obvious in the specular fits, where there
seemed to be a trend of increased platinum roughness and substrate roughness for higher
working gas pressure.
It is diﬀicult to determine which of the working gas pressures
between 2·10−3 mbar or 3.4·10−3 (cs0304 and cs0307) mbar produced the lowest roughness
since the three different specular fits, i.e., the full range of data with all parameters free,
the shorter range of data with all parameters free, and the shorter range of data with
fixed substrate roughness, yielded only slightly different roughness estimates for the two.
The thickness of the platinum layer also seemed to be quite close to the target thickness
for both cs0304 and cs0307.
The higher working gas pressures slightly increased the
thickness to 10.2 or 10.3 nm. The other characterization types did not really show any
trends with respect to the working gas pressure. The only other thing was that cs0316
and cs0319 showed circular structures in some of the large AFM scans and also small
particles scattered across the surface. This was not seen on the other samples in the AFM
measurements, but it could also be a coincidence, since the areas are very small.
7.4
REFS and GenX
GenX and REFS turned out to be quite diﬀicult to work with when they had to be com-
pared, due to their different inputs and the fact that they used different optical constants.
However, the main difference was the non-specular simulations, which indicated that they
use very different underlying models. It is very diﬀicult to tell which model is the most
accurate, also because REFS was not able to actually fit the non-specular measurements.
However, due to REFS being very much a ’black box’ and GenX being open-source where
custom models can also be defined using Python, GenX is probably the best choice of
the two. The definition of materials in GenX also seems to be a lot more flexible than in
REFS. There are probably more options to define the materials yourself than what was
found. GenX also yielded specular simulations closer to IMD, which is the ”standard”
fitting tool for specular measurements at DTU Space.

7.5
The PSD analysis
The original thought behind finding the peak positions in the PSDs was that they could
possibly yield information about the correlation length or other features of the measure-
ments. But that seems not to be the case. Only very few of the peaks somewhat corre-
sponded to features’ sizes, and that may be a coincidence. Therefore, it was concluded
that most of them are probably noise.
The PSD fitting with the ABC model and the fractal model proved to be challenging, as
the parameters, especially the parameter C seemed to be unconstrained for the applied
model and priors. The rms roughness values estimated from the ABC models generally
seem to be off. For all of the samples, they varied a lot. This was even the case for the
small scans, which showed very similar PSD shapes for the individual samples. The fractal
model seemed more stable, but also had a few instances where it was unconstrained. This
did not seem to be a problem in [44] and [46] that used both the ABC and the fractal
models.
The problems therefore could be due to either the data or maybe the fitting
method. Late in the data analysis process it was found that Gwyddion had some in-built
functions to determine the correlation length and rms roughness of the PSD and the ACF.
Unfortunately, there was not enough time to look into this in detail. But that may be
something for the future.
7.6
Methodology and recommendations
From the analysis that has been made in this it is clear that the different measurement
types together can yield a more comprehensive understanding of surface roughness and
structure. Especially the non-specular measurements and the AFM topography images
yielded a good understanding of the differences between the three different sample types.
The PSD analysis needs to be investigated further to obtain meaningful fits.
For future investigations of the platinum-coated mirrors for NewAthena, it could be ben-
eficial to make the routine specular measurements but also include non-specular measure-
ments and AFM measurements to address the lateral surface roughness as well as it seems
like it grows differently than iridium.
To analyze the non-specular measurements, GenX would probably be the best choice.
Using GenX would allow for looking into further into the underlying model, since it is
open source, getting a better understanding of how everything works.
The surface contamination may also need to be addressed so that it can be minimized.
Since most of it could be wiped away, it is something that is lying on top of the coating.
Maybe the witness samples need to be cleaned by other means than the airgun or they
need to be stored differently.

8
Conclusion
In this report, several types of characterization methods have been utilized to analyze the
surfaces of uncoated, iridium-coated, and platinum-coated samples systematically. The
methods include X-ray reflectivity measurements, X-ray scatter measurements, atomic
force microscopy, and lastly optical microscopy.
The platinum witness samples were part of a study to investigate the effect of one of
the coating parameters; the working gas pressure. The main differences between the six
samples appeared in the specular fits, where they showed a trend of increasing roughness
for both the platinum layer and the substrate with increasing pressure. The roughness
differences were not substantial at low pressures, and therefore 2·10−3 mbar and 3.4·10−3
are probably suitable levels for the working gas pressure for future coating runs. The
non-specular measurements of these samples were identical, indicating that the lateral
roughness is quite similar. This was also found through AFM measurements, where only
the samples coated at the highest working gas pressure showed differences from the other
samples in the AFM topography images.
The rms roughness from the specular measurements was found to be slightly higher for
the platinum-coated witness samples than for the iridium-coated witness sample. The
difference is below 0.1 nm for the least rough platinum samples.
However, this study demonstrates that these estimates do not provide a complete picture.
The other types of measurements, non-specular and AFM, contain valuable information
on the surface roughness of the samples that the specular measurement does not probe.
The non-specular measurements and the AFM measurements yielded information about
the lateral roughness, revealing that the platinum coating had a much more wavy surface
than that of iridium. The underlying cause of the differences has not been established
in this work and will need further research. But the analysis that has been made in this
project could help define the future methodology for quality assurance of the NewAthena
mirrors.
As a part of the analysis, GenX and REFS were compared. This was done both through
fitting the data and trying to get similar results, but also through simulations. It was
found that they used different optical constants and that underlying models, primarily for
the non-specular measurements, were different. The specular fits mostly varied below the
critical angle and in the valleys between the kiessig fringes. Due to other issues in REFS
it was found that GenX is the best choice for future fitting of non-specular fits, due to the
flexibility of the software.
Lastly, it was also found through optical microscopy that there was a lot of surface con-
tamination on the platinum-coated witness samples.
Since a lot of the contamination
could be removed by wiping the samples with a cloth, it is most likely something that
comes from either the storage or handling, which should probably be adressed to avoid.
8.1
Future work
Since this project was time limited, there was not time for everything and priorities were
made. The main result of this was probably that it was diﬀicult to go into all the details
of all the analysis methods that were used. Not only because of the time, but also because
it is diﬀicult to go into the details of everything while still keeping a good overview.

Therefore, it would probably be very beneficial in the future to look more into the under-
lying models of both the non-specular fitting in GenX, but also the PSD analysis. Doing
this could yield a more detailed and better understanding of the samples and what is seen
in the measurements. For example, why do platinum coated samples show broad specular
peaks and how can this be modeled using the prior information? Or how can one get bet-
ter estimates of the correlation length and rms roughness from the PSDs? Maybe there
exist other and better ways to obtain the correlation length and rms roughness from the
AFM measurements. Fx using software such as Gwyddion and similar programs.
It would also be interesting if a custom model was created for simulating non-specular
measurements in GenX. This way it could be implemented exactly the way one wants to
and maybe limit the model to a smaller amount of parameters, e.g. removing interdiffuse
roughness.
It would also be beneficial to use a more powerful pc for the non-specular fits in GenX.
The computation times were very long for some of the datasets and it could take hours to
go through fx 200 generations in some of the fits. This was also the reason for why most
of the non-specular fits were only fitted on shorter ranges of data. The amount of data
that is included in the fits can probably be extended with a more powerful pc.
Lastly, further investigation into the growth of platinum coatings definitely is needed, and
also how this affects the performance of the mirrors if they are used in a telescope such as
NewAthena.
