Abstract
This study focused on the solubility of FeCO3 as a means to predict the the corrosion and scaling
that occurs in the the oil producing wells in the Halfdan ﬁeld, using the thermodynamic model
extended UNIQUAC. It was concluded that a decrease in temperature or an increase in CO2
would increase the solubility of FeCO3. Furthermore, a temperature change at lower tempera-
tures had a greater eﬀect than the same change at a higher temperature. It was concluded that
the formation of CaCO3 had an inhibiting eﬀect on the solubility of FeCO3. The precipitating
salts included FeCO3, BaSO4, CaCO3, and in some extreme cases SrSO4. Examinations showed
that salts with the anion Cl– or SO 2–
4
generally had an increasing eﬀect on the solubility of
FeCO3, while addition of salts with the anion HCO –
3
or CO 2–
3
generally had an decreasing
eﬀect on the solubility of FeCO3. The HDA-29 and HDA-38 wells were also examined. It was
concluded that the temperature had a greater inﬂuence on the solubility of FeCO3 than the CO2
pressure throughout the well. It was observed that the formation waters, Formation Water 1
and Formation Water 2, did not show an equilibrium with the reservoir, when the reservoir con-
ditions were modelled. Thus, alternative compositions were determined for the two formation
waters. For Formation Water 1, Ca2+ was changed from 2209 to 1923 ppm, Ba2+ was changed
from 1 to 0.75 ppm, and HCO –
3
was changed from 400 to 915 ppm. Furthermore, 722 ppm
CO2 was added in order to match the 1.5 bar CO2 in the reservoir. For Formation Water 2,
Ca2+ was changed from 286 to 550 ppm, Ba2+ was changed from 11 to 3 ppm, and HCO –
3
was
changed from 400 to 1341 ppm. Furthermore, 847 ppm CO2 was used in order to match the 1.5
bar CO2 in the reservoir. The weight loss experiment that was conducted, conﬁrmed that an
increase in CO2 lead to an increase in corrosion rate. The temperature had a similar eﬀect, i.e.
an increase in temperature resulted in an increase in corrosion rate, caused by the presence of
oxygen.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background and the cost of corrosion
Corrosion is the process of converting a metal into a more chemically stable form, resulting in
degradation of metal equipment and tools. It has been a problem as long as metal has been in
use, although it was not until 1763 that the ﬁrst solution to a corrosion problem was suggested.
The problem was the corrosion of the ship HMS Alarm. It had a hull covered by copper, which
was attached with iron bolts. The corrosion process that occurred on the ship was what is today
known as galvanic or bimetallic corrosion. By making sure that the two metals were not in
direct contact, the corrosion problem was solved. [1]
In the beginning of the 1800, corrosion was ﬁrst identiﬁed as a electrochemical phenomenon,
and since then more studies has been conducted within the ﬁeld of corrosion.[2]
The corrosion in the industry today is costly, as it degrades equipment and create the risk
of environmental, personal, and economic cost. A study from 2002 estimates the cost of corro-
sion to be 137.9 billion US$ in the USA alone every year, with the cost of corrosion in Oil and
Gas exploration and production is estimated to be 1.4 billion US$ annually. [3]

1.2
Aim of the study
This report will focus on the corrosion of the production wells in the oil industry, speciﬁcally
the Halfdan ﬁeld situated in the North Sea.
The aim of this study is to examine the scaling and corrosion that occur in a production well.
This will be done using the thermodynamic model extended UNIQUAC (UNIversal QUAsi-
Chemical) to predict solubility of FeCO3 as a function of the partial pressure of CO2 and with
variations in temperature. The solubility curve will be created for diﬀerent ﬂuids present in the
production well. The graphs will be used to determine under which conditions corrosion will
occur, and which will yield scaling (precipitation). The model will be able to determine the
nature of the scale, based on the ﬂuid composition.
The extended UNIQUAC will also be used to examine the eﬀect that diﬀerent salts in the ﬂuids
have on the solubility of FeCO3.
Furthermore, well data will be implemented into the model in order to determine possible scaling
or corrosion for speciﬁc wells.
The compositions used to compute the solubility graphs will be evaluated and optimized.
The study also include an experimental part that aims to conﬁrm the predictions made by the
extended UNIQUAC.

2. CASE
2
Case
As mentioned, this report is focusing on the Halfdan ﬁelds in the North Sea. The ﬁeld has been
operated since 1999, and has a high porosity, 25 −30%, and a low permeability, 0.5 −2 mD.
The temperature is around 80◦C and pressure is 29 MPa. [6]
The ﬁeld consists of horizontal wells 3048-4572 m long i an alternating pattern of water injection
wells and production wells. The spacing between wells is 183 m. [6]
The water ﬂooding method is used to increase production, and the material for the wells is
L80-1. This steel type is less expensive than other variants, but subject to corrosion [7].
Figure 2: Halfdan ﬁeld with alternating water injection wells and production wells. HDA-01XD
is a section that was examined in [6].
While water injection is a commonly used tool in the North Sea, Halfdan is facing a number of
problems.
As the water is injected into the formation it travels through the reservoir. While the water is
in the reservoir it is heated due to the higher temperature in the reservoir, and when it reaches
the production well it is around 75 ◦C. [6]
If there are larger fractures from an injection well to a production well, the water will reach
the production well faster (breakthrough time is shorter). This will result in less oil produced,
and colder water of 60 ◦C ﬂowing into the production well. The problem with a decrease in
temperature is the increase in solubility of FeCO3 that could otherwise form a protective layer.
If the protective layer of FeCO3 is not formed, more corrosion will occur. As the Halfdan ﬁeld
is know for its low amount of CO2 and H2S [6][8], the formation of a protective layer is already
3 of 94
==PAGE:12==
limited.
Thus, it is of interest to determine how dependent the solubility of FeCO3 is on temperature
and CO2 concentration.
H2S and the mechanisms it preforms is beyond the scope of this study, and will not be discussed
in this report.

3. THEORY
3
Theory
This section includes a introduction to the basics of modern oil production as well as mechanisms
and expressions used in order to interpret the results.
This includes diﬀerent EOR methods, the corrosion mechanism in the well, phase diagrams,
Pourbaix diagrams, salting-in and -out, and the equilibrium constant.
3.1
Oil production
The process of extracting oil from the subsurface becomes increasingly more diﬃcult and com-
plicated as the easily accessible reservoir is depleted. Today, the reservoirs are both onshore and
oﬀshore and at great depth. This means that the cost of extracting the oil increases, and thus
the oil companies want to extract as much as possible, in order to make a proﬁt.
When starting production, the pressure in the reservoir enables the oil to ﬂow by itself. However,
after a while the pressure in the subsurface has decreased so much, that the oil can not ﬂow by
itself.
3.2
EOR
A way to solve the problem with the insuﬃcient pressure is to apply EOR or Enhanced Oil Re-
covery methods. It covers methods that enhances the recovery beyond the primary production,
and aims to extend the lifespan of a ﬁeld.
Figure 3: An overview of the type of EOR method used in the diﬀerent types of reservoir. [9]
What kind of EOR method is used, depend on the reservoir type. The two main types of reservoir
5 of 94
==PAGE:14==
is sandstone and carbonate/chalk as seen in Figure 3. The ﬁgure also shows what type of EOR
is most commonly used for each reservoir. The chart clearly shows that sandstone reservoirs are
much more common than a chalk reservoir, which is in agreement with the literature. [10]
Although, as mentioned earlier, the North Sea reservoirs is located in chalk.
3.2.1
Fracturing
The production from a reservoir depends heavily on the porosity and permeability of the rock
in the reservoir. While chalk has a high porosity in the range of 0.25 −0.30, it has a low perme-
ability, 0.5 −2 mD. These values are in good agreement with the values observed in the ﬁelds in
the North Sea, including the Halfdan ﬁeld[11]. In contrast, the porosity of sandstone is typically
between 0.05-0.25 [12] and permeability is 1 −100 mD [13] [10].
The reservoirs in the North Sea is located in chalk, and in order to produce anything of signiﬁ-
cance, the oil must be able to ﬂow. Fracturing the reservoir is a method used to achieve a better
ﬂow. Either by injecting HCl to dissolve the chalk, or by hydraulic fracture. Hydraulic fractur-
ing is done by pumping down water and applying pressure to the rock until the the strength of
the rock is exceeded, and fractures are created.
3.2.2
Water ﬂooding
Water ﬂooding is done with an injection and production well. The injected water travels through
the reservoir, pushing the oil to the production well, maintaining a pressure in the reservoir that
is high enough for production.
An illustration of this is shown in Figure 4. The water will only move the mobile oil, and leaves
behind the immobile oil. This oil might be caught in a dead-end pore, or trapped between the
grains as seen in Figure 4.
While water ﬂooding is easily implementer, with seawater being easily available oﬀ-shore, there
are a few drawbacks to this method.
While in theory, the water will go through the reservoir in a uniform way, this is not the case,
as a reservoir is not uniform. This will result in water ﬂowing faster through sections with
high permeability, creating what is known as ﬁngers. This will result in a faster breakthrough
time, and thus a lower recovery rate. This eﬀect can be an unwanted consequence of fracturing,
but does also occur naturally. Adding polymers to the injection water is one way of lowering
breakthrough time and increasing recovery. This will be discussed in Section 3.2.3.

Water ﬂooding is widely used in the industry, as the method is simple and easy to imple-
ment. While water ﬂooding is not part of the primary production, it is implemented early on in
the life cycle and categorized as secondary production, not EOR which is also known as ternary
recovery.
3.2.3
Polymers
As mentioned above, adding polymers to the injected water is a way of increasing the recovery
rate. The polymers work as a seal in front of the water, ensuring a uniform progression through
the reservoir. This is illustrated in Figure 5.
Figure 5: Water injection with and without polymers. Top picture has no polymer, resulting in
ﬁngers, shorter breakthrough time, and less recovery. The bottom picture shows added polymer
that ensures a uniform ﬂooding and thus a higher recovery [14].
The polymers work by forming a plug at the front, forcing the water to ﬂow through channels
that would otherwise not experience ﬂow. In this way, more oil is moved by the water, and more

==PAGE:16==
oil is recovered from the reservoir.
3.2.4
Thermal recovery
Thermal recovery is deﬁned as the methods that uses heat to produce oil. This deﬁnition covers
both steam injection and in-situ burning. Thermal recovery is the dominant form of EOR in
sandstone, as seen in Figure 3.
Steam injection is the most commonly used method, and as the name suggest, steam is injected
into the reservoir. By heating the oil, the viscosity is lowered, allowing the oil to ﬂow more
easily. Steam injections are used in reservoirs with light oil.
In-situ burning is not a widely used method for either carbonate rock or sandstone. [15] However
it is identiﬁed as thermal recovery, and is therefore is mentioned here.
The method works by injecting oxygen into the reservoir, and then igniting some of the oil in
the reservoir. Heating up the remaining oil will lower the viscosity, making it easier to produce.
The method is combined with other methods, such as water injection. [16]
In-situ burning is used with heavy oil, but the process is complicated, limiting its use.
3.2.5
Gas injection
Injection of gas follows the same principles as other injection methods: injection of gas into an
injection well, which result in a greater production at the production well.
As the name suggest gas is injected into the reservoir, altering the properties of the oil. Gas
injection is often used in reservoirs containing light oil and in HPHT (Hight Pressure High Tem-
perature) wells. Mainly two types of gas is used for gas injection: N2 and CO2.
In HPHT wells and light oil reservoirs, N2 can reach miscible conditions, which increases the
recovery. It is not a requirement that N2 can reach miscible conditions when used as injection
gas in wells, so N2 is also used when miscible conditions are not reached. The use of N2 in gas
injection is associated with high capital and operational cost. The gas is therefore not widely
used, and it is not expected to change. [17]
Another alternative to N2 is CO2. This gas has been successfully implemented, and the success
is tied to the accessibility of the gas. [17]
While gas injection is not widely used in sandstone reservoirs, the method is the most commonly
used EOR method in carbonate reservoirs, as seen in Figure 3.

3. THEORY
3.3
What is corrosion and what parameters have an inﬂuence
As mentioned in the introduction, corrosion is the process of converting a metal into the most
chemically stable form at the relevant conditions. The rate of corrosion depend on a number of
factors, these are discussed in Section 3.3.1. These include composition, temperature, pressure,
and ﬂow in the well.
Corrosion can be divided into two diﬀerent types: sweet and sour corrosion. In sour corrosion
sulfur corrodes the material [18]. In sweet corrosion, the corrosion is caused by the presence of
CO2 [19].
For the reservoirs in the North Sea, it is primarily sweet corrosion that occur[20], and thus sour
corrosion will not be discussed further in this report.

3.3.2
Iron
The casing in the Halfdan ﬁeld is the L80 steel[6]. As previously mentioned, this is a low grade
steel, and thus it is vulnerable to corrosion. The low grade steel is used due to the low cost.
The perfect solution would be to have a casing that would not corrode. Materials such as stain-
less steel is known for its corrosion resistance, and would be an obvious choice. The one major
draw back is the price as a casing with a more complex composition is more expensive to make.
Using the low corrosive material would make the production unproﬁtable. While these pipes
will corrode, a protective layer of FeCO3 would inhibit the corrosion, making the low grade iron
pipe a much more viable option.
3.3.3
Temperature
The solubility of FeCO3, and thus corrosion of the iron pipe depends on temperature. [22][23]
The solubility increases with a decrease in temperature, meaning that if the temperature is too
low, FeCO3 will not precipitate and form a protective layer. If the opposite is true and the
temperature is to high, a very thick layer of FeCO3 will form. This is also known as scaling,
and can block the ﬂow in the well.
3.3.4

of FeCO3, as an increase in pressure will lead to an increase in CO2 dissolved in the liquid,
following Reaction (2), which in turn will lead to an increase in H2CO3 with Reaction (3), and
thus more FeCO3 [24].

3.3.5
Composition
Composition of the ﬂuid ﬂowing through the tube has a big inﬂuence on how fast the corrosion
occurs, and which reactions will occur. When producing oil, there are primarily two types of
water: sea water and formation water. The sea water is present, as it is pumped down to the
reservoir as part of the water injection process.
The other type of water present is the formation water. This is water that was already present
in the reservoir from the beginning. Since it is water that has been trapped and isolated along
with the oil and gas, the composition diﬀers from that of the sea water.
3.3.6
Sea water
Sea water is used to reduce the time needed to produce the oil. This is done by pumping the sea
water down in order to maintain a reasonable pressure in order to produce the oil. Before the
sea water is injected into the well, it is treated in order to minimize wear on the well. Materials
removed includes solids, gas, biological material, and dissolved solids (sulfate). [25]
Solids in the surface water could potentially block the well, while removal of gas such as oxygen
or sulﬁdes will decrease the corrosion rate. While some things are removed from the ﬂuid, others
are too expensive, meaning that the ﬂuid is still corrosive when injected into the reservoir.
3.3.7
Formation water
A conventional reservoir contains both gas, oil, and water. They are generally separated, but
have reached an equilibrium over the millions of years spent trapped underground. Due to the
isolation from other water sources, and presence of both oil, gas, and the reservoir rock, the
composition of formation water will diﬀer from that of sea water.
Table 1 show the composition of the sea water, and two diﬀerent compositions for the formation
waters found in the Halfdan ﬁeld. The samples are from the same ﬁeld, but have been recovered
from diﬀerent depth, resulting in the variation.

much corrosion will occur.
The faster the ﬂow, the faster the oil, gas, and water will move through the pipe, which is
important in regards to the formation and existence of the protective FeCO3 layer.

3.7
Salting-in and -out
The addition of electrolytes (salts) in water has been studied for a long time, and is known to
change the solubility of non-electrolytes.
The phenomenon where the solubility of non-electrolytes is increased is known as ”salting-out”,
while the phenomenon where the solubility is increased is know as ”salting-in”.
Salting-in is often relevant when studying proteins.
Salting-in occurs when small amounts
of salts are added to a solution with a non-electrolyte (in this example a protein). The salt
will associate with the hydrophobic areas of the protein, shielding it from the water molecules.
This will increase the activity of water as less water molecules are required to interact with
the protein. In turn, this means that more water molecules are free to interact and dissolve
non-electrolytes [38] [39].
Salting-out occurs at higher salt concentrations than salting-in and is explained as follows [40]:
With an increase in dissolved salt, more water molecules are occupied with interacting with the
salt. This leaves less water molecules to interact and dissolve the non-electrolytes.

Inﬂuence of parameters and evaluation of composition
The large amount of CaCO3 that precipitate according to the extended UNIQUAC for Forma-
tion Water 1 was not expected. Neither was it that almost no CaCO3 precipitated for Formation
Water 2. It would be expected that the formation waters are in equilibrium with the chalk reser-
voir, and thus are saturated at reservoir conditions, also referred to as bottom hole conditions.
The unexpected predictions could indicate an incorrect composition. Determining an alternative
composition was possible with the extended UNIQUAC.
As mentioned in Section 5.2.2 the solubility graphs that were created by the extended UNI-
QUAC are based on a number of assumptions regrading, temperature, pressure, and composi-
tion. While temperature changes for each graph, the compositions are predetermined.
If these predetermined values were not correct, the model would give a prediction that does not
ﬁt the data from the well.
It was assumed that the samples of the formation waters that were analysed were purely liquid,
and that the formation waters were in equilibrium with the chalk reservoir at bottom hole con-
ditions.
There were no data available about how the sample was extracted, stored, or analysed, and thus
it was diﬃcult to evaluate the the given composition, and thus the large precipitation of CaCO3.

6.5
Conclusion and improvements for future experiments
The aim of the experiment was to determine if the trends observed in the model could be
conﬁrmed experimentally.
The experiment was successful in proving that a higher corrosion would occur with a higher CO2
content. It did, however, not show the expected trend in the corrosion rate. That the trend
showed an increase in the corrosion rate with an increase in temperature, instead of a decrease
is expected to be due to unwanted oxygen in the experiment.
The corrosion rate was generally lower than expected. Initial corrosion for mild steel in marine
conditions at atmospheric pressure is around 0.2−0.3 mm/year for 20-25 ◦C [52][53]. Meanwhile,
a higher corrosion rate is expected for the experiment with CO2 present, due to the acidiﬁcation
of the ﬂuid, and thus a more corrosive environment.
The reason why the experimental corrosion rates are so low may be explained by the following:
• The setup of the experiment did not account for the ﬂow in a well or removal of saturated
ﬂuid. This leads to a decrease in the corrosion rate and high scale formation.
• The setup also did not provide a way to accurately determine the amount of dissolved CO2.
The 3 bar CO2 was applied to each relevant sample at the beginning of the experiment, and
the CO2 subsequently dissolved into the ﬂuid. However, there was no way of accurately
determining the dissolved CO2 concentration in the ﬂuid.
There was, however, still a
visible and quantiﬁable diﬀerence when CO2 was applied.
• The removal of the scale proved insuﬃcient, resulting in an estimated corrosion rate that
was lower than the actual corrosion rate.
A more accurate result could have been obtained by a more eﬀective scale removal. This could
be done by studying the rate at which the HCl removes the scale, and identifying at what point
it starts to remove the uncorroded sample. This would allow all the scale to be dissolved in the
acid, while leaving the uncorroded metal. Alternatively, a diﬀerent concentration of HCl could
be used to remove the scale.
The setup is more diﬃcult to change, as investing in the correct equipment might prove expen-
sive.
If it were possible to change the setup, it could be improved in the following ways:
• A stirring element, such as a magnet could be added to an appropriate container, in order
to prevent the layering of dissolved sample in the ﬂuid, that will otherwise build up at the
bottom of the container.
67 of 94
==PAGE:76==
• In order to ensure that the pressure in the container is kept at 3 bar, resulting in the CO2
concentration being constant, CO2 could be bubbled trough the ﬂuid continuously, instead
of applying CO2 at the beginning. This would ensure a more accurate CO2 content in the
ﬂuid, throughout the experiment.
In order to ensure that no oxygen was present, the following steps could be taken:
• The oxygen in the deionized water should be removed. This could be done by purging the
water with N2 [54].
• The preparation of the ﬂuids should be done in a oxygen depleted environment.
• The containers should be ﬂushed with N2 before the ﬂuid and sample are added, and
should be kept in an inert environment a few hours before the experiment.
• The experiment should be conducted in an inert atmosphere, such as an N2 atmosphere.
• The handling of the samples after the experiment should be done in an inert atmosphere.
Despite the shortcomings of the experimental setup and procedure, it did successfully provide
results proving that if CO2 is added, the corrosion rate will increase.

Discussion
The report was split into three main parts: introduction and theory relevant for the project,
solubility estimations made with the extended UNIQUAC, and a weight loss experiment. This
section will summarize the main points discussed in those parts.
The ﬁrst major part of this study was the literature study where EOR, corrosion, the weight
loss experiment, and Pourbaix diagrams were introduced. As this part was mainly theory, there
were no results to discuss.
The second part of the study was the interpretation of the solubility graphs created using
the extended UNIQUAC. The study examined the trends of the solubility of FeCO3 in Water,
sea water, Formation Water 1, and Formation Water 2 as a function of the partial pressure of
CO2. The study also encompassed a change in temperature in order to determine the inﬂuence
on the solubility of FeCO3 and thus corrosion in the well. These results were compared with the
literature, in order to validate the trends observed. The trends for CO2, HCO –
3 , and CO 2–
3
was also examined, in order to ensuring that they behaved as expected, which further validated
the results.
Formation Water 1 deviated signiﬁcant from the other ﬂuids, which was believed to be caused
by the high Ca2+ concentration, as CaCO3 precipitated in the CO2 pressure range where the
solubility changed the least. The mechanisms behind the dissolution and equilibrium constants
were used in order to explain this behavior.
The eﬀects that the salts had on the solubility of FeCO3 were examined individually in order to
determine if removing one before injection of sea water would have an eﬀect. The solubility of
FeCO3 as a function of NaCl was observed to behave diﬀerently, compared to the other salts.
The solubility of FeCO3 displayed a maximum, increasing or decreasing, depending on the NaCl
concentration.
As this behavior of the FeCO3 solubility was unique for NaCl, experimental
validation of this prediction was needed. Although experimental data for FeCO3 did not exist,
data for CaCO3 was found. As the chemical structure of these compounds are similar, the ex-
perimental data for CaCO3 was deemed representative for FeCO3. Under this assumption, the
model prediction was shown to be in agreement with the experimental data.
A comparison between the four pure ﬂuids, Water, sea water, Formation Water 1, and For-
mation Water 2 was made in order to clarify the diﬀerence of FeCO3 solubility between the
ﬂuids, as well as determine why they diﬀered. The diﬀerences could be explained by the diﬀer-
69 of 94
==PAGE:78==
ence in the concentration of the diﬀerent ions such as SO 2–
4
and Cl–. Sea water has the highest
concentration of these speciﬁc ions, and is also the ﬂuid with the highest solubility of FeCO3
at all of the examined pressures and temperatures. This interpretation is supported by the salt
study done in the report.
The extended UNIQUAC was also used to examine the solubility of FeCO3 in a scenario where
the ﬂuids would mix, which should result in a more realistic picture of the solubility. In addition,
the inﬂuence each of the ﬂuids had on the solubility of FeCO3 were discussed. Especially the
high concentration of Ca2+ in Formation Water 1 had a big inﬂuence on the solubility of FeCO3
in the sea water/ Formation Water 1 mixture.
After both the pure ﬂuids, salts, and mixed ﬂuids were examined, two examples from actual
wells in the Halfdan ﬁeld were used as examples. This was done in order to determine how the
model could be used to predict scale in an actual well. The examples were chosen based on
the geographically close proximity and the diﬀerence in produced formation waters. The plots
were done by assuming a linear change in pressure and temperature throughout the wells, same
depth for both wells, and that the wells only produced water with a composition corresponding
to Formation Water 1 or 2. The wells were HDA-29 and HDA-38, both part of the HDA section
in the Halfdan ﬁeld.
The two wells had diﬀerent pressures and temperatures in the well as stated in the report.Although,
the temperature in the two wells did not diﬀer signiﬁcantly, and thus did not contribute to the
diﬀerence in the solubility of FeCO3. The pressures in the wells did diﬀer, and thus they could
have an inﬂuence on the solubility of FeCO3. However, the biggest diﬀerence in the compositions
of the two formation waters was the Ca2+ concentration, and thus the inﬂuence of Ca2+ was
examined.
Overall, the results found for the solubility of FeCO3 and precipitation of salts did not be-
have as expected, as the compositions for the formation waters had either too much or too little
Ca2+. This inaccuracy was identiﬁed, as the formation waters did not show equilibrium between
the ﬂuids and the reservoir, when the reservoir conditions were modelled. The extended UNI-
QUAC also showed that BaSO4 precipitated at reservoir conditions for both ﬂuids, and thus it
was altered for both ﬂuids, using a similar method as for the Ca2+ adjustments.
Adjustments were made to the two formation waters, by decreasing the Ca2+ and Ba2+ content
in Formation Water 1, and increasing the Ca2+ content while decreasing the Ba2+ content in
Formation Water 2. The adjusted values resulted in an equilibrium between the ﬂuids and the
chalk reservoir. The new compositions were not used in this study, but could be used in future
projects.

7. DISCUSSION
The last part of the report focused on the experimental setup, method, execution, and in-
terpretation of the results. The aim of the experiment was to simulate the well conditions, and
thus verify the trends that were observed in the modelling.
The experiment was designed, so that the eﬀect that the temperature, CO2, and composition
had on the solubility of FeCO3 could be determined.
The CO2 dependency was easily determined with the experiment. The temperature dependency
showed the opposite trend of what was expected. It showed that the corrosion rate increased as
the temperature increased. This trend is in agreement with the expected corrosion rate in an
oxygen rich environment.
The hypothesis regarding this trend is that Fe2+ oxidized to Fe3+ due to the presence of un-
wanted oxygen. If Fe3+ was present it would precipitate as Fe2O3, and the amount of Fe in the
aqueous phase would drop.
The container with sea water with added Fe and 0 bar CO2, that was conducted at 25 ◦C showed
no corrosion of the sample. As such, the Fe content was deemed representable of how much Fe
was dissolved before the experiment was initiated. The Fe content was 200 ppm Fe, however, an
analysis after the experiment showed less than 200 ppm Fe in the ﬂuid. This further supports
the hypothesis about the presence of oxygen and Fe3+.
The inﬂuence of the ﬂuids proved hard to determine, due to lack of data, as the experiment
was only completed twice due to time limitations. Two data points for each ﬂuid were deemed
unsatisfactory in regards to determining the inﬂuence of the composition.
The ﬂuids were, however, analysed in the extended UNIQUAC, and showed precipitation of
CaCO3 at 0 bar CO2 for all ﬂuids except the one with added FeCl2. For this ﬂuid, the model
predicted a precipitation of FeCO3, although, this precipitation was limited due to the oxidiza-
tion of Fe2+ to Fe3+ which then precipitated as Fe2O3. Some FeCO3 might still have precipitated,
as there was still Fe2+ present in the aqueous phase, although, the scaling was not analysed and
identiﬁed.
At 3 bar, no CaCO3 was expected to precipitate, and FeCO3 was expected to precipitate for the
sea water with FeCl2 at 50 ◦C.

Conclusion
This report focused on the FeCO3 in the well in the Halfdan ﬁeld. By studying the solubility of
FeCO3 it was possible to estimate the corrosion/scaling in the wells.
The solubility of FeCO3 was modelled for four diﬀerent ﬂuids: sea water, Formation Water 1,
Formations Water 2, and pure Water.
The examination showed for all ﬂuids, that a change in 1 ◦C at lower temperatures had a greater
inﬂuence on the solubility of FeCO3 than a change in 1 ◦C at a higher temperature.
The inﬂuence of CO2 on the solubility of FeCO3 was examined. It was found that an increase
in dissolved CO2 would lead to a higher solubility.
Removing salts from the sea water was a possible solution to lowering the corrosion observed
in the production well, and thus the eﬀect that possible salts in the ﬂuid had on the solubility
of FeCO3 was examined. The examination concluded that increasing the concentration of salts
with the anions Cl– and SO 2–
4
would increase the solubility of FeCO3 (except for NaCl). Fi-
nally an increase in the concentration of salts with the anion CO 2–
3
or HCO –
3
would result in
a decrease of the solubility of FeCO3.
The salt NaCl increased or decreased the solubility of FeCO3 depending on the concentration
of NaCl. For NaCl concentrations between 0 and 127,000 ppm, adding more NaCl resulted in
an increase in the solubility of FeCO3. For NaCl concentrations between 127,000 and 360,000
ppm the solubility of FeCO3 decreased. The examinations were done for 25 ◦C, but the trend
was observed regardless of the temperature. The trend was in agreement with the literature.
Comparing the ﬂuids, showed that at very low CO2 concentrations, Formation Water 1 was the
ﬂuid with the highest solubility of FeCO3. The addition of CO2 resulted in sea water having
the highest solubility, and Formation Water 1 having the lowest.
The extended UNIQUAC was used on two well scenarios. One well that produced Formation
Water 1 (HDA-29), and a well that produced Formation Water 2 (HDA-38). It was concluded
that the solubility of FeCO3 was lower in HDA-29, primarily due to the high concentration of
Ca2+ and thus precipitation of CaCO3 throughout the well. It was also concluded, that the
change in temperature of the well had a higher inﬂuence on the solubility, than the change in
dissolved CO2.
Alternative compositions for the two formation waters were determined, assuming equilibrium
with the chalk reservoir at bottom hole conditions. For Formation Water 1, the Ca2+ concen-
tration changed from 2209 to 1923 ppm, Ba2+ was changed from 1 to 0.75 ppm, and the HCO –
3
concentration was changed from 400 to 915 ppm. For Formation Water 2, Ca2+ was changed
from 286 to 550 ppm, Ba2+ was changed from 11 to 3 ppm, and HCO –
3
was changed from 400
to 1341 ppm. These new compositions can be used in a future study.
The weight loss experiment successfully proved that an increase in CO2 leads to an increase in

Future Work
The work in this report was heavily based on the solubility predictions made with the extended
UNIQUAC. The original compositions for the formation waters used in the extended UNIQUAC,
resulted in predictions that did not ﬁt the proﬁle that would be expected for a ﬂuid in equi-
librium with the chalk reservoir. Thus, new compositions for the two formation waters were
suggested. These compositions could be used in a future project.
This study also examined how the solubility of FeCO3 behaved in a well, by using actual data
from two wells from the Halfdan ﬁeld. Due to time limitations, only two wells were examined,
and only one scenario was applied to each well. A more in-depth analysis could be conducted,
examining a wider variety of wells at diﬀerent conditions in order to clarify the eﬀect of tem-
perature, CO2, and composition of the produced ﬂuid.
Finally, the experimental part concluded that oxygen had been present during the experiment,
and that it had an inﬂuence on the result. New experiments could be conducted, with focus on
limiting the exposure of the sample to oxygen, as described in the report.
