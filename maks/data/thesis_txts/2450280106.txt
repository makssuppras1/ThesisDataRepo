Abstract
The foreseen high penetration of wind power into the power systems imposes ad-
vanced requirements to sustain the frequency stability in the network. One of this
requirements for wind turbines is to provide synthetic inertia, which emulates the
behaviour of a synchronous generator during frequency disturbances. For the provi-
sion of this synthetic inertia, wind turbines can also be supported by energy storage
systems.
This thesis investigates the effect of introducing energy storage systems in the power
system in order to support the synthetic inertia or fast frequency response from wind
turbines. Results are analysed utilising the genetic algorithm and fmincon as opti-
mization methods for the fast frequency response control. In addition to this, the
impact of two control system approaches as close loop and open loop is studied at
wind speeds below and close to the rated speed of a wind turbine.
Results have shown that the introduction of energy storage systems in the power
system is beneﬁcial for the support of provision of FFR from wind turbines. Fur-
thermore, the genetic algorithm and close loop approach returned a more adequate
frequency response in the power system than fmincon and the open loop approach.
This is mainly noticeable at wind speeds close to the rated speed of the wind tur-
bine.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During the last century, the world has experienced a great change in the energy
scenery. This evolution has been possible mainly due to an increasing investment
in renewable energy, impulsed at the same time by a greater demand of energy and
international agreements such as the Kyoto Protocol, the Montreal Protocol or more
recently the Paris Agreement, all of them aiming at reaching a sustainable devel-
opment and reducing the greenhouse gas emissions. Over the years and due to an
evolution in the different renewable energy technologies, renewable energy is start-
ing to compete in price, and thus getting cheaper compared to conventional ways
of generation. According to [1–3], it is expected that by 2020, they will become def-
initely cheaper. As consequence of this, the share of the so called ”green” energy
has increased considerably in countries as China, India, United States, Germany or
United Kingdom, being Europe and Asia the regions that have driven this change
for the last ten years [4].
However, the introduction of renewable energies brings into play new challenges
for the electric power system. One of the most important ones, presented in [5]
and [6], is the accomplishing of the so called ancillary services, requirements which
were previously carried by conventional generators and that will have to be per-
formed by these newcomers, wind turbines among them. New grid code require-
ments have been recently developed by the European Union [5] and member states
as Denmark [7], deﬁning how the new renewable technologies installed should be-
have in order to support the grid. Some of these ancillary services consist on inertial
response, frequency or active power control, fault current contribution, fault ride
through and low voltage ride through. The inertia response given by a system inﬂu-
ences the way in which the frequency and active power in that system is controlled.
In order to improve them and reach a greater power system ﬂexibility, some ideas
are proposed in [8], such as getting a better control on demand response, improve
the interaction with non electrical energy networks, being able to get more accurate
renewable energy source forecasts and the integration of energy storage.
In this context, emphasis in this thesis is on analysing the option of implementing
energy storage systems, focusing on its contribution to improve the synthetic inertia
from wind turbines.
1.1
Motivation and Problem Formulation
One of the main challenges in power systems with large scale of renewable genera-
tion is to keep the frequency of the system as close as possible to the nominal value.
These challenges arise as consequence of an increasing displacement of conventional

plants trip and stop delivering power to the network. Another cause of the reduc-
tion in inertia of the power system is the replacing of conventional generation units
by renewable power plants.
Given this scenario, the research and development of new technologies that could
support the grid in case of faulty situations, in which frequency is affected, is of great
importance. Recent advances in energy storage systems (ESS) show that this equip-
ment is not only able to provide energy during intermittences of the wind [10], but
also is a back up for the grid in critical circumstances, as it improves energy avail-
ability, system’s stability and provides some of the aforementioned ancillary services
such as inertial response and frequency or active power control. Added this to the
fact that its cost is being reduced every year [11] and that the trend is expected to
continue in the future, ESS presents a huge potential to develop. In addition to this,

it is an option for improving the frequency response capabilities of the power sys-

Speciﬁcally in the case of wind energy, ESS are utilised in order to improve different

1.2
Objectives
The overall objective of this project is to investigate how the combination of wind
turbines with energy storage systems can provide an improved frequency control
support to the power system.
In order achieve that objective, wind turbine and energy storage system models will
be implemented. Furthermore, a study of the fast frequency response will be done,
reaching a further understanding of the behaviour of both components when work-
ing together.
An optimization is used to enhance the frequency response of the power system
under frequency disturbance situations. This is done due to the fact that a wrong
implementation of the frequency control might lead to greater issues in the power
system. In this way, parameters playing a major role in the fast frequency response
controls will be optimized. This is relevant, as during the frequency response to the
fault, wind turbines usually provide an overproduction of energy at the cost of re-
ducing the angular speed of the rotational mass [12]. After the power unbalance, the
overproduction drops in an undesired attempt from the wind turbine to recover its
previous rotational speed, reducing the injection of power to the network [12]. This
drop, also known as recovery period, will be targeted in this work too as one of the
effects to mitigate.
1.3
Assumptions and Limitations
Due to the broad and complex nature of the topic that is being considered in the
present thesis, a series of assumptions and limitations have been taken into account:
• The wind turbine operates at constant wind speed during the whole process of
simulation. Due to the fact that the scenarios under investigation in the present
thesis are not developed in a long span of time, the wind speed adopted for
each simulation is taken as stable magnitude. In reality, wind speed changes
because of gusts and small wind ﬂuctuations. However, for small ranges in
the order of one minute, these variations are not relevant enough to affect the
outputs extracted from the studies.
• Voltage in the network formed by the wind turbine and the energy storage
system is considered to be constant. As stated before, the main objective in
this project is to improve the frequency response. Therefore and in order to
focus in its analysis, the voltage from the grid will be taken at a steady value.

• Following the same reasoning, ESS will only focus on providing frequency
support. As consequence, all the power supplied from this device will consist
on active power, being the reactive power set at zero. This will be further
explained during the discussion of the different models.
• The present work does not consider aerodynamic and structural loads. There-
fore, features related to loads and fatigues are left out of the scope of this study.
• Likewise, the economical aspects are not included in the present project, as it
focuses on reaching the best possible frequency behaviour from the compo-
nents. Therefore, aspects such as reduction in costs for the ESS model or the
energy dispatched will be considered as topics that should be addressed in
future works.
1.4
Structure
The present thesis is divided in the following sections:
Chapter 1: Introduction. A general introduction to the project is presented, focusing
on describing the problem of providing fast frequency response from wind turbines
and the motivation to introduce ESS as possible solution to enhance the frequency
response of the power system. Finally, the objectives to be accomplished by the end
of the thesis are stated.
Chapter 2: State of the Art. This chapter provides the state of the art both for wind
turbines and ESS technologies, as well as for frequency control methods and op-
timization approaches. In addition to this, each part is put in the context of fast
frequency response studies.
Chapter 3: Modelling for Fast Frequency Response. The model control blocks and
their functioning are introduced in this chapter. Firstly, the power system presents
the different types of generation units and their construction based on transfer func-
tions. The wind power plant is modelled as an upscale single wind turbine, describ-
ing its controllers. Likewise, the main control blocks for an ESS are explained.
Chapter 4: Optimization Methods for Fast Frequency Response. In this chapter,
the optimization methods implemented for solving the simulation problem, fmincon
and genetic algorithm, are described. Furthermore, two approaches that are applied
in the simulation (open loop and close loop) are explained, presenting their charac-
teristics and the differences between them.
Chapter 5: Fast Frequency Response Optimization of Wind Turbines with Battery
Energy Storage Systems. The incorporation ESS to wind turbines is studied. This
is done by evaluating several scenarios for the provision of fast frequency response.
Different approaches and optimization methods are employed. Finally, a sensitivity
study of the effect of a delay in the provision of fast frequency response is presented.
Chapter 6: Conclusion. The main conclusions of the thesis are presented. This is
done by reviewing the objectives set at the beginning of the work and comparing
them with the results obtained in chapter 5. Finally, different options for future
works related to the topics discussed in the project are included.

2.1
Overview of Wind Turbine Technologies
It was in the 1980s when wind energy started to stand out, in part because of the
research by institutions and the increase of energy prices after the oil crisis, which
evidenced the need of renewable sources of energy. These wind turbines complied
with a standard or determined set of characteristics, being known as Type I. The
main feature of these machines is that they operate at ﬁxed rotational speed regard-
less the wind speed [13]. However, that speed is imposed by the frequency of the
network, as the induction generator (wound rotor or squirrel cage) is directly cou-
pled to the grid. Being very robust, simple, reliable and low cost in the electrical
components are some of the advantages that this solution presents. Nevertheless,
ﬁxed speed machines have the disadvantage of showing a unique point of maxi-
mum efﬁciency, for which the wind turbine is designed. Due to this, some models
included two different conﬁgurations for the pairs of poles. Thereby, the maximum
efﬁciency can be reached at least in two operation points: four pairs of poles (for
lower wind speeds) and two or three pairs of poles (at high wind speeds). Another
important aspect of Type I wind turbines is the large amount of reactive power that
they consume because of the inductive behaviour of the generator. This is partly
compensated with capacitor banks. The capability of these turbines to provide an-
cillary services is almost null. Not only most of the ﬂuctuations from the wind are
transmitted to the grid in the form of power ﬂuctuations, but also these machines
do not provide a good performance for fault ride through situations, as well as its
short circuit current contribution is high and can damage part of the machine. This is
translated into an even greater consumption of reactive power under fault scenarios,
which can contribute to a greater unbalance in the grid. As depicted in Figure 2.1
Type I is equipped with a gearbox and a soft-starter that reduces inrush current dur-
ing connection and disconnection of the turbine to the network.

The term ﬁxed speed is usually associated to the power control technique known as
stall control, which can be differentiated in active and passive stall. In the case of
passive stall, is the simplest and most robust method, as the blades are bolted to the
hub at a ﬁxed angle. When the wind speed is higher than the designed limit, the
blades start to stall, limiting the output power of the turbine. This power control is
slow, which leads to a smoother power output. However, it is obtained at the cost
of a reduced efﬁciency during lower wind speeds and a maximum power at steady
state that might vary due to the grid’s frequency and air density. In the case of active
stall, the control is actively done by pitching the blades. Therefore, during low wind
speeds, the system is controlled by pitching towards or against the wind, depend-
ing on the desired attack angle, so that maximum efﬁciency is achieved. Active stall
control differs from pitch control because during high wind speeds, the blades are
pitched in opposite direction, getting a smooth power output. Last Type I models
implemented another power control technique: pitch control. In the same way than
active stall, the blades can be pitched to reach the maximum efﬁciency during low
wind speeds. At high ones, the output power is kept very close to the rated one, al-
though with fast ﬂuctuations. In addition to this, the high complexity of this method
adds some difﬁculties to the system. The main advantages of this type of control are
easier emergency stops and start ups of the turbine and a power control close to op-
timum.
Nowadays, stall control is mainly related to ﬁxed speed in the same way that vari-
ables speed is related to pitch control due to power limitation reasons. This consid-
eration comes from the fact that stall control (active and passive) in a variables speed
turbine limits the capability of reducing output power in a fast way. Because of this,
in case of a strong gust with the machine running at maximum rotational speed, the
torque can increase exceeding the critical value, leading to a possible runaway situ-
ation.

that can be obtained re around 10 to 16%, depending on the technology. Regarding
power quality aspects, it is still close to Type I, as it shows a high consumption of re-
active power and has limited ways to compensate it and the capability for fault ride
through is reduced, although it is slightly better as a consequence of the variable

A new conﬁguration appears for Type III. It uses gearbox and a doubly fed induction
generator, as it is connected to the grid through both the stator and the rotor. While
the stator has a direct connection, the rotor is coupled to the grid by means of a
partial-scale frequency converter, which makes possible to increase the speed range
to 30%. The frequency converter is composed by a rotor-side converter, in charge of
controlling the active and reactive power exchange of the stator with the grid, and
a grid-side converter, responsible of keeping the voltage in the DC link constant.
This last converter is also able to control the exchange of reactive power with the
grid through the rotor. However, in most of the cases the reference is set to zero,
so that the whole reactive power exchange is done through the stator. Finally, both
converters are joined by a DC link with capacitors that keep the voltage stable. Type
III machines are mainly used for onshore facilities. The main advantages of Type III
turbines are the low transmission of aerodynamic power ﬂuctuations to the grid and
its capability to compensate the reactive power consumption due to the partial-scale
converter. Furthermore, ancillary services as fault ride through are now possible, as
the power converter controls the reactive power and voltage of the machine, so it
does not get disconnected from the network. On the other hand, the introduction
of power electronics results in the injection of harmonics to the grid, depending on
the quality of the converter, and the loss of capability to provide inertia to the power
system, as the rotor of the generator is decoupled from the grid due to the power
converter. Moreover, this solution requires additional protections as a chopper re-
sistance in the DC link or a crowbar in the rotor of the machine and the short circuit
current contribution is still high, as the generator is still directly connected to the
grid through the stator.

2.2
Overview of Energy Storage Technologies
As power plants and technologies evolve and renewable energy increases its rele-
vance in the power mix, new challenges arise. Two of these questions are the de-
creasing inertia in the system, as conventional power plants are being substituted
by alternative sources which use power electronics, and the fact that there can be
enough wind for wind generation or sun for solar generation, but if the demand is
not high enough, that power will be lost due to curtailment, as injecting that power
in the grid could also cause instabilities. As consequence of this situation, energy
storage systems appear to offer a solution, as although they are converter driven de-
vices, they can help with frequency and voltage regulation in case of imbalance or
fault. At the same time and as its name indicates, they are able to store the energy
that otherwise would be curtailed, so that it can be used during periods in which
the power demand was greater than the generation and there was no production
enough from renewable energy and conventional units.
Furthermore, the relatively recent emergence of energy storage technologies added
to the fact that depending on the application it is better implementing a different
alternative, have caused the development of many topologies.

2.3
Fast Frequency Response and Synthetic Inertia
As it was already mentioned in the introduction, frequency is the parameter that
measures the balance between generation and consumption of power. If the system
is not at the nominal frequency, this is, a deviation occurs, the system will require
or reject energy. This energy is usually stored in the rotating masses of the genera-
tors that are connected to the network, so that they slow down when the energy is
required by the system (frequency below nominal value) or accelerate when there is
excess of energy (frequency above nominal value). When a fault occurs, the inertia
of the system, formed by the rotating mass of the synchronous generators, avoids a
sudden change in the frequency. With the integration of more renewable energy in
the power system, technologies that are driven by power electronics such as wind
or solar energy are reducing the total inertia of the system, as the incidence of the
synchronous generators is lower.
There are three main parameters which are typically analysed in transient phenom-
ena, as they indicate the robustness of the system. Therefore, by enhancing these
three factors, the frequency behaviour will be improved:
• Rate of change of frequency (RoCoF). It can be observed right after an inci-
dence occurs in the system. It indicates the rate at which the frequency drops
during the transient. With low inertia, RoCoF reaches greater slopes, falling
much faster. This means that the capability of the system to react against this
imbalance has to be fast as well. Otherwise, the frequency would continue
dropping, increasing the possibilities of observing several generator discon-
nections or even a blackout. The greater the inertia, the slower the slope, in-
creasing the time in which the system can react.
• Minimum instantaneous frequency or nadir frequency. During transient phe-
nomena, is the lowest value reached for frequency. In systems with high iner-
tia, RoCoF is less steep and nadir can be observed at higher frequencies than
in systems with low inertia, in this value is usually lower.
==PAGE:28==

• Steady state frequency. It is the ﬁnal value around which the frequency sta-
bilizes after the transient. In situations with high penetration of renewable
energy, the steady state frequency is lower than in scenarios where there is a
greater inﬂuence of synchronous generator driven units.
Under some circumstances, a second depression of the frequency can be visible, also
called recovery period, right after the minimum instantaneous frequency. This oc-
curs because the generator tries to recover the kinetic energy that has been injecting
to the grid in the ﬁrst moments after the disturbance as well as its previous rotat-
ing speed, so part of the power that the turbines are generating, is actually used to
accelerate the rotating mass. This can be a challenge, as a system with high wind
penetration might experience a second frequency drop even larger than the initial
nadir frequency.
In the moment in which a power converter is installed in a wind turbine, the gen-
erator is decoupled from the grid, isolating the rotating mass and loosing in that
way the natural inertia of that turbine. However, the converter is also able to em-
ulate the release of kinetic energy, due to the frequency independence of both sides
of the full scale converter in Type IV turbines. This additional power can be ob-
tained by providing an electrical torque that counters the RoCoF, balancing change
of frequency [19]. This process, denominated in the literature as synthetic inertia or
synthetic inertial response, has been thoroughly studied by the research community
in the last years.

As a consequence of the wind energy dominance in the power system over other

clariﬁcations have been given, it can be derived that synthetic inertia or synthetic
inertial response refer to the reaction observed in a generation unit in order to emu-
late the behaviour of a synchronous generator. Hence, synthetic inertia is a form of
control enclosed in fast frequency response.
For a complete visualization of the frequency response, it is also important to bear in
mind the different stages of frequency control necessary for providing appropriate
ancillary services. This stages, represented in Figure 2.7, are deﬁned as:
• Primary frequency control. Consists on a response given from a generating
unit with a governor droop control, that adjusts the output power depending
on the frequency divergence with respect to the nominal value and the droop
parameter. Its ﬁrst missions is to stabilize the frequency at a reasonable level
in the ﬁrst seconds after the disturbance. Therefore, it is activated in the ﬁrst
seconds after a disturbance is detected and actuates between 30 seconds and 1
minute, moment in which the secondary control takes over the responsibility.
This primary stage is also known as droop control [21].
• Secondary frequency control or automatic generation control (AGC) has the
objective of restoring the nominal frequency in a synchronous region by inject-
ing power from the frequency containment reserves. In addition to this, the
error in tie line power ﬂow is also targeted to be reduced as much as possible.
Starting once primary control is deactivated, it can last up to 10 or 15 minutes.
• Tertiary frequency control is the longest frequency control method, as it is ac-
tivated around 15 minutes after the incidence and can take up to some hours
until it is completely deactivated. It aims at restoring the frequency contain-
ment reserves used during secondary control, in case another anomaly hap-
pens. Under some circumstances, it can actuate instead of secondary control.
Furthermore, this method is activated manually from the transmission system
operators.

2.4
Optimization Algorithms
One of the objectives set for project is to optimize the frequency response given by
the power system, focusing on the power that the BESS can provide. In this section,
a ﬁrst approach to different optimizations will be presented.
Optimization is a process that has as objective to reduce efforts or to get the maxi-
mum the proﬁt from an activity. Its origins can be tracked back to Newton, Lagrange
or Cauchy in the XVIII century, with the minimization of functions. More recently,
optimization processes have become much faster and sophisticated, mainly due to
the arrival of computational tools in the XX century, which allowed the development
of new areas of investigation and optimization theories [22].
Optimization problems are primarily deﬁned by three components:
• Objective function. Declares in which way the optimization is going to be im-
plemented. Usually it consists on maximizing or minimizing the output.
• Variables. All the parameters that have any inﬂuence on the value of the out-
put of the objective function are considered as variables or unknowns.
• A set of constraints, that restrict the value of the variables to certain values.
These constraints are introduced as a need to obtain optimized results which
make physical sense. Even though constraints are not indispensable for the
deﬁnition of optimization problems, they practically appear in all of them.
There exist many different ways to classify optimization algorithms, such as by type
of constraints, nature of the variables, nature of the equations that deﬁne the objec-
tive function, structure of the problem or even the number of objective functions that
==PAGE:31==
16
Chapter 2. State of the Art
form the problem. In this case, they will be split into different categories in a simpler
way: classical and advanced methods [22].
Classical or numerical methods are those algorithms that use differential calculus
to give solution to problems that are based in maxima or minima of continuous and
differentiable functions. These have little application on practical problems, as they
do not comply some of that requirements. Nonetheless, classical algorithms are the
basis for more advanced approaches. Some of these optimization techniques are:
• Linear programming. In this technique, the objective function is linear. Like-
wise, the variables that deﬁne the objective functions have to be deﬁned in
terms of linear equations or inequalities.
• Integer programming. As its name indicates, this method has the restriction of
having all its variables as integers.
• Quadratic programming. Similar to linear programming, its variables can only
be deﬁned implementing linear equations and inequalities. On the other hand,
quadratic terms can be found in the objective function.
• Non-linear programming. In this optimization technique, at least one non-
linear equation has to be stated in order to determine the objective function,
the constraints or even both.
• Stochastic programming. Independent on how the objective function is de-
ﬁned, this method uses stochastic or random variables to describe the con-
straints. Due to this, constraints depend on values that come out of random
phenomena.
• Dynamic programming. This technique is based on making smaller problems
out of the main one, so that those smaller problems are simpler and faster to
solve.
• Combinatorial optimization.
It is focused on issues that can be solved by
means of discrete solutions or can be simpliﬁed to them.
• Inﬁnite-dimensional optimization. This optimization algorithm analyzes prob-
lems in which the possible solutions belong to an inﬁnite-dimensional space.
• Constraint satisfaction. Focuses on scenarios where the objective function is
constant. This method is very popular in problems related to artiﬁcial intelli-
gence.
With the appearance of computation, optimization algorithms increased in complex-
ity and added new characteristics. Besides this, other relevant factors as reduced
computational times and costs were introduced, changing this ﬁeld. These recent
techniques are denominated as advanced methods and in most of the cases, they are
named from activities that can be observed in nature, as the way in which their so-
lution is implemented resemble those processes. Some of the main algorithms that
are considered in this category are:
==PAGE:32==
2.4. Optimization Algorithms

• Hill climbing. This graph searching technique consists on nodes and paths.
The problem is optimized by ﬁnding nodes that are closer to the solution than
the previous path’s end. This optimization method is broadly used in the arti-
ﬁcial intelligence ﬁeld. Furthermore, small changes can be made to the starting
node to obtain related algorithms.
• Simulated annealing. It is created from the material science process, in which
the heating ﬁrst and the slow and controlled cooling in second place, help to
increase the crystal size of different materials, reducing in that way the defects.
In the optimization technique, the aim is to reach a solution with the minimum
internal energy point, where a physical system would reach an equilibrium.
Therefore, the objective function would be considered as the internal energy
and each possible solution in the search space would be a possible state of the
system.
• Genetic algorithm. Although there are many types of evolutionary algorithms,
the genetic one has been extensively used. Based on biological techniques of
recombination, inheritance and mutation, the genetic algorithm is formed by a
random initial population which has descendants. From those, the most valid
ones (the ones that ﬁt better for a solution) are taken and crossed again until
a ﬁnal output is obtained, so several iterations have to be considered. It is
important to note that the solution will be approximate and might vary if the
process is repeated, as mutations in the descendants are not always the same.
• Ant colony optimization. When ants are looking for food, they walk randomly.
In the moment in which they ﬁnd it, they come back to the anthill releasing a
trace of pheromones, which is used and reinforced by other ants until they
reach the place where the food was found. If the path is no longer used, the
pheromone track is lost. Likewise, the ant colony algorithm is implemented to
ﬁnd possible answers, until the followed path is optimum (a feasible solution
that is at a ’short distance’ from the initial state). In the same way, the fading of
the trace does also help the exploration of different solutions, as otherwise in
the moment in which the ﬁrst possible solution is found all solutions would try
to follow that path. This method has been thoroughly applied in transportation
systems.

3.2
Wind Turbine Model
The model of the wind turbine that has been implemented for the fast frequency
response simulation is the one of a Type IV, as it was already presented in chapter 2.
According to the standard IEC 61400-27-1 [27], there are two model speciﬁcations
that are recommended for the implementation of these wind turbines: Type 4A and
Type 4B. The ﬁrst one does not consider any power oscillations, as its mechanical
and aerodynamic models are not taken into consideration; while the second option,
4B, contemplates power oscillations, as it includes a 2-mass system in its mechanical
model. However, this is done at the cost of assuming constant aerodynamic torque.
As the output power is of interest for this study, the selected model for its implemen-
tation is based on the Type 4B model, taking into account the variable speed wind
turbine (VSWT) model already proposed in [24]. The parameters used for the wind
turbine model are obtained from [25]. These values are presented in Appendix A.
In Figure 3.3, the operational concept of a VSWT can be observed, where 2 zones can
be differentiated: the ﬁrst one, delimited from A to C and denominated as power
optimization region; and the power limitation region, deﬁned from C to D. The op-
timization zone receives its name from the objective of maximizing the aerodynamic
efﬁciency, i.e. the power coefﬁcient (Cp). Therefore, from A to B it is shown how
the turbine starts at the cut-in wind speed, at around 3 m/s, with the minimum ro-
tational speed, at a maximum Cp that is maintained and at a pitch angle very close
to zero degrees. As the power increases with the wind speed, the rotational speed
increments until the nominal value is reached or this variable is limited. Then, from
B to C, the power continues increasing, but pitch angle and rotational speed are kept
constant. As consequence, the Cp will start decreasing its value. The power limi-
tation zone has an opposite target to the optimization one. In this case, from C to
D, the wind speed is considerably high and loads and stress on the turbine compo-
nents might lead to some issues. As result of this, the aerodynamic power has to be
limited, aiming at getting a constant output power at the rated value. This is done

Maximum Power Point Tracking Table
The target of the MPPT table is to return the optimum value possible for the gener-
ated power to the input rotational speed of the generator. It is important to remark
that each wind turbine type has a different MPPT table, as this characteristic in a
ﬁxed speed machine is quite limited and depending on the VSWT, the generator
speed that can be covered varies considerably.
FIGURE 3.8: Maximum Power Point Tracking Control Strategy [25]
In Figure 3.8, it can be observed that at lower wind speeds, the generator speed in-
creases gradually, so that the optimum power, which also increments its magnitude,
is followed. At large wind speeds, the generator speed is kept at its rated value and
as consequence of this, the power that the MPPT table returns is also the rated one.
Nevertheless, when the machine is operating at generator speeds close to nominal,
some issues can arise. Under ideal conditions, the control strategy to be followed
is to produce the rated power at point C right after the generator speed has slowly
reached its nominal value at point B. As this situation leads to errors in the sim-
ulation, the strategy implemented for the MPPT table present in the wind turbine
model consists on creating a very steep slope, from points B’ to C’ in Figure 3.8. As
a result of this new approach, the simulation issues are solved and a close approxi-
mation to reality is achieved. Moreover, large power oscillations operating close to
rated speed in the ideal case are reduced.
Similarly to the Cp table seen in the aerodynamic model, the MPPT table is based

Fast Frequency Response Control
The fast frequency response model has the purpose of giving a reference power out-
put depending on the frequency deviation that is being received at the input. Two
parts can be observed in this model: the FFR activation and the FFR actuator. In
order to create a model which deals results closer to reality, a rate limiter is im-
plemented as well. Therefore, in case of really steep slopes coming from the FFR
actuator, the output power is limited to a certain maximum and minimum.
FIGURE 3.11: Fast Frequency Response Controller
The FFR activation block is in charge of triggering a signal so that the frequency
response is activated when there is a frequency deviation in the system. In that
way, turbines and batteries start delivering power reserves. As it can be seen in Fig-
ure 3.12, this controller presents some restrictions for the activation of the triggering
signal. This is done with the objective of not providing a response in occasions in
which small deviations of the frequency occur during the daily power system op-
eration. Therefore, to activate the FFR, RoCoF and ∆f have to be greater than a
threshold value. Another limitation is the rotational speed. This variable cannot be
lower than its minimum value, as operation below this cause stall of the blades. If
this situation is reached, FFR is disabled. The S-R ﬂip-ﬂop is a set/reset block that
activates the output Q if RoCoF and ∆f are above the threshold and the rotational
speed is greater than the minimum limit. In case that any of this conditions is not
accomplished, the ﬂip-ﬂop goes to the reset state (Q is deactivated). Finally, the
EnableIR parameter allows to decide externally if it is wanted to deliver the FFR or
not.

frequency, a second dip in the frequency signal and the steady state. By reducing the
nadir frequency, the easier it is to redirect the disturbance and reach the nominal fre-
quency again. The second dip is a problem that arises mainly in systems that, as in
the case of wind turbines, need to recover part of the power injection that provided
in the ﬁrst moments after the disturbance. As consequence of this, a new distur-
bance can appear in the system’s frequency. The third component, the ﬁnal steady
state frequency time, indicates how fast can the system recover from an imbalance.
As result of this, if the the steady state frequency is reached in a short time, that is a
sign of a robust network. As the algorithm implemented in the simulation optimizes
a single objective, these three components have to be formulated in a unique objec-
tive function. Due to the different importance of the nadir frequency, and steady
state frequency for the simulations, they receive different weights, being nadir the
most relevant one (as a very low value can cause a blackout in the network), the
second dip the next one (as the reduction of the recovery period by injecting active
power from the BESS is one of the aims of this project) and ﬁnally, the steady state
frequency. According to all the previous reasoning, the objective function is deﬁned
as:
ObjFunc = α · fNadir + β · f2nd dip + γ · TSS
(4.1)
α = 0.5
β = 0.35
γ = 0.15
With all this variables, the optimization algorithm computes the parameters that re-
sult in a value for the objective function. The outcome from that optimizations is
then compared and a ﬁnal solution is achieved, were the objective function has the
lowest value.
This objective function was provided by DTU, based on the work done in [24].

4.3
Genetic Algorithm Optimization
Compared to fmincon, the genetic algorithm (GA) is a complex optimization, as it
includes a more advance technique when selecting the values for the optimized
variables. As the previous algorithm, this is a nonlinear optimization for both con-
strained and unconstrained problems, which guarantees to ﬁnd a solution for the
FFR, as it cannot be linearized accurately and needs to run the simulation in order
to identify the proper parameters. At the contrary than fmincon, the GA is indepen-
dent from the initial conditions given to the problem, which results in more accurate
outputs. Moreover, as it was already stated in chapter 2, GA is thoroughly used for
the study of power systems and is relatively fast compared to other optimization
method. The GA ﬂowchart is depicted in Figure 4.2.
FIGURE 4.2: Genetic Algorithm Flowchart
The GA is an optimization method based on natural selection and evolution. After
the initialization of the problem, the ﬁrst population is created randomly, already
==PAGE:53==
38
Chapter 4. Optimization Methods for Fast Frequency Response
taking into account all the restrictions and constraints for the variables to optimize.
Then, the behaviour of those variables is studied after being applied to the problem,
assigning them a ﬁtness value. Those members that belong to the ﬁrst population
and have better genes (i.e. their ﬁtness value is higher than the average), are selected
to reproduce, obtaining the so called children. There are three types of children: elite,
crossover and mutation. The ﬁrst type is formed by the parents with the greatest
ﬁtness values and due to this, all the members of this group are selected to advance
to form the next population. Crossover children are those descendants created as a
combination of a pair of parents. Mutations are applied as well in individual parents,
introducing extra variety to the population. As consequence of the implementation
of this optimization, a ﬁnal result (the last child with high ﬁtness value) is obtained
after several iterations, once the change of the ﬁtness value regarding the parents is
less than the stated tolerance. This method reduces simulation time and maintains
the accuracy of the result by combining the most feasible variables, instead of calcu-
lating all the possible results by varying the parameters randomly [30].
The GA, similarly to fmincon, presents sets of options to deﬁne how to make the
optimization apporoach to the problem. Some of the GA option settings relevant for
this study are:
• Reproduction options. Speciﬁc approaches to the creation of children are pro-
posed, as elite count (the number of individuals that belong to the elite group
in each iteration and, as result, have direct access to the next population) or
crossover fraction (fraction of individuals from the next generation that are
obtained as result of a crossover).
• Population options, that modiﬁes the criteria of the population present during
the iterations along the optimization. The main parameters are the type of
population, the size of that population or initial parameters that can be used
for the initialization of the GA.
• Stopping criteria options. These are the criteria that make the optimization to
stop once they have been achieved. The most relevant criteria are the number
of iterations, time limit or the tolerance.
For the present optimization process, the GA has been given upper bounds and
lower bounds in which the parameters should be found. In the same line than fmin-
con optimization, the function tolerance has been augmented from the default value
(1 × 10−6) to 1 × 10−3. On the other hand and although it can be introduced as ad-
ditional setting, no initial parameters were proposed.
This optimization method is utilised in the MATLAB script provided by DTU, based
on MathWorks’ genetic algorithm [30] and the work done in [24].
4.4
Open Loop Approach
In the optimization process for the open loop approach, there are ﬁve parameters
that are optimized so that the resultant power shape is the one that will lead to the
best frequency behaviour. This parameters are four time variables (t1, t2, t3 and t4)
and a power limitation variable Pmax, IR. Those were chosen due to the fact that the
most convenient reference power shapes to optimize and implement are rectangular
==PAGE:54==
4.5. Closed Loop Approach
39
and triangular responses. Considering these variables, at t1 the FFR is activated,
ramping up until t2 is reached. This can happen because of two reasons: whether
the maximum limit of energy released from the rotating mass has been reached, or
the optimization returns a value Pmax, IR which is below this limit. This maximum is
set at 0.1 pu for the wind turbine, as above this value, both mechanical and electrical
fatigues and loads can be quite harmful for the machine. This does not apply for
BESS, whose maximum bound is 1 pu. The ramp from t1 to t2 is linear and its slope
is constant, depending only on the variables t1, t2 and Pmax, IR. In a similar manner t3
is the moment in which the power release starts decreasing and t4 the time at which
there is no need of delivering more power for the frequency response, or there is no
more power available that can be delivered. It is expected to obtain a greater slope
at the ﬁrst ramp, as it is the moment in which the fault is detected and most of the
power provisions are released so that the impact of RoCoF and the nadir frequency
can be countered. The ramp down, on the contrary, reduces the reference power in
a slower way, releasing gradually less energy. An example of the reference power
shape can be seen at Figure 4.3:

Closed Loop Approach
In the case of the close loop approach, similarly to the open loop approximation,
the maximum power that can be delivered by the wind turbine as consequence of
the FFR is 0.1 pu, avoiding mechanical and electrical stresses that could damage the
machine. In the battery case, it is designed to deliver as much power as needed un-
der faulty situations and thus, this limitation is 1 pu. As it was already introduced
in chapter 3, in the close loop approach the frequency deviation signal at the input
of the FFR control is not only a signal to trigger the FFR, but also the variable used

5.1.1
Fast Frequency Response Control Applying a Derivative Gain
During faulty situations, wind power plants have to provide ancillary services, i.e.
they have to collaborate in the balancing of the power system, to reach pre-fault val-
ues. There exist different methods used in the literature that have been proven to
work. In [34] a close loop approach is proposed to improve the frequency response
of the system. As presented in chapter 4, the FFR is active for a time Td, a constant
Kd is applied to the RoCoF (obtained from the derivative of the frequency deviation)
and a factor Kp is multiplied to the frequency deviation ∆f. However, a simple con-
trol that can be applied consists on setting Kp to zero, giving a target time during
which the FFR from the wind turbine will be activated and adjusting kd, the deriva-
tive gain, to a ﬁxed value.
Figure 5.2 shows the results of applying different values to Kd with Kp
= 0 and
Td = 15s for 50% of wind energy penetration at a wind speed of 7 m/s. It can be ob-
served that if no Kd is introduced, a scenario with no FFR is obtained. With greater
values for the derivative gain, nadir frequency values are enhanced. Likewise, an
augment of this constant is translated into a release of the kinetic energy available
in the rotor, increasing the output power of the wind turbines. This can also be ap-
preciated in the rotor speed, as its magnitude decreases during the extra injection
of power. A particular effect that can be seen at speeds below the rated wind speed
is the so called second dip in frequency, studied in [33–35] and produced as conse-
quence of the recovery period of wind turbines. This recovery period is a span of
time during which the output power is lower than the pre-fault value, as the rotat-
ing mass of the machine is recovering its initial spinning reserve. As result of this,
the rotor speed is restored to its pre-fault value and a second dip can be seen. This,
present in wind speeds below rated, is not observed at higher wind speeds due to
the fact that the increase of output power can be obtained from pitching the blades.
Therefore, as the energy is not being taken from the rotating mass, there is no need
to restore it and that second dip does not appear.
As it has been shown in Figure 5.2, a simple control with a derivative gain can im-
prove the nadir frequency. However, new issues appear that require a solution. For
that reason, a balance has to be found between the enhancement of nadir frequency
and the appearance of the second dip, which will be investigated by means of an
optimization.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Future Work
The objective of this project has been to investigate the support that energy storage
systems can provide to wind turbines when they are contributing with synthetic
inertia or fast frequency response to the grid. For this purpose, an optimization
methodology has been developed and implemented in order to enhance the FFR
provided by both WT and ESS in the power system. Furthermore, the model of
a power system is implemented in the software tool Simulink, detailing the differ-
ent controls of a WT and ESS. Several scenarios have been simulated utilising this
model, analysing the FFR provided by both WT and ESS.
In ﬁrst place, a general overview of the WT topologies and ESS technologies is pre-
sented, discussing their advantages and drawbacks. The most suitable option is
chosen to be implemented in the model developed in this project. This is done by
means of an analysis where models such as the wind turbine Type 4B (based on the
IEC standard [27]) or a battery energy storage system, focused on the provision of
FFR, are selected. Likewise, two control system approaches are deﬁned to investi-
gate FFR: a close loop approach (which acts according to the system’s frequency on
each moment) and an open loop approach (which uses the system’s frequency in
order to activate the FFR from WTs and BESS).
Once the models have been implemented in the simulation tool Simulink, the op-
timization process is presented, where the objective function that sets the aim of the
optimization is explained. The aim of the optimization is to enhance the frequency
response from WTs and ESS after a frequency event by improving the following
characteristics: nadir frequency, second dip and time lasted to reach the steady state
frequency. Furthermore, a description and explanation of the genetic algorithm and
fmincon optimization methods is introduced. The ﬁrst one is selected due to its ex-
tended use in power system studies, while the second one provides results in a short
computation time.
Finally, the simulation scenarios and results obtained from these simulation scenar-
ios are presented. This is done by explaining the challenges that high penetration
of renewable energy (as it is the case of wind energy) bring to the power system. In
addition to this, a description of solutions that have been proposed in literature such
as synthetic inertia from WTs is provided, reaching the conclusion that an optimiza-
tion of the FFR could greatly improve the frequency response in the power system.
Similarly, the inclusion of BESS could help to achieve the target of enhancing further
the frequency response in the power system, while reducing the stress generated in
the WT caused by the provision of FFR from WTs and BESS.

Chapter 6. Conclusion and Future Work
The scenarios in which the FFR provided from WTs and BESS is analysed, focus
on two different wind speeds, 7 m/s and 11 m/s, for which the second dip in fre-
quency is an issue. At 7 m/s, it can be derived that the fmincon optimization does not
return feasible outputs to the given problem, as it uses a non-convex search space
which returns several and very pronounced nadir frequencies that do not improve
the system’s response to a frequency disturbance. In addition to this, it is deduced
that the genetic algorithm is the most suitable option for optimization problems in
which the frequency response of the power system is analysed. This comes as result
of the enhancement for each of the proposed scenarios in which this optimization
method was utilised. At 11 m/s, only the genetic algorithm is applied, as fmincon
was not returning the expected results. For the two scenarios presented, the inclu-
sion of BESS in the power system was more beneﬁcial than for the scenarios at lower
wind speed.
Likewise, the two approaches investigated in this thesis have been compared. Con-
trary to what was expected, open loop approach dealt a greater performance in terms
of enhancing the frequency response rather than the close loop approach for the sce-
narios analysed at 7 m/s. Due to the characteristics of the close loop approach, which
acts on the frequency deviation and RoCoF according to the system needs, this FFR
actuator option was intended to return a greater enhancement for the frequency re-
sponse and variables from the WT in the power system. Results at 11 m/s met the
expectations showing the advantages of close loop approach. For this FFR actuator
option, the fast frequency response was greatly improved respect to the cases with
no BESS in the power system. This can be explained due to the appearance of issues
such as a second dip, present with an open loop approach, avoided with a close loop
approach. Furthermore, the energy injected from BESS to the grid was lower in the
case of a close loop than in the open loop approach. This also denotes a greater ef-
ﬁciency of the close loop approach when providing the FFR from WTs and BESS to
the power system.
As main conclusion, it can be stated that the introduction of BESS in the power sys-
tem is beneﬁcial for the support of provision of FFR from WTs. This is deducted
from the reduction that can be observed in output power from the WT and the ro-
tor speed. This considerably decreases the possibilities of reaching the minimum
rotor speed allowed (which would shut down the turbine) and attaining the max-
imum provision of power from the WT’s FFR (which could cause a great stress on
the machine). Additionally, it can be concluded that BESS is more beneﬁcial for the
support of FFR from WTs at wind speeds close to the rated wind speed of the WT
than at lower wind speeds. While the frequency response of the power system is
improved at 7 m/s with only WTs providing FFR to the network, this enhancement
is quite reduced at 11 m/s. However, the introduction of BESS in order to support
the FFR provided from WTs to the power system, allows to reach much greater im-
provements for the system’s frequency response, WT’s rotor speed and WT’s output
power at 11 m/s.

6.1. Future Work
67
6.1
Future Work
The objectives stated for this thesis are achieved within the scope and limitations.
However, some suggestions for future work are proposed. One of the options that
could be followed to extend the conclusions presented here are the study of econom-
ical aspects that involve the introduction of BESS in the system, broadening the un-
derstanding of need of this technology, deciding if it is economically viable to install
this technology in the network. Likewise, the fmincon optimization implemented
could be improved for provision of FFR purposes by considering a convexiﬁcation
of its search space, being able to reach feasible solutions for power system related
problems. A last suggestion for future works related to the topic presented in this
project is the sizing of BESS. An optimization of BESS size would be done, setting
a reference for the storage that is needed for each scenario. This would lead to an
optimal BESS size to enhance the frequency response in the power system for most
of the contingency situations.
