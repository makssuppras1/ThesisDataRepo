Abstract
This project uses numerical hydrodynamical simulations to study the small-scale mixing
effects in galactic discs in the early Universe. Particularly the impact of these effects on
the spread of rapid neutron capture process (r-process) elements is investigated. Based
on stellar nucleosynthesis theories four models for the likely production sites of r-process
elements are further investigated. The models each assume: ordinary core collapse super-
nova explosion (CC-SNe), exotic CC-SNe, neutron star merger (NSM) and time delayed
NSM as the primary production sites of the r-process. Mg is used as a tracer element
of the relatively well understood Оұ elements to test the validity of the simulations while
Eu is used as a tracer element of the less well understood r-process. By comparing the
distributions of these two elements to the observed abundances of the halo stars in the
Milky Way (MW) this project finds that: i) it is highly unlikely that these two elements
are synthesized in the same event, ii) both exotic CC-SNe and NSM could be the site the
r-process nucleosynthesis, with NSM being the marginally more likely model.

CHAPTER1
Galactic Chemical
Evolution
Only the very lightest elements (H, He and trace amounts of Li) were synthesized in the Big Bang, the
first generation of stars, the population-III (POP-III) stars, formed from a pristine gas of these primordial
elements. These stars are believed to have grown much larger than most of the stars we observe today and
therefore followed a much more rapid evolution. During their lifetimes these massive stars burnt the light
nuclei to heavier elements, some of which were returned to the interstellar medium (ISM) when these stars
died, likely in supernova explosions (SNes). From this mildly enriched gas a new generation of stars, the
population-II (POP-II) stars, formed. The gas from which these stars formed was likely only enriched by
the elements synthesized in a single SNe, therefore, the POP-II stars have extremely low content of heavier
elements (metals). Many of these stars have since evolved to the ends of their lives leaving unobservable
remnants behind, but the smallest of them still shine and can be observed today. They serve as an important
fossil record of the evolution history of the galaxy.
The complicated processes through which stars are born from the ISM and inject energy, mass and freshly
synthesize elements back to the ISM, launch winds and eject material out of the galaxy is at the heart of
the subject of galactic chemical evolution (GCE) [42].
1.1
Mixing in galaxies
Figure 1.1 is an illustration of a galaxy and the complicated patterns of material recycling taking place
around it. The disc of the galaxy (yellow-ish disc, at the center) is home to the majority of the galaxyвҖҷs stars.
It is here the bulk of star-formation takes place and where the metals synthesized in stars are returned to
the ISM. When the most massive stars die in CC-SNes they impart significant amounts of momentum on
the ISM, capable of launching winds of material out of the plane of the galaxy (e.g.[17]). This sets up a
galactic fountain in which enriched material from one part of the galaxy is recycled to an other part, thus
working to mix the gas in the galaxy across large distances.
If the winds are sufficiently strong, part of the out-flowing material will not return to the disc but be ejected
into the circumgalactic medium (CGM). By this process the otherwise pristine gas in the CGM is enriched
by the metals created in the stars. The galactic disc receives pristine gas from the CGM, replenishing the
galaxyвҖҷs supply of gas from which to form stars.

1.2
Simulations of galaxy evolution
This thesis focuses on understanding the small-scale mixing in galaxies in the early Universe. By small-
scale is meant the mixing taking place in the ISM within the disc of the galaxy, i.e. on order pc distances.
Special attention is paid to the mixing of r-process elements. The mixing processes are studied using hy-
drodynamical simulations of the interactions between massive stars and the ISM.
This work adds to the body of other work modelling the chemical evolution of galaxies. Several simu-
lations exists modelling the large scale interactions outlined above, notable examples of this include the
Illustris (and Illustris TNG) simulations which model a vast network of galaxies and their interactions,
element tracing has been carried out for some elements (Mg and Eu, among others) in this framework (e.g.
[39, 44]). Also the Feedback in Realistic Environments (FIRE) project studies the interactions between a
galaxy and its environment (e.g. [24] on the CGM). These cosmological scale simulations can model the
evolution and interactions of several 100 galaxies in complex set ups requiring vast resources and time.
An other approach to modelling large scale mixing effects are cosmological zoom-in simulations. In these
types of simulations a large number of galaxies are evolved in a cosmological context at low resolution.
From these a few candidate galaxies are selected for further modelling. The evolution of these galaxies is
then studied in detail including effects of hydrodynamics and stellar feedback, among others. A notable
example of such a simulation in the context of this work was recently published by Safarzadeh et al [51].
This work also investigates the mixing effects on the predicted distribution of r-process elements in the
galaxy, with a box size on order Mpc and a resolution limit of вҲј500 pc this model is unable to capture the
physics at play on the small scales at which stellar abundance enrichments are expected to take place.

1.3 Thesis objectives
3
At small scales (very near or even within the galactic disc) many studies have focused on the feedback from
SNe and their interactions with the ISM (newer examples include [17, 35, 36]). Studies of element mixing
at these scales include, Petit et al who modelled metal transport by turbulent mixing effects at a resolution
of 20 pc [43] and Yang & Krumholtz who modelled the mixing of a single metal scalar field in a periodic
(infinite) disc [66].
To the best of my knowledge this study is the first that focuses explicity on the impacts of small-scale mixing
on the distributions of r-process elements.
1.3
Thesis objectives
The goal of this project is to study the small-scale mixing effects in galactic discs in the early Universe (i.e.
at low metallicities). Emphasis is placed on mixing in the ISM due to feedback from SNe and the impact
of mixing on the spread r-process elements in the ISM. The primary goals of this thesis are:
I To model the mixing of Eu (as a tracer of r-process elements) assuming CC-SNe vis a vis NSM as the
primary astrophysical site of r-process production
II To compare the predictions of abundance spread in the ISM with abundance data of metal poor halo
stars, in the hope to contribute to the body of work trying to constrain the likely source of r-process
nucleosynthesis.
1.4
Structure of this thesis
This chapter has provided a brief outline of the chemical recycling processes in galaxies, the following
chapter will explore the life-cycle of stars, their interactions with their environment and the nucleosynthesis
processes that power the star through its life and the ways in which the synthesized elements are returned
to the ISM. This sets the stage for chapter 3 to introduce the observed abundances of metal poor stars in
the MW. The chapter introduces the basic concepts needed to discuss stellar metalicities and outlines the
general trends observed in the oldest known stars in our galaxy. The chapter concludes with a presentation
of the set of observational data which will serve as the measuring stick against which the results of this
project will be evaluated. Chapter 4 opens with a review of the equations governing fluid flow, leading into
a presentation of the numerical methods applied to solve them and the details of the specific simulations
run for this project. The chapter ends with an overview of the four models set up for this project. The
results of the simulations are presented in chapter 5. Discussion of the implications of the findings is saved
for chapter 6. Finally, a summary and outlook to future work concludes this thesis.

CHAPTER2
Stellar Nucleosynthesis
Since the 1930s it has been known that stars shine by the energy released in nucleosynthesis processes in
their cores [21]. In the 1950s the theory of stellar nucleosynthesis as the origin of the elements heavier than
He became widespread with the publication of the seminal work by Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler and Hoyle
[8]. Hence, the nuclear processes taking place in the cores of stars play a vital role not only for the stars but
the evolution of the galaxy in which they exist. In this chapter the life cycle of stars is explored following the
chronology of the stellar evolution, at each stage attention is paid to the most important nuclear processes
related to that evolutionary stage. Before these can be explored a short review of the basic nucleosynthesis
processes is in place.

For elements heavier than Ni the coulomb barrier becomes too large to be overcome in an exothermic pro-
cess if two (or more) charged nuclei are to be involved in the process. Instead these elements are formed

Figure 2.2 shows a section of the (N, Z) plane of atomic isotopes. Stable nuclei are found in the so called

2.2.2
Kelvin-Helmholtz contraction
Stars are born from clouds of gas and dust in the ISM. In order to form proto-stars the gas must contract.
This can only happen if the self-gravity of the gas is strong enough to overpower the pressure in the gas
which works to inflate the gas cloud. As gravitational attraction increases with increasing density and
pressure decreases with decreasing temperature, only gas clouds at sufficiently high density and low tem-
perature will be able to form stars.
Throughout the MW a number of such cold, dense gas clouds (nebulae) can be identified. Typical densities
are on the order nH вҲј100 /cm3 and the temperature is generally T < 100 K. A single nebula contains
вҲј1 Г— 103 MвҠҷwithin a volume вҲј(10 pc)3. Each nebula can give birth to a large number of stars in a
single generation [21].
When the appropriate conditions are reached the gas cloud will collapse due to the force of its own grav-
ity, this contraction will cause the temperature of the gas to rise. Eventually the temperature will become
hot enough for hydrogen fusion to begin in the core of the proto-star (hydrogen fusion is described in
more detail in section 2.2.3). As hydrogen burning becomes more and more efficient it releases a large
amount of energy which provides sufficient pressure to balance the gravitationally driven contraction of
the proto-star, and establish hydro-static equilibrium. Once both core hydrogen burning and hydro-static
equilibrium are establish the star is considered to have joined the MS and will stay on the MS as long as
both these requirements are fulfilled.
The typical proto-star stage lasts вҲј2 Г— 104 yr вҲ’2 Г— 107 yr with more massive stars moving more quickly
through this (and all subsequent stages) than their less massive counterparts.

the number of stars, N, formed with a given mass, M. A commonly used IMF is the Salpeter IMF which

2.2.4
Post-main sequence evolution
Once all the H in the core of the star is used up the star leaves the MS. As H is exhausted the rate of energy
release slows and the star contracts to re-establish thermal equilibrium. As the core contracts it heats,
heating the bottom layers of the envelope which react by expanding and cooling on the surface.
If the temperature, near the center of the star, becomes high enough shell H burning may ignite in the
layer of the envelope just outside the H depleted core. As shell H burning adds more He mass to the core
it continues to contract until it reaches temperatures of T вҲј108 K at which point core He burning begins.
This proceeds through the triple Оұ process.

2.3
Core collapse supernova
The exact explosion mechanism of a supernova (SN) is still not well understood [42, 21, 41]. The commonly
accepted picture is that: nucleosynthesis above the iron peak elements involves endothermic processes,
hence the moment the last of the Si fuel is exhausted the star runs out of its energy source. The sudden stop
in energy input causes the collapse of the core on a dynamical time scale, П„dyn, [42]. This collapse leads to
the distingeration of the iron peak elements into Оұ-particles, which subsequently disintegrate into protons
and neutrons. The core continues to collapse enough to force the electrons onto the protons forming
neutrons, eventually most of the core consists of neutrons alone and the collapse is halted by the degenerate
pressure of the neutrons. The sudden resistance of the core to further collapse leads to a bounce and the
formation of a shock front when the inner layers of the envelope come crashing onto the now rigid core.

2.3.2
Supernova Remnants
At the onset of SN the exploding star becomes separated into two distinct parts divided by the mass cut:
the stellar core collapses to form a compact object at the center of the explosion while the outer layers and
the envelope are violently expelled into the ISM. It is the light from this expanding material that can be
observed as a (type II) SN [55].
The shock front that expels the outer layers will continue to propagate through the ISM sweeping up inter-
stellar matter on its way and leaving behind a low density region in its interior. This is a SNR and can be
observed for centuries following explosion. The evolution of a SNR depends on the nature of the surround-
ing medium, but can be relatively well understood in a simple picture assuming a homogeneous ISM[10,
6, 12, 61], in this simple picture the remnant goes through four distinct phases:
1. The first phase is free expansion: during this phase the mass of the ejected material from the progen-
itor star is much larger than the total mass swept up by the shock front. Therefore the surrounding
mass does not yet pose a great inhibition to the expansion of the front which proceeds at a constant
velocity. This phase ends when the amount of mass swept by the front becomes comparable to the
ejected mass from the star.
For a star ejecting 6.8 MвҠҷinto a 0.1 atoms/cm3 density ISM the radius of the shock front is approx-
imately Rs = 9 pc when the two masses are equal. If the velocity during this phase is assumed to be
vs вүҲ1 Г— 104 km sвҲ’1 this phase will last approximately 850 yr.
2. As the shock front continues to propagate the surrounding medium poses a barrier that is increas-
ingly difficult to overcome. This causes the shock to decelerate which gives rise to a second shock
front travelling backwards into the interior of the SNR, this second front is called the reverse shock
front. The reverse shock heats the material behind the forward shock front to temperatures that are
sufficently high for the gas to glow in x-rays [55]. This second phase of the SNR evolution is known
as the Sedov-Taylor phase (after the two physists who first described it) or the adiabatic blast wave
phase as the shock front (blast wave) is expanding adiabatically, i.e. the material is not (yet) radiat-
ing significant amounts of energy.
As more and more cold interstellar matter is swept up the temperature of the SNR begins to drop. At
T вҲј2 Г— 105 K some electrons are able to recombine with nuclei and cooling through line emission
becomes a very effecient energy sink [55].
3. Once cooling becomes important (defined as the time when the remnant is radiating away a signifi-
cant fraction of its energy) the SNR has entered the third phase: pressure driven snow plough phase
(also some times called the energy conservation phase). The loss of energy to radiation causes the
shock front to further decelerate [37].

canonical NS with MвҲ—= 1.4MвҠҷand RвҲ—вҲј1 Г— 106 cm). Any r-process material synthesized on or near
the surface will need to overcome this potential barrier to escape and become available to enrich future
generations of stars. However, the presence of two NS of nearly equal mass and size may work to reduce the
tangential escape velocity of the system allowing for material to leave the system in tidal tails [58]. Using
3D hydrodynamical models of the merger event several authors have estimated the amount of mass that
is accelerated to high enough velocities to become unbound. Rosswog et al estimated M вҲј3 Г— 10вҲ’2 MвҠҷ
based on simulations of symmetric binaries [50], Goriely et al report expected unbound matter in the
range 1 Г— 10вҲ’3 MвҠҷ-1 Г— 10вҲ’2 MвҠҷ[23] and Roberts et al investigated multiple binary compositions in-
cluding symmetric and asymmetric DNS, as well as, NS-BH binaries and found the ejected mass to be
close to 5 Г— 10вҲ’2 MвҠҷ, irrespective of the masses of the binary members [48].

2.4.1
r-process synthesis
In a simple picture the r-process is able to operate in the dynamical ejecta of a NSM because of the high
densities of neutrons and relatively high temperatures achieved as the material expands from nuclear den-
sities (ПҒ вҲј1 Г— 1014 g/cm3 in NS). When the material becomes unbound by the tidal forces, it expands on a
hydrodynamical time-scale thereby significantly lowering the density. At lower densities the neutrons are
able to leave the nuclei, which then become unstable to ОІ-decay. Rapid radioactive decays ensue releasing
вҲј1 MeV/event and raising the temperature to T вҲј1 Г— 109 K. Eventually fission decay sets in causing
the temperature to rise very rapidly to вҲј1 Г— 1010 K, at these temperatures, with the expected density of
free neutrons the conditions are now favourable for r-process nucleosynthesis [58].
Employing a slightly more complex model Kasen et al find that the fate of the newly formed compact object
at the center of the NSM has important impact on which elements are synthesized in the r-process [26].
An illustration of two of their models1 is reproduced in figure 2.10, in this model a total of three seperate
components of the ejecta may apply. As the NSs merge tidal forces fling material into tails of debris (seen
as the orange torus in the figure), material at the contact surface between the two stars may be squeezed
into the polar regions and ejected along polar jets (the blue cones in the figure). Finally, some material
may become locked up in an accretion disc around the newly formed compact object at the center of the
merger, some of this material may later be expelled in winds.
The material in the tidal tails is expected to have high neutron densities and thus synthesize the heavy
r-process elements. While the squeezed material in the polar regions is expected to synthesize lighter r-
process elements. Finally, the material in the disc may synthesize heavy r-process elements if the object
collapses quickly to a BH (model b, in the figure)), whereas, if a long-lived NS results a high degree of neu-
trino irradiation is expected to lower the neutron content of this material and thus only allow the synthesis
of lighter r-process nuclei (model a).
The ability of NSM to synthesize r-process elements in solar abundance ratios has been shown, using nu-
merical simulations of nuclear reaction networks, to be quite independent of the initial conditions for the
NSM [23]. This is in contrast with many of the models of exotic types of SNe which require very specific
initial conditions in order to produce r-process elements in a solar abundance pattern (see also section
2.3).

The matter ejected from a NSM expands into the ISM in an explosive manner, Montes et al have carried

GW170817
On August 17th 2017 the advanced laser interferometer gravitational wave observatory (aLIGO) and ad-
vanced Virgo (aVIRGO) detected a вҲј100 s gravitational wave signal consistent with the merger to two

sGRB have long been suspected of association with merging DNS and the correlation in time and sky-
position of the events intensive searches in multiple electromagnetic wavebands followed soon after these
announcements. Approximately 10 hrs after the gravitational wave (GW) detection, an optical transcient
was discovered in the galaxy NGC4993, this detection was first given the name SSS17a (Swope Supernova
Survey) and later the designation AT2017gfo [2].

confidence [2] and the mass of the dynamically ejected matter to 1 Г— 10вҲ’3-1 Г— 10вҲ’2 MвҠҷ[1]. Follow-up
observations in nearly all electromagnetic bands (x-ray, UV, optical, infra-red and radio) showed the event
to be consistent with theoretical predictions of a NSM [26].
Key parameters determining the peak luminosity and light curve evolution of the transient are: the ejected
mass, Mej, the velocity with which this mass escapes, vej, and the chemical composition of the mate-
rial [25, 26]. Kasen et al find that the spectra of AT170817 are consistent with two ejecta components:

primarily consisting of heavy r-process elements. Over a year later the multi-messenger observations of
GW170817/GRB170818A/AT170817A are still the best evidence that NSM events are able to produce r-

4.2.2
Cooling function
Cooling of the gas in this context refers to the addition of a sink term in the energy equation (4.14) account-
ing for the loss of energy through radiation from the constitutents of the gas. More elaborate schemes are
possible which take into account the full coupling between the fluid dynamics and radiative interactions
(including heating due to radiation absorption), in these cases also the momentum equation (eq. 4.7) will
be modified by these inclusions. For the purposes of this project a simple approach is sufficient and cooling
is implemented as a sink term on the right hand side of the energy conservation equation.

4.3
ramses
ramses is an open source computer program intended for simulations of self-gravitating fluids using adap-
tive mesh refinement (AMR) on massively parallel computers [60]. The code was developed with the in-
tention of studying structure formation in cosmology, which requires very a large dynamical range, for
which the AMR approach is well suited [59]. Many of the capabilities of the ramses code are not utilized
in this project and will not be discussed further here.
The simulations in this project were run using RAMSES version 3.10. The patch to the ramses code that
was used for this specific project is available for download from https://bitbucket.org/ankolborg/
ramses/src/master/. This patch is a modification of an existing code-patch by D. Martizzi to the orig-
inal code developed by R. Teyssier at the University of ZГјrich.

4.4.2
Simulation domain and cell size
In addition to an appropriate time step also the size of the grid must be well chosen to ensure a usable result
of the calculations. ramses includes a method to implement AMR in which cells are adaptively refined into
smaller cells using user-defined criteria. This approach allows simulations to achieve higher resolution in
regions where it is required to appropriately solve the problem. AMR was not employed in the simulations
run for this project as an important aspect of the mixing problems investigated herein is the turbulence
which develops in both the disc and the winds of the galaxy. An AMR grid would preferentially increase
the resolution in the disc of the galaxy, but not the winds, because of the greater density in the disc. This
is not desirable when one is interested in the turbulence in both regions.
Instead a static grid of fixed cell size is employed over the entire simulation domain and throughout the
entire evolution time. The chosen grid is a Cartesian cubic grid of 2563 cells. The simulation domain is a
cubic box with side length L = 1000 pc, resulting in each cell having a size вҲҶx = вҲҶy = вҲҶz = 3.9 pc.
In their 2016 paper Martizzi et al [36] carried out convergence studies a very similar ramses code patch
including the same galaxy set-up and the same SN model (see section 2.3), but not including metals (see sec-
tion 4.6). They found that this cell size was appropriate to ensure convergence of the disc vertical structure,
turbulent velocity dispersion, galactic wind speed and the thermal structure of the atmosphere. Because
the metals injected into the gas flow in this projectвҖҷs simulations mainly advect along with the fluid and

Total evolution time
The simulations are allowed to evolve for T вҲј120 Myr (except for one which ends at T вҲј75 Myr).
Only that part of the simulation which can be characterised as after the on-set of steady state and before a

4.5
Boundary and initial conditions
In the previous sections the Euler equations have been derived and their sources terms introduced, fur-
thermore the basic scheme for solving the set of equations numerically has been presented. The last thing
missing in order to obtain a solvable set of equations are the boundary and initial conditions describing
how the fluid is distributed at t = 0, as well as, how it is to behave at the edges of the domain. These
conditions as they apply to this project are described here.
Initial conditions
At the initial time all the gas is assumed to be in hydrostatic equilibrium with the gravitational potential
at a temperature of T = 1.32 Г— 104 K. In addition to this the density of the gas in the mid-plane of the
galaxy is defined, initially this is set to ПҒ0 = 3.469 Г— 10вҲ’23 g/cm3.
Boundary conditions
The simulation domain is sketched in figure 4.5, the mid-plane of the disc is indicated by the light gray
plane in the middle of the box. Using the numbering convention given by this figure boundaries 1 and 2
are equipped with out-flow boundary conditions, meaning that gas is allowed to leave the domain through
these surfaces but material cannot flow into the domain through these surfaces. Boundaries 3-6 have
periodic conditions, meaning that the material which leaves the domain through boundary 4 returns to
the domain through boundary 6 and vice versa (similarly for boundaries 3 and 5). This effectively sets up
a model of an infinite disc in the (x, y) plane.

Physical interpretation of boundary conditions
The chosen boundary conditions imitate an infinite, non-rotating galactic disc. This is of course rather un-
physical when comparing to real galaxies. However, considering that the horizontal extent of the domain
is quite small (L = 1000 pc) compared to the typical diameter of a disc galaxy (D вҲј45 вҲ’60 kpc for the
present day MW) a patch of disc of this size in the middle of the galactic disc may see the disc as having
infinite extent.
Real galaxies also rotate about their gravitational center, this rotation sets up a shearing motion in the
fluid of the disc which causes mixing of the gas. This mixing mechanism is ignored here. The time scale
considered in these simulations is fairly short compared to the rotation period of a galaxy, which is the time
scale on which shear motion mixing is effective. A simple estimate for the rotation period of the galaxy can
be obtained by assuming a test particle is in circular orbit around the center of the galaxy at some radius,
r, in the plane of the disc. For such a particle the orbital period, Torb, is given by:

4.6
Additional scalar fields
The fluid equations developed in section 4.1 can be supplemented by a number of scalar fields allowing one
to model a number of different aspects of the fluid. For each scalar field included an additional equation
of evolution is added to the set. For a simple scalar field this equation is:
вҲӮ
вҲӮt (ПҒx) + вҲҮВ· (ПҒxu) = C вҲ’D
(4.22)
Here x refers to the scalar quantity of interest, C is a creation term while D is a destruction term.
In this project three such scalar fields have been added to the code to trace each of three groups of elements:
Оұ, Fe peak and r-process.
No destruction terms are included, once the elements are introduced into the simulation volume they only
leave through advection out of the domain. It should be stressed that significant amounts of metals may be
lost in this manner, due to the vertical boundary conditions. The creation term corresponds to the input
of fresh elements by each astrophysical process. The details of this process are expanded upon in section
4.7.4.
4.7
Supernova feedback
Throughout the simulation a number of SNe and NSM explode in the simulation domain, each explosion
injects mass, thermal energy, momentum and metals. In this way the explosions alter both the chemical
composition of the gas in the box, as well as, the dynamical development of the galactic disc. The following
sections describe the modelling choices made for the injection of these explosions into the simulation
volume. Many of these choices were made prior to this projectвҖҷs initiation and therefore lie outside its
scope, but, because of their strong influence on the results of the simulations they are discussed here.

4.7.1
Explosion sites
The explosion sites of both the SNe and NSM are determined by a random number generator in which
every point in the (x, y) plane has equal probability to host an explosion. In the vertical direction the
random number population is subject to a step function probability, such that below the scale height of the
explosion type each point has equal probability to host an explosion, while above the scale height there is
zero probability of an explosion. For the SNe the scale height is set to HSNe = 150 pc, while for NSM it is
slightly higher HNSM = 1.33HSNe = 200 pc, to model the ability of these objects to wander further from
their birthplaces.
The coordinates of the explosions are determined before any of the simulations are run and are saved in a
seperate file which is re-used for all the simulations. In this manner explosion number i will have the same
(x, y) coordinate in all the simulations, if this explosion is of the same type in each simulation it will also
have the same z coordinate. If the explosion is an NSM in one simulation the z coordinate is adjusted to
reflect the higher scale height of this type of explosion.
4.7.2
Injection model
As was discussed in section 2.4.2 NSM have similar remnant evolutions as SN and are resonably well de-
scribed by the same models. This is utilized here.
Once the code has determined that it is time for an explosion to happen and has found the coordinates of
the next explosion it will evaluate the conditions in which this explosion will go off. Depending on these
conditions the remnant of the explosion will be better or worse resolved. Explosions which happen in
lower density media will be larger by the time they evolve to the mature stages of evolution (discussed in
section 2.3.2) and thus be better resolved. In cases where the Sedov-Taylor phase of the remnant will be well
resolved (defined as when Rcool > 3Rinj [35]) the explosion is injected as an addition of Mej mass with the
appropriate yields of metals and 7.1 Г— 1050 erg of thermal energy and 9.6 Г— 1042 g cm sвҲ’1 of momentum
(equivalent to 3.4 Г— 1051 erg of kinetic energy) into a spherical region of radius Rinj = 8 pc вүҲ2вҲҶx. When
the remnant is well-resolved the code is able to follow the evolution and convert the appropriate amount of
thermal energy into momentum transferred to the ISM. When, on the other hand, the explosion happens
in a denser medium the Sedov-Taylor phase of the remnant evolution is not well resolved and addition
of this division of energy and momentum would result in too much thermal energy being radiated away,
with too little momentum transfer for the ISM.
This could be solved by increasing the resolution of the code, but since that involves decreasing the grid
size in all three directions this would increase the computation time by a factor of N 3 where N is the factor
by which the resolution is increased. As an alternative to this a sub-grid injection model can be applied,
this is a model which provides a fit to the evolution of the explosion and gives a prediction of what fraction
of energy and momentum should be injected based on the medium in which the explosion happens.

4.8
Simulation suite
A total of four different simulation set-ups have been designed to investigate the likely astrophysical site of
r-process production. Each simulation emulates a different model of nucleosynthesis:
Model 1, assumes CC-SNe are responsible for both Оұ, Fe peak and r-process enrichment and that r-process
production happens equally in all CC-SN.
Model 2, explores the вҖқexotic-CC-SNeвҖқ model in which a subset of CC-SNe produce r-process elements
and the rest do not.
Model 3, is the fiducial model of this project. It assumes r-process enrichment only happens in NSM, which
produce no Оұ or Fe peak elements.
Model 4, is a version of model 3 including a time delay between the enrichment from CC-SNe and NSM.
For all simulations the rate of CC-SNe will be ЛҷnSNe = 300 /kpc3/Myr and the yield of Оұ and Fe peak
elements are: yОұ,SNe = 0.17 and yFe,pk,SNe = 0.01. The total evolution time, T вҲј120 Myr (except for
simulation 4 which ends after T вҲј75 Myr) and the time between snapshots is вҲҶt = 5 kyr. The box size
is 1 kpc3 and the gravitational potential is unchanged, there is no self-gravity of the gas and the cooling
scheme always follows the same prescription.
In simulations 1-3 the initial background metallicity for all three metal fields is set to ZОұ = ZFe,pk = Zrp =
10вҲ’20ZвҠҷ. In simulation 4 the Оұ and Fe peak metallicities are initial set at higher values to emulate that
enrichment of these elements have been on-going for a while before NSM become important.
The parameters of the four set-up are:
1. Model 1: only CC-SNe each yielding 10вҲ’5MвҠҷof r-process material per event. All explosion have
the same scale height, total ejecta mass and rate.
2. Model 2: 1 in a 1000 CC-SNe is an exotic type providing 10вҲ’2MвҠҷr-process material (in addition to
iron and Оұ elements), the rest do not provide r-process material at all. All explosions have the same
scale height and total ejecta mass.
3. Model 3: 1 in a 1000 CC-SNe is a NSM. The NSM have a lower ejecta mass (0.01MвҠҷ) and provide
only r-process material, while the CC-SNe have the usual ejecta mass (6.8MвҠҷ) and yield Оұ and iron
peak elements according to Kobayashi, but no r-process material. The scale height of the NSM is
1.33 times that of the CC-SNe.
4. Model 4: is the same as simulation 3 but with an initial metallicity ZОұ = ZFe,pk = 1 Г— 10вҲ’2 and
Zrp = 1 Г— 10вҲ’20 to mimick a time delay

CHAPTER5
Results
As stated in chapter 1 the goal of this project is to study the spread of Оұ and r-process elements in the ISM
assuming CC-SNe vis a vis NSM are the primary astrophysical site of r-process element production. It is
the aim to compare the simulation results with observational abundance ratios.
Four different simulation set-ups have been designed (introduced in chapter 4): model 1, assumes CC-
SNe are responsible for both Оұ and r-process enrichment and that r-process production happens equally
in all CC-SN. Model 2 explores the вҖқexotic CC-SNeвҖқ model in which a subset of CC-SNe produce r-process
elements and the rest do not. Model 3 assumes r-process enrichment only happens in NSM, which produce
no Оұ or Fe peak elements. Finally, model 4 is a version of model 3 including a time delay between the
enrichment from CC-SNe and NSM.
This chapter presents the main results from these simulations. Only key findings are presented in this
chapter, when all four simulations yield similar results only the result from the fiducial simulation (3) are
given here. A full set of corollary results can be found in appendix A.

connection to the real galaxies and is therefore discarded. The end of the transient phase is identified as
the time at which the velocity dispersion of the gas no longer changes drastically over small time intervals.

5.4
ISM Phase Development
Phase diagrams of (nH, T) show the distribution of cells in the simulation volume which have a specified
combination of density and temperature. These diagrams are useful to discern structure develops in the
gas. The purpose here is to investigate whether there is a connection between gas phase (combination of
density and temperature) and the metallicity of that gas.
Figure 5.6 (see page 50) shows the density-temperature phase space of all the gas in the simulation domain
at the end of the evolution time. the cells are weighted by their metallicity. Furthermore, two different ver-
sions of the diagrams are calculated, one in which the cells are also weighted by their mass and normalized
to the total mass in the box and one in which they are weigthed by the their volume and normalized to the
total volume. Thus, the вҖқalgorithmвҖқ for creating the phase diagrams is:
1. Define an interval of density and temperature of interest and define the resolution in this interval
2. Loop over all the cells and determine which bin the cell belongs to
3. When a cell belongs to a bin add to that bin the value of Zj,iVвҖ“ i or Zj,iMi for volume and mass
weighting, respectively. j refers to the metallicity group Оұ, Fe peak or r-process, i refers to the cell
number.
4. Normalize each distribution by the total volume or total mass as appropriate
Figure 5.6 shows the mass weighted diagrams on the left and volume weigthed on the right. Only the fidu-
cial simulation is included here as these figures are qualitatively very similar for the four simulations.
The mass weighted phase diagram tends to emphasize the denser gas in the disc, while the volume weighted
puts more emphasis on the diffuse, hot gas surrounding the disc. Comparing the two side-by-side can give
insights into the distribution of metals in the two phases.
Each of the diagrams have a similar overall shape with a diagonal running from low density and high
temperature (upper-left-hand-corner) towards the middle of the space where it intersects with a nearly
horizontal branch of narrow temperature differences over a range of densities. This lower branch corre-
sponds to the dense, cold gas found primarily in the disc, while the upper branch corresponds to the diffuse,
hotter gas surrounding it. This is corroborated by the two regions being more clearly emphasized in mass
and volume weighted distributions, respectively.
The Fe peak and Оұ element groups show relatively small peaks in each phase of the gas, whereas, the r-
process metallicity appears to be more evenly distributed among gas of different phase - i.e. shows less
correlation with gas phase than the other two groups do.

CHAPTER6
Discussion
The intention of this project has been to use numerical simulations of regions of a galaxy to investigate
the small-scale mixing effects on the distribution of r-process elements (traced by Eu) in the ISM. Four
models of enrichment have been tested: model 1 assumes all enrichment is produced in CC-SNe, model 2
investigates the possibility that most CC-SNe do not produce elements beyond the iron peak, but a subset
are an exotic type and do synthesize r-process elements, model 3 is the fiducial case in this project and
assumes that r-process elements are produced only in NSM which do not produce lighter elements, these
events are furthermore assumed to have a higher scale height than the exotic SNe counterpart in model
2. Finally, model 4 is a version of model 3 in which the initial metallicity of Оұ and Fe peak elements is
raised to model a delay time of the NSM relative to the CC-SNe. The results of all 4 models were presented
in chapter 5 and will be discussed here, starting with model 4 which showed signs of issues in the model
set-up, followed by the simplest model, namely 1. Finally, models 2 and 3 are discussed together towards
the end of this chapter.
6.1

The formation channels of CC-SNe and NSM (see chapter 2) suggest that there should be a substantial delay
time between the onset of CC-SNe and the time of the first NSM, model 4 of this project was intended to
investigate the effects of this delay. For this model the initial metallicity of the Оұ and Fe peak elements was
set to ZОұ = ZFe,pk = 10вҲ’2. Scaling this initial Fe metallicity to the solar pattern yields:

and galactic fountain effects could be expected to become important.

6.2
Model 1: Both Mg and Eu are produced in ordinary CC-SNe
Over the nearly six decades discussions have prevailed about the astrophysical site(s) of the r-process. Early
work (notably [8]) expected CC-SNe to be the site of frequent but small scale production. Although this
picture has been challenged by models of rare SNe and NSM, which are more easily compliant with the
observations, model 1 from this projectвҖҷs simulation suite is intended to investigate the ability of ordinary
CC-SNe to produce r-process element distributions consistent with the observed abundances of low metal-
licity stars.
The model assumed that each CC-SNe yields Оұ, Fe peak and r-process elements at fixed mass ratios. Fig-
ure 5.12 in chapter 5 showed that this model is able to reproduce the observed spread in [Mg/H] reliably-
The [Eu/Fe] distribution produced by the simulation of this model, on the other hand, resembles a delta
function and is much more narrow than the observed spread in the halo star abundances.
A very narrow distribution suggests a well-mixed ISM. Such a chemically homogenous gas cannot be ex-
pected to form chemically distinct stars and as all the gas in the entire simulation volume is accounted
for in the distribution of the model there is no gas (either more pristine or more enriched) for this gas
to mix with and form the halo stars with higher or lower enrichments. Following this logic the results of
the simulation of model 1 rules out the scenario that both Fe peak and r-process elements be produced in
ordinary CC-SNe.
6.3
Models 2 and 3: Can we distinguish between exotic CC-SNe and NSM?
Several authors have argued for either exotic types of CC-SNe or NSM as the primary source of r-process
nucleosynthesis. Arguments for the exotic SNe model include this processвҖҷ short formation time, which is
necessary to explain the abundance of r-process elements in low metalicity stars, while arguments for the
NSM model include the robustness of the r-process expected in these events and the observation of the
kilonova AT170817A which confirmed that these events are indeed able to synthesize these metals in the
expected masses.
This project attempts to use mixing effects of the elements in the ISM to further the understanding of
which of these astrophysical sites could be the birth place of the r-process elements. Model 2 borrows
the assumptions of the exotic SNe explanation and combines it with the, observationally constrained, rate
of r-process enrichment to set up a scenario in which the majority of CC-SNe yield only Оұ and Fe peak
elements and a small subset (1 in a 1000) yields these element groups, as well as, r-process elements. Model
3 on the other hand assumes that r-process production only happens in NSM which are modelled to not
yield any lighter elements at all.
In both models the total mass of Eu (and r-process elements more generally) per event is the same (MEu вүҲ
1 Г— 10вҲ’5 MвҠҷ/event) and the rate of r-process yielding events relative to ordinary CC-SNe is the same
(frp,event = 10вҲ’3). The primary differences between the models are: the total ejecta mass per r-process
yielding event are different (Mej,SNe = 6.8 MвҠҷand Mej,NSM = 0.01 MвҠҷ) and the scale heights of the two
events are different by 33 % (HSNe = 150 pc and HNSM = 200 pc).
Figure 5.12 in chapter 5 showed very similar distributions of both [Mg/H] and [Eu/Fe] from these two
models. Both simulations resulted in [Mg/H] distributions exactly like the distribution seen in model 1 (see
previous section), while both distributions of [Eu/Fe] were broader than the distributions of the observed
halo stars. Various factors could be at play here causing the differences between modelled distributions
and the observed ones, these will be discussed in turn in the following sections.
6.3.1
Missing mixing effects
Firstly, it is important to think about the missing physics in these models. As was discussed in chapter 1
the mixing effects in real galaxies are complicated and include effects of both the galactic fountain mixing
==PAGE:69==
6.3 Models 2 and 3: Can we distinguish between exotic CC-SNe and NSM?
59
enriched gas across large distances in the galaxy, as well as, mixing due to turbulence induced by the shear
forces in the disc from the rotation of the galaxy. Neither of these effects are modelled in this project and
they could both work to increase the mixing of the gas, thus making it more chemically homogeneous
leading to a more narrow distribution of the abundance ratios.
On the other hand the distributions of [Mg/H] obtained from the simulations of the simple models here
show excellent agreement with the observations, thereby suggesting that these simulation boxes, in spite
of their limitations are able to capture the relevant physics and produce reliable predictions of element
distributions.
6.3.2
Commonality of uncommon events
Also the rates of the exotic events have impact on the width of the final distribution. In chapter 5 it was
seen clearly from the time evolution of both [Mg/H] and [Fe/H] (e.g. figure 5.10) that the distributions of
these two elements grow more narrow as more and more events add these elements to the ISM gas. This
suggests that higher rates of an event leads to a more narrow distribution of the elements produced by that
event. Applying this to models 2 and 3 it could be expected that the distributions of [Eu/Fe] would grow
more narrow approaching the observational distribution if the events were more common.
The relative rates (and hence the overall rates) of both the exotic SNe and the NSM were fixed based on the
observationally constrained total r-process yield per year in a galaxy (1 Г— 10вҲ’7 MвҠҷ/yr) and the expected
ejecta masses of NSM (1 Г— 10вҲ’3 MвҠҷ-1 Г— 10вҲ’2 MвҠҷ) coroborated by the AT170817A observation. Increas-
ing the rates of these events would imply lowering the mass per event to balance the total mass per year
and could lead to conflict with the models of ejecta mass from these events.
6.3.3
Comparing gas and stars
A final factor that is important to keep in mind when interpreting the difference between the distributions
from the simulations and those of the observation is the fact that the observations give information about
stars while the simulations give information about gas in a galaxy. The two are comparable under the
understanding that, at least some of, the gas in the galaxy will eventually form stars and that those stars,
when they form inherit the abundance ratios of the gas from which they were born. As was discussed in
section 2.2.2 the gas must first cool and become sufficiently dense to form stars, considering the distribution
(see figure 5.3) and phase (figure 5.6) of the gas in the simulation large quantities of this gas is not at found
at temperatures or densities where it can be expected to be star-forming. On its way to becoming a nebula
the gas is likely to mix with pristine gas from other regions of the galaxy and thus become diluted in
element abundance, this could help to explain the larger spread, on the high end side of the mean, of the
gas compared to the stars.
On the low end side of the distribution two important factors concerning the observations should be kept
in mind. The first is that, due to observational bias towards more highly enriched stars the observations
could be missing a low end tail. This is due to the fact that it is more difficult to detect smaller amounts of
metals and therefore observations of very metal poor stars can result in upper limits on the enrichment in
stead of a qualitative measurement. Since upper limits are excluded from the data set for this thesis there
could be information missing from the observational distribution, which if included could move the two
distributions closer to agreement with one another.
Furthermore, the galaxy patch simulated in this project models the disc part of a galaxy and the extremely
metal poor stars are found primarily in the halo of the present day MW. This is not necessarily a problem for
the comparison if one assumes that the galaxy is relatively, chemically homogenous on large scales, however,
in their observations Reddy et al find that there is a significant difference between the [Eu/Fe] abundances
observed in thin disc stars compared to thick disc and halo stars [46]. Specifically, these authors find that
the metal poor stars in the thin disc of the galaxy exhibit lower [Eu/Fe] abundances than their counterparts

in other regions of the galaxy. Although the stars in these authorsвҖҷ sample were not as metal poor as the
ones considered here (вҲ’1.2 < [Fe/H] < вҲ’0.3 in the Reddy et al sample) their observations could point
to an interesting distinction between the abundances of stars formed in different regions of a galaxy and
could be an interesting hypothesis to investigate in larger scale models.
Ultimately, the distributions from the simulation results are best interpreted as an upper limit on the stellar
abundance spread which can be produced from gas enriched by the modelled event.
6.3.4
Degeneracy in production site
The previous sections have discussed the level of agreement between the simulated distributions and the
observed ones, both models 2 and 3 have been considered jointly in these sections due to the very simple
reason that it is nearly impossible to tell these two distributions apart.
It is interesting to note that the difference in 33 % of scale height between the SNe and NSM enrichment
events apparently does not translate to a difference in distribution of the metals in the gas. The argument
could be made that the scale height of the NSM is simply not great enough here to make this difference
apparent. Safarzadeh et al investigated the influence of strong natal kicks on the distance travelled by a
DNS pair prior to merger and found that the majority of the pairs which are able to merge do so with the
central 100 pc of their host galaxy [52] - this should be compared with the scale height modelled for NSM
in this project of 200 pc.
The statistics of the distributions, summarized in table 5.13, made apparent some differences between the
visually similar distributions from the two models. The ratio of the variance to the mean for the observa-
tional set was found to be 0.72 while the same measure for model 2 was 1.01 and 0.86 for model 3. This
points in the direction that the NSM model for r-process enrichment is better able to explain the observed
spread in [Eu/Fe] of low metallicity stars than is the model of exotic SNe.
CC-SNe are an attractive site for r-process nucleosynthesis because their formation channels easily allow
them to enrich the ISM already at very early stages of galaxy evolution. On the other hand this pictures
is challenged by the many simulations of r-process nucleosynthesis in SNe requiring very specific initial
conditions in order to produce r-process elements in solar abundances. This is not a problem when NSM
are assumed to be the site of synthesis of the heaviest elements as models of these events show them to
be able to produce the expected ratios of elements over a wide range of initial conditions. Furthermore,
the data from the only NSM observed to date shows excellent agreement with the expectations from these
models, underlining that NSM are certainly able to produce r-process elements in the expected ratios. The
major difficulty to be overcome for the theory that NSM are the primary site of r-process enrichment are
the long delay times expected due to their formation channels. Several population studies and dynamical
simulations are being carried out in the search for realistic, fast formation channels of NSM to reconsiel
the delay time of these events with the observations indicating early enrichment.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
As stated in chapter 1 the goal of this thesis was to study the small-scale mixing effects in galaxis in the early
Universe. Particularly mixing of r-process elements in the ISM and its impact of the spread of r-process
elements has been the focus. The primary goals set up for this project were:
I To model the mixing of Eu (as a of tracer r-process elements) assuming CC-SNe vis a vis NSM as the
primary astrophysical site of r-process production
II To compare the predictions of abundance spread in the ISM with abundance data of metal poor halo
stars, in the hope to contribute to the body of work trying to constrain the likely source of r-process
nucleosynthesis.
The observational set of halo stars was presented in chapter 3, where it was also argued that the significantly
larger spread of Eu (as a tracer of r-process elements) compared to that of Mg (a tracer of Оұ elements)
suggests that these two (groups) of elements are not produced in the same astrophysical site.
Chapter 2 reviewed the theories on stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis processes. Based on these, four
different models for the r-process production was set up:
1. Enrichment of all element groups is due to CC-SNe
2. Enrichment of r-process elements is due to an exotic type of CC-SNe, which has a lower rate, but is
otherwise similar to the ordinary CC-SNe
3. R-process elements are synthesized in NSM
4. The same basic model as 3, but trying to model a delay time of the NSM relative to the CC-SNe
Numerical simulations of a region of a galaxy were carried out to investigate the spread of Mg and Eu in
each of the four models.
In chapter 5 the predicted distributions of these elements were presented and compared to the distribu-
tions of the metal poor halo stars. These results were discussed in chapter 6, the main conclusions of this
discussion were:
вҖў No support is found for the model that CC-SNe can produce both Оұ and r-process elements
вҖў Models 1-3 are able to reproduce the observed spread in Mg, indicating that these simulations are
able to accurately capture the important physics influencing the mixing
вҖў Model 4 does not accurate capture the spread of Mg at low metallicities due to too high initial metal-
licity in this model
вҖў Both models 2 (exotic CC-SNe) and 3 (NSM) are consistent with the observed abundance spread.
Preliminary statistics points to NSM as the more likely model
Further work could be done on:
==PAGE:72==
7 Conclusion
62
вҖў Making the abundance distributions of the simulations more comparable with the observations. This
could be done, for example, by evaluating the statistics not in each cell of the simulation but over a
group of cells containing the amount of mass over which the elements are expected to mix before
they form stars.
вҖў Studying the effects of delay times in the early Universe, i.e. at low metallicities. This could be done
e.g. by self-consistently evolving the simulation to appropriate metallicities before NSM are turned
on.
Other enticing avenues of enquiry include: migrating models 2 and 3 to global galaxy simulations retaining
similar resolutions in order to study the joint mixing effects of SNe feedback, galaxy rotation and galactic
winds on the distribution of elements in the ISM.
