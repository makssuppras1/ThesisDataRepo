Abstract 
In modern organic chemistry the synthesis of complex molecules in few steps is of high interest. One 
of the more promising methods for achieving this goal is transannular reactions in which fused ring 
systems are created from a large macrocycles. This project concerns a classic reaction, the Mannich 
reaction, which has recently been applied in a transannular setting. Although studies have proven this 
reaction successful, the obtained product is not always the only item of interest. Speculations have 
risen questioning if this reaction is truly a Mannich reaction, since another reaction mechanism which 
would yield the same product is possible. The objective of this project is to elucidate the reaction 
mechanism of this transannular reaction, which is attempted accomplished through both experimental 
synthetic work and computational chemistry using Density Functional Theory. The computational 
calculations were hoped to distinguish these two reaction mechanism through modelling of diastere-
oselectivity, but this proved both mechanism to yield the same diastereomer. However, energy studies 
have shown the Mannich reaction being of overall lower energy than the alternatively suggested 
mechanism. Concerning the synthetic work, this was unfortunately stalled quite a bit due to problems 
with a Suzuki reaction. Alternative synthetic pathways are currently being attempted and are showing 
promise due to only one or two synthetic steps required to obtain the target molecule. 
Resumé 
I moderne organisk kemi er det vigtigt at udvikle metoder til at syntetisere komplekse molekyler i få 
skridt. En af metoderne til dette er transannulære reaktioner som lukker sammensatte ringsystemer 
fra makrocykler. Dette projekt omhandler en klassisk reaktion, Mannich reaktionen, som for nyligt 
har været afprøvet i transannulær sammenhæng. Selvom denne reaktion har vist sig at være succes-
fuld, er det ikke altid kun produktet som er af interesse. Der er kommet overvejelser om denne reak-
tion egentlig er en Mannich reaktion, da en anden mekanisme kan give det samme produkt. Formålet 
med dette projekt er at præcisere hvilken reaktionsmekanisme der finder sted, hvilket bliver gjort med 
en sammensætning af organisk syntesekemi og computerberegning via Density Functional Theory. 
Det var håbet at computerberegningerne kunne skelne de to reaktionsmekanismer på baggrund af 
deres diastereoselektivitet, men dette var ikke muligt da mekanismer ville give den samme diastere-
omer. Det viste sig dog at Mannich reaktionen havde lavere energiniveauer end den alternative me-
kanisme. Syntesearbejdet blev desværre hindret en del på grund af en række Suzuki reaktioner som 
ikke var mulige. Til gengæld er arbejdet på to nye synteseveje blevet påbegyndt og de ser lovende 
ud, da der mangler et til to syntesetrin for at opnå det krævede molekyle.

1 Introduction 
In modern chemistry one important focus is the development of methods for synthesizing complex 
molecules consisting of two or more fused rings. One of the main methods for this has been the classic 
intramolecular reaction in which two functional groups are fused into one under the closing of a ring 
as demonstrated in Scheme 1. A vast amount of these reactions exists, varying from simple substitu-
tion reactions to more advanced reactions like the alkene metathesis (Scheme 1). However, another 
method is also of great interest; transannular reactions. In these reactions a fused ring system is closed 
from a large ring, a macrocycle, under a powerful intramolecular reaction. These reactions are of 
special interest since many of them can occur under mild conditions. Some examples of these are the 
Diels-Alder reaction (Scheme 1) and the aldol condensation  - these are some of the most used reac-
tions for transannular application.1  
 
Scheme 1: Examples of intramolecular and transannular reactions 
Though the transannular reactions are effective for the formation of fused ring systems, one of the 
larger challenges is the synthesis of the macrocycle. For this task one of two methods is usually ap-
plied; either an intramolecular reaction as described above or (as exemplified in this project) a ring 
enlargement of a fused ring system. 
In recent years, theory on a new type of transannular reaction has been suggested: the transannular 
Mannich reaction which is the focus of this project.2 
1.1 The Mannich Reaction 
The Mannich reaction is a well-known reaction using three functional groups: two carbonyls and a 
secondary amine. During this reaction an iminium ion is firstly formed, initiated from a nucleophilic 
attack from the amine to one of the carbonyls (Scheme 2).

The formation of the iminium ion is followed by a reaction with the other carbonyl in its enolized 
form (Scheme 3).

With two carbonyls involved the regioselectivity of the Mannich reaction can be an issue. This issue 
is often countered by using formaldehyde for the formation if the iminium ion since this is the most 
reactive carbonyl and it cannot enolize. The formation of an iminium ion from formaldehyde and 
dimethylamine is very often used and can be purchased as a salt known as Eschenmoser’s Salt. The

The Mannich reaction with formaldehyde is a powerful tool for synthesis of amine-ketones and is 
advantageous compared to the classic aldol reaction, since an aldol reaction with formaldehyde is far 
too reactive and can cause multiple reactions where the Mannich reaction only adds a single amine 
group.3

1.2 The Transannular Mannich Reaction 
It has recently been considered if the Mannich reaction can be used in a transan-
nular setting in order to synthesize fused ring systems from macrocycles.2 Studies 
have already been done in which a 13-membered ring was attempted closed to a 
tricyclic compound like Cylindricine D seen in Figure 1. The strategy was to syn-
thesize a 13-membered ring containing the three required functional groups needed 
for the Mannich reaction and let it undergo suitable reaction conditions. This was accomplished in a 
previous study4 in which the macrocycle was synthesized through a ring enlargement of a fused ring 
system by ozonolysis as seen in Scheme 5. 
 
Scheme 5: Ring enlargement by ozonolysis 
The transannular reaction has been tested in house according to the reaction conditions seen in 
Scheme 62. 
 
Scheme 6: The test transannular reaction 
As seen, this reaction not only gives a high yield (70 %) but is also fully diastereoselective towards 
the cis diastereomer which was proven by Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. This 
method appears as a very promising way of synthesizing complex molecules like cylindricines. 
1.3 Questioning the transannular Mannich reaction 
The transannular Mannich reaction shown in Scheme 6 seems to be a very convenient reaction with 
its ability to form fused ring systems diastereoselectively in high yield. However, it can be questioned 
whether this is actually a Mannich reaction. Due to the structure of the macrocycle, an alternative 
reaction pathway, which yields the exact same product, is possible. This alternate mechanism is 
shown in Scheme 7.

This mechanism happens if the initial formation of the iminium ion does not occur, and is replaced 
by a transannular aldol reaction. The compound from this aldol reaction can condensate to a conju-

The purpose of this project is to distinguish between these two reaction mechanisms and to provide

2.1.1 Computational Chemistry 
As stated the energy of a system can be calculated through the Schrödinger equation. This is highly 
advantageous in chemistry since systems can be simulated and it is then possible to estimate many 
different attributes of the system in question. Computational chemistry has many different uses, from 
the simulation of the folding of macromolecules, like proteins, to the simulation of the reaction paths 
of organic reactions. However, due to the complexity of the Schrödinger equation and the inability to 
solve it, several different methods have been constructed in order to approximate the energy of chem-
ical systems. In this project, only Molecular Mechanics (MM) and Density Functional Theory (DFT) 
has been used, and only these methods will be discussed. However, since DFT is known as a post 
Hartree-Fock (HF) method, HF will also briefly be described. 
For this project, DFT has been used practically to elucidate organic reaction mechanisms whereas 
less attention has been paid to the mathematical background of these calculations. Hence, DFT will 
be described in general terms without in depth elaboration on the underlying mathematics. 
2.1.1.1 Molecular Mechanics 
MM is a branch of computational chemistry wherein quantum chemistry is completely disregarded 
and fully replaced with Newtonian mechanics. This is done by applying a chemical Force Field, a set 
of equations describing the interaction of the different atoms. Simplified, the atoms are connected by 
springs, which makes it possible to complete calculations in minimal time compared to the quantum 
mechanical calculations. This method is often used for the simulation of macromolecules, like pro-
teins, which makes it possible to simulate folding, docking etc.5

2.1.1.2.1 Basis Sets 
A basis set is a set of mathematical functions used to construct a wave function or an orbital function 
for chemical systems (assuming the atom is in the definition range of the basis set) and the basis set 
is generally described in a term similar to: X-YZG(*/**)(+/++). This implies that the core orbitals 
are composed of a linear combination of X primitive Gaussian functions, and the valence electron 
orbitals are composed of two functions each: a linear combination of Y primitive Gaussian functions 
and primitive Gaussian functions.  
The * represent the addition of Polarization Functions, which are added basis functions increasing 
the mathematical flexibility of the wave functions. Here, two asterisks (**) means that these function 
are applied to all atoms and one asterisk (*) means that hydrogen and helium atoms are excluded.  
The + sign represents Diffuse Functions, which are functions that can be added in order to accommo-
date highly excited atoms and molecular orbitals of high energies. Again two plusses (++) means all 
atoms are affected and one plus (+) refers to the situation when H and He are excluded.5 
The basis set used for all calculations in this project is the 6-31G** basis set. 
2.1.1.3 Density Functional Theory 
One of the most challenging parts of computational chemistry has been that the larger the chemical 
system, the larger the calculation. The major problem here is that the computational time required for 
these calculation rises exponentially with the increasing number of electrons in the system, hitting 
what is referred to as “The Exponential Wall”. One of the methods to overcome this problem is by 
using Density Functional Theory (DFT). The Thomas-Ferme theory, the most fundamental DFT, was 
surprisingly developed shortly after the publication of the theory on the Schrödinger equation in 1926, 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
however it received very little notice until 1964-65 when Walter Kohn, Pierre Hohenberg and Lu J. 
Sham published the Hohenberg-Kohn variational principle and the Kohn-Sham self-consistent single-
particle equations. This led to Walter Kohn receiving the Nobel Prize in chemistry along with John 
A. Pople in 1998.7 
The principles of DFT involve not viewing the chemical system as a wave function but rather con-
structing a density matrix, ρ(r), a property that can be visualized in three dimensions compared to the 
more abstract wave function. The energy is hereafter calculated. The principle of this type of calcu-
lation is rather complex, but can be described as first dividing the energy into five terms seen in 
Equation 4. 
𝐸(𝜌(𝑟)) = 𝑇𝑛𝑖(𝜌(𝑟)) + 𝑉𝑛𝑒(𝜌(𝑟)) + 𝑉𝑒𝑒(𝜌(𝑟)) + ∆𝑇(𝜌(𝑟)) + ∆𝑉𝑒𝑒(𝜌(𝑟)) 
Equation 4: The total energy of a chemical system divided into fragments 
The first three terms of the equation are, from left to right, the kinetic energy of noninteracting elec-
trons, the nuclear-electron interaction and the classically calculated electron-electron repulsion. The 
last two fragments are the correction to the kinetic energy originating from electron-electron interac-
tions and the all non-classical corrections to the electron-electron repulsion energy. 
For calculating the electronic energy from Equation 4 the Kohn-Sham methodology is applied. This 
is a method of calculating the electronic energy of a system where the electrons are not interacting, 
i.e. the first three fragments of Equation 4. The two last fragments are joined together and denoted as 
the exchange-correlation energy, 𝐸𝑥𝑐, which is calculated through a hybrid functional. Disregarding 
the exchange-correlation energy, it is possible to adapt the Hamiltonian operator in the Schrödinger 
equation in order to accommodate a non-interacting system, which can then be used to calculate the 
energies of the three first fragments of Equation 4.

2.2 Computational Methods 
For modelling purposes the programs MacroModel* and Jaguar8† have been used for MM and DFT 
calculations respectively. Both programs have been used through the Maestro‡ interface. 
2.2.1 Molecular Mechanics 
MM simulations were performed using the program MacroModel (part of the Schrödinger suite), 
using the OPLS05 force field with a chloroform solvent model (CH2Cl2 not being available as a sol-
vent and chloroform being the closest analogue). For conformational searches, the energy window 
for saving structures were set high (500 kJ/mol) in order to obtain all possible conformers. 
2.2.2 Density Functional Theory 
All DFT calculations were done with the program Jaguar (part of the Schrödinger suite) using the 
B3LYP hybrid functional, the 6-31G** basis set and a PBF CH2Cl2 solvent model. Optimizations 
were run until converging energies were obtained. The SCF accuracy level was set at ultrafine. For 
transition state (TS) searches, the initial guess of the TS was obtained from coordinate scans and the 
criteria of a successful TS search were that the structure had only one imaginary frequency. Coordi-
nate scans were in general run from equilibrium position to a distance of 1.5 Å between the reacting 
atoms (the approximate length of a C-C sigma bond).

‡ Schrödinger Release 2013-3: Maestro, version 9.6, Schrödinger, LLC, New York, NY, 2013.

TSs were further optimized by locking the atoms involved in the TS and performing a conformational 
search using MM and optimizing all obtained structures using DFT (atoms still locked). Then the 
structures were subjected to another TS search (atoms unlocked) yielding all possible conformers of 
the TS. The search with the lowest energy was selected as the final TS. 
Thermodynamic energies for compounds were obtained from a MM conformational search from 
which all conformer were optimized using DFT. The lowest obtained energy yielded the thermody-
namic energy. 
2.3 Theoretical Strategy 
One of the major advantages of using computational chemistry to elucidate reaction mechanisms is 
that it is possible to examine every single step along the two reaction pathways.  
The main focus of this project has firstly been the diastereoselectivity of the final steps of both reac-
tion pathways. This is a crucial factor to determine since a direct correlation to the experiment can be 
made. 
Secondly, the regioselectivity of the Aldol reaction has been thoroughly examined in order to prove 
that this reaction would yield the required product for the mechanism to function. 
Thirdly, the chemoselectivity of the initial step of the reaction (Aldol versus Mannich reaction) will 
be studied and focused on finding a method, which could distinguish between the reactivity of the 
two reaction types. 
Then a full overview of the energies of the reactants, intermediates and products of both reaction 
paths has been examined, to see if one of the pathways was favored thermodynamically. 
Lastly, some of the crucial results have been recalculated using a slightly different functional, 
B3LYP-D3, in order to investigate if correcting for the London dispersion energies would yield sig-
nificantly different results.

2.4 Mechanistic considerations: The Boc-group 
The mechanism shown in Scheme 7 is simplified in a manner that the Boc-group (Boc = butyloxycar-
bonyl, sometimes also written t-Boc) of the macrocycle is not present. The Boc-group protects the 
amine as a tert-butyl carbamate. The Boc-group is easily removed by reaction with fairly strong acid 
as shown in Scheme 8. 
 
Scheme 8: Removal of the Boc-group with strong acid 
Note that the elimination shown in red does not always occur. This is due to the high reactivity of the 
carbocation, which can react in numerous different ways. To avoid this a scavenger is often added to 
the reaction mixture whenever a Boc-group is removed. 
This aspect is important to consider for the modelling work. In the proposed reaction, the molecule 
is only subjected to a Lewis acid, borontriflouride etherate. This Lewis acid is not, by itself, strong 
enough to facilitate the removal of the Boc-group, meaning that the mechanism for the removal has 
to involve the reaction itself. This is considered mainly for modelling purposes where it is assumed 
that the Boc-group is removed during the iminium ion formation in the Mannich reaction and during 
the nucleophilic attack of the Michael reaction in the alternative reaction mechanism.

2.5 Results and discussion 
2.5.1 Determining the Diastereoselectivity of the Michael Reaction 
If the reaction goes through the alternatively suggested mechanism, the diastereoselectivity determin-
ing step will be the Michael reaction. This selectivity occurs when the second chiral center is formed 
during the final keto-enol tautomerism shown in Scheme 9. 
 
Scheme 9: Formation of the second stereocenter during the Michael reaction 
In order to determine the outcome of this reaction, the transition states of the cis and trans proton 
transfers will be constructed. A model for this proton transfer can be done in different ways, and it is 
difficult to make an exact energy prediction of the TS due the many options for this proton transfer. 
However, for the purpose of determining the outcome of the reaction, only the energies of the TSs 
relative to each other are relevant, which makes the simplified models used in the following section 
feasible.

determine the steric outcome of the reaction as stated earlier.

The addition of a water molecule to the proton transfer showed lowering of the transition state, and it 
is expected that adding two or more water molecules and letting the proton move as in the Grotthuss

trans transition states was almost identical for the two models (13.6 kJ/mol versus 12.9 kJ/mol). 
Therefore, it was concluded that no further models for this system was required. Furthermore, it was 
concluded that if the reaction follows the alternatively suggested mechanism, the product would be 
the cis diastereomer (which was also the case in the experiment).

Scheme 21: Synthesis of 4 
In order to obtain 1, 4 had to be coupled to 1,2-dibromocyclohexene, 3a, through two reactions: first 
a hydroboration followed by a Suzuki reaction forming 2 and then a Weinreb acylation as seen in 
Scheme 22, in order to close the 9-membered ring. 
  
Scheme 22: First synthetic approach 
In the first reaction, the Suzuki reaction, the alkene on 4 is subjected to a hydroboration with 9-BBN, 
which can the then through a palladium catalyzed coupling react with a vinylhalide (or other alkenes 
with a suitable leaving groups). An illustration of the catalytic cycle of the Suzuki reaction is shown 
in Scheme 23.  
==PAGE:53==
45 
 
 
Scheme 23: Catalytic cycle of the Suzuki reaction 
The Suzuki reaction uses palladium in oxidation state 0 as catalyst. The mechanism for the reaction 
is initiated through an oxidative addition of the palladium catalyst to the vinylbromide (or similar 
alkene) oxidizing palladium to Pd(II). This is followed by a ligand exchange and then a transmeta-
lation with the borane. The product is then formed through a reductive elimination, restoring the 
palladium to Pd(0).15,16 
After the Suzuki coupling, the idea was to close the 9-membered ring in 1 through a Weinreb acyla-
tion. The mechanism for this reaction is shown in Scheme 24. 
 
Scheme 24: Mechanism of the Weinreb acylation 
The mechanism for this reaction builds on a metal insertion of lithium or magnesium to the vinyl 
bromide. This can then act as a nucleophile, attacking the electrophile carbonyl carbon of the Weinreb 
amide. The formed intermediate does not regain the carbonyl under elimination of the hydroxylamine, 
but forms a 5 membered ring intermediate with the metal ion. This makes the Weinreb acylation a 
superior ring closing mechanism compared to something like a Grignard reaction, due to the rigid 5 
membered lithium intermediate which only collapses with quenching.17,18 This prevents the eventu-
ality of a double Grignard addition, which is possible if using an ester or carboxylic acid, or the 
reduction to a secondary alcohol if an aldehyde was used – sparing the oxidation step which would 
be needed in order to obtain 1. 
==PAGE:54==

Predicting whether this ring closing mechanism is favored or not is difficult beforehand due to the 
closing of 9-membered rings being outside the definitions from Baldwin’s Rules.19

4.1.2 NMR spectroscopy experiments 
Another approach could be recording NMR spectra throughout the reaction. H-NMR would probably 
be the least effective since the reaction mostly involves quaternary carbons. C-NMR might, however, 
be able to detect the alkene peaks of the aldol intermediate.

nitrogen atoms are solely examined. Looking at the reaction, 15N NMR spectroscopy might prove 
effective for distinguishing between the two reaction mechanisms. Since there is only a single nitro-

alternative mechanism the tertiary carbamate reacts directly to the tertiary amine. The chemical shifts

5 Conclusion 
Since the synthesis is not yet complete, a conclusion concerning the mechanism is unavailable from 
this point of view. It can however be concluded that the first synthetic approach by ring closing with 
a Weinreb acylation along with its alternative synthetic path should be discarded, due to the unreac-
tivity of 4 towards hydroboration and the unreactivity of 3a/b towards Suzuki couplings with tert-
butylallylcarbamate. At the moment the second synthetic pathway is showing promise with only the 
final ring closing remaining, however suitable conditions for this reaction as not yet been found. 
Lastly, work on a synthetic pathway using an intramolecular Michael reaction is still being examined 
but with no substantial results yet due to the oxidative rearrangement encountered. 
From the computational calculations it was unfortunately not possible to distinguish the reaction 
mechanism on the background of their diastereoselectivity due to both mechanism theoretically yield-
ing the cis diastereomer. This does however proof correlation between the computational method 
applied and the observations made in the laboratory. 
It was however seen that the Mannich reaction was favored thermodynamically overall when com-
paring to the alternative mechanism, as well as being kinetically favored when examining the initial 
reaction step. This does point towards the transannular Mannich mechanism being the true path for 
the reaction.
