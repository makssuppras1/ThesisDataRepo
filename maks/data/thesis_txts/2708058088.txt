Abstract
This thesis is centred around Gaussian Processes and their application in model-based
Reinforcement Learning.
PILCO is a data-efficient model-based policy search method
that utilizes stationary GPs to reduce model bias. Due to its data efficiency, PILCO is
well suited to control robots. However, robots often possess complicated and discontinuous
dynamics. Therefore a GP can be insufficient to model robots. Input Warped GP is a
method that extends the stationary GP to express discontinuity and non-stationarity. This
thesis combines PILCO and IW-GP, creating the RL algorithm IW-PILCO. IW-PILCO is
compared to PILCO on the mountain car control problem.

Introduction 1
Learning a policy to control any system toward a desired behavior/state by simply
initiating an algorithm.
Developing such an algorithm is the goal of Reinforcement
Learning (RL).
RL is a framework for experience-driven autonomous learning. In this framework, an agent
is constructed to control a given system. Through interaction, the agent learns a policy to
minimize a given cost function. With this framework RL algorithms have defeated Chess
and Starcraft champions, reduced Google data center cooling bills by 40%1, solved Rubik
cubes using a robotic hand2 and more.
RL algorithms are divided in two main categories: Model-Free (MF) and Model-Based
(MB). MF algorithms suffer from data inefficiency [2], making them impractical for
controlling some robots and physical systems.
Therefore this thesis focuses on MB
algorithms, as they obtain better data efficiency by building a model of the environment
dynamics. However, MB methods can suffer from model bias - if the agent is too confident
in the dynamical model, it will make poor decisions. To mitigate model bias, one can model
the system dynamics with a probabilistic model. Several probabilistic dynamical models
have been considered such as; Gaussian Process (GP), Bayesian deep neural networks,
and Probabilistic neural networks. GPs learn faster than neural networks; however, a GP
scales poorly with data due to computational complexity. Neural network models are more
expressive and can model complicated dynamics, including discontinuities. Furthermore,
neural networks scale well with experience, but learning is slow.
In 2010 Deisenroth [5] introduced PILCO, a MB policy search that uses a GP as the
probabilistic dynamical model. When introduced, PILCO showed unprecedented data-
efficiency in terms of policy learning.
Currently, PILCO uses a GP with a stationary
squared exponential kernel. Consequently, it can not model complicated and discontinuous
dynamics, which are typical for robots [2]. In this thesis: We choose to work with PILCO
and equip it with a more expressive dynamical model.
We choose the Input-Warped
Gaussian Process (IW-GP) as our dynamical model, as this choice keeps us in the realm
of GPs, and hence closer to the nature of PILCO.

1.2
Limitations
In 2017 Polydoros and Nalpantidis [10] referred PILCO as state of the art in MB RL.
However, recent bench-marking on more complicated environments an MB RL algorithm
called PETS outperform the remaining field of MB algorithms [13].
PETS uses a
probabilistic neural network [2]. Despite recent bench-marking, we choose to constrain the
focus on PILCO. The argumentation is twofold: (1) Several modifications have already
been built on PILCO, indicating relative maturity. (2) PILCO has been used to control
physical robots3, unlike simulations. Hence PILCO has shown results in the real world.
In this thesis, IW-GP is only tested on synthetic regression problems to illustrate its
advantage in non-stationary problems. Furthermore, IW-PILCO is only compared to the
original PILCO on the mountain car problem.
1.3
Significance of the Study
If RL becomes more popular in the real world, it will most likely have a considerable
impact on the world. The world is transitioning towards green energy. However, a world
with superfluous resources is unlikely.
Therefore, RL algorithms capable of efficiently
controlling complicated systems will be pivotal in utilizing our resources, such as reducing
Google data center energy consumption. PILCO is a step in this development. Perhaps
PILCO with a more expressive model can increase this step and handle more complicated
control problems than previously.

1.5. Thesis Outline
PILCO is a MB policy search RL algorithm [3][4][5]. Conceptually PILCO consists of 4
main steps. (1) the dynamical system is estimated with a stationary GP from data. (2)
long-term state predictions are approximated. (3) the policy is improved by minimizing
the accumulated cost, using analytic gradients. (4) the learned controller is applied to
the environment. Repeat from step 1.
DeepPILCO
DeepPILCO replaces the GP with a Bayesian neural network dynamics model with
approximate variational inference. The goal is to reduce computational complexity and
to control high-dimensional systems [6]. Personal communication indicates that [6] has
reproducibility issues.

This chapter introduce GPs in its generality for two reasons. (1) originally PILCO use a
stationary GP to model the dynamics, and it is therefor a key component in PILCO. (2)
GPs are the framework in IW-GP and hence IW-PILCO.
A Gaussian Process (GP) is a stochastic process that generalizes the Gaussian distribution.
As a modeling method for random processes, the GP is a non-parametric Bayesian method
used for regression and classification tasks.
This chapter first introduces GPs mathematically. We then excite a GP with an uncertain
input and approximate the predictive distribution using Moment Matching (MM).

multi-modality will move the mean away from the mode because MM works as a smoother.

PILCO 4
Probabilistic Inference for Learning Control (PILCO) is a data-efficient MB policy search
algorithm that originally uses a GP for the dynamical model. This chapter define the
setting in which PILCO operates and then a detailed description of the steps in PILCO.
However, first a high-level introduction to RL.
The general idea behind RL is visualized in Fig. 4.1. RL is, in many ways, a game between
a constructed agent and the given environment. Using an action, the agent receives a new
state and a cost. The agent reflects on its action and alter its future actions to minimize
the cost.
Figure 4.1.
The concept of RL visualized.
The agent learn a policy through
interaction with the environment.
4.1
Markov Decesion Process
Suppose we wish to control a given discrete-time dynamical system.
Such control
problems can be constructed as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). An MDP is a 4-tuple
(X, U, ftrue, c) where:
• X is the state space.
• U is the control space.
• Transition probabilities: assume that the true transition dynamics are deterministic
and determined by the function ftrue : X × U 7→X, which only depend on the
previous state and control (the Markov Property).
yt = xt −xt−1 = ftrue(xt−1, ut−1),
xt−1 ∈X, ut−1 ∈U
• The immediate cost, c, given after transitioning to xt from xt−1 using control ut−1.
We will assume that only the current state is penalized and not the control or previous
state hence c : X →R.
35
==PAGE:42==
4. PILCO
Denote the MDP S = (X, U, ftrue, c). An agent, A, is constructed to control the system.
We define A as a tuple that inherits the elements X, U and c from S, but not the true
dynamics ftrue. Instead A need to estimate ftrue, denote this estimate f. The agent is
also equipped with a policy, π : X 7→U, mapping from state- to action-space. Hence A is
characterized by the 5-element tuple A = (X, U, c, f, π).

Exploration vs. Exploitation: is the grass greener on the other side of the fence? is an
open question in both life/philosophy/mathematics and consequently RL. This question
is the core in the exploration-exploitation trade off. Should one exploit the current policy
which work, or should one explore the policy space further, with the opportunity to find a
better policy, or worse. The variance of the cost function Vxt[c(xt)] measures the potential

This chapter compares PILCO and IW-PILCO on the mountain car problem, a standard
testing domain in RL. The objective is to move a car up a hill and reach the flag, as seen
in Fig. 6.1. However, the car engine is not powerful enough to drive the car up the hill.
Therefore, the car has to build potential energy to climb the hill. The car must move away
from the flag to build up potential energy, and hence the agent must endure an initial cost.
In this thesis, the Python library Gym from OpenAI is used to simulate the mountain
car.
The 2D state-space X consist of the position p ∈[−1.2, 0.6] and velocity v ∈[−0.07, 0.07]

the environment is initialized the car is placed uniformly in the set [−0.6, −0.4] with zero
velocity. Therefore the initial state distribution is x0 ∼U([−0.6, −0.4] × [0, 0]). The car
is controlled by a 1D control signal u ∈U = [−1, 1]. u = −1 is full throttle left and u = 1
is full throttle right. The rollout end if the car reaches the flag position p = 0.45 or if the
horizon H is reached.

Conclusion 7
This chapter will first conclude on the thesis objectives and then, as final remarks, discuss
possible future work.
Implement IW-GP and compare to GP on synthetic non-stationary regression
problems:
IW-GP was successfully implemented using GPyTorch in Python.
In
Section 3.2 IW-GP was demonstrated on three synthetic non-stationary regression
problems. The synthetic problems were designed such that the lengthscale varied spatially.
It was showed that the stationary GPs, would overfit in some regions. However, the IW-GP
could adapt the lengthscale, and therefor outperformed GPs on these synthetic regression
problems, as expected.
IW-GP expands the traditional GP with a lot of parameters and hyper-parameters.
Therefore, IW-GP is more expressive but also more complex and is more likely to have a
multi-modal objective function NLML.
Implement and describe approximate long-term predictions in IW-GP: In
Section 2.2 the long-term predictions {p(xt)}H
t=0 was inferred in closed form using MM
for a GP model. This result is used in PILCO to compute analytic gradients dE[c(xt)]
dθ
.
Therefore, it was necessary that IW-GP could infer {p(xt)}H
t=0 in closed form which
required linearisation of the input warping as explained in Section 3.3.
This method
was successfully implemented.
Implement PILCO: A big part of this thesis is spent on understanding PILCO as well as
implementing PILCO this was successfully done using Python and PyTorch as the main
library, the autograd feature in PyTorch was used instead of explicit analytic gradients.
Combine PILCO and IW-GP to implement IW-PILCO: In Chapter 5 the theory
in PILCO and IW-GP was combined to form IW-PILCO. Using the MM procedure for
IW-GP we incorporated IW-GP into PILCO, creating an implementation of IW-PILCO.
Compare IW-PILCO to PILCO on the mountain car control problem: PILCO
and IW-PILCO were compared on the mountain car problem in Chapter 6. Conditioning
on the chosen hyper-parameters, the 10 runs of PILCO and IW-PILCO showed that:
• IW-PILCO found more successful policies than PILCO, however, these policies did
not achieve a lower cost than PILCO.
• Examining the failed PILCO run in Fig. 6.6 and the less successful IW-PILCO run in
Fig. 6.8 showed that: PILCO and IW-PILCO sometimes have long-term predictions
that do not reflect rollouts - The reason being unclear. Perhaps better tuned hyper-
parameters can solve this issue.

7. Conclusion
• IW-PILCO generally modeled the dynamics better than PILCO in the NLML sense.
• 10 PILCO and IW-PILCO runs are not sufficient to give a definite conclusion on the
comparison between PILCO and IW-PILCO on the mountain car problem.
It was unexpected that several PILCO runs yielded policies that did not solve the mountain
car problem consistently. The issue might be poorly tuned hyper-parameters. Hand-tuning
is not optimal, and a more structured approach when tuning hyper-parameters would be
beneficial.
IW-PILCO was fed hyper-parameters hand-tuned for PILCO, which favors
PILCO. Nevertheless, IW-PILCO yielded more successful rollouts than PILCO, showing
that IW-PILCO might have the potential to improve PILCO.
In summary:
4 out of 5 objectives were reached in this thesis. More work is required to
determine if IW-PILCO is an improvement on the mountain car problem and potentially
other control problems.
7.1
Future Work
As mentioned, it is inconclusive whether IW-PILCO is an improvement over PILCO.
Two actions are suggested to provide a definite answer: (1) It is believed that better-
tuned hyper-parameters will result in a higher success rate and consistent policies for
both IW-PILCO and PILCO. Therefore, a more structured approach when tuning hyper-
parameters is obvious.
Random grid-search is suggested because it is a relatively
straightforward/simple tuning method. (2) A more complicated test-bed, perhaps with
discontinuous dynamics, might shed light on IW-PILCOs performance and the expressivity
of IW-GP in a control context. For example, the python library Gym offers a 6-axis robotic-
arm test-bed.
Recent research in DKL i.e. IW-GP where g is a DNN, shows that: In some cases DKL
suffer from overfitting, despite using the NLML objective function[9].
Ober et al. [9]
suggests that a fully Bayesian treatment can rectify the performance. One might consider
merging this development into IW-PILCO in future work.
