Abstract
LiNi0.5Mn1.5O4/Graphite full cells are contenders for high energy battery storage ap-
plications of the future by virtue of their high operating potential resulting in high
energy density and the absence of cobalt in the cathode active material, resulting in
low costs and low toxicity.
One of the main challenges facing the large-scale commercial implementation of
LNMO is the instability of available electrolytes at such high potentials, meaning
that a fundamental understanding of the decomposition reactions involving the in-
teraction between electrodes and electrolytes is needed.
The analysis of gases evolved during cell operation as a result of the decomposition
of electrolyte solvents during the creation of solid electrolyte interface (SEI) layers
on the anode and the cathode, is a way of characterising these processes.
Different electrolyte formulations’ effect on SEI formation have been investigated
using Differential electrochemical mass spectrometry (DEMS), in situ pressure mea-
surement, Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OEM)
and galvanostatic chronopotentiometry.
It has been found that vinylene carbonate (VC) additive in particular, increases gas
evolution tenfold compared to a standard system, contributing to the formation of
a signiﬁcant cathode electrolyte interface (CEI), while hardly affecting the SEI for-
mation. It has also been shown that the evolution of gas species only accounts for a
small portion of side-reaction in LNMO/Graphite cells.

Scope of the Thesis
This thesis has three main working objectives
The ﬁrst is to adapt the DEMS and pressure setup to the use with Li-ion batteries,
and through preliminary experiments establish its suitability for the purpose.
The second is to characterise the LNMO/Graphite system both electrochemically
and physically, with different electrolyte conﬁgurations.
The third is to obtain high-quality DEMS and pressure data that will enable a de-
tailed understanding of both electrode-electrolyte interface layer formation and on-
going electrolyte decomposition reactions leading to cell degradation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
From the hearing aids empowering our elders, to powering the drones that will be
transporting our descendants, batteries are becoming an increasingly important
part of our lives. With the electriﬁcation of our society as a whole along with a push
towards intermittent renewable energy sources, the need to store that energy for
portable and stationary applications is becoming increasingly evident, highlighting
the need for developments in energy storage technologies for both large scale grid-
storage and high power mobile and transport applications.
The large scale adoption of electric vehicles, as just one technology among many
that will see the use of batteries rise to unseen levels, will pose new challenges in
terms of surmounting current limitations on cost and raw material availability. De-
veloping technologies to overcome these will be what sets both companies and aca-
demics apart in a race to come up with the best solutions to these truly global prob-
lems.
LiNi0.5Mn1.5O4 (LNMO) as a cathode material is a candidate to become a main com-
ponent in future batteries by the merit of its high energy, low cost and low toxicity.
One of the main factors preventing the widespread adoption of LNMO is its high
operating potential beyond the stability window of current electrolytes. Gaining a
fundamental understanding of the interaction between cathode, anode and elec-
trolyte will prove essential in designing better battery systems in the future.
One of the consequences of electrolyte decomposition is the evolution of gas as one
of the products of side-reaction in high-voltage cells. The analysis of these evolved
gas species permits an insight into the mechanisms causing them, and has an ap-
plication in research into battery degradation in general, in addition to speciﬁcally
focusing on the troubles caused by gas evolution in its own right. Differential elec-
trochemical gas spectrometry (DEMS) and accurate in situ pressure monitoring of
operating cells have been a cornerstone of this thesis, to investigate solid electrolyte
interface (SEI) layers on the anode and cathode electrolyte interface (CEI) layers on
the cathode.

2.1
Li-ion batteries
2.1.1
The fundamentals of Li-ion batteries
A battery is a device made up of one or several electrochemical cells, which each
consisting of two electrodes with different redox potentials, connected electrically
by an external circuit and internally by an ionically conducting electrolyte between
the two electrodes. A cell’s function is to store electrical energy as chemical energy,
exploiting a series of redox reactions to perform the conversion back and forth.[1]
A distinction is made between primary batteries that operate only in discharge mode
and secondary batteries where the redox reactions are reversible, permitting the
cells to be both charged and discharged. The two electrodes have different redox
potentials, with the positive electrode having the highest redox potential, and the
negative electrode having the lowest. The difference between the two (equilibrium)
redox potentials of the electrodes yields the equilibrium, or open circuit, cell volt-
age.
During discharge, an oxidation reaction happens at the negative electrode, i.e. at
the interface between the electrode and the electrolyte, while a reduction reaction
simultaneously happens at the positive electrode. During charge the opposite is
true, such that the oxidation takes place at the positive electrode, and the reduc-
tion happens at the negative. Charge balance is maintained by the movement of
positively charged Li-ions within the battery, and electrons in an external circuit, as
illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1.1.

2.1.2
Current Li-ion technologies
This subsection is borrowed from a similar introductory section on the basics of
Li-ion batteries written in 2016 during my own and Malte Bjerg Petersen’s Bachelor
project on "Development of in situ X-ray diffraction and electrochemical character-
isation method of LNMO cathodes".[3]
"Lithium cobalt(III) oxide (LiCoO2) is the most widely used cathode material for
Li-ion cells, which was ﬁrst commercialised in 1991 by Sony. [4] The drawbacks
of this material is its high toxicity, and high cost. [5] Lithium manganese oxides
provide a viable alternative, due to their non-toxic nature, considerably lower pric-
ing, high voltage and high electronic conductivity, making them ideal for cathode
materials."[3]
"LiMn2O4 (LMO) spinel and its subspecies provide higher discharge potentials than
both the incumbent LiCoO2 cells, and the layered structure of the LiMnO2 material.
[6] The spinel structure allows three dimensional ion diffusion, translating into sig-
niﬁcantly faster charge/discharge rates than for layered structures. [7] Furthermore
spinels can reach high potentials with Li/Li+ (notation for cells with metallic Li used
as anode, and Li ions used as active species in the cathode) as a reference - 4.0 V and
4.7 V for LMO and LNMO respectively, compared with the lower 3.0 V and 3.6 V of
LiMnO2 and LiCoO2 respectively.[8]"[3]
"High voltage cells offer numerous practical advantages in applications where high
voltage battery packs are desired.[9] Fewer cells are needed in series in a battery
pack to obtain high voltages, and anode material can be saved thereby further re-
ducing the size of the cell as a whole, even though the energy density of the cathode
itself is not increased. Mn based spinels offer good results from this perspective,
especially when doped with other transition metals such as nickel, iron, chromium,
cobalt and copper.[9] This partial replacement of the Mn atoms on the 16d octa-
hedral sites offers the added beneﬁt of reducing the amount of trivalent Mn in the
structure, thus limiting Mn dissolution in the organic electrolyte, a phenomenon
which constitutes one of the main degradation factors for Mn based spinel cath-
ode materials. [10][11] When doping the LMO cathode material with Ni, 25% of Mn
(predominantly trivalent - Mn3+) is substituted, leaving mostly tetravalent Mn."[3]

2.1.3
LNMO/Graphite full cell
When working with full cells instead of half cells, the potential measured will be
the potential difference between the cathode and anode, instead of the potential
vs. Li/Li+, ie. the potential difference will be reduced by the potential of the anode
(graphite in this case) towards Li/Li+.
In ﬁgure 2.1.2 the potentials of the graphite anode and the LNMO cathode against
Li/Li+ is shown. The potential of the full cell is the potential difference between
the cathode and the anode, so when plotted as the potential against Li/Li+ as in the

2.2
Properties of LNMO
LNMO is a promising material since it’s high operating potential gives it a high en-
ergy density. Because it operates in a multi phase system (see section 2.3), most of its
electrochemical activity happens at a voltage plateau of 4.7 V vs Li/Li+. The speciﬁc
energy density of a material is given by the product of the cell’s operating voltage
and it’s gravimetric capacity, yielding an energy density of 700 Wh/g for LNMO. Us-
ing a high voltage cathode material in a full cell has the added beneﬁt of reducing
the amount of anode needed, since each Li+/e– pair stores more energy than a cell
of equal capacity but with a lower operating potential.

2.2.2
C-rate
The concept of C-rate is a way of describing the rate at which a cell is charged or
discharged as a function of a cells theoretical capacity, in galvanostatic (constant
current) chronopotentiometry. The cells theoretical capacity is in this case the the-
oretical capacity of the capacity-limiting electrode. In all the measurements per-
formed in this thesis the cathode is always the capacity-limiting electrode, and so
C-rate is deﬁned from the capacity of the LNMO cathode.
A C-rate of 1C is deﬁned as the time it takes to fully charge or discharge a cell in 1
hour. In reality this is taken to signify the time it would take if the full theoretical
capacity was obtained, and so in practice a 1C discharge for instance will typically
take less than one hour. A C-rate of 0.5C would then signify a charge/discharge
time of 2 hours, and 10C of 6 minutes. A cell with a theoretical capacity of 1 mAh
charged at a C-rate of 1 would be charged at a current of 1 mA, and if it reached its
full theoretical capacity, this would take an hour. The same cell cycled at 0.5C would
be cycled at a current of 0.5 mA.
This allows cells of different capacity to be cycled at equal rates, and thus be com-
pared. C-rate can however be ambiguous, since it is deﬁnied by the theoretical ca-
pacity. If a cell undergoes a large capacity fade in the initial cycles for instance, it
will effectively cycle at a higher C-rate than intended during its remaining lifetime.
In this thesis cells where typically formed at either 0.1C or 0.2C for three cycles, and
then charged at a C-rate of 0.5C, and discharged at a C-rate of 1C.

2.4
Electrolytes
Electrolytes in batteries serve as electronically insulating ionic conductors, allow-
ing the transport of ions from one electrode to the other. These can be based on
acids, bases, polymers or other organic compounds with dissolved salts. In Li-ion
batteries, electrolytes are based on polymers in solid, gel or liquid form, with the
most common being liquid organic carbonate based solvents. Lithium salts are dis-
solved in these, and additives can furthermore be added for a number of purposes
including increased temperature stability, increased stability at voltage extremes,
increased conductivity, to favour the formation of a beneﬁcial solid electrolyte (SEI)
and/or cathode electrolyte interface (CEI). These additives can thus decrease gas
evolution and other degradation mechanisms in some cases, but may also show in-
creased gas evolution as side effects of SEI/CEI formation.

2.4.1
Choice of electrolyte solvents
In a review from 2004, Xu deﬁnes 5 requirements for the ideal electrolyte solvent
used in Li-ion batteries, that sound as follows: "(1) It should be able to dissolve salts
to sufﬁcient concentration. In other words, it should have a high dielectric constant.
(2) It should be ﬂuid (low viscosity), so that facile ion transport can occur. (3) It
should remain largely inert to all cell components, especially the charged surfaces
of the cathode and the anode, during cell operation. (4) It should remain liquid
in a wide temperature range. In other words, its melting point should be low and
its boiling point high. (5) It should also be safe (high ﬂash point), nontoxic, and
economical."[14]
Aqueous solvents have the ability to dissolve the necessary amount of salts, have a
high dielectric constant and a low viscosity. Waters narrow stability window how-
ever, only going to 1.23 V, means that it reacts too readily with the strongly reduc-
ing and oxidising surfaces of the graphite anode and typical Li-ion battery cathodes
(e.g. LNMO), respectively, reducing the choice to non-aqueous solvents with polar
groups, including ether-linkage (−O−), sulfonyl (S−−O), carbonyl (C−−O) and nitrile
(C−−−N). [14]. Typically cyclic carbonates such as ethylene carbonate (EC) or propy-
lene carbonate (PC) are combined with linear carbonates such as ethylmethyl car-
bonate (EMC), diethyl carbonate (DEC) or dimethyl carbonate (DMC). The cyclic
carbonates are characterised by high dielectric constants, and in the case of EC,
a narrow liquid temperature range. Combining them with linear carbonates with
lower conductivity, but higher stability in a wider range of both temperature and
potential, creates a synergistic effect and delivers a solvent that both has a high ca-
thodic and anodic stability, high viscosity and ionic conductivity, and high solvation
of Li salts.[14]
The formation of an SEI layer is an essential part of the successful operation of a Li-
ion cell. Its main function is to avoid the further decomposition of solvents on the
anode surface. A good SEI layer is formed during the initial cycles, and stops grow-
ing after that, since continuous growth would lead to a continuous loss of Li and a
low coulombic efﬁciency. Since the SEI layer does not conduct electrons however,
and since it keeps electrolyte solvents away from the surface of the anode, only let-
ting Li+ ions through, a good SEI layer naturally prevents its own excessive buildup
while letting the cell cycle efﬁciently.[15][16] Browning et al. show that this happens
in particular when an EC:DMC mixture is used as solvent. The SEI layer serves as
an inhibitor of these processes, while allowing conduction of Li-ions. Its creation
during the ﬁrst cycle, and especially during the ﬁrst lithiation of the graphite anode,
is thus essential for a well-performing cell.[16][17]
Tavassol et al. show a large irreversible mass uptake on the SEI layer with EC:DMC
as electrolyte solvent. On a model system of Au electrodes, at potentials between 2

2.4.2
Vinylene carbonate as an electrolyte additive
Vinylene carbonate (VC) is widely used as an additive in commercial Li-ion batter-
ies, for its role in forming a stable polymer SEI ﬁlm on graphite anodes, and thus
increase cycle stability of full cells, especially at elevated temperatures. Dong et al.
investigated VC as an additive in LNMO/Graphite full cells, and found that it in-
stead decreased cell performance, both in terms of capacity fade rate, and in terms
of low coulombic efﬁciency, symptomatic of a continuous growth of the SEI layer,
thus resulting in a reduction in available Li+.[19]
VC polymerises to form part of the CEI and SEI layer on the cathode and anode
respectively, through two separate processes. A study done on LCO and LFP cells by
El Ouatani et al. shows that for cells cycled at potentials within the stability window
of the electrolyte. the reduction of VC on graphite anodes happens at approximately
1.25 V vs Li/Li+ (and is not observed on LTO anodes, since they operate at 1.55 V vs
Li/Li+). Interestingly the polymerisation on the cathode seems not to depend wholly
on the potential, but also on the chemical composition of the cathode surface, since
oxidation is observed at 4.2 V on LCO cathodes, but not on LFP cathodes at 4.5 V.[20]
Self et al. have surveyed the effect of VC additives on gas expansion in NMC pouch
cells. Their data further implies that an initial gas evolution at the start of the ﬁrst
charge cycle is greatly reduced, with however, an increase in gas evolution at high-
voltage (>4.5V)[21], which would imply challenges for implementation of VC in LNMO
cells.
This is in fact also what Dong et al. demonstrate, and that the polymerisation of VC

2.5.1
Gas evolution studies on high-voltage Li-ion batteries
NCM/graphite and LCO/Graphite cells, being some of the most widely used com-
mercial cathode and anode combinations, are naturally also the most studied Li-
ion systems in literature. Since they operate at relatively high potentials, and use
the same electrolytes as in LNMO/Graphite cells, a lot of information can be gained
from those studies. The most relevant distinction to have in mind when comparing
gas evolution in these cells to that in LNMO/Graphite cells, is that LNMO cells, in
contrast to NCM and LCO cells, are cycled to potentials beyond the stability win-
dow of the carbonate based electrolytes, leading to a greater driving force for gas
evolution and other side reactions in such cells.
Berkes et al. ﬁnd that in NCM/Graphite cells, gas evolution is related to mainly two
processes, including the desirable formation of SEI formation on anodes, and from
oxidation mechanisms on the cathode. It is also linked to a decreased coulombic
efﬁciency. By studying the pattern of gas evolution over more than 30 cycles, they
observe that H2 and CO are especially evolved during the initial cycles, and at lower
potentials, while the onset of CO2 evolution happens at potentials above 3.84 V, and
increases over time, peaking after the 20th cycle.[23]

Humidity content in the electrolyte above 10 ppm H2O catalyses a series of degra-

as shown in equation 2.14. LiF is insoluble in the carbonate based electrolyte, thus
thickening the SEI layer, and HF heavily degrades the SEI layer, rendering it more
fragile.[38] To avoid the introduction of any humidity into the cell, they are thus as-
sembled in an Ar ﬁlled glove box with under 0.1 ppm H2O, and all components are
dried for 12 hours under vacuum, at 120 ◦C.
After use all the relevant components are cleaned in an ultrasound bath and thor-
oughly rinsed in acetone (O-rings are rinsed in ethanol). The pipes are purged using
pressurised air and then Ar to remove any remaining species.

3.3. ELECTROCHEMICAL CHARACTERISATION
3.2.3
Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry
Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OES, but referred
to as ICP throughout this thesis) is an elemental analysis technique used to detect
chemical elements in a sample with great precision.
The operating principle of this type of emission spectroscopy is to use a plasma
to ionise sample atoms that then emit electromagnetic radiation at a characteristic
wavelength.
ICP has been used in this thesis to analyse the elemental composition of used elec-
trodes. This has been done by dissolving the electrodes in acid, and spraying the
solution into the plasma torch, Ar in this case, subsequently measuring the wave-
length emitted by the recombination of the ionised atoms in the sample and their
electrons. The elemental composition of the sample is then found from the inten-
sity of the signal at a given wavelength. When investigating samples such as entire
cathodes, where there is several orders of magnitude difference between analysed
elements, the resulting distortion of the output spectra needs to be accounted for
by careful calibration.
The ICP measurements themselves, along with the interpretation of the results and
determination of the exact elemental compositions in this thesis has been performed
by Pia Villadsen in the Analysis laboratory at Haldor Topsøe A/S. The reported error
rate of the results is below 2 %.
3.3
Electrochemical characterisation
The main electrochemical characterisation techniques used in this thesis are gal-
vanostatic (constant current) chronopotentiometry and potentiostatic (constant volt-
age) chronoamperometry.
Cycling cells at a constant current ensures that the redox reactions on either elec-
trode happen at a constant rate [41], with the voltage being the potential difference
between the cathode and the anode, corresponding to the relevant redox couple. In
an LNMO half cell charged towards Li/Li+ this means that the potential stabilises
to 4.0 V while transitioning from Mn3+ to Mn4+, then swiftly rises to 4.7 V when all
Mn is in the tetravalent state to instead begin oxidising Ni from Ni2+ to Ni3+, where
the potential will once again be stable during that transition. The next redox cou-
ple operates at a slightly higher voltage of 4.75 V vs Li/Li+, and corresponds to Ni3+
to Ni4+.[42] When this oxidation is complete, and all Li-ions are correspondingly
deintercalated from the cathode, the potential will increase to sustain the applied
current, determining the cut-off potential set to the cell.
The cells investigated here are typically subject to a constant voltage step after the
constant current step, meaning that once they have reached a given cut-off volt-
age, the cell is held at that voltage while the current necessary to keep it there is
measured. That current will typically fall as most Li-ions are extracted, and a cutoff

4.4
Evaluation and quantiﬁcation of gas leaks
In the following, the leak rate is described in terms of a pressure drop by unit of time,
i.e. mbar/h. During the rest of this thesis the gas evolution measured by pressure
has been converted into moles.
In his work on this same DEMS setup, at DTU Risø, Jonathan Højberg [2] observed
leak rates of 0.2 - 20 mbar/h. In his situation, studying Li−O2 cells over periods usu-
ally not exceeding 9 hours, leak rates of 5-10 mbar/h were acceptable since they do
not exceed 10 % of total gas evolved (corresponding to gas evolution of 100 mbar/h).
The cells studied in this thesis however, evolve gas in the order of 0.5 - 2 mbar/hour,
and the cells are sometimes cycled for over a 100 hours.

An added challenge is that temperature variations in the laboratory over time can be
signiﬁcant, and can have a detrimental effect on the accuracy of the measurements.
This is especially the case in pressure measurements where even small variations
in temperature cause pressure variations of the same order of magnitude as the
gas evolution, meaning that they have been performed in a temperature controlled
environment. For DEMS measurements, that have not been possible to tempera-
ture control, it has proven sufﬁcient to log the temperature for use when calculating
evolved gas from the ion currents.
In both types of measurements, the quality of the data, and the ability to extract
good quantitative information, is dependent on the gas not leaking out of the cell
during operation. As described in section 3.1.1, the cell is connected to the rest
of the setup via Swagelok DESO quick-connects. Furthermore, the rest of the pipes
connecting the different sensors, valves and pumps are all connected with Swagelok
ports. In the DEMS setups, a total of 38 connections, and in the pressure setup, a
total of 9 connections make a completely gas-tight system very difﬁcult, with very
small leaks possibly occurring. One of the big challenges in this project has been to
ensure that the setup is hermetic enough to not adversely affect the quality of the
relatively weak signal obtained from the two types of experiments.
This low gas evolution entails a need for a setup that is as hermetic as possible. The
main source of leaks is the system of O-rings expanding against a fused silica ferrule,
as seen in ﬁgure 3.2.2, sealing the electrode tips. The O-rings can not be tightened
arbitrarily hard, as it results in the glass ferrule breaking.
During pressure tests, the leak rate of a cell is quantiﬁed before beginning cycling,
by keeping the cell at OCV for 5 hours before beginning to charge the cell. After
an initial stabilisation of the pressure, presumably due to reaching an equilibrium
between the temperature of the cell and the temperature of the pressure sensor, a
cell is deemed sufﬁciently tight if the leak rate is below 1 mbar/h, and preferably
below 0.5 mbar/h.
Another consideration in relation to gas leaks is the possibility of a contamination
of the cell from air getting in. When working in pressure mode, the cell is always
operated at over pressure, to ensure that any leak results in gas leaking out, instead
of air leaking in. In the cases where the leak rate is below 1 mbar/h, it is safe to
assume that the amount of air getting into the cell is negligible. When working in
DEMS mode, parts of the setup will be under vacuum, and will thus draw in outside
air from any eventual leak. Since the cell itself is regularly purged and reﬁlled with
fresh Ar, the effect is not deemed to be detrimental to the cell itself.

5.2
Discussion
Based on the results obtained in the previous section, the SEI and CEI formation
processes will be described, along with parasitic reactions also taking place.
5.2.1
SEI formation
The initial cycles are responsible for a large part of SEI build up, but there are indica-
tions of a continuous build-up consuming Li. This is shown by four main elements;
Firstly the coulombic efﬁciency recovering quickly, secondly by ICP data of cells af-
ter formation and after 43 cycles, thirdly by pressure measurements showing high
initial gas evolution tapering off, and ﬁnally by DEMS measurements not showing
signiﬁcant levels of CO2.
The coulombic efﬁciency for the ﬁrst cycle as seen on the voltage curve in ﬁgure
5.1.2 is 87 % and quickly rises to above 99 %. The subsequent evolution during the
next 600 cycles is shown for two identical cells in ﬁgure 5.1.3. It takes 100 cycles
for the efﬁciency to stabilise at 99.7 %, matching the steeper capacity fade shown in
ﬁgure 5.1.1, indicating additional reactions taking place for a signiﬁcant period of
time, which could be due to SEI and CEI formation.
The ICP data in the discharged state (table 5.1.2) show that after 6 cycles 15 % of Li
remains on the anode, while 29.5 % remains after 43 cycles. Assuming that this is all
Li incorporated in an SEI layer, the rate of Li consumption is around 0.5 % per cycle
even after 43 cycles, conﬁrming the relatively low coulombic efﬁciency until cycle
100. This distribution of Li is illustrated in the scheme in ﬁgure 5.2.1.
The pressure data shown in ﬁgure 5.1.4b show an initial gas evolution during the
ﬁrst charge cycle. This initial gas evolution, although signiﬁcantly larger than dur-

5.2.2
CEI formation
No signiﬁcant CEI layer is observed, contrary to what authors like Edstrom et al.
have observed in LMO/Graphite cells, a spinel material closely related to LNMO
[33]. This is supported both by electrochemical data, ICP data and DEMS measure-
ments.
Firstly, the voltage proﬁle in ﬁgure 5.1.2 does not indicate signiﬁcant oxidation reac-
tions on the cathode happening at high voltage. If this where the case the constant
voltage step at 4.9 V could be expected to be longer, sustained by an eventual elec-
trolyte decomposition reaction. The 145 mAh/g charge capacity is still signiﬁcantly
higher that the discharge capacity of 135 mAh/g, meaning that some side reactions
do happen.

Secondly the ICP data in table 5.1.3 shows that there is no increase in Li bound on
the cathode between cycle 6 and cycle 43, at a cutoff at 4.9 V, with 13 % and 10.4 % Li
remaining respectively. Furthermore, in the presence of signiﬁcant oxidation reac-
tions further binding Li in a CEI layer, this could reasonably be expected to increase
with cells held at higher voltages. The opposite trend is seen here, indicating again
that no signiﬁcant oxidation takes place, as part of CEI formation.
Third and lastly, the known oxidation reactions on high-voltage cathodes mostly
evolve CO2 by oxidation of EC (CO can also be evolved, but is believed to be by a
less favourable path). The data in ﬁgure 5.1.4a indicate that very little CO2 i evolved,
even at 4.9 V, lending itself to the conclusion that in the standard electrolyte, high-
voltage oxidation does not seem to be a large problem, nor a large contributor to the
formation of a CEI layer.
5.2.3
Parasitic reactions
There are indications of a reduction of trace amounts of H2O during the initial cycle,
supported by pressure measurements during the ﬁrst cycles, and by DEMS mea-
surements showing H2 evolution.
An interpretation of this preliminary ﬁrst gas evolution is that it happens as a conse-
quence of a reduction reaction on the graphite anode, since the potential of graphite
quickly descends below 1 V, potential at which many of the reduction reactions be-
gin.
Since the onset of the ﬁrst gas evolution comes shortly after applying a current, but
does not appear in subsequent cycles as seen in ﬁgure 5.1.4b, a good example of
a one-off reaction would be the reduction of trace amounts of H2O described in
equations 2.15 and 2.16, which would then not appear again if the reduced H2O
originated from trace amounts in the electrolyte rather than H2O leaking in from
the surrounding air.
The DEMS measurements, with their low time-resolution, have not been sufﬁcient
to capture the such an initial evolution reliably, also the ﬁrst data point shown in ﬁg-
ure 5.1.4a does show H2O with the largest contribution. This lack of H2 signal could
be explained if the H2O reacting in the pressure cells was in fact introduced when
connecting the Swagelok DESO quick connect ports between the pressure sensor
and the cell. This would then still create a one-off reaction as explained in the para-
graph above, but would not happen in the DEMS cell, where small amounts of H2O
introduced when connecting the cell would quickly be ﬂushed away.
This interpretation would then signify that the second rise towards high-voltage in
ﬁgure 5.1.4b is an indication of a little oxidative activity on the cathode, which is
also indicated by the ﬁnal rise of the CO2 signal in ﬁgure 5.1.4a.

5.3. CONCLUSION
5.3
Conclusion
From pressure measurements, DEMS measurement, long-term cycling in coin cells,
and ICP measurements on post-mortem cells, it has been found that gas evolution
is not the primary symptom of degradation mechanisms happening in LNMO/-
Graphite full cells with standard electrolyte, but can be used to backtrack some of
the possible solvent decomposition and polymerisation reactions involved in the
formation of SEI layers.
The SEI layer build up on the anode has been found to be signiﬁcant, and continu-
ously uses available Li for up to 100 cycles, increasing the initial fade rate. CEI build
up and oxidative reactions seem not to be as important.

Electrolyte additives
Having investigated the standard LNMO/Graphite system, with 1 M LiF6P in EC:DMC
used as electrolyte in chapter 5, this section will focus on the use of electrolyte ad-
ditives, and in particular investigate the use of Vinylene Carbonate.
To quantify the effect of additives in standard EC:DMC and EC:DEC:DMC based
electrolytes, the same array of testing methods have here been used, beginning with
elemental analysis of post-mortem electrodes using ICP-spectrometry, and then in-
cluding chronopotentiometric and amperometric cycling in coin cells, in-situ pres-
sure measurements during formation cycles to gauge total gas evolution, and ﬁnally
DEMS measurement of initial cycles, to identify which speciﬁc gases are evolved in
those crucial formation cycles. These different measurements can then be used to
give an indication as to which decomposition reactions the cell undergoes during
the formation of the SEI and CEI layers, and what effect the use of an electrolyte
additive like VC has to that process, as well as to the cells cycle stability. The de-
composition reactions and ensuing gas evolution observed i VC cells is found to be
dramatically different from the effects observed in the standard system.
The ﬁrst section will focus on the result obtained in cells with additives, held up
against the results from the standard cell shown in chapter 5. This will lead to a
discussion in the second section that will draw out the differences between the two
electrolyte compositions.

6.2
Discussion
Based on the data presented above, the formation of SEI and CEI layers is discussed
in terms of the clear increase in gas evolution in the VC cell compared to the stan-
dard cell.
6.2.1
Vinylene Carbonate as the main subject of investigation
VC was chosen as the main object of further investigation as additive for three main
reasons. Firstly it is one of the most widely used electrolyte additives in high-voltage
Li-ion cells [19], secondly the early indications of signiﬁcant CEI build-up were in-
teresting, as to the authors knowledge the appearance of a signiﬁcant CEI layer on
LNMO cathodes has not been widely reported in literature, and thirdly, the limita-
tions of the used setup necessitated the investigation of a system evolving more gas
than the standard system.
Being one of the most widely used electrolyte additive in high voltage batteries, in-
vestigating its effect further in LNMO was seen to be of interest, especially if the
initial loss in capacity could be traded for lower capacity fade from the formation of
stable interfacial layers on the electrodes. Dong et al. already investigated the use
of VC in LNMO, and found some of the same increased fade rates seen here [19].
Compared to the other electrolytes investigated using ICP spectrometry, the data
shown in tables 6.1.1 and 6.1.2 show a signiﬁcantly larger retention of Li on the
delithiated cathode than the other electrodes, while at the same time showing a
low retention on the anodes, opposing the notion that VC forms a thick SEI layer on
graphite, and introducing the notion of a signiﬁcant CEI layer.
From the DEMS and pressure measurements performed on standard cells, it was
clear that the poor quality of the data obtained was in part due to being very sen-
sible to very small external variations affecting the measurements. Attempting to
measure the effect of a reduction of gas evolution by focusing on mitigating it would

6.2.2
SEI formation
This subsection will analyse and discuss the SEI formation process, with factors in-
dicative of a larger SEI buildup than in a standard cell, and with counteracting ar-
guments indicating that the SEI layer is in fact not affected much by the addition of
VC to the electrolyte.
Indications of SEI formation
Four main arguments indicate that a SEI layer of a certain importance is formed.
Firstly, a slight over-potential during the ﬁrst charge indicates added resistivity in
the cell. Secondly pressure measurements show that gas evolution begins in the
early stages of the charge cycle, and ﬁnally more CO2 is detected early on than in
the standard cell.
Looking closely at the ﬁrst charge of VC cells and standard cells in ﬁgure 6.1.3b, it
can be distinguished that the two VC cells charge at a slightly higher potential than
the standard cell. This begins at 50 mAh/g, coinciding with the shoulder indicating
that the graphite anode has reached its low-potential plateau seen in ﬁgure 2.1.2.
The gradual rise in over-potential could be interpreted as being due to added in-
ternal resistance in the cell as a SEI layer is formed on the anode. The cell is still
undergoing the same electrochemical activity as its standard counterpart, as can
be seen by the features characteristic of LNMO (transition from the Ni2+/Ni +
3 redox
couple to the Ni3+/Ni4+ redox couple at 95-100 mAh/g) visible in both cells. This
over-potential can only be qualiﬁed as such because two cells for each conﬁgura-
tion, identical but for the electrolyte additive, show completely reproducible results.
The pressure measurements shown in ﬁgure 6.1.6c show that precisely at the 50
mAh/g point the VC cell begins to evolve more gas than the standard cell, point
at which the initial gas evolution in the standard cell stagnates, as seen on the ﬁrst
charge in ﬁgure 6.1.6a.
From the work of Dong et al. on VC polymerisation in LNMO/Graphite cells [19] the
main expected gas species evolved as one of the products of VC polymerising in the
SEI layer is CO2. Looking at the detailed DEMS data in ﬁgure 6.1.5b however, the
CO2 level remains low until the 100 mAh/g point, also when taking the time delay
into account. The pressure increase observed in ﬁgure 6.1.6c seems instead to be
caused by either CO or C2H4. C2H4 is known to be evolved as a consequence of EC
reduction (reaction 2.12), but could possibly also arise from a reaction involving VC.

6.2.3
CEI formation
There are strong indications that VC used in a high-voltage cell like LNMO/Graphite
forms a thick CEI layer, and consumes large amounts of both the available Li as well
as surplus electrons provided during charge. This is shown by four factors; Firstly
the electrochemical data show a lot of high-voltage activity during the initial cycles
of VC cells, coupled to a low coulombic effectivity. Secondly DEMS measurements
show that CO2 is the dominant gas evolved, matching previous ﬁndings reported in
literature. Thirdly the DEMS measurements show that gas evolution begins rapidly
after the cell reaches high voltages. This is supported by pressure measurements
showing an accelerated gas evolution beginning at this point. Fourthly, ICP data
show a large buildup of Li on the delithiated cathode, and the consumption of extra
Li from the electrolyte.
Electrochemical high-voltage activity
Beginning with the electrochemical data; cycling data sampled in coin cells, DEMS
cells and pressure cells point at a lot of oxidation at high voltage. Looking ﬁrst at the
voltage proﬁle of the standard and VC cells in ﬁgure 6.1.3a, it is clear that the cells
undergo the same activity during the initial phase of the ﬁrst charge, as explained
in section 6.2.2. Even though the slight over-potential that can be observed in ﬁgure
6.1.3b is likely an indication of an increase in resistivity in the cell due to the creation
of a slightly thicker SEI layer than in the standard cell, the onset of a wholly differ-
ent reaction begins at the "inﬂection point" indicated by the green arrow in ﬁgure
6.1.3b. At this point the majority of the Li-ions in the cathode have been deinterca-
lated, and the remaining ions are harder to access, increasing the voltage necessary
to "pull" them out. This is what happens in the standard cell, where the voltage in-
creases until the set cutoff of 4.9 V, while the remaining Li-ions are then pulled out
while the current under the constant voltage step is falling.
In the VC cell however, it is more energy favourable to begin another process, showed

In the DEMS measurements (ﬁgure 6.1.5a) the rise in gas evolution likewise comes
after the cell reaches higher potentials.
Large Li buildup on the cathode after formation
Part of the reactions decomposing solvent and VC molecules evolve gas as one of
the products, while up to 80 % of the remaining surplus charge goes into reactions
that do not evolve gas, and can thus not be qualiﬁed in that way. Most of the reac-

VC polymerisation do not incorporate Li, most of those reactions being reduction

closely related to LNMO, as well as on LCO.[33] The exact reactions leading to this
buildup however are not clear.

poor performance of that particular cell (this can be seen on the voltage proﬁle of
the last charge before disassembly shown in ﬁgure 6.1.1b), so around 40 % would be
expected instead if the measurement was repeated with a properly performing cell.
This is a clear indication that the CEI layer is far more signiﬁcant in the VC cells than
in the standard cells, and likewise far more signiﬁcant than the SEI layer in terms of
Li consumption
Continuous Li consumption
It has just been explained that around 40 % of available Li can be expected to be
bound in the CEI layer after just six formation cycles. The slow recovery of coulom-
bic efﬁciency shown in ﬁgure 6.1.4b indicates that this never fully recovers, with VC
cells operating at a constant low efﬁciency of below 99.5 %, indicative of a continu-
ous CEI build up consuming Li.
Indeed during the whole lifetime of the cell the efﬁciency of the VC cell is low com-
pared to that of the standard cell. This could indicate a continuous VC polymerisa-
tion as suggested by Dong et al. [19], sustained by the fact that the coin cell conﬁg-
uration used here is assembled with plenty of electrolyte, and can thus be sustained
for 600 cycles without exhausting the surplus of fresh electrolyte. This argument is
also proposed by Aktekin et al. from Upsala University, who base their explanation
of capacity fade in LNMO/LTO cells on parasitic reactions on the electrodes lead-
ing to limitation of available cyclable Li. Coulombic efﬁciency can be considered
the most important factor for characterising long term cyclability in laboratory test
cells assembled wit plenty of electrolyte, since a low coulombic efﬁciency is a symp-
tom of a large occurrence of parasitic reactions, causing continuous Li loss that cycle
tests alone may not expose.[45]

6.2.5
Complimentary techniques
Better evaluation of reactions not producing gas needs to be done to properly un-
derstand the CEI and SEI formation process in LNMO/Graphite cells, since it has
been found that a minority of those reactions evolve gas, accounting only for up to
a third of the total.
Other authors use especially X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) on cycled elec-
trodes to be able to distinguish which polymer bonds are present on the surface,
and thereby gain a better insight into which species from the electrolyte end up in
the interfacial layers on either electrode. ICP used in this thesis proved essential in
mapping where Li was bound in these layers, but is limited by only being able to

6.3
Conclusion
From electrochemical cycling of cells, ICP analysis of electrodes after formation,
pressure measurements and DEMS measurements on LNMO/Graphite cells with
VC additive in the electrolyte, it has been found that electrolyte formulation plays
an outsized role in SEI/CEI formation in a high-voltage battery system such as LN-
MO/Graphite. This has been demonstrated in the four following ways.
Gas evolution was found to be increased tenfold in VC cells compared to control
measurements on standard cells.
A large amount of electrolyte decomposition was found to arise from oxidation re-
actions on the cathode, leading to the build up of a signiﬁcant CEI layer.
It was found that no signiﬁcant SEI layer on the anode is created, contrary to what
many authors in literature report about other Li-ion batteries in conjunction with
the use of VC.
Finally gas evolution was found to account for less than a third of side-reactions in
the VC cell, and even less in standard cells, highlighting the need for other charac-
terisation techniques to map the processes contributing to CEI/SEI formation, and
to cell degradation.

Chapter 7
Challenges and future work
The main challenges throughout this thesis have been to obtain a reliable mea-
surement of gas evolution. Beginning with characterising the standard system, that
turned out to release signiﬁcantly less gas than the VC cells, added to the difﬁculty.
In summary, there are three factor that need to be improved to improve the reliabil-
ity of this setup for characterising systems with relatively low release of gas. The ﬁrst
is to accurately account for temperature variations, the second is to make mitigat-
ing leaks easier by making the cell more hermetic, the third is to get a larger signal,
thus improving both the signal to noise ration and in general making the monitor-
ing of gas evolution less susceptible to the aforementioned factors of small leakages
in particular.
Temperature variations have in the past been accounted for by monitoring the tem-
perature next to the cell, and by simple use of the ideal gas law, correct for the effect
of temperature variations. The main challenge arises when performing pressure
measurements, where very small variations in temperature cause relatively large
variations in pressure. Monitoring the temperature locally next to the cell did not
prove sufﬁcient, since the physical space occupied by all the pipes and pressure
sensors can also be subject to local temperature variations. In appendix B a plot
showing temperature alongside pressure shows the different attempts at correcting
for temperature. the solution has been to move both pressure sensor and cell into
a temperature-controlled oven, however with the reduced functionality of not be-
ing able to ﬂush the cell before use, or of combining pressure measurements with
DEMS measurements.
A signiﬁcant improvement to be made would be to design or purchase a cell that
was more airtight, partly by using a tighter clamping mechanism. The EL-cell has
that advantage, but has a large internal volume reducing the size of the resulting
signal.
Indeed increasing the signal compared to the internal volume into which the gas is
evolved could be done by increasing the capacity of the cell. This would have to be
by a large factor if the behaviour of systems releasing little gas need to be reliably
investigated. Using pellet electrodes instead of coated cathodes and anodes would

Chapter 8
Conclusions
Electrochemical characterisation using chronopotentiometry and chronoamperom-
etry, in situ measurement of pressure during cell operation, Differential electro-
chemical mass spectrometry measurements, as well as Inductively coupled plasma
optical emission spectrometry measurements on post mortem electrodes have all
been performed on LNMO/Graphite full cells. Using these techniques ﬁve different
electrolyte formulations have been screened, including three with additives. Based
on this the additive vinylene carbonate has been selected as the primary focus for
further investigation of the effect electrolyte additives can have on the formation of
surface interface layers on both the cathode and the anode.
Compared to the standard electrolyte formulation of 1M LiF6P in 1:1 vol.% EC:DMC,
2 %wt. VC has been shown to provoke a tenfold increase in gas evolution in LN-
MO/Graphite cells, arising especially from signiﬁcant oxidation reactions occurring
at high voltage on the cathode, leading to high levels of CO2 evolution, and the for-
mation of a signiﬁcant cathode electrolyte interface layer. Solid electrolyte interface
buildup on the anode was however found to be similar to the standard electrolyte
conﬁguration. Only a tenth of excess charge during the initial cycle was found to
contribute to gas evolution in standard cells, and a third in VC cells, underpin-
ning the need for investigative methods other that gas evolution characterisation
to properly understand the processes leading to SEI/CEI formation, and cell degra-
dation.
Finally, a modiﬁcation of the current setup was proposed if mitigation of gas evo-
lution were to be investigated. Measurements performed on the standard system
where close to the detection limit, so if a decrease of gas evolution were to be reliably
detected, a cell with a tenfold increase in capacity would be needed. Furthermore,
a sealing mechanism not reliant on expanding O-rings against a fragile glass ferrule
would permit a more reliably hermetic cell for future tests.
