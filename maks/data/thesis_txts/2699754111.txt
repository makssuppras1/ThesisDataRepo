Introduction
In the 1930s, H. Eyring and M. Polanyi pioneered the development of Transition State
theory and its subsequent application in the evaluation of reaction rate constants. Some
30 years later in 1969 J. Polanyi, the son of M. Polanyi, and coworkers published a
series of papers in which they examined the dynamics of bimolecular exchange reactions.
They found that depositing energy in specific degrees of freedom would promote reaction,
an observation that since have become known as the Polanyi rules.
Molecular beam
experiments have shown that some reactions seemingly do not conform to these rules.
With advances in both computing power and quantum reaction dynamics, theoretical
calculations have corroborated the experimental results.
In a recent study, Zhang et
al. [1] observed deviation from the Polanyi rules in the lower energy regime calling into
question their generality.
The objectives in this thesis are twofold: we are interested in elucidating the applica-
bility of the Polanyi rules and, if any, quantify the effect by evaluation of the quantum
mechanical exact reaction rate constant and compare it to that of standard Transition
State theory. The computational study comprise a quantum mechanical treatment of
triatomic bimolecular reactions confined to a collinear configuration whose dynamics are
described by relatively simple two-dimensional potential surfaces, in the spirit of Polyani’s
original paper. The surfaces feature either a so-called early or late barrier and to mea-
sure the effect of barrier location, reaction probabilities are calculated. The quantum
mechanical rate constant is evaluated based on the calculated reaction probabilities and
compared to results from Transition State theory which predicts that the magnitude of
the rate constant is independent of barrier position.
The first chapter provides a short introduction to a few key concepts in quantum
mechanics before we turn to some important elements of reactive scattering. The chapter
ends with an introduction to reaction rate constants from the viewpoint of quantum
mechanics and Transition State theory.
In Chapter 2, we introduce the bimolecular
reactions that will be explored in the computational study and the LEPS model potential
from which the potential surfaces are constructed. Chapter 3 is dedicated to the numerical
methods employed in the modeling of the chemical reaction dynamics. The Split-Operator
method is introduced and the combined flux operator/absorbing potential approach for
computing cumulative reaction probabilities is presented. We end the chapter with a
short investigation of the stability and numerical precision of the propagation algorithm.
The final chapter presents the results obtained from the simulations where we examine
the effect of barrier location on reaction probabilities and compare the exact quantum
mechanical rate constant to the Transition State equivalent.

1.2.1. Adiabatic Representation
Solving the TDSE, Eq. (1.2.1), pertaining to a given molecular system involves 3n de-
grees of freedom, where n = N + K (nuclei and electrons). The full system quickly
becomes high-dimensional and even for small molecules it is necessary to simplify the
representation of the system.
In the clamped nucleus representation of the Hamiltonian, we attempt to separate the
electronic and nuclear degrees of freedom. To that end, the molecular Hamiltonian in Eq.
(1.2.2) is partitioned according to, ˆH = ˆ
TN + ˆHe, where ˆTN is the nuclear kinetic energy
operator and ˆHe the electronic Hamiltonian defined
ˆHe = ˆTe + ˆVee + ˆVeN + ˆVNN
(1.2.10)
where the operators retain their original meaning but with the important distinction that
the nuclear coordinates are held fixed. As such, the electronic Hamiltonian depends only
parametrically on nuclear coordinates, R.
Introducing the electronic Hamiltonian into Eq. (1.2.4), one may solve the TISE for a
given nuclear configuration R
ˆHeψn (r; R) = En(R)ψn (r; R)
(1.2.11)
Solution of this particular electronic Schrödinger equation, Eq. (1.2.11), yields the so-
called adiabatic electronic states. The electronic wavefunction ψn(r; R) associated with
the nth adiabatic electronic state is seen to depend only parametrically (indicated by
“;”) on the nuclear coordinates, R, with corresponding electronic energy En(R). Thus,
the electronic energy is a function of nuclear coordinates and gives rise to the concept of
potential energy surfaces, as we shall see shortly.

1.3. Potential Energy Surfaces
From the preceding section, it is evident that the potential energy surface (PES) plays
a key role in molecular reaction dynamics. After all, a chemical reaction is a dynamical

process and as such the fate of a chemical system is determined by the forces operating
within the system, i.e. F = −dV (R)/dR, describing the interactions between all particles

1.3.1. Barriers and Minimum Energy Path
As stated previously, the potential energy surface represents the total electronic energy
with the electrons in some state α (plus nuclear-nuclear coulombic interaction energy),
as a function of internuclear coordinates and thus, dictates the dynamics of the isolated
chemical system. The important features of the potential surface are, in general, associ-
ated with the various local/global extrema and saddle points located on the surface. Of
particular interest for chemical reaction is the presence of saddle points, stationary points
that locally are minima in all directions except one, in which it is a local maximum (first-
order saddle point). Mathematically, we say that the Hessian determinant evaluated at
the stationary point is indefinite, implying that the Hessian matrix has both positive and
negative eigenvalues.
A saddle point along a reaction path connecting reactant minima and product minima,
can be interpreted as representing an energy barrier which must be surmounted in order
for reaction to take place. To illustrate, Fig. 1.3.1 depicts the potential energy curve
along the minimum energy path (reaction coordinate) for an endothermic bimolecular
reaction. Here, the classical barrier height, Ecl, is the difference in electronic energy at the
saddle point and the electronic energy of the reactants. The effective barrier height, E0,
accounting for the vibrational zero-point energies at the saddle point, represented by an
unstable activated complex (see section 2.5.1), and the reactants, are often slightly lower
than the classical barrier height. For more complex reactions, there may be several saddle
points present along the reaction coordinate with interspersed local minima, representing
(meta)stable intermediates. However, here we mainly focus on direct reactions with a
single saddle point.
The minimum energy path (MEP) referred to above, is the path along which the
gradient of the potential is parallel, ∇V = (∇V · −→n ) −→n , where −→n is the unit vector
tangent to the MEP. In other words, it is the path of lowest possible energy connecting
reactants and products going through the saddle point. Thus, the saddle point represents
the point of maximum energy along the reaction coordinate. Figure 1.3.2 depicts contour
plots of the hypothetical potential energy surfaces for the exchange reaction, H + H2 →
H2 + H, in the collinear configuration along with the minimum energy path (red line).
Notice how the two surfaces differ only in the position of the barrier (asterisk in figure).
In figure (a) the barrier is in the reactant channel (abscissa), a so-called “early barrier”,
whereas in figure (b) it is located in the product channel (ordinate), a “late” barrier.
The significance of classifying barriers as “early” or “late” is captured in the observation,
that translational energy tends to be more efficient at promoting early barrier reaction,
whereas vibrational energy promotes late barrier reaction, the so-called Polanyi rules [7].

2.2. Model Potential
In order to describe the behavior of a specific chemical reaction, a high-accuracy potential
surface is necessarily required, depending on the nature of the properties one wishes to
elucidate. In theory, high-accuracy potentials may be obtained from ab initio electronic
structure calculations but is rarely feasible in practice for most systems owing to the
tremendous computational cost. Alternatively, one may determine local regions of the
potential surface for the most important geometries using ab initio calculations and then
construct the global PES by fitting to various functional forms [13]. For high-dimensional
systems, e.g. bio-molecular systems, it may be necessary to describe the PES in terms of
classical force field models which group different atom types according to their interactions
or reactive force fields (e.g. reaxFF [14]) in case of bond breaking and formation.
However, in the present case we are not interested in any particular chemical system,
as such, but are more concerned with the topology of the surface and the effect it has
==PAGE:23==
2.2. Model Potential
19
on chemical reaction. To be more specific, we are interested in how the position of a
barrier along the reaction path affects the rate of chemical change of a general bimolecular
exchange reaction. As such, the accuracy with which the surface describes a given reaction
is not critical as long as the interactions are qualitative reasonable. What is important to
us, however, is the ability to adjust the shape of the surface and the position of barriers
along the reaction coordinate. On that basis, the model potential chosen was that of the
LEPS potential which is described in the following.
2.2.1. The Extended LEPS Potential
The potential energy surfaces employed in the simulations of the triatomic systems were
constructed using the semi-empirical extended LEPS potential (London-Eyring-Polanyi-
Sato). From a historical point-of-view, the LEPS potential is one of the earliest surfaces
used to describe the hydrogen exchange reaction. It is based on the well-known Valence
Bond theory and was conceptualized through the early work of Heitler and London in
1927 [15]. In their treatment of the H2 molecule, using the variational theorem, Heitler
and London were able to obtain singlet and triplet state energies of H2. In subsequent
work, London obtained an expression for the lowest adiabatic potential energy surface of
H3, in terms of Coulomb and exchange integrals between the pair of atoms. The formula
by London was the basis for Eyring and Polanyi’s semi-empirical LEP potential [16], in
which the sum of Coulomb and exchange integrals are expressed in terms of a Morse
potential. One of the major issues with the LEP potential is that it predicts a potential
energy well for the symmetric H-H-H configuration, when ab initio calculations predicts
a barrier of ~0.4 eV [9]. In 1955, Sato [17] introduced a new parameter S to account
for the neglect of the orbital overlap integral in previous models as well as including an
approximate form of the H2 triplet state. The Sato parameter S is simply applied as
a factor multiplying the original LEP equation and these modifications lead to a LEPS
surface with a symmetric barrier, as expected. In the extended LEPS model three Sato
parameters are introduced, one for each diatom, and takes the form

2.2.2. LEPS Parameters
Since we are interested in investigating how the position of the barrier affects the reaction
dynamics, it is necessary that the topology of the potential surface can be modified in a
consistent manner. The LEPS potential affords us some degree of control over the surface
and location of barriers via two main avenues. One could adjust the various spectroscopic
constants that enter the LEPS function or adjust the Sato parameters until the surface
takes on the desired shape. Either way, it is important to emphasize that, in doing so,
one runs the risk of ending up with a surface describing a hypothetical reaction. This
is particularly true, when discussing early and late barriers in connection with a specific
reaction. For example, in a symmetric exchange reaction by shifting the barrier location
into the reactant or product channel, the barrier is now located asymmetrically and is
no longer appropriate to the reaction. Nevertheless, we may still gain some qualitative
insight into to the topics discussed in the text.

3. Numerical Methods
In this chapter we discuss the various numerical methods employed in the propagation
of the wave packet. Implementation of the algorithms was done in Matlab and while
perhaps not an obvious choice for such a computationally demanding task, nonetheless
offers an integrated scripting environment with well-optimized libraries. Matlab code
for the construction of grids as well as the split-operator scheme was adapted from work
previously done by Ph.D. Alexandre Paolo Voute and was subsequently heavily optimized
for the work carried out in the present project.
In order to solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, we have to resolve to nu-
merical methods. The first step involves discretization of the spatial representation of
the otherwise continuous wavefunction.
Many such schemes have been developed [6,
21], here we employ a grid-based approach related to the Fourier method which is the
topic of the first section. The second step, is the propagation of the initial wavefunction
|Ψ(t0)⟩, which amounts to evaluation of the time-evolution operator ˆU = e−i ˆHt/ℏoperat-
ing on |Ψ(t0)⟩. Again, several propagation algorithms have been developed [22] but the
so-called Split-Operator method combines particularly well with the Fourier method and
is discussed in the second section. We then turn to some of the issues encountered in
employing grid-representations and how to handle these difficulties by a combined flux
operator/complex absorbing potential approach. In the last section of the chapter, we
briefly examine some of the potential sources of error and the stability of the propagation
methods described in the following.

As mentioned earlier, the kinetic

In summary, the full time-step ∆t propagation is a multi-step process:
• Multiply the wavefunction in discrete coordinate space by, e−i ˆV ∆t/2ℏ
• Fourier transform to discrete momentum space, Ψ(R, t) →˜Ψ (p, t)
• Multiply the wavefunction in discrete momentum space by, e−i ˆT∆t/ℏ
• Inverse Fourier transform to discrete coordinate space, ˜Ψ(p, t) →Ψ (R, t)
• Multiply the wavefunction in discrete coordinate space by, e−i ˆV ∆t/2ℏ
These steps are then repeated iteratively until the desired propagation time is reached.
3.3. Complex Absorbing Potentials and Flux Analysis
In order to evaluate the reaction probabilities in Eq. (1.4.10) the wave packet must be
propagated a sufficient length of time such that no amplitude remains in the interac-
tion region. However, within this time the scattered wave packet may become highly
delocalized in coordinate space when fast moving components of the wave packet travel
long distances. The spatial delocalization quickly becomes a problem in the asymptotic
regions where the wave packet is reflected at the grid boundaries and re-enters the inter-
action zone due to the periodic nature of the Fourier-grid. An obvious solution to the
problem would be to introduce a longer grid to accommodate the spread of the scattered
wave packet. However, this comes at the cost of computational expense and the required
grid length may be so great that it is becomes computationally untenable as illustrated
in Fig. 3.3.1.

deemed that a propagation time-step of ∆t = 0.025 fs and a grid size of 1024 × 512 pro-

4.3. Reaction Probabilities and Barrier Position
One of our objectives is to investigate the influence of barrier position on the cumulative
reaction probabilities and see how well they agree with the Polanyi rules. As mentioned
earlier, the wave packets were propagated with initial translational energies over the
range Etr = [0, 2Ecl] for each barrier type. This allows us to express the transmission
probability as a function of relative translational energy.
We start by examining the reaction probabilities for the simplest bimolecular reaction,
Reaction I: H + H2 →H2 + H, as it provides a good point of reference for comparison.
The reaction probabilities as a function of relative translational energy Etr is shown in
Fig. 4.3.1, for the initial wave packet in (a) the vibrational ground-state and (b) the first
excited state. Figure (a) depicts the graphs corresponding to the reaction probability
for an early barrier (blue graph), a late barrier (red graph) along with the probability
of a Gaussian (yellow graph) having components with energy greater than the effective
potential, E0. As discussed previously, the effective barrier is just the classical barrier
height corrected for the difference in zero-point energies at the saddle point and reactant,
and is indicated by the dash-dotted line. The potential surfaces associated with the two
barrier types are provided in Fig. 4.3.2, for easy reference.

Examining 4.3.1 (a) with the wave packet in the vibrational ground-state, the over-all
picture is clear; the early barrier facilitates reaction to a much greater extend compared
to the late barrier. However, reaction probabilities for the two barriers follow each other
closely (and are non-zero) for translational energies below the effective barrier, Etr < E0,
a clear indication of significant tunneling offered by the light hydrogen atoms. At these
low initial translational energies there is seemingly no discrimination between barrier type
and translational energy is equally effective in crossing an early barrier and a late barrier
in contrast to the Polanyi rules. This agreement starts to deviate around Etr = 0.42 eV,
as a fraction of the plane wave-components in the translation wave packet (Gaussian)
carries energies greater than that of the effective barrier, as shown by the yellow graph.
Interestingly, the reaction probability never reaches unity for energies past the effective
barrier as one would expect from a classical point-of-view. In fact, oscillatory behavior is
observed with a decreasing transmission probability, P(Etr > E0) < 1. Such oscillations
are not an uncommon occurrence in bimolecular reactions and are often attributed to
quantum resonance phenomena characteristic of so-called metastable states as discussed
in [27]. Nonetheless, the continued decrease in transmission is surprising and is most
likely a result of the “sharp turn” the wave packet must undergo in crossing the barrier,
as can be seen from the potential surface in Fig. 4.3.2. With increase in translational
energy, less time is afforded for the excess energy to re-distribute among the various
accessible degrees of freedom and the wave packet reflects off of the potential wall and
re-crosses back into the reactant channel, reducing transmission.
Turning now to the reaction probabilities associated with the vibrational excited state
in Fig. 4.3.1 (b). First, it is important to mention that the total energy E = Etr + Ev
is considerably larger with Ev=1 ∼0.78 eV as seen in Table 4.1.
Thus, the energy
in the vibrational degree of freedom of the reactant diatom H2 alone is larger than
the effective barrier E0 ∼0.49 eV. Nonetheless, it is clear that the excitation in the
vibrational coordinate facilitates late-barrier reaction to a much greater extend, even at
very low initial translational energies. Which is not so surprising given that the excess
energy is primarily associated with the vibrational coordinate and lies almost parallel
to the reaction coordinate over the late barrier in the product channel.
Conversely,
there is no early barrier reaction at low initial translational energies. Again, this makes
sense since excess vibrational energy offers little in terms of surmounting a barrier lying
predominantly along the translation coordinate in the reactant channel. However, as the
initial translational energy increases Etr > 0.2 eV transmission increases - well before the
effective barrier. Presumably due to tunneling effects becoming significant and relaxation
of vibrational energy redistributing into the translational degree of freedom. As Etr →Ecl
transmission for the two barrier types attain similar values and the increase in Etr start
to impede reaction analogues to the ground-state wave packet.
Despite deviations from the Polanyi rules at low initial values of Etr, owing to the
quantum mechanical effect of tunneling, the main points of the Polanyi rules still stand;
vibrational energy tends to be more efficient in promoting late barrier reactions and
translational energy promotes early barrier reaction which redistributes into vibrational
degrees of freedom in the product. An example of the latter is clearly evident in the fully
propagated wave packet for Reaction II shown in the preceding section, Fig. 4.2.2 (f).

4.4. Reaction Rate Constants
In this section, we compare the rate constants as evaluated by classical Transition State
theory and those evaluated based on the reaction probabilities calculated from the pre-
ceding quantum mechanical treatment.
Before presenting the results, its worthwhile to take a brief moment and discuss what
one might expect from such a comparison. Firstly, with the Transition State theory as
applied in this work where no quantum mechanical corrects have been made, it would
be reasonable to expect some deviations in the calculated rate constants. Particularly,
in the lower energy regime due to the quantum mechanical effect of tunneling, which
we have already seen examples of, and anticipate that tunneling effects will be most
pronounced for reactions involving light atoms. Thus, it is sensible to expect that TST
will severely underestimate the value of the rate constant when tunneling is a factor.
Secondly, TST assumes no barrier re-crossing takes place and again, this is at odds with
the results reported earlier where re-crossing, presumably due to reflections, appeared to
be a significant factor in the higher energy regime. Furthermore, in TST it is assumed
that the trajectory follows the reaction coordinate and thus, that the rate constant can be
evaluated from an normal mode analysis at a single point, the saddle point. Yet, we have
seen that the trajectory is not, in any way, confined solely to the reaction coordinate and
the wave packet “shortcuts” through higher-lying barriers. These considerations suggests
that TST either underestimates or overestimates the value of the exact rate constant or,
by chance, the two competing effects could possibly cancel each other out.
Let us again start by examining the results for Reaction I: H +H2. The calculated TST
and early/late barrier QM rate constants as a function of temperature are shown in Fig.
4.4.1 and below it in Table 4.3 values of the three rate constants at different temperatures
are highlighted. As expected, Transition State theory underestimates the rate constants
by a factor of ∼102 −104 at lower temperatures. Likely due to the aforementioned
tunneling effect which we saw, in the preceding section, was significant at lower collision
energies. Interestingly, kTST < kearly
QM for all temperatures considered with respect to the
early barrier, despite the occurrence of reflections at higher values of Etr which resulted
in a decreasing reaction probability. One would expect that the TST assumption of “no
recrossing” would mean that kTST approach and possibly surpass that of kQM at higher
temperatures. To investigate this a little further, Fig. 4.4.2 depicts a series of plots of
the integrand in kQM(T) as a function of energy, i.e.

Conclusion
60
Conclusion
When Polanyi and coworkers first published their paper on the dynamics of bimolecular
reactions, more than 50 years ago, the theoretical work was based on classical equations
of motion. In recent years, experiments and theoretical results obtained from quantum
reaction dynamics indicate that the Polanyi rules break-down for some energy-regimes.
The results obtained in the present computational study corroborates these findings and
suggests that the main contribution to this discrepancy is due to quantum mechanical
effects, in particular, tunneling at low collision energies. While this disagreement may
have important consequences for some low-energy applications it is safe to say that, for
the hypothetical reactive systems considered in this work, the discrepancy did not affect
the overall reaction probabilities to a significant degree. The main point being made is
the Polanyi rules still provide a very good guide as to how energy in the various degrees
of freedom may affect reactivity.
From a more general perspective, calculations of the cumulative reaction probabilities
displayed clear quantum mechanical effects. Tunneling was significant at low collision
energies for light-atom reactions and quantum reflections were observed for most reactions
at high energies. Step-like or oscillatory behavior was also observed often attributed to
resonance phenomena in the literature.
The reaction rate constants obtained from the cumulative reaction probabilities and
results based on Transition State theory were in remarkable agreement, especially at
intermediate temperatures, despite the relative simplicity of TST. While the reactions
considered might be highly hypothetical, the main conclusions still stand: the Polanyi
rules break-down at low collision energies when tunneling is significant and Transition
State theory offers a simple and computationally cheap method of estimating reaction
rates at intermediate temperatures.

A. Jacobi Coordinates and the Kinetic Energy Operator
For an arbitrary configuration of N atoms there is a total of 3N degrees of freedom. If
the configuration is restricted to a plane, the total degrees of freedom is reduced to 2N.
In the collinear case, the configuration of atoms is further confined to lie along a line
segment and we are left with a total of N degrees of freedom. Therefore, the atomic
positions of the collinear arrangement of the A + BC →AB + C reaction with respect to
some fixed coordinate system can be written
RA = xA, RB = xB, RC = xC
(A.1)
That is, there is a total of 3 degrees of freedom to be considered. Evidently, consider-

a significant reduction in dimensionality, however, this can be further reduced by sepa-

B. Normal Mode Analysis
Normal mode analysis (NMA) is a method often used to describe the collective motion
of atoms within a molecule and as such, NMA can provide information about accessible
