Abstract
This thesis explores the mathematical modelling of project networks, focusing on phase-
type distributions as a stochastic approach to project modelling. The research investigates
how these distributions compare to traditional methods, such as the Successive Principle
and the Critical Path Method, in describing project durations and to some degree costs.
The study begins with a theoretical foundation covering phase-type distributions, their
properties, and their utility in modeling. It then introduces process networks and macro
chains, emphasizing their role in representing task dependencies and transitions. Tradi-
tional approaches like PERT and the Successive Principle are presented to provide context
for this development and enable comparability.
Central to this thesis is an algorithm designed to model task concurrency and transitions,
integrating principles of graph and set theory. The algorithm’s performance and sensitivity
are analyzed through simulations and case studies, including a detailed exploration of a
telecom network example. These practical implementations demonstrate the flexibility
and accuracy of the phase-type approach.
Finally, the study examines potential extensions of the methodology to multivariate models
incorporating cost and time, as well as the algorithm’s scalability. A discussion of the
limitations and possible avenues for future research concludes the thesis.

Introduction
The world is in a state of constant change, driven significantly by projects of various scales.
According to the Project Management Institute, organizations waste an average of 11.4%
of investment due to poor project performance [13]. Before these projects can commence,
they require meticulous planning and deliberation. It is crucial to estimate the duration
and cost, along with the certainty of the estimations. For well-defined projects, these
estimates can be made. Well-defined project plans can be modelled using deterministic
and stochastic methods. The choice of method can impact estimated project durations
and costs.
This thesis explores the mathematical modeling of project networks, focusing on phase-
type distributions as a stochastic approach. Stochastic modeling of project networks is
not a new concept. This research investigates how phase-type distributions compare to
traditional stochastic methods, such as the Successive Principle, Project Evaluation and
Review Technique, and the Critical Path Method, in describing project durations and, to
some extent, costs.
The Critical Path Method (CPM), developed by Morgan R. Walker and James E. Kelley
Jr. in the late 1950s, is based on the idea that the project duration is determined by the
path with the longest duration through the project network [27]. CPM is often used in
conjunction with other methods such as the Project Evaluation and Review Technique
(PERT), which was developed by the US Navy in the 1950s to model project completion
times[22]. PERT employs three time estimates for each task to account for uncertainty
and assumes that the time of a task follows a Beta distribution.
Another Stochastic modelling approach, often used alongside the CPM, is the Successive
Principle, which is used mainly in Northern Europe. The Successive Principle (SP), was
developed primarily by Steen Lichtenberg, to estimate the total duration or cost of a
project [17, 18]. This approach is iterative, reducing uncertainty progressively. It assumes
that task durations or costs can be described by Erlang distributions, leading to the idea
that the total duration or cost of a project can be described by a phase-type distribution.
D. Meisch’s PhD thesis explores the use of phase-type distributions in modeling project
durations and costs [21]. A well-known inaccuracy of using CPM is the mishandling of
merge events and the Merge Event Bias (MEB) [10]. Several solutions already exist to
handle this problem either partly or completely [11, 12, 17].
By modeling projects using phase-type distributions, the MEB is accounted for. However,
some projects are not easily modeled using phase-type distributions. While D. Meisch
shows an approach to how even these complex networks can be modeled, it requires a
good understanding of phase-type distributions.
The main result of this thesis is the
ability to model these complex networks by way of an algorithm that can find the phase-
type distribution of the total duration of a project.
The thesis begins with a theoretical foundation covering phase-type distributions, their
properties, and their utility in modeling. This foundation supplies the reader with the
needed knowledge for later. Process networks and macro chains are the introduced, em-
phasizing their role in representing task dependencies and transitions. After introducing
the networks, traditional approaches like PERT and the Successive Principle are presented.
The purpose, of introducing these, is to provide context and enable comparability.

Modelling with phase-type distributions is then explored, presenting several cases to show
how the total duration of a project can be described by a phase-type distribution and how
the distribution of the total duration can be determined. The cases start with some simple
and fundamental networks and then move on to more complex networks. The focus will be
on the duration rather than other variables such as cost, since the dependency structures
of costs is rarely complex. For the more complex cases where the phase-type distribution
of the total duration is not easily found, an approach termed here as the macro chain
approach is shown in detail. The macro chain approach is a systematic way of finding the
phase-type distribution of the total duration of a project.
The main outcome of thesis is an algorithm. The algorithm determines task concurrency
and transitions for a given project, integrating principles of graph and set theory. The
algorithm’s performance and sensitivity are analyzed through simulations and case stud-
ies, including a detailed exploration of an example concerning a telecom project. These
practical implementations demonstrate the flexibility and accuracy of the phase-type ap-
proach.
To conclude the thesis an example of a multivariate model of a network is presented. The
example shows how the algorithm in tandem with multivariate phase-type distributions
can be used to model a project with both time and cost as variables. The telecom example
and multivariate example are used as a jumping point for a discussion of the limitations
of the method and possible avenues for future research.

The Continuous phase-type distribution (PH)
For an irreducible Markov Jump Process (MJP), X(t), with a finite state space E =
{1, 2, ..., n, n+1}, where state n+1 is absorbing, a state i is said to be recurrent if P(ηi <
∞) = 1, where ηi is the first time the state i is returned to, i.e. ηi = inf{m ≥1|Xm = i}. If
a state is not recurrent it is transient and P(ηi < ∞) < 1 meaning that there is probability
of absorption before state i is revisited.

The distribution of the absorption time τ = inf{t ≥0|X(t) = n + 1} follows a phase-type
distribution and can be given a phase-type (PH) representation (α, T) with the initial
distribution α and the sub-intensity matrix T which is also known as the sub-generator
matrix. The initial distribution α is the probability of starting in each transient state and
is α = (α1, α2, ..., αn) where 0 ≤αi ≤1. If the probability of entering the absorbing state
from the start is 0 then the atom at 0 is said to be of size zero else the atom at zero will
be of size 1 −αe = αn+1, where e is the column vector of ones.
Given the representation of a PH distribution, (α, T), the probability density function
f(t) is and the cumulative distribution F(t) are known, and a proof of this is given in
Bladt and Nielsen (2017) [4]. The probability density function f(t) of the absorption time
τ is given by
f(t) = α exp(Tt)t,

yielding t = −Te.
And the cumulative distribution F(t) of the absorption time τ is given by
F(t) = P(τ ≤t) = 1 −α exp(Tt)e.
A property of the class of PH distributions is that it is closed under both finite convolutions
and finite mixtures [23, 2].

2.4
Properties of Phase-types (PH)
Theorem 3.1.11 and Corollary 3.1.12 in Bladt and Nielsen (2017)[4] states that the sub-
intensity matrix T of a PH is invertible if and only if the first n states of the underlying
MJP, {Xt}t≥0, with state space E = {1, 2, ..., n, n + 1}, are transient.
A PH representation is said to be irreducible if the probability of visiting any transient
state from the initial distribution is positive. So for representation PH(α, T), with n
transient states, to be irreducible it must be that (α exp(Tx))i > 0 for i = 1, .., n, and all
x > 0. It can also be stated that a PH representation is irreducible if and only if T + tα
is an intensity matrix of an irreducible MJP (Lemma 5.4.1 [4]).

An important property of the class of PH’s is that it is closed for order statistics [4].
2.5.1
Minimum of PH distributions
The following argument provides a simple proof for the minimum of two independent
random variables with phase-type distributions.
Let X1 and X2 be two independent
phase-type distributed random variables with the initial distributions α1 and α2 and sub-
generator matrices T1 and T2, where the orders of X1 and X2 are m and n respectively.
Let Y = min(X1, X2), then the survival function of Y is given by
P(Y > y) = P(X1 > y, X2 > y)
= P(X1 > y)P(X2 > y)
= (1 −FX1(y))(1 −FX2(y))
= (α1 exp(T1y)em) · (α2 exp(T2y)en)
= (α1 exp(T1y)em) ⊗(α2 exp(T2y)en)
= (α1 ⊗α2) · (exp(T1y)em ⊗exp(T2y)en)
= (α1 ⊗α2) · (exp(T1y) ⊗exp(T2y)) · (em ⊗en)
= (α1 ⊗α2) · exp((T1 ⊕T2)y) · emn
= γ · exp((L)y)emn,
where γ = α1⊗α2 and L = T1⊕T2. Hereby the minimum of two independent phase-type
distributed random variables is also a phase-type distribution random variable with the
representation (γ, L).

2.12
Definitions of Networks and Macro Chains
This section introduces key terminology and foundational concepts from project planning
and process networks, ensuring clarity and consistency throughout the thesis.
A network is understood as a collection of tasks or activities that must be performed
in a specific order. Mathematically, it can be represented as a directed graph denoted
by G = (V, E), where V represents the set of nodes (vertices), corresponding to tasks or
activities, and E represents the set of edges, denoted as directed pairs (A, B), indicating a
dependency where Task A must be completed before Task B can commence. Dependencies
are critical for modeling project workflows, as they dictate the permissible sequence of task
execution. A path is a sequence of tasks where each task is a predecessor of the next task
in the sequence. The initial vertex in a path must be a task with no predecessors, and the
final vertex must be a task with no successors.
The networks considered in this thesis are Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs).
This is
essential because the total duration of a project corresponds to the maximum duration
across all paths in the network. Cyclic dependencies would lead to possible infinite loops,
making it impossible to compute a finite project duration.
The focus of this thesis is primarily on modeling the duration of tasks and the overall
project duration, considering these durations as random variables. Although the primary
focus is on time, the methodology can be extended to other measurable variables, such as
costs. The reason for focusing on task durations rather than costs is that the dependency
structures of cost in most cases are simpler, where the total cost is just the sum of the
costs of the tasks. However, when cost and duration are considered together, the cost
becomes more interesting as delays in tasks can lead to increased costs.
Beyond the network structure, the thesis also introduces the concepts of macro states and
macro chains to simplify the analysis of complex project networks. A macro state rep-
resents a set of states that are logically or structurally related. These could correspond to
states representing different stages within the same task or states representing concurrent
execution of multiple tasks. In the case of two tasks running alongside one another the
macro state encompasses all possible combinations of the states of the two tasks.
A macro chain is a sequence of macro states, capturing task dependencies and transitions
between macro states. For a network each macro chain is unique, and represents a specific
sequence of events, meaning tasks finishing and beginning. Every macro chain for a given
network will share the same initial macro state, but the final macro state will depend
on the specific sequence of tasks in the chain. In later sections when the macro chains
come into play, an approach called the macro chain approach is introduced. The macro
chain approach is understood as the analysis of macro chains and macro states made to
construct a phase-type representation. The macro states and chains can be put together
to form a network of macro states where the chains are the paths through the network.
The macro states are not Markovian in general only the individual states are Markovian.
Transitions between macro states occur when a task is completed and any new tasks,
which depend on the completed task, are initiated. These transitions must be found and
analyzed to construct the sub-intensity matrix, which governs the stochastic behavior of
the projects total duration.
When analyzing the phase-type (PH) distribution of project durations, macro states and
macro chains play a crucial role.
Suppose Task A has a duration described by a PH
distribution with representation (αA, TA), and Task B also follows a PH distribution with
22
Mathematical Modelling of Process Networks the Phase Type Case
==PAGE:31==
representation (αB, TB). Then, TA and TB represents the intensity matrices for the macro
state where Task A and Task B are occurring individually. If Tasks A and B are executed
concurrently, the macro state intensity matrix becomes TA ⊕TB. If the states of Task A
and Task B are entered with the probabilities αA and αB, respectively, the states, in the
macro state of (A, B), are entered with the probabilities αA ⊗αB.
In summary, a project network can be represented as a DAG, where tasks are nodes and
dependencies are directed edges. The project duration is the maximum duration across
all paths in the network. The concepts of macro states and macro chains simplify the
analysis of complex project networks, enabling the construction of tractable and accurate
stochastic models for project durations.

3.1
Introduction
This chapter will introduce the process of finding the phase-type distributions of network
durations. The chapter will start by introducing the Critical Path Method (CPM), Project
Evaluation and Review Technique (PERT), and the Successive Principle (SP) as methods
for planning and managing projects.
The chapter will then introduce how phase-type
distributions can be used to model the duration of tasks in a project. The chapter will
introduce the concept of lead times. The chapter will conclude with a concrete example of
how the phase-type distributions can be used to model the duration of tasks in a project
network.
When planning a project, several factors are important.
The planners often need to
account for price, time, and other resources. All these will, in most cases, depend somewhat
on each other.
Furthermore, an important part of the planning is the uncertainty of
everything.
Where a project with a low price and time estimate may at first sound
attractive, it would likely lose some of the appeal if it were discovered that the uncertainty
of these estimates was very large. One management tool that has been developed to handle
these issues is the Successive Principle by Lichtenberg. This is a method that is primarily
used in Scandinavia. It has its fundamental techniques from subjective statistics, which
helps it yield a more realistic estimate of the project. The main elements of the Successive
Principle are the triple estimates, a medium-sized heterogeneous analysis group to produce
the estimates, and an iterative process in which the tasks that have been identified are
further broken down into smaller tasks based on which tasks have the largest uncertainty
and therefore the biggest decrease potential. Lichtenberg uses the Erlang distribution as
the underlying distribution of the duration and cost of each task and the sum of these
tasks’ duration as the total project time. By having a sum of random variables, the central
limit theorem can be applied.
The Successive Principle (SP) shares concepts with the Project Evaluation and Review
Technique (PERT) method, and both of these methods are based on the idea that each
task can be represented by a random variable.
These tasks and their flow can then
be represented as process networks.
These networks and their variables, such as task
durations, can in some cases be modeled by phase-type distributions that describe each
task/variable and the combination of these distributions is a phase-type distribution as
well. The assumption that task costs or durations follow phase-type distributions limits
the types of distributions that can be used to represent task resources. For example, the
beta distribution often used in PERT is not a phase-type distribution. In his Ph.D. thesis
David Meisch worked on representing the tasks’ durations as phase-type distributions and
the entire project duration as a phase-type distribution[21]. This modeling included lead
times and using Multivariate Phase-type Distributions (MPH*) as defined by Kulkarni[14].

3.2.1
Critical Path Method (CPM)
In many cases, PERT and SP rely on the Critical Path Method (CPM) to determine the
total duration of the project. The CPM focuses on finding the Critical Path (CP), which
is the path through the network that has the longest duration. The duration of a path
is found by summing the expected durations of each task on the path. It is also often
described as the path with no float/slack. Float/slack is the time a task can be delayed
without delaying the project. Any task on the critical path is considered a critical task.
The total duration of the project is then the sum of the durations of the critical tasks.
When Kelley and Walker in [27] introduced the CPM, it had two versions, one for deter-
ministic schedules and one for stochastic schedules. The deterministic version is the one
most commonly used today. For the stochastic version, the tasks’ durations has solutions
reliant on integrals, and Kelley and Walker suggest an approximation with histograms,
alternatively Monte Carlo simulations are often employed to determine the critical path.
Another approach to determine the CP is through a subjective evaluation of which tasks
are critical to the completion of a project.
This thesis uses an interpretation of the CPM that is deterministic in its choice of path
based on the expected duration of the tasks and does not account for the uncertainty of
the tasks when deciding the CP. This interpretation of CPM is not new; Malcolm et al.
(1959) [20] employed the same method of finding the critical path of a project by selecting
the path with the highest total of mean durations.
While CPM typically focuses on one path in a network, there are versions that also consider
near-critical paths. These paths can be identified using the criticality index (CI). The CI
describes the probability that a task will be critical, i.e., that the task belongs to the
critical path. The CI can be estimated subjectively or by Monte Carlo simulations. If
each path in the network has a given probability of being the critical path, the CI of a task
is the sum of the probabilities of the paths that contain the task. By also observing the
near-critical paths, the Merge Event Bias (MEB) can be somewhat included in the final
estimates. The criticality index, combined with the variance of a task, yields the Priority
Figure (P), which is a way to find how big a contribution a task’s variance has to the total
variance.

The Successive Principle (SP) was developed by Lichtenberg in the 1970s and has been
continually refined since then. The main focus of SP is cost and scheduling. Like PERT,
this method is based on the idea that each task in a network has a resource which can

An important aspect of the Successive Principle (SP) is the iterative process in which
identified tasks are further broken down into smaller tasks based on the sub-tasks with
the largest uncertainty, i.e., the biggest priority figure and therefore the greatest potential

effectively use subjective statistical properties, the defined tasks must be independent.
The independence must also be true for any set of subtask which are made tasks. This
process aims to minimize epistemic uncertainty —uncertainty due to lack of precision or
knowledge in the modeling — so that the remaining uncertainty is mostly aleatoric, which
is the inherent uncertainty of what is being modeled.

allowed uncertainty. The allowed uncertainty is the uncertainty that is acceptable for the
project.

3.2.5
Merge Event Bias (MEB)
Merge Event Bias (MEB) refers to the systematic delay introduced in project durations
when tasks are interdependent and must converge at a merge point. The Critical Path
Method (CPM), which underpins both PERT and SP, often fails to capture these delays
accurately, resulting in underestimation of the expected total project duration (MacCrim-
mon and Ryavec, 1964)[19]. This would make sense as the total duration will either be
the sum of durations on the critical path or a path which has been realized with a longer
duration than the critical path. MacCrimmon and Ryavec (1964) state that if the task
durations are symmetric and with compact support the standard deviation of the total
project duration is overestimated by the CPM. As the distributions of task durations in
this thesis will be right skewed we can not comment on how the standard deviation is
affected by the CP approximation.
When multiple tasks converge at a single event, the total duration is not merely the sum
of mean task durations, but is affected by the uncertainty of the duration of each merging
task. At such merge points, delays arise because a subsequent task cannot proceed until all
preceding tasks have been completed. Mathematically, the expected duration at a merge
event can be expressed as:

3.3
Successive Principle as Phase-type
For the Successive Principle Lichtenberg uses Erlang distributions to describe the length
or cost of tasks. The Erlang distribution is, as mentioned earlier, a special case of a phase-
type (PH) distribution. What D. Meisch did was use phase-type distributions to describe
the length of the tasks and hereby the length of an entire project. By doing this exact
results based on underlying assumptions can be obtained and the CLT can be avoided.
It also allows for the use of more general distributions than just Erlang distributions. By
using phase-type distributions the modelling can maintain any skewness, as in longer right
probability density tails, that might be present in the data.
3.3.1
Exact PH Distribution of Total Duration
If the PH distribution of the total duration is straight forward, meaning that the total
duration can be expressed as sums and maximums of the task durations in a way, so
that no task duration is repeated, the exact PH distribution can easily be constructed.
In this straight forward scenario the CP-PH approach, described in 3.2.1, would also be
exact. In contrast, if the PH distribution, of the total duration, is not straight forward
to construct, the PH distribution of the total duration of the project can be constructed
by the macro chain approach, which is the approach exemplified in the comming Section
3.10. The macro chain approach is based on analyzing the network to find macro states
and transitions between those.
Having found the macro states and transitions a PH
representation of the total duration can be constructed. The macro chain approach is able
to construct the true PH distribution of the total duration of the project in cases where
the CP-PH and CP approaches are not able to. The approach can however easily become
computational expensive compared to Monte Carlo (MC) and CP.

3.9
Lead Times
Another important concept to have in the toolbox when working with process networks is
the concept of lead times. Lead time refers to the period during which a successor task can
run simultaneously with its predecessor. Lead time is often used in project management
to describe partial overlaps of tasks. Lead time can mainly be understood in two ways.
In this section, we will explore the two interpretations of lead time and discuss how it can
be modeled in project networks. We will later use this concept of lead time in the case
study presented in Chapter 6.
The first interpretation is that lead time represents the portion of a successor task that
can start independently of the predecessor.
In this case, we can think of the task as
being composed of subtasks: subtasks that can run independently of the predecessor and
subtasks that cannot. We would assume that the lead time is the duration required to
complete the subtasks that can run independently of the predecessor. In a simple case,
this would translate to moving from a structure like Figure 3.5a to a more realistic model
as shown in Figure 3.5b.

The other version of lead time refers to when an entire successor task depends only on
the partial completion of the predecessor task.
This is the version Meisch (2014)[21]

two subtasks: the part needed for the successor to start and what Meisch defines as
the alternative lead time, which is the part of the predecessor that the successor is not
concerned with.
In any interpretation of lead time, it should be possible to split one task into two subtasks:
one independent and one dependent. There are a few considerations to keep in mind when
splitting tasks.
Firstly, the method for choosing or modifying the distributions that describe the new
task durations depends on the available information. In the Successive Principle (SP),
a negative triple estimate is often provided for the lead time. If a task has an Erlang

From the matrix above it can be seen that the memoryless property of the exponential
distribution is used since the Erlang distribution is composed of k exponential distributions
and so the individual steps are memoryless despite the entire distribution not having this
property.
When using an Erlang distribution to describe a task’s duration one interpretation of
the task is that the task consists of several subsequent sub-tasks. The sub-tasks are all
completed in a time which is exponentially distributed with some mean µ. With a shape
parameter k it would be k sub-tasks, which are completed in a time, which follows an
exponential distribution with mean µ.
Now, if the sub-tasks had durations with different expected values µi, this would result in
a mixture of Erlang distributions. As described in the earlier section on PH distributions
2.7, this is also known as a generalized Erlang distribution, which can also be described
as a PH distribution.
A different case could be that different alternatives of how to perform the tasks are con-
sidered with different probabilities and these alternatives each would complete the tasks
with different expected durations. Assuming the alternative solutions all have completion
times which are exponentially distributed with mean µi, this case could be modelled as task
duration which follows a hyperexponential distribution, which is also a PH distribution.

More Network Structures
To better understand the modelling of process networks with phase-type distributions,
it is meaningful to consider additional network structures. This chapter explores various
network structures and their corresponding Phase-type (PH) distributions. The PH dis-
tributions of these networks’ total duration will be derived. The primary objective is to
generalize and analyze the PH distribution of the total duration of a project based on the
network structure.
Previously, four different network structures with their corresponding PH distributions
have been defined.
The first structure is a simple sequential network, exemplified by
a network with two nodes and a single edge, as discussed in Section 3.4.
The second
structure is a parallel network, exemplified by a network with two nodes and no edges, as
described in Section 3.5. The third structure is a simple merge event network with three
nodes and two edges, as detailed in Section 3.6. The fourth structure is a network with
four nodes and three edges, as shown in Section 3.7 and illustrated in Figure 3.4. This
structure includes a merge event and warrants the macro chain approach to construct a
sub-intensity matrix representing the PH distribution of the total duration.

In this chapter, an algorithm for finding all macro states and transitions between them is
presented. Before introducing the algorithm, it is essential to present relevant concepts
from graph theory and set theory. These concepts - some of which, such has the basic
concept of graphs, were already introduced in section 2.12 - provide a foundational frame-
work for understanding the macro states of a network, as they share structural parallels
with the mathematical properties described in set and graph theory. By considering these
connections, we can apply established results to our algorithmic approach.
5.1
Graph Theory and Set Theory
In set theory, a directed acyclic graph (DAG) is referred to as a partially ordered set
(poset). A poset is a set of elements equipped with a binary relation that is reflexive,
antisymmetric, and transitive [25]. In the context of a network, the tasks represent the
elements of the poset, and the binary relation denotes the precedence relationship between
tasks.
Reflexivity means that every task is related to itself, ensuring that each task is included
in the poset. Antisymmetry ensures that if Task i precedes Task j, then Task j does not
precede Task i. The transitive property ensures that if Task i precedes Task j and Task
j precedes Task k, then Task i also precedes Task k, i.e.,
i →j
and
j →k
⇒
i →k.
The transitive property is crucial for defining the macro states of a network, as it establishes
the order in which tasks can be executed.
Set theory also introduces the concepts of chains and antichains. A chain is a set of tasks
where each task is a predecessor of the next task in the set, corresponding to the graph
theoretical concept of a path, as used in network theory and up to now in this thesis. An
antichain, on the other hand, is a set of tasks where no task is a predecessor of another
task within the same set. The set of macro states of a network corresponds to all possible
antichains. Every individual node in a network forms an antichain, ensuring that the set
of macro states will always include all tasks individually as macro states.
A covering of a poset is a set of chains that together cover the poset, which is equivalent
to a transitive reduction in graph theory. The transitive reduction of a graph is the graph
with the fewest possible edges that preserves the reachability relationships between nodes.
In the context of a network, the covering of a poset corresponds to the set of transitions
between macro states that together cover the network.
A node is said to cover another node if there is a directed edge between them and no other
node is simultaneously covered by the first node and covers the second node. This means
that the covering relation is minimal and direct, ensuring that the essential precedence
relationships are maintained without redundancy.
If all non-initial tasks have no atom at zero, the network can be reduced to the covering
of the poset equivalent to the transitive reduction of the graph. For example, consider
a network with three edges: A →B, B →C, and A →C. Here, the edge A →C is
64
Mathematical Modelling of Process Networks the Phase Type Case
==PAGE:73==
redundant as it is covered by the other two edges. In terms of tasks, if Task B can only
start after Task A is finished, and Task C can only start after Task B is finished, then
Task C can only start after Task A is finished. The last statement holds no additional
information and can be omitted. This principle can be applied to more complex networks
as well.
For the complexity analysis of the algorithm, we will assume that any given project/net-
work is a single, weakly connected network. This means that if we ignore the direction of
edges, there is a path between any two nodes. We never have strong connectivity, which is
when a path exists between any two nodes considering the direction of the edges, since this
is not possible for a DAG. This assumption of weak connectivity is reasonable for most
project networks, as they are typically designed to be completed in a single, cohesive flow.
If weak connectivity is not present the project would actually be multiple independent
projects, which could and should be analyzed separately.
The topological order of a network is a linear ordering of its vertices such that for every
directed edge A →B, node A comes before B in the ordering. The topological order
is essential for defining the macro states of a network, as it establishes the precedence
relationships between tasks. The topological order also ensures that the network can be
traversed in a systematic manner, moving from one task to the next based on the defined
order.
The width of a graph, denoted as w, represents the size of the largest layer, which is
equivalent to the size of the maximum antichain [26]. According to Dilworth’s theorem,
the width of a poset is equal to the minimum number of chains required to cover the poset.
In other words, the width of a network is equal to the minimum number of paths needed
to cover a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG).
The minimum number of chains required to cover a DAG is a well-studied problem, and
these chains can be identified in linear time. Although the number of chains in a DAG
varies based on its structure, the width consistently measures the largest antichain. Ac-
cording to C´aceres et al. [6], the width can be computed using parameterized algorithms,
which offer significant efficiency improvements, especially when the width w is small rel-
ative to the number of vertices. These methods facilitate more effective computation of
the width, serving as an important indicator of the algorithm’s complexity.
5.2
Properties of the Adjacency Matrix
An adjacency matrix, A, with elements aij, is a binary representation of a network, where
each entry indicates whether a connection exists between two tasks. If a vertex i has a
directed edge to vertex j, then entry aij is 1; otherwise, the entry is 0. Additionally, the
adjacency matrix is always square, and in this framework of process networks, its diagonal
is zero, as no task can be its own predecessor. For any adjacency matrix of a DAG, which
represents a network, the initial macro state is determined by identifying tasks with no
incoming edges, which correspond to columns with all zero entries. Similarly, the final
macro states are identified by tasks with no outgoing edges, corresponding to rows with
all zero entries.
In general, any node with a predecessor will have a non-zero column vector. The sum of
the column gives the number of direct predecessors. Likewise, any node with a successor
will have a non-zero row vector, which sums to the number of direct successors.
Since the networks under consideration are DAGs, the adjacency matrix will always be
upper triangular (or can be permuted into an upper triangular form). This structural
Mathematical Modelling of Process Networks the Phase Type Case
65
==PAGE:74==
property ensures that no task depends on tasks appearing later in the topological order.
When networks are represented as adjacency matrices, any permutation of the matrix
is a valid isomorphism. This is because any permutation will maintain the structure of
dependencies and therefore still represent the same network.
5.3
The Algorithm
In this section, we present an algorithm for identifying all macro states and transitions
between them in a network.
This algorithm, inspired by traversal strategies such as
breadth-first search (BFS) and depth-first search (DFS), operates on a network represented
by an adjacency matrix. The algorithm outputs two primary lists: one containing all macro
states and another capturing the transitions between these macro states. Each transition is
represented by the originating macro state and the resulting macro state reached through
task progression. Although a similar algorithm was not found in the literature study of
the author, it is possible that one exists that addresses the same problem.
The algorithm starts by identifying the initial macro state, containing tasks with no ingoing
connections. It then successively examines each macro state by removing each task in the
current macro state. For each task, the corresponding row and column are removed from
the adjacency matrix, effectively pruning the network. The resulting reduced adjacency
matrix is analyzed to identify the resulting macro state. If a new macro state is discovered,
it is appended to the macro state list. The transition is recorded regardless of the novelty
of the reached macro state. After having done this for each task in the current macro
state, the algorithm proceeds to the next macro state in the list. This process continues
until all macro states and transitions have been explored.
Key steps of the algorithm:
• Initialization: Define and initialize the structures to store macro states, transitions,
reduced adjacency matrices, and remaining tasks.
• Identify Macro States: Find tasks with no incoming edges in the adjacency matrix
to form macro states.
• Prune Tasks: Systematically remove active tasks from the current macro state and
adjacency matrix to discover macro states.
• Record Transitions: Maintain a record of transitions between macro states as
tasks have been removed.
• Terminates: Stops when all macro states and transitions are explored.
The following section describes in detail the steps of the algorithm.
5.3.1

1. Initialization of the data structures:
• Macro States (MS): Initialize a list to store macro states, starting with the
initial macro state. The initial macro state is the set of tasks with no ingoing
connections (zero-filled column vectors).
• Transitions (Transitions): Initialize an empty list to store transitions be-
tween macro states.
• Adjacency Matrices (MS matrix): Initialize a list to track reduced adja-
cency matrices corresponding to macro states, starting with the original adja-
cency matrix.

making the algorithm unnecessary since the total duration would simply be the maximum
of all durations. Therefore, the number of steps the algorithm will perform is at most

trivial PH representation where the total duration is DT = Dsingular +max{Drest}, where

tasks. The representation can be easily found using convolution and max operations on
the distributions of the durations.
Given the complexity of the problem, it is recommended to reduce the network as much
as possible before applying the algorithm. Well-defined subnetworks can be reduced to a
single node, maintaining a well-defined Coxian distribution, assuming that all the original
distributions are Coxian. For instance, if two nodes are connected and the predecessor has
only one successor and vice versa, these nodes can be reduced to one. If both are Erlang
distributed, the new node will be generalized Erlang distributed, following the rules of
convolution as described in section 2.7.
Pruning nodes on noncritical paths can also improve performance and reduce the size of
the final sub-intensity matrix, though it may affect accuracy.

The Telecom Example
6.1
Modelling a Telecom example
This section aims to implement models for the telecom example that Lichtenberg (2000)
introduces in [17].
The problem at hand involves estimating the duration of a project for releasing a new
product in a telecom company. This project spans from the initial design phase to the sale
and shipment to major markets. The analysis and estimates of the project do not consider
the risk of major accidents or disasters. At the time of Lichtenberg’s writing and analysis,
the project had already been underway for a few months, with a remaining deadline of
117 weeks.
The project was initially defined to consist of 28 activities where the 28th is the aggregated
contingency corrections also known as the overall corrections. Dependencies between the
activities’ ability to start are given. There are three overlaps of activities, that in this
modelling are considered as lead times. The lead times have, in accordance to the earlier
section 3.9 on lead times, been handled such that the predecessor is split into two. The
modes are adjusted given the estimates of overlap.
For example, Tasks 4 and 8 have been found to have an overlap. The mode of Task 4 is
given as 9 weeks and the mode of Task 8 is estimated to be 15 weeks. The lead time is
then given a mode of -8, so to handle this in the modeling, Task 4 is split into two tasks.
One task, named 4 1, with a mode of 1 and the other task, named 4 2, with a mode of
8. Now Task 8 only depends on 4 1 and Task 4 2 has no successors. This is not the most
correct way of handling the lead times since it should be done with the means and not
the modes, but since the modes are constant regardless of the shape parameter those are
easier to handle in this case.
However, due to the complexity of the model and the number of states which are generated
when using the PH distribution the model is simplified by disregarding Task 4 2. The
simplification is justified because the likelihood of a task in the second layer of the network,
which has no successors, being the last incomplete task is very low when the network has a
depth of 10 or 11. Similarly, the lead time between Task 2 and Task 11 is also disregarded.
Furthermore, some tasks have a triple estimate of 0, due to the project already being
underway when the project was analyzed. These tasks are therefore excluded from the
modeling. The model also disregards Task 27 since it also has 0 as the triple estimate.
Lastly, the contingency corrections do not have a mode given but a mean of 10.2 is given.
From this mean a mode of 10 is used for the overall corrections in the modelling. All tasks,
with an assumed mode different from 0 and the assumed modes are given in Table 6.1.

Modelling with Hyperexponential Distributions
As discussed earlier in section 3.11, the choice of distribution for task durations can be very
impactful on the results. In this section, we model task durations using hyperexponential
distributions. Given that the triple estimates in the telecom example are intended for
Erlang distributions, translating these to hyperexponential distributions is not straight-
forward.
As described in the theory, a hyperexponential distribution is a mixture of exponential
distributions. For this analysis, task durations are modeled with hyperexponential distri-
butions where the mode is taken as the mean of an exponential distribution with a prob-
ability of 0.98. Additionally, the minimum and maximum values from the triple estimates
are used as the means for two other exponential distributions, each with a probability of
0.01. This means that each task duration is modeled as a mixture of three exponential

Conclusion of Telecom Example
In this chapter, we have explored various models to estimate the duration of a telecom
project.
We started with the full model with a low and identical shape parameter for all 26 tasks
and then extended it to include Erlang distributions with different shape parameters.
By using Monte Carlo simulations and Phase-Type (PH) distributions, we were able to
compare the results and observe the impact of different shape parameters on the mean and
variance of the project duration when using the full model. As a proof of concept, HPC
was also used to compute the results for the full model when the computation became too
heavy for a regular computer.
We also considered a reduced model focusing on the six near-critical paths and compared
it with the full model. The results showed that the reduced model provides a good ap-
proximation while greatly reducing computational complexity. Additionally, we examined
the critical path model, which, while useful, carries a higher risk of underestimating the
total project duration compared to the partially reduced network.
Furthermore, we incorporated the triple estimates to account for uncertainty in task du-
rations. This approach provided more realistic estimates and highlighted the importance
of considering uncertainty in project planning.
Finally, we modeled the task durations using hyperexponential distributions and compared
the results with those obtained using Erlang distributions. The hyperexponential models
yielded higher mean durations and variances, indicating that the choice of distribution
can have a substantial impact on the results.
Overall, this chapter demonstrates the importance of selecting appropriate distributions
and parameters for task durations and the benefits of using reduced models to simplify
complex project networks. The findings can help project managers make more informed
decisions and better manage project risks.

Multivariate Networks and MPH*
In many projects, multiple variables are of interest, such as time, cost, and their interplay.
Using the theory of phase-type distributions, we can model multivariate project networks
that incorporate both time and cost components. This approach provides valuable insights
into the dependence and covariance between time and cost, which is of importance to the
project management and risk assessment. However, a closed-form solution for the joint
distributions of MPH* is not yet available in the current theory of phase-type distributions.
The cost must be linear with time to use MPH* distributions. That means if Di is the
random variable describing the duration of Task i, then Ci is the cost of Task i, which
can be written as Ci = ci,1 · Di + ci,2, where ci,1 is the time-dependent cost rate and ci,2 is
any constant cost. The cost rates ci,1 should all be non-negative. The total project cost
is then

7.1
How the Reward Matrix is Constructed
Given the macro states, we can construct the reward matrix from the cost per time unit of
each task. The reward matrix is constructed such that one column of the reward matrix
is filled with ones, representing the time reward rates, while the other column(s) is/(are)
filled with the cost rates of the tasks. The cost rate of a macro state is the sum of the cost
rates of the tasks running in that macro state, in the case that the cost rate is constant
for any task. If each state of a Task i has a different cost rate, expressed by the vector
ri,2, the cost rate vector of a macro state, MSl, with n tasks is then
RMSl,2 = rMSl1,2 ⊗eMSl2 ⊗· · · ⊗eMSln + eMSl1 ⊗rMSl2,2 ⊗· · · ⊗eMSln + · · · + eMSl1 ⊗eMSl2 ⊗· · · ⊗rMSln,2.
Using our algorithm to determine the existing macro states, we can easily construct the
total reward matrix, R, given the constant reward rate for each task. We assume that
time always yields a reward rate of 1, and the cost of a task is the reward rate times the
time spent doing the task. The time reward column is filled with ones, and the cost in
a macro state with multiple tasks is the sum of the rewards of each task in that macro
state. Thus, we can find the means, variances, and covariance between time and cost for
a project.

into compartments, calculate the time and cost of each compartment separately, and then
combine them, as suggested by Lichtenberg (2000)[17].

Discussion and Further Work
8.1
Discussion and Perspectives
This thesis has focused on the mathematical modeling of process networks using phase-
type (PH) distributions, illustrating their capability to account for complexities such as
Merge Event Bias (MEB) and stochastic dependencies. The results emphasize both the
potential and the limitations of this approach in real-world project management scenarios.
8.1.1
Fixed modes or Fixed Means
The telecom example was based on fixed modes, meaning that the task duration means
were allowed to vary. This resulted in different total project means for the same network
when the shape parameter of the Erlang distributions for each task was changed. This
variation reduces the comparability of the results, especially since the main comparison
metric was the mean rather than the mode. If the modes given by Lichtenberg and the
triple estimates had instead been interpreted as means, the rate parameter for a given
shape parameter k would be determined as
λ =
k
mean
rather than
λ = k −1
mode.
For example, in the section using the critical path, the total mean would always be 100,
assuming that the modes are the means.
Not fixing the means also limits the value of the comparison between using Erlang and
hyper-exponential distributions. This was evident as the two versions yielded different
choices of near-critical paths. However, if we reference the earlier example in Section 3.11
where the means were held constant when using different distributions, the results support
the point that the assumption and choice of distribution are important to both mean and
variance.
In most cases, not having similar means weakens the comparability and conclusions of the
results.
The least affected comparisons are those between the full model, the near-critical path,
and the critical path when comparing with the same shape parameter, as these models
will share the means of each task duration.
8.1.2
Simulation vs Analytical Approaches
Simulation remains a dominant method for project network analysis due to its ease of
implementation. However, managers often lack the technical expertise to utilize these tools
effectively as stated by Douglas, David E (1978) [8]. The model proposed in this thesis
offers a mathematically rigorous alternative, albeit one requiring substantial understanding
of PH distributions. Despite this, it aligns closely with traditional project management
tools like CPM and PERT while addressing some of their limitations, such as MEB and
their approximate nature. The telecom example showed how triple estimates can be used
with the PH distributions, meaning a program can be setup to take just the triple estimates
and the network structure and then, by use of the algorithm, yield results using the PH
distributions.

8.1.3
Practical Implications and Managerial Tools
An important aspect of this research is its potential to enhance managerial decision-
making. By enabling detailed task descriptions beyond traditional triple estimates, the
model allows for more accurate representations of task durations. Integrating this with
iterative frameworks like the Successive Principle could further refine criticality indices
and decision-making processes.
Tools that translate the technical underpinnings of this model into intuitive interfaces for
project managers could drive wider adoption. For example, graphical tools that visualize
critical paths, task probabilities, and distributions could make the PH-based models more
accessible to non-experts.
8.1.4
Algorithmic Complexity and Heuristic Approaches
A significant contribution of this thesis is the algorithm for determining task concurrency
and transitions in project networks. Initially, the theoretical complexity of finding macro
states and transitions was thought to be the most limiting factor.
However, practical
examples like the telecom project demonstrated that the size of the sub-intensity matrix
posed a greater challenge. Developing methods to construct super-networks or employing
heuristics to reduce network size could alleviate computational burdens while preserving
critical information.
For instance, Goman (2021)[9] developed a heuristic to obtain more precise estimates
and identify important paths in a network. Using such heuristics to reduce network size
could be more effective than the near-critical path approach used in the telecom example.
Additionally, Hartley and Wortham[12] provide insights into exact solutions for specific
networks like the Wheatstone Bridge, demonstrating the potential for applying tailored
algorithms to reduce computational challenges in specific contexts.
Combining these heuristic approaches with near-critical path analyses could further en-
hance computational feasibility for larger networks. Near-critical paths have shown utility
in reducing network complexity without significantly compromising the accuracy of re-
sults. This was seen in the telecom example, where when using near-critical paths most of
the information of the full model was retained while make the computations less complex.
Monte Carlo (MC) simulations are a pragmatic alternative to PH-based analytical meth-
ods. While comparable in results for smaller networks, their precision diminishes as net-
work complexity increases. Increasing the number of simulations can address this, but at
the cost of computational efficiency.
8.1.5
Finding the Criticality Index with PH
At no point in the examples has the criticality index(CI), and thereby the variance, been
explicitly considered. As mentioned earlier, the criticality index quantifies the likelihood
of a path being the most constraining in terms of project completion time, by assigning
each task a probability of being critical. This index can be either subjectively assigned
or calculated using probabilistic methods. A common approach for approximating the
CI involves Monte Carlo simulations, where the project network is simulated multiple
times. For each simulation, the path with the longest total duration is identified, and
the probability of a given path being the critical path is estimated as the proportion of
simulations in which it is the path with the longest duration.
A promising avenue for further work involves leveraging phase-type distributions (PH) and
the associated algorithms to directly calculate the probabilities of each path being critical.
This could potentially simplify the project networks and hereby reduce the computational
complexity of networks, while also providing insights into which paths are most likely to

constrain project completion. These results also have the value that they would not be
approximations but exact probabilities given the assumptions of the model.
The probability of any given macro chain i, consisting of the macro states MSij, where
j = 0, 1, ..., n for the n macro states in the macro chain and ij gives the indices from the
list of all macro states, can be determined using the expression:
αMS0(−TMS0)−1TMS0→MSi1(−TMSi1)−1TMSi1→MSi2(−TMSi2)−1 · · · TMSin−1→MSineMSin,
where MS0 represents the initial macro state, MSin is the final macro state, TMSij →MSil
denotes the transition intensity between macro states, and eMSin is a vector of ones corre-
sponding to the dimension of MSin. The probability of Task d being the last to complete
is then obtained by summing over all macro chains where d is the final task.
Using this probabilistic framework, it should become feasible to calculate the critical prob-
ability of each path. For further direction on this, the reader is referred to an article on
conditional distributions of phase-type by Andersen, Neuts, and Nielsen (2000) [1].
8.1.6
Expanding Network Models
This thesis lays a foundation for extending the framework to more complex network struc-
tures, such as those involving advanced order statistics.
Incorporating a minimum or k-th order statistic could model scenarios where only k of n
task are needed to be completed for a project to progress. For example, if at the planning
stage, the team identifies that they only need the k shortest tasks from a set of n to be
completed, this knowledge could be incorporated into the framework by constructing a
new ”task” whose distribution corresponds to the k-th order statistic.
As shortly discussed in section 2.14 our modelling is not adapted to allow for atoms at
zero having a size bigger than 0. Allowing for this would be another interesting extension.
It would as mentioned add some complexity especially in cases where it is non-initial
tasks which have atoms at zero, but it should be a very possible extension both for the
algorithm to record transitions and macro states where tasks are possibly skipped, and for
the construction of the PH representation. This feature would account for activities that
might not need to be performed with a certain probability, providing a more realistic and
flexible representation of project networks.
Exploring these and other similar avenues could enhance the modeling of task prioriti-
zation, conditional dependencies, and dynamic decision-making in project management
scenarios.
8.1.7
Multivariate Extensions
The multivariate phase-type (MPH*) distribution, demonstrated in this thesis, opens av-
enues for modeling networks with multiple interdependent variables, such as time and cost.
Future work could explore optimizing project schedules within this framework, balancing
trade-offs between variables based on project priorities like expected completion time, cost
uncertainty, or a mixture of both. This approach offers the potential for deriving optimal
scheduling strategies tailored to specific project objectives. The use of the modelling to
choose a subjectively optimal project structure is also applicable in the univariate case.
Multivariate modeling would also be interesting to account for inflation, exchange rates,
or other economic factors that could impact project costs and durations. Knowing the
time until a purchase is made and hereby the inflation of prices based on the time of the
purchase could be a valuable tool in project management.

8.1.8
Iterative and Successive Improvements
The successive principle aligns with the structure of this modeling approach, offering
potential for applications like determining criticality indices within the framework. Using
the successive principle in conjuncture with phase-type distribution could refine estimates,
making the model increasingly robust as new data or insights are incorporated. The ability
to identify the criticality index has not been developed in this thesis but could be a valuable
tool to expand.
8.1.9
Extending to the Bilateral Domain
Expanding the model to include bilateral PH distributions would greatly widen its appli-
cation scope. The bilateral PH distributions would especially be useful in cost or other
resource modeling cases where negative values are more prevalent. This is not to say that
the duration could not also benefit by allowing for negative durations. It was for example
seen that Lichtenberg had negative triple estimates for the overall correction factors in the
telecom example.
The rewards in a multivariate phase-type distribution can be bilateral, see for example
chapter 8.5 in Bladt and Nielsen (2017)[4]. Extending to allow for costs to have bilateral
values would be a natural extension of the model, making the modelling more versatile
and applicable to a wider range of project management scenarios.
8.1.10
Broader Applications Beyond Project Management
While this thesis focuses on project management, the PH modeling approach has broader
applications in fields such as logistics, healthcare, and reliability engineering.
For example, Hartley and Wortham[12] discuss networks modeling the Wheatstone Bridge
from electrical engineering, demonstrating how exact solutions can be derived for specific
configurations. The network structure of a Wheatstone Bridge is analogous to the non-
regular case discussed in this thesis. This shows how the structures of the project network
also could represent other areas such as patient journeys through healthcare systems or
workflows in logistics, providing valuable insights into process optimization.
Additionally, the thesis did not explore areas such as PETRI nets, which could offer
meaningful perspectives for future research. Investigating further whether algorithms for
constructing macro states and transitions already exist in related fields could enhance the
methodology and bridge interdisciplinary gaps.
8.1.11
Conclusion
The comprehensive exploration of PH distributions in this thesis provides a robust founda-
tion for advancing stochastic modeling in project management. By addressing computa-
tional challenges, expanding the framework to incorporate advanced statistical tools, and
exploring applications beyond project management, this research opens numerous avenues
for future work. The insights and methodologies presented have the potential to enhance
the accuracy of project network analyses across a wide range of domains.

9
Conclusion
This thesis has explored a structured approach to modeling project networks using phase-
type distributions, with a primary focus on project duration. Given the inherent com-
plexity of project durations and their natural connection to networks and phase-type
distributions, a theoretical foundation was established. The fundamentals of phase-type
distributions were introduced alongside key graph and network theory concepts to for-
malize project networks. The introduction of macro states and macro chains provided a
systematic framework to describe the various pathways a project may take to completion.
To contextualize the findings within existing project planning methodologies, techniques
such as the critical path method, PERT, and the successive principle were examined.
Additionally, the concept of near-critical paths was introduced, encompassing multiple
paths rather than a singular critical path, thereby preserving essential information about
merge events and their biases, which impacts estimation accuracy.
Through detailed case studies, various network structures were analyzed to illustrate their
complexities and to demonstrate how phase-type representations can be constructed, even
in cases where simple convolutions and maximum operations are insufficient. The macro
chain approach played a crucial role in formulating these representations.
To the author’s knowledge, a novel algorithm was developed to automate the identification
of macro states and the transitions between them, enabling the phase-type representation
of project networks to be generated without manual intervention. However, the compu-
tational feasibility of the algorithm is constrained by the possibly large number of macro
states and transitions. The time complexity, bounded by O(n2), presents a limitation, par-
ticularly as the sub-intensity matrix grows large. The computational challenge of evaluat-
ing matrix exponentials was highlighted in the telecom example, where high-performance
computing (HPC) and Monte Carlo simulations were employed to extract meaningful re-
sults. By leveraging model reduction techniques to retain essential paths while minimizing
computational burden, it was possible to compute the matrix exponential efficiently while
maintaining accuracy. Comparisons between the critical path, near-critical paths, and
the full model demonstrated that near-critical paths offered a superior approximation,
preserving crucial information often lost in the critical path approach.
Further refinements to the model included considerations for task duration distributions.
Initially, an approach with Erlang-distributed task durations, assuming a common shape
parameter was used. Subsequent refinements incorporated variability in shape parameters
to better reflect the uncertainty in triple estimates, revealing the importance of accurately
interpreting these estimates. The impact of distribution choice was also explored by tran-
sitioning to hyperexponential distributions, underscoring the challenges and implications
of any underlying modeling assumptions.
Finally, the thesis presented a multivariate phase-type distribution framework as a proof
of concept, demonstrating how project cost and time could be modeled simultaneously.
This was exemplified through a small-scale building project, where a reward matrix was
constructed to analyze correlations between time and cost. The results emphasized the
importance of considering such dependencies in project planning.
Potential avenues for further research include refining the algorithm, extending the ap-
proach to broader variety of networks and models, and exploring real-world applications

where phase-type modeling could enhance decision-making in project management. By
integrating these methodologies, phase-type distributions offer a robust and flexible frame-
work for capturing the stochastic nature of project durations and dependencies, paving
the way for more precise and informative project planning strategies.

Macro Chain Approach for a Regular Layered
Network
A network with five nodes can be created by adding a single node and edge to the network
from Figure 4.2. The new node is connected to have an edge going into it from the first
layer, placing the node in the second layer. This would create a network with five nodes
and five edges as exemplified in Figure A.1.
1

Figure A.1: Network with five nodes and five edges
The network’s total duration can be expressed by the following expressions.
D = max{D1 + max{D3, D4, D5}, D2 + max{D4, D5}}
= max{max{D1, D2} + max{D4, D5}, D1 + D3}.
From the expressions, dependencies between the durations of the tasks are evident, and
the macro chain approach is therefore used to construct a PH representation.
The network will always start in the macro state {1, 2}, where Tasks 1 and 2 are running.
From this macro state, the network can transition to two different macro states:
• If Task 2 finishes first, the network will transition to a macro state, where Task 1 is
running alone, since all the tasks 3, 4, and 5 are dependent on Task 1 completing
before they can start. This is therefore the macro state {1}.
• If Task 1 finishes first, Task 3 can commence, and Task 2 will continue running. This
is the macro state {2, 3}.
From the macro state {1}, the only macro state which can be reached is the macro state
{3, 4, 5}.
From the macro state {2, 3}, the network can transition to two different macro states:
• If Task 3 finishes first, the network will transition to a macro state, where Task 2
continues running alone. This is the macro state {2}.
• If Task 2 finishes first, the network will transition to a macro state, where Task 3
