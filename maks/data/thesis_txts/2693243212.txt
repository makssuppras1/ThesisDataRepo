Abstract 
________________________________________________________________________________ 
This report examines whether an optimization of a current dDNP-NMR methodology can be 
accomplished to observe additional glycolytic metabolites with higher signal intensity. The 
optimization was performed with a model system of S. cerevisiae in the form of commercially 
available freeze-dried yeast granules. The dDNP-NMR assay was addressed with regarding glassing 
agent, cell density/ substrate ratio and with the application of [1-13C,D] glucose as substrate. The 
impact of data acquisition settings including receiver gain and different pulse programs was assessed. 
Lastly, dDNP-NMR was tested as an analytical method to compare metabolic activity in different 
yeast strains. It was found that the dDNP-NMR assay benefits from omitting glycerol as glassing 
agent. The optimal ratio of cell density/substrate was found to be 3.6:1 [𝑂𝐷600/mM glucose]. The 
application of [1-13C,D] glucose as substrate enabled the observation of two additional glycolytic 
metabolites (F-6-P and F-1,6-BP).  It was verified that the current dDNP-NMR methodology was 
operating with optimal RG setting for the respective current pulse program and substrate 
concentration. An avg. of ~80 % improved signal intensity could be obtained with a pulse program 
inducing a 16° flip angle and with 2 sec. repetition time at the expense of a 2-fold lower time 
resolution. The application of a 90° pulse program increased the signal intensity compared to the sum 
of signal intensity gain with repeatedly 10 ° pulses. The application to use dDNP-NMR to designate 
strain specific metabolism activity was found to be successful but quantification should be performed 
tentatively. It was found that degradative glycolysis is favored over the pentose phosphate pathway 
in ratios of 1:0.35 and 1:0.16 for S. cerevisiae and S. boulardii, respectively.

1 Introduction 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Cells are the basic units of life and unicellular microorganisms are the oldest form of life. Such  
unicellular microorganisms play important roles in fermentation of food, production of enzymes, 
fuels, chemicals and sewage treatment.  Through combination with bioengineering, it has become 
possible to utilize cells as the ultimate factory for a bulk production of a broader selection of specified 
products (Kiyotaka Y, et al., 2014). To modify and optimize cells into a cell factory, a detailed 
understanding of the ongoing systems-biology is required. Currently, the use of in vitro kinetics of 
purified enzymes is the foundation of the most advanced systems-biology models (Smallbone, et al., 
2013). Corresponding in vivo data for mechanistic models remain hard to obtain and test for their 
predictive value. Even for S. cerevisiae, one of the best studied model organisms, the kinetic 
modelling of the central metabolism remains a formidable challenge, which needs to be addressed to 
develop better cell factories. Rather recently, hyperpolarized NMR has emerged as a technique that 
uniquely enables tracking of reaction pathways by observing resolved signals from the different 
pathway chemicals with sub-second time resolution in living cells (Lerche, et al., 2015). This project 
will examine if the current dDNP-NMR methodology for the recording of the glycolysis in S. 
cerevisiae (Meier, et al., 2011) can be optimized to achieve higher signal intensity and quantification 
of more metabolites. Biological, NMR-spectroscopic, and experimental parameters will be optimized 
using an experimental model system for S. cerevisiae in the form of commercial dry yeast. The 
optimization to increase signal intensity and the number of observable glycolytic intermediates aims 
to gain a better understanding of the glycolysis in S. cerevisiae. Lastly, the biological findings of the 
glycolysis in S. cerevisiae made enable with dDNP-NMR data are described.

2 Introduction to the Methods Applied  
2.1 DNP-NMR Enables the Recording of In-Vivo Metabolic Data 
NMR is a well-established analytical method with a broad range of applications. Catalytic monitoring 
by conventional NMR has for a long time proven to be a valid method to probe catalytic activity. In 
addition, NMR spectroscopy is a non-invasive method allowing insight into intact cells. Conventional 
NMR has shortcomings, due to its limited sensitivity and time resolution. These shortcomings can be 
addressed with dissolution dynamic polarization nuclei magnetic resonance (dDNP-NMR) for 
suitable systems.  
2.1.1 Fundamental Principles of NMR  
NMR utilizes the phenomenon that a magnetically active nucleus with spin can absorb energy in a 
quantized process. All nuclei possessing either an odd mass, odd atomic number or both has a 
quantized spin.  Having a quantized spin induces an angular magnetic moment and the nuclei are said 
to be magnetically active (L. Pavia, et al., 2015). The number of spin states that a nucleus may adopt 
is determined by the nuclear spin quantum number I, where 2𝐼+ 1 spin states are allowed. Outside a 
magnetic field, these states are degenerated, but in an external magnetic field alignment with the 
magnetic field in the z-direction of the net magnetization is a preferable low-energy state contrary to 
the high-energy state aligned antiparallel to the  instrument magnetic field. Magnetization in the 
direction of the magnetic field is also called polarization. The spin state alignment with the magnetic 
field is a dynamic process. The time to build up equilibrium net magnetization parallel to the external 
magnetic field is described with the spin-lattice (longitudinal) relaxation time constant, T1 (D. W. 
Claridge, 2016). The energy difference Δ𝐸, between two spin states depends on the applied magnetic 
field strength B0, and the magnetogyric ratio 𝛾 constant of the nuclei of interest.  As different spin 
states process a different energy relative to the thermal energy, the population of the spin states can 
be described with the Boltzmann distribution using Boltzmann’s constant k (L. Pavia, et al., 2015):

2.2 Critical Experimental Design Parameters for dDNP-NMR 
The success of an dDNP-NMR experiment depends on several additional factors that differ from 
conventional NMR. Therefore, the most impactful factors are further elaborated in this section. 
Critical experimental design parameters in dDNP-NMR include the following to parameters:  
1. The solid phase build-up  
2. The data acquisition  
2.2.1 Solid Phase Build-up 
The sample preparation determines the level of the solid state polarization obtainable. During the 
polarization, after a flip-flop interaction an electron needs to be repolarized to keep pushing the 
polarization of the nucleus. Paramagnetic ions, most often used are Gd-complexes, can be used to 
speed up the polarization process by shortening the electron T1. Shortening the electron T1 makes the 
repolarization of the electrons faster, but it also increases the relaxation rate of the nuclei (Karlsson, 
et al., 2012). The amount chosen is a compromise between keeping laboratory work time-efficient 
and obtaining a suitable polarization level. The free electrons needed are provided in the form of 
stabilized radicals. It is possible to oversaturate the sample preparation with radicals to a point, where 
only low polarizations are possible due to nuclear spin relaxation. For a certain sample preparation, 
the optimal ratio of paramagnetic ions and radicals must be found. Lastly, for the sample preparation, 
a glassing agent might be needed to ensure high homogeneity of the radical distribution (Karlsson, et 
al., 2012).  
2.2.2  dDNP-NMR Data Acquisition  
In vivo data acquisition is not performed under the same conditions as those, where the 
hyperpolarization is accomplished. As result hereof, a dynamic artificial spin population distribution 
of the nuclei of interest is generated upon hyperpolarization. The spin population distribution will 
decay towards the surroundings Boltzmann equilibrium spin population distribution, decreasing the 
signal enhancement. The rate constant for the decay is the inverse of spin lattice relaxation time 1/T1. 
This limits the effective data acquisition time domain to approximately 3xT1. During data acquisition, 
the pulse applied contributes to pushing the artificial spin population distribution towards the 
Boltzmann equilibrium distribution. To conserve polarization for later data points, small flip angle 
pulses must be applied, as a hard 90° pulse would consume hyperpolarization and the spin population

2.3 The dDNP-NMR Experiment  
This brief introduction will aim to present a general dDNP-NMR experimental set-up to gain an 
insight in how dDNP-NMR is performed. Figure 4 outlines a schematic illustration of the experiment 
subject to this project.  
Figure 4: Schematic illustration of a general dDNP-NMR experimental set-up. 
The experimental set-up is divided into two parts: The solid-phase build-up (a) and the NMR part (b), for which the steps are denoted 
Xa and Xb, respectively. (Made in Chemdraw) 
Ia) A sample preparation containing substrate, Gd-complex and stable radical is placed in a sample 
cup. Commonly used is Gadoteridol and Trityl OXO63. The sample cup is hereafter inserted into 
the polarizer and flash-frozen in liquid helium.  
IIa) The sample is irradiated with microwaves enabling the dynamic buildup of a new distributed spin 
population equilibrium. The state of the dynamic buildup is monitored by applying a small-flip-angle 
pulse with a constant time interval to measure the magnitude of the polarization currently achieved. 
IIIa) A buffer solution is injected into a heating chamber connected to the polarizer.  
IVa) The buffer solution is pressurized and heated. The sample is ejected from the polarizer chamber 
and the hot buffer solution flushes out of the polarized sample into a receiver vessel. The dissolved 
sample is sucked into a syringe attached to the receiver vessel. 
Ib) Before step IVa is reached, a biological specimen of interest is added into an NMR tube. The tube 
is connected to an injection line before it is inserted into the NMR coil.  
IIb) In continuation of step IVa, the syringe containing the hyperpolarized sample is used to inject it 
via the injection line to the biological assay.  
IIIb) During the sample injection to the NMR spectrometer recording can start.

2.4 Visualization of the Glycolytic Pathway by dDNP-NMR 
The glycolytic pathway is visualized in the dDNP 
NMR experiment by the recording of a series of 
1D 13C spectra with a defined time interval. This 
series of 1D spectra is combined into a pseudo-2D 
13C spectrum allowing the establishment of a time 
domain. The time domain enables minoring of the 
kinetic reaction progress in the glycolysis. At the 
very beginning the hyperpolarized 13C enriched 
glucose is the only potential signal present. As the 
glycolysis proceeds, the hyperpolarized 
13C-
enriched glucose is converted into intermediates 
and further end products. These molecules are 
built up of hyperpolarized 13C-enriched glucose 
having a high capacity to induce an NMR signal. 
Figure 5 (Harris, et al., 2013) schematically 
displays the fate of 
13C atoms in glucose 
participating in the glycolytic pathways. The 
product of glycolysis itself is two molecules of 
pyruvate. Pyruvate is a key intermediate in the 
network of other metabolic pathways and 
therefore other metabolites are expected to be 
observed from pyruvate. The hyperpolarized 13C in the intermediates and end products is 
distinguishable from one another as they have different chemical shift, 𝛿. The difference might not

4 Results 
This chapter describes the results obtained for optimizing the sample assay, new strategies and 
biological findings.  
4.1 Starting Point of the Optimizing Process  
The starting point was a well-defined protocol. A short representation hereof is given in the following: 
30 mg of a DNP sample preparation, equivalent 50 𝜇mol [U-13C,D] glucose, is hyperpolarized for 
approximately one hour. Besides the glucose, the sample also contained glycerol as a glassing agent, 
Gd complex and a stable radical. A total volume of 200 𝜇𝑙 of a yeast suspension made of 66 mg dry 
yeast granules and 440 MES buffer pH 5.65, compose the biological assay. The assay is transferred 
to a Shigemi NMR tube and connected to an injection line before it is inserted into the NMR 
instrument equilibrated to 310 K.  
5 ml of MES buffer solution is used to flush the hyperpolarized glucose sample out of the polarizer. 
The dissolved sample is pulled out from the receiver vessel into a syringe. The liquid level in the 
syringe is adjusted manually to 400 𝜇l and injected via the injection line to the yeast suspension. 
Subtracting the dead volume of the injection line, this amount is equivalent to a final glucose 
concentration of ~6.4 mM in the final volume.  
Prior to the injection, a series of 1D 13C spectra is started. The spectra are recorded with a 10º flip 
angle and a repetition time of 0.5 sec. After the series has been recorded, the NMR tube is ejected 
from the spectrometer, and the total volume is measured before cleaning the NMR tube. The injection 
line is flushed with air and the heat chamber of the polarizer and receiver vessel is washed with 
EDTA(aq) before of N2(g) is sent through the dissolution stick of the Hypersense to flush out any 
residuals.

concentration should be, where the glucose uptake becomes saturated. Here, the uptake is the limiting 
factor for the signal intensity. Beyond this concentration, additional precious isotope enriched glucose 
would be wasted. At lower glucose concentrations, the whole cell catalytical potential would not be 
utilized.  It was expected to find an approximately linear correlation between signal intensity and 
glucose concentration until saturation was reached. The results hereof are presented in Figure 12.

4.3 Optimizing for Observable Metabolites   
The strongest peaks in all the spectra originated from the glucose, as expected. Glucose has several 
anomeric forms causing a comprehensive assortment of peaks. These peaks stretch over a wide 
chemical shift range, thus obstructing the detection of metabolites with similar chemical shifts as 
some of the glucose carbon atoms. Different approaches and efforts have been made to tackle this 
resolution problem.  
4.3.1 Magnetization Quenching 
For in-vivo NMR measurements, the cell membrane serves as natural boundary dividing the sample 
into an extra- and intercellular environment. The addition of a compound that perturbs the 
extracellular environment, but which does not diffuse into the intracellular environment enables 
selective modifications to differentiate between inter- and extracellular metabolites. This principle 
has been utilized for in-vivo dDNP-NMR (Reineri, et al.) (Karlsson, et al., 2016). Attempted here, is 
the use of Prohance, a Gd relaxation agent, to quench the magnetization of the extracellular, 
hyperpolarized glucose. Consequently, optimally no signal should emerge from glucose and 
previously hidden intermediates might appear. The effect was tested at two different concentrations 
of Prohance, namely at 5 mM and 10 mM in the final volume. Relative to the protocol/reference 
experiment, Prohance was mixed into the cell suspension prior to the injection of hyperpolarized 
glucose. The glucose signals were quenched within ≈12.5 sec and ≈3.0 s at Prohance concentrations 
of 5 mM and 10 mM, respectively. Stack plots of the spectra are shown in Appendix F. For both 
attempts, the glucose peaks were still present in all spectra and no additional intermediates were 
found. Hence, the attempts were judged to be unsuccessful for the intended purpose.   
4.3.2 NMR Shift Agents  
A different approach to modify the glucose peaks was the usage of NMR shift agents. Lanthanide-
induced shift (LIS) has proven to successfully shift 13C NMR signals of sorbitol, an open chain 
reduced form of glucose, with values of 16 ppm upfield at a ratio 1:2.5 [sorbitol/Ln(NO3)3] (Kieboom, 
et al.). Such a shift of the glucose 13C NMR signal would potentially reveal intracellular signals from 
glucose derivatives.

4.4.2  Optimizing the Flip Angle and Repetition Time  
Performing a dDNP-NMR experiment, the magnitude of the pulse and the repetition time between 
the pulses needs to be balanced. This balance implies not to use the hyperpolarization at once by 
applying a 90° pulse, but also not to lose the hyperpolarization by natural decay. In addition, 
conserving the hyperpolarization through longer repetition times enables the metabolism to be 
monitored over a longer time frame.  
A series of protocol experiments was conducted modifying the flip angle together with the repetition 
time with paired values of (10o, 0.5 sec), (16o, 1 sec) and (22o, 2sec). These values come from 
simulations in MatLab describing pairs of flip angles and TR that give maximum signal for a 
metabolite with T1 of 10 seconds and typically observed metabolic rates. The aim was to investigate 
whether the hyperpolarization can be put to better use, giving higher signal intensity at higher flip 
angle.

From the data in Figure 16 it appears that using a flip angle at 16° every second gives a higher AUC 
than the two other settings (10o, 0.5 sec and 22o, 2 sec). Higher flip angle normally translates directly 
into higher signal, but the short time frame of the early glycolytic metabolites might counteract this 
benefit of a larger flip angle as the reptation time comes too large. Indeed, using 22o 2 sec. gave a 
lower AUC for all measured metabolites. An average improvement of ~80% signal intensity for all 
metabolites was found for a 16° pulse with repetition time of 1 sec, compared to the reference pulse 
program setting (10o, 0.5 sec). However, the 80% improve in signal intensity comes at the expense 
of 2-fold lower time resolution.       
4.4.3 Remarks on Optimizing NMR Related Parameters   
It was proven that different values for NMR acquisition parameters than those commonly employed 
(e.g., in the reference/protocol experiment) can be used to gain signal intensity at the cost of time 
resolution. The RG setting was found to adjust to an optimal setting with respect to the concentration 
and pulse angle.

4.6 Biological Aspects of the dDNP-NMR Data  
dDNP-NMR data collected throughout the optimization process have given unique insight of the 
glycolytic metabolism in S. cerevisiae. It was tested whether the dDNP-NMR method could be 
applied to other yeast species to examine if the method is able to identify characteristic glycolytic 
performance related to the examined yeast species.  
4.6.1 Quantification of different metabolic pathways 
In the spectra recorded with [1-13C,D] glucose, signals for both CO2 and ethanol(C2) were observed. 
The glucose(C1) during degradative glycolysis is converted to ethanol(C2). The CO2 produced by 
this pathway origins from glucose(C3,C4). Therefore, the CO2 signal for [1-13C,D] glucose cannot 
be caused by degradative glycolysis. The CO2 signal must arise from the pentose phosphate pathway 
(PPP), as the CO2 formed here derives from glucose(C1) (R. Jensen, et al., 2016). The dDNP-NMR 
data of [1-13C,D] glucose can thereof be used as a directed measurement of the favoritism of the two 
different metabolic pathways. An AUC ratio of CO2/ethanol was calculated from spectra recorded 
with [1-13C,D] glucose fed to dry yeast (S. cerevisiae) and fresh grown cultures of the yeast S. 
boulardii. The results are shown in Figure 21.

4.6.2 A View into the Cellular Redox State  
The metabolic control in glycolysis occurs on a time scale of seconds and has been correlated with 
variations in the NAD+/NADH ratio (Theobald, et al., 1997). For S. cerevisiae it has been shown that 
the accumulation of 3PG is facilitated under conditions with increased NAD+ /NADH ratios (R. 
Jensen, et al., 2019), in contrast, the accumulation of DHAP is facilitated under conditions with 
increased NADH/NAD+ ratios (Cronwright, et al., 2002 ). The 3PG and DHAP dDNP-NMR signals 
can hypothetically be interpreted to reflect the current cellular redox state. The cellular redox state 
for dry yeast (S. cerevisiae) and fresh grown cultures of the yeast S. cerevisiae strain BY4743 was 
examined and compared. The results are shown in Figure 22.

5 Discussion  
5.1 Discussion of Assay Optimizing 
The assay optimizing was performed with a model system of S. e in the form of commercial freeze-
dry granulates.  The yeast granulates makes the experimental set-up more convenient to perform as 
no incubation is needed. Initially, concerns of this form of yeast would impact the experimental 
reproducibility as the purity and the homogeneity of the freeze-dry granulates was unknown. These 
concerns emerged to be minor validated by lesser standard deviation present in the dDNP-NMR data. 
However, the model system yeast seemed to initially faster expose glycolytic flux compared to all 
incubated yeast cultures. Hereof, suspicion of the model system is not truly representative for pre-
steady state glycolytic kinetics. The assay was prior optimized regarding to radicals and electron 
relaxing agents and is therefore out of scope of this report.  
5.2 Discussion of Optimizing for Observable Metabolites 
The desired effect of extracellular magnetization quenching of glucose with Prohance has proven to 
be unsuccessful, however improvements to the experimental set-up could be implemented to give the 
desired effect better conditions. In the executed experimental set-up, Prohance was mixed into the 
cell suspension prior to the injection of hyperpolarized glucose. Hereby, the yeast is in direct 
competition with the magnetization quenching to uptake the hyperpolarized glucose before it is 
completely quenched. Therefore, a smaller amount of hyperpolarized glucose would be fed into the 
yeast compared to an improved experimental set-up in which Prohance is injected separately with a 
defined time delay between the glucose injections. This improvement would challenge the 
reproductivity of the experimental set-up with the current instrumental settings available as the 
precision of the manual injections is of concern. Currently, work on making the manual transfer of 
the hyperpolarized sample to the NMR spectrometer automatic is in progress.  This would aid the 
reproductivity of the suggested improved experimental set-up and re-examination would be of 
interest. 
The NMR shift agents tested induced an insufficient magnitude of shift for current use. The literature 
covers various NMR shift agents that could be of interest (Forsberg, 1996). However, the studied 
applications of NMR shift agents described in literature are niche and no direct equivalent application 
has been found in relation to the issue presented in this report. The 13C chemical shift values for

The application of [1-13C,D]glucose to observe F-6-P and F-1,6-BP is of great biological interest as

and LC/MS of isotope label substrate (Tanner, et al., 2018) (Parker, et al., 2016). These examinations  
require extensive laboratory work. The dDNP-NMR found here enable a direct observation with 
laboratory work in a time scale of 1 ½ hr.      
5.3 Discussion of Optimizing Data Acquisition 
The signal intensity gain obtained with the 16° pulse and 2 sec. repetition time has a high expense of 
a 2-fold lower time resolution. The lower time resolution is critical as the early metabolites are 
observable in a relative short time frame of approximately ≈7 sec. Hereof, the more interesting time 
points are dramatically decreased reducing the advantages in gaining a 80 % signal improvement.

5.5 Discussion of Biological Aspects  
The dDNP-NMR suggests more realistic data compared to classical in-vitro studies of purified 
enzymes in isolation as enzymes do not operate in isolation inside the cell (Teusink , et al., 2000). 
The observed accumulation of F-6-P in the glycolysis is consistent with other observations from 
literature; whether it can be singled out as the bottleneck is still discussed (Boscá, et al., 1994).

6 Conclusion  
It was found that the dDNP-NMR assay benefits from omitting glycerol as glassing agent. The 
optimal ratio of cell density/substrate was found to be 3.6:1 [𝑂𝐷600/mM glucose]. The application of 
[1-13C,D] glucose as substrate enabled the observation of two additional glycolytic metabolites (F-6-
P and F-1,6-BP). It was verified that the current dDNP-NMR methodology operated with an optimal 
RG setting for the respective current pulse program and substrate concentration. An avg. of ~80 % 
improved signal intensity could be obtained with a pulse program inducing a 16° flip angle and with 
2 sec. repetition time. However, this is at the expense of a 2-fold lower time resolution. Applying a 
90° pulse program increased the signal intensity compared to the sum of signal intensities obtained 
with repeatedly 10 ° pulses. Highest signal intensity was found with a time delay of 3 sec. between 
the 90° pulse and glucose injection. It is concluded that the experimental set-up reproducible to 
assemble distinct 90° pulse data time points to obtain kinetic information of the glycolysis. The 
application to use dDNP-NMR to designate strain specific metabolism activity was found to be 
successful but quantification comparation of metabolites should be performed tentatively. It is found 
that the degradative glycolysis is favored over the pentose phosphate pathway in ratios of 1:0.35 and 
1:0.16 for S. cerevisiae and S. boulardii, respectively. The validity dDNP-NMR data analysis of 
glycolytic intermediates to track the current cellular redox state is concluded to be of questionable 
validity.

7 Further Perspectives  
dDNP-NMR offers unique possibilities to gain insight of intracellular reactions. Enabling better 
quantitative analyses of metabolic signals will aid dDNP-NMR to become a superior method for 
determining the vast cascades of reaction kinetics that unfolds in metabolic pathways. It is suggested 
to pursue the need for a better understanding and determination of intracellular T1. Ideally, an accurate 
T1 determination would enable data treatment to deduct the signal decay caused by the natural loss 
of polarization. Hereby, dDNP-NMR signals will remain as true values to be directly translated into 
genuine kinetics rates.

Enhanced 13C NMR Detects Extended Reaction Networks in Living 
Cells  
Pernille Rose Jensen1*, Francesca Sannelli2$, Ludvig Tving Stauning1,2$, and Sebastian Meier2* 
Insights into intracellular reaction kinetics, thermodynamics and 
regulation have remained sparse, but would be impactful for the 
advancement of biomedicine and bioproduction. A suitable 13C 
NMR approach provides improvements in sensitivity that make 
extended reaction networks and assay time windows, previously 
inaccessible cell densities and relative flux measurements 
accessible. 
All living entities coordinate their energy and material demand 
through metabolism. These tasks are intertwined with the need 
to balance redox chemistry and combat oxidative damage.1 The 
most central metabolic reaction pathways play crucial roles in 
providing energy and biomass, and continue to attract attention 
due to their promise for providing therapeutic targets and 
future sustainable production.  
 
While metabolism had been belittled as biochemical 
textbook knowledge to be mastered and put aside in some 
disciplines, the mechanistic details even of the most central 
pathways remain incompletely understood.1-3 Mechanistic 
understanding of intracellular reaction kinetics and product 
control hinges on suitable analytical capabilities. Analytical tools 
that provide insight into intracellular reaction kinetics, 
thermodynamics and regulation remain, however, sparse. Such 
analytical capabilities would therefore promise to be impactful 
for the advancement of biomedicine and bioproduction. 
 
Cells form the basic functional units of life. This functionality 
has been forged over billions of years. Insight into intracellular 
reaction chemistry under natural conditions hinges on the use 
of minimally invasive methodology employing natural or near-
natural substrates. NMR spectroscopy has been employed to 
study cellular reactions since more than four decades,4 as NMR 
spectroscopy provides a high spectral resolution and detects 
nuclear isotopes rather than bulky non-natural groups. 
However, NMR spectroscopy suffers from the presence of NMR 
active background signals and from rather low signal amplitudes 
relative to the noise from the detection electronics. These 
problems can be somewhat mitigated by the use of 
cryogenically cooled detection electronics5 and of isotope-
enriched reactants for 13C NMR. Nevertheless, in-cell NMR 
studies require high cell densities and acquisition times that 
prohibit the detection of pre-steady state kinetics.6 In addition 
to the minimally invasive isotope labelling,7 a physical labelling 
approach 
termed 
hyperpolarization 
has 
gained 
some 
popularity.8 Hyperpolarization temporarily enhances nuclear 
spin polarization by four orders of magnitude. This non-
equilibrium state fades however with the nuclear spin T1 time 
and is consumed by each experimental observation, thus 
limiting the timescale and number of observations during which 
sensitive observations are possible. As a consequence, the most 
common analytical tools for studying metabolism still use 
extracellular detection with NMR spectroscopy9-11 or mass 
spectrometry,12,13 after samples have been stabilized by the 
quenching of cellular activities.14, 15 
 
The limited sensitivity of in-cell NMR spectroscopic 
observations, the need for high cell densities or the need for 
quenching of cellular activities leave a biological blind spot (Fig. 
1). Reactions occurring on the several seconds to few minutes 
timescale are not easily observed. Thus, hyperpolarized

NMR spectroscopy has mainly been used to observe one or few 
reaction steps16-18 or fast glycolytic reactions.19-22 The current 
study hypothesizes that cellular assays under reproducible 
conditions may benefit largely from closing the gap in time 
scales and sensitivities between intra- and extracellular 
measurements. To this end, an in-cell assay at discrete time 
points is proposed, where each time point corresponds to a 
sensitivity-optimized single 13C NMR experiment (Scheme 1). 
Compared to conventional data acquisition schemes, this 
approach enhances the signal-to-noise ratio by orders of 
magnitude, especially for measurements after the one minute 
time window. Beyond extended observation time spans, this 
approach brings measurements at low cell densities into the 
realm of possibility, and is shown to enable the observation of 
extended reaction networks rather than short reaction 
sequences or fast pathways only. 
 
Hyperpolarized glucose was produced with the dissolution 
DNP (D-DNP) methodology using [U-13C,2H] and [1-13C,2H] 
isotopologues. The D-DNP methodology polarizes 13C nuclear 
spins of glucose to 40% of full nuclear spin alignment. Further 
improvement leaves a theoretical improvement of 2.5, but 
improvements of spin polarization were outside the scope of 
the current study. Instead, the use of time-discrete 
measurements detecting all hyperpolarization was pursued 
herein, to complement conventional kinetic measurements that 
use small flip angle () pulses for time-resolved measurements

1A,B).23,24

avenues for functional assays in living cells: (i) Signal 
enhancements are particularly striking for the later time points 
of an intracellular reaction and the time scale of hyperpolarized 
NMR (seconds) and of non-hyperpolarized NMR (minutes) can 
be bridged; (ii) more expanded parts of the intracellular 
reaction network should become observable; (iii) lower cell 
densities should become tractable under biologically more 
relevant settings, and (iv) accurate quantifications of network 
responses should become viable. Thus, a sensitive detection

The measurements of Fig. 3 show that new network 
products become observable with the applied approach. 
Comparison to authentic standard spectra and data bases show 
that these signals do not derive from glycolytic intermediates. 
Rather, authentic standard spectra indicate that signals at 174.5 
ppm and 181.3 ppm (relative to pyruvate at 26.6, 170.3 and 
205.1 ppm) can be tentatively assigned to glutamate (Fig. S2). 
Rapid injection 1H-13C spectroscopy validates that citric acid 
cycle intermediates are labelled on the low minute timescale 
(Fig. S3). Notably, data acquisition for the in cell 1D 13C NMR 
spectra requires 0.3 seconds, opposite to the 1H-13C spectra of 
2 minutes duration. The sidechain carboxylic acid signal at 181.3 
ppm partly overlaps with another non-glycolytic signal that is 
consistent with the formation of acetate. Difference spectra 
between labelling of cellular metabolites after 10 seconds and 
after 20 seconds distinguish between end products from a more 
extended reaction network (Fig. 4, positive signals) and 
glycolytic intermediates (Fig. 4, negative signals due to 
decreasing labelling after 10 seconds).  
 
Notably, all glycolytic intermediates and other metabolites 
beyond carbon dioxide and bicarbonate would be on the brink 
of being non-detectable with real- time measurements of Fig. 2 
after 20 seconds. With the suggested acquisition scheme, these 
metabolites remain detectable also beyond one minute after 
injection of tracer (Fig. S4). The main compounds that are 
labelled with hyperpolarized magnetization after one minute 
include pyruvate, alanine and glutamate. This observation is 
consistent with previously determined metabolite pools 
indicating that pyruvate is the most abundant glycolytic 
metabolite (at 10-2 M), while alanine and glutamate, which are 
accessible from pyruvate, are the most abundant amino acids 
(2.2×10-2 M and 3.9×10-2 M, respectively).2  Hence, observed 
distributions of glucose signal one minute after injection was 
consistent with a redistribution towards the steady state pool 
of intracellular chemicals. The increased sensitivity of the

Finally, we assessed relative signal influx into different 
catalytic pathways, one of which had been considered 
inaccessible to D-DNP NMR assays in yeast.22 The carbon 
dioxide and bicarbonate signals that are formed from [U-13C,2H] 
glucose can derive either from glycolysis and alcoholic 
fermentation, or from the pentose phosphate pathway. In order 
to distinguish between the use of the two pathways, [1-13C,2H] 
glucose was used (Fig. 5).25 The 13C label ends in the C2 position 
of ethanol in alcoholic fermentation, but in CO2/HCO3- in the 
pentose phosphate pathway, which has been considered non-
accessible in D-DNP assays in yeast.22 However, we find that 
hyperpolarized [1-13C,2H] glucose can be found to enter the 
pentose

Hence, accurate flux distributions can be obtained for pathways 
that were deemed hardly accessible with time-resolved D-DNP 
NMR measurements.
