Summary 
 
 
The goal of the thesis  was to examine two specific CNS disorders DLB and NPSLE each under different 
scopes. DLB was investigated in the treatment field  and NPSLE in the field of diagnostics. For better 
organization the thesis was split into two separate projects. One was related to treatment while the 
other to diagnostics. 
In the first project, the aim was to find a correlation between the microRNA miR-107  and DLB. miR-
107 is a post transcriptional regulator which is associated with Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) the most 
known Dementia related disease.  To establish such a connection, antisense oligonucleotides will be 
designed in order to silence it and by knocking it down, its role in DLB will be identified. However, 
unmodified oligonucleotides have low chances of making it threw the Blood-Brain-Barrier and even 
though phosphorothioates are incorporated on the first and last three oligonucleotides of the strand, 
the risk of enzymatic degradation is not eliminated. These concerns can be addressed by 
bioconjugating, through Copper-Catalyzed-Azide-Alkyne-Cycloaddition (CuAAC) click chemistry, an 3´-
alkyne-modified oligonucleotide with a Cell Penetrating Peptide (CPP) with an azide group on its C-
terminus. The CPP due to its bulkiness will protect the oligonucleotide from enzymatic degradation in 
the plasma and will also enhance the drugs ability to enter the Blood-Brain-Barrier due to it´s cationic 
nature. Furthermore an intracellular Cathepsin cleavable motif of Glu-Val-Cit is incorporated in the 
linker of the peptide and the oligonucleotide with the intention of freeing the oligonucleotide from 
the peptide delivery system after it crosses the Blood-Brain-Barrier.  
On the second project, a pool of 11 synthetic antigens DNA/RNA/LNA was used to their high reactivity 
with SLE serum samples. The same antigens were used to test their affinity with 73 NPSLE serum 
samples. The goal is to highlight the degree of affinity between the antigens and the NPSLE samples 
and how their reactivity compares with the SLE samples. Furthermore one of the antigens, the reduced 
version of Apolipoprotein H was synthesized, characterized with Gel Electrophoresis (GEL) and purified 
with a Molecular Cut off 30kDa filter. For this to be achieved, Indirect Enzyme linked immunosorbent 
Assay (ELISA) experiments were conducted with IgG and IgM as the secondary autoantibodies.

1. Introduction 
 
CNS Diseases 
 
Central Nervous System (CNS) Diseases are a wide range of ailments that hamper the function of either 
the brain or the spinal cord and are caused by infections, structural defects, blood flow disruption, 
tumours, autoimmune disorders or degeneration of neuronal tissues (1)1. Some primary examples of 
CNS conditions are Lewy Bodies Diseases such as Parkinson´s Disease (PD) or Dementia with Lewy 
Bodies (DLB) (degenerative disease), Alzheimer´s Disease (AD) (degenerative disease), Brain Stroke 
(blood flow disruption) , Cerebral Palsy, Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis or Lou Gehrig´s Disease 
(degenerative), Poliomyelitis (infection), Brain Tumours (tumours), Systemic Lupus Erythematosus 
(SLE) (autoimmune disorder) and many more (2).  Identifying the causes of newly found CNS diseases, 
which could be derived from a combination of genetic dispositions and environmental factors, are 
known to be an immensely challenging task. Furthermore, discovering a potential treatment can, 
sometimes, prove to be even more demanding simply because there are no reliable technologies 
known that can help the developed therapeutic substance penetrate the Blood Brain Barrier (BBB), a 
homeostatic defence mechanism designed to protect against pathogens. There is also the tedious 
chain of steps that need to be taken for a drug to be made (optimizing synthetic process, in vitro & in 
vivo studies, clinical studies, fine tuning scale up, product certification and marketing) which make the 
treatment of CNS diseases even more difficult. And even though symptoms of a disease can be 
replicated with consistency in test animals by either applying changes in their behaviour, in their 
physiology through surgery, or in their genome through genetic modification, one other obstacle is 
that there will likely be a discrepancy in the way that different species handle treatment for that 
disease, making it more difficult to draw concrete evidence of the drug´s effectiveness in a human 
organism (Rockenstein et al 2007). 
There is a huge need for advancement in treatment for such diseases as up until today there are no 
medications that lift off the symptoms without any repercussions to the patient. For patients who 
suffer from depression the common treatment is the use of antidepressants such as Zoloft, Effexor 
and Lexapro which function as serotonin reuptake inhibitors. These medications, although they have 
proven to alleviate patients from these symptoms, can also have negative effects such as the serotonin 
syndrome, a reaction based on increased levels of serotonin on the CNS which can lead to diarrhea, 
tremor, muscular abnormalities, enhanced agitation, rapid heart rated, muscle rigidity and many more 
(3).  
For Parkinson’s Disease standard treatment consists of commercial drugs such as Levodopa (L-Dopa) 
a dopaminergic drug whose role is to help with the supplementation of dopamine in the caudate 
nucleus and putamen by helping dopamine cross the BBB. However, the drawback to this drug is that 
most patients with L-Dopa experience complications including motor fluctuations and dyskinesia. In 
search for new ways of overcoming this global epidemic, gene therapy has been on the forefront of 
new therapeutic approaches as it has the potential of providing long term improvements to the PD 
patients conditions. In essence, adeno-associated viruses are the most widely used viral vectors and 
they can be implemented to deliver therapeutics into the brain cells such as aromatic I-amino acid

Project Goals 
In this thesis, the goal is to focus on examining two specific CNS disorders and to merge the fields of 
treatment and diagnostics. To address the issue of possible confusion between those different themes 
of research, the thesis will be split into two separate projects. One will be devoted to treatment while 
the other in diagnostics. 
Project 1: Towards Novel Treatment of Dementia with Lewy 
Bodies 
On the first project, the aim is to find a correlation between the microRNA miR-107 and DLB. miR--
107, is known for its connection with Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) the most known Dementia related 
disease and a disease with histopathological overlap with DLB.  To establish such a connection, a 
peptide-oligo conjugate will be designed in order to silence it and by knocking it down, its role in DLB 
will be identified. 
The peptide-oligo-conjugate was chosen to ensure penetration across the Blood-Brain-Barrier. Free 
oligonucleotides have low chances of making it threw the Blood-Brain-Barrier mainly due to their 
hydrophilic nature and negative net charge that they carry due to the presence of the phosphodiester 
backbone. This makes it difficult for them to approach and interact with the similarly negatively 
charged luminal membrane proteins of the Blood-Brain-Barrier. Furthermore, even though 
phosphorothioates are incorporated on the first and last three oligonucleotides of the strand, the risk 
of enzymatic degradation is not eliminated. These concerns can be addressed by conjugating, through 
Copper-Catalyzed-Azide-Alkyne-Cycloaddition click chemistry, a 3´-alkyne-modified oligonucleotide 
with a Cell Penetrating Peptide (CPP) with an azide group on it´s C-terminus. The CPP due to its 
bulkiness will protect the oligonucleotide from enzymatic degradation in the plasma and will also 
enhance the drugs ability to enter the Blood-Brain-Barrier due to it´s cationic nature. More specifically, 
as the drug approaches the Blood-Brain-Barrier, the drug will interact through Coulombic interactions 
with the negatively charged phospholipid bilayers of the luminal membrane. The linker of the peptide 
and the oligonucleotide consists on intracellular cleavable motif of 3 amino residues which will free 
the oligonucleotide from the peptide delivery system after it crosses the Blood-Brain-Barrier.  
Project 2: Diagnostics for NPSLE 
 
In this project, a pool of 11 synthetic antigens DNA/RNA/LNA were used to their high reactivity with 
SLE serum samples (Samuelsen et al 2018),(Klecka et al 2018). The same antigens will be used to test 
their affinity with 73 NPSLE serum samples. The goal is to highlight the degree of affinity between the 
antigens and the NPSLE samples and how their reactivity compares with the SLE samples. Furthermore 
one of the antigens, the reduced version of Apolipoprotein H was synthesized, characterized with 
Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis (PAGE) and purified with a Molecular Cut off 30kDa filter. For this 
to be achieved, Indirect Enzyme linked immunosorbent Assay (ELISA) experiments were conducted 
with IgG and IgM as the secondary autoantibodies. ELISA assays have been used widely for SLE 
diagnostics (Walsh et al 2000) and it is a widely known diagnostic assay which displays the interaction 
of antigens with autoantibodies with measurable color change.

Dementia with Lewy Bodies Treatment 
 
Dementia 
According to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM)-IV, dementia is defined 
as a decline in memory with impairment of at least one other cognitive function, such as skilled 
movements (limb apraxia), language (aphasia) or executive function (e.g., planning, attention and 
abstract reasoning) (Scott et al 2007). According to the World Health Organization (WHO) study it was 
found that for 2015  there was one new case every 3.2 seconds, a 30% increase compared to a 2012 
WHO study for 2010.  
 
The demographics below show the number of annual cases of people over 60 years old for various 
regions and worldwide (Prince et al 2015).  
Europe : 1,754,536 (25 % ) 
Asia : 4,863,827 (49%) 
North & South America : 1,754,536 (18%)  
Africa : 818,106 (8%)  
World : 9,912,623 
 
Main Types of Dementia Syndromes 
 
Alzheimer’s Disease 
Considered the most common dementia disease, accounting for 60-80% of the total cases. Timely 
identifying the disease’s symptoms can enhance the effectiveness of the applied treatment which has 
demonstrated to greatly improve the function of the patient’s mental capacity and thus, the quality 
of his/her life. (Scott et al 2007)(5) 
Vascular Dementia 
This dementia category mainly is linked to insufficient blood delivery to neural tissues due to minor 
ischemic strokes that have a causative relationship with gradual cognitive decline and memory 
impairment, trouble of speech and listening comprehension, lack of orientation, and vision loss. The 
disease is also coined as multi-infarct dementia or vascular cognitive impairment. (Van Jonnes et al 
2014)(4) 
Dementia associated with parkinsonism like Dementia with 
Lewy Bodies and Parkinson Dementia Disease 
 
Lewy Body diseases tend to be age related with symptoms usually surging in patients over 65 years of 
age although there have been cases which contradict this propensity and are more prevalent in men 
than women (Walker et al 2015). 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
12 
In a systematic review, statistics show that patients of dementia with Lewy bodies account up to 23% 
of all dementia cases however this number is miscalculated since diagnostic methods for this type of 
dementia are still not definitive and many cases  end up being misdiagnosed ( up to 50% of them) (Van 
Jonnes et al 2014), (Palmqvist et al 2009), (Zaccai et al 2005). The major challenge in the diagnosis of 
dementia with Lewy Bodies is lack of knowledge for symptoms that appear before the inevitable 
cognitive and memory problems and the unknown differentiation of its symptoms with Alzheimer’s 
disease.  
  
Up to 80% of patients with Parkinson’s disease progress to dementia and the point-prevalence of 
dementia is roughly 25% in patients with Parkinson’s disease. These two clinical syndromes differ in 
the sequence of onset of dementia and parkinsonism, but with progression both syndromes and 
underlying pathological changes become similar and can be viewed as a continuum rather than 
dichotomous entities. 
  
The characteristic histopathology of DLB cases is the build-up of synaptic protein alpha-synuclein 
aggregates, the main component of the Lewy Bodies and Lewy Neurites, in the brainstem, the limbic 
system and the neocortical regions. There isn’t much knowledge on the Lewy Bodies neuroprotective 
or neurotoxic role and their contributions to the disease since many patients with the aforementioned 
histopathological symptoms may not demonstrate clinical symptoms correlated with any type of 
dementia (Parkkinen et al 2008). 
 
Symptoms of DLB 
 
Dementia with Lewy Bodies is known for having a profound effect in the patient’s cognitive abilities 
who manifests a progressive cognitive decline while also may display signs of parkinsonism, sensory 
malfunction like visual hallucinations, delusions, mood alteration  and decline of motor skills and 
coordination. With respect to the patient’s cognitive ability, most common problems occurring in 
cognitive assessment are increased deficits in attention, executive function and visuospatial skills, 
which are in contrast with the more frequently occurring deficits in episodic memory identified in 
people with AD. Visual hallucinations are a main hallmark when it comes to DLB symptoms making 
them trustworthy signposts of  diagnosis for clinicians and they appear in conjunction with 
deteriorated visual awareness and cognitive fluctuations (McKeith et al. 2017), (Cagnin et al. 2013). 
When it comes to delusions, patients can develop many different types such as phantom delusion ( 
phantom boarder syndrome) , theft delusion and the Capgras syndrome a special type of phantom 
delusion. Phantom delusion, also known as misidentification illusion, can be described as the patient’s 
false impression that imaginary people exist and live in their homes while the theft delusion is when 
the patient thinks that a certain possession of his/hers was taken away from them.  Lastly, the Capgras 
syndrome is when the patient is under the impression that some of his/hers family members are being 
replaced by an imposter. This syndrome is most likely expressed in the moderate stages of the disease. 
In a review of 47 studies and reports,it was found that phantom delusion was primaly expressed in 
DLB while theft delusion was identified as a familiar pattern in AD patients instead (Simard et al 2000). 
RBD (Rapid Eye Movement Sleep Behavior Disorder) is considered a common clinical feature of DLB 
and one of the main clinical diagnostic criterias since it is highly specific and sensitive in DLB compared 
to other forms of Dementia. Furthermore, mood symptoms (depressive, anxious and/or apathetic 
behaviour) were found  to be correlated with DLB with a prevalence ranging from 20-65 % .(Borroni 
et al 2008).

Ways of Assessment for Cognitive Malfunctions 
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the usual go-to choice for the assessment of cognitively 
impaired patients as it can provide evidence for patterns of cerebral atrophy and as well to count out 
intracranial pathologies like brain tumors. 
 
There were also neuroimaging studies which intended to image the dopamine transporter (DaTscan) 
with the use of the tracer 123I-N-fluoropropyl- 2b-carbomethoxy-3b-(4-iodophenyl) nortropane (123I-
FPCIT) and it was discovered that DaTscan can be considered as a potential biomarker since it was 
reduced in the caudate, posterior and anterior putamen compared to AD patients and healthy 
controls. 
 
Another method which can monitor the status of the DLB patients is functional imaging using 
perfusion SPECT and fluoride oxyglucose (FDG) PET highlights the characteristic patterns of cerebral 
blood flow and glucose metabolism. 
 
 
Modes of treatment 
 
Treatment for cognitive symptoms 
 
According to many randomized-controlled trials, greater improvements in the cognitive function of 
DLB in comparison with AD patients were observed when patients were treated with cholinesterase 
inhibitors such as donepezil and rivastigmine (Aarsland et al 2012), (Ikeda et al 2013). 
 
Treatment for neuropsychiatric symptoms 
 
Even though cholinesterase inhibitors, as previously mentioned, have shown  therapeutic ability to 
alleviate cognitive impairment, they are not considered the first course of action for treating 
neuropsychiatric symptoms. In the early stages it is encouraged to begin  with non  pharmacologic 
interventions like physical activities, caregiver training, socializing and environmental modification 
(Aarsland et al 2012),( Matsunaga et al 2015)(Mori et al 2012). 
 
Treatment for motor symptoms 
 
Levodopa is mainly used for treating motor symptoms in Parkinson’s Disease. However, since PD and 
DLB are heavily correlated with each other, it makes sense that treatment for overlapping symptoms 
could possibly coincide. More specifically, it was shown that administering the Levodopa to DLB 
patients has generated some positive results when it comes improving motor skills (benefited around 
30% of DLB patients), however these positive results may be overshadowed by the increase in the risk 
of psychosis and hallucinatory episodes. (Aarsland et al., 2012; Goldman et al., 2008; Lucetti et al., 
2010).

MicroRNAs 
 
Definition, Function and Regulation 
  
 
MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small non-coding RNAs that regulate protein output post-transcriptionally 
meaning that they do not take part in the transcription process (Bartel, 2009). 
 
They are produced from precursor molecules that can form local hairpin structures, which ordinarily 
are processed (via the Dicer pathway) such that a single miRNA molecule accumulates from one arm 
of a hairpin precursor molecule. Micro RNAs may trigger the cleavage of their target molecules or act 
as translational repressors. An abundant amount of targets are managed under the surveillance of 
miRNA a fact that displays their influence in the majority of protein coding genes and that shows that 
they are responsible for regulating every biological process (Friedman et al 2009). Ways of 
understanding the roles of miRNAs is by the effects that is causes on cells when it is over-expressed 
or knocked down. By constructing gene knock-down experiments many correlations have been 
established between miRNAs and many types of processes such as cell proliferation, differentiation, 
normal development, homeostasis and diseases (Lu Y et al 2007). However there has not been any 
evidence of phenotypic consequences when tampering with the expression of miRNAs besides two 
miRNA genes ( miR17-92 and miR-96 ) which have been shown to bring about developmental defects 
in humans upon mutation (Mencia et al2009), (de Pontual et al 2011). Therefore, after extensive 
research of miRNA behavior a conclusive presumption is that rather than acting as genetic switches 
where they would be responsible for completely shutting the expression of certain genes they act as 
rheostats meaning they are fine tuning expression of multitude of different types of genes with the 
aim of determining the cells fate (Vidigal et al 2015). 
 
miR-107  
 
The miR-107 like all miRNAs is a post transcriptional regulator which is found to be interconnected 
with many different types of diseases such as Alzheimers disease (Nelson and Wang, 2010), leukemia 
(Ruan et al 2015), chronic Lymphocytic  and many different of cancer and furthermore it was found 
to play a variety of roles in different tissues and cells, including cell division, metabolism, stress 
responses, and tumor angiogenesis (Jiang, 2017). 
Cancer 
 
Many studies have demonstrated that miR-107 overexpression promotes tumor cell proliferation in 
cancers such as hepatocellular carcinoma as well as the EMT process and tumor cell metastasis in 
gastric cancer cells. According to one research, miR-107 is overexpressed in tissues that have 
hepatocellular carcinoma as it was found targeting AXIN2 a protein that was inhibited in HCC cells. 
The targeting was identified by using algorithms, Western Blot Analysis and Luciferase assay results 
(Zhang et al 2015). Besides that, a different study discovered overexpression of miR-107 in gastric

Neurodegenerative Diseases 
 
Biochemical validation indicates that miR-107 may be involved in accelerated disease progression

Antisense Oligonucleotides and Double 
Stranded RNA Interference 
To silence the miR-107 there are two novel technologies mentioned in the literature for target 
recognition, effective binding and mRNA gene knock down. One is the technology of Anti-Sense 
Oligonucleotides (ASO) which is based on a single stranded deoxyribonucleotide chain complementary 
to the target mRNA.  Through Watson-Crick base pairing, the two strands bind upon meeting each 
other, form a DNA/RNA duplex. Once binding occurs, various silencing mechanisms can take place. 
The other technology is called RNAi interference where the therapeutic is an RNA molecule and with 
the help of the RNA induced silencing complex (RISC), will find the RNA target and bind with it.  
Precautions need to be taken for effective use of ASOs. One important detail when working with them 
is being aware of possible interactions with other nontarget mRNAs. Even partial binding could lead 
the ASO cross reacting with the wrong target activating the RNase and cleaving the inadvertent duplex, 
thus defeating the therapeutic substance’s original purpose as the mRNA target would still be active. 
Furthermore, the secondary and tertiary structure of the target mRNA needs to be taken into account 
in order to prevent unsuccessful binding. A great way of finding an optimal candidate is the use of an 
RNA folding program (Di Fusco et 2019).

Pre-hybridization 
 
Studying the pre-hybridization events is just as significant of a task as it is studying the other two steps 
as it can help elucidate what factors influence the concentration of the PS-ASOs (Phosphorothioate-
Antisense Oligonucleotides) on the target site. By identifying these factors, the chemistries of the PS-
ASOs can be modified to achieve better circulation of the therapeutic on the intracellular 
environment.  
 
The distribution of the ASO is depended on how it travels across the cell and changing the levels of 
proteins, known to bind to PS-ASOs, can demonstrate the change in the subcellular localization and 
the degree of PS-ASO activity. One example of such experiment can highlight the usefullness of this 
method. PS-ASOs are known to interact with TCP1β protein when entering the nucleus. Furthermore, 
once these two bind together, PS bodies start to form, an effect that has shown to decrease the 
potency of PS-ASOs. To validate this observation, if the levels of TCP1β protein reduce it is observed 
that the PS bodies decrease as well and the activity of PS-ASOs become enhanced. Other sites that 
have found to deactivate PS-ASOs are the paraspecles and lysosomes.(Liang et al 2015),(Crooke et al 
2016). 
 
Furthermore, better understanding of which sites in the cell membrane interact with the PS-ASOs can 
provide more clues for identifying the molecular mechanism of entrance.  The key facilitators for cell 
internalization were found to be surface proteins (Liang et al 2015),(Juliano et al 2014),(Juliano et al 
2015) who bind with the PS-ASOS and lipids which indirectly assist in their interactions with the cell 
proteins. All these factors play a role in whether the PS-ASOs will end up in a location which will either 
enhance or dampen its activity (Crooke, 2017). 
 
Hybridization  
As the PS-ASO arrives to the nucleus, it tracks the target RNA and with the help of various proteins 
and lipids whose identity is still not known(Crooke, 2017), binds with it through Watson-Crick base 
pairs. 
Post Hybridization 
 
 
After hybridization has occured, the degradation of the DNA/RNA is the next step. The enzymes  
responsible for that process belong to RNase H family, with them being RNase 1 and RNase 2. Out of 
the two thought it is found that only RNase H1 takes care of the duplex cleavage, a surprising fact 
considering that RNase H2 is much more abundant (Wu et al 2004). One plausible explanation could 
be that RNase 2 is bound to chromatin while RNase H1 is free in locations like the nucleus, cytoplasm 
and mitochondria. In order to confirm RNase H1´s unique role, a mice experiment was conducted were 
they were subjected to hepacytes that specifically knocked-out RNase H1. The end results validated 
that hypothesis since there was no sign of the R-loop preribosomal RNA/DNA duplex cleavage, one of 
its main biological functions (Lima et al 2016). 
 
Furthermore, the enzymes ability to cleave these duplexes can be compromised as there are certain 
types of proteins who tend to compete with the enzyme by binding with the duplex and blocking its 
access to it (Vickers et al 2014). Other mechanisms that can sabotage RNase H1 is the inhibition of 
5´cap formation and splice switching (Bennet et al 2017),(Chan et al 2006). With alternating splicing 
the ASOs will bind near the vicinity of one of the pre-mRNAs exons, sterically denying any access to

RNA interference 
 
There is a different type of gene silencing mechanism when the therapeutic substance is either a 
phosphorothioated RNA or a double stranded small interfering RNA (siRNA). In both cases once the

two oligonucleotide chains are bound, the catalytically active region of the Argonaute protein 
compartment, AGO2,  hydrolyze´s the target with a process called ´´slicing´´ since it cuts the target 
RNA into smaller fragments (Pratt and MacRae, 2009),( Filipowicz, 2005).

Blood Brain Barrier 
History and General Characteristics 
 
The Blood-Brain Barrier is a layer of capillaries surrounding both the Central Nervous System and 
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) and the medium of interaction between the internal environment 
of the CNS and the bloodstream. The blood brain barrier was first discovered by accident from the 
experiments of Paul Ehrlich who observed a lack of colouring in brain tissues of mice after being 
stained with hydrophilic dyes. The intent of the experiments had nothing to do with that discovery as 
his original plan was to estimate the oxygen consumption of the bodily organs. To achieve that he had 
to equally distribute the dyes to the rest of the body by injecting it intravenously. The results of his 
experiment showed that all the organs of the mouse was stained besides the brain, giving him the 
suspicion that there is impermeable layer in the brain that, somehow, protects its cells from the rest 
of the body (Erlich 1885). Any structural analysis of that barrier came much later when the electronic 
microscope was utilized in the works of Reese and Karnovsky (Reese et al 1967). 
 
Today, it is being reported that its surface area can range from 12 to 18 m2 for an average adult making 
it the largest surface area of interaction between the blood and the brain (Begley et al 2005).  There 
is also the blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier, a secondary interface located inside the brain at the 
choroid plexus however in the topic of drug deliver the Blood Brain Barrier is regarded as the favoured 
route for administration since the distance between that interface and the neurons is very short, 
approximately 25 μm. In case of the Blood-Brain -Barrier, it possesses continuous capillaries with 
endothelial cells closely united with the help of tight junctions. These junctions consist of  many types 
of different proteins such claudins, occludins and zonula occludentes (ZO1,ZO2,ZO3) that act as a 
molecular filter, a sieve, that is responsible for blocking most molecules that attempt to pass in 
between the endothelial cells. One particular type of protein that is necessary for the formation of 
those junctions are called cadherins that cluster together and form their own junctions called adhering 
junctions. These help in keeping structural integrity and connection of the endothelial cells intact 
(Abbott et al 2010).

Function of the BBB 
The Blood-Brain-Barrier is built to serve many different uses.  
Ion Regulation 
 
Through the use of specific ion channels and transporters the Blood-Brain-Barrier is able to sustain a 
balance of the intracellular ion concentrations for optical synaptic signalling functioning and the 
stabilization of neuronal environment. Examples of ions that are regulated are K+, Ca2+ which are 
known to be important ions for neuronal function, Mg2+ and H+. However, regulatory function is 
correlated with the age of the mammal. According to one study, where ion-selective electrodes were 
used to measure the ion concentration of anaesthesized animals in situ, it was found that making 
immature mice hyperkalaemic, would raise the K+ concentration on the Interstitial Spinal Fluid (ISF), 
pointing to the conclusion that the increased plasma K+ concentration had an effect on the ionic 
environment of the brain. The same study found the same causative effect on Ca2+ levels when making 
immature hypercalcaemic (Butt et al 1992). 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
23 
 
Neurotransmitters 
 
The Blood-Brain-Barrier controls the influx of neurotransmitters, small chemical messengers essential 
for neurotransmission, which most of them are prevented from entering the brain at the luminal 
membrane of the endothelial cells. The small amount that does enter however, gets immediately 
chemically processed through deamination or O-methylation depending on the type of vessel they go 
through. Deamination occurs in brain microvessels while in pial vessels, which are intracranial vessels 
on the surface of the brain within the pial arachnoid (leptomeninges) (Jones et al 1970), O-methylation 
occurs (Hardebo et al 1980). Excess influx of neurotransmitters can have toxic effects on the 
intracellular environment of the nerve cell. For example, if glutamate, the most used neurotransmitter 
in the body, is released into the brain interstitial fluid in an uncontrollable manner, it could result in 
neuroexcitatory damage to the neuronal tissue. The Blood Brain Barrier also manages to maintain the 
levels of neurotransmitter produced in the brain by controlling their outflux to the bloodstream since 
they are essential for proper brain and body function (Abbott et al 2010).  
 
Toxins 
 
The Blood-Brain-Barrier prevents most macromolecules from going in since it has been reported that 
the concentration of proteins in the CSF is significantly lower than that in the plasma (Begley et al 
2007). It is also protecting the CNS from neurotoxic substances that could fit into the category of 
endogenous metabolites, proteins or substances ingested from food sources. Examples of neurotoxic 
proteins are prothrombin and plasminogen whose presence in the nerves intracellular environment 
can cause neuronal damage. These compounds are, by themselves, harmless to the nerves well being 
and homeostasis. However, its the conversion to the toxic thrombin and plasmin, with the help of 
Factor XA and plasminogen activators respectively, that can have a detrimental effect to the nerves 
physiology and function as it can cause  seizures, glial division and even apoptosis (Gingrich, Traynelis 
et al 2000). Any acceleration to the rate of nerve cell death can be quite destructive as it will most 
likely, outpace, the slow neurogenetic process of an adult organism, increasing the chances for 
neurodegenerative diseases. 
Nutrition  
 
Regarding nutrition, the BBB can either help selectively specific water soluble nutrients and 
metabolites to passively diffuse through or can use specific solute carriers ( SLCs) to pass them throw 
by carrier mediated reflux and the direction of the transport being depended upon nutrients 
concentration gradient (Abbott 2010).

Ways of BBB penetration 
 
Passive Partitioning 
 
 
Many types of lipophilic molecules enter the CNS through passive diffusion and there are many factors 
that influence a molecules candidacy for such a penetration mechanism. From in-silico studies, a 
correlation established between the distribution function and the rate of passive diffuse of molecules 
has been established with the distribution function being a logarithmic ratio of a compound´s 
concentration in the immiscible phases octanol/buffer with both its ionized and deionized form taken 
into account (Known Y, 2001),(Clark et al 2003). Restricting factors could be considered the presence 
of rotatable bonds, the bulkiness of the molecule (molecular weight over 450 Da) (Levin, 
1980),(Pardridge W. et al 1995), high polar surface area molecules and multiple hydrogen bonding 
molecules. Even though polarity does not assist in the BBB permeability, positively charged molecules 
like strong protonated bases have a higher chance of passing threw as they interact with negatively 
charged proteoglycans and phospholipid head groups located on the outer layer of the luminal 
membrane of the endothelial cells (Abbot et al 2010). With regards to blood gases like oxygen and 
CO2 their permeability is blood flow dependent while negatively charged bicarbamate has a very slow 
passive permeability. 
 
ABC transporter efflux 
 
 
Some of the passively diffused molecules however can be stopped by many different type protein 
families who all have a common characteristic. Through the expenditure of adenine triphosphate 
(ATP), the known energy providing molecule and famously coined the molecular unit of currency, 
these proteins abduct undesired molecules such as lipophilic endogenous toxins or xenobiotic 
molecule that have successfully passed the endothelial cells, and effluxing them out into the 
bloodstream. Furthermore, these proteins can efflux substances even with high distribution function 
showcasing how unclear the criteria for successful BBB penetration can be. Sometimes increasing the 
lipophilicity of a drug may make it a better candidate for the ABC proteins to dispose from the CNS. 
(Begley, 2004) 
 
All of them fall under the umbrella of the protein family called ATP- binding cassette transporters, or 
ABC proteins in abbreviation. Under that spectrum lay a variety of protein subfamilies such as the P-
glycoproteins (PGP) which are placed on the luminal membrane and act as guards of the endothelial 
cells, the Multidrug Resistance Associated Proteins (MRP), which depending on its isoform can be 
located either in the luminal or abluminal membrane or even both (Roberts et al 2008), and the Cancer 
Resistance Proteins.  
 
Carrier Mediated Influx 
 
Solute carriers are used to transport polar and large molecules like glucose and amino acids to the 
CNS. These carriers are transport proteins and their location in the endothelial membrane is depended 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
25 
on the type of that proteins as some tend to be expressed on the luminal membrane, some on the 
abluminal membrane and others can be present in both of them. Furthermore, their directionality was 
found to affect the selectivity of the substrates being transported and the direction to which they are 
transported. There are many types of mechanisms when attempting to describe how solute carriers  
transport their substrates. Firstly, there is the unidirectional pathway where the carrier can get the 
molecule both in and out of the cell. Besides that, molecular permeation can be facilitated by ion 
exchange or by a concentration gradient. Lastly, Blood-Brain-Barrier crossing through Carrier 
Mediated Influx can be a reversible process that becomes depended on electrochemical gradients 
(Abbott et al 2010). 
 
Transcytotic Mediated Influx 
 
Larger molecules such as peptides and lipids can pass through the Blood-Brain-Barrier by either 
binding to a receptor or through adsorption. This differentiation slightly alters the mechanism of 
penetration but the end result is the same. In Receptor – Mediated Transcytosis, the macromolecules 
ligands interact with a receptor bound on the outside surface of the luminal membrane and after them 
cluster together a caveolus forms, kickstarting endocytosis. Then, after the caveolus completely 
surrounds them it later turns into a protective vesicle. In Adsorption – Mediated Transcytosis, mostly 
cationic molecules get involved as the positive charge is required to approach, interact with the 
negatively cell surface proteins and induce endocytosis.  All in all, the end result is formation of a 
vesicle that helps the macromolecules to travel across the endothelial cells while at the same time 
staying away from the lysosomal compartments in order to avoid enzymatic degradation. Smaller 
peptides do not have a clear mechanism of entrance in the CNS. The possibilities are many without 
much foundation explanation on why such differentiations in the mechanism occur. They could loosely 
enter the endothelial cells through liquid phase endocytosis, which can be characterized as a 
continuous flow of fluids from the cells environment, or by receptor - mediated transcytosis. They 
could, furthermore, be transported by peptide-specific transporter proteins located on the inside of 
the luminal membrane (Kasten et al 2003). 
 
 
Mononuclear Cell Migration 
 
Besides macromolecules, nutrients and blood gases mononuclear cells have the ability to enter the 
Blood Brain Barrier however it is not a common incident. These mononuclear leukocytes enter either 
by the process of diapedesis, which is when they approach in the endothelial cells in close proximity 
to the tight junctions with the use of their pseudopodia, or from either paracellular or transcellular 
route upon modification of the tight junctions. These modifications occur during inflammatory states 
in the Blood-Brain-Barrier while diapedesis happens when the Blood-Brain-Barrier is infection-free. 
Furthermore, diapedesis can be triggered by the functions of surface proteins such as CD99 and 
junction associated molecules (JAMs) and during that time once the leukocytes enter the cytoplasm 
the luminal membrane quickly shuts off. That way when the abluminal membrane opens to release 
the mononuclear cell, it would prevent fluid filled channels from being created (Wolberg et al 2005). 
On the other hand, the tight junctions become loose from the reogranization and repositioning of the 
claudins and occludins and this effect can induced by either naturally from the signalling of 
neighbouring cells or pharmacologically.

Gene Therapy and the Blood Brain Barrier 
 
Gene Therapy is one of the new branches in the field of medicinal chemistry and is still in its infancy. 
Scientists are continually pressing to develop new ways of effectively delivering genes to the target 
tissues by either tampering with the properties of the therapeutic substance or by partnering it with 
molecular carriers that can act as a protective compartment. These are called delivery systems and 
they are specifically designed to enhance the cellular uptake of the therapeutic and to prevent it from 
degrading from plasma proteins, increasing its bioavailability. Many different types of carriers have 
been used such as viral vectors (Adenoviruses, Adeno-Associated Viruses, Lentiviruses etc.) which, in 
brief, are virus mimickers that enter the target cells and release the gene that they are designed to 
carry. They are excellent at infecting cells, including Brain cells (Hermann et al 2001, Do Thi et al 2004) 
however it could also be the culprit of a myriad of immune responses (Nayak et al 2010). Furthermore, 
a wide variety of nanoparticles are also utilized for delivering gene therapeutics to the brain such as 
polymeric nanoparticles like cationic polyetheimine-DNA polyplexes (Rao et al 2015) and 
poly(amidoamine) (PAMAM). These two are of common use since their presence in the cells tends to 
increase the chloride concentration which results to swelling of the endosome and better release of 
the therapeutic in the cell (Sonawane et al 2003). Focused ultrasound is a unique and non-invasive 
way of adding gene therapeutics into the Blood-Brain-Barrier and it is based on applying acoustic 
energy on specific areas of the brain in order to loosen the tight junctions of the endothelial cells. By 
loosening them up it provides an open avenue for the genetic material to enter and there are studies 
that have achieved such results. (Hsu et al 2013), (Burgess et al 2012), (Wang et al 2015).

There are possible many more ways in which a gene can be assisted inside the brain cells. However, 
this thesis will focus on a different class of carriers called Cell Penetrating Peptides. 
 Cell Penetrating Peptides 
 
The idea of Cell Penetrating peptides came about the time when scientific teams where investigating 
out why certain proteins had the ability to be easily internalized by cells.  More specifically, the 
discovery of TAT protein being able to efficiently penetrate cells started an extensive research 
regarding which structural components of such proteins are responsible for cellular uptake. In the late 
90s, scientists found domains that facilitate transfection called protein transduction domains (Vives 
et al 1997). For TAT, that region is are the 49-57 residues (Park et al 2002). After other similar 
discoveries were made, these special sequences of amino acids were coined Cell Penetrating Peptides, 
a chain of 5-30 amino acids long than can pass membranes through various types of mechanisms.  
 
These penetration pathways can be generally divided into to two main categories (Guidotti et al 
2017). The first type is direct penetration, involves multiple steps and is based on the Coulombic 
interactions of the positively charged CPPs and the negatively charged phospholipid bilayers.  The 
second type of mechanism, which is generally considered as the most accepted way of penetration is 
endocytosis  and it can be divided to caveolin dependent and independent endocytosis. From the 
above description it can regarded as similar to transcytotic mediated influx however, endocytosis is a 
more general term. Furthermore,  the type of mechanism can be related with the size of CPP and its 
physicochemical properties (Maolo et al 2005), (Koren et al 2012). 
 
These amino acid chains can be differentiated into three categories. There are the cationic CPPs which 
are characterized by their Arginine and Lysine rich motifs. There are also amphipathic where besides 
the positively charged basic amino acids, non polar side chain groups exist like those of valine, alanine, 
leucine, isoleucine and proline. Amphipathic peptides can be further categorised into primary, 
secondary and proline-rich peptides (Milleti et al 2012).  Lastly, there are pure hydrophobic CPPs 
which contain only non polarized and neutrally charged residues, critical for the peptides ability to 
enter cells however they are poorly studies in comparison to the other two classes (Guidotti et al 
2017)  
 
CPPs, since then, have been given considerable attention and for good reason. They have proven to 
be reliable delivery factors that can transport a variety of biologically active conjugates like DNAs, 
peptides or proteins and small interfering RNAs, covalently or non covalently (Bechara et al 2013). 
Non covalent complexes rely upon electrostatic and or hydrophobic interaction of negatively charged 
cargoes, like oligonucleotides, and positively charged CPPs. They can, also, effectively bypass the ABC 
protein transporter P-glycoprotein in the BBB and can be therefore be implemented as vectors for the 
delivery of drugs that are substrates of that protein. Lastly, CPPs have been found to increase 
therapeutic effectiveness of drugs whose target tissue is difficult to reach. This has been proven and 
tested with antimicrobials, anti-inflammatory drugs, antineoplastic and neuroprotective agents. One 
other encouraging fact about CPPs is that some of their therapeutics have been even prompted for 
clinical trials ( Meyer-Losec et al 2008).

Enzyme Linked Immunofluorescent Assay (ELISA) 
The diagnostic method used to test the antigen affinity to NPSLE samples was the Indirect ELISA assay 
using IgG and IgM as secondary antibodies. The ELISA assay, an abbreviation for Enzyme Linked 
Immunosorbent Assay, is the most widely used assay method for detecting and measuring 
antigen/antibody binding. The assay is usually carried out in 96 well polystyrene microtiter plates and 
there are two antigen detection methods in ELISA experiments. The first, is the direct detection 
method where an antibody specific to the antigen under examination is conjugated to an enzyme 
substrate and the second is the indirect detection method where an enzyme linked secondary 
antibody is added to the primary antibody. It must be noted that the secondary antibody must have 
specificity not only for the antibody species but also for the isotype of the primary antibody that is 
used. In both cases of ELISA detection methods, a substrate is added to the well to react with the 
detection enzyme and produce a color change (i.e. fluorescence , chemiluminescence, or color 
production). The direct detection method is a simpler and faster procedure which eliminates the 
possibility of cross reactivity of the secondary antibody. However, it is inferior in sensitivity when 
compared to the indirect detection method. The latter in addition to the increased sensitivity, offers 
the possibility of signal amplification which proves quite useful when there are small antigen amounts. 
Furthermore, the potential of cross reactivity of the secondary antibody can be reduced by an 
additional purification step of the secondary antibody that will block the parts that bind to unintended 
IgG thus reducing the existence of non-specific binding (Konstantinou, 2017), (Aydin, 2015),(Castro 
and Gourley, 2010).

Synthesis of 3´alkyne modified antisense oligonucleotides  
 
Antisense oligonucleotides KAS103 and KAS104 were synthesized on automated DNA synthesizer 
PerSpective Bio-systems Expedite 8909. Synthesis was done with standard DMT-Off protocol, and a 
scale of 0.84 μmol of a 3´-alkyne-modifier serinol CPG support. Cleavage of the oligonucleotides from 
the CPG support was performed with the addition 1 mL of ammonium hydroxide solution at 55 oC

times with cold acetone. The oligonucleotides identity and purity were identified by MALDI-TOF mass

Peptide Synthesis on Merrifield Solid Support 
 
Initiating Coupling 
 
0.1 mmol of Chloro-(2'-chloro)trityl Polystyrene Resin is weighed and placed in a plastic reaction 
vessel. The resin is drenched with 2 mL of DCM with the aim of swelling the polymeric beads and 
making them more free-flowing. The resin is stirred on and off for a duration of 15 minutes to prevent 
the beads from forming clusters. 0.4 mmol of Fmoc-protected Amino Acids are weighed in vials and 
dissolved in roughly 1 mL of DCM. Later for activation, 0.6 mmol of DIPEA are added which acts as a 
strong base. This process lasts a total of five minutes. The beads are dried through vacuum suction 
and then the activated amino acid is added to the resin for the first coupling reaction. The resin is 
stirred on and off every 20 minutes in the time span of 2 hours. After the first coupling reaction is 
completed the resin goes through 3 washing cycles with DMF as the solvent, 3 washing cycles with 
DCM and then, again, another 3 washing cycles of DMF. Each cycle lasts for 2 minutes and consists of 
rapid stirring of the resin with the aim of removing unreacted AA from the space between the 
polymeric beads. DMF is used due to its strong interaction with the AA´s making it highly efficient in 
drawing the unreacted AAs away from the solid support. DCM is then used to further expand the 
beads and drag furthermore unreacted AAs which were trapped in between the beads. 
After the washing is done a second batch of activated AA, dissolved in DCM with DIPEA, is added to 
the resin to ensure that all reactive sites will be coupled. The reaction lasts for 2 hours and occasional 
stirring is required. After the second coupling reaction is completed the resin goes through the same 
washing procedure as previously mentioned. When the washing procedure is completed, the resin is 
swelled in DMF. 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
35 
Deprotection of First Amino Acid 
 
After the addition of the first Amino Acid, the next step is cleavage of the Fmoc-protecting with the 
addition of 1mL of 20% piperidine in DMF solution. At first the aforementioned solution is added and 
removed after 2 minutes of stirring for washing purposes. Then it is added, again, but this time for 18 
minutes for successful deprotection. After the deprotection the resin is washed with the same 
procedure as previously mentioned. 
 
Elongation of peptide chain 
 
Next step is the elongation of the peptide. Here, 0.4 mmol Fmoc-AA´s are mixed with 0.4 mmol of 
HATU and dissolved 1 mL of DMF instead of DCM. For activation 0.6 mmol of DIPEA are added. The 
activation of the peptide lasts for 5 minutes. Once the resin is dried, the activated AA is added to the 
resin for a 2-hour coupling reaction. After the coupling, the resin is washed with the same procedure 
to remove unreacted AAs and then the attached AAs are deprotected the same way as the first AA (2 
min washing and 18 min for deprotection). After the deprotection, the familiar washing procedure 
occurs. After the addition of the final amino acid and it´s successful deprotection, the resin is washed 
more thoroughly with 3 cycles of DMF/DCM/DMF/DCM. It is important for the washing solvent to be 
DCM in order to remove traces of DMF in the resin, a solvent with a very high boiling point. This will 
also prevent false yields. 
Cleavage from resin 
 
For cleavage of the peptide, 1 ml of a 20% HFIP in DCM solution is added to the resin. The reaction is 
exothermic and gives a characteristic red color to the resin. The cleavage lasts for 30 min and the liquid 
phase is being extracted from the reaction vessel. This procedure is repeated three times. Later the 
resin is washed thrice to ensure that all the cleaved peptides are being retrieved. All the extracted 
fractions are accumulated in a weighed vial and then they are reduced in N2 flow for the removal of 
HFIP. The vial is weighed again, and the yield is calculated. 
 
Cyclization Reaction 
 
1 eq. of crude peptide is dissolved in a volume between 2 and 4 mL of DMF and is added to a syringe. 
8 eq. of HATU are being dissolved in approximately 10 mL of DMF and later 12 eq. mmol of DIPEA are 
added to the dissolved HATU mixture. The dissolved peptide is added dropwise to the reaction 
mixture. Slow addition decreases the possibility of the peptides from cross-reacting as the solvent will 
more easily interfere between the unreacted peptides and inhibit dimerization. After the peptide is 
added, the reaction is being left overnight to ensure a high yield. The whole reaction is done in room 
temperature and under magnetic stirring. The identity of the cyclic peptide is validated with LC-MS 
chromatography.

Aqueous Work-up of Peptides 
 
The reaction mixture is added in a separatory funnel and washed with 25 mL of Brine solution and 25 
mL of DCM. The mixture is mixed thoroughly with vigorous shaking and after that the organic layer is 
accumulated. This process is repeated 3 times. The organic layer is dried with MgSO4, filtered with a 
pleated filter and added to a weighed round bottom flask. The organic layer is condensed in the rotary 
evaporator with temperature set to 40oC.  
 
Deprotection of Boc-protecting groups 
 
The crude peptide is dissolved in DCM and transferred into a vial. 95% aqueous solution of TFA is 
added to the solution. The amount of acid depends on the volume of the peptide solution. 20% of 
trifluoroacetic acid is the ideal concentration. The vial is sealed with a glass stopper since plastic 
stoppers will degrade and contaminate the reaction mixture. The reaction is done in room 
temperature with magnetic stirring. Validation of full deprotection is done by drawing roughly 5 drops 
of the solution, dissolving it in an appropriate mixture of Acetonitrile/H2O with 0.1 % HCOOH and 
running an LC-MS spectra. After the peptide is fully deprotected the mixture is reduced with nitrogen 
gas. To ensure full removal of the trifluoroacetic acid, the peptide is re-dissolved in DCM and reduced 
in nitrogen flow once more.  
Azide Coupling Reaction  
 
1 eq of peptide, 2 eq. of HATU and 3 eq of DIPEA are dissolved in 5 mL of DMF for activation. After 5 
minutes, 2 eq of 3-azidpropanamine are added to the solution. The reaction is left in room 
temperature, overnight with magnetic stirring.  
 
Deprotection of Peptide-Azide 
 
After the work-up both peptides were dissolved in approximately 4 mL of DCM and later treated, 
initially, with 10 mL of TFA solution with scavengers (88% TFA, 5% H2O, 5% Phenol, 2% TIPS) and left 
overnight under magnetic stirring. 
Copper(I)-catalyzed-alkyne-azide-cycloaddition (CuAAC) 
 
Alkyne modified oligonucleotide (1 eq.) was dissolved in water (500 μL) and transferred to a small 
glass vial equipped with a magnet. Peptide azide (3μM, 3 eq.) was added from a stock solution in milliQ 
water. At the same time aminoguanidine (0.05M, 10 eq.) was added. The reaction mixture was 
degassed for 10 min. with argon before addition of freshly prepared aqueous ascorbic acid (0.05M, 15 
eq.) followed by aqueous Cu(II)TBTA ( 0.01M, 2.5 eq.) and aqueous TEAA buffer (500 eq.) The reaction 
was left stirring overnight at room temperature. Conversion was measured using MALDI-TOF and

3. Results and Discussion 
 
Synthesis of Oligonucleotides 
 
miR-107 is a post transcriptional regulator whose sequence (Figure ) was discovered from the analysis 
of 256 small RNA libraries that were prepared from 26 distinct organ systems and cell types of humans. 
(Landgraf, Rusu, Sheridan et al 2007). It has been associated with many diseases such as Alzheimers 
(Nelson and Wang 2010), cancer (Zhang et al 2015) and leukemia (Ruan et al 2015). miR-107 was 
found to be decreased in the early stages of Alzheimers however there are no reported correlations 
with Dementia with Lewy Bodies. The goal is to investigate whether downregulation of miR-107 will  
enhance DLB symptoms in mice brain cells.  To find the function of the miR-107 in DLB, it has to be 
knocked down by either using ASOs, small-interfering RNAs (siRNAs) or miRNAs. ASOs silence the RNA 
target with antisense mechanism while the siRNAs and miRNAs with the RNA interference mechanism. 
ASOs  were chosen over siRNAs for this study due to simplicity of their synthesis, lower cost and 
robustness (Watts et al 2012). The sequence of the designed ASO is complementary to the mature 
miR-107 and it binds with it through Watson-Crick base pairs. Addition of three phosphorothioates to 
the 5´and 3´end of ASOs increase resistance to enzymatic degradation. In total, four oligonucleotides 
(KAS103,KAS104,KAS105,KAS106) were synthesized with an automated DNA synthesizer PerSpective 
Bio-systems Expedite 8909 on a . KAS103 and KAS105 where the ASOs while KAS104 and KAS106 
where designed to be used as controls by having scrambled sequences.  Furthermore, two amino 
modifiers were added with the aim of incorporating fluorophores to KAS105 and KAS106. These would 
help with trafficking the peptide-oligonucleotide conjugates journey inside the brain cells as well as 
the speed at which it travels.

Characterization of oligonucleotides 
 
MALDI-TOF 
 
One of the main methods for characterizing the identity of oligonucleotides is through MALDI-TOF 
mass spectrometry which detects the mass to charge ratio of analyte ions. These ions are formed 
when the analyte is mixed with a matrix, a small acidic organic compound. After mixing the matrix and 
the analyte together, crystals begin to form. These crystals are irradiated by a pulsed laser with a 
wavelength close to the absorption band of the matrix. The matrix role is to transfer the absorbed 
energy to the analyte. The absorbed energy prompts both the analyte and the matrix to evaporate 
and then, in the gas phase, proton transfer reactions take place leading to the development of single 
charged molecules. Once they are formed, they are guided to an accelerator and their time of flight is 
measured from a mass analyzer. DNA Oligonucleotides, in comparison to peptides, tend to get limited 
signal intensity and mainly due to interaction between the negatively charged phosphate backbone 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
46 
and the alkali cations (Na+,K+) present in the analyte solution. These cations inhibit the excitation of 
the analyte by interfering with the energy transfer process which in turn reduces the signal intensity 
and quality. Furthermore,  DNA oligonucleotides fragment when desorbing excess laser energy. More 
specifically, during proton transfer reactions, nucleobases can become protonated and detach from 
the DNA strand which can dampen signal intensity as well.  
In these experiments,  crude oligonucleotides were characterized on a Autoflex speed Bruker MALDI-
TOF mass spectrometer by mixing 0.5 μL of matrix (aqueous 3-hydroxy-picolinic acid) with 0.5 μL of 
oligonucleotide sample in a MTP Plate AnchorChip 384.

corresponds to the peak of the oligonucleotide while the peak at 3382.606 m/z matches with the half 
mass of the oligonucleotide. The latter occurs commonly in oligonucleotides as the laser can cause the 
DNA chain to fragment in half. Therefore this spectra validates that KAS103 was successfully

Synthesis of Peptides 
 
Synthesis of Linear Core Peptides #1,#2 
 
Through solid phase peptide synthesis, core peptides #1 and #2 were synthesized on a 2-chloro trityl 
resin with the aim of designing a circular peptide core. These peptides contain similar sequences of 
with the only difference being that peptide core #1 has a proline residue while core #2 has a glycine 
residue. Proline will provide more rigidity to the structure of the peptide while glycine will add 
flexibility.  Both options were tested in order to investigate their ability to cyclize and how the single 
change of an amino acid residue can affect the peptides solubility a common problem in solid phase 
peptide synthesis (Behrendt et al 2016). Phenylalanines and tryptophans were used for UV activity 
while the lysines were incorporated to attach the cell penetrating peptides TAT and T7 on the branches 
of the cyclic core. One of the lysines has an Aloc protecting group which is orthogonal to the other 
Boc-protecting Lysine side chains, as it was intended to connect the peptide core with the oligo 
through a peptide-azide cleavable linker (Figure 3.10b). The cleavable linker contains a tripeptide that 
has a sequence H2N-Glu-Val-Cit-OH and it is recognized by an intracellular cathepsin enzyme (Anami 
et al 2018). However, the synthesis never got past the deprotection of side of the lysine and 
tryptophan Boc protection groups.

Synthesis of TAT and T7 with Glu-Val-Cit Cleavable Motif 
 
Due to time constraints, the TAT49-57 transduction domain (sequence: YGRKKRRQRRR) and the T7 
peptide (sequence: HRPYIAH) were synthesized as alternative cell penetrating peptides. Both of them 
have been tested to assist cargos across the Blood Brain Barrier with great results. For example, TAT 
was connected to a cholesterol-terminated poly(ethylene glycol) with the aim of improving the 
delivery of antibiotic to the brain endothelium. The result was the self-assembly of polymeric micelles, 
resembling bile salts, which showed improved cellular uptake in brain cells due to the enhanced 
affinity between the hydrophilic PEG-TAT region and the surface proteins of the brain luminal 
membrane (Liu et al 2008). In another study, cholesterol-TAT nanoparticles, which were meant to 
have antimicrobial activity, showed that they could cross effectively the Blood Brain Barrier for brain 
infection treatment (Wang et al 2010). Furthermore, it was found that when TAT is connected with 
poly( ethylene glycol)-DNA nanoplexes through poly(ethylene glycol) spacers, the cellular uptake and 
gene transfection both increased in primary neurons (Suk et al 2006).  
==PAGE:59==
 
 
59 
 
In case of the T7 peptide, its sequence is reported to show affinity with the transferrin receptor and 
comparable to that of the transferrin protein (Tf) (Lee et al 2001). This receptor is found to be overly 
expressed in the brain in comparison to other cells (Ji et al 2006),(Pang et al 2011),(Zhang et al 2012), 
increasing the possibility that the peptide will meet it´s target. Furthermore, the T7 peptide does not 
compete with the transferrin protein for the occupation of the receptor sites as both interact in 
different locations of the site and the binding of Tf to TfR is reported to assist in the transportation of 
T7. In conclusion, the T7 is found to be perfect for interacting with brain tumor as it can bind both 
effectively with glioma cells and healthy brain cells. In one study, when T7 is conjugated with a poly-
lysine dendrimer and PEG, it acts as a delivery system for a DNA oligonucleotide and showed dual 
targeting capabilities (Kuang et al 2013).

CuAAC reaction: T7-azide and 3´-alkyne modified 
oligonucleotide conjugation 
 
There are many option bioconjugation reaction that connect peptide-azides with alkyne modified 
oligonucleotides. When considering a bioconjugation reaction between the T7 peptide and the 3-
alkyne-modified oligonucleotides, many methods can be utilized starting from the popular CuAAC click 
reaction to the Stain Promoting Azide Alkyne Reaction (SPAAC) and and the Diels Alders reaction, 
whether it be normal or inverse electron demand. 
When considering the Diels Alders reaction, one of its pros is that is metal free, avoiding the raising 
the levels of toxicity in cells. However a big downside to such a method is that it provides heat for high 
conversion, something that can lead to degradation of the oligonucleotides. SPAAC involves a 1,3-
dipolar cycloaddition between an azide and a cyclic alkyne. The strain of the alkyne gives an extra 
driving force forming the desired product. However, the lack of a metal catalyst does decrease the 
rate of the reaction. Lastly the CuAAC reaction is a cheap quick way of clicking the two biomolecules 
but with one negative side being its proclivity towards cytotoxicity when scaling up in cells. In these 
cases in order to avoid such a problem with the use of Tangential flow filtration (Raja, McDonald et al 
2007). In small scaled however, the residual copper does not harm the cells, thus making it a great 
method for the T7-azide and the purified 3´-alkyne-modified oligonucleotides. A depiction of catalytic 
cycle of the CuAAC reaction is shown in Appendix A5.

ELISA Assays 
 
The thesis is about CNS diseases investigating them from two different scopes. Project 1 was focused 
in the treatment of Dementia with Lewy Bodies by investigating the effect the miR-107 knockdown 
with DLB pathology.  Project 2 is concerned with another CNS disorder, the Neuropsychiatric form of 
SLE, by identifying antigens that can timely detect the progression of SLE to NPSLE. The way to do that 
is to conduct indirect ELISA experiments with the use 11 antigens correlated to SLE and determine 
which of them could be used as a diagnostic tool  that could differentiate SLE to NPSLE. 
Reduction of ApoH 
 
According to studies, the disulfide bond formed by termination of cysteine in domain V of ApoH can 
be reduced by thioredoxin-1. Such reduction can create a novel antigen that may have the potential 
of diagnosis in Autoimmune Pathologies.

Indirect ELISA Assays 
 
Indirect ELISA experiments were conducted to measure the degree of affinity between the pool of the 
synthesized 11 antigens and the autoantibodies of NPSLE human patients. The affinity depends on the 
intensity of fluorescence produced by the TMB enzyme that binds to the secondary autoantibody. 
Therefore, there are two factors that come into play. The first is how well the NPSLE antibodies can 
bind with the antigens used for this assay. The second is how well the secondary conjugated 
autoantibody binds to the primary antibodies. That is why all sera samples were examined with two 
different secondary antibodies which were IgG and IgM. The secondary autoantibody which will 
produce the more favorable results, will regarded as a better choice for indirect NPSLE ELISA assays. 
Furthermore, the results of NPSLE assays will be compared with those of SLE and healthy controls in 
order to examine if there are any differences in the reactivities between those two diseases. Different 
reactivities will certainly help in making distinctions between SLE and its Neuropsychiatric as it can 
open the door for the designment of separate diagnostic methods.

4. Conclusions – Future Perspectives 
 
In this thesis the aim was to address CNS diseases from two different points of view The first would 
be the treatment of Dementia with Lewy bodies and the second is the diagnosis of NPSLE.  
MiR-107 is known for its association with Alzheimers showing decrease in its expression during the 
initial stages of the disease.  No studies as of yet have shown any correlation of that mRNA with DLB. 
In view though that these  two types of dementia are known to have histopathological overlap, it was 
decided to investigate whether miR-107 downregulation will also cause an increase DLB symptoms.To 
achieve antisense oligonucleotides were chosen as inhibitors over siRNA and miRNAs due to simplicity 
in their synthesis, low cost and resistance in plasma nucleases. Furthermore phosphorothioated 
phosphoramidites were included in the two ends of the DNA strand to further increase resistance to 
enzymatic degradation. In total, four oligonucleotides (KAS103,KAS104,KAS105,KAS106) were 
synthesized through automated solid phase oligonucleotide synthesis with an 3´-alkyne modified 
serinol CPG support. Two of them where antisense to the sequence of mature miR-107 (KAS103, 
KAS105) and while the other two, having scrambled sequences,  were used as controls (KAS104, 
KAS106). Oligonucleotides KAS105 and KAS106 were further modified with the addition of two 
different amino modifiers which would later be attached to fluorophores for tracking. 
Oligonucleotides KAS103,KAS104 and KA105 were successfully characterized with MALDI-TOF mass 
spectrometry and purified with Reverse Phase HPLC. However in the case of KAS106 there were 
difficulties in deprotecting its amino modifier and characterizing it. The alkyne functional group was  
used for the bioconjugation of the oligonucleotides with cell penetrating peptides which assist the 
oligonucleotides to cross the Blood Brain Barrier. For future oligonucleotide synthesis, measuring the 
coupling efficiencies would help compare the yields of the obtained oligonucleotides with the general 
benchmark since there is a correlation between that and the coupling efficiency (6). 
In the beginning linear core peptides #1 and #2  were synthesized by Fmoc-solid phase peptide 
synthesis and later cyclized in solution in presence of HATU and DIPEA. Successful cyclization was 
validated by LC-MS analysis and after aqueous work up, were treated with 20% TFA solution for Boc 
deprotection. The deprotected cyclic peptides were successfully characterized with MALDI-TOF mass 
spectrometry and purified with RP-HPLC.  
Furthermore, TAT-Glu-Val-Cit and T7-Glu-Val-Cit peptides were successfully synthesized and 
characterized with MALD-TOF mass spectrometry but weren’t able to be purified with RP-HPLC. In 
order to be bioconjugate with the 3´.alkyne modified oligonucleotides, the crude peptides were 
coupled in solution with 3-azido-1-propanamine for the introduction of an zázide group on the C 
terminus. After work up of the coupling reaction, the peptide-azides were treated with a TFA solution 
with scavengers (88% TFA, 5% H2O, 5% Phenol, 2% TIPS) for full deprotection. The T7-azide was 
successfully deprotected, characterized and purified while on the other hand when the TAT peptide 
underwent purification, there were no signs of the fully deprotected compound in the examined 
fractions. One possible explanation could be the peptide was decomposed after being subjected to a 
week long of exposure of concentrated TFA solution. Therefore to increase the ease of full 
deprotection of TAT and avoid long exposure to harsh acidic conditions, a different protecting group 
than MTR should be used to protect the guanidino group of arginine. A good option would be PBF as 
it has been reported to be easily removed in acidic conditions (Carpino et al 1993). 
Lastly, the purified oligonucleotides KAS103, KAS104 and KAS105 were successfully conjugated with 
the T7 peptide through CuAAC click reaction forming peptide-oligonucleotide conjugates. Even though 
the products were purified by RP HPLC and their structures identified by MALDI-TOF mass 
spectrometry, the results could not be quantitated due to very low yield. This could be attributed to 
the insufficient purity and amount of the T7 peptide which proved to be contaminated with impurities 
when tested with RP HPLC.  
In conclusion, it has been proven that such peptide-oligo-conjugate can be formed. For better yields, 
sufficient amount of  T7 should be obtained and its purity should validated before starting its 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
77 
bioconjugation with oligonucleotides. Furthermore, the yield of peptide-azides could be raised more 
by having the azide-reaction being done in DCM rather than DMF. That way the following workup step 
would less challenging compared to when using DMF where there was difficulty in distinguishing and 
extracting the two layers. 
Once the peptide oligonucleotide conjugate is produced and purified the next step is to transfect brain 
DLB mice cells  with the aim to inhibit miR-107 and record changes in DLB symptoms. These symptoms 
which are connected to neurodegeneration are not yet well understood  however, according to clinical 
data, symptoms may be explained by synaptic pathology. More specifically a decrease in 
neurotransmitter release could be a potential biomarker for DLB pathology as the overexpression of 
a-synuclein can lead to synaptic dysfunction. 
Lastly, the cell penetrating peptides could be connected to a synthetic replica of miR-107, instead of 
an ASO, with modifications  that will ensure stability of the designed RNA molecule, in order to observe 
the effect of miR-107 overexpression in DLB mice brain cells. If the results demonstrate that its 
overexpression alleviates DLB symptoms it will validate the hypothesis the miR-107 is responsible for 
the downregulation of the β-amyloid-cleaving enzyme 1 which is known for production of neurotoxic 
proteins. 
 
 
Neuropsychiatric lupus (NPSLE) is the most prevalent form of SLE yet probably the least understood. 
It affects 14% to over 80% in adults and 22% to 95% in children (Aniala et al 2001),(Brey et al 2002) 
and most of its clinical manifestations, are nonspecific complicating the diagnosis, clinical assessment 
and treatment planning (Popescu and Kao, 2011). It is therefore evident that early diagnosis of NPSLE 
would prove to be very useful for clinicians to tailor treatment modalities for the 
syndrome.Furthermore, It is estimated that 28% to 40% of adult NPSLE manifestations develop before 
or around the time of the diagnosis of SLE and 63% occur within the first year after diagnosis (Hanly 
et al 2007). Thereby, developing diagnostic tools for NPSLE, not only will set the foreground for its 
treatment but it will also serve as a forewarning for the onset of SLE pathology. 
 
Indirect ELISA assays with a pool of 11 synthetic antigens, known for their reactivity with SLE, where 
conducted with the aim to compare their affinity to NPSLE autoantibodies. This will highlight 
difference in reactivities between NPSLE and SLE.For HRP secondary autoantibodies IgG and IgM were 
used to find the most suitable secondary antibody for those assays. In total 73 NPSLE blood samples, 
20 SLE and 16 healthy controls were used and each sample was duplicated for reproducibility 
purposes. The results for IgG indirect ELISA experiments showed that for antigens dsD4, dsD5 and 
dsHUV the mean absorbances of NPSLE samples were greater than those of healthy controls with 
dsHUV showing the most significant difference. These antigens had lower affinity for NPSLE samples 
compared to SLE. Thus the results show a distinction between NPSLE vs healthy controls and NPSLE 
vs SLE. 
For the IgM secondary antibody indirect ELISA assays, only two antigens, ApoH and reduced ApoH 
showed enhanced affinity of NPSLE samples compared to healthy controls with ApoH showing the 
greater reactivity. In addition, there was no significant difference between the affinities of NPSLE and 
SLE samples making the differentiation of these  two more difficult. In conclusion IgG is the preferred 
secondary autoantibody since it gave more positive results giving better distinction between NPSLE, 
SLE and healthy controls. 
At this point it is noteworthy to point out that in order to enhance the statistical validity of these 
assays it would be preferable to increase both  SLE and healthy control samples to numbers similar to 
those of NPSLE samples. Another improvement in this assay to use other secondary autoantibodies 
such as IgE since the presence of IgE autoantibodies to dsDNA has been reported previously in several 
lupus patients (Bayry, 2016). Furthermore more clinical data such as age, gender,race in the examined 
samples would provide more clues that would elucidate developmental tendencies of NPSLE in 
comparison to SLE.

A2. Reaction Mechanisms of Oligonucleotide Synthesis  
 
Step 1. Detritylation 
The TCA catalyzes the removal of the DMT protecting group by protonation of the 5´oxygen group. 
The cation formed on the oxygen makes the nucleotide a very good leaving group as the π*c-o is 
conjugated with the π system of the aromatic rings. Furthermore, the presence of methoxy groups 
provide an extra push to the breaking of the bond between the tertiary carbon and 5´oxygen. The 
positive charge in the oxygen of the methoxy group can be stabilized through resonance making it a 
stable carbocation.(6) 
 
 
==PAGE:87==
 
 
87 
Step 2. Activation of Phosphorus (III) 
In order for the new nucleotide to be added to the oligonucleotide backbone, the 5´end of the terminal 
nucleotide has to attack the phosphorus through a Sn2 reaction. For this to occur however the 
phosphorus needs to have a good leaving group. Since the tertiary amine is a poor leaving group, the 
propensity of a nucleophilic attack to the phosphorus is decreased since the nitrogen cannot stabilize 
the negative charge after it breaks its bond with the phosphorus, thus increasing the chances of 
nitrogen reconnecting with the phosphorus and making the coupling reaction reversible. To avoid 
that, the new nucleotide is first mixed with DCI (4,5-dicyanoimidazole) whose role is to activate the 
phosphorus as it is a great leaving group compared to the tertiary amine, thereby improving the 
phosphorus´s electrophilicity (6). 
 
 
 
 
Step 3. Coupling Reaction 
After the tetrazole activates the phosphoramidite, the 5´end attacks the phosphorus, elongating the 
oligonucleotide backbone. Later on, an acid base reaction occurs. 
 
==PAGE:88==
 
 
88 
 
 
 
Step 4. Capping of Unreacted Oligonucleotides 
In case any 5´hydroxy groups remains unreacted it could pose a threat for undesired side reactions 
with future nucleotide couplings. In order to prevent that from happening, the hydroxy groups are 
acetylated by treating the resin with a mixture of acetic anhydride and methyl imidazole. The aromatic 
compound is used as a catalyst for the reaction as it makes the carbonyl of the acetyl group more 
prone to 1,2-additions. Once the oxygen attacks the electrophillic carbonyl the C=O breaks and later 
reforms, kicking out the methyl imidazole (6) 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
89 
 
 
 
 
Step 5. Oxidation of Phosphorus (III) 
 
The final step of the synthetic cycle ensures the stability of the oligonucleotide backbone. Phosphorus 
(III) is very reactive and needs to be oxidized to keep the oligonucleotide chain safe from breaking 
apart. In this case the oxidizer used is a Iodine/Water/Pyridine/THF solution. Molecular iodine firstly 
approaches phosphorus (III) and reacts with a Sn2 type mechanism. The presence of pyridine initiates 
ionization of water molecules and the resulting hydroxy groups attack the positively charged 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
90 
phosphorus (III). The P-I bond breaks fairly easily as iodine is a great leaving group. Lastly, pyridine 
deprotonates the hydroxy groups and the resulting extra electron pair fills the πp-o molecular orbital, 
creating a very stable P=O bond. 
 
 
A3. Reaction Mechanisms for Solid Phase Peptide 
Synthesis 
 
 
 
Step. 1 Coupling Reaction onto Merrifield Resin 
 
DIPEA is the activator of choice for this coupling reaction as it is a strong non-nucleophilic base. After 
the deprotonation, the amino acid is added to the resin and a rapid exothermic reaction occurs with 
the carboxylate attacking the tertiary carbocation which is in equilibrium with the formation of the C-
Cl bond. 
 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
91 
 
 
 
 
Step 2a. Elongation of Peptide. Activation of newly added Amino Acid 
Prior to the coupling of the new amino acid with the anchored peptide, it has to become activated. 
This time however, the carboxylic group will act as an electrophile rather than nucleophilic carboxylate 
in the first coupling reaction. The presence of the OH makes the carboxylic group nucleophilic mainly 
due to its instant deprotonation giving form to a carboxylate. Dissolving the amino acid with HATU 
helps making that change in its chemistry by turning the carbonyl group into a very good electrophile. 
Firstly, the carboxylate attacks the imine through a 1,2-addition and later one of the tertiary amines 
lone pair fills the πc-N molecular orbital, forming a stable C=N bond and releasing the 1-hydroxy-7-
azabenzotriazole. This triazole later hydrolyzes the ester group with another 1,2-addition releasing 
tetramethylurea.  
==PAGE:92==
 
 
92 
 
 
Step 2b. Elongation of Peptide. Coupling Reaction 
Once the activated amino acid is added to the solution, the lone of the pyridine nitrogen, since it does 
not contribute to the aromaticity of the ring, is more susceptible to hydrogen bonding with the N 
terminal of the attached peptide. This interaction brings the primary amine closer to the activated 
carbonyl group, improving the chances of peptide bond formation. The amine attacks the carbonyl 
group and the 1-hydrozy-7-azabenzotriazole is released right after the C=O is reformed. 
 
 
==PAGE:93==
 
 
93 
 
 
Step 3. Fmoc Deprotection through treatment with 20% piperidine solution 
The deprotection is initiated with the deprotonation of the only sp3hybridized carbon in the entire 
aromatic section of the protecting group. The enhanced acidity of this proton can be showcased 
through resonance structures as the tertiary carbanion can be stabilized across the aromatic system. 
The result of this E1cb reaction is the formation of an alkene, CO2 and the deprotected amino acid.
