Abstract
Since 1975, transplantations have been a vital element of the medical field. The core
of matching the patients with donors of organs or stem cells is fitting them based
on HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) types. With vast polymorphism and minimal
dissimilarities between different alleles, determining types of antigens is a challenging
matter in this field. Long-read sequencing approaches can compensate for these issues.
With the sequencing precision of the Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) improving
yearly, it is an ideal base for HLA typing.
A bioinformatic pipeline was developed in Nextflow - a software suitable for work-
flow management, incorporating a k-mer mapping alignment tool - KMA for ONT
sequencing. With double typing against the IMGT-HLA database, it returns G groups
of the diploid genotype alleles for seven loci: HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, HLA-DRB1,
HLA-DQB1, HLA-DQA1, and HLA-DPB1.
The pipeline was evaluated on data consisting of samples from 90 Danish individ-
uals. The average concordance accuracy of 93.8% was observed for six loci (excluding
DPB1). For all of the loci, the accuracy was 81.75%. After manually analysing incor-
rectly typed loci (mainly not matched with any G group), the concordance accuracy
was determined to reach over 95.8%.
With such promising results, the presented pipeline can serve as a basis for a
reliable research method or in a clinical setup achieve a quick and reliable matching
of a donor to a stem cell transplant receiver.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Transplantations, particularly hematopoietic stem cell transplantations, have been
a prominent element of medicine since 1975 [1].
The crucial milestone in setting
this new era was understanding the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) and
matching the donor with the patient [2]. The human MHC is a set of genes encoding
human leukocyte antigens (HLAs), which are a part of the innate immune system and
are crucial for information transmission to the adaptive immune system. Matching
patients by HLA gene alleles significantly decreases graft rejections [3].
Gnomic sequence segments of HLA alleles are very polymorphic. Therefore, align-
ment to reference genomes has been largely unfeasible in typing methods utilising
next-generation sequencing (NGS) methods [4]. It has also been challenging to cor-
rectly phase alleles - identify alleles from paternal and maternal chromosomes. If an
individual gets two different genetic variants from parents, they are heterozygous at
the locus. When both alleles are exact, they are homozygous. Increased coverage en-
ables heterozygous positions’ phasing [5]. Therefore, a need for long-read sequencing
has emerged. The third-generation sequencing methods do not rely on the alignment
of short sequences, and Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) offer long enough reads
to cover the complete HLA genes.
Developing a non-commercial system for HLA typing for Rigshospitalet was the
primary motivation for this thesis.
However, a research incentive was to make it
possible to cheaply and in a high-throughput manner analyse the genomes of the
participants of the Copenhagen Aging and Midlife Biobank (CAMB) project [6].
This chapter introduces the background knowledge needed to understand the
project thoroughly.
The first section covers biological topics with a focus on the
role of the HLA complex in the immune system and its importance in transplanta-
tions. The second section sheds light on the technical information - development of
sequencing technologies and alignment methods.

1.1
Biological Background
1.1.1
The Immune System
A pathogen invading the organism first has to overcome surface barriers, such as skin,
mucus, and enzymes. If it succeeds, it faces the next level of defence - the immune
system. The most substantial role of the immune system is to defend the organism
against diseases. It is an organisation of specialised cells and molecules, and there
are two main types of responses to attacking microorganisms - innate (natural) and
adaptive (acquired) [7].
The innate immune system is responsible for the early immune response.
It
consists of natural killer cells, phagocytic cells (macrophages, monocytes, and
neutrophils), inflammatory mediators-realising cells (basophils, mast cells, and
eosinophils), and specific proteins [7].
While innate responses lack immunologic memory, adaptive responses improve
based on exposures to different pathogens [7].
Cells responsible for the acquired
responses include T lymphocytes, the effectors of cellular immune responses, and
B lymphocytes, antibody-producing cells. After lymphocytes are developed in the
thymus and bone marrow, they move to the spleen and lymph nodes, which capture
antigens from lymph and blood. The adaptive responses, which usually begin in these
two sites, are often influenced by signals from the innate immune system. They can
be provided indirectly by antigen-presenting cells or directly by circulating pathogens.
As lymphocytes are very mobile, they can then migrate to different sites of the body
to employ effector functions [8].
Proteins that serve as a pivotal bridge between the innate and adaptive systems
belong to a major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class. These proteins bind short
peptide fragments of pathogens and introduce them to T cells. Specifically, CD8+
T cells (cytotoxic T cells) interact with the MHC class I, and CD4+ T cells (helper
T cells) interact with the MHC class II. The T cells then trigger the immunological
response. As the system is very heterogeneous, it can protect the organism against
diverse types of pathogens: the cells infected by viruses are killed, macrophages are
activated to kill bacteria, and activated B cells produce antibodies that eliminate
extracellular pathogens [3,8,9].
1.1.2
Human Leukocyte Antigen
The human version of the major histocompatibility complex is the human leukocyte
antigen complex (HLA). Notably, it is known for prodigious polymorphism, which
reflects a vast diversity of the responsible genes within a population. The importance
of examining genetics behind the HLA lies in its role in determining transplantation
outcomes, as it governs tissue compatibility and thus influences whether the recipient’s
body embraces or spurns the grafted tissues [3]. Variations in the complex are also
associated with diseases, such as autoimmune diseases or cancers [10].

1.1.2.1
HLA Gene Complex
The gene complex of HLA is a 3.6 million base-pairs-long genomic region located on
chromosome 6 [11]. It consists of more than 200 genes, most of which are not involved
in immunity. Just over 40 genes encode leukocyte antigens (Figure 1.1).
Figure 1.1. Location of HLA genes on chromosome 6 (From [12])
The human leukocyte antigens fall into three categories: class I, II, and III. They
differ structurally and functionally. Classes I and II are formed of an α- and a β-chain.
In class II, both are encoded on chromosome 6 and are equivalently large, whereas
a gene of the smaller β-chain of class I, beta2-microglobulin, lays on chromosome
15 [12]. The HLA class III genes are located between the genes of classes II and I.
The α-chain of class I consists of five main domains: α1 and α2, the peptide-
binding domains; α3, the immunoglobulin-like domain; a trans-membrane region;
and a cytoplasmic tail (Figure 1.2). Exon 1 encodes the leading peptide. Exons 2,
3, and 4 encode for the domains of α1, α2, and α3, respectively. Exon 5 encodes for
the trans-membrane domain, and exons 6 and 7, the cytoplasmic tail [13]. There are
over 20 loci of class I genes of the HLA complex. They are expressed by somatic cells.
The three major genes (classic class I genes) are HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-C [12].
Both of the class II chains, α and β, have four domains: α1 and β1, peptide-
binding domains; α2 and β2, immunoglobulin-like domains; the trans-membrane re-
gion; and a cytoplasmic tail (Figure 1.2). For both chains exon 1 codes for the leader
sequence, exon 2 for α1 and β1, and exon 3 for α2 and β2. Exon 4 is responsible for

Nomenclature of HLA alleles
Gene fragment encoding domains of both classes
are distinguished by numbers in their names, HLA-A1 or HLA-DRB1. An asterisk
separates the gene from allelic information, consisting of four numbers isolated by
colons. The first field corresponds to the serological antigen carried by the allotype.

field, their nucleotide sequences differ, and the encoded proteins alter. If the coding
region sequences differ only by synonymous substitutions (the amino-acid sequence
remains the same), the variants are distinguished by the third field. Alleles which
differ in the intronic region are differentiated in the fourth numeric field.

the differences in the gene expression levels. The used letters are: “N” - not expressed,
“L” - low cell surface expression, “S” - soluble, secreted protein not found on cell

1.1.2.2
HLA Protein Structure and Function
The HLA class I and II proteins, antigens, form structures suitable for transporting
peptides. Chains α1 and α2, by folding into α helices, create a so-called peptide-
binding groove for class I, and chains α1 and β1 for class II (Figure 1.2). The groove
of class I is closed and it houses shorter peptides (7-15 residues). In the endoplasmic
reticulum, the HLA class I antigens are loaded with peptides from proteins typically
degraded within the cell. These peptides are carried to the surface of the cell by the
antigens. There, they present the peptides to cytotoxic T cells (CD8+). They play
an essential role in the immune response against infected or malignant cells [12].
The class II pocket is open on both sides and can therefore bind longer chains.
The HLA class II proteins are carried in lysosomes to the cytoplasmic region. The
lysosomes intersect with endosomes, where proteases degrade exogenous proteins, cre-

then exposes it on the cell surface for helper T cells (CD4+). The HLA class II anti-
gens are found in antigen-presenting cells and are vital in responses to extracellular
pathogens [12].
1.1.2.3
HLA and Transplantations
Already in the 1960s, antibodies to HLAs were recognised as a cause of rejections of
allografts [17,18]. MHC molecules are known for their ability to protect the organism
against pathogens recognised as non-self. Unfortunately, they can also induce immune
responses against MHC proteins of non-identical organisms when grafting tissues
or organs.
HLA-specific antibodies can be developed after blood transfusions or
during pregnancy. Patients can also develop donor-specific antibodies de novo after
transplantation. Developing the HLA-specific antibodies results in a high probability
of rejecting a graft, which raises the importance of detecting anti-HLA antibodies [3].
A study by Williams et al. analysed over 189.000 deceased donor kidney transplant
cases between 1987 and 2013, using the United Network for Organ Sharing data. A
significant linear correlation between the number of HLA mismatches and hazard
ratios was identified, which impacts the survival of allografts, with 13% increase in
transplantation failure for one mismatch and 64% for six mismatches (considering
only HLA-A, -B, and -DR) [19].
Hematopoietic stem cell transplantations offer immense hope for numerous pa-
tients, yet it remains a procedure entangled with various complexities. One of the
challenges is the management of graft-versus-host disease risks. Immune cells can
recognise whether cells are “self” or “non-self”, and information about it is encoded
in the HLA genes. When the immune cells of the transplanted tissue recognise the host
cells as “non-self”, they proliferate to cause tissue damage to the host’s organs [20].
Some of the genes are less polymorphic than others, thus, the risk of mismatching
them between the patient and the donor is significantly lower. As genomic sequencing
is costly, typing the whole HLA genomic region would be supernumerary. Therefore,
only the most polymorphic genes are examined - classic class I genes: HLA-A, -B,
and -C, and three class II genes: HLA-DR, -DQ, and -DP. The most important are
polymorphisms within regions of residues forming peptide-binding grooves, which for
class I are exons 2 and 3, and for class II is exon 2 of β-chain genes. The α gene of DR
is not polymorphic, and for DQ and DP it shows a much lower level of polymorphism
in comparison to the β gene [21].
1.1.2.4
HLA-associated diseases
HLA genes are documented to be associated with susceptibility to various diseases.
The MHC is linked to more diseases than any other genomic region. These diseases
fall into three principal categories: autoimmune, infectious, and malignant.
Over 50 years of studies have shown a strong association between autoimmune
diseases and HLA class II especially. It is essential to underline that they were mainly

studied in a white population. Type I diabetes is connected with DRB1 and DQB1
genes, and particularly combinations of DQA1*05:01-DQB1*02:01 and DQA1*03:01-
DQB1*03:02 [22]. Furthermore, DRB1*15:01 is the primary risk factor corresponding

and inflammatory bowel disease [30,31].
Even though no HLA genes have been associated with the incidence of cancer,
some alleles are known to worsen the disease’s progression. Tumours can alternate
and dysregulate the HLA genes, causing aberrant expressions. Especially A*02 is
linked to poor prognosis for lung, melanoma, ovarian, or prostate cancer.
Non-classical HLA class I molecules G, E, and F play a vital role in regulating the
immune system. Tumours can manipulate these molecules to inhibit the activity of
the system and thus save the affected cells, particularly by causing null alleles of the
respective genes. For the suppressive role of these molecules, they are investigated as
a potential target for anticancer immunotherapy [32,33].

1.2.1
DNA Sequencing
1.2.1.1
First-Generation Sequencing
Frederick Sanger was the first to determine a sequence of insulin protein in the 1950s
[34]. With further developments of Edman [35], this novel method started a new era
in biology, and with different approaches, it was further designed for examining RNA
and DNA [36–41].
The DNA sequencing starts with preparing single-stranded DNA templates and
annealing short primers. They are then added to a reaction mixture of DNA poly-
merase, deoxynucleotides (dNTPs) and small amounts of labelled chain-terminating
dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs).
New DNA strands are synthesised.
Incorporated
ddNTPs prevent elongation of the chain as they lack a hydroxyl group at the 3’
carbon. The final product is a mixture of DNA strands of different lengths. The frag-
ments are separated by gel electrophoresis by their sizes. Analysing the radioactive or

fluorescent labels of ddNTPs, the sequence of nucleotide bases can be determined [42].
The principles of the method are shown in Figure 1.4.
Later presented by Staden [43] and ameliorated by Messing [44], shotgun se-
quencing, incorporating random clone sequencing and alignment assembly, majorly
increased the pace of generating sequences.
Automated by Smith et al. [45], the
method incorporated fluorescence detection with different fluorophores specific for all
DNA bases. These innovations were a starting point for massively parallel sequencing,
also called second-generation or next-generation sequencing (NGS).

1.2.1.2
Second-Generation Sequencing
A key shift compared to previous solutions is utilising multiplexing, which is sequenc-
ing many samples simultaneously. The second-generation sequencing methods do not
use bacterial cloning, but in vitro amplification, and use “sequencing-by-synthesis” -
replicating template sequences by DNA polymerase [46,47]. The read length for tech-
nologies of this generation varies from 35 to 700 base pairs [48]. An NGS workflow
consists of three steps: library preparation, sequencing, and data analysis [49]. NGS
enabled the generation of large volumes of data with every run and reduced the cost
per base. The main limitation of these technologies is a short length of reads [50].
Leading technologies were developed at Illumina, Ion Torrent, and Roche 454.

fluorophore labels after excitation by laser for reading the sequence [51]. Ion Torrent

1.2.1.3
Third-Generation Sequencing
The third generation of sequencing characterises the ability to sequence long reads.
The two technologies representing this generation are PacBio and Oxford Nanopore
Technologies (ONT).
In the PacBio workflow, a double-stranded DNA, with hairpin-like adaptors lig-
ated to the ends of the molecule, is transformed into a single-stranded circular DNA
called SMRTbell. The SMRTbells are loaded on a chip - an SMRT cell consisting
of sequencing units called zero-mode waveguides. They provide just enough volume
suitable for light detection. Replication is possible with a single polymerase immo-
bilised at the bottom of each unit. Nucleotides, labelled fluorescently, are added to
the cell, and every time a base is incorporated into the strand by the polymerase, a
light pulse is emitted. The recorded colour pulse sequences are translated to DNA
sequences [55]. A scheme presenting the idea is shown in Figure 1.6.
Figure 1.6. The principle of the PacBio sequencing technology. Single-stranded circular
DNA transforms are located in the wells of the SMRT cell chip. Each well has a polymerase
secured at the bottom. When the polymerase attaches fluorescently labelled nucleotides to
the DNA strand, light pulses of different colours are emitted. (From [55])
Contrastingly, The ONT technology is based on membrane channel proteins de-
rived from bacteria, which have been shown to detect ionic current blockades by
homopolymers of nucleic acids. They consist of a nanopore and a motor protein. The
latter controls the flow of the nucleotide strand. The nanopores utilised in the devices
have diameters of around 1.2nm and are laid in a synthetic membrane [56].
The membrane with nanopores separates two chambers with differently charged
solutions. Voltage is applied between the two chambers, inducing an ionic current
to flow through the pores.
With the current, single-stranded, negatively charged
molecules are driven through the pores, from the negatively charged side to the pos-
itively charged side of the membrane (shown in Figure 1.7(a)). The motor protein
is employed with the pore, slowing down the flow of the DNA strain by ratcheting.
The pores are sensitive to masses of the nucleobases, and as a single strain of a DNA
molecule moves through it, the electrical field shifts, and the ionic current changes
depending on the nucleobase, as seen in Figure 1.7(b) [56,57].
==PAGE:21==

Figure 1.7. Nanopore sequencing principles. With the ionic current, a negatively charged

The most significant advantages of the technology are sequencing in real-time,
shorter sequencing time, shorter library preparation, and the portability of the devices
[58]. The main limitation is the relatively high error rate, with spurious insertions and
deletions often observed. The base-calling accuracy of the reads attains 91−97% [59].
Besides the random errors induced by base-calling algorithms’ defects, systematic
errors are noticed for homopolymer regions. They occur as the speed of moving the
molecule through the pore is not entirely constant [60]. However, the error rates are
being lessened yearly [61].
1.2.2
Alignment
Sequence alignment is a fundamental method in bioinformatics used to identify similar
regions between sequences that may indicate functional or structural aﬀinities. It is
also crucial for understanding evolutionary relationships between genes or proteins.
There are two main alignment approaches - global and local.
Developed in 1970, the heuristic Needleman-Wunsch algorithm represents the
global alignment of two sequences. A matrix where one sequence is horizontal and the
other vertical is constructed. An optimal alignment is found by creating pathways
and maximising a similarity score, with interruptions of the pathway penalised [62].
The Smith-Waterman algorithm was built upon the principles of the Needleman-
Wunsch algorithm in 1981 and was designed for local sequence alignment. Distinctly
from the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm, instead of complete sequences being aligned,
shorter similar segments are sought [63]. It is especially advantageous in comparisons
of sequences with conserved regions but significantly different otherwise.

1 Introduction
1.2.2.1
K-mer Alignment
One of the challenges in the current sequence alignment is the redundancy of many
databases. There is a lack of explicit methods for decision-making when there is a
tie for templates that match the best. Fast and precise mapping methods, such as
Bowtie2 or Minimap2, select the best matches randomly if a tie occurs [64,65]. KMA
has been developed to combat this challenge and is now a renowned bioinformatic tool
for sequence alignment using k-mers. It utilises the ConClave score, which handles
the occurrence of ties of the best-matching templates. Obtained alignment scores for
all the query sequences are summed to form the ConClave score.
KMA uses heuristics to accelerate the mapping process. To avoid the slow, de-
tailed alignment of the sequences, the algorithm maps raw reads against the template
sequences to determine if the query sequences match anything in the database. It
allows for discarding reads that are very different from the database. K-mers - sub-
sequences of length k from the query sequences are compared to the database. If
matches are found, they are awarded positive scores - equal to k. Mismatches are
penalised with −1. Queries are accepted for the alignment only if they have positive
scores, where the threshold is equivalent to the minimum identity of (k + 1)−1 in
k-mer space [66].
All sequences that pass the heuristic mapping are indexed in a hash map and are
used as seeds for alignment starting points. Extended pieces of sequences that give
the highest scores are aligned against the template sequences with the Needleman-
Wunsch algorithm, shown in Figure 1.8 [62]. The alignment results are formed into
four files: .res - with the template matches and parameters such as score, depth,
coverage, .fsa - with the consensus sequences, .frag.gz - a fragment file, and .aln -
with the consensus alignment sequences.

CHAPTER 2
Methodology
This thesis aims to develop a pipeline for HLA typing for ONT sequencing data. The
program was written in Nextflow [67], a framework perfectly suitable for bioinformatic
pipelines. It can be run locally or with Docker [68].
This chapter presents the data used for evaluating and deploying the program and
explains the implementation of the pipeline.
2.1
Benchmarking Dataset
The pipeline was developed and tested based on data provided by Frederikke Byron
Pedersen from Vævstypelaboratoriet at Rigshospitalet in Copenhagen. The experi-
mental work was conducted by Jacob Flemming Hansen, Laura Studnitz Andersen,
and Signe Blomsterberg.
This dataset encompassed genetic information from 96
stem cell donors, each characterised by two alleles across seven loci: HLA-A, -B, -C,
-DRB1, -DQA1, -DQB1, and -DPB1. The sequences were acquired with the Native
Barcoding Kit 96 with Amplicons from ONT and sequenced on the GridIon [69]. The
base-calling was performed using Guppy [70]. For HLA class I, the whole length of
the gene was sequenced, while for class II, depending on the locus, only exons 2 and
3 were included for HLA-DQ and exon 2 for -DRB1 and -DPB1. The allelic types
of the baseline dataset were obtained using NGSengine Turbo software [71]. These
results were benchmarked against data provided by HistoGenetics, where Illumina
sequences were analysed [72].
2.2
Database
The developed program employs the IPD-IMGT/HLA Database for HLA typing,
which serves as a specialist resource for over 35,000 sequences of the human major
histocompatibility complex (MHC) and includes the oﬀicially designated sequences
recognised by the WHO Nomenclature Committee for Factors of the HLA System,
and is therefore the biggest and the most reliable source of MHC sequences and their
types [73,74]. The database comprises different representations of the sequences - the
whole genomic DNA sequences, nucleotide coding sequences, and protein sequences.
It also incorporates information on G and P groups for each allele.

The input data are .fastq files that combine sequences of all loci being the objects

The sequences are tagged with locus-specific primers.

2.3.1.3

Files with separated sequences are then fed into KMA along with the matching in-
dexed database files [66]. Since the sequencing process differed slightly for each HLA

quality below ten are discarded, and twenty bases are trimmed off on both ends of
the remaining reads. By setting other parameters, the program adjusts for calling
suspicious Nanopore-typical deletions, which forces each query to match only one
template, and minimises the risk of existing matches between the templates in the
database.

2.3.3
Decision threshold
Ideally, if an individual was homozygous at a locus, one allele would be the most
abundant in the analysis, and the residual alleles would be sequenced with a much
lower depth. With a heterozygote, two gene alleles would have the most significant
abundance and be sequenced at a similar depth. Such evident diversification would
make it possible for an effortless differentiation between a heterozygote and a ho-
mozygote. Unfortunately, with PCR being biased, allele imbalance is often observed,
which makes it very diﬀicult to draw a clear line between the two possibilities. As
this varies between PCR setups, it is possible to affect the decision process of calling
a pair of genes homo- or heterozygous.
KMA utilises an accumulated alignment score called ConClave score [66]. For
each template, alignment scores are summed so that the score represents a cumulative
strength of the match. This score is conditional on the depth and quality of reads, so
it is more robust than using only depth to score the results.

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
HLA is the most essential element of the puzzle required for successful transplan-
tations.
Since it is known to be a very complex set of genes, the typing process
is immensely challenging. There are well-recognised typing methods for short-read
sequencing, but they have limitations, including such issues as the phasing of HLA
genes. Hence, methods for long-read sequencing are currently being sought, as they
address the phasing problem. Nanopore sequencing especially enhances the overall
process, leading to a reduction in cost. Unfortunately, ONT sequencing introduces
a high error rate.
However, with the technology developing rapidly and improve-
ments being observed yearly, the Nanopore sequencing-based HLA typing methods
can already compete with second-generation sequencing approaches.
The main aim of this thesis is to develop a method for HLA typing at the G
group resolution utilising Nanopore sequencing data. Unlike other available methods,
the presented pipeline incorporates mapping with KMA. KMA aligner is a heuristics-
utilising tool very prominent for quick alignment, especially for templates with long
records. The pipeline is written in Nextflow, a framework facilitating parallel pro-
cessing, ideal for processing data from multiple patients at once, hence shortening
the analysis time. It contains Python-based scripts for some operations, such as de-
multiplexing. The demultiplexing process, which splits the reads based on primers’
sequences, eliminates reads from other loci, which might come from contamination
in the post-PCR mixture. Moreover, considering the allele imbalance, phasing of the
haplotype was the most formidable challenge. Other solutions, potentially incorpo-
rating existing tools, should be assessed to tackle this diﬀiculty.
This method was developed and evaluated on data from 96 individuals provided by
Vævstypelaboratoriet at Rigshospitalet. 7 loci were analysed: HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1,
-DQB1, -DQA1, and -DPB1 with focus on exon 2 for HLA class II. The concordance
accuracy of 81.75% was acquired. When excluding DPB1, the accuracy was at a
level of 93.8%. The primary sources of errors were slightly incorrect calls for which
sequences were encoding the same proteins. For DPB1, the benchmarking dataset
had many alleles, which the pipeline tended to assign types not classified into any
G group yet, but which amino-acid sequences for the peptide-binding domain were
correct. It caused a lot lower accuracy scores than for other alleles. Additionally, the
zygote determining dilemma contributed to the lower accuracy score substantially.
After an in-depth analysis of the incorrectly typed alleles, it was determined that
the potential concordance was 93.49% at the P group level. According to Hurley

and Ng, making clinical decisions should be based on the antigen-presenting groove
genes only. Therefore, the obtained result could, in theory, be considered clinically
relevant [76].
With the acquired results looking promising, a comparison to state-of-the-art tech-
niques is necessary. Two of the most renowned approaches are the works of Stockton
et al. for both HLA classes with 98.4% accuracy for two-field resolution and 97.5% for
three-field resolution, and Liu et al. for class I only with 100% accuracy for two-field
resolution [77,78]. It is generally easier to determine the types of class I loci as their
genes are less polymorphic than some of the class II genes. In comparison to these
methods, the introduced technique lacks precision.
Aiming to improve HLA typing accuracy, an alternative sequencing technology
worth considering is PacBio. It offers long-read sequencing with much lower error
rates. Nevertheless, since the equipment is exceedingly expensive and the cost per
sample is very high, it is still financially unavailable for many laboratories. Another
drawback of the technology is its inflexibility in the sequencing process. Unlike ONT,
it does not allow interruption of the sequencing process when enough reads have been
acquired.
4.1
Applications
The presented pipeline can contribute to a continuously growing HLA typing field,
significantly decreasing the hazard ratio for transplantations.
Being able to run 96 samples simultaneously and owning a method for meticulous
typing, Rigshospitalet would be equipped with a rapid technique for donor matching.
The improved tool would be of substantial improvement in the current matching pro-
cess. It would lessen the waiting time for stem cell matches and contribute to reducing
the risk of graft-versus-host disease. Currently, the hospital sends the DNA material
for analysis to Histogenetics in the USA. Were any regulations passed not allowing
for shipping the genetic material outside of Denmark, it would be very advantageous
to own an internal system for HLA typing.
Hopefully, the method will be used by other medical institutions as well.
To
begin with, this open-source method creates competition with other, expensive tools.
Accessibility enables bioinformaticians in such institutions to perfectly understand
the principle of the pipeline and alter it depending on their needs. This pipeline can
be run on any ONT sequencer or personal computers with systems based on Linux
or iOS.
Besides transplantations, the developed pipeline can be employed in further stud-
ies of the HLA complex. The potential accuracy for four-field resolution would bring
remarkably exact data at a relatively low cost for various studies.
Since there are strong associations between the HLA genes and autoimmune dis-
eases, such as type 1 diabetes or multiple sclerosis, infectious diseases like malaria
and AIDS, as well as cancer and rare diseases, this pipeline could serve as a practical

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis has demonstrated the feasibility of the development of a bioinformatic
pipeline, which addresses the challenge of HLA typing for Nanopore sequencing, with
results close to state-of-the-art approaches. With improvements mentioned further,
the developed program can compete with available tools. The presented program’s
main advantages are open-source access and the rapidness of acquiring results. The
future outcome is of great importance for Rigshospitalet and, when deployed, can
save a lot of resources and facilitate logistic undertakings.
5.1
Future perspectives
The pipeline should be improved in a few aspects. The presented program is only a
first step to a more thorough work. Suggestions for future improvements are listed
below.
Firstly, the pipeline should include a script for the files’ down-sampling. The lack
of it has significantly slowed down the typing process. As shown by Liu et al., above a
certain depth level, the down-sampling does not impact the preciseness of typing [78].
The minimum depth required for typing with the NGSEngine-Turbo is 2000 reads
per sample and 100 reads per locus. Similar values are expected to be needed.
Secondly, as only exon 2 of the class II loci was the point of interest, the sequences
should be mapped only to the G group database.
Moreover, the typing method should be improved.
A suggestion is that more
sequences with the best scores should be chosen for further analysis, and consensus
sequences could be created using the selected hits. Due to time limitations, other
approaches were not examined. Also, the returned values should be types at four-
field resolution for HLA class I.
Furthermore, the method for calling a zygote should be re-conceptualised. Em-
ploying available methods such as HapDup or WhatsHap should be considered.
Additionally, it should be possible for the user to choose the database files for
the loci the user desires to use. Currently, the selected files are defined in the code.
However, users should be able to define it from the command line as one of the run
parameters.
As a further matter, in a clinical setup, prompt data collection is crucial. To
save as much time as possible, the pipeline should be improved from the angle of

operations performed, as well as translating the Python scripts to languages with

Lastly, a user interface should be designed for the program. It would allow every-

database system with HLA types being filled in automatically for each patient after
a typing process.

def smith_waterman_alignment(primer, sequence):
# Perform Smith-Waterman alignment between primer and sequence

f"{primer_prefix}.fastq")
prefix_handles[primer_prefix] = open(output_file_path, 'w')
if primer_prefix not in primer_prefixes:
primer_prefixes[primer_prefix] = []
primer_prefixes[primer_prefix].append(primer_sequence)
# threshold for the acceptable alignment score
# 20% of error tollerance
alignment_threshold = len(primer_sequence) * 2 * 0.8
