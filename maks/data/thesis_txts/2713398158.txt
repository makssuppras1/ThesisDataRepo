Abstract
With the ever present push towards subsidy free electricity market mechanisms, innovation and col­
laboration are important to preserve the investment case for distributed energy resources (DER). In
a market with hourly netting of production and consumption it can be difficult for agents to take full
advantage of their DER as the utility company offers a much lower price for export electricity than they
do for import electricity. This can result in bad investment cases and the deincentivazation of DER
such as solar photovoltaics (PV) and energy storage (ES). In this thesis, cooperative game theory is
used to construct an energy grand coalition, in which a group of agents pool their resources and use
optimal ES control to minimize their coalitional energy cost. The additional surplus from the group
optimization can then be reallocated back to the agents via a Shapley value calculation. This thesis
specifically examines the discounted cash flow of investments of a DER technology and uses KPIs
such as net present value (NPV) and complex payback (CPB) to indicate the feasibility of a given DER
investment. Using these KPIs, direct comparisons can be done between investments in a cooperative
and non cooperative setting in order to quantify the long term value of cooperation for the different
agent types.
Several case studies and sensitivity analysis will be performed based in California. California is cur­
rently undergoing net energy metering (NEM) legislation reformation, policy makers are trying to drive
a shift from yearly to hourly netting. The solar industry claims this bill will kill the business case for
residential solar while non solar owners complain of inequity as Californians pay over $3 billion per
year to support NEM programs. This thesis proposes coalitional energy management as a remedy for
both parties and is investigated with sensitivity to CAPEX, debt financing structure, electricity export
price, and DER penetration rate.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivations
For as long as society has existed cooperation has been levied to expedite processes and catalyze
growth. On the international scale successful cooperation is required to eradicate corruption and to
combat omnipresent threats to global society. One of the biggest threats to life as we know it is global
warming. If the world continues to burn fossil fuels at current rates, earths average temperature may
rise to catastrophic levels. Climate change resulting from a world 3 to 8 degrees °C warmer than
pre­industrial levels will cause changes in rainfall patterns, the disappearance of glaciers, droughts, a
rise in sea levels, the loss of islands and coastal wetlands, as well as increased flooding [1]. Because
the effects of climate change cannot be attributed to each specific country that causes greenhouse
emissions, all countries are at the same time culprits and victims of the problem, albeit to different
degrees. In this case, international cooperation can provide a framework to address this collective
problem. While international governments collaborating to fight climate change, such as the Paris
Accords, help to kick start the fight; the real battle is fought by the everyday person. To fight climate
change the average person is only equipped with their decisions. Some choose to eat vegan, others
may choose to install home solar to reduce their carbon footprint. US households account for ap­
proximately 20% of the energy consumed in the United States [2]. The US EPA further states that in
2019, 25% of US GHG emissions came from electricity production and 62% of US electricity comes
from fossil fuels. While home electricity is far from the whole picture when it comes to the scope of
emissions, it is a sector where an impact can be made.
The advances in distributed energy resource(s) (DER) over recent years have made them more and
more attractive as an investment. Additionally, historically high electricity prices are motivating people
to adopt solar PV and even battery storage. However, DER penetration remains relatively low. In 2019
the US broke 2 million home solar installations [3] but this is only out of 79.6 million homes in the US
[4]. The solar energy industries association projects home installations to increase to 4 million by
2023, but this would still only be about 5% of US homes. So, what are the reasons for slow adoption
and what can be done about it?
In some areas, DER simply are not financially feasible. Perhaps, the average homeowner needs
to believe that purchasing a DER will be also good for their wallet and not only good for the planet.
This is where cooperation can come into play. In recent research the idea of energy communities
has been explored where agents work together by optimizing their DER operation to minimize the
electricity bill of the group, not just each individual operating to minimize their own bill. These energy
coalitions have been shown to financially benefit all parties involved, saving them money compared
to operating individually [5]. This savings that the group is able to achieve can then be reallocated to
the members of the group using methods that are designed to redistribute the surplus in a fair way.
The players then pay an electricity bill equivalent to what they would’ve payed minus their share of
the savings. These savings payments from the coalition serve as positive cash flows on top of the
savings the member already achieves from operating its DER. These savings combined with the initial
investment cost, operation and maintenance costs, and a discount rate create a traditional discounted
cash flow. Depending on the type of savings included, both the individual investment cash flow and
the cash flow under a coalitional framework can be constructed. From the discounted cash flows
many different financial metrics can be calculated and compared across a verity of factors including
player DER type and an array of input sensitivity analysis designed to uncover the best parameters
for the players in the coalition.
This cooperative framework may play a pivotal role in accelerating the adoption of DER by giving
people a better investment case. In order to conclude on the frameworks potential financial impact it
must be thoroughly examined under a number of test cases. To my knowledge, this thesis is the first
time a financial model extension has been added to the cooperative framework to quantify the long

1.1.1
NEM modernization in CA ­ potential issues for DER investment
This research comes at a very opportune time given the current political developments in California
surrounding how DER link to the grid and are financially compensated.
Current situation
California currently offers solar customers net metering under their NEM 2.0 guidelines. This allows
homes with solar to net out their consumption and production on a yearly basis. Meaning as long
as the consumer produces as much power as it consumes over the course of the year, its electricity
bill will be zero. Consumers can even get a credit on the order of two to four cents per kilowatt hour
(kWh) [6] if they produce more than they consume. This policy comes with a slew of problems for
the utilities, environment, and the tax payers. It is expensive for the grid to take in energy and to
balance the energy the grid must provide solar customers electricity at a loss. Due to the fact that
the utilities in California are public entities, Californians today spend more than $3 billion a year to
support NEM programs [7]. Additionally, due to transmission losses all ratepayers pay as much as 10
times more for exported NEM energy than for other sources of renewable energy [7]. Furthermore,
from a carbon perspective, the current carbon accounting practices that net out electricity yearly only
achieve 40 — 70% decarbonization of the off­taker’s actual electricity consumption [8]. In order to
reach 100% there is a need for one­to­one matching of consumption and production, something an
energy coalition incentivizes.
Proposed changes
The Public Utilities Commission of the State of California (CPUC) is considering proposed rule changes
to the state’s current net energy metering tariff (NEM 2.0). In what has been described as a “contro­
versial proposal”, the proposed successor net energy metering tariff (NEM 3.0) represents a shift from
net metering to net billing. Under the proposed rule, “imports and exports will be calculated based
on instantaneous netting of consumption and production and will be trued­up on an annual basis”
[9]. This means that instead of netting production and consumption annually, it would be done on an
hourly basis. The CPUC also says the the new rule, ”sends more accurate price signals based on
avoided costs that will incent storage and provide value to the grid when it is most needed during net
peak” [7]. The CPUC also is proposing a new way to value exported energy. Previously, at a yearly
true up, any net excess energy was rewarded with a relatively low credit rate of 2­4 cents/kWh [6].
The proposal for the new valuation method is the avoided­cost calculator, or ACC, and while it’s been
used for decades to measure the cost­effectiveness of energy­efficiency investments and programs,
this will be the first time it’s been put to use to directly compensate rooftop solar for its grid value
[10]. Avoided cost is defined by the CPUC as the “marginal costs a utility would avoid in any given
hour” if a distributed energy resource provided power instead of the utility. This is essentially a large
utility wide calculation that would be needed to be performed every hour to determine the value of
incoming distributed solar energy. While this may seem cumbersome, it would generally give pro­
sumers a better price than they are receiving for net exports now, though the change to hourly netting
would ensure that the solar customers are indeed worse off than in NEM 2.0. The ACC has been
investigated by consulting firm E3 and they have published a number of resources including a spread
sheet to estimate the export price of electricity. Those resources can be found here [11] and have
been used to estimate export prices of electricity in this study.
For environmentalists NEM 3.0 is seen as a win due to the hour­on­hour demand matching which
ensures close to 100% decarbonization. Non solar owners also support the legislation in an attempt
to iron out the extreme inequity the NEM 2.0 policy has created in favor of solar owners.
However not all are in favor of the rule change. The solar industry has coined the bill, ”the solar
killer” and claim that it will completely deincentivize the solar industry. Tom Beach, principal consul­
tant at Crossborder Energy and a consultant to the Solar Energy Industries Association, claims that
the plans would extend the payback period for rooftop solar systems well beyond today’s four to six
years and out to 10 to 15 years. This could drastically reduce the uptake of rooftop solar, he said [12].
Groups that see a cost­shift problem in today’s net­metering regime also recognize this problem. The

Natural Resources Defense Council and The Utility Reform Network have both proposed reducing
net­metering compensation, but they are also promoting a “market transition credit” to pay upfront

Takeaways
It is clear that policy modernization is needed, for both economic and environmental reasons. If
distributed solar as a technology is to one day become completely standalone, the policy training
wheels need to be taken off. This means moving away from NEM 2.0. While NEM 3.0 offers a more
equitable solution, there are major concerns regarding the continued feasibility of solar under the
change. This is perhaps where coalitional energy management can come into play, as shown in [5]
energy coalitions can be utilized to minimize prosumer energy cost. But the question is how much of
an affect will this framework have over the lifetime of a DER? This thesis will conduct case studies in
California in order to conclude on the potential improvement a cooperative framework can have for
potential solar investments under the proposed NEM 3.0.
1.2
Literature review
1.2.1
Energy Communities built on cooperative game theory
The development of photovoltaic solar (PV) with energy storage (ES) and continued advancement of
smart metering technologies have given rise to the emergence of various types of peer­to­peer (P2P)
energy markets in recent years [13]. One of them is prosumer1 energy markets, enabling these pro­
sumers to trade energy among themselves [14]. However, these market structures tend to disrupt
the grid as it is very costly for the grid to absorb energy from the local level. To discourage excess
prosumer generation, utilities generally pay prosumers significantly less for excess generation than
they charge for energy usage. Under this electricity pricing assumption, where import is valued higher
more than excess distributed production, forming local energy coalitions is proposed in [15] to quantify
the economic benefit to the prosumers for sharing their excess production with their neighbors instead
of selling to the grid. It is further shown in [5] that ES system owners can operate collaboratively to
minimize the total coalitional energy cost, the Shapley value and nucleolus are explored as surplus
reallocation functions in a cooperative game theoretic structure. While the Shapley value is consid­
ered to be a fair reallocation of coalitional utility where agents receive payments proportional to their
marginal coalitional contribution, it is shown that the Shapley value does not stabilize the cooperative
game, meaning the agents may receive a higher payoff by deviating from the main group and form­
ing smaller coalitions. While computationally intensive, the nucleolus is empirically demonstrated to
be stabilizing [5], which incentivizes prosumers to stay within the grand coalition2. Now that payoff
functions have been investigated the next step to quantify the benefit that these economic structures
can bring to the individual prosumer over the lifetime of a DER investment.
1.2.2
DER Investment
The science and literature around basic investment decisions is solid and abundant. However, emerg­
ing technologies present new challenges and variables for the consumer to consider. This section will
review previous research done on the four agent types in an energy community and describe what
has been found to impact the adoption of their given technology.
No PV or ES
The simplest case is no technology investment. This agent type can be treated simply as a bystander
who will be happy with the new framework if it benefits him/her. The cooperative game theory frame­
work has already been showed to reduce the energy cost of this agent type, thus they are guaranteed
to be economically satisfied.

PV
The investment case for solar has been studied for many years and there is plentiful research on the
subject. Home solar has a much higher adoption rate than home batteries and governments are even
beginning to implement policies forcing its adoption. The California Energy Commission [16] intro­
duced the California solar mandate which requires rooftop solar photovoltaic systems to be equipped
on all new homes built on January 1, 2020 and beyond. Under net metering (NEM) 2.0, the typical
solar customer pays for the solar energy system through energy bill savings in 3­5.5 years depending
on utility [7], and then receives bill savings/elimination for the remainder of the current 20­year tariff.
The majority of customers purchasing solar PV also choose to finance their purchase, despite 100%
cash being the cheapest payment option overall [17]. Additionally, research from Berkeley lab [18]
states that compared to the general population, 2020 solar adopters are 58% more likely to have
higher income then their counties respective median and 81% more likely to live in a rural area. Anal­
ysis of this agent type will focus on improving the NPV/payback time of the solar system under the
new NEM framework and under cooperation.
ES
Standalone energy storage is rather uncommon and typically where ES is found it is accompanying
PV. The current literature of factors affecting battery adoption is sparse but there are a number of
quality works out of Australia which boasts one of the highest ES penetration rates in the world. [19]
analyzes a survey of over 500 households and interviews with 51 homeowners in Tasmania which
were used to ascertain motivations for installing ES. The study found that there is a high interest in
purchasing batteries yet the time frame for purchase is largely dependent on a satisfactory payback
period. Nearly 80% of respondents are considering batteries within the next two years, but on average
stated that a payback of 7 years would be required to make the investment palatable, at least from
a financial standpoint. The study suggests that as the technology becomes cheaper with improved
efficiency, more households will turn to batteries as a means to reduce household power bills. When
analyzing this agent type it will be important to examine the potential of the cooperative framework to
accelerate the business case feasibility for prosumers.
PV and ES
Data suggests that people who already own PV are much more likely to purchase batteries as well.
[20] uses linear probability and probit regressions with data from an Australian household survey of
1812 solar users to assess a range of economic, policy, social, and locational explanatory variables.
They find that capital and policy are important determinants. Households with smaller solar systems
are less likely to install batteries, an example of a physical capital effect. Households facing financial
pressure, indicative of financial capital constraints, are also less likely to install home batteries. There
is also evidence of a policy impact, as smaller solar feed­in tariffs motivate actual battery uptake.
When analyzing this agent it will be important to make direct comparison to those with the PV agent
type to determine if the ES has a positive or negative affect on the investment case.
1.3
Research gaps and contributions
Most of the current research points to rather trivial results regarding investment decisions into ES/PV.
It makes a lot of sense that people under financial pressure are less likely to install these systems
as their runway may not be very long and a ”large investment” into a PV/ES could cause financial
hardship. It is assumed that finances is one of the most important factors when looking at investment
decisions. That is not to also say there are some exterior motivators such as the environment involved
but it would be naive to assume the population is completely altruistic in nature. Some thresholds for
financial KPIs can be extracted from the literature such as the 7 year payback time required for a ES
system from the Tasmanian study [19], or the 10 year payback time for PV as stated by the Natural
Resources Defense Council [21]. The problem with these numbers is that they are highly dependent
on demographics, location, and financial situation of survey respondents. Research on investment
decisions and cooperative prosumer framework, to my knowledge, has not been merged. The goal of

Concept and definition of cooperative game
theory
Cooperative game theory is the study of group formation. It attempts to examine why people form
groups and, through particular mechanisms, can incentivize people to stay together or split up. A
cooperative game is a mandatory binding contract that can be reached by all parties on the basis
of information exchange. Moreover, a cooperative game is a mechanism to establish cooperative
consciousness, mutual trust, restraint and commitment through negotiation and communication [22].
There are four main points:
• Common interests
• Necessary information exchange
• Voluntariness, equality, and mutual benefit
• Compulsory contract
The main questions that this chapter will aim to answer are: which coalitions will form and how should
the coalitions divide its surplus?
2.1
Definition of coalitions
A cooperative game is given by specifying a value for every coalition. Formally, the coalitional game
consists of a finite set of players N, called the grand coalition, and a value function v(S) : 2N →R
from the set of all possible coalitions of players to a set of payments that satisfies v(∅) = 0 (the value
of the empty set is zero) [22]. Where S is a subset of the grand coalition.
A group of N players can built up 2N possibilities if every player is given the option to join the group
or not. Due to this, the possible number of coalitions becomes gigantic. For example, a group of 10
players has 1,024 subsets while a group of 20 has 1,048,576.

2.2
Superadditive games
If a game is superadditive it weakly implies that the highest surplus is achieved by the grand coalition.
A game G = (N, v) is superadditive if for all S, T ⊂N, if S ∩T = ∅, then v(S ∪T ) ≥v(S) + v(T )
[23]. This means that if there are two strict subsets of N with an empty intersection (they have
entirely different agents), then adding the players into one coalition is at least as good as having
them separate. The cooperative energy management game this thesis revolves around happens to
be super additive because additional players cannot lower the value of the coalition, the worst case
would be a player with 0 marginal value (value function to be described in Chapter 3). This means
every game analyzed will be the game played by the grand coalition where all possible agents are
participating. This is guaranteed to bring the highest surplus possible but it is not guaranteed that all
agents will be satisfied and want to join the group.
2.3
Stability
The question of which coalitions should be formed turned out to be rather easy as long as the focus
remains on superadditive games, as this thesis will. However, the more difficult question is once
the coalition is formed and a surplus is achieved, how should the members divide this surplus? For a
game to be stable (all players are satisfied) it must be impossible for any multilateral deviation (players
splitting to form new coalitions) to result in a higher payoff. Stability is dependent on the reallocation
method used.
2.3.1
Imputation
An imputation x ∈RN is a payoff allocation whose value xi represents the payment to prosumer i ∈N.
An imputation must satisfy the efficiency and individual rationality axioms. Efficient meaning the sum
of the payoffs is equal to the value of the coalition P
i∈N ϕi(v) = v(N). Individually rational meaning
no player’s payoff is less than what they would get on their own ϕi(v) ≥v({i}), ∀i ∈N. An imputation
can be seen as the bare minimum required by the players. However, it does not guarantee everyone
to be satisfied as prosumers may be able to achieve higher payoffs by forming smaller coalitions.
Under an imputation the payoffs may be extremely unfair with one player receiving all the surplus with
all the others just receiving enough to satisfy the imputation condition. These players who are getting
the bare minimum will look at the player receiving the most and want a piece of their pie. They can
form a new coalition without the greedy player and redistribute the surplus in a more fair way, thus
achieving a higher individual payoff.
2.3.2
Excess
To measure a coalition’s dissatisfaction with respect to imputation x the notion of excess is used
looking at the sub coalitions S ∈N:

This says that the sub coalitions excess under the payoff allocation x is equal to the value of the sub
coalition minus the sum of the payoffs for all players in the sub coalition. The payoffs in this case are
coming from the reallocation function in the grand coalition. If a sub coalition has a positive excess,
it indicates S can obtain higher payoffs by breaking off from the grand coalition.
2.3.3
Core
The core of a cooperative game C is defined as the set of imputations such that no sub coalition has
a value greater than the sum of its members allocated payoffs:
C := {x ∈I|ε(x, S) ≤0, ∀S ∈2N }
This means that an imputation is in the core if and only if no sub coalitions have a positive excess.
Therefore, no sub coalition has reason to leave the grand coalition and receive a larger payoff. A
payoff allocation x is stabilizing if x ∈C. However, it should be noted that some games have empty
cores, meaning regardless of payoff allocation there will always be dissatisfaction.

Reallocation of surplus ­ Shapley value

1. (Efficiency) P

Axiom 1) and 2) qualify the Shapley value as an imputation meaning it is efficient and individually
rational. Axiom 3) says that if two players are the same (they have the same marginal value) then
they will have the same Shapley value. Axiom 4) says that if the marginal value of a player is zero
then its Shapley value should also be zero. Axiom 5) says that the Shapley value of a player in a sum
of two games is the sum of the allocations to the player in each individual game.
For the specific energy management game this thesis will deal with it has been shown that the Shapley
value is often in the core but not always [5].
2.5
Alternative reallocation functions
An alternative allocation function is the nucleolus which is an individually rational distribution of the
surplus of the grand coalition in which the maximum dissatisfaction/excess is minimized [24]. The
nucleolus has been shown to have stabilizing properties for the prosumer game [5], which is very
desirable. However, calculating the nucleolus is extremely computationally intensive. [5] states ”In
order to implement the said cooperative game for larger size prosumer coalitions, the development
of sampling algorithms and more efficient nucleolus calculation methods to reduce the computational
burden is needed.” In this previous research the maximum possible number of agents was 14, and
this took roughly a day to compute a 24 hour game.
2.5.1
Why Shapley?
Due to the added computational intensity of the nucleolus, this thesis will use the Shapley value as
the reallocation function for the prosumer game. While the Shapley value has been shown to not
always be in the core of the prosumer game (meaning prosumers could be dissatisfied and benefit
by deviating from the grad coalition), it often does lay in the core [5]. From a purely academic stand
point the stabilizing nature of the nucleolus is very elegant. However, when examining things from a
more practical lens, such as this work aims to do, it can be argued that in real life scenarios prosumers
would not deviate from the grad coalition when outside the core under a Shapley value payoff function.
There are a few reasons to this:
• Information asymmetry ­ Prosumers will not have complete information of their neighbors load­
/generation profiles and they will not be able to know if a deviation can benefit them without
complete information.

• The cost of action ­ Coordinating a deviation from the grad coalition can be costly in both time
and money, this cost of action may be enough to deter agents from deviation.
• Reaction time ­ One day it may be in the prosumers best financial interest to deviate, and in
the very next day it may be best to be in the grand coalition. It is unreasonable to expect agents
to be able to react to changes in real time and it is much more realistic for them to sit in one
coalition continuously.
The challenges outlined above describe why it would be difficult for prosumers to know when to devi­
ate, and even if they were able to map a deviation plan in real time, coordination and implementation
would be a gigantic logistical task.
Due to these reasons this work will solely focus on a Shapley value payoff function. The time saved
by omitting the nucleolus can be allocated to extensive sensitively analysis on investment decisions
plus experimentation with larger time frames and coalition sizes which is intended to be this works
novel contribution.

Financial model and metrics
In order to quantify the benefit of joining an energy coalition, two primary metrics will be utilized,
net present value (NPV) and complex payback (CPB). This chapter will begin with an explanation
and formulation of the metrics and then elaborate on additional financial definitions and modeling
considerations.
4.1
Financial metrics
Net present value (NPV) is the difference between the present value of cash inflows and the present
value of cash outflows over a period of time [25]. It is commonly used in investment analysis to
determine if a certain investment is feasible by finding the present value of a future stream of costs/­
payments. NPV is defined as follows:
NPV =

Where CFt = net cash inflow/outflow during a single period t, r = discount rate or return that could have
been earned in alternative investments, and t = number of time periods. When calculating the NPV it
is also important to add the investment cost or capital expenditure (CAPEX) which is undiscounted.
A positive NPV indicates that the projected earnings generated by a project or investment—in present
dollars—exceeds the anticipated costs, also in present dollars [25]. It is assumed that an investment
with a positive NPV will be profitable. An investment with a negative NPV will result in a net loss.
When using a mixture of debt and equity financing the discount rate will be the weighted average cost
of capital (WACC). For an individual prosumer, the WACC is calculated as follows:
WACC = r = Re(1 −Dp) + RdDp
Where Re is the cost of equity, Dp is the percentage of debt financing used, and Rd is the cost of debt.
This all together gives a total rate for the opportunity cost of the project to be used in discounting of
future cash flows. This thesis utilizes the NPV function in the NPFinancial package available for free
in Julia.
The complex payback period is a modified version of the payback period that accounts for the time
value of money. It gives a time in which the initial investment will be recovered while discounting
future payments in accordance to the WACC defined earlier. The time period when the cumulative
sum of discounted cash flows is equal to zero is the CPB. This metric may be easier to understand
compared to the NPV as it quantifies the benefit in a simpler way.
As there was no CPB function in Julia packages, a function has been created to calculate the CPB
given the discounted cash flow and number of periods. Appendix section A.2 shows this function that
returns the year which the prosumer recovers their investment cost else returns ”I” for infeasible if the
investment cost cannot be recovered in the lifetime of the DER.
The internal rate of return (IRR) is a metric used in financial analysis to estimate the profitability of
potential investments. IRR is a discount rate that makes the net present value (NPV) of all cash flows
equal to zero in a discounted cash flow analysis [26]. Generally speaking, the higher an internal rate
of return, the more desirable an investment is to undertake. The formula for IRR is as follows:

4.2
Loan amortization
An amortized loan is a type of loan with scheduled, periodic payments that are applied to both the
loan’s principal amount and the interest accrued. An amortized loan payment first pays off the relevant
interest expense for the period, after which the remainder of the payment is put toward reducing the
principal amount [27]. In an amortized loan the payment in each period is the same but the mixture
of interest and principal payments changes throughout the life of the loan. Debt financing is quite
common in DER financing and some customers even choose to set 0% down [17]. These customers
then receive a loan with a repayment plan of 10, 15, or 20 years. The payment in each period P for
an amortized loan is calculated with the following formula:
P = Di(1 + i)n
(1 + i)n −1
Where D is the amount borrowed, i is the interest rate of the loan, and n is the number of payment
periods. This study used the PMT function in the NPFinancial Julia package to calculate loan pay­
ments.
4.3
Extrapolation simplification
Due to the computational complexity of the Shapley value calculation, it is unrealistic to model every
year in the life of the energy community. This study will use 20 year lifetimes for financial analysis
and in order to simplify the computation there will only be two years analyzed, the base year and final
year. Between these end points a linear extrapolation simplification will be made. This means that the
cash flows between year 1 and 20 will be a linear deprecation/appreciation due to DER degradation
and inflation. The function used to extrapolate between the vector of all players net cash flow in the
final year (B) and all players net cash flow in the first year (A) can be seen in appendix section A.3.

To calculate the shapley value a list of energy costs was created for all sub coalitions. Each sub
coalitions value is then calculated, as described in section 3.2, and then put into a dictionary that is
keyed by a list of players in the coalition (adding in v(∅) = 0). Then looping through all players i in
the game, start with a list of all players and remove the player i from the list. Then create a list of all
subset combinations of the new player list without the player of interest, this is S\{i}. Then add player
i into each subset in S\{i}, this represents every coalition player i is a part of in the subset S. To see
this process in a flow chart example, see Figure 5.2. Finally, to calculate the shapley value of player i,
for each value in S\{i} calculate the value difference between the coalition which i is in and the same
coalition with i removed (including the empty set which has a value of 0). This represents the value
that player i brings to each coalition it is in. Then each value contribution must be weighted by the
length of the coalition with i minus one factorial, times the total number of players minus the length
of the coalition with i factorial, all divided by the number of players factorial. The players awarded
shapley value is the weighted sum of all of its value contributions to each coalition it is in. The julia
function used to calculate the Shapley value for a given number of players can be seen in Appendix
section A.6.

and once in the final year of operation including degradation and inflation. Then linear extrapolation is
used to construct 20 years of shapley values. The CAPEX, OPEX, loan amortization, WACC, inflation
rate, energy savings, and Shapley value can all be combined to a discounted cash flow which is then
converted to the different KPIs described in chapter 4.

5.3
Sensitivity Analysis
5.3.1
CAPEX sensitivity
To examine the potential impact of DER price cuts/hikes, a CAPEX sensitivity analysis was performed.
The goal of this analysis is to see how prosumer NPV/CPB react to changes to CAPEX in cooperative
and non cooperative environments. Because both technologies in this study are subject to tax credits
that are diminishing year after year, it is also interesting to examine the affect of removing the tax
credit as this will have immediate impact on the consumer as early as 2024. Currently the tax credit
is at 26% of the installed value in the year 2022. Next year, in 2023, it will decrease to 22% and
the year after in 2024, it will be 0% [40]. Because of technological improvements the cost of PV and
ES systems have been steadily falling over the past 20 years. It is reasonable to assume this trend
will continue given all the current research on efficiency, manufacturing, and design. The sensitivity
analysis was broken up into four scenarios:
• 1: All CAPEX is 20% cheaper (­20%)
• 2: All CAPEX is 20% more expensive (+20%)
• 3: All CAPEX no longer receives a tax credit (efficitvly +26%)
• 4: All CAPEX remains the same as described (base case)
Each scenario is run 50 times in order to have an appropriate sample size.

Due to the computational intensity of running the shapley algorithm and optimizing across all subsets

5.3.2
Debt sensitivity
As it is important to consider all socioeconomic backgrounds in the analysis, looking at the percent
of debt financing can show the inequity that exists between the people who have money and those
who don’t. Just as the CA NEM 2.0 policy created inequity for the early adopters who were supported
by tax payer money, inequity can still exist in NEM 3.0 despite one of its main goals being to reduce
inequity. This analysis will look at the affect of debt ratio under cooperative and non cooperative
frameworks. As a solar broker stated in an interview, ”the best option is always to go all cash, but
very few people have the means to do that” [17]. It is expected that 0% debt will be the best option,
but it will be interesting to see how much the NPV/CPB falls off as debt increases.
The sensitivity analysis was broken up into five scenarios:
• 1: 0% debt (all cash)
• 2: 25% debt
• 3: 50% debt
• 4: 75% debt
• 5: 100% debt (no money down)
Similar to the CAPEX analysis, each scenario is run 50 times in order to have an appropriate sample
size. Each time a run is done consumption profiles and DER systems are randomly assigned, ensuring
one player always has PV+ES. Then all five scenarios are run with the same assignments in order to
have comparable results. For all runs of each scenario, each players cooperative and non cooperative
NPV and CPB are recorded into a data frame which is written to a .csv file.
Now with five scenarios in each run, the computational demand has gone up by 25%. Now each
script with 10 runs must solve 210 · 2 · 5 · 10 = 102, 400 optimization problems. Using the HPC, each
script takes around 15 hours to complete, but thanks to parrelization the total computation time can
be reduced by 80%.

is often higher than $0.04/kWh. In fact, the average sell price in the base calculator for 2022 was
$0.0722/kWh. For this reason an increase of electricity sell price will be focused on.

5.3.4
DER penetration sensitivity
All of the previous analysis were conducted with 20% penetration rate of PV and ES. However, the
penetration rate of a technology will also be a key determinate to how a player is rewarded by the
shapley value. As penetration rates go up it is expected that DER will be less valuable to the coalition
and NPVs of investments will go down. The goal of this analysis is to quantify the impact additional
systems can have on players and identify key synergies of player types that provide the most value.
Another goal will be to look at optimal coalitions for each type of player and identify for each investment
what is the best type of coalition to join. In 2019 around 6.6% of utility customers in California had
installed solar, according to additional data collected by the California Solar and Storage Association
[42]. By now in 2022, this number may be closer to 10% while numbers for home battery systems lag
behind. This analysis will give a road map to the future and test the viability of DER investments as
penetration rates increase under cooperative and non cooperative frameworks.
Two types of analysis were conducted. The first looked at the big picture with gradual increase of
DER and examined the affect on all players. This was done with the group of 10 players using the
base case inputs. Consumption profiles were randomly assigned at the beginning of the script and
held constant throughout the analysis. Initially only player 1 had PV, while the other players had no
resources. In each iteration more DER were added to the community. In the second iteration, player
1 now has PV+ES while the remaining players have nothing. Next player one has PV+ES and player
2 has PV while the remaining have nothing. This trend continues, adding PV then ES to the players
in order, until all players have PV+ES as seen in Table 5.3. This is a total of 20 iterations, for each
one each players NPV in the coalition and total number of DER was recorded. This script must solve
210 ·2·20 = 40, 960 optimization problems which takes around 6 hours on the HPC. In order to reduce
the impact of the initial random consumption profile selection, 10 runs of the script were performed.
The results analyzed is the average of all 10 runs. This analysis provides a high level overview of
DER penetration and its impact on players that have not invested as others choose to invest. It also
tracks early investors and tracks the change in value of that investment if others also choose to invest.

6.3
Impact on CA NEM policy
The claims that the solar industry have been making saying that the new NEM 3.0 will increase they
pay back to 10 plus years appears to be correct, as seen in Table 6.1 where the current situation (non
cooperative) has an average pay back of 11.2 years. While this work cannot quantify the reduction
in investment interest as a result of NEM 3.0, it has shown that, with cooperation, it is possible to
return the investment case of PV back to NEM 2.0 levels, again seen in Table 6.1 in current situation
(cooperative) where the average payback for a PV player is 4 years. One caveat to this is that the
cooperative case is reliant on ES players that currently have an infeasible investment case. This can
be remedied by government subsidies in the medium term while hoping for technology to improve
and CAPEX to reduce. Or the players in the coalition can elect to go for higher penetration of PV+ES

Conclusion
7.1
General conclusion
Under NEM 3.0 in the base case without cooperation PV+ES is the best investment followed by
PV, No DER, and then ES. Once introducing cooperation the bottom two stay the same, but the top
two flip. PV becomes a better investment than PV+ES due to the reduced CAPEX and access to
energy storage resources within the coalition. ES players have a negative investment case in both
frameworks but the cooperative structure can give them a $3,000 boost in NPV. No DER players
have 0 NPV without cooperation and receive a boost of around $1,500 in the base case for joining a
coalition.
There are a several factors that can improve the players NPVs under cooperation. A reduction in
CAPEX can be used to incetivize investment in DER. This can be done by governments through tax
credits and is already in place in California. A lower debt share was also shown to improve NPV. This
data must be communicated to potential investors in attempts to increase financial literacy regarding
DER investments and decision making. A higher export electricity price was shown to increase NPV
for PV and PV+ES players, but reduced the NPV of ES players. As mentioned earlier the pricing
mechanism of export electricity will likely be left in the hands of the ACC which prices the energy
based on the avoided cost for the utility. Prosumers can take advantage of this information by tracking
the ACC (if implemented) and trying to limit the export of electricity to hours with high export prices.
Synergies and coalition composition can also greatly affect the NPV of a DER investment. What
helps and what hurts a players investment when it comes to synergies and composition boils down to
understanding how the Shapley algorithm value players. The Shapley value is assigned based on the
players marginal contribution to the coalition. That means how much better/worse would the coalition
be off if the player left. Based on this there are a three factors the Shapley value rewards greatly:
• Uniqueness
– When a player is unique in the coalition and has something no one else has, giving the
coalition a reduction in cost. This could be a player that is the only one with PV thus is the
only one able to produce excess energy.
• Value addition
– When a player brings something to the table for the betterment of the coalition by reducing
its cost. This could be a player who brings additional PV production that the coalition can
utilize. This player won’t be rewarded as much as if they were the only one with PV because
they’re not as unique, but the coalition can still appreciates the investment.
• Synergies
– When a player brings a DER to the coalition that compliments the existing DER in the
coalition. This could be a coalition with 50% PV penetration and then a player decides to
invest in ES. The new ES is now not only unique but also complements the existing PV
resources by providing the ability to shift load.
Finally, relating to NEM policy, this study was able to show that under NEM 3.0 conditions, the coop­
erative framework was able to return investment value to prosumers. While energy coalition coopera­
tion networks may initially present logistical challenges, once the public is properly educated and the
concept is implemented, prosumers can expect to see a quantifiable improvement in their DER in­
vestment case. This shift to NEM 3.0 will also save the California tax payers billions of dollars. Money
that can be returned directly to their pockets or used to further fund research on the improvement of
DER technology, specifically ES.

7.2
Future work
Because of the near infinite degrees of freedom the analysis has there are many opportunities for
further work. A deeper dive into penetration rates may reveal more information. This thesis dealt
with holding one player fixed and changing the remaining players one at a time to the same type of
DER player. For a more complete picture all possible combinations would have to be explored by the
sensitivity analysis. This provides a huge feasible space, with 10 players and each player has 4 types
there are 410 = 1, 048, 576 possible coalition compositions. This can be reduced by only looking at
unique coalition compositions but the problem is still too big to be ran on a resource like the HPC. A
reduction of coalition size must be done or some type of metahuristic can also be developed in order
to optimize the collective investment case for the entire coalition.
The study also deals with same time investments, all players decide to invest in their DER at the same
time. Another dimension that could be added is mixed time investment. This would look like some
players investing now, and other players investing later at different times. How would this affect the
NPV of the original investor as more DER comes in to the coalition and last the investor as the original
investors DER becomes decommissioned. Again, the feasible space for this analysis is gigantic and
scenario reduction and or heuristics would need to be developed in order to conduct an effective
analysis.
One assumption this study makes is fixed electricity sale price. In reality the new proposition of NEM
3.0 involves variable export pricing based on the ACC. To include this measure, network affects of the
entire power grid must be simulated and put into real time. Building a model to replicated the ACC can
be a thesis in itself but could provide important information and increase accuracy of the coalitional
simulations.
The final area for additional work revolves around stochasticity. The model used in this study is
determinant, meaning it has the ability to see perfectly into the future and make current decisions
based on certain future outcomes. This future data is solar production and player consumption. The
battery owner can operate the battery perfectly with out any uncertainty regarding the future. This is
of course not very realistic. An extension to the model can be to add stochasticity to the consumption
and production profiles. This can either be in the form of scenarios with a weighted probability or by
a distribution of outcomes.
