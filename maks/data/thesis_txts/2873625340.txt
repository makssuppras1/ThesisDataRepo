Abstract
In the modern world of rapidly evolving techniques, patients with ocular diseases such
as diabetic retinopathy are still treated by highly skilled doctors who manually segment
lesions from retinal scans. While this approach remains effective, it is time­consuming
and labor­intensive. To address this, recent efforts have turned toward leveraging deep
learning models for seamless and accurate lesion detection.
Deep learning models for medical image segmentation depend on accurate, pixel­level
annotations. However, the quality of masks created by experts is rarely checked in detail
before being used. This thesis explores whether differences in annotation quality can be
detected in binary lesion masks using the FGADR dataset. It examines how deep learning
methods can be used to find and understand these differences using both supervised and
unsupervised techniques.
Several research questions are addressed through specific experiments.
The lesion
masks are first manually labeled by trained professionals based on visual inspection. A
Convolutional Autoencoder with residual blocks is designed to test whether the hidden
features it learns reflect structural differences related to quality. Dimensionality reduction
is used to verify if clear clusters appear in these features. In addition, a supervised clas­
sifier is trained to check whether quality labels can be predicted from the structure of the
lesion masks.
The results of these experiments are presented and discussed to understand how anno­
tation quality differences show up in both unsupervised and supervised settings.
Keywords:
Annotation quality, diabetic retinopathy, lesion segmentation, autoencoder, classifier, FGADR,
medical image analysis, deep learning

Introduction
Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a prevalent complication of diabetes that damages retinal
blood vessels. This can lead to vision impairment or blindness [1]. The retina, a light­
sensitive layer in the eye, converts visual stimuli into neural signals for the brain. High
blood sugar levels over time can damage the small blood vessels in the retina. This
causes lesions such as microaneurysms, hemorrhages, and exudates [2]. In 2019, the
World Health Organization estimated that 463 million people had diabetes globally. Over
77% of them were affected by DR [2]. By 2045, diabetes cases may exceed 700 million.
This will increase the global burden of DR [3].
Early detection of DR is vital to prevent irreversible vision loss [4]. Ophthalmologists use
fundus photography to monitor retinal lesions. However, manual analysis of these images
is time­consuming and costly. It is also prone to human error [5]. DL offers a solution by
enabling automated lesion segmentation and annotation quality assessment. This thesis
uses the FGADR dataset, which includes 1,842 high­quality fundus images with pixel­level
lesion annotations [4]. It develops a convolutional autoencoder and classifier framework
to assess annotation quality for the segmentation of DR lesions. The goal is to enhance
diagnostic accuracy and efficiency.
This work explores latent space representations to reveal patterns in lesion structure and
annotation quality. It addresses challenges like noisy annotations and class imbalance.
The study aims to improve the reliability of automated DR screening. Ultimately, it sup­
ports scalable and accurate diagnostic pipelines in clinical practice.
1.1
Motivation
Age­related Macular Degeneration (AMD) is typically diagnosed through the manual eval­
uation of retinal fundus images by ophthalmologists or trained professionals [6]. As spec­
ified by Köse et al. in [7], the diagnosis process for AMD often involves segmentation per­
formed manually by clinicians. When signs of degeneration are detected in the macular
area, the professional outlines the affected regions directly on the image. These images
are acquired using a fundus camera, after which a clinician visually inspects them to iden­
tify any pathological changes in the macula. The severity of the condition is estimated by
quantifying the segmented areas of degeneration. This process is time­consuming and
requires a high level of expertise, especially due to the subtle nature of early­stage AMD
features.
To assist in this process, various image processing techniques have been explored. Among
them, mathematical morphological operations have been extensively used as a foun­
dational approach for image segmentation, aiming to isolate and analyze pathological
features in retinal scans [8]. However, traditional techniques often rely on hand­crafted
feature extraction and predefined rules based on expert knowledge, which may lack ro­
bustness across different patient populations and imaging conditions [8].
In recent years, machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) have emerged as
promising approaches for medical image analysis. ML refers to algorithms that learn
patterns from data to make predictions or classifications, while DL is a subfield of ML
that uses neural networks with multiple layers to automatically learn hierarchical feature
representations [9]. These data­driven methods offer the potential for more accurate and

automated diagnosis of ophthalmic diseases, including both AMD and DR [10]. In par­
ticular, DL models are capable of learning complex features directly from retinal images
without requiring manual feature engineering [3].
Despite their promise, these techniques face significant challenges in medical applica­
tions. High­performing DL models typically require large training datasets with detailed,
high­quality annotations. However, acquiring such data in clinical contexts is difficult [11].
Annotating medical images, especially for retinal diseases, is a labor­intensive task that
demands expert knowledge. It is also a costly process, as it requires expert knowledge,
specialized tools, and careful review to ensure accuracy [12]. Furthermore, medical imag­
ing datasets often include noisy or low­quality scans, which further complicates training
and evaluation.
Given these challenges, this thesis focuses on quality assessment of lesion annotations in
DR using DL. The study uses the FGADR dataset, a large­scale, fine­grained annotated
DR dataset comprising 2,842 fundus images, including 1,842 images with pixel­level le­
sion masks (Seg­set) [4]. The proposed methodology involves designing an autoencoder
architecture with residual blocks in the encoder and inverse residual blocks in the decoder.
The model learns a latent representation of the lesion masks, which is then analyzed to
determine whether it forms meaningful clusters based on annotation quality or lesion type.
The latent space is further examined using both unsupervised and supervised settings to
evaluate the extent to which annotation reliability can be inferred from the learned fea­
tures.
1.2
Purpose and Contributions
This thesis investigates supervised and unsupervised DL techniques for assessing the
quality of binary segmentation masks for lesions in DR. Specifically, it explores whether
learned latent space representations of lesion masks can reveal patterns related to an­
notation quality and lesion type. The study uses the FGADR Seg­Set, which includes
pixel­level annotations for six lesion categories. Notably, annotation quality is labeled
at the image level, not for each individual lesion, which presents a unique challenge in
evaluating model performance.
The main idea is to employ a convolutional autoencoder to learn compact latent repre­
sentations of lesion masks in an unsupervised manner. These embeddings are then vi­
sualized to examine whether they naturally form clusters based on segmentation quality,
lesion type, or both. To introduce supervision, the autoencoder is adapted into a clas­
sifier. This allows for a comparative analysis of how label supervision affects the latent
space structure. Furthermore, reconstruction loss and classifier uncertainty are analyzed
to investigate their relationship with annotation quality.
While the high­quality masks identified through this process have the potential to improve
downstream segmentation tasks (e.g, U­Net segmentation), this thesis focuses primarily
on the quality assessment framework, rather than evaluating the performance of end­to­
end segmentation.
To guide this investigation, the following research questions are posed:
(RQ1) Can the learned latent space representations of an autoencoder on lesion masks
be separated based on segmentation quality?
(RQ2) Does incorporating label supervision improve the separability and structure of clus­
ters in the latent space with respect to both annotation quality and lesion type?

Thesis Structure
The thesis has the following structure:
• Literature Review (Chapter 2): Reviews prior research on DL methods for annota­
tion quality assessment, latent space analysis, and robust training strategies in the
presence of noisy labels.
• Clinical Background (Chapter 3): Introduces diabetic retinopathy, its clinical sig­
nificance, and the retinal lesions associated with it. It also covers key concepts in
medical image segmentation for retinal imaging.
• Technical Background Knowledge: (Chapter 4): Discusses annotation quality in
binary lesion masks, the fundamentals of image and lesion segmentation, and the
role of DL in medical image analysis.
• Methodology (Chapter 5): Presents the proposed autoencoder and classifier frame­
work, including architecture design, pre­processing steps, and techniques for latent
space visualization and analysis.
• Implementation (Chapter 6): Describes the experiments conducted to explore the
research questions, including unsupervised clustering, supervised latent structure,
joint label supervision, and multi­view analysis.

mance.

and lesion type.

of findings, acknowledges limitations, and outlines potential directions for extending
the work, including alternative model designs and broader applications.
The aim of this research is to support the development of scalable and reliable DL methods
for assessing the quality of lesion annotations in DR, with a focus on analyzing latent space
representations to inform automated quality control in medical imaging.

2.1
Deep Learning for Quality Assessment of Annotations
The Importance of Annotation Quality in Medical Imaging
The field of ML has become vital in medical imaging. Segmentation is a task where each
pixel is categorized into a particular anatomical structure, and computer­aided diagnosis
is the process in which a label or continuous value is predicted for an entire image. These
tasks are performed with ML methods [13].
Despite the abundance of unlabeled data that is available, a common issue in applying DL
methods to medical image analysis is the lack of accurately labeled datasets. Obtaining
reliable annotations is a labor­intensive and costly process that often requires professional
knowledge, which is typically not available in routine clinical environments. As a result,
there is a shortage of high­quality annotations, and thus many datasets are restricted to
a particular area of research and in turn inaccessible to the broader scientific community.
This limitation has boosted the development of learning paradigms that use both labeled
and unlabeled data, such as semi­supervised learning, multiple instance learning, and
transfer learning [13].
Considering the example of a radiography implementation, as summarized by the author
Guo et al. in [14], we see that radiograph interpretation is a time­consuming task with
limited professionally trained radiologists in many healthcare clinics. It is a challenge to
find accurate and reliable ground truth labels for the training of DL models due to the lack
of skilled annotators. According to the study, the CheXNeXt model performed similarly in
terms of diagnosis of 11 out of 14 diseases when tested against 9 practicing pathologists.
Some differences were observed in three groups, which unraveled the variations that arise
in human annotators [14]. While medical imaging datasets have been growing in size, a
frequent challenge that is faced by supervised ML algorithms is the lack of annotated data
[13].
Although this discussion focuses on classification tasks, the issue of noisy labels also
affects segmentation problems which is even worse as pixel­level annotations require
precise and consistent labeling. Many segmentation tasks are dependent on certain clas­
sification steps or in some cases, share data across tasks. In such cases, errors in classi­
fication labels can propagate to segmentation results. Inaccuracies in classification labels
can lead to errors in predictions at the classification stage. These misclassifications, com­
promise the reliability of segmentation outputs. Thus, many of the approaches aimed at
improving classification label quality are also relevant to segmentation [15].
While each strategy tackles the issue of annotation quality from a different angle, a space
opens to explore the combined knowledge of the two approaches to improve the annota­
tion quality across a range of medical imaging applications.
Noisy Annotation Labels in Medical Datasets
Recent advancements in DL have been driven by various factors such as technical ad­
vancements in network architecture design, better parameter initialization techniques,
more efficient training procedures. The availability of powerful computational hardware
allows training of large models on massive datasets. Additionally, there has been a con­
tinuous effort to increase access to large, well­annotated datasets, which are crucial for
training DL models. However, a prominent challenge in this area is the presence of noisy

labels i.e. labels which are incorrect, inconsistent, or even imprecise. This can degrade
the model performance and limit the effectiveness of the training techniques [16].
While large and well­labeled datasets are available for certain applications, collecting and
accurately labeling such datasets remains challenging in many areas. A solution that is
becoming popular is to employ non­expert humans or automated systems to label large
datasets. However, these datasets have very minimal applications in medical imaging as
they typically suffer from very high label noise [16].
The challenge of obtaining large datasets with accurate labels is vital in medical imag­
ing. The availability of this data is typically small, and access is hampered by factors
such as patient privacy and clinical policies. However, labeling medical images is very
resource­intensive as it requires expertise in the domain. In some applications, labels
from multiple experts must be obtained and combined to address significant inter­observer
variability. Some studies have even employed numerous experts to annotate large med­
ical image datasets according to Karimi et al. in [16]. This, however, depends on large
financial and logistical resources, which are not available in many fields. A few studies
have used automated mining of medical image databases, like hospital picture archiving
and communication systems (PACS), to build big datasets for training [17]. This method
is not always applicable, as historical data doesn’t always include the desired labels or
images. Such datasets are also expected to have higher noisy labels when compared to
expert­labeled datasets as stated in [16]. Certain studies then resorted to crowd­sourcing
methods where the labels were obtained from non­experts. However, the scope of this
approach was narrow, as in many medical applications, non­experts are unable to provide
useful labels. Even for tasks that are relatively simple, such as segmentation tasks, com­
puterized systems generated significantly less accurate labels when compared to experts
and crowd­sourced non­experts [16].
A general understanding is that there exists a lack of large datasets with trustworthy labels.
This is one of the biggest challenges being faced while deploying DL methods in the
medical field. A long­standing challenge in DL has been learning from noisy labels. A
lot of studies have shown that the impact that noisy labels have on the performance of a
model can be more significant than that of feature noise. The complexity of label noise
distribution varies a lot by the application. Label noise can be categorized into 3 different
types: class independent, class dependent and feature dependent. The methods that
have been proposed to handle these noisy labels usually fall into one of the following
three categories:
1. Some models, such as Naïve Bayes and random forest, are more robust to noisy
labels compared to decision trees and SVMs. Bagging methods offer more robust­
ness, while boosting methods worsen the label noise effect. It has also been shown
in studies that simpler loss functions like 0­1 loss are more accepting of label noise
than smoother alternatives. Researchers then proposed modifying standard loss
functions and applying re­weight techniques during training [16].
2. Label noise reduction approaches mainly focus on finding and eliminating incor­
rectly labeled data points from the training set. This approach involves training a
classifier to potentially identify mislabeled data based on prediction discrepancies.
Approaches such as ensemble voting and k­nearest neighbors have been used re­
cently for detecting noisy labels. Sophisticated methods leave­one­out methods,
which examines how each sample affects the training process. Finally, some meth­
ods are similar to outlier detection techniques. These techniques define some cri­
teria to reflect the complexity of a data point and prune those training instances that

exceed a certain threshold on that criterion [16].
3. Some methods perform classifier training and label noise modeling in a unified net­
work. The methods that were used in the previous 2 categories can be used here as
well. Methods in this class focus on improving standard classifiers such as support
vector machines, decision trees, and neural networks. On the other hand, different
forms of probabilistic models have been used to model the noisy labels and then
improve various classifiers [16].
Noisy Labels for Deep Learning Models
DL models typically require much more training data than traditional ML models. In many
fields, the training data is labeled by non­experts and in some cases even by automated
systems which leads to high levels of label noise, compared to smaller and carefully cu­
rated datasets. While DL models, including Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), are
capable of memorizing large datasets in certain conditions, empirical studies state that
these models prioritize learning shared patterns from correctly labeled data before over­
fitting to noisy labels [18]. Evidence has shown that CNNs can handle significant label
noise, although this robustness diminishes with more complex datasets or highly concen­
trated noise [16]. Contrary to this, there are studies which have found that training on
small, clean datasets outperforms training on large, noisy ones.
Researchers also explored how noise affects feature representations. It said that local
intrinsic dimensionality decreases initially during the training and then increases once the
model starts fitting noisy labels. As a whole, CNNs can sometimes withstand noisy labels,
but their sensitivity depends on factors such as the richness of the dataset, the level of
label noise, model architecture and training procedures. The theoretical and practical
exploration of this trade­off is currently a booming area of research [16].
Recent investigations, including those by Arpit et al. in [18] and Rolnick et al. in [19],
suggest that although CNNs have the vast capacity to memorize data, they tend to learn
certain dominant patterns in correctly labeled samples during the early phases of training.
This delays the process of overfitting to noisy labels. Such prioritization allows CNNs to
be categorized as robust noise supervision models, especially when the label noise is
distributed randomly. However, this property of the model deteriorates when the noise
is dependent on the class, as shown by Drory, Avidan, and Giryes in [20]. Finally, the
empirical work by Wang et al. (2018a) showed that models trained on smaller but clean
datasets often performed better than those trained on larger noisy labels, especially in
domains such as face recognition [21].
The theoretical aspects of this phenomenon are still evolving. For instance, Ma et al.
in [22] analyzed local intrinsic dimensionality (LID) and figured out that during training,
LID usually decreases when models generalize from clean data but increases once the
overfitting to noisy labels starts. This proposes the idea that changes in learned feature
space can indicate when a model begins to memorize noise in the data. Similarly, Chen
et al. said that the test error increases at a quadratic rate [23]. That is, doubling the noise
could lead to a fourfold increase in the error. This emphasizes the need to supervise
the label quality of data. From these findings, we can say that a CNN’s sensitivity to
noisy labels is influenced by various factors such as the complexity of the dataset, the
structured vs random nature of the noisy labels, and the training procedure. While several
architecture strategies and normalization techniques have been incorporated to delay or
reduce the memorization of noise, exploring the trade­off between the capacity of learning
and robustness has been an important area of research [16].

The paper also discusses the studies that addressed label noise in training DL models for
medical imaging. Some of the techniques analyzed are discussed below.
1. Label Cleaning and pre­processing
Pham et al. (2019) used label smoothing for the classification of thoracic diseases
from chest X­ray scans, which gave positive results. It led to improvements of up to
0.08 in the area under the receiver operating curve (AUC) when compared to simple
methods such as ignoring noisy samples [16].
2. Network Architectures
Bekker and Goldberger (2016) proposed a noise layer that was used for breast
cancer detection in mammograms. This slightly improved the detection accuracy
[16].
3. Loss Functions
In segmentation tasks with noisy annotations, standard loss functions like cross­
entropy may penalize models too harshly for discrepancies that occur due to incor­
rect labeling. Matuszewski & Sintorn (2018) used a modified Dice Loss that ignored
uncertain regions in various particle masks, which were derived from an approxi­
mation of the center rather than precise boundaries. On the same line, Rister et al.
(2018) observed an improvement in the segmentation of abdominal organs in CT
scans by using IoU Loss. This loss was more tolerant of annotation errors, espe­
cially around the boundaries of objects. These adaptations in terms of loss helped
models generalize better to label noise [16].
4. Data Re­weighting
In cases where annotation noise is present, identifying and adapting to the quality of
samples during training becomes important. If low­quality or potentially mislabeled
data can be detected, for instance, through high loss values, uncertainty measures,
or inconsistencies in learned features, then training strategies can be adjusted ac­
cordingly.
Data re­weighting techniques aim to reduce the influence of noisy labels by assign­
ing lower weights to such unreliable samples. Xue et al. applied loss­based pruning
for skin lesion classification, eliminating high­loss samples in each batch under the
assumption that they were incorrectly labeled. This allowed the model to focus on

training robustness [16].

6. Training Procedures
Training loops that aim at handling noisy labels usually involve a structured learning
flow. They guide the model to learn reliable patterns before introducing it to uncertain
data.
Curriculum learning is an effective strategy that trains with the most reliable and
most confident labeled samples and gradually incorporates noisy samples. This is
very similar to the way humans learn, helping the model develop a robust feature
representation before encountering ambiguous data [16].
Another process that is commonly used is fine­tuning. In this approach, a model is
first trained on a smaller, high­quality dataset and then fine­tuned on noisier data.
This avoids a model from directly memorizing incorrect labels and helps in general­
izing better by starting with a more stable base [16].
Some studies use pseudo­label refinement. In this, a pre­trained model generates
updated labels or confidence scores for noisy data. These refined pseudo­labels are
then used to guide training, often improving the consistency and reducing overfitting
[16].
The staged training strategies take advantage of how DL models evolve. Early
training captures general patterns, while later stages have the risk of memorizing
noisy labels. By structuring the training process carefully, these methods are able to
improve robustness and generalization, especially in sensitive domains like medical
imaging [16].
Label noise is difficult to avoid in many medical imaging datasets. While significant progress
has been made in handling label noise using DL models, particularly CNNs, several chal­
lenges remain. A major limitation is the lack of large­scale, publicly available datasets
with allowable levels of label noise, making benchmarking difficult. Furthermore, many
noise­handling techniques proposed in the general ML field are not directly suited to the
unique challenges of medical data, such as small sample sizes, multi­class segmentation
tasks, and variable annotation granularity. Future research should focus on developing
domain­specific methods that integrate clinical expertise, accommodate heterogeneous
annotation protocols, and model inter­observer variability. There is also growing inter­
est in semi­supervised and weakly supervised learning frameworks, which can leverage
small amounts of clean data to guide training on large, noisy datasets. Another promis­
ing direction is improving model calibration and uncertainty estimation, as overconfident
misclassifications can be dangerous in clinical settings. Therefore, understanding how la­
bel noise affects DL representations in medical imaging remains an open research area,
critical to ensuring the safe and effective deployment of AI systems in healthcare [16].

imaging datasets [28]. The two approaches discussed illustrate the shift from traditional
methods to using GAN­based synthetic data generation and confident learning for label

Weighted Sampling and Noise­Robust Training
The field of medical image segmentation often suffers from ambiguity in the quality of data
annotations. This can originate from ambiguous imaging features, observer variability, or
inconsistent annotation protocols [21]. This is evident in DR datasets such as the FGADR
dataset, where it is difficult to delineate small, low­contrast lesions. Delineation is hard
for experts, too. This varied annotation labelling degrades the performance of the model
and affects generalisation.
In classification tasks, label noise means that the wrong class is assigned to an image.
But when it comes to segmentation tasks, the identification of label noise is complex. It
appears as either a missing part of the lesion mask or blurry boundaries of the lesion, or
incorrect areas marked in the mask. These types of mistakes are harder to fix, and the
model, in turn, learns from low­quality samples. Recent studies have explored specialised
strategies that past standard label correction to address partial or incorrect annotations.
This results in the risk of overfitting to spurious segmentation while the model is learning.
It ultimately reduces clinical utility [12][21].
To address this, numerous studies have looked into annotation­quality aware training. It
is where models explicitly account for the reliability of their supervision signals. Sample­
specific reweighting is the process where each mask is assigned a dynamic weight based
on its quality. Rather than assuming all the annotations are equally reliable, the method
prioritises learning from correctly annotated masks and downweights those that contain
certain annotation noise [21][16][29][30].
Guan et al. proposed a confidence­weighted loss using sample weights, which were deter­
mined from the consistency of embeddings. This way, the model can iteratively attenuate
the impact of noisy segmentation masks. Similarly, as discussed, Li et al. introduced the
auto­encoder framework­based technique that reduced the weight of samples that had a
higher reconstruction error. This made it possible to eliminate uncertain training masks.
From this, we saw that these methods aligned with the findings of Cheplygina, de Bruijne,
and Pluim, who states that DL models trained with limited supervision can still learn mean­
ingful features when it is combined with sample reweighting or semi­supervised learning.
In such environments, the model is trained to rely on consistent samples, improving the
robustness to imperfect ground truth.
Noise­robust training goes hand­in­hand with weighted sampling by modifying the function
to reduce the sensitivity on incorrect samples. Bootstraping methods blend the model’s
own prediction with the ground truth to soften the label noise during training. Further,
techniques like label smoothing and consistency regularisation make sure that the model
doesn’t become overconfident in potentially flawed labels. Domain­specific loss func­
tions such as Generalised DICE loss and Tversky loss have been shown to be effective
in dealing with class imbalance and noisy annotations by weighting false positives and
false negatives effectively, in segmentation tasks [33]. In this research, the latent­space

3.1
Diabetic Retinopathy: Overview and Clinical Significance
People with diabetes are prone to an eye disease called Diabetic Retinopathy. In diabetic
patients, the excessive growth of glucose levels in the blood impacts the retina, the part of
the eye that converts light into electrical signals [2]. The brain interprets these signals to
produce the images that we see. Blood vessels carry oxygen and nutrients to the retina,
and having diabetes affects these blood vessels in numerous ways [34]. Over time, blood
vessels in the retina start leaking fluid, which causes swelling and blurred vision in DR
patients [2]. As specified by Sushith et al. in [3], DR not only affects the individual health
of patients and the quality of life but also increases the economic burden in terms of
medical operational cost and loss of productivity. The progression of DR is invisible until
it introduces significant damage to vision.
DR has two primary stages: non­proliferative DR (NPDR) and proliferative DR (PDR).
Non­proliferative DR can be classified into mild, moderate and severe stages. Microa­
neurysms develop in the retinal blood vessels in mild NDPR. As the disease advances,
blood vessels that are blocked can cause blurred or fluctuating vision, and microaneurysms
are then clearly present. Once the NDPR becomes severe, it leads to more significant
blood vessel blockage, which leads to the presence of exudates and retinal hemorrhages.
PDR is characterized by neovascularization, the formation of abnormal blood vessels on
the retina, which is a key diagnostic indicator of disease progression. If untreated, these
fragile vessels can lead to complications such as retinal detachment and eventual blind­
ness [5].

This phase of the research incorporated an unsupervised learning method via a convolu­

5.3.1
Architectural Overview
The goal of this autoencoder framework is to extract meaningful latent representations
from binary lesion segmentation masks in the FGADR dataset. These vectors support
both accurate mask reconstruction and, importantly, unsupervised quality analysis through
32

==PAGE:43==
clustering and visualization in the latent space. The final architecture is a fully convolu­
tional autoencoder with residual blocks. The architecture consists of 3 main elements:
1. An encoder with multiple stages that transforms binary masks into a highly abstract
latent space
2. A bottleneck layer that captures the most compact and informative representation
3. A decoder that reconstructs the original mask using inverse residual blocks and
upsampling
The design of the autoencoder was influenced by the necessity to capture structurally
meaningful and quality­relevant representations of binary lesion masks. In the early de­
sign phases, several standard convolutional autoencoder configurations were explored.
While they offered a basic framework for unsupervised learning, certain limitations were
observed with regard to the difficulty in reconstructing fine lesion boundaries. These in­
sights motivated a refined architecture involving residual blocks and up­sampling tech­
niques, striving to improve reconstruction masks and learning features relevant to the
annotation quality.
To address these constraints, the architecture took inspiration from deep metric learning
[52], residual design [53], and VAEs for multi­modal data representation. These areas
provided resources for developing compact, semantically rich embeddings, which prove
to be effective for quality assessment in medical imaging.
Key Architectural Design Influences
1. Compact Representations: Motivated by the principles of Deep Metric Learning
and Disentangled Representation Learning, the bottleneck was limited to a compact
latent space (128x1x1) to encourage abstraction and differentiation of mask quality
features. This reflects approaches such as the Deep Metric VAE (DMVAE), which
uses tight bottlenecks to enforce latent structure separation and class­awareness in
multi modal settings. [54][55]
2. Preserving Structure in the latent space: Variational and contrastive autoencoder
models strive to organize the latent space so that similar inputs are clustered closely
together, while differing ones are separated. This concept was implemented in the
architecture of the autoencoder to check whether high­quality and low­quality masks
create separate clusters when visualized with techniques such as t­SNE or UMAP.
[54][56]
3. Residual Learning: The encoder utilizes Residual blocks, while the decoder uses
Inverse Residual Blocks to address the vanishing gradient and enhance the recon­
struction accuracy. Residual networks, proposed by He et al.[53], are recognized
for enabling deeper and more expressive models. This is essential for preserving
intricate details in sparse lesion masks.
4. Medical Image Autoencoders for Quality Detection: Li et al.[57] and Sundgaard
et al.[54], demonstrated the effectiveness of residual and VAE­based autoencoders
in detecting abnormalities, segmenting lesions, and assessing annotation accuracy
without supervision [57][58]. These studies validated the approach of using bottle­
neck embeddings for interpreting quality assessment. The findings aligned directly
with the goals of this thesis.

Encoder: Dimensionality Reduction and Feature Extraction
Figure 5.3: Encoder Architecture
The role of the encoder is to transform the input binary masks into a compressed represen­
tation by enriching feature extraction while progressively reducing the spatial resolution.
It is composed of 5 encoder blocks:
• Initial Block (encoder block 1): A single convolution layer with a stride of 2 which
decreases the size of the image to half its original size. Followed by batch normal­
ization, a non­linear ReLU activation, and finally a max pooling layer to reduce the
spatial dimensions.
• Subsequent Encoder Blocks (encoder blocks 2­5): Each of these incorporates a
Residual Block aimed at capturing higher­level semantic features while maintaining
low­level spatial information. Each residual block is made up of two convolutional
layers that include Batch Normalization and Leaky ReLU activations. A skip con­
nection allows the input feature map to be added directly to the output of the block.
This promotes continuous gradient flow. When there is a discrepancy between input
and output dimensions, a 1x1 projection is applied to align them. Following each
residual block, there is a MaxPool2D layer that reduces the dimensionality of the
image by half progressively.
The encoding layers reduce the input masks from a size of 256x256 to a compact repre­
sentation of size 128x4x4, enabling the model to retain the essential structural information
of lesions while eliminating spatial redundancy.
Bottleneck: Latent Space Representation
At the bottleneck, the 128x4x4 encoder is compressed using a single 4x4 convolution
layer, producing a latent vector of shape 128x1x1. This represents the most abstract form
of the input. That means a compact representation that is meant to capture all essential
information needed for reconstructing and evaluating the segmentation mask.
The latent vector serves two purposes:
• Reconstruction Input: It serves as the foundation for recreating the original mask
through the decoder.
• Feature Embedding: It is extracted and used as input for unsupervised tasks such
as clustering, quality classification, and dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g,
t­SNE or UMAP) to examine inherent patterns in the annotation quality.

A transpose convolution layer is used to invert the bottleneck compression prior to de­
coding. This is done by mapping the latent vectors back into a spatial configuration of
128x4x4.
The strong dimensionality reduction at this phase encourages the encoder to focus on
the most relevant features, particularly capturing lesion structures and boundary details,
instead of relying on pixel­level memorization.
Decoder: Binary Mask Reconstruction
Figure 5.4: Decoder Architecture
The decoder replicates the encoder while performing spatial up­sampling to bring back the
original input resolution. Instead of relying solely on transpose convolutions, the model
follows a common practice of combining up­sampling operations with convolutional layers.
This is done to improve reconstruction quality [59]. This model incorporates an approach
that involves:
• Up­sampling: Each decoding phase starts with spatial enlargement via bilinear
interpolation. This technique improves the image resolution by predicting interme­
diate pixel values from neighboring ones, providing a smooth scaling experience
and preventing checkerboard artifacts. Unlike transpose convolutions, bilinear in­
terpolation is mathematical and helps avoid the checkerboard effect often seen in
deconvolutional layers. [59]
• Inverse Residual Blocks: After up­sampling, an Inverse Residual Block is ap­
plied. These blocks follow the same principle as the standard residual block but
are adapted for transpose convolutions. They consist of two transpose convolu­
tional layers that include Batch Normalization and LeakyReLU activation. This is
combined with a skip connection that may include a 1x1 transpose convolution to
align dimensions.
This design helps the model:
• Maintain semantic consistency while enhancing spatial resolution
• Prevent vanishing gradient
The final decoder block up­samples the feature map to 128x128 using bilinear interpola­
tion, followed by a transpose convolution that restores the image to its original 256x256
resolution. A sigmoid activation is applied at the final layer to ensure that the model’s out­
put values lie in the range [0,1], matching the normalized input binary masks. The masks
are converted to grayscale and normalized by ToTensor(), which scales pixel intensities
from [0,255] to floating­point values in [0.0, 1.0].

are from the ground truth values and guide the training to improve.

Generalized DICE Loss
The Generalized DICE Loss is a part of the MONAI library. It is a specialized DL toolkit
developed for medical imaging. It is an enhanced version of the conventional Dice Loss
designed to address imbalanced datasets, where certain classes appear significantly less
than others. A regular loss function, such as the MSE, might have its main focus on the
background, as that’s what dominates the image. Whereas, this loss function is designed
for medical image segmentation, especially when the region of interest is very small or
sparse. It focuses on making sure the predicted and actual masks overlap as much as
possible. It works well in our case because lesions take up only a small part of the entire
image. Hence, focusing directly on the shape and area of the lesion, not just pixel­by­pixel
accuracy [60].
In MONAI’s implementation, this loss assigns higher weights to under­represented classes,
ensures each class contributes equally to the final loss, regardless of the size, and can
be used with or without sigmoid [60].
Binary Cross­Entropy Loss (BCE)
This loss function is a commonly used loss function for binary classification tasks, where
the aim is to predict whether each input belongs to class 0 or class 1. In the case of this
project, it would be the background and the lesion. In segmentation, BCE is applied per
pixel, making it relevant for pixel­level accuracy tasks [61].
Mathematically, it is represented as,
BCE(y, ˆy) = −[y · log(ˆy) + (1 −y) · log(1 −ˆy)]
This formula ensures that incorrect predictions are penalized more heavily as they get fur­
ther from the correct label. BCE is well­suited because it treats each pixel as a separate
binary classification task, helps the model focus on accurately classifying each pixel, and,
unlike DICE loss, which estimates the overall overlap between the predicted and ground
truth masks, BCE focuses on each pixel independently. This makes BCE especially ef­
fective at capturing fine­grained details, such as sharp edges or small lesions, where local
precision is important. [62]
Combination of Loss Functions
In this phase of the project, the goal is to train an autoencoder that not only reconstructs
binary lesion masks accurately, but also has the capability to learn fine structural de­
tails. These fine structural details include small edges, isolated dots, and irregular lesion
shapes. To ensure both structural similarity and reconstruction of lesions are maintained,
two complementary loss functions were combined:
Total Loss = [0.6 × DiceLoss + 0.4 × BinaryCross −Entropy(BCE)Loss]
This way, Generalized Dice Loss directly measures the overlap between the predicted
and ground truth masks. It is ideal for small, sparse lesion areas. Due to this property of
the loss function, it is ideal for medical images where lesions make up only a tiny portion of
the full image. BCE Loss, on the other hand ensures pixel­wise accuracy, rewarding the
model for getting each pixel that it gets correct, particularly along the lesion boundaries.
By assigning more weight to the Generalized DICE Loss (60%), the training emphasizes
structural similarity and better reconstruction of lesions, while still preserving local detail
by including 40% from BCE to refine local detail.

Latent Space Visualization and Analysis
The latent space is central to every representation learning system. In this study, it func­

Latent Space Analysis
In the realm of medical imaging, especially when dealing with binary masks, raw pixel
values alone provides limited insights into the anatomical or pathological entities they de­
pict. Thus, it is crucial to investigate whether the features captured by deep networks,
particularly in the patent space, embody meaningful and distinctive patterns that corre­
late with clinically significant concepts such as lesion classification of annotation quality
assessment.
The following hypotheses were put forth:
• Unsupervised models, like autoencoders, trained for image reconstruction, may nat­
urally learn to group similar samples into clusters within the latent space. These
clusters are likely to emerge based on common visual characteristics or structural
elements, even without explicit label guidance.
• Supervised models, particularly those trained with explicit class or quality labels,
are anticipated to create more distinctly separated and organized latent spaces.
Supervision imposes task­specific constraints on the encoder, guiding it to organize
features in a way that aligns with human­defined boundaries.
• The formation, compactness, and uncertainty of clusters within the latent space indi­
cates the encoder’s capability to extract high­level, semantically meaningful features
from the input data. In medical scenarios, these abstractions may pertain to tissue
abnormalities, lesion shape, or the visual quality of annotation masks.
• By examining the configuration of the latent space, we seek to reveal qualitative
evidence of the model’s comprehension and differentiation between these clinical
categories. Moreover, such analysis can reveal whether noisy or low­quality annota­
tions disrupt the cluster formation, offering insights into the reliability and robustness
of feature learning in the presence of label imperfections.
t­Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t­SNE)
To represent the 128­dimensional latent vectors in a manner understandable to humans,

the encoder into a two­dimensional space, facilitating visual analysis of cluster develop­
ment and class separability [64].

Discussions
This chapter discusses the major findings derived from the experimental phases of this
study, highlighting how the interplay between autoencoder­based reconstruction, latent
space structure, and supervised classification yields valuable insights for quality assess­
ment in medical image annotation. The research was conducted particularly in the con­
text of Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) using the FGADR dataset. The investigation aimed to
assess whether representations learned from binary lesion masks, independent of expert­
labeled fundus images, could implicitly capture both annotation quality and the structural
characteristics of lesions.
The results from the experiments provide numerous important insights that go beyond the
limited focus of binary mask classification. They suggest potential pathways to improve
the reliability, robustness, and interpretation of medical image segmentation systems.
This discussion reflects on those findings, with a particular emphasis on how they sup­
port future research directions in DR diagnosis and the broader field of medical imaging.
8.1
Autoencoder Reconstructions
The initial set of experiments focused on training a convolutional autoencoder to recon­
struct binary lesion masks. Various loss functions were tested, and it was observed that
the BCE + Dice loss produced visually better reconstructions than other configurations. In
particular, lesions reconstructed with this loss combination appeared more distinct, with
smoother edges and better preservation of shape, especially for larger lesions. These
reconstructions were qualitatively more faithful than those produced with MSE or Dice
alone.
While this improvement in reconstruction quality was not formally quantified across anno­
tation quality levels, the overall visual fidelity indicated that the BCE + Dice loss enabled
the model to more effectively capture and reproduce lesion shape information from the
input masks.
This raised a question: ”If the model can reconstruct lesion masks more accurately using
this loss, does that mean the encoder has learned useful internal representations of the
mask structures?”
To investigate this, we extracted the latent vectors from the autoencoder that was trained
using BCE + Dice and visualized them with t­SNE. This enabled us to assess whether the
internal representations captured any semantically significant patterns associated with
lesion type, annotation quality, or both.
Implications
While the reconstruction quality does not provide definitive insights into the internal repre­
sentation of annotation quality, the observed qualitative enhancement in BCE + Dice loss
suggests the model may have acquired the ability to encode more lesion­specific struc­
tural information. This suggests the possibility that the encoder may be extracting shape
and structural information that extends beyond mere surface­level pixel similarity.
If these internal representations turn out to group together masks of the same lesion type,
or even the same quality class, it would suggest that:
• The model is not just memorizing patterns but learning to compress the masks in a
way that preserves clinically relevant features.

• Or they capture different levels of difficulty, that is, the autoencoder focuses on

Conclusion
The findings of this study provide several important insights into how deep learning models
trained on binary lesion masks can capture meaningful representations of both lesion
types and annotation quality. Through a combination of unsupervised reconstruction and
supervised classification tasks, the study demonstrated that annotation precision leaves
detectable structural patterns in the masks, and that these can be learned and exploited
for downstream classification and latent space organization.
Unsupervised training using a convolutional autoencoder revealed that reconstruction
quality improves significantly with the use of BCE + Dice loss, enabling the model to bet­
ter preserve lesion shape and detail. However, visualizations of the latent space showed
that annotation quality alone was not cleanly separable under this unsupervised setup,
suggesting that quality is a more abstract or higher­order feature that does not emerge
without explicit guidance.
Supervised classifiers, on the other hand, successfully learned both annotation quality and
lesion type from binary masks alone. The quality classifier achieved strong performance,
and the lesion classifier showed moderate success, especially given the constraints of
binary input. The combined­label classifier, despite lower accuracy due to class imbal­
ance, produced the most well­structured latent space, highlighting the benefit of multi­
dimensional supervision in organizing feature representations.
A quality­stratified evaluation of the lesion classifier further confirmed that annotation qual­
ity has a measurable impact on classification performance. The model performed signifi­
cantly better on high­quality masks than on low­quality ones, reinforcing the importance of
data quality in training and evaluation. Lastly, while the exploratory comparison between
reconstruction loss and classifier entropy revealed no clear correlation, it opened potential
for future investigations into combining structural and semantic uncertainty measures.
9.1
Future Work
While the current study focused on visual and qualitative analysis of model behavior, sev­
eral promising directions remain for future research:
1. Quantitative analysis of cluster structure in latent space:
Future work could compute distances between the centroids or midpoints of high­
and low­quality clusters in the latent space to measure how well the model separates
annotation quality internally. This would provide a more rigorous understanding of
class separability and embedding quality, especially across different training config­
urations.
2. Measuring intra­cluster vs. inter­cluster distances:
Calculating the compactness of clusters (intra­class distances) and the distance be­
tween clusters (inter­class) could reveal how consistent or diverse each annotation
group is, offering a way to identify noisy or overlapping categories.
3. Label noise detection:
Ambiguity or overlap in t­SNE clusters may not always reflect modeling limitations,
it may also indicate mislabeled data. Quantitative metrics such as Mahalanobis
distance from class centers or cluster boundary analysis could be used to flag po­
tentially mislabeled or outlier samples.

4. Latent space uncertainty metrics:
Building on the loss­entropy comparison, future studies could explore more sophis­
ticated metrics to estimate model confidence directly from the latent space, possibly
integrating them into active learning pipelines or label quality auditing tools.
5. Extension to image–mask pairs:
While this work focused on binary masks alone, future architectures could combine
both fundus images and their corresponding lesion masks to assess how pixel­level
features complement structural annotation quality.
6. Improved multi­task learning frameworks:
Instead of combining lesion type and annotation quality into a single label, future
work could employ multi­head architectures to predict each label separately but
concurrently. This could reduce class imbalance issues and improve model inter­
pretability.
7. Automatic feedback for human annotators:
With sufficiently high­performing quality classifiers, the system could be extended to
provide real­time annotation feedback, guiding annotators toward more consistent
and structurally rich lesion outlines.
Together, these future directions aim to build on the current study’s foundation by develop­

the reliability, transparency, and trustworthiness of annotation­based workflows in medical
image analysis. By leveraging both learned representations and quality­aware modeling,
future research can contribute to more robust segmentation systems, support efficient
data curation, and ultimately facilitate more accurate and scalable diagnostic pipelines in
clinical practice.
