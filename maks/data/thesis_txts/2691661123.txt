Executive summary
The European power system is currently undergoing a radical change owing to the growing
market share of variable renewable energy technologies. Several challenges follow the transition,
including an increasing need for flexibility measures and declining market values of solar and
wind energy, challenging the competitiveness of these energy sources. The rapid cost reductions
in battery energy storage in recent years have stimulated interest in coupled renewable-battery
systems to mitigate the value drop and to provide overall system value benefits. This thesis
intends to assess to what extent coupled batteries can improve the market value of wind and
how this relates to other mitigating measures. In addition, the system value of wind hybrids is
compared to that of independently sited wind and battery units.
The assessment is based on simulations performed for the European energy system using the
energy system optimisation model Balmorel. The system is optimised until 2050, corresponding
to the year when the climate-neutrality target of the European Union should be met.
The
assessment framework is based on a marginal case study, where hybrid configurations of 3-4
MW capacity are added exogenously to the model after the system has been optimised. This
enables a detailed assessment of the marginal value and operational patterns of hybrids, having
close to zero impact on the overall energy system and price. Configurations have been placed
in nine regions (West and East Denmark, North West and South Germany, Spain, France, the
UK, South West Norway, and South Sweden) to assess the value potential across varying system
characteristics. Eight scenarios have been evaluated combining different turbine designs and
storage capacities, including both standalone systems and hybrids.
Optimising the European energy system resulted in a total battery storage capacity of 141
GW in 2050, with the largest deployment in countries with high solar generation shares. For
wind-dominated countries, increasing flexibility offered by electric vehicles, power-to-X and inter-
connection capacities largely reduced the need for batteries. Moreover, the operational patterns
of hybrid and standalone batteries demonstrated a low arbitrage potential due to the regular
wind patterns, featuring long periods of surplus generation or calms. Evaluating the value factor
development of wind towards 2050 revealed a stabilising tendency after 2030 as a consequence
of increasing solar generation shares, standalone battery storage deployment and demand-side
flexibility.
Solar PV significantly impacts the value factor, as it both decreases the average
electricity price and acts as a key component in making battery storage profitable. As a con-
sequence of more volatile prices, hybrid wind-plus-battery configurations are found to be more
valuable in systems with combined wind and solar shares.
Comparing the competitiveness of hybrids to standalone wind turbines show a general better
business case for both high and low wind speed turbines. Low wind speed turbines are able to
provide similar value gain to that of hybrids but at a substantially lower cost. Consequently,
the additional total value of advanced turbine design compared to traditional turbines ranges
between 0.4-9 €/MWh, while hybrids result in added cost of 0.9-4.2 €/MWh. Likewise, compar-
ing the value of hybrids to independently sited wind and batteries exhibit a considerable system
value loss for all regions and all years, related to the coupling constraints of hybrids. The value
loss, referred to as the coupling penalty, averages 19 €/MWh. The value corresponds to the cost
savings and/or additional revenue gain required for hybrids to be competitive with independent
systems of comparable size. Admittedly, the penalty is subject to considerable uncertainty due
to the limitations of the method/model and varies also widely on a regional basis, meaning that
roles for coupled projects could potentially exist in the future energy system.

Preface
This thesis was developed in the department of Technology, Management and Economics at the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The thesis completes a Master of Science in Industrial
Engineering and Management, with a specialisation in Management and Analysis of Operations.
The project, representing 30 ECTS, was carried out from 4th of January 2021 to 18th of June
2021.
The thesis is a result of a collaboration between DTU and Ea Energy Analyses, in the framework
of work package 3 (WP3): Explorations in the Value of Wind Power, of IEA Wind Energy Task
26 Extension (2018-2021).
The main supervisor at DTU was Lena Kitzing, while co-supervisor was Postdoc Stefanie Buch-
holz. From EA Energy Analyses, Alberto Dalla Riva supervised the thesis development.

market and the total wind production (including curtailed energy) for a specific time period.
Essentially, it represents the average revenue per unit of wind produced. It is also sometimes
referred to as captured price, and is calculated as follows:

The market value of storage is calculated similarly, however the revenue includes both sale of
electricity and cost of charging.
Moreover, energy production correspond to the discharged
electricity from the battery.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Research motivation
In accordance with the global climate action goals of the Paris Agreement, the International
Energy Agency (IEA) released in May 2021 a roadmap for realising net-zero carbon dioxide
(CO2) emissions in the energy sector by 2050.
The IEA emphasises that decarbonising the
energy system relies heavily on a largely based renewable power sector, complemented by a
rapid phase-out of coal, oil and gas supply. In their pathway to net-zero, almost 90% of global
electricity generation in 2050 stems from renewable sources, with solar PV and wind accounting
for nearly 70%. Moreover, facilitating a carbon-free energy system requires huge increases in
electricity flexibility, such as batteries, demand response, hydrogen-based fuels and hydropower
to ensure reliable supply [1].
The increasing penetration of renewable energy sources is, however, not a new phenomenon to
the world. In recent years, renewables have dominated new installations of electricity generation
in Europe and globally, driven by consideration of environmental impacts, policy decisions and
significant cost reductions in particular wind and solar photovoltaics (PV) technologies. In many
markets, the cost has fallen to the point of which these technologies have become cost-competitive
with conventional thermal generation [2]. As a result, several countries have experienced an
exponential increase in wind power deployment in recent years. In 2019, 15% of the electricity
demand of the EU was covered by wind energy, with Denmark being the world-leading country
with a share of 48%. By 2030, a number of countries within the EU are expected to reach shares
above 30%, with some countries covering over half of their demand from wind energy [3].
However, the expected increasing wind penetration levels pose several challenges to the operation
of the electric power system and the market dynamics.
A number of properties specific to
the variable nature of wind energy are problematic for system integration, of which the most
important is the fluctuating availability of the source over time. Such integrating challenges can
materialise through grid expansion or increased balancing needs, both influencing the economics
of wind power. Nevertheless, the most significant economic impact is likely to be the reduced
market value of wind [4].
For wind energy, and variable renewable energy (VRE) technologies in general, the abundance
of the source itself tends to depress market prices during periods of high resource availability,
often referred to as the self-cannibalisation effect [5]. Having almost zero marginal cost, wind
energy pushes the market equilibrium towards lower prices in the hours of which wind power
plants are producing, following the merit order effect [6]. With higher penetration rates, wind
energy will continue to shift the residual load curve further to the left, causing an even larger
price drop. The result is a polarisation of prices, of which low prices occur during periods of
high wind availability and high prices occur when wind resources are absent. Consequently, wind
power producers will see a lower market price in hours of production compared to the average
annual price. This means that 1 MWh produced from wind power will on average be worth less
than 1 MWh produces from a constant source [5].
The market value drop that materialises with increasing VRE penetration is detrimental to the
competitiveness of wind energy, potentially endangering the decarbonisation of the power system.
This is a pressing issue in several regions already today and is eventually also expected to become
so globally [5]. Several mitigation measures have been presented in literature, particularly the

use of advanced turbine design for new wind power installations [5, 7, 8]. These so-called low
wind speed turbines shift the generation profile towards lower wind speed hours in which market
prices tend to be higher, thus enhancing the market value of wind. However, as the market
evolves and technologies develop, other measures to boost the value of wind may become more
beneficial, such as the integration of utility-scale storage.
In recent years, several energy storage applications, in particularly lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries,
have witnessed rapid cost reductions and technology advancement [9]. This has further lead to
stimulating interest in integrating utility-scale batteries into the grid. The necessity for energy
storage has previously been limited and less economically attractive, as the electricity generation
mix almost exclusively relied on fossil fuels [10]. However, with the increasing share of VREs and
the further cost-reduction potential in battery technologies, energy storage is on the rise. By co-
locating such battery energy storage at the wind farm, wind producers can shift generation from
periods with low energy prices to periods with higher prices, perhaps at an even greater extent
than low wind speed turbines. The more volatile wholesale prices associated with increasing
levels of variable renewable output will indeed make such energy arbitrage even more profitable.
The storage unit can also be used to absorb excess wind power production to further counter
the merit order effect. Previous research has identified several drivers of co-locating wind and
battery systems compared to siting them independently, including construction cost synergies,
better utilisation of transmission capacity and operational synergies through co-optimisation.
1.2
Research questions
The thesis aims at answering the following main and sub research questions:
To what extent can hybridising wind with battery energy storage increase the market value of
wind in the European energy system? How does this compare to the value obtained by other
system options, such as deploying advanced wind turbines or standalone batteries?
• What is the role of energy storage in the future European system?
• How is the potential value of hybrids changing based on different system characteristics
across Europe?
• How does the market value of hybrids relate to that of independently sited systems?

Electricity markets differ substantially from other commodity markets due to the physical char-
acteristics of electricity; large volumes of electricity cannot yet be economically stored, electricity
flows cannot be controlled easily and eﬀiciently due to capacity limits of power grids, and de-
mand and supply must match at all times [27]. To manage eﬀicient operation where reliable

ranging from derivative markets with mid to long-term financial contract trading, to the real-

The day-ahead market, often referred to as the spot market in Europe, is the largest market for
energy trade, often used to adjust long-term positions closer to delivery. The day-ahead market
consists of one pan-European auction at noon for the 24 hours of the next day [27]. Participants
can buy and sell energy for the following day hour-by-hour or in block time products. Producers
submit their offer at the minimum selling price (willingness-to-accept), while consumers submit
their bid at the maximum buying price (willingness-to-pay). The market stops trading a day
before delivery, at gate closure time (12:00 CET). The market operator thereafter determines
the aggregated supply and demand curves by sorting offers according to increasing price and
purchase bids in the inverse order. The clearing price and traded volume, corresponding to the
point of equilibrium, is determined by the intersection of these marginal merit order curves, and
is established for all bidding areas and for each hour of the following day. The equilibrium price
corresponds to the marginal cost of the last power plant dispatched to balance the demand and
supply, and will therefore vary on an hourly basis [24, 28].
Once the day-ahead market is cleared, the intraday market opens for trading up until one hour
before real-time delivery [30]. The intraday market provides a last opportunity for participants to
adjust their scheduled bids. After the intraday market closes, the transmission system operator
(TSO) is responsible for ensuring balance of supply and demand at all times by correcting
forecasting errors occurring in real-time, while also guaranteeing system reliability and adequacy
[27]. Depending on the real-time imbalances, settlements are applied after delivery based on
discrepancies between operation and initially scheduled bids. The intraday and balancing market
will become increasingly important in the future due to the expected rising penetration of
fluctuating renewable energy, causing increasing challenges related to accurate forecasting.

2.2.1
Impact of wind on the electricity market
As the marginal cost of renewable energy sources like wind and solar is close to zero, all offers
from these producers enter the aggregated supply curve from the left-hand side. In other words,
renewable generation is dispatched before conventional power producers and as a result their
output directly influences the market price. Due to the variable nature of wind energy, the
supply curve will be shifted depending on the wind availability at every hour. During periods
of low wind power production, the supply curve will be shifted to the left and conversely to the
right during periods of high production [31]. As illustrated in Figure 2.3, this will affect the point
at which the supply and demand curve intersects. Higher production levels result in a lower
market clearing price, as expensive generators are pushed out of the market and substituted by
wind power. Consequently, there is a negative correlation between hourly wind production and
wholesale power prices, which exacerbate with higher wind penetration. This is referred to as
the merit order effect [24]. The same applies to solar PV, having an even lower marginal cost
than that of onshore wind.
Figure 2.3: Impact of wind power on the market price for different demand levels [31].
Another important factor concerning the impact of wind production is connected to the price
elasticity of electricity. With the electric utility industry being a natural monopoly and electricity
being an essential good for the existence of modern civilisation, the demand is considered to be
inelastic. This means that a change in supply can cause rather drastic price differences without
having a large effect on demand. The impact of wind on the equilibrium price also depends
on the demand levels, which vary throughout the day. Figure 2.3 illustrates this effect. At
night hours, the demand curve will intersect at the flatter part of the supply curve, leading to a
smaller impact of wind power on the spot price. However, in peak hours the merit order effect
is considerable due to the steepness of the curve, causing the price difference to be rather large
when wind availability is low [31].
2.2.2
Impact of battery energy storage on the electricity market
Also, the operation of battery energy storage impact the merit order curve, however in a slightly
different manner than electricity generating units. Even though energy storage does not generate
electricity but only shifts it to other hours, it still impacts the clearing of the market. The
definition of marginal power price is given as ”the price per MWh if a marginal additional unit
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Hybrid energy systems
9
of demand would need to be supplied”. This would mean that the battery would be able to
charge 1 MWh less in this hour and therefore also discharge 1 MWh less in a subsequent hour.
For the hour where the storage usually would supply energy, the marginal producer of this
hour would have to supply 1 MWh additional power. The marginal cost of the battery storage
when charging is therefore determined by the marginal cost of the alternative option in the
hour it discharges. Consequently, the deployment of utility-scale batteries in the energy system
leads to more uniform prices, as the shift in energy usage alleviates the extreme price volatility
levels while price increases during hours of charging. The resulting price formation favours wind
producers, diminishing the merit order effect.
2.2.3
Future variations in wholesale electricity prices
The overall power prices in the European Continent is expected to rise towards 2030, driven
by higher fuel and CO2 prices.
In addition, with the phase-out of nuclear, coal and lignite
power plants, it is expected that natural gas set the price during more hours. From 2030 and
onwards, average prices are predicted to stabilise due to faster development and increasing share
of renewables. This development will however impact the short-term price variation in decades to
come. The variability in prices is anticipated to increase as VRE technologies begin to dominate
the energy mix, fuel and CO2 prices rise and more thermal plants are decommissioned. This
will further lead to an increased number of hours with high price spikes, in which the price is
set by flexible consumption with high willingness to pay, back-up generators and conventional
plants with high marginal cost. At the same time, the number of hours in which the price is low
or even zero is anticipated to increase, set mainly by the increasing share of VRES [32].
Even though additional demand-side flexibility and access to energy storage will dampen the
variations, their contribution to the system will most likely not prevent short-term variations.
This is reasoned by the fact that the magnitude of capacity necessary to develop suﬀicient
flexibility to remove the largest price impacts of VRE is presumably not profitable. This is
particularly the case for the winter months in which wind generation tend to vary significantly.
Price spikes during winter days with cold and still weather is already common today; the UK
reported its highest hourly price on record on the 5th of January 2021, reaching a spike of
£1,000.04/MWh [33].
Moreover, Statnett reports expected price spikes up to 500 €/MWh
in Norway in 2040 [32]. In comparison, the highest day-ahead price reported in Norway by
NordPool in 2019 was 110 €/MWh [34].
2.3
Hybrid energy systems
Due to the rapid growth in variable renewable energy and the fast-pace cost reductions in storage
technologies, the use of utility-scale energy storage in the electrical power grid has entered
the spotlight in recent years. With the simultaneous declining value in VRE technologies, an
increasing interest towards developing so-called Hybrid Power Plants has emerged. By definition,
hybrids can apply to any project combining multiple energy generation sources and potentially
storage, however this study will only focus on the coupling of wind and battery storage at the
same geographic location [22]. The battery storage unit of the hybrid system can either function
as a supportive element to the VRE asset or as an independently operated unit. The difference
rest on whether the battery is eligible to charge from the grid or not. For the scope of this thesis,
the hybrid battery can only charge from the co-located wind generator.

increase value by affecting the price formation to be more favourable for wind producers.
• Balancing services and forecast error mitigation: Due to the variability and uncertainty
related to VRE, the participation of wind and solar producers in balancing markets is
very limited. With the fast response time of battery storage, coupling batteries to wind
generators could enable balancing services such as frequency regulation. In addition, the
hybrid battery can reduce imbalance charges and penalties related to unfulfilled output
commitment.
• Reserve capacity: Being independently operated, neither VRE producers nor batteries
usually participate in the capacity market due to the risk of performance penalties. Due
to the characteristics of VRE, both the battery and the wind producer faces the risk of
having insuﬀicient energy during the performance period. Hybridising storage with VRE
reduces the risk for both systems, as the battery levels out the output while the generator
increases output duration [37]. Consequently, hybrids can earn additional revenues from
capacity payments, while also indirectly defer investments in expensive reserve capacity.
Indeed, as VRE penetration increases, the revenue earned from capacity and balancing
markets are anticipated to increase [2].
• Ramp control: Large change in wind and solar production output can lead to curtailment
and need for reserve services. A battery hybrid system can level output to avoid large ramp
rates, potentially increasing production, reducing cost of expensive thermal generation and
reducing maintenance cost of the renewable generator [37].
• Transmission benefits: Hybrid configurations are able to maximise the use of the grid
connection point by increasing the capacity factor of the system. This is related to the
variable nature of wind production, in which transmission lines are underutilised at low
production, while causing curtailment in hours where production exceed line capacity.
Coupled systems can therefore both increase sales and reduce construction costs due to
lower required transmission capacity.

As confirmed by numerous studies [37, 38, 39, 40], the maximum value of hybrid systems is
obtained through co-optimised operation, gaining revenues from multiple services. It should
be noted that the extent of additional revenue depends on the constraints posed on the hybrid
configuration, such as the geographical siting, the sharing of interconnection capacity and the
potential restriction on grid charging.

Barriers of hybridization
Despite the numerous benefits that hybrid power plants can provide, the commercial viability of
these systems is still limited. This is mainly due to the characteristics of the European energy
systems, where overcapacity of generation and a well-connected transmission grid creates small
electricity price differences and, thus, limited incentives for energy arbitrage. Regulatory barriers
are also constraining the deployment of such systems, including among others double network
charges, lack of legal definition for storage units and rules for participation of energy storage in
balancing markets. Wind energy asset owners in several European countries also have to reapply
for support schemes when adding energy storage to an existing wind farm, hindering producers
from hybridising in the fear of loosing subsidies [10].
Furthermore, hybridisation can lead to sub-optimal operation and siting compared to standalone
systems.
Firstly, the geographic location differs as batteries are often placed in areas with
congestion and high demand in contrast to remote locations where the highest wind resources
are based. Limiting the output of the hybrid to the point of interconnection capacity can also
reduce the value compared to standalone devices, as there may occur hours where the battery
discharge is restricted due to large wind generation [11]. Another revenue loss of co-locating can
accrue if the hybrid is only eligible to charge from the wind plant, as assumed in this work. This
entails that cost-reduction synergies related to co-location need to exceed the potential revenue
loss that these operational constraints comprise.
In order to deal with the regulatory barriers imposed on hybrid power plants, countries need to
also include these systems in their national regulatory framework. Procedures and requirements
should be standardised and formalised in order to accelerate the deployment of hybrid systems.
This also accounts for battery storage units in general, as lack of policy frameworks and clear
definitions make any business case for hybrids risky and unpredictable [21].

Methodology
The methodology section is split into three parts. First, the energy modelling tool used as the
basis of this analysis is described, together with the framework, assumptions and limitations of
the model. Secondly, the value assessment framework used to evaluate the impact of hybrids
and their competitiveness against standalone systems is introduced. This framework relies on
four different metrics. The third section presents the set-up for the hybrid configurations, along
with the different scenarios used to answer the research questions.
3.1
Energy system modelling in Balmorel
Energy models are increasingly used to provide insight into the future development of energy
systems, considering the radical changes in technologies and markets expected in the years to
come. A wide variety of models has emerged since the wake of the oil crisis, in which both
industry and policy makers realised the importance of long-term energy planning. In recent
years, the diverse challenges concerning security, affordability and resilience of energy supply, as
well as environmental concerns, have been incorporated in the model-based analysis of energy
systems. This particularly includes climate change policies targeting the integration of large
fractions of fluctuating renewable energy. Successful integration relies on complex interactions
between energy production, storage, distribution and consumption, which needs to be analyzed
under a range of scenarios [45].
Depending on the scope of the analysis, the system considered and the policy questions being
addressed, there is a wide range of energy modelling tools and typologies available to choose
between. For the aim of this work, the Balmorel model was selected on the basis of several
requirements. Firstly, it is able to perform scenario analysis towards 2050, which is important
to capture the increasing renewable penetration in decades to come.
It is an optimisation
model that allows for successive layers of simulation; from investment planning simulations
with optimised investments in new capacity and technologies, to day-ahead market simulations
where operation and market equilibrium is modelled based on economic dispatch. The hourly
resolution of the last-mentioned simulation is imperative for the comprehension of VRE and
storage operation, which is fundamental for this work.
Moreover, as this study intends to
answer how the benefits of hybrid power plants differ based on varying system characteristics,
the ability to model multiple countries is essential. Finally, the model is open-source and has
been continuously developed since 2000, making it a robust tool [46].
Balmorel is a bottom-up, partial equilibrium energy system optimisation model, with a detailed
characterisation of the electricity and heat system. Noteworthy, it is not a general equilibrium
model as it does not include all sectors, such as the transport sector. In addition, Balmorel is
implemented as a mainly linear programming optimisation problem [46]. It is coded in GAMS
(General Algebraic Modelling System), a high-level modelling system for mathematical program-
ming and optimisation [47].
3.1.1
Analysis and model framework
The analytical framework can vary between short-term and long-term perspectives, depending
on the assumptions made concerning the existing capital stock. For this work, a medium-term
perspective is used [12], in which existing infrastructure is given but the system can adapt
to evolving conditions through endogenous investments and decommissioning. The Balmorel

3.1.2
Modelling assumptions
Simulation process and time resolution
Three successive simulation layers have been executed, namely investment optimisation, full-
year optimisation and day-ahead optimisation. These refer to three different so-called ”Balbase
(Bb)” options. The sequence of simulations along with respective time resolution is illustrated
in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Sequence of simulations with respective time resolution. S = weeks, T = hours.
With a medium-term approach, the model first performs an investment optimisation (Bb2) where
generation and transmission capacity is planned and optimised in order to satisfy energy demand
and meet policy targets for emissions and renewable penetration levels at lowest cost.
The
investment into additional capacity, as well as decommissioning of existing non-competitive units,
are optimised for each decade from 2020 to 2050. The model converges to the optimal solution
by means of linear programming, meaning no binary or integer variables are considered. In
addition, due to the high computational heaviness of running investment simulations over a long-
term horizon, the time resolution has been reduced. This is achieved through time-aggregation,
where one year is composed of 26 weeks and each week consists of 12 hours. Consequently, the
hourly resolution of one year is aggregated down to 312 hours.
==PAGE:31==
3.1 Energy system modelling in Balmorel
17
The full-year optimisation run (Bb1) simulates one year at a time, taking the capacity invest-
ments from the Bb2 run as exogenous parameters. The purpose of the run is to accurately model
long-term operational decisions that cannot be optimised for in the short-term day-ahead opti-
misation. This includes planned maintenance of thermal generators, as well as the use of hydro
reservoirs and other seasonal storage. As capacity investments are fixed in the Bb1 optimisation,
a more detailed time resolution is feasible. The number of weeks simulated is therefore doubled,
including each of the 52 weeks of a year. One week is still aggregated to 12 hours, resulting in
a total of 624 hours.
Due to the highly fluctuating nature of VRES, the temporal resolution of the investment and
full-year optimisation is not suﬀicient to accurately model the impact of renewables on the
energy system, as well as the market value of these energy sources. In addition, the value of
storage highly depends on the price volatility from hour to hour, as the battery will charge
in hours of high price and discharge in hours of low price. In order to capture the complex
interaction between renewables, conventional generators, storage, distribution and consumption,
an hourly resolution is required. A third optimisation is therefore performed for the hourly
energy dispatch (Bb3), where the capacities optimised for in the investment simulation are fixed.
The hourly (day-ahead) optimisation also relies on the results of the full-year optimisation, which
determines the availability of the units and the initial storage content that can be utilised at
the beginning of each week. With regard to time resolution, one year is assumed to consist of
52 weeks, corresponding to 8736 hours. The optimisation run replicates the principles of the
day-ahead markets in Europe, where bids are scheduled according to the merit order curve and
generators are dispatched based on their marginal cost. In addition to determining the supply
of each generator, the market clearing price for each hour and each region correspond to the
shadow price of the energy balance constraint of that region.
Finally, the analysis is limited to the day ahead market, neglecting both the intra-day market,
the balancing market and forward and futures contracts. Consequently, the benefits and costs
of these markets are not considered. This is further discussed in the following section.
3.1.3
Balmorel limitations
Even though Balmorel is chosen as the energy system model for this thesis work, it is important
that the reader is familiar with the limitations of the model to understand the comprehensiveness
of the results. First of all, Balmorel is a deterministic energy system model assuming perfect
foresight. As such, the model does not represent the stochastic behaviour of variable renewable
energy production and forced outages of dispatchable units. The imperfect predictability of
key parameters, such as hydro inflows, wind speeds, solar irradiation and demand levels can
therefore not be captured. Consequently, the model might provide optimal solutions which in
reality is not practically feasible. This also includes an artificially high eﬀiciency of battery
storage as prices are known from the beginning. Uncertainties related to political decisions on
energy and environmental systems are also outside the dimensions of the model.
Secondly, the model assumes an atomistic market structure where energy producers act as
price takers without any strategic market behaviour. This entails that there is no exercise of
market power and such, the optimal bidding price of each market agent corresponds to their
marginal cost/utility. In markets where the clearing conditions differ largely from those under
perfect conditions, the Balmorel model is less suited. It should however be mentioned that the
assumption of perfect competition rests on a heavy tradition and have been applied in numerous
studies [48].
==PAGE:32==
3.1 Energy system modelling in Balmorel

Thirdly, several transmission grid-related issues are not covered in Balmorel. As further ex-
plained in Appendix A.1, Balmorel does not include any representation of the internal grid
within a region. By considering each region as an electrical ”copper plate”, neither distributional
power flows, congestions or bottlenecks can be defined within the regions [49]. Consequently,
issues such as voltage and angle stability, as well as primary and secondary reserve management
cannot be accounted for. The value that energy storage units can provide in terms of ancillary
services can therefore neither be quantified. In addition, it is not possible to capture the effect
of location between hybrid configurations and standalone systems, in which standalone batter-
ies would most often be placed in close proximity to congested areas instead of remote areas.
Lastly, there is no guarantee that the resultant flow is feasible from a grid perspective, as the
flow within a region is only limited by the maximum transmission capacity connected to the
region and not the inter-regional grid connections.
In addition to the limitations and assumptions presented above, some additional remarks are
mentioned:
• The balancing market have not been modelled as the Balmorel model used in this work
has no accurate representation of how these markets function in the European energy
system. Consequently, it is not possible to quantify the value that batteries can gain from
providing energy or capacity balancing services.
• As for all models simulating the future energy system, there is a high uncertainty related
to projections of costs and performance of future technologies. The results therefore highly
depend on the accuracy of these data inputs.
• The investment optimisation only optimises for one year at a time, meaning that the
potential profit of long term investments and conditions of the future energy system is not
accounted for. The model only considers whether the power plant is able to pay back the
annuity of the investment, and if so, the plant is invested in.
Consistency in wholesale electricity prices
As previously mentioned, long-term investment models are dependent on time aggregation, which
poses a number of challenges to the model. For Balmorel, the problem is rooted in the fact that
8,736 hours are aggregated into 312 time segments, meaning that the investment optimisation
is not able to capture the extreme cases of variability in hourly dependent sources, such as wind
and solar. Including energy storage in the model adds another layer of complexity, which is
diﬀicult to accurately capture with aggregated timesteps. The same applies for demand-side
flexibility options provided by power-2-x (P2X), industrial heating and smart charging from
electric vehicles (EVs). The flexibility offered by these measures is, just like batteries, highly
dependent on the hourly variations in prices.
Running the model without performing any modifications to the modelling of energy storage or
other flexibility measures results in a system with negligible battery storage investments. This
is the consequence of time aggregation, as it significantly limits the arbitrage trade opportuni-
ties. Several measures have been considered to incentivise investments in battery storage (see
Appendix A.2). In the end, a reduction in capital and fixed operation and maintenance (O&M)
cost of batteries were imposed on the investment optimisation run, corresponding to a factor
of 1/4. This is indeed the current set-up used at Ea Energy Analyses. The reduction is later
adjusted for in the hourly Bb3 simulation when calculating the levelized cost of storage.

3.2
Value assessment framework
In order to analyse the impact of hybridisation and to compare value provided by different
configurations, four metrics have been developed. The first two metrics, Market Value (MV)
and Levelized Cost of Electricity/Storage (LCOE/LCOS), is used to evaluate how the value and
cost of hybrid systems relate to high and low wind speed turbines across different markets in
Europe. They also serve the purpose of defining the most cost-effective hybrid configuration,
considering different energy-to-power (E/P) ratios. This is presented by the ratio between MV
and LCOE, also defined as the Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR). The third metric, Value/cost adder,
aims to quantify the additional value/cost of either adding the most favourable hybrid battery
to the HWST or replacing the turbine with a LWST. Comparing the value adder to the cost
adder act as a measure for the competitiveness of the different configurations. Lastly, a firth
metric is developed to assess the relative system value of hybrids compared to independently
sited wind turbines and batteries, referred to as the Coupling penalty.

While the storage value adder serve as a good measure for comparing standalone wind to hybrids,

Following the definition proposed in [22], the coupling penalty is calculated by subtracting the
market value of the hybrid from the market value of the independent wind turbine and battery, as

3.3
Set-up of market value analysis
In order to answer the research questions presented in Section 1.2, a two-step approach has been
pursued. The first step aims to quantify the need for storage in the European system and how
this differs across countries. The second step investigates the value that hybrid configurations
can provide compared to standalone devices, assuming different system characteristics. This
is conducted as a marginal case study where all configurations are added exogenously to the
model after the system has been optimised. Moreover, only marginal capacities are considered,
meaning that the implementation has close to zero impact on the overall energy system and
price. The set-up for the two approaches is described in detail in this section.
3.3.1
Role of energy storage in the European system
As the analysis conducted in this work depend highly on the conditions of battery energy storage
in the system, a preliminary study has been conducted to evaluate the deployment of batteries.
The need for such an analysis is particularly important considering the limitations that this
study has identified concerning the modelling of storage in Balmorel. Towards 2050, battery
energy storage is anticipated to cover large parts of the flexibility needs, especially in countries
where interconnection capacity and other flexibility measures are limited. For evaluating the
battery storage deployment, an investment optimisation (Bb2) is performed using the common
framework of the Balmorel model, described in Section 3.1.1 and Appendix A.1.
Based on the results, the flexibility offered by different measures is evaluated in order to deter-
mine the contribution from battery energy storage. Other measures considered are demand-side
flexibility from: end-users, industrial heating, P2X and smart charging from EVs. A description
of the different means can be found in Appendix A.4. As the flexibility needs across countries
in Europe varies highly depending on the energy mix, the demand break-down and the intercon-
nection capacity, each country/region is evaluated separately. Based on the findings, potential
adjustments are made in order to ensure alignment with the expected battery storage capacities
reported in literature.
3.3.2
System value of hybrids
In the second step, the marginal value of wind and battery coupled systems are evaluated
for different energy system characteristics across Europe. The purpose is to: 1) determine to
what extent hybridising wind turbines with energy storage can increase the market value of
wind, also taking into consideration the additional cost of implementing battery storage units,
and 2) determining the system value of hybrid configurations compared to the value offered by
independently sited wind turbines and batteries. The main focus for the choice of the scenarios
in this analysis is related to the design of wind turbine technologies and the capacity of battery
storage units.

Scenario definition
Based on the described turbine and battery configurations, a number of scenarios have been de-

different E/P ratios are assessed. Moreover, the capacity ratio between the turbine and the bat-

fewer hours where all of this production is stored, the power capacity of the battery is usually
considered to be lower than the capacity of the turbine. This result in more eﬀicient utilisation
of capacity, as well as lower investment costs. Based on literature review, the capacity ratio
often ranges between 0-40% [56, 57]. Finally, a total of eight scenarios have been simulated for

Development in market value and value factor of onshore wind
From Figure 4.10 it can be observed a decreasing market value (MV) and value factor (VF)

largest increase, from 19% to 66% in the 10-year span. Moreover, results show a lower value drop
in relative terms compared to the absolute market value. This is related to the development
in average wholesale prices, mainly affected by the increasing shares of solar generation in the
overall energy mix.

drop of 15% in the first decade compared to a 2% drop in relative terms. Considering that
wind generation shares only increase by 2% in the same time period, the absolute value drop

cannibalization of wind. Similar tendencies can be observed for France and the UK, with lower
value gaps for the wind value factor. Noteworthy, the drop is still present due to simultaneously
increasing wind shares. Consequently, solar PV has a beneficial impact on the value of
wind in relative terms, while in absolute terms it contributes to further price depression.
Finally, Southern Sweden and Southwestern Norway have a limited value gap, both in real and
absolute terms, due to modest wind deployment.

With new wind farms being installed in the energy system, decisions must be made whether to

hybrids indeed can increase the market value of wind, however the system penalty of coupling
wind and batteries is considerable for all markets and all years.

4.4
Sensitivity analysis - Interconnection capacity
As results have illustrated, the value of hybrids are highly dependent on the flexibility needs
and the price volatility of different markets. Countries characterised by high interconnection
capacity to neighbouring countries tend to have less fluctuations in prices and therefore lower
value of energy arbitrage. Moreover, increased transmission positively impacts the value factor of
wind, as also confirmed by numerous studies [59, 60]. In order to evaluate the trade-off between
transmission capacity and battery storage deployment, a sensitivity is conducted on the allowed
investments in interconnectors across Europe. A special focus is directed towards Germany and
Denmark, which features high wind shares but no initial battery storage deployment.
With the current set-up of the Balmorel model, new transmission capacity investments of 3
GW are allowed for every 5-year period. The internal capacity within Germany is an exception,
with 6 GW allowed each 5-year. Moreover, capacity expansion planned before 2030 is fixed
exogenously based on information provided by Europe’s and Germany’s Network Development
Plan (TYNDP and NEP). These plans include large investments between Southern and Northern
Germany. Based on the intitial set-up, the sensitivity includes the following modifications:
• Reducing endogenous investments in transmission capacity by 66%. Corresponds to al-
lowed investments per 5-year of 1 GW (2 GW for internal Germany).
• Reducing exogenous planned expansion between Northern and Southern Germany from
2025-2030 by 50%. Corresponds to a reduction from 10 GW to 5 GW.

should be noted that results also show an increase in capacity to some regions in which the
initial expansion did not reach the allowed 5-year restriction. This is for example the case for
Northwestern Germany, whereof capacity and flow to Sweden and Norway increases.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This chapter first addresses the results of this thesis work compared to previous studies on
battery energy storage and hybrid systems, with a special focus on the recently published report
by Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL) on the value of coupled renewable-battery
power plants in the U.S. [22]. Secondly, limitations of the analysis are presented, with a particular
focus on the consistency issues between the investment and hourly optimisation run. A simplified
optimization model is presented as an alternative to the Balmorel model, in order to quantify to
what extent a less complex set-up could answer the research questions. Moreover, a discussion
follows on different factors that have not been possible to quantify, and how these impact the
competitiveness of hybrid power plants.
5.1
Comparison of results to previous studies
Overall value of energy storage
The future role of energy storage in the modelled European system is rather limited, with
only considerable installations in countries with large solar generation shares. This includes
among others Spain, France and partly Southern Sweden and the UK. By 2050, Denmark and
Southern Germany also acquire a fair share of solar generation, however the significant expansion
of electric vehicles and P2X demand devalue batteries, as they offer free demand-side flexibility
to the system. Together with large interconnection capacity to neighbouring countrie,s the need
for batteries is eliminated. The total power capacity installation of 141 GW in Europe in 2050
is consistent with that presented in [14], ranging between 90-200 GW at 50% and 90% VRE
shares.
Evaluating the battery operation of the standalone and hybrid system, a lower utilisation is
observed in regions with high wind generation shares. With wind frequently setting the price
on the market, the arbitrage opportunities of batteries are limited owing to the fact that high
and low wind availability often occur over longer, consecutive hours. The results of [14] and
[16] present the same tendency, in which almost no (or very limited) batteries were installed
in scenarios highly dominated by wind power. The regular wind patterns, with long periods
of surplus generation or calms, suggest that other integration measures might be more suited,
such as increased transmission capacity or hydrogen energy storage. This is also confirmed by
the sensitivity study conducted, in which batteries were still not profitable in wind-dominated
regions even with a large reduction in interconnection capacity. In markets where solar domi-
nates, the price volatility is greater, following low prices during mid-day hours and high prices
in remaining hours. The daily generation pattern enables a high number of storage cycles which
benefits battery energy storage. Moreover, independently sited batteries will gain higher value
for these systems thanks to the ability to also store solar energy. These results are aligned with
previous studies on the topic, emphasising the complementarity of batteries and solar PV [14,
15, 16].
Impacts on the market value development of wind
As presented in Section 1.3, several studies have emphasised the drop in market value of wind
towards 2030 as a result of increasing wind penetration. The findings of this study confirm the
expected trend, with wind value factors ranging mainly between 0.9 and 0.7 in 2030, depending
on the region. Summarising the results, the value dropped to 0.86 (-12.4%) in Southern Germany,
to 0.69 (-20.7%) in Northwestern Germany and to 0.78 (14%) in Denmark from 2020 to 2030. At
this point, onshore wind shares corresponded to 23% (Southern Germany), 42% (Northwestern
48

49
Germany) and 25 % (Denmark). Noteworthy, the increasing offshore wind capacity in Denmark
and Northwestern Germany further suppresses the wind value factor, but at a lower rate than
onshore wind [7, 13].
Comparing the mitigating effect of hybrids and LWSTs, slightly higher value adders are observed
in general when coupling a battery to the turbine rather than changing turbine design. Nev-
ertheless, the adder is largely dependent on different system characteristics. In markets with
high solar generation shares, hybrids seem to be the preferred mitigation option due to a more
beneficial price profile for energy arbitrage. For wind dominated systems, the value provided by
hybrids and LWSTs is often comparable due to lower arbitrage opportunities and a considerable
merit order effect. With a value gap of 0.06 and a 9% value increase of LWST in Northwestern
Germany, the results are well aligned with previous studies, reporting a 10-15% increase for
similar turbine types [5, 7]. Finally, the fact that hybrids are more beneficial in systems with
large price volatility make them less effective for the purpose of mitigating the wind value drop,
as such price profiles are rarely observed in markets where wind is largely dominating.
Previous studies conducted on the wind value factor has mainly focused on the years up to
2030, in which storage deployment is found to be more or less negligible. This study reveals
a change in development from 2030 onwards, following higher solar generation shares, battery
installations, demand-side response and interconnection capacity. Due to the correlation effect
of solar, wind producers will benefit from systems characterised by generation from both energy
sources compared to wind dominated ones. The presence of solar PV decreases the average price
in the market, whereas wind power plants continue to produce during high price hours. Conse-
quently, the value factor of wind increases. This is particularly observed by the development in
wind value factor in Spain towards 2050, but is also true for the remaining countries as solar
generation shares rise. The larger deployment in battery energy storage also provides additional
value to wind, enabling higher wind generation and more favourable price formation for wind
producers. Finally, results indicate that also the increasing demand-side response and intercon-
nection capacity towards 2050 is contributing to the mitigated value drop, as also underlined by
[59] and [60].
Hybridisation versus standalone wind and battery
The magnitude of the storage value adder reported in this work can be compared to that found
in [22], presented in Section 1.3. At first sight, the additional value offered by hybrids seems to
be rather modest for the system modelled in this study, with adders ranging from 0.3 €/MWh
to 9 €/MWh, compared to $3-$22/MWh in [22]. However, as mentioned in Section 1.3, the
assumptions and framework of the two studies are rather different. Firstly, the zonal pricing and
subsequent market-coupling in Europe lead to more eﬀicient utilisation of cross-border capacity
and reduced price differences, compared to the U.S. Consequently, the arbitrage opportunity
for batteries in Europe is lower.
Moreover, historical prices are generally characterized by
higher volatility than simulated prices, as also presented in the following section for European
prices. The study also included capacity market value in contrast to only energy market value
as estimated in this work. The lower battery-turbine capacity ratio used in this study further
reduces the value potential of batteries. Taking these aspects into consideration, the storage
value adders can be considered comparable. Furthermore, a storage value adder of 3 €/MWh in
the UK is enough to increase the value factor by 0.07 in 2030, from 0.78 to 0.85. This indicates
that larger value adders are not necessarily required to make a difference towards the value of
wind in the modelled European system.

5.2 Reflections and limitations of the analysis
50
Despite the mitigating effect of hybrid on the value deflation of wind, results show diminishing
returns compared to both high and low wind speed turbines, related to the additional cost
of installing a battery. These results align with [17, 18, 19, 20], all addressing the value of
battery energy storage in electricity markets. Their results indicate that additional revenue
streams, besides that obtained by energy arbitrage, is required to reach competitiveness. This
study confirms that batteries coupled with wind generators are no exception.
Nevertheless,
the additional value gain required is largely site-dependent. In France, the high price volatility
results in large value adders from arbitrage, corresponding to 3.5 €/MWh in 2050. The value loss
of 0.9 €/MWh constitutes 25% of the arbitrage value. The share of capacity value versus energy
value presented for the California ISO in [22], suggest that capacity payments could potentially
cover this value loss. Countries such as Germany and Denmark, where the additional required
value is larger than that obtained from energy arbitrage, will likely not reach competitiveness by
offering reserve capacity, as arbitrage is anticipated to constitute the largest share of revenues
also in the future [2]. The question of whether additional revenue streams from e.g. capacity
payments and reduced balancing costs can outweigh the cost adder for some regions is essential,
and is a topic that should be addressed in further studies.
Similar to the findings of [22], the coupling of batteries and wind generators result in a substantial
value loss compared to standalone systems. The coupling penalty essentially provides a threshold
for the amount of synergy savings or additional value gain that is required for the hybrid to be
competitive with that of independently sited systems. Several assumptions made when designing
the hybrids can potentially change the results presented here. [22] has addressed the impact of
some of these assumptions in their sensitivity study (see Figure 1.4). Due to the time-consuming
and complex setup of the simulation process used in this study, along with the limitations of the
model, it has not been possible to quantitatively determine the impact of these assumptions. A
qualitative discussion on the potential impact of different factors is presented in the following
section.
5.2
Reflections and limitations of the analysis
The suitability of Balmorel
As described in the methodology section, several Balmorel simulations have been conducted in
order to evaluate the marginal impact of hybrid systems on the market value of wind. The whole
simulation process is rather time-consuming, as it takes about one day to run the investment
optimisation (Bb2), 0.5 days for the full-year run (Bb1) and 2 hours per day-ahead simulation
(Bb3). The last-mentioned optimisation is performed for each scenario and each decade from
2020 to 2050, accumulating to a total of 24 runs (48 hours).
Any minor changes to input
parameters entail re-running the model. The limitations with regard to modelling of energy
storage add to the question of whether the Balmorel model is the best fit for this purpose.
Accordingly, it has been investigated whether a simplified model could provide similar results.
The benefit of such a model is greater time eﬀiciency, in which parameters such as turbine size,
storage capacity, point of interconnection capacity and hybrid cost components can easily be
adjusted. The market value of wind can be calculated based on exogenous input data given to
the Balmorel model, and would in theory not require the complex methodology used in this work.
The challenge of the simplified model is finding a method to accurately represent the charging
and discharging behaviour of the hybrid battery. This has been accomplished by developing an
optimization model in GAMS, in which historical day-ahead prices and hourly wind generation
profiles are used as input. The mathematical model is described in Appendix A.7.

Limitations of marginal case study
This section addresses the limitations of the marginal case study, discussing the elements that
have not been possible to quantify with the current methodology and how these potentially
could have altered the final result. These limitations mainly relate to potential revenue streams
of both standalone and hybrid systems that have not been accounted for. Moreover, impacts
concerning the coupling constraints of hybrids are reflected upon.
Due to the complexity of this scope and the need for investment optimisation, the use of unit
commitment has not been feasible. The model therefore only includes economic dispatch, lacking
constraints related to minimum/maximum up- and downtime and ramp limitations of the power
plants. Adding these elements could provide additional value to batteries. For example, ramp
restrictions could limit the up-scaling of power plants during scarcity hours, requiring expensive
reserve capacity to cover demand. Similarly, steep ramp rates of wind generation increase the
need for response requirements from conventional generators, and can also lead to significant
curtailment. Consequently, under the constraints of unit commitment, batteries can increase
production and reduce the need for expensive reserve capacity.
Additionally, having perfect foresight about VRE production, hydro inflow and demand levels
removes the uncertainty of these parameters. In reality, the dispatch in terms of market outcome
and the investment into reserve requirements would largely be affected by the supply-demand
mismatch that such uncertainty could cause. Assuming perfect foresight presents in a way the
maximum arbitrage revenue that can be obtained by batteries, as complete certainty about
market prices leads to artificially high eﬀiciency. In line with the findings of [22], imperfect
conditions would result in lower storage value adders.
Due to the mismatch between the Bb2 and Bb3 run, as well as the lack of unit commitment
and imperfect conditions, the price profiles resulting from the hourly optimisation are subject
to uncertainty. As demonstrated by the comparison of real and simulated prices in 2020, the
Balmorel model is not able to accurately represent the true volatility in prices. As evidenced
both in this work and in numerous other studies (e.g. [22, 40, 61]), high price fluctuations act
as the most important driver for batteries to be profitable. As also confirmed by the simplified
optimization model, real-world value adders of batteries and hybrids will likely be higher than
those presented in this study. Moreover, results show a substantially higher value of standalone
batteries in regions with large fluctuations, such as France. The impact of real prices might
therefore cause larger value differences between hybrids and standalone systems.

Besides energy arbitrage, battery energy storage can also earn revenues from participating in
the balancing market.
However, the additional value depends on the VRE penetration and
flexibility needs in the market, which differs across Europe. The potential increases with higher
price volatility, indicating more frequent dispatch of reserve capacity. Even though this revenue
stream has not been possible to quantify in this work, it is expected that batteries will to a greater
extent take part in offering ancillary services and reserve capacity in the future, promoted by
the increase in VRE shares. For battery coupled systems, this also includes reducing imbalance
charges and penalties of wind producers by means of capacity firming. Nevertheless, including
balancing markets in the model is expected to benefit standalone batteries more, owing to the
coupling constraints of the hybrid. This is also confirmed by [22], where enabling regulation
reserves increased the average coupling penalty from $12/MWh to $23/MWh. The regulatory
structure for providing frequency response and reserve capacity may however benefit hybrid
systems more, as the likelihood of meeting performance requirements is higher.
One of the main limitations of the marginal case study is related to the system on which it
is established. Being a marginal study, hybrids are installed exogenously after the system has
been optimised, meaning balance of supply and demand is secured. Consequently, the system is
more or less saturated with batteries, and the value of adding a hybrid system is therefore lower
compared to a system in need of flexibility. The storage value adder of hybrids from 2035 and
onwards might therefore be underrepresented, as a result of increasing system battery capacity.
For accurate analysis, the hybrid systems should be considered as an alternative for standalone
batteries instead of a supplement. However, it should be mentioned that differences in terms of
value would mainly be observed in countries with battery deployment, meaning that the value
of hybrids in Germany and Denmark would likely remain unchanged.
As Balmorel considers each region a ”copper plate”, the opportunity cost of siting a storage
unit at a potentially less valuable location to be near the wind asset has not been possible
to assess.
Based on results presented by [22], the siting constraint imposes a rather large
impact on the coupling constraint, endorsing even higher values of independently sited batteries.
Moreover, the lack of inter-regional representation disables the opportunity to assess the impact
of grid charging allowance for the hybrid, as this would entail the same configuration as for the
standalone battery. In accordance with results from [22], allowing hybrids to charge from the
grid is expected to improve the storage value adders.
In this study, the transmission limit of the hybrid configuration has been restricted by the
maximum capacity of the VRE. Consequently, if the turbine produced at maximum capacity,
the battery would not be able to sell additional energy. [22] show that for the U.S. system,
increasing the POI capacity to the total capacity of the wind and battery device could provide
additional value, in their case $1.6/MWh. However, considering the load duration curve of the
hybrid versus HWST presented in this study, the hybrid battery mainly increases output at lower
wind production. Accordingly, the POI capacity is rarely restricting output, and an increase
would likely result in lower capacity utilisation and higher cost without a significant increase
in value. Taking this into consideration, it could oppositely be of interest to run a sensitivity
on reduced POI capacity. With a higher capacity factor of the transmission line, hybrids can
facilitate cost savings as less transmission capacity is essentially required. This can further have
a significant impact on the larger system value owing to the potential of transmission deferral
for future wind power plants. Due to time limitations of this thesis, the impact of different POI
capacities has not been quantified.

For simplicity reasons, a turbine rating of 3 MW for the HWST is assumed for all years up
to 2050. With continuous development in turbine technology, it is acknowledged that larger-
capacity turbines will likely enter the market in years to come, as has been seen in previous years
[62]. If the same turbine-battery ratio is assumed, a larger rating would also indicate greater
battery capacity, and thus increasing costs. However, as larger turbine capacities are primarily
beneficial in high-quality wind regions, hybrids would likely become even less profitable due to
the demonstrated low arbitrage potential. At the same time, regions with favourable conditions
for batteries mainly feature low wind resources and would therefore benefit more from low wind
speed turbines with lower rating.
Lastly, cycle induced degradation of batteries are not accounted for in the model. If a degrada-
tion penalty were to be imposed on the battery operation, the value adders of both standalone
batteries and hybrids would decrease. However, the difference would likely be greater for stan-
dalone batteries due to more frequent charge and discharge, especially in high-volatile countries.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
Concluding remarks
Promoted by climate change challenges and ambitious decarbonisation targets, the expected
surge in variable renewable generation can favour investments in hybrid power plants in the
European energy system.
The topic is increasingly relevant for ensuring competitiveness of
VRE technologies, owing to the reported market value drop. This thesis work therefore aimed
to analyse the power system impact of hybridising wind and battery storage, with a special focus
on its mitigating effect on the declining wind value. Additionally, the system value provided by
hybrids was compared to that of independently sited devices.
The day-ahead market simulation firstly reveals a less pressing need for mitigating measures
than initially expected. With technology development and substantial cost reductions, larger
investments in solar PV is expected in most European countries towards 2050. Lead by the
correlation effect, the presence of solar generation largely benefits wind producers as average
prices are reduced while wind generation frequently occurs during high price hours. Moreover,
solar provides profitable conditions for battery storage due to more volatile prices. The operation
of batteries results in price profiles more beneficial for wind producers, mitigating the merit order
effect. Together with the extensive expansion in interconnection capacity that is anticipated
across Europe, the need for additional measures is somewhat alleviated.
Moreover, the ability of hybrid power plants to substitute some of the above-mentioned means
is found to be unprofitable for the model framework of this thesis. Solar generation is naturally
built in the system, as it is a generation technology offering electricity at very low cost. Ex-
panding transmission capacity is also considered to be more favourable than installing battery
storage in many cases. This is related to the arbitrage potential of batteries, which is proved
to be moderate in countries with high wind shares. The sensitivity study further underlines
the claim, where batteries remain unprofitable even with a large reduction in interconnection
capacity. With wind patterns often being characterised by long periods of surplus generation
or calms, other integration options such as long-term energy storage are likely more suitable.
Conversely, large battery energy storage deployments are found in solar dominated countries
due to the complementary nature of these sources. In general, favourable conditions for battery
operation is found in countries with high price volatility, correlated with the daily patterns of
solar availability. Consequently, solar PV has both a direct positive effect on the value factor of
wind, as well as an indirect impact by facilitating more favourable conditions for batteries. As
such, hybrid configurations provide largest storage value adders for these countries.
Comparing the hybrid system to independently sited wind show a less competitive business case.
In particularly, LWSTs are able to provide similar value adders as hybrids at a substantially
lower cost.
Nevertheless, results indicate that hybrids can potentially become cost-effective
in regions with high price volatility, if additional revenue streams from balancing markets are
included. Moreover, it can be noted that that only hybrid configurations featuring a HWST is
considered. However, results show that LWSTs can provide higher value than HWST for most
countries, also at a substantially lower cost. Consequently, further work should investigate the
business case of coupling LWSTs with batteries, particularly in regions featuring low wind speeds.
Finally, the coupling of wind turbines and batteries result in a considerable value loss compared
to independently sited devices, averaging 19 €/MWh (26.1 €/MWh in 2050) or a 32% reduction
in value.
The penalty is driven by limitations on grid charging and shared interconnection

capacity. Moreover, the estimated cost savings required to make hybrid systems competitive
with standalone devices are much higher than what literature suggest reasonably.
As this study is limited to the day-ahead market, with price profiles being less volatile than what
is expected in the future energy system, both the real-world storage value adder and the coupling
penalty may be higher or lower than those estimated in this work. Easing the grid charging
constraint is likely to cause larger storage value adders for hybrids. Additionally, the inclusion
of revenue streams from higher realized price volatility and balancing services can improve the
business case of hybrid power plants. This is, however, also the case for standalone batteries.
More optimal siting can also provide additional value, considering both transmission losses
related to remote wind assets and the siting of standalone storage in congested areas. Ultimately,
hybrid power plants can be beneficial from a business-profitability perspective, however results
show that independently sited wind and battery units are likely to provide greater system value.
6.2
Future work
With continuous development in renewable energy sources and storage technologies, along with
the large uncertainty of future energy systems, many research questions continue to remain
open. Firstly, the market structure might change significantly as VRE shares continue to grow,
potentially making balancing markets increasingly more important in the future. Quantifying
the additional storage value adder from providing such services and reducing imbalance charges
can provide valuable insight into the competitiveness of both hybrids and standalone batteries
in different system characteristics.
Moreover, the detailed configuration of hybrid systems has not been addressed in this thesis.
Different system topologies exist depending on the design of the electrical infrastructure, which
will further impact the potential synergy savings that can be obtained from coupling assets.
This should be optimised depending on the functionality of the hybrid system.
As results show limited arbitrage value of battery energy storage in wind dominated systems,
other coupling configurations should be investigated. This could potentially include solar-wind
power plants owing to the correlation effect and complementary nature of these sources. Adding
batteries can also increase profitability, as solar provides favourable conditions for batteries,
which further form price profiles beneficial for wind producers. Other configurations can involve
the coupling of wind and hydrogen storage, owing to the typical wind patterns of low or high
availability for longer consecutive hours. Researchers should investigate the competitiveness of
different coupling configurations in terms of cost reductions and improved eﬀiciencies.
Lastly, a more in-depth analysis on the operational benefits and cost of hybrid systems is needed,
in which both unit commitment and stochastic representation of variable renewable energy
should be captured. The assumption of full foresight result in fully optimised storage operation
planning and sizing, which in reality would never have the same accuracy. Solving the problems
related to inconsistency between aggregated and hourly optimisation runs is also imperative to
accurately represent the hourly operation of battery storage. Different aggregation schemes could
potentially provide more coherent generation and prices between the Bb2 and Bb3 simulation.
Further research should also be conducted on potential synergy savings related to wind-battery,
wind-solar-battery and wind-hydrogen coupled systems.

APPENDIX A
Appendix
A.1
Balmorel Model Structure
The structure of Balmorel model is described in detail in [63]. This section describes some of
the main dimensions in the model, including input data and overall model configuration.
Time
The time resolution has three entities: Year, Season and Timestep.
One year consist of a
number of seasons, which again consists of a number of timesteps. Season and timestep are
both flexible entities, meaning that they can be changed based on the desired time resolution.
The current default time-aggregation in Balmorel involves 26 weeks segments within a year, with
12 hour representation for each week (investment optimisation) or 52 weeks segments with 168
hours within a week (dispatch optimisation). The most detailed time segment available is the
timestep, representing hourly resolution. Assuming that a year consist of 52 weeks, an hourly
simulation of a year contains values for 8736 hours. This is slightly less than a standard year
(8760 hours), which is caused by the fact that the number of weeks within a year is in reality
not an integer number.
Geography
There exist three different spatial geography layers in Balmorel: Country, Region and Area.
Country level is the highest geography entity, constituting one or more regions which again
consist of a number of areas. Every area has to be connected to a specific region, and every
region belongs to a given country. At country level, national policies and targets are defined. At
regional level, the energy market is cleared and the corresponding electricity balance is defined.
It is therefore also at this level where the transmission lines are connected and any congestions or
bottlenecks between regions are defined. Furthermore, regions are considered electrical ”copper
plates”, meaning there are no flow representation, and thus no power transmission bottlenecks,
within a region. The area level contains the highest degree of detail. For the electricity system,
areas represent a virtual geography with information about generation technologies, investment
options, full load hours and variation profiles for RE resources. For heat systems, areas act in
the same way as regions do for electricity systems, i.e. this is where the heat balance is solved.
The definition of an area can vary widely depending on the scope of the analysis, but represents
e.g. large district heating grids, offshore wind parks or specific land characterised by high wind
speeds.
Case of Germany
The modelling of Germany is different from today’s day-ahead market setup, which only consist
of one price zone. Due to the well known bottlenecks that limits the flow of energy across the
country, Germany has been divided into numerous market zones in order to better represent
the internal congestion in the grid. In reality, congestion are managed by counter-trading or
redispatching by the TSO after the market clearing. To account for this congestion management
in the dispatch model, Germany is considered to have four different price zones: NW (Northwest),
NE (Northeast), CS (Central South) and ME (Mideast). This division is especially important
when considering the market value of wind, as the four regions have rather different system
characteristics, with high wind resources in Northern Germany. Consequently, the regions will
also have different prices, especially in hours of high wind generation. Noteworthy, the resulting
market value of wind and storage in the four regions should not be interpreted from an investor
point-of-view, but rather from a socio-economic perspective.

Input data
Balmorel requires a set of quantities to be defined exogenously. Firstly, the existing system is
defined with respective generation fleet and transmission capacity, as well as committed capac-
ity that is planned with high certainty. Other input data include demand projections, fuel and
emission costs, policy targets and renewable production profiles by area. The method used for
calculating the availability of wind resources in a given hour is described in detail in Appendix
A.8. Finally, technology data for each of the units are gathered from the technology catalogue,
provided by The Danish Energy Agency and Energinet. This includes information on e.g. fi-
nancial costs (CAPEX and OPEX), asset lifetime and eﬀiciencies, of which the model bases
future investment on. Any operational constraints for the generation and storage units are also
provided as exogenous input to the model.
The generation units that the model can choose between depend both on technology specification
as well as year it is accessible. For example, for future onshore wind installations, three different
technology design are available in the default model, respectively low, medium and high wind-
speed turbines. For each technology design, six different configurations exist based on the year
it will be invested in. Thus, different turbine technologies are available in e.g. 2020 and 2030,
due to expected technology and cost development.
Overall model configuration
The objective function of the base model in Balmorel minimises total system costs, including
investment costs in new generation, transmission and storage capacity, electricity and heat gen-
eration costs, transmission costs, operation and maintenance costs, fuel and emission taxes,
generation taxes and subsidies, changes in consumers’ utility relative to electricity and heat con-
sumption, as well as infeasibility penalties. Main constraints include energy balance equations,
ensuring that supply and demand for heat and electricity is equal in all timesteps and for all re-
gions and areas; generation capacity constraints for all units; storage restrictions, including both
dynamic equations, as well as upper limits for loading and energy capacity; fuel consumption
and transmission capacity restrictions; and VRE resource restrictions, limiting the generation
from VRE to their production profiles. Other structural constraints that relate more to the
diverse system restrictions are maximum investment and fuel capacities as well as policy targets
such as emission limits, share of renewable generation and cap on fossil fuel consumption. A
graphical overview of the most import input data, along with the main structure of the model is
given in Figure A.1. The flow chart represents the investment optimisation, where the objective
function and constraints are looped over the simulated years. As such, all capacities invested in
2020 are included as existing capacities in the 2025 run, and so on.

A.2 Modelling of energy storage in Balmorel

A.2
Modelling of energy storage in Balmorel
As mentioned in Section 3.1.3, several measures have been investigated in order to compensate
for the limitations that time aggregation poses on the model, in particular on the modelling
of energy storage. One of these measures include the introduction of a new parameter in the
storage volume equation, referred to as cyclesin. The parameter, representing the number of
cycles per season, is used to provide storage units with additional artificial volume capacity. This
is accomplished by dividing the loading and unloading of the volume equation by the cyclesin
parameter, allowing for additional charging cycles within the same time segment. Equation A.1
defines a simplified version of the new storage volume constraint.
V olumet+1 = V olumet + Length(Timestep) · (Loading−Unloading/νunload)/Cyclesin (A.1)
The higher cyclesin values, the more charging cycles is possible within the same time segment.
With cyclesin set to e.g. 8, the battery can achieve 8 cycles within a timesegment compared
to the one cycle achieved with cyclesin equal to 1. Accordingly, the energy storage capacity
of the battery can be 8 times smaller and still deliver the same generation amount within the
same timesegment. This makes investment in batteries much more favourable as one can more
effectively utilise the energy capacity of the battery.
The fundamental problem is illustrated in Figure A.2, with price duration curves for Italy, Spain
and the UK and a cyclesin value of 8. Due to the more favourable conditions of battery storage,
the total amount of batteries that the model choose to invest in is considerable. This results in
Bb2 price duration curves with relatively shallow slopes. In contrast, the duration curves of the
Bb3 simulation is much more volatile, with more hours of both higher and lower prices. The
inconsistency in prices is a result of poor representation of hourly operation from wind, solar
and storage technologies. This can lead to hours of insuﬀicient capacity in the Bb3 simulation.
These deficits are often covered by backup capacity, which yields a much higher marginal cost.
In addition, the removal of the cyclesin parameter in the Bb3 simulation leads to additional
price differences as the model is not able to utilise the volume capacity to same extent as in the
Bb2 run.

The large deviations in prices between the two simulation runs further lead to a mismatch in

limitations of lower storage volume availability.
The inconsistency in prices is a serious problem with regard to the reliability of results obtained
from the model. The market value of VRE technologies are highly affected by the prices in the
system, and the differences between the models can therefore cause results to be misrepresenting.
The cyclesin parameter has therefore been set back to 1, such that the available volume capacity
of the batteries within a given timesegment is the same for the Bb2 and Bb3 run. As mentioned
in Section 3.1.3, the battery cost has instead been reduced by a factor of 1/4 to make investment
more favourable in the Bb2.

A.4 Competing means of flexibility

A.4
Competing means of flexibility
With the energy system evolving towards a future VRES-dominant one, the need for flexibility
to ensure balance between demand and supply becomes increasingly important. Energy storage
is taking on an increasing role as flexibility provider, while also offering several other benefits to
the system. However, the growing request for system flexibility can also be offered through other
measures, such as strengthening inter-regional transmission connections or increasing demand
side response. These are all competing measures in terms of capacity investments, and there
deployment will therefore impact the need for battery storage in the system. The most important
sources of flexibility included in the Balmorel model are:
• General flexibility from end-users
Demand side flexibility can be offered in terms of flexible electricity consumption from end
users, such as residential, commercial and industrial sectors. To account for this, a certain
percentage of the average classical demand is allowed to be increased or decreased by the
means of a virtual storage.
• Industrial heating
Electrification of the industry heat sector, by means of heat pumps and electric boilers, is
also seen as a promising solution for increasing flexibility. By replacing other boilers in
periods of high VRE supply and corresponding low electricity prices, electricity demand
increases, leading to higher prices and hence VRE market value.
• Smart charging of electric vehicles
The expanding fleet of electric vehicles, following the progressive electrification of the
transport sector, does not only increase the electricity consumption in the long term, but
it also provides an additional source of flexibility. In the model, the EV batteries act as
a virtual storage for which consumption from the grid can either increase, by charging
outside natural patterns, or decrease, by refraining from charging. For each country, the
amount of charge, discharge and volume capacity is limited by a fraction of the total EV
capacity.
• Production of Power-to-X
A certain amount of electricity is consumed in order to produce and cover the demand of
Power-to-X (P2X). The model does however provide the option of shifting production to
hours of low electricity prices, by the means of hydrogen storage. This provides another
source of flexibility to the system, which increases as the demand for P2X expands. The
P2X electricity consumption can also be redistributed between countries, which provides
additional flexibility.

European system.
As can be observed, the classic demand from the household and service
sector is expected to remain constant, while electricity demand from Electric Vehicles (EVs)
and Power-to-X (P2X) is expected to increase. From 2030 onwards, a rather large amount of
electricity used for P2X production is assumed in the model, reaching over 2,000 TWh in 2050.
This significant increase will further provide additional flexibility to the energy system.

A.8
Modelling wind power production
In this section, the methodology used for modelling wind power production in the Balmorel
model is described.
Area specific data input
In order to accommodate for varying wind characteristics and resource potential, different wind
areas are defined within each Balmorel region. Each area is represented by a wind speed time
series existing of 8736 values, corresponding to a Balmorel year. The wind data is extracted
at a certain height, expressed in m. The last parameter used to describe the wind resource is
the shear factor, representing the wind shear profile at the specific height. The parameter is
dimensionless and depends on the atmospheric stability and terrain type of the site.
Wind technology specific input
In order to assess how the optimal design of wind turbines change with hybridisation, different
wind technologies have been implemented in the various system configurations. Each technology
is described by a power curve and a hub height. The model can choose to invest in a number of
future technologies, for which each region has a predefined list of which technologies the model
is allowed to invest in. For example, only high wind speed turbines can be installed in Denmark,
while new investments in Germany solely include low wind speed turbines. Data inputs for
future technologies are based on projections given by Energistyrelsen’s technology catalogue.
As the implementation of wind power is defined on an area basis, an aggregated power curve is
used to represent the entire production within the area. In reality, a large number of turbine
models would be installed within a region, having different production levels at the same wind
speeds.
The aggregated power curve used to define the resource potential for a specific area is defined by
a logistic function, as given in Equation A.12. This function essentially translates wind speeds
into wind power production. Each technology defined is represented by its own aggregated power
curve.
P =
1
1 + e−g·Kw·(u−M−ϵ)
(A.12)
– u: wind speed at specific height
– g: maximum slope of logistic curve
– M: wind speed at maximum growth
– Kw: smoothening parameter accounting for wind distribution within area
– ϵ: wind speed offset parameter to represent real output compared to theoretical power
curve from manufacturers.
