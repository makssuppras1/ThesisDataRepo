Abstract
The study focused on the wake characteristics of Hywind Scotland’s 6 MW floating wind tur-
bine.This analysis used data from the upwind lidar,with inflow conditions parameterized in
terms of the mean horizontal wind vector,shear,and veer,with the effect of pitch and roll an-
gles in account.Similarly,wake analysis was performed using the downwind scanning lidar’s
PPI and RHI scan data.Wake characterization was performed for two turbines to compare
and analyze variations in wake behavior under each inflow cases.This study compared four
inflow cases,resulting in dramatically different wake behaviors.The analysis revealed that in-
flow conditions are the primary factors in varying wake behaviours,with variations in vertical
wind shears making a significant difference in wake recoveries,as well as the cross wind compo-
nent.Turbulence intensity plays a role in wake recovery,but it is also affected by other inflow
parameters.One of the cases was analyzed using WRF-LES,where the necessary inflow recon-
struction model was applied to extract the inflow parameters and identify which simulation
hour of the WRF-LES closely replicate the observed inflow condition.This particular hour was
used for wake analysis and compared the wake behaviors of both the observed and the sim-
ulated,where the wake analysis of both showed that the WRF-LES could able to predict the
observed flow at far wake with some discrepancies.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Amidst the Earth’s struggle with climate change,present-day patterns showcase an amazing
revolution shaping the energy sector throughout the globe.Countries all over the world are
recognizing the urgent need to reduce carbon emissions and adopt cleaner and sustainable
alternative fuels,hence,driving fossil fuels,which have been the primary source of energy for
many years worldwide,to the edge of a steep decline.This change in perception is underpinned
by a deepening understanding of the deadly danger brought about by global warming whereby
each year sets higher records for the average temperature of our planet while climate-related
adversities become harsher.
Wind power has become a beacon of hope in this time of environmental crises since it is
an alternate source of energy that can significantly help in dealing with global warming.The
reason why there are many wind turbines throughout the world today is attributed to latest
technological development in addition to its wide spread application arising from reduced costs
of production.
From huge onshore farms meant to generate electricity by using air currents moving over land
areas usually flat at low altitudes above sea level or sometimes hilly regions where they are
erected in lines parallel one another,extending hundreds meters long each way depending on
space availability;to ambitious offshore installations—sometimes even larger — set up along
coastlines or far out into oceans,often requiring substantial investment but capable of producing
vast amounts of power more consistently than their land based counterparts due to stronger
offshore winds which blow more continuously at higher speeds throughout year as compared
with those over land areas—the past few years have seen an unprecedented boom within the
industry thus sealing its position as a key player towards achieving low carbon emissions.
In addition,the vast majority of European countries,along with several key industries,signed a
European wind charter in 2023,agreeing to promote Europe’s wind sector and accelerating wind
deployment in the EU,both onshore and offshore.So far,cumulative offshore capacity totals 16.3
GW,and new offshore installations must climb to around 12 GW per year on average to meet
European demand,which is ten times more than the 1.2 GW installed in 2022 [9].
According to Eurostat,wind energy contributed for one-third (37.5%) of all electricity generated
from renewable energy resources in the EU in 2022.However,this is insuﬀicient to meet Europe’s
energy needs in 2030.As a result,the EU and the wind sector have committed to increasing both
onshore and offshore projects in the coming years,with the number of jobs expected to reach
936,000 by 2030 [9].
These figures demonstrate the critical role that wind energy plays in the energy transition,and
in order to continue improving the situation,we must concentrate on the development of new
technologies pertaining to the wind industry.This might simplify and improve the utilization
of wind power.But the progress lies in developing technologies that enhance the eﬀiciency of
wind power plants and improve the understanding of the physical phenomenon to refine the
accuracy of the models used in the design and execution of wind projects .
As a result,eﬀicient wind farm planning is critical since it provides significant economic ben-
efits to developers and investors.However,this diﬀicult endeavor presents various technological
hurdles that may lower the project’s profitability over its expected lifetime.And this paves the
way for precisely planning and optimizing the wind farm in terms of technical feasibility and

economic viability,as well as maintaining competitiveness in wind projects.
Wake losses on the other hand are the main factor to reduce the amount of energy the wind
farm produces.This is because when the wind flow past the turbine it creates slower moving

measurements can provide valuable data for numerical modelling and improve the accuracy of
the wake simulation tool [20].
On the other hand,the design of floating offshore wind farms,which are intended to capture

accurately model the wake behavior downstream of the turbines.So,in order to fully comprehend
the intricate relationship between the floating offshore wind turbines and their surrounding
environment,advanced measurement and monitoring techniques become essential.

2.1
ABL
This dynamic environment is a part of the ABL where the atmosphere that directly inter-
acts with the Earths surface.The ABL is the lowest part of our atmosphere characterized by
turbulence when it interacts with the Earth’s surface.This boundary layer can stretch from a
few hundred to several kilometers,depending on the surface characteristics.For offshore wind
farm,understanding this ABL is necessary as this influences everything from resource assess-
ment to turbine performance and maintenance.
Particularly,in marine environment the ABL is bit complex as the oceans high thermal inertia
that is its ability to store and release heat slowly makes the marine ABL less sensitive to daily
temperature changes when compared to the land.During the day,the marine ABL remains
shallower having weak turbulence but during the nights it has less stability.This behaviour
impacts the wind patterns and turbulence which are the key essential factors for the offshore
wind farm [19].

Wave boundary layers are the lowest part of the marine ABL which is in direct contact with the
ocean surface.The ocean waves modulate the wind flow through form drag,dynamic forcing and
energy dissipation.And the depth of this boundary layer can extend from 1 to 3 meters.Also,this
layer is responsible for the deviation from the logarithmic wind profile in the surface layer.
Surface layer extends from the top of the wave boundary layer to 10% of the total ABL depth.In
this layer the turbulence flux and momentum are constant with the height.Also,the wind speed
profile is quite logarithmic and influenced by the surface roughness and thermal conditions[19].
Mixed layer extends from top of the surface layer to the temperature inversion (zi).This layer
has large turbulent eddies which dominate the transport of the momentum and other quantities
where their scale is determined by the ABL depth.
Convective ABL which is observed during the day time,the solar heating of the ocean surface
causes the warm air to rise leading to the development of thermal convection.Due to this a
significant vertical mixing occurs throughout the marine boundary layer.The potential tem-
perature θ(z) increases with the height indicating that the warm air near the surface rises
and mixes with cooler air.This mixing is done through the action of buoyant forces where this
warmer air rises and the cooler air sinks and due to this,it results in a well mixed layer where
the temperature and other atmospheric properties remains almost constant.This is the reason
to get the fairly constant θ(z) upto certain height.After that,the convective process weakens
leading to the temperature inversion where the potential temperature increases with height
indicating a stable layer and suppressing the vertical movement of the air particle.
Stable ABL,which is observed during the night time or generally over cooler ocean surfaces has
minimal vertical mixing as it lacks solar heating.As a result,θ(z) increases with the height indi-
cating a stratified atmosphere where cooler air which is denser lies below the warmer air.This
intact suppresses the vertical movement leading to less turbulence but more layered wind pro-
file.Moreover,from Figure 2 stable ABL could be seen where the boundary layer is much shal-
lower when compared to the CBL.In this case the boundary layer height could be below 200
meters and thus the wind turbine rotor layer can span across the multiple layers of wind speeds
and directions,resulting in complex wind shears and veers.This change can pose challenges in
the wind turbine operation where this increased fluctuation in wind speed may potentially in-
crease the loads on wind turbine components.Additionally,the diurnal and seasonal variations
play a significant role in alteration between the CBL and SBL.

2.2
Wakes
Figure 3: Wind speed vertical profile in wake of the wind turbine [16]
The incoming flow characteristics of this Atmospheric Boundary Layer (ABL) are directly
impacted by the presence of the earth’s surface,including surface frictions and heat transfers
over the course of a day.According to Newton’s third law,these activities are balanced out by
reactions on wind flow,which alter the wake region’s properties (i.e., the volume downstream
of the turbine) [8].
A wind turbine’s wake is typically separated into two regions:the near wake and the far wake
[13].This wake is determined by the ABL and it depends on the direction and speed of the
incoming wind as well as the point at which the undisturbed wind approaches the wind turbine.
The flow expands when the wind strikes the turbine,taking on shape from the rotor’s geometry.A
short distance downstream,turbulent diffusion propels the flow.In addition to affecting the
wake,the rotor also affects the incoming wind flow in the so-called ”induction zone”,which
is the area upstream where the wind expands and experiences an increase in pressure.This
extends upstream of the turbine by two rotor diameters[10].Thus,the rotor blade tip and root
vortices,as well as the vortices shed from the tower and nacelle,dominate the flow in the near
wake [5].
The decrease in kinetic energy that precedes the near wake lowers the wind speed.The velocity
gradient from the wakes edges causes an annular shear layer to emerge in the wake zone’s near
wake,as can also be observed in the Figure 3 .
The wake has the lowest centerline speed in this area up to two rotor diameters[10].After a
few downwind distance, the helical tip vortices shed from the blade tip,finally breaking down
and creates turbulent mixing of the flow,where momentum takes center stage.As a result,the
shear layer surrounding the wake slowly grows due to turbulent mixing,and the vortex structure
decays.

==PAGE:19==
The wake expands out more laterally and vertically as a result of turbulent mixing with the
surrounding environment at the transition zone,which is where the flow from the near to far wake
occurs.Therefore,as the vortices break down because of their instabilities,the wake’s turbulence
strength rises,which in turn encourages the wake’s effective mixing with the undisturbed airflow
outside of the wake’s boundary layer.At this point,the slower-moving wind inside the wake
exchanges momentum with the faster-moving wind outside the wake layer.Additionally,as the
wake moves farther away,its shape broadens and its edges become more diffusive,and its initially
highly skewed velocity profile in the near wake becomes more uniform and symmetric in the
transition zone.
The far wake zone,where the recovery of the velocity deficit occurs, begins once they meet four to
six times the rotor diameter[5].It resembles a gaussian profile and the turbulence there appears
to be more isotropic.A large range of wind turbine wake models are taken into consideration in
this far wake[13].Furthermore, since the far wake wind will be striking the rear row turbines,it is
even more crucial to understand the wind characteristics at this point.Numerous assumptions,
including self-similarities and statistical unviersalities,can be made in this area [5].
The wakes mentioned above are thought to follow a straight path and are presumed to be
stationary.However,in the actual world,wakes are dynamic and require consideration of the di-
rection of the wind,which causes the wake to meander.When the wind direction fluctuates,there
is an increase in the misalignment between the turbine and the incoming wind, causing the wake
to meander and only partially cover the rear row turbine.In a wind farm,this may be referred
to as many wakes where wake-wake and wake-turbine interact [16].

WRF-LES
WRF is the widely used numerical weather prediction model,designed for atmospheric research
and operational forecasting with capabilities from the microscale to the mesoscale.LES is a
computational fluid dynamic technique applied to the simulation of turbulence by resolving the
large eddies,having a significant influence on the transport and mixing processes,while smaller
scales are modeled using subgrid scale model(SGS).WRF-LES would make this a comprehen-
sive atmospheric dynamics model that closes the gap between microscale turbulence and the
mesoscale weather system.A microscale weather system is an atmospheric motion,in the form
of eddies or vortices,occurring over a short distance and timescale,while mesoscale weather sys-
tems are large-scale phenomena that stretch from tens to hundreds of kilometers in space and

last from hours to days [12].
However,these results from simulation must be validated against field measurements to make
sure that the physics used in the simulation are correct.Large discrepancies might appear on
account of the domain extent, reconstruction of turbulent inflow conditions,and aligned turbine
configuration [20].Grid resolution is also important because it represents the level of detail
used to depict atmospheric processes [17].Fine grid resolutions are needed to capture wake

Integration of Lidar & WRF-LES
Previous research conducted on floating wind turbines in Hywind Scotland utilized two nacelle-
mounted wind lidars to examine the characteristics of upwind and downwind flow fields associ-
ated with a 6 MW turbine installed on a ballast platform,and wake characterization was detailed
in [3].Additionally,for various inflow scenarios, the models derived from wind lidar readings were
scrutinized using engineering wake models[3].In a study carried out by[20],greater discrepancies
in wake recovery were identified between LES and lidar in the far wake zone.An investigation
into the observed disparities underscores the necessity for improved modeling of meso-scale
effects, possibly requiring a finer simulation grid to capture the smaller scales of wake turbu-
lence.Now,LES emerges as a crucial tool due to its ability to resolve in high resolution and
depict detailed representations of turbulent structures,while also facilitating parameter explo-
ration that impacts wakes.Large eddy simulations (LES) and other numerical simulation tools
have seen widespread application in recent years for simulating and examining turbine-wake
interactions in wind farms[8].The diurnal variation of wind speed and direction was effectively
reproduced in the LES model,demonstrating the simulation’s accuracy,with the propagation of
the wake being notably well-replicated,showcasing its capability.Nevertheless,comparing full-
scale wind turbine wake observations to model predictions poses challenges.The diﬀiculties in
comparing full-scale measurements and model predictions of wind turbine wakes stem from
issues related to accurately establishing free stream flow characteristics,where precision in en-
suring equivalent time averaging in models and measurements is crucial.These challenges often
arise from two major factors:horizontal inhomogeneity and non-stationarity of the atmospheric
flow[23].Recently,wind turbine wakes have been investigated using either Plan Position Indica-
tor (PPI) or Range Height Indicator (RHI) scans conducted by nacelle-based lidars aligned with
the rotor[6].Additionally,evaluation methods such as a direct comparison of line-of-sight (LOS)
velocities with projected LES velocities are being studied for a more comprehensive assessment
of atmospheric and wake turbulence[21].Therefore,in this current study,the projected velocities
obtained through WRF-LES must be validated against solid measurement data observations
(lidar) to enable validation and error analysis and to ensure the reliability and accuracy of such
numerical solutions.
The field data for this wake investigation has already been collected, with Wind Iris measuring
inflow wind and Gallion lidar measuring downstream wind [3].This information will be used for
further analysis.

Methodology
Upwind radial speed Model [3]
The upwind radial speed model is intended to parameterize inflow wind conditions using the
following assumptions and approaches,all of which are based on [3].
Assumptions:

Conclusion
The comparisons between these cases highlight the importance of inflow conditions in deter-
mining wake performance.Positive vertical shear (du/dz) promotes recovery by drawing greater
momentum air into the wake,as observed in RHI scans,reducing the velocity deficit downstream,
whereas negative vertical shear aids slower recovery.Both positive and negative crosswind ver-
tical shear (dv/dz) improve lateral mixing,with higher negative shear producing better tur-
bulence and wake dissipation, as seen.The higher the turbulence intensity,the faster the wake
recovers,although moderate turbulence intensity with more negative dv/dz and positive du/dz
recovered the wake much faster.Though both turbines have the same inflow conditions,they be-
have differently,with HS2 appearing to vary significantly with the inflow conditions,which could
be attributed to the difference in wind direction changes along the downwind distance.Even
small differences in wind direction can lead to large differences in the wake behavior.
The significance of the upwind radial model was highlighted by analyzing case 1 using the WRF-
LES.This model contains certain assumptions that made it diﬀicult to fit the LES-extracted
radial velocities because the top beams fluctuated with the values.As a result,the constant shear
assumption did not fit those beams well,but it did fit the bottom beams—which were aligned
to the hub height,which was necessary to measure the free stream conditions.In addition,even
though the hub height had been set according to the 6 MW turbine,the wake behaved differently
in the simulation since it used the prefixed DTU 10 MW turbine,which has different specs.With
some inconsistencies,the simulation predicted the real data from X/D=5 to 9.

Further study
So far,the wake characterization for both turbines has been performed and analyzed for below
rated wind speed.The same could be done for the above-rated wind speed when the blades begin
to pitch to reduce thrust on the turbine,and so analyzing their wake behavior could provide
some insight.Currently,the inflow lidar was only deployed on one turbine,so the assumption of
homogeneous flow was made.Multiple inflow lidars can be deployed to study the effect of local
atmospheric conditions on each turbine,allowing for the observation and analysis of inflow vari-
ations seperately.Furthermore,this thesis analysis focuses on single turbine wakes i.e)when the
incoming wind is wake free,but this could be extended to include multiple turbine wake stud-
ies(wind farm).This significantly improves our understanding of wake-wake and wake-turbine
interactions.These could still be assessed using the WRF-LES, giving us the opportunity to
fine-tune the underlying physics used in the simulation to closely match the observed data.
