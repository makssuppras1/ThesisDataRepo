Abstract 
This thesis explores the integration of Lean Management, the Socio-Technical Model (STM), 
and the Humanware approach in driving digital transformation within restaurant kitchen 
environments. Focusing on the case of Remi, a kitchen operations app designed to coordinate 
tasks, improve workflow, and support sustainability and the study investigates how digital 
tools can enhance both operational efficiency and team dynamics in a fast-paced hospitality 
context. 
Through a single-case qualitative study conducted at a Copenhagen-based restaurant, the 
research draws on semi-structured interviews and STM-aligned observations to assess how 
technology reshapes human interactions, workflows, internal culture, and sustainability 
practices. The findings highlight both the enablers and friction points of digital adoption, 
including role clarity, interaction burden, cultural alignment, and external operational 
pressures. 
By applying the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework, the thesis also evaluates the broader 
impact of Remi on people, planet, and profit dimensions. The results suggest that when Lean 
principles and Humanware design are embedded into digital tools, they can significantly 
support decision-making, reduce inefficiencies, and foster shared accountability—even in 
decentralized and traditionally analog kitchen settings. 
The study contributes to existing literature by adapting the STM from healthcare to hospitality 
and demonstrating how Humanware-aligned tools like Remi can act as catalysts for 
organizational learning and sustainable transformation in the restaurant industry.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
The restaurant industry faces growing pressure to improve operational efficiency, sustainability 
performance, and team coordination—while remaining highly reliant on manual labor and 
traditional workflows. With tight profit margins and rising consumer expectations, the sector is 
slowly embracing digital transformation. However, the path to effective digital adoption 
remains challenging. 
Technological tools such as digital Point-of-Sale systems, inventory platforms, and AI-based 
analytics have the potential to enhance resource allocation, reduce food waste, and streamline 
operations. Yet, implementing these solutions often requires overcoming workforce resistance, 
training barriers, and organizational fragmentation. Many restaurants, particularly small and 
independent ones, lack the digital infrastructure and standardization found in larger chains, 
making widespread technological adoption difficult. 
The complexity of managing front-of-house, kitchen operations, and customer service adds 
further barriers. Importantly, the most significant challenges—and opportunities—for digital 
transformation are concentrated in the kitchen. This environment, the epicenter of culinary 
operations, is traditionally resistant to change but critically in need of solutions that support 
real-time coordination, efficient communication, and sustainability. 
Successfully integrating technology into kitchens while maintaining the human touch is 
essential. Tailoring solutions to meet the cultural and workflow-specific needs of kitchen teams 
is vital for improving business performance and workforce well-being. 
 
1.1 Problem Statement 
The restaurant industry faces growing operational, environmental, and social pressures. 
Margins are tight, staff turnover is high, and manual coordination often leads to inefficiencies. 
While digital tools have transformed other industries, restaurants—especially independent 
ones—are often slow to adopt them due to limited resources, rigid hierarchies, and the fast-
paced nature of kitchen environments. 
The few existing approaches to digitalization and sustainability often overlook the high-
pressure and unpredictable environment of restaurant kitchens. Workflows are nonlinear, 
communication is often verbal and fragmented, and cultural resistance to change is deeply 
rooted. Existing research tends to focus on business or innovation-related themes, with 
relatively few studies addressing the social, psychological, or organizational dynamics within 
kitchens (Yeh & Huan, 2017). Seminal contributions such as Fine’s Kitchens: The Culture of 
Restaurant Work (1996) and recent empirical studies from Denmark (Pathak & Bartaula, 2021) 
highlight the complexity of kitchen workflows, hierarchical structures, and the everyday 
pressures that shape how new routines and tools are (or are not) adopted. 
To better understand how digital tools can support—not disrupt—kitchen environments, this 
thesis draws on Lean Management principles and the Humanware approach, which 
emphasize process efficiency and human-centered adaptability. In parallel, the Socio-Technical 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
Model (STM) provides a structured systems lens to examine how people, technology, and 
organizational factors interact in complex environments. Lastly, the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) 
framework is used to evaluate how digital transformation initiatives affect people, planet, and 
profit—ensuring a holistic view of sustainability outcomes. While Lean and Humanware are 
not socio-technical theories per se, they can be embedded within a socio-technical framework 
(STM) to analyze how human-centered operational tools interact with digital systems in 
complex kitchen environments. 
Despite the relevance of these frameworks, their integration and application in the context of 
restaurant kitchens remains underexplored. This thesis addresses that gap by studying how a 
newly developed digital tool—Remi—can improve communication, coordination, and 
sustainability performance in a real-world kitchen setting. 
 
1.2 Research Gap 
The absence of an adapted socio-technical framework for restaurants limits our understanding 
of how digital tools, human factors, and sustainability considerations interact in kitchen 
operations. Most studies on Lean Management focus on manufacturing contexts and do not 
account for the behavioral and cultural complexity of service environments. 
Furthermore, while the concept of Humanware—emphasizing the integration of human and 
technological systems—offers promise, its application in kitchens remains conceptually and 
empirically underdeveloped. There is a clear need to understand how Lean and Humanware 
principles can be effectively combined to support digital transformation efforts in professional 
kitchens. 
This study seeks to bridge this gap by developing a scalable framework that aligns digital tools, 
operational goals, and workforce adaptability using a Socio-Technical perspective. 
 
1.3 Research Objectives and Question 
The overarching objective of this thesis is to explore how the integration of Lean Management 
Principles and Humanware into a Socio-Technical framework can improve communication, 
coordination, and sustainability in restaurant kitchen operations. 
Specifically, the study aims to: 
1. Investigate operational inefficiencies in restaurant kitchens, focusing on 
communication gaps, task coordination challenges, and unsustainable practices 
such as food waste and inefficient resource use. 
2. Examine how the practical application of Lean principles and Humanware 
elements, supported by digital tools, influences coordination, decision-making, and 
sustainability outcomes in kitchen operations. 
3. Develop an integrated model combining socio-technical thinking, Lean principles, 
and Humanware logic to align operational performance, workforce well-being, 
and sustainability in professional kitchens.

Chapter 2: Literature Review 
This chapter reviews the academic literature on the digital transformation of restaurant kitchens. 
It examines how restaurants function as complex sociotechnical systems, integrating 
operational processes with human and technological elements. Key theoretical approaches, 
including the Sociotechnical Model (STM), Lean Management, and Humanware, are presented 
to support the analysis of digital tool adoption, coordination practices, and sustainability in 
kitchen environments. 
2.1 Digital Transformation in Restaurant Kitchens 
2.1.1 What is a Restaurant? 
A restaurant is far more than a place where food is prepared and served. It is a complex, living 
system designed to deliver a coordinated gastronomic experience, while also functioning as an 
economic and cultural institution. At its heart, a restaurant operates as a system of systems, 
where multiple interconnected subsystems—from procurement to service—collaborate to 
create not only meals but memorable moments for diners (Adrià, 2019). 
These subsystems include procurement, preparation, cooking, plating, service, communication, 
and post-service feedback. Each restaurant’s system adapts to its unique gastronomic offering, 
defined by its resources—products, techniques, and tools—and supported by key elements such 
as management, finance, and customer experience (Adrià, 2019). 
At the center of this operation is the kitchen, conceptualized not just as a physical space but as 
a production unit, akin to an industrial manufacturing environment. It transforms raw 
ingredients through structured processes, human expertise, and coordinated roles into culinary 
outputs Basque Culinary Center. (2019). This transformation depends on the interplay of 
people, materials, tools, and time—all requiring synchronization under pressure. 
One of the most enduring organizational models in professional kitchens is the brigade system, 
originally developed by Auguste Escoffier. It remains a foundation for modern kitchen 
hierarchy and workflow, structuring tasks around specialized roles (e.g., saucier, garde-manger, 
pâtissier), with clearly defined leadership lines from the head chef to line cooks Basque 
Culinary Center. (2019). While this structure enables clarity and speed during peak service, it 
can also present resistance to change and rigidity—particularly when introducing digital tools 
that may disrupt established routines and communication flows. 
Modern restaurants also depend heavily on mise en place, the philosophy and practice of having 
“everything in its place.” This pre-service organizational logic not only supports efficiency and 
consistency but also reflects a mental model shared across kitchen teams Basque Culinary 
Center. (2019). Digital systems that align with or enhance mise en place (e.g., real-time prep 
tracking or visual task lists) are more likely to be accepted and successfully adopted. 
Importantly, restaurants must be understood as adaptive socio-technical systems. Each 
element—from inventory tracking to dish execution—requires coordination between human 
skills, tools, and environmental constraints. As technological innovations such as AI-driven 
inventory, real-time task assignment, or robotic support tools enter the kitchen, the balance 
between efficiency and human creativity becomes increasingly vital. Tools must not simply 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
“fit” the kitchen’s workflow—they must support its culture, pace, and tactic knowledge 
systems. 
Therefore, understanding the restaurant not just as a service venue but as a dynamic production 
and coordination system is foundational for analyzing the potential and limits of digital 
transformation in this domain. 
2.1.2 The State of Digital Transformation in the Restaurant Industry 
Digital transformation, defined as the integration of digital technologies into all areas of 
business operations, is reshaping industries worldwide. In the restaurant sector, this 
transformation is driven by increasing demands for operational efficiency, sustainability, and 
enhanced customer experiences (Alt, 2021).  
However, the pace and extent of adoption vary widely across the industry. The restaurant 
industry is not a uniform sector. It comprises a wide variety of operational formats—ranging 
from fast-food chains and dark kitchens to fine dining restaurants and food trucks—each with 
differing capacities, needs, and paces for technology adoption.  
While some technologies such as CRM systems or online reservation tools are widely used 
across formats, others—like robotic chefs or AI-powered inventory systems—may be more 
feasible in modular, scalable environments such as quick service or ghost kitchens. This study 
focuses on a casual dining restaurant with a traditional kitchen setup, where human 
collaboration and real-time coordination remain central. As such, it explores how digital 
transformation can be adapted to a dynamic, human-centered kitchen environment rather than 
applied uniformly across the sector.  
By focusing on back-of-house dynamics in a medium-sized, European casual dining context, 
this study contributes to closing a critical gap in digital transformation literature, which often 
prioritizes front-of-house innovation or chain-level automation strategies. 
Large restaurant chains have leveraged technology to standardize processes, adopt modular 
automation, and implement AI-driven tools for inventory and demand forecasting (Deloitte, 
2025; Tech Landscape Report, 2024). In contrast, small and independent restaurants often face 
significant barriers, including limited financial resources, lack of digital infrastructure, and 
skepticism regarding the return on investment (Alt, 2021; Deloitte, 2025).  
To develop a holistic understanding of technological integration in the restaurant industry, it is 
essential to review key industry reports and empirical studies that illustrate how human-
computer interactions are shaping operational practices and driving business transformation. 
However, their integration into high-pressure, fast-paced environments like kitchens presents 
both opportunities and challenges. 
2.1.3 Key Technologies and Trends in Kitchen Digitalization 
The use of Information and Communication Technologies (ICTs) in hospitality has evolved 
from basic operational tools, such as Point-of-Sale (POS) systems, to more strategic systems 
that include real-time analytics, customer relationship management, and workforce 
optimization (Law et al., 2020; Xiang, 2018). Yet, many restaurants still operate with 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
fragmented systems, lacking interoperability between front-of-house and back-of-house 
operations, which limits the overall effectiveness of digital tools (Alt, 2021).  
Recent developments highlight that robotic chefs are assisting with food preparation to enhance 
precision and speed, while AI-driven order processing optimizes queue management and 
reduces wait times. According to the National Restaurant Association Tech Landscape Report 
2024, restaurant operators are rapidly adopting smart kitchen technologies, including AI-
powered inventory systems to reduce food waste, predictive analytics for demand forecasting, 
and cloud-based coordination platforms that integrate front- and back-of-house workflows. 
Building on these trends, recent industry reports emphasize that data analytics and digital tools 
are becoming integral to restauarant operations. Deloitte (2025) reveals that independent 
restaurants are now leveraging AI-based scheduling and IoT-driven inventory control to tackle 
operational inefficiencies and rising costs. Similarly, predictive analytics, real-time data 
monitoring, and stock tracking allow kitchens to streamline supply management and reduce 
spoilage (Deloitte, 2021). 
Moreover, in larger restaurant chains, analytics tools are increasingly used to optimize resource 
allocation and identify process bottlenecks, enabling managers to implement continuous 
improvements in workflow efficiency (Ozturk et al., 2023). Empirical studies, such as Banker 
et al. (1991), underscore how early digital tools like POS systems enhanced kitchen operations 
by supporting real-time, data-driven decision-making. While the broader impact of advanced 
analytics in small restaurant kitchens remains underexplored, these findings highlight the 
foundational role of integrated systems in supporting operational improvements—particularly 
when aligned with organizational workflows and complexity. 
Together, these technological developments reflect an emerging trend, particularly among 
larger chains, towards more data-informed operations. While often promoted in industry reports 
and trade media, evidence of widespread adoption remains uneven, especially in independent 
restaurants. Nonetheless, this direction reinforces the relevance of a socio-technical perspective, 
where digital solutions are evaluated in terms of their alignment with human decision-making 
and operational complexity in kitchen environments. This convergence supports improved 
coordination, sustainability, and operational resilience, which are key objectives in today’s 
competitive restaurant landscape. 
While these advances showcase the potential of digital tools, much of the industry’s focus 
remains still on front-of-house innovations that directly enhance customer engagement. In 
contrast, the digital transformation of back-of-house (BOH) operations—where the real 
complexity of kitchen environments lies—remains widley underexplored. Despite increasing 
access to data, many restaurants lack a coherent big data strategy, with only a minority 
leveraging analytics to drive efficiency and growth (BCG, 2018). Advanced data utilization has 
been linked to revenue increases of 5–10%, cost reductions of 10–15%, and significant 
EBITDA improvements. However, fragmented IT infrastructures and reliance on multiple 
infromation technology systems limit scalable digital integration. 
Digital transformation in the restaurant industry increasingly targets not only customer 
engagement but also the optimization of back-of-house (BOH) operations, where kitchen 
efficiency, task coordination, and sustainability are critical. Industry fast-food leaders such as 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
Domino’s and McDonalds have invested heavily in BOH technologies—including data-driven 
labor planning, real-time inventory management, and AI-based forecasting—to streamline 
workflows and reduce operational costs (BCG, 2017; BCG, 2018). 
Moreover, the strategic value of data extends deep into the kitchen, where real-time analytics 
enable chefs and kitchen managers to make informed decisions on task prioritization, resource 
allocation, and waste reduction. The use of dynamic data systems, combined with agile testing 
methods, has become essential for continuous improvement in BOH performance (BCG, 2017). 
In the evolving restaurant industry, data is becoming a currency as vital as cash, shaping 
competitive advantage. Restaurants that fail to develop robust data management capabilities 
risk marginalization, while data-literate competitors seize growth and profitability 
opportunities. 
2.1.4 Barriers to Digital Transformation in Kitchens 
Despite the promise of digital tools, several persistent barriers hinder their effective adoption 
in restaurant kitchens: 
• 
Financial Constraints: High initial costs for hardware and software implementation 
deter many small businesses, especially in uncertain economic climates (Deloitte, 
2025). 
• 
Technical and Skills Gaps: Many kitchen staff lack formal IT training, and high turnover 
rates in the industry make continuous upskilling difficult (Alt, 2021). 
• 
Cultural Resistance and Staff Adaptation: Cultural resistance and staff adaptation 
remain significant challenges in many restaurant kitchens, particularly in traditional or 
independent settings. These kitchens often rely on deeply embedded routines and 
informal coordination, leading to hesitation toward tools perceived as disruptive or 
unnecessary (Flink and Beling, 2017). However, in fast casual and fast food 
environments, where processes are more standardized and technology integration has 
already advanced, resistance has been notably lower. Still, across the sector, adaptation 
remains complex, especially given the need to balance speed, precision, and flexibility 
in daily operations. 
While proponents argue that digital transformation enhances efficiency and sustainability, 
critics caution against over-reliance on technology in hospitality, an industry deeply rooted in 
personal interaction and craftsmanship. Some researchers warn that excessive automation may 
erode the "human touch" central to the dining experience (Flink & Beling, 2017). Furthermore, 
the actual return on investment for many technologies remains uncertain, particularly for small 
and independent operators with limited data and scalability (Alt, 2021; Deloitte, 2025). 
2.1.5 Relevance to This Study 
Given these dynamics, this study situates digital transformation within the broader context of 
restaurant operations, focusing particularly on kitchen environments. The integration of 
technology in kitchens must be understood as part of a socio-technical system, where human 
factors, organizational culture, and technological capabilities interact continuously. This 
chapter establishes the foundation for applying the Socio-Technical Model (STM) in analyzing

how digital tools like the Remi app can support improved communication, coordination, and 
sustainability in restaurant kitchens. 
2.2 Socio-Technical Systems Theory (STM): How Technology Adoption Interacts with Human 
and Organizational Factors 
2.2.1 Understanding STM and Its Relevance to Restaurant Kitchens 
The concept of socio-technical systems originates from the work of Trist and Bamforth (1951), 
who first explored the interaction between social elements (people, teams, culture) and technical 
components (tools, processes, technologies) in organizational settings. Their research, initially 
focused on coal mining, revealed that technological changes alone often fail unless they align 
with human and organizational needs. This dual focus on technology and human organization 
became a foundational principle for analyzing complex work environments where neither 
technical efficiency nor social dynamics alone can explain system performance (Bauer & 
Herder, 2009). Over time, this theory has evolved, incorporating broader dimensions such as 
goals, culture, infrastructure, and external environments (Münch et al., 2022), forming a 
complex, interdependent framework for understanding how work systems operate. 
Socio-technical theory has since evolved into a widely accepted framework across various 
industries, especially in contexts where rapid technological change creates new 
interdependencies between tools, tasks, and teams. Li et al. (2020) emphasize that socio-
technical systems are particularly relevant in the digital era, where the integration of advanced 
technologies with human-centered processes defines the success of modern organizations. 
These systems allow for a structured understanding of how digital tools can support, rather than 
disrupt, existing workflows and cultural practices. 
Moreover, STS research has proven highly valuable in environments characterized by high 
complexity and variability, such as manufacturing, healthcare, and increasingly, service 
industries like hospitality. Soliman and Saurin (2017) argue that lean production principles, 
when applied to socio-technical systems, highlight the importance of aligning technological 
interventions with human adaptability to manage variability effectively. Similarly, Li et al. 
(2020) highlight that digital transformations are successful only when social readiness and 
human skills development are addressed alongside technical advancements. 
While socio-technical systems theory provides a foundational, this study specifically adopts the 
Socio-Technical Model (STM) — a practical, structured framework. An early conceptualization 
of such models was presented by Leavitt (1965), who identified four key interacting 
components in any organizational work systems: people, tasks, structure, and technology. These 
dimensions highlight how changes in one area inevitably affect the others, making holistic 
system thinking essential. 
This distinction is critical. Whereas socio-technical systems theory offers broad conceptual 
guidance, STM provides a concrete, multi-dimensional tool that enables systematic analysis of 
how technology, people, and workflows co-evolve in real-time. Its structured nature allows for 
direct application to field research, making it ideal for this study, which aims to explore and

Given the proven relevance of sociotechnical systems theory across various industries, it is 
essential to contextualize the choice of the sociotechnical model (STM) relative to other 
technology acceptance frameworks. While STS theory provides a broad perspective for 
analyzing the interplay of social and technical factors, specific models such as the STM enable 
a more detailed analysis of the impact of these interactions on organizational dynamics. This 
precision is particularly important in environments such as restaurant kitchens, where real-time 
coordination and operational adaptability are of central importance. To further justify the use of 
the STM in this study, it is compared below with two frameworks commonly used in digital 
acceptance research: the Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) framework and the 
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM). 
Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) Framework 
The TOE framework (Tornatzky & Fleischer, 1990) explains technology adoption based on 
three key contexts: technological capability, organizational readiness, and environmental 
pressures. It is particularly useful for evaluating external factors such as market competition 
or regulatory requirements (Oliveira & Martins, 2011). However, TOE does not extensively 
address the internal dynamic adjustments within organizations as they integrate new 
technologies into daily practices. 
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) 
TAM (Davis, 1989) centers on individual users’ perceptions, specifically focusing on the ease 
of use and perceived usefulness of technology. This model is effective for predicting initial 
acceptance, especially in user-centered systems, but it lacks the depth to explain how 
technologies transform workflows, social interactions, and organizational structures over 
time. 
Socio-Technical Model (STM) 
In contrast, STM captures both the adoption and ongoing integration of technology within 
organizations. It focuses on how technology reshapes communication flows, task 
coordination, and system-wide performance. Given the interdependent and dynamic nature of 
restaurant kitchens, STM allows for a more pragmatic and operationally grounded analysis of 
digital transformation, beyond the predictive capabilities of TOE or TAM.

While the STM was originally developed for healthcare environments, it offers strong

environments where technology must be carefully integrated with human behavior and 
organizational processes—challenges that are equally present in professional kitchens. This 
makes STM a relevant and transferable framework for examining digital transformation in 
hospitality settings. 
These insights can be logically extended to restaurant settings for several reasons:

2.3 Lean Management in Hospitality 
2.3.1 Lean Management Principles and Their Potential in Restaurant Operations 
Lean Management, rooted in the Toyota Production System (TPS), is a process-driven 
philosophy focused on the elimination of waste, continuous improvement, and value creation 
for the customer (Krafcik, 1988; Womack & Jones, 1996). Initially developed for 
manufacturing, particularly in the automotive industry, Lean principles have since been adapted 
to various service sectors, including healthcare, logistics, and increasingly, hospitality (Dekier, 
2012). 
At its core, Lean emphasizes: 
• 
Just-in-Time (JIT) production: producing only what is needed, when it is needed. 
• 
Jidoka: building quality into processes by empowering workers to stop production to 
fix problems. 
• 
Kaizen: continuous improvement through incremental changes. 
• 
Elimination of non-value-added activities: identifying and removing process 
inefficiencies. 
2.3.2 Historical Evolution and Broader Application 
The origins of Lean can be traced back to early 20th-century innovations by Henry Ford, but 
were more systematically developed by Sakichi Toyoda, Kiichiro Toyoda, and Taiichi Ohno at 
Toyota. Their work integrated flexibility, fault detection, and pull production methods, which 
significantly improved production efficiency under conditions of low demand and limited 
resources (Dekier, 2012). 
Over time, Lean evolved beyond manufacturing. Womack and Jones (1996) codified Lean 
principles applicable to any process-oriented industry, advocating for Lean as a universal 
management philosophy adaptable to service environments. However, the application of Lean 
in small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), including restaurants, remains limited due to 
perceived complexity and resource constraints (Alkhoraif et al., 2019). 
The distinction between lean and traditional production systems, as first articulated by Krafcik 
(1988), offers valuable insight for understanding restaurant operations as hybrid environments. 
Lean systems, characterized by minimal inventories, cross-functional teamwork, and 
continuous improvement, have been empirically shown to outperform traditional buffered 
systems in quality, productivity, and adaptability. These attributes closely align with the 
demands of modern restaurant kitchens, where real-time coordination, efficiency under 
pressure, and minimal waste are essential for success. Applying Krafcik’s insights, restaurant 
kitchens can be viewed as lean production environments that require dynamic resource 
management and a strong emphasis on team-based problem solving. This further validates the 
relevance of Lean Management principles in enhancing both operational performance and 
customer satisfaction in hospitality contexts. 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
2.3.3 Lean and the Hybrid Nature of Restaurant Operations 
Restaurants are unique hybrid organizations, combining manufacturing-like food production 
with high-contact service delivery (Muller, 2012). Although Lean Management aligns 
conceptually with restaurant operations, its practical implementation across the sector remains 
limited and uneven. Recent research highlights that Lean has seen broader application in hotel 
operations than in restaurants, yet even in hotels, Lean use is underdeveloped and lacking 
methodological consistency (Perdomo-Verdecia et al., 2022). In the restaurant sector, Lean 
adoption is more systematic in fast food and quick-service environments, where high volumes 
and standardized routines support tools like workflow mapping, cycle time analysis, and 
inventory control (Gładysz et al., 2020). In contrast, fine dining and casual restaurants face 
barriers such as operational variability, reliance on skilled labor, and resistance to procedural 
standardization (Perdomo-Verdecia et al., 2022). Nonetheless, Lean-inspired practices like mise 
en place or Kanban-style task signaling are already embedded informally, showing potential for 
structured Lean adaptation. 
Gładysz et al. (2020) provide a useful breakdown of Lean waste typologies tailored to service 
contexts such as HoReCa (Hotels, Restaurants, Catering), highlighting errors in service 
delivery, miscommunication, duplication, and unclear instructions as key waste sources. Their 
study also reinforces the critical role of human resource management, noting that Lean systems 
depend on the motivation and acceptance of employees to be successful. These insights stress 
the need for human-centered Lean implementations, particularly in high-pressure environments 
like kitchens. 
Key Lean concepts already inherent in many kitchen practices include: 
• 
Just-in-Time (JIT): Food is prepared to order, minimizing inventory and spoilage. 
• 
Kanban systems: Guest checks act as order triggers, guiding workflow from prep to 
plating. 
• 
Mise en place: Represents stored labor and work-in-progress inventory, reflecting 
Lean’s focus on minimizing delays during service (Muller, 2012). 
• 
Pull production: Each station or employee is both a customer and supplier, emphasizing 
interdependent flow and responsiveness. 
Despite this natural fit, most restaurants apply these principles informally, without leveraging 
the full strategic potential of Lean methodologies (Muller, 2012). The lack of formalization 
limits opportunities for systematic improvement, cross-functional collaboration, and error 
management. 
2.3.4 Empirical Evidence and Adaptation Challenges 
Empirical studies in Lean consistently demonstrate efficiency gains, cost reductions, and 
quality improvements in sectors adopting these principles (Buer et al., 2021; Alkhoraif et al., 
2019). However, smaller operations like restaurants face specific barriers: 
• 
Resource constraints: Lean tools require time and management commitment, often 
scarce in SMEs. 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
• 
Customization needs: Lean must be tailored to the restaurant’s specific workflows, 
which are dynamic and varied (Alkhoraif et al., 2019). 
• 
Cultural resistance: Staff may resist structured changes, preferring familiar routines. 
Furthermore, debates exist about the compatibility of Lean with digitalization. While some 
argue Lean’s low-tech origins conflict with modern IT solutions, emerging views suggest digital 
tools can enhance Lean, especially in data collection and real-time process monitoring (Buer et 
al., 2021). This synergy, when managed carefully, can foster continuous improvement and error 
reduction in complex environments like kitchens. 
2.3.5 Conclusion: Lean's Role in Modern Kitchens 
Lean Management offers a powerful framework for addressing inefficiencies and improving 
coordination in restaurant kitchens. The dual nature of restaurants as both production and 
service entities makes them ideally suited to benefit from Lean, yet underutilization persists due 
to operational and cultural barriers. Integrating Lean into kitchen operations, especially when 
supported by digital tools, holds promise for enhancing workflow efficiency, reducing waste, 
and improving team collaboration. Recognizing the kitchen as a Lean environment provides a 
critical opportunity for restaurants to achieve greater resilience and sustainability in a 
competitive industry. While Lean Management underscores the importance of waste reduction 
and workflow optimization, its success in dynamic environments like restaurant kitchens 
ultimately depends on how effectively human workers interact with and adapt to technology. 
This necessitates a closer examination of the human-technology interface, which is explored 
through the Humanware approach. 
2.4 The Humanware Approach and Tech-Human Integration 
The integration of Lean Management principles with the Humanware approach offers a 
powerful framework for enhancing operational efficiency in restaurant kitchens. While Lean 
focuses on eliminating waste and optimizing processes, which is crucial for achieving high 
productivity in fast-paced environments, its emphasis on efficiency can sometimes overlook the 
essential role of human factors. This chapter explores how the Humanware approach 
complements Lean thinking by prioritizing workforce adaptability, cognitive ergonomics, and 
emotional well-being during digital transformation. 
2.4.1 Understanding Humanware: Technology Designed for People 
The Humanware approach, introduced by Shimada (1986), emphasizes the integration of 
human capabilities with technological advancements, ensuring that digital tools enhance rather 
than disrupt workforce efficiency. This approach emerged from the Japanese automobile 
industry, where the interplay between human resources and technology became critical for 
achieving high productivity and quality. Shimada describes Humanware as a self-generating 
innovative interplay between human resources and hardware technology, arguing that 
technology alone cannot drive success; it must be supported by skilled and motivated workers 
(Shimada, 1986). At its core, Humanware recognizes that employees are not passive users but 
active agents whose skills and adaptability significantly impact technological adoption. This 
perspective challenges traditional views that separate hardware and social organization, 
advocating instead for a holistic understanding of how these elements interact to drive 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
performance (Fabre et al., 2019). In the context of restaurant kitchens, where human expertise 
and creativity are vital, the Humanware approach offers valuable insights into how to integrate 
technology effectively.  
Humanware also shares conceptual ground with Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) research, 
which provides valuable insights into how technology can be designed to harmonize with 
human workflows (Hewett et al., 1992). Early HCI research focused on usability and 
functionality, but the field has since evolved to emphasize the broader concept of technology as 
experience (McCarthy & Wright, 2004). This paradigm shift recognizes that technology is not 
just a tool but an integral part of users' personal and professional lives. In restaurant kitchens, 
this means designing systems that not only improve efficiency but also enhance the lived 
experience of kitchen staff. Both frameworks advocate for tools that are intuitive, empowering, 
and emotionally attuned to users. 
2.4.2 Historical Evolution of Humanware 
The origins of Humanware can be traced back to the early developments in Japanese 
manufacturing. During a period of intense competition with American automakers, Japanese 
companies adopted innovative production systems that relied heavily on human resource 
effectiveness. Key principles emerged, including: 
1. Human Control of Production Processes: Workers were empowered to monitor and 
adjust production systems in real time, which not only enhanced quality but also fostered 
a culture of continuous improvement. 
2. Skill Development and Adaptability: Japanese manufacturers invested significantly in 
training programs, developing a multi-skilled workforce capable of adapting to 
changing production requirements (Shimada, 1986). 
3. Motivation and Participation: The success of the Humanware approach depended on 
creating an environment of trust and collaboration, where employees actively 
contributed to the improvement of production systems (Barley, 1986). 
Although initially applied in industrial contexts, these principles have since found relevance in 
service industries. Yet, their application in restaurant kitchen operations remains under-
researched, despite clear parallels in terms of time sensitivity, variability, and team 
interdependence. 
2.4.3 Humanware in the Context of Digital Transformation 
The relevance of the Humanware approach has significantly increased in the era of digital 
transformation, where emerging technologies such as new communication tools, artificial 
intelligence (AI), the Internet of Things (IoT), and robotics are reshaping operational 
environments. While these technologies hold the potential to improve efficiency and reduce 
costs, their success fundamentally hinges on their alignment with human capabilities and 
workflows. As Lozada et al. (2020) emphasize, technology design must prioritize cognitive 
ergonomics, ensuring that systems support human decision-making rather than overwhelming 
it. Similarly, Shimada’s (1986) principle of human-technology synergy remains crucial in this 
context. 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
Concrete examples illustrate how Humanware principles can be applied to the digitalization of 
restaurant kitchens. AI-driven inventory management tools, for instance, can automate routine 
stock control tasks, thereby freeing chefs to focus on higher-value activities such as quality 
control and culinary creativity. Similarly, collaborative robots (cobots) can assist with repetitive 
and physically demanding tasks like slicing or plating, allowing staff to dedicate their attention 
to customer engagement and service excellence. Furthermore, communication and task-
planning applications, designed with Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) principles, can help 
kitchen teams manage workflows more effectively by providing clear, intuitive interfaces that 
reduce mental load.  
These technologies exemplify how digital tools, when properly aligned with Humanware ideals, 
can augment human judgment, support workflow continuity, and facilitate knowledge transfer 
through intuitive, minimally intrusive systems. By simplifying complex tasks and offering real-
time feedback, such systems can significantly reduce cognitive load, allowing kitchen teams to 
maintain high levels of performance even under intense operational pressure. 
2.4.4 Challenges and Opportunities in Applying Humanware to Kitchens 
Despite its promise, implementing Humanware in kitchens is not without obstacles. Emotional 
adaptation plays a crucial role: digital tools can evoke anxiety, resistance, or fatigue, particularly 
when introduced without adequate training or consultation. While Shimada focused primarily 
on motivation and skill, modern workplace studies highlight the importance of emotional 
resilience and user experience in successful tech adoption (Lozada et al., 2020; McCarthy & 
Wright, 2004). Additionally, the informal communication structures and cultural dynamics of 
restaurant kitchens may clash with the procedural logic of many digital tools. Kitchens often 
operate with informal communication patterns and established routines, leading to resistance 
against tools perceived as intrusive. Change management strategies should therefore include 
participatory design, staff feedback loops, and on-the-ground testing to avoid friction. 
Fragmented IT systems also pose a barrier. Many independent kitchens operate without 
centralized infrastructure, making it difficult to integrate new tools without significant upfront 
investment and training. 
2.4.5 Conclusion 
While Shimada’s (1986) Humanware theory originated in manufacturing, its core principles are 
highly applicable to modern kitchen environments undergoing digital transformation, because 
of the interplay between human resources and technology. This study builds on Shimada’s 
insights by examining how Humanware principles can be adapted to address the unique 
challenges of kitchen environments, where the integration of digital tools must balance 
efficiency with the preservation of human creativity and teamwork. 
The Humanware approach represents a critical evolution in understanding how technology and 
human resources can be integrated to achieve sustainable performance improvements. Its 
emphasis on human adaptability, cognitive ergonomics, and participatory practices makes it 
particularly relevant in the context of digital transformation.  
By incorporating insights from socio-technical systems theory and Lean thinking, the 
Humanware approach provides a balanced and future-ready framework—one that 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
acknowledges that lasting performance improvement in kitchens depends not only on 
technological advancement, but on the people who bring that technology to life. 
 
2.5 Sustainability in Restaurant Operations: Applying the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) 
Framework 
2.5.1 The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) Framework in Restaurant Sustainability 
The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework, introduced by Elkington (1998), expands 
traditional business performance metrics beyond financial outcomes to include People, Planet, 
and Profit. This model is particularly relevant for evaluating sustainability in restaurant 
operations, where social responsibility, environmental stewardship, and economic resilience 
must intersect. 
• 
People: TBL emphasizes fair labor practices, employee well-being, and ethical working 
conditions. For restaurants, this includes fair wages, manageable workloads, and 
training programs that enhance staff development and retention (Feinstein, 2024). 
• 
Planet: Environmental sustainability is addressed through actions like reducing food 
waste, improving energy efficiency, sourcing locally, and minimizing single-use plastics 
(Lee et al., 2024). 
• 
Profit: From an economic perspective, sustainable practices can reduce operating costs 
and improve profitability. Initiatives such as efficient inventory management and waste 
reduction not only align with environmental goals but also contribute to long-term 
financial health (Dey et al., 2011; Bansal & DesJardine, 2014). 
2.5.2 Sector-Specific Challenges and Industry Trends 
While the benefits of sustainability are well-documented, restaurants face unique operational 
constraints. Financial limitations often hinder the adoption of green technologies, particularly 
among small and limited-service restaurants (Cantele & Cassia, 2020). Additionally, supply 
chain complexities and a lack of standardized ESG regulations create uncertainty and 
inconsistency in implementation (Kasim & Ismail, 2012; Gazzola et al., 2024). 
Despite these challenges, shifts in consumer preferences are driving the industry toward more 
sustainable practices. Diners increasingly favor restaurants that demonstrate transparency in 
sourcing, ethical operations, and environmental responsibility (Madanaguli et al., 2022). 
Michelin-starred restaurants, for example, have begun integrating formal sustainability policies 
in response to market and reputational pressures (Gazzola et al., 2024). 
2.5.3 Innovative and Community-Based Approaches 
Restaurants are also beginning to serve as agents of cultural change. Community-driven 
efforts—such as Portugal’s Fruta Feia initiative, which uses imperfect produce to reduce food 
waste—illustrate how sustainability can align with both environmental and economic goals 
(Ribeiro et al., 2018). Some establishments are now embedding sustainability into their core 
identity, offering employee training, partnering with local farmers, and educating customers 
about responsible consumption (Higgins-Desbiolles & Wijesinghe, 2018). 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
2.5.4 Conclusion and Critical Perspective 
Using the Triple Bottom Line framework to examine restaurant operations reveals that 
sustainability is a multidimensional challenge requiring more than environmental compliance. 
It must also encompass social equity and economic viability. However, real-world 
implementation is frequently constrained by high costs, operational complexity, and fragmented 
regulation. 
To make sustainability more accessible and scalable across the restaurant industry, future efforts 
should focus on context-aware, low-cost, and high-impact solutions. These include simple 
waste-tracking systems, community sourcing models, and inclusive staff engagement strategies. 
In this way, TBL can serve not only as a theoretical framework but also as a practical guide for 
transforming restaurant operations toward a more sustainable future. 
2.6 Conclusion and Conceptual Gaps 
This literature review establishes a foundation for understanding digital transformation in 
restaurant kitchens. STM provides a holistic lens for system analysis. Lean and Humanware 
frameworks offer tools for managing change, while TBL and Stakeholder Theory evaluate 
impact. Together, these theories guide the development of an integrated model to analyze and 
improve restaurant kitchen operations. 
While STM, Lean Management, Humanware, and sustainability frameworks offer valuable 
insights individually, few studies have integrated them into a cohesive model tailored to 
restaurant kitchen environments. The literature lacks empirical investigations into how Lean-
Humanware integration functions within socio-technical systems, particularly in high-pressure, 
service-oriented contexts. 
Moreover, most digital transformation studies focus on user acceptance or macro-level adoption 
strategies, without capturing the post-adoption impact on communication, coordination, and 
sustainability outcomes. This thesis addresses these conceptual gaps by developing and 
applying a hybrid framework that connects system-level theory with operational realities in 
professional kitchens.

By combining these qualitative insights with quantitative data, the research aims to deliver both 
depth and breadth in analyzing digital transformation processes, providing actionable, 
theoretically grounded recommendations relevant to practice and future research.

methods approach combining ethnographic observation, semi-structured interviews with chefs,

4.2 Research Design: Justification for a Single-Case Approach 
This study adopts a single-case study design, focusing on a Copenhagen-based casual dining 
restaurant. The decision to rely on a single case is rooted in the need to analyze depth rather 
than breadth—to uncover the socio-technical and organizational mechanisms underpinning 
technology adoption in high-pressure kitchen environments. As Gerring (2004) argues, case 
studies are particularly powerful for identifying causal mechanisms, contextual depth, and 
temporal process tracing. This makes them ideally suited for research questions that prioritize 
explanatory insight over statistical generalization. 
Building on this rationale, Stake (1995) and Merriam (2009) emphasize that the strength of 
single-case studies lies in their bounded and intensive nature. Such designs allow researchers 
to develop rich, holistic understandings of the subject of inquiry—especially when the case 
is purposefully selected for its relevance, accessibility, and potential to generate analytic 
insights. In this thesis, the selected restaurant serves as a bounded system where human, 
technological, and managerial interactions can be studied in situ. 
Furthermore, Gustafsson (2017) highlights that single-case designs are appropriate when the 
goal is to understand a complex phenomenon in its natural setting, enabling researchers to 
explore not only observable outcomes but also underlying behaviors, tensions, and 
adaptations. This aligns with the research aim to investigate how Lean Management and digital 
tools affect communication, coordination, and sustainability in the lived reality of a restaurant 
kitchen. 
However, it is also essential to recognize the limitations of this approach. Single-case designs 
inherently carry risks related to generalizability, replicability, and researcher bias 
(Gustafsson, 2017). While the findings from this study are not intended to be statistically 
 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
representative, they offer valuable conceptual and theoretical insights that can inform 
broader discussions about digital transformation in hospitality settings. These limitations are 
mitigated through methodological triangulation (observation, interviews, app data) and 
reflexivity in data collection and analysis. 
The selected restaurant provides an appropriate “bounded system” where the interplay between 
human, technological, and organizational factors can be observed holistically. Its casual dining 
format, team size, and operational scale make it representative of many urban European 
restaurants, while still offering a manageable scope for deep fieldwork. This aligns with the 
idea that case studies excel in revealing how phenomena unfold over time and in context—what 
Gerring (2004) refers to as “process tracing.” 
While acknowledging limitations regarding statistical inference, this design enables a rich, 
contextualized understanding of the mechanisms at play. Moreover, it provides a foundation for 
future research using multiple cases or comparative designs. 
In sum, the single-case study provides the methodological depth needed to uncover nuanced, 
embedded insights into the digital transformation of restaurant kitchens. It also lays a solid 
foundation for future research using multiple-case comparisons or longitudinal designs. 
 
4.2.1 Case Description: Restaurant Profile and Selection Rationale 
This study is based on a single case: El Goldie, an independent, chef-driven restaurant located 
in Copenhagen. The restaurant offers a creative, Mexican-inspired tasting menu at a fixed price 
of 300 DKK and operates exclusively in the evening. It employs a small team of approximately 
10–14 (6 chefs) employees and accommodates 60–80 guests per night. The kitchen team 
includes one head chef, one sous chef, and several line cooks, operating with a semi-formal 
brigade structure. The kitchen is located in the back of the restaurant and is not visible to guests. 
While the menu changes frequently to allow for creativity and flexibility, it is not strictly 
seasonal due to the limitations of sourcing Mexican ingredients in Denmark. 
El Goldie was purposefully selected as a strategically relevant and information-rich single 
case (Stake, 1995) for several reasons, all aligned with the research design and objectives of 
this thesis: 
• 
Operational Complexity and Socio-Technical Fit: Despite its small scale, El Goldie 
operates in a dynamic, high-pressure kitchen environment. Execution of a multi-course 
tasting menu with a compact team requires tight coordination, effective communication, 
and adaptive task distribution. This setting is ideal for analyzing how digital tools 
interact with existing workflows, communication patterns, and informal decision-
making processes—key concerns of socio-technical systems theory. 
• 
Lean and Humanware Alignment: The restaurant functions with inherent lean 
characteristics—minimal stock, short prep windows, limited staff capacity—and relies 
heavily on informal, trust-based coordination. These features reflect common patterns 
identified in independent restaurants globally, where “low-margin environments force a 
natural lean mentality, often without formal systems” (Deloitte, 2025, p. 14). Such 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
settings offer ideal conditions to examine how Humanware principles influence the 
integration of digital tools in ways that enhance rather than disrupt team performance. 
• 
Representative of Independent Urban Restaurants: El Goldie shares key features 
with a broader group of independent restaurants identified in Deloitte’s 2025 study: 
small team size (5–15 staff), non-standardized processes, reliance on the partly chef-led 
model, and high levels of creative autonomy. Deloitte notes that 73% of independent 
European restaurants fall into this “owner-led, flexible-format” category (p. 10), making 
El Goldie analytically representative of this wider population. 
• 
Digital Maturity Gap: As highlighted in the same report, over 60% of independent 
restaurants in Europe “lack structured digital workflows for planning, inventory, and 
prep coordination” (Deloitte, 2025, p. 9). El Goldie had no formal digital system in place 
for kitchen task management prior to this research. This low digital maturity provides a 
clean pre-intervention baseline for evaluating the introduction and perceived usefulness 
of Remi. 
• 
Agility and Cultural Fit for Experimentation: The same report highlights that 
independent restaurants, while often under-resourced, show higher levels of flexibility 
and cultural openness to testing new tools, especially when ownership is directly 
involved in operations (Deloitte, 2025, p. 7). El Goldie, as a chef-run, non-chain 
operation, offered this type of agile, low-barrier-to-entry environment, enabling iterative 
learning and observation of tool adoption in context. 
• 
Scientific Validity Through Deep Access: The research team was granted full access 
to El Goldie’s kitchen across two testing phases—before and after the introduction of 
the Remi app. This access enabled in-situ, real-time observation of kitchen practices 
during both prep and service, enriching the empirical dataset and supporting an 
embedded interpretive methodology aligned with socio-technical system analysis. 
• 
Strategic Fit with Current Industry Gaps: Independent restaurants make up the vast 
majority of hospitality businesses globally but remain underserved by digital 
infrastructure and underrepresented in academic research on digital adoption (Deloitte, 
2025, p. 7). Moreover, these businesses are uniquely agile, allowing researchers to 
observe both adoption and adaptation in real time. At the same time, they are deeply 
reliant on informal structures, making them ideal for studying the integration of socio-
technical tools into everyday practice (Deloitte, 2025, p. 18). 
In summary, El Goldie’s profile—small, chef-driven, digitally underserved, operationally 
lean, and culturally open to innovation—makes it an ideal case to explore the integration of 
digital tools into human-centered workflows. As Deloitte (2025) concludes, independent 
restaurants “represent both the most vulnerable and most responsive segment when it comes to 
digital transformation” (p. 6), offering a uniquely valuable lens for understanding how systems 
like Remi can support more sustainable, structured, and people-centered kitchen operations.

4.3 About Remi: A Kitchen Operations App 
4.3.1 Purpose and Design Rationale 
Remi (https://www.thisisremi.com/) is a digital kitchen operations platform developed to 
support kitchen teams in planning, coordination, and communication. It aims to reduce food 
waste, lower stress levels, and enhance collaboration and decision-making in professional 
kitchens. 
The app was developed between October 2023 and February 2025 through an iterative, user-
driven process that included interviews, prototyping, and testing with over 35 chefs from 
various restaurant formats (fine dining, casual dining, and fast casual). This co-development 
process aligns with Human-Centered Design (HCD) principles and reflects the needs of a 
dynamic, high-pressure work environment. 
4.3.2 Core Features, User Workflows, and Roles 
Remi is designed around the practical realities of professional kitchens, providing a digital 
companion that supports rather than disrupts established workflows. At its core, the app offers 
three integrated functions that reflect daily kitchen operations: menu planning, task 
management, and team communication. The menu planning feature enables chefs to build, 
modify, and maintain dynamic menus, including detailed breakdowns of each dish, which 
enhances clarity for prep and service. Through the task assignment and prep planning

To mirror the structure of real-world kitchen hierarchies, Remi supports three primary user

4.3.4 Relevance to the Research 
Including a detailed description of Remi is essential for understanding how the app functions 
as a socio-technical intervention within the studied restaurant. The STM framework 
emphasizes that tools are not neutral—they mediate communication, shape coordination, and 
can either support or obstruct workflows. Understanding how Remi was designed and used 
during the observation period is therefore critical for interpreting the results in Chapters 5 and 
6. 
 
4.4 Data Collection Methods  
4.4.1 Rationale for Participant Observation 
Participant observation was selected to enable the researcher to be embedded in the kitchen 
environment—not merely as an external observer, but as an active participant. This immersive 
approach increases access to tacit knowledge, informal team dynamics, and everyday workflow 
patterns that are often difficult to capture through interviews or surveys (Geertz, 1973; Spradley, 
1980). The objective is to observe firsthand how routines, interpersonal interactions, and digital 
interventions unfold during actual prep and service shifts. 
This method is aligned with the Socio-Technical Model (STM), which emphasizes the 
interdependent relationship between human, technological, and organizational subsystems 
(Baxter & Sommerville, 2011). By engaging directly with staff and integrating the Remi app 
into operations, the researcher is positioned to observe socio-technical interactions in context—
including emergent norms, adaptations, and unintended consequences. 
To enable comparative insights, the observation period was structured into two distinct phases: 
• 
Phase 1: Two weeks of standard kitchen operations without the Remi app 
• 
Phase 2: Two weeks of operations with the Remi app integrated into the workflow 
This design allowed for contextual comparison of communication, task planning, and 
coordination practices across pre- and post-intervention conditions—providing a more robust 
basis for identifying both challenges and improvements enabled by the digital tool. 
Observation will focus on: 
• 
Team interaction and communication patterns 
• 
Use and non-use of the Remi app 
• 
Workflow bottlenecks and Lean-like routines 
• 
Informal feedback and cultural attitudes toward technology 
Field notes will be recorded in two streams: (1) descriptive accounts of observed actions, and 
(2) analytical memos capturing impressions, questions, and emerging patterns. 
This section provides a detailed and practical framework for how participant observation will 
be applied as a core methodological tool in this study. Drawing from socio-technical theory and 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
interpretivist ethnography, this approach involves immersive, hands-on fieldwork in real-time 
kitchen environments where the Remi app is actively used (Geertz, 1973; Spradley, 1980). 
 
4.4.2 Observation Focus Areas (Aligned with STM) 
The following dimensions, adapted from STM, structure what the observation will focus on: 
• 
Hardware/Software Infrastructure: Where and how the Remi app is accessed (e.g., 
shared tablet, mobile phones). 
• 
Human-Computer Interaction: Ease or difficulty of using the app during rush periods, 
app avoidance behaviors, multitasking. 
• 
Workflow Coordination: How Remi affects task assignment, handovers, or planning 
rituals. 
• 
People and Roles: Who engages with the app and who doesn't; informal leadership 
patterns. 
• 
Internal Culture: Humor, resistance, encouragement, or team norms around app use. 
• 
External Pressures: Health inspections, customer expectations, food waste policies. 
• 
Feedback & Data Monitoring: Whether data or task completion is reviewed after 
shifts, and how teams reflect on performance using the app. 
Please see Appendix D(TableD.2) 
 
4.4.3 Structured Observation Documentation 
All observations will be documented in a structured, time-stamped format using two parallel 
streams: 
1. Descriptive Field Notes: Factual details of what happened—who did what, where, 
using what, and in what sequence. 
2. Analytical Memos: Reflections on why the behavior occurred, what it reveals about 
tech adoption or team dynamics, and how it fits into STM or Lean-Humanware thinking. 
The field logs will include: 
• 
Who was involved (role or pseudonym) 
• 
What happened (specific actions, behaviors, words) 
• 
When it occurred (exact or relative time) 
• 
Where it occurred (location in the kitchen) 
• 
Why it might matter (operational or cultural insight) 
• 
How it links to broader themes or theories 
This structure ensures rigor, transparency, and alignment with qualitative best practices 
(Emerson et al., 2011).

4.4.4 Use of Technology in Participant Observation 
Because Remi is a central part of the intervention being studied, the observation will treat digital 
tools as an “active agents” within the system. This includes: 
• 
Where phones are placed 
• 
Which features of the app are most and least used 
• 
Whether tasks are confirmed digitally or still verbally 
• 
Timing patterns (e.g., morning planning vs. in-shift adjustments) 
This mirrors Baxter and Sommerville’s (2011) socio-technical approach, where digital 
infrastructure is embedded into the cultural and procedural fabric of work. 
 
4.4.5 Researcher’s Role as Participant Observer 
The researcher will perform basic support tasks in the kitchen (e.g., prep, checking inventory) 
to gain trust and better understand the context. This participation: 
• 
Enables better access to unfiltered routines and informal communication 
• 
Builds rapport with the team 
• 
Surfaces frictions and adaptations that passive observation might miss 
Throughout, reflexive memoing will document positionality and emotional reactions (Emerson 
et al., 2011). These notes serve both as analytic input and as transparency for potential bias or 
influence on the field. 
 
4.4.6 Ethical Practices 
• 
All participants will be informed about the research purpose and give consent 
• 
Identities will be anonymized in notes and transcripts 
• 
Sensitive data will only be recorded if explicitly permitted 
• 
Observation will not interfere with service or stress teams unnecessarily 
 
4.5 Semi-Structured Interviews 
Semi-structured interviews are employed in this study to explore the lived experiences, 
meanings, and practices of kitchen staff, chefs, and managers who interact with the Remi app. 
This interview style is particularly useful in interpretivist research because it balances structured 
inquiry with flexibility, allowing respondents to voice unanticipated views and reflect on their 
experiences in their own terms (Brinkmann, 2013; Kvale & Brinkmann, 2009). The method 
supports open-ended exploration while maintaining alignment with core themes of the research 
— including digital adoption, workflow coordination, and sustainability in kitchen operations. 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
This approach is preferred over structured interviews, which restrict participants to predefined 
questions, limiting depth and nuance. It is also more targeted than unstructured interviews, 
which lack consistency and make thematic synthesis across cases difficult. As GoodTape (2023) 
emphasizes, semi-structured interviews are ideal when the goal is “to hear people reflect on 
their practice while still ensuring the researcher collects comparable insights across 
participants.” This balance makes it a strong fit for this study’s aim: to understand how Remi 
impacts kitchen culture, communication, and sustainability. 
 
4.5.1 Interview Objectives 
The purpose of the interviews is to: 
• 
Understand how kitchen staff experience and evaluate the Remi app in daily operations. 
• 
Uncover communication and coordination challenges in kitchen workflows. 
• 
Investigate how sustainability concerns shape attitudes toward digital tools. 
• 
Capture the influence of Lean principles and Humanware elements on daily practices. 
 
4.5.2 Interview Design and Structure 
The interviews follow a flexible three-part structure: 
1. Introductory Phase – Building rapport and discussing the participant’s background and 
role. 
2. Descriptive Phase – Exploring concrete examples of workflows, communication, app 
usage, and challenges. 
3. Reflective Phase – Eliciting interpretation and evaluation of the app, changes in work 
dynamics, and broader reflections on digital transformation. 
Questions are grounded in the theoretical underpinnings of the Socio-Technical Model (STM),

Cultural norms and resistance to change

Operational efficiency and stress 
• 
Sustainability outcomes (e.g., food waste, energy, well-being)

represent a broad spectrum of roles and responsibilities in the kitchen, including head chefs,

4.6 Data Analysis Approach: Thematic Analysis 
The interview data will be analyzed using thematic analysis, a hybrid deductive-inductive 
approach (Braun & Clarke, 2006; Green et al., 2007). This method was chosen because it can 
capture both expected patterns and unexpected themes in a heterogeneous group of participants. 
In contrast to content analysis, which tends to focus on counting the frequency of codes or 
categories, thematic analysis focuses on interpreting meaning in participants' language and 
behavior. Grounded theory was also considered but ultimately not selected because the goal of 
this study is not to develop a new theory but to interpret experiences through existing conceptual 
lenses—specifically, STM, lean humanware, and sustainability theory. 
Thematic analysis allows to: 
• 
Map findings to pre-existing frameworks (deductive coding), 
• 
Surface emerging insights from real-world use (inductive coding), 
• 
Identify cross-cutting patterns in how technology is integrated into kitchen workflows. 
As noted in GoodTape’s guide (2023), thematic analysis is especially appropriate when 
researchers seek to “explore how people understand or experience a particular situation — 
especially across different roles and contexts.” 
Initial codes are developed around key theoretical dimensions such as: 
• 
App usability and cognitive load 
• 
Communication breakdowns or improvements 
• 
Workflow coordination and real-time task management 
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
• 
Emotional responses (stress, adaptability, motivation) 
• 
Reflections on food waste, efficiency, and well-being 
Coding is done manually through iterative reading and memoing. Themes are triangulated with 
observation notes and Remi app usage logs to strengthen credibility and depth. 
 
4.6.1 Ethical and Practical Considerations 
All participants provided informed consent after being briefed on the research aims and their 
rights. Participation was entirely voluntary, and anonymity was ensured throughout the process. 
Participants were informed that they could skip any question or withdraw at any time without 
consequence. Interviews were conducted in person at the case site, typically post-shift or during 
quiet periods in the kitchen. Conversations lasted between 25 and 65 minutes, allowing for in-
depth reflection on the app experience and kitchen workflows. 
 
4.6.2 Document/Tool Analysis 
While this study originally aimed to integrate backend analytics to track in-app user behaviour 
and time-stamped actions, technical constraints prevented full implementation. As an 
alternative, key usage metrics were manually captured during field observations and app 
walkthroughs. 
These metrics include: 
• Time taken to create a mise en place (MEP) list or assign tasks 
• Time required to create or update menu items 
• Number of tasks assigned per user per day 
• Frequency of notes, priorities, or changes logged in the Remi app 
Timing was measured using a stopwatch during live sessions, with contextual annotations. 
Interaction frequencies were retrieved from the app interface during daily debriefs with the 
chefs. 
Although these metrics are not backend-automated, they offer real-time, in-situ performance 
indicators and serve as a proxy for adoption intensity and tool integration. This quantitative 
lens complements the thesis’s qualitative field notes and interviews, enriching the overall 
analysis of operational coordination and user-centered digital transformation in restaurant 
kitchens. Please see Appendix B(Table B.1).

4.11 Limitations 
While this study offers valuable insights into the socio-technical integration of Lean and 
Humanware principles in a kitchen setting, several limitations must be acknowledged: 
• 
Single-Case Design: The focus on one restaurant provides depth and contextual insight 
but limits the generalizability of findings to other kitchen types, cultures, or 
organizational scales. 
==PAGE:47==

• 
Observer Effect: The researcher’s presence as a participant-observer may have 
influenced staff behavior, particularly during the early observation phases. Although 
efforts were made to reduce intrusiveness, some degree of reactivity is likely. 
• 
Limited Time Frame for Digital Adoption: The Remi app was introduced only two 
weeks prior to the post-intervention observation phase. This short timeframe did not 
allow for full organizational adoption, which typically requires longer cycles of training, 
resistance, normalization, and iteration. As such, the findings reflect early-stage 
interaction with the tool rather than long-term digital integration. 
• 
Manual Data Collection: Due to technical constraints, certain quantitative metrics 
(e.g., task planning time, number of assignments) were collected manually rather than 
via backend analytics. While still valuable, this limits precision and may introduce 
observational bias. 
• 
Sample Size for Interviews and Fieldwork: Although the sample included various 
team roles, the number of interviewees and field observations remains relatively small, 
which may restrict the diversity of perspectives captured. 
Despite these limitations, the study contributes meaningful, empirically grounded insights into 
how kitchen teams experience and adapt to digital transformation. It lays the groundwork for 
future research that could include longitudinal case studies, multi-site comparisons, and more 
advanced analytics integration.

6.1 Key Finding 1: The Importance of Cultural and Leadership Alignment for Digital Adoption 
One of the most decisive factors shaping the adoption of digital tools in restaurant kitchens is 
not their functionality but the cultural and leadership context into which they are introduced. At 
El Goldie, the introduction of the Remi app revealed how leadership behaviour and cultural 
norms influenced its adoption and effectiveness. While junior chefs such as Alicia and Magnus 
were eager to use the app and described it as a helpful structure for autonomy and planning, the 
mixed signals from senior chefs disrupted its consistent use. Juan Carlos was ostensibly a role

"At the beginning, it was very difficult to adopt another technology… when you're under

ambiguity is minimized. At El Goldie, junior chefs like Alicia showed readiness and motivation

their workflow. Andersen et al. (2022) argue that leaders serve as ‘meaning-makers’ in digital 
transitions—shaping how tools are understood through symbolic and behavioral cues. At El 
Goldie, the absence of unified signals from leadership created interpretive uncertainty. 
This lack of clarity in leadership shaped the team's behavior. On days when both leaders actively 
used Remi, the completion rate of digital tasks rose above 80%. On days with inconsistent

modeling, the app was ignored and relied on verbal handoffs, with completion rates falling

This fluctuation highlights how closely digital adoption is linked to cultural reinforcement and 
team norms. The inconsistent uptake of the Remi app at El Goldie can be located within the

Theoretically, this finding reinforces the Humanware approach, which emphasizes the

undermining the app’s potential to reduce waste and improve efficiency. This observation is

cultural alignment and leadership commitment are critical factors during the early phases of 
implementation (Saghafian, Laumann, & Skogstad, 2022).In particular, the lack of role clarity

restaurant leaders should: 
1. Model consistent use of digital tools during all phases of operation, including high-
pressure moments. 
2. Establish clear expectations and routines around tool usage, such as daily check-ins or 
task reviews. 
3. Provide ongoing training and support to address resistance and build confidence among 
staff. 
4. Involve staff in the design and refinement of digital tools to ensure alignment with real-
world workflows and cultural norms. 
Ultimately, the Remi app served as a mirror to existing dynamics: when leadership was aligned 
and culture supported its use, the tool empowered autonomy and improved coordination. When

also influences Triple Bottom Line outcomes. By improving consistency in planning and 
reducing task confusion, resource usage can be optimized—leading to less food waste (Planet),

“You always have to, like, pick out your phone and go into the app… instead of just looking 
across your shoulder at the physical list... Every time, I feel like it would take more time.” — 
Simon 
This illustrates a broader usability mismatch between digital systems and real-world kitchen 
rhythm. According to Eze et al. (2019), ease of use—defined as the degree to which a system 
can be used with minimal skill and effort—is crucial not only in the initial inscription of 
technology but also in its framing and stabilisation within real workflows. In the Remi case, 
while adoption occurred initially, the lack of frictionless real-time use prevented the tool from 
stabilizing into everyday kitchen practice. 
Closely linked is the concept of adoption cost, which Eze et al. define not just in financial terms, 
but also in operational and cognitive costs. In this study, the "cost" of using Remi during 
intense periods came in the form of disrupted flow, additional steps, and increased mental 
workload. As Eze et al. note, such hidden costs often become decisive adoption barriers in 
SMEs. 
The app's friction became especially visible as shifts progressed. While during pre-shift 
routines, Remi helped chefs independently plan mise-en-place without repeated verbal 
confirmation, its lack of speed and flexibility became apparent during service. Features such as 
visible task assignments and color-coded priorities aligned well with Lean principles like waste 
==PAGE:73==
73 
 
reduction and decentralization. Yet the app’s rigidity during high-pressure moments hindered 
its full integration. 
These challenges are mirrored in healthcare settings, where high workload and time pressure 
similarly reduce engagement with digital systems (Keyworth et al., 2018). The study highlights 
that successful technology adoption requires explicit instruction on how to perform the 
behaviour, clear relevance to daily routines, and visible benefits for performance improvement. 
Without these, even well-designed systems can be bypassed under stress. 
On May 12, a dessert preparation error occurred because the app lacked real-time recipe access 
and task linking. The issue resulted in miscommunication, stress, and wasted ingredients. 
In response, chefs reverted to analog workarounds—calling out tasks, scribbling notes, or 
referring to printed or mobile-stored recipes. These informal adjustments align with Saghafian 
et al. (2022)’s notion of workarounds: adaptive deviations from formal systems when 
technology fails under real-world stress. In the Remi case, such deviations protected 
performance but simultaneously signaled a failure of full integration. 
As noted by Keyworth et al. (2018), effective implementation of technology-based 
interventions depends on coherence (clear purpose), cognitive participation (active 
engagement), and collective action. In the Remi case, when stress rises, these elements break 
down, pushing chefs to disengage and fall back on familiar analog methods. 
During pre-shift routines, Remi enabled chefs to independently manage their mise-en-place 
(dialy task planning) without requiring repeated verbal confirmation. Features like visible task 
assignments and color-coded prioritization helped reduce planning friction and aligned well 
with Lean objectives. For instance, chefs could quickly identify their responsibilities and 
prioritize tasks effectively before the shift started. However, as the day began, the limits of the 
app became increasingly visible. The inability to quickly edit task and check task wihout loosing 
time, adjust and add information such as order lists, or the fact that critical informaiton such as 
recipes were not visiabel in the app often forced chefs to revert to traditional, analog 
workarounds — such as shouting tasks or scribbling on paper lists and look for their recipes in 
books or pone photos. This shift toward analog fallback methods exemplifies what Saghafian 
et al. (2022) describe as workarounds: informal, adaptive deviations from formal systems when 
tools do not support core tasks under pressure. These workarounds are not necessarily deviant, 
but rather serve as practical coping strategies during high-stress or high-urgency moments. In 
Remi’s case, the lack of fast interaction modes during peak periods led to procedural drift — 
chefs bypassed the app to protect service quality and flow.  
The dessert preparation error on 12.05, caused by the missing recipe feature and not 
communicated inforamation, vividly demonstrated the risks of this disconnect. A single 
oversight, exacerbated by time pressure and the app’s static design, led to wasted resources, 
unnecessary stress, and a breakdown in trust. 
From a theoretical standpoint, this key finding reinforces the Humanware principle that 
effective tool integration depends not just on functional design but on alignment with a Deep 
understanding of work practices and its enviroment. Kitchens are palces where pshicality, 
attention, and memory interact rapidly. Any digital tool that cannot keep pace with this rhythm 
risks being ignored or abandoned at the most critical moments. In Lean terms, adaptability is 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
central to maintaining flow and preventing bottlenecks. When the system becomes rigid under 
stress, it inadvertently generates waste — in the form of repeated instructions, lost time, rework 
and financial loss. 
Practically, the implications are clear. Tools like Remi must evolve to support the unique tempo 
of restaurant operations. Features such as faster real-time task editing and updates, prodcut 
tracking via AI vision, recipe integration, and hands-free interaction — via voice commands or 
wall-mounted tablets — are not luxury add-ons but essential components of resilient digital 
infrastructure. Moreover, supporting dynamic workflows requires interfaces that allow for fast 
interaction without interrupting service rhythm. Chefs should not have to choose between 
accuracy and efficiency. 
This finding also speaks directly to the research question. The answer lies in designing systems 
that adapt as rapidly as the work environment they aim to support. Static planning alone is 
insufficient. Kitchens need digital companions that move with them — not against them. 
In sum, this key finding illustrates a broader truth for digital transformation in hospitality: 
resilience is not just about robustness but responsiveness. Systems that can flex under pressure 
— without compromising usability — will be those that chefs not only use but rely on. The 
Remi app’s limitations during high-pressure moments highlight the need for digital tools to 
evolve beyond static planning aids into dynamic, real-time coordination platforms. By 
addressing these gaps, tools like Remi can become indispensable partners in the fast-paced, 
high-stakes world of professional kitchens. 
This chapter thus reinforces that real-time adaptability is not a secondary feature — it is 
essential for digital tool adoption in hospitality. A system that is usable only during calm phases 
will not achieve full integration. Digital tools must match the speed, pressure, and rhythm of 
frontline kitchen work to remain relevant when it matters most. And finally, the convergence 
between healthcare and hospitality underscores a broader truth: digital tools must be introduced 
with not only user-friendly features but also behavioural reinforcement, organisational 
alignment, and clarity of purpose to remain relevant during peak operational moments. 
 
6.3: The Data-Driven Potential of Feedback Loops for Kitchen Performance and Sustainability 
One of the most forward-looking insights from this study is the untapped potential of digital 
tools, such as Remi, in establishing meaningful feedback loops—not merely for usability 
improvements but as foundations for truly data-driven kitchen operations. Although informal 
insights were frequently exchanged among chefs, their systematic collection and structured 
analysis were limited. This gap prevented the app from evolving into a robust tool capable of 
tracking performance, supporting coordination, and advancing tangible sustainability goals. 
Empirical observations highlighted a stark contrast between traditional paper-based task lists 
and digital assignments via Remi. Printed lists frequently lacked clarity and real-time 
accountability. Alicia vividly described the uncertainty with paper workflows: "Sometimes 
[Juan Carlos] forgot to put the name. Salsa for Alicia? No. Churros for Fabian?" Remi, 
conversely, clarified task ownership explicitly: "Now I don’t need to ask him anymore." This 
==PAGE:75==
75 
 
clarity not only improved individual accountability but also provided a critical foundation for 
performance tracking and workflow optimization. 
The transformative potential of structured, data-driven feedback was further underlined by Juan 
Carlos. Reflecting on the fragility and mental overload induced by paper-based processes, he 
explained: 
"I have just half an hour to sit down and make the orders... and suddenly I realize I'm out of 
lists. So I have to go to the computer, change the color of the paper, print it, and then start my 
process maybe 10–15 minutes late. Do you know how many mistakes I've made in those 
moments? And then we're wasting paper." 
Juan Carlos also explicitly tied kitchen inefficiencies to sustainability concerns: 
"80% of food waste is due to poor decision-making... It could be solved completely if we were 
a bit more organized." 
This observation closely aligns with findings from Awan et al. (2021), who highlight how data-
driven insights can profoundly enhance circular economy outcomes by systematically capturing 
operational knowledge and fostering sustainable decision-making practices. Translating this 
insight to the kitchen context suggests that digital tools, through structured feedback loops, 
could systematically reduce food waste and improve resource efficiency. 
Drawing further parallels to smart manufacturing practices discussed by Tao et al. (2018), 
kitchen operations can significantly benefit from real-time data analytics and predictive 
monitoring. By embedding IoT-like principles into Remi, such as real-time task completion 
updates or predictive analytics for inventory and ingredient usage, kitchens could shift from 
reactive troubleshooting to proactive, data-informed decision-making—significantly reducing 
waste, errors, and operational downtime. 
The socio-technical dimensions of this transition were strongly reinforced by Colombari and 
Neirotti (2024), who argue for leveraging frontline employees' knowledge to improve 
operational data-driven decision-making. In kitchens, this implies a pivotal role for head chefs 
and sous chefs, who would not only collect operational data but interpret it, foster collective 
sense-making among the team, and implement insights in daily practices. For Remi, this could 
mean structured in-app prompts or dashboards explicitly designed to facilitate team discussions 
and joint decision-making. 
To operationalize these insights effectively, Remi should integrate lightweight, chef-friendly 
feedback mechanisms inspired by smart manufacturing principles: real-time performance 
dashboards, predictive shift analytics, or structured monthly reviews involving frontline staff 
and managers. Such features would translate everyday kitchen operations into actionable, 
continuous improvements. 
However, potential challenges must also be acknowledged. Implementing structured feedback 
loops demands not only technological adjustments but significant organizational and cultural 
shifts. Resistance from staff unused to data transparency or additional administrative tasks 
could emerge. Addressing this requires careful training, clear communication of benefits, and 
phased implementation to gradually build trust and proficiency among kitchen teams. 
==PAGE:76==
76 
 
In summary, embedding structured data collection and feedback mechanisms into tools like 
Remi transcends mere technical enhancement. It is about transforming kitchen operations into 
adaptive, learning-oriented systems. By tracking task performance, operational habits, and 
sustainability metrics systematically, kitchens can evolve from reactive, fragmented spaces into 
proactive, resilient, and strategically sustainable environments. 
 
6.4 Validity, Reliability, and Limitations 
To ensure the credibility of this research, multiple strategies were employed to enhance both 
the validity and reliability of findings. Nevertheless, given the nature of the study — a single-
case analysis of a live kitchen environment during early-stage digital adoption — certain 
limitations remain and should be acknowledged. 
 
6.4.1 Validity 
Validity in this study was strengthened through triangulation of data sources, including field 
observations, in-depth interviews with various kitchen roles, and usage data from the Remi app. 
By drawing on these different types of evidence, the analysis could establish stronger empirical 
grounding and avoid over-reliance on individual accounts. The socio-technical model (STM) 
provided a structured analytical lens, ensuring that findings were interpreted within a coherent 
theoretical framework. 
Still, construct validity may be influenced by the novelty of the tool and the short testing 
period. Since Remi was in a beta phase, some observed outcomes may reflect transitional 
dynamics rather than long-term behaviors. Additionally, internal validity could be affected by 
the presence of the researcher during testing, which may have introduced subtle observer effects 
— such as heightened app usage during observed shifts. 
 
6.4.2 Reliability 
To enhance reliability, field observations followed a consistent structure across multiple test 
days, and interviews were transcribed and cross-checked to reduce misinterpretation. Coding 
categories for STM analysis were derived deductively from theory and refined inductively 
through engagement with the data. Where possible, statements were linked to specific dates or 
app usage patterns to ensure analytical traceability. 
Nonetheless, replicability is inherently limited in qualitative single-case research. The 
outcomes of this study are context-specific and closely tied to the team culture, leadership style, 
and workflows of El Goldie. Another kitchen with a different team structure or digital maturity 
level might experience very different outcomes with the same tool. The findings should 
therefore be understood as transferable insights, not generalizable truths. 
 
==PAGE:77==
77 
 
6.4.3 Limitations 
Several structural and methodological limitations must be acknowledged: 
• 
Single-case scope: While depth was prioritized over breadth, the lack of comparative 
kitchens limits the ability to contextualize or benchmark results. 
• 
Short implementation phase: The Remi app was tested over two weeks, a time frame 
that may be too short to fully capture cultural shifts or habit formation. 
• 
Incomplete data integration: Due to technical constraints, some usage metrics could 
not be captured automatically and relied on manual tracking, reducing data granularity. 
• 
Participant bias: Chefs were aware that their feedback might influence the app’s 
development, which may have encouraged either overly critical or overly positive 
responses depending on individual motivations. 
Despite these limitations, the study offers valuable insights into the real-world dynamics of 
digital tool adoption in professional kitchens. Its strength lies not in statistical power but in 
contextual richness, grounded analysis, and the integration of lived experiences into broader 
socio-technical reflection. 
 
6.5 Concluding Remarks 
This master thesis set out to explore how digital tools can improve coordination, 
communication, and sustainability in restaurant kitchens through the lens of a socio-technical 
model. Using the Remi app as a case study, the research examined not only the technical 
functionalities of the tool, but also the cultural, emotional, and organizational contexts in which 
it was deployed. The results confirm that technology in kitchens cannot be seen in isolation it 
is inseparable from the people who use it, the workflows it aims to support, and the cultural 
conditions that shape its adoption. 
Through field observations, interviews, and in-app usage data, it became clear that digital 
transformation in the restaurant sector is neither linear nor universal. Instead, it is shaped by 
dynamic interactions between leadership behavior, team culture, interface usability, and real-
world constraints. When digital tools like Remi align with team routines and emotional 
rhythms, they can empower staff, reduce stress, and increase operational clarity. When this 
alignment falters — whether due to missing menu updates, inconsistent leadership modeling, 
or a lack of adaptability during peak service the very same tool can become a source of friction 
or even be bypassed altogether. 
The socio-technical model (STM) proved to be a valuable analytical framework. It helped 
situate Lean and Humanware principles within a broader system logic — one that recognizes 
digital tools as mediators of behavior, not just containers of information. The findings not only 
demonstrate the need for human-centered and culturally aware digital design, but also 
emphasize the potential for sustainability gains if coordination frictions and communication 
breakdowns are systematically addressed. The final STM working model (Chapter 5.9) 
synthesizes these findings into a systemic framework that links empirical observations with 
==PAGE:78==
78 
 
practical and theoretical implications offering a structured guide for future interventions in 
kitchen digitalization 
While this research is limited to a single case, it offers a high-resolution lens into the complex 
realities of restaurant work. The insights generated here are not only relevant to kitchen software 
developers and restaurant managers but also contribute to the academic discourse on 
digitalization in small-scale, high-pressure service environments. 
In a time where labor shortages, sustainability imperatives, and customer expectations put 
increasing pressure on kitchens, digital solutions will likely play a growing role. But their 
success depends not only on technical robustness it hinges on the human systems into which 
they are introduced. The road ahead requires a fusion of thoughtful design, adaptive leadership, 
and inclusive practices that recognize chefs not just as users of technology, but as active co-
creators of digital change. 
 
6.5.1 Future Research and Practical Implications 
This study explored the intersection of lean thinking, human-centered design, and socio-
technical systems in the context of digital transformation in a professional kitchen. While the 
research focused on a single case study, its findings offer broader relevance for both academic 
inquiry and practical innovation in the hospitality industry. 
6.5.2 Future Research 
The limitations of this thesis also point toward promising avenues for future investigation. First, 
further studies could expand the empirical scope by including multiple restaurant types, such 
as fine dining, canteens, or hotel kitchens, to examine how different cultural and organizational 
structures influence digital tool adoption. Such comparative designs could reveal whether the 
socio-technical patterns observed at El Goldie are context-specific or more broadly applicable. 
Second, the role of feedback loops emerged as a critical yet underdeveloped area. Future 
research could explore how digital tools can facilitate real-time learning within kitchen 
environments not only through post-shift evaluations but also via embedded analytics, usage 
data, and AI-generated insights. These studies might also investigate how such feedback 
mechanisms can support chefs’ decision-making, prevent bottlenecks, and improve 
sustainability outcomes. 
Third, there is a need for more longitudinal studies that trace the evolution of digital adoption 
over time. While this study captured two intense weeks of use, digital transformation is often a 
slow and layered process. Longer-term engagement with teams could shed light on habit 
formation, resistance, and the shifting role of leadership in sustaining or reshaping adoption. 
6.5.3 Practical Implications 
For restaurant owners, digital designers, and policymakers, this thesis offers several takeaways. 
First, the success of digital tools in kitchens depends not only on functionality but also on 
cultural legitimacy. Practical adoption is shaped by leadership behavior, peer modeling, and 
daily routines. Design teams should therefore co-create tools with chefs from multiple ranks 
and roles not only during prototyping but throughout the implementation journey. 
==PAGE:79==
79 
 
Second, tools must be adaptable to the high-pressure, sensory-rich nature of kitchen work. 
Features like real-time task editing, recipe linking, and hands-free interaction are not merely 
conveniences; they are necessary to prevent breakdowns under stress. Tech developers should 
approach kitchens not as static workflows but as living systems, where priorities shift rapidly 
and attention is often fragmented. 
Third, this thesis reinforces the potential of kitchens as data-rich environments. If properly 
implemented, digital tools like Remi could help track errors, identify workflow inefficiencies, 
and support sustainability monitoring in ways that are currently impossible with analog 
systems. For this potential to be realized, however, structured feedback and digital routines must 
become part of the kitchen’s daily fabric not just temporary add-ons. 
This thesis began with a simple question: what happens when digital tools enter the heat and 
rhythm of a professional kitchen? Through close observation, user engagement, and theoretical 
reflection, it became clear that technology alone does not drive transformation, people do. The 
case of Remi shows that digital adoption in hospitality hinges on trust, routines, and cultural 
alignment just as much as it does on technical design. As kitchens continue to modernize, the 
challenge will not only be to invent smarter tools, but to foster environments in which those 
tools are meaningfully and sustainably used. This thesis contributes a small but focused step 
toward that future.

Appendix A: Field Observation Guide 
A.1 Overview and Purpose 
This guide outlines the procedures for conducting rigorous and ethical participant observation 
in restaurant kitchens testing the Remi app. It aligns with socio-technical theory, interpretivist 
methodology, and feedback from field supervisors. 
A.2 Observation Method Used: Participant Observation 
The researcher will actively participate in kitchen activities to build trust, access tacit 
knowledge, and observe digital tool integration in context. 
Tasks may include: 
• 
Assisting with food prep 
• 
Supporting service flow 
• 
Observing Remi usage during handovers and post-shift reviews 
A.3 Structured Documentation Dimensions 
Each observation entry will contain the following dimensions: 
Element Description 
Who 
Roles of people involved (e.g., sous chef, dishwasher) 
What 
Specific behaviors or events 
When 
Time of day or relative shift point 
Where Physical kitchen location 
Why 
Relevance or impact on workflow or team dynamics 
How 
Method of communication, coordination, or app interaction 
A dual-column note format will be used: one for factual detail, one for interpretive reflection. 
A.4 Observation Focus Areas 
• 
Use of the Remi app (access points, hesitations, adaptation) 
• 
Communication around task assignment and updates 
• 
Cultural signals: jokes, resistance, leadership behavior 
• 
Lean indicators: time-saving strategies, workarounds 
• 
Points of digital vs. manual conflict (e.g., reminders, planning) 
==PAGE:87==
87 
 
A.5 Technology in Observation 
Devices are not neutral—they shape the work environment. Observations will track: 
• 
When and how tech is introduced into the workflow 
• 
Whether the team uses the app voluntarily or only when prompted 
• 
How Remi supports or disrupts flow 
This approach treats tech as part of the system and not a separate layer. 
A.6 Researcher Role & Reflexivity 
Reflexivity will be ongoing. The researcher will: 
• 
Log emotional and positional responses (e.g., discomfort, identification) 
• 
Reflect on potential biases and power dynamics 
• 
Use memoing to process learning and adjust field focus 
A.7 Ethics and Integrity 
• 
Full transparency with teams about research intent 
• 
Consent obtained for observation and note-taking 
• 
No photos or recordings unless explicitly approved 
• 
Confidentiality maintained in all outputs
