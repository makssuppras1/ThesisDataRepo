Abstract
As onshore wind energy keeps on growing, a prospective shift in development of wind farms from
ﬂat to complex terrains can be foreseen. This is a direct impact of saturation, as ideal ﬂat terrains
are limited in existence and majority of them are already occupied. This motivates the need for un-
derstanding how wind turbines behave in a complex terrain. Speciﬁcally, the interaction between
turbines and terrain is of great importance, as the ﬂow in a complex terrain is strongly affected by
the inhomogeneous topography of a particular terrain. In addition, wind turbine wakes are recog-
nized as one of the most complicated ﬂow phenomenon generated by the turbines and responsible
for major power losses in a wind farm.
In the current study, we examine the interaction between the wake generated by a wind turbine
and complex terrain. The idea is to get a basic understanding of how wind turbine wake behaves in
a complex terrain inside a computational domain. A double ridge conﬁgured complex site named
Perdig˜ao located in Portugal is chosen for this study. A domain of size 5120 × 2560 × 3000 m3
is simulated using Large-Eddy Simulation, where, turbines are modelled using an actuator disk
model. The atmosphere is kept neutrally stratiﬁed. Moreover, the effects of Coriolis force and
buoyancy are also neglected. It is to be mentioned here that this thesis is part of a collaborative
research effort and focused on analyzing the data harvested from LES; carrying out the simulations
is not part of this work.
As the ﬂow around a complex terrain is a strong function of the terrain itself, we employ a compar-
ative mode of analysis to characterize this terrain dependence. For this purpose, we study terrain
with two different levels of complexity. In addition, the turbine location and number is also kept
variable - with the top of the two ridges chosen as turbine positions. Finally, in the context of the
computational setup, we note the shortcomings in the current LES setup focusing mainly on the
grid resolution.
Obtained results show signiﬁcant differences in velocity proﬁles from a ﬂat terrain, including the
speed up across the ridges, ﬂow separation behind the ridges and faster wake recovery due to higher
mixing promoted by higher ambient turbulence. The two complex terrains are observed to show
similar velocity proﬁles with the steeper terrain showing higher turbulence generation. The turbine
located on the second ridge is observed to be greatly affected by the wake of the ﬁrst ridge but has
no impact from the upstream turbine due to the large distance and faster wake recovery. The grid
resolution affects the velocity speed up across the ridge, showing a 15% difference at the hub height
of the turbine on top of the ﬁrst ridge. The suspected reason for this difference is also discussed.
Moreover, instantaneous data at speciﬁc points in wake is used to visualize the energy spectra,
as well as, to visualize the probability distributions of velocity ﬂuctuations at two different time
scales.

Chapter 1
Introduction
World energy needs have been on a continuous rise since the industrial revolution. The annual
energy outlook 2017 predicts a 28% increase in the global energy consumption between the years
2015 and 2040. However, with petroleum and other liquid fuels boasting the highest share in pro-
viding for energy demand of the world, renewable energy is identiﬁed as the fastest growing energy
source of the world [1].
This prospective shift towards renewable energy can be associated to the following major issues
with the conventional energy sources. For one thing, the Earth possesses a limited supply of fossil
fuels, which means we will run out of these resources at some point in the future. Shaﬁee & Topal
evaluated that Earth today has fossil fuel reserves for up to 2112 with coal being the only one avail-
able after 2042 [2]. The other major issue and arguably the biggest challenge facing us today is
the impact of fossil fuel consumption on enhanced release of carbon dioxide and other hazardous
gases like oxides and dioxides of sulphur and nitrogen in the free atmosphere. The alarmingly high
levels of these gases, alternatively known as greenhouse gases, give rise to the phenomenon known
as global warming. The immediate impact of global warming on our atmosphere can be observed
from abnormal rise in the sea levels, increase in mean Earth temperatures, and depletion of fresh
water sources.
Preserving the atmosphere and reversing the impact of global warming while fulﬁlling the world
energy needs in a sustainable manner are the chief reasons which make clean renewable energy
sources an attractive alternative to the conventional methods of harvesting energy from fossil fuels.
While wind and solar energy are the ﬂag bearers of renewable energy sources, they also face chal-
lenges which go beyond their ﬁnancial feasibility and social acceptance. The inherent intermittent
nature of these resources requires a much efﬁcient integration of energy from different sources in
order to ensure a consistent supply meeting the demand. Moreover, the strong variability in the
performance of these sources depending on the site speciﬁc conditions presents another challenge.
This not only creates a distinction between the sites based on quality, but also, demands for a much
deeper understanding of a wide selection of variables governing energy yield of a speciﬁc site.
Turning our focus towards wind energy; it can be identiﬁed as the energy source of present &
future, with a huge role to play in our journey towards a sustainable and clean environment. Wind
energy has been undergoing a continuous growth since the dawn of this century. The global wind
energy report 2016 indicates a global installed capacity of 486 GW by the end of 2016, with more
than 50 GW of wind power added each year since 2014 (see ﬁgure 1.1). To add to this, being the

Wind energy offers the possibility to be installed both off and on-shore. Offshore environment of-
fers relatively better wind conditions due to persistently stable atmospheric conditions, low rough-
ness length of water, resulting in higher wind speeds and lower ambient turbulence when compared
to onshore conditions. However, due to high installation and maintenance costs in offshore condi-
tions, onshore wind energy holds the title for being the cheapest renewable energy source currently
available to mankind [5].
The performance of wind turbines placed in a wind farm is affected by a number of factors de-
pending on ambient atmospheric conditions, as well as, on individual and collective interaction
of wind turbines with the free atmosphere and terrain in which they are located. One of the key
factors and a major source of power losses in wind farms is the wake effect generated by turbines.
Wake can be deﬁned as the region of velocity deﬁcit and enhanced turbulence generated in the
downstream of a turbine. This affects the performance of turbines located in wake in two ways:
ﬁrstly, the reduced wind speed in the wake means lesser power production by the in-wake turbine.
Secondly, higher turbulence means an increase in mechanical loads such as fatigue loads on the
turbine standing in wake. Generation of wake behind a turbine can be associated with two key
features of a turbine: 1) the blocking effect of a turbine in the form of a forward thrust force, as
it is essentially a structure placed in the path of free air; 2) vortical wake structures formed due to
rotation of wind turbine blades, e.g. root and tip blade vortices.
The sheer complexity of the topic and its crucial importance in understanding interaction between
turbines, atmosphere and terrain, as well as, in resource assessment studies for wind energy site
planning has led it to become focus of a wide range of research from analytical and experimental to

1.1.1
Wake modelling
As mentioned earlier, wake regions are characterized by velocity deﬁcit and enhanced turbulence
generation resulting in power losses and increased mechanical loads on the turbines located in the
downstream. Two regions of interest in wake studies are known as near and far wake regions. The
near wake is the region in the immediate vicinity of turbine, where the impact of rotor geometry
is dominant. This region is also marked by high gradients of velocity and pressure ﬁelds, as well
as, expansion in wake width [6]. The far wake region is located further downstream and enhanced
turbulence is the dominating feature of this region [7].
Historically, a lot of work has been done on modelling and capturing wakes on different levels
of complexity. The following list categorizes some popular wake models in the increasing order of
complexity and therefore computational cost:
1. Empirical models e.g. Jensen Model
2. Linearized Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) e.g. Fuga
3. Other models e.g. Larsen model or Dynamic Wake Meandering model

4. Nonlinear RANS e.g. RANS coupled with two equation turbulence model and turbine model
5. Large-eddy simulation with a turbine model
Empirical models
These models earn their popularity from their relative simplicity and practicality in engineering
applications. One of the earliest and most popular of such models is the Jensen wake model [8, 9].
This model assumes a top-hat uniform inﬂow velocity proﬁle and the wake expansion is related
to downstream distance in a linear fashion (see ﬁgure 1.2). The mass ﬂow between rotor and
downstream plane is given by continuity.
Figure 1.2: Wake development as per control volume technique employed by Jensen model (source:
[6]).
There have been many implementations of Jensen model over the time, one important application
is the Park Model, which is implemented in WAsP and takes into account the effect of wakes from
multiple turbines [10]. Different variations of this model also include impact of wake decay and
different mechanisms of wake generation in a wind farm.
Another model worth mentioning is the inﬁnite wind farm boundary layer model developed in 1992
by Frandsen [11]. This model takes into account the wake effect by a deformation in the velocity
proﬁle across the rotor plane. This is exhibited in ﬁgure 1.3.
Linearized RANS
Fuga is a useful engineering tool developed for studying wakes which is based on linearized RANS
[12]. It utilizes quasi-steady RANS equations with a surface layer closure. The linearization of
RANS equations is achieved by employing Taylor expansion and disregarding the terms with order
higher than one. This enables the use of Fourier transformation, facilitating the process further.
Fuga is commonly used in combination with WAsP in site assessment studies, especially, for off-
shore sites.

Other models
This category includes models which are more advanced than linearized RANS, but, are not based
on fully nonlinear RANS equations. Two notable models in this category are: 1) Larsen model
[13, 14] and 2) dynamic wake meandering model [15].
The Larsen model is based on axis-symmetric RANS, where, a shear-layer approximation is im-
plemented. Prandtl’s mixing length model is employed to close the system and pressure term is
neglected for further simpliﬁcation. An improved version of this model presented in 2009 includes
changes in boundary conditions and an approach to model multiple wake scenarios.
The dynamic wake meandering model, captures wake characteristics based on three ﬂow char-
acteristics: 1) velocity deﬁcit, 2) turbulence generated by rotor and 3) wake meandering.
Nonlinear RANS
This approach utilizes the elliptic Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes equations with turbulence
models such as two equation models. The wind turbine is modelled using actuator disk or line
models or fully resolved turbines. An example of such models is its current implementation in
DTU’s in-house CFD code EllipSys3D. The turbine rotor is parameterized using an actuator disk
model [16]. The code can be coupled with a number of turbulence models, of which the latest
implementation is a k −ε −fp model [17]. This model enables good prediction of velocity deﬁcit
in near wake region when compared to the traditional k −ε model.
Large-eddy simulation
Large-eddy simulation offers the most detailed and accurate look into the wake phenomenon on
expense of high computational costs. Large-eddy simulation runs a spatial ﬁlter, where, the large
scales of ﬂow are directly resolved using ﬁltered Navier-Stokes equations. The smaller (ﬁltered)
scales are parameterized using appropriate sub-grid scale models. Turbines models are similar to
the ones used in RANS approach described in the above section.

1.1.2
Flow over complex terrain
Understanding ﬂow over complex terrain has been a topic of analytical, experimental and numerical
research for over four decades now. This section presents an overview of highlighted research on
the topic in the last 40 years. The review is further divided into three parts concerning experimental,
analytical & numerical works respectively.
Experimental work
The experimental studies carried out on complex terrain ﬂows can be further sub-divided in two
categories: 1) ﬁeld measurement campaigns and 2) wind tunnel studies.
Mason and Sykes performed ﬁeld measurements over a circular and isolated hill in England and at-
tempted to extend the Jackson-Hunt 2D theory [20] to the three dimensional case, reaching a good
agreement between theory and measurements [21]. Sacr´e studied ﬂow over a hill with gentle slope
and found that closer to the surface overspeed is affected comparably by both local topography
and mean hill slope [22]. Another series of studies under British Meteorological Ofﬁce featured
ﬁeld observations around different hills such as Black Mountain, Bungendore ridge and Nyland
hill [23, 24, 25]. These studies focused on studying the overspeeding and turbulence characteris-
tics across the hill, with an attempt to relate them with theoretical studies and with thermal stability.
Over the past 40 years, the Askervein hill study has emerged as a classical benchmark for ﬂow
over complex terrain. The ﬁeld experiment was carried out in two phases and involved measure-
ments with more than 50 meteorological masts of varying heights acquiring a total of 150 hours of
data. Details of the study are given in [26]. Mickle et. al. presented results from the study showing
vertical proﬁles of wind speed and turbulence across the hill and compared with numerical predic-
tions [27]. Walsmley and Taylor used the Askervein hill case to study growth of boundary layer
and impact of changes in roughness length [28]. The Askervein hill project has been a classical
testing benchmark for analytical and numerical models for complex terrain studies [29].
However, an extensive ﬁeld measurement campaign carried out at the Bolund peninsula in Roskilde,
Denmark in 2007-2008 has now become a new benchmark to validate numerical models attempt-
ing to capture complex terrain ﬂows [29, 30]. Although, the height of Bolund hill is just 12 m, the
isolated location and absence of signiﬁcant thermal effects and Coriolis force facilitate the possibil-
ity for up-scaling in the neutral atmosphere [31]. The study found dramatic increase in turbulence
intensity over the escarpment. This provided basis for the argument suggesting ﬂow models able
to at least capture important temporal and spatial scales of turbulence should be used for numerical
investigation of the ﬂow around complex terrains. A comparison of results from ﬁfty seven differ-
ent studies for Bolund hill showed a wide variation of results and thus exposing limits of current
models in predicting ﬂow around complex terrains [29].
Turning towards wind tunnel studies of complex terrain ﬂows, Pearse et. al. conducted wind tunnel
study over two dimensional bell-shaped, sinusoidal and triangular hills and found that intermediate
slope hills showed highest mean velocities instead of steepest hills [32]. The idea of studying two
dimensional hills was further propagated by Britter et. al. They studied the speed up in velocity

dimensional hill [33]. Regarding the Askervein project, Teunissen et. al. simulated Askervein

parameters on results [34]. Gong et. al. and Finnigan et. al. further enhanced the study of ﬂow

dimensional hills have been studied by several groups under different stability conditions [37, 38].
Analytical work
The ﬁeld of analytical solutions to ﬂow over complex terrain was pioneered by Jackson and Hunt,
who proposed an analytical model attempting to relate hill size and shape to variation in velocity
and turbulence across the hill [20]. This model was frequently used in following years in order
to compare experimental data with the model predictions. This also included several attempts to
extend the theory to three dimensional hills.
Sykes also presented a theory to predict ﬂow around an aerodynamically smooth two dimensional
hill in the limit of high Reynolds number [39]. Moreover, Hunt presented a solution of turbulent
ﬂow around two and three dimensional hills [40].
These studies, however, are presented for low hills (having a small slope) and are linear in na-
ture. This results in their inability to take into account the complex nonlinear phenomena, such as
ﬂow separation, happening in realistic complex terrains with much steeper slopes.
Numerical work
In order to account for nonlinear effects, numerical tools have been developed over time with vary-
ing levels of complexity and accuracy. The ﬁrst of such numerical studies was carried out by Taylor
and Gent, who developed a numerical model based on mixing length turbulence closure [41]. A
limitation of this model was its inability to simulate ﬂow separation. Following this, Deaves for-
mulated ﬂow equations in terms of two variables and solved in them in a code based on ﬁnite
difference scheme [42]. He regarded his approach as much more general than previous works, due
to its ability to model any slender hindrance to ﬂow.
The widely implemented technique of terrain following coordinate system was ﬁrst utilized in
the context of complex terrain ﬂows by Clark [43]. He developed a ﬁnite difference code to simu-
late ﬂow over small hills and complemented it with his derivation of governing equations. Further
works in this area include numerical approach of Zeman and Jensen [44]. They developed a nu-
merical model to simulate turbulence characteristics over a two dimensional hill and compared the
numerical results for Askervein case with experimental observations.
Numerical modelling of atmospheric ﬂows using RANS has been another topic of interest over
the last 30 years. It offers ﬂow solution at a much more cheaper cost than LES. However, the use
of RANS in ABL ﬂows has faced certain crucial issues. Firstly, use of RANS with standard two
equation turbulence closures has been reported to predict incorrect streamwise gradients [45, 46].
In addition, choice of the speciﬁc turbulence model also affects the results predicted by the simu-
lations. k −ε model is the most widely used model in this regard and the issues with prediction of
ﬂow using this model (mainly an undesired dissipation of energy) are well known. Several studies

1.1.3
Wake interaction in terrain
Study of wake interaction in a terrain can be sub-divided in two parts: 1) ﬂat and 2) complex
terrain studies. In this regard, an overview of past studies based on experimental work, as well as,
numerical studies is presented.
Flat terrain studies
Robert and Walker performed one of the earliest ﬁeld measurements on full scale large horizontal
axis wind turbines (HAWTs) [55]. They measured wake ﬂow in stable conditions and wake deﬁcits
at 9D downstream distance. In addition, they performed validation of results with two wake models
reaching a good degree of agreement.
Wind tunnel studies, on the other hand, have been the prevailing experimental tool in studying wind
turbine wakes in atmospheric conditions. Builtjes performed the earliest of such studies using a
static simulator [56]. Ross and Ainslie studied the interaction between wakes of multiple turbines
such as in a wind farm using laser Doppler annemometry inside a wind tunnel [57]. Milborrow and
Ross studied wake interaction focusing on the impact of ambient turbulence on wake characteris-
tics [58]. Medici and Alfredson performed wake measurements using hot wire annemometry [59].
They resolved three velocity components to study vortex shedding and found that the phenomenon
is similar to one found in bluff bodies. Following this, Chamorro and Port´e-Agel studied the effects
of boundary layer turbulence on wind turbine wakes and found wake shows a non-axisymmetric
behavior due to non uniform inﬂow [60].
More recently, ﬁeld measurements of turbine wakes using Lidars are carried out showing strong
ﬂuctuations in ﬂow in the near-wake around the turbine top [61]. Further, lidar measurements and
wind tunnel measurements are used for wake characterization showing faster recovery in convec-
tive condition in comparison to neutral stability [62].
Turning toward numerical simulations, two techniques based on RANS and LES, respectively,
have been widely implemented. RANS offers an attractive numerical approach to solving atmo-

numerical studies mostly used RANS for modelling. Xu and Sankar proposed a hybrid Navier

become a popular tool to study wake interaction with terrain. Jimenez et. al. developed an LES
code and modeled turbines using concentrated drag forces [67]. They compared results of simu-
lation in ﬂat terrain with experimental data, obtaining good agreement between the two data sets.
Subsequently, LES studies of cluster of wind turbines have been carried out in recent years. Calaf
et. al. performed LES of fully developed boundary layers due to wind turbine arrays [68]. Port´e-

experimental tests [69]. Best agreement between experiment and simulations was obtained for tur-
bine models which induce rotational effects of turbines. Abkar et. al. used large-eddy simulation to
study change in structure of turbine wakes due to the inherent diurnal cycle [70]. Finally, Allaerts
and Meyers studied the affect of height and strength of inversion layer on performance of a wind
turbine array using LES [71].
Complex terrain studies
Although the earliest studies on interaction of wind turbine wakes in complex terrain were doc-
umented in the 1990s, the topic didn’t gain much attention from researchers until recent years,
especially, when compared to its counter-part: ﬂat terrain. Part of the reason for this apparent dis-
parity is the way scientiﬁc research works. Our understanding of physics begins from the simplest
scenarios and different layers of complexity are then added gradually to the existing work. Through
continual exploration of wakes in ﬂat terrain, we today have a (nearly) complete understanding of
the situation in ﬂat terrain and researchers are now focusing on the issue in complex terrain.
The ﬁrst study on wind turbine wakes in complex topography was performed using wind tun-
nel experiments by Taylor and Smith who used static turbine models on a 2D ﬂat top hill to study
boundary layer ﬂow [72]. Major result from their study was the delay in ﬂow separation due to
presence of turbines in the system. Hemon et. al. took a numerical approach to study dynamics
of wind turbines in a complex terrain [73]. They applied the vortex method to calculate vorticity
generated by rotor blades and modelled hill by induced velocity computed from a straight vortex
ﬁlament. Voutsianas et. al. considered the curvature in streamlines using a velocity correction term
[74]. Further work in the 90s was done by Helmis et. al., who carried out measurements in the
near-wake region of a turbine in complex turbine [75].
Moving on to more recent years, Lee et. al. performed numerical simulation of ﬂow through a
wind farm using RANS with k −ε model in complex site located in Korea [76]. They studied ﬂow

2.1
Numerical Simulation of Turbulent Flows
Turbulent ﬂows are characterized by three dimensional eddies evolving in time and space and span-
ning over a wide range of scales with an inherent chaotic nature. In three dimensional turbulence,
the phenomenological description proposed by Richardson gives a naive insight into evolution of
these eddies (ﬁgure 2.1) [84]. According to this phenomenology, energy is added into the system
in the form of large eddies (L) which then break into smaller eddies. These smaller eddies then
break into even smaller eddies and the process continues until a scale (η) is reached where viscous
effects become dominant and energy is ﬁnally dissipated in the form of heat.

at which it is dissipated is obtained by looking at the energy spectrum. In the case of three dimen-

can be associated with the integral and dissipation length scales of ﬂow - which are the largest and
smallest scales in the ﬂow respectively. This Reynolds number dependency is given by:

2.2
Large-Eddy Simulation Framework
LES is a high-ﬁdelity approach to turbulence simulation, which is cheaper than the DNS but much
more expensive than the RANS approach. However, a major advantage of LES over RANS is its
ability to resolve the ﬂow in space and time. The concept of energy spectrum is crucial to the dis-
cussion of LES. The fundamental idea behind LES is that the largest scales in the ﬂow are the ones
containing energy and thus are important to the turbulent energy transport. These (large) scales of
the ﬂow are directly resolved using a ﬁltered form of Navier-Stokes equations. The smaller (non-
resolved) scales cannot be simply ignored as energy has to be dissipated somewhere in the system,
for which dissipation scales have to be taken into consideration. These small scales are, therefore,
parameterized using suitable models called sub-grid scale models (SGS models). The resolution of
grid is another important parameter in the discussion of LES, as it is commonly used to deﬁne the
cutoff between resolved and parameterized scales. By increasing the grid resolution, more scales
of ﬂow can be resolved at an expense of higher computational cost.

2.2.5
Numerical schemes
This section presents an overview of some chief numerical characteristics of the code, including
spatial and time discretization and numerical scheme for pressure solution.
Spatial discretization
The computational space is simulated by estimating spatial derivatives using a combination of
pseudo-spectral and ﬁnite difference schemes. The horizontal (ξ,γ) derivatives are computed us-
ing pseudo-spectral method, whereas, second-order centred ﬁnite difference scheme is employed
in the vertical (ζ) direction, as described in [90].
Key solution variables ( ˜ui, p∗,θ,e) are collocated at cell centers to build a compact differencing
formation. To ensure tight coupling of velocity and pressure variables, contravirant ﬂux velocity
components (U,V) are located on cell centers and (W) on cell faces.
Time marching
An explicit Runge-Kutta scheme of third order with dynamic time stepping operating at a constant
Courant-Fredrichs-Lewy number is utilized for forward time marching. The CFL number chosen
in the current study is 0.5.
Poisson equation
A major challenge in the numerical scheme is to formulate and solve the Poisson equation for
pressure while maintaining consistency with the incompressible ﬂow equations under Boussinesq
approximation. Equation 2.15 states the Poisson equation for pressure. However, this equation
cannot be solved directly and therefore, it is solved in an iterative manner by employing equation
2.12, which gives the following scheme:

2.3
Turbine Parameterization
The effect of wind turbine on the ﬂow can be parameterized using various models, which vary on
the level of detail with which they capture the ﬂow interaction with turbine. Three most commonly
used models for this purpose are brieﬂy introduced here, along with the details of the model imple-
mented in the current study.
The simplest model to represent the turbine induced forces is an actuator-disk model as imple-
mented by Froude on momentum theory for propellers [16]. This model assumes the load to be
uniformly distributed over the rotor area. The effect of the turbine is taken into consideration with
an axial thrust force in the direction opposite to the direction of ﬂow. No rotational force compo-
nent is considered in the model and therefore it is commonly referred to as ‘actuator disk model
with no rotation’ [69].
An improvement over the previous turbine model is the ‘actuator disk model with rotation’. This
model utilizes the blade-element momentum theory [94] to account for the lift and drag forces
induced by the turbine, which are then integrated over the rotor disk. The rotational effects intro-
duced by turbine rotation and non-uniform distribution of forces over rotor area are also captured
==PAGE:32==
20
CHAPTER 2. PROBLEM DEFINITION & METHODOLOGY
utilizing this model.
The ﬁnal model in this category is the ‘actuator line model’. As described above, the actuator
disk models integrate forces over the whole rotor area and thus do not capture the helicoidal vor-
tical structures generated at blade tip and root. The actuator line model captures these structures
by employing the BEM theory to compute lift and drag forces and distributes them on lines repre-
senting the blades. This model is speciﬁcally useful when loading on turbine is under consideration.
A comparison of three models described above shows that the actuator disk model with no ro-
tation overestimates the velocity at wake center and underestimates turbulence intensity at turbine
top in the near wake region [69, 95, 96]. The actuator disk model with rotation and actuator line
models, on the other hand, show good agreement with measurement data. Despite this fact, the
actuator disk model with no rotation is still the most widely used turbine model, especially, in the
studies focusing on turbine generated wakes [83, 18, 3]. This is due to the simplicity of the model
and the fact that associated differences are small and limited to the near wake region. In addition,
the computational costs associated with the actuator line or actuator disk with rotation models are
quite higher than the actuator disk model without rotation. Moreover, the actuator line model is
extremely useful for studying loads on wind turbine blades and to study unsteady wake dynam-
ics. For studies focusing on stationary ﬂow dynamics and wake interaction with terrain or other
turbines, actuator disk models give satisfactory ﬂow estimates.

Where indicated, a normalization of computed ﬁelds is performed with the value at a ﬁxed reference

2.7.2
Upstream ﬂow conditions
An investigation of vertical velocity proﬁles at different upstream locations is done to characterize
inﬂow velocity conditions. Figure 2.12 shows normalized velocity proﬁles at various upstream lo-
cations. For the purpose of comparison, velocity proﬁles for the two (steep and smooth) cases are
plotted together with a log-law proﬁle and ﬂat terrain case. The log-law proﬁle is evaluated using a
u∗value of 0.3 m/s. This value is smaller than the value of 0.6 m/s speciﬁed earlier while evaluating
the driving pressure gradient. This is explained by the complexity of terrain and the fact that the
pressure gradient is no more balanced only by u2
∗/H (as would be in ﬂat terrain). Instead, there is
an additional contribution from the form drag generated by the presence of ridges, primarily due
to the pressure difference across the ridges. This additional pressure gradient reduces the friction
velocity. However, it must be mentioned here that the proﬁles are insensitive to the chosen value
of friction velocity due to normalization with a reference velocity.
The velocity proﬁles show a good agreement in terms of quantitative values. However, the slope
of simulated proﬁles is different from log-law for complex terrain cases. This can be explained
by the fact that the log-law is ideally applicable in a ﬂat terrain, whereas, in the complex terrain,
deviations can be expected. In addition, periodicity of boundary conditions in horizontal direction
also affects the ﬂow conditions in the upstream and may contribute to the observed deviations.
Moreover, the grid resolution also has an impact on the captured velocity proﬁles and a relatively
coarser grid (20 m) is expected to show more deviations than a ﬁner grid.
2.7.3

An important parameter to quantify the Gaussian/non-Gaussian nature of velocity ﬂuctuations is
to plot the probability density function of the velocity ﬂuctuations at different time scales. For

constructed. Two time scales used in the current study correspond to the smallest and largest time
scales in the ﬂow. The smallest time scale is taken to be the average time difference between two
consecutive velocity computations and largest time scale is taken to be the through time of ﬂow i.e.
17 minutes.

Chapter 3
Results & Discussion
This chapter documents results from the study along with a discussion on governing ﬂow physics.
The results presented are further categorized into three main divisions:
1. Ensemble averaged ﬂow ﬁelds
2. Single point turbulence characteristics
3. Self-similarity of wind turbine wake
As the terrain is complex, it is crucial to identify the sensitivity of results to the speciﬁcs of a
particular case setup. In this regard, terrains with varying levels of complexity, as well as, vary-
ing location of turbine are compared with each other and key differences in the ﬂow physics are
highlighted. In addition, as LES works on a spatial averaging ﬁlter, the sensitivity of results to a
particular ﬁlter width is also analyzed.
3.1
Ensemble Averaged Flow Fields
Ensemble averaged ﬂow ﬁelds are studied to understand the stationary ﬂow characteristics. Main
parameters under consideration are: streamwise velocity component, turbulent kinetic energy pro-
duction, SGS energy contribution and axial induction factor. The two dimensional plane in x and z
directions passing through the turbine location in y direction, referred to as the ‘main transect’, is
mainly focused for this study.
3.1.1

In order to facilitate the comparative mode of study, the steep case with 20 m horizontal grid
resolution and a single turbine on top of the ﬁrst ridge is chosen as a base or reference case. Main
ﬂow characteristics for this case are described in this section.

3.1.2
Terrain complexity
Unlike a ﬂat terrain, the ﬂow characteristics around a complex terrain are affected by the speciﬁc
details of the terrain itself. This, in turn, is also expected to inﬂuence the wake characteristics of
a turbine. The impact of terrain complexity on wake behavior is studied by smoothing the terrain
further through the spatial exponential ﬁlter described in section 2.4. This relatively smooth terrain
is compared with the reference terrain presented in the previous section. The two complex terrain
cases are additionally compared with an ideal ﬂat terrain case. The horizontal grid resolution for
this comparison is 20 m.
A comparison of velocity ﬁelds between the two complex terrain cases around the ﬁrst ridge shows
a slightly higher speed up in the smoother terrain (ﬁgure 3.7). Pearse et. al. [32] found simi-
lar results where hills having intermediate slopes showed higher mean velocities than the steepest
hills. This can be explained by the fact that the wake from the ridge starts at the same point for
the two cases, however, the smooth case has a wider peak which causes the wake to grow slightly
higher than the steep case. This in turn gives rise to a bigger low pressure region on the lee-side
of the ridge in smooth case, slightly enhancing the velocity speed up. In addition, the ridges are
asymmetric and non-Gaussian, which also has an impact on velocity distribution across the ridge.
Focusing on the wake from the turbine, a major difference between the two cases is the sudden
upward shift in the orientation of wake in far wake region for smooth case. This shift is purely
due to the shear generated by the ﬂow separation region behind the ridge. As the separation region
grows higher than the ridge top, it causes an initially straight wake to shift in an upward direction.
Flow characteristics across the ridge
The ﬂow across the ridge for the two complex terrains is almost the same with the smooth case
showing slightly higher values. Starting from turbine location (G), a velocity deﬁcit is observed.
This velocity deﬁcit is equal for the two cases at the turbine position, however, the wake recovery
is slightly faster in the steep case than the smooth one. An important thing to notice is the relative
position of velocity deﬁcit regions with respect to the position of turbine rotor. It can be observed
that the smooth case shows a higher upward shift in the velocity deﬁcit (wake) region than steep
case. To visualize things from the perspective of a ﬂat terrain, respective velocity proﬁles from
the ﬂat terrain case are also added. Signiﬁcant difference from ﬂat terrain can be seen in complex
terrain cases. This includes the speed up across the ridge, the appearance of recirculation region
in complex terrain and the upward shift in the velocity deﬁcit region. The magnitude of velocity
deﬁcit is also higher in complex terrain cases than the corresponding ﬂat case (ﬁgure 3.8).
The total turbulent kinetic energy for different cases is plotted in ﬁgure 3.9. Comparing total
TKE in complex terrains without turbine shows similar trend, except in far wake, where the steep
case shows a higher peak than the smooth terrain. Once the turbine is added to the ﬂow, a change
in total TKE is observed, with steep terrain showing a higher production of turbulence than smooth
from turbine position to the far wake region. It is also observed for both cases that in the near wake
region, the TKE produced by the turbine is higher than that by the ridge. The roles are reversed in
the far wake, with TKE produced by the ridge being higher than that of the turbine. Comparison
with the ﬂat terrain case shows that the TKE produced by the turbine itself in complex terrain is
signiﬁcantly smaller than the ﬂat case. This shows that the complex terrain suppresses the produc-
tion of TKE in wind turbine wake.

of second turbine is unaffected by the ﬁrst turbine. However, lesser TKE across the major part
of the second turbine’s rotor in the presence of the ﬁrst turbine means lesser loading across the
rotor. Thus, putting a turbine on the ﬁrst ridge can actually prove beneﬁcial for the second turbine.
However, drawing a strong conclusion at this point can be misleading and this point will be further
elaborated in the next section.
As was shown earlier in ﬁgure 3.14 (b), the TKE production due to the second turbine is much
smaller. This is complimented from ﬁgure 3.16, which shows a higher TKE for the second turbine
just at rotor top in the near wake region. As we move further downstream, the TKE production is
almost same across the rotor top. The TKE production in the wake of the ridge itself is also lesser
in the presence of two turbines for all locations under consideration.
This is in contrast to what is observed for TKE production across ﬁrst ridge, where, TKE due
to the ridge is same with and without the turbine for the considered locations. This can be due to
the reduced TKE by the ﬁrst ridge in the presence of a turbine, which causes a reduction in the
ambient turbulence level.
Following this, velocity ﬁelds with and without the turbine are compared. Figure 3.18 shows the
proﬁles of axial induction factor for the two turbines. The two proﬁles seem to match quite well
for all locations which is justiﬁed by the fact that the two turbines are located in the same terrain
under same atmospheric conditions.
Finally, the development of wake in a plane parallel to rotor plane for the two turbines is shown
in ﬁgure 3.19. Higher velocity levels can be seen for ﬁrst turbine than second turbine. Moreover,
the wake recovery is much faster for the second turbine than the ﬁrst turbine. The strength of
separation region is also higher for the ﬁrst turbine.

As mentioned before, LES works on a spatial ﬁlter, the width of which is a function of grid reso-

are resolved i.e. the contribution of SGS energy to total TKE is reduced. In order to quantify the
impact of grid resolution on resolved and parameterized scales, we deﬁne the following quantity:
TKEgridratio = TKE20m −TKE10m
TKEre f,20m
(3.1)
where, TKEgrid ratio quantiﬁes the normalized difference between ratio of SGS energy to total
TKE of the two grid resolutions. In addition, TKE20m & TKE10m denote the respective ratios of
SGS to total energy for 20 and 10 meter resolutions. Figure 3.20 shows the computed difference
ﬁeld. As the grid resolution in the horizontal direction is unequal, linear interpolation is used on the
coarser grid to makes the two ﬁelds compatible. From the computed ﬁelds it is observed that the
difference is higher than zero for most part of the domain, with higher values closer to the ground.
This shows that SGS contribution in 20 meter resolution is higher than the 10 meter case.
Flow characteristics across the ridge
To compare the ﬂow characteristics of the ﬁrst turbine, velocity and total TKE proﬁles across the
ﬁrst ridge are plotted in ﬁgures 3.21 & 3.22 respectively. It is clear that the resolved velocity in the
ﬁner grid is higher than the coarser grid. As the grid resolution is increased, the terrain also gets
ﬁner and thus changes slightly. This is why the rotor positions are not exactly on top of each other
at certain locations. The wake recovery seems to be a bit slower in the case of ﬁner grid resolution.
While, some of the observed difference in velocity proﬁles can be due to the change in the terrain
itself, this also somewhat indicates the inability of the coarser grid to fully resolve the velocity

proﬁle. To elaborate further, consider the balance between pressure gradient, friction velocity and
form drag. As the terrain is more detailed and slightly modiﬁed in the ﬁner grid, the contribution
of form drag is higher than the coarser grid. This causes a decrease in the friction velocity in the
ﬁner grid than the coarser one. As was mentioned in the section 2.7.2, a u∗value of 0.3 m/s gives
the closest ﬁt between the log proﬁle and the inﬂow in the LES domain for the coarser grid. In case

has a signiﬁcant consequence for the largest time scales in the ﬂow. For a velocity of 5 m/s, the
time scale corresponding to the largest eddy that can ﬁt inside the domain is approximately 2.75
hours & 4.65 hours for the coarse and ﬁne grid respectively. This is a huge difference in the largest
time scales in the ﬂow for the two grid resolutions. We observe a slight trend in the 30 minute
averaged velocity ﬁeld of the ﬁner grid, which indicates that complete stationarity is not achieved
in this case. To avoid this trend, 35 ﬁelds of 30 minute averages are averaged together to gain a total
averaging time of 17.5 hours for the ﬁner grid (as indicated in table 2.2). A total averaging time
of 17.5 hours is sufﬁcient to resolve the smallest to intermediate time scales in the ﬂow, but, the
largest time scales might not be fully resolved resulting in a slight non-stationarity in the results.
Although, the coarse grid resolution of 20 m along with the impact of grid resolution on time scales
might explain the discrepancy in the velocity proﬁles, more investigation in this area is required to
quantify the exact reason for this difference.

separation region, the total TKE proﬁles show deviations from each other (for both with and with-

to the added detail in the grid, which contributes to a higher total TKE production on the lee-side
of the ﬁrst ridge.

observed in the coarser grid is absent in the ﬁner grid. At further downstream locations (O & P),
the phenomenon is more complex with ﬁner grid showing higher total TKE closer to the ground.
However, as we move upward, the total TKE is higher in coarser grid.
To further understand the reason behind the smaller total TKE in the ﬁner grid across the sec-
ond ridge, the ratio of total TKE for the ﬁner to coarser grid is shown in ﬁgure 3.25. It can be
observed that in the far wake of the ﬁrst ridge, the mentioned ratio is less than 1. This can be
attributed to the higher form drag generated in the ﬁner grid due to a more detailed terrain, which
further reduces the effective friction velocity. This causes lesser TKE production behind the ridge
in the ﬁner grid than the coarser one.

3.2
Single Point Turbulence Characteristics
Turbulence characteristics in the wake of the turbine are explored. The instantaneous data described
in section 2.5.2 is used for this analysis. Following the comparative mode of study, differences in
the turbulence characteristics due to the presence of the turbine, as well as, due to the changes in
the model set up are discussed. Two quantities are focused in this context: compensated energy
spectrum and velocity ﬂuctuation PDFs. Two downstream locations deﬁned as near and far wake
region are focused for this study. The near wake is assumed to be at a downstream distance of one
rotor diameter from the turbine, whereas, the far wake is assumed to be at a downstream distance of
four rotor diameters due to the short-lived nature of the wake itself. At each downstream distance,
three points in the vertical direction corresponding to speciﬁc locations around the rotor top, hub
and bottom are studied. The smooth case is only included in the comparison for terrain complexity,
whereas, rest of the comparisons are based on the steep terrain.
3.2.1
Compensated Energy Spectra
Compensated energy spectrum facilitates the visualization of the inertial sub-range, as the -5/3
slope essentially becomes a horizontal straight line in this formulation. It is important to note
that the computation of energy spectra is based on resolved velocity ﬁeld and thus represent the
distribution of resolved energy. The contribution of SGS energy in the presence or absence of a
turbine at a certain point remains approximately the same and thus allows the observation of the
impact of the turbine itself on overall energy at a point and its distribution among different scales.
However, as the SGS contribution is a function of spatial location, it is expected to affect the
resolved velocity ﬁelds at different points. This will be further explained in the discussion below.
The spectra are computed using the following formulation:
ε = (S(f)f 5/3/Ck)3/2
(3.2)
where, ε is the rate of dissipation, S(f) is the energy spectrum, f is the frequency and Ck is a
constant.
Figures 3.28 (a) & (b) show comparison of energy spectra in the near & far wake for the two
terrains, with and without a turbine on top of the ﬁrst ridge. The grid resolution in the horizontal is
20 m. In both wake regions, the energy is higher in the presence of a turbine for all the points in
the two terrains. This is expected, as the turbine enhances turbulence production in its wake. In the
near wake region, however, highest energy is observed at the hub height in the presence of turbine.
This is rather unexpected, as the turbulence production should be higher closer to the surface and
thus the point at the bottom should ideally exhibit the highest energy. This apparent disparity can
be explained by the increased contribution of SGS energy at the point corresponding to the bottom
of the rotor. A higher SGS contribution would leave lesser resolved energy and thus we observe
lesser energy in the spectrum in the bottom. In the far wake, however, highest energy is displayed
by the points closest to the surface. This further supports the argument made earlier, as the terrain
is complex and thus the absolute difference between the surface and the points increases as we
move downstream. This reduces the SGS contribution sufﬁciently enough to exhibit the expected
energy distributions. It is also observed that the energy spectra in the presence or absence of tur-
bine show higher difference at points corresponding to hub and top of the turbine. The spectra at
the bottom point display much closer energy distribution. This can be explained by the fact that
the TKE production due to the turbine itself is signiﬁcantly high at the rotor top and decreases as
we move to the rotor bottom (see ﬁgure 3.6 & 3.14 (b)). Moreover, comparing the energy spectra

of the two terrains, it is observed that the smooth terrain possesses higher energy than the steep
one. Regarding visualization of inertial sub-range, any conclusive statement is hard to make, as all
spectra display varying regions which can be considered comparable to the straight horizontal line
on top. Finally, comparing the spectra in the near and far wake, it is observed that the energy distri-
bution at the smallest time scales for different locations and terrains has a wider spread in the near
wake than the far wake. This indicates higher mixing and thus a more homogeneous distribution
of energy in the far wake at the smallest scales than near wake.
Impact of turbine location is discussed by comparing energy spectra in the wake of the two tur-

near wake, the second turbine shows higher energy than the ﬁrst one. This can be explained by the

ﬁrst turbine shows the highest amount of energy at larger time scales, but has a higher slope as we

Moving on to the impact of grid resolution, we expect to see higher energy in the ﬁner grid due to
added detail in the terrain, as well as, due to the fact that the resolved scales are higher in the ﬁner
grid than the coarser one. To visualize this, we compare the energy spectra of respective turbines
for different grid resolutions. Figures 3.28 (g) & (h) compare spectra of turbine on the ﬁrst ridge,
whereas, ﬁgures 3.28 (j) & (k) show the same comparison for the turbine on the second ridge. Fo-

scales also display a higher amount of energy than the 20 m case. The (almost) ﬂat region is also
observed to increase for certain spectral curves in the ﬁner grid. The energy spectra for second
turbine, on the other hand, display much tighter spread of energy at larger to intermediate time
scales. The difference in the energy in smallest scales is, however, clearly visible.
In summary, we observe that the presence of a turbine increases production of TKE at almost
all scales. This difference in energy is higher at the points corresponding to the rotor top and hub,
whereas, points at bottom display much closer energy distributions. The contribution of SGS en-
ergy is higher closer to the surface, which causes the points at the bottom in the near wake to have
lesser resolved energy than the points at the hub height. As expected, increasing the grid resolution
increases the resolved energy at almost all the points.

3.3. SELF-SIMILARITY OF WIND TURBINE WAKE
59
3.3
Self-Similarity of Wind Turbine Wake
Wakes behind wind turbines are known to show self-similar behavior in a ﬂat terrain under different
atmospheric conditions. Xie & Archer [109] showed wakes to be self-similar in neutral conditions.
Abkar & Port´e-Agel found self-similarity under various stability classes [110]. This was further
explored by Xie & Archer, who then checked for self-similarity in a ﬂat terrain under different
stability conditions in the presence of Coriolis force [111]. This self-similar behavior of wakes in
a ﬂat terrain is not limited to horizontal axis wind turbines, as Abkar & Dabiri found that wake
behind a vertical axis wind turbine is also self-similar [112]. In this study we attempt to look for
self-similarity in a complex terrain under different model variations as presented before. However,
ﬁrst we check for self-similarity in the ﬂat terrain case to verify consistency with previous studies.
To check for self-similarity, we normalize the streamwise velocity deﬁcit proﬁles in the down-
stream of the turbines and plot them against the normalized distance. The velocity deﬁcit in the
wake denoted by ∆U is normalized with its maximum value for a particular proﬁle ∆Umax. As the
wakes are expected to be asymmetric, the centered distance on each side of the wake is normalized
with the wake half-width of the respective sides.
Figure 3.31 shows self-similar velocity deﬁcit proﬁles in the lateral direction at different down-
stream distances. The ﬂat terrain, as expected, shows the self-similar behavior for the longest
distance with a symmetric proﬁle. The complex terrains also depict self-similarity but for shorter
distances, primarily due to faster wake recovery and also due to the non-uniformity in the terrain,
which deforms the proﬁles as we go to further downstream distances. The turbine located on the
ﬁrst ridge shows self-similarity in different terrains with a very tight spread of proﬁles throughout.
The turbine on top of second ridge, however, shows a wider spread towards the tails of the proﬁles
for the coarser grid resolution. Another observation is that the self-similarity in the coarser grid
holds from the turbine location to a certain downstream distance. This is probably due to the fact
that grid points across the turbine rotor in the lateral dimension are not enough to resolve detailed
wake structure. As the grid resolution is improved, the self-similarity is observed from one rotor
diameter downstream to three rotor diameters for both turbines. In addition, the spread of proﬁles
for the second turbine also becomes tighter in the ﬁner grid.
Following this, self-similarity is checked in the vertical direction (see ﬁgure 3.32). The proﬁles
in complex terrain show self-similarity with a much wider spread between the different down-
stream locations. In addition, the proﬁles are relatively more asymmetric than the lateral proﬁles.
This can be attributed to the fact that the vertical proﬁles have an impact of the surface and face
vertical wind shear. In addition, as the terrain is complex, varying levels of slope lead to varying
distances between the projected rotor area and the surface, which eventually gives rise to high vari-
ation among proﬁles at different downstream locations.
In general, it can be stated that velocity deﬁcit proﬁles preserve self-similarity in the wake of
the turbines in a complex terrain. The region of this preservation is, however, much smaller than
the ﬂat terrain counter-part. This is attributed to the deformation of wakes in the far wake region
by varying heights of terrain, as well as, to the faster wake recovery in the complex terrain. Finally,
vertical proﬁles show wider spread and higher asymmetry than the lateral ones.

Chapter 4
Summary & Future Work
Interaction between wakes generated by a wind turbine and the complex terrain was the focus of
this study. For this purpose, we modelled a complex site named Perdig˜ao inside an LES domain
under neutral atmospheric conditions. Different cases varying in terrain complexity, turbine loca-
tions and numbers, as well as, grid resolution were studied. Averaged, as well as, instantaneous
data has been utilized for the purpose of different analysis.
Averaged data ﬁelds were analyzed to study the stationary ﬂow characteristics. The terrain is
challenging with massive ﬂow separation in the wake of the ridges. Placing a turbine on top of the
ﬁrst ridge in the steeper terrain shows that the shear generated by the ridge itself does not allow the
wake to follow the terrain or move in a straight path. Rather, the wake has an upward orientation.
Comparing the two different terrains shows a difference in the orientation of wake in the stream-
wise direction. In addition, the velocity deﬁcit in the near wake of the turbine is observed to be
similar in the two terrains. The steeper terrain, however, shows a higher turbulent kinetic energy in
the near wake. The orientation of the wake in the plane parallel to the rotor plane is also observed
to be similar in the two terrains for the same turbine location.
Comparing the two turbines located on top of each ridge shows signiﬁcant differences. The ori-
entation of the second turbine’s wake in the streamwise direction is somewhat terrain following,
due to the smaller ﬂow separation region behind the second ridge. Moreover, the turbulent kinetic
energy produced by the turbine itself in the wake is much smaller for the second turbine than the
ﬁrst turbine. The orientation of the wake in the rotor plane is also different for the two locations,
which might indicate some dependency on the terrain characteristics in the lateral direction.
As the grid resolution is changed in the horizontal, it has an impact on the terrain itself due to
the added detail. This affects the friction velocity in the terrain. We observe a higher speed up
across the ﬁrst ridge for the ﬁner grid, but the total TKE remains almost the same. While, some
difference is predicted due to the change in the terrain itself, this also somewhat indicates the in-
ability of the coarser grid to fully resolve the velocity proﬁle. Although, further investigation in
this area is recommended to ﬁgure out the exact reason for this difference.
Following the study of stationary ﬂow characteristics, turbulence characteristics in the wake of
the turbines are analyzed. For this purpose, compensated energy spectra at different vertical posi-
tions in the near and far wake of the turbine are analyzed. Higher energy in the far wake is observed
than the near wake. The impact of SGS contributions is also signiﬁcant in the near wake, where

the distance between the surface and chosen points is relatively smaller. The ﬁner grid is observed
to have more energy, as expected. In addition, the inertial sub-range is also more highlighted in the
ﬁner grid as compared to the coarse grid.
The velocity ﬂuctuation PDFs are plotted for all the cases in the near and far wake for two dif-
ferent time scales. The results shows the LES is able to capture some non-Gaussian behavior at the
smaller time scale, depicted in the tail heavy PDFs. The larger time scale is, however, observed to
collapse on the Gaussian proﬁle. The ﬁner grid does not show much of an improvement over the
coarser grid showing inability of both grids to resolve the smallest scales of ﬂow (expected).
Finally, normalized velocity deﬁcit proﬁles in the wake of the turbines are plotted. This is done
to check if the wakes preserve self-similarity in the complex terrain. Obtained results show self-
similarity for a shorter distance than the ﬂat terrain. In addition, the lateral proﬁles show a much
more symmetric shape and tighter spread than the vertical proﬁles. This is due to the fact that the
vertical proﬁles are impacted by the surface generated shear. As the distance between the rotor hub
height and surface increases in the downstream of the turbine, it also has an impact on the surface
shear and its interaction with the turbine wake.
Through this work, we have extended one of the earliest LES studies of wind turbine wakes in
a realistic complex terrain. By comparison of the wake characteristics under different terrain con-
ﬁgurations we pointed out the similarities and differences in ﬂow characteristics induced by the
speciﬁcs of the varying topography. We also showed that the energy distribution among different
scales is dependent on the locations of the points and on the presence/absence of the turbine. Fi-
nally, we found that the wind turbine wakes preserve their self-similar behavior inside a complex
terrain under all variations.
4.1
Future Work
The work presented in the current study is fundamental in nature, with a very limited set of variables
under simpliﬁed ﬂow and atmospheric conditions utilizing a coarse grid resolution. This study is,
therefore, intended to be a preliminary study for further investigation of wakes in a complex terrain.
Due to the unexplored nature of this topic, we suggest following ideas which are crucial to increase
our understanding of the problem and to the development of simulation strategies capable enough
to predict the ﬂow with sufﬁcient accuracy:
1. Validation of results: Performing an experimental investigation of the problem inside a wind
tunnel is crucial, as it not only enables us to understand the problem in detail under a con-
trolled environment, but also, gives us an experimental data set to validate our LES setup.
Validating the simulation setup is necessary, if further studies in this domain are to be per-
formed.
2. Sub-grid scale modelling: As the terrain is highly complex with a lot of ﬂow separation, it
would be interesting to compare different SGS models such as dynamic Smagorinsky model
[113], Lagrangian scale-dependent dynamic model [114] and Deardorff model [93]. In addi-
tion, modiﬁcation of some existing models to improve the modelling in the complex terrain
can also prove useful.
3. Turbine parameterization: Comparison of different turbine models, such as actuator disk and
actuator line has been performed in the ﬂat terrain [69, 95, 96]. Such comparisons have
==PAGE:77==
4.1. FUTURE WORK
65
shown that actuator disk without rotation overestimates the velocity somewhat in the near
wake region. It would be interesting to see if the differences observed in the ﬂat terrain are
signiﬁcant enough in the complex terrain or not due to the short-lived nature of the wake.
4. Additional turbines: Adding more turbines in the form of a wind farm can give an insight
into how a wind farm might interact with the atmosphere. In addition, changing the inﬂow
wind direction to be parallel to the ridges or to have staggered and array conﬁgurations of the
wind farm will be enlightening for the collective interaction of a wind farm with a complex
terrain and ambient atmosphere.
5. Atmospheric stability: One of the most important and fundamental studies can be designed
on how the atmospheric stability affects the wake interaction within the complex terrain.
Moreover, the impact of diurnal cycle, as well as, of the low level jets on the wake behavior.
Also, taking the Coriolis force into consideration can give a very detailed insight into the
ﬂow characteristics in conditions very close to the real atmosphere.
6. Terrain heterogeneity: In the current study the roughness length is assumed to be constant
throughout the domain. In reality, however, a domain this big would never have a constant
roughness length. Therefore, having a heterogeneous terrain, with internal boundary layers
impacting the ﬂow is suggested.
7. Dimensional scaling: Studying the scaling of second and higher order moments in the wake
of turbines located in a complex terrain can be interesting to verify the validity of the scaling
laws under wake conditions in the complex terrain.
8. Capping inversion: Finally, taking into account the inversion layer on top of the atmospheric
boundary layer would be interesting. Studying the impact of a wind farm on the inversion
layer inside a complex terrain can also yield interesting results. The top-down approach can
also be utilized to quantify the impact of capping inversion.
