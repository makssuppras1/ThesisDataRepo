Abstract 
The emergence, selection and spread of resistant bacteria is an increasing problem, posing a 
serious threat against human welfare. Conventional methods to characterize these bacteria have 
not evolved very much and are still time consuming to perform and the results are difficult to 
reproduce. While next generation sequencing (NGS) has shown promising results over the past 
years, and the costs of doing NGS has decreased. 
To prove the use of NGS technologies for clinical diagnostics and surveillance of antimicrobial 
resistance, a novel mapping method, KMA, was developed. This method allows direct analysis of 
raw sequence reads, from both whole genome sequencing (WGS) and metagenomic sequencing. 
The computing can be handled by a standard desktop computer. 
Mapping of sequence data against various databases in this study, showed a high concordance 
compared to conventional methods. It was in addition possible to identify pathogen bacteria in 
cases where the conventional methods failed. An increased accuracy and speed was also found in 
comparison with state of the art tools for NGS analysis. 
In conclusion NGS is a realistic tool to aid clinical diagnostics and antimicrobial surveillance.

Introduction 
Since the discovery of penicillin by Alexander Fleming in 1928, antibiotic development and 
treatment have revolutionized the human welfare.1 Today antibiotics are widely used to treat and 
prevent infectious diseases, as to ensure rapid growth of live-stock.2 Meanwhile the usage of 
antibiotics have created favorable conditions for the emergence, selection and spread of resistant 
bacteria, creating a threat against the human welfare.1  
In order to countermeasure the growing population of antibiotic resistant bacteria, antimicrobial 
susceptibility testing (AST) have been used and standardized since the 1940’s.3 However, the 
different methods of AST includes a lot of laboratory work, the results are hard to reproduce with 
more than 95% concordance and takes 2-3 days and often returns inconclusive results which then 
requires retesting.4 Therefore new reliable and rapid diagnostic tools are needed in order to type 
bacterial pathogens, and analyze their antibiotic resistance pattern. Here NGS has shown 
promising results, on both WGS and metagenomic sequence data.2,4–6 
This project describes methods for analyzing the vast amounts of data generated by NGS, going 
from WGS data through simple metagenomic sequence data, such as urine samples, and at last 
the more diverse metagenomic samples, such as fecal samples.  
The main focus of this project is the detection of the bacterial species composition, resistance 
pattern and to some extent the virulence factors of each sample and sample type. To accomplish 
this, a novel mapping method KMA (k-mer (fragment of DNA with length k) alignment) has been 
developed in order to map raw reads directly against a redundant database. This enables accurate 
analysis of bacterial features, and distinguishes the highly similar sequences seen in bacterial 
sequence analysis.

metagenomic sequencing data.2,6–9 For all datasets, clinically relevant attributes have been 
obtained using traditional microbial diagnostic methods. Where these covers species composition 
/ determination, phenotypic susceptibility testing and detection of virulence factors. The

Sequence data: For second generation sequencing, as used in this study, the DNA is sheared 
before sequencing. This technique results in a large number of short DNA fragments, reads, which 
is sequenced (reads) and stored in fastq-format. The fastq format consists of 4 lines for every read 
sequenced: 
1. A header line, containing information about the read and possible barcodes. This line has 
to begin with “@”. 
2. The read / sequence. 
3. An information line, where any extra information may be written, usually this line is empty. 
This line has to begin with “+”. 
4. A quality score of the read. This line has to have the same length as the read, as every 
character here corresponds to the quality of each base call in the read. 
The data in a fastq file is often trimmed before use, where low quality bases are removed using 
the quality scores on the fourth line. 
Another common format of sequence data is the fasta-format, which is stored over at least two 
lines. The header line (must start with “>”) containing some information on the sequence, while 
the sequence is portrayed on the following lines. Usually the fasta format contains long pieces of 
sequence, and may be obtained by an assembly of raw reads.  
 
Alignment: In bioinformatics, the first, the oldest and probably the single most important tool, is 
the alignment of one or more sequences. Alignments tell us how our sequence data look and how 
much there is of each specific pattern. If the found pattern are known, it can be deduced what it is 
or most likely is. The proper choice of alignment method is thus critical, for the further 
investigation of the problem you seek answers to.10 
For this reason, a broad variety of alignment methods have been developed since the 1970’s, in 
order to solve a broad variety of problems commonly encountered in bioinformatic analysis.11 The 
most commonly used alignment methods today are: BLAST (basic local alignment search tool)12 
and BWA (Burrows Wheeler alignment),13 briefly explained below.

Despite a broad variety of alignment methods, the correct one for analyzing the microbial content 
in metagenomic sequencing does not exist. As they will fail on at least one of the most important 
characteristics of microbial metagenomic sequencing, which are: 
1. Large amounts of sequence data. 
2. Low depth. 
3. May contain contamination. 
4. High similarity of templates within the databases of interest. 
Even current implementations of both BLAST and BWA, will fail on at least one of the above 
characteristics. Therefore, a novel alignment method, KMA, was developed, specifically to solve 
the problem of microbial metagenomic sequencing.  
 
BLAST: BLAST is designed to accurately find and align the best matching templates in a database to 
a query sequence. To gain high accuracy BLAST uses the Smith-Waterman algorithm,11,14 while 
speed is achieved using k-mer seeds to start alignments from.10 
The Smith-Waterman algorithm search for the optimal alignment between two sequences, and 
uses a scoring matrix to decide whether a match, mismatch or gap should occur on each position 
between the query and template sequence. For the optimal alignment of the sequences, a correct 
choice of scoring matrix is thus needed. Where the scoring matrix should reflect the similarity 
expected between the query sequence and template sequence. This part of the BLAST algorithm 
gives BLAST the precision, but is also the slow part of the algorithm, as Smith-Waterman alignment 
runs in O(n2).12,14 
To circumvent the use of the Smith-Waterman algorithm, BLAST uses a k-mer table to look for 
hotspots between the query and template sequence. A single k-mer lookup is O(1), thus a lookup 
of the entire query and template database will be O(n) (n = database size + query size). As a result 
BLAST will only start alignment with Smith-Waterman from these k-mer seeds, which brings BLAST 
close to a runtime of O(n).10,12 
The problem with BLAST and metagenomic sequencing, is that BLAST performs a local alignment 
of sequences. This requires assembly of the sequence data, which potentially obscure accuracy 
because of the low depth.5 Direct alignment with raw sequence reads would require a global

BWA: BWA was developed to meet the requirements for a rapid alignment method, that was able 
to map raw read data directly against a large reference. To do this BWA uses suffix arrays from a 
Burrows Wheeler transformation (BWT), allowing it to fast and accurate look for similarity 
between a query and template sequence in a memory efficient manner.13 
Originally BWA was developed to map against the human genome, where each template has a lot 
of redundant regions. As a result of this, it may be impossible to find one best matching template 
for each read, or even find one correct place within each template to start alignment from. To 
solve this, the first priority of BWA is to find the best matching template and position for each 
read. If one such template or position does not exist, a random one is chosen among the best 
matching templates.13 This is, for human genome mapping a fair choice to take, as one would then 
get a uniform distribution of identical reads over the redundant areas. However, for microbial 
mapping this behavior is unfortunate, as it will decrease the mapping depth of the correct 
template if there are many highly similar templates. For example, when mapping resistance genes, 
a beta-lactamase gene often share high similarity between hundreds of other beta-lactamase 
genes.15 This similarity between templates will force the reads to be uniformly spread over these 
similar templates, and the resulting depth of the wanted beta-lactamase gene will be hundreds of 
times lower than the actual depth.16 
Attempts to work around this problem has been made, where the databases are clustered based 
on sequence similarity, and a consensus sequence drawn from each cluster. The consensus 
sequences of each cluster will then be uniquely represented, and BWA can be used to look for 
these.16 
BWA is probably the best suited candidate for microbial metagenomics analysis at the moment, as 
it handles most of the characteristics.17 The problem encountered with BWA is the unfortunate 
random choice of templates, unless the database is clustered. However, clustering opens new 
threats to the data analysis, where a clustering of the database assumes that there will only be 
one hit within each cluster of sequences. The mapping is further challenged if an incorrect choice

KMA: KMA was developed to handle the characteristics of microbial metagenomics, instead of 
working around the problems by using existing methods. To achieve this, KMA has to be fast in 
order to handle the vast amounts of data, handle the fact that the depth of the sequences is low, 
handle contamination so that it does not mask the results and finally be able to pull the best 
template candidates from a highly redundant database.  
To do this the KMA algorithm has been implemented in three main steps (see figure 1), which 
work independently of each other. The two first steps have the main focus of limiting the data 
size, and make interpretation of the data easier for the final steps (see figure 1).

align. To avoid this, KMA extracts the k-mers of each template in the database and links them back 
to their template. This way the second step can go through the query sequence, and check for

The realignment of sequence pieces between matching k-mers is continued recursively, until k is 
non-positive. When k becomes non-positive the two sequences are known to be completely 
different, and the alignment is filled with mismatches and gaps to fit the size of the longest 
sequence piece. 
By the end of the alignment, an alignment score is calculated. A match gives a score of 1 while a 
mismatch / gap is given a penalty of 3, similar to what is used by BLAST and BWA.10,13 Using this 
alignment, gap openings have no extra penalty, as there is not discriminated between gaps or 
mismatches with KMA.  
This alignment score is then updated for all best mapping templates for each read, over all reads 
in the input data. This way KMA can use the alignment score obtained from all the input data to 
choose the best matching template for each read, by iterating over all mapping reads again.

Sparse KMA: Alignment with KMA requires a database of templates to be split into k-mers, which 
requires a lot of memory for big databases. For this reason, an option was added to the KMA 
algorithm, to perform a sparse mapping similar to that of KmerFinder.4,20 Where a sparse KMA 
mapping of k-mers only considers k-mers with a certain prefix, this way limiting the size of the 
template database. KmerFinder analyses the co-occurrence of k-mers between a query sequence 
and a template database of known k-mers, and uses this to find the most similar template 
sequences in a database. To perform this sparse mapping with KMA, the KMA algorithm is 
terminated at step2, and the k-mer content is analyzed instead of aligning the sequences. To avoid 
multiple hits due to similarity between templates, a “winner takes it all” strategy was 
implemented.8 Where k-mer hits of top scoring templates, is removed from lower scoring

HashMap: Analyzing big data, such as NGS data, often involves looking something up in a 
HashMap (also known as hash list or hash table). A HashMap is a data structure which is able to 
connect “keys” to “values”, so called key-value pairs. Depending on the implementation of the 
HashMap, these key-value pairs can contain anything, e.g. k-mers to templates pairs.  
For a lot of NGS methods, most of the computation time is spent on a sequential HashMap lookup, 
even though lookup in HashMaps are fast (O(1)). Hereby the speed of most NGS methods is 
dictated by the implementation of their HashMaps. Unfortunately, no HashMap implementation 
exist that will solve every problem perfectly, which is the reason why C does not contain any 
standard HashMap implementations.

HashTree: The HashTree offers constant lookup time like the HashMap, but has some different 
features which makes it better suited for the alignment part of the KMA algorithm.  
Instead of having a long table storing the key-value pairs like the HashMap, the HashTree stores 
the keys in a tree structure, with corresponding values at the end of each branch. In case of 
storing k-mers, the HashTree will then have a height of k. To look up any value in the tree, one will 
at max have to check k nodes to find the wanted value. Each node in the tree will in this case have 
5 branches (one for each base and the “N”-base), where each node will lead to a new node, a 
value or the special NULL-value. If the NULL-value is reached, it means that the key you seek does 
not exist (see figure 3).  
The tree structure itself also gives the possibility of finding partly matching k-mers, in which case 
fewer nodes are visited before a value is returned that determines if the k-mer exists or not. This 
feature come in handy when using the recursive alignment method of KMA, where k is 
decremented and partly matching k-mers is needed. 
 
Figure 3; A hash tree for k-mers, here with k=3, bold headed arrow indicates a null-pointer. Key-value pairs are arbitrary chosen. 
Compared to the HashMap the lookup in a HashTree is a bit slower, even though the lookup time 
is kept constant (O(1)). In addition to this it uses more memory, due to the tree structure. This is 
why it is only used for the alignment part, where partly matching k-mers can be used beneficially.

KmerResistance: In order to analyze the resistome of WGS samples, KmerResistance identifies co-
occurring k-mers between a database of resistance genes, a species database and a query 
sequence. To account for different sequencing depths and possible contamination, 
KmerResistance adjusts a threshold for depth and template coverage (depth correction and 
template correction), using the identified species in the sample to set dynamic thresholds using 
the exponential survival function (see equation 6). The species is predicted as the one with the 
highest number of co-occurring k-mers between the query sequence and a species database, 
equivalent to choosing the species with the highest query coverage. Hereby a detected species 
with low depth will adjust a threshold for predicting genes, by setting a low threshold on depth 
and template coverage. Whereas a species prediction with high depth, will give a high cutoff on 
depth and template coverage of the resistance gene predictions. This adjustment of thresholds 
allows KmerResistance to accurately predict and associate resistance genes in WGS samples, even 
at extreme depths.5

Databases: As a database containing a sufficient amount of full bacterial genomes would use too 
much space for use on most standard computers, therefore two bacterial genome databases were 
built for sparse KMA mapping.  
One was created to analyze WGS data, and another for metagenomic analysis. The database for 
analysis of WGS data were built from 8676 full bacterial genomes, downloaded from NCBI. For 
metagenomic analysis of fecal samples, the database was expanded with genomes identified in 
fecal metagenomic samples by Nielsen et al.21 As the bacterial contents of the human gut have not 
yet been fully sequenced and assembled, the majority of the bacteria in fecal samples will not be 
in a database of complete genomes from NCBI. 
Both of the bacteria databases were created with a prefix of “ATG” to limit the database size, and 
with a k-mer size of 19. Previous studies have shown promising results with a k-mer size of 16, 
here a k-mer size of 19 was chosen here to limit the number co-occurring number of k-mers 
between the bacteria database and the human genome.4,20 A k-mer size of 16 randomly matches 
48% of the human genome, whereas a k-mer size of 19 only matches 2%.  
In order to predict antimicrobial resistance in the samples, a full indexed database was created on 
the resistance genes from the ResFinder database.15 Virulence genes was collected according to 
Joensen et al.,6 and indexed for alignment with KMA. Both the resistance and virulence database 
was created with a k-mer size of 16, as suggested by Clausen et al.5 to be the optimal for 
databases of this size.  
To be able to account for human contamination in the samples, the databases were modified with 
the human genome, where a special annotation was added to each k-mer in the databases that 
co-occurred in the human genome. This way the database would more or less keep the same size, 
and contamination could be recognized by k-mers already in the database.

Data analysis: Thresholds for predicting species, resistance genes and virulence genes in the 
samples was found using a 5-fold cross-validation. With 5-fold cross-validation a training data set 
is chosen, and split into 5 parts. Then a threshold is optimized on 4 of the 5 parts of the data, and 
tested on the remaining part. This training and testing is then repeated five times, so that each 
part is used for training as well as testing. A final threshold is then chosen, as the mean of the five 
obtained thresholds.10 
For the WGS data analyzed by KmerResistance-2.0 the Zankari dataset served as a training data, 
while the Stoesser dataset was kept for evaluation of the obtained thresholds. 
A threshold for species prediction in metagenomic samples was found by training on the Peabody 
dataset, and were tested on the Hasman and Joensen dataset.  
In order to assign found resistance genes to bacteria in metagenomic samples, each found 
resistance gene was assigned to the species with the most similar depth. This approximation of 
assigning resistance genes will therefore not deal with every factor of metagenomic sequencing, 
as e.g. it is ignored that plasmids may have a different depth than the chromosome of its host, but 
it will work as an approximate measure, for where each gene is located. 
A final prediction threshold for pathogenicity was performed on the Joensen data set, where 
pathogen bacteria in the samples were identified by mapping of virulence genes. A threshold for 
this was found by 5-fold cross-validation on the C. difficile positive samples, while the rest of the 
Joensen data set were kept for evaluation.

Discussion 
For the WGS datasets and simple metagenomic datasets, high concordances were achieved when 
compared to conventional methods.  
The species predictions by conventional methods on the Zankari and Stoesser datasets were not 
the main focus when these datasets were created, as opposed to the phenotypic susceptibility 
tests. This may explain the false predictions of species in these datasets, as the species predicted 
by NGS were highly similar to those assumed by conventional methods. The six false positives of 
aadA1 in E. coli and the two aac(6’)-aph(2’’) corresponds with the ones found by Zankari et al.,2 
which were deemed to have degenerated promoter regions. The remaining 4 false positives might 
be due to contamination as described by Clausen et al.,5 or due to incorrect readings of the 
phenotypic susceptibility tests.2 The Stoesser dataset mainly consisted of beta-lactamases, which 
are rather complex resistance genes where a single SNP might alter the gene function completely. 
Some of the isolates in the Stoesser dataset have also been shown to contain chromosomal 
resistance genes,7 which is not included in the ResFinder database and were therefore not 
analyzed.15 
 
==PAGE:23==

For the Hasman dataset, it is hard to say how high the false positive rate is, as the samples in that 
dataset have not been thoroughly tested. However, all bacteria found were associated with UTI’s, 
indicating that the findings were true. In cases where several bacteria were identified, some 
inconsistencies were found on antimicrobial resistance as well, as these were most likely assigned 
to the wrong bacteria. This behavior was however expected, as plasmid information was not been 
included. For diagnostics of UTI’s, the correct assignment of resistance genes is however of less 
importance. As the urinary tract is thought of as sterile, all bacteria found here may be considered 
pathogen or at least unwanted. An antibiotic able to treat all of these bacteria, is thus favored. 
Clinical diagnostics of fecal samples are rather complex, as it involves isolation and growing of 
single colonies from a diverse sample. This process is then further obscured by anaerobic bacteria 
or even non-culturable isolates, therefore a large amount of conventional tests are filled with false 
negatives.6 Even for well-known pathogens such as C. jejuni it has been shown that only 50% of 
the conventional tests detects the presence of C. jejuni.22 Therefore it is not unlikely that the 
samples predicted to contain C. jejuni by NGS in this study does actually contain C. jejuni, even 
though twice as many samples were tested positive by NGS, compared to the conventional 
methods. In all 15 cases where the conventional methods failed to detect pathogens, the NGS 
methods used in this study were able to detect at least one possible pathogen that would explain 
the symptoms of the patient. This implies that sequence analysis is more sensitive than the 
standard conventional methods, while only few pathogens were detected in the healthy controls.  
While the costs of sequencing are decreasing, the computer power needed to analyze the 
sequence data has dropped dramatically. All analysis performed under this study, does not require 
more than 16 GB of RAM and can therefore be carried out on a standard computer, as long as only 
one sample is analyzed at a time. Meaning that with the coming advances of Nanopore 
sequencing, it is not only possible to sequence anywhere, but also analyze sequence data 
anywhere.

Conclusion 
High concordances between NGS based diagnostics and conventional methods were achieved in 
this study, revealing that NGS technologies have a huge potential in clinical diagnostics and 
surveillance. While some false positives occurred with the NGS methods, false negatives where 
rare, showing the still increasing sensitivity and accuracy of NGS based methods.
