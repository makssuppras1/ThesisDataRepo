Abstract
Technological advances and reduction of costs have led to a progressive introduction of robots in many
different ﬁelds during the last years. One of the applications is vehicle autonomous navigation which is
lastly achieving satisfactory results for outdoor environments not only in military but also in commercial
domains such as private transportation.
The wide availability , high information content and suitability for human environments of low-cost color
cameras convert the computer vision in an appealing sensor for many robot platforms. This thesis presents
different methods for vision-based object recognition of a set of landmarks in the robot’s vicinity with the
purpose of using them for situational awareness and give the robot the possibility of navigate by the use of
recognizing landmarks.
The object recognition task is divided into 4 modules: Image segmentation, Classiﬁcation, Generation of
candidates and Object detection. Besides, a chapter dedicated to localize landmarks with prior knowledge of
their dimensions is presented.
The results show that the object detection is successful even though there is room for further improvements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
Technological advances and reduction of costs in the recent years have led to a progressive introduction
of robots in many different ﬁelds until the point where nowadays robots are seen as a normal device that
even families have at their homes such as a robot vacuum or garden robot lawn mower. Contrarily, just
few lustrums ago, the robots just performed simple tasks such as robot arms in production lines, but the
development of high accuracy sensors and computers allows the performance of more complicated works
and the robots appearance is very varied depending of the task to accomplish.
Most of commercial autonomous robots though, work in indoors environments such as transportation
robots navigating into storage warehouses or through halls in ofﬁce environments. The result of research
into mobile autonomous robots in the outdoor environments is lastly giving good results and considerable
progresses towards the commercialization of them for non-military purposes.
In a near future, transportation tasks around the cities and even for long distances could be carried out
by autonomous vehicles. The effort invested in navigating autonomously along the streets cohabiting with
non-autonomous vehicles and humans has given a boost in the safety of using this method, that even big
companies are considering to adopt autonomous robots for performing their duty. One of this cases, is the
expectations that U.S. Postal Service have in the positive impact that introducing the autonomous vehicles
for postal delivery would give to their continuous ﬁnancial losses (USPostal 2016).
Recently in addition, autonomous aerial vehicles (AAV) broaden its ﬁeld by adding as well high loads
or even human transportation for mid-short distances with the release of Ehang 184 (Ehang 2016), able to
transport up to 100 kg, to an average speed of 100 km/h with an autonomy of around 20 minutes.
1.2
Goals of the project
The objective of this thesis is to recognize by vision static objects that appear repeatedly in the running
track around DTU in order to use them as a landmarks and create a map of them that in the future can be
used for robot localization during autonomous drives along the path. The main objectives are:
• Segment the images taken from the camera such that for further processing it yields the best result.
• Classify and process the images in order to ﬁnd the best candidates for object detection

• Recognize a set of predeﬁned objects within the images
• Calculate the position of objects with previous knowledge of their dimensions for mapping them.
• Detect consecutively the objects ensuring the continuity of the detection broken by failures, and
rejecting false detections.
1.3
Problem formulation
Sorting the GPS accuracy derives of localization in a forest environment and avoiding the use of laser
due to the low-cost of the cameras brings to a vision-based navigation by mimicking the human navigation
method where the detection of the environment gives a broader spectrum of the situation awareness, and
detecting landmarks increases the positional information. The ﬁnding of the road is essential for route
following behavior of the robot.
Given the singularity of some natural objects that change its appearance depending of the time of the
year and the weather, the landmarks are chosen to be strong against this factors.
In this thesis is treated the segmentation of the images using different methods. The method presented in
Section 3.3 was implemented few weeks before the deadline and even though it shows interesting results it
need more work on it in order to use it in the following procedures of object recognition. This is the reason
why the next chapters are done following the segmentation from Section 3.2.
The problems that are solved overall are the segmentation of the interesting features in the images,
achieving high rates of correct detection, specially in a mid-short range distance, of the predeﬁned land-
marks, the mapping of the relative position from the robot of those landmarks and gaining information from
correlation frame-to-frame of the object in successive images in different lightning conditions and different
seasons of the year.

CHAPTER 3
Image Segmentation
3.1
Introduction
The ﬁrst part of the object recognition procedure is to convert the raw image received from the camera
into a more useful representation of it. The image segmentation goal is to divide or cluster the pixels of the
image into meaningful structures.
The object recognition procedure depends on the characteristics of the structures or regions that are of
interest within the picture. Therefore, the outdoor environment plays a key role in the image segmentation.
The environment is uncontrolled, i.e. the background of the picture is not designed to improve the
visualization of the objects, and the illumination changes constantly. In addition, focusing more on the DTU
running track, generally objects such as trees or buildings differ from each other in geometrical shape, even
they look different depending on the robot perspective. Furthermore, the different seasons of the year present
the vegetation in the outdoors environment diversely. Hence, the variant appearance of some objects discard
pure geometric or template assumptions for detection.
The image segmentation objective is to join all these same objects that differ in shape into an only cluster.
Achieving perfect image segmentation is barely impossible for the outdoor environment the robot drives in.
Under-segmentation or over-segmentation may occur. In the ﬁrst case, a cluster contains pixels of more than
one object, while for the second case, one object is divided in more than one cluster. Both cases can be solved
with further processing explained in the following chapters, but is more desirable to have over-segmentation
as is easier to join clusters than to remove the non-wanted part of a cluster.
To sort the above explained difﬁculties, the image segmentation is based in the HSV color model of
the image. Therefore the segmentation is depending only on the color of the objects and the pixels with
similar color are grouped in the same cluster. Therefore the geometric differences does not apply for this
segmentation. On the other hand there is the possibility of having different objects with the same color, or
objects having different colors.
The colors are affected by illumination, but for different lightning condition the colors are assumed to
change accordingly to it, therefore ideally for a homogeneous change of lightning, the whole picture will
change of color, and the same objects will still share it, but if the lightning is not equally distributed like
it happens with the shadows of objects it introduces another change of color on the shadow region. The

3.2
K-Means clustering
K-means is one of the most widely used and simple unsupervised clustering algorithms, especially for
large data sets. For the purpose of speeding up the algorithm the images are resized up to 20% (154x204
px.) of the original size (768x1024 px.). Smaller sizes can still be used but would need an adjustment of the
parameters set up in this report.
The procedure follow a classiﬁcation of a data set X into k clusters previously ﬁxed. The idea is to place
k points to be the initial centers of the clusters, but there are several ways to place them at the initialization
of the algorithm.
Figure 3.1: Graphical procedure of K-means clustering (Algorithm 1). Source: Myrhøj (2016).
After initialization, using euclidean distance, every point in the data set is assigned to the nearest cluster
center. Then the cluster centers are recalculated, which are the average position of all the points belonging to
the same cluster.
The last steps are iterated until the algorithm converges. The result do not guarantee the optimal
solution, and it can vary depending of the initial placement of the initial cluster centers. Therefore, the
number of clusters and its centers initial placement are the major issues to take into account for this algorithm.

3.2.1
Choice of K decision and seeding
The choice of the number of K and the seeding of the K-Means algorithm are closely related and should
be discussed together. It is known that with a correct choice of initial cluster centers, the execution time
is reduced due to a faster convergence, and the possibility to end up with wrong clusters is diminished
(Arthur and Vassilvitskii 2007). Hence thresholding 4D histogram is used to seed the K-Means algorithm
and explained below.
By grouping pixels with similar values for all H, S and V and therefore similar color, can be assumed that
those belong to the same object. To do so, the histograms for each layer of the picture, that can be seen in
Figure 3.3 are combined together into a 3D space being the axis the 3 image channels. For faster inspection
of the histogram the intervals of the 3 axis are limited to 25, and the number of pixels laying inside every
one of those 25x25x25 bins is the 4th dimension on the 4D histogram, as can be seen in Figure 3.4.
In order to determine the seeds, a weighted 3D structure is dragged along all positions in the 4D histogram
so as to ﬁnd the presence of large weights that presumably are prominent peaks in the histogram where the
number of pixels in the central position of the structure is bigger than its neighbors within the structure,
hence ﬁnding local peaks. The weights are calculated following next equation:
w = 6H(x1, x2, x3) −H(x1, x2, x3 −1) −H(x1, x2, x3 + 1) −H(x1, x2 −1, x3)
−H(x1, x2 + 1, x3) −H(x1 −1, x2, x3) −H(x1 + 1, x2, x3)
(3.1)
That ensures that the weight will be big when the central value is large compared to the neighbors and
will tend to zero or negative when neighboring values are alike or bigger respectively.
Once the weights have been calculated, are stored in a list in descending order for ﬁnding the peaks and
seed the clustering algorithm. Then, the coordinates for the ﬁrst and larger weight are stored as a cluster
center for the seeding. All the weights found in the vicinity of a 3x3x3 cube placing the center on the
examined weight, are then removed from the list to avoid having 2 clusters for 1 object. The process is
iterated through the list until a certain threshold for the weight value is reached. The outcome of the 4D

For the region splitting the quadtree, a hierarchical data structure representation, is used. It works with

The images input to the algorithm are the HSV images including the 3 channels, therefore the uniformity

further splitting. Therefore, the large regions are good candidates for splitting since they may include many
different elements. Furthermore, the criteria of splitting have to be easy to accomplish rather than difﬁcult.
If a homogeneous region is split into 4 sub-regions, this is a mistake that the merging procedure can easily
compensate the over-split. On the other hand, if inside a quadrant there are more than one homogeneous
areas, the error recovery is not possible.

4.1
Introduction
For the outdoor navigation of the robot, detecting the features that can be used as landmark is one of the
objectives. Therefore those features on the DTU running path that are static and repetitive are chosen.
The next step on the detection process is to create object candidates of the landmarks, that will serve
to feed the object detection module in next chapter which will either accept or reject them as being a true
candidate. In this chapter the classiﬁcation of the clusters and the post-processing of them are treated.
The result from the image segmentation is expected to contain each of the features in the image separated
in clusters. The next operation of the object recognition procedure is to classify them as interesting clusters
or discard them. That is, if they meet the requirements of potentially being one of the recognizable landmarks
around the running track, which for this case are the red signs, the trash cans, the light posts, the trees and
the road.
The classiﬁcation process can join more than one cluster solving an excessive segmentation caused by
over-segmentation in the previous process. The color expected for every of the objects after several tests sets
the classiﬁcation.
The red signs have a different situation. Depending on their size, they will or won’t have their own
cluster if appearing in the image. But given that is an object with a very distinctive color compared with the
rest of the environment where mostly is expected to perceive green (vegetation), brown (road and tress) or
light colors (sky), a pixel classiﬁcation instead of a cluster classiﬁcation is carried out in the image for red
pixels to solve the situations where the red sign does not have its own cluster.
It can also happen that the classiﬁcation result is not successful and two clusters containing different
objects are joined, or noise is included, or the objects are not fully included in the cluster. Post-processing
with morphological operations is applied to the classiﬁed cluster images in order to remove as much as
possible the effects of noise and non-wanted structures and also to strengthen geometrical assumptions of
the objects.

As the name of this color model states, the ﬁrst channel is dedicated to exclusively weight the color of

4.5.1
Morphology
Morphological operations are used to reaching this objectives (González and Woods 2002)(Ch. 9,
519-560). For this purposes the sub-images are binary images . In particular, the opening and closing
operator, region ﬁlling and the extraction of connected components.
Opening and closing
Those are two different operations that combined together they help to both remove outlying structures
in the binary image and close holes and notches. The two operations are composed of other 2 operations:
dilation and erosion. For the use of them is required to deﬁne a structure element, the shape of which will be
decided according to the wanted result of the operations.
Dilation enlarges components, and is the result of dragging the structure element through all the boundary
of the components with the center in the boundary pixels. All the outer pixels from the component that lied
inside the structure element while doing this operation are relabeled to 1. This procedure can be visualized
in Figure 4.11.
Figure 4.11: Left: Component - Middle: Structure element - Right: Result of dilation component with
structure element. Source: (González and Woods 2002).
Erosion is the dual complementation of dilation, the principle is the same, but instead of enlarging the
component, this gets its pixels that lied inside the structure element removed hence labeled as 0. Figure 4.12
shows the erosion method.

straight line the result of erosion with structure element. Source: (González and Woods 2002).

Connected components
It is of capital importance to ﬁnd all the pixels that are connected in the binary clustered sub-images
since ideally they would be one object of interest. The degree of connectivity will determine the result. For
1D images like the binary images that are treated in this chapter, there is the choice of 4-connectivity and
8-connectivity. The ﬁrst only treat as connected the neighbor pixels that are on the same row and column,
while the second one considers also the diagonals, as can be visualized in Fig. 4.15.
In order to ﬁnd the connected components, an algorithm called Flood Fill (Bond 2011) is used and will
be explained shortly.
For a sub-set of pixels marked as 1 in the image set, one is chosen to start and from this point the
connectivity chosen is used to ﬁnd nearby connected pixels. This action is performed recursively until all
the connected pixels are labeled with the same label. Afterwards, this action is repeated for another pixel
marked as 1 in the binary image that have not been labeled as a connected component yet, until all the pixels

CHAPTER 5
Object Detection
5.1
Introduction
In this chapter is described the last module of the detection process. Throughout the process from the
input image from the camera until object detection module, the obtained object candidates are subject to
assumptions that will determine either if they are accepted as correct detection or are discarded.
The recognition assumptions are based on shape, size, localization, and in the case of the red signs also
the object that holds them.
For the good functioning of the object recognition algorithm during the navigation of the robot, it is
desired to have a larger success rate in detection. Therefore the detection assumptions are chosen restrictive
against the wrong detections during the testing.
For representing the road, that can be treated as different of the rest of the objects since it is a continuous
path that covers the whole running track and is expected to be found in every single image for correct nav-
igation, a polygon that uniﬁes the boundaries of the road is chosen due to its unpredictable shape in the image.
Contrarily, the rest of the objects have a predeﬁned shape that ﬁts for them a representation with a box
bounding them.
5.2
Road
Among the detection the road is a special case. The assumptions followed in the cluster classiﬁcation
section are actually the same as for the detection. Given the singularity of the road in the images, due to the
non-uniformity of the road shape along the running track together with the robot ability to navigate the road
margin sides, the smaller components in comparison with the largest one are not rejected. Hence the only
operation performed is to ﬁnd the road boundaries.
The procedure followed to ﬁnd the boundaries is performed for every of the connected components
in the road sub-image and correspond to Algorithm 4. The pixel situated closest to the origin in every
component is chosen to be the starter one. Then its 4-connectivity neighbors are checked in a predeﬁned
order whether if they are road or not. The predeﬁned order is: Up, Right, Down, Left. This order leads
to a clockwise extraction of the boundary. If the upper neighbor checked is not a road pixel, then the
following neighbor following the inspection order is checked. When a road neighbor is found and is not

Canny edge detector
The Canny edge detector takes as input a gray-scale image, and the output is an image showing the
intensity discontinuities at their original position.
The ﬁrst step of the procedure is to smooth the image by Gaussian convolution and then differentiate. As
property of convolution, this is equivalent to convolve the image with the ﬁrst derivative of the Gaussian
function.
Since the ﬁrst derivative is directional, two ﬁlters fV (x, y), fH(x, y), horizontal and vertical are deﬁned
for convolving the image and detect edges on arbitrary directions. By convolution, the gradient components
of both directions are obtained RV (x, y), RH(x, y).
At this point, edges give rise to ridges in R(x, y), which is the result of squaring and summing the
gradient components. Those peaks are marked if they surpass a certain threshold and the rest of the pixels
are set to 0.
Afterwards, the non-maxima suppression to thin the edges is performed, since during the smoothing
might have been broadened. Checks the non-zero components, and if both neighbors in the direction of the
gradient are smaller, sets them to 0.
As a last step, two thresholded images T1, T −2, with different thresholds τ1 < τ2,are used to obtain
binary images. Image T2, with larger threshold will have then less noise and fewer false edges but larger gaps
between edge segments. To form continuous edges, the end of the segments in T2 are tracked in T1, in or-
der to ﬁnd a neighbor pixel that continues the edge segment until bridging the gap with another edge segment.

Hough transform
The classical Hough transform is a technique used to ﬁnd regular curves that can be expressed in
parametric form. In this case, it is used to ﬁnd lines, whose parametric form is:
y = k ∗x + m
(5.1)
However, the polar coordinates as in Eq. 5.2, are more convenient for the application of the Hough
transform.
x ∗cos(θ) + y ∗sin(θ) = ρ
(5.2)
Where θ is the rotation of r with respect to the x-axis and r is the the length of a normal to the origin.
Therefore, a line is represented with a point in the ρθ-space.
Plotting all the possible lines (ρ, θ) for the known points (x,y) in the Cartesian space, sinusoidal curves
are the result in the polar space. The intersection of them in a certain (ρ, θ) location, means that those points
are aligned by a straight line deﬁned by intersection parameters.
An accumulator matrix to store how many points intersect at each location allows to ﬁnd the most
signiﬁcant lines in the location where the value of the accumulator matrix is larger. In Figure the sinusoidal
curves in the ρθ space and the 5 largest values in the accumulator matrix can be observed.
By applying the Hough transform on the edge image, the 5 bins in the accumulator matrix with larger
magnitude are selected, and the rest are discarded. That is, the 5 most signiﬁcant lines. The goal is to extract
the 4 edge lines of the poles, but depending of what appears in the background or the output of the edge
operator, other lines can appear as more signiﬁcant, hence the 5 lines choice. Then, the lines are required to
be vertical, with a margin of ±8° in order to give ﬂexibility to the algorithm when the pictures are taken
with a small roll rotation on the mobile robot.
To give validity to the detection, the lines are classiﬁed as being in the right-half or left-half of the
sub-image. The condition for detection is that the lines are long because it is assumed that the edge lines

6.1
Introduction
In order to increase reliability over a set of consecutive pictures that the object that has been detected is
actually the same for all the sequence, or to overcome a failure in the detection in a punctual image of the
sequence, a template matching operation is carried out.
The purpose is to ﬁnd the template (object) extracted from the previous image, in the current image.
The template matching is not scale invariant, therefore it is assumed that the consecutive pictures are taken
in a relatively short period of time in comparison to the mobile robot velocity, thus the sizes of the object
in both images are not too different and the template matching gives a correct result. The normalized
cross-correlation method (NCC) is used for the template matching (Lewis 1995).
To accelerate the process of the template matching, instead of calculating the similarity of the template
all over the new image, a Region Of Interest is deﬁned. This ROI is calculated according to the odometry
taken from the robot’s motion model and the object distance taken from the camera perspective model. To
calculate the object distance, a previous knowledge of the size of the object is required. Therefore for this
chapter, only the objects with known size and repetitiveness of it over the whole track are used as landmarks.
The trees consequently are not used, as well as the light posts, which are discarded because their height cut
the upper part from the images when they are close enough to the camera to be detected. The testings have
been performed only for trash cans and the small and big red signs.
From the previous robot pose, the previous distance and orientation from the object related to the camera,
and the pose of the robot at the moment to take the next picture in the sequence, the placement of the object
can be translated in the new image.
Assuming that all the above mentioned calculations do not have maximum accuracy, the area of the
object is enlarged in order to include neighboring areas to form the ROI in which the object is searched by
template matching.
The use of the template matching in the algorithm is to ﬁnd the objects previously found in the following
situations:
• in an image of the sequence where the detection fails
• check a detection in an unexpected area in the image, i.e. the object is found outside of the ROI.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
7.1
Conclusions
The segmentation results based on K-Means clustering based on peaks in the 4D HSV color histogram,
show satisfying result on clustering the different groups of objects appearing in the images where the sunlight
does not interfere.
The application of an adaptive color-based threshold in the peak selection for seeding the clustering
algorithm maintains generally the correct object grouping in clusters for images where the sunlight does
affect despite the probability that shadowed areas split the objects in different clusters, mainly the road. It
has also been observed that for some cases, the adaption of the threshold causes over-segmentation in the
picture.
The classiﬁcation of the clustered images based on the composition of the HSV components of the
pixels that form them, shows overall a success in grouping together different clusters containing parts of the
same object due to over-segmentation. The classiﬁcation, based in color assumptions acquired manually
performing several segmentations of different pictures, show good and repeatable results for artiﬁcial objects
and the road. In the case of trees, however, the correct classiﬁcation do not held the same repeatability
shown by the rest of the objects, specially when in the cluster are included many background trees, or the
over-segmentation split different parts of the trunks, that due to his unequal distribution of the color since is
a natural object do not join together.
For the red color search combining two thresholding methods together, generally only with the RGB
thresholding the segmentation of the red signs is successful, but the combination with the HSV thresholding
sort the cases in where the ﬁrst one was not sufﬁcient.
The object detection strongly depends on the above mentioned classiﬁcation, therefore is not a surprise
that the trees show results less satisfying than the rest of the objects, where if there is no obstruction and the
object, is placed in a short-mid range, and the neighboring area does not share color, the recognition of the
object is highly probable.
The template matching method and the setting of a region of interest, combined together give satisfactory
result for the tests with the set-up of robot speed and picture capture time interval from the analysis chapter.
Even though the sizes of the template do not match with the size of the matched area due to robot approaching,

7.2
Future work
• Progress in the Split and Merge segmentation. As explained in the chapter of segmentation, further
merging procedures and grouping of the regions can be applied, as well as trying other methods that
not only rely on color, for example (Ojala and Pietikainen 1999) or (Nammalwar et al. 2010) that use
a mix of color and texture.
• Thresholding for tree clusters. As the results showed the lowest success ratio for detection, new
thresholds, like extending the adaptive color-based threshold for this case can improve its detection.
• Introduce roll sensor. Read the robot roll sensor in order to automatically rotate the pictures if
needed.
• Camera distance calculation study and calibration. The current distances are merely based on the
pin-hole projection model and the results are approximated, but have not been calibrated with the real
distance.
• Identiﬁcation of the crossing roads. For the autonomous navigation of the robot, it would be a good
idea to stop when reaching a crossing with a pavement road where cars and other heavy motorized
vehicles circulate, and give the control to a user with the remote for the safety of the robot.
• Extend ﬁeld of vision. Extend the ﬁeld of view of the robot by using the 3 cameras.
• Study the minimum distance detection. As is stated in this report, for objects placed far from the
robot, the mapping of the objects lose accuracy. Perform a study to determine a maximum distance for
detection.
• Create a map of detected objects. In order to inspect how the landmark detection works for robot
localization.
