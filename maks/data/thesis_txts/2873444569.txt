Abstract
The integration of carbon capture, utilisation and storage technologies (CCUS) can help
to achieve a reduction in carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions. Optimising the carbon capture
process is important to improve the efficiency and reduce the energy consumption. A CCUS
pilot plant using an alkaline solution for absorption and electrochemical desorption via bipolar
membrane electrodialysis (BMED) has been installed at a refinery in Romania. This thesis
discusses the performance of the pilot plant. The pilot plant utilises the pH-swing concept
to influence the carbon equilibrium. The capture module comprises an absorption column,
where CO2 is absorbed in alkaline aqueous KOH solvent, and a desorption module, where
by applying a pH-swing in a BMED stack CO2 is desorbed and the solvent is regenerated.
The two modules were operated in island mode; the decoupled operational mode allowed for
individual analysis of their performance. Three experiments were conducted in both modules,
and the resulting data was analysed to investigate the operation. Additionally, a method was
developed to examine solvent samples in the laboratory.
In the capture module, CO2 was absorbed, and a high solvent loading of up to 𝛼= 0.89 was
achieved. The obtained capture efficiency was lower than values reported in the literature;
this may be attributed to errors during data recording, suggesting a potential for improvement.
In the regeneration module, different current densities were applied to the BMED. There is
a significant impact of current density on the performance of the regeneration module. At
a high current density of 300 A/m², the experiment terminated after three hours. When the
current density was halved, the desorption time doubled. However, when the current density
changed from 150 A/m² to 300 A/m², the power consumption increased significantly. Water
flux across the membranes was observed across all three experiments. This flux reduced
the efficiency of the plant, as dissolved CO2 is transported into the wrong compartment. The
results showed that there is a significant trade-off between reaction rate and power consump-
tion.

Project Description
Carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions into the atmosphere have been a concern for serious cli-
mate change in recent years. The production of flue gas in industrial facilities, like power
plants, oil refining plant, is the main source for CO2 emissions. The use of amine as absorb-
ent is an efficient method to reduce CO2 emissions, but with additional issues with volatility,
degradation, foaming and corrosion. CO2 capture by potassium hydroxide (KOH) solution
will be an important alternative technology for carbon management due to its low degrada-
tion rates and low corrosivity. The current studies on CO2 capture in KOH solutions at pilot
scale are limited. It is indispensable to evaluate the CO2 capture behavior at small scale
comparable to large-scale plants. The successful outcomes of this study will facilitate the
transfer of this knowledge into industrial sites.
The project allows a comprehensive understanding of the CO2 capture process from the in-
dustrial flue gas using KOH solution at OMV Petrom, Romania. The project involves KOH
based CO2 Capture coupled with electrochemical regeneration of the solvent. This is novel
technology, and the knowledge obtained in this project will help understand the behavior of
the pilot plant. The pilot plant was operated in an “island mode” where the capture module
and the regeneration module were operated independently of each other. Such an opera-
tion helps in understanding the saturation point of the KOH solution and the behavior of the
electrochemical cells.
This project is aimed at acquiring practical and theoretical knowledge of chemical engin-
eering principles, unit operations such as absorption, electrochemical reduction, gas-liquid
separation, membrane technology, etc. Your task will entail analysing the pilot data for the
capture and regeneration modules, analysis of the liquid samples in the lab which will feed
into the data analysis. You will disseminate your results in a report.
Learning Objectives
1. Collect and overview literature which discusses KOH and K2CO3 based CO2 Capture
in relation to post combustion carbon capture and discuss current pilot work status on
such a technology (electrochemical CO2 capture).
2. Describe the fundamental principles involved in pH-swing CO2 Capture
3. Organise the data collected from the pilot plant operations
4. Distinguish between coupled operation and island mode operation of the pilot plant
5. Analyse liquid samples in the lab for CO2 loading and concentration of various anions
and cations using the IC
6. Analyse the pilot data for island mode operation, especially in terms of osmotic water
transport
7. Compare the results with any existing literature data
8. Summarize and discuss the obtained results
9. Disseminate the results in a report and presentation

Introduction
According to the International Energy Agency [1], in 2022, 3,593 Mt of carbon dioxide (CO2)
were released to the atmosphere in Europe by the combustion of fossil fuels. This accounted
for 10.5% of the global CO2 emissions. Even though the trend in emissions is decreasing,
CO2 is a classified greenhouse gas and its release is a driving force of climate change [1].
Figure 1 shows the distribution of CO2 emissions according to different sectors. The biggest
sector was the electricity and heat production, which accounted for approximately 33% of
the total emissions, the industry sector accounted for almost 13.7% of the CO2 emissions in
Europe [1]. If the CO2 emissions from these two sectors could be omitted, a total of 1,679
Mt of CO2 could have been avoided from being released into the atmosphere.
Figure 1: Pie chart showing the distribution of CO2 emissions by fuel combustion in 2022,
attributed to different sectors according the the International Energy Agency [1].
CO2 can be captured on emitter sites with so-called carbon capture technologies, which have
gained increased research interest in the past years. Carbon capture, utilisation and storage
(CCUS) technologies offer a solution by capturing the CO2 at the source. The captured CO2
can either be repurposed for other industrial applications or stored permanently underground.
Consequently, the release of CO2 into the atmosphere is avoided [2].
There are several CO2 capture methods which can be applied to reduce CO2 emissions.
Post-combustion methods are the most studied and advanced technologies; they capture
CO2 from flue gases exiting industrial sites. Flue gas usually consists of CO2 along with
other gases such as air, nitrogen and water vapour.
In typical post-combustion capture
methods, CO2 is selectively removed from flue gas using absorption, adsorption or mem-
brane processes. Other capture technologies, such as oxy-fuel combustion, involve burning
fuel in pure oxygen instead of air, resulting in a flue gas consisting mainly of CO2 and water
vapour, from which CO2 is easier to separate. Pre-combustion capture technologies remove
CO2 from the fuel before combustion by converting the fuel into a mixture of hydrogen and
CO2 through gasification or reforming [3].
In the attempt to improve carbon capture technologies, the ConsenCUS project [4] was
launched. The project includes the installation of an electrochemical post-combustion car-
bon capture pilot plant at industrial sites, where they capture CO2 from the flue gas. This
project concerns a carbon capture pilot plant which is installed at an oil refinery in Romania

[4] and is designed to capture up to 0.1 ton of CO2 per hour. Figure 2 shows the CCUS plant
installed at OMV Petrom in Romania.
Figure 2: Electrochemical carbon capture pilot plant installed at OMV Petrom in Romania.
At a refinery, crude oil is being transformed and refined into products such as diesel fuel,
gasoline or fuel oils. Romania was the first country in the world where oil production was
registered. The Petrobazi refinery is situated in Romania and is operated by OMV Petrom.
The plant has a refining capacity of 4.5 MN tons of crude oil annually, making it one of the
biggest refineries in Eastern Europe [5].
1.1
Principles of Post-Combustion Carbon Capture
Absorption refers to the separation process in which a specified gaseous substance is re-
moved from a gas stream by the use of a liquid agent, called the solvent. During chemical
absorption, the solvent and the target gas form weakly bonded intermediate compounds.
The most researched and widely applied post-combustion CO2 capture method is absorp-
tion, using chemical solvents such as aqueous amine solutions. Monoethanolamine (MEA)
is the benchmark solvent for this application [3].
Figure 3 shows a simplified flowsheet of an absorption carbon capture plant using MEA
as the solvent. The process involves two main steps; the first step occurs in the absorber
column. Flue gas and solvent are transported into the column where the solvent selectively
reacts with the CO2 present in the flue gas. The absorption is carried out at low temperatures
ranging from 40 °C to 60°C. The resulting CO2-rich stream is transported to the desorption
column, while the cleaned CO2-lean flue gas is released into the atmosphere. The reboiler
at the desorption column heats the CO2-rich mixture to 100 °C to 120°C; at this temperature,
the chemical bonds between solvent and CO2 are broken, releasing pure CO2. The cleaned,
lean solvent can be recycled back to the absorber column. A heat exchanger between the
lean and rich solvents pre-heats the rich solvent before it enters the desorber column [6].
When using amines, a thermal swing is applied to absorb and desorb CO2.
4
==PAGE:18==

Figure 3: Simplified flowsheet of a typical carbon capture system using chemical solvents
such as amines.
Pilot-scale tests of CO2 capture using 30 wt% of MEA at a waste-to-energy facility in Denmark
carried out by Vinjarapu et al. [7] showed a capture efficiency of up to 95%. The thermal
swing needed to successfully absorb and desorb CO2 has a reported energy consumption
between 3.86 and 6.82 GJ per tonne of CO2 according to Sharifian et al [3].
Solvent selection is a crucial step in designing a chemical absorption carbon capture unit.
The desired solvent should have favourable qualities such as a low energy consumption, low
toxicity and high CO2 solubility. Additionally, quick kinetics are favourable to avoid excessive
space demands for the absorber column [3].
Amines are not used in their pure form; they are mixed with water to improve the density,
viscosity, surface tension and thermal expansion coefficient of the amines. The addition
of water increases the energy demand for solvent regeneration; however, high amine con-
centration leads to higher viscosity, which makes solvent handling difficult. Additionally, the
increasing degradation rate poses environmental concerns [3]. MEA specifically, is known
to be corrosive [8] and degradable into substances such as heat stable salts, after contact
with other gases and impurities such as oxygen, sulphur dioxide or nitrous oxides [6]. The
aim is to find a solvent which offers more durability and energy efficiency, to optimise the
carbon capture processes while keeping an optimal capture efficiency.
1.2
Electrochemical Carbon Dioxide Regeneration
Instead of forming intermediate compounds, aqueous alkaline solvents can be used in which
CO2 is absorbed. Shifting the pH of the solvent, affects the equilibrium of dissolved CO2
species. At a high pH CO2 is chemically bound as bicarbonate and carbonate, which can
not be released as gaseous CO2. By shifting the pH to a lower level, the equilibrium is driven
toward the formation of dissolved CO2, which can be released from the liquid stream. The
recovered CO2 is in a gaseous state and has a high purity. In order to use the full potential
5
==PAGE:19==
Analysis of island mode operation of electrochemical CO2 capture pilot
of the described pH effect, an additive is required, which is used to influence the pH of the
solvent.
KOH solvents have been reported to be effective for capturing CO2, due to increased solvent
capacity. KOH solvents are reported to have a high chemical stability, thus degradation is
less of a concern. However, the high alkalinity can cause corrosion and complicate solvent
handling [9]. Instead of thermal desorption, the CO2 can be released and the solvent regen-
erated by adjusting the pH of the solvent. The advantage of a so-called pH-swing concept
is that absorption and desorption of CO2 can occur at ambient conditions, which may result
in a decreased energy demand compared to MEA processes [3].
The pH-swing can be applied with electrochemical methods. During this post-combustion
capture method, the absorption of CO2 is the same compared to using MEA solvents (see
Figure 3), however CO2 is desorbed and the solvent regenerated in an electrochemical cell.
Electrochemical CO2 regeneration has been realised by several methods, where electrolysis
or bipolar membrane electrodialysis (BMED) are the most studied. In electrolysis, the pH-
swing is enabled in the vicinity of two electrodes; this method is energy intensive due to
the intrinsic irreversibility of the gas evolution redox reactions [3]. BMED technologies util-
ise acidic and alkaline compartments which are formed by superposing bipolar membranes
(BPM) and cation exchange membranes (CEM). The pH swing is induced by water disso-
ciation on the BPM. BMED technologies are reported to have a lower energy consumption
compared to electrolysis [10]. If combined with renewable energy sources, a more sustain-
able method for carbon capture can be achieved [3].

rendering of the pilot plant, introducing its three modules. The plant is separated into 3 main
parts: capture, regeneration and utilisation. The capture module includes pre-treatment of

The two modules can be operated in different operating modes: coupled operation and island

the regeneration module, the impact of the current density is investigated to analyse the
extent of desorption efficiency as well as power consumption.

2.1.2
Absorption Behaviour and Solvent Selection
In order to use the full potential of the pH effect on CO2 solubility in water and to overcome the
slow reaction kinetics of the binary system, an additive is required. In the absorption step,
CO2 is to be removed from the gas stream; thus, a solvent with high pH should be used.
Solutions such as aqueous KOH solvents are suitable. Assuming that KOH fully dissociates
in water, the pH of the solvent is expected to lie at approximately 14. The amount of K+ ions
in the solution stays constant, as K+ is not involved in the equilibrium reactions.
The absorption process is exothermic; therefore, temperature in the absorption column af-
fects both the solubility and absorption of CO2. At increased temperatures, the solubility
of CO2 in water decreases. However, both the gas-phase and liquid-phase mass transfer
coefficients increase along with temperature, enhancing the rate at which CO2 is transported
from the gas phase into the liquid. This increase in mass transfer coefficients is attributed
to the higher diffusivity in the liquid phase at increased temperatures. In addition to the tem-
perature, the pressure has an effect on CO2 loading. At higher pressures, mass transfer is
improved and the CO2 loading increases. However, the removal efficiency decreases with
increasing the initial pressure [13].
2.1.3
Electrochemical Regeneration of Carbon Dioxide with Bipolar Membranes
To remove CO2 from the solvent, the pH has to be reduced. Electrochemical methods such
as BMED can successfully apply a pH swing in the solvent. The solvent cycles through two
compartments, an acidic and an alkaline compartment, which are separated by a cation ex-
change membrane (CEM). The two compartments are also between two bipolar membranes
(BPM).
The BPM consists of two exchange layers, the anion exchange layer (AEL) and the cation
exchange layer (CEL). The region where the two layers meet is called the interface layer
(IL). An electric field is applied to the membrane, from which H2O dissociates into protons
H+ and hydroxide OH – ions. Equation (5) shows the dissociation of water occurring on the
membrane without gas formation. For the dissociation of water to occur, a minimum voltage
of 0.83V has to be applied to the system [3].
H2O ⇌H+ + OH−
(5)
Figure 8 shows an electrochemical cell, with the two compartments separated by the CEM
and BPMs on the side. Through the anode and cathode compartments, an electrode rinse
solution is recirculated to convert the electric current into ionic current by oxygen and hydro-
gen evolution reactions [10]. The ionic current is carried through the cell by the ions moving
through the layers [14]. The water splitting on the IL works under reverse bias; the H+ ion
leaves through the CEL layer while the OH – ion leaves through the AEL layer. Across the
CEM, the K+ ions are transported from the acidic compartment into the alkaline compartment.
The solvent is transported through the acidic compartment and afterwards it is cycled through
the alkaline compartment.

The moving anions and cations are the drivers of the pH change in the system [14]. The
acidic H+ ion transfers from the CEL into the acidic compartment, which reduces the pH of the
solvent in the acidic compartment. Consequently, the equilibrium in the acidic compartment
shifts from carbonate to bicarbonate and reaching a pH lower than 4.5, where dissolved CO2
[15] is the main species. The dissolved CO2 can then be separated as gaseous CO2 from
the solvent. Meanwhile, while basic OH – ions transfer from the AEL into the alkaline com-
partment, K+ ions are transferred over the CEM from the solvent in the acidic compartment
into the alkaline compartment. In the alkaline compartment, the OH – ions and K+ recombine
forming the lean KOH solvent. The lean solvent is ready for reuse in the absorption column
[12].
The current density is the amount of electric current that flows through a unit area. The
current density applied to the system impacts the energy consumption as well as the capture
efficiency. With increasing current density, the CO2 desorption efficiency is increased due to
the higher water splitting efficiencies at the BPM. At high current density, the potential drop
is larger, resulting in an increased energy consumption. At low current density, the voltage
drops, leading to a decrease in the efficiency of water dissociation at the BPM [10].
It is reported that an increased temperature has a positive effect on the Faradaic efficiency.
Additionally, the reaction rates are improved at elevated temperatures. However, ion ex-
change membranes and BPMs have a limitation on high-temperature processes [3]. Adition-
ally, performing the process at high temperatures would lead to increased power consump-
tion.

Island Mode Operation of the Pilot Plant
The above sections have explained the operation of the two modules when they are operated

Results
The pilot plant was operated in island mode, and the two modules were investigated sep-

loaded with CO2. Subsequently, the regeneration module was operated in island mode. In

parameters which were calculated to discuss the performance. The specific electrical con-
sumption of the regeneration module was expected to decrease when the used solvent had
high loading; thus, the aim of the experiments in the capture module was not to obtain an
optimal capture efficiency but to reach maximum solvent loading.

Discussion and Outlook
This study aimed to analyse the performance of the capture and regeneration module of a
CCUS plant. Experiments were carried out in island mode operation, where both modules are
operated independently. The discussion is supported by results obtained with data analysis
as well as sample analysis using IC and Titration.
5.1
Capture Module
In the capture module, three experiments were carried out, two of them lasting for three
hours and the third experiment for five hours and 45 minutes. The operational parameters
were kept consistent across the three experiments. The goal was to achieve a high solvent
loading.
The CO2 concentration was measured in the gas stream at the inlet and the outlet of the
absorber column. However, only one recording unit was installed for two sensors, thus giving
only one value of CO2 concentration at a time. At the start of the experiments, only one of
the probes was active. Data collection was excluded until both sensors had been activated.
The amount of water vapour in the inlet and outlet gas stream was determined with the as-
sumption of a fully saturated gas stream. With that knowledge, the amount of water absorbed
in the solvent can be calculated. The more water absorbed, the more dilute the solvent can
get, which affects the pH and consequently affects the absorption capacity and efficiency.
The obtained capture efficiency of the experiments was calculated with the CO2 amount in
the gas streams. Due to the reduction of the data recording the CO2 sensor, the capture
efficiency is likely an underestimation of the actual value. Shortly before the end of each
experiment, a sample was taken. With titration, the loading can be determined. The aver-
age values of capture efficiency and solvent loading are presented in Table 21, along with
values presented in literature for comparison purposes. The capture efficiency obtained in
this project is lower than values from literature, the solvent loading is higher than the experi-
ments using 0.91M KOH absorbent as well as the experiments using MEA solvent, as seen
in Table 21. The results shown by Rastegar et al. [13] are from experiments conducted at
an absorption column operated at 8 bar. This elevated pressure increases the CO2 solubility
and thus enhances capture efficiency. However, applying a higher pressure increases the
power consumption of the capture module. The results by Vinjarapu et al. [7] using 30 wt%
MEA showed a high capture efficiency, however, a low solvent loading.

5.2
Regeneration Module
In the regeneration module, the current density was varied between 75, 150 and 300 A/m².
The current density affects the reaction rate, the applied voltage and influences the energy
consumption.
At the BPM, water dissociates, which is driving acidification and alkalization in the acidic and
the alkaline loop. A minimum amount of voltage has to be applied to a cell in order for water
dissociation to occur. This minimum voltage was surpassed for experiments with 150 and
300 A/m²; however, most of the time, the voltage in the experiment with 75 A/m² was below
the limit.
In order to reach sufficient CO2 desorption, the carbon equilibrium inside the acidic loop has
to be shifted towards dissolved CO2, which then can be removed through vapour stripping
in the acidic tank. At a pH below 7.5, the equilibrium shifted for CO2 desorption. At a pH
below 4.5, all carbonate and bicarbonate present in the solvent have reacted to dissolved
CO2; at this pH, full desorption can be obtained. Figure 52 shows the recorded pH level in
the acidic loop over the operational time. The benchmark pH was met in two experiments.
With 150 A/m², the pH dropped successfully after 6 hours in the experiment. With 300 A/m²,
the pH drop was much faster, after two hours and 17 minutes. The reaction rate increased
significantly with the applied current densities. The experiment at 75 A/m² showed a slow
change in pH, indicating that the experiment was terminated too soon.
Figure 52: Recorded pH level in the acidic loop over the operational time, across three
experiments with varying current density (J).
In coupled operation, the tank levels are controlled with an overflow valve, which keeps
a constant solvent amount in both loops while constantly transporting lean solvent to the
capture module. In island mode experiments, the overflow valve is closed. However, the
tank levels still changed during the island mode operation of the plant. The water flux is
occurring from the acidic loop towards the alkaline loop. Water is moving alongside the K+
ions being transported over the CEM. The generated concentration difference between the
two compartments also initiates osmotic water transport. The applied current density induces
electro-osmotic flux. These three effects increase with increasing current density. To account
for the water transport into and across the BPM, the model approach as listed by Weiland
et al. [19] was applied. The calculations provided insights on the operation of the BPM, by
determining diffusive and electro-osmotic flux as well as the water consumption for water
74
==PAGE:88==

dissociation. This model was based on the current density and the water content across the
membrane. The water flux across the CEM and BPM increased with current density.
Alongside the water flux, dissolved CO2 was also transported from the acidic to the alkaline
loop. This transported CO2 could not be regenerated; consequently, the efficiency of the
regeneration module is reduced. The experiment at 150 A/m² was split over two days, in-
cluding a shutdown of the plant overnight. During the shutdown, changes in solvent levels
in both loops were still observed.
The obtained Faraday’s efficiency in this project was 45.5% for the experiment at 300 A/m²,
which is very close to the result obtained by Li et al. [22], who reported an efficiency of
50% at the same current density. As expected, desorption efficiency decreased from 150
A/m² to 34%. Due to unproportionate CO2 desorption values in the 75A/m² experiment, the
Faraday’s efficiency was higher for that experiment.
There is a notable trade-off between the power consumption and the removal efficiency, as
the power consumption does not linearly increase with the current density. While the J300
experiment showed optimal pH shifts in the two loops, as well as fast desorption of CO2,
the power consumption is significantly larger than for the other experiments. The SEEC is
similar for the experiment with 150 A/m² and the experiment for 300 A/m²; the power at 300
A/m² was consumed in just two hours compared to five hours at 150 A/m². Thus, continuous
operation at higher current densities will result in significantly higher overall energy consump-
tion. The power consumption for the Experiment at 300A/m², the SEEC was calculated to
be 9.13 GJ/tonne of CO2. Vinjarapu et al.[7] reported an energy consumption of the MEA
regeneration to be around 3.04 GJ/tonne of CO2.
From the results, it can be concluded that 75 A/m² is too low for sufficient CO2 desorption.
However, from this experiment, insights about the water flux could be obtained which oc-
curred, regardless of the speed of pH change. A current density of 150 A/m² is sufficient;
however, the reaction rate could be improved. The power consumption of 300 A/m² is signi-
ficantly larger than for the experiments at lower current densities investigated in this study of
75 and 150 A/m².
5.3
Recommendations
In order to improve the island mode tests and to further operate the pilot plant in coupled
mode as efficiently as possible, several recommendations can be made.
Due to the data recording of the CO2 content in the flue gas stream, the full extent of the
absorption could not be included in the capture efficiency; thus, repeated tests could advoc-
ate for an improved removal efficiency. To mitigate problems with the CO2 sensor in the
capture module, sampling can be increased. These samples can also be used to validate,
water transfer from the gaseous phase into the solvent by using analytical measurements
such as Karl Fischer titration. Increased, sampling could also allow to determine the time
until high loading was reached. Additionally, the absorbent concentration can be varied, as
literature has stated, to obtain improved results regarding the solvent loading with lower KOH
concentrations.
The results of the regeneration module showed a significant trade-off between power con-
sumption and reaction rate. The optimal operational parameter might lie between 150 A/m²
and 300 A/m². Therefore, it is recommended to carry out similar tests with a range of current
densities between the two current densities to further investigate the optimal trade-off point
of power consumption versus reaction rate.
As the water flux was an unexpected observation, the flux across the membranes requires

further investigation. It is recommended to validate parameters such as the electro-osmotic
permeability, the ion exchange efficiency and the diffusion coefficient of water through the
CEM. The permeability of the used membranes should be tested on carbonic species, as
a transport of CO2 from the acidic to the alkaline compartment is undesirable. The model
of the water flux as well as consumption regarding the BPM can be improved further. It is
recommended to validate the water gradient across the layers and the impact of the current
density.

Conclusion
The capture module and regeneration module of an electrochemical CCUS plant installed at
a refinery in Romania were investigated. The performance of the two modules, operated in
island mode, was analysed using data analysis and sample analysis. To assess the samples
in the laboratory, a method was developed for ion chromatography IC and titration. Three
experiments were carried out in each module. For the capture module, the three experiments
differed in duration. In the regeneration module the current density was varied in-between
experiments.
In the capture module, high solvent loading of up to 𝛼= 0.89 was achieved, which surpassed
the solvent loading of conventional MEA solvents of 0.53 [7]. However, the capture efficiency
was low between 4.5 and 6.7 %, while Vinjarapu et al. [7] reported a capture efficiency
of 95% when using MEA. Due to issues with the CO2 sensors for the gas streams in the
capture module, the initial amount of CO2 in the gas was unknown, which may have under-
represented the calculated capture efficiency. To improve the analysis of the capture module,
it is recommended to repeat the experiments with additional samples and a more accurate
CO2 sensor.
The current densities affected the reaction rate, applied voltage, and the overall energy con-
sumption of the plant. In the experiment with the lowest current density, the applied voltage
was below the required minimum for water dissociation in the bipolar membrane. Thus, the
pH shift in the compartments was minimal, resulting in low amounts of CO2 desorption, and
solvent regeneration was not possible. At the higher current densities, complete CO2 desorp-
tion was achieved. However, at a current density of 300 A/m² the desorption was completed
in 2 hours and 17 minutes, at a current density of 150 A/m² the desorption time was 6 hours.
The downside of performing the desorption at high current densities is the increased energy
consumption. More testing in the range between 150 and 300 A/m² is advised to find the
optimal operational values. Unexpected water flux across the membranes was observed
in all three experiments. Across the cation exchange membrane, osmotic, electro-osmotic
and hydration flux could be quantified. Additionally, water transport inside the BPM and its
respective anion and cation exchange layers was also discussed.
The experiments were carried out successfully and gave insights about the performance of
the two modules. Nevertheless, several recommendations for improvement can be made. To
confirm the capture efficiency, experiments in the capture module, with increased sampling
of the solvent is advised. In the regeneration module at current densities between 150 A/m²
and 300 A/m² are advised to further evaluate the relationship between the reaction rate and
power consumption. To further assess the water flux across the membranes, experiments
to determine the electro-osmotic flux, as well as general water transports, should be made.
