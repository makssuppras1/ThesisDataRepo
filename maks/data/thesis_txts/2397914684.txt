Weather Impact on Power System Load Shedding
by David NIEVA MORENO
In the last years, the decentralization of the power systems is taking place. Specif-
ically, this change is due to the high penetration of distributed generators (DGs) in
the system. The majority of them are renewable energy sources, mainly PV panels.
The increasing number of DGs leads to different technical challenges regarding
the security of the power systems. The power output of these renewable DGs de-
pends on the weather conditions. This thesis investigates the weather impact on the
functioning of under-frequency load shedding (UFLS) through the DGs.
Under-frequency load shedding is a system protection scheme used as the last
resource for handling frequency instability in a power system. The UFLS objective
is to re-establish the equilibrium between generation and demand by tripping loads
at the distribution level. This is automatically realized when the system frequency
reaches the set thresholds during its drop. However, the DGs connected to the load
feeders are also disconnected. Therefore, the net load shed can be considerably re-
duced depending on the shed distributed generation.
In order to study this effect, the Continental Europe synchronous area is selected.
First, the share of DGs is investigated in the associated countries. Afterwards, dy-
namics simulations leading to the necessity of UFLS activation are realized for the
whole year 2015 on an hourly resolution. The simulations focus on the study of split
islands produced by the occurrence of an out of range incident. Two independent
study cases are investigated. In both cases, the system split results in a deﬁcit gen-
eration within the analyzed island where the UFLS scheme is activated. The success
of the scheme is studied based on different variables, namely the frequency nadir,
the total load shed from the customers and the DG shed. Finally, the relationship
between the results and the distributed generation is investigated.
Relevant results were obtained regarding the analyzed study cases. In Study
Case 1, where a small island (West Denmark and Germany) is considered, the se-
curity of the system is not ensured and the risk of a blackout is signiﬁcant. Fur-
thermore, it is demonstrated that the system is vulnerable in the morning hours of
Sundays along the summer months. This occurs due to the high share of DG for
those hours when the system load is relatively low. In Study Case 2, where West
Europe is considered, the system is not vulnerable to blackouts due to the size of the
analyzed island. However, it is demonstrated how the UFLS scheme can be signiﬁ-
cantly improved by safeguarding the DGs during the sunlight hours.

is deﬁned starting with a description of its limitations and assumptions. Finally, a

1.1
Background
Recent events in the last years have changed the future of the energy production in
most countries of the world. Especially, the challenge of solving the evident pollu-
tion problem due to conventional energy source emissions is leading to the deploy-
ment of alternative sources of energy.
Additionally, the constant reduction in the renewable energy costs, the created
distrust around nuclear energy after Fukushima accident together with the uncer-
tainty around fossil fuel reserves among others, seems to predict an entire change
of the energy production system towards renewable sources [44]. This change has
already started to be noticeable in most countries. Indeed, important events such as
the Paris Agreement for the mitigation of the greenhouse gas emissions are a clear
evidence of this.
As a consequence of the previous facts, nowadays Renewable Energy Sources
(RES) are extensively growing around the world as an alternative for the traditional
scenario of centralized power generation based on large conventional power plants
[4]. However, a high percentage of these renewable generation units, especially
photovoltaic (PV) panels, are connected to the distribution system. These sources
of power are known as Distributed Generators (DGs) and their penetration in the
power system is leading to different technical challenges. In particular, regarding
the current defence plan procedures against disturbances in the power systems.
Power systems are planned, built and operated to stand credible contingencies.
Then n-1 rule was developed for ensuring that a normal contingency is managed
without serious consequences for the system [33].
The operation condition of the power system is normal when all electrical vari-
ables are within the predeﬁned limits. In these conditions, the n-1 rule ensures that
the loss of a single system element does not lead to the violation of the operational
limits and the demand is fulﬁlled. If an incident occurs, the operation condition
might change to alert state if the n-1 criterion in not ensured anymore, although the

Only if these limits are violated, the emergency operation condition is reached
and consequences such as the loss of stability or the interruption of supply may oc-

However, even if power systems are protected by the n-1 criterion, the expected
credible contingency might eventually be exceeded through an unexpected event
or by the sum of the cascading effects of several events. Furthermore, during the
emergency state, although initially there is not a blackout, the power system does not
fulﬁll its functions. Therefore, measures must be taken to restore it to secure states
and to prevent a blackout [24].
Defense plans are a set of coordinated automatic measures, known as Individ-
ual System Integrity Protection Schemes (SIPS), intended to ensure that the overall
power system is protected against these major disturbances. Their goal is to min-
imize the consequences of the low probability events, not covered under the n-1
criterion, and to prevent a system blackout [26].
One of these SIPS is the under Under-Frequency Load Shedding (UFLS). This
SIPS is a measure for handling the frequency instability phenomena. Frequency sta-
bility is the ability of the power system to reach and maintain a stable new operating
state after a severe disturbance occurs. In large interconnected power systems, such
as the Continental Europe (CE) Synchronous Area, frequency instability usually oc-
curs after the split of the system into islands [20].

1.2. Motivation
3
The UFLS objective is to restore the system frequency to an acceptable level fol-
lowing a major disturbance which conducts to a generation deﬁcit. This is accom-
plished by tripping loads from the electrical power system. The shed of load is car-
ried out by under-frequency relays at distribution stations where most of the load
feeders are automatically controlled by tripping breakers when the set threshold fre-
quencies are reached [31].
Nevertheless, there is an obvious drawback when UFLS is applied in power sys-
tems. The shed of loads affects mainly the households since industrial loads and
other speciﬁc loads such as hospital are tried to keep connected due to their evi-
dent importance. For this reason, these loads are provided with dedicated feed-
ers [31]. Consequently, UFLS can solve frequency instability phenomena but on the
other hand creates discomfort in the end users and economic losses, since consumers
might be compensated when they are disconnected from the power system.
1.2
Motivation
As described in the previous section, although the increase of RES has evident ad-
vantages, it also leads to new challenges regarding the security of the electrical
power systems due to the large penetration of DGs. Regarding the analyzed SIPS
in this project, i.e the under-frequency load shedding, the consequences of the inte-
gration of these generators might put at risk its correct functioning.
As previously stated, the vast majority of these DGs are renewable sources of
energy, mainly PV panels and less often small wind farms or isolated wind turbines.
The increasing connection of these generators to the distribution system entails the
risk of overestimating the effect of shedding load.
In particular, when UFLS is activated, the underfrequency relays trip the break-
ers of load feeders, disconnecting not only the desired load but also all the DGs
associated to the same feeder. As a consequence, the desired amount of load shed
for overcoming the frequency drop might not be accomplished. Accordingly, the
UFLS response is not the expected one, increasing the probability of expanding the
frequency instability. Moreover, this effect could be even larger if the disconnected
feeder in that moment has a net exceed of distributed generation, i.e the load shed is
less than the generation supplied to the distribution network.
On the other hand, the variability and unpredictability of the weather is well-
known. This fact makes UFLS success dependent on weather conditions since the
distributed generation will vary with the amount of wind blowing or the sun shin-
ning. It must be noted that the term variable RES will be utilized for referring to wind
and solar energy. In this way, a distinction with other RES such as hydro-power is
realized.
Furthermore, not only DGs but also the variable RES in general are affecting the
power system conditions. The high penetration of variable RES implies evident con-
sequences for the power systems characteristics. For instance, it clearly inﬂuences
the system inertia [48]. This issue has been superﬁcially taken into account in the
designed model as it will be explained in the following chapters.

analyze the inﬂuence of DGs in the UFLS scheme of an electrical power system. For

nated by the Union for the Co-ordination of Transmission of Electricity (UCTE), and
nowadays operated by the European Network of Transmission System Operators

It is assumed that frequency is always at its nominal value, 50 Hz, before ap-

Some deﬁnitions have been already presented in Chapter 1 during the introduction
of this thesis. In this chapter, a detailed literature review of the concepts utilized
through the development of the project is provided. Since the considered area for
realizing the presented study is the Continental Europe synchronous area, the def-

2.1
Power System
A power system has been classically deﬁned as a network that connects generation
units with loads through a grid of transmission lines, including all the associated
equipment connected to this network [5] . Hence, its function is to convert energy
from the available sources to electricity and to transport it to the consumers.
The transmission network is usually divided into different levels. Speciﬁcally,
the transmission level operates at the highest voltage levels (230 kV and above) and
interconnects the main generation units with the main load buses. The generation
voltage (from 11 to 35 kV) is stepped up to the transmission level. Afterwards,
these voltages are stepped down for meeting the consumption level. This is done
by means of two more levels, the subtransmission system (69 to 138 kV) where some
large industrial consumers are directly connected and also some generation units,
and the distribution system, where the power is provided to individual consumers
at different voltage levels, from 4 to 34.5 kV (primary feeders) to 120/240 V (sec-
ondary feeders) [34]. It is important to note regarding the scope of this thesis that
DGs are connected to both of these last sorts of feeders.
In order to accomplish its function, the power system must be properly con-
trolled. In every moment, the generation must meet the consumption that varies
continuously according to the demand. Furthermore, the power supply must be
provided within some minimum standards which guarantee the quality of the sup-
ply such as the constancy of both voltage and system frequency, which is the vari-
able under analysis in this project. The frequency quality is deﬁned by ENTSO-e
throughout different parameters which are presented in Chapter 3.2 [18].
A power system is in synchronous operation if all its connected synchronous
machines are spinning at the same speed, i.e at the system nominal frequency. The
imbalance between the instantaneous consumption and the production at any mo-
ment alters the system frequency. If the consumption is higher than the production,
the system frequency decays, and vice versa [30]. Therefore, a disturbance, such
as the sudden connection of large loads, the disconnection of a generation unit or a
==PAGE:27==
10

fault in the transmission network such as the loss of a line can affect this equilibrium,
thus putting in risk the stability of the power system.
2.2
Frequency Reserves
The main objective of frequency reserves is to stabilize the frequency at a new steady-
state value within the admissible quality values after the occurrence of a disturbance
in the system [17].
The goal of the primary reserve is to stabilize the frequency drop to a new steady-
state by means on the re-establishment of the balance between the generation and
the demand. However, its purpose is not to restore the frequency to its nominal
value. The primary reserve only contains the frequency drop [17]. For this reason,
depending on the considered literature, it can be also referred as Frequency Contain-
ment Reserve (FCR) [18].
In fact, the restoration of the frequency is realized by means of the secondary
control reserve, also named as Frequency Restoration Reserve (FRR) [18]. This is
done by the re-distribution of the load among the generating units available. The
secondary control action is slower than the primary control, within 30 seconds after
the disturbance, and it is delivered within 15 minutes after the frequency drop [17].
Furthermore, tertiary control can be used for restoring the secondary control range
when required. Due to this fact, it is referred as Replacement Reserve (RR) [18].

depicted in Figure 2.1:

2.3
Defence Plans
A ﬁrst deﬁnition of defence plans was given in Chapter 1 according to the Interna-
tional Council on Large Electric Systems (CIGRE) [26]. However, depending on the
literature, the deﬁnition entails slight differences [6].
==PAGE:28==
2.4. Under-Frequency Load Shedding
11
In Europe, ENTSO-e deﬁnes a Defense Plan as the combination of coordinated
and mostly automatic measures to prevent the system blackout when a rare extreme
contingency takes place. Futhermore, ENTSO-e classiﬁes in turn these actions in
Special Protection Schemes (SpPS) and in System Protection Schemes (SyPS) [20].
Particularly, Special Protection Schemes (SpPS) are schemes activated by an event
or as a response after normal or exceptional contingencies with the aim of avoiding
the system to enter in emergency state. On the other hand, SyPS are event-based
schemes activated after an out of range contingency, which leads the system to the
emergency state. Their objective is to stabilize the system and to avoid a system
blackout [6, 24].
Finally, the concepts of normal, exceptional and out of range contingencies have
been previously mentioned. According to ENTSO-e Operation Security Network
Code [16, 19], the contingencies or incidents are normal if they are initiated by a
single failure and they must be handled in a secure manner without the violation of
the deﬁned security limits. Exceptional contingencies are the result of the combina-
tions of several normal incidents. Although Load Shedding might be necessary, the
system security must be ensured against these contingencies. Finally, out of range
incidents, such as the combination of multiple incidents splitting the system, could
have critical consequences or even lead to a system blackout.

emergency state, the UFLS is intended in order to prevent the system from a black-

Under-frequency load shedding is a well-known economic and effective tech-
nique in order to protect power systems from frequency instability. A ﬁrst deﬁnition
of UFLS was provided in Chapter 1. Accordingly, the main goal of UFLS is to ensure
the security of the power system after an incident which leads system frequency to
drop. It is considered a last resource regarding the system stabilization since it ac-
complishes its objective by the automatic shed of load in order to re-establish the
equilibrium between the generation and the demand [26].
According to the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers (IEEE), UFLS is
performed for minimizing the risk of a severe uncontrolled system separation, loss
of generation or system blackout [31]. Furthermore, IEEE states the advantages of
UFLS in the restoration philosophy after a disturbance in the Guide for the Applica-
tion of Protective Relays Used for Abnormal Frequency Load Shedding and Restora-
tion [31], where also the functioning of the different under-frequency relays utilized
for UFLS can be consulted. However, this project based on dynamic simulations
does not reach the level of detail of how these relays are activated. Nevertheless, it
must be noticed that already IEEE states the formulated problem in this document,
advising to have into account during the design of UFLS schemes the possible shed
of generation.
Frequency instability usually appears in large system when they split into differ-
ent islands [2]. This fact is also considered by ENTSO-e [20]. The reason is that the
split of large power systems causes the sudden deﬁcit and surplus of large quantities
of power in the remaining islands. Therefore, the analysis carried out in this thesis
will focus on different islands within the CE Synchronous Area.
UFLS schemes can be categorized in three groups: Traditional Load Shedding
schemes, Semi-adaptive schemes [34] and Adaptive schemes [1]. The traditional
scheme is the most extended nowadays and the one utilized regarding the created
model. It is based on simple under-frequency relays which shed a certain percentage
of load when the set frequency thresholds are reached during the system frequency
decrease. If the load shed is not enough for stabilizing the system frequency and it
continues decreasing, the thresholds of the following load shedding stages might be
reached, and consequently more load would be shed. The set thresholds and per-
centages of load shed in each load shedding stage are decided based on previous
experience and different simulations [7].
Regarding the CE Synchronous Area, ENTSO-e states some recommendations
and minimum requirements regarding the UFLS schemes that TSOs apply in each
country [20]. Due to its relevance for model created in this thesis, this information is
provided and further analyzed in Chapter 3.2.

consequences of the automatic disconnection of DGs from the grid which occurred
in the past in some countries within the CE synchronous area. This issue was alerted
by ENTSO-e through its report "Assessment of the System Security with respect to

The aforementioned report notiﬁed the disconnection of signiﬁcant PV capacity

in the functioning of UFLS schemes could be the weather conditions that determine

This chapter deals with the description of the model created in this thesis. Firstly,

4.1
Introduction
As previously stated, this thesis is focused on the study of under-frequency events
where UFLS schemes need to be utilized. Therefore, the disturbance introduced in
the model will always lead to a situation where production does not meet the load.
It must be noted that situations where generation exceeds the consumption are eas-
ier to handle and less threatening to the system security [30].
When a frequency deviation occurs, it is ﬁrst offset by the kinetic energy stored
in the rotating machines, generators and motors. On the other hand, the existence
of frequency-dependent loads also adds some stabilizing effect to the system. Af-
ter a disturbance occurs, these kinds of loads are able to regulate themselves. This
effect is modelled by means of the system damping (D) [34]. However, the sys-
tem inertia and the damping might be not enough to handle severe disturbances.
For this reason, regulating units perform automatically primary control if the devia-
tion is greater than the value speciﬁed for the primary reserve activation in Chapter
3.2. The primary reserve goal is to re-establish the balance between the generation
and the consumption, stabilizing the system frequency at a new steady-state value.
However, the primary control does not restore the frequency to its nominal value
[22].
If the primary control does not fulﬁll its goal of stabilizing the system frequency
after the disturbance, the system frequency could reach the threshold for the activa-
tion of the UFLS scheme. By means of this system protection scheme, the balance
between the generation and the consumption is accomplished by the successive dis-
connection of load feeders at the distribution level. However, as previously com-
mented, due to the increase of distributed generators, not only load is shed but a
signiﬁcant share of generation might be also disconnected.

the grid response to a power deviation is modelled through the equation of motion,
depending on the system inertia and damping. For any disturbance introduced in
the system, there will be a frequency deviation from the nominal value. Finally, if
the size of the disturbance is enough, primary control and UFLS algorithms might
be activated for stabilizing the frequency drop.
In this model, the power disturbance is implemented as a step signal. The size
of the disturbance can be varied for different dynamic simulations. As shown in the
Sum Block, it is implemented with a negative sign meaning a loss of power gener-
ation in the system. On the other hand, both the primary control output and the
shed of load are introduced as positive signals since their effects are respectively
equivalent to the increase of generation or to the decrease of the load. However, the
primary reserve might be negative if the frequency surpasses the nominal value.

Primary Reserve Equivalent Unit
It is of great importance the correct modelling of turbine-governor models when
dealing with frequency control and stability analysis in power systems. The rea-
son is that the primary reserve is implemented through this kind of models. As a
consequence, the frequency response after a disturbance in the model is directly de-
pendent on the correct functioning of the selected turbine-governor model.
As previously commented, the primary reserve is modelled by means of a single
generating unit equivalent to all the generators that comprise the primary reserve
in the power system. This can be done under the assumption that all of these units
share the same type of generating technology, thus they have the same modelling
parameters. This assumption is commonly accepted in frequency control and stabil-
ity analysis since it simpliﬁes the models without obtaining signiﬁcant differences.
Furthermore, in reality, the vast majority of units providing primary control share
the production technology, i.e fossil fuel power plants. According to this, and fol-

UFLS Algorithm
As shown in Figure 4.1, the UFLS block has a single input, the frequency deviation.
On the other hand, the output is the per unit net load shed, i.e taking into account
the shed of distributed generation.
The UFLS algorithm is presented in Figure 4.6 by means of a ﬂowchart. For
the understanding of the designed algorithm, the following considerations must be
taken into account:
• Instantaneous Frequency finst: During each dynamic simulation, the instanta-
neous system frequency is calculated by adding the nominal frequency to the
frequency deviation.
• Instantaneous Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF): Furthermore, the frequency
derivative is calculated and ﬁltered. This practice must be done when deal-
ing with the RoCoF calculation within dynamics simulations in order to avoid
noise and zero values. These facts could lead to the wrong functioning of the
model.

(fk) for the activation of each UFLS stage (k). The UFLS stages are stated in Ta-

According to the algorithm, the UFLS scheme is only activated if the instanta-
neous frequency drops under the frequency thresholds. Furthermore, it is checked
that the instantaneous RoCoF is negative, i.e. frequency continues dropping. On the
other hand, if the frequency starts to increase (RoCoF > 0) after the UFLS action, it
is ensured that the last percentage of load shed is maintained. This is done by im-
plementing a high stopping frequency (fStop).
In fact, if this last measure is not implemented, oscillations around the threshold
frequencies would be created. Indeed, the load shedding action would be discon-
nected, or return to the previous stage percentage every time that the frequency
increases. Consequently, the frequency will drop again reaching the same threshold,
entering in a loop of oscillations around the threshold. For the same reason, the al-
gorithm internally stores the last percentage of load shed and compares it with the
new one, maintaining always the largest amount of load shed during the clearance
of the disturbance.

Commonly, under-frequency load shedding occurs after events that result in large
interconnected power system split into islands. A well-known example is the UCTE

FIGURE 5.3: UCTE split into three islands during 4th November 2006
disturbance [35]

they suffer a frequency drop to about 49 Hz. The decreasing frequency was stopped
by the automatic activation of the under-frequency load shedding schemes in the
affected countries.
On the other hand, the North-Eastern Area countries handled a surplus of gen-
eration that led to an over-frequency situation where frequency reached 51.4 Hz.
Finally, the South-Eastern Area countries encountered a slight deﬁcit of power
which conducted the frequency to about 49.7 Hz without serious consequences.

ing the UFLS action increased, even more, the initial mismatch between the genera-
tion and the consumption. This disturbance was cleared with around 17 GW of load
shed and almost 11 GW of tripped generation in the Western area [20]. One of the
main conclusions regarding the 4th November disturbance was the absence of con-
trol regarding the decentralized generation and the necessity of having into account
DG when designing the defence plans in power systems [35].

5.4.4
Suggestion of an improved UFLS Scheme
In this Study Case, the considered island for the dynamic simulations has a greater
size than in Study Case 1, comprised only by West Denmark and Germany. Further-
more, as shown in Table A.1 in Appendix A, there are signiﬁcant differences in the
PV capacities among the countries forming part of Study Case 2. These both facts
can be utilized for improving the load shedding scheme together with the previous
conclusions and the characteristics of the Study Case 2.
==PAGE:86==
5.4. Study Case 2
69
The idea is to redistribute the percentage of load that each country must shed in
each UFLS stage by taking into account its particular characteristics. Speciﬁcally, by
considering their PV capacities. In this way, those countries with more distributed
generation can be avoided of shedding high percentage of loads, because of the PV
panels connected to the distribution network. This decrease in their participation in
the load shedding scheme would be assumed by countries where distributed gen-
eration is not very extended. However, in order to design a fair UFLS scheme that
pleases all the involved countries, the countries with less PV capacity must be com-
pensated somehow.
As previously discussed, the majority of the DGs are PV panels. In fact, this
project only considers this technology connected to the distribution network. There-
fore, it is concluded that distributed generation only affects during the sunlight
hours. This fact can be considered in order to propose a fair load shedding scheme.
Speciﬁcally, those countries with large PV capacity could assume higher percentages
of load shed during the night hours, while they can maintain their distributed gen-
eration during sunlight hours by shedding less load in comparison with the rest of
countries.
It must be clariﬁed that this proposed UFLS scheme is realized from an academic
point of view. The involved countries and the CE synchronous area coordinator,
ENTSO-e, would be in charge of discussing the possibility of implementing this kind
of UFLS schemes. Therefore, the proposed UFLS is just a demonstration of how the
security of the system could be improved by safeguarding the distributed genera-
tion of being shed in a system island like the one presented in Study Case 2.
Based on the previous reasoning, the countries involved in the Study Case 2 are
divided into two groups which will switch their UFLS scheme depending on the
hour of the day. Moreover, the relative load of each country in the whole system
was taken into account when making the division, in order to have two groups with
a similar amount of consumption.
• Group 1: Countries with large PV capacity. These countries would shed more
load during the nights if the UFLS is activated. In turn, during the sunlight,
they would shed less load and their distributed generation would be safe-
guarded by the rest of countries. This ﬁrst group is comprised by Italy, Spain
and France.
• Group 2: The rest of the countries of the Study Case 2 would assume the pro-
tection of the system during the sunlight hours, shedding more load in case of
the UFLS activation. On the contrary, during the night these countries would
shed less load than the countries in Group 1.
The proposed load shedding scheme is stated in Table 5.7. This load shedding
scheme has been designed base on ENTSO-e rules. Therefore, according to the reg-
ulation, every country shed at least 5% of their load at 49 Hz. Furthermore, there is
not any UFLS stage in which the additional shed of load surpasses the 10% of the
load. Finally, the hours where the UFLS changes among the two groups of countries
were decided based on the PV generation proﬁles for the analyzed case.

6.1
Main Conclusions
This thesis analyzes the impact of the weather on under-frequency load shedding in
power systems. Speciﬁcally, the weather impact is measured by means of the chang-
ing renewable distributed generation. The analysis focuses on isolated areas with
generation deﬁcit after the occurrence of an out of range incident which splits the
CE synchronous area. In the following, the main conclusions reached are summa-
rized:
Firstly, in view of the veriﬁcation test it is concluded that the created model be-
haves according to the ENTSO-e requirements. Therefore, the developed model is
valid for the simulation of the CE synchronous area. Furthermore, with the aim of
continuing with this work, the model was created ensuring that is easily adaptable
for simulating new study cases.
An analysis was carried out for determining the share of renewable DGs within
the countries that comprise the analyzed system. This investigation highlighted the
necessity of an accurate inventory of renewable DGs within the CE synchronous
area. The inventory should contain a detailed list of DGs with crucial information
such as the disconnection settings of these units. This information would help to the
realization of more accurate studies. In this way, the distributed generation which
continues without fulﬁlling the disconnection requirements could be appropriately
modelled. Furthermore, the model could use the exact distributed generation capac-
ity instead of estimating it. Consequently, not only PV distributed generation could
be included but also wind and other distributed sources of energy.
Regarding the obtained results, it has been demonstrated the importance of tak-
ing into account the increasing distributed generation within the CE synchronous
area when designing the under-frequency load shedding schemes. Furthermore,
in both the cases analyzed it is demonstrated the relevance of the system load for
the correct functioning of the UFLS scheme. In fact, the vulnerability of the system
depends directly on how the system is loaded. When the system load is high, the
impact of the distributed generation is less signiﬁcant and the stability of the system
is not compromised. Nevertheless, the DGs could lead to the disconnection of more
UFLS stages. The individual conclusions for each case are the following:
==PAGE:93==
76
Chapter 6. Conclusions & Future Work
• Study Case 1: West Denmark and Germany: According to the previous con-
clusions, a small isolated area like the one proposed in this study case is more
vulnerable due to its lower system load. This fact is even more crucial in the
weekends when the customer’s consumption is lower. In fact, the vulnera-
bility of the system is endangered during the sunlight hours of these days in
the warmer months (from April to September). The reason is the signiﬁcant PV
distributed generation in the mentioned period. Finally, it is also demonstrated
the risk of blackout during the Sunday’s mornings of these months since they
are the less loaded hours along the year. The blackouts occur in those morn-
ings when the PV distributed generation is very large.
• Study Case 2: West Europe: The size of the area considered in this study case
is larger compared to the previous one. Consequently, due to the higher load,
the stability of this system is not compromised. The UFLS scheme can han-
dle the disturbance by using its two ﬁrst stages. However, the disconnection
of distributed generation during the sunlight hours leads to reach the second
UFLS stage.
A second UFLS scheme is proposed in order to improve the obtained results.
Speciﬁcally, based on the distributed generation capacity, two groups of coun-
tries switch their contribution to the UFLS scheme depending on the hour of
the day. In this way, the countries without signiﬁcant share of distributed gen-
eration safeguard the DGs by shedding more percentage of load during the
sunlight hours. On the other hand, during the night they shed less load if
a disturbance occurs. It is demonstrated that this strategy is successful since
the nadir keeps always above 49 Hz. Furthermore, the shed of load and dis-
tributed generation is reduced.
6.2
Future Work
As previously stated, this thesis demonstrates the relevance of distributed genera-
tion regarding UFLS schemes. However, further analysis can be realized.
Firstly, it would be important to have available an accurate inventory of the
distributed generators within the CE synchronous area. This is crucial in order to
consider the exact amount of generation disconnected during the simulated distur-
bance. In fact, since only PV distributed generation has been considered in the sim-
ulations, the inclusion of wind distributed generation and other DGs might lead to
more severe results. Therefore, although it was estimated that wind DGs capacity is
not so signiﬁcant, the analysis should be carried out considering all DGs.
On the other hand, it was commented the difﬁculty of knowing in advance the
islanding patterns after an out of range disturbance. Furthermore, only two individ-
ual cases were analyzed within the CE synchronous area. The two analyzed cases
in this thesis are representative and relevant conclusions were reached. However,
more cases might be simulated emphasizing on the analysis of the splitting borders.
==PAGE:94==
6.2. Future Work
77
Finally, it was demonstrated that the proposed UFLS scheme for study case 2
leads to the improvement of the results. However, it was also mentioned the difﬁ-
culty of implementing such a scheme in a fair way for all the implicated countries.
The same reasoning for creating the proposed UFLS scheme could be used at a na-
tional level. Instead of creating groups of countries, the safeguard of distributed gen-
erators could be realized within each country by its different regions. This could be a
solution easier to implement. However, it would be necessary an accurate inventory
with the location of the DGs and a further individual analysis for each country.
Due to the ﬂexibility of the created model, all the proposed future work could
be realized with the same model. The new inventory of DGs could be easily used
instead of the estimations realized in this thesis. Furthermore, using the main model
as template, all the new desired cases (different isolated areas) could be simulated
by selecting the countries comprising the new analysis. Finally, regarding the appli-
cation of the proposed UFLS scheme within a country, the country should be split
in the regions considered in the model. This last implementation could be the most
challenging since the hourly load proﬁles of each region would be necessary. How-
ever, a ﬁrst estimation based on each region characteristics, such as its population or
industrial activity among others, could be utilized.
