Abstract
In the era of digitization, computing devices have become integral to various
operations, with the operating system (OS) playing a pivotal role in dictating
eciency, security, and adaptability. While mainstream OSs cater to general-
purpose tasks, there exists a specialized category known as Real-time Operat-
ing Systems (RTOS). These are designed for mission-critical applications where
precise timing is paramount.
The research herein delves into the intricacies
of RTOS, focusing on Zephyr, known for its thread-based model, and Lingua
Franca, a reactor-based programming approach. The study aims to understand
Zephyr's platform for real-time timings and the enhancements Lingua Franca
can oer. The methodology involves an overview of RTOS concepts, dissecting
various programming models, and rigorous testing of timing prociencies. The
research also contrasts insights from the RTOS realm with the more ubiquitous
OS environment, particularly Linux, to assess the adaptability of RTOS mod-
els in broader computational contexts. The ndings aim to shed light on the
potential of these systems in real-time operations and propose forward-looking
solutions.

Introduction
In today's digital age, computing devices are essential for both everyday and
specialized tasks.
Central to these devices is the operating system (OS), a
crucial software layer that not only facilitates user-hardware interactions but
also dictates the eciency, security, and adaptability of computational tasks.
Mainstream operating systems such as Windows, MacOS, and Ubuntu cater to
general-purpose tasks, balancing user experience, application support, and re-
source management. In contrast, specialized OS categories, like Red Hat Enter-

However, there's a unique and critical OS category: the Real-time Operating
Systems (RTOS). Employed in mission-critical applications ranging from medi-
cal devices to aerospace systems, RTOS is crafted for tasks where precise timing
is non-negotiable.
The hallmark of an RTOS is its unwavering commitment to meeting system
deadlines, with its timing capabilities being of utmost importance.
This re-
search dives deep into the intricacies of RTOS, highlighting programming mod-
els that cater to their specialized requirements. Specically, we are intrigued by
Zephyr, known for its thread-based operational model, and Lingua Franca (LF),
a reactor-based programming approach. Our objective is twofold: rstly, to un-

Chapter 2
Real-Time Operating
Systems
This chapter delves into Real-Time Operating Systems, emphasizing their pre-
dictability and timing. We distinguish between real-time systems and real-time
operating systems, categorize them based on their deadline requirements, and
explore the unique design principles of RTOS. The chapter also covers the role

ing thread execution. Finally, we discuss various scheduling policies tailored for

2.1
Introduction to real-time systems
Real-time systems, not to be confused with 'real-time operating systems', are
computing systems that must adhere to stringent timing constraints, ensuring
that tasks are completed within a specic, predened time frame. The den-
ing characteristic of these systems is not necessarily their speed but their pre-
dictability and consistency in meeting deadlines. Failing to meet these deadlines
could result in degraded performance, system failures, or, in critical applications,
catastrophic outcomes.
==PAGE:18==
4
Real-Time Operating Systems
Real-time systems are prevalent in a myriad of applications, from industrial
automation and telecommunications to multimedia and nancial systems. They
integrate both software and hardware components, often employing specialized
operating systems (Real-Time Operating Systems) and programming languages
designed to handle real-time requirements eciently.
2.2
Deadlines (Dierent real-time systems)
In the realm of real-time systems, they are classied into three distinct cate-
gories: soft, rm, and hard real-time systems. The type depends entirely on the
system's requirements. Factors to consider are the repercussions if the system
fails to meet its deadline and whether such failures have mild or severe impacts
on the environment.
1. Soft Real-Time System:
 Denition: A soft real-time system is one where the deadlines are
important but missing an occasional deadline is acceptable and does
not cause catastrophic failure.
It is more focused on throughput
than on strict timing guarantees. Provides good average response
time, with occasional deadline misses. The system performance might
degrade, but the system will continue to operate.
 Example: Streaming media (like video playback) is a common exam-
ple. If a few frames are dropped occasionally, the overall experience is
not largely compromised, although the quality may diminish slightly.
2. Firm Real-Time System:
 Denition: In rm real-time systems, missing a deadline is undesir-
able and might lead to degraded performance, but it does not have
catastrophic consequences. However, frequent misses are not toler-
able. Provides a guarantee that deadlines will be met with a high
probability. Infrequent deadline misses might be acceptable, but re-
peated misses are not.
 Example: Some robotic control systems where occasional misses might
mean decreased eciency or quality but not a complete system fail-
ure.
3. Hard Real-Time System:
 Denition: A hard real-time system is one where it is absolutely im-
perative that responses occur within the required deadline. Failure to

meet the deadline can result in catastrophic outcomes. Provides strict
guarantees that deadlines will always be met. Any missed deadline
is considered a system failure.
 Example: Control systems in industries, medical life support systems,
or avionic control systems where missing a deadline might result in
the loss of life or serious damage to equipment or the environment.
2.3
Real-time operating systems
Real-time systems require reliability, consistency, and adherence to strict dead-
lines. Real-time Operating Systems are engineered specically to cater to these
needs, providing an environment that ensures the system can reliably meet their
stringent time constraints. This section delves into the unique design principles
of RTOS that enable them to prociently support real-time systems. Real-time
systems are often mission-critical. The RTOS, thus, is built with certain dening
features that cater to these requirements[OHT16]:
 Predictability: Unlike general-purpose OSes, an RTOS is designed to
provide guaranteed response times, ensuring that tasks complete within a
known timeframe.
 Minimal Overhead: To reduce latency, RTOSes have a minimal memory
footprint and ecient task-switching mechanisms.
 Network Readiness: For applications like IoT devices, RTOSes have
built-in networking capabilities.
 Energy Eciency:
As many real-time applications run on battery-
operated devices, RTOSes focus on power eciency.
 Security: Given the critical nature of real-time applications, RTOSes
come equipped with security features to prevent unauthorized access and
tampering.
The characteristics previously described serve as guiding objectives for an RTOS.
However, the weight given to each characteristic can vary based on the specic
environment the system is deployed in. Achieving these objectives necessitates
an comprehensive design process that accounts for a multitude of aspects. Ev-
ery design decision has an eect throughout the operating system, aecting
everything from its real-time performance to its development approach.

Real-Time Operating Systems
To provide a comprehensive understanding, we will explore not just the technical
design aspects but also the comprehensive lifecycle of an RTOS. We will try to
show the dierent facets that formulate an reliable RTOS in practice, and show
what impact they have on the timing aspect. As a case in point, we will delve
into the Zephyr OS, elucidating its design emphasis and the rationale behind its
development choices, and how well they support reliable timing mechanisms.
The comprehensive survey detailed in [OHT16] outlines the essential design
features of IoT devices. Interestingly, many of these features overlap with the
requirements of an RTOS. With slight modications, we can shift our focus to
emphasize the real-time capabilities. These design features are divided into two
main categories: technical properties and non-technical properties.
Technical properties
 General Architecture and Modularity:
The kernel type determines the system's architecture and modularity.[OHT16].
 Microkernel: The microkernel adopts a distinct approach, striv-
ing to run the majority of the system's services in user space. This
isolates dierent functionalities and minimizes the code running in
kernel space. Such separation can bolster robustness and exibility;
a failure in one service does not necessarily compromise the entire
system. Additionally, it facilitates system extensions and modica-
tions without impacting the core kernel. On the ip side, the height-
ened modularity might introduce overhead, potentially diminishing
performance relative to monolithic kernels.
 Exokernel: Designed to oer minimal abstraction between the hard-
ware and the application layer, the exokernel allows applications to
directly manage and access hardware resources. This direct access
can enhance performance and eciency due to reduced overhead.
However, the downside is that applications must be meticulously de-
signed to safely and eciently manage hardware resources.
 Monolithic Kernel: In a monolithic kernel, all OS services operate
within a unied address space: the kernel space. This encompasses
everything from le system management to device drivers. This in-
tegrated design can be highly ecient, given the swift inter-process
communication and the elimination of frequent switches between user
and kernel modes. However, a drawback is the system's vulnerability;
a malfunction in one service (e.g., a driver crash) could jeopardize the
entire system. Moreover, updating or modifying a monolithic kernel
can be intricate, as changes in one segment might inuence others.

 Scheduling Model:
The scheduler aects energy, real-time capabilities, and throughput. There
are preemptive (interrupts any task) and cooperative (tasks yield them-
selves) schedulers.
Preemptive scheduling divides CPU time, while in
cooperative, tasks manage themselves.
 Programming Model:
OS programming models included multithreaded programming model, event
driven, and more. Programming models play a pivotal role in operating
systems, shaping how computations are organized, scheduled, and exe-
cuted.
 Driver Model and Hardware Abstraction Layer:
IoT devices interact with the environment, requiring diverse drivers and
hardware abstraction for ease of porting. A well-structured hardware ab-
straction and driver model aids design but has overhead costs.
 Debugging Tools:
Depending on the programming language, debugging tools vary, but hard-
ware limitations can dictate their use. Standard languages allow for well-
known debugging tools, but hardware might need simpler methods.
 Testing:
IoT OS development requires rigorous testing, especially in distributed
workows. Emulators and simulators help with hardware and network-
related tests.
Testing for IoT is challenging due to its distributed and
embedded nature but remains vital.
Non-Technical properties
 Documentation:
Crucial for understanding and maximizing the OS, especially for embedded
systems where compromises might be made. Comprehensive documenta-
tion is essential for eective OS usage.
 Maturity of the Code:
A reection of project age, number of contributors, and users. This is also

2.6
Scheduler
The scheduler in an operating system is instrumental in directing and coordinat-
ing thread execution, ensuring optimal resource use and timely task completion.
To address the complexities of concurrent execution, the scheduler integrates
synchronization mechanisms. It supports mechanisms like locks, mutexes, and
semaphores to allow threads to safely access shared resources, preventing in-
consistencies or deadlocks. Additionally, the scheduler ensures fairness in CPU
time allocation, setting priorities, and maintaining overall control.
Threads are executed based on their priority, ensuring critical tasks receive im-
mediate attention and less urgent ones queue up. In single-core systems, one
approach is for the scheduler to use time-sharing, allocating each thread a spe-
cic time slice. When a thread's time expires, it re-queues, and the next thread
gets CPU time, simulating parallelism. Synchronization mechanisms, such as
locks and mutexes, are used to prevent simultaneous access to shared resources,
which can cause data inconsistencies and deadlocks. Techniques like priority in-
heritance can also be employed by the scheduler, where a lower-priority thread
holding a resource needed by a higher-priority thread temporarily inherits the
higher priority. External events or interrupts might prompt the scheduler to
adjust the current execution order, allowing urgent tasks to preempt ongoing
threads.
However, the scheduler's objectives and principles can vary based on the oper-
ating system's design and intended use.
In an RTOS scheduler, predictability is paramount. Tasks have strict deadlines,
and the scheduler ensures timely starts and completions. Missing a deadline,
especially in a hard RTOS, can be disastrous. Conversely, Non-RTOS Sched-
ulers, like the "SCHED_OTHER: Default Linux time-sharing scheduling"[Lin],
prioritize fairness and throughput maximization, ensuring equitable CPU dis-
tribution for all processes. In the pursuit of maximizing throughput, non-RTOS
Schedulers might curtail preemption, with some adopting a cooperative schedul-
ing approach.
Conversely, an RTOS Scheduler will readily preempt an active task when a task
of higher priority is queued, a measure essential for adhering to stringent timing
constraints. While non-RTOS Schedulers might dynamically adjust thread pri-
orities, often based on wait duration and emphasizing fairness, RTOS Schedulers
rigidly adhere to task priorities to ensure predictability and determinism.

highest priority to the task with least slack time, which is the task closest

precise knowledge of how long task runs.

For highly static systems with minimal external inuences, a Time-driven

When the runtime of each task is known, LST can also be a viable option in

The event-driven programming model in operating systems is a design approach

The Actor model is a paradigm that provides a high-level abstraction for con-

The Actor model adopts the philosophy that everything is an actor. An actor is
a computational entity that primarily responds to messages by sending messages
to other actors, creating new actors, or changing its state. The implementation

by having separate memory spaces. This separation ensures that corruption
in one actor's memory does not necessarily corrupt another actor's memory.
Coordination between actors is achieved through message passing. Every action
in the system is a reaction to a response. Combining this with a total ordering
of events allows for a lock-free concurrent system.
The Actor model inherently supports concurrency and parallelism. Since actors
operate independently and communicate via messages, they can run in parallel,
making the model suitable for distributed and multi-core systems.
The advantages of this model include scalability, fault tolerance, and modularity.
Each actor is an independent execution unit, allowing the system to spread
across not only one processor but also a network of machines. The actor model's

4.5
Synchronous reactive programming model
The Synchronous Reactive (SR) Programming Model is fundamentally anchored
on the "synchrony hypothesis" [BG92]. This hypothesis suggests that a system's
reactions to external events are perceived as immediate, regardless of the internal
computation time. In other words, the intricacies of internal processing times
are abstracted away, presenting the illusion of instantaneous response.
Execution within the SR model is divided into distinct "logical instants." Dur-
ing each instant, the system performs a sequence of actions: it reads inputs,
processes computations, and produces outputs. Interestingly, from an external
perspective, all operations within an instant seem to occur at once. This model's
dening feature is its unwavering determinism. Even when multiple threads or
concurrent operations are in play, the SR model guarantees that a given set of
inputs will always result in the same outputs, regardless of the internal operation
order.
Communication in the SR model is predominantly event-driven, relying on sig-
nals. These signals can be both generated and evaluated within a single logical
instant, enabling real-time feedback mechanisms. Additionally, the model sup-
ports the parallel execution of tasks. To preserve the essence of synchrony, all
concurrent tasks are synchronized at the end of each logical instant, setting the
stage for the subsequent instant.
Esterel, a programming language, epitomizes the principles of the Synchronous
Reactive model. At its heart, Esterel is designed to "react" to external events,
mirroring the SR model's synchrony hypothesis.

ing, computation, and output generation. Esterel's communication mechanism
is built around signals, facilitating swift responses and feedback loops. The lan-
guage also supports parallelism, allowing for concurrent threads of execution.
Crucially, Esterel ensures that these threads are synchronized at each instant's
conclusion, reinforcing the model's deterministic nature. As a result, Esterel
provides the assurance that given inputs will predictably produce consistent
outputs.

4.6
Extended Reactor model of LF
The Reactor model, as described in [LMBL21][JLD+23], introduces a unique
approach to synchronous reactive model4.5. At its core, the Reactor Concept
distinguishes reactors from actors. Unlike actors, reactors do not communicate
directly with their peers.
Instead, they employ named and typed ports for
interconnections. These ports serve as conduits for events; when one reactor
generates an event, it is observed by any other reactor connected to that specic
port. The system is designed such that events enter through input ports, while
reactions are responsible for sending out events via output ports.
Adding to the complexity, reactors introduce "actions," a nuanced version of
ports. These actions dier from standard ports in their visibility; they remain
concealed from other reactors and cannot be connected in the traditional sense.
Their primary role is twofold: scheduling future events within the same reac-
tor and synchronizing between the reactor's code and external asynchronous
triggers.
A dening feature of the reactor model is its embrace of Logical Time. Every
event within this system carries a distinct tag, and the reactions to these events
are synchronized to this tag's logical time. This synchronization ensures that
during a reaction, time remains static. Furthermore, the system is designed
to prevent any input port from holding more than one event at a given logical
time. This meticulous tagging ensures that the output from a reaction and its
triggering input event are logically simultaneous.
Lastly, the Reactor model champions Deterministic Execution. It is structured
to promote both modularity and the potential for distributed operations. By
intertwining the logical time model, reactors ensure a deterministic outcome.
This deterministic nature is further amplied when logical time is mapped to
real-world time, facilitating the creation of distributed reactor systems that
operate with predictability[LMBL21].

6.4.2
Kernel Timing Implementation
The kernel's internal timing is fundamentally based on a tick-based mechanism.
Each tick serves as a unit of time that the kernel uses to schedule threads,
manage timeouts, and perform other time-dependent operations. The system
timer module oversees this function. In our context, the system timer is tied
to the SysTick timer, which is an inherent component of the Cortex-M0+ CPU
and acts as the primary source for the system's timekeeping.
The kernel uses a global doubly-linked list, known as timeout_list, to manage
all time-dependent events. Each node in this list is of type struct _timeout
and contains information about when a specic event is supposed to occur. This
list is sorted by the time at which these events are scheduled to happen.
SysTick Implementation of Timeout
The SysTick timer is congured to
generate an interrupt at regular intervals, the duration of which is determined
by the CONFIG_SYS_CLOCK_TICKS_PER_SEC setting. When this interrupt occurs,
the sys_clock_isr() function is invoked. This function serves as the Interrupt
Service Routine (ISR) for the SysTick timer and is directly hooked into the
vector table, bypassing any ISR wrappers.
The behavior of sys_clock_isr() varies depending on the operating mode of
Zephyr tickless or non-tickless. In non-tickless mode, the function simply calls
sys_clock_announce(1), indicating that one tick has passed. In tickless mode,
the function calculates the number of ticks that have elapsed since its last invoca-
tion, denoted as dticks, and passes this value to sys_clock_announce(dticks).
The sys_clock_announce() function is responsible for managing the timeout_list.
It dequeues the struct _timeout elements whose time has come (i.e., their tick
count is less than or equal to the current tick count) and invokes their associated
callback functions. After handling these immediate timeouts, it looks ahead in
the timeout_list to determine when the next timeout will occur and recong-
ures the SysTick timer accordingly.
This architecture ensures that all kernel modules and applications that rely on
time-based events are served in a timely manner. The timeout_list serves as a
centralized mechanism for managing all such events, making the system ecient
and easier to debug.

An alternative to the previously mentioned solution using semaphores is pro-

Zephyr's Work Queue API oers a robust mechanism for asynchronously exe-

the initialization of work items with specic handler functions and their sub-

in Appendix B. The basic functions are.
 k_work_init(): Initializes a work item.
 k_work_submit(): Submits a work item to the system work queue.
 k_work_init_delayable(): Initializes a delayable work item.
A work queue in Zephyr is a kernel object designed to manage and execute
work items in a FIFO (First-In, First-Out) manner. It operates on a dedicated
thread, the priority of which can be congured. This thread processes work
items in the queue, executing a specied function for each item.
The state
of each work item is managed through ags, with atomic operations ensuring

7.1
Introduction to Lingua Franca
Lingua Franca (LF) is a polyglot coordination language designed for concurrent
and potentially time-sensitive applications. These applications can range from
complex embedded systems to expansive cloud and edge computing solutions.
LF programs orchestrate interactions between components known as "reactors,"
each of which is implemented in one of the supported target languages: C, C++,
Python, Rust, and TypeScript. The language aims to facilitate deterministic
interactions while managing timing aspects explicitly. A dedicated code gener-
ator translates LF programs into the chosen target language, which can then be
compiled using standard toolchains.
LF has been ported to run on top of the Zephyr real-time operating system.
This integration combines the deterministic and performance benets of LF
with Zephyr's real-time capabilities.[JLD+23].

7.2
Core Concepts of Reactors
Reactors serve as the computational units in LF, and their functionality is di-
vided into "reactions." These reactions are written in the target languages and
are triggered by events, timers, or actions. This section provides an overview of
the key components and features of reactors.
 Components: Reactors consist of triggers, input ports, actions, timers,
output ports, local states, parameters, and reactions.
 Reactions: These are procedures in a target language that execute in
response to a trigger. They must declare all inputs and outputs.
 Events: Events are timestamped triggers that can be messages, timers,
or actions. These events activate reactions at specic logical times.
 Flow of Time: Reactions execute at strictly increasing logical times, and
unread messages are discarded.

 Advancement of Time: Time progresses to the next event's logical time
after all initial reactions are completed.
 Superdense Time: Allows multiple events at the same numerical time
but dierent microsteps.
 Execution Order: Reactions in the same reactor triggered simultane-

The logical time in LF follows the real-time (physical time in LF). However

The LF scheduler employs two distinct queues, the reaction queue and the event
queue.

triggers at consistent logical time intervals. For example, an action might be
planned to occur 200ms after an input is detected. it is important to note that
these actions are always scheduled within the reactor that introduces them.
Conversely, Physical actions aim to schedule reactions based on real-world
time, using the local physical clock. For instance, an action might be set to
occur precisely when an input is detected. External sources, like other threads

for receiving input from the surrounding environment.

Before LF begins executing reactors and their associated reactions, an initial-

Fast and Keepalive Properties
In LF, the keepalive and fast properties have specic impacts on program
execution. The keepalive property ensures that the LF program remains active
even in the absence of scheduled events, allowing it to respond to unpredictable
external inputs. Conversely, without keepalive, the program would terminate
once all events have been processed.
The fast property enables the program to execute without real-time synchro-
nization, processing events as quickly as possible. This is particularly useful for
simulations where real-time synchronization is unnecessary.
7.6.3

The single-threaded LF execution revolves around two queues: the reaction
queue and the event queue. The reaction queue holds a sorted list of reactions,
prioritized by Earliest Deadline First (EDF) in this implementation. The event
queue contains all scheduled events.

queue. The timer module and logical time are initialized.

The PREEMPT_RT (Real-Time Preemption) patch is a set of kernel patches that
transform Linux into a real-time operating system.
The main goal of the
PREEMPT_RT patch is to minimize the latency of the system and make it more
predictable. Here is a brief overview of what the PREEMPT_RT patch does to
achieve this:
 Fully Preemptible Kernel: One of the primary features of the PREEMPT_RT
patch is that it makes almost every part of the kernel preemptible, with the
exception of a few very small regions of code (raw_spinlock_t). This
means that real-time tasks can preempt non-real-time tasks, and even
parts of the kernel, ensuring that they get CPU time when they need it.
 Priority Inheritance: Priority inversion is a problem where a higher-
priority task is blocked waiting for a lower-priority task to release a re-
source. The PREEMPT_RT patch introduces priority inheritance for spin-
locks and semaphores. This means that if a low-priority task holds a lock
==PAGE:80==
66
Dive into Linux
that a high-priority task needs, the low-priority task temporarily inher-
its the higher priority to nish its work and release the lock, preventing
priority inversion.
 High-Resolution Timers: The patch introduces high-resolution timers,
allowing for more precise control over when tasks are scheduled to run.
 Threaded IRQ Handlers: In standard Linux, interrupt handlers run in
a special context that cannot be preempted. With PREEMPT_RT, interrupt
handlers are threaded and run as standard kernel threads, allowing them
to be preempted if necessary. This ensures that high-priority tasks can
run even if there's a lot of interrupt activity.
 Modied Spinlocks: In the standard Linux kernel, spinlocks will busy-
wait (spin) until they can acquire the lock. In a real-time system, this
can introduce unpredictable latencies.
The PREEMPT_RT patch replaces
traditional spinlocks with sleeping spinlocks, which put the task to sleep
if the lock is not available, allowing another task to be scheduled.
 Soft IRQs as Threads: In the standard kernel, Soft IRQs run in inter-
rupt context and cannot be preempted. The PREEMPT_RT patch makes Soft
IRQs run as threads, allowing them to be preempted by higher-priority
tasks.
The PREEMPT_RT patch signicantly enhances the real-time capabilities of Linux.
It achieves this by reducing latency and allowing more aggressive preemption.
However, it does not extensively address the variables that can inuence the
upper bounds of the aforementioned factors.

Zephyr as a Native Posix Application
One of the standout features of Zephyr, referenced in 6.1, is its capability to
function as a native POSIX application, enabling it to run on any operating
system that supports POSIX.
Zephyr's native POSIX architecture is crafted to allow Zephyr applications to
run natively on POSIX-compliant operating systems like Linux. This design em-
ulates a single-threaded CPU or MCU by associating each Zephyr thread with
a POSIX pthread. This ensures that only one software thread is active at any
moment, under the guidance of the Zephyr kernel. Such mapping retains trans-
parency for the application, presenting an identical interface to the Zephyr kernel
as other architectures do. During compilation, the code is natively compiled for

the host system, often as a 32-bit binary. All threads employ a standard Linux
pthread stack, while Zephyr thread stack allocation is exclusively for thread
management.
The execution begins with the board's primary function, which manages pre-
liminary tasks like parsing command-line arguments and initializing hardware
models. The "CPU simulation" starts by generating a new pthread, momen-
tarily pausing the original thread. As the embedded software advances, more
Zephyr threads can emerge, each linked to a specic pthread. A hallmark of
this design is its employment of simulated time, separate from real-time, giving
the Zephyr application an illusion of running on an ultra-fast CPU, independent
of the host CPU's actual speed. No simulated time passes during the application
or kernel code's execution.
The native POSIX utilizes a hardware model implemented with distinct threads.
These threads mimic various hardware modules, like timers and device drivers.
Software threads, dened by the application, handle the application's execution,
such as the primary thread, threads initiated by the main thread, and worker
queue threads. As highlighted earlier, the native POSIX architecture simulates
a single-threaded CPU. Given this, combined with the fact that software threads
are non-interruptible by the application, no hardware threads can operate until
a software thread enters a sleep state. This design ensures determinism and
eectively makes software threads cooperative with locked interrupts.
Interrupt management and triggering are also replicated by the hardware mod-
els. They can initiate interrupts to the simulated CPU. These are processed in
the context of the software thread that receives them.
By operating as a native POSIX application, Zephyr gains access to host li-
braries and the host's le system. Linux communicates with the base hardware
via the virtual lesystem, enabling the Zephyr application to interface with
hardware through the lesystem. However, frequent lesystem access can in-
duce overheads, as le operations are overseen by the kernel, leading to context
switches with each le access.

8.3.1
Time in Native_POSIX Zephyr
As described earlier simulated time operates fully independent of the real host
time and progresses as rapidly as the host's computational power permits. This
is ideal for debugging or batch testing but not when interfacing with external
systems that rely on real host time. The Zephyr kernel is solely aware of the sim-
ulated time, as provided by the hardware models. Consequently, any standard
Zephyr thread will only recognize this simulated time.
The sole connection between the simulated time and the real/host time, is es-
tablished by the clock and timer model. This model can be adjusted to de-
celerate the execution of native_posix to real-time. This can be achieved by
CONFIG_NATIVE_POSIX_SLOWDOWN_TO_REAL_TIME.
Enabling the conguration CONFIG_NATIVE_POSIX_SLOWDOWN_TO_REAL_TIME merely
forces the HW model to wait before triggering the next system tick interrupt un-
til the corresponding real/host time is reached. If native_posix operates slower
than real-time, this model will "catch up" as soon as possible by not delaying
subsequent ticks. Thus, if the host load is excessive, you might observe the
simulated time lagging behind the real host time. This approach ensures that
regular runs remain deterministic while oering a semblance of real-time to the
observer.
Without this conguration we are not able to come close to an real-time appli-
caion, without employing a second application to send periodic signals to the
native posix zephyr application, but this would increase the eect that Linux
has on the runtime, since it would potentially double the amount source of
inconsistencies.

Related work
The paper introduced by Akhilesh Murikipudi, V. Prakash, and T. Vigneswaran[MPV15]
underscores the paramount importance of timing in real-time applications. They
they show that in real-time systems, tasks are dierentiated based on their tim-
ing attributes, such as sporadic timing, response time, and deadlines.
The
distinction between hard and soft real-time systems was highlighted, with the
former requiring strict adherence to deadlines, and the latter demanding per-
formance assurances.
The general architecture of an RTOS, as presented in the same study, oers
insights into how timing is managed and maintained. The architecture ensures
that tasks are scheduled and executed based on their priority and timing re-
quirements, ensuring that no deadline is missed.
A comparative analysis between the General Purpose Operation System (GPOS)
and the Real Time Operating System was conducted using a mobile robot as
a real-time application.
The results indicated that RTOS, with an average
response time of 20µs, outperformed GPOS, which had an average response time
of 102µs in a real-time environment. This dierence underscores the dierence
of timing capabilities of RTOS compared to GPOS.
Other studies have delved into the application of RTOS in dierent domains.
For instance, research on the economics of Linux adoption in developing coun-
tries highlighted the increasing use of Linux in real-time applications due to
its multitude of timing capabilities. Another study presented a comparison of
various RTOSs, analyzing parameters like worst-case response time, interrupt
latency, and latency jitter. Such comparisons provide valuable insights into the
timing performance of dierent RTOSs.
In conclusion, the timing in RTOS is a critical aspect that determines the sys-
tem's reliability, and suitability for real-time applications. The study mentioned
above provide a comprehensive understanding of the importance of timing in
RTOS and its implications in various applications.

10.3.4
Discussion
Basic
We begin by discussing the basic results. As observed in Figure 10.1,
the ISR was the fastest, leading by a noticeable margin. This result allows us
to estimate the additional workload managed by the system.
All reaction methods in both LF and Zephyr utilize the same interrupt trigger
in the GPIO, implying they must perform at least the minimum operations of
the ISR, which consists of an interrupt and signaling, taking 10 µsec.
Using the worker queue and subsequently context-switching to that thread in-
curs an additional 34 µsec, as derived from 10.2 "Time from ISR to executing
a dierent thread" and the last 21 µsec are contributed by interacting with the
worker queue.
Comparatively, if we subtract the time required for ISR and signaling, the LF
methods uses an additional 149 µsec to react. This is over four times longer
than the worker queue in Zephyr.
The primary contributor to this extended reaction time for LF is its operational
structure. From 10.2, we understand that switching from ISR back to the inter-
rupted thread consumes 24 µsec. While awaiting an event, LF eectively runs in
a loop, checking a list of conditions as outlined in 7.8. This introduces potential
delays, as depending on the loop's position when the event queue updates, LF
will still process all logic before rechecking the event, leading to added latency.
This delay arises because the LF runtime isn't alerted when an event enters the
queue.
As detailed in 7.8, if there are no reactions in the queue, the system concludes
its operations unless the keepalive property is set. In such cases, while the
reaction queue is empty, LF runs the next() function until the event queue
populates. During the execution of the next() function, interrupts are disabled,
and they are only re-enabled at its conclusion. This can introduce a response
time delay to the interrupt signal.
Once the event queue updates via lf_schedule(signal,0) with a value of 0,
the LF scheduler remains unaware of this update until it revisits the loop's start.
When next() runs again and the event queue isn't empty, the system enters
wait_until as per 7.8, executing all sleeping code before assessing the event's
sleeping duration, contrary to being given a delay of 0.
In summary, the LF code executes a signicant amount of superuous code
before processing the reaction triggered by the ISR.

As previously mentioned in 8.2, numerous factors can inuence real-time execu-

eects, especially when aiming to run Zephyr as a native POSIX application on
Linux while retaining as much real-time capability as possible.

114
Results and discussion
 Cache: Cache coherence can be an issue. Since the Linux kernel is sched-
uled to run and its subroutines are cached (assuming a standard cache
policy), this might inuence Zephyr's runtime. If Zephyr's data is evicted
from the cache, performance could degrade.
 Dynamic Buers: Dynamic buers in Zephyr, when running on Linux,
might be subjected to the memory management policies of the host OS.

potentially aecting real-time guarantees.

 Interrupt Service Overhead: Interrupt servicing can introduce addi-

 Delayed Response System Call: There's no guarantee that a system

for the system to read or write les can vary based on several factors, such

Analysis of Timer Error
An error we discovered in LF, that either causes the system to terminate, or
caused the scheduler to calculate the wrong time.
==PAGE:131==
10.8 Analysis of Timer Error
117
The root cause of the observed error lies in the interplay between the function
next() and the timer's behavior. Specically, within the next() function, just
before invoking wait_until(), which in turn calls lf_sleep_until_locked()
(responsible for time retrieval), we enter a critical section:
int next(void) {
// Enter the critical section.
if (lf_critical_section_enter() != 0) {
lf_print_error_and_exit("Could not enter critical section");
}
}
In the unthreaded version, entering this critical section locks the IRQ:
int lf_critical_section_enter() {
if (_lf_num_nested_critical_sections++ == 0) {
_lf_irq_mask = irq_lock();
}
return 0;
}
We remain in this critical section until the semaphore is taken:
k_sem_take(&_lf_sem, K_FOREVER);
During this critical section, if we call lf_clock_gettime(), and the timer
reaches its maximum value, the ISR, which is supposed to increment the epoch:
static void _lf_timer_overflow_callback(const struct device *dev, void *user_data
_lf_timer_last_epoch_nsec += _lf_timer_epoch_duration_nsec;
}
does not get triggered. Consequently, the timer value resets to 0, while retaining
an outdated _lf_timer_last_epoch_nsec value. This discrepancy manifests
when calculating the current time in get_time:
*t = now_nsec + _lf_timer_last_epoch_nsec;
==PAGE:132==
118
Results and discussion
The result is that get_time(t) assigns a smaller-than-expected value to t. This
discrepancy, in turn, leads to an inaccurate computation for the sleep duration:
lf_clock_gettime(&now);
interval_t sleep_for_us = (wakeup - now)/1000;
In our observations, this caused the computed value to exceed the counter's
maximum value. Even in congurations where this does not cause an overow, it
introduces signicant inaccuracies. This error also disrupts the timer's expected
upper bound, with the magnitude and likelihood of the error being inuenced
by the counter's top value. The larger the top value, the greater the potential
error, though the probability of occurrence diminishes since the top value is
reached less frequently.
In essence, the delayed ISR, which should update the epoch, results in the
system retaining an outdated notion of the current time. Given that this timing
is crucial, it jeopardizes the reliability of all event-based operations.
As of August 14, based on the nightly build commit 6b4bb4c, no x has been
implemented. Notably, repeated tests with various print functions revealed that
the timing of this error varies. However, if the code remains unchanged, the
failure consistently occurs at the same point.
The issue was resolved by relocating the critical section, ensuring that the timer
could interrupt and update the global variable. This guarantees that we always
obtain the most recent time and never revert to a previous time. While this
solution is secure in single-core systems, its safety in multi-core systems cannot
be guaranteed.

Central to our exploration of Zephyr was the timing capabilities, which serve
