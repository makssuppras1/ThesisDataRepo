Abstract
Visualization techniques in machine learning are an important class of techniques used to visualize
high-dimensional data by embedding it in 2 or 3-dimensional space. In this thesis, a new visualization
technique is presented. The new technique, named Rie-SNE, is specifically developed for visualizing
data that resides in the latent space of deep generative models. The driving assumption of this technique
is that the data to be embedded lie on a Riemannian manifold. After giving some background into
existing theory and models that will be made use of, the method of Rie-SNE is presented, and afterwards
two approximation methods are given that use k-means clustering and nearest neighbor search to speed
up computations. Results are presented by visualizing data in a latent space induced by a variational
autoencoder trained on classic data sets. The performance of Rie-SNE is evaluated by comparing it to
the performance of an existing technique, t-SNE, on the same data and determining the quality of the
visualizations. The expectation was that Rie-SNE would outperform t-SNE in a clear manner, but such
was not the case. Rie-SNE’s performance was in general good, always on par with t-SNE and often
times better. After having analyzed Rie-SNE’s performance, some key concepts and assumptions are
discussed, as well as some thoughts on why Rie-SNE did not perform as well as was originally thought.
The thesis is concluded with a brief overview of Rie-SNE’s core concepts, some remarks about what
went well are given and promising avenues for future work are laid out.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Visualization techniques in machine learning are algorithms that can embed high-dimensional data into
2D or 3D space, while preserving some intrinsic structure or relationships that exist within the data for
the purpose of visualization.
Specifically, it can be of interest to visually explore the latent space of certain generative models, since
they might encode some hidden structure between data points that could prove to be useful to see.
Latent spaces of generative models are typically an encoded or compressed version of the data space,
and as such have lower dimensionality than the original data. However, they can still be of higher
dimensions than humans are able to visualize, so visualization techniques need to be employed. More
often than not these techniques will rely on a distance measure between data points, which is typically
the Euclidean distance.
The aim of this thesis is to come up with a new visualization technique, one that does not assume the
high-dimensional data lie in Euclidean space. The hope is that this will give better visualizations of
latent spaces, specifically latent spaces of deep generative models, since it has been shown that assuming
the latent spaces of these models have a Riemannian structure rather than a Euclidean one can give
better model performance [1, 12].
Generative models are statistical models that aim to learn how data was generated in order to be able
to generate new values that will faithfully resemble the original data. Then, deep generative models
are generative models implemented as deep neural networks, where deep neural networks are used
to learn the parameters of the generative process. Two of the most popular deep generative models
are variational autoencoders [15] and generative adversarial networks [7]. In this thesis the focus will
be on visualizing the latent spaces of variational autoencoders, due to their simple architectures and
widespread use.
For developing the new visualization technique the approach will be to take an existing technique
popular in the field, t-SNE, and alter it in such a way that it assumes a Riemannian structure over
the high-dimensional space holding the data to be embedded. t-SNE is a non-linear dimensionality
reduction technique that aims to preserve local structure within the high-dimensional data that it
embeds in low-dimensional space [19]. It does so by computing probability distributions over the points
in high-dimensional space and in the low-dimensional embedding and then moving the embedded points
around such that the two probability distributions are as similar as possible.
More concretely, t-SNE wants to position the points in the low-dimensional embedding in such a way
that the Kullback-Leibler divergence, a similarity measure of probability distributions, between the two
distributions is minimized. Letting P be the probability distribution representing similarities between
the points in the high-dimensional space and Q be the probability distribution representing similarities
between the points in the low-dimensional embedding, t-SNE aims to:
min KL(P||Q)
In practice this is done using gradient descent, taking the gradient of the KL-divergence with respect
to the embedded points, where a positive gradient means an attraction between the low-dimensional

CHAPTER2
Background
This chapter will present in more detail the theory underlying the mathematical models and tools used
throughout the thesis.
First, a detailed account is given of the deep generative models known as variational autoencoders,
with both the probability theoretical foundations being explained as well as some of the neural network
implementation details.
Secondly, t-SNE will be given a good deal of attention, since it will serve as a basis for the new technique.
Some basic concepts from Riemannian geometry must also be explained, mainly what a Riemannian
manifold is and how to find shortest paths between points on such a manifold.
Finally, Brownian motion on a Riemannian manifold is explained, since this will dictate how probabilities
in the new technique are computed.
2.1
Variational autoencoders
Variational autoencoders, first proposed by Kingma & Welling in 2014, are a neural network archi-
tecture and approximation techniques for doing inference and learning tasks in directed probabilistic
graphical models with intractable marginal likelihoods and posterior distributions [15]. To get a firmer
understanding of variational autoencoders, a primer is given on the type of probabilistic models that
motivate this type of neural network architecture, then the main points of the model are presented.
As a starting point, it is helpful to keep in mind the purpose of probabilistic models. That is, given a data
set of n observations D = {x1, x2, ..., xn} where each observation is generated from some true probability
distribution with probability distribution function ptrue(x), it is of interest to find a probabilistic model
pθ(x) that approximates the true distribution. Then, for any observation:
pθ(x) ≈ptrue(x)
And in the same manner it is often of interested to find some conditional model pθ(y|x) that approxi-
mates some true conditional distribution ptrue(y|x).

between them. More precisely, a Bayesian network represents a factorization of the joint probability of
all of the network’s random variables [3], i.e.:

graphical model with observed variables x and latent variables z is the joint probability distribution
pθ(x, z) [14].

This section gives a detailed overview of t-SNE and its key ideas.
t-SNE, proposed by van der Maaten & Hinton in 2008, is a non-linear dimensionality reduction technique
for visualizing high-dimensional data by embedding it into 2 or 3 dimensions, with the aim of faithfully
representing local structure found in the high-dimensional data in the low dimensional embedding [19].
t-SNE is a variant of another technique called SNE, or Stochastic Neighbor Embedding [8]. However,
instead of relying on a Gaussian distribution for computing low-dimensional probabilities like SNE,
t-SNE uses a Student’s t-distribution for the low-dimensional probability computations [19].
So, t-SNE computes probabilities for how close any two points are in both the high dimensional space
and the low dimensional embedding. This gives two probability distributions, P and Q, one for the

high-dimensional space are preserved in the low-dimensional embedding. Therefore, t-SNE wants to
minimize the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the two distributions, i.e. t-SNE wants to minimize:

As is evident, t-SNE needs to deal with computing pairwise distances between data points quite a lot

2.2.1
Approximating input similarities using vantage-point trees
Two main approximation techniques exists that can improve the computational efficiency of t-SNE [18].
The first one is a sparse approximation for computing the joint probabilities of P, pij, since computing
all pij has complexity O(n2). The key thing to note for this approximation is that if data points xi
and xj are dissimilar, i.e. they have a large distance between them, then their corresponding joint
probability pij will be close to zero.
Then, instead of computing all pij with O(n2) complexity, it
suffices to take some number of nearest neighbors for each point xi and computing only pij values for
those nearest neighbors, setting the rest to zero.
A value of τ = ⌊3 · perplexity⌋is used for the number of nearest neighbors of each point xi. This is an
arbitrary value picked by van der Maaten in [18] based on empirical results suggesting it gives sufficiently
good approximations of P. This brings the complexity for computing all pij to O(τn). There is however
additional cost in finding the nearest neighbors of each point that must be factored in. Finding the τ
nearest neighbors of each point xi can be done by constructing a vantage-point tree [30] over the data
and performing a nearest neighbors search on the resulting tree. Constructing the tree and performing
a nearest neighbor search for τ nearest neighbors has time complexity O(τn log n).

The Barnes-Hut algorithm [2], originally developed for approximating N-body simulations in astro-

doing it the naive way, by computing the force exerted on a given body by each and every other body

structure to approximate the forces that far-away bodies exert.

far away enough from a given body, then all the bodies within that cell can be said to exert the same
force on the given body. In two-dimensional space, this tree data structure is a quadtree. Quadtrees
are trees where each node represents a rectangular cell with a particularly sized bounding box in a
given two-dimensional space. Each non-leaf node has 4 children that split the parent node further up
into four rectangular quadrants. A leaf node contains at most one point in its cell, and the root node
contains all the points. Each node also stores the number of points that are within its cell and their
center of mass. For a three-dimensional space, the idea is the same except a so-called octtree is used to

For developing the new technique, the key assumption is that variational autoencoders encode input
data into a Riemannian latent space [1, 12]. In order to visualize latent space data that is on a Rieman-

shortest distances between points on a Riemannian manifold are of interest, but they are not trivial
compute. In order to explain how to compute shortest distances between points on a Riemannian mani-

Riemannian are given. In addition, the length of a curve on a Riemannian manifold is defined, which
prompts a discussion about the metric choice for a latent space of a variational autoencoder and how a
shortest curve between two points in such a latent space can be found.

CHAPTER3
Method
This chapter will present the new visualization technique, named Rie-SNE, or Riemannian Stochastic
Neighbor Embedding. First, the general steps of Rie-SNE will be outlined. The naive approach is first
introduced as a starting point, and after having shown that the naive approach has an undesirable
running time, some approximation methods will be developed. These approximation methods address
the biggest bottlenecks of Rie-SNE, which arise from working with geodesics to determine shortest
distances between points. The purpose of the approximation methods is to reduce the time complexity
of Rie-SNE, such that it can run in reasonable time (meaning hours instead of days).

As a first attempt at an optimization, it is worth noticing that the geodesics between any two points

starts at point zj and ends at zi. Given this, finding all pairwise geodesics actually requires finding
only n(n−1)/2 geodesics rather than n2. However, this optimization still results in a quadratic running
time, so as n grows large a quadratic running time multiplied by the very large constant c still needs
to be dealt with. In practice this does reduce computational time though, so it is worth implementing.
3.3
Approximating geodesics using k-means clustering
In order to have a reasonable running time for Rie-SNE, ways of approximating finding all pairwise
geodesics are needed. An approximation method based on k-means clustering is presented here. The
method goes as follows:
1. Group latent points into k clusters using k-means clustering where distances are measured with
the Euclidean metric and k is a user-defined value with a speed/precision trade-off.
2. Find all geodesics between cluster centers using (2.9). The number of geodesics that have to be
found is then k(k −1)/2, which given a reasonably defined value for k is k << n(n −1)/2 for
large n.
3. Since curves are represented as cubic splines, i.e. a collection of piecewise third-order polynomials,
an approximation of a geodesic between two points zi and zj can be constructed in the following
way. Take the geodesic that goes from the cluster center of the cluster containing zi to the cluster
center of the cluster containing zj and change the endpoints of this geodesic to zi and zj. If zi
and zj are within the same cluster, approximate the geodesic between them as a straight line.
4. The resulting curve will serve as an approximation for the true geodesic between zi and zj.
Using this method gives a fixed complexity for finding geodesics that is bound by k, and does not grow
with the input size n, keeping the running time reasonable. Although it is possible to find all necessary
geodesics in O(k2) time, the computation of the geodesic distance matrix D is still quadratic with
respect to the input size n, since it is still necessary to iterate over all pairs of latent points to construct
the geodesic approximations and compute their lengths. The lengths of the approximate geodesics are
computed using equation (2.6) and will serve as an upper bound on the true geodesic lengths. Even
though the computation of D is still quadratic with respect to n, the k-means approximation method
gets rid of the very large constant c associated with finding all pairwise geodesics.
A thing to note here is that the optimal number of clusters isn’t necessarily the number that gives the
best clustering. That is, the quality of the clustering isn’t what needs to be maximized, if that was the
case methods like the elbow method [26] or the average silhouette method [24] could help determine the
optimal number of clusters. Rather, the optimal number of clusters using this method is the number
that gives the best approximation of geodesics while having a reasonable running time, and is therefore

up to the user to decide. If k is chosen to be large, say k = n, then each latent point is in its own
cluster, turning this approximation method into the naive approach. However, if k is chosen to be low,
the result is a fast method for finding geodesics but poorer approximations. Empirical results show
that a k-value of around 100 gives good geodesic approximations close to that of the naive approach as

3.4 Nearest neighbor approximation
25
3.4
Nearest neighbor approximation
With an approximation available for the biggest bottleneck of Rie-SNE, the attention is turned towards
the next most time consuming computation, which is to actually compute the lengths of the geodesics
that have been found. The length of curves on a Riemannian manifold (M, g) can be computed using
equation (2.6).
In practice, computing the length of a curve γ in latent space is done in the following way. First, the
curve γ is discretized, meaning it is broken up into t same-sized linear pieces. Then, those pieces get
decoded by the variational autoencoder into data space. There the Euclidean distance of each of the t
pieces is computed and the resulting sum of these distances will serve as the approximate length of the
curve.
Computing the lengths of all pairwise geodesics (or their approximations) has quadratic time complex-
ity, O(n2). However, similar to the constant associated with finding geodesics, there is also a constant
associated with computing the length of a curve, so a more accurate depiction of the complexity can
be written as O(mn2), where m is a large constant. This constant m represents the time complexity of
computing the length of a curve using equation (2.6). The added constant makes the quadratic time
complexity even harder to deal with, especially for a large input size n. Therefore, for a large input
size, some further approximation methods should be considered.
To approximate having to compute all pairwise lengths of geodesics, a method that expands upon
the idea of approximating input similarities using vantage-point trees from section (2.2.1) is presented.
Instead of only computing a sparse distribution P (P is represented in matrix form), the same idea
can be used to compute a sparse geodesic distance matrix D and a sparse Riemannian volume measure
ratio matrix H0, which then results in a sparse P distribution. The method goes as follows:
1. For each latent point zi, find τ of its nearest neighbors by constructing a vantage point tree [30]
over the latent points and performing a nearest neighbor search using the Euclidean metric, and
store the indices of all nearest neighbors for each zi.
The number for the amount of nearest
neighbors of each latent point is the same number used in section (2.2.1), i.e. τ = ⌊3 · perplexity⌋.
2. For each zi, only find geodesics of the τ nearest neighbors of zi (using the k-means approximation
method from (3.3)) and compute their lengths. This yields a geodesic distance matrix D of size
n × τ.
3. In the same manner, only compute Riemannian volume measure ratios of the τ nearest neighbors
of zi. This yields a Riemannian volume measure ratio matrix H0 of the same size, n × τ.
4. Finally, compute P using the n × τ sized D and H0 matrices. Then, flatten P to dimensions
1 × nτ and perform a compressed sparse row format reconstruction using the stored indices from
step 1. This gives a n × n sized P matrix with values for the τ nearest neighbors in each row, and
zeroes everywhere else.
By using this approximation method, the quadratic time complexity multiplied with the large constant
m associated with computing all pairwise geodesic lengths is no longer an issue. The geodesic distance
matrix D, the Riemannian volume measure ratio matrix H0 and the sparse P distribution can all be
computed in O(τn) time. Constructing the vantage point tree and finding the τ nearest neighbors of
each latent point zi has complexity O(τn log n).

4.1
Experimental setup
Experiments will be conducted by visualizing data in the latent space of a variational autoencoder
trained on the classic MNIST [17] and Fashion-MNIST [29] data sets. After training, data points will
be encoded into the latent space of the variational autoencoder and then Rie-SNE and t-SNE will be
used to visualize the latent space data. The variational autoencoder will be made up of three neural
networks and has the following architecture:
• µϕ will be the encoder network, consisting of:
- A linear input layer the size of the data with a softplus activation function.
- A linear hidden layer of roughly half the size of the input layer with a softplus activation
function.
- An linear output layer the size of the latent space.
• σϕ will be a learnable parameter in the encoder network.
• µθ will be the decoder network, consisting of:
- A linear input layer the size of the latent space with a softplus activation function.
- A linear hidden layer of roughly half the size of the output layer with a softplus activation
function.
- An output layer the size of the data with a sigmoid activation function.
• σθ will be a separate neural network.
In general, using a network architecture like the decoder architecture described above to learn the pa-
rameters of σθ will result in poor extrapolated variance estimates in latent regions where there is no
data [1]. Ideally, there should be large variances in regions of no data, which is not guaranteed using
this approach. Instead, the following training scheme is adopted.
First, the parameters of the encoder network µϕ and the decoder network µθ are learnt jointly, fixing
σθ = [0.01, 0.01, ..., 0.01]D for the time being where D is the data dimensionality.
Then, a separate radial basis function network [4] is used for σθ. The RBF network is used to learn the
parameters of σθ after the parameters of µϕ and µθ have been learnt. This ensures that variances σ2
θ
will be large in latent regions of no data [1].

CHAPTER5
Discussion
In this chapter the results of Rie-SNE will be discussed, along with some thoughts on what the expec-
tation was and what could have gone better.
The performance of Rie-SNE when visualizing latent space data is generally good. Most often Rie-SNE
performs on par with t-SNE on the same data and a few times slightly better. An interesting result was
the perplexity resilience of Rie-SNE. As the perplexity parameter is given different values, Rie-SNE is
very consistent with its visualizations, keeping the same general structure and relative locations of the
visualized clusters. t-SNE on the other hand is more sensitive to changes in the perplexity parameter,
changing the structure and relative locations of the visualized clusters for different values of perplexity.
Visualizing latent points in the latent space of the Bernoulli variational autoencoder also gives some
interesting results, the clusters formed by Rie-SNE’s k-means approximation method on that data had
particularly nice shapes. Rie-SNE’s performance in higher-dimensional latent spaces seems to have
some inaccuracies, particularly placing some embedded points uniformly apart from one another. A
proposed solution is given in section (3.6), which is to perform the computations of conditional proba-
bilities pj|i in log space. However, the solution could not be fully realized. This is because performing
the computations of pj|i in log space fundamentally changes the way the variance parameter t has to
be found. Before, it was possible to determine values for t with a great deal of accuracy using binary
search, but performing the pj|i computations in log space makes this infeasible, so other methods are
needed.
The time benchmarks show that the running time of Rie-SNE’s approximation methods is fairly slow,
especially on the CPU. However, by utilizing the GPU together with the notion of batching, significant
speedup can be achieved, especially for computing the Riemannian volume measure ratio matrix H0.
The expectation was that Rie-SNE would outperform t-SNE very clearly on latent space data, due to
the result that assuming latent space data is on a Riemannian manifold gives improved model perfor-
mance [1, 12]. This means that Rie-SNE was expected to give visualizations that had better clustering
structure, meaning either clearer boundaries between clusters or better cluster shapes, or just a better
sense of data being split up into clusters according to class labels. This ended up not being the case
however, so it is best to examine why.
The most obvious possibility is the fact that in order for Rie-SNE to run in reasonable time, approx-
imation methods needed to be employed. Both the k-means approximation method and the k-means
and nearest neighbor approximation method are just that, approximation methods. However, empirical
results show that the k-means approximation method performs on par with the naive approach when
cluster size is around k = 100 or higher, whereas the k-means and nearest neighbor approximation
method has poorer performance compared to that of the naive approach.
Another possible reason is poor model performance. If the underlying model is poorly trained then
geodesic distances in latent space might be less meaningful. The variational autoencoder in section
(4.1) does show some signs of good training, the loss functions get lower as the epochs progress and
they do reach some level of convergence. The autoencoder’s ability to reconstruct MNIST digits is
rather good as well. The latent space variances seem to be in line with what was expected by using the
==PAGE:59==
5 Discussion
51
RBF network to learn the parameters of σθ, i.e. variances are larger in regions of no data. However,
more resources could be spent on validating model performance, making sure a model is sufficiently
trained before visualizing its latent space data using Rie-SNE. Also, testing Rie-SNE on different net-
work architectures, whether it be a different architecture for a variational autoencoder or another type
of deep generative model, like generative adversarial networks, could be interesting.
For the k-means approximation method, two factors come to mind that might affect the quality of the
final visualizations. Firstly, the Euclidean metric is used to determine how k-means performs the initial
clustering of the latent points (not the final clustering shown in the visualizations), which is done so
that only geodesics between cluster centers need to be found. This could be problematic however, since
the assumption is that Euclidean distances are not the true distances between points in latent space, so
an alternative metric should perhaps be used. Secondly, the assumption is taken that the straight line
distance is the shortest distance between latent points that are within the same cluster. Although the
majority of pairwise distances will be the ones that are between clusters, this could still have some effect
on the final visualization. For latent points that are in two separate clusters, the case where either one
point or both points reside on their respective cluster’s boundaries could give rise to an approximate
geodesic that is too long compared to the true geodesic between the points and this could distort the
final visualization somewhat, depending on the size (radius) of the clusters.
For the k-means and nearest neighbor approximation method, the τ nearest neighbors of each latent
point are determined using the Euclidean metric, which like with the k-means approximation method,
could prove problematic. Also, the number of nearest neighbors seem to be chosen rather arbitrarily.
The original t-SNE paper used τ = ⌊3 · perplexity⌋, giving good empirical results, and the experimental
results produced in this thesis had the value set at τ = 2·⌊3·perplexity⌋. Further investigation into this
parameter could prove useful in improving the performance of this particular approximation method.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
In this thesis, a new visualization technique was developed, named Rie-SNE. Specifically, the technique
was developed for visualizing data embedded in the latent space of deep generative models, namely
variational autoencoders. The underlying assumption driving the technique is the result that assuming
latent spaces of deep generative models are Riemannian rather than Euclidean can give improved model
performance [1, 12]. Therefore, Rie-SNE assumes the data it wants to embed is on a Riemannian man-
ifold. This is achieved through a probability density function based on the heat kernel of a Brownian
motion on a Riemannian manifold given in equation (2.10).
Rie-SNE operates on, amongst other things, pairwise distances between latent points. Because of the
Riemannian assumption, shortest paths between latent points are now geodesic curves. In terms of time
and memory, although mostly time, geodesic curves are expensive to find and computing their lengths
is also expensive. Since Rie-SNE needs to find these geodesic curves and compute their lengths, it ends
up having a poor running time. Because of this, two approximation methods where developed in order
to speed up Rie-SNE. The first method, the k-means approximation method, utilizes k-means clustering
in such a way that the number of pairwise geodesic curves that have to be found is greatly minimized.
The other method, the k-means and nearest neighbor approximation method, utilizes a combination of
k-means clustering and nearest neighbor search. Like with the first method, it uses k-means cluster-
ing to greatly minimize the number of pairwise geodesic curves needed to be found, but in addition it
uses nearest neighbor search to greatly reduce the number of length computations that have to be done.
Rie-SNE’s performance was tested on various configurations of the MNIST and Fashion-MNIST data
sets, and compared to t-SNE’s performance on the same data. The result was that Rie-SNE produces
good visualizations, on par with t-SNE and sometimes even better, especially with the k-means ap-
proximation method. However, the expectation was that Rie-SNE would greatly outperform t-SNE
on visualizing data assumed to lie on a Riemannian manifold. Such was not the case, but this is a
good start. An unexpected but nice result was the perplexity resilience of Rie-SNE. That is to say,
Rie-SNE seems very resilient to changes in the perplexity parameter, giving mostly the same consistent
visualizations for different values of perplexity, whereas t-SNE does not. Rie-SNE seemed to have some
problems though when the latent space dimensionality was increased, causing some embedded points
to appear uniformly far apart from one another.
For future work, it would be interesting to implement another method of finding desirable values of
variance t for the computations of conditional probabilities pj|i in log space, in an attempt to make
Rie-SNE more robust to computations in high-dimensional latent spaces.
This would probably be
a more fine-grained method, like gradient descent or simulated annealing, since halving the search
space each time easily overshoots good values of t and ends up in a bad local minimum (in log space).
Another avenue to explore is changing out the Student’s t-distribution density for the low-dimensional
probabilities. Perhaps going back to the original SNE algorithm [8] and using a Gaussian kernel for the
low-dimensional probabilities could improve performance. However, since the Student’s t-distribution
is more heavily tailed than a Gaussian, it works better for eliminating crowding effects, i.e. it allows for
moderate distances in high-dimensional space to be modelled further away in the embedding [19]. So,
maybe this is not such a likely approach for better performance, but it is worth looking into. Exploring
alternative metrics to use for the approximation methods would also be of interest.
