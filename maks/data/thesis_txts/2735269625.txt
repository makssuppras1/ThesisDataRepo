Abstract
Since 2020, there has been a surge in technologies automating neural network (NN) ac­
celerators for both field­programmable gate arrays (FPGA) and application­specific in­
tegrated circuits (ASIC). With the overarching goal of enabling the development of spe­
cialized AI chips for a noninvasive medical device, this thesis explores state­of­the­art
methodologies for translating convolutional neural network algorithms (CNN) into cor­
responding chip layouts.
The focus is on end­to­end automation, emphasizing cost­
effectiveness and reliance on open­source software. The project presents a conceptual
framework for building novel NN­to­Silicon solutions and an experiment within this frame­
work leading to a plug­and­play automation flow using TensorFlow in Python, the High­
level Synthesis (HLS) tool Vivado HLS, the NN­to­FPGA compiler HLS4ML, and the RTL­
GDSII tool Openlane2. SkyWater Technologies’ 130 nm PDK (sky130) is utilized. The
project successfully produces layouts for two simple NN examples below 1000 total pa­
rameters, containing dense and activation layers, and 2D convolution layers. Ultimately,
it is concluded that current open­source tools are capable of end­to­end automation of
small, low­complexity NN architectures at the cost of larger chip sizes, and will for large­
scale NN architectures often be better suited for automating individual building blocks.
Keywords: Automation, open source, NN accelerator, CNN, FPGA, HLS, ASIC, PDK.

Introduction
In broad terms, this thesis is concerned with studying methodologies that road map how to
take a given NN algorithm, and create its corresponding circuitry on a microchip layout. In
addition, a novel method for this is experimented on, utilising new advances in FPGA au­
tomation technology as an intermediate step. This experiment is under the constraint that
the method should be free (monetarily) and be build primarily upon open­source software
in a high­level programming environment.
During development, general NN­to­Silicon methodology was referred to as flows, and
encompasses several stages which tackle sub­problems for achieving the layout of a NN
accelerator.
This thesis is in essence a specialised study with the overall aim of facilitating future
custom development of application specific AI microchips for a small, wireless, battery­
driven medical device, which seeks to be a noninvasive alternative to current colon and
endoscopy procedures.
1.1
Background
This study is built upon the 2022 paper penned by Sahafi et al., wherein they designed an
AI­empowered wireless video endoscopic capsule to address the challenges of gastroin­
testinal (GI) tract diseases; Including colorectal cancer.[1] The capsule features real­time
image processing using a DNN, enhanced visualisation of the mucous layer, on­the­go
task modification, and bi­directional communication with a patient’s electronic devices
(e.g. smart phone), while also having OTA programming capabilities. In an in vivo test on
a pig, all novel features were successfully validated.
Figure 1.1: [1] Shows the mockup/concept of the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule in four
stages. Stage 1: Shows patient about to swallow the capsule. Stage 2: Shows the cap­
sule is connected to patient’s smart device (here a phone) over BLE. Stage 3: Shows the
capsule’s journey through the intestines. Stage 4: Visual findings of intestinal anomalies
are sent from the capsule to smartphone for further examination.
The development of the AI­empowered wireless video endoscopic capsule (henceforth
referred to as Wireless Endoscopy Capsule for the sake of brevity) is in response to GI
tract disorders becoming more prevalent in both the young and elderly populations around
the world. In Europe alone, they cause approximately 1 million deaths annually. Cases of

1.2
Motivation
AI chips are specialised alternatives to a generic CPU, often designed to handle parallel
processing, which comes with its own memory and IO. The prime feature of the AI chip is
implementation of structures tailored for handling matrix operations common in NN; These
structures are called NN accelerators.[2]
The hypothesis
As for most micro electronics, reducing footprint and reducing battery size is of great
consideration, albeit not always a mandatory one. But in the pursuit of creating a less

invasive medical device, shrinking these parameters directly contribute to reducing the
technology’s physical invasiveness.
Considering the cell batteries of the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule accounts for roughly
60% of its size, the hypothesis is that the smaller area and power­use of ASIC as com­
pared with other out­of­the­box technologies, could reduce the amount of space needed

ASIC design also comes with its own distinct set of advantages. Typically, the cost of
ASIC development is tremendous, but comes with a trade­off of cheaper high­quantity
production – enabling large­scale fabrication. Typically, the reduced flexibility of ASIC
design comes with the ability to operate faster and at lower power.
To address ASIC development, an open­source approach is favoured. The promise of
open­source technology for the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule is essentially two parted,
(1) monetary, (2) experimental. In the advent of the open­source PDK, and the increased
maturity of free EDA tools, developing production­ready ASICs without paying for any
software seems more feasible then ever. This opens up the possibility for incredibly broad
collaboration – akin to what is found in the software world – between everyone from senior
engineers, to researchers, to students. In short, it is an exciting new area, and there is
a potential to make an impact on not only the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule project, but
also on the space as a whole.
The objective
On the behalf of Sahafi et al., I aim to investigate open­source tools for ASIC design,
specifically to implement the NN accelerator part of an AI chip with the goal of laying the
groundwork for a future custom AI system for the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule project.

1.3
The open­source design space
In essence, four essential components are needed for creating open­source NN acceler­
ators.
1. Programming environment for creating NNs.
2. Creating corresponding circuit equivalent of NN.
3. Designing layout of circuit equivalent.
4. Choice of process node.
While these components could be managed manually, the ideal flow aims to establish
a streamlined process that automates as many individual components as feasible. This
approach aims to diminish factors such as time­to­market, speed up prototyping, and
ensure functionality by implementing criteria throughout the development process.
The flow could leverage existing tools to address the issue, incorporating established
solutions into the process.
1. Programming environment for creating NN: Though there is no unified way of
creating NNs, the preferred method for many developers involves coding in a Python
environment using popular APIs such as Keras, TensorFlow, PyTorch, or JAX.
2. Creating corresponding circuit equivalent of NN: The realm of digital electron­
ics provide great help with this. Diverse HLS tools, like those from Xilinx and Intel,
alongside open­source options like PandaBambu, facilitate the translation of soft­
ware descriptions in languages such as C/C++ into hardware descriptions written in
Verilog/VHDL.
3. Designing layout of circuit equivalent: In this realm, the key player is ’the Open­
ROAD project,’ a comprehensive suite of technologies designed to automate the
layout creation of Verilog/VHDL descriptions. Derived from ’OpenROAD’, Efabless’
’OpenLane’ and Python­interface ’OpenLane2’ represent forks offering no­human­
in­the­loop workflows.
4. Choice of process node. The availability of PDKs from different semiconductor
companies in different sizes is a well­established concept. However, the emergence
of open­source PDKs, such as Global Foundries’ 180nm and Skywater Technolo­
gies’ 130nm PDKs presents a fresh opportunity to align with the desired open­source
philosophy.
To conclude, with the introduction of OpenROAD Project and its forks — an automation
tool for creating physical layouts from Verilog RTL — as well as the wide availability of
Python libraries for NNs, e.g. TensorFlow and PyTorch, along with the increasing doc­
umentation on how to implement NNs on ASIC, as well as FPGA, there has been an
enabling of creation processes for open­source NN accelerators; Effectively going from a
Python machine learning script all the way to a physical layout.

Research Goals
This project’s research question is concerned with developing a novel method for solving
a known problem of custom ASIC design. The statement is as follows:
For implementing NN in Wireless Endoscopy Capsule, can current open­source tools be
used to create a simple NN accelerator chip, as to facilitate future custom chip develop­

ment in the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule design?
For this follows some key procedures that need investigation:
1. How to design a template NN algorithm and its corresponding circuit architecture
and have it scalable such that future developers can use it for more complex / large­

2. A review of available open source PDKs which allows for production of transistor­

The key procedures were broken down into a list of deliverables:

used.
3. Choice of open­source PDK: The most promising is Skywater Technology’s sky130

(a) Is is possible to interface with the input and output?
(b) Does the chip compute what is expected?
Ultimately ending on a final system description of; Input and output signals, volt­
age/current/power requirements, operating temperature and size. With an evalua­
tion of feasibility of using the designed process for future development.

2.1
Project background
This section introduces the NN for the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule project, providing

2.1.1
The NN of the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule
In the Wireless Capsule Endoscopy project, the current NN implementation is a highly opti­
mised CNN of approximately 3.2MB (due to an 8MB restraint), made to detect and localise
colorectal polyps. Inspired by MobileNet32 and YOLO (see Figure 2.1), the CNN employs
the non­maximum suppression (NMS) algorithm, filtering candidate regions based on a
confidence score. Images which satisfy the threshold for significant findings, undergo
further analysis and is transferred OTA with BLE unit.
The CNN uses 8­bit precision and a clock frequency of 400MHz. When using the inter­
section over union (IoU) criterion, it reaches an average precision of AP25 = 99.5% and
AP50 = 95.8%, with its current colorectal polyps test set.
Tertiary Wireless Endoscopy Capsule specifications
The Wireless Endoscopy Capsule features optimisations often found in tiny devices or
IoT devices such as:
• Low­power mode: It is programmed to routinely enter an idle state. And can stay
active for longer periods if findings are deemed critical.
• Regulates images taken: It regulates images taken per second (FPS), and will
thus choose to increase FPS if significant findings are continually met.
• RF on significant findings: Only images with significant findings are sent over
BLE. If no significant findings are detected, the device will still sent every 5th image
such that location of the pill is constantly known, and a later reconstruction of the
bowel can be achieved.
Key takeaways
Based on the paper by Sahafi et. al., the NN can be broadly characterised by being a
highly optimised CNN using multiple blocks of Depthwise­Separable convolution akin to
the architectures like MobileNet.

2021: VeriGOOD­ML
An abbreviation of Verilog Generator (Open­source, Optimized for Designs for Machine
Learning), VeriGOOD­ML is a no­human­in­the­loop methodology for generating machine
learning­optimized Verilog from a given ONNX­file.[4] It addresses various key challenges
in creating hardware descriptions for ML algorithms and layout designs for both small,
simple, and large, complicated networks. Esmaeilzadeh et al. adopted a platform­based
approach, categorising ML algorithms into three separate groups and choosing three dif­
ferent approaches to address each.
1. For Non­DNN ML algorithms: It utilises the template­based framework TABLA by
Mahajan et al.[5], specifically designed to simplify the development process of NN
accelerators for FPGAs, focusing on supervised learning algorithms (both classifi­
cation and regression). TABLA operates on the idea that various learning algorithms
can be formulated as stochastic optimization problems, employing stochastic gra­
dient descent as a fixed solver to minimise an objective function over training data.
Consequently, only the specific objective function varies for different learning algo­
8

==PAGE:19==
rithms, significantly reducing development time.
2. For DNN algorithms: Here, VeriGOOD­ML utilises its own compiler, GeneSys,
which translates an ONNX description (a graph network) into a new graph descrip­
tion. During this transformation, the compiler has the opportunity to replace the orig­
inal nodes with template optimisations for common DNN structures such as dense,
convolution, and various activation layers. The result of this is a hardware­aware
reconstruction, which e.g. builds a binary tree for the L2 norm, or optimises the flow
of data for convolution.
3. For small specific ML algorithms: It employs its own compiler named Axiline, a
hard­coded engine designed for specific small ML algorithms. The rationale is that
for certain commonly used ML algorithms, investing time in complex computations
from scratch, as in the TABLA or GeneSys cases, may not be a worthwhile invest­
ment. This method can thereby increases developer productivity in specific cases.

The paper demonstrates successful layout generation for classic ML algorithms, namely
SVM, ResNet50, and a logistic regression algorithm, as a proof of concept on the GF12LP
13 metal layer technology using all 13 layers. This achievement was accomplished using
a blend of commercial and open­source automation tools for tasks such as place­and­
route, CTS and PDN generation.
The key findings from VeriGOOD­ML I have concluded as the following:
• Generic IO files As a file type, ONNX is an industry standard with comprehensive
documentation. This feature facilitates the utilisation of the workflow by numerous
developers, given that popular Python ML libraries can generate this format. Re­
gardless of the method or ML algorithm employed, the ultimate output is in the form
a JSON, string or binary file.
• Platform­based design: The utilisation of multiple tools tailored to various types
of ML algorithms is a notable aspect of the approach. The inclusion of the Axiline
engine for smaller algorithms is particularly clever, acknowledging that not all imple­
mented ML algorithms are large­scale or groundbreaking. This approach effectively
saves some developers a great amount of time.

2022: SODA­Opt
[6] An abbreviation for Software Defined Accelerators Optimizer, SODA­Opt by Agostini
et al. is an open­source framework that generates NN­optimized hardware descriptions
for implementation in either FPGA or ASIC design. Similar to VeriGOOD­ML, it follows
the approach of converting from popular file formats (demonstrated with both Tensor­
Flow and PyTorch) to layout, through an intermediate step of converting the model from
Python/ONNX to an optimized equivalent. This equivalent is then processed by an HLS
tool to create Verilog and ultimately goes through the RTL­to­GDS tool OpenROAD.
Figure 2.4: [6] Shows the SODA­Opt flow
In contrast to VeriGOOD­ML’s platform­based design, Agostini et al. chose to design a
single compiler, the SODA compiler. This compiler translates the Python NN algorithm
into efficient machine code using the MLIR framework found in the LLVM project (see
annotated bibliography for details). The SODA compiler leverages built­in dialects (i.e.,
IR structures) to optimise computation and reduce redundancy.
The SODA­Opt compiler accomplishes the following:

• Loop Optimisations: Tiling and unrolling loops, when combined, typically lead to
the merging of multiple loops into smaller, regular­sized block structures. This re­
duces control overhead, enhances parallelism, and improves cache locality and data
reuse.
• Redundancy Optimisations: (1) Early Alias Analysis, (2) CSE, and (3) DCE, re­
spectively aims to optimise the NN’s code by (1) determining whether two pointers
in a program can refer to the same memory location, (2) reducing the computation
of expressions occurring multiple times, (3) and removing code that has no impact
on code results, respectively.
As a result, what the compiler produces is a very bare­metal and optimized equivalent
of the input NN algorithm, which can then be parsed to the open­source HLS tool Pand­

C/C++ code and MLIR intermediate representations (which can be compiled through C­
compilers such as GoLang) into corresponding Verilog/VHDL. Bambu comes with built­in
verification tools for simulation and analysis, similar to those found in software like Xilinx’s
Vivado. A LeNet CNN was transformed from Python code to a layout on the open­source
theoretical 45nm FreePDK PDK using OpenROAD.
The key findings from SODA­Opt I have concluded as the following:
• Generic input, generic output: Akin to VeriGOOD­ML, having a clear and standard
IO­type allows for easier integration with external tools.
• Compiler optimisations: This is where the magic happens. It is ingenious yet
reads as a simple idea. Considering most HLS tools prefer C/C++ as their input file,
PandaBambu is unique from the pack as it also supports LLVM and its language­
agnostic MLIR framework. Adding an intermediate step where the focus lies on
taking the Python code through a process of optimisation on a compiler­level results
in finely crafted machine code that can be compiled into most languages. As a result,
this simplifies the process of creating HLS­compatible code and, in principle, allows
for the code to be put into any desired target language if needed.
Future work
The paper is mostly focused on expanding the scope beyond general­purpose processors
and HLS­generated accelerators, as well as integration with other open­source tools, to
create more specificity towards particular FPGA platforms. From my own analysis, con­

2.4
Modern advances in FPGA NN accelerators
This section features a quick exploration of FPGA NN accelerator developments, as well
as some tools for the task of NN­to­Verilog translation.
The typical CNN as described in Appendix A.1 is a stack of layers where the output of
each layer is used in the subsequent one. As pointed out in 2020 paper by Chen et al.,
due to the feed­forward nature of the CNN dataflow, it lends itself well to efficient hard­
ware structures. [10] They also state that 90% of operations for general NN accelerators
are matrix multiplications and convolutions, leading to developing efficient topologies for
handling matrix multiplications being paramount in CNN acceleration development, and
thus also the primary focus of optimisation.
2.4.1
Hardware­aware design
Intriguing in the context of NNs is its similarity to something like filter design: multiple ar­
chitectural approaches can achieve the same output from a given input, albeit with distinct
hardware implementations. Consequently, many researchers advocate for a co­design
strategy, where the NN’s architecture and its feasibility for hardware implementation (both
FPGA and ASIC) are given equal weight in consideration.[11]
2.4.2
FPGA design for ASIC design
Characteristic for FPGA is of course the ability to synthesise digital logic typically written in
an HDL. An FPGA has typically three types of core resources at its disposal, DSPs, LUTs,
and BRAM. The NN accelerator architecture of the CNN utilises the FPGA by dividing
the heavy use of multiplications into parallel resources.[12] Naively, this is done through
ascribing DSPs to each node, thus using a considerable amount of float multipliers for
larger networks.
This method has in time been replaced with the now more contemporary approach to
designing NNs for FPGAs, pioneered in 2020 by Wang et al. [13], later refined in 2023
[14]. LUTnet, is a methodology for FPGA NN optimisations. For graphical representation,
see Figure 2.6.
On FPGA, the modern optimisation flow is as follows:
• Hardware­aware NN model: Designing, training, testing, and pruning NNs is done
by leveraging TensorFlow. Deliberate reductions in the total amount of parameters
in the NN, at a cost of some accuracy.
• From DNN to BNN: [12] The optimisation on the FPGA itself involves transforming
the weights and biases of the NN to binarised versions. Reduces computational
complexity. This is accomplished through a combination of Python­written scripts
and HLS tools.
Implementing a DNN on Silicon for ’Intelligent Wireless Capsule Endoscopy’ using Open­source tools

==PAGE:26==

on FPGA technology.
• From logic gates to LUTs: Replacing as many conventional multipliers (DSPs) with
LUTs as possible, it has been shown the circuit complexity and footprint is reduced,
which as a result leads to less fan­in (ie. inputs) at points of summation. Saves area
and resources as compared with DSPs in an equivalent scenario.
• Training on FPGA An additional benefit, LUTs can be updated on the FPGA, thus
backpropagation is possible, also allowing for hardware­specific training.
For validation of performance, efficiency pertaining to use of hardware resources and
circuit latency are simple measures most consider. Four key parameters are (1) Reuse,
ie. blocks can be for single­use or be reused in the circuitry, (2) Latency, ie. taking gate
delay into account etc., (3) Power, (4) Area.
The relation between the the hardware specific parameters lead to a trade­off between
parallelism (to get low latency, it must have low reuse factors), and size (high reuse factors
results in higher latency). In FPGA, the speed at which memory can be accessed is a
bottle­neck for many NN accelerators when latency is of particular importance.[10]

FPGA advancements in an ASIC flow
Significant efforts have been dedicated over the past five years to optimising NNs for
FPGA platforms, particularly focusing on hardware­aware implementation. Can these
optimisations be leveraged in the ASIC domain?
When a LUT is translated for ASIC implementation, it results in a circuit composed of
gates. This raises the question: What is the purpose of employing LUTs initially? Despite
this transition, there remain compelling reasons to explore this avenue in ASIC develop­
ment.
Firstly, integrating FPGA testing into the ASIC development process can offer an addi­
tional layer of testing and validation. As the resources needed to test NNs on FPGA
platforms is tremendously less.
Secondly, LUTs on FPGAs are adaptable, enabling backpropagation and facilitating hardware­
specific training. Those being one level closer to representative hardware then a CPU/GPU
can provide.
What the flow for the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule could do is leverage
1. CPU/GPU for training, testing of NN
2. LUTnet or similar FPGA­optimised methods for reducing NN size and complexity,
while also allowing for training on hardware for further optimisation
3. Translate the final Verilog RTL directly to GDS. Wherein one can consider parasitics,
static timing analysis, and IR drop.
ASIC implementation removes the programmable aspect inherent in FPGAs, yet it does
not preclude its utilisation. With emerging advancements, such as the work by Amarú
et al. in 2021 [16], which delineates a LUT­based optimisation methodology customised
for ASIC synthesis rather than FPGAs (further refined in 2022 [17]), this approach could
potentially become prevalent.
2.4.3
Key takeaways
Overall, it seems there lies an opportunity in using the advances in FPGA NN­to­Verilog
design flows as a part of the development of NN on ASIC. This could potentially come
with an extra layer of validity, as the proposed NN could then be tested in high­flexibility
environments of CPU/GPU and FPGA, before committing to the low flexibility of an ASIC
design. It could also potentially leverage future advances in FPGA, and the emerging
crossover between FPGA and ASIC as shown by Amarú et al.
The drawback of this is approach is two­fold, it assumes the general ’non­ASIC­specific’
LUT­based optimisations will be good enough to match other ASIC­specific methods. It
also assumes it is possible to make gains to model accuracy for ASIC design, by exten­
sively testing NN on FPGA platforms. On both assumptions, I have no clear answers.
With this in mind, I explored methods for designing NN­to­Verilog.

3.1
Research Discussion
This section presents a discussion of the introduced research, with a focus on biases and
gaps.
3.1.1
Research biases
For this study, in order to keep the scope manageable, I aimed to quickly establish some
guidelines for the research, particularly concerning the first three research goals, where
most of the key decisions are made.
The scope of the research for the first three research questions was confined to the fol­
lowing areas:
1. Design template NN algorithm: The Wireless Endoscopy Capsule operates through
image recognition, hence making CNN highly relevant. As CNN also incorporates
standard layers universally found in NN, such as dense and activation layers, along
with various optimisation methods to enhance parameters like model size and effi­
ciency, CNN provides a project­relevant framework with room for increasing com­
plexity.
2. Convert NN algorithm to digital circuit design: With the above constraint, this
also implies that whichever methodology is decided upon should support CNN and
common layers in this domain.
3. Choice of open­source PDK: In this case, production­ready PDKs should be of
higher priority, ie. PDKs where the production of ICs has been successfully demon­
strated. PDKs designed for research (for theoretical purposes) or proprietary PDKs
were largely left out, but since proprietary PDKs also fulfils the marker of production­
ready, support for these should not be excluded from the used methods.
Thus the study was biased towards CNN implementation, and proven production. This
was done within a space where I (the author) had experience with relevant technologies,
and or saw opportunity for introducing novel ideas. This leads into the next set of biases.
Bias of time period
Due to the 2020 release of the OpenROAD project and open­source PDKs, my reviews
focused on methodologies similar to my own research goals from 2020 onward. This
thesis does not fully explore the NN accelerator landscape but rather a portion of it from
2020 onward. I favored freely available resources online.
Bias towards digital architectures
Due to the OpenROAD bias, it’s evident that leading methods like VeriGOOD­ML, SODA­
Opt, and ’AI by AI’ leverage existing digital NN accelerator architecture for ASIC design,
overlooking the potential of analog or mixed­signal approaches. My proposed flow follows
a similar path. I chose to prioritise exploring improvements within the established digital
framework. Additionally, I expanded my search to the FPGA domain to look for novel
ideas.

Bias of authorship
My bias towards certain methods stemmed from my experience with specific tools. I

have some proficiency in Keras and TensorFlow, my expertise in machine learning is still

3.1.2
Research gaps
No major unknowns arose during my research in the fields of writing NN, NN on FPGA,
or RTL­to­GDS. These areas have been extensively studied and have shown continuous
growth throughout the project. HLS tools, developed primarily for FPGAs, have demon­
strated success in ASIC design as well.
However, I must note a lack of documented hands­on experience with some of the tech­
nologies mentioned in the literature reviews.
The lack of hands­on experience
The potential of VeriGOOD­ML and SODA­Opt is indeed significant, with both being fre­
quently cited in papers and surveys exploring the NN­to­Silicon landscape, especially
SODA­Opt. However, I observed in some surveyed papers that they reference the re­
sults from the source papers, rather than providing their own practical experience with
the tools. This suggests a limited hands­on familiarity with the tools, despite their promis­
ing advancements. No online examples of self­taught experts were found, and the tools
had barren GitHub communities.
Assuming marketing alone cannot explain this phenomenon, I identified several probable
reasons why this might be the case:
1. The steep open­source learning curve: I have personally experienced the strug­
gle of familiarising myself with the aforementioned frameworks as they appeared
during my literature review. Simple tasks can become excruciating battles, high­
lighting the daunting learning curve inherent in these open­source tools:
• Installing the software: Certain tools, like SODA­Opt and PandaBambu, suf­
fer from documentation leaving little margin for error. Additionally, tools like
HLS4ML have install guides that lack crucial information about dependencies,
further complicating the setup process.
• Working bug­free: Many of these tools depend on precise package versions,
not just for functionality but for accurate operation. Failure to closely monitor
the output can lead to the mistaken belief that a non­functioning tool is actually
working as intended.
• Navigating the output: While it is understandable that Python­to­C/C++ trans­
lation tools like Keras2C and HLS4ML prioritise optimisation over human read­
ability, this often results in outputs that are not easily understandable. This
reliance on ”trusting the machine” can limit one’s ability to make modifications
tailored to specific scenarios.1
2. Everything is a Docker container: SODA­Opt, PandaBambu, OpenROAD, etc.,
are commonly distributed as Docker containers. While this aligns with industry stan­
dards and offers convenience, it also implies the difficulty of building these frame­

Why this gap?
The disparity between the demonstrated capabilities of these technologies and their lim­
ited adoption is somewhat concerning, albeit understandable. The steep learning curve,
lack of hands­on experience, and reliance on Docker containers may contribute to their
underutilization. Users may struggle with initial setup, and subsequent contribution, cre­
ating a gap between the tools’ potential and their practical use. Larger companies may
also have established workflows, hindering widespread adoption of these alternatives.
Addressing the gap
Addressing these issues presents a monumental challenge. It requires cutting­edge re­
searchers to devote more attention to packaging, which may not be their expertise or
interest, or for general users to significantly enhance their proficiency with specialised
tools to reduce the barrier of entry.
Moreover, most reviewed frameworks (excluding HLS4ML) have not received updates on
their GitHub pages since their release. HLS4ML’s regular updates could be due to CERN
sponsoring its development.
The disparity between the capabilities of current open­source tools and the average de­
veloper needs attention. Continued progress in this domain relies on active participation
from those able to contribute.
An additional consideration is needed
To address this gap in relation to the research goals, an additional consideration is added
to the workflow. There should be extra emphasis on easy installation of dependencies and
a simple interface to increase the likelihood of adoption. A step­by­step guide with clear
examples becomes essential. Routine updates and the opportunity for open dialogue with
developers when issues arise are mandatory. However, even with these considerations
in mind, usage is not guaranteed, as shown in the case of both SODA­Opt and ’AI by AI’.
Based on the aforementioned, I aspire to make the experimental work very easy to dupli­
cate, while still withholding the criteria set for the project. A way I sought to do this is by
including a full­fleshed install guide and example, with practical tips and considerations
sprinkled through out.

NN architecture
Looking to the Prior Works chapter inspired a couple of different approaches to NN model
architecture.
First, I considered these two networks:
1. ’AI by AI’: Uses an extremely small CNN for inference of the MNIST 1­channel 28

produced a 2000 x 2000 µm IC with sky130 PDK.
2. Wireless Endoscopy Capsule: Also utilises an extremely small CNN, though on
a completely different scale. The 3.2 MB CNN, utilises 15 blocks of separable con­
volution with batch normalisation, on 3­channel 240 x 240 px images, at a 400 MHz
clock frequency.
A challenge of any flow methodology is run­time. It can take weeks to complete large IC
structures with automation tools. As such, to incentivize more testing, and less waiting, I
chose to use the flow in two ways:
1. Debug template: Testing whether it was possible to complete the flow on the dense
and activation layers. Functions like a template example which can be run multiple
times an hour for debugging.

it is possible for the FPGA­optimised flow to perform at the same level on the
same PDK. For overview of model layers, see Figure A.7 in Appendix A.4.

fidelity. Used as a way to gauge compatibility. For overview of model layers,
see Figure A.8 in Appendix A.4.

accelerator design, as well as to test if the flow could produce a complete NN accelerator
end­to­end.

Result findings
The following is a summary of the produced results.
Debug template
The debug template primarily showed:
• It is possible to complete the flow on very simple architecture with the current setup.
• The tools used in Flow 1 provide plenty of individual validation methods, which can
be used to access problems throughout the four automation phases.
• The FPGA­targeting HLS4ML can be used to at least automate the creation of dense
and activation layers.
• Openlane2 features plenty of documentation reports pertaining to layout violations,
and has the tools to address these violations.
• It was possible to generate a layout for the debug template with 75% pruning and
reuse factor 16, on a 1244.445 x 1244.445 μm area, using all 5 metal layers of the
sky130 PDK.
Baseline NN
The baseline NN primarily showed:
• It was possible to complete the floorplanning phase of a very simple CNN architec­
ture with the current setup. Extracting reports for layout violations was not possible.
• The FPGA­targeting HLS4ML is not optimal for saving area. Though the model was
already small, and multipliers were heavily reused (by factor 16), it resulted in a
die area 234.83% larger then ”AI by AI”’s bare­metal implementation, and the clock

speed was off target by 15.23% with an uncertainty of 12.50%. The model exhibited
large­scale latency issues, with 0.256 ms delays.
Compatibility NN
The compatibility NN primarily showed:
• Even though separable convolution layers, and the single depthwise convolution
layer are supported by HLS4ML, in practice the HLS compiler will produce unusable
results, often ending in failure / shutdown.
• The clock speed of 400 MHz used by the current Wireless Endoscopy Capsule
project is too tall an order for both Vivado HLS and Openlane2, as it was so er­
ror prone neither an acceptable Verilog project could be synthesised, or a layout
attempt could be produced.
3.5
Discussion on Flow 2
This section briefly outlines the challenges encountered while using PandaBambu and
Keras2c.
I attempted to convert the baseline neural network from ’AI by AI’ using the Keras2c library.
However, when attempting to use PandaBambu, the HLS tool encountered errors related
to analysing the code’s headers, stemming from a compiling error on PandaBambu’s side.
After spending considerable time debugging this issue, I decided to abandon this ap­
proach, as I deemed the encountered errors not sufficiently interesting to warrant further
documentation.
While PandaBambu theoretically offers similar functionality to Vitis/Vivado HLS, in prac­
tice, its usage proved significantly different. Generating C/C++ code compatible with HLS
is already a complex task, particularly aiming for closer­to­bare­metal implementations.
Therefore, employing abstraction libraries like TensorFlow/PyTorch necessitates a sophis­
ticated translation process. PandaBambu’s documentation includes an example demon­
strating the conversion of an ONNX project to a .ll­file using TVM (a hardware compiler
for deep learning), followed by the creation of corresponding Verilog for a 2D convolution
block. However, replicating this example posed a significant challenge in practice, and
I have not progressed beyond this specific example using the TVM method. Due to the
inability to scale this approach to larger networks or develop it into a template, I discon­
tinued experimentation with PandaBambu for this thesis report. Nonetheless, remnants
of the implementation can be found on the project’s GitHub page.[23]
Regarding the translation of Python to C/C++, utilising TVM would be ideal, as it is theo­
retically and documentarily sound.

Discussion
The following presents an evaluation of the project with a focus on discussing challenges
and hindsights. This is followed by a section addressing the original research problems.
And lastly is a section on future work and recommendations for the future.
4.1
Evaluating the project
The ambition to achieve an end­to­end NN­to­Silicon flow proved too ambitious. Despite
efforts, the goal to create an automation flow starting from a high­level environment, which
allowed for manufacturing of an NN­accelerator on open­source PDK, was not met within
the project period.
Challenges
Challenges included quantifying ideas like FPGA optimisations applied to the ASIC do­
main. Sahafi highlighted the need to integrate components cohesively, foreseeing itera­
tive adjustments throughout the process. This, in fact, turned out to be exactly the case.
The functions connecting the flow components were straightforward, being focused on
format conversions, and manual parameter setting. However, the relatively low complex­
ity of the solutions were completely at odds with the high complexity of the process of
deducing the issues, which required reverse engineering across Python libraries, Verilog
projects, and layouts, along sifting through any available documentation.
The project spanned NN models, FPGA, and IC design, each with their own unique prob­
lems and terminologies. Though pre­project, I considered myself well­versed in these
domains, the project was a humbling experience.
Time­wasters
Regularly when doing projects like this with a lot of software dependencies, one would
work within a virtual environment, as to ensure package consistency throughout the entire
work period. I knew it would be dangerous to venture outside of this norm. Although
package updates did cause some delays, the upside was that updates often resolved
previous issues. Today, installing Flow 1 components on an Ubuntu machine seamlessly
integrates with any global Python packaging handler, ensuring smooth execution of the
flow script.
A monkey wrench
At the halfway point of the project, ’AI by AI’ by Baungarten et al., and the incredibly com­
prehensive survey of NN accelerator automation tools by Ferrandi et al. were released.
This prompted an expansion of the thesis objective beyond simply creating an NN accel­
erator workflow, into an end­to­end automation flow for CNN in a Python environment,
with a focus on verification. Though I liked the challenge, I see how this objective came
at a cost.
The unaccomplished
Many desired outcomes remain unaccomplished, including robust FPGA test­benching
and ASIC verification processes. Addressing issues with Python libraries, some now
deprecated (see Appendix A.5 for details), consumed time, as did deciphering installation
and usage instructions for open­source software, though the process is actually fairly
simple, just sparsely documented (for full installation guide, see Appendix A.7). More on
suggested improvements in Section 4.4.

Project plan
From a project planning standpoint, the original project approach prioritised iterative com­

4.2
Practical application of the experimental results
The experimental results show that Flow 1 as a whole is best suited as a lightweight
plug­and­play script, which enables one to get familiar with relevant terminology, and to
get practical intuition for how relevant software work. Flow 1 is a fairly simple­to­install
framework, all­encompassing of the NN­to­Silicon process, which has not produced any
results which can be manufactured.
To answer the question of whether HLS4ML is a good resource in an ASIC flow, I find the
answer to trend towards no.
”Yes” would be due to the high amount of transparency and customisation layer to layer.
Working with the tool is simple and elegant, and provides many quality of life features and
verification stages. Many great examples of CNN on RGB datasets exist on their GitHub
page, and though this experiment does not replicate these results.
”No” would be because the baseline comparison showed that ”AI by AI”’s bare­metal C
translation far surpasses the HLS4ML­generated equivalent on all relevant parameters;
Timing, delay, die area, not to mention the distinct lack of violation reports. In addition,
personal experience with using TensorFlow’s depthwise convolution, and/or separable
convolution layers threw many undocumented errors, though the HLS4ML documentation
does state these are supported.
In this case, what is optimal for an FPGA was not equivalent to what was optimal for an
ASIC.
The results were sadly very clouded by a bottleneck of the experimental work; Computing
power, as many flows stopped due to lack of system resources (thus the need for com­
pression in all experimental cases). As such for future endeavours, one should consider
the use of HPCs from the get­go, and be comfortable with flows like these taking any­
where between 12 hours to multiple days before any assessments can be made. This is
not to say that the computing power issue solves the aforementioned problems, just that
it would enable a more definitive comparison.
4.3
Revisiting the research question
Returning to our original research query – Whether or not current open­source tools can
be used to create a simple NN accelerator chip, as to facilitate future custom chip devel­
opment in the Wireless Endoscopy Capsule design – this study suggests the answer is
yes.
1. Recent methodologies, including one fully open­source tool[6], another predom­
inantly utilising open­source tools[4], and a flow incorporating open­source APIs
and software for RTL­to­GDS translation[7], demonstrate the capability to automate
hardware­aware design for NN accelerators on modern architectures with digital
circuitry.
2. These methodologies offer a foundation for a robust approach.
Leveraging ad­
vancements in open­source frameworks for Python ML libraries[24], bare­metal trans­
lation/compiler code optimisation, HLS, and RTL­to­GDS flows[25], it is increasingly
46
Implementing a DNN on Silicon for ’Intelligent Wireless Capsule Endoscopy’ using Open­source tools
==PAGE:57==
evident that existing open­source tools can be harnessed to develop methodologies
enabling developers to operate exclusively in high­level language programming en­
vironments for NN to produce silicon equivalents.
3. Though with limited results, the experiment conducted for this thesis shows that it is
possible to completely automate the design process of common NN blocks to layout
in a Python interface with a single button press, leveraging advancements in FPGA,
with a predominantly open­source approach.
Whether or not the Wireless Endoscopy project should use current open­source tools
depends on a couple of factors.
1. NN Scale: While results have demonstrated successful implementations for con­
temporary architectures like LeNet[6] and simple two convolution layer CNNs[7],
fully automating CNNs at the scale of current Wireless Endoscopy Capsule NNs
to silicon has not been achieved. The experimental findings indicate that networks
larger than a few megabytes, with clock frequencies surpassing 100 MHz, could
potentially take weeks to process in Openlane2. Not to mention this thesis’ lack­
luster results with HLS tools of NN models beyond 10.000 parameters. The minimal
human intervention in these processes means that any errors or misconfigurations
of critical parameters could set the design process back significantly. Moreover,
these processes may necessitate high­performance computing resources, as cur­
rent RTL­to­GDS tools lack optimisation for multi­threading and exhibit poor memory
management.
2. Limitations of open­source PDKs: As of today, utilising the sky130 PDK is still
part of an experimental phase of Google’s open­source IC production. It is con­
stantly reiterated that sky130 is in its infancy.[26] Though credible designs have
been produced using set PDK, there is no insurance of functionality.
3. Open­source tools? Open­source designs: As selecting a PDK, is also selecting
who should produce the IC, agreeing to Google’s terms of use, one also agrees to
share the design to other users and Google itself. Depending on the desired level
of discretion, this needs to be considered.
4. Licence issues: Under section 4 of the End User Licence Agreement of Vivado,
concerning use of bit­streams generated, it is written that ”No right is granted here­
under to use the Software or any Bitstream generated by use of the Software to
program or develop designs for non­Xilinx Devices[...]”[27]. Yet Vitis HLS falls un­
der the Apache License, Version 2.0. In online discussions on the AMD forums, and
in online community spaces like Reddit, there is generally quite a bit of confusion on
what users are allowed to do, thus professionals from AMD should be addressed
with this issue.
5. What about the rest of the ASIC? Efabless provides an ASIC template with SPI,
but this template is limited to 2000 x 2000 nm designs, and limits the use of M5
layer of the sky130 PDK. It would thus be advised to look into alternative methods
for creating memory, IO etc. More on this in future works, Section 4.4.
Key Takeaway
To summarise, findings show that automation tools can be used at each stage of NN ac­
celerator development, with multiple options for NN development platform and HLS tools,
though only one RTL­to­GDS tool. Even the relatively simple FPGA­inspired flow from
the experimental work, shows promise in accelerating development time of key blocks.

When one considers that RTL­to­GDS is (as of now) the most popular way of generating
NN accelerators, this presents a future wherein people with expertise in Verilog and asso­
ciated EDA software, are more then capable of creating improvements in this landscape.
4.4
Future work & recommendations
For this particular project, the focus was on automating NN accelerators. In this domain
there are several points of entry for future work:
1. FPGA testbench: As a complete Verilog project is produced, it would be a missed
opportunity to not use e.g. Vivado and write a testbench to validate the digital cir­
cuitry and produce timing diagrams before the RTL­to­GDS process.
2. Open­source HLS: Replacing Vivado HLS with the open­source Vitis HLS (which
HLS4ML claims to work with) would be an obvious improvement, assuming there is
some transparency surrounding licensing and use.
3. Test on other PDKs: Openlane2 allows for adding other PDKs, including propri­
etary ones, thus if one wishes to go the extra mile, there lies an opportunity in further
decreasing the size of the developed layout, having more options for addressing
parasitics etc. Alternatively, it also allows for working with different vendors.
4. Create a complete CNN design: As the experimental work only managed to use
a simple debug template for most of the testing, it makes sense to port Flow 1 (or
similar) to an HPC to test if larger designs are possible and accelerate the speed at
which these designs are completed.
5. Further violation fixing: An additional improvement would be to take the address­
ing of layout violations further by providing own constraints, thus allowing for a de­
sign which can be manufactured.
6. Manufacture the layout: Manufacturing a complete layout and doing physical tests.
If not enabled by a template SoC design like Efabless’ 2000 x 2000 nm Caraven
template, through one’s own design.
The last point brings us to the next. In the domain of creating the rest of the components
needed to complete the ASIC design, there are also several points of entry for future work:
1. Implementation of OpenRAM: To address memory, open­source framework Open­
RAM allows one to create custom SRAM. This is partly integrated into the sky130
PDK.
2. RISC­V framework: It has been demonstrated that the RISC­V CPU architecture
can be produced through OpenROAD and its derivatives. Even using Arduino.[28]
This could potentially be used to as a communication and control layer for an AI
ASIC.
3. Open­source IP cores: Projects like OpenCores is a user­driven community, shar­
ing everything from communication protocols like I2C, to encryption like AES. Build­
ing from this could present an opportunity, while keeping in­line with the open­source
spirit.

Conclusion
This study provides grounds for cautious optimism. Recent advancements and method­
ologies leveraging open­source frameworks demonstrate promise in automating hardware­
aware design for convolutional neural network accelerators. While challenges persist,
including scaling to larger networks, the demonstrated ability to automate common NN
blocks to layout signifies progress towards enabling developers to operate exclusively
in high­level language programming environments for NN­to­Silicon translations. In as­
sessing the effectiveness of HLS4ML within an ASIC flow, the evidence leans towards
a negative evaluation. While HLS4ML offers transparency and layer­by­layer customisa­
tion, a comparative analysis with a bare­metal C translation from ”AI by AI” by Baungarten
et al. reveals significant disparities in crucial metrics such as timing, delay, die area, and
error reporting. Additionally, challenges encountered with TensorFlow’s depthwise and
separable convolution layers underscore the limitations of HLS4ML in handling certain
NN architectures effectively. Though falling short in producing viable results for manu­
facturing, the produced experiment, Flow 1, emerges as an accessible tool for gaining
familiarity with NN­to­Silicon processes, offering ease of installation and an introductory
understanding of relevant concepts.

A.1
Primer on NN layers for CNN
Figure A.1: [29] Shows a simple feed­forward CNN with its most common layers. The
following section describes the individual layers shown here. Note how the input should
be any arbitrary image with a vehicle, and the output should be the probability this given
vehicle belongs to one of several classes; Car, truck, van etc. This is generally the purpose
of a multi­class CNN which outputs a probability distribution.
Convolutional layer
Unlike traditional fully connected NN, CNNs are specifically structured to handle grid­like
data, hence its repeated use in image recognition tasks where the input is a pixel­by­pixel
grid ie. matrix or tensor. This is done through the use of a smaller size pixel­by­pixel filter
(or kernel) that convolves (or slides) across subsets of the input image, starting from the
top­left corner, ending at the bottom­right corner. The distance by which the kernel slides
across the image is referred to as its stride. By performing element­wise multiplications
this way, a CNN ultimately produces a feature map of summations of the element­wise
multiplications, as seen on Fig. A.2.
The feature map highlights regions in the input data that correspond to specific patterns or
features that the filters are designed to detect. The convolutional concept also works for
colour images. If an image is in RGB­colour it is said to have a depth of 3 (or 3 channels),
and correspondingly, convolution with a 3­depth filter is performed on each channel. This
can be used to detect specific variations in colour. When combined with edge detection
one could detect specific objects e.g. a blue car.
Pooling layer
Used to reduce the spatial dimensions of the input and decrease computational com­
plexity, the pooling layer typically either retains the maximum value (max pooling) or the
average value (average pooling) in a local region.
Batch normalisation layer
For the training process of an NN, a typical problem is determining initial values of the
weights and biases in the layers. The purpose of batch normalisation layers is to simply
normalise input values (ie. zero­mean, divided by standard deviation). This effectively
puts all values on the same scale, thus reducing the dependency on the specific values
of the initial weights.

Normalising values like this also reduces covariate shift, which is the situation where there
is a numerical discrepancy between test and training data set, and real­life input data
which might be on a different scale.
In addition, for some networks it results in faster convergence during the training process,
ie. when weights have reached a local optimum, thus it can also be a time saver.
Activation layer
In the CNN context, activation layers are situated after convolutional and dense layers.
Its purpose is to transform one layer’s output value into a specific type of input value for
the subsequent layer or as a final output entirely.
There are several methods for activation layers. Some relevant ones with a short descrip­
tion are
• ReLu: Replaces all negative values in a feature map with zeroes. Often used be­
tween layers.

• SoftMax: Converts a network’s raw multi­class output into a probability distribution.
Often used as a final output.
• Sigmoid: Converts a network’s raw output into a value between 0 and 1. Used in

• Tanh: Similar to Sigmoid, but now between ­1 and 1.
Flatten layer
Entails transforming a matrix structure into its vector representation, either row­wise or
column­wise. The effect of this is for CNN is that it creates compatibility with dense layers
by going from 2D to 1D.

Metrics for validating performance
There are many ways to validate the performance of NNs, if purely interested in its ability
to make correct predictions, typically two parameters are used:
1. Value accuracy: How accurate the model is at making correct predictions on un­
seen data. Closer to 100% the better
2. Entropy loss: A measure used to discriminate confident wrong predictions. Closer
to zero the better.
Optimisation
The data science world has given us robust and time­tested libraries such as TensorFlow
and Torch, offering frameworks for development in both ML and NN contexts. Both come
with C++ and Python libraries. Additionally, the Keras API for Python utilises both Tensor­
Flow and Torch as back­ends, providing an even more user­friendly interface. Keras also
has a derivative, QKeras, which focuses on quantisation for mobile and edge devices on
specific FPGA platforms.
For all the aforementioned frameworks, model optimisation strategies are inherent, allow­
ing for optimisations directly on the model before eventual code translation, HLS, or layout
creation.
Common strategies such as
• Weight pruning: Involves identifying and removing connections (weights) in the
neural network with low magnitudes, ie. set them to zero. Sparse networks with
pruned weights lead to reduced model size, faster inference, and lower memory

57
==PAGE:68==
requirements. In addition, most HLS tools have dead code detection and removes
these zeroes during synthesis, thus leading to an equivalently smaller digital circuit.
• Quantisation: Involves reducing the precision of weights and activations from higher
bit representations (e.g., 32­bit floating­point) to lower bit representations (e.g., 8­bit
integers). This reduces memory footprint and speeds up inference, and under some
circumstances, without any significant reduction in model accuracy.
And specifically for CNN, an alternative to standard convolution as seen in the Wireless
Endoscopy Capsule is called depthwise separable convolution, and consists of two steps:
• Depthwise Convolution: Offers a significant reduction in computations compared
to standard convolutions by independently applying convolutions across channels.
In a traditional convolutional approach, a single filter with depth = 3, is applied across
all three channels of an image, thus the computations performed increase 9­fold.
However, with depthwise convolution using three separate filters, one for each chan­
nel, the computations are only increased by a factor of 3.
• Pointwise Convolution: As the output of the depthwise convolution is now three
feature maps, this step is required to combine the information into a single feature
map. As the name suggests, this is done by convolving each individual value (point­
by­point).
Differences in pooling strategies can also be incorporated. This process is generally de­
cided more on a per network basis, as the effectiveness varies depending on the task at
hand.
In combination these optimisations lead to improved efficiency, and is generally used in the
domain of Mobile and Edge devices; Fitting in with the goals of the Wireless Endoscopy
Capsule project, as well as giving a potential edge at the synthesis and layout stage.

Practical Tip: Do not include comment sections with """ in the flow class, as it will pro­

The way this works is that by referring (or tagging) to the last run of the flow, Openlane2
instantiates all the relevant directories, then when running the flow from a later step (like
the Parasitics Extraction step), all the relevant files will be accessible and it can run with
all the ”last run” files.
This also allows for modifications to later­step configuration parameters, e.g. if a specifi­
cation is not met and produces an error, we can add a modification to the configuration
and retry the step.
