Abstract 
For the past decades nucleic acid structure and interactions have been studied using time 
consuming enzyme amplification of test sequences to generate a large enough amount of 
substrate that could give a read signal. PCR and qPCR have mostly been used for amplification. 
To fully understand to a full extent the model of nucleic acid folding and functions at low 
abundance levels, we must design methods to achieve this. 
Synthesis of nucleosides analogues provides a wide range of possibilities to detect viral sequence 
because they are enzyme-free and not dependent on a systematic specific modification. Synthesis 
of analogues that contain fluorophores and probes which selectively bind and sense the U(T) and 
C nucleotides will be performed and incorporated to a larger project which also includes A and G 
for human identification. 
With the use of developed designs and synthetic strategies, we aim at novel, unbiased and 
hybridization based assays that can detect STRs in a sequence for identification at low 
abundance levels. These novel assays are very reliable and they provide a robust validation by 
’’overlapping’’ probe approach, and a simple workflow to provide low cost. 
Amphilic amino glycopids which will be used as transfection agent (a process of directly 
introducing nucleic acid into cells) is synthesised and then tested for its tranfection ability on our 
nucleosides analogues. 
The goal is to provide novel tools with the objective of going beyond enzymatic amplification 
when assessing biologically and clinically actionable nucleic acids. Also successful synthesis of 
the Transfection agent. 
Synthesis of modified fluorescence oligonucleotides used for amplification free detection of 
nucleic acid sequences and transfection of these nucleotides into cells to characterised the 
transfection abilities of the amphilic sophorolipid amino acids. 
This multidisciplinary thesis blends organic synthesis with Chemical biology and its application 
in forensic genetics.

INTRODUCTION 
Chapter 1 
Introduction 
The has been a revolution in our knowledge of Nucleic Acid (NA) sequences at the turn of the 
21st century with sequencing of the human genome being at the fore front. The analysis of 
genomic DNA can facilitate the learning process about evolution, relationships between 
individual organisms, the means by which genes are controlled, hereditary disease, identification 
of individuals (humans) and the encoded information within DNA sequence. Analysing DNA 
sequences have led to applications such as DNA fingerprinting which have transformed forensic 
science for in identification of individuals (1-5). 
Most of the established methods including DNA sequencing, genetic and forensic analysis 
depends on labeled oligonucleotide, and they require DNA polymerization. PCR (a process of 
amplifying a specific region of DNA sample using PCR) can be use in genetic analysis in short 
tandem repeat (STR) analysis (6). 
Short tandem repeats (STRs) are short repetitive sequences (repeats) that are 2 to 6 base pairs 
long used in DNA profiling for identification of different regions (loci) individually in human 
DNA.  Human genomic STRs contain base pairs that are repeatedly arranged in a specific gene 
sequence (7).  
STR analysis has been the most widely used method for identifying individuals in forensics. STR 
polymorphism enhances the probability of identifying individuals since it involves large number 
alleles which allows for the analysis of multiple loci. Standard STR typing is performed by 
polymerase chain reaction (PCR) in which the regions at the beginning and at the end of the 
repetitive region are used to bind the primers to determine the size of the amplicon (Allele) (8-13). 
Modified oligonucleotides have been used extensively to detect and study nucleic acid sequences 
by  labelling it with a fluorophore which when incorporated into a probe sequence emits 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
radiation (fluorescent) upon successful hybridization with a target sequence or do not 
fluorescence if there is a mismatch. The fluorescence change on the base in the complementary 
strand can be read fluorometrically without the need to wash or separated the strands. Under 
standard conditions, nucleobases are not fluorescent therefore require modification to become 
fluorescent(14-21).  
Saito et al 2003, had used pyrene-labelled base-discriminating fluorescent DNA Probes for 
homogeneous SNP typing. They synthesised novel nucleosides containing pyrenecarboxamide 
chromophore linked by a propargyl linker (22). Other studies had used Polymerase chain reaction 
to amplify sequence in other to have a high enough concentration for a read-out. 
This study will be focused on the chemistry aspect of synthesising fluorescent nucleosides 
involving deoxyuridine (dU) and deoxycytosine (dC) to be incorporated into a probe sequence to 
target STRs in human Y chromosomes for identification. Here we aim to synthesised rhodamine 
B-carboxamide connected by an N-propargyl linker to enhance the fluorescent properties of 
Rhodamine B. This fluorophore was choosen because it forms a more rigid structure with the 
nucleoside thereby increasing fluorescence intensity. We will attempt to attach the rhodamine B-
carboxamide to synthesised nucleosides dU and dC which will provide a read-out when 
incorporated into a probe sequence and hybridized with a target. This modification will also be 
less susceptible to quenching. Hypothetically, using these modified nucleosides we can eliminate 
the amplification process which is time consuming to provide an instant readout identifying 
individuals. This project is part of a larger project also involving deoxyadenosine and 
deoxyguanosine. 
As much as we are interested in the modification of nucleic acids to elucidate its structural 
properties and develop potential applications, the delivery of genes into cells has also been of 
great interest in this field. Modified genes or gene segments are delivered into cells through 
transfection in other express the encoded genetic information in the genes. With the discovery of 
lipofection in 1987, there has been an increase in the interest of developing a non-viral gene 
delivery carrier to emulate the more efficient viral carriers which are very toxic (23-31). 
Cationic gene carriers have been developed and used extensively for this purpose but they are far 
less efficient than viral gene carriers. These carriers form a complex (lipoplexes) with the anionic 
phosphate backbone of DNA through electrostatic interactions and are taken up into cells 
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
through endocytosis (32-37). One of the major characteristics of good transfection agent is its net 
positive charge after formation of the complex with the gene. This can either enhance or decrease 
the transfection efficiency of the agent. The negatively charged DNA is repelled by the 
negatively charged cell surface which makes it difficult for these genes to enter cells. Developing 
a gene carrier with the right balance of positive charge will lead to a higher affinity of the 
negatively charged cell surfaces towards it(38-46). 
Size of the tranfection agent also plays a major factor for endocytic cellular uptake. Very large 
particles are difficult to diffuse into the vascular periphery. Targeted delivery of genes with 
specific ligand-receptor interaction must be taken into account. Hepatic liver cells are a typical 
target for target delivery because they posses an asialoglycoprotein receptors for 
oligosaccharides bearing galatose residue. Modification of cationic transfection agents can alter 
its properties leading to a difficult to predict scenario during formation and interaction of the 
complex with DNA(49).  
To address those problems above, here we aim for the chemical synthesis of a novel transfection 
agent: amphilic amino glycolipid derivative. Inspiration was drawn from the enzymatic synthesis 
of amphilic sophorolipids which are been used as surfactants. Swapping the sophorose sugar 
head (amphilic head) with lactose to make economically feasible and using ricinoilic acid (with 
an extended hydrophobic backbone) can be glycosylated with the sugar moity at the secondary 
12-hydroxyl carbon while allowing for amide coupling of amino acids at the acid labile at carbon 
1 to give the derivatives. The lactose moity acts as a charge-masking, complex formation 
promoter and lowers the overall toxity (50-53). These amino acids can become positively charged 
thereby increasing the overall positive charge during formation of the carrier-DNA complex(55). 
This study will use L-valine tert-butyl ester to synthesis one derivative.

Background 
2.1 Chemically Modified Oligonucleotides 
There are many applications of oligonucleotides in biology and forensic science. They are used 
to reveal and study the presence of a specific nucleic acid sequence in DNA sequencing and 
mutation detection. In these applications DNA and oligonucleotides are labelled with radioactive 
nuclei via radioactive deoxyribonucleotide triphosphates (3H, 125I and 14C) or fluorescent dyes so 
that their presence is identified. ATDBio Ltd outlines the bases for suitable modification as 
follows(2-5): 
 
The label should be easy to attach under mild conditions using a simple, cheap and 
reproducible protocol. 
 
The method of attachment should not interfere with the signal-generating properties of 
the label or affect the hybridization of the oligonucleotide/DNA to its target nucleic acid. 
 
The label should be detectable at low concentrations (using suitable instrumentation). 
 
In some cases it may be advantageous to add multiple labels to a single oligonucleotide; 
the label should be compatible with this. 
 
The label should be stable to hybridization conditions e.g. elevated temperatures (up to 
96 °C), detergents, and aqueous buffers up to pH 9. 
 
The label should be stable to long term storage at −20 °C and be easily disposable. 
 
It should be possible to simultaneously detect several different labels simultaneously and 
to differentiate between them (multiplex detection).

2.3 Nucleic acid structure and function (one use of fluorescent nucleotides)  
Nucleic acids can be found in different forms, aggregated states, and polymorphs, which could 
be related to a specific cellular function. Appropriately selected fluorescent nucleosides, 
strategically placed within the nucleic acid strands, can photophysically signal hybridization, 
folding, and conformational changes. Additionally, fluorescent nucleoside analogs have been 
used to fabricate assays that monitor enzymes operating on nucleic acids (e.g., polymerases) or 
nucleic acid-based enzymes (e.g., ribozymes). 
2.4 Fluorescent base analogues  
Standard nucleobases (A, G, C, T and U) are not fluorescent under standard conditions. 
Fluorescent base analogues are structural analogues of the standard bases that are fluorescent, 
while still forming hydrogen bonds with standard bases (13-17). 
Fluorescent labels are commonly attached to the double helix at the end of a linker, which places 
the fluorophore relatively far from the DNA bases. This is useful in many circumstances, but 
sometimes it is necessary to incorporate a fluorophore closer to the DNA or RNA double helix, 
without perturbing the helix. As fluorescent base analogues are located rigidly in the double 
helix, their movement is restricted. This restricted movement results in a more predictable

2.6 The Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) 
The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a technique used widely in molecular biology,

of a DNA sample. PCR can amplify a few molecules of a precious DNA sample (e.g. at the

2.7 DNA Fingerprinting 
DNA fingerprinting was invented in the 1980s by Sir Alec Jeffreys at the University of Leicester. 
Human DNA can be analyzed by this method to identify individuals at the genetic level with a 
far greater degree of certainty than previous forensic methods such as blood group determination 
or traditional fingerprint analysis. DNA fingerprinting (also called DNA profiling, DNA typing 
or genetic fingerprinting) can also be used to determine the relationship between individuals (e.g. 
paternity testing). 
2.8 Short Tandem Repeat (STRs) Genotyping 
STRs also known as microsatalites are short, repetitive sequences (repeats) 2-6 base pairs long. 
These repeats vary from person to person and it is usually found in the intron (non-coding) 
region of genes. This difference in repeats of homologous STR loci between individuals leads to 
polymorphism. STR polymorphism has made it possible to be able to determine genetic profiles 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
of individuals especially in forensic cases. Y-chromosome STRs (Y-STRs) found on Y-
chromosome are widely used for analysis. Capillary electrophoresis (CE) and Next-Generation-
Sequencing (NGS) are mostly used for the analysis of STRs loci. Some factors can interfere with 
these repeats and cause mutation. Mutation in the sequence motif can also offer forensic 
applications of STRs analysis. 
2.8.1 STR Analysis 
STR analysis uses PCR to measure the number of repeats at specific loci. Primers bind to the 
DNA at specific STR loci and, are extended by PCR. The length of the PCR product depends on 
the number of repeats. If the PCR primers are labelled, the PCR products will be labelled, 
allowing the products to be detected at the end of the reaction. For each STR locus, there will be 
two PCR products (one for each of two alleles). 
2.8.2 Fluorescent STR analysis 
In a more modern variant of STR analysis, the PCR primers are labelled with fluorescent dyes. 
Primers for different STR loci are labelled with different fluorescent dyes, adding a second 
dimension to the assay As it has so far been possible to develop only a limited number of 
fluorescent dyes with well-resolved spectral characteristics, three different fluorescent dyes are 
typically used.

Key reaction Mechanisms in the Projects 
 
Sonogoshira Crosscoupling  
 
 
The Sonogashira cross-coupling reaction is the organic reaction of an organohalide with a 
terminal alkyne to give the coupled product using a palladium catalyst, a copper catalyst, and 
base. The palladium cataly-zed mechanism begins with oxidative addition of the organohalide to 
the Pd(0) to form a Pd(II) complex. Transmetalation with the organocopper reagent, formed from 
the terminal alkyne and the copper catalyst, then follows. The alkynyl anion replaces the halide 
on the palladium complex and regenerates the copper halide catalyst. Reductive elimination then 
gives the final coupled product, regenerates the palladium catalyst, and the catalytic cycle can 
begin again.[1]

3.1.6.2  Capping (Step 2) 
Capping prevents deletion mutation in solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis. It is difficult to 
achieve 100% yield during each step of synthesis of oligonucleotides and this therefore leaves 
the posibity of some 5’-hydroxyl unreacted groups that may get involved in coupling of the next 
nucleoside. This results in deletion of bases and at the end of the oligonucleotide synthesis, a 
complex mixture of oligonucletides will be synthesised carrying the wrong genetic information. 
Capping is carried by use of two reagents in the synthesiser, these include acetic anhydride and 
N-methylimidazole (NMI). Both are dissolved in tetrahydrofuran with pyridine (small amount) 
to form electrophilic mixtures that rapidly acetylate the unreacted 5’-hydroxyl group rendering 
==PAGE:34==

them inert. The use of pyridine is to keep the pH basic to prevent detritylation by acetic acid 
formed during mixing.
