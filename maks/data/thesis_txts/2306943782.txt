Abstract
Climate change is expected to cause a 30 % increase in rainfall intensity for rain
events with return period of 10 years by 2100, leading pluvial ﬂooding to be con-
sidered one of the most important impacts in terms of climate change in Denmark.
Urbanized areas with high fraction of impervious surfaces are challenged both in
terms of compliance with service level and ﬂood management. Low impact devel-
opment (LID) has gained attention since it helps to restore the hydrological water
cycle before intense urbanization by enhancing inﬁltration and evapotranspiration.
This study aims to investigate the beneﬁts of combining LIDs and cloudburst so-
lution in order to achieve the climate change adaptation goal.
Tåsinge Plads, in Copenhagen, was used as case study in order to identify beneﬁts
and drawbacks of two new tools in MIKE URBAN for modelling LIDs technologies,
i.e. LID Screening tool and soakaway node. 7 models were created, analysed and
evaluated based on the expected behaviour of the system in reality. Finally, a com-
prehensive hydraulic model was proposed to investigate the hydrological impacts of
the LIDs on the surrounding area. The eﬀects of the LIDs were analysed in three
diﬀerent domains, i.e. everyday rain, design rain and extreme events.
The Climate Change Adaptation Plan produced by the Municipality of Copen-
hagen was analysed in diﬀerent perspectives, identifying the diﬀerent goals in terms
of climate change adaptation. Skt. Kjelds district was chosen as case study since it
is characterized by a high fraction of impervious surfaces and shown to be sensitive
to climate change. The branch of the Copenhagen Cloudburst Management Plan
for the area of Skt. Kjelds represented the backbone for further development of a
comprehensive plan aiming at improving the annual water balance, complying with
the service level in future climate and mitigating ﬂood damages. The objectives
were pursued by implementation of LIDs (by utilizing the LID Screening tool) and
surface solutions together with disconnection of areas from the existing sewer sys-
tem to cloudburst pipes. Applying a rainfall-runoﬀmodel B, a hydraulic model
was established in MIKE URBAN in order to evaluate the proposed plan.
We present that the soakaway node is the best tool to represent both the bio-
retention system and basin system at Tåsinge Plads due to the implemented in-
ternal overﬂow structure and basins coupled in series, respectively. Moreover, this
study shows that the LIDs placed in Tåsinge Plads are overdimensioned compared
to common use of LIDs solutions, since they are able to manage a CDS rain with
return period T = 100. If the LIDs are disconnected from the combined sewer
system to a separate system in a future scenario, this study shows the discharge to
Lynetten wastewater treatment plant is reduced by 55 %.
The presented solution for Skt. Kjelds shows great potential in everyday, design
and extreme rain by strategically combining solutions with diﬀerent goals. The
water balance for the area of Skt. Kjelds is tangibly improved, reducing the annual
accumulated discharge to Lynetten wastewater treatment plant by 20 %. The pro-
posed plan allows the sewer system to comply with the service level. It is shown
that the ﬁnal solution dramatically reduces ﬂooding. This goal was achieved by
reducing 73 % of the volume of water ﬂowing on the surface, and handling part of
it with proper surface solutions.

Sammenfatning
Regnintensiteten for en 10 års hændelse i Danmark forventes at stige med omkring
30 % i 2100, grundet klimaændringer. Det gør oversvømmelsesrisikoen fra skybrud
til den største påvirkning af klimaforandringer i Danmark. Byområder med store
dele impermeable overﬂader er særligt udfordret, når det kommer til at overholde
serviceniveau og oversvømmelseshåndtering. Lokal Aﬂedning af Regn (LAR) har
derfor fået stigende opmærksomhed, da de grønne overﬂadeløsninger hjælper med
at genoprette den naturlige hydrologiske cyklus ved af lade vandet inﬁltrere og
fordampe. Dette speciale undersøger fordelene ved at kombinere LAR og skybrud-
sløsninger, så målene i Klimatilpasningsplanerne kan nås.
MIKE URBAN leverede i starten af 2016 to nye metoder at modellere LAR løs-
ninger på, nemlig ”LID Screening tool” og ”Soakaway node”. Fordele og ulemper
ved at anvende værktøjerne er i dette speciale undersøgt, hvor Tåsinge Plads i
København er brugt som case study. 7 modeller er etableret, analyseret og eval-
uret baseret på den forventelige opførelse af systemet.
På den baggrund er en
omfattende hydraulisk model foreslået, hvor eﬀekten af de implementerede LAR
løsninger på de omkringliggende områder undersøgt. Eﬀekten af LAR løsningerne
er undersøgt i tre forskellige domæner, nemlig hverdags, design og ekstrem situa-
tionen.
Københavns klimatilpasningsplan er analyseret og målene identiﬁceret. Byområdet
Skt. Kjelds er valgt som case study, da området er karakteriseret af store imperme-
able overﬂader og området i nyere tid har oplevet problemer med oversvømmelse.
Vandoplandet for Skt. Kjelds repræsenteret i Københavns skybrudsplan er brugt
som rygrad for implementeringen af de foreslåede løsninger, der søger at forbedre
den årlige vandbalance, at overholde serviceniveauet samt at mindske skaderne
fra oversvømmelse. Målet er forfulgt ved at implementere LAR løsninger (ved at
bruge ”LID Screening tool”) og overﬂadeløsninger foreslået i skybrudsplanen samt
afkoble arealer fra det eksisterende kloaksystem og koble dem til de i skybrud-
splanen foreslåede skybrudsledninger. Regn-afstrømning model B er brugt i den
hydrauliske model i MIKE URBAN.
Vi konkluderer, at ”soakaway node” er det bedste værktøj til at repræsentere både
vejbedene og bassinet implementeret på Tåsinge Plads. Grunden er, at vejbedene
har interne overløbsbygærker implementeret, hvor bassinerne er implementeret i
serie. Vi viser, at LAR løsningerne implementeret på Tåsinge Plads er overdimen-
sioneret i forhold til den normale anvendelse af LAR, da både vejbedene og bassinet
kan håndtere en CDS regn T = 100. Derudover viser vi, at afstrømningen til det
eksisterende system kan reduceres med 55 %, hvis LAR løsningerne afkobles det
eksisterende system og kobles til det separate skybrudssystem i fremtiden.
Den præsenterede løsning for Skt. Kjelds viser stort potentiale i alle tre domæner
ved at strategisk kombinere løsninger med forskellige mål. Vandbalancen for Skt.
Kjelds er forbedret markant, da den årlige akkumuleret aﬂedning til Lynetten ren-
sningsanlæg er reduceret med 20 %. Vi viser, at den foreslåede plan overholder
serviceniveauet for området.
Derudover konkluderer vi, at den endelig løsning
dramatisk reducerer oversvømmelse. Vand på terræn er reduceret med 73 % sam-
menlignet med udgangspunktet og er delvist håndteret ved overﬂadeløsninger.

1
Introduction
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) aﬃrmed that global
warming is certain (IPCC, 2015).
On the 12th of March 2016 NASA released
shocking data concerning the temperatures registered for the month of February
2016: the records shows 1.35 C above the 1951-1980 global average for the month
(Masters and Henson, 2016). Moreover, a forecast released by the UK Met Oﬃce
believes 2016 is going to beat the records set in 2014 and 2015 concerning the in-
crease in average temperature compared to the period 1961-1990 (MetOﬃce, 2016).
According to Zhou et al. (2012), one of the main issues related to climate change
is the global modiﬁcation in precipitation patterns, where an increase in both an-
nual precipitation and in occurrences of extreme events in Northern Europe is
expected (DMI, ND; Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2012). Furthermore, the size of extreme
events are predicted to increase in the future. Speciﬁcally, Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2012)
reports a 30% increase in intensity for 10-year rain event and 40% increase in
intensity for 100-year rain event in 2100 in Denmark. Consequently, Refsgaard
et al. (2013) states that pluvial ﬂooding is likely to be the most important fac-
tor of climate change impacts to consider in Denmark. Land use changes such as
increased urbanization in the city with high fractions of impervious surfaces will
increase the amount of rain water-runoﬀ(Pauleit et al., 2005), where the stormwa-
ter systems are likely to undergo surcharge more frequently. Already, stormwater
management has gained increased attention in Copenhagen, since the capital has
experienced several severe rainfall events in recent years causing massive ﬂoodings
with great economical looses as a result (Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2011; Arnbjerg-Nielsen
et al., 2015). Combined, it appears an obvious necessity to upgrade and maybe
redesign urban drainage systems.
In order to make Copenhagen more resilient to climate change and cloudburst
events, The City of Copenhagen developed a Climate Change Adapatation Plan
(CCAP) in 2011, and a Cloudburst Management Plan (CMP) in 2012. The plans
aim to disconnect storm water and daily rain water from the sewer system (Copen-
hagen City, 2011a) and build preferential surface routes for discharging water to
the sea during heavy rain events (Copenhagen City, 2012b). Meanwhile focusing on
protecting the city from cloudbursts, the city aims to enhance the livability of the
city promoting green and blue infrastructure (Copenhagen City, 2011a). Low Im-
pact Development (LID) technologies appear to be a central element of the plans,
since the CMP states that when implementing adaptation measures against plu-
vial ﬂooding green solutions are preferred over grey solutions (Copenhagen City,
2012b). This approach is conﬁrmed in the more recent Climate Change Adapta-
tion And Investment Plan, where 300 planned projects aims to provide the city
with improved urban spaces besides making the city resilient to climate change
and cloudburst-proofed (Copenhagen City, 2015a).
LIDs represent green technologies within the stormwater management, aiming at re-
ducing annual runoﬀfrom paved areas and partially restoring the pre-development
hydrological conditions by modifying the water balance (Fletcher et al., 2015).
Meanwhile serving the stormwater management purpose, they can provide better
urban spaces and eventually amenities for the society, embodying a recreational
value (Lloyd et al., 2002).
1
==PAGE:24==
There is a general conﬁdence in the LIDs potential to reduce annual runoﬀfrom
developed areas, enhancing the natural water cycle and decreasing CSOs and stress
to WWTP. Even though a recently ﬁnalized master thesis project carried out by
Azad (2016) at DTU Environment investigated the performance of LIDs, in terms
of service level compliance, studies evaluating the hydraulic performances of LIDs
in order to meet the service level of a conventional combined sewer system seem to
exist to a lesser extent.
1.1
Aims and objectives
The thesis aims to investigate the hydraulic performances of LIDs in the domain
of every day rain, design rain and extreme rain. Tåsinge Plads acts as a speciﬁc
case study in order to investigate diﬀerent modelling approaches in the modelling
tool MIKE URBAN that is widely used in Denmark. The main objective is to
investigate the beneﬁts of combining LIDs and cloudburst solutions. To obtain
this, the present work will focus primarily on the following underlying objectives:
• Analyse the conﬁguration of LIDs installed in Tåsinge Plads and investigate
modelling approaches in MIKE URBAN 2016 in order to propose the best
practical approach for modelling the square.
• Utilize the proposed model in order to compare the hydraulic performances of
Tåsinge Plads before and after implementations of LIDs in terms of annual
water balance (historical rain data), compliance with service level (T=10)
and ﬂood mitigation (T=100).
• Identify and analyse the stormwater management goals of Copenhagen, specif-
ically of the Cloudburst Management Plan (CMP) for Skt. Kjelds district,
with focus on green solutions proposed.
• Investigate the possibility of implementing the Cloudburst Management Plan
of Copenhagen (extreme domain) in such a way that it serves the goals of
the Climate Change Adaptation Plan of Copenhagen (every day and design
domain), with focus on LIDs.
• Diﬀerent scenarios are put forward and the performances of the proposed
system are evaluated in terms of: annual water balance (historical rain data),
compliance with service level (T=10) and ﬂood mitigation (T=100).
• The thesis will primarily investigate hydraulics performances. However, as-
pects such as water quality and recreational or social values will be taken
into consideration for an overall evaluation.

2
Background
2.1
Wastewater Management in Denmark
Governmental and European law plays a role when the municipalities creates plans
dealing with wastewater management. The European Union has adopted the EU
Water Framework Directive (WFD) 2000/60/EF (Vandrammedirektivet) that es-
tablishes the framework to protect groundwater, streams, lakes and the ocean of
the European countries (Kallis and Butler, 2001). The EU directive is implemented
in Danish law by law of Environmental Goals (Miljømålsloven) that aims to create
joint national water planing. The environmental goals are determined by the Min-
istry of Environment in national Water Management Plans (WMP) (Vandplaner)
in order to reach the common established set of goals. The WMP imposes among
other that the improvement can be obtained by reducing the pollution loads from
Wastewater Treatment Plants (WWTP) and Combined Sewer Overﬂow (CSO).
This is addressed in Wastewater Mangement Plans (WWMP).
2.1.1
Wastewater management plans
The municipal council should according to the law of Environmental Protection
(Miljøbeskyttelseslovens) § 32 establish WWMP that aims to give a joint overview
of the existing and future management plans of wastewater (Naturstyrelsen, 2015).
The plans should moreover illustrate the impacts on the environment and ensure
the goals of the WMP is obtained together with projected economical costs for the
supply companies. The wastewater management of Copenhagen is presented in the
following.
Wastewater management plans of Copenhagen
The WWMP of Copenhagen is adopted in 2008 and operates with a plan period of
12 years. Consequently it will be valid to 2019 and then replaced by a new, revised
WWMP. The economy of the activities throughout the plan period constitutes of
2,165 million DKK by the price level of 2007 (Copenhagen City, 2008).
The overall goal is to dispose both rain- and wastewater in a way that the health
status of the population is maintained, that the environment is not exposed to
damaging aﬀects and that the fundamental view on ecology and recycling are met.
Speciﬁcally, it is stated that the sewage system shall be operated so that the wa-
ter cycle of Copenhagen is maintained (Copenhagen City, 2008). Furthermore, it
is stated that where it is technical, economical and environmentally beneﬁcial for
the City of Copenhagen new public wastewater installations should be established
based on LID principle. The established goals should apply under climate change,
coordination with other planing should be obtained, speciﬁcally with the EU WFD,
rat control should limit the spread of rats and focus is put on work environment
for the employees in the sewer supply company (Copenhagen City, 2008).
According the WWMP of Copenhagen, the frequency in which wastewater is al-
lowed surcharged from the sewer on the terrain depends on the pollution level of
the wastewater, i.e. rainwater runoﬀwith or without diluted sewage water. Pipes
transporting rainwater runoﬀin a separate system is not allowed to surcharge more
frequent than once every 5 year, whereas pipes in a combined sewage system is not
allowed to surcharge more frequent than once every 10 year (Copenhagen City,
3
==PAGE:26==
2008).
Combined and separate sewerage systems are widely used in Copenhagen, where
the fractions of combined and separate systems constitutes of 90 % and 10 %, re-
spectively according to Sørensen et al. (2006). The reason is that old systems tends
to apply the combined system, whereas newer installed systems in cities tend to
apply the separate system. According to Sørensen et al. (2006), up until 2006 the
majority of the sewer network of Copenhagen was at least 100 years old. With this
data in mind, the dimension criteria of ﬂooding no more than once every 10 years
apply in most cases in Copenhagen.
When pipes and basins are dimensioned safety factors (sf) are often used to account
for e.g. uncertainties and climate change. In the WWMP 2008 of Copenhagen,
statistical uncertainties together with the projected increase of rain intensities due
to climate change are assigned a value of 1.2 and 1.3, respectively, leading to a
total safety factor of 1.56. Exactly how to deal with climate change adaptation
is formalized in the municipality established Climate Change Adaptation Plans
(CCAP).
2.1.2
Climate adaptation plans
CCAP are established by the municipalities according to the agreement between
the Government and Local Government Denmark (KL) in 2012 (Klimatilpasning,
2015). The plans are established in order to adapt to the projected changes in
climate. The eﬀort of climate adaptation varies between municipalities also when
the above mentioned governmental established WMP is taken into account. Ac-
cording to Hellesen et al. (2011) factors such as distance to the ocean, land use,
infrastructure, living patterns, economy and politicians plays an important role,
when the level of climate change adaptation are set in the municipalities. In the
following, the CCAP of Copenhagen is presented.
Climate adaptation plans in Copenhagen
The city of Copenhagen compiled a CCAP in 2011 that outline the challenges the
city faces both in short and long term. Moreover it points out solutions that from
current knowledge seems most appropriate (Copenhagen City, 2011c). The aim of
the plan is ﬁrst and foremost to act due diligence and make sure to avoid mistaken
investments, which is obtained through a high professional quality by including
model simulations.
One result shows that adaptation by increasing sewerage system in order to make
space to the extra rainwater will contribute to negative socio-economic gain com-
pared to the base scenario (no adaptation). However, a scenario in which 30% of
the sewerage system is disconnected by the means of a combination of separating
and LID solutions shows that the level of ﬂood damage is heavily reduced, while
costs are kept low and consequently creates a great socio-economic gain (Copen-
hagen City, 2011c).
The CCAP recommend this solution proposal in order to
reduce the extent of ﬂood damage. These elements can contribute to a greener city
by soakaways, swales, lakes and green channels.
The CCAP also deals with a "Plan B", i.e. minimizing damage costs when ﬂooding
occurs. This is done by terrain based solutions that alter the ﬂow of the water on
4
==PAGE:27==
the surface to locations where it does no or only little damage. Designation of
areas is determined while taking the many stakeholders into account. This scoping
is formalized in the Cloudburst Management Plan (CMP).
2.1.3
Cloudburst management plans
In order to cope with the increasing intensities of heavy rain events, i.e. cloud-
burst, today and in the future climate, municipalities are able to create CMP. The
CMP itself is not legally binding for homeowners, the municipality or the supply
company but should be incorporated in other plans, such as the municipal plan,
local plans, WWMP and other sector plans (Copenhagen City, 2013). Thus, only
one municipality in Denmark has made a CMP, Copenhagen. Ayfer Baykal, former
Technical and Environmental Mayor of Copenhagen, stated in 2012 that the plan
is globally one of the most ambitious in respect of cloudburst management ever
made (Klimatilpasning, 2012). The plan is presented in the following.
Cloudburst management plans in Copenhagen
The CMP of Copenhagen was established in 2012 and divides the city into 7 wa-
tersheds (Copenhagen City, 2012c).
In total, the plan consists of 300 projects
that combines surface solutions where possible and elsewhere underground pipe
solutions. The major cloudburst tunnels forms the backbone of the plan and are
constructed within the next 7-9 years, whereas the entire plan is planned to be
conducted within the next 20 years (Copenhagen City, 2015a). In total, the CMP
will have a total cost of 11 billion DKK, that is less compared to the cost of "no
action"-scenario and "expansion of sewer network"-scenario, which have projected
cost of 16 and 20 billion DKK, respectively.
The 300 projects are according to Copenhagen City (2015a) prioritized in the fol-
lowing order
• Cloudburst tunnels that will function from day one
• High risk areas
• Areas where solutions are simple to implement
• Areas where other construction activities are in progress
• Areas with synergy eﬀects
The ambition level of the plan is that a maximum water depth of 10 cm is allowed
on public roads when a 100-year rainfall event occurs. Speciﬁcally appointed cloud-
burst roads are however excepted in this requirement (Copenhagen City, 2015a).
2.2
Three Point Approach
The Three Point Approach (3PA) was according to Fratini et al. (2012) ﬁrst pre-
sented in the 11th International Conference of Urban Drainage in 2008. The aim of
3PA is to provide managers and decision makers an intuitive tool to communicate,
discuss and reﬂect about possible future scenarios, when dealing with solutions
within wastewater management. Hence the name, three domains are presented,
where climate adaptation can take place, domain one, two and three that corre-
sponds to domain of technical optimization of water system, urban resilience and
5

spatial planning and day-to-day values, respectively. Figure 1 presents the three
domains, where ﬂood return period constitutes the x-axis and costs constitutes the
y-axis.
Figure 1: The 3PA scheme. Note that both axes are on a logarithmic scale. The x-axis represents the
return period of ﬂood and the y-axis represents the size of the rain in terms of costs of maintenance and
damaging urban infrastructure (Fratini et al., 2012).
Point one represents the design domain for sewers and other drainage infrastruc-
ture. The systems should be able to prevent damage for such deﬁned design storms
and obey the political established service level. The CCAP of Copenhagen is cov-
ered by this domain.
Point two represents the extreme domain, where extreme events such as cloud-
bursts storms occur. The aim in this domain is to mitigate the eﬀect of possible
future events together with adaptation to future scenarios. The CMP of Copen-
hagen is covered by this domain.
Point three represents the daily situation and includes dry periods where speciﬁc
attention is put on the way the urban space is used on a daily basis. This domain
suggest that implementation of projects should be able to improve the recreational
values in the area in order to meet the needs for the stakeholders. Fratini et al.
(2012) reports that social participation and engagement by locals is considered
important in order to create acceptance of new urban infrastructure and ensuring
solutions are preserved and maintained.
2.3
Low Impact Development
Low Impact Development (LID) represent an emerging approach to storm water
management that aims to minimize the runoﬀfrom urbanized impervious surfaces
through management of stormwater as close as possible to its source. It is done
by aﬀecting the water balance, enhancing detention, inﬁltration and evapotraspi-
ration.
While serving the purpose of climate adaptation and helping the goals
set for cloudburst adaptation, LIDs oﬀer great possibilities for improving urban
spaces and their liveability. Also, LIDs have shown promising results in order to
retain pollution loads generated from runoﬀ. Examples of LIDs structure are soak-
6
==PAGE:29==
aways, inﬁltration trenches, rain barrels, wet or dry detention basins, green roofs
and more. Terminology varies globally when dealing with this new approach and
Fletcher et al. (2015) collect and analyze the origin and the development of these
terms. Some examples are Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD), Sustainable
Urban Drainage System (SUDS) and Low Impact Development (LID).
According to Fletcher et al. (2015) the term WSUD appeared for the ﬁrst time
in the 1990s, in Australia.
Lloyd et al. (2002) deﬁnes WSUD as philosophical
approach to urban planning and design that aims to minimize the hydrological im-
pacts of urban development on the surrounding environment, where stormwater
management is seen more as a branch of WSUD, focused on preventing ﬂooding
and improving water quality. The term WSUD is currently spreading outside Aus-
tralia, especially in New Zealand and UK (Ashley et al., 2013). The term SUDS
appeared for the ﬁrst time in UK in 1997 (Fletcher et al., 2015). SUDS refer to a
group of technologies meant to deal and manage stormwater in a way that is be-
lieved more sustainable compared to the traditional end-of-pipe approach (Fletcher
et al., 2015). The leading concept is to replicate the hydrological water balance
before intense urbanization. LID is the most used term in North America and
New Zealand and it appeared for the ﬁrst time in 1977 (Fletcher et al., 2015).
LID has been used to deﬁne a small scale and local stormwater treatment system
characterized by technologies located close to the runoﬀsource, in contrast with
the traditional wide catchment scale drainage system (Fletcher et al., 2015). In
Denmark, the term LAR (Lokal Aﬂedning af Regnvand) is widely used in order to
characterize elements that manage rainwater runoﬀclose to the source (Teknolo-
gisk Institut, ND). This study use the term LID, since it is the most used term in
literature within this topic, as shown in Figure 2. Furthermore, it is used in the
applied modelling tool, MIKE URBAN, which moreover constitute the reasoning
behind this choice of terminology.
Figure 2: Diﬀusion of urban drainage terminology (Fletcher et al., 2015).
In many studies, LIDs appear to be promising regarding runoﬀreduction and cli-
mate change adaptation measures. Holman-Dodds et al. (2003) showed that mod-
ifying urbanized landscapes, rerouting runoﬀfrom impervious area to adjacent
7
==PAGE:30==
pervious surfaces, decreases the hydrological impact of a catchment in compari-
son with the traditional fully connected drainage systems. Many studies already
pointed out the capability for LIDs to mitigate CSOs from existing systems (e.g.
(Villarreal et al., 2004; Semadeni-Davies et al., 2008)). From an extensive liter-
ature review carried out by Ahiablame et al. (2012) emerged great potential for
mitigating the eﬀects of urbanization and land development in terms of both hy-
draulics and water quality when implementing LID elements. Locatelli et al. (2014)
estimates the annual runoﬀvolumes from green roofs to be 43-68% of the total pre-
cipitation. Particularly, the runoﬀintensity was reduced by 40-78% for 0.1-1 year
return period. A case study of a small catchment in Copenhagen, shows the imple-
mentation of bioretention units could ﬁlter and attenuate 75% of the annual rain
(Lerer et al., 2015). Soakaways dimensioned for 0.1 year return period (every day
domain) were seen to inﬁltrate more than 68% of the annual stormwater runoﬀ.
However, if combined with detention storage it was shown to avoid sewer surcharge
for a rain event with a return period of 10 years (design domain), being able to
reduce annual runoﬀby 68-87% (Locatelli et al., 2015a).

3.1
Rainfall-Runoﬀ
The Rainfall-runoﬀmodel is of fundamental importance in hydraulic modelling,
since it computes the rainwater runoﬀgenerated by a catchment. The fraction of
rainwater converted into runoﬀis highly depending on the hydrological processes.
The crucial hydrological sub-processes are identiﬁed by Linde et al. (2002) to be
wetting loss, surface storage, interception by vegetative surface cover, inﬁltration
and evapotranspiration. Figure 9 shows a schematic overview of the hydrological
processes.

and interception process some times are combined denoted evapotranspiration.
The inﬁltration and evapotranspiration terms become of great importance when
focusing on LID solutions, since one of the driving concepts of LID is the possibility
to reduce the runoﬀfrom impervious areas enhancing inﬁltration and evapotranspi-
ration locally. It appears relevant to focus on how these processes are described and
can be modelled. In the following, the inﬁltration and evapotranspiration processes
are explained and the governing equation describing them presented.

3.1.1
Horton Inﬁltration
The inﬁltration term describes the fraction of precipitation that enters the soil. One
of the most used theories regarding the inﬁltration process was presented by Robert
E. Horton. According to Horton, who studied the inﬁltration thoroughly around
the 1930s, not all precipitation intensities can inﬁltrate into the ground (Horton,
1941).
The maximum inﬁltration capacity expresses the maximum intensity of
precipitation the soil can inﬁltrate. Moreover, Horton asserted that this property
decreases with time, since it depends on the amount of water present in the soil,
i.e. soil saturation. Equation 1 present the mathematical expression.

3.1.2
Evapotranspiration
Evapotranspiration is the form of evaporation due to intercepted precipitation and
transpired water from plant surfaces. Out of the several phases in the hydrologi-
cal cycle, evapotranspiration is one of the most diﬃcult to quantify (Shaw et al.,
2010). It is due to the fact that several factors including type of vegetation, tem-
perature, vapour capacity in the air, saturation deﬁcit and wind speed aﬀect the
physical process, where water convert from liquid into vapour state. This entails
that evapotranspiration is highly depending on climate and might vary seasonally.
3.2
Rainfall runoﬀmodels
In order to describe the phenomena rainfall runoﬀin hydraulic modelling, speciﬁc
models are established. The type of model chosen might diﬀer simulated runoﬀsig-
niﬁcantly due to computation concept as well as diﬀerences in the amount and com-
plexity of input data and model parameters (DHI, 2016e). Four diﬀerent rainfall
runoﬀmodels exists in MIKE URBAN: Time/Area method (model A), Kinematic
Wave (Model B), Linear reservoir model (model C) and Unit hydrograph model
14
==PAGE:37==
(UHM). In the following, model A and B are presented, since they are widely
applied rainfall runoﬀmodels in urban drainage context in Denmark.
3.2.1
Model A
Model A (Time/Area method) is widely used in the modelling practice in Den-
mark. Thus a short overview of the concept is presented.
The amount of runoﬀis controlled by the rain event together with the contributing
area (Ared). The contributing area is calculated by multiplying the total catchment
area (A) with the runoﬀcoeﬃcient (φ). φ varies between 0-1 and depends of on
land use. When φ approaches 1, the fraction of impermeable surfaces is high, which
will lead to increased runoﬀcompared to area where φ is low (Linde et al., 2002).
The shape of the resulting runoﬀhydrograph is determined based on the time-
area curve, which expresses fractions of the catchment contributing to runoﬀat
a given time, t. The shape of the time-area curve depends on the shape of the
catchment and the distribution of permeable/impermeable surfaces. It calculates
the fraction of the calculated runoﬀthat is discharged at time t, based on the time
of concentration (tc).
In model A, the inﬁltration process is approximated to be proportional to the
rainfall intensity, i.e. the inﬁltration capacity is assumed constant over time. How-
ever, Horton proved in Horton (1941) that inﬁltration capacity indeed changes over
time. The eﬀect of describing the runoﬀfrom green areas with a higher accuracy
is currently carried out by Envidan A/S with support from The Foundation for
Development of Technology in the Danish Water Sector (VTU) aiming to monitor
the surface runoﬀfrom green areas (EnviDan, ND). However, since the present
paper focuses on the hydraulic performances of LID technologies and the majority
of LID solutions are enhancing inﬁltration and retention, detailed description of
the hydrological processes seems crucial. Rainfall-runoﬀmodel B enables the use
of the Horton inﬁltration equation, which is thus used in the present paper as the
rainfall-runoﬀmodel and explained thoroughly in the following section.
3.2.2

amount of runoﬀis determined by the rainfall event, size of the contributing area

tion determines the shape of the hydrograph (DHI, 2016e). A schematic overview

3.3
Network Model
Once runoﬀis computed from the catchments, it is assigned to a manhole in the
network system that connects the runoﬀcontribution to the network. The theory
and computation scheme of the network model is beyond the scope of this study,
however, the following will present main concepts of ﬂow in links in MIKE URBAN.
MIKE URBAN 2016 allows to describe the ﬂow in pipes by the kinematic wave ap-
proximation, diﬀusive wave or dynamic wave equation. The kinematic and diﬀusive
wave are simpliﬁcations of the full dynamic wave equation. The reason to chose the
simpliﬁed ﬂow description is to reduce computation time. In general, the dynamic
wave computation is recommended to be used by DHI (2016d), which thus is the
chosen ﬂow description of this study. Moreover, the dynamic wave computation
describes backwater eﬀects, which is not always accounted for in both diﬀusive and
kinematic wave computation (DHI, 2016d).
In order to describe the ﬂow, the full Saint Venant equations are applied.
It
computes an unsteady 1D ﬂow by vertically integrating the continuity and mo-
mentum equations. The derivation of these equations are not presented here but
is shown in various textbooks and scientiﬁc papers according to DHI (2016d). All
forces aﬀecting the ﬂow conditions are taken into account and water level in nodes
and discharge in links are enabled to be computed.
18
==PAGE:41==
Moreover, MIKE URBAN is able to model both pressurised ﬂow and free sur-
face ﬂow in order to cope with situations where the pipe is full and partially full,
respectively. MIKE URBAN applies pressurised ﬂow conditions if the hydraulic
gradeline is above the pipe in the longitudinal direction by adding a ﬁctive slot on
top of the pipe.
DHI (2016d) and DHI (2016c) present further information on network computation.
3.4
Flood Computation
MIKE URBAN is able to compute water ﬂow on the surface, once subsurface pipes
surcharge. The ﬂow on the surface is computed based on a 2D overland ﬂow model
using MIKE 21 (DHI, 2016c). The 1D pipeﬂow model, a Digital Terrain Model
(DTM) and coupling points between the two models (typically manholes) forms the
backbone when combining the 1D and 2D modelling schemes. This combination is
also known as MIKE FLOOD.
MIKE FLOOD is used to simulate the interaction between the sewer system and
the surface (DHI, 2016b).
In the present project, the urban link (coupling) is
designed to describe the exchange of water between network and surface when a
manhole surcharges or when overland ﬂow enters the network. The linkage can also
be used for other purposes, e.g. in order to describe interaction between a sewer
system and a collection basin described using the topography (DHI, 2016b), or to
link features of the network system discharging into the surrounding area through
a pump or over a weir. The coupling of the two models is carried out by linking one
or more cells of MIKE 21 with 1 feature in MIKE URBAN, e.g. manhole, pump,
basin etc. (DHI, 2016b)
In order to compute the water ﬂow and depth on the surface, a 2D model solver
is used, either the Single Grid (rectangular or rectangular multi-cell solver) or the
MIKE 21 Flood Screening Tool (FST) is chosen. The current study applies Single
Grid.
3.5
Precipitation
Precipitation plays an important role in hydraulic modelling, since it acts as the
input to the models. The Water Pollution Committee of The Society of Danish
Engineers (SVK) has for more than 60 years developed methods and recommenda-
tions regarding precipitation for dimensioning of sewage systems (Gregersen et al.,
2014). In their latest release, publication 30, an updated regional model is presented
where annual mean precipitation and mean values of extreme daily precipitation
constitute the two interpretative variables. Since both variables are deﬁned based
on spatial location, a set of coordinates are required as input to the model, in order
to generate a synthetic rainfall event.
3.5.1
CDS rain
In Denmark, Chicago Design Storms (CDS) are widely used as rain input when
dimensioning sewer systems. An example of the shape of the artiﬁcial generated
rain series is presented in Figure 11. The duration of the CDS is chosen based on

3.5.2
Historical rain
Historical rain series are often used as input to hydraulic models, when annual
water balances, CSOs and surcharges are investigated. In order not to simulate
an entire historical rain series with a ﬁxed time step, Long Term Statistics (LTS)
can be used. When dealing with CSOs and surcharges, LTS is powerful since it
enables to eliminate irrelevant periods of simulation (i.e., dry periods) and thus turn
the continuous historical period into a discontinuous series of relevant rain events
DHI (2016c). This reduces simulation time without losing signiﬁcant information.
However, when establishing water balances smaller events and dynamics in dry
periods, i.e. inﬁltration and evapotranspiration, becomes important, which makes
LTS less applicable.
3.6
Modelling LIDs in MIKE URBAN
Until the end of 2015 no speciﬁc tool was available in MIKE URBAN to model LID
solutions. In order to investigate the eﬀects of LID technologies in a catchment
diﬀerent workarounds were needed, where e.g. the pervious-impervious ratio of
green roads could be modiﬁed. When studying the eﬀect of implementing green
roofs and water tanks for rain harvesting, parameters representing initial losses were
often modiﬁed. Another possibility to represent LIDs structure was to implement
a normal basin, where a time series for losses could be applied in order simulate
inﬁltration. However, with the release of January 2016, MIKE URBAN delivers
new features supporting two new approaches for modelling LID structures:
• LID screening level – catchment based approach
• Soakaway node level - network based approach
The two approaches are fundamentally diﬀerent. The catchment-based approach
is carried out within the runoﬀcomputation, whereas the network based approach
20
==PAGE:43==
is carried out within the network simulation. Note that the LID Screening tool is
strictly applicable in runoﬀmodel B.
3.6.1
LID screening level
The catchment-based methods are based on the LIDs feature available in SWMM,
where the LIDs controls were introduced in 2009 according to a research published
by US EPA (DHI, 2016c). The LID screening level is intended to be used as a
support tool to dimension the magnitude of inﬁltration and rain harvest necessary
within a catchment to reach a predetermined goal (DHI, 2016c). LIDs are able
to be implemented in catchments with the desired design and properties given by
the user (LID control). Currently, 7 diﬀerent LID structures are able to be im-
plemented, i.e. Bio-retention cells, Inﬁltration trenches, Porous pavements, Rain
barrels, Vegetative swales, Rain gardens and Green roofs (DHI, 2016c). Depending
on the type of LID chosen, diﬀerent layers are modelled in the LID structure: sur-
face, soil and storage. Depending on the complexity of the chosen LID layout many
parameters are required, mainly describing the properties of the diﬀerent layers.
Furthermore, a drain system can be applied to the storage connecting the LID to
the network.
When a LID structure is deployed in a catchment (LID deployment) it is pos-
sible to deﬁne the number of units, the dimensions of the LID (area and width)
and collecting area, which is the area of the catchment that will be drained by the
LID. The possibility to deﬁne a speciﬁc collecting area allows directing to the LID
only a fraction of the runoﬀgenerated by a catchment. If the collecting area is set
equal to the extent of the catchment, all the runoﬀgenerated by the catchment is
directed to the LID. However, when dealing with catchment not 100% impervious,
the maximum collecting area that can be selected for the LID is the total impervi-
ous area of the catchment.
The runoﬀgenerated by the selected catchment is directed to the surface layer
of the designed LID, which also represents the area of the LID. Here, the hydro-
logical processes describing the fate of the water through the diﬀerent layers is
described using an uniﬁed approach of compartments. If the capacity of a LID
structure is exceeded, the surface runoﬀfrom the LID is directed to the connected
manhole of the sewer system. The diﬀerent mathematical formulas applied in the
tool are not described in the present project.
When the runoﬀsimulation is computed, MIKE URBAN returns a summary ﬁle
where the accumulated volume for diﬀerent hydrological processes are presented,
both for the catchment and LIDs deployed. Table 1 shows the processes displayed
in the summary ﬁle for both the catchment and the LIDs.

All catchments were assigned a length of 100 meters and a slope of 10 %. Ideally,
length of polygons should have been measured and slopes diﬀerentiated between
e.g. roofs and roads, since these parameters are used in the kinematic wave, as
seen in Equation 11. However, the length of a polygon depends on the shape and
inclination towards a manhole. The slope of polygons vary greatly depending on
land use, where roofs are typical steep and roads and green areas ﬂat. In both
cases, a manual procedure would have been necessary. A manual procedure was
in the present paper not an option due to time constraint with the numbers of
polygons in mind (3,326). An overestimation of length and slope will lead to a
lower and higher peak of Q(t), respectively as seen from Equation 11. A thorough
investigation of results when diﬀering lengths and slopes for catchments was car-
ried out and details are available in Appendix Q. In conclusion, results presented
in Section 5.4 showed that the diﬀerences in discharge and water level at diﬀerent
points in the catchment are very similar, meaning that the converted model B is
similar to the somewhat calibrated model A. Thus the presented assumptions are
considered reasonable.
Comparison of model A and B
The translation from model A to B posed some challenges, due to the radical dif-
ference between the two runoﬀcomputation schemes. In model A green areas are
given an impervious fraction of 0, thus no runoﬀis generated. On the other hand,
model B computes runoﬀfrom green areas, as explained in Section 3.2.2. When the
soil of a green area reaches saturation, it can generate runoﬀ. Green areas are then
expected to have a little response for low intensity rain, whereas they are expected
to contribute to the discharge when high intensity rain events occur. Model A and
B were compared in order to understand diﬀerences and the implication of using
model B in the present study. Model A, provided by HOFOR, is believed to be
calibrated for everyday rain events. For rain events with return period between
0.1 and 1 years model B is expected to perform similarly to model A, whereas
for event with higher return period (e.g. T=10) model B is expected to generate
higher runoﬀvolumes than model A.

not to experience bottle necks. According to Edmonton City (ND) the minimum
slope required for separate system is 0.10 %, when the diameter is at least 525mm
and thus respected for all scenarios.

Scenario 2
In scenario 2, runoﬀfrom roofs facing inner yards and the inner yards located close
to the CP were disconnected. The generated runoﬀfrom the inner roofs is led
through an LID placed in the inner yard, more speciﬁcally a bio-retention cell with

connected to the CP.
The LID Screening tool was applied as modelling approach. Given the discussion
of the two methods available, that is provided in Section 3.6, and the knowledge
from the case study of Tåsinge Plads, the LID screening tool was believed to be the
best method in this planning phase of the project. Once the desired layout of bio
retention was created, many units could be deployed in the area with a reasonable
time eﬀort. In this way it was possible to preliminary evaluate the potential and
the impact of installing the planned LIDs in the area. For this project, it would
not have been reasonable to utilize the soakaway node method, since each LID
deployed should include construction detail such as bottom level, ground level and
invert level together with pipe connections.
The disconnected roof catchments, together with the inneryards were directed to
the same manhole in the CP. The initial plan was to design bio-retention cells with
a drain applied in the bottom layer. However, it was found that the drain was
not implemented correctly in MIKE URBAN. Due to this ﬁnding, no drain was
applied to any bio-retention cell. This entails a longer emptying time, only depend-
ing on rate of inﬁltration and evapotranspiration. Inﬁltration through the bottom
was applied in the bio retention cell. However, inﬁltration is only possible where
the groundwater table is suﬃciently deep. Locatelli et al. (2015b) found that the
performances of inﬁltration trenches are aﬀected by shallow groundwater tables.
SEPA (1999) reports that groundwater tables should be greater than 1.8 m when
inﬁltration is applied in a bio-retention cell. Areas where inﬁltration was assumed
allowed in Skt. Kjelds district is presented in Appendix I.
Currently, KK requires cleaning of roof water runoﬀfrom roofs, gutters, dormers
or ﬂashings that contains Zink (Zn), Lead (Pb) or Cubber (Cu) (Copenhagen City,
2016). The same report suggest LIDs with e.g. engineered soil as treatment mea-
sure. In fact, an experiment was carried out by Teknologisk Institut (2015b), where
synthetic road water runoﬀwith relatively high concentrations of the worrisome
heavy metals (i.e. Zn, Pb, Cd, Ni and Cu) was led through engineered soil in a
swale. The conclusion was that the substances in the outlet were reduced by 84-100
%. Thus bio-retention cells were in this project also applied as treatment measure.

period of T=0.5. This entails according to Spildevandskomiteen (2015) the surface
area of the LIDs to be approximately 3 % of the connected roof area. Appendix

5.2
Tåsinge Plads
5.2.1
Bio-retention system
The selection of the model to be implemented when representing the bio retention
system in Tåsinge Plads was carried out based on the expected behaviors of the
system in reality and a qualitative analysis of the results obtain by the 4 models
proposed.
5.2.1.1
Expected behaviours of the system in reality
Water balance
In terms of water balance the system is expected not to have great eﬀect. In fact,
no inﬁltration occurs and very little evapotranspiration is expected due to the short
time the water spends in the bio-retention cells before being returned to the sewer
network.
Service Level
The system is expected to reduce the peak of the runoﬀfrom the roads for rain
event with T<10. In fact, the water is collected, stored and slowly returned to the
sewer system. The system has been dimensioned for a return period higher than
10 years (Copenhagen City, 2014). For this reason, no overﬂow is expected with
a CDS T=10. When the precipitation is too intense to be accepted by the soil,
the overﬂow structures on top of the soil layer let the water bypassing the soil and
45
==PAGE:68==
reach directly the storage. In this way, no overﬂow is expected until the maximum
storage capacity is reached.
Pollution
It is proven by many studies that engineered soil has great removal potential for pol-
lutants such as heavy metals (Ahiablame et al., 2012; Teknologisk Institut, 2015b).
However, since road water runoﬀcannot be inﬁltrated due to potential presence of
salt, a large fraction of the water treated by the engineered soil layer in the bio-
retention system is send to the sewage system. The beneﬁts of pollutant removal
in the soil layer is thus lost.

5.3
Status model: From MOUSE to MIKE 1D
When the engine was switched from MOUSE to MIKE 1D, MIKE URBAN initially
returned errors. The cause is imprecision in the network structure. MIKE 1D is
more rigorous than MOUSE, returning errors that are not detected by MOUSE.
Two example of errors are boundary conditions applied where not possible and cross
sections (CRS curves) built without respecting the criteria required by MIKE 1D.
The MOUSE engine will accept these data errors and display warnings, whereas
the MIKE 1D engine would compute errors. The solution to the problem was to ﬁx
the errors manually. This operation was indeed very time demanding since MIKE
1D returns errors in a sequential way. The errors are not shown all at once, and
new errors are returned once ﬁxes are carried out.
Eventually, all error messages were solved and the model run successfully with
MIKE 1D when a CDS rain T=10 with a 4 hour duration was applied. However,
the model showed high instability. A very little time step was necessary in order
to run the network simulation successfully, i.e. 5 seconds. This poses a huge limi-
tation for the usability of the model when it comes to long term period simulation
with historical rain data. In fact, such a small time step requires an extraordi-
nary high computational time and an enormous memory capacity when running
network simulation. It was not possible to simulate a network computation for a
period longer than 1 month for the entire status model. In conclusion, the status
model could be used for simulating CDS rain event (12 hours duration) but not for
the purpose of simulating historical rain series, meaning boundaries conditions for
the area of Skt. Kjelds could only be created for CDS simulations.

Discussion
6.1
Tåsinge Plads
6.1.1
Modelling of LIDs
The diﬀerent modeling approaches were critically analyzed and compared with fo-
cus on the expected behavior in reality. The model that best served the purpose of
reproducing the expected behavior of LIDs in Tåsinge Plads was chosen. However,
the lack of measured data of the water ﬂow in the pipe network around Tåsinge
Plads did not make it possible to calibrate and validate the proposed model for the
LIDs installation in the square.
A sensitivity analysis carried out in a MsC project at DTU by Schiolborg and
Lindbladh (2016) showed that the most inﬂuential parameters are the area ratio
between the LID and the catchment, and the hydraulic conductivity to the sur-
rounding soil. The conductivity of the native soil was also found to be the most
sensitive parameter by Chui et al. (2016). For the bio-retention model proposed
in the present project, the uncertainties related to the area ratio are only due to
the area of the catchment. In fact, the area of the LID was precisely implemented
in the model based on technical drawing (Copenhagen City, 2014), whereas it is
not possible to deﬁne the area of the road that is actually drained to the LID with
absolute conﬁdence, which can vary over time. For the basin system, more uncer-
tainties are related to the total area of the basin system, due to the complexity of
the system, which provides a combination of open basins installed in series with
diﬀerent shapes. Also the width varies within the same basin. However, as well as
the bio-retention system, the area of the basin system was modelled based on data
from technical drawings (Copenhagen City, 2014). The conductivity of the soil is
believed to be a very uncertain parameter, since it can vary within few meters due
to the inﬂuence of many factors. In the present project, the hydraulic conductiv-
ity of the native soil was chosen based on hydraulic conductivity tests carried by
Teknologisk Institut (2015a) in an area of Copenhagen with moraine clay as native
soil type.
Bio-retention system
The simpliﬁcation of model 1 into model 5 showed very positive results, with almost
no diﬀerences between the two models. The bio-retention system in model 5, when
modelling with MIKE URBAN, was seen to be able to cope with the runoﬀgener-
ated by a CDS rain with T=100, which initially might look questionable since LIDs
are usually dimensioned for low return periods, i.e. 0.2 - 1 years. A comparison
was carried out with SVK LAR spreadsheet (Spildevandskomiteen, 2015), in order
to understand if the obtained results were reasonable. It was found that according
to SVK LAR spreadsheet, given the dimensions of the bio-retention system, the
system should not overﬂow for a T=100. This ﬁnding is in accordance with the
projections returned by the model proposed in this project. Another key factor of
the high performances of the systems was found to be the drain capacity of 5.2 L/s
applied.
The results indicate that the bio-retention system installed in Tåsinge Plads has
a capacity much higher than the common standard for LIDs installations. LIDs
are usually dimensioned for low return period, as suggested by the Danish design
71
==PAGE:94==
guidelines for rainwater harvesting systems according to Mikkelsen et al. (1999)
presented in Lerer and Sørup (2016). No service level is deﬁned for LIDs, but it
was shown that they retain 75% of the accumulated annual runoﬀwhen dimen-
sioned for T=0.2, and up to 95% when designed for a return period of 5 years
(Lerer and Sørup, 2016)
This very high capacity does not entail that the system is wrongly dimensioned,
but rather that it is not exploited as much as it could. The space occupied by LIDs
in Tåsinge Plads does not reduce accessibility and usability of the square, on the
contrary it enhances it. However, under a cost-beneﬁt point of view, it does not
seem eﬃcient to build such a huge LID system which would reach full capacity only
once in approximately 100 years. The investments in space and money could be
optimized by increasing the amount of surfaces connected to the system. A larger
collecting area entails the system will fail more often than it does at the present
conﬁguration, but at the same time the system would be able to collect and delay
a larger fraction of the runoﬀfrom the surrounding area. The driving aim should
be lowering the return period for overﬂows down to 5 years, which according to
Copenhagen City (2008) is the service level for separate system. Currently the col-
lecting area of the bio-retention system in Tåsinge Plads is 2,100 m2. If overﬂow
from the bio-retention system is accepted with a frequency of once every 5 years,
it was estimated that an area of 7,000 m2 could be drained by the bio-retention
system.
According to Copenhagen City (2012b), Tåsingegade will be transformed in a cloud-
burst road, in order to transport water from Tåsinge Plads down to the CP branch
located Østerbrogade. Due to this planned surface solution, it might be considered
to reduce the return period for overﬂow from the LIDs even below 5 years. In fact,
the water overﬂowing would reach the street and be transported downstream by
the cloudburst road. This option would allow connecting even more area to the
existing LIDs, enhancing the annual water balance, being more runoﬀcaptured by
the LIDs. However, a terrain analysis showed that it seemed reasonable to increase
the collecting area of the bio-retention units to approximately 4,000 m2, which
corresponds to a 90% increase compared to the current situation (i.e. 2,100 m2).
This value is far from 7,000 m2 that is the size of the collecting area that would
make the bio-retention system overﬂow once every 5 year.
Basin system
The choice of merging the capacity given by the basins in series into one single
structure returned positive results, making it possible to simplify the model. As
with the bio-retention system, the basin system also appeared to cope with a CDS
rain T=100 years. SVK LAR spreadsheet was again used in order to understand
if the simulated results were reasonable. With the given dimensions of the basin
system, it appeared reasonable for the system to handle a rain event with return
period of 100 years. The same considerations made for the bio-retention system
apply to the basin system: the collecting area could be increased in order to better
exploit the capacity of the basin system. Speciﬁcally, according to SVK LAR, the
collecting impervious area of the basin could be increased up to approximately 8000
m2, if a failure is accepted with a return period of 5 years.
Diﬀerent from the bio-retention system, the aim of increasing the collecting area
72
==PAGE:95==
of the basin system seemed more feasible. As explained, only the roofs facing the
square are drained to the basins. By changing the gutter system of the buildings
surrounding the square, it might be possible to collect more runoﬀfrom a wider
area of roofs. It was estimated that if for every roof already connected to the basin,
the part facing the inner yard was also connected to the LID, the collecting area of
the basin system would rise to 2,800 m2. Moreover, by slightly expanding the area
of the roof connected to the basin system for all the buildings already connected,
the impervious collecting area for the basins system would increase up to 6,000 m2,
closer to the estimated 8,000 m2 necessary to observed overﬂow with a frequency
of once every 5 years.
LID modelling tool
Even though LID screening tool is believed to best represent the hydrological pro-
cesses through the diﬀerent layers of LIDs, for this speciﬁc case study, the soakway
node tool was identiﬁed as the best tool for reproducing the LIDs installed in
Tåsinge Plads. With regards to the bio-retention system, the choice was driven
by the necessity to represent the overﬂow structure that lets the water bypass the
soil layer if the rain intensity is too high. This feature is not possible to be imple-
mented with the LID screening tool. Regarding the basin system, the complexity
of the combination of the basins in series was seen to be more reproducible with the
soakway node. Furthermore, in this speciﬁc case study, details of the installations
were available for both LID systems, e.g. bottom, invert and ground level, drain
pipe diameter etc., allowing to model them in detail within the network. By doing
this the interaction with the network was well represented. In a planning phase,
when the aim is to evaluate the possible impact of LIDs implementation in an area
and construction details are not available yet, the LID screening tool is considered
to be the best choice. This aspect is discussed in more detail in the next section
regarding Skt Kjelds.
In terms of evaporation, the soakaway shows some limitations.
However, the
modelling approach used in the present project did account for evaporation. The
soakaway nodes representing the LIDs corresponded to underground point with
deﬁned geometry properties. The evaporation process was then accounted for in
the rainfall-runoﬀcomputation, using model B. In fact, the surface of the LIDs
where evaporation takes place was represented when the land use of the square was
modiﬁed, according to the new conﬁguration of Tåsinge Plads.
6.1.2
Hydrological impacts
The evaluation of the eﬀects on the water balance given by the LIDs changed rad-
ically depending on the reference chosen. When considering only the catchments
undergoing modiﬁcations within Tåsinge Plads, tangible improvements on the wa-
ter balance were seen, whereas when considering the impact of the current Tåsinge
Plads on the larger hydraulic catchment very little change was observed. In both
cases, the biggest achievement was given by the hypothetical disconnection of the
drain from LIDs in future Tåsinge Plads. In fact, the total discharge to existing
sewer system would be reduced from 1956 m3 (current situation) to 533 m3 (fu-
ture situation), on an annual basis. In the total annual water balance context, the
separation of the drain would entail that the fraction of discharge to the combined
sewer would decrease from 49% to 13% when considering only the square, and from
54% to 51% for the hydraulic catchment.
73
==PAGE:96==
Bio-retention system
The only eﬀect given by the bio-retention system regards the peak of the runoﬀ
hydrograph from the road. The system delays and cuts the runoﬀpeak from the
road catchment. However, due to the small size of the road area compared to the
total area discharging to the network pipe located in Tåsinge Plads, the peak re-
duction is very little. Currently, the bio-retention system makes little sense in a
water balance context. It does not inﬁltrate and the water captured by the LIDs
is almost completely returned to the sewer system, except for a little fraction that
gets captured by the soil and evaporates. A regulation does not allow road runoﬀ
to be inﬁltrated due to the presence of salt in the winter period (Copenhagen City,
2016). In the future, the possibility to change the regulation and inﬁltrate the
water from the bio-retention system should be investigated more into detail. In
fact, it is a low traﬃc intensity road and the runoﬀis led through the engineered
soil, which showed high performances in pollutant removal. Teknologisk Institut
(2015b) tested the engineered soil capacity with synthetic road water runoﬀ, which
contained relatively high concentrations of heavy metals (i.e. Zn, Pb, Cd, Ni and
Cu), concluding that these substances in the outlet were reduced by 84-100 %.
Ermilio (2005) provides promising removal eﬃciency values for bio-inﬁltration cell:
90% removal of TSS and between 93%-98% for Lead and Zn. In a ﬁeld test, Jia
et al. (2015) investigated the urban runoﬀcontrol eﬀectiveness of a low-impact de-
velopment best management practice (LID-BMP). Concerning water quality, the
bio-retention cell showed a removal eﬃciency for Zinc close to 100 %, 69 % for
copper, 51% for NH3-N (ammonia nitrogen) and total nitrogen (TN) 49%. Con-
sidering all these promising ﬁndings and the fact that groundwater in the city area
is not used for drinking purposes and is classiﬁed as "presume polluted" (Copen-
hagen City, 2016), the regulation that forbid to inﬁltrate road runoﬀfrom LIDs
seems outdated. If inﬁltration was approved, the delay eﬀect given by the storage
could be increased by lowering the drain capacity, so the retention time would in-
crease enhancing evaporation and inﬁltration, which will improve the annual water
balance.
Consideration on the bio-retention system
As a result of the present study, the overﬂow structure appeared to be a smart fea-
ture to be included in a new layout for a bio-retention cell. Thanks to the possibility
for the water to bypass the soil layer, the unit can also be useful with relatively
high return period. In an everyday rain scenario, the water is entirely collected
in the surface storage. By inﬁltration through the soil layer, the runoﬀis cleaned
and inﬁltrated to the native soil from the storage layer. When a rain event with a
rainfall intensity that undermines the capability of the soil in accepting the water
occurs, the water collected in the surface storage can bypass the soil, and reach
the storage directly if capability is still available. In this way, the bio-retention
unit would also have a delaying eﬀect on the runoﬀfrom more intense rain. Of
course, the frequency of the internal ﬂow should be carefully evaluated for each
case, depending on the nature of the collecting area, since the runoﬀreaching the
storage through the internal overﬂow structure would be inﬁltrated or discharged
to separate system without treatment.
Basin system
Diﬀerent from the bio-retention system, the basin system allows inﬁltration and
74
==PAGE:97==
thus has a bigger positive impact on the annual water balance. The results pre-
sented in this project show that the implementation of new Tåsinge Plads increases
the losses by 730 m3 on an annual basis. This increase is not only due to LIDs.
Inﬁltration from the basin system accounts for 55% of the increase of losses, and
the remaining 45% is given by the wider extension of green area in the square after
modiﬁcation. This ﬁnding underlines the importance of the role of pervious area
in a urban context: even without installing LIDs, changing the land use beneﬁts
the water cycle. Increasing green areas helps the process of reproducing the water
cycle of pre-urbanization, enhancing inﬁltration and evapotranspiration, and thus
decreasing runoﬀ.
The little change in water balance observed for the hydraulic catchment suggests
that Tåsinge Plads is not suﬃcient as it is now to improve the water balance con-
siderably. Either more catchments have to be drained by the LIDs installation,
given the possibility discussed in Section 6.1.1, or more LIDs have to be spread
around the area in order to obtain signiﬁcant improvements.
6.2
Runoﬀcomputation: conversion form model A to model B
The translation from runoﬀmodel model A (Time/Area method) to model B (Kine-
matic Wave) was necessary in order to implement the LID screening tool. When
shifting the runoﬀcomputation scheme from model A to B, more parameters are
required, as explained in detail in Section 4.3.2. Some parameters are related to
catchment properties and others are related to hydraulic properties of diﬀerent land
uses. An investigation on the inﬂuence of varying length and slope showed that in
some cases they can aﬀect the results tangibly. However, in the present project the
aim was to set up a rainfall-runoﬀmodel B able to compute runoﬀsimilar to model
A, which is believed to be calibrated by HOFOR. The goal was reached successfully:
the two models return very similar result for a CDS rain T=0.5, meaning model
B could be implemented with a good degree of conﬁdence. The same comparison
was carried out for a CDS rain T=10, which shows bigger diﬀerences: model B
computes an accumulated runoﬀ15% higher than model A.
The parameters that showed to change radically the runoﬀcomputation results
for model B were the maximum and minimum Horton inﬁltration capacities of the
soil. Initially, a value of 10−6 mm/hr and 10−10 mm/hr were chosen, according to
values presented in IDA/SVK (1994), for maximum and minimum inﬁltration ca-
pacity for moraine clay, respectively. With these parameters, rainfall-runoﬀmodel
B returned a total accumulated runoﬀapproximately 50% higher than model A.
The values were changed to 1.14 ∗10−5 mm/hr and 9.19 ∗10−6 mm/hr, for max-
imum and minimum Horton inﬁltration capacity, respectively.
The two values
were chosen based on conductivity values for moraine clay measured in an area
of Copenhagen (Teknologisk Institut, 2015a). Since the goodness of the ﬁtting be-
tween model A and B serves the purpose of the present study, it was decided not
to carry out a deeper analysis of the required parameters due to time constraints.
Model B can be considered more conservative than model A in terms of runoﬀ
from green areas. Model A does not compute runoﬀfrom green areas, which are
seen to contribute dramatically to the runoﬀwhen exposed to high return period
rain events, as showed in detail in Section 4.3.2. Furthermore, in the model used
for the simulation, all the green areas are connected to the sewer system. In reality
75
==PAGE:98==
this is probably not always the case. In some cases green areas are not directly
connected to the sewer system (e.g. public parks), and in some places the water
gathers in the terrain depression. Green areas represent a very uncertain topic
(EnviDan, ND), and in the present project it was decided to connect them to the
sewer network in order to provide very conservative results. However, an increased
surface storage of 1 cm was assigned to green areas in order to account for this
assumption.
6.3
Solution for Skt Kjelds District
6.3.1
Compliance with service level and cloudburst management
The ﬁnal solution is exposed to both a CDS, T = 10 and CDS, T = 100 with s.f.
1.56 in order to investigate compliance with service level and the goal of cloud-
burst management, respectively. However, several aspects arises when evaluating
the hydraulic performance. In both cases, boundary conditions are implemented
in project borders in order to account for inﬂuences outside the project area. How-
ever, non of the planned cloudburst solutions are modelled outside the project area
entailing more runoﬀand thus higher pipe ﬂow than expected. Another source of
overestimation is that all catchments in the model (except the lake Kildevældssøen)
are connected to the sewage system. This itself is an overestimation, since some
areas in the system are not connected in reality. Moreover, the generated runoﬀis
send to relatively few manholes along the pipe system in the model, whereas many
more connection points along the pipe system (grids) are existing in reality. This
phenomena is especially important to keep in mind in upstream parts of the system.
In the CMP of Copenhagen, storage capacity of green retention roads are esti-
mated. However, this project disregards the ﬁgures presented. The main reason is
that it is not known how surface storage as retention roads and retention spaces are
implemented in the CMP, see Copenhagen City (2015a). If vegetative swales are
implemented as presented in this project, the dimensions in the CMP seems too big.
In addition, it is unknown for the authors, which areas are planned to be drained
by the surface solutions, which makes implementation challenging. Combining the
two factors, an overall underestimation of surface storage is implemented compared
to the CMP. If the ﬁgures of surface solution were implemented according to the
CMP it is likely that the same service level could be achieved with slightly smaller
cloudburst pipes than presented in the proposed solution.
It is shown that despite the fact of overestimating water ﬂowing in pipes and
the underestimation of surface capacity, the ﬁnal solution is able to cope with a
CDS, T = 10. When considering the CDS, T = 100 ﬂooding is very limited, where
the vast majority of the ﬂooding is between 10-20 cm.
6.3.2
Water balance
As presented in Section 4.4.6, the paved area disconnected from the combined sewer
system to the CP in the ﬁnal solution constitutes 29 % of the entire catchment lead-
ing to similar fraction of runoﬀdirected to the harbour on an annual basis. It must
be noted, that the impact on the annual water balance would have been greater
if the catchments outside the project area were excluded from the model. In fact,
no solution is applied to these catchments, even though they still contributes with
76
==PAGE:99==
discharge to the project area. However, the annual water balance of catchments
having a LID deployed has a more considerable change. On average, 63 % is inﬁl-
trated and only 4 % is evaporated when looking at runoﬀfrom catchments having
a bio-retention cell assigned. Lerer et al. (2015) conﬁrms this substantial fraction
of ﬁltered and attenuated runoﬀby presenting results in the same magnitude (75
%) when evaluating the eﬀect of implementation of bio-retention cells in a road on
the annual water balance.
Accumulated overﬂow from the implemented LIDs (runoﬀnot treated by LID)
constitutes around 30 % of the annual runon to the LIDs. Findings in Lerer and
Sørup (2016) show that 10 % of the annual runon to the LIDs is expected to be
accumulated overﬂow, when designing the LID for a rain event with return period
of T = 0.5. However, the reason for the higher accumulated overﬂow is believed to
lie in the fact that approximately 30 % of the LIDs in the catchment are not able to
inﬁltrate, due to too high groundwater tables. In addition, drains are not applied
to the bio-retention cells in the model. As presented, the LID Screening tool does
not compute the outﬂow from the drain correctly, which forced the authors not to
apply drain when deploying bio-retention cells in MIKE URBAN. Since drain and
inﬁltration is not applied, water can only leave the LIDs due to evaporation, which
is shown to be a minor hydrological process (4 % of the annual water balance). Due
to the long emptying time, accumulated overﬂow from the non-inﬁltrating LIDs is
expected to be signiﬁcant. LIDs located in non-inﬁltrating areas should have a sig-
niﬁcantly higher surface area in order to cope with a rain event of T = 0.5. However,
the surface area of LID being 3 % of the collecting area is kept constant for all LIDs
in this project, which explains the increased total accumulated overﬂow from the
implemented LIDs in the proposed solution. Note that with the proposed dimen-
sions of LIDs the lack of inﬁltration and implementation of drain does not make it
possible to reach the goal of treating 90 % of runoﬀset by Copenhagen City (2016).
The proposed solution suggests a constant area ratio between the area of LID and
catchment area of 3 %. However, the area ratio is based on one speciﬁc K-value. K
has shown to be a highly inﬂuential parameter (Schiolborg and Lindbladh, 2016)
and can vary greatly for moraine clay within a short distance (Teknologisk Institut,
2015a). Thus, in project phase, hydraulic conductivity tests are suggested to be
carried out.
6.3.3
Hydrological performances of LIDs
When evaluating the performances of LIDs in high-intensity rain, this study shows
that the peak of the runoﬀfor a CDS T=10 is reduced by approximately 20 %.
The result shows that even though LIDs in practice are designed for smaller events,
peak ﬂow reduction also occur in bigger events. Similar ﬁndings are presented in
Rosa et al. (2015) where it is shown that LIDs are likely to have storm-ﬂow ben-
eﬁts even during high intensity rains. It moreover corresponds with the strategy
presented in the CCMP of Copenhagen (Copenhagen City, 2011a). As mentioned,
the peak of a rain event with T = 10, is in Danish climate expected to increase
by 30 %. With the given dimensions of the LIDs (i.e. surface area of LID being 3
% of the collecting area) the expected increase in peak intensity is almost entirely
mitigated. If opportunities of disconnecting area from the existing combined sewer
system to the CP are not existing but available space for LIDs is present, this result
shows that LIDs can be eﬀective climate change measures.
77
==PAGE:100==
When considering the eﬀect of LIDs on peak ﬂow from a catchment when extreme
event (CDS, T = 100) is simulated, no diﬀerences are observed. This ﬁnding corre-
sponds well with the general understanding of performances of LIDs on storm-ﬂow
in the extreme domain (see e.g. (Rosa et al., 2015; Holman-Dodds et al., 2003)).
The result implies that the cloudburst system constitute the key element when
dealing with ﬂood measures, rather than the implemented LIDs.
6.3.4
Environmental assessment
As presented, the larger the disconnected area, the less water ﬂowing to the WWTP
and thus the less operational cost. However, from an environmental point of view
both drawbacks and beneﬁts are present if the ﬁnal solution is implemented. On an
annual basis, less water will ﬂow in the combined sewer system, with a lower ﬂow
rate compared to the baseline scenario. This might increase lifespan of pipes due
to less corrosion. In terms of climate, it will lower annual energy consumption due
to less pumping and treatment eﬀort. Most importantly, less stress to the Lynetten
WWTP will result in less bypass in both number of events and volume. Vezzaro
et al. (2014) predicts that 88 bypass event occurs on an annual basis at Lynetten
WWTP with a total volume of around 2.4 mio. m3 untreated WW discharged into
Øresund. This entails that rain events with a return period of only 0.012 yr or
higher will result in bypass at Lynetten WWTP.
Moreover, it seems that treatment of "ﬁrst-ﬂush" runoﬀ(the initial and most pol-
luted runoﬀfrom a rain event, see e.g. Lee et al. (2002) and Peng et al. (2015)) is
not managed optimally due to the overall system conﬁguration. Two pressurized
pipes from Kløvermarken (Amager) and Strandvænget (Østerbro) constitute the
main inlet to WWTP Lynetten, where no storage volume is available. Due to the
pressurized pipes, WW is already present in the pipes before a rain event occurs.
Consequently, once ﬁrst-ﬂush runoﬀarrives to the WWTP it is likely that capacity
is exhausted, meaning that the most polluted part of the runoﬀwill be discharged
into the environment (Vezzaro et al., 2014).
On the downside, runoﬀfrom disconnected areas is directed to the harbour without
monitoring water quality, which potentially pose a negative impact on the environ-
ment. However, LIDs such as bio-retention cells are able to treat ﬁrst-ﬂush runoﬀ
due cleaning capability of the engineered soil and once capacity is exceeded the
subsequent, cleaner runoﬀis discharged without treatment. Thus, any stress re-
moved from the WWTP Lynetten seems to be of high importance and an overall
environmental beneﬁt is expected if the ﬁnal solution is implemented.
Copenhagen is aiming at being the world’s ﬁrst CO2 neutral city by 2025 (Copen-
hagen City, 2012a). The ﬁnal solution concentrate the wastewater ﬂowing to Lynet-
ten WWTP, since a fraction of the rainwater runoﬀis removed. Thereby it is easier
to extract the energy from the WW in order to form biogas that can be used to
town gas. This implies that the ﬁnal solution have a positive eﬀect on reaching
this goal.
The technical and environmental administration of Copenhagen has presented a
plan, where the city is to plant 100,000 trees over the coming 10 years (Copen-
hagen City, 2015b). If the plan is executed it is likely that extensive irrigation is
78
==PAGE:101==
required. The relatively clean rainwater runoﬀplaned to be discharged into the
harbour by the ﬁnal solution could be utilized for irrigation purposes to support
the success of the plan and minimize environmental impacts. In fact, runoﬀcould
be stored along the cloudburst pipe system in separate basins.
6.3.5
Economical assessment
It is not known, what is included in the assessment of the ﬁxed treatment price of
6.29 DKK/m3 at Lynetten. Fees for discharging compounds such as nitrogen (N)
and phosphor (P) that is present in the eﬄuent from the WWTP is not expected
to be included in the treatment cost. If included, the price would increase, leading
to an increased saving by the ﬁnal solution.
Vezzaro et al. (2014) presents a study where the sewage system is optimized when
an economical fee is assigned to the discharge of untreated wastewater through
CSO and bypass (varying from 1-25 e/m3 depending on discharge location). How-
ever, the Act on charge of sewage, LBK nr. 938 of 27/06/2013 (Bekendtgørelse af
lov om afgift af spildevand) states that WW from CSO is exempted from the fee.
Unfortunately this create a ﬁnancial incentive to direct untreated WW through
CSO rather than treat the WW, which might result in an unwanted and unneces-
sary negative environmental impact. Flow sensors at the most signiﬁcant bypass
and CSOs could be implemented in order to monitor amount of WW discharged
without treatment and subsequently determine a fee.
Flood economy is not included in the present study due to time constraint. How-
ever, further work is suggested to include this in order to asses a realistic and
meaningful cost-beneﬁt analysis of the ﬁnal solution. This is carried out in Copen-
hagen City (2015a). The importance is more clear when bearing in mind that the
cloudburst hit Copenhagen in the summer of 2011, had a total cost of 8 billion
DKK (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2015). This is more than 70 % of the total cost of
the ambitious CMP of Copenhagen (Estimated 11 billion DKK (Copenhagen City,
2015a)).

6.4
Future Work
Based on the ﬁndings in this thesis, we suggest that the following points could be
investigated in future work.
• Measure data (ﬂow in under drain, local rain measures, inﬁltration test) in
order to calibrate and validate the model at Tåsinge Plads.
• Monitor water quality in the eﬄuent from the LIDs to back up the choice of
discharging runoﬀfrom LIDs directly to the harbour without further treat-
ment.
• Investigate the possibility to represent surface solutions, i.e. cloudburst roads,
retention roads and spaces etc., in a 2D overland model.
• Investigate the full potential for LIDs in Skt. Kjelds area, since the maximum
potential for LIDs implementation was not studied.
• Investigate the eﬀect of up scaling the proposed scenario, by expanding the
solutions to other coastal areas of Copenhagen.
• Investigate how the proposed solution will aﬀect Copenhagen’s plan to be the
ﬁrst CO2 neutral city by 2025.
• Investigate how the cleanest runoﬀtreated by LIDs can be optimally stored
and distributed along the CP in order to accommodate the plan of planting
100,000 trees in Copenhagen.

7
Conclusion
Based on a comparison between the newest LID modelling features in MIKE UR-
BAN, this study concludes that the soakaway node feature is the best modelling
practice in order to represent both the bio-retention units and the basin system
implemented in Tåsinge Plads. The main reasons were shown to be the need for
representing the internal overﬂow structure and the complexity of the basin system
in series, for the bio-retention system and the basin system, respectively. In both
speciﬁc cases the LID screening tool showed limitations.
Investigations on hydrological performance of Tåsinge Plads using the proposed
model showed that the implemented LIDs were able to handle a rain event with
a return period higher than 100 years, implying that the square is able to meet
the service level and even the ambitious goal of ﬂood management in Copenhagen.
A comparison of Tåsinge Plads before and after renovation showed that the drain
from the LIDs should be disconnected to the future separate cloudburst system in
order to have a tangible impact on the annual water balance (reducing sewage dis-
charge from around 65 % to around 15 % on an annual basis). The impact on the
hydraulic catchment of Tåsinge Plads is shown to be negligible, and we recommend
to increase the collecting area of the existing LIDs in order to exploit its capacity,
and/or increase the number of LIDs deployed in the hydraulic catchment.
The rainfall-runoﬀmodel A in the hydraulic model of Skt. Kjelds was success-
fully converted into model B. When comparing the two models for a CDS rain T
= 5, hydrographs of water levels in manholes and discharge in pipes showed minor
diﬀerences in the range of 0 - 2 %. Instead, when comparing the two models for a
CDS rain T = 10, the diﬀerences were higher, in the range of 2 - 15 %. Model B
generated the highest values. Moreover, model B showed 15 % higher total accu-
mulated runoﬀvalues.
The hydraulic model of the existing sewer system in Skt.
Kjelds showed that
the service level is not met, neither today nor in the future, since around 14 %
and 46 % of manholes are surcharging when exposed to a CDS rain, T = 10 for
a current and future climate (2116), respectively. The implementation of scenario
4 reduced the surcharging manholes down to 1 %. It was shown that due to im-
plementation of LIDs that enhances losses, together with disconnection from the
existing sewer network to the cloudburst pipes of 49% of the catchment area (29%
impervious and 20% pervious), rainwater runoﬀdirected to Lynetten WWTP was
reduced by approximately 30 % on an annual basis. The implementation of the
proposed ﬁnal solution is expected to reduce treatment cost at Lynetten WWTP,
reduce damages costs due to ﬂooding, reduce environmental impacts by reducing
bypass at Lynetten, both in terms of number and volume and increase ecosystem
services and liveability in the city.
This study concludes that the ﬁnal solution is estimated to reduce the volume of
surface water in Skt. Kjelds by 73 % when exposed to the extreme event (T=100)
compared to the baseline scenario. The highest fraction of the remaining ﬂood-
ing is observed on areas with low vulnerability, e.g. parking lots. Since model B
slightly overestimates runoﬀcompared to model A and the implemented surface
solutions are strongly under-dimensioned relative to the values proposed in the
81
==PAGE:104==
CMP, the ﬁnal solution presented seems promising. The implementation of cloud-
burst road in the ﬁnal solution allowed to reach the ambitious goal of the the CMP.
Neither calibration or validation of the models have been possible in this project,
entailing that interpretation of results must be done with caution.

Detailed explanation of implementation of LIDs
In the following, the dimensioning approach of LIDs is presented together with the
implementation in MU (Scenario 2 is used as an example).
• A thematic illustration of both pipes and catchments are performed in order
to identify the cloudburst pipes and roofs facing inner yards, respectively.
• All catchments of the roofs facing inner yards located near the cloudburst
pipes are merged. The inner yard it self is merged as well. Both merged
catchment are connected to the same nearest manhole of the cloudburst pipe.
• Bio Retention Cell is chosen as LID solution and assigned a merged roof
catchment.
• The Bioretention Cell has storage capacity both on surface and in soil and
storage, while the SVK spreadsheet presented in Gregersen et al. (2014)com-
putes dimensions for a soakaway, i.e. strictly storage capacity. Figure J.1
presents the diﬀerences in concept.
• This project suggest to implement an internal overﬂow structure in the bio-
retention cells that neutralizes the inﬁltration rate through the soil ensuring
full exploitation of storage capacity.
• This report presents surface and soil height of 30 cm respectively and a poros-
ity of 1 and 0.35, respectively. In order not to overdimension the bio-retention
cells, the storage capacity from the three layers are taken into account in the
SVK spreadsheet. Equation J.1 presents the inclusion of storage capacity
from all layers in the bio-retention cell.
• In order to dimension the bio-retention cells from the SVK spreadsheet, Table
J.1 presents the used parameter values. Note that the inﬁltration rate through
the bottom is lower, than the one used in MU (9.19 ∗10−6 m/s), since it
was updated later in the project phase. The consequence is that the lower
inﬁltration rate contributes to a higher surface area of LID, i.e. the model is
overdimensioned.
• It is shown that the surface area of LIDs represents approximately 3 % of the
assigned reduced catchment area, see Table J.2 for varying catchment sizes.
• The reduced area of the roof and the area of the LID is used in the "LID
deployment".
Note that 5 m2 are subtracted every reduced roof area, to
ensure the collecting area not to be bigger than the impervious area of the
catchment the LID is assigned, since the LID screening tool in MU strictly
drains the impervious area.

N
Flood computation
N.1
Coupling parameters
When coupling the manholes of the 1D model to the 2D surface model, a maximum
ﬂow to the manhole has to be deﬁne. A Qmax=1 m3/s was applied to the manholes
of the existing system. The value was increased from 0.1 to 1 m3/s. In fact, an inlet
represented by 1 manhole in the model it corresponds actually to many manholes in
reality and it is then believed 0.1 m3 understimate the maximum water ﬂow to the
system. Varying this parameter of course does not increase the volume capacity of
the system, when the system will be full, the runoﬀwill be diverted to the surface.
For the manholes of cloudburst pipe system the Qmax value was set to 3.5 m3/s.
The value was set based on the maximum ﬂow observed in the lateral cloudburst
pipe branch, going from East to West to Osterbrogade. In this way, there will be
less ﬂooding due to limitation of the water in entering the system ubefore it gets
full. This modiﬁcation is fundamental especially for the manholes of the CP system
that for modelling reasons presents manholes with a huge number of catchments
connected. For the manholes within the link representing the CR instead the value
was set to 10 m3/s. In fact, no limitation should be present for the water to reach
the street.
N.2
DTM generation
Initially, a Digitial Elevation Model (DTM) of the terrain is downloaded from the
webpage of the Danish Geodata Agency. The "DHM/overﬂade (0.4 m grid)" is
downloaded from the Danish Geodata Agency. It is a raster based set of data,
where every pixel of 0.4*0.4 m has a value that represents the height with sea level
as reference. The DTM describes the surface without building, vegetation etc.
Afterwords, a polygon (shape ﬁle) representing the buildings in the area is con-
verted to raster and assigned an elevation of 20 meters
The two rasters, DTM and polygon are merged together in order to create a new
DTM with resolution 1.6 m containing also information about the building. Figure
N.1 shows the diﬀerent steps explained.
