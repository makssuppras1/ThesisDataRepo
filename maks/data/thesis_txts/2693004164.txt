Abstract
Vaccines have played a tremendous role in reducing mortality caused by infectious
diseases ever since it was ﬁrst introduced in 1796. Most vaccines are administered
parenterally, but oral delivery of vaccines and drugs is of great interest due to high pa-
tient compliance and low cost. However, the development of orally delivered drugs
and vaccines faces several challenges that need to be overcome. The GI tract, which
the orally administered drug or vaccine needs to travel through, is protected by enzy-
matic, chemical, and physical barriers. Microcontainers have been developed for oral
delivery of drugs and vaccines. Microcontainers protect the drug from the external
environment and can be sealed with a polymer lid to control the release of the drug
and vaccine.
In this project, the transit time of microcontainers in mice was investigated. The micro-
containers were loaded with the X-ray contrast agent barium sulfate and coated with
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid. X-ray imaging and CT scanning were used to quantify and
visualize the microcontainers. The study showed a fast transit of the microcontainers
through the GI tract. After 30 minutes, the microcontainers were primarily located
to the stomach and small intestine, and after 120 minutes, most were localized to the
cecum.
The release of paracetamol from chitosan-coated microcontainers was investigated. A
full release was seen after 71 minutes, which was much faster than previously reported
studies on chitosan. The thickness of the chitosan was measured and an experiment
on what effect the coating thickness has on the release time was conducted. However,
no conclusion can be drawn from these experiments.
The last part of the project aimed to investigate the effect of Eudragit® L100-55 coated
microcontainers loaded with CTH522 vaccine formulation. An in vitro release study
was conducted where the loaded and coated microcontainers were placed in a solu-
tion at pH 4.76 and afterward transferred to a solution at pH 6.64. The results showed
an immediate release at pH 4.76 and a release of 5,000%. At pH 6.64, the release was
5,000% and increased up to almost 45,000%. The different components related to the
release experiments were tested, and their UV spectrum was recorded. Only the anti-
gen showed to contribute to the UV spectrum. An uncoated chip loaded with CTH522
vaccine formulation was investigated, which showed a release of 1,800% and thus
might explain the high release.

Introduction
Vaccines are considered one of the most successful medical inventions introduced. Ac-
cording to the World Health Organization, more than 2.5 million lives are saved every
year because of vaccines [1]. The preferred route of administration for drugs and vac-
cines is the oral route due to the minimal invasiveness, painfulness, and no need for
trained health care personnel [2]. In addition, oral vaccines are more cost-effective
as they do not require the same extensive puriﬁcation as parenterally vaccines do [3].
Oral delivery of drugs and vaccines are facing enzymatic, chemical, and physical barri-
ers which have to be overcome for effective drug and vaccine delivery [4] for example,
by using an oral drug delivery system to protect the drug and vaccine from the outside
environment.
1.1
Gastrointestinal tract
The gastrointestinal tract (GI-tract) digests and absorbs nutrients as well as providing
an efﬁcient barrier from foreign substances [5]. The GI tract consists of the mouth,
pharynx, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus (Figure 1).
The small intestine is further divided into the duodenum, the jejunum, and the ileum,
and the large intestine can be divided into the cecum, colon, and rectum [3].
Figure 1: Illustration of the human gastrointestinal tract consisting of the mouth, pharynx, esophagus,
stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus. Created in BioRender.com.
1
==PAGE:9==
Oral delivery of drugs encounters several challenges as the drug is exposed to a harsh
environment throughout the GI tract. Drugs have a high risk of being degraded by var-
ious digestive enzymes present in the stomach and small intestine. The pH varies sig-
niﬁcantly throughout the GI tract, ranging from 1.0-7.5. The stomach is highly acidic
in fasted state (pH 1.0-3.0) whereas in fed state the pH is much higher (pH 3.0-7.0)
[6]. The small intestine has a more neutral or slightly alkaline pH (6.0-7.5), and the
pH of the colon varies from 5.0-7.0 [3], [7]. Exposure to this variation in pH values
along the GI tract can result in hydrolysis, oxidation, or deamidation, thereby leading
to deactivation of the drug [7].
1.2
Small intestine
Absorption of drugs occurs mainly in the small intestine. The duodenum in humans
is only 25-30 cm long; thus, the passage of the drug is quickly resulting in poor absorp-
tion. The jejunum has highly active peristalsis, which favors absorption and uptake of
nutrients. Leftover drug will be absorbed in the ileum, which has a similar absorption
capacity as the jejunum [8]. The small intestine is covered by an epithelial cell layer
which is arranged into villi, and crypts [9]. A wide variety of cells cover the small in-
testine, including enterocytes, goblet cells, paneth cells, endocrine cells, and microfold
cells (M cells) (Figure 2) [10].

antigens from the lamina to the lamina propria, where they will come across different immune cells.

Enterocytes are the most abundant cell type in the small intestine and are responsible
for the majority of the absorption of nutrients, and drugs [11]. Mucus-secreting gob-
let cells are the second most common cell type of the small intestine. They contribute
to the protective mucus layer covering the epithelial layer, which contains secretory
immunoglobulin (sIg) A antibodies. Paneth cells are located at the base of the crypts,
and their function is to secrete antimicrobial proteins and peptides. Endocrine cells
secrete hormones important for digestive functions. Less than 1% of the total cells in
the small intestine are comprised of M cells. They are located at the follicle-associated
epithelium (FAE) that covers Peyer’s patches and play an essential role in the uptake
and transport of antigens from the lumen to the lamina propria, where they will en-
counter different immune cells. In contrast to the epithelial layer, the mucus layer is
absent in the FAE, resulting in efﬁcient uptake of particles [10], [12], [13].
1.3
Intestinal mucus layer
Enzymatic, chemical, and physical barriers protect the GI tract from the daily exposure
of foreign pathogens while still allowing an exchange of nutrients. The enzymatic and
chemical barriers include digestive enzymes and the variation of pH values across the
GI tract. The physical barrier is the mucus layer protecting the intestinal epithelium
[14].
The mucus is a complex hydrogel and consists of water, proteins (mainly mucin), car-
bohydrates, lipids, salts, and antibodies. Mucin monomers link together via disulﬁde
bonds to form large dynamic viscoelastic gels and provide protection and lubrication
of the GI tract, thus maintaining a healthy homeostasis in the GI tract [3], [12], [15].
The mucus layer consists of two layers: a ﬁrmly adherent mucus layer covering the
epithelial cell layer and an outer loosely adherent mucus layer. The thickness of the
mucus layer varies a lot throughout the GI tract. The stomach has a very thick layer,
whereas the small intestine has the thinnest layer to allow a higher uptake of nutrients
and drugs; thus, the small intestine is considered the best target for oral drug and
vaccine delivery [12], [16], [17]. As the thickness of the small intestine varies according
to location, an overview of the thickness for different parts of the small intestine can
be found in Table 1.

The mucosal tissue act as a barrier and restricts the penetration of foreign particles,
i.e., vaccines. An approach that has been exploited to overcome the mucus layer is to
increase the mucoadhesion of the drug and vaccine delivery systems, thus improving
the residence time and increasing the local drug concentration and absorption from
the mucosal surface [16], [18].
1.4
Vaccines
In 1796 Edward Jenner discovered that inoculation of cowpox protects against infec-
tions with smallpox. Jenner decided to name the procedure vaccination from the Latin
word vacca meaning cow, and vaccinia meaning cowpox [19]. Vaccination has since
then played a tremendous role in preventing disease outbreaks, thereby reducing the
mortality rate. In the United States alone, over one million people are saved each year
[20].
Vaccination aims to provide long-lasting and protective immunity against disease-
causing pathogens [21], [22]. Vaccines can be divided into three types; live attenuated,
inactivated, and subunit vaccines [23].
A live attenuated vaccine contains a weakened form of the pathogen and effectively
elicits a robust immune response and generates long-lasting immunity. However, the
weakened pathogen might revert to its virulent form [24]. Inactivated vaccines con-
tain inactivated pathogens and are therefore not able to replicate and cause disease
[25]. Subunit vaccines only contain components of the pathogen that are required in
order to generate an immune response and are thus considered to be the safest vac-
cine type [3], [26]. Subunit vaccines have a high safety proﬁle; however, they have
poor immunogenicity and a shorter duration of protection compared to live attenu-
ated vaccines and may require booster injections or co-administration with adjuvants
for sufﬁcient protection [25], [27]. An adjuvant is a component that stimulates the
immune response towards the active ingredient of the vaccine [22], [26], [28].
Subunit vaccines consist of a puriﬁed antigen from the target pathogen, which can be
proteins, polysaccharides, or virus-like particles, if the pathogen is a virus [25], [29].
4
==PAGE:12==
The components of a subunit vaccine are very fragile and are easily degraded in the
GI tract by the constant change in pH and the presence of proteolytic enzymes. Thus,
subunit vaccines require delivery systems in order to deliver the intact antigen to the
small intestine [3].
1.4.1
Oral vaccine delivery
Today, 87 vaccines are licensed in the United States however, only ﬁve of them are oral
vaccines [30]. Developing orally administered vaccines is challenging as indicated by
the low number of approved oral vaccines.
Most vaccines are delivered parenterally but oral delivery of drugs and vaccines rep-
resents an attractive route of administration due to the high patient compliance, and
reduced costs [27], [31]. Oral delivery of vaccines eliminates the need for needles and
thus the high risk of cross-contamination, which is a huge problem, especially in de-
veloping countries where diseases such as HIV and hepatitis are common [32].
The main advantages of oral vaccines are the ability to induce both a mucosal and a
systemic immune response [31]. However, opposite to parenteral vaccines, oral vac-
cines need a higher dose of antigen in order to induce a immune response, which
increases the risk of inducing tolerance. Because of the enzymatic, chemical, and bio-
logical barriers, the orally delivered drugs and vaccines need to be protected in order
to pass through the stomach and reach the small intestine intact [3].
1.5
Microcontainers
Microcontainers are cylindrically shaped polymer-based devices in micro size range
(Figure 3).
Figure 3: Scanning Electron Microscopy image of a SU-8 microcontainer [33].
5
==PAGE:13==
The drug or vaccine of interest can be loaded inside the cavity of the microcontainer
and obtain protection from the outside environment. The microcontainers are only
open at one end, and will therefore provide a unidirectional release of drug and vac-
cine. This will result in a more controlled drug and vaccine release compared to micro-
and nanoparticles where release can happen from all angles [34].
The open end of the microcontainers can be sealed with a polymer lid to control the re-
lease of the drug and vaccine [35]. pH-sensitive polymers are widely used to facilitate
drug and vaccine release in selected regions of the GI tract, especially the small intes-
tine [36]. Eudragit polymers have the advantages of being stable at gastric pH values,
while dissolving upon contact with intestinal pH values [37], [38]. It has previously
been shown, that the microcontainers have mucoadhesive properties [34], thereby pro-
longing the transit time throughout the GI tract. To further increase the transit time,
mucoadhesive polymers can be applied as a protective lid on the microcontainers. One
such polymer is chitosan, which is a hydrophilic cationic polysaccharide that shows
mucoadhesive properties [3], [39], [40].

2
Hypothesis and Aim of Studies
The hypothesis investigated in this master thesis is that the transit time of microcon-
tainers can be prolonged and that the release time of drugs and vaccines can be con-
trolled using different polymers as a coating on the microcontainers.
This project aims to estimate the transit time of microcontainers in mice and investigate
if the release time can be prolonged. This is divided into three parts:
• Estimate the transit time of orally dosed microcontainers in mice followed by CT
scanning and X-ray imaging of the mice.
• Investigate if the thickness of chitosan as a coating on the microcontainers inﬂu-
ences the time it takes for the drug to be released
• Study how a Eudagit L100-55 coating controls the release of CTH522 vaccine
formulation from microcontainers

Materials and Methods
Barium Sulfate (BaSO4) was obtained from Alfa Aesar (Ward Hill, MA, USA).
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA, MW 38,000-54,000, 50:50 PLA:PGA), low molecu-
lar weight chitosan, acetic acid, isopropanol (IPA), dibutyl sebacate (DBS), potassium
dihydrogen phosphate (KH2PO4), phosphate buffered saline (PBS) tablets, and parac-
etamol were acquired from Sigma-Aldrich (St.Louis, MO, USA). Ethyl acetate was ob-
tained from VWR Chemicals (Radnor, PA, USA). Eudragit® L100-55 (EL100-55) was
aquired from Evonik Industries (Darmstadt, Germany). Mice capsules were obtained
from Torpac Inc. (Fairﬁeld, CT, USA). Milli-Q water was acquired from a milli-Q
water puriﬁcation system (Merck Millipore Darmstadt, Germany). The CTH522 vac-
cine formulations (CTH522 vaccine) were prepared by PhD student Philip H. Ronøe
Carlsen. The vaccine formulations contain the antigen CTH522, 10 mM Tris and 10%
trehalose. The vaccine formulation with adjuvant contains, in addition, the adjuvant
alpha-Galactosylceramide.
SU-8 microcontainers on Si-wafers and Ti-Au-wafers and silicon wafers were pro-
duced and provided by micro and Nanofabrication specialist Lasse Højlund Eklund
Thamdrup and, IDUN research center, DTU Health Technology (Lyngby, Denmark).
3.1
Mice
25 g male-speciﬁc CB6F1 mice were obtained from Envigo (Indianapolis, IN, USA).
The mice had free access to food and water at all times. The mice were housed at
DTU’s animal facilities and cared for by the animal keepers at DTU.
3.2
Loading of Drug and Vaccine into Microcontainers
The microcontainers were ﬁlled manually in a similar manner as described previously
[34], [36]. A shadow mask was placed and aligned onto the cavity of the microcon-
tainers. Either BaSO4, PLGA, or CTH522 vaccine formulation was deposited on top
of the shadow mask and pushed inside the cavities of the individual microcontainers
using a ﬁne make-up brush. The shadow mask was gently removed, and excess pow-
der was brushed away. The microcontainers were weighed before and after loading to
determine the amount of drug or vaccine ﬁlled into the microcontainers.

3.3
Preparation of Polymers Used for Spray Coating
PLGA: 902 µg of PLGA was mixed with 100 mL ethyl acetate and stirred until com-
plete dissolution was observed.
EL100-55: 2 g of EL100-55 was mixed with 150 mL IPA and stirred at low speed. 107
µL DBS was added and the solution was stirred overnight. IPA was added so the total
volume reached 200 mL.
Chitosan: 1.16 mL acetic acid was diluted in 200 mL MilliQ water. 500 µg of chitosan
was mixed with 80 mL acetic acid and stirred at 45-55 °C overnight. Acetic acid was
added, so the total volume reached 100 mL. The solution was centrifuged at 12.000
rpm for 30 minutes and ﬁltered with a 5-13 µm sized ﬁlter using vacuum suction.
3.4
Spray Coating of Microcontainers
The drug or vaccine-loaded microcontainers were spray coated using an ExactaCoat
spray coating system (Sono-Tek, NY, USA) equipped with either an AccuMist nozzle
or a Vortex nozzle. The coatings used were either EL100-55 (dissolved to a 1% (w/v)
solution in IPA with 5% (w/w) DBS relative to EL100-55), PLGA (dissolved to a 1%
(w/w) solution in ethyl acetate), or chitosan (dissolved to a 0.5% (w/v) solution in 0.1
M acetic acid). Prior to spray coating, the system was cleaned with solvent (approxi-
mately 15 mL), dependent on what polymer was going to be used, so ethyl acetate for
PLGA, acetic acid for chitosan, or IPA for EL100-55. Afterward, the polymer solution
was connected to the system and washed through (approximately 5 mL). The chips to
be coated were placed on a hot plate inside the spray coater. The settings for the spray
coating program are listed in Table 2.

3.5
Scanning Electron Microscopy for Evaluation of Loading and Coat-
ing of Microcontainers and Release from Microcontainers
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was utilized to examine the microcontainers after
loading and spray coating using a TM3030Plus Tabletop Microscope (Hitachi, Japan)
to ensure sufﬁcient drug loading and coating of the lid. In addition, SEM was used to
check the integrity of the coating after drug release studies. The microcontainers were
mounted on a 45°tilted metal holder using double-sided carbon tape. The examination
was done using the SE detector and an acceleration voltage of 15 kV to obtain high-
quality images.
3.6
Loading of Capsules with Microcontainers
SU-8 microcontainers were scratched off the wafer with a scalpel, and the released
microcontainers were ﬁlled into mice gelatine capsules by using a dosing kit. Approx-
imately 80 containers were ﬁlled into each capsule.
3.7
Study of Transit Time of Microcontainers in Mice
12 male-speciﬁc CB6F1 mice were used for the study, and the experiment was carried
out under the license number IDUN_SSI_21_01. The study consisted of four groups,
with three mice in each group as shown in Table 3. Two capsules were used for each
mice and were administered by oral gavage. The study was carried out for two hours
after dosing, and the mice were euthanized after 30, 60, 90, or 120 minutes, respec-
tively, according to Table 3. The mice were stored at -20 °C until further analysis.
3.7.1
Evaluation of PLGA Coating at Simulated Gastric and Intestinal pH Values
To investigate if pH values corresponding to the gastric and intestinal environment
of a mouse have an effect on the coating, a chip loaded with BaSO4 and coated with
PLGA was ﬁrst submerged into a 10 mM KH2PO4 solution adjusted to pH 4.6 with
HCl for two hours on a shaker with 120 rpm and afterward submerged into a 10 mM
KH2PO4 solution adjusted to pH 6.52 with NaOH for additional two hours. The chip
was examined with SEM every 30 minutes.

3.7.2
CT Scanning of Intact Mice and X-ray Imaging of Removed GI-tracts
Three frozen mice, one from each group from the in vivo study, were visualized in 3D.
The 3D visualizations were created from single scans using 1572 projections with two
frames per projection with a scan time of 0.5 seconds resulting in a ﬁnal scan time of
approximately 26 minutes. The GI tracts were removed from all the mice and placed
in a Petri dish for X-ray imaging. The X-ray imaging and CT scanning were carried out
using a Nikon XT H 225 scanner (Nikon Metrology, Tokyo, Japan), and X-rays were
generated using a voltage of 70 kV and a power of 30 W. The X-ray imaging and CT
scanning were conducted by PhD student Rolf Beck Kjeldsen.
3.8
Release Study of Microcontainers Loaded with Paracetamol and
Coated with Chitosan
In vitro release of paracetamol from microcontainers coated with chitosan was inves-
tigated on a µDiss proﬁler (pION INC, MA, USA). The channels were calibrated with
their own paracetamol standard curve prior to the experiments. Samples of a parac-
etamol stock solution (2 mg/mL) were repeatedly added to a 10 mL PBS solution to
obtain a range of deﬁned standard concentrations. A UV spectrum of each concentra-
tion was recorded.

4.1
Loading and Spray Coating of Microcontainers
Microcontainers were loaded with either BaSO4, paracetamol, or CTH522 vaccine for-
mulation as described in section 3.2. BaSO4 was used for estimating the transit time
of the microcontainers as BaSO4 is an insoluble X-ray contrast agent with low toxic-
ity, thus allowing the microcontainers to be easily tracked [42]–[44]. Paracetamol was
used as the model drug for the release studies involving chitosan. Paracetamol was
chosen as it is highly water-soluble, thus releasing from the microcontainers easily in
aqueous media [37]. Furthermore, as chitosan is hydrophilic, hydrophobic drug and
vaccine formulations are therefore not able to be loaded into the microcontainers [39].
The CTH vaccine formulation was used in the study of how EL100-55 can control the
release. The loaded microcontainers were visualized using SEM (Figure 4).
Figure 4: Scanning Electron Microscopy of microcontainers before and after loading with drug. (A)
Empty microcontainer. (B) Microcontainers loaded with BaSO4. (C) Microcontainers loaded with parac-
etamol. (D) Microcontainers loaded with CTH vaccine formulation.

The amount loaded onto each chip varied a lot. The weight of the BaSO4-loaded mi-
crocontainers were 6.93 ± 0.45 mg (n = 6), the weight of the paracetamol-loaded mi-
crocontainers were 3.12 ± 0.27 mg (n = 6), and the weight for the CTH vaccine-loaded
microcontainers were 2.57 ± 0.53 mg (n = 4). The variation could be due to some of
the microcontainers being damaged and fall off during alignment and removal of the
shadow mask. One chip weighs approximately 208 mg, which corresponds to 332.8
µg per microcontainer. The drug loading method used requires practice in order to
obtain a sufﬁcient loading, and this could therefore be an explanation for the variation
in the amount. For up-scale production, it would be beneﬁcial to consider automation
to obtain a more consistent loading of microcontainers. Automation could be in terms
of a machine that can load the drug into the microcontainers automatically.
After drug loading, the microcontainers were coated with either PLGA, chitosan, or
EL100-55 (Figure 5). PLGA was used for coating BaSO4-loaded microcontainers, chi-

CTH522 vaccine formulation.

Transit Time of Microcontainers in Mice

The PLGA coating was evaluated at different pH levels simulating the gastric and the

After 30 minutes, the microcontainers were primarily located in the stomach and the

from the stomach to the intestines. After 60 minutes, the pattern is quite similar to that

==PAGE:25==
of 30 minutes, albeit most of the microcontainers from the small intestines were from
the proximal part. After 90 minutes, over 90% of the microcontainers were found in the
small intestines, with the majority of them being in the distal part. After 120 minutes,
the majority was localized in the cecum. These ﬁndings are similar to a study by von
Halling Laier et al.[41]. Their results showed that after 60 minutes, the majority of
the microcontainers had passed through the stomach and were distributed along the
small intestine. Their ﬁndings at 90 minutes, however, do not correspond with the
result from this study. They found that the majority of the microcontainers recovered
were present in the cecum and the colon. The variation could be from different species
of mice used in the experiments or between the mice, as both experiments showed
large variability. von Halling Laier et al. used an optical microscope to count the
microcontainers, and some might have been overlooked with this method. This study
used X-ray imaging, which could explain the variation between the studies, although
errors also could occur with this method due to manual counting.
Considering the large variation between the mice, including a larger number of repli-
cates for each time point could be advantageous in terms of statistics. Another study
of transit time could include euthanasia time points at 15 and 45 minutes to further
investigate the transit time from the stomach to the small intestine and at 105 minutes
for investigating the transit time to the cecum. As drug absorption mainly occurs in
the small intestine, this could provide a more extensive understanding of the transit
time through the small intestine.

4.3
Release of Paracetamol from Chitosan Coated Microcontainers
4.3.1
Optimization of Chitosan Coating Settings
As no one previously had used chitosan combined with the Vortex nozzle, different
settings on the spray coater were investigated on a silicon chip prior to the release
experiments. First, the dwelling time, i.e., how much time there is between each loop,
was measured. Acetic acid was sprayed on a silicon chip, and the time it took for it
to dry was measured. The experiment showed that a 30 second dwelling time was
sufﬁcient for the chip to dry. However, the dwelling time was only measured on a
couple of layers on the silicon chip. A longer dwelling time might be necessary to
ensure the polymer is completely dry when spray coating many layers. Next, the
infusion rate, i.e., how much polymer the nozzle is spraying, was tested. Four different
settings were tested (Figure 12).
An infusion rate of 1.5 mL/min and 200 loops provided the smoothest surface on the
silicon chips and was therefore chosen. Other settings were not tested but compared
to the settings used for PLGA. Further tests could include path speed, i.e., how fast
the nozzle moves.

Release of Paracetamol
The release of paracetamol from chitosan-coated microcontainers was investigated to
determine a time range for expected drug release. The release experiment was con-

mean weight expressed in percentage of the total load of paracetamol present in the
microcontainers throughout the experiment (Figure 13).
The release study showed 100% release after 71 minutes. The result is not similar to
previous studies. Kamguyan et al [47]. conducted an experiment of chitosan-coated
microcontainers. Their result showed a release of 88.8 ± 6.5% in pH 7.4 after approx-

a study on chitosan microspheres, and their results showed a release of ibuprofen ap-
proximately 95% after 12 hours. Both of these studies have in common that they were
conducted ﬁrst at a low pH (2.5 and 1.4, respectively) and after at a higher pH (7.4
and 7.2, respectively), thus simulating the environment throughout the GI tract. The
degree of swelling inﬂuences the rate of drug release. A high swelling rate results in
a delayed drug release [49]. Huanbutta et al.[49] showed that the swelling of chitosan

the opposite of what was expected. These ﬁndings could be from a too wet coating

introducing a higher dwelling time between each loop and conduct the study in vitro

5
Conclusion
This project aimed to estimate the transit time of microcontainers in mice and investi-
gate if the release time can be prolonged.
BaSO4 was loaded into the microcontainers and coated with PLGA for the transit time
study. X-ray imaging and CT scanning were used to quantify and visualize the micro-
containers. The transit time appears to be quite fast in mice, with the microcontainers
located in the stomach and the small intestine already after 30 minutes, and after 120
minutes, most were located to the cecum. The transit study showed a signiﬁcant vari-
ation which could be in between the mice, as well as the counting method which can
be imprecise.
Paracetamol was loaded into the microcontainers and coated with chitosan to inves-
tigate the release time. The release study showed a full release after 71 minutes, in-
consistent with previous studies showing a much longer release time. The degree
of swelling increases as pH decreases which results in delayed drug release. This
might explain the differences from this study compared to previous studies as they
conducted their experiments in low pH values followed by neutral pH values. In con-
trast, this study only did the release experiment in neutral pH value.
Furthermore, this study investigated if a thicker coating of chitosan could prolong the
release time. Three different layers were tested, and the thickness of the layers was
measured. The results did not make it possible to draw any conclusions, most likely
due to the coating being too wet when the next layer was applied, resulting in the
coating running out over the edge.
CHT522 vaccine formulation was loaded into the microcontainers and coated with
EL100-55 to evaluate the effect of the coating on the release. First, a PBS buffer with
pH 7.4 was used for the release study, which showed a peak followed by a decrease in
concentration. This was thought to be of eudragit dissolving, but no previous studies
showed the same results. Afterward, an in vitro release study was conducted where
KH2PO4 solutions of pH 4.76 and 6.64 were used. The experiment showed an imme-
diate release, although the experiment was conducted at 4.76, in which the eudragit
coating should not dissolve. The study using PBS buffer and the in vitro release study
showed an exceptional high release of more than 14,000% and 5,000%, respectively.
The different components related to the release experiment were tested, and only the
antigen showed a peak. An uncoated chip loaded with CTH522 vaccine, was tested
which showed a release of 1,800% increasing to 9,200%. This is a possible explanation

for the high release percentage, but further experiments are needed to conﬁrm this.

6
Future Perspectives
Oral drug and vaccine delivery are of high interest, but some challenges remain to be
solved.
The loading of the microcontainers was inconsistent from chip to chip, and future
aspects could include automation of the process or consideration of another loading
method to improve the loading of the microcontainers. Automation of the counting of
microcontainers is another thing to be looked into, as this would minimize the errors
related to manual counting.
Further investigations of the thickness of chitosan are needed to conclude if a thicker
layer results in a delayed drug release. As a starter, the dwelling time between each
loop can be increased to achieve a sufﬁcient dry layer before applying the next one on
top.
Additional studies on the CTH522 vaccine formulations need to be conducted regard-
ing why a release of far more than 100% is measured and what can be done in order to
avoid this.
