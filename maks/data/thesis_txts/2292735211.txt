Abstract
The Mean Sea Surface is an important reference for
several geophysical applications.
In the Arctic this
surface is employed for the sea ice thickness deriva-
tion, fundamental parameter in the climate change
challenge. DTU13MSS is the MSS released by DTU
in 2013 and is derived by merging 20 years of altime-
try data. The model shows dubious patterns over the
region with latitudes higher than 82N which will be
investigated in this project. In this work an updated
version of DTU13MSS, the DTU15MSS will also be
presented. DTU15MSS is based on DTU13MSS and
integrates with a 3-step method four years of SAR
and SARin CryoSat-2 data and the geoid model
EGM2008. The main purpose of DTU15 construction
is the correction of the artefacts found in DTU13MSS.
The quality of the new model will be discussed by
comparing the surface with UCL13, the MSS released
by the University College London.
In addition, a
study on the sea level variability has been carried
out with CryoSat-2 data, since the MSS height is af-
fected by this parameter. The analysis has been im-
plemented during a training period at ESRIN, and
it has been focused on satellite’s operational mode
performances in terms of time and in relation to the
distance to coast.

Introduction
During the last decades of the XX century the theory of climate change became
widely accepted, and the debate on global warming due to a rapid increase of
human activity brought towards a major sensitivity for the environment. With
a long-term global coverage, satellites play a fundamental role in the climate
change challenge, being a resource for the development of tools that allow to
understand and predict the Earth system variations [36]. The European Space
Agency is actively involved into Earth Observation since 1977, and at present it
is a principal contributor in the climate change monitoring with its Living Planet
Programme and the European programme Copernicus [12]. ESA’s Living Planet
programme comprises seven Earth Explorer missions which guarantee, with the
combination of Copernicus satellites, a continuous and robust data retrieval of
all Earth’s systems. One of the most successful missions of the Programme is
CryoSat-2 satellite, designed for monitoring land and marine ice. However, the
high potential of CryoSat’s primary onboard instrument SIRAL (SAR/Interfero-
metric Radar ALtimeter) grants investigations also on water, giving the chance
to develop products for several scientiﬁc applications over coastal, polar, and
open ocean [16]. The aim of this project is to contribute to the derivation of
a new Mean Sea Surface (DTU15MSS) for the Arctic, exploiting information
retrieved by CryoSat-2 on the Arctic Ocean. The MSS is deﬁned as the sur-
face that best approximates the geoid, and it is computed by averaging over
time sea level data obtained from satellite altimetry. The diﬀerence between the
geoid and the MSS model gives the Mean Dynamic Topography (MDT), that
describes the ocean currents and volume variations due to temperature. The
MSS is a reference for numerous geophysical applications, and in particular, in
the Arctic regions it is crucial for the derivation of sea ice thickness, fundamen-
tal parameter in the climate change observation. In 2013 DTU Space released
its last version of a global-coverage MSS, the DTU13MSS, which is based on a
20-year period of satellite data provided by diﬀerent missions. The comparison
of DTU model with UCL (University College London) MSS showed dubious fea-
tures at high latitudes that could belong to DTU13MSS. In Ch.2 these issues
will be presented and analysed. The new surface will be based on DTU13 and
CryoSat-2 data from 2010 to 2014, and it is built with the main objective of
correcting DTU13’s problematic aspects. The surface is computed with a 3-step
method, which involves the elimination of orbital and range errors of CryoSat
data and the integration of the geoid model for the complete coverage at lati-
4
==PAGE:7==
tudes 88o −90o. In Ch.3 the method of DTU15MSS derivation is illustrated.
In addition, during the period of my thesis, I had the chance to work as a trainee
at ESRIN. There, I studied the performance of CryoSat-2 in terms of annual sea
level variability. Sea level is a crucial indicator of climate change and global
warming, and according to [2] it increases of 3.6 mm per year. This parame-
ter can aﬀect the value of the MSS and it must be accounted in its derivation.
The challenge of measuring the sea level trend with CryoSat over certain zones
of the Earth stays in SIRAL’s observation modes, that change according to a
deﬁned geographical mask (in Ch.1 an overview of CryoSat-2 satellite and the
details about SIRAL modes are illustrated). The derivation of sea level time-
series over an area where diﬀerent modes occur implies that the modes have a
similar performance in time, so that continuity and consistency of the results
is maintained. At present, this aspect has not been investigated yet, especially
over the Polar regions, where the mask changes often, in relation to the ice ex-
tent. For this reason, my research in Frascati was focused in testing SIRAL’s
performances by comparing LRM and SAR mode’s timeseries over Bangladesh
coasts. In Ch.4 the sea level variability project is introduced and discussed. The
choice of the area is driven by the length of SAR and LRM timeseries: over the
Arctic the mask is updated every two weeks, that makes diﬃcult a monthly or
annual trend analysis. On Bangladesh instead, the mask has the duration of two
years in LRM mode and almost three years in SAR. This fact allows not only
an annual trend derivation on complete years, but also to a monthly compari-
son within the modes. Indeed, this study could be considered as a preliminary
work that could be applied, in future, on the Arctic. Moreover, with this study
I had also the opportunity of measuring the quality of CryoSat data over the
coast, which is an element of high concern in ocean altimetry. Finally, a com-
parison of CryoSat results with CCI timeseries will be carried out. CCI is the
ESA’s Climate Change Initiative Programme that encloses Earth Observation
archives of the Essential Climate Variables (ECVs) - fundamental parameters of
the Earth system - and are characterised by a continuous, long-term and global
coverage records merged from diﬀerent missions [8]. A similarity in CryoSat and
CCI behaviour would open the possibility to utilise CryoSat data in CCI ECV
timeseries, and therefore guarantee the continuity of a 20-year sea level history.
The entire project has been realised using MatLab, GMT (Generic Mapping
Tool) and AWK programs. For the derivation of DTU15MSS the DTU13 Suite
of Geophysical data is employed, whereas for the main processing in sea level
variability the CryoSat-2 L2 data have been obtained from the online processing
platform SARvatore (SAR Versatile Altimetric Toolkit for Ocean and Exploita-
tion), released by ESA. In the plots all the scales are in metres unless it is not
speciﬁed.

Chapter 1
CryoSat-2
The CryoSat-2 mission has been designed with the main purpose of monitoring
ice variations on a global scale. Such interest arises from the widespread concern
about global warming and its eﬀects on the Earth systems, in particular the
cryosphere, which is undoubtedly highly sensitive to temperature rise. CryoSat-
2 is a fundamental resource for a better understanding of cryosphere and the
related water masses behaviour, and it is a necessary contributor to a long
and uninterrupted time series of Earth observation datasets. The satellite was
launched on the 8th of April 2008 as a replacement of CryoSat, which was lost
during launch in 2005 [13]. The mission was supposed to last three years, but
surprisingly the data are still retrieved at present. With a low-Earth, non-Sun-
synchronous orbit and a mean altitude of 717km, CryoSat-2 fulﬁls the mission
objective of studying the global ice extent, reaching, for the ﬁrst time for a
remote-sensing satellite, the coverage of ±88o of latitude [14]. The novelty in the
ESA’s ice mission stays in the payload: CryoSat’s SAR/Interferometric Radar
Altimeter (SIRAL) is able to operate into three diﬀerent modes according to
a geographical mask that is deﬁned in relation to the type of surface observed
ﬁg.(1.1). In particular, the mask is updated every two weeks in line with the ice
extent changes over the polar regions. In the next section the SIRAL instrument
and its operational modes are illustrated.
1.1
SIRAL and mode masks
SIRAL is a unique radar altimeter that operates in Ku band. Its unprecedented
design allows the enhancement of the resolution and the opportunity of observing
steep and complex areas with accuracy. The instrument is characterised by a
high Pulse Repetition Frequency (PRF) which enables an along-track coherent
sampling, fundamental feature for obtaining higher resolution [9]. The antenna
subsystem consists of two Cassegrain antennas: one works as both transmitter
and receiver, while the other is only a receiver [15]. The two antennas form
an across-track interferometer, which will be employed over slopes and irregular
coastal areas. SIRAL operates in the following modes [15]:
6
==PAGE:9==
Figure 1.1: Global map of SIRAL modes
 Low-Resolution mode (LRM): LRM mode is analogous to a pulse-limited
altimeter and it is characterised by a low PRF of 1970 Hz. It is mainly
used over ocean and ﬂat areas.
 SAR mode: in this case the PRF measures 17.8 kHz. Such high frequency
allows the coherence between diﬀerent pulses. SAR mode, respect to LRM,
works with burst pulses, meaning that for each burst 64 pulses are trans-
mitted with frequency of 17.8 kHz. This process enhances the along-track
resolution because the same area is illuminated multiple times along the
ﬂight direction (multilook process). In other words, for one burst each of
the 64 pulses (or Doppler beams) illuminates the surface with stripes of
250m along-track width (Doppler cells) ﬁg.(1.2). For more bursts a given
Doppler cell is illuminated by several beams coming from diﬀerent bursts,
obtaining a higher amount of information from each cell ﬁg.(1.3).
 SARin mode: SARin mode exploits SAR interferometry to reach a higher
resolution also across-track, because by comparing the phase angle of both
the receivers it is possible to determine the arrival direction of the reﬂected
signal. For this reason SARin operates over complex or steep surfaces.
In DTU15MSS derivation SAR and SARin mode data are exploited, since
over the Arctic the presence of complex surfaces such as ice leads and coastal
areas requires higher resolution. Insead, in sea level variability analysis LRM
and SAR modes are utilised.

DTU13 and related problems
2.1
The DTU13MSS in the Arctic Ocean
The DTU13MSS is a global map of the mean sea surface height based on a
20-year dataset, and comprises altimetry data from 1993 to 2013. The model
is derived from the previous version DTU10MSS, which covers a period of 17
years, and its precursor DNSC08, released by the Danish National Space Center
in 2008. The surface has been built by merging datasets from diﬀerent satellite
missions.
Every mission is necessary for a temporal continuity of the mean
surface and likewise contributes to additional information in terms of latitude
coverage, as it can be seen in table (2.1). DTU13MSS is derived with a two
step procedure illustrated in [4] and the result is a map with a global coverage.
For the Arctic, the MSS considered goes from 60◦to 90◦, ﬁg.(2.1). It must be
noticed that for higher latitudes than 88◦there is no satellite coverage, therefore
EGM2008 geoid data from 88◦to 90◦are employed. In the previous version the
geoid comprises latitudes from 86◦to 90◦because of the lack of Cryosat data.

DTU13MSS is used as a reference surface in diverse altimetric applications,
such as in ocean, for the derivation of the Mean Dynamic Topography, or in
cryosphere, for the determination of the sea ice thickness, fundamental param-
eter in the climate change challenge. As a result of a combination of several
missions, with e.g. diﬀerent references, techniques, and time frames, the MSS
shows issues that must be analysed and possibly corrected. When a comparison
by subtraction was made with the MSS released by the University College Lon-
don (UCL) i.e. UCL13, few problems were individuated. UCL13 is a Mean Sea
Surface that integrates 2 years of CryoSat SAR and SARin data, from Septem-
ber 2011 until September 2013, to the previous UCL MSS version released in
2004 which comprises ERS-2 data (ca. 1995-2004) until 81.5o of latitude and a
geoid model plus Mean Dynamic Topography (MDT) model until 90o [A. Rid-
out, Personal Communication and technical notes]. However, the last version
of UCL contains data only until 88o. By subtracting the two surfaces with pre-
viously applying to both a resolution of 6-by-6 km - this passage is necessary
because the available versions of UCL13 and DTU13MSS had a diﬀerent origi-
nal resolution - a good match can be found for areas lower than 80o, especially
for longitudes ranging [−80o, 60o], while a diﬀerence of around 20 cm can be
found at higher latitudes ﬁg.(2.2). At constant latitudes of 82o and 86o the plot
shows circular artefacts that highlight a discontinuity in the DTU13MSS that
could correspond to ICESat laser altimetry data. Other dubious marks appear
from 86o until 88o; these features could be attributed to the presence of the
geoid in the DTU model. Such hypothesis comes from the fact that also when
DTU10MSS is compared with UCL13 the same situation occurs ﬁg.(2.3).
Another issue that can be observed is the presence of track stripes at high
latitudes, again matching with ICESat observations.
Moreover, on the Beaufort Sea an area with higher values appear: according to

the location, this feature could be attributed to the Beaufort Gyre (BG). The
BG is a wind-driven circular current that occurs in the north of Alaska and
it is responsible for the sea ice and freshwater accumulation in that part of the
Arctic ocean [27]. Since the 1990s the Beaufort Gyre has gained 8000±2000km3
of freshwater [29] and in particular more than 5000km3 were stored during the
period 2003-2012 [30]. An explanation why this phenomenon can be observed in
the diﬀerence plot could be that the Mean Sea Surfaces have a diﬀerent average
time: for DTU13 the middle point between 1993 and 2013 is 2003, while for
UCL13 is around 2004, therefore higher values due to a water gain are expected
for UCL13.
However, it must be taken into account that UCL13 has not a
continuous timeseries (there is a gap of ∼6 years between ERS-2 and CryoSat-2
observations), therefore it is actually diﬃcult to state whether this hypothesis
holds in this case. On the other hand, applying the reasoning to the comparison
between DTU13 and DTU10 (whose mean time is in 2001.5), in ﬁg.(2.4) it can
be found that higher values appear in the BG area probably due to the higher
average time of DTU13MSS. In the same plot it can be observed that diﬀerences
of 10-20 cm occur at latitudes higher that 82N, showing a clear problem with
ICESat data and the geoid integration.
The correctness and the continuity of the MSS is a fundamental ingredient
for altimetry applications because the presence of irregular values could highly
aﬀect climatic results. A clear example of the importance of this surface is shown
in ﬁg.(2.5) and ﬁg.(2.6): the same features discovered in DTU13MSS above 86o
appear also in the study of sea ice thickness, threatening the results of the
entire research. For this reason, a correct version of DTU13MSS, also called
DTU15MSS must be released. In the next chapter the method for DTU15MSS
derivation is illustrated.

The DTU15MSS
The aim of DTU15MSS derivation is to operate on two crucial aspects, that are
the correction of DTU13MSS’s high-latitude artefacts, and the introduction of
CryoSat-2 data, which contribute to a higher stability of the surface in terms of
time. DTU15MSS is based on DTU13, which already contains information from
Cryosat SAR mode. The novelty in the new surface is the integration of SARin
data, that appear as a ﬁxed mask along the Canadian islands and Greenland
shores ﬁg.(3.1), and constitute a fundamental element for a higher deﬁnition of
MSS over coastal areas.

3.1
The ﬁrst approach
The ﬁrst approach is based on a program written by L. Stenseng. The purpose
of the script is to correct the DTU13MSS with adding a residual grid derived
by the along-track mean of the sea level anomalies. In this project, the along-
track ﬁtting concept has been exploited, using the linear regression instead of
the mean. The next paragraphs describe the stages of DTU15 computation and
its improvements.
3.1.1
1st step
After merging 4 years of SAR and SARin point data with removing ocean and
lead oﬀsets, DTU13MSS is subtracted from CryoSat data: the surface is inter-
polated over each CryoSat track and removed from each data point, obtaining
the diﬀerence ∆, which is shown in ﬁg.(3.9). In ∆outliers are removed, limiting
the acceptance value to a range of ±1 m.

Chapter 4
Annual sea level variability
In this chapter the project realised at ESRIN is illustrated. The purpose of the
work is to map the annual variability of sea level along the Bangladesh basin
by employing CryoSat-2 products. The sea level, also called Sea Level Anomaly
(SLA), is deﬁned as the Sea Surface Height (SSH) - the measured height of the
surface with respect to the reference ellipsoid - minus the Mean Sea Surface.
This analysis is also an opportunity for the study of SIRAL’s performance above
coastal areas with diﬀerent operational modes. Moreover, diﬀerent waveform re-
trackers will be compared in terms of sea level timeseries.
The choice of the region is justiﬁed by the objective of comparing LRM and
SAR masks, indeed both the modes have covered the same area at diﬀerent
time frames: LRM from 2010 to October 2012 and SAR from October 2010
to present. The area of interest is a box ranging [88o, 91o] of longitude and
[20o, 24o] of latitude that can be seen in ﬁg.(4.1). In ﬁg.(4.2) the SAR mask
applied since October 2012 is shown.
Bangladesh is characterised by a complex landscape, mostly covered by water
and mangrove forest in the southern region. This wetland ecosystem is originated
by the presence of three river deltas (Ganges, Brahmaputra and Meghna), which
cover ∼80000 km2 of Bengal Basin and are known for their massive ﬂow rate
[3]. Moreover, this part of the country, which hosts fertile plains, is an area with
strong climate change susceptibility because of the frequent tropical cyclones,
tornadoes and monsoons [3]. Such climatic conditions and the rivers’ discharge
are responsible for surges and local sea level oscillations, which can highly aﬀect
sea level trends.
The behaviour of sea level through years will be described by diﬀerent
datasets. All the data are presented in Sec.(4.1). In Sec.(4.2) the methodol-
ogy employed to process the data is illustrated and the results will be discussed
in Sec.(4.3).

4.1
Data
With the purpose of studying the performance of diﬀerent retrackers, the data
employed for computing the annual sea level variability are extracted from dif-
ferent datasets: SARvatore, DTU, RADS and CCI. The following sections will
shortly describe the characteristics of such datasets.
4.1.1
SARvatore dataset
SARvatore is an online processing tool developed in ESA/ESRIN for the sci-
entiﬁc community that applies SAMOSA retracking model on CryoSat SAR
waveforms. SAMOSA is a physically-based model that estimates the epoch, the
amplitude, and the Signiﬁcant Wave Height (SWH) [11]. With this output, a
3-parameter least squares ﬁtting is implemented and information on SLA is sup-
plied by SARvatore. The Sea State Bias (SSB) correction is not provided with
the ﬁnal product, thus a possible approximation is found for this project. This
issue is discussed in the next section. SARvatore is the main dataset analysed
in this work, and it will be employed to study the SAR performance along the
coast and respect to other retrackers.
4.1.2
DTU dataset
DTU13 Suite dataset contains Cryosat-2 data processed with eight diﬀerent
retrackers from ESA level L1b products. It must be taken into account that
these retrackers are designed for complex waveforms such as ice leads and sur-
faces and are based on empirical models, therefore an indicative measurement
of SLA can be derived, but no other ocean parameter (e.g. SWH dependence
or SSB correction) estimation is reasonable [L. Stenseng, Personal Communi-
cation]. Moreover, from a study presented in [33], it can be seen that power
peak retrackers are dramatically inﬂuenced by the sea state condition: for waves
higher than 1m, higher ﬂuctuations are registered in SLA values, showing that
a physical retracker e.g. SAMOSA is a more stable solution for open ocean,
ﬁg.(4.3) and ﬁg.(4.4).

4.1.3
RADS dataset
The Radar Altimeter Database System (RADS) encloses altimetry data of 9

4.2
Method
The annual sea level variability can be derived from the expression of the Sea
Level Anomaly deﬁned in [37]:
hSLA = H−Robs−∆hdry−∆hwet−∆hiono−∆hSSB−htides−hatm−hMSS (4.1)
Where Robs is the range, H the height of the spacecraft, and ∆hdry, ∆hwet,
∆hiono, ∆hSSB, htides, hatm are the geophysical corrections that must be applied
to the range, and represent respectively the corrections for dry troposphere, wet
troposphere, ionosphere, Sea-State Bias, tide height, and dynamic atmosphere;
hMSS is the height of the Mean Sea Surface that can be identiﬁed as the ”time-
wise stable” part of sea height that must be removed in order to study the sea
variations, which are of the order of cm, and are invisible respect to the total
sea level. In this project hSLA refers to the Mean Sea Surface DTU13MSS, and
the corrections applied on all the datasets used are [18]:
 Dry and wet tropospheric corrections, derived from ECMWF models
 Dynamic atmospheric correction, MOG2D model
 Ionospheric correction, GIM model
 Ocean tide, long-period equilibrium ocean tide and ocean loading tide
corrections, FES2004 model
 Solid Earth tide, Cartwright model
 Polar tide, Historical pole location ﬁles
As it can be seen comparing eq.(4.1) with the list above, the SSB correction
is not applied from the beginning. The reason why this parameter is not directly
inserted into the data stays in the fact that there is not a unique and deﬁnite
model to estimate SSB. The Sea State Bias represents the state of the sea surface
and can be deﬁned as a function that depends on the Signiﬁcant Wave Height
SWH, which characterizes macro-roughness of the ocean, and on the wind speed
U10, which is the driver of micro-roughness of the sea surface. In the case here
analysed the function is expressed in one dimension, therefore SSB depends only
on SWH [10]:
SSB = f(SWH)
(4.2)
SSB can be also described as the function that ﬁts the relation between SLA
and SWH, thus the linear or quadratic regression line that approximates the
data cloud generated by SWH vs SLA. In ﬁg.(4.5) the SSB derived with a linear
and quadratic ﬁt is represented. For the ﬁt, the resolution of the data is chosen
at 1Hz, since higher variance of data occurred at a 20Hz resolution. Because of
a small slope in the linear ﬁt and a too rapid variation of the quadratic trend
for high SWH, both the solutions are discarded, and a SSB with a 4% slope is
chosen. Finally, this correction is applied by summing the SSB ﬁt to the SLA

Another issue that must be considered when analysing data along the coast
is the inﬂuence of land on the signal. When the altimeter footprint covers both
ocean and land, the waveform, which depends on the backscatter coeﬃcient (σ0),
changes according to the surface type and the ocean signal is contaminated by
land [37] ﬁg.(4.6).
In order to study how the coast aﬀects the signal, an algorithm that computes
the distance of the data from the coast has been built. This algorithm is already
implemented in SARvatore and it has been reproduced for the other datasets
with the aim of extending the study in two cases: coastal zone and open ocean.
The distance to coast has been computed by centering a circle of radius r = 10km
on an ocean data point (ocean and land characterizations are made according
to the ocean/land mask provided by the datasets). Within this radius all the
land points are detected and the distances from the ocean point are calculated.
The minimum distance between the ocean point and the land points represents
the distance to coast ﬁg.(4.7) [S. Dinardo, Personal Communication].
Such
procedure is then applied to every ocean point. It must be clariﬁed that the
land mask is not supplied in RADS and CCI dataset, therefore the algorithm
has been implemented by employing the mask provided by SARvatore.

Conclusion
The Mean Sea Surface and the sea level trend are two crucial elements in the
climate change challenge. The MSS is a fundamental element for several sea
and ice applications, while sea level variability is one of the indicators of global
warming.
In this project two main experiments have been carried out: the
derivation of a new MSS for the Arctic and the calculation of the sea level trend
over Bangladesh. The entire work has been based on information retrieved by
CryoSat-2, a satellite thought for the cryosphere but with high performances
also on ocean.
With the ﬁrst investigation DTU13MSS has been corrected over high latitudes,
and 4 years of CryoSat data, including SARin mask, have been integrated. Two
approaches have been used for the DTU15MSS derivation. Both the approaches
follow a 3-step method summarised as follows: a ﬁrst adjustment over long
wavelengths is implemented using orbital linear regression residuals; afterwards,
the short wavelengths are corrected by computing the linear regression residuals
over tracks in boxes 10o-by-2o, and ﬁnally the integration of a synthetic MSS for
latitudes 88o −90o is realised adding the geoid model EGM2008 with a linearly
interpolated MDT. The approaches diﬀer in the way of adding the residuals to
DTU13MSS. Over the Polar regions the grids in geographical coordinates are
not regularly spaced, because at high latitudes each cell decreases its size in
longitude, losing information. For this reason, in the ﬁrst approach the polar
stereographic projection is used to operate over regularly spaced grid. Indeed,
the residuals are, in both 1st and 2nd step, projected and interpolated over
stereographic grids and then added to DTU13, that is also set in stereographic
coordinates. However, an analysis on GMT projection performance shows not
completely satisfying results, and for this reason it has been tried with another
approach: since residuals are computed on points, also the sum with DTU13 oc-
curs point-wise, avoiding the projection stage. In the comparisons with UCL13
both the techniques show an improvement of DTU13MSS, that is corrected over
86o, but also an increase in the striping and the presence of an artefact at 82o.
Moreover, an oﬀset of 15 cm between the surfaces is registered.
A prelimi-
nary analysis regarding these two features has been realised by observing the
1-D proﬁles along longitudes and latitudes. No particular cause was found for
the problem at 82o. Along longitudes an irregular proﬁle has been found for
DTU15MSS respect to UCL13, but oscillations of higher amplitude has been
also observed in UCL13. The plots include DTU10MSS proﬁle, demonstrating
62
==PAGE:65==
a clear improvement of DTU15MSS.
After these considerations the following questions arise, introducing the topic for
further studies: since interpolation does not preserve data from degradation, is
the point-approach more performing than the surface approach? How to avoid
surface striping and the artefact at 82N? What is the cause of the 15 cm oﬀset
between DTU15MSS and UCL13?
In the second experience the annual sea level variability of Bangladesh has
been computed and the behaviour of diﬀerent retrackers has been compared.
The performance of CryoSat along the coast has been investigated evaluating
single SLA ocean values according to the distance to land. The distance of each
point from land has been computed with the algorithm implemented in SARva-
tore, and it has been extended to all the datasets. The sea level shows elevated
values and high variance within 5km from the coast, while a more uniform dis-
tribution can be observed at longer distances. For this reason the derivation of
sea level variability has been divided according to distance to coast. With this
approach, it was possible to learn that for coastal data higher ﬂuctuations occur
in monthly averaged values respect to open ocean data. The general monthly
and annual trend shows an increase in sea level. On the other hand, because
of a short time frame data - 2 years of LRM and 2 years of SAR - the result
obtained by the linear regression cannot be considered a deﬁnitive value. How-
ever, the slopes have been employed for comparing coastal and open ocean data,
20Hz and 1Hz resolution, and SSB-corrected and uncorrected values.
It has
been observed that the major diﬀerence occurs for 20Hz data between coastal
and open ocean values, while the 4% slope SSB correction contributes only with
few millimetres. The comparison amongst the retrackers has been carried out
by observing the monthly averaged timeseries of each dataset. Good agreement
appears between PLRM and SAMOSA, while DTU retrackers contain uncertain
values. LRM data at the same geolocation as SAR are available only from 2010
to October 2012, therefore values outside the SAR mask are selected for the
following years, in order to test the continuity of the timeseries after the mask
change. A similar behaviour can be observed between SAMOSA and LRM from
end 2012, even though diﬀerent slope values are measured. This is an important
result, because it allows a reasonable continuity between datasets and the pos-
sibility of prolonging CryoSat’s sea level timeseries. As a future work, the same
study could be implemented over the Polar regions, where the rapid change of
mask represents the main challenge. Finally, it has been observed that SAR data
have a good agreement with CCI values, showing the opportunity of combining
SAR and CCI for the continuity of sea level records. This concept is of crucial
importance, because it responds to the strong need of uninterrupted monitoring
of climatic parameters in order to understand their impact on diﬀerent scales
and prevent possible eﬀects on the eco-socio-economic aspects.
