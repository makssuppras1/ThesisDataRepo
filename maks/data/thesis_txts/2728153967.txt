Abstract
This report focuses on further development of a model, describing the load carrying
structure in a wind turbine blade.
A model has been constructed, that can handle the use of multiple materials as well as
non-uniform distributions of these, in order to calculate the distribution of material in the
load-carrying structure of a wind turbine. In this project, combinations of two materials is
explored. It is also investigated whether non-uniform distributions of material will contribute
to the optimization of the load carrying structure.
Further, it is intended to study the
combinations of diﬀerent materials, as well as determine the characteristics and feasibility
of bio-based materials. Especially bamboo displays promising properties, for construction
purposes, particularly because bamboo grows quickly, and the material is relatively cheap
to produce. Moreover, combining carbon ﬁber with bamboo, presents promising results.
A dependency of certain materials upon length, is also investigated, in order to determine
optimal material compositions for practical use. It is noted that the priorities of materials
varies very little with the length of the blade.
Lastly is investigated, if a non-uniform
distribution of material along the blade is advantageous. It is noted that the total percentage
of materials, is the primary inﬂuence upon the total mass of the load carrying structure.
The variation of materials can, however, be utilized in order to move material from the
tip of the blade, to the root, by placing stiﬀer material at the tip. Diﬀerent variations of
uniform material is shown, and the feasibility of non-uniform material distribution will be
determined.

Introduction
This project will investigate, and elaborate on the calculations, created by José Alberto
Navarro Martínez in 2019 [14], based on the model by Mikkelsen [15], for determining the load
carrying structure of a blade consisting of a single material. The model will be expanded, in
order for the simple beam model to handle multiple materials. The composition of materials
are considered to be uniform with unidirectional ﬁbers and with the same composition, over
the length and thickness of the blade. Hereafter the model is further elaborated in order to
handle a non uniform material-distribution along the length of the blade, which will enable
an investigation of varying properties.
Further, diﬀerent compositions of materials will be explored, with the intent of deter-
mining the optimum composition of materials for the load carrying structure, with respect
to environmental impact, cost and mass of the blade. The purpose of the project is to create
a plug-and-play tool for simple and quick optimizations to the load-carrying structure in a
wind turbine blade. This model has to take into account multiple aspects of production re-
garding mass, cost, and environmental impact. Further, as wind turbine blades may consist
of multiple materials, it is the goal to develop this model, so that more than one material
can be handled. Investigations of combinations of diﬀerent materials, such as bio-materials,
Carbon Fiber and Glass Fiber will be done. Along with these, investigations upon the de-
pendency of these conclusions, with variations in length of the blade, as well as non-uniform

Motivation
Historically, the selection of materials for production of wind turbines has been based
upon cost, availability, mechanical properties, and ease of manufacture. Lately, however,
it is becoming more and more apparent, that environmental considerations and sustainable
material-design, should play a much larger role, when considering a wind turbine life cycle.
It should be noted, that materials with more sustainable properties, or less problematic end
of life scenarios, normally have shortcomings regarding mechanical properties. Therefore
careful examination of the interaction between materials is important, so that the optimum
combinations of materials can be obtained, fulﬁlling both mechanical properties, as stiﬀness
and fatigue, and at the same time optimize production and end of life scenario. It should also
be noted that the eﬀects of end-of-life are not the focus of this project, as they are diﬃcult
to quantify, even if they are signiﬁcant, and should not be ignored.
This is speciﬁcally
relevant for glass ﬁber, as this is currently one of the primary components, and this is often
disposed as landﬁll, at end of life. Therefore only the energy-consumption, cost, mass, and
CO2-emissions are quantiﬁed and presented.
As new materials or options emerge, it is important to quickly determine the feasibility
of these in construction of new turbines. As these turbines consist of multiple materials, it is
important that the model can handle the use of multiple materials while still enabling quick
calculations.
Python has been chosen for the calculations, both because the original calculations, that
this project builds upon, has been made in Python, as well as the accessibility of Python
as an open source, free to use, tool. Also Python is less computationally heavy, than eg.
Matlab.

Materials
The choice of materials, for load carrying structures of wind turbine blades, is part of
the scope of this project. The following section will describe the materials chosen, for this
thesis, as well as the changes from the project by Martinez [14].
Metals are excluded from this work. According to Martinez [14] and Hau [9] metals
are too heavy, compared to mechanical properties, too costly, and also diﬃcult to combine
with ﬁber-materials, due to material-interactions. Also, metals have relatively poor fatigue-
strength and are prone to corrosion. Metals, such as steel or aluminium, have been used for
wind turbine blades, but are now conﬁned to the smaller rotor sizes. This is described by
Hau et. al. in [9]. The combination of ﬁber-enforced materials, used in the model, described
in the following section, considers the combined material to have the mixed ﬁbers uniformly
distributed. This is diﬃcult to achieve with metals. Additionally, as the load of the carrying
structure is primarily in the longitudinal direction, unidirectional ﬁber-materials are ideal
for the construction.
The necessary signiﬁcant characteristics, for construction materials, in order to specify
the load carrying structure of a wind turbine blade, are: The stiﬀness, described by Youngs
Modulus: E = σ
ε , the speciﬁc strength σ0, that the material can withstand for 107 cycles,
the density ρ, leading to the mass, when the volume is known, the cost of production of one
kg of material, the embodied energy spent in production, and the CO2-emissions in kg per
produced kg of material. The perfect material, for construction of a wind turbine blade,
would have a large strength pr kg, stiﬀand fatigue resistant, ideally also at a low cost. For
the purpose of this project, the materials should also have small emissions pr. produced
kg, also, production should not be energy intensive. The number of load cycles have been
obtained from Mikkelsen, [16].
As for the fatigue strength σ0, this is calculated for a structure of 107 cycles, same as
in Martinez [14]. As the radius, used in this work, is larger, in theory a smaller number of
cycles could have been used. This is due to the lower number of total rotations (cycles) for
a larger turbine with the same tip speed ratio. However this is chosen as a safety factor, and
for the ease of comparison between results.
The Considered materials in this project are:
• Glass Fiber
• Carbon Fiber (Commercial and aerospace-grade)
• Bamboo strips
• Plywood

Carbon ﬁber
For this project, two types of carbon ﬁber materials are used. Firstly, as described by
Martinez [14], commercial grade carbon ﬁber, and secondly AS4 carbon ﬁber. In general
Carbon Fiber has superior mechanical properties compared to Glass Fiber, with a lower ρ,
a larger E and a respectable ε0, though at a higher ﬁnancial cost. Thus Carbon Fiber is
primarily used for the load carrying part of the wind turbine.
For the commercial carbon ﬁber, a 60% volume UD layup with Epoxy-matrix, with
ε0 = 0.25%, is used.
Considering the data for AS4 Carbon ﬁber, the data for E = 170 GPa, σ0 = 1436 MPa
(for 107 cycles) and ρ = 1790 kg/m3, stem from not yet published work at DTU [10]. The
remainder of the material parameters are therefore not all strictly precise. For the CO2-
emission- and energy-consumption-characteristics, similar behaviour to commercial grade
carbon ﬁber, utilized in Martinez ([14],[1],[4], [18]) is assumed. As for cost of AS4 Carbon
ﬁber, the cost of a seemingly similar material from Performance Composites inc., is used,
250 $/LB or ≈550 $ pr kg. The characteristics of the priced material are slightly weaker,
meaning that the assumption of cost could be conservative, however large scale purchase,
could lead to the opposite assumption. As the characteristics for AS4 stem from as yet
unpublished work, the characteristics will not be explored or elaborated further. It should
be noted that AS4, is in any case superior to commercial carbon ﬁber, however signiﬁcantly
more costly. All parameters are presented in table 4.2. AS4 has been included, in order to
test the feasibility of Aerospace-grade carbon ﬁber, for use in modern wind turbine blades.

Blade optimization for a single material-blade
5.1
The simpliﬁed model
In this section, a short description of the simpliﬁed beam model, will be provided. This
model has been provided, from an earlier project by Martinez [14], which is based on the
work of Mikkelsen [15]. This model is predicated upon the load carrying structure of the
blade, being viewed as carried by four load bearing areas inside the blade, as seen in ﬁgure
5.1, in order to counteract the stresses in the ﬂapwise and edgewise direction. In the latter
sections, the modiﬁcations to the model will be described.
Simply put, the structure is
ﬁrstly optimized in order to fulﬁll fatigue criteria for ﬂapwise stress, secondly a stiﬀness
calculation is made, so that the maximum deﬂection of the blade tip, does not exceed the
maximum calculated deﬂection, which is deﬁned in later sections. Lastly, edgewise stresses
due to gravitational eﬀects are calculated, and material is added, so that the edgewise stress
criteria are met.
5.2
Simpliﬁed crossection and blade geometry
The non-dimentionalized geometry for the wind turbine-blade, is taken from the DTU
10 MW reference turbine [3]. As decribed by Martinez [14], the crossection in this model, is
deﬁned by the part of the structure that carries the normal stresses in the material of the
blade. The crossection is represented by four areas, as shown in ﬁgure 5.1. Herein, the load
bearing areas are also deﬁned. This enables the simpliﬁcation of optimizing upon only Aflap
and Aedge. The blade is calculated as a cantilever beam, fully ﬁxed at the hub.
5.3
Loads on the blade
The loads on the blade, arise from aerodynamic loads, in the ﬂapwise direction seen in
ﬁgure 5.2, and from gravitational loads, due to rotation, in the edgewise direction as seen in
ﬁgure 5.3. For this project, these are the loads considered.

The algorithm
The algorithm, simply put, takes into account the above calculations, ﬁrstly the ﬂapwise
area is optimized, so that the fatigue criterion is fulﬁlled. Thereafter, the tip-deﬂection is
calculated, using the ﬁnite diﬀerence method. Provided that this is lower than the limit

wmax, the Aedge is calculated, so that σ0 is satisﬁed. If the deﬂection criterion is not fulﬁlled,
ﬁrstly, mass is added to Aflap, until this criterion is satisﬁed, whereafter Aedge is calculated

• The steps of the algorithm
1 Aflap is optimized with respect to mass, using (5.18)
2 The blade deﬂection is calculated, using the ﬁnite diﬀerence method. as described in

3 If the tip-deﬂection is too large, material is added through optimization, so that the

known.

7.2.1
Manual correction when mixing the materials
It should be noted, that when mixing materials with dissimilar ε0, mixing smaller
amounts of the material with the smallest ε0 will result in a dramatic increase of mass
in the structure. There would be a few options for handling this issue. In this work, the
region with increased mass, compared to the initial material is ignored. The reason for this
is that, for practical purposes, extra material would not be added, so as to make a less than
optimal structure. Thus, in the ﬁgures, the ignored regions are represented by results from
material with the highest ε0. This means that the load carrying structure of blade would
consist of only the one material. The region is deﬁned, as the region where the mass is larger
than the singular material. It is noted that other characteristics may also be inferior to the

This could be mitigated by statements in the code, so that the carbon ﬁber is not introduced,
in the case, that the blade becomes heavier by doing so, in theory material could be added,
just in order for the entire structure not to fail. In practice this would not be advantageous
though, as failures in the carbon ﬁbers will create stress concentrations that will change the
properties of the material.

Comparison of the diﬀerent solutions
In this section, the diﬀerent transitions will be compared, in order to ﬁnd the combina-
tion(s) with respect to diﬀerent criteria, that present desirable solutions for the load carrying
structure of a wind turbine blade. There are diﬀerent focus points to consider, when design-
ing the load carrying structure of a wind turbine blade. First and foremost, cost is always
a primary concern, and will be one of the decisive factors for the design. Secondly, environ-
mental concerns, such as energy consumption and CO2-emissions, during the lifecycle of the
material can be assumed to make an even larger impact upon design, as larger energy-costs,
and potential CO2-taxes will impact the economy of production as well. Lastly mass is also
an important consideration, not only for minimizing material in the load carrying structure,
which again leads back to the cost. As the mass of the load carrying structure for the blade
decreases, the remainder of the structure, hub, Nacelle, and tower, can be down-scaled as
well, which would impact the economy of the remainder of the structure.

8.1
Uncertainties and limitations
8.1
Uncertainties and limitations
Firstly there are some uncertainties, that need to be addressed. In the model described,
the combinations of materials is calculated as a combined, homogeneous, material. This
can obviously not be the case for practical applications. There will be material interaction
eg. change of fatigue properties in transition-areas, that is not considered in this work. It
should also be noted, that this model, and the consideration of the material as a homoge-
neous structure, would beneﬁt combinations of materials with similar ε0 whereas this will
not always be the case for a sandwich construction. While it could, potentially be possible
to combine ﬁber-based materials in a homogeneous material, this is obviously not the case
for plywood, which could only be applicable as a part of a layered structure.
The costs of the revised bamboo, is rather uncertain, as it has not been possible to
obtain updated data for this, and the cost is assumed equal to the poplar-MOSO-design.
As both bamboo materials are currently only produced for experimental purposes, it will be
diﬃcult to obtain costs of the material when produced on an industrial scale.
Production methods also contribute with signiﬁcant uncertainty, as it has not, for this
project, been investigated what resin would eventually be used, in order to combine the
materials.
The cost of AS4-carbon-ﬁber is also not certain, however, as aerospace-grade is used,
the highest cost from Performance Composites inc. [18] has been chosen. For a design on a
wind turbine or multi wind turbine scale, costs of production could conceivably be negotiated
downward, however, even halving the cost, would not change the conclusions signiﬁcantly.
It should be noted, that there is signiﬁcant uncertainty related to emissions and energy
consumption, that are assumed equal to the commercial carbon ﬁber.
It should also be noted that this project only concerns the load carrying structure of the
blade, and thus not the ﬁgures for the entire blade are presented. Additionally, the model
itself is simpliﬁed, making for quicker calculations.
Lastly, the discussion of CO2-taxes relies on the danish Carbon-taxing-template. It is
however highly uncertain, if and how, taxes or trading schemes may be implemented on a
global scale. There are many unknowns in this implementation, as a global implementa-
tion of taxation, would conceivably be administered diﬀerently across the globe. A likely
solution would be a market for emission-quota, whereby these are traded between relevant
emitters. However, for this project, the level of the danish emission-taxes has been chosen
as a reference.

8.3
Discussion of designs
Depending upon tolerances and priorities, there are a few options for making a design
that ﬁts certain criteria. Once again a comparison with a pure glass ﬁber construction is
chosen as basis for comparisons. If the designer focuses exclusively upon environmental pa-
rameters and cost, the single material bamboo design would be the simple choice. In this
design, however the mass is ≈19 % larger than with a pure Glass Fiber design. Providing
that the designer could accept a 10 % increase in mass, a mix of Bamboo and Glass Fiber
could be considered, as this would provide better economic and environmental character-
istics. At 70 % bamboo 30 % Glass Fiber, the mass is 10.5% larger, whereas the cost is
reduced to 54.5 % which would be considered signiﬁcant, as well, the energy consumption
and CO2-emissions are reduced by 63.4 % and 55.8 % respectively. In the case that a larger
mass could be acceptable, pure bamboo appears to be the most suitable choice, as this
presents the desirable characteristics, except for mass.
If the mass is required to be below that of pure Glass ﬁber, and the cost should also be
considered, this excludes any combination of Glass Fiber. The cost would eliminate the AS4
combination as a viable option. As mentioned above, the cost at 20% AS4 would be a factor
of 10 higher than any of the other design options. Even if this design has signiﬁcant advan-
tages, considering both mass and environmental impacts, it is considered absolutely unlikely

that any designer would choose this option. For the AS4 option to be viable, the cost would

Based on the above, the following list of criteria is made and used as basis in the
following.
Criteria
1. Mass must be smaller than or equal to Glass Fiber Design
2. Cost must be smaller than or equal to Glass Fiber Design
3. CO2-emissions must be 50 % of the emissions from a Glass Fiber Design or less.
4. Energy consumption must be 50% of that for a Glass Fiber design or less.
This list of criteria would leave, only the combination of commercial carbon ﬁber and bam-
boo, as a viable option. For the entire transition, excluding the irrelevant region, from 10%
Carbon Fiber and upwards, criterion number 1 is fulﬁlled. For cost, criterion 2 a mixture
of 65% Carbon ﬁber would yield a cost of 84.505 k$, which means that criterion 2 is just
fulﬁlled. The same mixture would yield a CO2 emission of 82.54 tonnes, also fulﬁlling cri-
terion 3. For criterion 4, the above mixture will result in 1275497 MJ which is a 47.2 %
reduction. For criterion 4 to be fulﬁlled, a mixture of 58% Carbon ﬁber and 42% bamboo
is needed, resulting in an energy consumption of 1208980 MJ, which is a 50.0 % reduction.

to that of glass ﬁber, for the mixtures between 10% and 58% commercial carbon ﬁber, as
all of the results for Embodied energy, CO2-emissions, cost and mass perform better. Thus,
the evaluation that needs to be made, is whether the designer prioritizes mass, or cost and
environmental impact. - In this, it should once again be noted that reductions in mass,
in the load carrying structure of the blade, can lead to reductions in the remainder of the
construction, ie. hub, tower and nacelle. Also, the combination of carbon ﬁber and bamboo,
would signiﬁcantly reduce issues, concerning end of life scenarios.

Conclusion
In this report, it has been indicated, based upon the criteria of Mass, cost, energy
consumption and CO2-emissions, that bamboo has potential, as a material for construction
of the load carrying structure of smaller wind turbine blades. If combined with commercial
carbon ﬁber, the bamboo can contribute to an environmentally superior production of the
load carrying structure in a wind turbine. It has, in this work, been shown, based upon the
simpliﬁed model, that all parameters considered, the uniform combination of commercial
carbon ﬁber and bamboo, presents reasonable characteristics. The mixture can be varied, in
order to vary the diﬀerent characteristics, of mass, cost, and environmental considerations,
depending upon priorities for the design. As mentioned in section 4, CO2 sequestration is
not included or investigated, and thus, for environmental considerations, the feasibility of
bamboo-design may in reality be even better.
Unsurprisingly, it is advantageous to combine materials with similar max strain charac-
teristics, as the materials will tend to behave in the same manner, this is especially the case,
when the model, as is, considers a homogeneous material. It should be noted, as would be
expected, that small fractions of fatigue-controlled material, introduced in large quantities of
deﬂection controlled material will result in a signiﬁcant increase in mass. This results from
the maximum allowable strain of the fatigue-controlled material not being large enough.
In section 8.5 it is concluded, that bamboo, and bamboo mixtures are viable, especially
when considering smaller turbines. Though, for larger turbines, the beneﬁt of signiﬁcantly
larger swept area per mass, outweigh the considerations of materials cost for the rotor,
to some extent. Moreover, as Carbon taxes do not rise above 10 % of the total cost of
production, at the investigated levels, this would be expected to have very limited eﬀect
upon the design of future wind turbines.
Energy consumption has also been considered, this is deemed to be relatively insignif-
icant, as the energy payback time is in any case less than 2.5 production days.
When
considering energy consumption and CO2-emissions from production, it should be noted
that the eﬀects can be further mitigated by utilizing green-energy for production.
In section 8.4 it is seen, that varying the material distribution along the blade has very
little inﬂuence upon the total mass of the load carrying structure, compared to a structure,
with the same total volume fraction and uniform distribution. It is shown in section 8.4,
that a stiﬀer material-distribution close to the root, will result in a lighter structure than
when the distribution is reversed. It should be noted that a stiﬀer tip, will move material
towards the root.
As seen in section 8.5 the conclusions in this work, do not change when the length of
the blade is varied. Except for with very small blades, when a Glass ﬁber blade is slightly
cheaper pr kW than some mixtures of commercial carbon ﬁber and bamboo. This is seen in
ﬁgure 8.22.
Depending upon the parameters for the design of the turbine, diﬀerent materials are
advantageous. If mass is not an issue, and one can accept a heavy structure, bamboo is
superior in all other aspects. Thus a much less costly blade, can be made utilizing bamboo,
than any of the other tested materials.

Future Work
The model described, is useful, in order to gain a quick overview of diﬀerent materials.
However, in order to improve the accuracy of the mechanical results, one could implement
laminate calculation models, which would take into account, the interaction between mate-
rials. Thus one could get an even better prediction of materials behaviour albeit sacriﬁcing
swift calculation times. Moreover, mechanical testing of the actual mixtures, could conceiv-
ably vastly improve the accuracy of the data-input, for the calculations.
This project has not taken material decay, for real world applications into account.
Mechanical fatigue has been calculated, however impact from the surroundings have not
been considered, for the life cycle of a wind turbine.
Within this project, the model has been employed to calculate structures that meet
the speciﬁed criteria. For real life applications, though, safety factors must be applied, this
would conceivably add mass, material and expense to the structure.
Sandwich construction calculations could be introduced into the model, which would
yield more accuracy in the results while presumably raising the calculation time signiﬁcantly.
In this model, only linear variations are investigated, with limited gain, with respect
to overall mass of the structure.
Conceivably, a distribution that increases the stiﬀness
of the material, in the sections where the stress is the highest, could result in superior
mass-characteristics. As this is neither in the root, nor in the tip of the blade, the linear
characteristics-distribution will not be able to determine this.
This model has shown the potential for Bamboo as a viable material for production of
wind turbine-blades, however, should be validated by larger scale mechanical tests.
Arguments could be made, for examining Paulownia wood, as a future material of wind
turbines. This has not been done in this work.
Lastly, more exhaustive environmental impact analyses should be done, for the chosen
materials, eg. for use in other constructions at end of life[21].
