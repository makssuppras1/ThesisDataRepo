Abstract
This paper serves to investigate the potential for integration of wind power in mini-grids in rural set-
tings, particularly these in an African context.
The ﬁrst sections outline the scope of the research which was undertaken and what the aim of the
project was, with background given to support why such a project is necessary. Following this, an
overview of the case study from which all data was sourced is given.
The next section deals with data extraction, analysis and validation, and the methods and processes
involved in readying the data for use. This same section details the methodology used in the develop-
ment of the software which was used to ultimately investigate the potential of wind energy.
With the software deﬁned, the following section outlines the various simulations which were carried
out and the results of these, detailing if and how wind can be included in these settings. Finally,
conclusions are drawn and discussed before listing various options for taking this research further in
future iterations of these sorts of projects.

Introduction
The ﬁrst chapter serves to introduce the project, with a description of the motivation behind the
project and what the ultimate aim of the research is, via the problem statement.
1.1
Motivation
The purpose of the project is the investigation into the potential for and the beneﬁts (and possible draw-
backs) of inclusion of wind energy solutions in isolated mini-grids in rural areas. These rural areas, and
in the case of this study those in Africa, are often not connected to national-grid-level electricity supply,
usually due to the cost of extending the grid out to reach them, and when assessing these projects
against the cashﬂows returned from the low consumption generated by the connected end-users [1]. As
a result, a solution to bringing electricity supply to these communities is often achieved through the
rolling out of isolated mini-grids. These mini-grids are not connected to the main national grid, and the
associated power from these is both generated (by a variety of diﬀerent sources) and consumed on site.
In Africa, these grids are largely driven by photovoltaic solutions which are connected to battery
storage for the load during non-daylight hours. The investigation assesses whether and to what extent
wind energy can complement solar energy in these systems in a variety of ways. These potentially
could include energy supply in times of low irradiation and high wind, which could represent either
those inevitable hours of darkness or simply suboptimal solar conditions, such as on cloudy days.
Storage solutions constitute a signiﬁcant proportion of the capital cost of the mini-grid, not least
due to the fact that current battery technologies sometimes need replacing far more frequently than
other components. That being said, if wind can then provide enough of a supplement of power to
the system (and critically, at the right time of day), the price of the grid solution as a whole can be
reduced by reducing the storage size, and access to these grids and their beneﬁts could be enjoyed by
a greater number of people.
The case study for the basis of the project is that of a small mini-grid which is in place on a Kenyan
island named Ndeda, on Lake Victoria in the South-West of the country. This grid provided the ﬁrst
access to electricity for the approximately 4,000 residents on the island, and has recently been ﬁtted
with a small wind turbine to supplement the photovoltaic-battery system which had originally been
installed. It is with data from this wind turbine and the facility as a whole that analysis into the value
of wind power is performed.
The project is carried out at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), and is in conjunction
with DTU Elektro, DTU Wind Energy and the partnership between the United Nations Environment
Programme (UNEP) and DTU. The project also runs in parallel with a Bachelor project using the
same case study, and shares data and ﬁndings from this project.
1.2
Problem Statement
The problem consists of two major parts:
• The ﬁrst problem involves the development of tools which take in the solar and wind inputs,
demand proﬁle and various system parameters. Using these, the system then either evaluates
what a turbine should cost for it to be a viable addition to the system, or on the other hand,
with a given turbine, what the wind proﬁle should look like for the given system to be viable.
• The second task is the investigation into optimisation options for the system. With information
from the ﬁrst part of the toolbox, the analysis is as to how the physical parameters of the turbine,
such as cut-in speed or rotor diameter, can be optimised to improve the value of the wind power.

Background
A large portion of the world’s inhabitants continues to live without access to electricity. While more
and more electrical connections continue to be made, the fact is that around one billion people are still
without power as of 2017. Though this number is declining, much remains to be achieved if the UN’s
goal of universal access to electricity is to be realised by 2030 [2].
This access to electricity continues to be limited in Africa in particular, with sub-Saharan Africa
having the unenviable position of the world’s worst in this regard. It is home to around 13% of the
world’s population, whilst at the same time constitutes nearly half of the world’s population without
access to electricity [3]. An overview of the situation for the entire continent is given in Figure 1.
Figure 1: African Countries and their Respective Access Levels to Electricity [4]
A signiﬁcant contribution to these ﬁgures is the vast divide between urban and rural areas, which
see electriﬁcation rates of 71% and 22% respectively [5], with the population of the region which was
living in rural areas in 2017 at just over 60% [6]. A major driving force for the slow growth in rural
areas is the sheer distance involved in extending the national grids out to service these communities.
Considering the size of the communities and their relative isolation from urban centres, the economic
case for installing a transmission line out to supply electricity simply is not feasible.
2
==PAGE:9==
This has led to a recent push towards distributed energy technologies in isolated mini-grids, not at-
tached to the national grid and where power is generated and consumed on site, in order to attempt
to bring access to a broader population [5]. These mini-grid solutions are not without their own prob-
lems, which include limited policy and legislation, some reluctance to invest from the powers-that-be,
or simply aﬀordability of the power generated at these sites for the people who reside there [7]. Over-
all, however, in terms of access, these provide the most straightforward and the cheapest means of
electriﬁcation to these communities [8].
An increase in access to electricity for these rural communities is directly linked to an increase in
access to possibilities and facilities taken for granted by many of those who have had the privilege of
living their whole lives with access. These include improved levels of gender equality, increased facility
for education, better healthcare potential and general increases in prosperity, to name but a few.
Africa also has the advantage of vast sources of renewable energy, which include solar, wind, hy-
dro and even geothermal power in certain areas. Solar energy, of which there is multiple terawatts of
available resource, is dominant. Wind is more expensive than solar however, but in certain areas has
the potential to provide power at times of day that solar simply cannot [7]. With wind and solar in
tandem, such as is the focus of the case study investigated for this project, the hope is that costs for
these systems are brought down further.
The ultimate intention here is that this will lead to more aﬀordable access to electricity, allowing
for an acceleration in the provision of access to electrical power and as a result an acceleration in
empowerment of people.
2.1
What is a Mini-Grid?
Mini-grids, which are also often called Micro-grids (with these terms seeming to be interchangeable
in some cases, and not in others), have a variety of deﬁnitions, depending on the source. The United
States Department of Energy’s Microgrid Initiative deﬁnes a microgrid as "a group of interconnected
loads and distributed energy resources within clearly deﬁned electrical boundaries that acts as a single
controllable entity with respect to the grid. A microgrid can connect and disconnect from the grid to
enable it to operate in both grid-connected or island mode" [9]. This deﬁnition implies a connection
to a greater grid, which is not always the case, and for the purpose of the case being studied, deﬁnitely
is not.
The International Council on Large Electric Systems (CIGRÉ) deﬁnes these systems as "electricity dis-
tribution systems containing loads and distributed energy resources, (such as distributed generators,
storage devices, or controllable loads) that can be operated in a controlled, coordinated way either
while connected to the main power network or while islanded" [10].
While each of these deﬁnitions cover the bulk of what is meant by the term ‘mini-grid’, there is a
reasonably clear implication in most cases that these are connected to a main national grid. In the
African cases however, this is often not how these systems are constructed. These deﬁnitions also tend
to blur the line between mini- and micro-grids, this classiﬁcation often a function of installed capacity
but which varies from source to source.

2.2
Mini-Grids in an African Context
As previously mentioned, the huge distances involved in reaching some rural communities in Africa
drive up the costs of bringing access to electricity to these areas. If the goal of universal access (that is,
access to power for 100% of the population) has even a slight chance of being met in time, alternative
means of electrifying communities will be required.
Factors which make mini-grid solutions more attractive include both price relative to national grid
extension and time taken for construction and commissioning. Mini-grids are inherently smaller-scale
than national grid connections, and these can also be constructed in a modular manner and be installed
and providing power within weeks [11].
Each of these factors, amongst others, make these an attractive option for those who are involved
in providing access to power for these rural communities.
2.2.1
Current Status
According to the Africa Mini-Grid Developer Association (AMDA), the 140 million Africans (of 315
million) living in rural areas who will gain access to electricity supply by 2040 will do so by means of
between 100,000 and 200,000 new mini-grids.
Mini-grids are being installed in a variety of African countries, with Kenya and Tanzania seemingly at
the forefront, as these countries have more developed infrastructure and had included mini-grid options
in their national electriﬁcation plans at an early stage. Nigeria has also included mini-grids as part of
its own electriﬁcation plans, and AMDA has plans to branch out into a number of other countries in
the near future [8].
2.2.2
Trends
Knowing that grid extension is not an option in so many cases, it is clear that mini-grids are here
to stay. A number of sub-Saharan countries have begun to include these options in their plans for
electricity access for all. Nigeria has made clear its plan of universal access by 2040, which will require
around 10,000 mini-grids to be installed [8]. Rwanda too includes mini-grids in its Rural Electriﬁcation
Strategy of 2016 [12].
Ethiopia’s National Electriﬁcation Program 2.0 includes hybrid and renewable mini-grids in the plan
going forward, and has indicated a pilot program for 12 sites. In all, based on their criteria of being
2.5-25 km from grid connection points, 1160 sites have been identiﬁed as potential options for mini-grid
deployment [13].
Tanzania’s ﬁrst electricity supply was achieved via isolated mini-grids in various larger population
centres in colonial times, and these were ultimately connected together to form what became their na-
tional grid. As of 2016, Tanzania had 109 mini-grids in 21 regions, serving around 180,000 customers.
While there seems to be less of an outright number put on what the plan is for mini-grids in Tanzania,
these do at least feature in their plans, such as the Scaling Up Renewable Energy Plan (SREP), which
includes a sub-plan called Renewable Energy for Rural Electriﬁcation (RERE).
The motive behind RERE, which has World Bank backing, is to roll out mini- and micro-grids, as well
as solar energy access in rural areas. The Tanzanian National Electriﬁcation Programme Prospectus
indicates that the plan leading to 2022 is a further 5500 settlements being given grid-connections and
6000 being given oﬀ-grid connections [14].

2.3
Kenyan Context
Kenya can be seen as something of a leader in the East African region in terms of the rate of electriﬁ-
cation (both urban and rural) that has been achieved to date, with the national rate of electriﬁcation
increasing from 7.9% to 64.5% between the years 2000 and 2016.
An indicator which gives an idea of the progress made thus far (and of Kenya’s relative dominance in
East Africa) is the diﬀerence between urban and rural electriﬁcation rates. In Kenya, the percentage
of the urban population which has access to electricity is 78.1%, while that for rural areas is 59.7%.
While this remains a large gap, the ﬁgure for the rural population represents a remarkable change, as
this stood at around 4% in 2002. Kenya’s immediate neighbours paint a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent picture
in this regard too, with the diﬀerences summarised in Table 1.

Wind Energy in Kenya
Wind power has been exploited in Kenya since the 1800s, at which point the primary end-use was
pumping of water. Electricity generation from wind energy is still relatively new to Kenya, though
interest is steadily growing.
The wind resource in the country is signiﬁcant, with the Ministry of Energy stating that around
73% of the country experiences average wind speeds of more than 6 m/s at an altitude of 100m above
ground level [19]. Figure 3 shows average wind speed data from the Global Wind Atlas’ website, while

region, though there are areas scattered around the country and along the coast which have a reason-
able resource too.
A number of commercial wind farms have been constructed in recent years, with the biggest of these
being The Lake Turkana Wind Power project, which at 310 MW is the biggest wind farm in Africa
at present and produces around 20% of the required power for the country. A handful of other farms,
signiﬁcantly smaller than Lake Turkana, such as the 25.5 MW Ngong Hills Wind Farm are also com-

The Ndeda Mini-Grid System
This chapter outlines what has been done on Ndeda Island to date, as well as what options may be
open for the future.
3.1
The Ndeda Community
Ndeda is a small island on Lake Victoria, situated in Siaya County, in the South-Western part of
Kenya. The island is home to around 4000 people, the majority employed in a subsistence capacity
in one way or another in the local ﬁshing industry, though this number tends to vary from season to
season, with more or fewer ﬁshermen on the lake at diﬀerent times [24] [26]. The location of the island
is shown as an inset on the complete map of Kenya in Figure 5.
Figure 5: Kenya, with Ndeda’s location inset [25]
The people of the island had no previous access to electricity before the introduction of their current
means, which is realised through a mini-grid. This grid was installed by Renewvia Energy, a United
States based solar energy system installer with a Kenyan subsidiary. The grid allowed for a proportion
of people living on the island to phase out their use of disposable batteries, which they used in
conjunction with their burning of kerosene and other fuels as their primary sources of energy, and
to replace these with the cleaner alternative of electrical power.
3.2
The Ndeda Mini-Grid
The mini-grid which was originally installed by Renewvia was a solar-photovoltaic (PV) and battery
system with 9.75 kW of PV and 48 kWh of battery capacity. This capacity is ostensibly more than
enough to serve the small demand from the proportion of people on the island who are currently con-
nected to the grid.
As an addition to the solar and battery grid, a small 6.1 kW wind turbine was installed to sup-
plement the system in times of low sunlight. The photovoltaics and the wind turbine both serve the
load and charge the batteries when they can, and the power is distributed via an inverter to the local
residents who are connected to the service.

==PAGE:15==
The service is oﬀered on a subscription basis, which is handled through a mobile network where resi-
dents can recharge as and when they need. It is estimated that between 20% and 45% of households
have subscribed to the service at this stage, but as this grows, the system will see more strain and
ultimately it remains to be seen whether the current installed capacity remains enough [26].
The mini-grid is connected to data loggers which provide a real-time view of the status of the system,
with visibility of solar and wind production as well as demand and battery state of charge. Wind
production is not explicitly shown, as this was added later, though can easily be seen as there are
moments where energy is being transferred to the battery whilst solar production is zero. The three
primary measurements (energy from solar, energy from wind, and energy to grid) are measured by use
of shunt connections, from which the data presented in the following chapter was sourced. An overview

3.3
Potential Future Developments
With new access to electricity for the islanders, accompanied by the low subscription rate at present,
the primary development is likely an increase in number of subscribers. Demand is extremely low
at this stage, but with the ﬁshing industry ﬁrmly in place, and requiring trips to the mainland or
to Uganda for supplies such as ice, various productive uses, such as local production of ice, could be
feasible for driving up demand. At present, the village has a small diesel-powered mill for ﬂour, which
is a possible direction in terms of electrical upgrades to systems on the island.
Also, as more and more residents have access to the grid, and those who currently do not have access
begin to see the beneﬁts of being connected, the likelihood is high that more subscribers will be added
to the system. Ultimately, class and gender equality as well as overall quality of life could be improved
with increased access to healthcare and education made available through the provision of electricity.

4.2.4
Batteries
The battery system was not modelled in very great depth for this particular study. From the perspec-
tive of the energy-balance algorithm, any surplus energy which could be stored in the battery or any
energy which was needed from the battery could just be added to and subtracted from the energy in
the battery at any given timestep.
The inputs which deﬁne the battery are its nominal voltage and its Ampere-hour (Ah) capacity. The
battery bank on the island consists of ﬁve sets of four 12 V, 200 Ah batteries in series, each of these
combinations of four constituting 48 V and 200 Ah. These ﬁve sets are then connected in parallel,
providing an overall capacity of 48 V and 1000 Ah. In nominal terms, this means that the island’s
battery system has a full energy capacity of 48 kWh.
In terms of the energy-balance algorithm and the portions of this which deal with sizing of the mini-
grid, some scaling of the battery is also necessary. Scaling the battery in increments of 48 kWh was
thought to be too coarse to see any substantial changes between diﬀerent conﬁgurations of the grid.
This was due to the fact that more often than not, with the inputs similar or equal to those seen on
the island, one battery would suﬃce in every conﬁguration. It would thus be diﬃcult or impossible to
see improvements in terms of the reduction of the number of batteries, as the system is guaranteed to
require at least one battery no matter what, and there would be no opportunity to scale this capacity
down.
To combat this, ﬁner granularity was introduced by scaling the battery units in smaller Ampere-
hour capacities, and for the studies simulated in the following chapter, 100 Ah capacity units were
used, with the costs scaled accordingly. The total number of battery units found as an optimal solution
is then multiplied by the unit costs and unit Ampere-hour capacity to provide a total storage cost and
a total size.

4.3
Algorithm
The basis of the feasibility toolbox is a simple energy-balance algorithm, around which the bulk of
the analysis revolves. The principle behind the energy-balance algorithm is just that - the balance
is calculated based on what power can be supplied from the solar, wind and battery systems and
what power is needed by the consumer. If the balance is positive (i.e. more power is generated than
consumed), energy can potentially be used to charge the battery. If however the balance is negative,
energy is required from the battery, and this is discharged as needed (and as capable).
The battery is initialised at a certain State of Charge (SOC), and the equivalent available energy
from the battery is calculated, based on knowledge of the nominal battery voltage and capacity in
Ampere-hours. Then for each time step t, the balance of the inputs and outputs to and from the grid
is calculated, based on Equation 1.
Balance(t) = Solar(t) + Wind(t) −Load(t)
(1)
Again, this balance is given in kilowatt-hours (kWh), as the hourly resolution makes the kilowatt (kW)
input and the resulting kWh values equal, which allows for simply adding and subtracting from the
energy storage capacity of the battery. The balance is eﬀectively either what is left over once the
demand has been met, or what is missing and cannot be supplied by solar or wind and is required
from the battery. With this information, the logic which is applied is as follows:
• If the balance is positive, and:
– If the battery has suﬃcient available capacity for it:
∗Add the balance to the battery
– If the battery does not have quite enough available capacity for it:
∗Add what energy the battery can take, and curtail the remainder
∗Battery is fully charged
• If the balance is negative, and:
– If the battery can supply all of what is needed without reaching its minimum energy value:
∗Take what energy is required from the battery
– If the battery cannot supply what is required without reaching its minimum value:
∗Take what energy the battery can provide
∗Battery reaches its minimum SOC
∗System in current format is infeasible

4.3.1
Feasibility Conditions
The simple energy-balance algorithm ultimately determines whether a particular combination of pho-
tovoltaics, wind turbines and batteries is a feasible option for meeting the demand on the grid. As
mentioned previously, this feasibility is determined based on the state of charge (SOC) of the battery
unit(s). The system starts the simulation at a particular SOC, for the testing phase often set at 80%,
and the balance is applied, adding to and subtracting from the battery energy.
A minimum SOC is also speciﬁed, which for testing was usually 50%. As the time-series progresses
and the energy-balance is applied, the battery SOC changes, and if at any time the energy level falls
to the level which constitutes the minimum, the system is ﬂagged as infeasible and thus not a viable
option for meeting the particular demand proﬁle.
The system is then expanded separately in the three possible dimensions, representing solar, wind
and battery, until feasible options for expanding technologies on an individual basis are found, result-
ing in a search space. This is simple, but is an eﬀective means of determining options for the system.
The same criteria are used for the feasibility of all of the combinations within the search space. This
50% depth of discharge threshold is used throughout the analysis, and is the value most often found
in other studies [27][28].
4.3.2
Search Space
For the search space algorithm, the energy-balance is performed in a modular manner, taking in as an
argument which particular technology is being investigated for each run. Each run takes in a diﬀerent
set of inputs and as such results in a diﬀerently-sized space. For the ﬁrst run, wind is excluded and
the system begins with 1 ‘unit’ of solar power (corresponding to scalar-multiplying the solar power
time-series by 1) and one battery ‘unit’ (corresponding to a user-speciﬁed battery ampere-hour value,
in this case 100 Ah, multiplied by its voltage). The balance is evaluated against the feasibility con-
ditions, discussed in Subsection 4.3.1, and if the system is found to be infeasible, the process is rerun
with the number of solar ‘units’ increasing until the system is feasible with a certain amount of solar,
no wind input and a single battery.
This is then repeated for the wind power search space, excluding solar power input and increasing
the number of wind power inputs in tandem with a single battery until the system reaches a fea-
sible conﬁguration. Lastly, the search space algorithm is run a ﬁnal time with a single solar input,
a single wind input, and an increasing number of battery ‘units’, until the feasibility conditions are met.
With these three values known (the number of ‘units’ of each technology), the upper boundaries
of the search space are deﬁned and the program can move on to the ranking of the feasible options by
evaluation of each of the combinations contained in the search space.
4.3.3
Energy-Balance and Feasible Combinations
With the search space deﬁned, the next step is to use the energy-balance algorithm again in a slightly
modiﬁed format, and to use it to evaluate the feasibility of every combination contained in the space.
Instead of increasing the number of units of a particular technology and keeping the others static,
each combination is tested against the feasibility conditions. After having been simulated for a prede-
termined length of time, which is a year for the testing phase, whether or not the system is a feasible
combination of technologies is noted as a binary yes or no, which is stored along with the number
of units making up that combination. This information is later fed into the portion of the algorithm
dealing with visualisation, which is discussed in Subsection 4.3.4.

5.2
The Eﬀect of Variation in Cost of Wind Power
The second set of simulations involves varying the cost of wind power, rather than when wind power
is available to be harvested.
As noted, wind power at this scale (and for the particular case of the island) is extremely, and in
most cases prohibitively expensive. It is for this reason that in a normal case, including normal solar,
wind and demand conditions, the expectation is that the energy-balance will return a least-cost grid
conﬁguration which would exclude wind power entirely. The wind speed being so low on average, cou-
pled with the cut-in speed of the turbine used in the analysis as well as its price, make for a distinctly
negative business case for wind.
This analysis aims then to determine what a turbine would need to cost for the energy-balance algo-
rithm to take note and begin including it in the system. The method which was used was to ﬁrst run
a simulation to conﬁrm that the algorithm behaves as expected under normal conditions - complete
exclusion of wind power due both to cost and wind conditions. Next, with this previous analysis as
a baseline, the demand is doubled and the simulation run for a number of iterations, each with wind
costing marginally less than before, until wind ﬁnds a presence in the energy-mix. This same analysis
was to be run a number of times - with average proﬁles and projected proﬁles - to determine how
repeatable this is, and with the necessary cost of wind noted for each run.
5.2.1
Base Case - Normal Conditions and Normal Costs
The ﬁrst run was carried out in order to obtain some idea of the options presented for normal condi-
tions at the site of the mini-grid. This is to say that the simulation is run for standard levels of solar
and wind energy inputs, and demand unchanged from real values seen on the island at the time of
writing. With wind turbines as expensive as they are relative to solar and batteries, the likelihood is
high that the algorithm would favour these over wind, and result in a mini-grid which relies on solar
input and battery storage to serve the load.
When this simulation is run, the program outputs a solar and battery mini-grid, as expected, with
the least-cost option taking shape as a 6 kW solar and 19.2 kWh battery system, with a total cost of
$30,092. The options are visualised in Figure 38, with the best option highlighted (with thicker edges).

5.4
Levelised Cost of Energy
This section looks into some of the ﬁnancial aspects of these sorts of projects, and into a commonly used
means of comparing these, known as the Levelised Cost of Energy (LCoE). This measure is deﬁned by
the United States Energy Information Administration (which calls it the Levelised Cost of Electricity),
as that which "represents the average revenue per unit of electricity generated that would be required
to recover the costs of building and operating a generating plant during an assumed ﬁnancial life and
duty cycle" [30]. Simply put, it is the cost at which electricity produced from a particular project must
be sold, in order for the project to be an attractive option over the course of its lifetime.
While LCoE is often used to compare technologies and their competitiveness with regard to one
another, in this case it is used on a project by project basis, and seeks to give both an understanding
of how including wind in a project inﬂuences this measure, and also whether and to what extent the
LCoE for these simulations align with real and recent ﬁgures.
Sources vary appreciably on what information is included in the calculation for LCoE. Some sources
include taxes, whilst some do not, and others go as far as including environmental costs and external-
ities. For the purposes of this project, a very simple LCoE calculation is used, using a simple discount
rate, Capital Costs (CapEx), Operational Costs (OpEx) and energy produced over the lifetime. The
method of calculation is given in Equation 4.

In the above, t represents the time periods involved, n is the maximum number of time periods -
representing the project lifetime, TCt is the total costs in time period t, r is the discount rate and qt
is the energy produced in time period t. The energy seemingly being discounted in the denominator
is simply a consequence of algebraic rearrangement [31].
The values for LCoE which are reported in other studies and literature vary quite substantially, due
to vastly diﬀerent assumptions and inclusions of diﬀerent costs. For instance, the values (albeit for a
larger grid, without wind power) reported by another DTU study vary between 0.41 e/kWh and 0.22
e/kWh, averaging to around 0.37 $/kWh at the time of writing [32]. For comparison, the LCoE for
residential solar as reported by IRENA, varies between 0.22 $/kWh and 0.15 $/kWh, depending on
the country [33], while a further study for mini-grids states LCoE ﬁgures of 0.57 $/kWh [34]. With all
of this kept in mind, the calculations for the LCoE for the various simulations were made, such that
comparisons could be drawn.
Using the costs for the grid conﬁgurations as given in the preceding sections, and taking the op-
erational costs as 2.5% of the initial capital cost, with a discount rate of 10%, with each of these
assumptions directly taken from the associated bachelor project [26], the LCoE ﬁgures for the bulk of

end of the values as reported in the literature.
It should also be noted that even though these results are in the same order of magnitude as those
reported in literature, it should be kept in mind that the various sources may also have included dif-
ferent types of cost. It should also be acknowledged that the estimated energy yields are potentially
not quite as accurate as they could be, with losses neglected and no indication of just how much is
curtailed in the energy-balance algorithm.

6
Discussions and Conclusions
The research which was carried out for this project allows for a variety of conclusions to be drawn.
Beginning with the overview of the situation in sub-Saharan Africa with regard to access to electricity,
it is clear that much remains to be achieved. If levels of education, equality, healthcare and job creation
are to be boosted, one way of achieving this would be to ﬁnd some means of bringing electricity access
to more of those who are currently without it.
It is also clear that grid extension is often not the way of going about providing this access, con-
sidering the distances and economics involved, and that isolated mini-grids make an excellent case for
themselves in this regard. It is also clear, however, that there is work to be done in acceleration of the
rolling out of these mini-grids, and that legislative hurdles and the like are major obstacles which need
removing ﬁrst.
All that being said, for the mini-grid investigated in the Ndeda case-study, a multitude of major
ﬁndings can be discussed. Firstly, the conditions present on the island and in the local area tend to
provide a much more suitable platform for solar and battery mini-grids than for those in which wind
is part of the generation mix. The low wind conditions, accompanied by the prohibitively expensive
cost of wind at this scale, make for diﬃcult conditions for wind energy to make its presence felt.
With this in consideration, however, a primary ﬁnding is with respect to when wind is available
for energy to be harvested. The ﬁndings in Subsection 5.1 show that there is potential for wind, even
with the low wind speeds as seen in the area, to almost halve the overall capital cost if the wind is
present at the right time of day with respect to when solar energy can be harvested. This ﬁnding is
hugely signiﬁcant for those individuals and companies which are involved in designing, installing and
commissioning these grids. These entities would be able to make an educated decision on where best
to place these grids and on the technology mix of the grids, in order to drive down the cost of energy
as far as is possible, which is currently a major obstacle for the end-users and their ability to connect
to grids in these rural communities.
The ﬁndings outlined in the preceding paragraph place substantial emphasis on knowing a lot more
about the wind conditions at this scale. This in turn emphasises a real need for improvement in the
measurement campaigns and rules for wind energy at this scale, in order to be able to make these
educated decisions and to bring costs down.
The second set of simulations proved what was said from the beginning - wind at this scale is just
too expensive to be a viable inclusion - and that costs need to be driven down signiﬁcantly for it to
be a feasible option in the energy mix. It was found that a greater than 90% reduction in capital
costs would be necessary before wind ﬁnds its footing in these sorts of grids, at least for the average
conditions seen in the particular area under consideration. For conditions in which some variation is
seen, more accurately representing the conditions seen over a year at this site, the required reduction
in wind cost can be improved, but is still almost prohibitive without drastic measures being taken in
terms of rethinking the way wind technology is manufactured at this scale.
Lastly, it was seen that while wind yields and the proportion of the total yield which comes from
wind energy can be greatly improved by making fairly minor changes to the turbine in question, this
has little positive eﬀect on the cost of energy being brought down, which should be the ultimate aim
for these grids, considering that this is such a barrier to access for the people who need it most.
It is hoped that this project provides something of a precursor to small-scale wind for mini-grid
applications being investigated in greater detail, and that changes to manufacturing of these turbines
result in costs being brought down suﬃciently as to reduce the LCoE of these projects.

6.1
Potential Further Developments
With the project where it is at present, a handful of ideas for further developments and extensions
going forward were formulated, much of which could be achieved using the code which is presented in
Appendix A as a foundation.
Algorithm Speed
At this stage, the algorithm for ﬁnding the maximum cost of wind such that it is a feasible addition is
fairly slow, and could be improved by altering part of the method. The method at present incrementally
reduces the cost and runs the energy-balance for each of these costs, which is basically a linear-search
algorithm (potentially going through the entire list). What could be done instead is adapting this to
work in a similar way to a binary-search algorithm. In this way, the maximum and minimum costs
would be evaluated (top and bottom of the list), and the energy-balance run. The algorithm then
would ﬁnd a cost halfway between these and run the energy-balance for this cost and the cheapest
cost. This process would then continue until the both ends of the list are costs for which it is feasible
to include wind, eliminating half the list each time. This is signiﬁcantly more eﬃcient than a linear
search and could be fairly easily adapted to what is necessary for the project.
Wind Speed Data
One aspect of the wind speed data which was not looked into in any great depth was the eﬀect of hub
height of the turbine. Considering that the Homa Bay dataset has wind speeds at two heights, it could
be interesting to compare the results for a turbine using the wind speeds present at a greater height.
Energy Losses in the System
As mentioned in the text, losses in the system are neglected in the analysis at present. In order to
make this more accurate, these could be included in future iterations.
Graphical User Interface
A graphical user interface could be developed for the toolbox, such that the functions are somewhat
more user-friendly. At the moment, changes to the parameters have to be hard-coded, and the various
running modes of the system (ﬁnd max-cost of wind, simulate time shifts in wind proﬁle, etc.) need
to physically be disabled and enabled as needed.
Solar Data
As mentioned previously, the original aim was to have the toolbox work with a latitude input which
would then link to a database for solar conditions, in turn allowing for the use of a more representative
year for solar data.
