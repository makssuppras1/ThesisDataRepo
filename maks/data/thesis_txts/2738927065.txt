Abstract
This master thesis includes the analysis of part of the FloatLab campaign 2024, related to loads
calculation in a cylinder fully and partially submerged. The experimental work was conducted at
the DHI facilities at Hørsholm, during May and June 2024. This report starts by analysing several
experimental cases to understand the physics related to when a structure has a forced vertical motion
or is exposed to waves. Both effects are combined and tested to simulate representative real-life
cases. The second part of the report presents a numerical model and their estimations in comparison
with the previous experimental analysis. The accuracy in both trends and order of magnitude can
be seen in the validation chapter where experiments and model results are compared.

1
Introduction
This master thesis is focused on analysing how a fully or partially submerged structure reacts to
different conditions using a cylinder which is exposed to several sea conditions and external heave
motions.
Although it is considered a single element for the numerical model and experimental
study, it must be supposed to be part of a bigger structure. This cylinder represents one of the
lower elements that make up the floater of a floating wind turbine. This report contains the defi-
nition of a force model and the primary analysis of the related experimental data.
1.1
Motivation
Over the last decades, energy consumption has increased significantly worldwide. In parallel, prob-
lems such as environmental pollution and resource scarcity have had a major impact on the de-
velopment to meet this demand. This is why numerous initiatives based on the Paris Agreement
attempt to define limits and measures to be taken to reduce the human impact on the planet as
much as possible [3].
In 2021, 49% of CO2 emissions in Europe came from the energy sector, being the largest producer
of these gases [4]. Renewable technologies have an important role to play on this path towards
decarbonisation, which is reflected EU’s targets for 2030. Considering a 23% share of renewables
in the European Union by 2022, it is expected to reach 42.5% by 2030 thanks to the numerous
incentives and the technology development [5].
Despite the emergence of new alternatives, the
wind energy sector is of undeniable importance in the European renewable energy landscape. This
is reflected in the leadership of the European market, which accounts for 67% of the world market
share [6]. Although wind turbines are a mature technology, new lines of research are emerging to
get the most out of the wind.
Although most of the wind farms are onshore, new offshore installations are becoming increasingly
common. As can be seen in figure 1.1, the capacity of offshore-based electricity has increased by
almost 5% between 2012 and 2021. This is mainly due to the lower associated costs as well as
increased investment and economic incentives.
Figure 1.1: Electricity capacity trends, wind energy data [1]
In recent years, the large offshore wind resource has begun to be exploited, driving the development
of numerous configurations. Although the vast majority of installations are fixed-bottom turbines,
floating technology is becoming increasingly attractive to investors. Thanks to the possibility of
installation in the open sea far from the coast, this technology has greater social acceptance [2].
Also, it allows the winds to be harnessed to supply nearby coastal towns and villages. Despite

==PAGE:12==
its advantages, floating wind does not currently account for a significant percentage of offshore
installations. In 2023, only 270MW of the world’s offshore operational capacity was floating tur-
bines [2]. However, significant growth is expected for this technology in the following years, as
shown by the large number of projects in progress. In Europe, Norway and the UK are the main
drivers of them. While in the Norwegian coast is located the largest floating wind farm with an
installed capacity of 88MW, the UK aims to reach 100MW in a new installation in the Celtic Sea
[2]. In addition to them, the southern European countries are also investing in this technology.
One example is DemoSATH Floating Wind, a Spanish project driven by Saitec Offshore Technolo-
gies, RWE Renewables and the Kansai Electric Power Co. Inc. The full-scale prototype is based
on a 2MW wind turbine with a barge-type foundation floating close to the Basque coast in Spain [7].
One of the main areas where most research is being carried out is in floater design. Currently, there
are four floater typologies as can be seen in figure 1.2: barge, semi-submersible, spar and tension-leg
platform. Given that it is not a mature technology, the foundation decision is not standardised and
there are different circumstances where each floater type is used.
Figure 1.2: Main categories of floating offshore wind turbine foundations [2]
This master thesis research and experiments are based on the floater design. The main objective
is to have a better understanding of the physics around the experiments. Significant effects must
be identified and explained in order to comprehend how forces are distributed along the cylinder.
Different environments will be used in the experiments, trying to simulate open sea conditions where
the floater cylinder is completely submerged but also when it goes a bit out of water. Although
the usual floating turbine system is defined by six degrees of freedom, this thesis will be based on
analysing the vertical component of the forces (heave direction).
1.2
FloatLab
The content of this thesis is part of the FloatLab project. DTU, DHI, Siemens-Gamesa Renew-
able Energy, Stiesdal Offshore, Stromning and Ørsted are the companies which take part in the
consortium. Considering that Denmark is one of the leaders in offshore wind turbine production,
some investment in floating wind energy is essential to maintain this leadership. This project aims
to analyse numerical models for both turbines and foundations that can be used in the future [8].
Also, it is expected to contribute to increasing the knowledge about the physics related to these
elements during their lifetime. However, not only the theoretical part is tested in the project but
also some experimental campaigns are conducted. Thanks to the DHI participation, the wave basin
can be used as a laboratory during the experimental steps.
The FloatLab test campaign 2024 was conducted between the 24th of May and the 2nd of July
2024. It is divided into two parts: damping effects based on different heave plate geometries and
the behaviour of a fully and semi-submerged cylinder in open sea conditions. Everything related to
heave plates and their hydrodynamic performance is analysed in [9], while the load estimations for
the cylinder are explained in this thesis.

only flows should be defined but also the forces appearing in the cylinder. Also, more contributions
should be considered, such as the inclusion of an actuator to allow movement of the structure.

will allow to characterise the wave movement based on prefixed environmental conditions and to
estimate the forcing response. Also, the actuator motion will be included to observe how the flow
is affected.

Numerical modelling
In order to estimate the response of the cylinder, it is important to define a mathematical model
that helps to calculate all necessary variables in the system. In this chapter, the scenarios are
defined, in addition to including the mathematical approach that justifies them.
Finally, some
comments about the assumptions made and limitations observed are included.
3.1
Scenario ideation
Before starting any data analysis is important to define the conditions under which the model is
developed. Therefore, the behaviour of the structure in the event of forced movements and inter-
action with waves is studied.
Before making any decisions on the division according to conditions, it is important to divide the
campaign on a set-up basis. As already mentioned, one of the objectives of this work is to analyse
the behaviour of the prototype both submerged and partially submerged. Then, the experiments
are classified
• Submerged cylinder
During this first part of the campaign, the cylinder is below water during the whole testing
time. Also, the starting point for the experiments is with the specimen completely horizontal.
Then, different actuator signals or wave climates are included in the environment.
• Semi-submerged cylinder
The second part of the experiments has some differences from the previous one. In order
to analyse the cylinder’s behaviour while it goes in and out of water, these experiments
are conducted. In contrast to the horizontal experiments, only one end of the specimen is
continuously below water. The starting point is at 12° from the horizontal which allows to
start the motion with the front of the cylinder above the free surface.

3.3
Model limitations and assumptions
During the model development for the different scenarios, it is important to mention that some
assumptions and simplifications were made to achieve a simpler model.
For the first scenario, the motion comes purely from the actuator, which means that the movement
will be vertical. Considering that the cylinder is fully submerged, the water body will generate a
reaction to this movement opposing such displacement. Although the water will act in all directions
around the structure, the model will only consider the vertical component of that force, allowing
the calculation of the equilibrium only on the z-axis. In addition to this, the reactions obtained
for point O will also be vertical, neglecting all horizontal components. Although it can work theo-
retically, it is important to expect some amount during the measurements. However, it should be
checked that measurements in the z-axis, are significantly higher than in the rest.
For the wave analysis, similar criteria are applied. Considering that wave velocity will be always
perpendicular to the cylinder, both components must be calculated. Taking as a starting point
the horizontal cylinder, only the vertical component of the velocity will be non-zero. However,
with higher values of the inclination angle not only one component but the two of them should be
considered. To deal easily with all the force components, the whole problem is decomposed into
the two directions independently.

Cylinder set-up
The experimental campaign is based on a system consisting of a steel cylinder 2.8m long and 0.2m

some tests that in addition to waves, include vertical motion from an actuator. It means that
while the cylinder is fixed at one end, keeping constant the z-coordinate, the other end is moving.
Although this value will be constant for the fully submerged campaign, it will not be the same for
the partially submerged experiments. During the first half of the tests, the non-moving end is fixed
at 40cm below the free surface, measured from the axis of symmetry of the cylinder. However, for
the inclined cylinder, the fixed end should be raised a bit, going up to 16cm from the axis.
During the assembly process, the tank is slightly emptied to facilitate the positioning. The following
figure shows the cylinder once it is placed in the support structure. In the appendix F, the most
representative cylinder blueprints are included.
Figure 5.5: Cylinder mounting
Once the cylinder is in place, the water level rises to a depth of 3m again. However, in order to start
with the fully submerged experiments, the specimen must be pushed under the water. Once the
fixed end is located at the predefined draft, the cylinder is ready to start testing. It is important to
mention that during the signal generation process for the actuator, the initial z-coordinate for the
moving end should be known. For the tests where the actuator is working, the signal must move the
cylinder until it is horizontal and then start oscillating. This must be considered not only during
the motion tests but also for the zero runs and the other scenarios. For the inclined cylinder tests,
the draft values are recalculated and included in the signal generator, ensuring that the inclination
is the correct one.

Considering that both coordinate systems (accelerometers and load cells) are inverted for the ver-

of the actuator will be positive and, although the buoyancy has the opposite sign, the system is
dominated by the motion due to the resultant having the same sign. Once the cylinder moves
down, the acceleration becomes positive as well as the actuator reaction force. In this case, both
buoyancy and water forces would be negative, so it is reasonable to have a positive reaction force
that balances the system.

6.2.1
Actuator motion

partly above water. Larger forces are expected due to the force that the actuator has to exert to
submerge the cylinder.
Figure 6.18 shows the experimental signal from one of the low-frequency cases. As can be seen
in the spectra, only the actuator frequency is excited, imposing a regular shape in all the forces.
Considering the 16cm draft at one of the ends and the actuator amplitude, it should be remembered
that the buoyancy force changes depending on the actuator position. Then, instead of having a
constant force which will be subtracted once the zero-run is applied, the buoyancy has a maxi-
mum and a minimum. This variable force may have significant effects on the structure if it is not
considered properly. Despite the stability, this may affect the load distribution in the structure or
even excited important frequencies causing resonance [17]. Although in these cases the specimen is
not properly floating, this variable buoyancy force must be considered during the future design of
floaters.
Comparing with the fully submerged cases, the same order of magnitude can be seen for the actuator
force while there is an increase for the fixed end reaction.
However, the conditions are quite
different between the two cases. As was stated before, once the vertical motion is introduced in
the system while it is completely submerged, the hydrodynamic forces appear along the whole
structure. However, in these cases, only the cylinder fraction which is underwater is accelerating
the water body. In addition to the changes in buoyancy, some pressure differences appeared based
on the cylinder inclination. However, this difference from the horizontal does not look significant

The restoring force can be considered zero for the fully submerged cases due to the buoyancy

8
Final remarks
The main objectives were to analyse the behaviour of a cylinder under fully and partially
submerged conditions and to develop a mathematical model to predict the forces under sub-
merged conditions. Both assumptions have been studied throughout the research period, both
theoretically and experimentally. The main remarks after the completion of the analysis are
presented below.
8.1
Conclusions
The project may be divided into two fundamental parts: experimental analysis and numeri-
cal model. Both of them have been of great help in enabling a better understanding of the
phenomena occurring in the system. In addition, this allows the model to be scaled up to a
real prototype for the design and validation stages.
On one hand, the experiments regarding the fully submerged and the inclined cylinder have
been beneficial in understanding how the forces develop along the cylinder and the loads that
appear. Despite the appearance of certain effects common to both, there are specific aspects
associated with each configuration. An essential aspect in the development of any float must
be the definition of its natural frequency. As can be seen, throughout the experimental phase,
exorbitant growth in the forces due to this phenomenon can be observed. As the natural
frequency is extremely close to the second harmonic of the waves, the structure feels excessive
loads. In the case of regular waves, the peak values can be up to 200 times higher than the
standard values, which can lead to the collapse of the structure. Although this resonance
problem is more evident in the case of regular waves, it is also seen in the case of irregular
waves. Another effect that can also be detrimental in the long run is the occurrence of beat
frequencies in cases where there are both waves and forced vertical movement. Due to the
superposition of two similar frequencies, the load values give a behaviour in which the signal
throttling typical of this phenomenon can be seen. Like resonance, this effect can cause al-
ternating loads leading to fatigue of the structure. In addition to all this, by comparing cases
where the cylinder is static and the waves are passing, different trends can be seen. While
regular waves tend to concentrate the values more and increase the value of this central range,
irregular cases tend to have higher extreme values. This implies that for a given probability of
exceeding, the variation of extreme values will be more pronounced in the irregular cases than
in the others. It can be seen from the graphs that the irregular cases have higher extreme
values.
In the case of the partially submerged cylinder tests, some of these effects can be seen, but
with certain considerations. For example, the consequences of resonance are visible but, unlike
in the first tests, they appear at the end which is always underwater. This is consistent since
it is the end exposed to these conditions, but it shows the need to define the system in detail
to avoid structural consequences wherever it appears. In the case of the beating phenomenon,
it manifests itself in both reactions, although the most marked is the one underwater. On the
other hand, despite the general increase in the loads at the submerged end, there is no drop in
the actuator loads. Although the actuator is mostly in the air, numerous effects occur around
it. Not to be forgotten are the forces exerted by the actuator to force the cylinder in and
out of the water or the numerous impacts of the cylinder against the waves during the tests.
Particularly interesting is the case of the stochastic actuator signal, where the set-up directly
affects the frequency spectrum. Due to the buoyancy changes of the cylinder with respect to
the zero runs, the frequency amplification effect based on the acceleration of the cylinder is
cancelled. Thus, there is no difference between the spectrum of the position signal and the
force signal. This movement of the significant frequency range can be seen when comparing
the actuator-only case with the one that includes waves as well. This is why the influence of
63
==PAGE:74==
the waves is demonstrated.
The other important part is the prediction of all these effects by numerical modelling. Despite
the differences from one case to another, it can be said that the proposed model allows for
estimating the expected force trends and to a certain extent, the values. For the cases where
there is only forced vertical movement, the calculation of the fixed point reaction is correct
except for some extreme values. For the force on the actuator, the model gives lower estimates
than the actual. This is due to the simplicity of the model, which does not take into account
disturbances occurring in the inner layers of the fluid, which can create local force increases.
In the wave-only cases, the model estimates correctly except for the peaks associated with
the natural frequency excitation of the cylinder.
For the irregular cases, there is also a
significant reduction in the frequency spectrum. In addition, the force on the actuator is also
underestimated. This may be due to the disturbances that occur when the wave hits the front
cover of the cylinder, which may increase the local loads. Finally, in the cases where both
motions are combined, the model predicts reasonably well. Although resonance appears in the
cases of regular waves (in irregular waves it is not so evident), the model does not take it into
account and predicts both the trend and the observed order of magnitude ignoring this effect.
Furthermore, the beats are also clearly visible in both the actuator and fixed support. This is
why although the model is based on basic equations, it can be used to estimate the order of
magnitude of the loads in the cylinder. Also, some phenomena such as beat frequencies can
be seen in the results.
8.2
Future works
Although some interesting effects have been explained more in-depth thanks to this study, it
is clear that there is still much research necessary in this field. The following are some options
for further research in the project in order to increase the knowledge and understanding of
hydrodynamic phenomena.
– Redefinition of the project Although the study has provided many useful insights,
the consequences of resonance in the case of waves are still unfavourable. Analysing
the prototype and conducting a new campaign to observe the results without this effect
could help.
– Model refinement As was said before, the model is based on simple equations and
force equilibrium, but it is clear that there are several effects not considered.
– Inclusion of CFD analysis Considering that there are effects related to the water
layers below the free surface and the interaction between the waves and the cylinder, a
CFD simulation could be useful in terms of checking the real importance of these effects.
– Application to real prototype Although the model is designed for a single cylinder,
some re-modelling could be done to increase complexity and obtain better results. Instead
of just a cylinder, try to simulate a simple structure.
– Increase number of degrees of freedom Assuming that this initial model only con-
siders heave and sometimes surge direction, it would be a good idea to extend the model
to three dimensions in order to calculate turns as well.
