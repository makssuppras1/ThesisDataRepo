Abstract 
 
vii 
In this thesis we deal with the speed optimization problem in container shipping. It continues the work 
of Giovannini and Psaraftis (2018), by extending and improving the current optimization model. The 
existing model assumes that  the fleet consists of identical vessels which always satisfies the demand 
per port call. In addition, the demand was considered independent on the parameters of the problem. 
However, in this report we propose a tactical-level profit maximization model for liner shipping 
companies, in which various types of containerships are taken into consideration and the transported 
demand is estimated as an increasing function of fleet capacity. In addition, we assume a scenario 
where the demand may not be satisfied by the fleet capacity and we adjust the model respectively. If 
such a case, the operator is penalized. Although the proposed model  is similar to the existing one, 
considering different types of ships and the demand as a function of fleet capacity, we succeed to verify 
the previous conclusions and obtain more accurate results. 
The model comprises a fixed route which is served by a fleet which consists of various types of ships.  
In contrast with most studies, the objective function instead of minimizing the costs, aims to maximize 
the daily profit of the operator. The decision variables of the model are four, (i) the sailing speed of the 
vessels along the route, (ii) the service period, (iii) the fleet size and (iv) the fleet mixture. The model 
among other parameters, considers the inventory costs, the handling costs at ports and the fixed 
operating cost of the vessels. As the objective function and the constraints are non-linear, we apply 
Wang and Meng (2012) algorithm, in order to convert the model into a linear one. In addition, we 
estimate a linear demand function of fleet capacity in order to obtain a polynomial solvable model. The 
profit maximization model is applied to actual routes provided by global liner shipping companies. 
Last, the thesis deals with environmental considerations, such as the CO2 emissions produced by 
vessels. 
We conclude that when the revenue remains independent of the decision variables, the optimal 
solutions, always deploys only the smaller vessels. Furthermore, the fleet size depends on the vessel 
characteristics the route distance, while it is also confirmed that by using flexible frequencies, we can 
get better solution, which includes a mix of vessels.

Chapter 1 – Introduction 
 
16 
The fact that oceans cover most of the landmass in our planet, makes ocean shipping one of the most 
important modes of transport in international trade. In comparison with other modes of freight like 
truck, rail or air, ocean freight services have the advantage of providing a  cost-effective means of 
shipping of all kind of products, reducing in that way the operational costs of the companies. In addition 
to that, companies are able to transport heavy goods such as automobiles, industrial parts, machineries 
etc, something which is difficult and very expensive to do by using air services. Among the different 
modes of ocean going transportation, container shipping is by far the most important one in terms of 
value of the carried goods compared to bulk, tanker and general cargo shipping (UNCTAD, 2017). 
In the last decades, container shipping companies were aiming to deliver their goods faster and with 
reliability. Even the rise in crude oil prices and as consequence in bunker costs (UNCTAD, 2017) 
couldn’t affect that tend. However, due to the economic crisis, the worldwide demand of transport 
began shrinking in an unexpected way, while the supply continued to be on an increase. At the same 
time, the tendency of shipping companies of ordering large number of ships or ships with large capacity 
has led to an imbalance between supply and demand of transport capacity. As a result, freight rates 
declined which forced shipping companies to take action in order to compensate the operational costs. 
A strategy that is followed widely is to slow down the vessels compared to their maximum speed. 
Although the idea is not new, it is the first time that this practise applies to such a large part of the 
worldwide fleet. Therefore, the need of studying the reduce of sailing speed and its impacts to the liner 
companies’ profits becomes critical.  
This thesis deals with an Operational Research (OR) problem, known as “the ship speed optimization 
problem”. The OR is a scientific approach that deals with the application of advanced analytical 
methods in order to take decisions. OR professionals aim to provide rational bases for decision making 
by seeking to understand and structure complex situations and to use this understanding to predict 
system behaviour and improve system performance. It employs techniques from other mathematical 
sciences, such as mathematical modelling or statistical analysis, thus, converges to an optimal or near-
optimal solution of a complex problem. Operational Research is often concerned with determining the 
maximum (of profit, performance, or yield) or minimum (of loss, risk, or cost) of some real-world 
problems.  
In this thesis the problem deals with the speed optimization of vessels of different types, which sail 
along a route. The objective is to maximize the daily profit of the liner shipping company. This can be 
accomplished by optimizing, apart from the speed, the service period, the fleet size and the fleet

Chapter 2 – Literature Review 
 
18 
As the liner shipping has become a popular topic of academic research worldwide, a great number of 
papers has been published, focusing on different aspects in this area. This chapter reviews and presents 
some of them, reporting their main objectives and conclusions.  
To begin with, there are various studies that comprises not only with the sailing speed but also with 
the emissions that are produced along the journey by the vessels. One of the early studies was from 
Faber et al. (2010), who rose the question whether the surplus of the transport capacity can lead to a 
reduction of the Greenhouse Gas emissions or not. They estimated that by taking advantage of the 
oversupply and reduce the sailing speed, the emissions of bulkers and containerships can be dropped 
by 30%. We also note Lindstad et al. (2011) who investigated the effects of the speed reduction on the 
CO2 emissions. For this study, they were driven by the debate that the International Maritime 
Organization (IMO) expressed, regarding the measures for the greenhouse gas reduction. They 
concluded that CO2 emissions can be reduced, while the total shipping costs can either decrease, as 
well, or remain constant. Another paper was published by Gkonis and Psaraftis (2012), who focused 
on both the optimization of the operational vessel speed (both laden and ballast) of a tanker, considering 
parameters such as the fuel price and freight rate and the emissions estimation. Up to that period, most 
models used fixed speeds in order to estimate the emissions. However, they proposed a model which 
includes two steps. The first optimizes the sailing speed along a given route and the second step 
estimates the annual emissions based on the output of the previous one. The emission limits that were 
imposed in some Emission Control Areas (ECAs) drove Fagerholt et al. (2015) to study the possible 
impacts which were caused on the sailing paths and speeds. Thus, they developed an optimization 
model, which minimizes the  operational costs of the liner company. The model is applied for a 
predefined sequence of ports and decides about the sailing paths and speeds. They showed that these 
regulations influence the vessel speed, encouraging the slow streaming strategy (see section 3.2.1) 
inside the ECAs. In addition, in many cases, the operators choose the longer distances so as to avoid 
the ECAs and the speed reduction. If such a case, they report that this might give an increase of the 
total CO2 emissions.  
Other relevant work is the assessment of Chang and Wang (2014), which is conducted on the available 
operating  strategies. The aim is identify an approach where the decrease of the speed can result both 
to the minimization of the costs and the reduction of the environmental impacts. Thus they developed 
a model, which identifies the optimum speed reduction strategy capable of minimizing costs and 
mitigate CO2 emissions. A wide range of factors were included in the model, such as the revenue loss,

The following papers comprise mainly with the sailing speed optimization. First we mention a survey 
from Psaraftis and Kontovas (2012), who presented a taxonomy of speed optimization models in 
maritime transportation. Among other things, they studied models where the speed is either an input 
or decision variables, or how the fuel consumption function is defined. Fagerholt and Ronen (2013), 
who described the basic bulk ship routing and scheduling problem with a sailing speed optimization 
extension. They proved that speed optimization provides a better solution, compared to the 
corresponding approach without an optimized speed. They also reported that the profit contribution 
depends on multiple factors, such as the fuel prices and the relation between fleet capacity and the sizes 
of the available cargoes. Hvattum et al. (2012) analysed and applied in actual shipping routes, an exact 
algorithm, regarding vessel speed maximization problem. The model included among others a given 
set of port calls by a ship with a corresponding time window and the fuel cost as a convex function of

3.2 The importance of speed  
A ship is constructed by having a specific minimum and maximum cruising speed, which defines the 
range of speeds, which it can actually sail. The sailing speed has been important for many economic 
and environmental reasons. It is a key determinant of fuel cost, thus of the operating costs and it is 
directly connected with the environmental dimension of shipping. During the stage of route planning, 
the sequence approach is very common. More particularly, a ship sails using a given speed which is 
assumed beforehand and later during the execution of the routes, the sailing speed is optimized based 
on many factors like the weather, the time windows that must be always respected etc. 
Regarding the Green House Gases (GHG), the 2009 GHG study by the International Maritime

of ship speed on GHG emissions. It can be observed, that containerships are the top CO2 emitters in 
the world fleet. This is perhaps something to be expected, given the relatively high speeds of these 
vessels (20–26 knots) as opposed to those carrying bulk cargoes (13–15 knots) and given the nonlinear 
relationship between speed and fuel consumption and thus emissions.

3.2.1 Slow steaming  
Quoting the captain of MARIE MÆRSK John Moeller Jensen: “The biggest issue I have had the last 
eight years is fuel savings and it is interesting to see how much one can gain by running the vessel as 
optimally as possible and by taking advantage of the wind, the currents and planning your route 
accordingly”. (Containerisation, 2017) 
Compared with the 1990’s, the average price of heavy fuel oil increased until the period of 2007/2008 
by more than 800%. Slow steaming refers to the practice of operating transoceanic cargo ships , at 
significant less than their maximum speed.  Shipping lines first introduced slow steaming in 2007 in 
order to reduce fuel consumption, given the prohibitive cost of bunkers at that time. What’s more, the 
last years, in an attempt to overcome supply and demand imbalance and low freight rate levels, carriers 
imposed several rounds of general rate increases all of which were unsuccessful and therefore, despite 
today’s low fuel prices, slow steaming has remained the practice used by carriers to absorb excess 
tonnage – increasing voyage times, reducing ship call frequency at a given port and optimizing the 
==PAGE:26==
Speed optimisation problems in container shipping 
 
Chapter 3 – Problem Description 
 
26 
operations of larger vessels by increasing their occupancy rate. Slow steaming is estimated to have 
absorbed some 2.5 million TEUs of nominal capacity since the end of 2008 (Kalgora and Christian, 
2016) 
Hence, it is clear that slow steaming benefits shipping companies by reducing the consumption of 
petroleum products in the combustion of the main engine. These products are not only the fuels but 
also the lubricating oils, which are used in large two-stroke engines. On the other side, there are 
drawbacks in operating vessels at lower speed, such as the extension of the traveling time. Longer trips 
affect shipping companies and their customers as the capital costs increase, making faster operated 
ships more attractive. Another side effect of speed reduction is that in the short run, freight rates will 
go up once the overall transport supply shrinks because of slower speeds. Reducing speed may help a 
depressed market, but it is the shippers who will suffer and in fact they will do so in two ways: they 
will pay more, and receive their cargo later (Psaraftis and Kontovas, 2013).

3.2.3 Inventory Costs 
Cargo inventory costs can be a major factor, mainly in the liner business which involves trades of 
higher valued goods than bulk trades, affecting shipping service decisions in the economic order 
quantity model in logistics literature. Inventory costs represent the loss of opportunity cost or of the 
value cost, because cargoes cannot be used or sold in the shipping process, and these costs are 
positively correlated with cargo volume, value of cargo, and length of storage time. It is related to the 
container shipping process and involve both waiting time cost and shipping time cost.  The waiting 
time cost is the cost related to the sailing frequency due to schedule delays, either from waiting in the 
loading port or at the place of production or origin. These costs depend on transit time and hence on 
speed, a lower speed entailing higher such costs. 
3.3 Contract Arrangements and Shipping Costs  
In the maritime industry there are three main key players. The shipowners who comes in the market 
with their available ships. The shippers or charterers are those who want to transport a volume of cargo 
from on origin port to a destination. Charterers could also be companies, who need extra ships to satisfy 
the requested demand.  Brokers, are those who put the deal between shippers and charterers. According 
to Stopford (2009)  there are four types of contracts that are commonly used. These include of the 
voyage charter, the contract of affreightment, the time charter and the bare board charter. Let us briefly  
explain their principles and differences.  
The voyage starter describes the transportation of commodities from port to port for a fixed price per 
ton, which is included in the contract between the shipowner and the carrier. The cargo should be 
transported within the contract terms, otherwise there is a claim between the charterer and the owner, 
depending on the divergence from the agreed schedule. Regarding the contract of affreightment, the 
main difference with the voyage starter, is that the carrier is the one who sets the price per ton for the 
transported cargo. This kind of contracts are preferred for dry bulk cargo. The third type of contract is 
the time charters. The shippers hire vessels for a specific period of time and they are responsible for 
the journey’s delaits, such as the route, serving ports, vessel speed etcetera. In the bare boat charter, 
the charterer hires the vessel for a specific period of time and they are in charge of it.  
Shipping costs can be divided into four main categories: capital costs, operating costs, fuel costs and 
port charges. Every contract appears a different distribution of costs between the shippers and the 
charterers. Table 3.4 presents those differences between the contracts in terms of the costs.

3.4 Types of shipping services 
The operation of commercial ships can be categorized into three modes, liner, tramp and industrial. 
The liner operating mode by its definition is the provided services by a steamship company or shipping 
line, under which cargo vessels operate according to a fixed schedule and publicly advertised freight 
rates (source: www.globalnegotiator.com). There are approximately 400 liner services in operation 
today, most providing weekly departures from all the ports that each service calls. Liner vessels, 
primarily in the form of containerships and roll-on/roll-off ships, carry about 60 percent of the goods 
by value moved internationally by sea each year. In contrast, a vessel engaged in the tramp trade is one 
which doesn’t follow a fixed schedule or published ports call and. Therefore, a tramp service is 
available at short notice (or fixture) to load any cargo from any port to any port. Due to the radical 
development of liner services and more specifically the containerisation since the 1960s, the tramp 
services have decreased. However, as shipping business has not lost its interest in tramp operations, 
cases of liner companies who invest into tramp ships to create a buffer when the market is down, are 
very common. Last but not least, industrial operators usually both own the cargoes shipped and control 
the vessels used to ship them. These vessels may  be either their own or on a time charter. Such 
operations are used in high volume of liquid and dry bulk trades of vertical integrated companies 
(Lawrence, 1972)

More particularly, about the liner services which this thesis deals with, containers are generally

port origin-to-destination pairs, meaning that both the origin and the destination of each pair are sea 
ports. 
3.5 Vessel Characteristics and types 
Vessels come in a variety of types, which are described as it follows.  
A Container ship is the most common mode of transport, used for transport all of their load in truck-
size intermodal containers, in a technique called containerization.  Container ship capacity is measured 
in twenty-foot equivalent units (TEU). Today, about 90% of non-bulk cargo worldwide is transported 
by container (UNCTAD,2017), and the largest modern container ships can carry over 21,000 TEU. 
The average design ship speed for containerships is between 16 – 25  knots.  
A Bulk carrier or bulker is a merchant ship designed to transport unpackaged bulk commodities like 
wheat, sulphur, iron ore, coal etc. Today, bulkers make up 15% - 17% of the world's merchant fleets 
and range in size from single-hold mini-bulkers to mammoth ore ships able to carry 400,000 tons of 
deadweight.  The average design ship speed for bulkers of Handy size and above is between 13.5 – 15 
knots. As bulk cargo can be dense, corrosive or abrasive, safety problems such as cargo shifting, 
spontaneous combustion or cargo saturation can threaten the ship. 
A Tanker is designed to carry liquids in bulk. Its design is similar to a bulker with the exception that 
the cargo holds have been replaced with tanks The larger ones carry crude oil while the smaller ones 
usually carry oil products, chemicals, and other liquids. The tanker segment represents about 30% of

served by the same lines, as if only one port invests in container-handling equipment while other ports

are not achieved. Another example is the ports in western Africa, namely, Benin, Nigeria. While

3.7 Environmental impacts 
There is no doubt that both human and environmental costs of shipping are crucial. Low-grade marine 
fuels contain 3,500 times more sulphur than road diesel, while large vessels pollute the air in hub ports 
in such a great scale. For example, pollution by shipping is accounting for one-third to half of airborne 
pollutants in Hong-Kong (Wan et al., 2013). Maritime transportation is by far the biggest transport 
polluter globally. We can only understand this if we consider that the word’s fleet (approximately 
90,000 vessels) burns 370 million tons of fuel per year, which results on 20 million tons of Sulphur 
Oxides emissions (SOx). At the same time, the 760 million cars in the world are emitting approximately 
79000 tons of SOx. One large ship alone can emit more than 5,000 tons of SOx per year. Hence, we 
may say that the maritime shipping operations contribute significant amounts of greenhouse gases 
(GHG) associated with the global  warming. For this reason, the International Maritime Organizations 
(IMO) has developed measures in both technical and operational level in order to regulate the CO2 
emissions. In addition, nowadays vessels are designed in such a way, being more environmental 
friendly, equipped with more energy efficiency engines. Another strategy that is applied in a great scale 
is the slow steaming. As it was mentioned in previous sections, shipping operators reduce vessel’s 
speed in order to drop the fuel consumption and as the conscience the fuel costs.  
At the same time, the maritime industry is on high pressure. The expected profits from the larger ships

3.7.1 Evaluation methods 
According to Psaraftis and Kontovas (2009), there are two main approaches on calculating the 
CO2emissions, which are produced by vessels. The two methods, which can give considerable different 
results, are described below.  
Bottom-up-approach 
The bottom-up-approach uses the fuel sales data in order to calculate the total emissions. First it models 
the fleet activity by using data such as travelled kilometres per year or day and then it multiplies these 
values with the respective emission factors. However, these emission factors may be difficult to be 
estimated, as they depend on many parameters, which appear uncertainties. For instance, the emission 
factor may depend on the fuel consumption which is function of sailing speed, the payload or other 
parameters, such as the weather condition. Another struggle it may be that vessels have different 
characteristics (i.e. sail with different speeds, different fuel consumption etc), which make it difficult 
to calculate the fuel consumption for the whole fleet. In the literature, there is an extent study on how 
this method can be simplified in order to calculate the CO2 emissions.  
Top-down-approach 
According to this method, the CO2 emissions can be computed by using simulation models calibrated 
on the ships activity. In particular, through the  fuel sales data , the amount of fuel that was sold is 
obtained and be multiplied by the corresponding CO2 emission factor in order to compute the produced 
emissions. The data are furnished the Energy Information Administration (EIA), the International 
Energy Agency (IEA) or the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). 
According to IMO (2014), the Bottom-up approach can provide better estimation of the emissions 
for Greenhous Gases (GHS). Therefore, the results of such an approach is recommended to be 
used as benchmarks. On the other hand, the top-down could be the most reliable method, however 
the required data are not always trustworthy (Psaraftis and Kontovas, 2009). 
For this report, all the data regarding the calculation of the CO2 emissions are refered to the bottom-
up approach.

4.1 Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP) 
A wide variety of practical problems can be formulated and solved using tools provided by operations 
research, such as the linear programming (LP) techniques. In particular, it is an optimization technique 
for a system of linear constraints  and a linear objective function. An objective function defines the 
quantity to be optimized, and the goal of linear programming is to find the values of the variables that 
maximize or minimize the objective function. A constraint is an inequality that defines how the values 
of the variables in a problem are limited. In order for linear programming techniques to work, all 
constraints should be linear inequalities. An integer programming problem is a mathematical 
optimization or feasibility program in which some or all of the variables are restricted to be integers. 
In many settings the term refers to mixed integer linear programming (MILP) in which the objective 
function and the constraints (other than the integer constraints) are linear 
In addition to that,  a subset of the integer programming problems are the binary ones, where each 
variable can only take on the value of 0 or 1. This may represent the selection or rejection of an option, 
the turning of or off of switches, a yes/no answer, or many other situations. 
4.2 Model formulation  
In this thesis we deal with the speed optimization problem in container shipping. It is an extension of 
Giovannini and Psaraftis (2018), where we extend and improve the current mathematical model. Thus, 
we propose a tactical-level profit maximization model for liner shipping companies, in which various 
types of containerships are taken into consideration and the transported demand is an increasing 
function of fleet capacity. In addition, we consider a case where the fleet capacity cannot satisfy the 
requested transport demand. If such a case, the liner shipping company is penalized respectively.  
The objective of the model is the maximization of the operator’s average daily profit of a fixed route. 
More particularly, a ship sails from A to B, or even on a multiple leg route in which the sequence of 
port visits is already determined but ship payload varies along the route. It is assumed that the route

4.2.1 List of assumptions  
As there are many challenges that must be faced in optimization theory and practice, a number of 
assumptions were made. Driven by the fact, that maritime industry tends to take action in order to  
reduce monopoly  and concentrated market power by establishing regulations and antitrust policies, it 
is assumed that our line has neither the monopoly nor the oligopoly of the market. Therefore, as ports 
are served by many shipping lines, freight rates and demand per call port can be considered as 
exogenous inputs,  which means that both are predefined for each port pair (𝑥, 𝑧), where by all means, 
cargoes are delivered from port x to z.  Thus, it is assumed that our shipping operator has no power on 
affecting both of their  values.  
As far as the freight rates are concerned, they are determined by the way supply capacity management 
is being handled or other developments and changes happen in the maritime markets. For instance, the 
recent trend for consolidation that developed in the container ship market, both in the form of new 
mergers and acquisitions, as well as through the emergence of mega liner shipping alliances and their 
implication on the market, affected the development of container freight rates, keeping them in that 
small (UNCTAD 2016). In addition, the assumption that freight rates depend on the travel direction 
(i.e. westbound or eastbound) can be also considered as reasonable, as it is widely documented in 
various markets. Figure 4.1 shows the westbound and eastbound freight rates of North Europe main 
ports – China market.

4.2.7 Mathematical formulation M3 
Fleet-capacity sensitive to container shipment demand – fixed fleet mixture 
The shipping network comprises a predefined route served by ships with different capacities, measured 
by twenty-foot equivalent units (TEUs). Therefore, the shipping company must assign the containers 
to a specific fleet which is defined by size and fleet capacity. While shippers prefer more frequent 
services, liner shipping companies seek to accumulate more cargos by providing less frequent services, 
in order to reduce the costs and increase their profit. According to the previous formulation, the income 
is assumed fixed. It depends on the freight rates and the quantity of transported cargo, which both are 
considered predefined and constant. In that way, the ship operator can take advantage of high freight 
rates and their fleet capacity by delivering more commodities within the given period of time.  
However, in the near-homogenous liner shipping market, an important differentiating factor can be the 
fleet capacity that shipping operator provides. In face  of the competitive pressure, shipping companies 
try to offer more fleet capacity, so as to satisfy more customers in the same period of time. This strategy 
may attract more customers, especially when the goods involved are quantity sensitive, which may 
lead to higher profits for the shipping company. Hence, the container shipment demand for a pair of 
ports (𝑥, 𝑧), assuming that cargo is delivered from port x to z, is dependent on the fleet capacity. We 
call it fleet-capacity-sensitive demand and we assume that is an increasing function of fleet capacity, 
schematically shown in Figure 4.3. Thus, the demand is zero when the fleet capacity is zero and as the 
fleet capacity gets higher values, the demand tends to become constant. In contrast, Figure 4.2 shows 
a case of a constant demand with no fleet capacity dependency, like in the previous formulations 
(Sections 4.2.5 and 4.2.6).

4.3 Linearization of the Objective Function  
It is observed that above problems are mixed integer nonlinear programming models with objective 
functions, which comprises a summation of convex and non-linear functions. In order to take 
advantage the MILP solvers, a linearization should be performed. The nonlinearity can be overcome 
by applying Wang and Meng (2012) algorithm, which is presented in the following section. The same 
methodology is used by Giovannini and Psaraftis (2018) and we also use it as it is an easy and effective 
way to overcome the non-linearity behaviour that fuel consumption function appears. It is seen that 
the fuel consumption increases exponentially with higher values of speed.  An efficient outer-
approximation method can be employed by use sum of many piecewise-linear functions to 
approximate the convex objective functions of the presented models. We also need to mention that the 
following  methodology is applied only to models that consider the fleet mixture 𝑛𝑠 as fixed and not 
as a decision variable, as it is harder to linearize a weighted exponential fuel consumption function.

frequency.

5.4.4 Handling cost at port  
The next term consists describes the handling costs at ports and consists of a cost parameter and the 
quantity of the handled cargo, both loaded and unloaded, at each port. There are many factors that 
affect the port handling, such as the congestion of the port (affecting vessel dwell time), labor costs, 
flexibility, the carrier or even the international leadership association (ILA). It is hard to get this kind 
of costs as most ports do not publish their charges. For the most part worldwide, terminal handling is 
about 110 – 350 USD/container, going up for special equipment like open tops containers, flat racks, 
platforms, reefers etcetera (TheWorldBank, 2017). However, The World Bank group provides cost to 
export and import for all countries as well as the time it takes for the relevant processes (i.e. documents 
etc.) and therefore we can estimate a value regarding our cases. Thus, for this report, we consider a 
handling cost of 120 USD/TEU for TP1 route and 150 for AE5. We assume that the handling cost for 
ultra-vessels is higher than Panamax.

5.4.7 Penalty for excess cargo  
As it has already mentioned, when the capacity of the vessel cannot satisfy the required demand, the 
liner shipping company is penalised by a fee. The penalty value, which is included in the objective 
value as a daily cost per excess TEU, should be higher than the corresponding freight rate. In addition 
to that, as the freight rate depends on the direction (freight rate for westbound is different than the 
eastbound one), we assume that we know in advance which legs will appear excess cargo and we 
decide to pay the competitor so as to deliver the least expensive one. In this case, we prioritize the 
transportation of the expensive commodities on order not only to keep the most important customers, 
but also to minimize the penalty costs. The following table presents the initial values that are imported 
in the model. In Chapter 6, a sensitivity analysis is performed in order to study how the model behaves

7.1 Conclusions  
The main conclusions and contribution of this study is reported below. 
A literature review of academic studies was presented regarding the speed optimization problem in the 
container shipping. Then the key parameters of the problem were discussed, focusing, among other 
things, on the importance of speed, the vessel characteristics and environmental effects.  
In addition, the profit maximization model of Giovannini and Psaraftis (2018), was extended so as to 
consider different types of ships and then it was applied in two actual routes. Furthermore, a model  
which considers the demand as an increasing function of fleet capacity was developed and examined. 
Last, an extension which penalise the liner shipping company in case of excess cargo was tested. 
The work concluded that when we consider different types of ships, the optimization model decides 
about four variables, namely, the speed of the vessel along route, the service period, the fleet size and 
the fleet mixture and thus, it cannot be solved in polynomial time. However, when the fleet mixture is 
considered fixed and given as an exogenous input, the problem transforms into the same as the existing 
one, developed by Giovannini and Psaraftis (2018). Differences appear on the structure of the 
functions, especially in those which describe fuel cost at sea, fuel cost at ports and operating costs. 
This happens because the model considers different parameter values for each type of ship.  
When we apply the model on TP1 route, the optimal solution for the commonly used service period 
(once per week), is obtained by deploying only small vessels with fleet size equals to the maximum 
allowed (the fleet size is bounded by the service period). It was observed, that as we deploy larger 
vessels, the daily profit decreases. In addition the fleet which consists of bigger vessels tends to show 
higher sailing speed along the route. As far as the fleet size is concerned, it is constant for every vessel 
type combination. An exception was observed, when Ultra-Large Container ships were deployed. 
Thus, it is concluded that when the capacity utilization reaches low levels, the model decides to deploy 
less ships, which are sailing in higher speeds. The analysis of the corresponding costs, shows that the 
handling costs dominates the others. It is shown twice as high as the fuel costs at sea and operating 
costs, which follow. Finally, in overall we could say that the optimal solution for TP1 route is obtained 
when we deploy only small vessels, which sail in slow steaming and the size of fleet is the maximum 
which is allowed.  
Applying the model on AE5 route, we observe almost the same trends. For once per week service, the 
optimal solution is obtained when we deploy only the small vessels. However, an important difference

is that, on the contrary with the TP1 route,  the model decides to deploy the minimum allowed ships. 
Therefore, we can conclude that when we deploy Ultra-vessels and we have long distances, the model 
minimizes the fleet size. 
Considering a set of fixed frequencies, the work concluded that the optimal solution is obtained when 
we have service every six days with a fleet size equals to five (maximum allowed). The interesting 
result is that the optimal combination of fleet mixture is when we deploy two small ships with three 
big ones. Therefore, it is concluded that when we optimizing the service period, the optimal solution 
includes both types of ships instead of only small vessels (Scenario 1). We can say with certain that 
the results of Giovannini and Psaraftis (2018) are verified, as we are obtained better solutions by having 
flexible frequencies.  
When the model considers a demand as function of fleet capacity, we observe that there is a trigger 
point. If the demand increases more than this value, the optimal solution is obtained by deploying only 
small vessels. Otherwise, the model chooses to deploy the larger ones. This trigger point, depends on 
the demand and the fleet capacity. For example, it is seen, than the trigger point is shown when the 
demand increases by 10% for a 6% increase of the fleet capacity. If the demand increases more than 
the 10%, the profit per TEU will begin to decrease. Otherwise, we see an increasing trend  
In case of having excess cargo along a leg, a penalty is charged to the liner shipping company. This 
thesis concludes that there is also a trigger point which is dependent on the Demand, the fleet capacity 
and the penalty per container. For example, in TP1 route, we found that it could be profitable to accept 
an excess cargo up to 1% of the vessels’ capacity, only if the competitor charges us up to 150% of the 
corresponding freight rate per TEU.   
7.2 Recommendations for further work 
This thesis only serves a part within the area of the speed optimization problems in container shipping. 
It deals with a profit maximization model which among other parameters, it considers different types 
of vessels. However, a limitation of the study is that when we consider the fleet mixture as decision 
variable, the objective function and the constraints become nonlinear and thus, the model is hard to be 
solved. Certainly, further work needs to be done in order to transform the model into a mixed integer 
one. In this way, the fleet mixture will be decided by the model and not be given as an exogenous 
input.   
In addition, this study assumes that all types of vessels sail along the legs with the same speed.  By 
doing so, we doesn’t change the relationship between the decision variables (service period, fleet size 
and vessel speed), which results to the same behaviour with Gioavannini and Psaraftis (2018) model. 
==PAGE:96==

A possible extension could be to consider different sailing speeds for each vessel type, along the legs. 
However, in this case, we have to be aware of the situation of one ship which overcomes the other. 
This results to its earlier arrival at the port, which also force the service period to change for the route.  
Finally, we tested a model which considers the demand as function of fleet capacity. In order to apply 
the model to an actual case, we estimated the demand function as a linear increasing one. However, 
this decision was clearly in terms of simplifying the estimation of the curve. Solve the model by 
assuming different demand functions (i.e. convex), would may lead to more accurate results and 
conclusions. In addition, another challenge would be to express the demand  as a function of the transit 
time. 
In addition to that, when we allow to have possible excess cargo, it was observed that always the same 
legs appeared not to be able to satisfy the demand. Therefore, a further research would be to check 
whether it is profitable to continue having this excess cargo, at the expense of the penalty, or deploy 
small vessels (i.e. feeders) in order to serve these specific legs.

% Linearization of objective function % 
function [m,A]=WangsModel(a,b,error,Umax,Umin) 
    Qr = @(u) (1/24)*a*u.^(1-b); 
    Qr1 = @(u) (1/24)*a*(1-b)*u.^-b; 
    Qrmax = Qr(Umax); 
    for k=1:10000 
        u(1)=Umin; 
        Q(1)=Qr(Umin)-error; 
        clear uc; 
        clear ubk; 
        if (Qrmax-Q(k))/(Umax-u(k)) >= Qr1(Umax) 
            A(k)=Qrmax-Qr1(Umax)*Umax; 
            m(k)=Qr1(Umax); 
            if A(k)+m(k)*Umax >= Qrmax-error 
                u(k+1)=Umax; 
                Q(k+1)=A(k)+m(k)*Umax; 
                break 
            end 
        else 
            fx=@(ubj) (1/24)*a*(1-b)*(ubj.^-b)*(ubj-u(k))+Q(k)-(1/24)*a*ubj.^(1-b); 
            ubj=bisection(fx,u(k),Umax,0.000001); 
            ub(k)=ubj; 
            Qb(k)=(1/24)*a*ub(k)^(1-b); 
            A(k)=Q(k)-u(k)*((Qb(k)-Q(k))/(ub(k)-u(k))); 
            m(k)=(Qb(k)-Q(k))/(ub(k)-u(k)); 
            Qnk=@(u) A(k)+m(k)*u; 
            if A(k)+m(k)*Umax >= Qrmax-error 
                u(k+1)=Umax; 
                Q(k+1)=A(k)+m(k)*Umax; 
                break 
            end  
        end 
        if (Qrmax-Q(k))/(Umax-u(k)) <= Qr1(Umax) 
            if Qnk(Umax)>(Qr(Umax)-error) 
                A(k)=Q(k)-u(k)*((Qb(k)-Q(k))/(ub(k)-u(k))); 
                m(k)=(Qb(k)-Q(k))/(ub(k)-u(k));
