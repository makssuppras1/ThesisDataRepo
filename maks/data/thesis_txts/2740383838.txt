Abstract:
Precision medicine seeks to personalize healthcare by integrating genomic, environmental, and
lifestyle data, with a focus on understanding genetic mutations and their impact on disease
severity. Germline mutations, classified by American College of Medical Genetics (ACMG)
guidelines, are categorized into five classes: benign, likely benign, likely pathogenic,
pathogenic, and variants of uncertain significance (VUS). The rise of next-generation
sequencing (NGS) has exponentially increased the identification of VUS, particularly in cancer,
creating challenges for clinical decision-making. Lynch syndrome (LS), characterized by
early-onset colorectal cancer (CRC) and microsatellite instability (MSI), is linked to germline
mutations in mismatch repair (MMR) genes, predominantly affecting MLH1, MSH2, and MSH6.
Over 90% of LS gene missense variants in ClinVar are classified as VUS or have conflicting
interpretations. Various functional assays and computational prediction tools have been
developed to address the challenge of classifying VUS. However, despite these advances,
functional assays are available for only a limited subset of variants, and computational
predictions often provide pathogenicity scores based on probability without elucidating the
underlying molecular mechanisms.In this study, we employed MAVISp, a structure-based
framework designed to predict the molecular mechanisms of VUS to analyze VUS mutations
reported in three different databases occurring in MLH1 and MSH6. MAVISp analyzes variants
in two modes: a simple mode using static structures, and an ensemble mode using molecular
dynamics (MD) simulations. It predicts the impact of the variant on protein stability,
protein-protein interaction, post translational modifications and allosteric effects. In simple mode,
MAVISp identified damaging effects from at least one module in 37 out of 59 pathogenic MLH1
variants and 5 out of 9 MSH6 variants.We validated MAVISp’s predictions for our system on
MLH1 variants G67R, R100P, and L749P using available experimental data.Additionally, it
predicted damaging effects from at least one module for 133 out of 1211 VUS variants in MLH1
and 539 out of 2713 VUS variants in MSH6 reported in ClinVar.We highlighted VUS with
damaging effects from at least one module for variants such as G65V, F530S (MLH1), W105R,
and P107H (MSH6).MAVISp can also identify benign variants, as demonstrated by the D12E
variant in MLH1.Given that MAVISp utilizes 1000 ns MD simulations, we evaluated MutateX’s
predictive performance using shorter simulations (100 ns, 500 ns) and clustering algorithms.
Our analysis found that 500 ns simulations provide an optimal balance between computational
efficiency and predictive accuracy (0.98 accuracy compared to 1000 ns), making them suitable
for high-throughput protein domain analysis. MAVISp’s ability to predict molecular mechanisms
can contribute in reclassifying VUS variants by guiding experimental validation, facilitating
patient stratification and personalized therapy development.

Introduction
The Role of Genetic Testing in Tailoring Precision Therapies
Technological advancements and novel research methodologies are accelerating the transition
of healthcare toward personalized medicine, an approach that optimizes disease management
by utilizing molecular profiling to tailor treatments to individual patients. This strategy aims to
enhance clinical outcomes, improve patient quality of life, and reduce healthcare costs by
aligning therapeutic interventions with the unique molecular and genetic characteristics of each
patient, ensuring precision in therapy administration (Aziz, 2024)(Ohashi et al., 2015).
High-throughput technologies such as next-generation sequencing (NGS), for the detection of
genomic alterations linked to disease pathogenesis (Lattanzi et al., 2021), and proteomics, for a
large scale analysis of the impact of genomic alterations on the genes products, play pivotal
roles in this paradigm shift (Ohashi et al., 201). Their utilization in the clinical setting allows
genetic testing becoming integral to disease diagnosis, prognosis, and management. (Thomas
& Shi, 2023)
Genetic testing is a type of test performed to determine if an individual is carrying a genetic
defect in the genome (Genetic Alliance & The New York-Mid-Atlantic Consortium for Genetic
and Newborn Screening Services., 2009). The three main types of genetic tests are cytogenetic,
biochemical, and molecular (Deo & MacRae, 2009). Cytogenetic tests identify chromosomal
abnormalities, biochemical tests assess protein function and metabolite levels, and molecular
tests analyze specific gene sequences, providing insights into genome architecture. The use of
genetic testing integrating NGS and proteomics enhances therapeutic precision, efficacy, and
pharmacovigilance. For instance, ivacaftor targets a specific mutation in the CFTR gene in
cystic fibrosis patients (Goetz & Schork, 2018).
Another notable example is imatinib, used to treat chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML) when
associated with the bcr-abl gene fusion. Imatinib inhibits the overactive tyrosine kinase enzyme
caused by this fusion, demonstrating the use of targeted, efficient therapies that improve drug
safety and minimize toxicity (Goetz & Schork, 2018).
Overall the inclusion of patient genetic information in the clinical setting enables more accurate
molecular diagnoses and facilitates the identification of prognostic and predictive biomarkers
essential for personalized treatment regimens. This individualized approach holds particular
promise for treating complex genetic disorders, by maximizing therapeutic efficacy while
minimizing the toxicities often associated with conventional treatments (Naylor & Chen, 2010)
(Jackson & Chester, 2014).
Despite significant advancements in Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) technologies, which
have led to substantial progress in understanding genome architecture and its alterations, fully
grasping the complexity and heterogeneity of the mutational landscape remains a considerable
challenge (Abbasi & Alexandrov, 2021). To comprehensively characterize genomic mutations,
which are defined as stable hereditary genetic alterations in an individual's genome, several
aspects must be analyzed, including heredity, size, effect, and cellular consequences (Abbasi &
Alexandrov, 2021).

Mutation Databases and Their Role in Disease Variant Classification
Mutations occurring in reproductive cells are known as germline mutations. These are passed
onto offspring, meaning that every cell of the individual will carry the mutation (Clancy, 2008). In
contrast, somatic mutations arise in non-reproductive cells during the individual's lifetime and
are transmitted to daughter cells via mitosis but are not inherited by future generations. Somatic
mutations generally affect only a small subset of an individual’s cells (Clancy, 2008). This
distinction is crucial for understanding the heritability and impact of genetic mutations on health
and disease. Another critical aspect is the extent of the genome affected by mutations (Clancy,
2008). Large-scale mutations, such as insertions, deletions, duplications, inversions, or
translocations, impact chromosome architecture as a whole and are often associated with
severe genetic disorders, such as Huntington's disease or various forms of cancer (Clancy,
2008) (Brown, 2002). On a smaller scale, point mutations involve changes in one or a few
nucleotides. Frameshift mutations, caused by insertions or deletions of nucleotides, disrupt the
reading frame and impair proper protein assembly. Substitutions, where one nucleotide is
replaced by another, are divided into three types: missense variants (also referred to as single
amino acid variations or SAVs), which alter the amino acid sequence; nonsense mutations,
which introduce a premature stop codon, resulting in truncated proteins; and silent mutations,
where nucleotide substitution does not lead to an amino acid change. Moreover, mutations can
have different impacts depending on which functional element of the genome—such as
promoters, coding regions, or enhancers—they affect. The complexity of mutations is further
increased by their varying cellular consequences. Depending on the cell type, its epigenetic
regulation, and the gene product involved, mutations in the same genomic element can have
entirely different phenotypic effects. (Brown, 2002) (Clancy, 2008). The mutational process is
also fundamental to species evolution (Barton, 2010). The introduction of small mutations, often
in the form of point mutations, drives the generation of unique individuals, thus promoting the
genetic diversity essential for population adaptation to environmental changes (Hershberg,
2015). In this context, the human genome varies between individuals through single nucleotide
variations (SNVs), previously referred to as single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), which
contribute to each person's genetic uniqueness (“Functional Genomics,” 2018) (Petrosino et al.,
2021). SNVs occur approximately every 300–400 base pairs in both coding and non-coding
regions, with a population frequency of about 1% (Petrosino et al., 2021). However, the majority
of mutations are neutral or even detrimental to an individual's fitness rather than beneficial (Bao
et al., 2022). Particularly concerning are recessive variants that remain hidden in the population,
making them difficult to eliminate. Furthermore, many diseases are multifactorial, depending on
alterations in multiple genes, meaning that it is often a combination of mutations, rather than a
single one, that is responsible for pathogenicity (Saito, 2011). This complexity complicates the
assessment of the roles played by specific mutations.
To address this, freely accessible databases, such as gnomAD (Chen et al., 2023), have been
established to collect and analyze SNPs within populations. Additionally, specialized databases
like COSMIC (Tate et al., 2019), cBioPortal (Cerami et al., 2012)(Gao et al., 2013)(Bruijn et al.,
2023), ClinGen (Rehm et al., 2015) and ClinVar (Landrum et al., 2014)have been developed to
compile variants associated with specific diseases. With the growing availability of genomic data
thanks to NGS technologies, and the resulting databases for mutation storage, it has become

necessary to classify mutations in terms of their pathogenicity (Pavlick et al., 2024). Particularly
for monogenic diseases, such as Mendelian disorders, expert panels from organizations like the
American College of Medical Genetics and Genomics (ACMG), the Association for Molecular
Pathology (AMP), and the College of American Pathologists (CAP) have issued guidelines for
hereditary mutation classifications (Richards et al., 2015). These guidelines incorporate a
rigorous evaluation process, drawing on evidence from the literature, population databases,
computational predictions, segregation analysis, de novo data, and functional assays. Based on
this evidence, variants are classified into five categories: (i) pathogenic; (ii) likely pathogenic; (iii)
benign; (iv) likely benign; and (v) variants of uncertain significance (VUS). A variant classified as
VUS indicates that there is insufficient or conflicting evidence to determine whether it is
pathogenic or benign, reflecting current limitations in understanding its clinical significance
(Richards et al., 2015; Hoffman-Andrews, 2017).VUS can hinder clinical decision-making,
leaving clinicians without clear guidance (Fowler & Rehm, 2023).Additionally, disclosing the
presence of VUS to patients can lead to a state of "genetic purgatory" and significant
psychological distress, particularly in cancer-related contexts (Burke et al., 2022;
Hoffman-Andrews, 2017). As the number of genomic variants discovered increases, the
proportion of VUS is rising exponentially (Federici & Soddu, 2020). Currently, 36% of variants
reported in ClinVar are classified as VUS, highlighting the challenges in accurate classification
(Fowler & Rehm, 2023). This growing prevalence of VUS presents one of the most significant
obstacles in clinical genomics, as uncertainty in the interpretation of these variants complicates
diagnosis, treatment decisions, and genetic counseling.
Hallmarks of Cancer
One of the multifactorial diseases most frequently associated with Variants of Uncertain
Significance (VUS) is cancer. Cancer development, or carcinogenesis, involves complex and
multifactorial molecular processes resulting from interactions between genetic and
environmental factors. This includes genetic mutations—both somatic and germline—as well as
epigenetic changes and exposure to carcinogenic chemicals. Hereditary cancer, caused by
germline mutations, often results in an increased risk of early-onset disease (Karidio & Sanlier,
2021; Piña-Sánchez et al., 2021; Rahner & Steinke, 2008).The complexity of cancer is captured
by a set of evolving hallmarks that distinguish cancer cells from normal cells. These hallmarks
include (Douglas, 2022):i)sustaining proliferative signals ii)evading growth suppressor,;
iii)activating invasion and metastasis,; iv)enabling replicative immortality,; v)inducing
angiogenesis,; vi)resisting cell death,; vii)dysregulation cellular energetics; viii)tumor promoting
inflammation,; ix)avoiding immune destruction, and, x)genome instability and mutation.These
hallmarks provide a framework for understanding the mechanisms of cancer and continue to
evolve with ongoing research (Douglas, 2022).
Cancer is a major global health issue and the second leading cause of death worldwide
(Piña-Sánchez et al., 2021; Karidio & Sanlier, 2021). In 2022, there were approximately 20
million new cases and 9.7 million deaths globally. In Denmark, cancer is the leading cause of
mortality, accounting for around 16,000 deaths annually (Gamborg et al., 2024). Colorectal
cancer ranks third in both incidence and mortality, following lung cancer, which holds the highest

Genomic Integrity in Lynch Syndrome: The Importance of DNA Repair Pathways
Lynch syndrome (LS), an autosomal dominant cancer syndrome, is characterized by early-onset
colorectal cancer (CRC) and high levels of microsatellite instability (MSI) in tumors (Bellizzi &
Frankel, 2009). This genetic instability, driven by significant DNA damage, is one of the primary
causes of colorectal cancer development (Ferguson et al., 2015). DNA damage refers to
changes in the DNA sequence that affect its coding properties or hinder normal transcription
and replication processes (Martin, 2008). These alterations can be caused by various factors,
such as physical stress (e.g., temperature fluctuations) or chemical agents like benzene or
carbon monoxide from tobacco smoke, as well as replication errors during DNA synthesis
(Chatterjee & Walker, 2017).
During DNA replication, DNA polymerase assembles nucleotides with high fidelity to minimize
errors. However, if damaged DNA is replicated, it can lead to mutations and genetic alterations,
undermining genomic stability (Ferguson et al., 2015; Douglas, 2022). To counteract this, cells
have developed DNA-damage checkpoints, proofreading systems, and repair pathways to
maintain genomic integrity (Hakem, 2008; Wiesmüller et al., 2002; Kunkel, 2004). DNA damage
checkpoints pause cell growth, allowing repair mechanisms to either fix the damage or, if the
damage is irreparable, trigger apoptosis. Various repair mechanisms address different types of
damage: non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), homologous recombination (HR), and
Theta-mediated end joining (TMEJ) repair double-strand breaks (DSBs); nucleotide excision
repair (NER) fixes bulky lesions; base excision repair (BER) targets base damage; and
mismatch repair (MMR) corrects replication errors. The choice of repair mechanism is
determined by the specific type of damage and cellular context (Ropert et al., 2024).
The base excision repair (BER) pathway addresses base damage through two mechanisms: i)
short-patch BER, which removes a single damaged base, and ii) long-patch BER, which
excises 2-13 nucleotides, both initiated by a DNA glycosylase enzyme but diverging in their
processing methods (Hakem, 2008). The nucleotide excision repair (NER) pathway, responsible
for fixing DNA damage from UV radiation, chemical mutagens, and chemotherapy, operates via
two sub-pathways: i) global genome NER (GG-NER), which detects lesions across the genome,
and ii) transcription-coupled NER (TC-NER), which targets actively transcribed genes. Over 30
proteins are involved in this repair process in mammalian cells (Hakem, 2008). The mismatch
repair (MMR) pathway corrects replication errors like insertions, deletions, and substitutions,
using protein dimers to detect and repair these mismatches, crucial for maintaining genomic
stability (Hakem, 2008). Together, these pathways form an intricate network that safeguards
genomic integrity by addressing various types of DNA damage.
Overview of the MMR Mechanism
The DNA mismatch repair (MMR) pathway is essential for maintaining genomic integrity, with
deficiencies often leading to microsatellite instability (MSI). This highly conserved pathway is

mediated by several key proteins functioning as heterodimers (Pećina-Šlaus et al., 2020). The
MMR process is initiated when the MSH2-MSH6 complex (MutSα) recognizes and binds to a
mismatched base (see Fig 1.A). MSH6, a 1,360-amino-acid protein (152 kDa), differs from
MSH2 by having a disordered N-terminal region comprising the first 340 residues (see Fig 2.D),
which is functionally distinct. Structurally, MSH6 is divided into six domains (see Fig2.C), with
the N-terminal region housing critical motifs such as the PCNA-interacting protein (PIP), PWWP,
and nuclear localization sequence (NLS) motifs.
The PIP motif, located at the extreme N-terminal domain (NTD) of MSH6, interacts with PCNA,
facilitating 3’ excision activity during DNA synthesis. Downstream of the PIP motif is the PWWP
domain, which plays a crucial role in non-specific protein-DNA interactions, a feature of
chromatin-associated proteins. This domain aids chromosome compaction and has a
hydrophobic pocket containing three aromatic residues, suggesting it may bind methylated
proteins and histones, implicating it in chromatin processes. Lastly, the NLS motif, identified as a
Ser-Pro-Ser sequence in the MSH6 NTD, ensures the nuclear import of the MSH2-MSH6
complex through nuclear pore interactions. Together, these motifs play essential roles in MMR
functionality and genomic stability (Edelbrock et al., 2013) (Binda & O Tollefsbol, 2023). Domain
1 of MSH6 is crucial for binding DNA mismatches, while domain 2 serves as a connector
between domain 1 and domain 3. Domain 3 functions as a lever that folds in two positions
relative to domain 5, which is responsible for ATPase activity. Domain 4 acts as a clamp,
enabling nonspecific DNA interactions. These five domains align with MSH2 to form an
asymmetric mirror image, where each domain is juxtaposed (Edelbrock et al., 2013). In the
absence of MSH6, MSH2 forms the MSH2-MSH3 complex (MutSβ), which plays a role in
repairing larger DNA defects (Li, 2008). MutSα creates a theta-like clamp structure around DNA
(see Fig 1.A), with two channels: one for binding DNA and another that remains unoccupied.
The MutSα DNA binding site consists of four mobile domains, including a central domain on
MSH6, specifically interacting with the mismatch. The ATP binding site is located distally from
the DNA binding site, driving significant conformational changes, such as opening the central
channel to accommodate the DNA. This conformational shift also creates a binding site for
MutLα (Bradford et al., 2020) (see Fig 2.B).

The Crucial Role of MMR in Lynch Syndrome and Its Associated VUS Challenges
The mismatch repair (MMR) pathway's significance is highlighted by its role in LS. Over 70% of
germline mutations linked to Lynch syndrome affect the MLH1 and MSH2 genes (Buono et
al.,2024). Mutations in MLH1 often result in the loss of endonuclease activity, leading to a failure
in repair mechanisms. Approximately 10% of germline mutations associated with Lynch
syndrome are found in MSH6 (Buono et al., 2024). Although other MMR protein mutations also
contribute to Lynch syndrome, they occur less frequently. Studies indicate that mutations in the
ATP binding sites of MSH2 and MSH6 have distinct impacts on MMR, DNA binding, and ATP
hydrolysis (Bradford et al., 2020; Schofield & Hsieh, 2003; Hakem, 2008). Early diagnosis is
crucial for managing LS, which can occasionally involve extracolonic cancers like endometrial
cancer.Studies show that LS, primarily associated with MLH1 mutations often causes CRC and
MSH2 or MSH6 mutations associated with extracolonic cancers (Sehgal et al., 2014). The
diagnostic methods include molecular screening of tumor specimens for defective MMR function
or high MS levels, and direct germline testing. CRC development at a young age may indicate
LS, but not all LS cases present with CRC. (Bernstein et al., 2023) Diagnosis strategies have
evolved from the Amsterdam and Bethesda criteria to include extracolonic cancers (Bernstein et
al., 2023). Identifying LS mutation carriers is vital for enhancing cancer surveillance, genetic
counseling, and treatment (Trujillo-Rojas et al., 2023; Dal Buono et al., 2024; Yurgelun &
Hampel, 2018). . However, LS remains under-diagnosed, with challenges in risk stratification
and management (Williams et al., 2023).
Currently, more than 90% of Lynch syndrome (LS) gene missense variants reported in the
ClinVar database are classified as variants of uncertain significance (VUS) or have conflicting
interpretations, reflecting the challenges in determining the pathogenicity of these variants
(Scott et al., 2022; Abildgaard et al., 2023). The rarity and heterogeneity of missense variants
complicate their classification (Gerasimavicius et al., 2020). To address this, high throughput
functional and biochemical assays defined as MAVE (Multiplex assay of variant effects) have
been developed. However, while useful in the process of variant interpretation, they are still
available for only a subset of variants. For this reason computational tools such as GEMME
(Laine et al., 2019), EVMutation (Hopf et al., 2017), and Alphamissense (Cheng et al., 2023)
although considered weak evidence by ACMG guidelines, often represent the only available
information regarding the impact of a variant. Moreover these approaches highlight the
limitations in understanding the molecular mechanisms that drive pathogenicity (Abildgaard et
al., 2023; Scott et al., 2022). Indeed, these methods typically provide pathogenicity scores
based on probabilities, without elucidating the specific molecular mechanisms of mutations.
Finally, these predictions rely on available data, which may be biased leading to false positives.
As a result, methods like Alphamissense can be limited in their accuracy due to potential data
bias (McInnes et al., 2021). In conclusion, understanding molecular effects underlying missense
variant’s pathogenicity is crucial in clarifying the mutation’s role in disease and improving patient
stratification for targeted therapies.
MAVISp: Unraveling the Molecular Mechanisms of VUS Under the Lens of Protein Structure
The amino acid substitutions in a protein structure can impact various functional aspects,
including stability, protein-protein interactions, long-range effects, and protein dynamics,
potentially altering overall protein function.In this regard, protein structure analysis methods are
highly valuable for investigating the molecular mechanisms of missense variants (Iqbal et al.,
2020). Structure-based analysis has traditionally relied on experimental structures obtained from
techniques such as NMR, X-ray crystallography, or electron microscopy. However, these
methods have certain drawbacks, including low throughput, poor scalability, and being
15
==PAGE:16==
time-consuming. The recent advancements in computational methods such as Alphafold2 has
emerged as a viable alternative to experimental methods (Jumper et al., 2021) (Varadi et al.,
2022). This study aims to elucidate the molecular mechanisms using structural biology by which
VUS missense variants in MLH1 and MSH6 may contribute to pathogenicity causing colorectal
cancer. More than 90% of missense variants in MLH1 and MSH6 reported in ClinVar are
classified as VUS (Abildgaard et al., 2019). We investigate the impacts of these variants on
protein stability, post-translational modifications, allosteric effects, and protein-protein
interactions. We utilized MAVISp (Arnaudi et al., 2022) a structural-based computational
framework specifically designed to analyze missense variants and its impact on the molecular
mechanism linking with pathogenicity of cancer. MAVISp operates in two modes: i) Simple
Mode, which analyzes individual protein structures, and ii) Ensemble Mode, which utilizes
multiple structural models to predict variant effects. Both modes include modules for structure
selection and variant analysis, allowing for comprehensive insights into the molecular
mechanisms affected by these variants.
We validated MAVISp predictions using experimental data on MLH1 and to further elucidate the
molecular mechanisms of VUS in MLH1 and MSH6. Various modules from Simple Mode were
employed to analyze allosteric effects, post-translational modifications, stability, and
protein-protein interactions. For variants requiring deeper investigation into stability and
interactions, Ensemble Mode modules were utilized. Due to the computational demands of
Ensemble Mode, which involves simulations of multiple structures, we also conducted statistical
analyses to evaluate the predictive performance for MutateX results relative to MD simulation
time(100ns, 500ns, 1000ns) and protein structural length.
Results and discussion
Analysis of variants identified as pathogenic or benign in ClinVar for MLH1
In ClinVar, for MLH1, 60 variants are reported pathogenic and 15 as benign. MAVISp could
predict damaging effects for 34 out of 60 pathogenic variants from at least one module in simple
mode (see Fig 3.C). At first, we focused on 57 out of 60 pathogenic variants classified with
review status > 3 in ClinVar for MLH1. We evaluate if modules used in MAVISp framework
predict at least one damaging effect for the variants annotated as pathogenic in ClinVar.
MAVISp predicts damaging effects on 24 out of 57 pathogenic variants on stability with a
consensus of FoldX and Rosetta whereas FoldX and RaSP consensus predicts 16 out of 57
pathogenic variants with damaging effects on stability. 1 out of 57 pathogenic variants was
predicted with destabilizing effects on MLH1-PMS2 interaction from the LOCAL_INTERACTION
module in MAVISp. The PTM_MODULE predicted 3 out of 57 pathogenic variants with effects
on stability on post translational modification. 6 out of 59 were predicted to have damaging long
range effects.Here we discuss three cases of pathogenic variants with review status >3 on the
performance of MAVISp to link the mechanistic molecular mechanism with pathogenicity. We
selected the G67R, R100P, and L749P variants to ensure case study coverage across both the
NTD and CTD of MLH1.

Analysis of mechanistic molecular mechanism (destabilizing) of pathogenic variant G67R in
MLH1
From literature study the ATPase domain of MLH1-NTD belongs to the GHKL superfamily (Ban
et al., 1999) (Dutta & Inouye, 2000). It consists of four motifs, namely the N box, G1 box, G2
box and G3 box and an ATP-lid following the Bergerat ATP-binding fold. The N box binds with
Mg2+ and the motifs G1 box, G2 box and G3 box bind with ATP. The conserved glycines of G1
box (G65 and G67) and G2 box (G98 and G101) form two hinges that confer the flexibility of the
ATP-lid (see Appendix B Fig12) (Dutta & Inouye, 2000). These motifs interact with ATP either
directly or indirectly mediated through water molecules.The ATPase domain plays a key role for
recruitment of MutS𝞪bound with damaged DNA. Mutation G67R located in a glycine rich box
has been experimentally identified with degrading the ATP binding site by distorting the native
17
==PAGE:18==
main chain torsion angles and side chain interactions causing loss of MMR function through
protein structure instability. We observe damaging effects on the STABILITY_MODULE. The
folding free energy predicted by FoldX, Rosetta and RaSP > 3kcal/mol indicates damaging
effects on the protein stability according to the classification in STABILITY_MODULE(see
methods). This is observed in both simple_mode and ensemble_mode. This indicates that the
MAVISp framework was able to capture the destabilizing molecular mechanism of a variant
G67R which is already classified pathogenic with protein instability (Ban et al., 1999) (Dutta &
Inouye, 2000) (Abildgaard et al., 2019).
Analysis of mechanistic molecular mechanism (loss of protein-protein interaction) of pathogenic
variant L749P in MLH1
MLH1 dimerizes with PMS2 in CTD forming MutL𝛂complex (Wu et al., 2015). This domain
plays a key functional role in endonuclease activity (Wu et al., 2015).The residue L749 (see
Appendix B Fig13) is identified as an interactor with PMS2. (Perera & Bapat, 2008). In this
study, we investigated the impact of the MLH1 missense mutation L749P on dimerization.
Experimental data confirmed that the L749P mutation significantly reduces the affinity of MutLα
dimerization, leading to the loss of mismatch repair (MMR) activity (Kosinski et al., 2010)
(Hinrichsen et al., 2013) (Perera & Bapat, 2008). Through the LOCAL_INTERACTION module
from the MAVISp framework, we observed damaging effects on MLH1-PMS2 interaction caused
by the L749P variant. The STABILITY_MODULE from MAVISp predicts uncertain effects in
simple mode and destabilizing in ensemble mode. Experimental data is only available for
dimerization of MLH1-PMS2 with which our results are consistent. This supports the MAVISp
framework in linking the protein-protein interaction with damaging effects.MAVISp ensemble
mode predicted destabilizing effects on the STABILITY_MODULE. Since experimental data is
available only for protein-protein interactions, MAVISp suggests further experimental analysis to
assess protein stability related to the L749P variant.
Analysis of mechanistic molecular mechanism (long range) of pathogenic variant R100P in
MLH1
The arginine residue at position 100 (R100) of MLH1 is located within the ATPase domain and is
positioned on the surface of the protein(see Appendix B Fig14). Experimental data indicate that
the R100P mutation exerts a detrimental effect on protein stability, resulting in a reduced
steady-state level of the protein(Abildgaard et al., 2023).
In the MAVISp framework, the simple mode predicts an uncertain effect on stability. However,
the ensemble mode captures the deleterious impact of the R100P mutation more
accurately.This supports the experimental data available on R100P resulting in reduced
steady-state level of MLH1. MAVISp LONG_RANGE_MODULE predicts mixed_effects for the
variant R100P with an allosteric effect on the residue V15(see Appendix B Fig15). Since proline
is smaller in volume than arginine, the mutation leads to a local compression at the mutation
site. The R100P variant induces compression in the MLH1 protein's elastic network, causing a
structural adjustment that propagates to distant regions, including the V15 residue. V15, is
located in the 𝛂A helix of the ATPase domain (Wu et al., 2015). This helix is a tightly packed
region which plays a crucial role in maintaining the structural integrity through side chain
18
==PAGE:19==
interactions with functional residues. Compression from the R100P mutation could disrupt these
interactions, potentially destabilizing the helix and impairing ATPase activity, thereby affecting
the overall function and stability of the protein.
The experimental study focuses on protein destabilization, which aligns with our MAVISp
results, where the STABILITY_MODULE predicts destabilizing effects.Additionally, MAVISp's
long_range module suggests that mutations in position 100 could affect V15 impairing the
ATPase activity of MLH1 even if the mutation itself does not have a destabilizing effect. This
highlights the need for further experimental analysis to investigate these potential functional
disruptions in more detail.
Analysis of MLH1 molecular mechanism of VUS using MAVISp framework
We validated for our system the modules of MAVISp performance predicting the underlying
molecular mechanism with available experimental data on pathogenic variants. We use this
framework to analyze molecular mechanisms of variants reported VUS in ClinVar for MLH1.
There are 1211 VUS reported in ClinVar for MLH1 missense variants(see Appendix A Fig 10).
MAVISp predicted damaging effects for 133 VUS variants reported in ClinVar from consensus of
FoldX and Rosetta in STABILITY_MODULE whereas 105 VUS variants were predicted with
damaging effects from the consensus of FoldX and RaSP in STABILITY_MODULE. 8 VUS
variants were predicted with damaging effects from the LOCAL_INTERACTION module on
MLH1-PMS2 interaction. 14 variants were predicted to alter the regulation provided by
phosphorylation. 5 variants were predicted with damaging effects on stability after removal of
post translational modification. 12 variants were predicted to be potentially damaging in terms of
removing the function related to phosphorylation.103 variants were predicted with damaging
effects from the LONG_RANGE module. We selected three VUS variants G65V, D12E, and
F530S as representative examples.

residues. The residues represented in orange (G65 and F530) are classified pathogenic by

Glycine at position 65 (G65) resides within a conserved region of the glycine-rich box (see
Appendix B Fig16), which is essential for conferring flexibility to the ATP-lid in the ATPase
domain of MLH1 (Ban et al., 1999). The glycine-rich motif is a hallmark of nucleotide-binding
folds and plays a critical role in facilitating flexibility and movement required for ATP binding and
hydrolysis. Consistent with this, the crystal structure of MLH1 reveals that G65 engages in
indirect interactions with ATP, supporting its functional relevance (Dutta & Inouye, 2000).
Computational predictions using MAVISp capture the molecular mechanism caused by variant
G65V as a destabilizing effect on ATPase domain of the protein.The substitution of glycine (G)
with valine (V) at position 65 in the MLH1 ATPase domain could disrupt the domain’s structure
and function due to critical differences in the size, flexibility, and steric effects of these two
amino acids, despite both being hydrophobic.Our structural analysis indicates that G65 is buried
within the protein core, with a solvent-accessible surface area (SASA) of 2.7% in the static
structure, suggesting no exposure to solvent. This buried nature is maintained during molecular
dynamics (MD) simulations, where the average SASA increases slightly to 6.439%, indicating
that G65 remains largely shielded from solvent throughout the protein's conformational
sampling. This suggests that the buried state of glycine residues plays a role in the stability of
the ATPase domain .Glycine is the smallest amino acid with a side chain of one hydrogen atom
providing exceptional flexibility required for dynamic conformational shifts in the ATP-binding
domain of MLH1. In contrast, valine has a bulkier side chain of two methyl groups which could
introduce steric hindrance, reducing flexibility and distorting local protein structure.While both
residues are hydrophobic, the valine side chain may introduce clashes that could distort the
local folding or create unfavorable interactions with surrounding residues. Glycine’s small size
does not contribute to such interactions, making it more structurally neutral. The ACPSN
analysis of the dynamic structure also indicates that G65 has zero hub nodes, signifying an
absence of side chain interactions. Additionally, pathogenicity scores such as AlphaMissense,
DeMaSk, and EVE classification all categorize the G65V variant as pathogenic.
Aspartic acid at position 12 (D12) is located within a non-conserved region (see Appendix B
Fig17)of the MLH1 N-terminal domain (NTD). The D12E mutation, involving the substitution of
aspartic acid with glutamic acid, is predicted to have neutral effects in MAVISp framework.The
STABILITY_MODULE initially suggests uncertain effects in simple mode but reclassifies them
as neutral in ensemble mode. Similarly, neutral predictions are observed across other modules,
such as LONG_RANGE , in both simple and ensemble modes.Pathogenicity prediction tools,
including AlphaMissense and EVE, classify this mutation as benign.The substitution of aspartic
acid (D) with glutamic acid (E) is considered neutral due to their structural and chemical
similarities. Both amino acids are negatively charged and polar, with similar reactivity and
interaction properties. Structurally, they differ only by a single methyl group—glutamic acid has a
slightly longer side chain (-CH₂-CH₂-COOH) compared to aspartic acid (-CH₂-COOH). Despite
this small difference, both contain a carboxyl group (-COOH) at the end of their side chains,
allowing them to maintain similar roles in protein stability and function.Given these structural
20
==PAGE:21==
and chemical similarities, the D12E variant is unlikely to significantly affect MLH1 function or
stability, suggesting a benign impact.
Phenylalanine at position 530 (F530) in the C-terminal domain (CTD) of MLH1 is located
adjacent to a critical MLH1-PMS2 interaction site (see Appendix B Fig18) and the variant F530S
has been predicted to be pathogenic by multiple scoring systems, including AlphaMissense and
DeMaSk. When phenylalanine is substituted with serine (F530S), a dissimilar and smaller polar
amino acid, the substitution is predicted to have a destabilizing effect, as indicated by the
STABILITY_MODULE of MAVISp. This prediction is consistent in both simple and ensemble
modes.The substitution of phenylalanine (F) with serine (S) is destabilizing the protein stability
due to significant differences in their structure and chemical properties. Phenylalanine is a large,
non-polar, and hydrophobic amino acid with a bulky aromatic side chain, whereas serine is polar
and hydrophilic, with a smaller hydroxyl-containing side chain (-CH₂OH). This difference in size
and polarity can disrupt the local protein environment, especially in hydrophobic regions, where
phenylalanine at position 530 resides.Structural analysis shows that F530 is buried within the
protein core, with a solvent-accessible surface area (SASA) of 6.4% in the static structure.
During molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, F530 remains buried, with a slight increase in
SASA to 17.694%, indicating limited exposure to the solvent possibly due to the aromatic ring of
phenylalanine.Replacing it with serine could alter the folding, stability, or function of the protein
due to changes in side-chain interactions and polarity. Moreover, analysis using acPSN reveals
that F530 serves as a hub residue, forming side-chain interactions with L525, E717, and Y721.
These interactions with the buried state of F530 could be important for maintaining the local
structural integrity.Therefore the introduction of a polar serine residue in place of hydrophobic
phenylalanine leads to local structural instability, potentially compromising MLH1's role in DNA
mismatch repair.
Overall, the analysis suggests that the MAVISp framework is capable of predicting mechanistic
insights into molecular mechanisms associated with various mutations in MLH1 protein. The
framework's modules identify damaging effects in alignment with two or more pathogenic scores
that classify the variants pathogenic, providing a good insight to guide experiments for
reclassification of VUS. This capability can aid researchers in prioritizing VUS variants that
exhibit damaging effects in at least one module for further experimental analysis.The results
from STABILITY_MODULE indicates towards experimental analysis to check for protein
abundance through methods like CHX assays, FACS techniques or VAMP-seq method for high
throughput approach. Similarly, the results from LONG_RANGE module guides researchers for
experimental analysis to study the allosteric effects which suggest strong evidence by ACMG
guidelines. For example, the G65V variant could be prioritized for experimental assays such as
CHX assay as it is predicted to cause damaging effects on the protein structure by the
STABILITY_MODULE keeping G67R a pathogenic variant with ClinVar review status 3 as
control. G65V could also be prioritized for functional assays such as MAVE for stronger
evidence to reclassify the variant.

Analysis of MSH6 variants classified as pathogenic or benign in ClinVar
In ClinVar, only seven MSH6 variants are classified as benign, with just three having a review
status of 3. Similarly, there are nine pathogenic variants with a review status of 3 reported in
ClinVar. However, a substantial number of variants 2,713 are reported as VUS (see Appendix A

MAVISp predicted five [V398E, L449P, V480E, L1063R, L1211P] out of nine pathogenic variants
with a damaging effect from at least one module.Out of nine variants, five were predicted to

both Rosetta and FoldX, as well as from FoldX and RaSP in simple mode.A review of the
literature highlights a significant gap in experimental and functional analyses of MSH6,
contributing to the bottleneck in the classification of VUS.
To address this, we applied the MAVISp framework to analyze MSH6 VUS, focusing on the
underlying molecular mechanisms. MAVISp predicts damaging effects from at least one module
for 539 out of 2713 VUS variants reported in ClinVar in simple mode.Our study concentrates on
the N-terminal domain of MSH6, which operates independently of cofactors. We analyzed as
example cases the variants W105R and P107H. We choose these residues as they are a part of

By leveraging MAVISp, we aim to provide mechanistic insights into MSH6 VUS that can guide
further experimental studies, helping to resolve the functional impact of these variants and
improve the classification of VUS in ClinVar.

The variant W105R is predicted pathogenic by at least two pathogenicity scores employed by

mode, predicts mixed effects for the W105R variant, resulting in down mutation(see Appendix C
Fig21). Due to arginine's (R) smaller volume compared to tryptophan (W), the mutation induces
local compression at the mutation site. This compression may alter the MSH6 protein's elastic

analysis highlights the need for functional experimental studies on mutations that may not
impact protein abundance but could exert allosteric effects on D89, D94, P138, K185, and L193.

structure, interactions, and overall stability of the protein.Our structural analysis of W105 shows

To establish a benchmark for comparing the performance of different ensemble generation
protocols, we utilized the standard protocol currently implemented in MAVISp. This involves
calculating the average folding/unfolding ΔΔG values from 25 frames extracted from a
one-microsecond molecular dynamics (MD) trajectory. Our primary objective is to identify an
alternative protocol that maintains prediction accuracy while reducing computational time.
We assessed various protocols for ensemble generation in FoldX calculations based on
conformations obtained from MD simulations. The protocols compared included: i) the default
MAVISp protocol, which extracts 25 frames evenly from a one-microsecond trajectory; ii) 25
frames extracted from trajectories of 100 nanoseconds; iii) 25 frames extracted from trajectories
of 500 nanoseconds; and iv) a clustering algorithm that analyzes pairwise main-chain Root
Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) from the one-microsecond MD simulation to identify centroids
from the three most populated clusters for FoldX calculations. This clustering approach is
currently utilized in MAVISp when stability scores are predicted using either the RaSP method or
the Cartesian protocol of Rosetta.
The overall metric analysis, using 1000 ns as the gold standard, shows that the 500 ns
simulation performs better than the 100 ns and clustering methods, as expected (see table3).
For different protein domains, MD simulations at 500 ns achieve 99.6% accuracy, while the 100
ns and clustering methods yield 98.87% and 98.8% accuracy, respectively, for predicting
destabilizing and neutral effects using MutateX.
To further analyze this, we assessed each protein domain's performance (see table4 and table
5) by comparing performance metrics against average backbone RMSD (nm)(see Appendix D
Fig22) obtained from the 1000 ns trajectories. The 100 ns simulation shows slight fluctuations in
accuracy, typically ranging from 0.97 to 1.0, but experiences a significant drop to 0.918 at an
RMSD of 0.57 nm (APOC1), suggesting that 100 ns simulations may underperform for
structures with larger deviations. In contrast, the 500 ns simulation maintains more consistent
accuracy, around or above 0.98, with no major dips, indicating more reliable performance across
a broader range of RMSD values. The clustering method also shows variability, with accuracy
dropping to 0.918 at RMSD 0.57 nm (APOC1) but remaining between 0.97 and 1.0 for most
other structures.

==PAGE:27==
Sensitivity analysis shows similar trends: 100 ns displays variability, with a sharp drop to 0.5 for
APOC1 at RMSD 0.57 nm, while 500 ns consistently achieves near-optimal sensitivity
(0.97–1.0) across all RMSD values. The clustering method mirrors the 100 ns pattern, with a
notable dip in sensitivity at RMSD 0.57 nm. Specificity for 100 ns also fluctuates but stays close
to 1.0 for most proteins. The 500 ns simulation maintains stable specificity around 1.0 across all
RMSD values, with minimal deviations, while the clustering method shows some variability.
Overall, the 500 ns simulation demonstrates the most consistent and reliable performance
across accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity metrics, making it a more robust choice for protein
domain analysis.
In conclusion, our analysis demonstrates that the 500 ns MD simulations provide the most
consistent and reliable performance in predicting destabilizing and neutral effects compared to
1000ns MD simulations. With accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity consistently near or above
0.98 across varying protein domains and structural lengths, the 500 ns simulations outperform
both the 100 ns simulations and clustering methods, particularly for structures with larger
deviations, such as APOC1. These findings suggest that 500 ns simulations strike an optimal
balance between computational efficiency and predictive accuracy, making it a preferred choice
for high-throughput protein domain analysis while maintaining robustness across a wide range
of structural variations.

Conclusion
This study enabled the prediction of molecular mechanisms linked to the pathogenicity of
Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS) associated with cancer in mismatch repair (MMR)
proteins, specifically MLH1 and MSH6. Using the MAVISp framework, we first predicted
damaging effects for 34 out of 60 known pathogenic MLH1 variants and 5 out of 9 pathogenic
MSH6 variants in the simple mode. We validated this framework by analyzing MLH1 mutations
(G67R, R100P, and L749P) and comparing the predictions to available experimental data.
Notably, MAVISp identified additional effects, such as allosteric disruption caused by the R100P
variant, for which there is no current experimental data, suggesting further experimental studies
should be conducted to explore these long-range impacts.
The ability of MAVISp to predict damaging effects was then applied to assess VUS variants. The
framework predicted damaging effects in at least one module for 539 out of 2713 VUS variants
in MLH1 and 539 out of 2713 VUS variants in MSH6, as reported in ClinVar. We selected
specific VUS variants for further analysis, including D12E, G65V, and F530S in MLH1 and
W105R and P107H in MSH6. While D12E was predicted to be a benign variant, G65V and
F530S in MLH1, along with W105R and P107H in MSH6, were classified as having damaging
effects by at least one module of MAVISp, indicating a potential pathogenic impact.
The molecular mechanisms predicted by MAVISp for variants like G65V, F530S, W105R, and
P107H could be instrumental in prioritizing these variants for further experimental and functional
analyses. Such analyses could provide crucial evidence for reclassifying these VUS variants as
pathogenic.By understanding how a genetic variant impacts molecular mechanisms,
researchers and clinicians can more effectively stratify patients and develop targeted therapies.
For patient stratification, variants that disrupt protein function, like MLH1 and MSH6 in colorectal
cancer (CRC), help identify high-risk individuals for enhanced monitoring and early intervention,
while those with benign variants can follow standard care. In drug development, understanding
these molecular disruptions allows researchers to pinpoint specific therapeutic targets, such as
stabilizing misfolded proteins or correcting pathway defects. This knowledge supports the
creation of variant-specific treatments, advancing personalized medicine and improving patient
outcomes. By linking molecular mechanisms with pathogenicity, MAVISp could play a key role
in improving the clinical interpretation of VUS variants and guiding future therapeutic strategies.
MAVISp employs molecular dynamics (MD) simulations of 1000 ns to generate structural
ensembles for its ensemble_mode. However, this process is computationally intensive and
time-consuming. To address these limitations, we evaluated MutateX's predictive performance
using shorter simulation times of 100 ns and 500 ns, as well as a clustering algorithm,
comparing them to the 1000 ns ensemble as the gold standard. Our analysis shows that 500 ns
MD simulations consistently outperform both 100 ns simulations and clustering methods,
particularly for proteins like APOC1, which exhibit larger RMSD values.
In terms of predictive accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity, the 500 ns simulations demonstrated
nearly equivalent performance to 1000 ns simulations, with scores consistently near or above
30
==PAGE:31==
0.98. In contrast, the 100 ns simulations and clustering approach exhibited more variability,
especially for proteins with greater structural deviations, leading to a decrease in predictive
accuracy. For instance, the 100 ns simulations showed a significant drop in performance for
proteins with RMSD deviations around 0.57 nm, such as APOC1, indicating that shorter
simulations may fail to capture sufficient structural fluctuations needed for accurate predictions.
These results suggest that 500 ns simulations offer a balance between computational efficiency
and predictive accuracy, providing a more scalable solution for high-throughput protein domain
analysis while maintaining robustness across a wide range of structural variations. By
significantly reducing computational time without sacrificing performance, 500 ns simulations
present a feasible alternative for studies requiring large-scale MD analysis.
Future prospects
Currently, the cofactors for MLH1 have been superimposed using PyMOL, as AlphaFold2 is
unable to predict the cofactors of proteins accurately. In this study, we aim to implement the
AlphaFill module to incorporate cofactors for MSH6, specifically focusing on the ATPase domain
located in the C-terminal domain (CTD). Our initial analysis includes only two protein-protein
interactions involving MLH1 in a simple mode, alongside the N-terminal domain (NTD) of MSH6.
However, we plan to extend this analysis to include additional protein-protein interactions,
specifically between MSH3 and MLH1, as well as MSH2 and MSH6. Furthermore, we will
explore SLiMs (Short Linear Motifs) mediated protein interactions for MSH6, allowing us to
conduct a more comprehensive examination in both simple and ensemble modes. This
approach will enable us to gain deeper insights into the molecular mechanisms impacted by
various genetic variants.
At present, our performance evaluation encompasses 14 proteins, with plans to incorporate
additional proteins in the future. This expansion will include a thorough investigation of the
variations in Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) of these proteins. Recognizing that proteins
are dynamic entities capable of undergoing a wide range of motions across different timescales,
it becomes crucial to integrate the aforementioned analyses with an in-depth examination of the
conformational changes observed during the original Molecular Dynamics (MD) trajectory
utilized for ensemble generation.
Initially, we will assess the conformational heterogeneity of each protein under investigation by
applying RMSD analyses over the simulation time, focusing on the overall backbone
movements. This foundational analysis will serve as a basis for our future endeavors, where we
plan to implement Root Mean Square Inner Product (RMSIP) to quantitatively assess the
similarity and correlation between the dynamic behaviors of different protein structures over
time. By incorporating RMSIP, we will be able to evaluate how distinct protein conformations
relate to each other dynamically, providing critical insights into their functional implications and
the impact of variants on protein behavior.

Methodology
Selection of initial structure for the study
In this study we use the STRUCTURE_SELECTION module to retrieve models for MLH1 and
MSH6. We use two sub modules in STRUCTURE_SELECTION for simple_mode and three sub
modules for ensemble_mode. The module includes structure discovery from protein data bank
(PDB) as well as AlphaFold database. We retrieve Alphafold model on careful structural
analysis of the predicted structure. We used the ENSEMBLE_MODULE submodule to collect
ensemble structures on all-atom MD simulations.We use the INTERACTOME submodule to
retrieve models for protein interactors for MLH1 and MSH6 by querying the Mentha database
and retrieving the models with pDOCKQ score.The pDOCKQ score is a metric that evaluates
the confidence of protein-protein interaction predictions, with values ranging from 0 to 1. Higher
scores indicate greater accuracy of the predicted interface, with 0.8-1.0 suggesting very high
confidence and below 0.4 reflecting low confidence. It helps prioritize reliable docking models
for further analysis or validation.This module is also used to identify Short linear motifs or
SLiMS. They play key roles in protein-protein interactions, signaling, and regulation by serving
as recognition sites for larger protein domains.
MLH1 and MSH6 protein structures retrieved from the AlphaFold database were trimmed based
on AlphaFold's predicted local distance difference test (pLDDT) scores, ensuring higher
structural confidence, and supplemented by relevant literature.The pLDDT score in AlphaFold is
a confidence metric that estimates the accuracy of individual residues in a predicted protein
structure, with values ranging from 0 to 100. Scores between 90-100 indicate very high
confidence in the structural prediction, typically reflecting well-structured and accurately folded
regions. Scores from 70-90 represent confident predictions with minor uncertainties, while
scores between 50-70 suggest lower confidence, often corresponding to regions that may be
flexible or disordered. Scores below 50 indicate very low confidence, signaling that these
regions are likely unstructured or poorly predicted. MLH1 consists of 756 amino acids, and for
our study, we trimmed it into two key domains: the N-terminal domain (residues 1-341) and the
C-terminal domain (residues 501-756). Similarly, MSH6, a protein with 1346 amino acids, was
trimmed based on its structural significance. Given the crucial role of the N-terminal region in
DNA binding, we studied the MSH6 domain spanning residues 89-194. This domain selection
aligns with established literature on MSH6's functional importance.
The N-terminal structure of MLH1 includes essential cofactors, such as Mg²⁺ and ADP, which
are not predicted by AlphaFold. To address this limitation, these cofactors were superimposed
onto the AlphaFold model by aligning it with an experimentally determined structure from the
Protein Data Bank (PDB) using PyMOL.
Dataset of variants used in this study
We analyzed missense variants for both MLH1 and MSH6, focusing on key functional domains:
residues 1-341 and 501-756 for MLH1, and residues 89-194 for MSH6. Data were sourced
from multiple databases, including ClinVar, cBioPortal, COSMIC, and germline variants from
32
==PAGE:33==
whole genome sequencing (WGS) of 198 childhood cancer samples, part of The MARINARA
study in collaboration with the Swedish Colorectal Cancer Genomes (Byrjalsen et al., 2020)
working group as well as a saturation scan(see Appendix A Fig8 and Fig9). In the case of
ClinVar, we also referenced the RefSeq ID: NP_000240 for MLH1 (UniProt entry P40692), which
corresponds to the canonical isoform of 756 amino acids. The same approach was applied for
MSH6, using RefSeq ID NP_000170 (UniProt entry P52701), consisting of 1360 amino acids.
Specifically, we identified 1,599 MLH1 variants in ClinVar, 399 in cBioPortal, and 273 in
COSMIC, while for MSH6, 3,450 variants were reported in ClinVar, 737 in cBioPortal, and 541 in
COSMIC. A saturation run was also performed to gather additional variant data. Across the
11,343 MLH1 and 20,997 MSH6 variants analyzed, approximately 96% were classified as
Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS), with only a small fraction—0.5% for MLH1 and 0.2%
for MSH6—classified as pathogenic in ClinVar (see Appendix A Fig10 and Fig11).
MD simulation
We use an all-atom MD simulation to generate ensembles of protein structures by capturing
their atomic-level movements over time, providing insights into conformational flexibility and
dynamic behavior. This method was used for the STRUCTURE_SELECTION module in
ensemble mode of the MAVISp framework.
We performed a one-µs all-atom molecular dynamics simulation in a water solvent box, utilizing
CHARMM36 force-field.The trimmed structure for MLH1 and MSH6 from the
STRUCTURE_SELECTION module was selected. MD simulations were performed individually
on domains collected after trimming the structure.
The system preparation before MD simulations was carried out. At first, a 10000 steps of energy
minimization step with a steep descent method was applied on the protein in the solvent box.
Next, we added counterions to neutralize the charge on the system followed by energy
minimization of 10000 steps with a steep descent method.
A 1 ns NVT equilibration was conducted using GROMACS (Abraham et al., 2015) with
positional restraints on the protein (-DPOSRES). The simulation used a 1 fs time step for
1,000,000 steps. Output for coordinates, velocities, and energies was saved every 5,000 steps,
with energy data logged every 1,000 steps. The Verlet cutoff scheme was applied with a 0.9 nm
cutoff for Coulomb and van der Waals interactions, using PME for electrostatics. Temperature
was maintained at 298 K using a v-rescale thermostat for both protein and solvent groups, with
a 0.006 ps relaxation time. Hydrogen bonds were constrained using the LINCS algorithm, and
initial velocities were generated at 298 K. This step equilibrated the solvent while keeping the
protein restrained.
The system underwent three equilibration steps using GROMACS. First, a 5 ns NPT
equilibration was performed with a Berendsen barostat to stabilize pressure, using a time step
of 2 fs, with temperature set to 298 K via a v-rescale thermostat applied to separate protein and
non-protein groups. The pressure was maintained at 1 bar with isotropic coupling, and hydrogen
33
==PAGE:34==
bonds were constrained using the LINCS algorithm. Electrostatics were handled with
particle-mesh Ewald (PME), and a 0.9 nm cutoff was applied for both Coulomb and van der
Waals interactions. In the second 5 ns NPT equilibration, the Parrinello-Rahman barostat was
used to improve pressure fluctuations, while keeping other settings unchanged. The final 5 ns
NVT equilibration was conducted with a reduced time step of 1 fs, maintaining constant volume
and temperature at 298 K, again using v-rescale coupling, to ensure thermal stability before
transitioning to production simulations.
For the production MD run, a simulation of 1000ns (1 µs) was performed using GROMACS. The
system utilized a 2 fs time step over 500,000,000 steps. Coordinates, velocities, and energies
were output every 200,000 steps, with compressed coordinate files saved every 10,000 steps.
The Verlet cutoff scheme was applied with a 0.9 nm cutoff for both Coulomb and van der Waals
interactions, using PME for electrostatics and optimizing FFT calculations. Temperature was
controlled at 298 K using the v-rescale thermostat with separate groups for protein and
non-protein components, each having a relaxation time of 0.2 ps. Hydrogen bonds were
constrained with the LINCS algorithm, and initial velocities were not generated. This setup
provided detailed trajectory data while maintaining temperature stability throughout the
simulation.
Histidine protonation
The protonation state of histidine was carefully selected during the preparation of the MD
simulations. The pdb2gmx utility from GROMACS version 2022 was employed to generate the
system topology which suggests the protonation state of histidine. These protonated states
were cross-referenced using H++ (Gordon et al., 2005)and visual inspection using pymol
(Schrödinger & DeLano, 2020).
Analysis of simulation
MD analysis was conducted to evaluate key structural properties of the protein system. RMSD
(Root Mean Square Deviation) was calculated for the backbone to assess structural stability by
aligning the trajectory with the initial frame and tracking deviations over time. The Radius of
Gyration (Rg) was computed to measure the protein’s compactness, providing insights into
folding or unfolding behavior. The PBC Minimum Distance (pbc_min_dist) was analyzed to
evaluate the proximity of the protein to the nearest solvent or ligand molecule under periodic
boundary conditions, highlighting potential interactions and binding events. Additionally, RMSF
(Root Mean Square Fluctuation) was calculated for each residue to identify regions of flexibility
and rigidity. All analyses were performed using GROMACS tools to capture comprehensive
global and local structural dynamics throughout the simulation.
We used NACCESS (Hubbard & Thornton, 1993)-based estimates for relative solvent
accessible surface (SASA) for side chain atoms and protein residues.We used PyInteraph2 to
calculate the electrostatic interactions such as hydrogen bonds, salt bridges and contacts
between hydrophobic side chains. We also employed PyInteraph2 (Sora et al., 2020) to obtain
an atom-contact-based Protein Structure Network (PSN) applied to MD ensemble.The key
34
==PAGE:35==
parameters included in network analysis were proximity coefficient of 1.0 for interaction
detection, a minimum interaction distance of 3.0 Å, and a cutoff distance of 4.5 Å for
interactions. Interaction prevalence was considered with a threshold of 50%, and interaction
strength was used for edge weighting. Furthermore, the downstream analysis of graphs was
implemented using the graph_analysis function of PyInteraph2. We also calculate the shortest
paths between each pair of nodes as implemented in NetworkX, using the path_analysis
function in PyInteraph2. We also calculate the normalized node betweenness centrality as
implemented in NetworkX using the centrality_analysis function.
MAVISp framework
The simple mode was used to carry out analysis on a single structure of protein whereas in
ensemble mode we carry out analysis on an ensemble of 25 frames. We used the modules from
this mode to analyze the stability, allosteric effects, post translational modification and
protein-protein interactions.
STABILITY_MODULE was used to analyze the stability effects of variants on the protein
structure. Here, we investigate the folding free energy with FoldX (Schymkowitz et al., 2005)
suite, cartddg2020 protocol with ref2015 energy function in Rosetta (Barlow et al., 2018). These
methods were implemented using workflows for high-throughput mutagenesis scans such as
MutateX (Tiberti et al., 2022), RosettaDDGPrediction (Sora et al., 2023) and RaSP (Blaabjerg et
al., 2023)on the dataset of variants collected. The BuildModel module from FoldX was utilized
over five independent runs whereas for Rosetta only one structure is generated at relax step
which is optimized on Cartesian space for five rounds .These methods were implemented using
workflows for high-throughput mutagenesis scans such as MutateX (Tiberti et al., 2022) and
RosettaDDGPrediction on the dataset of variants collected. We also employ a machine learning
model RaSP which performs on par with biophysics based methods and is less computationally
intensive. On collecting free energy predictions MAVISp applies a consensus approach to
classify the if mutations cause destabilizing, neutral, stabilizing or uncertain effects. MAVISp
classifies the variants as: stabilizing (ΔΔG <= - 3 kcal/mol with both methods, FoldX and
Rosetta or RaSP), destabilizing (ΔΔG >= 3 kcal/mol), neutral (-2 =<= ΔΔG <= 2 kcal/mol), and
uncertain (2 <= abs(ΔΔG) < 3 kcal/mol) as evidence (ref) suggests that variants with changes in
folding free energy below 3kcal/mol is unlikely to experience significant loss of stability or
protein function at a cellular level . A variant is classified uncertain if two methods have different
classification.
LONG_RANGE_MODULE was used to analyze the long-range structural communication which
underlies allosteric effects. This workflow is designed to be applied on an allosteric signaling
map generated from a structure-based statistical mechanic model implemented in Allosigma 2
(Tan et al., 2020). AlloSigma2 generates an elastic network model of the protein, where
residues are represented as balls and bonds as springs.The model classifies the variants into
up or down mutation based on the steric hindrance upon substitution.This workflow retains the
variants that are exposed to the solvent ( >20% relative solvent accessibility of the side chain)
with changes in allosteric free energy higher than 2 kcal/mol or lower than -2 kcal/mol to sites
that were at distances higher than 8 Å and which belongs to pockets as identified by Fpocket.
35
==PAGE:36==
This module classifies each mutation as destabilizing if it exclusively causes positive changes of
allosteric free energy, stabilizing if it exclusively causes negative changes of allosteric free
energy and mixed_effects if both conditions occur, or neutral if the mutation doesn’t cause any
significant change.
LOCAL_INTERACTION_MODULE in MAVISp framework identifies and utilizes structure from
INTERACTOME submodule for protein interactors and protein target by querying Mentha
database, PDB along with experimentally validated proteome wide Alphafold models.This
module is based on estimate of changes in binding free energy for variants at sites within 10Å
from the interaction site. The binding free energy effect of protein interaction is investigated
using the FoldX suite with the MutateX workflow. The workflow utilizes the BuildModel and
AnalyzeComplex module from FoldX suite and averages over five runs. Whereas, for Rosetta
the flexddg protocol with talaris energy function with RosettaDDGPrediction workflow is utilized
in this case. The workflow uses 35,000 backrub trials and a threshold for absolute score of
minimization convergence of 1 Rosetta Energy Unit (REU). Furthermore, the workflow then
generates an ensemble of 35 structures for each mutant variable and calculates the average
changes in binding free energy. The classification of effects on protein interaction with the same
consensus method applied in STABILITY_MODULE. However, this module classifies a variant
as stabilizing (both FoldX and Rosetta predict ΔΔG <= -1 kcal/mol), neutral (-1 kcal/mol < ΔΔG
< 1 kcal/mol) or destabilizing (ΔΔG >=1 kcal/mol). For cases with disagreement between the
methods or for variants where there is no prediction by both methods and SASA>=20% are
classified uncertain.
PTM_MODULE in the MAVISp framework currently only supports phosphorylation effects of
variants at phosphorylatable sites. The effects studied in this module are associated with loss or
change in phosphorylation sites affecting the regulation, stability and functionality of the protein.
This module uses information such as solvent accessibility of the mutation site, being part of a
phosphorylatable linear motif or predicted changes of free energy of stability or binding upon
mutation or upon modification to classify the mutation for each category. The module also takes
advantage of SLiMS from the INTERACTOME module as well as annotated in cancermuts
package. We also investigate if variants introduce new phosphorylation sites and the
accessibility for kinases to phosphorylate the residue. The classification in PTM_MODULE uses
a custom decision logic to classify each mutation as neutral, damaging, unknown effect,
potentially damaging or uncertain.
The MAVISp framework integrates pathogenicity scores from tools like DeMaSk, GEMME, EVE,
REVEL, and AlphaMissense using their default parameters. A standalone version of DeMaSk
was run with BLAST+2.13.0 following the public protocol on GitHub. GEMME predictions were
obtained through its web server, with JET iterations set to five. For EVE scores, MAVISp uses a
custom-built Snakemake pipeline, following the scripts, protocol, and parameters provided on
the EVE GitHub. AlphaMissense pathogenicity scores and classifications were sourced from the
dataset of predictions for all possible amino acid substitutions in UniProt canonical isoforms,
version 2.

Performance and metric analysis on simulation period
We conducted molecular dynamics (MD) simulations with three different durations (100 ns, 500
ns, and 1000 ns) to evaluate how simulation time affects protein structure predictions in folding
free energy. From these simulations, an ensemble of protein structures was generated of 25
frames, and MutateX was used to perform a folding free energy (ΔΔG) scan. We also applied a
clustering method on the trajectory of MD simulations from 1000ns for MutateX to perform
folding free energy calculations. Python 3.10 and bash scripts were employed to classify ΔΔG
values and assess the performance of each simulation time using a confusion matrix, with the
1000 ns simulation as the gold standard.
●
For analysis, only ΔΔG values classified as neutral or destabilizing, based on the
MAVISp framework within MutateX’s STABILITY_MODULE, were considered.
We compared MutateX’s predictive performance for the 100 ns and 500 ns simulations with the
1000 ns simulation by generating confusion matrices, with metrics defined as:
●
True Positive (TP): ΔΔG classified as neutral in both the shorter simulation (100 ns or
500 ns) and 1000 ns.
●
True Negative (TN): ΔΔG classified as destabilizing in both the shorter simulation and
1000 ns.
●
False Positive (FP): ΔΔG classified as neutral in the shorter simulation but destabilizing
in 1000 ns.
●
False Negative (FN): ΔΔG classified as destabilizing in the shorter simulation but neutral
in 1000 ns.
To further evaluate predictive performance, we calculated accuracy, precision, sensitivity
(recall), specificity, and the F1 score:
●
Accuracy: The proportion of correct classifications (both TP and TN) out of all
predictions.
●
Precision: The proportion of TP predictions out of all positive classifications (TP + FP).
●
Sensitivity (Recall): The proportion of TP predictions out of all actual positives (TP + FN).
●
Specificity: The proportion of TN predictions out of all actual negatives (TN + FP).
●
F1 Score: The harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balance between the
two metrics.
This comprehensive analysis allowed us to assess the predictive reliability of shorter simulations
compared to the gold standard 1000 ns simulation.
