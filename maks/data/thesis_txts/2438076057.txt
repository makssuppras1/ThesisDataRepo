1 Abstract  
 
Collecting data on our oceans is prohibitively expensive, yet information related to our 
oceans is crucial for a diverse group of users, from maritime industries, to those industries 
reliant on global supply chains, to scientists studying the impact of humanity on our 
planet. The shortage of oceanographic data is especially acute in certain areas due to 
holes in current data collection program coverage. These holes often correspond to 
areas where human activities, such as fishing, are most concentrated. 
The motivation for this Master’s Thesis was to investigate commercial fishing gear as a 
platform for the collection of oceanographic data. Even simple oceanographic data, 
such as temperature and salinity, is expensive and often labor intensive to collect. In 3.2 
Review of Oceanographic Data Collection Platforms, common and novel platforms are 
compared, followed by 3.3 Oceanographic Data Collection Programs Utilizing 
Commercial Fishing Gear, which reviews the few programs worldwide which currently 
collect data via fishing vessels.  
A case study follows, utilizing temperature data collected from a Danish shrimp trawler, 
fishing in the Skagerrak. First, a modeling exercise is presented, in which the negative 
effects of increasing temperature on shrimp catch rates are discovered. The model 
outputs demonstrate the utility for this type of fishery collected data for both the fishing 
industry and fisheries management. Integration of oceanographic data coupled with 
fine-scale commercial catch data would be a great step forward in advancing the 
goals of Ecosystem Based Fisheries Management (EBFM).  
Understanding species’ responses to shifting temperatures can help managers account 
for changes that are likely to occur due to climate change. Further applications and 
utility of fishery dependent data are discussed for both fisheries management and 
commercial operations. Second, the utility of this Danish trawler data was investigated 
from a broader perspective by comparing it to modeled temperatures from the FOAM 
AMM7 oceanographic model, 7 Comparison to FOAM AMM7 Modeled Data. Despite a 
lack of sub-surface data in the Skagerrak, the model temperatures performed 
surprisingly well. Moreover, oceanographers have a high demand for in-situ data to 
assimilate both into current models, and into future higher resolution models under 
development. Broader perspectives are examined looking at both the global 
distribution of ocean observation programs and trawling efforts, as well as their 
distribution in the greater North Sea area. 
Using commercial fishing gear as a data collection platform is a simple concept to 
understand and implement from a technical standpoint, and there is a complex 
network of both disparate and interdependent benefits that could be achieved. 
Oceanographic data collection via fishing vessels is today barely utilized; however, if 
properly implemented this data source could become a base for wholistic solutions to 
some of the world’s most pressing problems.

3 Background 
 
3.1 Introduction  
 
Humanity depends on the ocean, not only for fish and other food sources, but also for 
transportation, shipping, as a source of energy, and for the regulation of our climate. 
The shipping industry moves 90% of total global trade volume across the oceans (ICS 
2016). As of 2010, over one-third of oil and natural gas was extracted from offshore 
sources (WOR-1 2010). Offshore wind turbine installations in Europe increased over 100% 
in 2017, and wind now provides 11.6% of the EU’s total electricity (Wind Europe 2018). 
Global ocean circulation patterns play a vital role in regulating our climate on land. 
Without the Gulf Stream bringing warm equatorial water north to the Nordic region, 
much of Northern Europe would not be habitable (Palter 2015). Half of anthropogenic 
carbon has been sequestered in the ocean (Sabine, et al. 2004), and it has absorbed 
more than 90% of the excess heat from global warming (von Schuckmann, et al. 2016). 
Oceans provide food, tie the economy together, are a source of energy, and regulate 
climate. Understanding our oceans, and real-time monitoring to detect slight changes, 
is of the utmost importance to industries, power suppliers, fishermen, weather 
forecasters, scientists, and society as a whole. 
Satellite and the accompanying remote sensing techniques, triggered a revolution in 
oceanographic monitoring, modeling, and forecasting. Seeing the majority of the 
oceans simultaneously via satellite became possible, and a huge wealth of data on the 
surface of the oceans has become available in real-time. With reasonable accuracy, 
we can predict large basin scale conditions several years in advance (Payne, et al. 
2017). Marine weather forecasts provide accurate predictions, which increase the 
safety and reliability of maritime operations.  
However, we still have a long way to go before the oceans become truly predictable. 
The majority of losses at sea, including shipping, fishing, and transportation are still due 
to weather (Primorac and Parunov 2016).  While we are getting better at predicting 
weather and the oceans, climate change is pushing conditions outside of previously 
observed normal regimes. Anomalous conditions not only make predictions and 
forecasts more difficult and less reliable, but ever more important. 
Commercial fishing is subject not only to the un-predictability of the weather and sea-
state, but also to the classic boom or bust nature of fishing. Traditionally, overfishing was 
almost exclusively blamed for fisheries collapses. However, there has been a shift in 
thought that, while fishing is certainly an influence, environmental and ecological 
factors can be more important in governing the fluctuations of fish stocks (Cury, et al. 
2014, Lindegren, et al. 2013 ). For example, Lindegren, et al. (2013), reconstructed a 
millennium of sardine and anchovy explosions of growth and collapses corresponding 
to temperature fluctuations of the El Niño Southern Oscillation in the Pacific Ocean. 
==PAGE:7==
3-2 
 
      
 
Stock recruitment relationships, the relationship between the number of potential 
spawning parents and the number of young fish which are recruited into the fishery in 
following years, are a cornerstone of traditional fisheries management. The idea that 
more parents produce more offspring makes intuitive sense; however, in a global 
analysis of stock-recruit relationships, Cury, et al. (2014) found that on average, less than 
10% of the variability in recruitment could be explained by the spawning stock size, 
suggesting that the explanation to recruitment variability lies in ecosystem parameters 
affecting both the adult fecundity and the egg and larvae survival. The incorporation 
of factors from a widely defined ecosystem, including “the physical, biological, 
economic, and social interactions,” is the vision of Ecosystem Based Fisheries 
Management (NMFS 2017). 
Despite the recognized need to advance beyond traditional equilibrium single stock 
fisheries management, minimal progress has been made. While EBFM policy is formally 
adopted in most of the developed world, practice is far different. As of 2014, only 2% of 
marine stocks had any kind of ecosystem parameter incorporated into their 
management, recruitment or otherwise (Skern-Mauritzen, et al. 2016). Combining the 
results found by Cury, et al. (2014) and Skern-Mauritzen, et al. (2016), over 90% of 
variability in the reproductive capacity is left unexplained in more than 98% of 
managed fish stocks. Put another way, the number of new fish to enter a fishery is 90% 
effectively up to chance, while management controls the fishing effort to maintain the 
stock size which will only explain 10% of the stock size in the future. 
However, this fundamental failure in fisheries management is not due to lack of effort by 
fisheries scientists. Since the problem was first raised over 100 years ago (Hjort 1914), 
linking stock health to ecosystem parameters has been the ever-elusive goal of fisheries 
management (Myers 1998, Pepin 2016). A path to solving the stock-recruitment puzzle is 
slowly forming. Following a revolution in first oceanographic observation technology 
and then forecasting, a first cohort of successful marine ecological forecasts has been 
born (Payne, et al. 2017). Many marine forecasts are not for recruitment forecasting, 
and ecosystem effects drive fish stock dynamics at all life stages. The recruitment issue 
is, however, a particularly striking example of the need for incorporation of ecosystem 
effects in fisheries management. Natural mortality, growth rates, distribution, and 
phenology are all parameters which fluctuate due to ecosystem conditions but are 
assumed to be constant in traditional fisheries management (Lindegren, et al. 2013 , 
Hobday, et al. 2016, Jennings, Kaiser and Reynolds 2001). 
Once again, data is a limiting factor for the successful development and 
implementation of EBFM. A wide range of data types are needed for true EBFM, 
ranging from economic and social to biological and chemical (NMFS 2017); however, 
this thesis will focus on oceanographic and commercial fishing operations data. 
Hydrographic data, one of the simpler forms of oceanographic data concerning 
physical characteristics of water such as temperature and salinity, is the primary subset 
of oceanographic data type that this thesis focuses on. Hydrographic data is used both 
directly to understand species responses to hydrographic conditions, and indirectly via 
==PAGE:8==
3-3 
 
      
 
oceanographic models which can feed fisheries forecasts or advice (Payne, et al. 
2017). However, the hydrographic measurements used for these forecasts are rarely 
taken down in the water column where the fish are, but rather are taken at the surface 
where data is easier to collect, Payne, et al. (2017) and Hobday, et al. (2016), 
introducing a disconnect that increases the potential for failure. There is a serious 
shortage in oceanographic data, especially data from beneath the sea surface. The 
other data type that will be discussed is fisheries operations data (e.g. catches and 
locations). 
The shortage of oceanographic data creates a bottleneck for scientific 
advancements. The primary reason for the oceanographic data bottleneck is cost. A

underutilized, but direct, method of obtaining data precisely where the fish are. The

are reviewed below in 3.3 Oceanographic Data Collection Programs Utilizing 
Commercial Fishing Gear.

ocean observing network. 
 
3.2 Review of Oceanographic Data Collection Platforms  
 
While this thesis focuses on hydrographic data, oceanographic data as a whole is a 
broad category of data spanning bottom, or benthic, geological features and 
composition, surface metrological measurements like wind speed and wave state, sea 
ice coverage, to biological data like plankton and primary production. As a 
==PAGE:9==
3-4 
 
      
 
consequence of variety of oceanographic data types, the equipment necessary for 
taking measurements varies substantially, with some methods more specialized and 
cost effective, while others like research vessels are flexible but expensive. 
3.2.A Research Vessels: 
There are a wide range of methods and accompanying hardware for collecting 
oceanographic data. Traditionally, research vessel (R/Vs) deploying Conductivity 
Temperature and Depth (CTD) arrays were the primary method of collecting 
hydrographic data. R/Vs are able to perform the widest range of sampling techniques 
including, but not limited to, bottom trawl surveys for fish stock assessment, plankton 
recording, acoustic data collection both for fish stock assessment and for benthic 
mapping, and benthic sediment sampling. There are only approximately 300 R/Vs 
operating globally (Lauro, et al. 2014), due in most part to the prohibitive cost of owning 
and operating these vessels. The cost of operating the DTU Aqua R/V Dana is 
approximately 192,000 DKK per day ($30,000 USD) (Lisbjerg 2018). 
 
3.2.B Moored Buoys: 
A range of moored buoys are employed as platforms for sensors to measure 
both hydrographic and climatic variables from just above the sea surface. Many 
moored buoys have connected sensors going down the anchor cable to obtain data 
at depth (WHOI 2018). Buoys are ideal for creating time series of data from a single 
location. Data is usually transmitted automatically via satellite. Instruments need 
servicing on the order of twice a year via repair vessels, which are costly to operate 
(WHOI 2018).  
 
3.2.C Satellites: 
Using remote sensing techniques from satellites triggered a revolution in 
oceanography. Sea Surface Temperature (SST), salinity, primary production, and 
surface circulation currents can all be measured via satellites, NOAA (2017), on a 
previously unprecedented scale. A network of satellites is able to simultaneously 
observe the majority of the surface of the earth, providing a wealth of real-time data. 
Satellites require in-situ measurements to provide calibration for the satellite remote 
sensing observations in order provide accurate data. However, satellites are limited by 
not being able to see through clouds and by only obtaining data from the surface of 
the ocean. Furthermore, satellite remote sensing accuracy decreases in shallow coastal 
waters, as well at higher latitudes moving towards the poles (Brewin, et al. 2017). 
 
3.2.D Argo Floats: 
Argo floats are autonomous free-floating data collection platforms which adjust 
their buoyancy to create profiles up to 2000 meters deep (Argo 2018). Floats are 
primarily outfitted with CTD sensor arrays and have a designed lifespan of four years, 
==PAGE:10==
3-5 
 
      
 
after which they are not recovered. Argo floats take roughly 10 days to complete a 
profile cycle, at the end of which the float surfaces and transmits its data via satellite 
(Argo 2018). As of May 2018, there were 3,837 floats in operation producing over 
100,000 profiles per year (JCOMMOPS 2018), see Figure 8-1 in 3.2.G Opportunities for 
data collection via Fishing Vessels below. 
Argo floats were a drastic improvement in global ocean observation network (von 
Schuckmann, et al. 2016), with high precision profiles, which previously needed to be 
done via research vessels, being reduced in cost to approximately $200 USD per profile 
(Argo 2018). However, there are still serious limitations to the Argo float capabilities. 
Because the instruments are operating continuously without recalibration, there are 
serious issues with accuracy drift, especially with salinity (Ingleby and Huddleston 2007). 
Furthermore, Argo floats are only operable in the open ocean, resulting in holes in the 
observation network. These holes are often in marginal shelf seas often associated with 
high maritime activity where it is especially important to have data, such as the Bering 
Sea, the Indonesian Sea, The Sea of Okhotsk, the Grand Banks, the Argentine Shelf, the 
Yellow Sea, the Barents Sea, and the North Sea/Skagerrak (von Schuckmann, et al. 
2016, JCOMMOPS 2018). The holes in the Argo network are discussed in greater detail in 
8 Spatial distribution of current Observation Network compared with Trawling distribution 
 
3.2.E Recently Developed Methods:  
A host of autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), remotely operated 
underwater vehicles (ROVs), and autonomous surface vehicles have been developed 
for the collection of surface climate, hydrographic at depth, acoustic biomass, and 
acoustic benthic data. While these newer innovations provide cheaper alternatives for 
the collection of many of the data types formerly collected via scientific vessels, they 
can still be quite expensive. Saildrone, an autonomous surface vehicle that can collect 
a wide range of data types, costs $2,500 USD per day to lease for operation (Kwok 
2017), and does not have substantial underwater collection capabilities. A Wave Glider 
is an autonomous device made by Liquid Robotics with better sub-surface capabilities; 
however, the Wave Gliders costs approximately $300,000 per unit and $3000 on a daily 
use basis (Lam 2012). 
 
A range of other creative platforms and methods are utilized for the collection of 
oceanographic data. Aquatic animals can be tagged with small sensor arrays, 
providing data on both hydrographic parameters and animal behavior. Mammals 
provide an opportunity for satellite data transmission when they surface to breath. For 
example, bathythermographs have been outfitted on elephant seals (Ingleby and 
Huddleston 2007). Recreation is used as an avenue to employ citizen scientists on craft 
ranging from sailboats to surfboards (Brewin, et al. 2017). The United States Military has 
been developing sensors to use with icebergs as platforms, The Economist (2018) in 
order to monitor the polar regions, which can be especially difficult to monitor due to 
ice and cloud cover.

3.2.F Ships of Opportunity: 
An eXpendable BathyThermograph (XBT) is a simple probe that measures 
temperature as it falls through the water column at calculated rates to a specified 
depth, normally 460, 760, or 1800 meters (Ingleby and Huddleston 2007). The cost of an 
XBT increases with operational depth, with per unit pricing ranging from $315(USD) to 
$666(USD) at 460 and 1800 meters respectively (Sea-Bird Scientific 2018).  After 
deployment, an XBT remains connected to its vessel via copper wire and transmit the 
data back to the vessel via this wire (Talley, et al. 2011). However, once the XBT reaches 
the end of its wire, it continues to fall to the sea floor and is never recovered, hence 
expendable. As of 2013, approximately 20,000 XBTs were being put into the ocean 
annually, often by a Ship of Opportunity (SO), which uses non-research vessels including 
cargo ships, ferries, and recreational vessels to collect oceanographic data (NOAA 
2013).  
A variety of other SO programs exist. The most successful and widely 
implemented being the ferrybox. Ferries follow the same path on a regular interval, 
creating the opportunity for a consistent and regular time series of data. A ferrybox is an 
automated flow through system which takes measurements of physical and 
geochemical parameters at given time intervals while the ferry is underway (Haller, et 
al. 2015). Using fishing vessels as a platform for data collection is a variation of SO 
platform. 
3.2.G Opportunities for data collection via Fishing Vessels  
Using fishing vessels and their gear as a data collection platform is currently an 
underutilized method. The limited number of operational programs are reviewed in the 
following section, 3.3 Oceanographic Data Collection Programs Utilizing Commercial 
Fishing Gear. The fishermen are already doing the work of putting their gear out in the 
ocean and then collecting it again at regular intervals, offering a potential ride for a 
small sensor package. Traditional R/V based CTDs are prohibitively large and required 
skilled operators, which may have been an early roadblock to the implementation of 
fishing vessels for the collection of simple hydrographic data. However, sensors have 
decreased by orders of magnitude in size, and can function autonomously for years at 
a time, as demonstrated by Argo floats. 
While fishing vessels and gear could never have the diversity of collection capabilities 
that a R/V has, collection of simple baseline hydrographic parameters as well as 
acoustic data is feasible. This thesis will primarily focus on the baseline hydrographic 
parameters of temperature coupled with a depth reading. While temperature and 
depth are the simplest types of data, they are both prohibitively expensive to collect 
and the baseline data type, hence the most important. Below, Table 2 shows the 
comparative costs and capabilities of the discussed oceanographic data collection

direct comparison to fishing vessels as a platform for collection of data at depth. Argo 
floats are among the cheapest platforms for collection, and have quite high instrument

Of common commercial fishing gear types, trawl gear is the optimal choice for 
oceanographic data collection. Unlike many other gear types, the trawl net is 
constantly being towed through the water column. The net starts at the surface as it is 
set off of the vessel and travels down the water column until the desired fishing depth, 
often the bottom. At the end of the tow, the gear is hauled back up the water column 
and onto the vessel. The setting and hauling back provide the opportunity for two 
measurement profiles down and then up the water column, with depth or bottom 
temperature measurement in between. These downward and then upward profiles, 
combined with relatively high frequency of tows, make trawl gear the ideal fishing gear 
for hydrographic sensors. The systems and hardware already existing on most fishing 
vessels provides many auxiliary benefits to data collection, such as a power supply, 
large antennae for transmitting data, as well as human eyes to check that everything is 
functioning properly. Autonomous data collection platform such as drones, gliders, and 
Argo floats are all limited in power supply and communication capabilities, and failures 
are extremely difficult to remedy. 
Many commercial fisheries already monitor temperatures where they are fishing via 
sensors in the trawl net headrope sonar, due to fishermen’s knowledge that 
temperature affects fish behavior. Most, if not all, of the Danish shrimp trawlers in 
Skagen have trawl net sonar with temperature and depth sensors. However, none of 
this data is saved. Due to the minimal hardware likely needed to collect data with 
fishing gear, once set up, the cost per profile would likely be lower than any current 
offering presented above in Table 2. If the connection is made between the fishermen 
and scientists who need the data the fishermen can get, and the fishermen start saving 
their data, everyone stands to benefit. 
 
3.3 Oceanographic Data Collection Programs Utilizing 
Commercial Fishing Gear 
 
3.3.A The Fishery Observing System (FOS) and Fishery and 
Oceanography Observing System (FOOS) 
FOS was started in 2003 by the Marine Sciences Institute of the National Research 
Council of Italy (Martinelli, et al. 2016). This program consisted of purse seiner and 
trawler vessels in the Adriatic Sea equipped with Star-Oddi temperature sensors. In 2013 
the FOS was upgraded to the Fishery and Oceanography Observing System (FOOS) 
and expanded to the Sicilian Straight between Sicily and Tunisia (Patti, et al. 2016). The 
FOOS upgrade included substantial hardware and systems improvements: nke probes 
with the capability to record data on more parameters replaced the Star-Oddi sensors, 
the addition of electronic fishermen’s logbooks that integrated with the rest of the 
system, additional sensors for meteorological data, as well as near real-time data 
transmission capabilities (Martinelli, et al. 2016). Data is transmitted at the end of each 
fishing trip via a land-based station (Patti, et al. 2016). FOOS has developed a modular 
==PAGE:14==
3-9 
 
      
 
system designed to work with any vessel, automatically collecting and saving data with 
no work necessary from the fishermen. In addition to hydrographic data, the FOOS 
program collects a variety of surface weather data such as wind speed and direction, 
dew point, atmospheric pressure, and wave state (Patti, et al. 2016). Fishermen 
volunteer for the program in return for data analytics created from the data they 
collect (Patti, et al. 2016), which can be used to improve the efficiency of their fishing 
operations. The initial goals of the FOS/FOOS program were the improvement of 
oceanographic models and forecasts; however, the implications of this data for both 
fisheries operations and EBFM are also recognized (Ydoğdu, et al. 2016 , Patti, et al. 
2016).  
3.3.B RECOPESCA 
RECOPESCA is a program started in 2005 by the French Institute for Exploitation of the 
Sea (IFREMER) in cooperation with nke Instrumentation. Approximately 300 French 
fishing vessels, including trawlers, longliners, pot fishers, and gillnetters are outfitted with 
sensors (nke Instrumentation 2018). Different sensors have been designed for each 
fishery and gear type. Temperature and salinity, along with fishery specific data such as 
soak time, are recorded. The primary goal of the RECOPESCA project is the 
advancement of an ecosystem approach to fisheries management (Leblond, et al. 
2010).  
3.3.C Environmental Monitoring on Lobster Traps & Large Trawlers 
(eMOLT) 
The National Oceanographic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) and Northeast 
Fisheries Science Center, both in the USA, run the Environmental Monitoring on Lobster 
Traps (eMOLT) program which was started in 2001. The eMOLT program puts simple 
temperature and depth sensors, custom made by Lowell Instruments LLC, on 
approximately 100 lobster fisher’s pots along the coast of New England, USA (Manning 
2018 ). A more recent addition to the eMOLT program has been equipping trawlers 
both with trawl door temperature loggers and surface weather sensors. Currently, 17 
trawlers are equipped with door temperature loggers, and two vessels have surface 
weather arrays. All trawler data is sent from the vessels in near real-time via satellite 
(Manning 2018 ). The weather data is used by the US National Weather Service.  
The eMOLT program is intended to aid both fisheries science and the oceanographic 
and weather forecasting community; however, to date, the data has been utilized 
more in the oceanographic world. The eMOLT lobster pot data is assimilated into the 
Finite-Volume Community Ocean Model (FVCOM) (Li, et al. 2017). Furthermore, the 
eMOLT sensors were the first to detect an anomalous warming shift in the Gulf Stream in 
2011 (Gawarkiewicz, et al. 2012). This shift caused drastic consequences for not only 
marine ecosystems and fisheries, but also likely led to the third warmest New England 
winter on record (Chen, et al. 2014, Fisher 2015).

3.3.D  The Nearshore Temperature Monitoring Project 
The Nearshore Temperature Monitoring Project was started in 2011 by the Fishermen 
and Scientists Research Society (FSRS) in Nova Scotia, Canada. The goal of this project 
is primarily to collect data in order to understand the temperature effects of mackerel 
spawning migrations and lobster phenology, or seasonal lifecycle events (Fishermen 
and Scientists Research Society 2012). The data is also being fed into the Northeastern 
Regional Association of Coastal Ocean Observing System, operated in the United 
States. There is minimal information available on this project, with no information on the 
sensors or data collection methods. 
 
3.4 Temperature data from a Danish shrimp trawler 
 
The value and feasibility of oceanographic data collected via fishing vessel was 
explored in detail using a temperature data set collected in 2013-2014 using a Danish 
shrimp trawler fishing in the Skagerrak between Denmark, Norway, and Sweden. The 
targeted species is Pandalus borealis (Pb), which is a species of deep water shrimp that 
spans the globe in most northern latitudes from Alaska in the US to the Barents Sea off of 
northern Norway. 
Relationships to environmental and oceanographic conditions have been observed in 
Pb for many years (Rasmussen 1953, Parson and Colburne 2000, Oullet, et al. 2017 ). A 
high sensitivity to temperature has been documented multiple times ranging widely 
across the Pb distribution. For example, off of West Greenland in the colder northern 
extents of Pb ranges, Wieland (2005) observed an increase in recruitment and growth 
with increasing bottom temperatures. The Gulf of Maine in northeastern US is at the 
southern warmer edge of the Pb range, and in contrast to Greenland, recruitment of Pb 
in the Gulf of Maine has been shown to decrease with increasing temperatures 
(Richards 1996). The Gulf of Maine and the Skagerrak are both at the southern warm 
edge of the Pb preferential habitat window (Shumway, et al. 1985, Worm and Myers 
2003). The documented sensitivity to temperature makes temperature data potentially 
relevant for Pb management and advice.  
The primary goal of the following analysis was to determine if there was any relationship 
between temperature and catch rates. Higher catch rates would be indicative of 
preferential conditions for the Pb and could therefore be used by management to gain 
insight into how Pb will fare with impending temperature shifts due to climate change. 
The secondary goal was to explore the utility of fine-scale fishery dependent catch 
data coupled with temperature data.

4.1 Data Collection 
 
Data was collected from the 6th of April 2013 to the 12th of June 2014 aboard the 
Emmely Pilegaard S 226, sailing out of Skagen, the northern most point in Denmark. A 
temperature reading was taken every minute using a Hobo sensor which was attached 
to the trawl head rope. Over the data collection time period, Emmely Pilegaard

6 Shrimp Modeling discussion 
 
The predicted higher catches in shallower in-shore waters (Figure 5-1 & Figure 5-2) may 
be due to the shoreward spawning migration exhibited by Pb. However, the observed 
timing of this spawning event in late summer does not exactly match the literature that 
puts spawning time in the late fall for this region (Shumway, et al. 1985). The second 
large percentage model does not support this theory, as only large females make the 
shoreward spawning migration (Shumway, et al. 1985). The large percentage model 
barely shows any movement of the larger shrimp. If anything, this model predicts a 
deep migration in August, contrary to spawning theory. 
The GAM logged catch model showed that by separating the spatial and seasonal 
pattern, the effects of increasing temperatures were decreasing catches, in contrast to 
Figure 4-2. At the lower temperatures recorded during the two years of fishing is indeed 
where the highest density of shrimp is likely to occur. Higher catch rates at lower 
temperatures indicate Pb have a preference for lower temperature waters. 
Furthermore, this preference for cold water can be extended to the conclusion that 
cooling of Skagerrak bottom waters would be beneficial to Pb, while warming will be 
detrimental. While the majority of the shrimp seem to prefer the colder water, the 
percentage of large shrimp is higher in the warmer water. This result is contrary to initial 
intuition, as larger shrimp have higher mobility and a greater ability to move into their 
preferred habitat. Several studies have documented shrimp segregation by size 
grouping (Shumway, et al. 1985), which would affect and could help explain the results 
seen in the large percentage model, Figure 5-3. If the larger shrimp separate themselves 
from the smaller shrimp, they are separating themselves from the majority. 
The mapped predictions demonstrate that fine scale forecasting for shrimp distributions 
is feasible if the appropriate fine scale data is collected. The large shrimp percentage 
model shows that the larger shrimp get fished out in the areas where the heaviest 
fishing occurs. This makes sense, given the logistic curve selectivity pattern of trawl nets. 
The modeling results demonstrate value and utility of fine scale fishery dependent data, 
despite several limitations to using this type of data for predictions. The largest issue 
comes from non-random sampling. Fishermen only fish where they think shrimp will be. 
Furthermore, this data set is only one vessel for less than two complete years. A project 
to collect similar data for more or all shrimp vessels fishing in the Skagerrak would likely 
provide a wealth of results and far more applicable and reliable predictions and 
insights into phenology and ecosystem drivers of Pb than those presented above. 
 
6.1 Uses for fishing industry 
 
The seasonal depth distribution shifts are especially useful for fishermen, who might 
modify their fishing behavior to follow the shrimp to deeper waters in spring, and then to 
==PAGE:26==
6-21 
 
      
 
shallower waters during the late summer. The model also shows the greatest potential 
catches are in the coldest recorded bottom waters. However, there is a limit to this 
colder water preference, it just lies outside of the observed data. In conversations with 
the Danish shrimp fleet in Skagen regarding this work, it was noted that one year the 
bottom temperature was around 4°C and there were no shrimp. 
The predicted shifts in catch rates indicating distribution shifts demonstrate that data 
analytics and mapped predictions can be used to optimize fisheries operations. Current 
or predicted oceanographic conditions can be fed into a model to predict the best 
fishing grounds given the season and conditions. Having an idea of where the fish are 
likely to be not only helps fishermen have higher catches, but also spend less fuel and 
waste less time looking in the wrong areas for fish. 
 
6.2 Implications for fisheries management 
 
The models presented above demonstrate both the utility of high resolution fishery 
dependent data and the utility of oceanographic data coupled with that fishery data. 
The catch relationship with temperature indicates that increasing temperatures will not 
be favorable for either Pb or the fishermen who depend upon them. The temperature 
change predicted by long term oceanographic forecasts could be used in conjunction 
with the catch model to predict how the Skagerrak Pb distribution may look several 
years to decades into the future. Extrapolating these temperature relationships to other 
southern Pb regions such as the Gulf of Maine, is not an unreasonable extension and 
helps support of the theory of stock collapses due to the Gulf of Maine waters warming 
faster than 99% of the rest of the oceans (Kavanaugh, et al. 2017 ). 
The results of the large percentage model indicate that fisheries management may 
want to consider an increase in minimum mesh size. If the smaller shrimp that are now 
getting caught for animal feed are able to pass through the net mesh, they will have a 
chance to grow into large expensive shrimp suitable for the human market. Another 
alternative is a sorting grid which allows the smaller shrimp to escape, as is used in the 
Swedish Pb fishery. 
The mapped model outputs in Figure 5-1 are species distribution maps, which are 
crucial for the implementation of EBFM. Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) are a straight 
forward example of where species distribution maps can be used to protect seasonal 
spawning grounds or distribution of threatened species (Grüss, Chagris, et al. 2018). 
More sophisticated management techniques also rely of distribution maps. Spatially 
explicit ecosystem modeling frameworks such as OSMOSE, Ecospace, and Atlantis 
require distribution maps to map overlap between functional groups (Grüss, Thorson, et 
al. 2018, Grüss, Drexler, et al. 2018). While this shrimp distribution map only maps one 
species, overlapping species maps can illuminate seasonal and spatial trophic 
interactions, which is a key factor for understanding ecosystem interactions (Grüss, 
==PAGE:27==
6-22 
 
      
 
Thorson, et al. 2018). Furthermore, species distribution maps are used in biophysical 
modeling frameworks such as the Connectivity Modeling System (CMS) (Paris, et al. 
2013). Biophysical modeling frameworks are used to link physical parameters, such as 
temperature, to biological processes such as recruitment. Sensor data can be used to 
both improve understanding of biophysical links and to increase accuracy of 
hydrographic models. Then maps of the predicted oceanographic conditions can be 
used to predict future distributions and biological responses to environmental 
conditions. 
While there are inherent statistical issues using only fisheries data, the annual bottom 
trawl survey of Skagerrak shrimp, run by the Norwegian Institute of Marine Research, has 
its own set of problems. The primary issue is that this survey is only run once a year, 
currently in January (ICES 2016). While the fishing vessel data is not spatially randomly 
distributed, it is far better distributed temporally than the annual survey. ICES does take 
into account commercial landings data on a quarterly basis, but that is the only source 
of seasonal or fishery dependent data (ICES 2016). A hybrid sampling program utilizing 
both fine scale fishery data, and an official bottom trawl survey would provide the best 
of both worlds, as demonstrated by (Grüss, Thorson, et al. 2018). The fisheries 
independent trawl survey could fill in the gaps in distribution where shrimp are less likely 
to be, resulting in less extrapolation and more reliable predictions than those produced 
above. Fisheries independent trawl surveys also provide a consistent baseline to check 
for changes in fisheries catchability, for example, from gear technology improvement or 
smarter fishing practices. Independent trawl surveys are limited in resolution by the high 
cost of research vessel time, and often run into weather issues completing their 
assigned survey locations (Søvik and Thangstad 2016). While fishery data provides a 
cost-effective method to increase the resolution, getting fishermen to agree is likely to 
be a challenge in some instances. 
There were no “potential ecosystem drivers” discussed at the last ICES Pb benchmark 
meeting in 2016 (ICES 2016).1 While the modeling results indicate potential for 
assessment and advice based on including shrimp-temperature relationships, larger 
improvements would come from simply having spatially and temporally accurate 
catch data. There is substantial effort put into quantifying spatial and temporal fishing 
effort through less direct methods such as AIS and satellite data (Kroodsma, et al. 2018 , 
Eigaard, et al. 2016). 
Shifts in hydrography have caused large scale ecosystem shifts which cascade through 
interconnected trophic levels (Stige and Kvile 2017). Ecosystems that shift to regimes far 
outside any conditions previously observed render the traditional relationships, fisheries 
assessment, and management techniques less effective. For instance, mackerel in the 
North Atlantic underwent an abrupt range shift northward to the waters around 
Iceland, where there had never previously been any mackerel present. Rights to fish the 
mackerel stocks around Iceland sparked years of conflict between the EU nations, who

used to be able to fish the mackerel in their old distribution in international waters, and 
between Iceland, who now had the mackerel in the waters in their Exclusive Economic

Environmental data coupled with higher resolution catch data can enable managers 
to be more agile in their decision making, rather than relying on outdated relationships 
and models as happened in the Icelandic mackerel crisis. More data delivered faster 
can warn of changes earlier in order to enable smoother adaptation. 
 
6.3 Model extensions 
 
One of the largest hurdles in utilization of the Danish shrimp trawler data was the lack of 
a depth sensor coupled to the temperature measurements. The lack of a depth data 
made selecting the data points where fishing was occurring, as opposed to setting or 
hauling back the gear, quite difficult, see 12.2 Catch Log Book data merging. 
Adding catch sensor data or analyzing the trawl net sonar data for each tow could be 
a way to further increase the data resolution. Many of the tows were up to 12 hours 
long, and quite a bit of ground, differing habitats, and wide hydrographic ranges could 
be covered in a single tow. Using catch sensor data is would be possible to pinpoint the 
exact time on the tow, and thus the conditions, in which the shrimp entered the net. 
Integration of fishery independent trawl data points would help the predictions. For 
example, a few tows that are too deep for shrimp habitat would help to set realistic 
limits to the predictions, rather than forcing the model to extrapolate beyond its range 
of depths. Using fishery data provides higher resolution where the fish and shellfish are; 
and therefore, where the higher resolution Is needed. Using fishery independent trawl 
survey data, which are often conducted on an annual or semi-annual basis, paints a 
coarse picture of distributions and stock sizes. Using just fishery data is likely to create a 
host of issues. Using both data sources would provide the best way to get accurate 
stock assessments, especially as management strategies move from traditional single 
stock approaches to EBFM.    
Sediment type is quite important for shrimp habitat. However, precise enough maps of 
benthic habitats are not currently available. The mapping of habitat type and 
characteristics is another field which could be greatly aided by existing sonar data 
collected from fishing boats. Olex, a Norwegian sonar and software company, has a 
system in place to crowdsource sonar data from fishermen to map the sea floor (Olex 
2018 ). Furthermore, Centre for Environment, Fisheries and Aquaculture Science (CEFAS) 
in the UK is working on a machine learning neural-network habitat classification using 
Olex data (Fincham, J. Unpublished). This work classifying large areas of benthic 
==PAGE:29==
6-24 
 
      
 
habitats would be a great step forward for species distribution mapping. Benthic 
habitat data could allow models similar to the previously presented log catch and 
large shrimp percentage models to rely on habitat type rather than latitude and 
longitude to not only map the species distributions, but to understand the underlying 
mechanisms behinds the Pb habitat and distribution preferences. 
While temperature is the baseline hydrographic parameter, the collection of a wider 
range of parameters via sensors, e.g. salinity, dissolved oxygen, and pH would be 
relatively simple to add onto a temperature collection system. All of these have direct 
effects on the physiology of commercially exploited species, as well as on larger 
interconnected ecosystem factors. 
Simple temperature recordings coupled with fishing vessel catch data have great 
potential for use both in the fishing industry and for management. However, 
temperature data is just the tip of the iceberg. Fishing gear as a platform for sensors and 
higher resolution fisheries operations data is a largely untapped wealth of easily 
obtained information. Hydrographic data coupled with commercial catches is a 
baseline for solutions to many of the problems which have plagued fisheries 
management for over a century. The wholistic solution of fisheries collected 
hydrographic data extends far beyond the world of fisheries operations and fisheries 
management. The broader demands for oceanographic data create an opportunity 
for fishermen. In addition to fish, fishermen could begin “fishing” for data as a 
supplementary catch and source of income.

7 Comparison to FOAM AMM7 Modeled Data  
 
7.1 FOAM AMM7 Description 
 
Modeled bottom temperature data was extracted from the Copernicus Marine data 
portal (Copernicus 2018). This data comes from a re-analysis hindcast using the 
Forecasting Ocean Assimilation Model 7km Atlantic Margin model (FOAM AMM7). This 
model domain is a rectangle from 40°N, 20°W to 65°N, 13°E, using a latitude longitude 
grid of 1/15° and 1/9° resolution, which equates to approximately 7km squares 
(Copernicus 2018). The FOAM AMM7 model is a hybrid of the Nucleus for European 
Modelling of the Ocean (NEMO) model and the European Regional Seas Ecosystem 
Model (ERSEM) (Copernicus 2018). 
 
The FOAM AMM7 model assimilates data from a variety of sources using an 
“optimal interpolation scheme” to iteratively correct for model offsets from measured 
data (Haller, et al. 2015). Only SST measurements, coming from both satellites and in-situ 
measurements are currently assimilated into the model. Assimilated in-situ 
measurements come from a variety of sources including moored buoys, drifters, and 
ships (Haller, et al. 2015, King 2017). The ship data comes not just from R/Vs, but also SOs, 
including some ferrybox systems, see earlier review of oceanographic data collection, 
3.2 Review of Oceanographic Data Collection Platforms. Initially, only 2D surface 
measurements could be assimilated into the AMM7 model; however, as of 2017, the UK 
Met Office has developed an effective 3D assimilation scheme (King 2017). The in-situ 
measurements available for real-time assimilation in the Skagerrak can be seen below

7.2 Statistical Measures of Model Performance 
 
Each fishing measured temperature data point was compared with the modeled 
temperatures. However, the modeled temperatures are on a 7km grid at 24-hour 
intervals. The modeled temperatures were linearly interpolated in order to match the 
longitude, latitude, and time of the measured data points. Interpolation was performed

measurement associated with the trawl door temperature measurements, converting 
the measured point to potential temperature would have been simple. Instead, the

conservatively low bottom salinity of 33 PSU (Kristiansen and Aas 2015). The measured

measured temperatures were in potential temperature, direct comparisons were valid; 
however, the imprecision of the potential temperature conversion process is certainly a 
notable source of error.

7.3 FOAM AMM7 Comparison Discussion 
 
Given that there are no in-situ depth measurements currently assimilated into the FOAM 
AMM7 model, the modeled bottom temperatures are impressively accurate, a 
testament to the skill and sophistication of oceanographers and their modern methods. 
With the recent addition of 3D assimilation scheme, the AMM7 SST and depth 
temperature prediction RMSE has been reduced by 8%, and 25%, respectively (King 
2017). A 25% reduction in RMSE at depth is a drastic improvement for the AMM7 model 
and validates the need for more in-situ data at depth to take full advantage of this 
improved assimilation scheme. 
Even if the FOAM AMM7 model was perfect, 7km resolution is far from what is needed 
to capture the variety and fluctuations of conditions on the seafloor which make a 
==PAGE:36==
7-31 
 
      
 
difference to Pb habitat preferences. Fortunately, higher resolution data enables higher 
resolution modeling. The Met Office is currently building higher resolution AMM15 model 
(King 2017). Accompanying the development of the AMM15 model and the 
innovations in assimilation techniques is a call for more depth profiles from more 
sources, with the suggestion of fishing boats (King 2017). 
As the effects of global warming are beginning to change the climate at 
unprecedented rates, the necessity for oceanographic data becomes ever more 
critical. In both the Atlantic and the Pacific Oceans the basin-scale circulation patterns 
which we consider stable moderators of our climate are changing (Caesar, et al. 2018 , 
Bond, et al. 2015). Anomalous conditions force models like the AMM7 to unreliably 
extrapolate rather than interpolate within previously observed data. The best way to 
anticipate and mitigate against these shifts is to keep models grounded with more in-
situ real-time data.

9 Conclusions 
 
Fishing gear is a barely utilized platform for the collection of valuable oceanographic 
data that has incredible potential to contribute to solutions to endemic issues plaguing 
fisheries management, as well as contribute to the alleviation of problems in shipping, 
weather forecasting, and climate change monitoring.  
The high value of temperature and other hydrographic data is a reason for fishermen to 
save their data with corresponding times, depths, and GPS coordinates. Fishermen 
motivated to save and share their data could provide extremely high-resolution fishing 
activity data, compared to the commonly available landings data. Fishing activity data 
is a perennial point of conflict between fishermen and managers. However, a program 
where fishermen would get paid for the data and see the value in their efforts, rather 
than a government mandate of cameras or onboard scientific observers, would likely 
change that dynamic. 
There is an ever-strengthening call for more and a wider range of data necessary for 
the implementation of EBFM (Grüss, Drexler, et al. 2018, Kavanaugh, et al. 2017 , Patti, et 
al. 2016, NMFS 2017). Furthermore, advances from other fields for the utilization of “big 
data,” such as machine learning approaches, make handling complex and 
interconnected data sets with non-linear responses more feasible than ever before 
(ICES WKMLEARN 2018). Advances in modeling, data analytics techniques, and EBFM 
can make managers and modelers more agile, enabling them to better help the fishing 
industry, and others reliant on oceanographic data, adapt more rapidly to regime-shifts 
caused by global warming. 
Despite being in their infancy, oceanographic data collection programs in conjunction 
with fishermen have already demonstrated their utility as a first warning of 
oceanographic anomalies. In December 2011 three commercial fishermen in New 
England, US informed scientists at the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute, US, of 
strange warm waters on the edge of the continental shelf. Temperature sensors on 
lobster pots as part of the eMOLT program were able to verify the observed anomaly 
(Gawarkiewicz, et al. 2012). The warm Gulf Stream current had swung far westward 
from its normal path. Phenotypic shifts were observed in marine organisms the following 
spring, and the winter was one of the mildest winters on record in the Northeastern US 
(Gawarkiewicz, et al. 2012, Fisher 2015). Fishermen and the sensors on their gear were 
the first warning of a significant anomaly not only in the ocean, but throughout the 
adjacent land. 
Both the North Sea/Skagerrak and Georges Bank/Gulf of Maine, where the eMOLT 
program is in operation, are heavily trawled areas with a lack of real-time in-situ data. 
This pattern of heavily trawled areas with a lack of real-time data repeats itself across 
the globe. These coastal shelf seas which have the highest trawling intensity but a lack 
of in-situ observations are arguably the most crucial areas to have data. Shelf seas 
have a much higher use by humans than the middle of the ocean. Fishing, commercial 
==PAGE:42==
9-37 
 
      
 
and recreational vessel traffic, offshore oil platforms, offshore wind farms, and 
aquaculuture are all concentrated in coastal waters and shelf seas. Furthermore, the 
proximaty of these high intensity trawling areas to land makes the effects of maritime 
climate and weather on humans far more immediet and direct than the impact of 
conditions in the deep ocean off of the continental shelf. 
Fishing gear as an oceanographic data collection platform is a simple concept which 
can provide the base to solutions for a multitude of complex and systemic problems 
faced by traditionally unrelated parties. Just as EBFM calls for a systems approach to 
quantify and understand the interrelations in an aquatic ecosystem, an integrated 
ecosystems approach is necessary for solving the interrelated yet divergent problems of 
the earth and humanity. Collecting data with fishing vessels is a foundation which can 
be built upon to create agile and integrated systems for understanding, managing, 
and adapting to changes not only in our oceans, but on our planet as a whole.

12.1 Raw GPS and Temperature data merging 
 
Data was processed using the R programming language. Raw data came from three 
main sources: temperature logs from the Hobo logger, GPS points, and the catch log 
with tow start and stop times. Temperature data came in minute intervals in .hobo file 
format. GPS data was output in minute intervals in standard raw NMEA-0183 $GPRMC 
string format. Catch data was in a manually entered excel file.  
Two functions were created in one R script to first merge the GPS and temperature files, 
and then assign fishing trip and tow data to points corresponding to when the 
respective tow was taking place. See trip_merging4.R in supplementary material. 
For each grouping of trips by original .hobo files, a script named “Tur #_# fun.R” was 
created where # corresponded to the trips included in the original .hobo file, see R 
scripts in supplementary material. 
The individual Tur* scripts were largely the same, outputting several diagnostic plots for 
manual inspection of the data and the merging procedure. Unique to each Tur* script 
were the base temp and GPS files it loaded, as well as a time stamp correction factor 
of one or 2 hours depending upon daylight savings time. NMEA GPS files come with 
standard UTC timestamps, which do not change for daylight savings time. The Hobo 
logger time stamp was in the local GMT + 2:00 time zone, which did change for daylight 
savings time. The catch log time was also in local time, but the catch recorder forgot to 
change the recorded time for few tows after daylight savings time. All of the 
discrepancy between time stamp standardization required significant manual 
inspection for all data merging around the spring and fall switching on and off of 
daylight savings time. All time stamps were converted to UTC POSIXct objects in R.  
In the trip_merge function in the trip_merging.R script, merged temp and GPS data was 
separated into statuses with a for loop going through each data point. The status was 
first assigned to “transition” if the temperature difference between it and either the one 
following or the one ahead was more than 0.2°C.  If the row had not been assigned as 
transition, it was assigned to be “fishing” if the temperature was greater than 5°C and 
less than 8°C.  Remaining data points were assigned the status “air.”

Next, the merged temperature and GPS data set was merged with the catch log 
based upon the tow start times. The one line representing each tow was only merged

12.3 Supplementary factor creation 
 
Using the GPS coordinates and time stamp it was possible to supplement the merged 
data with a variety of supplementary potential explanatory variables to aid in the 
modeling. The only supplementary factor to be included in the final model was depth. 
Depths were taken from the NOAA bathymetry data base (NOAA 2018 ), using the 
marmap R package (Pante, Simon-Bouhet and Irrison 2018). 
Vessel speed was obtained geosphere package, calculating the distance between 
GPS pings (Hijmans 2017). This was ultimately a waste of time as speed is also included in 
the raw NMEA format GPS files. 
 
The percentage of the tow conducted during daylight was calculated using the R 
sunCalc package (Thieurmel and Agafonkin 2018) to assign each data point time 
stamp as either night or day. Then the percentage of day points for each tow was 
calculated. 
 
The fishing tow landing port was determined if the vessel entered a zone at the end of 
the trip defined by GPS points of south of 57.76°N, east of 10.66°E, and west of 9.9°E then 
the landing port was in Denmark. If the vessel entered a zone at the end of the trip 
defined by GPS points of North of 58.34 °N, south of 58.45°N, and east of 11.22 °E the 
landing port was in Sweden. Then these landing ports were dragged backwards by 
time step until an already filled in Port was reached. By this method, the final destination 
of each set of tows was dragged back from its corresponding landing port. The landing 
port was an attempt to discern if there were any differences in the targeted species 
and therefore fishing behavior due to different market prices in Sweden and Denmark. 
While there was only data for the shrimp landings, the Danish shrimp fishermen will land 
certain bycatch species, which in some cases can be higher value (exe. monkfish) than 
their targeted shrimp. 
 
Several versions of temperature anomaly were calculated using the FOAM AMM7 
modeled bottom temperature data (Copernicus 2018). These temperature anomalies 
were a quantification of the difference between the mean tow temperature and the 
modeled bottom temperature. The idea of this was to see if the shrimp were in a 
relatively cold or warm area. The most sophisticated version of the temperature 
anomaly, T_anom3, was calculated by taking the mean modeled bottom temperature 
in the red boxes in Figure 12-2 below.

12.4 Shrimp Catch Model output methods 
 
Mapped prediction outputs are run by inputting the NOAA bathymetry into the model 
in order to get latitude and longitude coupled with the corresponding depths (NOAA 
2018 ). Temperature and day of year are required as well for model input. In mapped 
predictions temperature is entered as constant for all prediction points. For each output 
map, the model predictions run through a loop which evenly divides up the part of the 
year required in output plots, see map_loop_pred.R in supplementary material (required 
catch_model.R to be run first. The model is only run on points with depths deeper than 
50 meters and shallower than 500 meters, even so, each map requires the model to 
predict outputs for 14,659 points, which is computationally expensive. The outputs are 
divided into three data frames with a standard error less than 0.7, 1.5, and 2.5. These 
==PAGE:54==
12-49 
 
      
 
three data frames are then plotted on top of each other with decreasing transparency, 
or “alpha” in ggplot2, in order to fade the prediction as the reliability decreases. 
The depth and temperature catch predictions are done in essentially the same way as 
the mapped predictions, with tempXdepth_loop_preds.R. However, latitude and 
longitude are held constant and a grid from the smallest to largest observed 
temperatures and depths, with a resolution of 0.5°C by 7 meters. This grid is only 2444 
points, so running time is quite a bit more manageable than with the mapped 
predictions. 
The large percentage model outputs are done in the same way except using 
large_ratio_model.R, map_loop_preds_LR.R, and temp_loop_preds_LR.R.
