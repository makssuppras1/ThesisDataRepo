Abstract 
Influent characterization of wastewater is an important aspect for design, optimisation and operation 
of activated sludge processes in wastewater treatment systems. In order to properly calibrate the 
BioDenipho process model at Lynetten, Denmark’s largest wastewater treatment plant, according to 
the ASM2d framework, influent characterisation is needed to translate necessary characteristic 
concentrations to model-compatible parameters. After performing two intensive measurement 
campaigns and standard laboratory analyses, influent and effluent COD, N, P concentrations were 
fractionated and translated into ASM2d compatible state variables. Total COD, total N and total P 
values which were found to be (743 ± 45) mg/l, (78 ± 6) mg/l and (15 ± 2) mg/l in the influent and 
(743 ± 45) mg/l, (78 ± 6) mg/l and (15 ± 2) mg/l in the effluent agree well with literature values. In 
the reactors, total N of (354 ± 11), total P of (178 ± 8), TSS of (4031 ± 262) and total COD of 
(6083 ± 30) mg/l are found. Comparing with data from Oti Faecal Sludge Treatment Facility in 
Ghana, it is observed that the three-stage treatment process using anaerobic, facultative and 
maturation ponds is very efficient in COD removal (92%), but needs to be optimized for nitrogen and 
phosphorus removal.

1.0 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Human life depends largely on the earth’s resources and the natural environment, which includes 
clean water. Over the years, global increases in industrial activities have resulted in an increase in the 
release of pollutants into the environment, leading to many detrimental effects, key of which is 
climate change (Adonadaga, 2014). The role of Environmental Engineering is to find the sources of 
these pollutions and mitigate, if not remove, them (Nazaroff & Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). One such 
discipline includes wastewater treatment processes for purposes such as preventing eutrophication in 
surface waters (Sathasivan, n.d.), agricultural purposes (Keraita & Drechsel, 2002) or even 
remediation of contaminated soil (Mouli, et al., 2004). 
1.1.1 Wastewater treatment in Denmark 
Danish waters have experienced significant advancement in pollution mitigation due to targeted 
efforts to improve efficiency of municipal and industrial wastewater treatment plants nationwide 
(NIRAS, 2015). The implementation of the first water action plan in 1987 and subsequent ones has 
led to  a substantial technological upgrade in wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs). Nearly 90% of 
Danish wastewater is treated at the 300 most advanced WWTPs in the country. In Danish WWTPs, 
pollutant concentrations are reduced in effluents through chemical and biological degradation of 
organic compounds, aqueous precipitation of phosphorous and sorption of metals, hydrophobic 
compounds, and nutrients in the organic sludge phase The sludge generated as a coproduct of the 
wastewater treatment process is increasingly seen as a resource rather than a waste material, which 
can be used for nutrients and energy recovery. Therefore, WWTPs represent multifunctional systems 
where technological, as well as management choices, influence the different functions and therefore 
the overall environmental performance. Plant types in Denmark are distinguished with regards to 
plant capacity,  mode of treatment (pure chemical precipitation vs. biological treatment), and 
production of biogas from sludge (anaerobic vs. aerobic treatment) (Niero, et al., 2014).  
1.1.2 Lynetten WWTP 
Lynetten, Denmark’s largest wastewater treatment plant, was inaugurated in 1980 (BIOFOS, 2015). 
The treatment process used to focus only on removal of organic pollutants, but has since 1997 been 
expanded, to include biological and chemical nitrogen and phosphorus removal, as well as harness 
resources in wastewater for electricity generation. Lynetten Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) 
serves 535000 inhabitants, out of the 569,557 living in Copenhagen (Stenstrop, 2015). The catchment

area is about 76 km2 and the plant treats a pollution load about 750000 people equivalent (PE). The 
wastewater treatment plant was designed for a maximum influent flow of 41500 m3/h and a maximum 
flow of 23000 m3/h into the biological treatment line. Treated water is released in Øresund (as seen 
in Figure 1), one of the three straits in Denmark that connect the Baltic Sea to the Atlantic Ocean 
(BIOFOS, 2015). 
 
Figure 1: The Lynetten wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) 
Wastewater is transported from households through a network of pipes to the WWTP. Upon arrival, 
it is first taken through grating and screening structures as well as a sand and grease trap to remove 
large solids, oils, grease and the like. It then continues to the primary clarification tanks where large 
solids that were not removed in the previous section settle. The supernatant, still very polluted with 
biomass, nitrogen, phosphorus, some heavy metals, pharmaceuticals and other colloidal solids, is then 
taken to the activated sludge biological treatment tanks - currently using the BioDenipho process – 
after which the effluent is sent to the secondary clarification tanks and later discharged to the Oresund.

1.1.3 The BioDenipho Process 
This is an activated sludge process largely used in Denmark to reduce phosphorus and nitrogen 
concentrations in wastewater to a minimum. It also removes organic matter and odour as the sludge 
is stabilized in the process. It consists of an anaerobic tank, two interconnected biological tanks which 
work in an alternating mode and are equipped with aerators, and a settling tank. It makes use of a 
technology called Phased Isolation Ditch (PID) technology, which is a family of continuous flow 
activated sludge processes differentiated by alternating flow patterns that are inherently linked to 
specific operating conditions within individual reactors to produce a high quality effluent. Nitrogen 
removal by nitrification and denitrification in the process is performed in a semi-batch manner by 
periodically changing the path of flow through two parallel aeration tanks, which are aerated 
according to a fixed (controlled) strategy (Bundgaard, et al., 1989). A typical process cycle comprises 
six phases, which are shown in Figure 3. The variation of nitrate and ammonia nitrogen concentrations 
in the two parallel aeration tanks is also indicated in Figure 3.

1.1.4 Wastewater Treatment in Ghana 
Some major challenges of rapid urbanization in Sub-Saharan Africa is the inability to keep up with 
supply of portable drinking water, scarcity of wastewater treatment facilities and limitation of access 
to the few existing ones. Typically, 4-5% of wastewater generated in urban areas in Ghana find their 
way into wastewater treatment plants due to poor sewerage networking. Most domestic wastewater 
are channeled through drains and gutters and finally disposed into water bodies without treatment 
(Keraita & Drechsel, 2002). Many farmers rely on these untreated wastewaters for informal irrigation 
of their crops, and even though there are by-laws in place for prevention of such practices, they are 
seldom enforced. According to IWMI-Ghana, more than 90% of perishable vegetables are sold in 
local markets and more 80% of agricultural production comes from smallholder family farms 
(International Water Management Institute, 2015). The risks, therefore, of exposure of farmers and 
consumers to pathogens are alarmingly high.  
According to the WHO/UNICEF 2012 report, drinking water coverage in Ghana has increased by 76-
90% in the rural areas and 91-100% in urban areas by the year 2012. At the same time, improvement 
in sanitation was the same or lower than it was in 1990, with 26-50% of the rural population practicing 
open defecation and a majority of the urban population relying on shared or public sanitation facilities 
(WHO & UNICEF, 2012). This led to cholera outbreaks over the years, with the 2014 incidence being 
recorded as the worst since 1982 (17000 cases, 150 deaths) as reported by GhanaWeb (GhanaWeb, 
2014). This is not surprising, considering the increase in population over the last 20 years due to high 
fertility and reduced mortality (Adlakha, 1996). With increasing population comes the demand for 
more stringent and effective waste treatment services. 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
Apart from the use of treated wastewater in irrigation, and further treatment to drinking water 
standards, it could also be used in the generation of hydrodynamic power. Furthermore, biogas 
production and electricity generation during treatment of wastewater using activated sludge (AS) and 
anaerobic digestion could be explored. Recent frequent power cuts in Ghana (Darko, 2015) attest to 
the fact that alternative sources of energy and methods of electricity production are needed for energy 
generation in the country.  
A study on wastewater treatment practices in Ghana shows that out of the total of 19 WWTPs in 
operation in Ghana (excluding 6 plants with low performance), activated sludge (AS) is the second 
most used technology, covering 26% of the total number of treatment plants. The most used 
technology is ponds, and the third is anaerobic digesters. Moreover, less than 5000 m3/day of 
wastewater is treated and feed flow rates at the treatment plants range between 1-25 m3/hr 
(International Water Management Institute, 2015).

institutional and similar facilities’ (Metcalf & Eddy, 2004). It includes both blackwater (mostly 
human excreta) and greywater (water from domestic activites, i.e. dishwashing, laundry, bathing). 
According to Henze, (2008), domestic wastewater consists mainly of microorganisms, biodegradable

2.2 Standard methodologies for wastewater examination 
The significance of producing representative, reproducible, defensible and useful data cannot be 
overemphasized, because only then can one accomplish the objectives of a sampling program. In 
order to fulfil these requirements, a detailed sampling protocol is needed, providing information such 
as sample plan, sample types and size, labelling and chain of custody, sampling methods, sampling 
storage and preservation, sample constituents and analytical methods (Burton & Stensel, 2004). The 
objective of this section is to outline such a protocol for use in the MSc thesis project. 
2.2.1 Methods of Analysis 
This section describes the methods used to analyze the wastewater after sample collection. Various 
methods are available depending on the desired wastewater constituent. According to Metcalf & 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
Eddie (2004), wastewater constituents can be grouped into different categories based on 
characteristics they possess: Physical, inorganic chemical and organic chemical characteristics 
(Burton & Stensel, 2004). While constituents with physical characteristics focus on such analyses as 
determination of Total Solids (TS), Fixed Suspended Solids (FSS), Volatile Suspended Solids (VSS), 
Total Suspended Solids (TSS), odour, temperature, pH and the like, Organic and inorganic chemical 
characteristics range from BOD and COD analyses to specific analyte concentration determination 
such as Nitrates, Nitrites, Phosphorus, Nitrogen, etc. It is important that the integrity of samples are 
retained in order to arrive at relatively true values after analyses. The use of analytical methods 
infused with Quality Assurance and Quality Control procedures increases the reliability of the results 
(American Public Health Association, American Water Works Association, Water Environment 
Federation, 1999). 
A. Physical Properties 
Alkalinity: Titration Method 
The acid-neutralizing capacity of water is it´s alkalinity. The concentrations of carbonate, bicarbonate 
and hydroxide content in surface waters is considered as the alkalinity because it is usually a function 
of these constituents. The titration method of determining alkalinity is based on Le Chatelier´s 
principle. If a standard  acid is added to water until it reaches an endpoint (due to the reactions between 
the acid and the hydroxyl ions present as a result of dissociation or hydrolysis), then the concentration 
of the acid added is the same as the concentration of the base present in the original water, and hence, 
a measure of its alkalinity. Alkalinity thus, depends on end-point pH used (American Public Health 
Association, American Water Works Association, Water Environment Federation, 1999). 
Temperature: Laboratory and Field methods 
It is important to determine the temperature of wastewaters we wish to analyze because changes in 
temperatures usually result in changes in biological and chemical processes. The Arrhenius equation 
is then used to correct for temperatures to account for changes that might have taken place.

Solids 
In wastewater analysis, the term ´solids´ is a broad qualification of dissolved, organic, inorganic, 
suspended, settleable and colloidal particles found in a given wastewater.  About 50 percent of all 
solids are organic. These are substances containing carbon, oxygen and hydrogen, some of which 
may be combined with nitrogen, sulfur or phosphorus (Mountain Empire Community College, 
2015)A "strong" wastewater can be defined as one containing a large amount of solids, predominantly 
organic solids.  A "weak" wastewater is one containing only a small amount of organic 
solids.  Inorganic solids are those substances not subject to decay, example, sand, gravel, silt, as well 
as mineral salt that introduces hardness in water. Suspended solids are visible and in suspension, and 
can be removed by physical or mechanical means such as filtration or sedimentation. Settleable solids 
are suspended solids which have enough weight to settle in a given period of time. While colloidal 
suspended solids are primarily the portion of total suspended solids that are not readily removed by 
physical or mechanical means and do not settle readily, they can be filtered out in a Gooch Crucible. 
Dissolved solids are smaller in size than colloidal and suspended solids and can pass through the filter 
(Mountain Empire Community College, 2015). 
B. Aggregate Organic constituents 
Chemical Oxygen Demand 
The chemical oxygen demand (COD) of a substance is the amount of oxygen needed to oxidize its 
organic contents, therefore the COD test is used to measure the oxygen equivalent of the organic 
material in wastewater that can be oxidized chemically using dichromate in an acid solution. COD 
differs from BOD because there are some organic substances that are difficult to oxidize biologically, 
but can easily be oxidized chemically. Also, dichromate oxidizes certain inorganic substances, hence, 
the COD of a substance is always higher than the BOD (Burton & Stensel, 2004).  
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
COD is determined using the Hach-Lange test kit, which makes use of sulphuric acid-potassium 
dichromate solution in the presence of silver sulphate as a catalyst. Adding this solution to the 
wastewater turns it green. The higher the oxygen demand, the greener the resulting solution. This 
green coloration is then evaluated in a photometer to give the COD value. 
Biochemical Oxygen Demand 
Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) is the amount of dissolved oxygen needed by aerobic biological 
organisms in wastewater to break down organic material present at certain temperature over a specific 
time period. In the determination of BOD, the principle underlying the standard procedure is that, 
given enough oxygen, all of the organic contents of the wastewater will decompose biologically 
through aerobic respiration of microorganisms. During the test, three things occur simultaneously. 
First, some of the organic content is oxidized to produce energy for cell synthesis and cell 
maintenance. Another portion is converted to new cell tissue using the energy released from the first 
process. Lastly, when the organic matter is used up, the cells begin to consume their own cell tissue 
to obtain energy for cell maintenance (endogenous respiration). If all three processes take place, the 
ultimate BOD (UBOD) is obtained (Burton & Stensel, 2004).  
The respirometric determination of BODn using Oxitop system is carried out for the purpose of this 
report. In this method, a volume of wastewater based on the expected quality is poured into a 
graduated measuring flask. The more the expected BOD, the less volume is required to allow 
sufficient oxidation in the bottle. A nitrification inhibitor, Allylthiourea (ATU) is added so as not to 
lose oxygen to the nitrification process and also not to record a higher BOD value. Since this system 
is a pressure measurement, to cause a drop in pressure, sodium hydroxide pellets are placed in a 
rubber sleeve fitted to the neck of the bottle with the purpose of absorbing carbon dioxide, an end 
product of respiration. A magnetic stirrer is added to ensure homogenous mixing. The bottle is then 
covered tightly and placed in an incubator and the measurement of the Oxitop head is started using a 
controller. Results are read after seven days (WTW, 2015) 
C. Inorganic Non-metallic constituents 
Nitrogen 
Nitrogen is a macronutrient. The average human body needs 2.0 grams of nitrogen per day. However, 
most people consume more nitrogen than needed (13.0 grams per day). As a consequence, a lot of 
nitrogen ends up in the waste stream as ammonia (Minnis, 2015), and high amounts in receiving 
waters can lead to eutrophication. 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
The sum of inorganically-bound nitrogen (NH3-N, NO3-N and NO2-N) and organically-bound 
nitrogen in wastewater is known as total nitrogen, TN. TN is a useful parameter in wastewater quality 
assessments because higher nitrogen concentrations in receiving waters could lead to eutrophication. 
Organically bound nitrogen is usually a small fraction of the total nitrogen present. The ammonia 
nitrogen, together with the organic nitrogen fraction gives the Kjeldahl nitrogen (ChemScan, 2015). 
NH3-N, NO3-N and NO2-N can be analyzed using the Auto Analyzer (AA). Since suspended solids 
can interfere with the results of these analyses, filtrated samples are used. If these nitrogen fractions, 
in addition to the total nitrogen are known, the organically bound nitrogen in the wastewater can be 
found. (Plosz & Smets, 2014) 
Phosphorus 
In Denmark, the Person Load (PL) of total phosphorus ranges from 0.8-1.2 kg/cap/yr and typical 
municipal wastewater phosphorus contents ranges from 6 to 25 g/m3 (Henze, n.d.). Phosphorus 
concentrations in wastewater can be analyzed using Hach-Lange test kits or Merck test kits based on 
the principle that phosphate ions react with molybdate and antimony ions in an acidic solution to form 
an antimonyl phosphomolubdate complex, which in turn is reduced by ascorbic acid to 
phophomolbdenum blue. The resulting solution is analysed in a photometer to attain the phosphorus 
concentration. It is important to note that without hydrolysis, only dissolved orthophosphate (PO4-P) 
is measured, but with hydrolysis, the total phosphorus (total-P) is measured. 
 
2.3 Sampling of Wastewater 
Wastewater sampling is a process used to collect a small portion of wastewater in order to determine 
the characteristics of an entire batch of wastewater, eg, municipal wastewater at a treatment plant. 
Sampling is necessary for testing effluent wastewater for regulatory purposes, as well as for 
improvement of the existing treatment techniques or methods. A sample must be small enough to be 
appropriately handled in the laboratory, but still representative of the wastewater going through the 
entire treatment plant. Care must be taken such that nothing is added to or lost from the sample taken 
and no change occurs in the sample characteristics from the time of sampling until laboratory analyses 
are over.

The first important rule in any wastewater sampling 
process is wearing rubber gloves to prevent contact 
of the wastewater with the skin. Additionally, poles

Types of samples 
 
Two main types of samples associated with wastewater are grab and 
composite samples. A grab sample is a single sample taken at a 
specific time. Grab samples may not be completely representative of 
the flow, but are preferred for some experiments such as pH and 
dissolved oxygen tests. Composite samples, on the other hand, are 
made up of several grab samples at different times (and sometimes 
different flows) put together, and are more representative of the 
characteristics of the wastewater in the treatment plant over a long 
period of time (Burton & Stensel, 2004).  
Composite samples 
Composite samples can be taken either by continuous sampling or by mixing discrete samples. They 
are further grouped into time-proportional and flow-proportional samples. Time proportional 
composite samples consist of equal volumes of discrete sample aliquots collected at constant time 
intervals into one container (Metcalf & Eddy, 2004). Flow proportional samples can be collected in 
two ways: collecting constant sample volumes at varying time intervals proportional to the flow; or 
collecting varying sample volumes proportional to the flow while maintaining a constant time 
interval. In the latter, the volume of flow at a time, say t, determines the volume to be sampled. 
Automatic samplers preset to take flow-proportional samples do this calculation and take correctly 
sized grab samples at all times (Metcalf & Eddy, 2004).

3.0 Methodology 
Two intensive measurement campaigns were carried out to collect influent and effluent wastewater 
samples from the Lynetten WWTP. Since this project aims at providing datasets for developing a 
model of the BioDenipho process, samples were taken after the pre-clarifiers (influent wastewater) 
secondary clarifiers (effluent wastewater). Automatic sampling was used instead of manual, and for 
the first measurement campaign (12/5 and 14/5), automatic samplers were started on the evenings of 
11/5 and 13/5 from 8:00 pm. For the second measurement campaign (25/5 and 27/5), sampling was 
started on the previous evenings at 7:00 pm. 
Laboratory analyses were used to determine the dynamic concentrations of total and soluble COD, 
Nitrogen, Phosphorus, as well as BOD, TSS/VSS, VFA, soluble ammonium and nitrates, and 
alkalinity of both influent and effluent samples, making characterization into ASM2d state variables 
possible.  
Some data were also acquired from the Tema Oil Refinery, Wastewater Treatment Department, and 
Oti faecal sludge treatment plant in Ghana for comparison with data obtained from the measurement 
campaign.  
3.1 Sampling method 
Time-proportional composite samples were taken from the pre-clarified influent and secondary 
clarified effluent of the BioDenipho process at the Lynetten wastewater treatment plant with an 
automatic sampler. Specifically, 12 samples per 1- litre bottle were taken within 5-minute intervals 
between samples and 1-hour intervals between bottles. The Automatic sampler was pre-set with 
specific conditions to depict the description above. All physical and chemical analyses were done in 
the Chemistry Laboratory of Building 115, DTU. 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
The wastewater analysis methods are chosen dependent on the fractions to be retrieved for the 
modelling of the Biodenitropho process at Lynetten wastewater treatment plant. 
3.2 Sample preservation and storage 
Plastic bottles were used for transfer of the samples to the laboratory, as well as for storage. To 
minimize potential for volatilization or biodegradation between sampling and analysis, samples were 
transported and kept in ice, even between analyses. To prevent changes in alkalinity, bottles were 
filled to the brim. BOD and filtered samples that were not analysed the same day were frozen at -
20℃, however, TSS/VSS tests, filtration and alkalinity tests were carried out right after sampling.

Guidelines for wastewater characterisation 
Guidelines as provided by Roeleveld and Loosdrecht (2002) for wastewater characterisation were 
followed in this project. Thus, physical-chemical methods were used to determine COD, N and P 
fractions, whereas a biological method was used to determine the biodegradable COD (BCOD) which 
is obtained from a BOD-analysis. However, instead of using the conversion factors for the N-
fractionation, organic fractions were obtained by carrying out physical-chemical tests on the soluble 
influent and effluent samples to obtain biodegradable soluble organic N and inert soluble organic N 
fractions respectively.

BOD-Automatic measurement system –Oxitop 
An automatic measurement system called Oxitop was used for the experimental determination of 
BOD concentration over time. The system included a series of measuring heads which register oxygen 
depletion from a sample due to the aerobic degradation of the carbonaceous substances.. A volume 
of wastewater based on the expected quality was poured into a graduated measuring flask. The more 
the expected BOD, the less volume was required to allow sufficient oxidation in the bottle. A 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
nitrification inhibitor, Allylthiourea (ATU) was added so as not to lose oxygen to the nitrification 
process and also not to record a higher BOD value. Since this system is a pressure measurement, to 
cause a drop in pressure, sodium hydroxide pellets were placed in a rubber sleeve fitted to the neck 
of the bottle with the purpose of absorbing carbon dioxide, an end product of respiration. A magnetic 
stirrer was added to ensure homogenous mixing. The bottle was then covered tightly and placed in an 
incubator and the measurement of the Oxitop head started using a controller. Results were read after 
seven days. A BOD degradation curve which was generated by the system was used afterwards to 
determine the ultimate BOD.

5.0 Conclusion 
Two intensive measurement campaigns (one for wet and the other for dry weather) were organised to 
collect influent and effluent samples from the full-scale BioDenipho process at Lynetten WWTP. 
After performing analytical experiments in the laboratory according to standard methods, influent 
and effluent COD, N, P concentrations were fractionated and translated into ASM2d compatible state 
variables. Total COD, total N and total P values found to be (743 ± 45) mg/l, (78 ± 6) mg/l and (15 ± 
2) mg/l in the influent and (743 ± 45) mg/l, (78 ± 6) mg/l and (15 ± 2) mg/l in the influent agree well 
with literature values. In the reactors, total N of (354 ± 11), total P of (178 ± 8), TSS of (4031 ±
262) and total COD of (6083 ± 30) mg/l are found. Data are reported with standard errors values 
and error propagation through the handling of data is quantified, rendering results reliable. Quality 
control measures in place in the laboratory for all testing methods ensure reproducibility of results. 
Upon comparison with data from Oti faecal sludge system in Ghana, it is observed that the three-
stage treatment process using anaerobic, facultative and maturation pond is very efficient in COD 
==PAGE:45==

removal, and satisfactory nitrogen and phosphorus removal. A simplified model in WEST of the 
BioDenipho process tends to imitate the dynamics of concentrations in the effluent of measured data.

5. Principle of analysis: 
Alkalinity:  
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
The ability of water to react with hydrogen ions. The alkalinity is determined by a titration with 
diluted sulphuric acid. During the titration the present weak bases are transformed to the 
corresponding weak acids.  
Under normal conditions, the major part of the bases will be the ions from the carbonate system: 
pH 6 –10:    HCO3- 
pH  > 10:    CO3-- 
 
Total alkalinity:  
The amount of acid, which will be needed to change the pH-value from the present pH to pH 4,5. It 
is measured in milli equivalents/ litre H3O+ (meq/l) and at the endpoint  pH 4,5 all the ions from the 
carbonate system are at CO2-form. 
 
Determination of total-alkalinity (TAL): 
In this schedule, there are 2 different methods to determinate TAL. The determination can be a simple 
manual titration with an indicator or a potentiometric end-point titration: 
 
1. Manuel titration with an indicator: 
The sample is titrated with sulphuric acid with an added indicator with a colour change at pH 4,5. 
This method will often do, but if there are many samples or the sample is turbid or coloured, it is not 
suitable. 
 
2. TAL after the Gran-method: 
 
When the TAL level is very low < 0,1 mmol/l , a titration after the Gran-method is the most precisely 
method. After titration to pH 4,5 the H+-concentration will increase after a linear function, with the 
added ml acid. From this linear function the equality point can be determined more precisely. 
 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
6.   Interference / precision / restrictions / suitability. 
Other bases which can react with H +-ions will contribute to the TAL result, fx ortho-phosphate, 
ammonia, borate, acetate, sulphide, humus. 
 
The magnetic stirring must be calm, so that the formed CO2 stays in the solution. 
 
 
7.   Sampling / sample preparation:

8. Preservation / durability:

amount of ion or biological activity in the sample it must be analysed within 2 hours.

14. Procedure  
 
1. Manuel titration with an indicator: 
A sample volume, fx 50 ml ( a ml) is transferred with a pipette to a conical flask. 6 drops of indicator 
is added and the sample is titrated with 0,0500 N sulphuric acid to the colour change from green/blue

Titrate quickly down to pH 4,5. 
Read the used acid volume and the pH-value. 
Add 0,05 ml acid and read the pH-value. Repeat the adding of 0,05 ml  at least 5 times or until the 
pH-value 3,5 is reached.
