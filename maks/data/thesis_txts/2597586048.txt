ABSTRACT
Ultraviolet light is a contributing factor for the degradation of constituent materials of photo-
voltaic modules, mainly the polymeric encapsulant Ethylene Vinyl Acetate (EVA), resulting in
reduced performance and lifetime of products. From a reliability standpoint, it is important to
determine the UV dose experienced by a panel. However, the measurements are not readily
available, unlike pyranometer global horizontal solar irradiation data. Therefore it is useful
to determine the UV dose using available pyranometer measurements. In this study, the UVA
(315-400 nm) and UVB (280-315 nm) doses in the horizontal plane were calculated.
A correlation analysis was carried out between the pyranometer and the UV irradiation mea-
surements obtained from the resources at Risø, Denmark. It was found that Global Horizontal
Irradiation > Solar Elevation Angle > Clearness Index > Air Mass in terms of linear correlation
with UVA and UVB irradiation. Based on these parameters, ﬁve models were developed which
included a machine learning model and the validation of an existing polynomial model. All ﬁve
models were validated using measurements obtained from Golden, Colorado. The multiple linear
regression model using three inputs made the best UVA and UVB irradiation predictions amongst
the non-machine learning models. The UVA model had a relative error in prediction of 11 %,
MAE of 0.760 W/m2, RMSE of 1 W/m2 and a R2 score of 98.9% and that of the UVB model of
244%, 0.054 W/m2, 0.074 W/m2 and 85%, respectively. All UVB models showed a high magnitude
of relative errors as a consequence of very small irradiation measurements (< 1 W/m2). The
predictions from this model were numerically integrated over the exposure time to determine the
UV dose. It was found that for the year 2019 at Risø, the UVA dose was 56.27 kWh/m2 and the
UVB dose was 0.87 kWh/m2.
This study can be broadened by predicting the UV dose in the plane-of-array. There is scope for
improvement in the accuracy of predictions by estimating the uncertainties associated with the
plane of array irradiation predictions. This study has to be supported with accelerated aging
tests, which will enable the determination of the service life of the constituent materials of
modules. It will also enable the quantiﬁcation of the durability of typical and colored building-
integrated photovoltaics, which is a major step in quality control leading to conﬁdence in potential
investment in the technologies.

C H A P T E R
1
INTRODUCTION
I
n today’s climate, the depletion of conventional sources of energy, coupled with the con-
temporary paradigm of sustainability, there’s an established need for renewable sources
for power generation. These sources are expected to meet the increased electric power
needs, peaks in demands, low costs and also being as environmentally friendly as possible. One
such energy source that meets these requirements is solar energy. The majority of the countries
in the world signed onto the Paris agreement to ensure that the global temperatures do not
increase above 2 ◦C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature
increase even further to 1.5 ◦C. [1] Following this agreement, goals were set to be implemented
by 2030, which includes the reduction of CO2 emissions by 40%. The Danish energy system is in
a transition towards reduced energy consumption with the increased utilization of renewable
energy. This adheres to the short-term goal of CO2 neutrality by 2025 and the long-term goal of
complete independence from fossil fuels by 2050. [2][3]
A solar PV capacity of 36MW is being installed every month, which makes Denmark reach the
target of 200 MW solar cell capacity ahead of time and it is estimated that the development
would reach a capacity of 3400 MW by 2030. [4] Innovation in the ﬁeld of solar PV along with new
incentives and policies, has led to the development of technologies such as building-integrated
photovoltaics (BIPV). The Energy Performance of Buildings Directive adopted by the European
Union aims at a nearly zero energy standard for new buildings by the end of 2020. [5] BIPV is a
technology that adheres to this directive and is a step towards obtaining clean, affordable, and
decentralized energy. [6] BIPV involves the integration of PV into the building envelope. It is a
relatively new technology that enables the implementation of PV not only as a power source but
also as a building element that contributes to its structural integrity and architectural aesthetics.
1
==PAGE:16==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
[6] As a building element, it must comply with both PV and building standards. [7] The nature of
BIPV allows for tailored solutions in terms of color, architecture, and design for integration into
the building sector. The new building of Copenhagen International School (CIS) (Figure 1.1) is
integrated with 6048 m2 of PV panels using Kromatix blue-green solar glass, with an installed
capacity of 720 kWp, making it one of the largest BIPV installations in the world. [8]
Figure 1.1: BIPV Installations - Copenhagen International School.
Developments in the solar PV ﬁeld apart from BIPV can be seen in the form of tandem cells
that show a 40% efﬁciency, ﬂoating photovoltaic (FPV) that as the name suggests, is installed
above water bodies, as a consequence show a 2.6% increase in the efﬁciency. [9] [10] Despite
major developments in the ﬁeld, from a reliability stand-point, PV modules are subject to
degradation. The module degradation could be due to early defects and/or during their operation.
With a high inﬂux of new systems, customers need to have complete information on their
investment. The module degradation could be due to early defects and/or during their operation.
[11] Degradation results in reduced performance, durability, and ultimately reduces the service
life of modules. Therefore quality control is a pivotal step to quantify the degradation effects. One
such degradation is the yellowing of modules as shown in Figure 1.2.
2
==PAGE:17==
1.1. MOTIVATION
Figure 1.2: Yellowing observed in PV modules.
Yellowing is a consequence of degradation of the photovoltaic polymeric encapsulant - EVA
(Ethylene Vinyl Acetate) which is a copolymer of ethylene and vinyl acetate (VA). In severe cases,
browning can be observed. This reduces the short-circuit current and consequentially the output
power of the module. Yellowing is one of the most common degradation modes found in aged PV
modules and is caused by UV exposure over long term ﬁeld aging. [12] It is therefore important
to determine the UV dose that a module is exposed to, which allows the quantiﬁcation of its
degradation effects. This project involves the determination of the UV dose experienced by a
module in the horizontal plane.
1.1
Motivation
The growing need for renewables, coupled with the developments in the PV ﬁeld, has contributed
to PV installations all over the world. A PV module has an average lifespan of 25 years, in which
it undergoes various stresses. [13] This thesis deals with the exposure of modules to UV stress.
The assessment of UV irradiation incident on the PV modules gives an insight into understanding
the degradation effects on modules, in turn enabling the determination of reliability and service
life of modules. [14] More often than not, the availability of UV measurements is limited, unlike
typical pyranometer measurements, making it difﬁcult to estimate the UV stress.
3
==PAGE:18==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
This project addresses the need for an uncomplicated method of estimation of UV stress. To that
end, a study has been carried out to develop models to predict UV irradiation in the horizontal
plane and subsequently estimate the UV dose. This is done with the intention that the models
can be generally applicable, which enables the understanding of UV exposure and its detrimental
effects on PV modules in different locations.
1.2
Problem Statement
The overall problem statement can be corroborated as follows:
’UV light is a contributing factor for degradation of the internal materials of photovoltaic modules,
and in-turn its performance and lifetime. However, the UV measurements are not easily available
like the typical global horizontal pyranometer measurements. It is therefore important to estimate
the UV dose experienced by modules, using pyranometer measurements. This project aims to
answer the question - can the UV dose on the horizontal plane be predicted using pyranometer
measurements?’
1.3
Thesis Approach
The thesis is organized as follows: Chapter 2 describes the theoretical background of the solar
spectrum, the scattering of light, the ultraviolet region of the spectrum, and its inﬂuence in
photovoltaic degradation. Chapter 3 describes the modeling approach followed to obtain the
objectives of this project, which include the collection and analysis of data from the resources in
Denmark, the different modeling approaches employed to predict UV irradiation in the horizontal
plane for Danish conditions, evaluation and validation of said models with available resources
from Colorado, and ﬁnally the prediction of horizontal plane UV dose in Denmark and Colorado.
In Chapter 4, the results from the models are compared using statistical indicators for both
Denmark and Colorado. Following this, the horizontal plane UV dose predictions in Denmark are
presented. Chapter 5 summarizes the thesis by drawing conclusions from the obtained results.
Prospects are presented in the same chapter to conclude the report. The literature references for
this project are presented.

THEORY
The sun radiates energy as a black body according to Planck’s radiation law at around 6000 K
and emits light at different wavelengths of the electromagnetic spectrum, particularly as UV,
visible, and the infrared (IR) light. The solar spectrum inside and outside the atmosphere is
shown in Figure 2.1. [15].
Figure 2.1: Solar spectrum.
The total irradiance obtained from the AM 0 spectrum is 1367 W/m2 which is termed as the solar
constant. As light travels through the atmosphere, the irradiation reaching the earth is affected
by reﬂection, absorption, and scattering. The phenomenon is represented in Figure 2.2. [15]
5
==PAGE:20==
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
Figure 2.2: Global irradiation.
The sunlight is sometimes reﬂected in the atmosphere that reduces the irradiation reaching the
earth’s surface. As seen from Figure 2.2, the global irradiation on a horizontal surface (GHI) is
made up of direct and a diffuse component. The direct component i.e direct normal irradiation
(DNI) is the irradiation that reaches a surface directly from the sun in a straight collimated path.
The diffuse component i.e diffuse horizontal irradiation (DHI) is the radiation that is scattered
by atmospheric particles. The scattering that occurs when light falls on particles that are smaller
than the wavelength of the radiation, is termed as Rayleigh scattering. Mie scattering occurs
when the irradiation is scattered by particles that are larger than the wavelength of light. The
particles include dust, smoke, aerosols, etc. [15] The GHI is 1000 W/m2 for AM 1.5 and ambient
temperature of 25 ◦C are the standard testing conditions for solar modules. The molecules such as
ozone (O3), oxygen, water, etc. are responsible for the absorption of light. The absorption differs
for different spectral ranges.
The ultraviolet spectrum is made up of three components - UV-A, UV-B, and UV-C. The UVC
has the shortest wavelength (100-280 nm) and is absorbed by O3. [16] The absorption in the
UVB spectral range (280-315 nm) is comparatively less signiﬁcant. The UVA has the longest
wavelength (315-400 nm), is hardly affected by absorption, and hence reaches the earth’s surface.
The UVB irradiation is much smaller compared to the UVA counterpart, reaching the earth’s
surface. The intensity of the UV radiation reaching a surface is approximately 5% of global
irradiation. [14] [17] UV irradiation plays an important role in accelerating the degradation of
the packaging materials of PV modules (Figure 2.3), especially the EVA. [18]
6
==PAGE:21==
Figure 2.3: Packaging materials of PV modules.
Over long outdoor exposures, the degradation effects can be observed in the form of yellowing
(Figure 1.2). The stability of PV modules to UV irradiation is tested according to IEC 61215 UV
preconditioning test. [19] A UV dose of 15 kWh/m2 is applied while maintaining the module
temperature at 60 ◦C for long exposure periods (typically 3000 hours). The stability is determined
by visual inspection and wet leakage current test deﬁned by the same standard, after exposure.
This is a low dose compared to the reality for outdoor exposures where the UV doses can reach
up to 120 kWh/m2. [17][20] The acceleration occurs due to the high temperature of 60◦C.
The analysis of solar irradiation data is the ﬁrst step in developing PV solutions. GHI is a mini-
mum parameter required, for the same. [21] Using GHI data, the direct and diffuse component
can be estimated. GHI data is typically available worldwide unlike UV measurements. The
spectro-radiometer used to measure UV irradiation are expensive meteorological instruments
and aren’t available in all locations. Therefore it is useful to determine UV irradiation through
modeling using available data. The quantiﬁcation of the UV spectrum is essential to characterize
its long-term degradation of effects on PV module materials.

3.2.1.1
Data ﬁltering
The combined data was ﬁltered in three stages - Baseline Surface Radiation Network (BSRN)
recommended Quality Control tests (v 2.0), removal of non-usable data by deﬁning physical
constraints, and ﬁnally the elimination of outliers using a ﬁve-number summary otherwise
known as box-plots.
BSRN Quality Control tests
The Baseline Surface Radiation Network (BSRN) recommended Quality Control tests (v 2.0)
were performed to obtain maximum beneﬁt and minimum impact of missing and bad data. The
tests are a series of deﬁning physically possible limits, extremely rare limits, and comparisons of
empirical relations of the measured parameters. These limits are used to account for all climates
and latitudes in the BSRN program. [24] The entire description of the tests can be found in the
Appendix 6.
Removal of non-usable data
After the BSRN tests were carried out, assumptions of some physical limits were deﬁned to
further ﬁlter out unreliable data. The ﬁlters used in this stage are enlisted as follows.
1. The irradiation values during the night time are below 0 W/m2 and were treated as
insigniﬁcant data. The following constraints were applied to remove negative irradiation
values.
i. GHI > 0.
ii. DHI > 0.
iii. DNI > 0.
iv. UVA Irradiation > 0.
v. UVB Irradiation > 0.

i. Elevation angles > 5◦.

Today, machine learning is extensively used in predictive analysis due to its ability to handle

AM, and Kt were used as inputs for this model to predict UVA and UVB irradiation. A random

Figure 3.18. [28]

Bootstrap aggregation or bagging is the process of sampling the given data set at random with
replacement. Bagging allows a better understanding of the variance with the data. The samples
that aren’t bagged are termed as ’out-of-bag’ samples. The learning is also done on the out-of-bag
samples and is evaluated using the out-of-bag (OOB) object score. The bagged samples are treated
as individual decision trees. Each tree is trained using different data samples with replacement.
The prediction from each tree is then averaged to obtain a ﬁnal prediction. It is an effective
machine learning model as it has high prediction accuracy due to the use of multiple decision trees
at random. It is ﬂexible to use, doesn’t require a thorough prerequisite knowledge in machine
learning, and has a low tendency of over-ﬁtting. The results of this model are discussed in Chapter

3.5
UV dose prediction
To predict the UV dose, trapezoidal integration, typically known as numerical integration, was
performed on UV irradiation over time. The irradiation predictions from the three input multiple
linear regression models were used. The data from 01st January 2019 - 31st December 2019 were
used for the same. The following steps were used to predict UV dose:
1. The UV dose was calculated for every day. This was done because the trapezoidal rule
returns one value after integration. It would yield a much higher value if done for the whole
year as the area under the curve (UV irradiation vs time) would be much bigger than that
for a day.

CONCLUSION
This study aimed to predict the UV dose experienced by a PV panel in the horizontal plane using
available weather and pyranometer data. On that account, the pyranometer, weather, and UV
data obtained from the resources at Risø were used. The UV dose predictions were made by
initially developing models to predict UV irradiation in the horizontal plane and numerically
integrating these predictions to determine the UV dose. To that end, the data records from 01st
January 2018 to 31st March 2020 were collected, for the same. The raw data was ﬁltered in three
stages - BSRN quality control tests, the deﬁnition of some physical limits, and removal of outliers.
The data, originally measured in 10-second intervals, were then averaged hourly to obtain a
functional data set for modeling.
A correlation analysis was conducted to study the inter-relationship between the pyranometer
and UV data. This was done using a correlation matrix to determine Pearson correlations and
pair-plots to study the nature of the correlations. This helped identify the variables that could best
predict UV irradiation. Based on the correlation matrix and pair-plots, GHI, SolarEl, Kt, and AM
were identiﬁed as predictors for UVA and UVB irradiation. GHI had the highest positive linear
correlation with UVA and UVB irradiation. The variables in descending order of highest linear
correlation was GHI > SolarEl > Kt > AM. AM had a comparatively high negative correlation of
-0.74 and -0.64 for UVA and UVB irradiation, respectively. From the pair-plots, it was found that
AM had a non-linear relationship with UVA and UVB irradiation. Bin-plots of UV irradiation
as a function of AM, grouped by different Kt were made to study the inﬂuence of AM. A clear
relationship between AM and UV irradiation was understood from the bin-plots and hence it was
still retained as one of the predictor variables.

CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
The data set was split into testing and training sets (20:80 split). The models were developed
using the training sets and evaluated on the test set. Five models were developed which included
a simple linear regression, two multiple linear regressions, one with 2 inputs and the other with
3 inputs, a 4th order polynomial ﬁt of RUV as a function of AM, and lastly a random forest regres-
sion. Two models of each type were developed to predict UVA and UVB irradiation, respectively.
The models were evaluated using statistical indicators which mainly include MAE, RMSE, and
MBE, the coefﬁcient of determination i.e the R2 score and the relative errors in prediction.
The simple linear UVA and UVB models using GHI as input showed MAE of 1.34 W/m2 and 0.07
W/m2, respectively. The R2 scores were 96% and 71%, respectively and the UVA model outdid the
model created by Al-Aruri et.al by 4%. The UVA model however was under ﬁtted by an almost
negligible MBE of -0.05 W/m2. The two input UVA and UVB models using GHI and SolarEl as
inputs showed on an average 38% and 23% decrease in MAE respectively. The models had a 2%
and 13% increase in R2 scores from the simple linear models. The three input UVA and UVB
models using GHI, SolarEl, and Kt as inputs, showed the best goodness of ﬁt with the lowest MAE,
RMSE among the non-machine learning models. The 4th order polynomial model had the least
R2 score. The UVB polynomial model severely underperformed compared to its UVA counterpart.
The random forest model using GHI, Kt, and AM as inputs, outdid the other models, having
the least MAE, RMSE, MBE, the relative error in prediction, and the highest R2 score for both
UVA and UVB predictions. However, these models proved to be disadvantageous as they do not
yield any weights or intercepts for them to be generalized for using solar data from other locations.
The machine learning UVA and UVB models had the least relative errors in prediction among
all developed models. All non-machine learning UVB models had a very high relative error
(compared to UVA models) due to very small actual UVB measurements (< 1 W/m2). The three
input UVA model had the least relative error of 11 % whereas the simple linear UVB model had
the least relative error of 204 %. Thus it was summarized based on the statistical indicators, R2
scores, and relative prediction errors that the three input multiple linear regression model was
the best among the non-machine learning models. The UVA and UVB irradiation predictions from
this model were used to predict the respective UVA and UVB doses. All models were validated
using a data set from Golden, Colorado, to verify the reproducibility of the models and UV dose
predictions were made. The UV doses were calculated for each day of the year 2019 by numerical
integration of UV irradiation over exposure time. The monthly averages were calculated using
the daily values and it was seen that on an average, in June there is a peak UVA and UVB dose
of 357.91 Wh/m2 and 5.86 Wh/m2, respectively. Using the daily values, the corresponding annual
UV doses were calculated to be 56.27 kWh/m2 and 0.870 kWh/m2, respectively.

5.1. FUTURE WORK
5.1
Future work
The possible modiﬁcations to this project for its improvement are presented in this section.
The accuracy of predictions of the 4th order polynomial model can be signiﬁcantly improved by
obtaining the polynomial through SMARTS simulations. In addition to modeling UV: TS ratios as
a function of AM, they can be modeled as a function of location, tilt, orientation to have a broader
understanding of other possible factors that inﬂuence the UV dose. Regarding the machine
learning model, random forest regression is capable of handling very large data with many
input variables. In addition to the three inputs (GHI, Kt, and AM), many others such as SolarEl,
extraterrestrial irradiation, ambient temperature, relative humidity, etc. can be incorporated into
the model. Despite being a black-box model, this helps to improve the predictions and identify
the importance of different factors in predictions. The UV doses in the plane-of-array can be
calculated, which would involve the transposition of existing models to plane-of-array (POA).
This opens up possibilities of determining accurately the most suitable transposition models
for speciﬁc scenarios. The accuracy of the POA predictions can be improved by determining the
related uncertainties in POA irradiation predictions. The time constraints of this project allowed
for validation of models in Golden, Colorado. However, the models can be validated for different
locations to identify potential scope for improvement of models and study the inﬂuence of location
on UV dose predictions. From an experimental standpoint, this study has to be supported with
accelerated aging tests according to different photovoltaic standards. It is an important step in
determining the lifetime of photovoltaic modules. As UV exposure contributes to the degradation
of packaging materials of modules, the service life of different materials can be studied by UV
exposure tests. As a result, UV degradation can be quantiﬁed. This can be applied to typical
and colored building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) which enables the study of UV degradation
for different color samples. In summary, the accelerated tests are the most important quality
control tests that can provide signiﬁcant information about the quality and service life of the
technologies, which is important for investment in the technologies.
