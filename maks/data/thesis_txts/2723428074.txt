Abstract
Congestion pricing is widely used to internalise the negative externalities and adverse impacts of
road traffic congestion. This thesis extends current literature on managing congestion of traffic
networks through congestion pricing by implementing and training dynamic price-setting models
using reinforcement learning algorithms. This is done in a simulation environment with a traffic
network with users making individual travel choices. The simulation environment used is a dynamic
day-to-day simulation with demand modelled using a logit-mixture model and supply modelled
using a trip-based Macroscopic Fundamental Diagram with heterogeneous trip lengths. The price
setting policies are learnt, using reinforcement learning, as a function of the departure flows and
time choices from the previous day.
The performance of the models trained with state-of-the-
art reinforcement learning algorithms (Proximal Policy Optimization and TD3) is compared to
the no-toll scenario, randomised tolls and traditional congestion pricing methods such as Bayesian
optimization. The results suggest that the development of adaptive congestion pricing models using
reinforcement learning algorithms could result in model performance comparable to traditional
methods and allow for diverse models that transfer performance to new scenarios and keep learning.
Keywords
Reinforcement learning; congestion pricing; traffic network simulation; demand management.

Introduction
The problem of traffic congestion is a growing concern around the world, with traffic networks
becoming increasingly congested year by year. Despite the decrease in travel during the COVID-19
pandemic, the total distance traveled by vehicles has risen quickly, resulting in further congestion of
traffic networks in both Europe and the US [32]. The impacts of traffic congestion extend beyond
just delays and frustration for drivers. They also have far-reaching effects on the environment,
the economy, and human health [12]. Congestion of traffic networks also lead to the use of more
fossil fuels as users will have to spend more time travelling [7]. These potential impacts highlight
the need to research and develop methods to alleviate traffic congestion and mitigate its negative
global effects on people and the environment. This serves as the motivation for finding methods to
decrease the congestion observed in traffic networks.
As traffic continues to increase, there is a growing concern over the rising number of traffic
accidents [27]. Ensuring the safety of people in and around traffic should be a top priority, and
reducing traffic accidents should be a key motivator for reducing congestion in traffic networks.
Studies have shown that reducing congestion can significantly reduce the number of traffic acci-
dents, with potential reductions of up to 45%, depending on the specific traffic network [27]. It
is therefore essential to prioritize research and development of methods that can alleviate conges-
tion and improve safety on the roads. This could have a significant positive impact on both the
environment and the well-being of people around the world.
According to INRIX, an analytics company specializing in traffic analytics, the economic costs
of congestion on traffic networks are on the rise. In 2021, congestion cost the US more than $53
billion [24], which rapidly increased to $81 billion in 2022 [32], showing a worrying trend. This
trend can also be observed in Germany and the UK, albeit to a lesser extent, with the UK’s cost
rising from £8 billion in 2021 to £9.5 billion in 2022, and Germany’s rising from e3.5 billion in
2021 to e3.9 billion in 2022 [24][32].
This trend highlights the importance of taking action before the issue becomes uncontrollable
and unsolvable. The significant economic costs, which are worsening, make it financially viable to
invest funds into reducing traffic congestion. Even a small percentage of the costs saved can make
a significant impact. Furthermore, the potential to mitigate associated societal and environmental
issues provides further incentive to explore and understand ways of handling traffic congestion to
alleviate its negative effects. In summary, the increasing costs associated with traffic congestion
demonstrate the urgency of developing effective solutions to this issue. By investing in research
and development of traffic management strategies, it is possible to reduce the negative impact of
congestion on society, the environment, and the economy.
Plenty of previous research has been conducted on the topic of congestion reduction, but with
the persistent trend of increasing congestion, it remains an unsolved and difficult problem. This
thesis aims to explore one method of handling congestion in traffic networks, namely congestion
pricing. The concept of congestion pricing was introduced by economist Pigou in 1920 [9]. It is used
to control user departure time and transport mode choices by imposing a tax on traffic networks
in the form of a toll [27]. The idea behind congestion pricing is that users of the traffic network
will plan their trips to limit their economic costs and travel time through their departure time and
mode choices. Consequently, the macro behavior of users can be controlled through the toll price,
which changes according to past choices of the users of the traffic network.
The ultimate goal of congestion pricing is to alleviate the negative effects of congestion in
traffic networks by limiting the number of users on the network at any given time. By doing so,
it has the potential to improve various aspects of people’s lives around the world. This method
of controlling congestion is economically viable and has been implemented in various cities around

the world with notable success [4][27]. Therefore, it is crucial to continue exploring and developing
congestion pricing as a potential solution to the ever-increasing problem of congestion in traffic
networks. This thesis will explore the concept of congestion pricing in more detail to evaluate its
potential effectiveness in reducing congestion and mitigating its negative impacts.
The implementation of congestion pricing should prioritize the maximisation of societal, envir-
onmental, health, and economic benefits to gain support from users, as such support is not always
guaranteed. The importance of highlighting the benefits when implementing congestion pricing is
demonstrated by the many critics of a new congestion pricing initiative in New York City [28].
Although a considerable amount of benefits may increase support, the political aspects of conges-
tion pricing implementation will not be considered in detail within this thesis as they are deemed
outside its scope.
This thesis proposes the following research question:
How can reinforcement learning models be implemented and trained for adaptive and
dynamic congestion pricing to effectively manage congestion of traffic networks
in a theoretical setting?
The goal of this framework is to reduce congestion and related problems, such as high travel
times for users of the traffic network, by optimising the total system travel time. The approach
involves first defining a modelling framework and simulating a traffic network environment. Dif-
ferent reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms will then be applied to train models within this
environment. The models will be quantitatively compared, and the best-performing approach will
be selected. To evaluate the effectiveness of the models, they will be compared against two scen-
arios: one with no toll and one with randomised tolls. A useful model should demonstrate better
performance than the no-toll scenario and the randomised tolls.
Therefore the objectives which can help in answering the proposed research question and reach-
ing the stated goal are as follows:
• Reviewing literature on congestion pricing, RL and RL for congestion pricing.
• Setting up a macroscopic traffic network model which can be used to simulate a traffic network
and users with the incorporation of dynamic demand-supply relationships.
• Apply different RL approaches and algorithms to train models capable of setting tolls.
• Evaluating trained models and comparing them to a scenario without a toll and a scenario
with a random toll.
• Testing RL approaches with different observation spaces, a different action space and changes
in the simulation such as drops in capacity.
• Providing recommendations for the implementation of congestion pricing in real-world trans-
portation networks based on the results of the study.
The best-performing models will be capable of setting a toll profile for every day, which will
be adjusted based on the observed traffic patterns of each day. The toll profile for each day, along
with the distance traveled, will determine the toll charged to each user of the traffic network. The
proposed system aims to maximise societal, environmental, health, and economic benefits to gain
support from users, as this is critical for successful implementation.

Literature Review
This section presents a review of recent and relevant academic literature which explores and defines
some areas of study necessary and useful in getting a deeper understanding of the challenges
present in developing RL for adaptive congestion pricing. This section gives an understanding of
the concept of congestion pricing and how, in theory, it can be implemented along with a look
at RL for congestion pricing and what it requires from a modelling framework standpoint. These
concepts are presented throughout this section through the review of academic literature.
2.1
Congestion pricing
One way to remedy the congestion of traffic networks is to implement a tax on the use of the
traffic networks which can be done in the form of congestion pricing [6]. Congestion pricing works
by having users of the traffic network pay for the amount of congestion and use of the traffic
network that they introduce. This forces the network users to accept the new and higher price of
their transportation needs or adjust their travel in all aspects including mode of transportation,
departure time and more [6]. Congestion pricing of traffic networks, when correctly implemented,
functions as a tool to partially control the choices of the traffic network users to optimise certain
factors as chosen by the implementer. These factors could include earned revenue, total travel time,
total number of users, average speed and more.
2.1.1
Implementation of congestion pricing
Implementation of congestion pricing is in its nature a complex problem with a variety of different
solutions. The chosen solution for a given situation should reflect both the given situation and the
desired outcome of the solution, whether it is to increase earned revenue, increase traffic flow or
another objective. [6] defines several different ways to implement congestion pricing depending on
what is needed for a specific traffic network. The scheme considered in this thesis is a distance-
based scheme. In a distance-based scheme, the users travel on the traffic network and pay a toll
based on the distance travelled [8]. The amount paid can also be changed based on the current
time of day and demand on the traffic network. One varying toll is known as a responsive toll as
it responds to current levels of congestion and changes in an attempt to relieve congestion [6]. The
concept of a responsive toll requires both the availability of real-time data from the traffic network
to assert the level of congestion and a model which is trained to change the toll in real-time. In this
thesis, the toll profile is set before the beginning of each day based on the time of day such that it
can be used in cases where real-time data from the traffic network is not available. This form of
pricing is known as a time-of-day toll and already sees use across the world in different ways [6].
One complication of setting the price for effective congestion pricing is the varying demand on
the traffic network within and across days. This thesis will attempt to deal with this extensively
studied complication, by employing deep RL to train a model to set a toll profile rather than a
single toll price. This toll profile, set by a deep RL model separately for each day, will attempt to
ensure that the price changes within and across days to accommodate differences in demand on the
traffic network. This could potentially deal with changes in demand depending on the time of day
but may fall short if a day is far from the norm due to a high-profile event, weather conditions or
other situations that could impact traffic conditions, capacity or demand on traffic networks.

2.2
Traditional congestion pricing methods
Bayesian optimization is used in [19] to set the price for congestion pricing in both an area-based
scheme and, as is the case in this thesis, a distance-based scheme. This is done to set a time-of-day
toll profile that is able to handle the costs experienced in the traffic network resulting from the
user’s use of the traffic network. Bayesian optimization is done in two steps: Updating Gaussian
process, where the probability distribution of the objective function is found, and optimising the
acquisition function, where the mean and variance of the probability distribution of the objective
function are used to find the optimal step for the next toll. In [19] this results in a significant
reduction in congestion when applying an adaptive toll profile using parameters from Bayesian
optimization compared to the no-toll scenario on the traffic network.
Marginal cost pricing is another traditional economic strategy for handling congestion. It re-
quires the toll for each traffic network user to be equal to the amount of cost experienced in the
traffic network as a result of the user. This is known as the marginal cost toll. Finding the right
toll to charge each individual user can be an incredibly difficult task and is not feasible. This is
especially true in real traffic networks, where it is not possible to make the assumptions necessary
for the exact marginal cost toll. This leads to having to approximate the marginal cost toll for
users which can perform better than without any toll at all with under-estimations of marginal cost
tolls [18].
2.3
Reinforcement learning
Research into RL started in 1957 when Bellman introduced the equation known as the Bellman
equation as an approach to solve the problem of creating a controller for dynamical systems [11].
This has evolved into a wide field which attempts to apply the same principles to solve problems
with various characteristics. This section presents some of the different approaches in theory with
relevant articles.
2.3.1
Model-based and model-free approaches
RL algorithms can be divided into two categories: model-based and model-free.
Model-based
approaches, where the RL model will learn probabilities of future states based on the actions taken
and the reward. Model-free approaches, where the RL model simply learns which actions give what
reward based on feedback from the environment [22]. The following subsections will present policy
gradient methods in general followed by Proximal Policy Optimization and Deep Deterministic
Policy Gradients.
2.3.2
On-policy and off-policy approaches
RL algorithms can further be divided in two categories: on-policy and off-policy learning. On-
policy RL algorithms evaluate and update the policy based on the current performance within
the environment. The same policy is used for selecting actions as well as evaluating the expected
reward and thereby the value of the actions. An on-policy RL algorithm, therefore, updates the
policy based on the rewards from the actions of the same policy. This is how the proximal policy
optimisation algorithm which is used in this thesis approaches the updates of the policy.
Off-policy RL algorithms evaluate and update the policy based on a different policy.
This
results in the algorithm using a policy to take actions and then evaluating the expected reward
from those actions and updating a separate policy based on that evaluation. This lets off-policy RL
algorithms learn from past experiences and policies which can lead to off-policy algorithms being
5

Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Congestion Pricing
more sample efficient than on-policy algorithms since they allow learning from past experiences.
The deep deterministic policy gradient algorithm, which is introduced later in this section, is an
example of an off-policy RL algorithm [17].
Both on-policy and off-policy algorithms will be presented and used in the following subsections.

Twin delayed deep deterministic policy gradient
Twin delayed deep deterministic policy gradient (TD3) is an RL algorithm introduced by Fujimoto
et al [16], as an improvement on the DDPG algorithm previously described. The issue of concern
that TD3 addresses is overestimations of Q-values, which gives DDPG a tendency to learn a poor
policy that exploits the Q-function. This issue is solved in TD3 by implementing the three following
techniques [21]:
• Two Q-functions: The twin part of the name comes from the fact that two Q-functions are
used as opposed to the single Q-function of DDPG. The TD3 RL algorithm then uses the
lower of the two Q-function estimations as the target in the Bellman equation.
• Lower frequency of policy updates: The TD3 RL algorithm updates the policy less
frequently than in DDPG, once for every second Q-function update [16].

2.4
Reinforcement learning for congestion pricing
RL models used to set the toll price for congestion pricing is not a novel idea. Sato et al [26]
and Pandey et al [22] present implementations of RL models that attempt to solve the congestion
problem of traffic networks. Both articles show that the approach is useful for solving the problem
without making assumptions of user behaviour, since this can be internalised within the RL model
and thereby handled within the toll profile parameters set. This allows for the creation of models
which can solve the problem of setting the price for congestion pricing without the need for a
complete understanding of how the users behave or making too many assumptions. This means
that a price setting model trained using RL could make decisions within and across days.
The implementation done by Sato et al [26] uses a Q-learning approach to train a model which
adapts the toll price each day based only on observable variables in the traffic network which would
be a requirement for a model to be useful in a real traffic network. The use of Q-learning limits
the model to using a discrete action space which means that the flexibility of change in toll for
each day is quite limited compared to using a continuous action space.
This could mean that
potentially a better action is available in certain situations but the model is limited to certain
actions only. The use of a discrete action space does however result in a smaller amount of actions
and an effective model should therefore need a smaller amount of computational time for training,
which may be beneficial depending on the training time of continuous models and the cost of
computational time. The approach taken was tested on both a traffic network model with a single
bottleneck and a traffic network model with multiple bottlenecks. The simulation used had users of
the traffic network wait for available capacity before going through a bottleneck and used RL with
the objective of optimising the wait times. The approach showed clear reductions in wait times in
both traffic network models although the improvements were better on the single bottleneck traffic
network model [26].
The implementation done by Pandey et al [22] uses two RL approaches based on policy gradient
methods, which are explained in detail in Section 2.3.3, namely PPO and A2C, and one approach
based on Q-learning, which is SAC, all of which are model-free approaches as explained in Section
2.3.1. The action taken by the RL models is continuous and sets the toll paid for each part of
the traffic network, which the authors have split into several different toll links, making the action
a vector of tolls, one for each toll link. Two different reward functions are presented; one which
rewards the RL model for maximising the revenue from the toll links and one which rewards the
RL model for minimising total travel time across the traffic network. Besides the two different
rewards functions and therefore different objectives, the RL models are trained and tested on four
different traffic network models. All three RL approaches are able to train models on all four traffic
network models, which can optimise total system travel time or revenue. The results are clearly
very dependent on the traffic network model as well as the RL approach with different approaches
being better for different traffic network models and objectives [22].
The implementations done by Sato et al [26] and Pandey et al [22] are made for different traffic
network models and are therefore not directly comparable. Both implementations do however show
that the problem of congestion of traffic networks can be at least partially solved using different
implementations of RL to set adaptive and dynamic congestion pricing. The successful use of Q-
learning also shows, that even a simpler and discrete approach can have some success in solving

difficult problems.
The problem of congestion of traffic networks and solving this with congestion pricing can be
viewed as a control problem for which an optimal solution, the solution with the lowest amount of
congestion, is to be found. Previous research has shown that RL can be effective in solving control
problems as in [15]. Here control problems are seen as problems to be solved by applying different
RL algorithms. Many RL approaches are presented, including the ones used within this thesis.
[15] also raises some considerations on the kinds of problems that RL algorithms can be applied

model.

Overall framework
This section will define the overall framework used to solve the problem of congestion using adaptive
congestion pricing with a toll profile that varies from day to day. The context of the framework
and the framework used for training and testing RL models will be presented. The parts of the
overall framework broadly presented here are presented in detail in Section 4 for the simulation
environment and Section 5 for the RL framework.
3.1
Context
The overall framework is set up by using a simulator, which includes a traffic network regulated
by an adaptive time of day toll with users who make mobility choices based on the supply they
experience on the traffic network. The users will learn and adapt to the traffic network’s experienced
situations, which will change their mobility choices in a day-to-day manner based on the previous
day. The users adapt their mobility choices differently depending on their own value of time as well
as the detriment of being either early or late.
Figure 1 shows the overall framework from a high-level perspective. The traffic network is at
the centre and is influenced by changes from the regulator that sets the adaptive time of day tolls
and the mobility choices of the users. The traffic network simulates the use of capacity based on
the mobility choices made by the users. The users pay toll payments to the regulator depending on
when and how far they travel and base their own mobility choices on their experience of the supply
coming from the traffic network. If the toll is more expensive at a certain time than the value of
arriving at the desired time for each user, the users will change their departure time accordingly.
The policy, which is a model trained by an RL algorithm, takes an observation from the traffic
network as input and outputs an action consisting of changes to the toll profile parameters which
the regulator uses to impose the adaptive time of day tolls for the next day on the traffic network.
This means that the policy makes relative changes to the toll profile parameters for each day based
on the observation from the previous day.

Reinforcement learning framework

Simulation environment
This section will define the simulation environment in which the behaviour of users on a traffic
network with tolling is simulated and it will further define the metrics which are used to assess the
performance of tolling in the simulation environment. The simulation script models supply using
a trip-based Macroscopic Fundamental Diagram (MFD) model, which will be presented in further
detail in this section. The simulation environment is created by using a modified version of the
script developed for the same purpose, which was introduced and used in [29]. The modifications
primarily consist of measurements of metrics for observations and adjusting to the object-oriented
paradigm.
4.1
Simulation framework
The simulation framework should simulate a traffic network, the regulator imposing tolls on the
traffic network as well as the users, who use the network each day. This includes simulating the
capacity provided by the traffic network, the mobility choices of the users and how the users learn
and adapt to changes in travel times based on both the mobility choices of other users and the
tolls imposed. The users should also be heterogeneous in their value of time, their value of arriving
early or late as well as their desired travel times and travel distances [29]. This is done by using a
trip-based MFD model.
The supply is simulated as shown in Figure 1 and briefly presented in Section 3 using a trip-based
MFD model. This ensures that when the number of users entering the traffic network increases,
the number of users exiting the traffic network will slowly increase after some time making for a
much more realistic simulation of a real traffic network. A trip-based MFD model also allows for
a much wider range of traffic network users in terms of travel distance, arrival times and the early
and late arrival penalties [29]. This makes it possible to charge tolls based on the travel distance
as well as time, meaning that a user who travels further than another user with the same traffic
network entry time, will pay a larger toll. This tolling scheme is known as a distance-based scheme
as presented in Section 2.
The framework simulates travel on a traffic network for a number of users for one day at a
time. This is done by having a demand, which is the total amount of users who use the traffic
network each day, and a capacity, which is the maximum number of users that the traffic network
can contain at once. In this thesis, a demand of 3700 users of the traffic network is simulated for
all cases with a capacity of 4500 unless otherwise stated. By having a capacity that is larger than
the demand, a state of gridlock is avoided, which would otherwise result in a speed of zero across
the traffic network[29]. This is done by first sampling from several Gaussian distributions to set
the attributes for each user individually for the first day of simulation. The attributes for each user
are defined in the following manner with units in brackets:
• Departure time - determining when a user enters the traffic network [minutes].
• Trip length - determining how far the user will travel [metres].
• Value of time - determining how valuable time is to the user [DKK per minute].
• An early arrival penalty - determining how detrimental an early arrival is to the user [DKK
per minute].
• A late arrival penalty - determining how detrimental a late arrival is to the user [DKK per
minute].

• Random utility - determining the unobserved factors contributing to the utility of the user
[DKK].

4.2
Performance metrics
Measuring the state of the simulation environment is done through four metrics which are measured
as the mean per user. These metrics are calculated based on observed measurements directly from
the simulation. This is done in order to be able to compare the performance of different models
and their ability to optimise the metrics in the simulation environment. These metrics are found
in [29] and the units are presented in brackets:
• Mean travel time - the mean travel time for users across a day [minutes]. This is expressed
as a positive amount of time spent by each user with a number closer to 0.0 being more
desirable.
• Mean travel time cost - the mean travel time cost for users across a day [DKK]. This is
expressed as a positive amount of cost imposed on each user with a number closer to 0.0
being more desirable.
• Mean social welfare - the mean of the sum of consumer surplus and the toll paid for users
across a day [DKK]. This is expressed as a negative amount of welfare with a number closer
to 0.0 being more desirable.
• Mean consumer surplus - the mean of the sum of observed utilities for users across a day
[DKK]. This is expressed as a negative amount of surplus with a number closer to 0.0 being
more desirable.
When assessing the effect of a toll profile on the state of the simulation environment it is
important to take the importance of the previous metrics into account. A model which attempts to
set toll profile parameters that minimise travel time across the traffic network, may not be effective
when measuring the impact on social welfare or consumer surplus. It is therefore important to be
aware of which metrics are the most important ones for comparing model performance and choose
to compare accordingly. The objective of the model taking actions should also be considered when
comparing across the four metrics.
The simulation environment is considered to have reached an equilibrium state once the user
mobility choices are close to constant on a day to day basis. Additionally, this means that the
metrics should be more or less constant across days after reaching an equilibrium state.
The
equilibrium state is reached when the perceived travel cost is equal to the experienced travel cost
for each user [29]. When testing the performance of a model’s ability to take actions which affect the
simulation environment, the metrics should be measured and calculated only after the equilibrium
state has been reached to avoid a decreased performance due to a worse starting toll profile or a
slowly converging simulation based on the actions from a model.
The simulation environment is used for training and testing RL models and the use will be
further presented in detail in the following section on RL.

Reinforcement learning
The RL modelling framework presented in this section will define the environment in which models
will be trained and tested. This includes the state space, the action space and the reward function.
The RL approaches, along with changed hyperparameter values, used to build and train models for
adaptive congestion pricing will also be presented.
5.1
Environment
This subsection defines the environment by the timestep and episode definition, the state space
variables, the observation space variables, the action space and the reward function which will be
used as part of the control problem of a traffic network with congestion pricing. This defines the
environment, how a model can perform actions within it and the response that the model will
receive after taking an action.
5.1.1
Timesteps and episodes
The finite timestep is modelled as a single day in this thesis. An episode consists of a number
of timesteps before the RL model weights are updated. In this thesis, an episode consists of 30
timesteps. The length of an episode should be set long enough to allow for the traffic network to
reach an equilibrium state where the mobility choices of users are not changing significantly on
a day-to-day basis. The length of an episode should also be kept as short as possible while still
maintaining the required length for the traffic network to reach the aforementioned equilibrium
state. This is done to allow a model to train with the lowest number of timesteps possible to keep
the computational time spent on training as short as possible.

5.1.3
Observation space
It is important to understand the distinction between the state and observation spaces. The state
space, as defined in the list above, contains all environment variables. The observation space is what
the RL models use as input and can be defined to contain some or all of the variables defined within
the state space. There are therefore important considerations as to what state space variables to
include when defining the observation space. One important consideration for this thesis is that
not all state space variables are observable in a real world system as they are in this simulation
environment. Different variables will be used as part of the observation space in this thesis but a
basic observation space will be used initially before testing with a broader observation space. The
basic observation space will be introduced in Section 6 on the methodology used for training and
testing. The broader observation spaces used for testing will be introduced in Section 7 when used
for testing.
5.1.4
Action space
A possible action is defined by the following three relative changes of the toll profile parameters
which will be set by the RL model. An action, therefore, consists of three separate values. These
values are limited and relative to the current toll profile and as such should be seen as the amount
of change imposed on the toll profile parameters. The absolute values of the toll profile parameters
are also limited and the limits are shown in brackets for each of the inputs in the following action
space:

• A: The relative change of the amplitude of the toll profile between −2.0 and 2.0 [min:0.0,
max:20.0].
• mean: The relative change of the mean of the toll profile between −3.0 and 3.0 [min:0.0,
max:90.0].
• sigma: The relative change of the standard deviation of the toll profile between −2.0 and 2.0
[min:0.0, max:50.0].
The starting toll profile parameters are randomly drawn from a uniform distribution one at a time
for every simulation environment reset. This means that A is drawn randomly between 0.0 and 20.0,
mean is drawn randomly between 0.0 and 90.0 and finally, sigma is drawn randomly between 0.0
and 50.0. The toll profile parameters are all bounded by 0.0 to allow for no toll. The combination of
randomly drawn toll profile parameters and a limited relative change limits the amount of control
that the RL models will have, as it could take several timesteps to reach the optimal toll profile
parameters. The limitation of actions however also ensures that the experienced toll for the users
will not change drastically on a day-to-day basis.
The toll rate is based on the departure time choice of each user. The chosen departure time
and distance travelled on the traffic network determine the amount of DKK paid for each trip. An
example of a toll profile can be seen in Figure 2. This toll profile would mean, that users with
chosen departure times around minute 50 would pay a much higher toll rate per meter than users
with a chosen departure time around minute 100. This would encourage users to avoid the high toll
rates by adjusting their departure times accordingly if their value of time is lower than the savings
due to the adjustments. The precise toll rate is taken as the toll rate from the first minute of a
15-minute departure time window. This means that if a departure time window starts at minute
60, then users departing at minute 65 would be paying the same toll rate for each travelled meter
as those departing at minute 71.

5.1.5
Reward function
The reward function should be made with an objective in mind. The reward function objective
could be to optimise the total system travel time, as Pandey et al found that it will utilise the traffic
network to full capacity at all times [22]. This will only work when assuming that the number of
users of the traffic network is constant from day to day, which will be assumed in this thesis unless
otherwise stated.
While there are many ways to construct a reward function which result in a good model per-
formance within a simulation environment, as defined in Section 4, it is important to define the
reward function such that it can be computed with only observable metrics for the models to be
usable and able to continue learning once deployed in the real world with only the observations
from a real traffic network available as inputs. This also possibly minimises the amount of training
needed on a simulation before deploying models to real-world scenarios as even a mediocre model
could potentially finish learning and improve performance while in use in the real world. The re-
ward function used in this thesis is formulated in the following manner:
reward = 100000 −TSTT,
(12)
Where TSTT is the total system travel time.
The reward function in Equation 12 is made with the objective to optimise the mean travel
time for users of the traffic network for each day. This optimisation is done by a negation of the
total system travel time which means optimisation can be done by maximising the value of the

6.3
Testing models
The performance of different models is assessed by testing the models which is done after training is
finished. The testing of models is done both manually and through the use of the evaluate policy
function in the stable-baselines 3 package. In all cases of testing, the actions of the models are set
to be deterministic to disallow any exploration of the environment during testing. This ensures
that the model reaches the best possible performance as the performance seen during training will
likely be an underestimation of the true performance due to exploration of the environment.
Given that models are trained with randomly selected starting toll profile parameters, the
testing of models is also done with randomly selected starting toll profile parameters, including
when testing with random actions. The models are tested across 10 episodes and therefore one

model is unlikely to have significantly worse starting toll profile parameters when compared to
other models across all 10 testing episodes.
The testing of all models as well as reference cases with no toll and random actions is also done
for 10 episodes to ensure that the results are representative. The metrics, which will be defined in
Section 7, are then calculated as the mean and standard deviation of those 10 episodes. The metrics

A baseline experiment is done which can be used for comparisons with all further experiments.

tolls. The consumer surplus is expected to be relatively high as the only cost imposed on users is
the travel time cost and no tolling.

7.6
Comparing with traditional congestion pricing
Understanding the significance of the results achieved with adaptive congestion pricing set by an
RL model can be difficult when only comparing to the results of no toll and random tolls as these
situations may be relatively easy to improve, upon even without a complex model.
Therefore
this section will compare the performance achieved by the models trained for this thesis with a
contextual Bayesian optimization (CBO) approach done with the code in [19]. The CBO with the
same observation space from the same traffic network is able to achieve a performance of 19.10
minutes of mean travel time per user after 131 iterations and 19.06 minutes of mean travel time per
user after 380 iterations. This a better performance than 20.17 minutes and 19.72 minutes from the
models trained for 225 episodes with PPO and TD3 respectively. The difference in results should
however be seen in a fair context as the approaches are not completely comparable.
The CBO is made specifically for this traffic network and would require significant changes for
use with other traffic networks, whereas the RL models can adjust to other traffic networks and
capacities and train to improve during use on an actual traffic network. This should be kept in
mind when considering whether to choose a CBO or RL approach for adaptive congestion pricing.
The actual difference in performance between the CBO, 19.06 minutes of mean travel time per user,
and the model trained for 225 episodes with TD3 using relative actions, 19.72 minutes of mean
travel time per user, is also relatively small when compared to the no toll and random toll mean
travel times. The model trained for 225 episodes with TD3 using absolute action reaches and even
better performance with a mean travel time per user of 19.59 minutes.
Comparing the models trained in this thesis to the CBO approach is slightly partial when
looking at the social welfare and consumer surplus metrics. This is due to the RL models not
having any incentive to optimise these metrics. This is done through the choice of reward function
which only incentivises reducing the total system travel time and thereby reduces both the mean
travel time and mean travel time cost for users. This is not the case for the CBO approach where
the incentive is to increase the mean social welfare per user, which the model does, but which also
manages to decrease the mean travel time and mean travel cost for users in the process. This is
why the two approaches are compared on mean travel time per user in this section as these metrics
are the most reasonable to use for comparison.
7.7
Discussion
This section discusses findings and the approach in the experiments and overall methodology.
Simulation environment
The simulation environment used in this thesis was based on the simulation script developed in
[29], as previously detailed in Section 4. This was designed with inspiration from a real traffic
network but is overall a fabricated scenario. Therefore the models could be trained to perform
closer to how they would perform in a real traffic network if the simulation environment was
developed with the exact parameters from a real traffic network scenario. This should be considered
when assessing the performance and especially the transferability to real traffic networks. The
models are not necessarily able to transfer the performance seen in this simulation environment to
another simulation environment with different parameters for user choices and learning. This is an
important consideration even though the models are able to transfer their performance between
51
==PAGE:66==
Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Congestion Pricing
two different scenarios, which can be seen in the experiment with the change in capacity of the
traffic network.
Effect of the reward function
RL models learn and change policies depending on the reward achieved from their actions. This
means that the reward function is the main factor in pushing the models to optimise certain metrics
in the RL environment. Given that the goal of this thesis is to reduce congestion by optimising
total system travel time and social welfare, this should be reflected in the reward function used to
train the models. The reward function used is presented in Section 5 in Equation 12. This reward
function only directly pushes the models to optimise the total system travel time and thereby the
mean travel time of users as these are directly related. The reward function does not successfully
push the models to optimise the social welfare in all cases seen in this results section, as social
welfare is only partially related by the total system travel time. Reducing congestion by optimising
total system travel time and social welfare would therefore likely be more successful by changing
the reward function to directly affect the social welfare in addition to the total system travel time.
How much training is needed?
Determining the amount of episodes and thereby timesteps necessary to train a model to solve the
problem of setting the toll profile parameters for adaptive congestion pricing, can be difficult and
requires weighting the amount of training, which is computational time, as well as the resulting
performance. The training of a model using either PPO or TD3 takes relatively long, mainly due
to the simulation script requiring between 15-20 minutes per episode on efficient hardware. This
makes it an important choice whether to train a model for 75 or 300 episodes due to the trade-off
between computational time and performance.
Making a decision on the amount of episodes to train a model should start by looking into the
differences in performance. For the sake of this discussion the model trained using PPO with the
basic observation space, consisting of the day index and the number of users in each departure
time window, is used for the performance comparison. This is assumed to be similar in cases with
models trained using broader observation spaces since the performance tendencies are similar. One
part of Table 2 can be seen in Table 8. When looking at travel time and travel time cost, it is clear
that the model improves significantly all the way up to 150/225 episodes of training with the 300
episode model actually performing slightly worse when measured on these metrics. In this case,
when measuring the performance on travel time and travel time cost, it makes sense to train for
either 150 or 225 episodes but not for 300 episodes. It is difficult to say whether the model trained
for 150 or 225 episodes is the better performing one due to the relatively high standard deviation,
which could mean that the extra 75 episodes of training from 150 to 225 would be worthwhile.
When measuring performance using the social welfare and consumer surplus metrics, the model
gets progressively worse from 75 episodes to 225 episodes of training. This however changes with
the social welfare and consumer surplus increasing from 225 episodes to 300 episodes of training.
Therefore if only travel time and travel time cost are relevant in the use of a model it may not be
worth it to train a model further, but if social welfare and consumer surplus are more important,
then 300 episodes or even more may be worthwhile.

8
Conclusions
This section sums up the findings and results of experiments done in Section 7 and concludes on
the research question posed in Section 1:
How can reinforcement learning models be implemented and trained for adaptive and
dynamic congestion pricing to effectively manage congestion of traffic networks
in a theoretical setting?
This section will also look into the limitations of the approaches taken in this thesis and what
future work could include and how it would be possible to add to and improve on the findings.
The findings of this thesis on the subject of using RL models to set toll prices for adaptive
congestion pricing in a traffic network can be seen as a combination of the results from the experi-
ments done in Section 7. The no-toll experiment is able to reach a state of equilibrium, where users
generally do not change their departure time choices between days. The experiment with random
actions for setting the toll profile parameters results in both better and worse performance than
the no-toll experiment depending on the starting toll. The toll profile parameters which result in a
worse performance than in the no-toll experiment are generally only seen when specifically setting
the parameters to achieve a poor performance.
The experiments with different observation spaces for models trained with the PPO and TD3
algorithms show that the basic observation space, which is simpler than the other observation
spaces, is able to achieve the best performance.
The trained models are generally all able to
achieve a better performance than in the no-toll experiment.
The experiments involving changes of the capacity of the traffic network show that the models
are able to keep their performance through a changing scenario that they have not been trained
on. Furthermore the models are able to achieve performance similar to models trained on the lower
capacity scenario without training on that specific capacity. This indicates that the trained models
have the ability to transfer their performance to a new scenario.
The experiments with a different action space, where the models use absolute actions to directly
set the toll profile parameters instead of relative changes, show a distinct difference between the
models trained using the two algorithms. The models trained using PPO are unable to learn much
and do not achieve a significantly better performance than the no toll experiment. The TD3 model
is able to achieve better performance than in other experiments with less training.
Overall the RL models are able to reach a performance close to the traditional congestion
pricing methods, including the CBO approach. The improvement offered by RL models in the
case of adaptive congestion pricing for traffic networks, lies in their ability to transfer performance
to different scenarios with a different capacity. The models are also able to continue improving
after being deployed to a traffic network without any outside interference. This means that RL
models are more flexible and useful compared to traditional congestion pricing methods, once a
sufficient performance has been reached in a traffic network simulation. Therefore using RL models
for congestion pricing could be useful and effective in cases where training time in simulation
environments does not need to be kept to a minimum, but transferability of performance and the
ability to keep improving while in use are important factors in the choice of congestion pricing
approaches.
8.1
Limitations
The approach taken in this thesis is generally successful in showing that adaptive congestion pricing
is possible using RL models. However, there are certain limitations which could be considered of
54
==PAGE:69==
Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Congestion Pricing
significant impact to the resulting models and their performance. This section will present the
limitations and the steps which could possibly remedy them.
The reward function used in this thesis, which was presented in Section 5, only rewards the
models based on the total system travel time. This means that the models only learn to optimise
the travel time which only has a partial effect on social welfare.
Since the stated overall goal
with adaptive congestion pricing in this thesis was to optimise both travel time and social welfare
experienced by the users of the traffic network, the reward function should include the social welfare
along with the travel time to ensure that models learn to optimise the social welfare along with the
travel time.
Possibly the most important limitation of the approaches used in this thesis is the computational
time needed for simulation of the traffic network and users. A large amount of computational time
needed for each simulated day, which is around 35 seconds with 3700 users, could be restrictive in
training models sufficiently to solve the problem of setting adaptive congestion pricing to reduce
the travel time and increase the social welfare of users.
8.2
Future work
The future work on the problem and solutions presented in this thesis could be centred around
several different topics:
1. Tuning of hyperparameters
2. Further training of models
3. Real-world testing of models
4. Interpretability of models
The tuning of hyperparameters in this thesis has been done in a limited sense and manually. The
automatic tuning of hyperparameters could open up opportunities to improve the performance of
the trained RL models and improve the data efficiency during training. This would be one way to
attempt to improve upon the work and research in this thesis.
Optimizing the simulation environment code to speed up training and testing could also unlock
better-performing models. The optimization and higher speed of the simulation environment code
could possibly improve performance of the models trained in this thesis by allowing for more training
episodes within the same amount of time. This would work in combination with the automatic
tuning of hyperparameters by improving both data efficiency and the speed with which the data
can be simulated, leading to a speed-up in several ways.
As some experiments with changes to the observation space have shown, models with access to
more information about the environment are not necessarily better than models with less informa-
tion. It would be interesting to allow these models to train further and see if the worse performance
is due to not having enough episodes to understand the extra information included in the broader
observation spaces or whether it is simply not adding any new information and introducing noise
to the models.
Putting the trained models to the test on a real traffic network would provide insight into
how performance can be transferred from the simulation environment to another scenario. This
would also make it possible to test whether the transferred model is able to continue training
and adapt to a new scenario and improve performance there.
This would indeed test whether
the improvements and conclusions arrived at in this thesis are simply theoretical and practical in

==PAGE:70==
Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Congestion Pricing
a simulation environment or if they can be achieved using the same approaches in a real traffic
network.
Work on interpretability and explainability of models to better understand how the models
work and learn about effective adaptive congestion pricing from the simulated traffic network would
help improve both the modeling approaches and simulators. This would aid in understanding the
reasoning behind actions and the problems that may hinder the trained models from reaching an
even more effective policy.
