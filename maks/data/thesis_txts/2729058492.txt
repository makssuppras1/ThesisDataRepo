Abstract
This master’s thesis project endeavors to analyze the potential of employing a hybrid
electrolysis­storage facility for the provision of ancillary services. The research involves
enhancing a publicly accessible linear programming model developed by Energinet to
align with the current configuration of the plant. The modified model will be utilized by the
author to assess the financial implications of operating a grid­connected electrolyzer and
battery, along with behind­the­meter renewable resources, to offer Frequency Contain­
ment Reserve (FCR), Automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR), and Manual
Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR) capacities. The study encompasses multiple
scenarios that aim to derive the most optimized sizing and operation strategies for the
integrated energy cluster’s various components. A comparative analysis of these simu­
lations will ascertain which scenarios yield the highest value for the project and which of
them may undermine its financial viability.

1.1
Motivation
In the pursuit of a sustainable and carbon­neutral future, green hydrogen has emerged
as a groundbreaking solution that holds immense potential for transforming the energy
industry. As nations strive to reduce their carbon emissions, especially in sectors where
electrification has more difficulties to reach, the deployment of green hydrogen has gath­
ered significant attention as a key enabler of the energy transition. In this context, Den­
mark, along with the European Union (EU), stands at the forefront to unlock the potential of
green hydrogen and lead the change towards a cleaner and more resilient energy future.
Green hydrogen is produced through water electrolysis, which harnesses renewable en­
ergy sources, such as wind and solar, to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. The pro­
cess yields a clean and emission­free energy carrier, with the ability to store and transport
renewable energy efficiently. Moreover, green hydrogen can also be used to produce
other sustainable products, such as synthetic fuels and chemicals that can replace the
use of fossil fuels. This is known as Power­to­X, where renewable electricity is seized to
create carbon­free end­products.
The potential of green hydrogen and PtX technologies cannot be overstated. They hold
the key to decarbonizing hard­to­abate sectors, such as transportation, aviation, and
heavy industry, where electrification cannot reach. As renewable electricity generation
expands, surplus power can be leveraged by transforming it into hydrogen and other en­
ergy carriers, therefore achieving a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions and fossil­fuel
dependency and promoting a well­balanced and flexible energy system.
Recognizing this transformative potential, Denmark, assuming a leading role, and the
EU have embarked on a collaborative journey to accelerate the adoption of green hydro­
gen and power­to­X technologies, fostering innovative approaches, and leveraging their
strategic advantages to spearhead global change.
However, a higher penetration of renewable energy in the EU countries´ enery mix poses
challenges for grid stability, due to the intermittent nature of these power sources. Vari­
ability in generation due to changing weather conditions can lead to imbalances between
electricity supply and demand, potentially causing disruptions in the power grid. This ef­
fect will be greater as the renewable share increases and here is where electrolyser and
batteries have emerged as promising solutions to address these challenges.
Both batteries and electrolysers are based on electro­chemistry, which offers fast re­
sponse times due to the fundamental nature of the chemical reactions involved, enabling
these devices to provide rapid frequency support and respond to imbalances in the grid
promptly. Electrolysers can switfly adjust their hydrogen production in response to grid
frequency signals, acting as flexible demand response tools and helping to balance the
system. Batteries can absorb or discharge electricity within milliseconds, making them
highly effective in addressing shorter­term fluctuations.
In this project, we dive into the pivotal role of electrolysers and batteries in the provision
of grid support services related to frequency imbalances and how they can impact the

1.2.1
Green hydrogen and Power­to­X in Denmark
Denmark global position in the green hydrogen and PtX development
Denmark has emerged as a frontrunner in the global transition towards sustainable and
renewable energy systems, including the development of green hydrogen and Power­to­
X (PtX) projects. With its commitment to combating climate change and reducing carbon
emissions, Denmark has strategically positioned itself as a hub for innovation, research,
and implementation in the field of green hydrogen technologies.
Denmark’s leadership in the development of green hydrogen and Power­to­X (PtX) projects
is driven by several key factors, such as a combination of abundant renewable resources,
forward­looking regulations, and a strong focus on innovation. With a robust wind energy
portfolio and project pipeline, both onshore and offshore, Denmark has a reliable source
of electricity for hydrogen production through electrolysis. The nation’s progressive regu­
latory framework offers incentives and subsidies, fostering investments in green hydrogen
and PtX technologies.
Moreover, Denmark’s commitment to research and innovation is evident in its collabora­
tive efforts among universities, research institutions, and private enterprises, resulting in
cutting­edge advancements in hydrogen production, storage, and utilization. Collabora­
tions between the public and private sectors have been key in the success of Denmark’s
green hydrogen initiatives, supported by both funding and investments. As an active
participant in global collaborations and initiatives, Denmark contributes to advancing the
adoption of green hydrogen and PtX technologies on both the European and international
stages. With a comprehensive national hydrogen strategy encompassing diverse sectors,
such as transportation, industry, and energy production, Denmark is well­placed to lead
the way in the integration of sustainable hydrogen solutions.
Denmark´s Power­to­X strategy
The Danish Government, following the ”Climate agreement for energy and industry” of
June 2020, published in 2021 a Power­to­X and Carbon Capture and Utilization (CCU)
strategy [1] with the aim to accelerate the advancement of PtX and CCU technologies and
support the establishment of this industry and the development of projects.
The four primary objectives that guide Denmark’s PtX strategy are listed and desccribed
below:
• Alignment with Climate Goals: PtX technologies must play a pivotal role in real­
izing the objectives set out in the Danish Climate Act, reinforcing Denmark’s com­
mitment to reducing its carbon footprint.
• Regulatory and Infrastructure Support: Establishing a robust regulatory frame­
work and necessary infrastructure to facilitate the long­term operation of the Power­
to­X industry within market conditions.

• Enhanced Integration: Strengthening the integration of Power­to­X technologies
into the Danish energy system, optimizing their contribution to the nation’s clean
energy goals.
• Export Potential: Fostering the development and export of PtX products and tech­
nologies to leverage Denmark’s expertise and position it as a key player in the global
green energy market.
Denmark’s PtX strategy is intricately linked to its vision for electrification. The country aims

teams and commercial beacons to establish local hubs for green energy and sec­
toral integration, with additional capital injection into the Danish Investment Fund.
1.2.2
European framework and the hydrogen­related Delegated Acts
While the project is situated within Denmark and falls under Danish regulations, its scope
transcends national boundaries to encompass the wider European landscape, impacting
both green hydrogen and Power­to­X (PtX) initiatives. The regulatory context for hydrogen
and Power­to­X (PtX) technologies in Europe is crucial as it sets the rules and incentives
that guide their development. Favorable regulations provide financial support, legal clarity,
and safety standards, encouraging investment and innovation.
The hydrogen and energy strategy adopted in 2022 by the European Comission, known
as REPowerEU, together with the delegated Act that defines Renewable Fuels of Non­
Biological Origin (RFNBO) are described in the following sections.
REPower EU
The REPowerEU Plan [2] , proposed by the European Commission, aims to reduce the
European Union’s reliance on imported fossil fuels, focusing on boosting renewable en­
ergy, energy efficiency, and sustainability to reduce dependency on non­renewable re­
sources. It includes measures such as increasing the EU’s 2030 renewable energy tar­
get to 45%, accelerating solar energy deployment, introducing a solar rooftop obligation,
promoting heat pumps, decarbonizing industries with electrification and renewable hydro­
gen, expediting renewable project permits, and raising the energy savings target to 13%
by 2030.

The REPowerEU strategy sets forth a dual target for renewable hydrogen production,
aiming for 10 million tonnes domestically and 10 million tonnes through imports by 2030.
The European Commission’s approach includes several key actions:
• Aligning Sub­Targets: The Commission called for aligning sub­targets for non­

• Hydrogen Joint Undertaking Investment The Commission aims to double the

• Industry Collaboration:
The Commission urges the industry to expedite the for­

use devices.
• Reporting and Monitoring Commencing in 2025, the Commission, in cooperation
with Member States, will provide regular reports on hydrogen uptake and its utiliza­
tion in challenging­to­decarbonize applications within industry and transport.
The REPower EU Plan also addresses the expansion of hydrogen infrastructure, which
would facilitate the production, import, and transportation of 20 million tonnes of hydrogen
by 2030. The report states that, While cross­border hydrogen infrastructure is currently
in its early stages, its progress is supported by its incorporation into the updated trans­
European networks for energy. The establishment of crucial hydrogen infrastructure, in­
cluding EU­internal pipelines and storage, necessitates estimated investments ranging

Regarding the strategy for importing up to 10 million tonnes of renewable hydrogen, the
Commission plans to establish three major hydrogen import corridors through the Mediter­
ranean, North Sea, and eventually with Ukraine.
First Delegated act
The first Delegated Act outlines the prerequisites for producing hydrogen and related fuels
to qualify as Renewable Fuels of Non­Biological Origin (RFNBOs) [3] under the Renew­
able Energy Directive (RED) II. This definition applies not only to hydrogen but also ex­
tends to derivatives like ammonia, methanol, paraffin, and other hydrogen­based e­fuels.
Despite being named as fuels, this delegated does not only apply to the transport sector,
but rather it suggests that RFNBOs could contribute to renewable energy targets across
sectors. This Delegated Act shapes the concept of green hydrogen and its derivatives
within EU law, thus playing a pivotal role in advancing the hydrogen economy.
This delegate mandates three important criteria that producers must fulfill to certificate
their products and RFNBO:
• Additionality This principle aims to ensure a continuous flow of new investments
in renewable electricity while preventing electrolyser deploymentfrom drawing elec­
tricity away from existing renewable plants, which could lead to a electricity grid
4
==PAGE:12==
with more carbon emissions. To achieve this, the additionality criteria state that the
renewable electricity (RE) plant must have become operational no more than 36
months before the electrolyser is connected. Additionally, the RE plant must not
have received any subsidies for its construction and operation.
• Temporal correlation: The goal of this principle is to ensure that hydrogen produc­
tion matches the generation of electricity from the contracted renewables. This has
to proved on a monthly basis until 2030, and from then, it must be done on an hourly
basis.
• Spatial correlation In order to fullfil the geographical correlation principle, the hy­
drogen production plant and the contracted RE plant are either: located in the same
bidding zone, or in interconnected bidding zones provided the zone of the reneww­
able plant has prices that are equal or higher than the zone where the renewable
fuel is produced, or the renewable electricity installation is in an offshore bidding
zone interconnected with the electrolyser’s bidding zone.
For supplying the electrolyser with renewable electricity, it can be source from the three
following situations:
• Direct Connection to the RE Plant This involves conducting electrolysis within the
same plant as renewable production, ensuring no electricity is drawn from the grid
for electrolysis and the RE plant has been operational for no more than 36 months
before the electrolyzer.
• Power from the Grid the electrolyzer must be situated in the same bidding zone as
the RE plant. Additional requirements include a renewable energy share exceeding
90% in the bidding zone or an emission intensity below 18 gCO2­eq/MJ, along with
temporal and geographical correlation.
• Power from the Grid through PPA or Own Power Electricity from the grid is con­
sidered renewable if generated by the electrolyzer operator or purchased through a
Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) from an RE plant.
Second Delegated Act
The second Delegated Act focuses on calculating life­cycle greenhouse gas emissions
for RFNBOs. [4] It outlines a methodology for calculating greenhouse gas emissions and
sets a target for RFNBO emissions reduction. This Act implements a greenhouse gas
savings target of at least 70% for RFNBOs from 1 January 2021 compared to conven­
tional fuels. The calculation covers the entire life cycle of RFNBOs, excluding machinery
and equipment emissions. The fossil benchmark for RFNBOs is set at 94g CO2­eq/MJ,
resulting in maximum emissions of 28.2g CO2­eq/MJ.
Emissions exceeding this benchmark can impact the marketability of hydrogen and hydrogen­
based fuels. The Act also addresses the classification of CO2 used in RFNBO production
and outlines various scenarios under which captured CO2 qualifies as an avoided green­
house gas emission.

2.1
European Synchronous Areas
The power grids of the different European countries are linked through a combination
of cross­border electricity interconnections, which are networks of high­voltage transmis­
sion lines and substations that facilitate the exchange of electricity between neighbouring
countries or regions. These interconnectors play a vital role in enabling the integration
of electricity markets and cross­border electricity trading, improving grid stability, and op­
timizing the use of diverse energy resources across the continent, including renewable
energy generation.
Here is a more detailed explanation of why interconnections between neighbouring coun­
tries’ power grids offer several benefits:
• Energy Security and Reliability: By diversifying energy sources and providing
backup support during demand spikes or disruptions, interconnections enhance en­
ergy security and grid reliability.
• Renewable Energy Integration: Interconnections enable countries to share ex­
cess clean energy from renewable sources like wind or solar, supporting the transi­
tion to a greener and more sustainable energy mix.
• Cross­Border Energy Exchanges: Interconnections allow surplus electricity from
one country to be exported and utilized by neighbouring countries with high de­
mand or energy deficits, ensuring a stable power supply throughout interconnected
regions.
• Electricity Market Integration: Interconnections foster regional or continental elec­
tricity markets, promoting cross­border trading, competition, and efficient utilization
of generation capacity, leading to cost savings for consumers.
One of the main points addressed in this project relates to the ancillary services and how
they can increase the profitability of a hydrogen production plant, at the same time that
they improve grid reliability. Therefore, the content of this project will mainly focus on the
first point presented above, which is grid stability.
In Europe, grid networks operate at a frequency of 50 Hz, meaning that the alternating
current (AC) electricity produced and consumed across most of continental Europe op­
erates at a frequency of 50 Hz. The frequency of a power system refers to the standard
rate at which alternating current (AC) oscillates within a wide­area synchronous grid.
In a well­regulated power system, both electricity production and demand are coordinated
to operate at the nominal frequency. Electricity production from power plants, whether
they are thermal, nuclear, hydroelectric, or based on renewable sources, is controlled
and adjusted to match the demand for electricity at any given moment. Grid operators
continuously monitor the balance between electricity supply and demand and adjust the
generation output to maintain the frequency close to the nominal value.

A synchronous area, also known as a synchronous grid, is a geographical region where
multiple power systems are interconnected and operate at the same frequency in syn­
chrony with each other. In a synchronous grid, the interconnections among regions’ power
systems enable electricity generation and consumption to be coordinated to ensure that
they operate at the same frequency, which is 50 Hz in all of Europe.
Europe has several synchronous regions due to historical, geographical, and technologi­
cal factors. The development of electrical networks, the history of power production and
distribution, and the proximity of nations all have an impact on the creation of synchronous
grids. In the European region, there are five main synchronous areas:[5]
Figure 2.1: European Synchronous Grid Areas [5]
• Continental Europe Synchronous Area: The majority of the nations of continen­
tal Europe are included in this area, which is the largest synchronous grid in all of
Europe. It covers nations like Germany, France, Spain, Italy, the Netherlands, Bel­
gium, Switzerland, Austria, Poland, Romania, Greece and more. Regarding Den­
mark, only DK1 zone is included in this synchronous area. To guarantee grid stability
and supply security, the transmission system operators in this region work closely

together through ENTSO­E (the European Network of Transmission System Oper­
ators for Electricity).
• Nordic Synchronous Area: The Nordic synchronous area covers several Nordic
countries, including the DK2 zone of Denmark, Sweden, Iceland, Norway, Finland,
and parts of Estonia. These countries are interconnected and operate as a single
synchronous system, allowing for efficient power exchange and coordinated opera­
tion.
• Baltic Synchronous Area: Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania are part of the Baltic
synchronous region. These nations connect their synchronized power system to the
Continental Europe Synchronous Area through high­voltage transmission cables.
• United Kingdom Synchronous Area: With England, Scotland, Wales, and North­
ern Ireland as its constituent parts, the United Kingdom has its own synchronous
region. This area is also connected to the Synchronous Area of Continental Europe
through a number of interconnectors.
• Ireland Synchronous Area: Ireland has its synchronous grid, and it is connected
to the United Kingdom Synchronous Area through interconnectors.
Although there are different synchronous areas throughout Europe, efforts are being made
to strengthen grid interconnection and encourage international electricity trading. A more
integrated and united European electrical market is the goal of initiatives like the Euro­
pean Network of Transmission System Operators for electrical (ENTSO­E), which aims
to enhance coordination and collaboration among various synchronous zones. However,
creating a single synchronous area for the whole of Europe would need major infras­
tructure development, regulatory alignment, and political cooperation among European
nations, which may imply major upfront costs and a considerable amount of time.
The hydrogen plant developed in this project is located in DK1 zone of Denmark, which
means that it is within the Continental Europe Synchronous Area coordinated by ENTSO­

2.2
Grid Inertia and Synchronous and Asynchronous
Generation
The inertia of the power grid refers to the ability of the system to resist changes in fre­
quency when subjected to disturbances, such as sudden fluctuations in electricity supply
or demand. In a stable grid, the frequency remains close to the nominal value (e.g., 50
Hertz or 60 Hertz) due to the rotational inertia of large generators. As these generators
maintain a constant rotational speed, they act as storage of kinetic energy, providing the
grid with inertia. This is highly valuable when a power plant fails, as it temporarily keeps
the frequency close to the nominal value, giving time to the grid operator to detect and
respond to the failure. Therefore, the inertia of a generator helps reduce the frequency
deviation and stabilize the frequency during fluctuations. However, not all generators are
able to provide the same inertia to the system, as it depends on the mass of the rotational
masses.
There are two types of electricity generation that impact the inertia of the system:
• Synchronous Generation: Synchronous generation sources, such as conven­
tional thermal (coal and natural gas), nuclear and hydroelectric power plants use
9
==PAGE:17==
steam turbines to produce electricity at a stable frequency, contributing significantly
to the grid’s inertia. These power plants have large rotating masses (rotors) that
store kinetic energy, and their rotational speed is directly linked to the grid frequency.
As a result, they provide inherent inertia, which acts as a stabilizing force for the grid.
The inertia from synchronous sources allows the grid to withstand disturbances, pro­
viding time for automatic control systems to respond and restore equilibrium.
• Asynchronous Generation Sources:
Asynchronous generation sources, which
include wind power, solar power, and battery energy storage, lack considerable ro­
tating masses and do not naturally provide inertia to the grid. Their electricity output
is typically variable, as they are dependent on external factors like wind speed or
sunlight, resulting in a non­synchronized generation.
Conversely to the past when the rotational speed of wind turbines was coupled to the
frequency of the grid, modern wind turbines used in large­scale wind parks are de­
signed to operate at variable rotational speeds based on wind conditions, requiring
power electronics to match the grid frequency. In the case of Solar PV, this tech­
nology produces DC electricity and therefore requires inverter devices to convert it
into AC at the grid frequency. [6]
The integration of asynchronous sources can reduce the overall inertia of the grid,
as these sources do not have large rotating masses matching the grid frequency.
Consequently, the ability of the grid to resist frequency deviations is diminished, and
faster frequency response times are required to maintain stability.
In the current context of an increasing share of renewable energy in the countries’ grid mix,
concerns regarding the loss of grid inertia due to a greater contribution from asynchronous
sources are arising. The higher the penetration of renewable energy, the more vulnerable
the grid may be to frequency changes during disturbances.
For the electricity system to remain stable and reliable, this problem must be addressed
and some measures must be taken to compensate for the decreased inertia. Deploy­
ing sophisticated control technologies, energy storage systems, demand response, and
adaptable grid management techniques are all part of this. These approaches support
grid resilience, improve frequency response, and facilitate the seamless integration of re­
newable energy sources.
2.3
Types of grid support services
Maintaining the frequency of the grid close to the nominal value is crucial for the resilience,
reliability and safe operation of the power system. Deviations from the nominal frequency
are caused by imbalances between electricity supply and demand, leading to grid insta­
bility and voltage fluctuations, which can provoke equipment damage. Frequency devi­
ations can cause sensitive equipment, such as motors, transformers, and electronics, to
malfunction, overheat, or even sustain irreparable damage.
Grid support services, or ancillary services, are additional services provided by grid op­
erators to support the stable and reliable operation of the power grid. These services are
essential for maintaining grid stability, balancing electricity supply and demand, and en­
suring the efficient functioning of the electricity system. There are three main categories
of ancillary services:
10
==PAGE:18==
• Services supporting frequency: These services are used by the Transmission
System Operator (TSO) to balance power supply and demand, maintaining the sys­
tem’s frequency close to the nominal value. They include frequency regulation,
spinning and non­spinning reserves, and frequency restoration.
• Services supporting voltage that empower the TSO to uphold the voltage stability
of the system, typically by regulating the provision of reactive power.
• Services enabling black­start that equip the TSO with the capability to restart the
system following a blackout.
This project will only address the Ancillary Services related to frequency support. The
other two are not included in the scope of the project.
2.4
Reserve Capacity
In power systems, mismatches between scheduled power supply and demand are practi­
cally expected on an hourly basis. Two main reasons drive supply­demand disturbances:
• Forecast errors in predicting renewable power supply and demand.
• Potential outages of generators, transmission lines, transformers, or other power
system assets.
As these mismatches are inevitable, given the impossibility of achieving perfect forecasts
or completely eliminating outages, Transmission System Operators (TSOs) must ensure
a backup power capacity to swiftly respond to these imbalances, thereby ensuring grid
stability and reliability.
To ensure there is always sufficient power capacity to balance the grid in case of fre­
quency disturbances, the Transmission System Operators (TSOs) have created reserve
markets, where the reserve is procured. In this market, power generators, demand re­
sponse providers, and other eligible participants bid to offer their ”reserve capacity,”
which is additional power capacity that can be called upon by the TSO to increase or de­
crease their production when the demand is higher or lower than the supply, respectively.
Conversely, a demand response provider, such as an electrolyser, operates in a balanc­
ing mechanism that is opposite to the previous scenario. It adjusts its load by decreasing
or increasing power consumption when the demand is higher or lower than the supply,
respectively.
The winning participants are rewarded for reserving power capacity, which has to be read­
ily available in case they are called upon a frequency disturbance. However, it does not
mean that this reserve will be triggered at all times, but it serves as an insurance mecha­
nism to meet unexpected frequency imbalances.
Given the fact that the project is assumed to be located in West Denmark, the electric­
ity market to which the plant belongs is DK1. Therefore, the management of the Ancil­
lary Services related to frequency support within this zone corresponds to Energinet and
ENTSO­e, as the zone belongs to the Continental Europe Synchronous Area. According
to the Danish TSO and ENTSO­e guidelines, there are three different types of reserve
capacities ­ FCR, aFRR and mFRR ­ with their respective reserve markets.

2.4.1
Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR)
The Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR), also known as primary reserve within the
Continental Europe Synchronous Area, is responsible for restoring the balance between
power generation and demand during frequency deviations at the first instance. Its objec­
tive is to stabilize the frequency of the grid, aiming for a value close to 50 Hz, and to reduce
the number of frequency dips/jumps while allowing slight deviations. The primary reserve
regulation operates automatically and involves generation or demand units equipped with
control systems that respond to changes in grid frequency. [7]
Technical conditions for FCR
The provision of the FCR services is subject to some technical requirements and condi­
tions:
• Frequency range and response time: FCR services must be activated when there
is an imbalance between generation and demand, and the grid frequency deviates
between 20 and 200 mHz from the reference frequency of 50 Hz, hence between
49.8 and 50.2 Hz. A permissible deadband of +/­20 mHz is allowed. [8]
A response start­up delay of up to two seconds is allowed. The reserve is supplied
linearly for frequency deviations between 20 and 200 mHz. It should deliver the first
half within 15 seconds and the remaining half within 30 seconds at a deviation of +/­
200 mHz. The regulation must be maintained for a minimum of 15 minutes, ensuring
stability in the power system’s frequency. Following the regulation’s completion, the
reserve must be replenished within 15 minutes to be prepared for subsequent fre­
quency deviations.[9]

– LER units/portfolios are required to have an approved ”Active Reservoir Man­

2.4.5
Future Developments in Denmark
The Nordics have a longstanding tradition of cooperation when it comes to balancing
their closely interconnected power grids. This collaboration has become even more cru­
cial as the region’s power system undergoes a significant transformation. The shift to­
wards greater reliance on renewable sources like wind and solar, along with increased
cross­border exchange, has led to the emergence of more refined markets with enhanced
temporal and geographical granularity. As a response to these evolving dynamics, En­
erginet and other Nordic Transmission System Operators (TSOs) are actively engaged in
the development of the Nordic Balancing Model (NBM).
In the pipeline are plans for the introduction of novel capacity markets for both aFRR
and mFRR, with the participation of DK1 and DK2. These shared capacity markets are
designed to enable each Nordic TSO to secure reserve capacity for aFRR and mFRR on
an hourly basis, across borders. The overarching goal of these capacity market auctions
is to optimize cost­efficiency at a Nordic level, ensuring that the reserve requirements
are met while also considering the implications of allocating interconnector capacity to
facilitate reserve exchange, rather than energy transmission.
Simultaneously, there are aspirations to establish unified energy activation markets for
aFRR and mFRR. In the case of mFRR, this involves an update to the existing common
Nordic regulating power market. The primary objective of these initiatives is to minimize
the collective expenses associated with the activation of balancing energy, all while fac­
toring in the constraints imposed by the grid.
Furthermore, at the broader European level, parallel efforts are underway to develop en­
ergy activation platforms dedicated to balancing energy. These initiatives are identified
as MARI for mFRR and PICASSO for aFRR, reflecting a commitment to harmonizing and
optimizing energy balancing practices across the continent.

3.1
Introduction to Green Hydrogen
Green hydrogen is produced in an electrolyser, which is a device that splits water molecules
into hydrogen and oxygen gas using electricity sourced from renewable energy genera­
tors . The electrochemical process of splitting water molecules into hydrogen (H2) and
oxygen gas (O2) is called electrolysis and it is a leading pathway to produce carbon­free
hydrogen using renewable energies.
Electrolysers offer opportunities for synergy with dynamic and intermittent power gen­
eration, which is characteristic of wind and solar energy. Therefore, the use of green
hydrogen can significantly reduce carbon emissions and the reliance on fossil fuels, help
combat climate change, and impulse EU’s energy independence and security of supply.
3.1.1
Green hydrogen applications
Green hydrogen has the potential to play a significant role in the transition to a more sus­
tainable and carbon­neutral energy system, offering a clean and versatile energy source
that can be used across multiple sectors. Some of the major applications of green hydro­
gen include:
1. Power­to­X:: In the power­to­X sector, green hydrogen can be used to convert
renewable energy into other forms of energy, such as:
(a) Power­to­Gas: Green hydrogen can be used to store excess renewable energy
as a gas that can be injected into natural gas pipelines or stored in gas storage
facilities.
(b) Power­to­Liquid: Green hydrogen can be used as a feedstock to produce syn­
thetic fuels, or e­fuels, aimed to be used as a substitute for fossil fuels in var­
ious sectors, including transportation and industry. The process of producing
e­fuels from hydrogen involves combining hydrogen with biogenic carbon diox­
ide (CO2) or carbon monoxide (CO).
(c) Power­to­Chemicals: Green hydrogen can be used as a feedstock for the pro­
duction of chemicals, such as ammonia and methanol, which can be used as
fertilizers, fuels, and in various industrial processes, such as the production of
pharmaceuticals or plastics.
2. Industrial processes: Green hydrogen can be used as a feedstock for industrial
processes:
(a) Steel production: Green hydrogen can be used as a reducing agent in the
production of steel, replacing fossil fuels such as coal and natural gas.
(b) Refining: Green hydrogen can be used in oil refining processes, replacing fos­
sil fuels such as natural gas and heavy oil.

3. Energy storage: Green hydrogen can be stored for later use, making it a valuable
source of energy storage that can be used to balance intermittent renewable energy
sources such as wind and solar.
4. Transportation: Green hydrogen can be used as a fuel for fuel cell electric vehicles
(FCEVs), which emit only water vapor as their by­product. FCEVs have a longer
range than battery electric vehicles (BEVs) and can be refuelled more quickly.
5. Power generation: Green hydrogen can be used as a fuel in gas turbines or internal
combustion engines to generate electricity. This can be particularly useful in regions
with limited access to renewable energy sources.
6. Heating and cooling: Green hydrogen can be used as a fuel for heating and cooling
buildings, replacing fossil fuel­based heating systems.
7. Energy export: Green hydrogen can be used as an energy carrier for export to
other countries, either via hydrogen pipelines or stored in tanks in compressed or
liquid forms. Moreover, if green hydrogen is converted to an e­fuel, particularly those
with limited access to renewable energy sources.
3.1.2
Types of Electrolysers
There are several types of electrolysers, including alkaline, PEM, SOEC, and AEM elec­
trolysers. Each type has its advantages and disadvantages and is suited for different
applications.
• Alkaline Electrolysers: Alkaline electrolysis is the oldest and most established
technology, and has been used in industrial applications for several decades. It
works by using an alkaline electrolyte, typically potassium or sodium hydroxide, to
facilitate the splitting of water molecules. They are reliable and have a relative low
cost and a long lifespan, making them a preferred option for large­scale industrial
applications. However, it is limited by its lower operating temperature and pressure,
which can lead to lower overall efficiency.
• PEM Electrolysers: PEM electrolysis is a newer but consolidated technology, com­
mercially available in large scale, that uses a solid polymer electrolyte membrane
to separate the electrodes. It operates at a slightly higher efficiency and lower tem­
perature than alkaline electrolyser. The technology is more compact, with a smaller
footprint, and offers a quick response to load changes, making it well­suited for
following renewable energy fluctuations. However, PEM electrolysers are typically
more expensive than alkaline electrolysers and have a shorter lifespan, which may
require stack replacement and higher operational expenses over the project’s life­
time.
• SOEC Electrolysers: SOECs are still in the early stages of development and are
currently being studied for use in large­scale industrial applications. These elec­
trolysers use a solid oxide electrolyte instead of a liquid electrolyte. They are more
efficient at producing hydrogen gas than alkaline and PEM electrolysers and oper­
ate at high temperatures, typically above 800°C, what makes SOEC electrolysers
suitable for PtX applications. However, they are more expensive and complex than
other types of electrolysers.
22
==PAGE:30==
• AEM Electrolysers: AEM electrolysis is similar to PEM electrolysis in that it uses
a membrane to separate the electrodes. However, AEM electrolysis uses an anion
exchange membrane instead of a proton exchange membrane. This allows for the
production of both hydrogen and oxygen gas at high purity levels, without the need
for a separate purification step. They operate at lower temperatures and pressures
and are more efficient at producing hydrogen gas. They are also more durable than
PEM electrolysers and do not require expensive precious metal catalysts. AEM
electrolysis is still in the early stages of development, but has the potential to be a
low­cost and efficient alternative to other types of electrolysis.
3.2
System components
The system consists of an electrolyser, an onshore wind farm a solar PV farm, a hydro­
gen storage tank and, in some cases, a battery energy storage system (BESS). These
components are explained in more detail below:
• Renewable Energy Assets The solar PV and wind farms are renewable energy
sources that generate electricity that can be utilized within the system to power an
electrolyser and/or charge a battery, acting as behind­the­meter generators, or they
can also feed the grid by selling the electricity on the spot market.
• Electrolyser The main component of the plant is the electrolyzer, which serves as
the core unit for the PtX (Power­to­X) process. The electrolyzer utilizes surplus elec­
tricity from the renewable sources or from the grid to produce hydrogen through the
process of electrolysis. The hydrogen produced by the electrolyzer is aimed to sat­
isfy the hourly hydrogen demand in the Power­to­X plant. The technology selected
for this project is the Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolyser, chosen for its
exceptional suitability in offering grid balancing services owing to its rapid response
capabilities.
When there is an excess of renewable electricity or when the spot price is relatively
low, the electrolyzer has the capability to produce more hydrogen than the imme­
diate demand. The surplus of hydrogen can also be sold directly in a hypothetical
hydrogen market at a market price or to third parties through bilateral contracts or
offtake agreements.
• Hydrogen storage tank Another optimal solution is to store the surplus of this ”low­
cost” hydrogen in a storage tank equipped with a compression system, which can
efficiently store and managed this gas. The stored hydrogen can be used to satisfy
the hydrogen demand in the Power­to­X plants at later periods or hours when is not
financially efficient to produce hydrogen, due to scarce renewable production and
high spot prices. This stored hydrogen can also be sold as explained above. The
compressor to increase the pressure required for storing hydrogen gas in the tanks
is assumed to be included here.
• Battery energy storage system (BESS) In addition to the renewable assets and
the electrolyzer, the system provides the option to integrate a battery. The battery
serves as an energy storage device and can be charged not only by the renewable
sources but also by the grid. This allows for the capture and utilization of low­price
electricity from both sources. The stored energy in the battery can then be dis­
charged to supply the electrolyser when renewable energy generation is insufficient
23
==PAGE:31==
and there is an increased demand for electricity and therefore the spot price is rel­
atively higher. The technology selected for the project is the lithium­ion battery,
chosen for its rapid response capabilities.
• Grid connection point and substation All the various assets are presumed to be
interconnected via a substation, which serves to transmit the generated electricity
from the renewable assets or facilitate electricity import from the grid when neces­
sary. The substation is assumed to be equipped with two voltage sides, namely
33kV and 150kV. The 33kV busbar functions as the connection point for the elec­
trolyser, battery, and renewable assets. Through this busbar, the renewable assets
can supply power to both the battery and the electrolyser. In contrast, the 150kV
side is linked to the transmission lines operated by Energinet. Via this grid con­
nection point, the battery and electrolyser offer reserve capacities, and electricity
transactions in terms of purchases and sales take place.
By combining the onshore wind farm, solar PV farm, electrolyzer, hydrogen storage, and
optional battery integration, the system enables efficient generation, storage, and utiliza­
tion of renewable energy. It supports the PtX process by satisfying hydrogen demand
while also providing grid flexibility and reliable power supply from both renewable sources
and the grid.

3.4
Assets providing grid services
The assets chosen to be included in the reserve capacity market are the electrolyser and
the battery. These two assets are based on electro­chemistry and therefore are able to
offer a very fast response to demand or supply changes.
Other assets, like a wind farm, could offer down­regulation services to the grid by partially
24
==PAGE:32==
curtailing their electricity production. However, the generation of electricity from such
assets is inherently volatile, and the bids submitted to electricity markets rely on forecasts
that often cannot accurately predict the actual generation. Consequently, there is a risk
of not being able to meet the submitted capacity if the production falls significantly below
the forecasted level. Therefore, the only two assets considered for providing ancillary
services in this project are the electrolyser and the battery.
On the one hand, the electrolyser can vary its load almost instantly, especially in the
case of Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) Electrolyser. By decreasing or increasing the
power supplied to the electrolyser, it can regulate the amount of hydrogen generated and,
consequently, the electricity consumed. This flexibility allows the electrolyser to respond
to grid needs and absorb or provide additional power when required during generation
excess of peak demand, respectively.
Similarly, a battery can participate in reserve capacity markets by adjusting its charging
or discharging rate. By controlling the flow of electricity into or out of the battery, it can
supply or absorb power from the grid as needed. During periods of high demand or grid
instability, the battery can discharge its stored energy to support the grid and maintain
system reliability. Conversely, during periods of excess generation or low demand, the
battery can be charged to store surplus energy.

Model
4.1
Description of the model
This model takes the form of a mixed­integer linear program, incorporating integer restric­
tions on certain variables. The aim of the model’s objective function is to minimize the
overall system costs while concurrently maximizing revenue streams, in order to attain
the most substantial positive cash flow feasible.
It is important to note that the model is designed in such a way that the participation of
distinct assets in the spot market is considered separately. This approach entails that
the costs or revenue generated by each asset are displayed entirely on an individual
basis. The model does not consider the net result of the combination of generation and
consumption of the different assets. This means that, for instance, all the generation
from the renewable assets is shown as sold in the spot market, or all the electrolysis
consumption is shown as procured from the spot market. This allows to have a holistic
view of that is the overall impact of each asset in the system

4.4
Objective function
The objective function aims to minimize the total cost in the optimization model. It consists
of the following components:
Cost and Revenue Terms:
The difference between the total costs/revenue incurred by each unit and the revenue
generated from ancillary services. In the case of renewable assets, the costs are negative
and therefore stand for revenue coming from power sales in the spot market. In the case
of the electrolyser, the costs are derived from power imports from the spot market.
Other cost/revenue components are included, such as hydrogen sales, curtailment costs,
storage power consumption costs, import costs, battery storage costs, and battery ancil­
lary service revenue.
Capacity Investment Terms:
• The investment costs of units based on their investment capacity and cost.
• The investment cost of hydrogen storage and discharge capacity.
• The investment cost of battery storage and discharge capacity.
• The investment cost of grid connection.
The objective is to minimize the overall objective function, considering costs, revenues,
and investments, to find the most cost­effective solution.

4.6
Assumptions and limitations
4.6.1
Perfect forecast
The model is created to simulate the operation of the plant during a full­year, knowing
beforehand the price of the spot market, the reserve prices, i.e. FCR, aFRR and mFRR,
and the hydrogen selling price in a common market for all the hours of the year.
The prices for the spot market and reserves have been taken from historical data from
2021 and therefore the model is run on these prices. The model hence simulates the
operation of the PtX plant during 2021 and optimizes the revenue from the participation
in the reserve markets.
Other simulations with 2025 or 2030 price forecast could be done to assess the revenue
potential from ancillary services, but as of today, there is much uncertainty regarding the
future price of both spot and reserve markets.
On the other hand, the price of hydrogen within a future market has been estimated and
may not reflect the hydrogen price when it is created.
All in all, having a perfect forecast of the different prices included in the model is definitely
unrealistic, but have been assumed to reduce the complexity of the simulations.
4.6.2
Ancillary Services Market
The hydrogen­PtX plant is located in DK1 and it will only provide support to the grid within
this zone. Therefore, the portfolio composed of the renewable assets, the electrolyser and
41
==PAGE:49==
the battery will solely participate in the FCR, aFRR and mFRR reserve markets within the
borders of DK1.
Exports to other areas such as the FCR cooperation zone, which has an export capacity
availability of 100 MW for Danish market participants, have not been considered in the
model.
Therefore, the revenue potential from providing ancillary services is limited compared to
a model that would include reserve markets from different zones. However, modelling
different markets for the same ancillary Service market has been excluded in this model
due to its complexity.
Moreover, as shown in table 2.2, the reserve volumes for FCR, aFRR and mFRR are 23,
100 and 284 MW, respectively. These volumes are expected to increase in the future due
to a greater electrification ­ and therefore a larger electricity demand in the Danish system
­, and a higher penetration of renewable generation in the electricity mix, bringing more
fluctuation on the supply side and hence and greater need of grid support services.
Still, large electrolyser plants should consider their participation in the ancillary service
markets more carefully, particularly in light of the expected rapid deployment of flexible
electrolysis capacity. It remains uncertain whether the price of reserves will decrease and
not be worthwhile ­ due to the larger availability of flexible capacity, or on the other hand
it will increase due to a phase out of current reserve providers while no new providers
replace them quickly enough.
4.6.3
Energy Activation Market
The purpose of reserve markets is to ensure that there is sufficient capacity should the grid
suffer an imbalance. The market players that are awarded capacity in the reserve markets
are then obliged to submit bids for balancing energy within the energy activation markets.
In this way, the capacity markets effectively serve as an availability payment mechanism,
enabling the TSO to secure access to bids within the energy activation markets.
The energy activation market is completely decoupled from the reserve markets and the
participants submit competitive bids that ensure the cheapest balancing energy when
required. Voluntary bids from participants that have not been awarded a capacity payment
are also accepted by the TSO. The clearing mechanism is marginal pricing for FCR and
mFRR, and will also be for aFRR in 2024.
However, the model does not incorporate energy activation markets due to the anticipa­
tion that the profit potential from energy activation markets for electrolysis will likely be
significantly lower than that of capacity markets. Furthermore, the system imbalances
are subject to stochastic variability. As a result, assuming perfect foresight is entirely
unrealistic, making it impossible to accurately predict when these imbalances will arise.
Incorporating the modelling of energy activation markets into this project would introduce
a substantial level of complexity, with the outcomes not being significantly affected.
4.6.4
Price­taker behaviour
The plant operation has been assumed to have taken a price­taker behaviour. However,
this could not reflect reality completely. In the current context, the small demand for FCR,
42
==PAGE:50==
which is only 23 MW in DK1 as of 2023, could be mostly covered by a 50­MW asset if no
limitations are applied.
If a single participant can cover most of the FCR demand, as a consequence it can influ­
ence the price and hence behave as a price maker. Nonetheless, the PtX plant is assumed
to behave as a price taker and adjust its operation to the hourly spot and reserve prices.

5.1.4
Hydrogen demand and off­take price
The electrolysis plant is conceived to supply a fixed hydrogen demand of 20 MWh per
hour to a Power­to­X plant, which will further utilize that hydrogen in chemical or e­fuel
synthesis, such as ammonia or e­methanol. Nonetheless, the power­to­X plant is not
addressed here as it is assumed to be operated by an external and therefore lies outside
the scope of the project.
If these 20 MWh are converted into mass units using the lower heating value (LHV) of
hydrogen, which is 120 MJ/kg, the resultant value is 666.67 kilograms of H2 per hour.
This demand for 20 MWh of hydrogen can be sourced either directly from the electrolyser
or from hydrogen storage tanks, where surplus hydrogen has been previously stored.
This storage mechanism effectively decouples the production and demand of hydrogen.
Additional hydrogen can also be sold in a hypothetical hydrogen market at a price of 450
DKK/MWh, which is equivalent to approximately 60.4 euros per MWh or, when consider­
ing the lower heating value (LHV) of hydrogen, approximately 2 euros per kilogram. This
pricing has been set to represent a pivotal point at which green hydrogen becomes com­
petitive with hydrogen derived from fossil fuels. IRENA notes that the cost of hydrogen
produced from fossil fuels, specifically coal and natural gas, spans between USD 1.1 and
USD 2.2 per kilogram [21]. Hence, the selling price of 2 euros per kilogram aligns with a
scenario in which green hydrogen can compete on par with carbon­intensive hydrogen,
assuming that the latter’s pricing remains unaffected by carbon­emission certificates or
rising feedstock costs.
However, the selection of this hydrogen sales price in an open market does not imply that
the production costs of green hydrogen, this particular case inclusive, would be below
2 €/kg. If production costs exceed this threshold, the project’s revenue would need to
originate from an alternative source. Potential sources could include revenue generated
from the sale of renewable electricity, earnings from providing reserve capacities in the
ancillary service markets, or even subsidies, such as the 3 euros per kilogram Clean Hy­
drogen Credit Tax in the United States, as defined in the Inflation Reduction Act [22].
5.1.5
Waste heat off­take price
As described in section 5.1.3, only 60 % of the electricity that is powering the electrolyser
is actually harnessed to produce green hydrogen. The rest is lost in inefficiencies of the
equipment, including both the electrolyser and balance­of­plant components. Out of that
40 % of electricity losses, half of it could be extracted from the system as waste heat
through heat exchangers and exported to district heating consumers.
Despite not being included as part of the objective function of the model, waste heat
can generate additional revenue if sold to district heating companies. In the results, the
revenue from the heat has been added as an extra to compare its potential against other

5.4.1
Analysis of electrolyser´s potential to generate additional revenue
through reserve markets
The electrolyser presents a substantial potential to harness its inherent flexibility, partic­
ularly in terms of rapid response capabilities, to strategically adjust its load and generate
revenue through the provision of Frequency Control Reserve (FCR), Automatic Frequency
Restoration Reserve (aFRR), and Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR) re­
serves. This flexibility is a key advantage that allows the electrolyser to swiftly modulate
its operations to meet the demands of the grid.
Preliminary assessments suggest that the electrolyser could yield an additional profit po­
tential of approximately 30% when capitalizing on the prevailing reserve prices in Den­
mark. This promising margin underscores the viability of tapping into reserve markets
to enhance the financial viability of electrolyser projects and reduce hydrogen production
costs, which in turn would untap the competiviness of green hydrogen against carbon­
intensive hydrogen in hard­to­abate sectors.
Furthermore, participating with the electrolyser in the reserve markets, as long as it com­
plies with all the qualification criteria, does not necessitate substantial additional invest­
ments in infrastructure when it is grid­connected. Rather, it primarily requires meticulous
planning and careful modeling of operational and bidding strategies.
In the context of DK1, the electrolyser is well­suited for providing mFRR reserves as En­
erginet exclusively procures up­regulation reserves for this service. The asymmetric na­
ture of the mFRR service allows the electrolyser to optimize its load on an hourly basis
for hydrogen production and actively participate in the mFRR market, strategically opti­
mizing its bidding strategy by offering its full capacity or slightly adjusted values based on
real­time market dynamics. This stands in contrast to services like FCR aFRR reserves,
where bidirectional power provision restricts the electrolyser’s suitability as it lowers its
utilization factor.
Nonetheless, it is noteworthy that prices associated with mFRR reserves are relatively
lower when compared to other reserve services. This pricing distinction is influenced by
the fact that mFRR is the last of the three services to be provided. The gradual nature of
mFRR’s response, compared to more immediate requirements of other services, fosters
a competitive market environment.
Furthermore, it is important to note that the model utilized in this study does not encom­
pass the intricacies of balancing markets. Typically, when a portfolio is awarded reserve
capacity, and therefore secures compensation through reserve markets, it is obligated
to participate in balancing markets as well. This additional layer of participation could
significantly impact the operational dynamics of the electrolyser.
The omission of the balancing market from this project introduces an important consider­
ation. The electrolyser’s response to load adjustments could potentially lead to an excess
or shortage of hydrogen production, especially if there is a fixed hydrogen demand to
meet. The size of the electrolyser and the capacity of its hydrogen storage system must
be meticulously planned and modeled to navigate these potential scenarios effectively.
In essence, while the electrolyser offers a promising pathway to leverage its flexibility for
revenue generation, it is imperative to recognize the complex interplay between reserve
and balancing markets. Any endeavor to participate in these markets requires careful
planning, encompassing factors such as electrolyser size, hydrogen storage capacity,
and the complex and stochastic dynamics of grid demands to ensure a robust and sus­
70
==PAGE:78==
tainable engagement.
5.4.2
Analysis of battery´s potential to generate additional revenue
through reserve markets
The potential benefits of developing a battery storage project to participate in reserve
markets are significant, as demonstrated by the outcomes of the simulation performed
in this project. The inclusion of a battery into the energy cluster could potentially dou­
ble the total project profit by contributing to reserve capacities in the FCR, aFRR and
mFRR markets. Particularly noteworthy is the prevailing profitability of the Automatic Fre­
quency Restoration Reserve (aFRR) market, where prices are approximately double and
nearly nine times those of the Frequency Control Reserve (FCR) and Manual Frequency
Restoration Reserve (mFRR) reserves. Assessing it individually, the incorporation of a
battery into the reserve markets within the current DK1 context has the capacity to gener­
ate annual revenue nearly five times its investment and operational costs, which unveils
its immense profit potential.
This potential is complemented by optimally operating the battery’s engagement in the
daily spot market, albeit on a smaller scale. Operating daily in the spot market, batteries
rely on their continuous charging and discharging cycles to generate revenue. However,
their energy storage capacity is limited, making them unsuitable for storing energy over
mid to long­term periods. For sustained profitability, batteries require the backdrop of
volatile spot prices that facilitate effective investment recovery. Moreover, when a battery
provides reserve capacities, its energy reservoirs are constrained due to specific qualifi­
cation criteria. This limitation is particularly pronounced in the case of Frequency Control
Reserve (FCR) within the context of DK1. In light of this, opting to offer reserves emerges
as the more profitable route for batteries.
However, certain risks and uncertainties are inherent in this pursuit. The unpredictability
of future prices is accentuated by the integration of renewables and the phase­out of
conventional generators, leading to increased demand for reserves. Conversely, the rise
of market participants through flexible assets like electrolyzers and batteries introduces
competitive dynamics.
Furthermore, changes in reserve auction mechanisms, such as the transition to weekly
aFRR auctions in DK1, and the emergence of new common capacity markets for aFRR
and mFRR introduce evolving market dynamics. The shift from pay­as­bid to marginal
pricing for aFRR reshapes pricing mechanisms.
This project has opted for an optimized battery size of 123 MW, which exactly matches
the combined volumes of FCR and aFRR procured by Energinet in DK1. While this con­
figuration could be ideal in a scenario with no new market entrants and perfect forecasts,
it lacks realism. Planning for a battery of this magnitude to fully cover both reserves ap­
pears overly ambitious. This perspective gains further traction considering the unrealistic
assumption of market stability and forecast accuracy.
In light of these considerations, planning for a battery of such size necessitates caution.
With the emergence of new players and the inherent unpredictability of the energy land­
scape, relying on revenue estimates from the current context of FCR, aFRR, and mFRR
reserves to offset the investment and operational costs of such a large battery becomes
exceedingly risky. On the other hand, the evolving nature of renewable energy penetra­
tion could potentially increase reserve requirements, thereby slightly mitigating this risk.
71
==PAGE:79==
Nonetheless, a more prudent approach should involve considering a smaller battery size.
This smaller battery can achieve a similar proportion of revenue in respect to its invest­
ment costs. While its impact on the overarching project might be limited, the strategy is
more adaptable to the dynamic energy market, enhancing overall project resilience and
long­term viability.
5.5
Sensitivity analysis
In order to provide a better understanding of how the different parameters can impact the
overall economics of the project, a small sensitivity analysis has been conducted. It has
the purpose of assessing the weight some input parameters have in the model and how
variations in them can affect the outcomes of the simulations. The parameters that are
going to adjusted are:
• FCR prices In 2021, spot prices soared to remarkable heights, recording an av­
erage day­ahead price of 88.14 €/MWh or 657 DKK/MWh in DK1. The following
year, 2022, witnessed further escalation in spot prices, maintaining an average of
88.14 €/MWh. Conversely, the year 2020 displayed a contrasting trend as prices
remained notably low, primarily due to reduced demand brought about by the Covid­
19 pandemic. Looking ahead, Energinet’s forecasts anticipate average prices of
approximately 50 to 60 €/MWh for the years 2025, 2030, and 2040 [16].
Therefore, 2018 is the year selected for running another simulation with different
spot prices. This year witnessed an average price of 44.05 €/MWh, slightly below
Energinet´s future estimations. Nonetheless, this choice offers valuable insights into
the potential effects of lower electricity prices on the project’s financial outcomes.
• FCR prices Energinet procures the required Frequency Control Reserve (FCR) in
4­hour blocks through the common Continental European market known as the FCR
cooperation. However, an increased penetration of renewables, characterized by
its inherent variability in production due to weather dependence, could result in a
heightened demand for FCR volumes to ensure sufficient grid balancing capacity.
Consequently, the likelihood of encountering elevated FCR prices becomes plau­
sible. For the sensitivity analysis, a price increase of two times the current rates
has been selected. Notably, even at this higher price point, it would still remain be­
low the FCR prices observed in 2020, before DK1 became part of the shared FCR
cooperation market [16].
• aFRR prices A future development for the aFRR market includes creating a unified
Nordic capacity market. This setup enables cross­border exchange of aFRR ca­
pacities, with the requirement that the aFRR capacity price difference exceeds the
day­ahead price difference by a set margin of 2 EUR/MWh. In theory, this devel­
opment could lead to a decrease in aFRR prices. This potential reduction is due to
an increase in market participants and the shift to hourly auctions. Therefore, an
aFRR price reduced by half have been chosen for conducting the sensitivity analy­
sis, closely resembling Energinet’s projections [16].
• CAPEX and OPEX The CAPEX and OPEX figures have primarily been sourced
from technology catalogs provided by the Danish Energy Agency [17] [18] [19], sup­
plemented by direct input from Energinet. All the CAPEX and OPEX values utilized
here are projected for the year 2030 and follow a declining trajectory. Nevertheless,
recent global events have shifted this trend upwards, introducing the possibility that
72
==PAGE:80==
these projections might not materialize. To factor in this risk, CAPEX and OPEX
figures have been elevated by 20% for the purposes of conducting the sensitivity
analysis.
• Hydrogen off­take price It has been assumed for this project that a common mar­
ket exists for the sale of green hydrogen by 2030. In this scenario, the selling price of
hydrogen has been set at 2 €/kg (equivalent to using 450 DKK/MWh in the model).
This price has been deliberately chosen to be competitive with the current prices
of carbon­intensive hydrogen, which is presently utilized in industrial applications.
However, achieving cost reductions for hydrogen production to reach 2 €/kg by 2030
presents a significant challenge. Consequently, to conduct a sensitivity analysis, the
hydrogen price has been doubled. This scenario might reflect a situation in a Eu­
ropean context where hydrogen consumers are mandated to purchase a specific
amount of green hydrogen, or where the prices of blue or gray hydrogen are artifi­
cially elevated due to higher carbon­emission taxes. Alternatively, it could involve
the provision of subsidies, such as 2 €/kg, to green hydrogen producers, similar to
what is done in the US with the Inflaction Reduction Act [22]. Any of these possibil­
ities could still render green hydrogen competitive at a price of 4 €/kg.
• Reserve Volumes As previously presented in Section 5.4, the chosen size of the
battery entails certain risks. Specifically, a 123 MW battery completely covers En­
erginet’s combined demand for FCR and aFRR capacities, at least as of 2023.

tery could provide the entire capacity—thus adopting a price­maker position—while
disregarding the adaptive strategies of current market participants or the potential
emergence of new entrants, could be deemed reckless.

scenario, the portfolio’s capacity will be restricted to 20% of each of the current
reserve demands in DK1, which would 5, 20 and 57 MW/h for FCR, aFRR and
mFRR, respectively.

Conclusion and future work
6.1
Conclusion
In summary, the analysis conducted in this study sheds light on the substantial poten­
tial of flexible assets, specifically the electrolyser and battery, to enhance the financial
feasibility of energy projects through their participation in reserve markets. The findings
underscore that integrating these assets can lead to significant revenue generation, par­
ticularly in scenarios involving aFRR reserves, which exhibit the highest profitability due
to their elevated prices. However, this potential is intricately tied to the shifting dynamics
of spot, reserve, balancing and hydrogen markets, as well as the unpredictable factors of
renewable energy adoption and market participant behavior.
The sensitivity analysis further highlights the necessity of considering various scenarios
to comprehend the influence of different input parameters. While reductions in reserve
volumes and market prices (i.e. Spot and aFRR) can yield considerable negative im­
pacts, increasing hydrogen prices and FCR prices demonstrate notable positive effects
on net balance. This reaffirms the importance of prudent decision­making in asset sizing,
integration strategies, and market participation to capitalize on revenue opportunities and
mitigate potential risks.
Specifically, a significantly more comprehensive analysis of the reserve volumes that a
single portfolio could be awarded within a competitive market context becomes neces­
sary. The uncertainty surrounding reserve prices remains notably high, influenced by
forthcoming market shifts impacting reserve capacities, coupled with the gradual decom­
missioning of existing generators and the introduction of new flexible assets.
Nevertheless, it is essential to acknowledge that the energy sector is constantly evolving
due to regulations, technology progress, and market changes. Therefore, energy projects,
and specifically hydrogen projects, should adopt adaptable strategies guided by thorough
analysis, market awareness, and the capacity to adjust to changing circumstances if they
aim to successful and profitable.
In essence, this study confirms the undeniable revenue potential of leveraging flexible
assets to provide reserve capacities in the ancillary service market. While this project is
centered in the Danish markets, regions with similar market frameworks also offer com­
parable potential for electrolyser and storage asset owners. For under­development elec­
trolysis or storage projects, participation in the reserve market should unquestionably be
contemplated, as it holds the capability to significantly amplify the project’s revenue. Nev­
ertheless, the decision­making process should rest upon thorough analysis and a compre­
hensive comprehension of the market dynamics. By upholding these principles, hydrogen
and storage projects can strategically position themselves to navigate uncertainties and
fully capitalize on the financial advantages associated with engaging in reserve markets.
6.2
Limitations and future work
The present analysis offers valuable insights into the potential of integrating flexible assets
like electrolyzers and batteries into reserve markets. However, there are certain aspects
Optimal operation of a hybrid electrolysis­storage system for hydrogen cost reduction in view of electricity
spot and balancing services markets
==PAGE:83==
that warrant consideration for refining the study and exploring its broader implications.
• Market price This model have used historical prices to provide a foundation for
the analysis. However, these price may not reflect the future reality and therefore,
incorporating projected price trends could yield a more accurate representation of
potential revenue. This would enable a clearer understanding of how changes in
market dynamics might influence the economic viability of the project.
• Qualification criteria for reserve markets Although this project has addressed
the requirements for participation in reserve markets, a comprehensive examination
rooted in the technical features of the electrolysis and storage assets should be c
meticulously undertaken.
• Energy balancing markets The project does not encompass the modeling of en­
ergy balancing markets. As a result, the influence of these markets on hydrogen
production and battery energy reservoir management remains uncertain. This rep­
resents one of the project’s notable limitations and opens avenues for future re­
search.
• Cross­border reserve demand Another significant limitation of the project is the ab­
sence of modeling concerning reserve demands from interconnected regions that
could potentially be exported from DK1. For instance, while the FCR demand is
only 23MW in DK1, participants in DK1 have the possibility of exporting an addi­
tional 100 MW to cross­border regions. Incorporating this aspect into the modeling
could substantially enhance the positive financial outcomes of these simulations, as
additional demand is introduced.
• Model´s level of detail The simulations in this project are conducted using hourly
data; however, it is important to note that renewable production exhibits fluctuations
within these hourly time steps. Consequently, an assessment of the electrolyser’s
ability to follow precisely such fluctuations becomes necessary, especially given that
the project does not account for potential ramping up/down factors due to the hourly
data granularity. Additionally, the electrolyser’s efficiency is not a constant value and
varies with the load. Enhancing the accuracy of the model by incorporating a pre­
cise representation of how efficiency evolves with the load could yield more precise
results. Moreover, the absence of a detailed model for the balance of plant further
underscores the potential for improvement. In essence, there exists a considerable
scope for enhancing result accuracy through these pathways.
• Others The accuracy of the input data also constitutes a limitation of the project.
While CAPEX and OPEX values are sourced from reputable references, their nature
as projections introduces an element of uncertainty. Similarly, the future hydrogen
market landscape remains highly uncertain, and the regulatory framework as well
as potential subsidies lack clear definition. Discrepancies in these factors could lead
to variations in the outcomes obtained from the project.
In conclusion, while the analysis has shed light on the potential of flexible assets in reserve
markets, addressing these limitations and expanding the study’s scope could offer a more
comprehensive understanding of the challenges and opportunities in integrating these
assets into the energy landscape.
