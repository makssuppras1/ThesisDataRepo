Abstract
Wind energy grows rapidly, yet there are many phenomena occurring in wind farms that are not
fully understood, such as wakes. Simultaneously, technological progress in Light Detection and
Ranging (LIDAR) provides sophisticated instrumentation for measuring the 3-dimensional wind
speed at any point inside a wind farm. However, the spatial and temporal structure of the LIDAR
measurements yields challenges in the subsequent analysis and interpretation.
During this project virtual LIDARs will be implemented on data derived from a Large Eddy Simu-
lation (LES). The LES will replace the observations against which the virtual LIDAR measurements
will be compared. The objective is to further improve future LIDAR measurement campaigns as
well as provide in-depth understanding of how to analyse LIDAR measurements and their limita-
tions, to extract valuable information about the wake behind a wind turbine.
The ﬁrst step of this master thesis is to create a stand-alone ground-based pulsed LIDAR tool
that can be applied to oﬄine LES data.
Utilizing this tool, the wind ﬁeld in wake conditions
can be monitored and various approaches will be examined to quantify the accuracy of the wake
statistics and wake detection. Similarly, any numerical errors and uncertainties introduced due
to the volume averaging of the LIDAR could also be identiﬁed and considered with the ultimate
purpose of improving not only the tool itself, but LIDAR campaigns in general. Hence, the second
objective of this thesis is to describe the best ways to employ LIDARs in order to measure in
highly turbulent situations and examine whether the acquired measurements can be used for wake
analysis.

1
Introduction
Wind energy is currently one of the most well-established sources of renewable energy and continues
to grow rapidly; in 2015 the cumulative global wind energy market growth was 17 % compared to the
previous year’s global capacity [19]. This rapid growth has become the driver for new technologies
to be developed and new places, suitable to facilitate large wind farms, to be discovered. Since
the generation of energy from wind is highly dependent on weather conditions, good quality wind
measurements are essential. These measurements are used in numerous applications during the
development and operational stage of a wind turbine or a wind farm. The most important are [50]:
• Wind Resource Assessment. It refers to the evaluation of the wind resources of the site under
investigation and the estimation of the power yield of a potential wind farm at that particular
site.
• Turbulence and shear measurements. Turbulence and shear inﬂuence signiﬁcantly the wind
turbine performance. Higher turbulence results in increased fatigue loads. Fatigue loads are
aﬀected in a similar manner by vertical shear, which will also increase the amplitude of the
1P rotor frequency.
• Power curve estimation. Wind turbine manufacturers need to clearly state the speciﬁcations
of each wind turbine; the power curve under given atmospheric conditions is one of those
speciﬁcations, perhaps the most signiﬁcant one.
• Load measurements estimation. Load measurements are used for fatigue and extreme load
calculations in order to estimate the lifetime of the structure and monitor its condition during
operation.
• Measurements for control purposes. The wind turbines need to be controlled not always with
respect to power yield optimization. For instance, during high aerodynamic noise or extreme
winds, the wind turbine has to be controlled in order to avoid disturbance of the residential
areas or to prevent potential damage in its components respectively.
For all of the above reasons, a great amount of research has been conducted in the wind measure-
ments ﬁeld and therefore a number of technological advancements have been accomplished [25][35][14].
One of the ﬁelds that has been developing rapidly is Scanning Remote Sensing Devices (SRSDs).
Up to recently standard anemometry, namely cup anemometers and wind vanes, was used for wind
measurements. Over the last few years however, SRSDs are broadly implemented and in some cases
even substitute the traditional meteorological mast. This thesis will concentrate on a speciﬁc type
of SRSDs, the LIDARs (LIght Detection and Ranging). SRSDs can serve all of the aforementioned
purposes while also demonstrating some advantages compared to their mechanical predecessors.
1. As their name suggests, they can measure from a distance. This is extremely useful in cases
where installing a mast at the location of interest can be challenging, such as in rough terrains
or oﬀshore.
2. They can, depending on the type of the SRSD, measure at multiple locations. This can be a
huge advantage especially in complex terrains or very large farms where the wind proﬁle may
vary signiﬁcantly between diﬀerent locations.
3. They are compact in size and easily transportable. That means that the same LIDAR can
be moved to diﬀerent locations within the same site -even though this is not considered as
“good practice”- or used in diﬀerent measurement campaigns.
4. They can be deployed much faster than a fully equipped meteorological mast.

cost eﬃcient. LIDARs and SRSDs in general will be discussed in detail in Chapter 2.
Obtaining accurate measurements is only one of many complications related to wind energy. An-
other one is the aerodynamic wake, a dynamic phenomenon where the ﬂow downstream of the wind
turbine is extremely turbulent and the velocity magnitude is signiﬁcantly lower due to the kinetic
energy extracted from the wind. The wake is caused from the aforementioned extraction of energy
as well as from the vorticity added by the wind turbine; it can also occur even when the wind
turbine is not operating because the structure acts as an obstacle that disturbs the ﬂow. Wakes
however, are not fully understood because of their dynamic nature. In addition, they can reduce
the total power yield of a wind farm by up to 20 % [3]. Moreoever, they can increase the fatigue
loads in a wind farm by up to 15 % [46] and result in the reduction of the turbines’ lifetime [51][43].
Therefore the need to comprehend and interpret the behaviour of wakes is becoming increasingly
important when it comes to wind power production. Understanding their behaviour will result in
improved power yield and higher lifetime expectancy of the wind turbines in terms of lower fatigue
loads. More information about the fundamentals of the wind turbine wake can be found in [49].
SRSDs and especially LIDARs, provide the possibility of investigating these two aforementioned
critical topics -i.e. measurements and wakes- at the same time; ﬁrstly comprehend the source of
the uncertainties in the devices themselves as well as identify best practices when performing a
measurement campaign based solely on LIDARs in order to accurately measure the wind; secondly
use LIDARs to detect and analyze the wake, which will result in increasing the total performance
of wind farms.
It should be noted that the use of LIDARs for obtaining measurements and analyzing wakes is
deemed even more necessary in oﬀshore sites. As mentioned at the very beginning, due to the
rapid growth of wind energy, there is a need for more rich-in-wind-resource sites, thus the wind
energy industry started expanding into oﬀshore locations as well. That was a reasonable evolution
also because of the numerous advantages that the oﬀshore sites have to oﬀer, which render them
noteworthy contenders against onshore areas. The most important advantages are:
1. The wind potential in oﬀshore locations is signiﬁcantly higher. Water has a very low surface
roughness and hardly any obstacles that can slow down or distort the wind ﬂow can be found
in the sea. In addition, atmospheric stability conditions tend to be more stable in oﬀshore
sites which corresponds to a vertical wind proﬁle that grows faster with height. These points
highlight that oﬀshore wind is a considerable option since it results into higher energy yield.

1.1
Problem Statement and Motivation
Even though the eﬀects of wakes both in power production and fatigue loads are well-known, a
standard method to detect and describe them is yet to be found. Most wind resource assessment
software packages use rather simple engineering tools that do not take under consideration impor-
tant aspects of the physics describing the wake, thus resulting in misleading results when trying to
predict the power yield [18].
The technological advancement of LIDARs oﬀers the necessary equipment to obtain measurements
in order to formulate a better understanding of the wake development and evolution; at the same
time however, the uncertainties involved in LIDARs and the accuracy of the ﬁnal output are not
determined in full detail mainly because of the stochastic nature of the wind. This problem becomes
even more pronounced in highly turbulent ﬂows such as the wake behind a wind turbine, where the
wind speed and direction change abruptly both with respect to time and space. All of the above
lead to the conclusion that the tools needed to analyze the aerodynamic wake are available; what
remains is to investigate the best way to use them in order to accurately describe it and quantify
the points along the process where uncertainty is introduced.
In [48], the need for the integration of a LIDAR simulator with a ﬂow model is highlighted. Accord-
ing to the same source, such a tool would improve LIDAR measurement campaigns by identifying
the best locations to position the LIDARs. In [42] a dual-Doppler LIDAR tool is investigated and
its ability to capture large coherent structures by obtaining virtual measurements generated from
a LES is shown. In [11], the advantages, disadvantages and uncertainties of a LIDAR measurement
campaign are demonstrated, while the potential of LIDARs is highlighted. Finally, in [20] it is
shown that it is possible to predict wake situations with the use of LIDARs.
All of the above being said, the main motivation behind this MSc thesis is the emerging need to
improve LIDAR measurement campaigns and also obtain a better understanding of wakes. As will
be explained in the next section, this motivation led to the formulation of the thesis objectives,

which are to combine these two topics by investigating and ameliorating the use of LIDARs and
given that, provide a deep insight of the behaviour and evolution of wakes.
1.2
Objectives
The aim of this project is to employ the aforementioned technological advancement in remote sens-
ing devices by implementing virtual LIDARs in a wind ﬁeld derived from a Large Eddy Simulation
(LES) to better interpret wakes and improve the current approaches to analyze them. A LIDAR
tool obtaining oﬄine measurements will be utilized for this purpose. The LES will also act as
reference against which the virtual LIDAR measurements will be compared. As will be later ex-
plained, the limitations of the LES do not aﬀect the investigation of wakes and as a result, no rough
assumptions are needed.
The goal is to further improve future measurement campaigns as well as provide in-depth under-
standing of how to analyse LIDAR measurements and treat their limitations to extract valuable
information about the wake behind a wind turbine. Note that the focus will be on ground-based
scanning LIDARs. More speciﬁcally, this thesis will concentrate on long-range pulsed LIDARs that
are more suitable for measuring in long downstream distances.
Furthermore, after establishing and quantifying the accuracy with which LIDARs can obtain mea-
surements, the wake will be analyzed in terms of turbulent statistics in an eﬀort to detect the
wake in the wind ﬁeld. Diﬀerent approaches, such as the wake centre and spectral analysis will be
implemented in order to assess the evolution of the wake in time from diﬀerent perspectives.

Scanning Remote Sensing Devices
Remote sensing is the process of acquiring data from a distance, i.e. not at the speciﬁc location
where the instrumentation is located. In wind energy, remote sensing refers to the measurement
of parameters related to the wind such as the wind velocity components. Scanning remote sensing
devices, are the devices that are utilized in remote sensing and operate by scanning a speciﬁc surface
or volume.
SRSDs are used for many applications such as aerial traﬃc control, topography and elevation
maps, air pollution and vegetation mapping to name a few [17][22][4]. The SRSDs used in wind
measurement campaigns are primarily LIDARs, SODARs (SOund Detection and Ranging) and
more lately also RADARs (RAdio Detection and Ranging) [10][9].
The LIDARs considered in
this MSc thesis are Doppler LIDARs and their operating principles are described in Chapter 2.1.
SODARs as well as RADARs are outside the scope of this thesis and will not be elaborated any
further.
2.1
LIDAR Technology
In this section the operation of Doppler LIDARs4 will be explained in detail. The examples concern
wind measurements, however the operating principles are the same regardless of application.
LIDARs rely on the Doppler eﬀect; that is the change in the wave frequency perceived by an
observer moving relatively to an emitting source [52]. In wind energy applications, the LIDAR
plays the role of the observer while the aerosol particles correspond to the emitting source -or more
accurately to the reﬂecting target since the particles technically do not emit anything. The LIDAR
sends a laser beam of a given frequency aiming at the measurement point. The wavelength, hence
the frequency, of the laser beam is ﬁxed and due to eye-safety restrictions it is usually close to
1550 nm [55]. When the beam collides with the dust particles convected by the wind, it is scattered
everywhere. Some of the scattered photons reach the point of origin, i.e. the LIDAR lens. Making
use of the Doppler eﬀect and the fact that the laser beam travels with a known speed5, the LIDAR
detects the diﬀerence in emitted and backscattered wave frequency and calculates the Line of Sight
(LOS) velocity or radial velocity6, i.e.
the projection of the 3-dimensional (3D) wind velocity
vector on the line connecting the LIDAR and the measurement point. It is evident that if the
dust particles are moving towards the LIDAR then the latter will perceive an increased frequency,
while the perceived frequency will be decreased for aerosol particles moving away from the LIDAR.
The formula connecting the radial velocity with the reﬂected and perceived frequency is given in
Equation (1).

2.3
Types of LIDARs
There are in principle 2 types of Doppler LIDARs, the continuous wave (CW) and the pulsed wave
LIDARs. The diﬀerence between the two types lies primarily in the laser beam they emit.
CW LIDARs send a continuous laser beam at the measurement point. The LIDAR lens focuses
at one point in order to measure the wind there. It should be noted that CW LIDARs measure
sequentially and in case measurement at a diﬀerent distance is required, the LIDAR has to re-focus.
A representation of the CW LIDAR focus process is shown in Figure 3.
Figure 3: CW LIDAR focus [7]. The probe volume is denoted by red color.
At this point it should be clariﬁed that even though a LIDAR focuses at a point, it measures over
a volume and averages over it. Therefore it is evident that the size of the volume is critical in the
resulting value. This volume is called probe volume and it varies with distance. More speciﬁcally,
the probe length increases quadratically with the focal distance. That means that the CW LIDAR
is very accurate in close range but with the accuracy dropping fast as the range increases. The
probe volume is indicated in red color in Figure 3 and it is deﬁned by the weighting function [12].
As the name suggests, this function applies a diﬀerent weight to the points along the probe length
over which the LIDAR averages in order to obtain the radial velocity. The weights are inversely
proportional to the distance from the measurement point. Hence, the averaging is weighted with
the center point of the probe being the most signiﬁcant. To avoid possible misinterpretations since
many deﬁnitions are used in relevant bibliography, in this report the term probe length refers to
the full length of the probe.
Pulsed LIDARs, just like the name suggests, shoot pulses of light. Depending on the distance they
have to cover, the pulses return to the LIDAR within diﬀerent time windows; as a result, multiple
points on the LOS can be measured simultaneously. This technique is called range-gating and is

Large Eddy Simulation
Large Eddy Simulation (LES) is a numerical model for simulating turbulent ﬂows. Other known
approaches to the solution of the Navier-Stokes (N-S) equations are RANS (Reynolds-Averaged
Navier-Stokes) and DNS (Direct Numerical Simulation). The LES is used in many applications
including the simulation of the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) which is most relevant to wind
energy. The ABL is the lower part of the atmosphere, i.e. the part that interacts directly with the
earth’s surface. It should be noted that the ABL is particularly turbulent.
LES is based on the premise that most of the energy and ﬂuxes of a ﬂow lie within the large-
scale8 motions of the ﬂow under consideration. Given that, the fundamental quantities such as
the velocity components, are explicitly calculated for the large-scale motions and modelled for the
smaller, less important ones. This technique is applied because the smaller scales of turbulence are
also more computationally expensive and an explicit calculation would only be possible for a very
small domain and a ﬂow with low Reynolds number. LES was named after the aforementioned
large-scale motions, also known as large eddies. It should be highlighted that the result of the LES
is ideally independent of the parameterization of the small scales, however this not always correct
in practice [44].
To apply the theory of LES, one ﬁrst has to deﬁne the limit between the large and small (sub-
grid) scales. The eﬀect of the sub-grid scales in the resolved ﬁeld is then considered through the
mathematical model that is used to describe them. The chosen threshold would ideally be the
Kolmogorov length scale, therefore the sub-grid scales would coincide with the dissipation range in
the energy spectrum, i.e. the range within which the turbulent kinetic energy (TKE) is converted
into heat [45]. The energy content of that range is signiﬁcantly smaller than in the rest of the
energy spectrum, hence the sub-grid scales would, as initially intended, not aﬀect the result of the
LES.
The use of a LES for the purposes of this thesis was considered the most appropriate choice. The
LES grants full knowledge of the entire ﬂow ﬁeld allowing the LIDAR tool to measure at multiple
points, as opposed to real wind farm measurements where only information at one point would be
available. This enabled not only more options concerning the LIDAR tool parametric study but
also more ﬂexibility to analyze the wake. Furthermore, according to the previous elaboration, a
LES fully resolves the larger scales of turbulence thus the ones that are closely related to wakes.
Hence, the scales that are modelled are not of major importance in the case of wake detection,
therefore the LES provided more than enough information and for this purpose -wake detection
and analysis- any inaccuracies compared to measurements were assumed to be negligible. More
information about Large Eddy Simulations can be found in [38][8][36][32].
The LES used during this MSc thesis, employed the actuator line (AL) technique [39]. According
to the AL technique, the rotor blades are modelled as lines along which forces are applied, which
eventually correspond to the loading on the rotor blades. This way, the mesh points that would
otherwise be dedicated to the reﬁnement of the rotor geometry, are instead dedicated to the accurate
capturing of the turbulent characteristics of the ﬂow within the wake. More details about the AL
technique as well as the principles and assumptions of the LES used for this thesis can be found
in [40].

Ground-based pulsed LIDAR Tool
The ﬁrst necessary step for this research topic was to develop a stand-alone LIDAR tool that would
later be utilized to obtain measurements from the LES. It should be noted that the measurements
are meant to be taken oﬄine, that is after the LES is completed. This means that the tool was
not implemented directly in the LES, rather used the LES results as inputs. This renders the tool
simpler, independent of the type of ﬂow simulation while the same LES results can be utilized
multiple times. The tool simulates the function of a ground-based pulsed LIDAR. Great attention
was given so that it is kept as generic as possible while providing the user with the necessary
freedom to customize the LIDAR positions, settings and operational mode.
Starting from the latter, the tool provides 2 modes of operation, Plan Position Indicator (PPI)
and Staring mode. When operating in PPI mode, the LIDAR keeps its elevation angle constant
while varying its azimuthal angle. In Staring mode, the LIDAR aims at a speciﬁc point and shoots
pulses at that point. Both the azimuthal and elevation angle of the LIDAR are ﬁxed.
A ﬂow chart of the MATLAB code structure and its most important points is shown in Figure 7.
The largest part of the code is common for both modes of operation; that was done intentionally
in order to maintain the code clear, short, eﬃcient and user-friendly for future works. The user
options and code diﬀerences in the two modes of operation will be discussed next, followed by the
description of the part of the code that is common in both PPI and Staring mode.

In the main script, called PulsedLidarSim, the user options are included. These are listed below.
1. Mode of operation. It is evident that the user ﬁrst must select whether the LIDARs will be
15
==PAGE:31==
scanning horizontal planes (PPI) or aiming at a speciﬁc point (Staring).
2. The shape of the weighting function. The options in this case are triangular and Gaussian.
The weighting function will be described in more detail in the following paragraphs.
3. The number of LIDARs. The tool is designed in such a way that it simulates the operation
of 2 or 3 LIDARs. Therefore the user is able to choose between the above options. It is
clear that the reconstructed velocity is dependent on the number of LIDARs, like shown in
Chapter 2.1. Hence, the number of reconstructed velocity components will be equal to the
number of LIDARs.
Then the user can customize the settings of the LIDARs. More speciﬁcally, for each LIDAR the
user can choose:
1. The probe length.
2. The minimum distance the LIDAR can obtain measurements. In reality this distance ranges
from 40 to 100 m depending on the probe length [7][6].
3. The resolution of the probe. In order to obtain measurements along the probe length, the
probe length is discretized into a number of points. For this MSc thesis this resolution was
chosen equal to 1 point per 4 metres of the probe length. The reasons for such a selection
were several; ﬁrstly, the LES grid resolution and the probe resolution should be of the same
order of magnitude to obtain an accurate representation of the wake; secondly choosing more
than 1 point per grid unit length would result in more than one interpolation per grid volume
element, hence increasing the computational time without providing additional information;
lastly the number of probe points should be in any case kept as low as possible to decrease the
number of interpolations inside the LES grid. Note that the interpolation is a highly time-
consuming procedure, especially when performed in 4 dimension (space and time). More
extensive reference to the probe length and probe resolution will be made in Chapter 7.
However, the user has the option of adjusting the resolution depending on the case under
investigation.
4. The LIDAR coordinates. It should be noted that even though cylindrical coordinates are
used by the LIDAR to detect the measurement points (or to scan over a plane) everything is
deﬁned in Cartesian coordinates.
5. The measurement point(s). In PPI mode, the user only deﬁnes one point. The LOS to that
point will be the symmetry axis of the LIDAR scanning sector. In Staring mode, the user can
provide as input a number of measurement points. These points can be anywhere within the
LES domain. Cartesian coordinates should be used as input for the measurement point(s).
In PPI mode four more options are available; the ﬁrst concerns the selection of the number of range
gates, the second the gap between consecutive range gates, the third the azimuthal angle span of
the sector that the LIDAR will scan and the fourth the azimuthal increment with which the scan
will be performed. The azimuthal angle span is graphically explained in Figure 8. The user is
deﬁning the half-azimuthal span φ/2, of the LIDAR sector. The LIDARs will then scan over twice
that angle. The value of φ/2 is common in all LIDARs.
As mentioned at the beginning of the current chapter, the functions work ﬁne regardless of whether
the LIDARs are operating in PPI or Staring mode. Hence, the following function explanations will
not make any distinctions between PPI and Staring unless necessary, in which case it will be
explicitly mentioned.
Since the input points are given in Cartesian coordinates, the ﬁrst step is to convert them into

This is done in CalcPhiThetaR function. It should be highlighted that for the conversion it is

Next, the range gates are calculated with calculateRangeGates function. This function, given
the selected probe resolution, calculates the number of probe points and their coordinates. Note
that the measurement point is always in the middle of a range gate, consequently the probe is
always split into an odd number of points. If the LIDAR is operating in Staring mode, only one
range gate is required. On the contrary, if the LIDAR is operating in PPI mode, the distance
between the LIDAR and the measurement point is automatically ﬁlled with range gates, based on
the user-selected, gap between the range gates, probe length and minimum measuring distance.
The range gates required for the scan of the whole sector are also calculated in a similar manner.
The coordinates of the range gate points are ﬁnally converted to Cartesian ones.
The conversion of the coordinates to Cartesian primarily serves into the next step of the code
which is the interpolation of the velocity components from the LES wind ﬁeld to the probe points
coordinates. It is evident that the points in which the probe length is discretized will not coincide
with the LES grid points. Therefore, an interpolation is performed in order to obtain the velocity
components at those points. This is done inside the InterpLES function. Note that InterpLES
uses MATLAB’s built-in griddedInterpolant function, which was set to perform a linear in-
terpolation. It is worth highlighting that the interpolation itself introduces some uncertainty in
the ﬁnal measurement. The coupling of the LIDAR tool with the LES output ﬁles will be further
elaborated in Chapter 6.
After interpolating the velocity components, the LOS velocity of each LIDAR is calculated following
the wind speed reconstruction procedure explained in Chapter 2.1. Note that in this case the wind
speed is projected and not reconstructed since the velocity components are known and the focus is
on the calculation of the LOS velocity; nevertheless the process is exactly the same, it is just the
unknown variable that diﬀers.

5
Parametric Study
Before applying the LIDAR tool to the LES data, its limitations should be clearly identiﬁed. To
accomplish that, a sensitivity analysis was performed, which investigated the eﬀect of diﬀerent
LIDAR parameters on the output velocity. Such parameters are for instance, the azimuthal angle
φ and the elevation angle θ. Similarly, the eﬀect of diﬀerent wind ﬁelds was investigated. It should
be mentioned that the focus of the study was on the Staring mode since it was considered more
appropriate for the following wake analysis; therefore the parametric study was performed using
this mode of operation. However, the derived conclusions can be applied to the PPI mode as well,
without additional adjustments.
A large number of cases was examined; hereafter the most signiﬁcant ones are presented. For all
the cases the probe length of the LIDARs was set to 20 m. A realistic probe length would be
in the order of 100 metres, however the cases investigated in the parametric study do not include
turbulence, therefore the results could be scaled with negligible diﬀerences. In addition, the smaller
probe length allowed the domain of the examined wind ﬁelds to be kept small, saving computational
time.
For the parametric study, U0 was arbitrarily chosen equal to 12 m/s. Finally, it should be noted
that the parametric study is independent of the wind turbine.

Lidar tool and LES coupling
A large part of the work done during this MSc thesis was dedicated to the coupling of the LES output
ﬁles with the LIDAR tool. The output of the simulation was a number of blocks that altogether
combined formed the LES domain. Hence, the ﬁrst necessary step was to merge all these ﬁles into
one ﬁle that would incorporate the complete LES domain. This process was repeated for all the
LES time steps.
Since the original LES code was written in Fortran, the output ﬁles were also in Fortran format.
The routine used to merge the ﬁles into one block, namely, OneBlock Cartesian.f was also
written in Fortran and adjusted not only to merge the ﬁles, but also to extract a smaller part of the
initial domain and a certain number of variables. This was done to reduce the size of the ﬁles that
would be later used as input to MATLAB. At this point it should be highlighted that choosing the
proper dimensions of the domain of the input ﬁles was a crucial decision for the rest of the analysis,
since these ﬁles would be loaded into MATLAB every time a time series would need to be extracted.
Hence, the ﬁles needed to be as small as possible to ensure that the LIDAR tool code would be
executed in a reasonable amount of time. Having said that, the OneBlock Cartesian.f routine
was set to save only the 3 velocity components inside the domain determined by the dimensions
shown in Table 1. As mentioned in Chapter 3, the wind turbine is positioned at x = y = 10, thus
the domain started 2R upstream.

This process decreased the size of the LES output ﬁles from 7.7 GB to approximately 50 MB each.
Next, the reduced ﬁles were converted into MATLAB format; the reason was that MATLAB reads
that format faster. No comparison was performed to quantify the speed diﬀerence between the two
formats, however loading the ﬁles in Fortran format and converting them every time the LIDAR
tool was utilized would eventually be computationally more expensive since for the extraction of a
single time series 3000 ﬁles needed to be read.
Besides loading the ﬁles into MATLAB, the part that required the most resources in terms of memory
was the interpolation between the grid points. Every probe consisted of a number of points at which
the values of the velocity components had to be evaluated. The evaluation was realized with linear
interpolation. As mentioned brieﬂy in Chapter 4, griddedInterpolant built-in function was
employed for that purpose. The advantage of this function is that the interpolation only needed to
be performed once, returning the interpolant of the dataset. One can think of the interpolant of a
point as the set of weights assigned to each grid location with respect to that point. It is evident
that the interpolant depends on the type of interpolation11. In this case the interpolant concerned
not a single point, but all the probe points required to measure at the pre-deﬁned measurement
points.
Hence the interpolant was calculated once and then applied to all the LES ﬁles that
were read sequentially by the code. Calculating the interpolant only a single time reduced the
computational and time requirements signiﬁcantly. More information about the interpolant and
the griddedInterpolant function can be found in [30].
It is obvious that all LES ﬁles could not be imported simultaneously as the amount of data would

be extremely large. Thus, a small number of ﬁles needed to be imported at a time, large enough
to allow a number of interpolations, i.e. a number of extracted time steps, but most importantly

5 ﬁles, i.e. approximately 250 MB, should be loaded each time.
Evidently, choosing a time step for the reconstructed time series that would be higher than the

7.3.2
Power Spectral Density
After examining the time series of the measurement points, the PSDs of the time series were
investigated. A few points should be highlighted regarding the calculation of the PSDs. Initially,
the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) was calculated and normalized against the length of the time
series, i.e. 3000 time steps. Only the frequencies up to the Nyquist frequency are saved; that is the
highest sampling frequency that will not cause oversampling [53]. In this case since the time step
is ﬁxed at dt = 0.1 seconds, the Nyquist frequency fNyquist will be:
fNyquist = 0.5 · 1
dt = 5 Hz
This can be also seen in the following ﬁgures where the horizontal axis extends from 0 to 5 Hz. The
Nyquist frequency is located at the middle of the spectrum generated by the FFT, hence since half
of the spectrum is discarded, the values of the remaining part have to be doubled in order for the
total energy content to remain intact; therefore all the FFT values besides the ones corresponding
to the DC13 component, i.e. f = 0 Hz and the Nyquist frequency, are multiplied by a factor of 2.
Finally, in order to obtain the PSD, Equation (12) is employed. There, the aforementioned doubled
values are utilized.
PSD =

function abs signiﬁes that only the modulus of the FFT is used for the PSD calculation.

Time-averaged u-planes
After calculating and visualizing the u-component y-z planes at 10R downstream of the wind turbine
for diﬀerent time steps, a similar process was conducted for time-averaged planes. In particular, the
time-averaged u-component planes for all the available downstream distances were calculated. The
relevant plots are presented in Figures 39 to 45. Again, only 8 contour levels were selected to avoid
artiﬁcial smoothing in the plots. The LES and LIDAR tool results are presented side-by-side for
easier comparison. It is evident that the wake deﬁcit decays with downstream distance. Moreover,
for small downstream distances the wake deﬁcit is split into two main areas. Moving away from the
wind turbine these two areas merge into one, which has decreased intensity and is located behind
the rotor centre. The transition from two separate areas to one is observed in Figure 41, that
corresponds to 6R downstream distance, while in Figure 42 (8R downstream) the wake deﬁcit is
already merged. The two areas in close downstream distances are a result of the high vorticity at
the root and at the tip of the blades [47]. However, it should be highlighted that the resolution of
the ﬁgures is too coarse therefore such details (e.g vortices) cannot be observed. When the wake
becomes fully turbulent, these two areas merge into one. The two areas are a sign of the near wake,
while when the areas have fully merged, the transition to the far wake is assumed to be completed.
Regarding the averaging introduced by the LIDARs, it is not visible in Figures 39 to 42 and 45
and it can be slightly seen in Figures 43 and 44. This is not strange since in this case the time
averaging has a much stronger eﬀect than the volume averaging. However, the main objective is
achieved since the patterns captured by the LIDARs are similar to the ones extracted from the
LES, while the near and far wake as well as the transition can be distinguished.

7.3.5
Spatio-temporal u-planes
So far the analysis in Chapter 7.3 did not take into account the measurement time and the time
needed to re-position the LIDAR lens when measuring at diﬀerent points. In reality, the LIDARs
measure at each point for a ﬁxed time period, for instance 1 second, sending a large number of
pulses; this process is necessary because just one pulse does not carry enough energy back to the
LIDAR to reconstruct the radial velocity therefore the CNR16 is too low.
In this section, these two times -the measurement and the re-positioning time- will be included in
the measurement process to investigate their inﬂuence in the wake detection. Two empirical values
were chosen, in particular the measuring time tmeas and re-positioning time tpos were assumed to
be 1 and 0.5 seconds respectively. Hence, the LIDAR tool required in total 1.5 seconds for the
reconstruction of the velocity components at each point only for one time instance.
The procedure followed to consider those two times is explained graphically in Figure 46. The points
located at plane x = 20 were considered similarly to Chapter 7.3.3 in order to be able to compare the
results of the two sections. Notice that the horizontal axis is time since the downstream distance of
the plane is ﬁxed. At ﬁrst, the 3 LIDARs shoot pulses at the ﬁrst point at (y, z) = (8.5, 1) (bottom
left) for time tmeas. Next, they re-position their beams to aim at point (y, z) = (8.5, 2) and for that
they need an additional time equal to tpos. The advancement in the time axis indicates exactly that
time-delay; switching from one point to the next, will cause the LIDARs to obtain measurements
at diﬀerent instances of the LES, consequently the wind ﬁeld at the plane at which the LIDARs are
aiming will have evolved. In Figure 46 only 5 time steps of this process are presented as an example,
between 50 and 56 seconds. The time period illustrated was chosen arbitrarily. After the scanning
of the whole plane is completed, the LIDARs start scanning again from point (y, z) = (8.5, 1) and
the process is repeated. Every vertical plane of points in Figure 46 corresponds to a diﬀerent time
step. Alternating colors have been used between consecutive time steps to denote the evolution in
time. Note that the term “scan” should not be confused with the PPI mode where the LIDAR
scans horizontal planes at multiple radial distances. Here the LIDARs are still “staring” and after
they measure at one point they proceed to the next one.
Since every measurement required 1.5 seconds and the plane consisted of 21 points, the beams
would return to the initial point after 21 · 1.5 = 31.5 seconds, hence the scan of one plane needed
31.5 seconds to be completed.
Thus, two consecutive measurements of the same point would
be 31.5 seconds apart from each other. Hence, to obtain 300 seconds time series at all points,
scatteredInterpolant built-in MATLAB function was used in order to estimate the velocities
at all points for the missing time steps. The interpolation performed was linear.
Note that in order to perform an interpolation at a point, two values are required, one before
and one after the time step under consideration. Hence, the ﬁrst and last 31.5 seconds of the
reconstructed time series were discarded because they were the result of extrapolation (and not
interpolation) from the following or previous values respectively. Similarly to Chapter 7.3.3, the
planes of the u-component as reconstructed from the LIDAR tool are presented in the right-hand
side plots of Figures 47 to 51. The left-hand side plots correspond to the full LES plots that also
appear in Figures 34 to 38 (top plots) and are repeated here for easier comparison.
As expected, the inﬂuence of the time delay deteriorated the accuracy of the results. In some
instances, for example for t = 100 s (Figure 48), the LIDARs are roughly capturing the shape of
the wake even if the wake deﬁcit magnitude is not so accurately reconstructed. However, in the next
instance (t = 150 s) presented in Figure 49, the u-component plane reconstructed by the LIDAR
tool does not correspond to the image generated by the full LES; therefore, examining Figures 47

Similarly to all the calculations until this point, Figure 52 was generated by utilizing normalized
velocity and dimension ﬁgures.
This is a common tactic when post-processing LES results for
reasons that have already been explained. However, this is not necessarily the case when work-
ing with LIDARs where units matter. The above reasoning enabled the previous analysis to be
repeated with non-normalized ﬁgures. The motivation was that by implementing units, the inter-
polation performed by scatteredInterpolant will change completely; the weights assigned by
scatteredInterpolant depend solely on the absolute values of the inputs, therefore multiply-
ing dimensions by R will have a direct impact on the end result. More speciﬁcally, the smallest
dimension will now be time since spacing is in the order of 102. Since scatteredInterpolant
treats all inputs as distances from the point of interest, time will be assigned with the largest weight
because MATLAB “assumes” that the time parameter is “closer” to the point, therefore it should
aﬀect it more. As a consequence, changes in time will have a greater impact on the interpolation
more than changes in space. The interpolation method was otherwise maintained the same.

8
Conclusions
The objective of this thesis was to improve future LIDAR measurement campaigns and to utilize
the outcomes of that investigation to provide an understanding about how wake analysis with the
use of LIDARs should be performed.
The ﬁrst essential step was to create a tool that would simulate the operation of ground-based pulsed
LIDARs in order to obtain oﬄine measurements from the LES. The LIDAR tool was subjected
to a parametric study in Chapter 5, according to which some useful guidelines were deduced; in
particular, the azimuthal angle formed by the LOS of two LIDARs should not exceed 150◦while
the elevation angle should remain below 50◦to avoid amplifying any inherent error. The error that
had the most severe impact on the reconstructed time series was the error in the LOS velocity.
No calibration is ﬂawless, hence it is crucial to limit the intensiﬁcation of potential uncertainties.
Similarly, if the CNR is also taken into account, staying within those suggested thresholds would
limit the intensiﬁcation of the noise in the radial velocities. The parametric study also revealed that
the LIDAR tool can perform accurate measurements in wind ﬁelds characterized by both vertical
shear and veer. The veer overall had a larger inﬂuence on the measurement error, however for the
range of shear exponents frequently met in wind energy, that is between 0.1 and 0.3, the error was
insigniﬁcant.
After completing the parametric study and extracting the necessary time series from the LES,
various statistics were calculated in an eﬀort to investigate whether wake analysis is possible with
the use of LIDARs and if yes, how accurately can the wake be measured.
The 1-point statistics returned very accurate results. The time series at point (20, 9, 2) showed
that the wake extent can be traced in terms of large drops in the velocity magnitude. The fast
ﬂuctuations of the velocity components were not captured but the overall patterns were similar
showing that the LIDARs can follow the large changes in wind speed that signify that the point
is aﬀected by the wake. Out of the 3 components, the reconstruction of the w-component was the
least accurate, probably because the ground is causing the ﬂuctuations in that component to be
very fast while their amplitude increases with height.
The spectral analysis revealed that the rotor frequencies, namely 3P and 6P, should be visible in
the near wake, even with the attenuation caused by the LIDARs. Note that a point at which the
3P and 6P frequencies are not visible is not necessarily located in the far wake but it can also be
located near the wind turbine in an area that is less aﬀected by the wake. The spectral analysis also
disclosed that the energy content measured by the LIDAR is not always less than the actual energy
at that point. The increased turbulence caused by the wake results in large and fast variations in
the wind speed. Hence, a point located inside the wake might have signiﬁcantly less energy than
its surroundings. In this scenario, it is very probable that this drop in energy will not be perceived
by the LIDAR, since the reconstructed velocity is inﬂuenced by the velocities at all points along
the probe, which in this case will not be as low. Consequently, the reconstructed PSD will not
capture the decrease in energy, similarly to Figure 29b. This case was observed in approximately
14 % of the cases most of which in the near wake, therefore it is a scenario that is not coincidental
and should be considered in LIDAR measurement campaigns.
The time-averaged planes displayed an adequate representation of the shape of the wake. They
also provided a rough estimate of the transition from near to far wake, while the wake deﬁcit was
also roughly captured since the time-averaging had a stronger impact than the volume-averaging.
It is important however to acknowledge that details regarding the physics describing the near and
far wake were not distinguishable not even in the ﬁgures returned by the LES, due to the coarse
spacing between the measurement points.
61
==PAGE:77==
The reconstruction of the u-planes at diﬀerent time instances showed that the LIDARs are able to
adequately capture the shape of the wake while the reconstructed magnitude was somehow reduced.
The wake centre time series between the full LES and the LIDARs demonstrated correlation greater
than 90 % for both coordinates, meaning that the wake centre can be detected with high level of
precision. Interesting was the fact that the correlation between the LES and full LES wake centre
time series was lower but that did not have a negative impact on the LIDAR time series. The
reason was that the limited number of measurements points caused some artefacts in the LES time
series which did not aﬀect the LIDAR measurements due to the volume averaging. Thus, in this
case the attenuation introduced by the LIDARs contributed to the accuracy of the measurements.
When measuring and re-positioning time were taken into account, the reconstructed u-planes re-
turned inconsistent results and the correlation between the wake centre time series declined to
slightly over 40 %. However, the artefacts occurring in the wake centre time series seemed to follow
a pattern; in particular, discrepancies were observed every 31.5 seconds, i.e. the time needed for the
complete plane to be scanned. Therefore, simple ﬁltering techniques that could be applied close to
those time instances to increase the correlation between the two time series were suggested; more
speciﬁcally the times when the ﬁrst or the second derivative of the wind speed equals zero could be
detected. Eventually, the ﬁltering could result in a time series that would describe the evolution of
the wake centre in a satisfactory manner for the purposes of wake detection.
Implementing the traditional method of reconstructing the u-planes, i.e. using physical values,
resulted in excessive smoothing, rendering the wake time series impractical. Hence, even though the
scanning using normalized values returned time series with artefactual peaks, it also had potential
for improvement. Therefore a re-evaluation of the traditional method to reconstruct the velocity
components when measuring at diﬀerent points should be considered if one intends to employ
LIDARs for measurements within a wake.
Overall, given the assumptions established at the beginning of this work, LIDARs demonstrated
signiﬁcant potential regarding wake detection and analysis. Disregarding the time necessary to
measure and re-position the LIDAR lens, the results were highly accurate. Including that time,
the ability of the LIDAR to follow the changes in the wind ﬁeld deteriorated, but improvements
can be made to ameliorate the correlation between the results.
Future work
The work performed during this MSc thesis also revealed areas that require further investigation.
Some suggestions are brieﬂy mentioned below.
At ﬁrst, similar analysis should be conducted using CW LIDARs.
Their advantage to obtain
measurements at close range can be proven valuable, especially when the focus lies on the near
wake.
Regarding the assumptions made, some of them could be revised or withdrawn, in order to inves-
tigate their impact in wake detection. The following are some relevant suggestions:
1. The probe should be modelled as volume and not as line.
Especially in the case where
CW LIDARs are implemented, this parameter should be taken into account, since the probe
volume will be a function of distance.
2. The weighting function could be elaborated further. A rather simple weighting function was
considered for the purposes of this MSc thesis. A more detailed function would be worth
investigating and implementing.
62
==PAGE:78==
3. The CNR should be also considered. A ﬁrst step towards that direction was the inclusion
of the measurement time in Chapter 7.3.5. The CNR, hence the measurement time, is a
function of distance which means that for diﬀerent ranges, diﬀerent measurement times are
required which might inﬂuence the result.
Furthermore, if possible, more measurement points should be examined per plane. That might lead
to the elimination of some of the artefacts and provide better representation of the u-planes.
Concerning the time delay, it is worth investigating the impact of diﬀerent weight distributions
to perform the interpolation.
As was previously described, the outcome was highly dependent
on the weights assigned on the time and space dimensions, thus estimating a combination that
accurately reconstructs the velocity planes as well as the wake centre time series, will add great
value to future LIDAR measurement campaigns (especially within turbulent ﬂows). More scanning
patterns should also be examined. The pattern implemented in this thesis scanned over all points
the same amount of times; patterns that focus on measuring primarily in the middle points of the
plane might be more appropriate since, despite its dynamic behaviour, the wake is more likely to
be found there, thus the reconstruction will be more accurate.
Finally, after investigating single wakes and identifying the areas that LIDARs are able -or unable-
to return accurate results, the next logical step would be to also examine multiple wake scenarios.
This would be one more step towards the investigation of wakes in real wind farms since nowadays
hardly any wind turbine can be found alone.
Concluding, the readers are encouraged to utilize the code created for the LIDAR tool and inves-
tigate diﬀerent scenarios, or even modify it and compare their results with the ones presented in
this report.
