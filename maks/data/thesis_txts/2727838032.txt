Abstract 
 
During the recent years Greek yogurt and acid­coagulated cheese consumption increase 
rapidly due to the health benefits and unique flavor. Acid whey is a by­product of acid­
coagulated cheese and Greek yogurt production. Acid whey can cause oxygen depletion 
in water, and the acidity of acid whey can lower the pH of water and soil, which make 
disposal of acid whey difficult. The most common way to process whey is to dry it 
using drum or spray dryers. However, acid whey cannot be spray dried and processed 
by membrane filtration due to its high lactic acid content. The aim of this thesis is to 
find a more economically and environmentally sustainable way to dispose of acid whey. 
 
In this thesis, a novel solution is proposed, biological “filtration”, where special bacteria 
are used to remove lactic acid, thus allowing for using the traditional whey processing 
technologies. The microorganism we focus on is Corynebacterium glutamicum, which 
is a well know platform organism used in the fermentation industry. Adaptive evolution 
method was used to provide an acidic pressure condition in CGXII medium for the wild 
type strain C. glutamicum ATCC 13032. After three months evolution, a mutation type 
strain was kept. The growth and lactic acid concentration of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 
and its low­pH adapted mutant in four different mediums were characterized. 
 
First, to investigate the effect of a low pH on growth of WT and MT, the strains was 
inoculated on solid and liquid CGXII medium containing 100 mM lactic acid and 
adjusted to pH 5.5/6/6.5/7. The growth and pH difference between MT and WT is 
significant, with MT showing a strong advantage. In order to present the lactic acid 
metabolize of WT and MT in acid whey, different initial OD values were selected to 
inoculate in acid whey with a pH of 5.5. The results suggested that MT could achieve 
optimal lactic acid metabolism with a lower cell count when the inoculation OD was 
set to 1. Additionally, tryptone was added in CGXII medium with lactic acid to 
investigate the function of shout chain peptides to promote the growth of C. glutamicum 
and aid in its faster adaptation to acidic environments. The result confirmed that 
tryptone can increase the growth for both strains under acidic condition.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background and motivation 
Cheese whey is the liquid that is generated when milk proteins coagulate and form curds. 
Cheese whey can be divided into sweet whey and acid whey. Sweet whey is formed 
when milk is coagulated using rennet, and is this is normally done at a pH that is similar 
to that of milk (pH ≈ 6.6). Acid whey, on the other hand, has a lower pH around 4.2 
to 4.5 since it is derived from acid coagulated milk, where the low pH is attained due 
to fermentation by lactic acid bacteria or through addition of organic acids, or mineral 
acids [21]. E.g., acid whey is a by­product of acid­coagulated cheese and Greek yogurt 
production [23]. When compared to sweet whey, acid whey has a higher content of 
minerals, such as calcium, potassium, and phosphorus. The composition of acid whey 
can vary depending on its source, the milk used, and the specific processing methods 
used. However, in general, acid whey contains the following components: lactose, 
protein, minerals, acid and water. When lactic acid bacteria are used, the acid whey 
contains lactic acid, which is formed when lactose is metabolized by the bacteria.  
 
Acid whey is a global challenge. The amount of acid whey produced has increased 
rapidly with the high production of Greek yogurt in recent years. Greek yogurt 
dominates more than 50% of the yogurt market in the United States and was the most 
popular yogurt style purchased in 2019 [41]. The amount of acid whey produced during 
the production of Greek yogurt and acid­coagulated cheese is huge. For Greek yogurt, 
100 L of milk can produce 33 L of yogurt and 67 L of acid whey [42]. For acid­
coagulated cheese, 1 kg of cheese generates 10 L of acid whey [43]. The European 
Union has the largest cheese market in the world, and an estimated acid whey 
production of around 40 million tonnes per year [44].  
 
The production of acid whey is substantial, and it cannot be directly discharged into the 
natural environment. The high levels of lactose and other organic compounds in acid 
whey can lead to oxygen depletion in water, which have a negative effect on fish and 
other aquatic organisms [24]. Additionally, the acidity of acid whey can lower the pH 
of water and soil. The high biological oxygen demand makes disposal of acid whey 
difficult, and expensive pre­treatments are needed prior to its release into the 
environment [39]. There is thus an urgent need for more economically and 
environmentally sustainable ways to dispose of acid whey.  
 
The most common way to process whey is to dry it using drum or spray dryers. Acid 
whey, however, is more challenging and cannot be spray dried and processed by 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
membrane filtration due to its high lactic acid content [40]. It agglomerates and forms 
lumps in the spray dryer [40]. One of the most common applications of acid whey is to 
use it as a fertilizer on farmland [45], but this application is associated with 
environmental risks, as acid whey can run off or leach into surface and groundwater 
[45] or cause accumulation of salts in the soil [46]. Acid whey can also used as an 
animal feed (pigs, sheep, cattle), but can cause animal salt toxicity and diarrhea [47]. 
General Mills (Minneapolis, MN) uses a biodigester to convert the acid whey into 
biogas which can be used for energy.  
 
In this thesis, we propose a novel solution, biological “filtration”, where special bacteria 
are used to remove lactic acid, thus allowing for use of the traditional whey processing 
technologies. The microorganism we focus on is Corynebacterium glutamicum, which 
is briefly described below.  
 
1.2 Introduction of C. glutamicum 
C. glutamicum is a well know platform organism used in the fermentation industry for 
large­scale production of amino acids [1]. C. glutamicum is a Gram­positive, aerobic 
bacterium within the Actinobacteria and has a short rod shape [69]. 
 
C. glutamicum was first discovered in 1957 by the Japanese researcher Dr. Takeshi 
Oishi [2]. He was trying to find microorganisms which could produce a large amount 
of glutamate, and he first reported the isolation and characterization of C. glutamicum 
from soil samples. Since then, C. glutamicum has become an important industrial 
bacterium used in the production of amino acids, organic acids, and other valuable 
chemicals. In addition to soil, C. glutamicum can also be found in water, air, fresh 
vegetables and fruits and fermented foods. 
 
C. glutamicum can metabolize a variety of sugars or organic acids. Besides being a 
highly important workhorse, C. glutamicum is considered as one of the important 
Gram­positive model organisms. 
 
1.3 C. glutamicum has an ability to produce amino acids 
C. glutamicum has several characteristics that make it an excellent model organism for 
studying bacterial metabolism and industrial production of various amino acids and 
other compounds. C. glutamicum can produce several amino acids, including glutamic

1960s, the company had developed a highly efficient production process and had

1.4 High growth rate of C. glutamicum 
C. glutamicum has a fast growth rate. The growth rate can vary depending on several 
factors, including the strain of C. glutamicum being used, and the growth conditions 
such as temperature, pH, oxygen concentration, carbon and nitrogen sources, and the 
presence of vitamins and minerals. C. glutamicum normally has a relatively fast growth 
rate when compared to other bacteria. The doubling time can vary depending on various 
factors such as the growth medium, temperature, and genetic modifications. Under 
optimal conditions, the doubling time of C. glutamicum is typically around 1 to 2 hours 
[48].  
 
1.5 Various substrates of C. glutamicum 
C. glutamicum can grow on a wide range of substrates, including sugars, organic acids, 
and alcohols due to its metabolic versatility and adaptability. This bacterium has a 
diverse metabolic pathway that enable it to use different substrates. This bacterium can 
sense the nutrient change and respond to the change by adjusting its gene expression 
and metabolic activity [6]. It has a complex regulatory system that allows it to respond 
to nutrient changes [7]. For example, glucose is one of the preferred carbon sources for 
C. glutamicum, and it can grow well on glucose as a sole carbon source. But when 
glucose is not available, C. glutamicum can switch to using other available carbon 
sources.  
 
1.6 Transport systems of C. glutamicum 
C. glutamicum possesses uptake systems for a broad range of compounds, e.g. for 
sugars such as glucose, fructose, sucrose and maltose, for organic acids like acetate, 
lactate, and pyruvate, and for nitrogen compounds such as ammonium, nitrate, and urea.  
 
1.6.1 PTS systems in C. glutamicum 
C. glutamicum has many transporters for importing different nutrients. For example, 
==PAGE:13==
5 
 
glucose can be easily transported into the cell through various glucose­specific 
transporters, such as GlcP and GlcU or by a phosphoenolpyruvate­dependent sugar 
phosphotransferase system (PTS), where the latter leads to concurrent phosphorylation 
of the glucose. In C. glutamicum, other sugars besides glucose are taken up by PTS, e.g. 
fructose and sucrose [8]. C. glutamicum has many regulators that control nutrient 
uptake, such as SugR, FruR, GntR1, and GntR2 [10]. For example, the regulator SugR 
can repress expression of the PTS genes during growth on substrates such as acetate 
and pyruvate [8]. Repression by the regulator SugR can be relieved when high levels 
of the intracellular PTS­sugar metabolites glucose­6­phosphate, fructose­1­phosphate, 
fructose­6­phospate, and fructose­1,6­bisphosphate are present [11].

1.6.2 Non-PTS systems in C. glutamicum 
In addition to the PTS, C. glutamicum uses non­PTS systems for the uptake of amino 
acids, peptides, and organic acids. One example of a non­PTS system in C. glutamicum 
is the ABC transporter systems, which enable C. glutamicum to transport various amino 
acids, peptides, and other nutrients. This system is composed of three components: an 
extracellular binding protein, a transmembrane permease protein, and an ATP­binding 
protein [12]. The binding protein recognizes and binds to the specific nutrient, and then 
delivers it to the transmembrane permease protein for transport into the cell. The ATP­
binding protein provides the energy required for transport. 
 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
The non­PTS systems also include the lactic acid transfer into the cell. One example is 
the monocarboxylate transporter (Mct), which is a lactate/pyruvate transporter that is 
involved in the uptake of lactic acid. This transporter is encoded by the mct gene, which 
is located in the C. glutamicum genome. The Mct transporter is composed of two 
subunits, MctC and MctP, which form a complex that is responsible for the transport of 
lactate and pyruvate. 
 
1.7 C. glutamicum lack of carbon catabolite repression 
(CCR)  
Carbon catabolite repression (CCR) is a regulatory mechanism in bacteria that allows 
the organism to use of the most favorable carbon sources for growth and metabolism. 
When the priority carbon source is present, CCR can inhibit the expression of genes 
involved in the utilization of other carbon sources [30]. In many bacteria, glucose is a 
preferred carbon source that can repress the expression of genes involved in the 
metabolism of other carbon sources such as organic acids or amino acids. Glucose is 
transported into the cell by the PTS system as mentioned before. Then it is 
phosphorylated by EIIglc and subsequently metabolized by the glycolytic pathway, 
generating energy and metabolic intermediates [32]. Fructose­1,6­bisphosphate is 
generated by glucose, which can lead to the activation of the CcpA protein. CcpA is a 
transcription factor that can bind to DNA sequences called catabolite response elements 
(cre) located upstream of target genes including the metabolism of alternative carbon 
sources [33]. CcpA is involved in the regulation of genes involved in the metabolism 
of various carbon sources, including glucose, fructose, lactate, and pyruvate. When 
CcpA binds to cre sites, it can recruit other proteins, which can inhibit the transcription 
of these genes [33]. Researchers have explored various strategies to engineer CCR 
regulation in bacteria, such as the deletion of genes involved in CCR or the introduction 
of mutations in regulatory genes to allow for more efficient use of alternative carbon 
sources [29]. 
 
However, C. glutamicum does not show any preference for specific carbon source like 
glucose, and thus co­metabolizes glucose with other sugars or organic acids [60]. 
Unlike the situation in some bacteria like E. coli and B. subtilis, there is little evidence 
of a global CCR system in C. glutamicum [61]. When C. glutamicum grows on a 
combination of glucose and fructose, it consumes both sugars simultaneously and the 
rate at which the bacteria take up sugars is higher when they are in a mixture compared 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
to when glucose or fructose are present alone [62].  
 
1.8 C. glutamicum is generally recognized as safe in 
industrial production 
GRAS status: C. glutamicum is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by regulatory 
agencies, which makes it a preferred organism for industrial production of food 
additives and other compounds for human consumption. For example, L­glutamic acid 
is one of the major amino acids used as a food additive and flavor enhancer in many 
kinds of foods [4]. According to a report by the European Commission, the global 
production of L­glutamate was around 3.3 million tons in 2016. More than 90% of 
global production of L­glutamate was using C. glutamicum as the microbial cell factory. 
 
1.9 Aerobic and anaerobic metabolism 
Respiration and fermentation are two processes which are used to provide energy to 
cells. C. glutamicum is nowadays described as a facultative anaerobic organism [70], 
which is capable of both aerobic and anaerobic metabolism. Aerobic respiration 
involves the use of oxygen to metabolize nutrients and generate energy. Oxygen works 
as exogenous electron acceptor for respiration. And under aerobic condition, C. 
glutamicum can produce various amino acids.  
 
Anaerobic respiration occurs in the absence of oxygen and typically involves the use of 
alternative electron acceptors such as nitrate, sulfate, or fumarate to generate energy. 
Some studies showing that strain ATCC 13032 and strain R can grow under anaerobic 
conditions when nitrate is worked as terminal electron acceptor [70]. Nitrate is reduced 
by the membrane­bound nitrate reductase to nitrite [71]. Since C. glutamicum lacks a 
nitrite reductase, nitrite accumulates in the medium, which have toxicity effect of C. 
glutamicum and slow down the growth [72]. 
 
Under oxygen­limited conditions in the absence of nitrate, C. glutamicum is able to 
produce various organic acids, including lactic acid, succinic acid, and acetic acid by 
using glucose as substrate in a mineral medium [26]. And the cell growth arrested under 
this condition. C. glutamicum can switch between these metabolic modes depending on 
environmental conditions and the availability of electron acceptors.

1.10 C. glutamicum and its tolerance to low pH 
Bacteria commonly face a lot of external environmental challenges, which can include 
fluctuations in pH, salinity, temperature, and osmolality [13]. The external pH 
challenges might affect the intracellular pH levels, further resulting in metabolic 
disorder and cause bacterial death. However, C. glutamicum possesses mechanisms, 
which allows it to adapt and survive at low pH. C. glutamicum can maintain its internal 
pH in the range between 7 and 8 when the external pH varies between 6.0 and 9.0 [14]. 
The minimum pH supporting growth of C. glutamicum is 5.5 [15]. 
 
Several studies have investigated the acid tolerance mechanisms of C. glutamicum, 
which include regulation of pH homeostasis, modification of membrane fatty acid 
composition, and activation of stress­responsive genes. C. glutamicum achieves pH 
homeostasis through a variety of mechanisms, e.g., by regulating proton extrusion, by 
producing alkaline compounds, and by altering metabolic fluxes [16]. One important 
regulator of pH homeostasis in C. glutamicum is the F1F0 ATP synthase, which acts as 
both a proton pump to expel protons and a proton­consuming enzyme [17]. Under 
acidic conditions, the ATP synthase will work as a proton pump to expel protons and 
promote uptake of cations such as potassium and sodium, which helps maintain 
intracellular pH. At higher pH values, the ATP synthase shifts its function to 
synthesizing ATP, which is needed in biosynthesis and for growth. Figure 1.3 shows 
the acid adaptive mechanisms in C. glutamicum. 
 
Figure 1.3 Strategies for acidic pH homeostasis in bacteria and C. glutamicum. 
General acid adaptive mechanisms in most bacteria are shown on the left, and possible 
molecular strategies adopted by C. glutamicum for coping with low pH are shown on 
the right[16]. 
 
Another important mechanism used to regulate pH in C. glutamicum is production of 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
buffering compounds such as glutamate. Glutamate is produced by the glutamate 
dehydrogenase (GDH) and glutamate synthase (GOGAT) pathways [18]. These 
compounds can act as proton acceptors or donors, depending on the pH of the 
environment.  
 
Several investigations have demonstrated that numerous cellular mechanisms are 
involved in resistance to acidic pH stress in C. glutamicum. For example, Follmann et 
al. showed the functional link between pH acclimatization, oxidative stress, iron 
homeostasis, and metabolic alterations [14]. Xu et al also reported that both repression 
of oxidative stress and sulfur assimilation play important roles in pH homeostasis [20]. 
C. glutamicum can over express some gene reduced intracellular ROS accumulation 
and eventually elevated cell survival at acidic pH condition [20].  
 
1.11 C. glutamicum tolerance to and metabolism of lactic 
acid 
C. glutamicum has several mechanisms for tolerating lactic acid, which is toxic at high 
concentrations. One of the primary mechanisms as mentioned above is the ability of C. 
glutamicum to regulate the pH in the cytoplasm. C. glutamicum has a variety of 
transporters and enzymes that can pump protons out of the cytoplasm or consume them, 
thus preventing acidification of the cytoplasm.  
 
Another mechanism that C. glutamicum uses to tolerate lactic acid is the production of 
specific enzymes that can detoxify lactic acid and utilize lactic acid as a carbon source 
for growth and metabolism. C. glutamicum can grow with lactate as sole or combined 
carbon and energy source [26]. Lactic acid is first imported into the cell through specific 
transporters, such as the lactate permease LldP or the proton­dependent lactate 
transporter LctP. C. glutamicum can produce lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), which 
plays an important role in the metabolism of lactic acid [66]. There are two different 
types of LDH enzymes found in C. glutamicum, LdhA, encoded by the ldhA gene, is 
responsible for production of l­lactate in C. glutamicum [27][28]. And the quinone­
dependent L­lactate dehydrogenase is encoded by lldD gene, which is a membrane­
bound enzyme that is essential for the metabolism of D­lactic acid [67]. This LDH can 
oxidize lactic acid to pyruvate while reducing NAD+ to NADH. Pyruvate can then be 
further metabolized through the central metabolic pathways to generate energy and 
biomass. This reaction not only generates energy for the bacterium, but also removes 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
lactic acid from the cytoplasm. The enzyme lactate dehydrogenase catalyzes the 
conversion of lactic acid to pyruvate, releasing NADH in the process. Finally, C. 
glutamicum will convert lactic acid into CO2. 
 
C. glutamicum can also produce other enzymes, such as the lactate racemase enzyme, 
which can convert L­lactic acid to D­lactic acid [25]. This conversion can decrease the 
toxicity of lactic acid to the bacterium, as D­lactic acid is less toxic than L­lactic acid. 
 
1.12 Adaptive laboratory evolution to enhance lactate 
metabolism of C. glutamicum 
Adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) is a method that is used to study how organisms 
adapt to changing environments over time [34]. In ALE experiments, C. glutamicum 
can be grown under specific selection pressures such as nutrient limitation, high 
temperature, low pH and so on, which prompts mutants that are better fit to the imposed 
conditions to arise. Bacteria accumulating mutations that confer a selective advantage 
in the given environment are selected for and become more prevalent in subsequent 
generations [35]. By analyzing the genetic changes that occur during ALE, researchers 
can identify the genes and pathways involved in adaptation and gain insights into the 
molecular mechanisms of evolution in C. glutamicum. Several studies have already 
bred C. glutamicum strains by using ALE. Lee et al adapted wild­type C. glutamicum 
under gradually increasing H2O2 conditions in chemostat culture and found the H2O2­
stress tolerance strain which contributed for constructing a strain with oxidative stress 
tolerance [36]. Oide et al carried out an ALE experiment on C. glutamicum under 
thermal stress [37].

2 Material and Methods 
2.1 Bacterial strains and adaptive evolution 
 
The wild type strain is C. glutamicum ATCC 13032. The mutation strain is from C. 
glutamicum ATCC 13032 adaptive evolution in CGXII medium with 100 mM lactic 
acid and the pH is 5.5. Single colonies were inoculated into BHI liquid medium and 
incubated overnight to obtain the preculture. Then 100 μL of the overnight culture was 
transferred into 5 mL of CGXII with 100 mM lactic acid and the pH is 6.5. After two 
days growth, CGXII with 100 mM lactic acid and the pH is 5.5 inoculating 10% of the 
previous culture. The procedure was repeated and this growth­and­dilution process was 
continued for about 3 months in CGXII with 100 mM lactic acid and the pH is 5.5. 
During the first month, subculturing was performed every two days, with each 
subculture inoculating 10% of the previous culture. In the second month, subculturing 
was conducted daily, and each subculture was inoculated with 5% of the previous 
culture. In the third month, subculturing was also done daily, with each subculture 
inoculating 2% of the previous culture. For every fifth passage, a culture sample was 
withdrawn and stored at −80℃ for future analysis.

3.1 Growth of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 and its low-pH 
adapted mutant on lactate supplemented solid CGXII 
medium 
 
C. glutamicum grows best at a neutral pH which may interfere with it being used for 
removing lactic acid in acid whey. In previous work we adapted C. glutamicum into 
growing better at lower pH in the defined medium CGXII. From this adaptation the 
mutant MT, which we characterize in the current study, was isolated and compared with 
its parent strain. To investigate the effect of a low pH on growth of C. glutamicum and 
its low­pH adapted mutant, the strains was streaked on solid CGXII medium containing 
100 mM lactic acid and adjusted to pH 5.5/6/6.5/7.  
 
Colonies gradually developed on the agar plates which after 48 hrs appeared as shown 
in Figure 3.1 (a). Based on the size of the colonies, it was apparent that the WT strain 
grows very slowly at all different pH’s when compared with MT. Basically, the WT was 
unable to grow within 48 hrs. This may be due to inhibition by the low pH of wild type 
C. glutamicum. For wild type C. glutamicum, the optimal pH range for growth is from 
7 to 8, and if the extracellular pH is outside this range, C. glutamicum will activate 
various metabolic mechanisms to maintain its intracellular pH [14]. For the MT strain, 
during the adaptive laboratory evolution, it become more acid tolerance than the WT. 
The final pH that MT was exposed to during the evolution was 5.5, which apparently 
had enabled it to grow faster than the WT strain on solid CGXII medium. Also the MT 
strain should be able to metabolize lactic acid faster than the WT since lactic acid was 
the only carbon source present during the adaptive laboratory evolution. The growth 
rate of MT varies depending on the pH of the medium. Based on colony size, growth at 
pH 5.5 was the slowest when compared to growth at pH levels. In Figure 3.1 (b) shows 
the colony morphology of C. glutamicum under Stereo Microscope (Olympus, Japan). 
Just like C. glutamicum typical colonies, which smooth, creamy, and convex in shape 
[49]. The colonies are circular, and the color of the colonies appear pale yellow and 
beige. Figure 3.1 (c) shows the MT strain growth on CGXII medium within 72 hrs. The 
colony shape is circular and with the increase of growth time, the colony becomes larger 
and remains round.

3.2 Characterizing growth of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 
and its low-pH adapted mutant in lactate supplemented 
CGXII liquid medium at acidic pH 
 
To characterize the growth of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 and its low­pH adapted 
mutant on lactate supplemented CGXII liquid medium, the strain was inoculated in 
CGXII liquid medium containing 100 mM lactic acid and with different start pH. The 
inoculation OD value is 0.1. The impact of each component of the CGXII medium on 
C. glutamicum has been confirmed [56]. Four different pH’s were selected with the 
main objective of comparing the differences in growth rates of C. glutamicum under 
different initial pH conditions. Lactic acid, as an acidic substance, lowers the pH of the 
medium, and 100 mM lactic acid is also the sole carbon source for C. glutamicum. 
Therefore, setting different pH gradients could observe the intensity of the inhibitory 
effect of the acidic environment on C. glutamicum. When the pH was below 5.5, neither 
the MT nor the WT could grow, so the following pH values were chosen: 5.5, 6, 6.5, 
and 7. 
 
As can be seen from Figure 3.2 (a), little growth of the wild type occurred within 9 
hours, while the mutant displayed no growth in the first 4 hours, followed by a slow 
increase in OD value after 4 hours, reaching around 0.5 after 8 hours. The pH of both 
the MT and WT cultures gradually increases after inoculation. The pH of the MT 
increases noticeably faster than that of the WT. During this process, C. glutamicum 
metabolizes lactic acid so the pH of the system increased. Additionally, in the first 4 
hours, although the OD value does not increase, when the pH reaches around 6, the OD 
==PAGE:26==

value starts to increase. Moreover, when the initial pH is 5.5, compared to other initial 
pH values, the pH change is the greatest within 9 hours. The pH for the MT culture 
increases from 5.5 to around 6.4, a rise of 0.9. The pH of the WT culture increases from 
5.5 to around 6.1, a rise of 0.6. In contrast, for other initial pH values, such as when the 
initial pH is 6 (Figure 3.2 (b)), the pH values of the MT and WT increase by 0.6 and 
0.3, respectively. When the initial pH is 7 (Figure 3.2 (d)), the pH change for both the 
MT and WT is only around 0.1. The pH should increase as lactic acid is metabolized. 
In the medium with a high pH such as 6.5 and 7, the OD value increases more rapidly, 
which will lead to a faster rise in pH. However, when the initial pH of the medium is 7, 
the pH increase is slow. When the pH in the CGXII is 5.5, it is extremely challenging 
for C. glutamicum. Therefore, it actively utilizes various mechanisms to resist the low 
pH environment. C. glutamicum has evolved a large number of strategies to handle the 
low pH during the natural evolution and these help to keep the intracellular pH within 
a narrow range [16]. But C. glutamicum can't change the pH in the medium by using 
these adaptive strategies. The reason for unusual pH changes may be related to the 
buffering substances in the CGXII medium. The main buffering substance in CGXII 
medium is MOPS (84 g/L) and (NH4)2SO4 (40 g/L), and it also contains a small amount 
of KH2PO4 (2 g/L) and K2HPO4 (2 g/L). The effective pH range of MOPS is 6.5­7.9 
which has a pKa is 7.2 [64]. Therefore, when the initial pH of the medium is 5.5, the 
buffering effect of MOPs is weaker, and the pH changes significantly within a certain 
period of time, especially in the range where the pH is below 6. Some study also shows 
the measured increase of the pH values depends on the initial lactic acid concentration 
and on the buffering capacity of the media [65]. The consumption of lactic acid (pKa 
3.86) cannot influence the buffering capacity of the medium at pH above 7 [65]. 
 
As the initial pH of the medium increases, the differences in OD values and pH values 
between the MT and WT become more significant. When the initial pH of the medium 
is 6 (Figure 3.2 (b)), the trend of pH in the MT and WT are very similar during the first 
3 hours. However, after 3 hours, the pH of the MT increases at a noticeably higher rate 
compared to the WT. Once the difference in pH values between the MT and WT 
becomes larger, at around 4 hours, their OD values also exhibit a significant divergence. 
The OD value of WT remains relatively unchanged, while the OD value of MT begins 
to increase rapidly. This change also demonstrates a correlation between pH variations 
and OD changes. In an acidic environment, a higher pH corresponds to faster OD 
growth.

In order to present the lactic acid metabolize of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 and its low­
pH adapted mutant in acid whey, different initial OD values were selected to inoculate 
in acid whey with a pH of 5.5. The initial pH of acid whey is 4.5, and it contains a large 
amount of protein flocculent precipitates (see Appendix A.2). Since C. glutamicum 
cannot grow in an environment with a pH below 5.5, we adjusted the pH of acid whey 
using 5 M sodium hydroxide. To minimize interference from other factors membrane

growth, changing the pH of the system caused precipitation of some proteins, and as a 
result, the acid whey environment became very turbid. Therefore, it was not possible to 
measure the OD value of the bacteria. Thus, the pH of the system and the consumption 
of lactic acid were chosen to be measured, which indirectly assess the bacterial growth. 
 
The HPLC chromatogram of acid whey is shown in Figure 3.5. The black line

the standard substances. HPLC analysis revealed that the lactate content was 8.2 g/L.

present in acid whey within a certain period. The pink line appears no peak of lactic 
acid, indicating its complete metabolism. Additionally, acid whey contains a significant 
amount of lactose, besides lactic acid. C. glutamicum cannot metabolize lactose and 
cannot grow in a minimal medium containing only lactose [55]. There is a small peak 
on the right side of the lactose peak in the black line, which disappears in the pink line. 
This small peak is likely to be galactose, as C. glutamicum cannot metabolize lactose

3.4 Characterizing the growth of C. glutamicum 
ATCC13032 and its low-pH adapted mutant in CGXII 
medium with Tryptone 
To characterize the growth of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 and its low­pH adapted 
mutant on lactate supplemented CGXII+tryptone liquid medium, the strain was 
inoculated in CGXII with 1 g/L tryptone liquid medium containing 100 mM lactic acid 
and with different start pH. In Chapter 3.2, the growth rate of MT in CGXII medium 
with 100 mM lactic acid is approximately twice that of WT. The growth difference 
between MT and WT is significant, with MT showing a strong advantage. Additionally, 
the pH increase in MT is faster compared to WT, indicating a clear advantage. However, 
in Chapter 3.3, the growth performance of MT and WT in acid whey did not show a 
significant difference. While MT generally exhibits slightly faster lactate metabolism 
in acid whey compared to WT, it does not demonstrate overwhelming superiority as it 
does in CGXII medium. In some conditions, WT even shows faster lactate metabolism. 
As mentioned earlier, this phenomenon may be due to the amino acids and peptides 
content in acid whey. The acid whey used in this experiment is untreated whey obtained 
from MAMMEN CHESS A/S (Fredericia, Danmark), a byproduct of dairy fermentation. 
This acid whey contains visible protein flocculent precipitates (see Appendix A.2), 
which are removed through 0.22 μm filtration. However, acid whey still contains some 
water­soluble free amino acids and short­chain peptides. This component could be the 
reason why the large difference between MT and WT growth characterization in acid 
whey and CGXII medium. These short­chain peptides and amino acides, which 
generated during milk fermentation, are likely to promote the growth of C. glutamicum 
and aid in its faster adaptation to acidic environments. 
 
1 g/L Bacto™ Tryptone (Gibco™) was added to the CGXII medium to simulate short­
chain peptides and some free amino acids found in acid whey. Tryptone is an animal­
derived pancreatic digest of casein. The amino acids and peptides in acid whey is from 
renneting action on casein [43]. In this new medium, apart from the additional Tryptone, 
the rest of the formulation is the same as described in section 3.2 of CGXII with the 
addition of 100 mM lactic acid. In previous experiments, both MT and WT strains were 
unable to grow in CGXII medium with a pH below 5.5. The OD value even slightly 
decreased over time, possibly due to cell lysis. However, it is hypothesized that tryptone 
can promote the growth of C. glutamicum and enhance its tolerance to acidity. 
Therefore, pH gradients of 4.5, 5, 5.5, and 6 were chosen to characterize the growth.

4 Conclusion 
 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate a novel solution to process acid whey. The lactic 
acid in acid whey will agglomerates and forms lumps in the spray dryer which makes 
acid whey discharge become more challenging. We focus on is Corynebacterium 
glutamicum to work as a biological “filtration”. This bacterium can use lactic acid as 
carbon source in acid whey thus allowing for using the traditional whey processing 
technologies.  
 
C. glutamicum grows best at a neutral pH which may interfere with it being used for 
removing lactic acid in acid whey. Adaptive evolution method was used to obtain a low­
pH adapted mutant from the wild type strain C. glutamicum ATCC 13032. From this 
adaptation the mutant MT, which was characterized in the current study, and isolated 
and compared with its parent strain in four different mediums CGXII solid medium 
with lactic acid, CGXII solid medium with lactic acid, CGXII liquid medium with lactic 
acid and extra tryptone and acid whey.  
 
In Chapter 3.1 and 3.2, to investigate the effect of a low pH on growth of C. glutamicum 
and its low­pH adapted mutant, the strains was first characterized on solid and liquid 
CGXII medium containing 100 mM lactic acid. The mediums were adjusted to different 
acidic pH 5.5/6/6.5/7. In both solid and liquid mediums, the growth and pH express 
consistent results, with MT showing a strong advantage in growth and metabolism 
lactic acid. Additionally, in Chapter 3.4, tryptone was added in CGXII medium with 
lactic acid to investigate the function of shout chain peptides to promote the growth of 
C. glutamicum and aid in its faster adaptation to acidic environments. The result 
confirmed that tryptone can increase the growth for both strains under acidic condition, 
especially in the high acidic conditions like the initial pH=5.5. 
 
Last, Chapter 3.3 present the lactic acid metabolize of WT and MT in acid whey, 
different initial OD values were selected to inoculate in acid whey with a pH of 5.5. 
The results suggested that MT could achieve optimal lactic acid metabolism with a 
lower cell count when the inoculation OD was set to 1. And the total time for run out 
all lactic acid in acid whey is around 13 hours with start OD=1.

5 Future research 
 
In this thesis the focus has been on characterize C. glutamicum ATCC13032 and its 
low­pH adapted mutant in CGXII solid medium, CGXII liquid medium, CGXII 
medium with tryptone and acid whey. The acid whey was supplied by one Danish dairy. 
However, acid whey can vary substantially in terms of composition depending on its 
source, and even from batch to batch [40]. It would be interesting to investigate how C. 
glutamicum ATCC13032 and its low­pH adapted mutant performs on different 
types/batches of acid whey. For example, some factories may pre­process acid whey, 
extract proteins, and then dispose of the remaining liquid. Would this have an impact 
on the growth of C. glutamicum and its metabolism of lactic acid. 
 
Furthermore, acid whey is characterized by its green­yellowish color, because of the 
presence of riboflavin (vitamin B12) [59]. It will be interesting to know whether 
riboflavin has any stimulatory or inhibitory effects on the growth of C. glutamicum. 
Riboflavin serves as a precursor for flavin mononucleotide (FMN) and flavin adenine 
dinucleotide (FAD), acting as cofactors for various enzymes, and these cofactors could 
promote cell growth [68]. Additionally, whether riboflavin influences the lactic acid 
tolerance ability of C. glutamicum in acidic environments.  
 
The plate colony counting was used in Chapter 3.3, the measurement of C. glutamicum 
growth in acid whey revealed that MT not only metabolizes lactic acid but also exhibits 
significant growth in the medium. In the future, a sub­batch fermentation model could 
be designed to process acid whey by considering the growth of microorganisms in acid 
whey, lactate metabolism, pH changes, and other relevant factors comprehensively. 
 
Urea had a strong influence on C. glutamicum growth. In CGXII medium, there is 10 
g/L urea [63]. In general, an excess amount of urea will inhibit the growth of C. 
glutamicum [56]. This effect was due to the increase of culture pH resulting from urea 
decomposition [56]. However, In the case of acid whey, we aim to rapidly increase the 
pH of the medium to reduce its acidity level. In acid whey, there is a natural urea content 
of approximately 0.06% [40]. Whether it is possible to increase the metabolism of acid 
whey by adding a small amount of urea could be investigated. 
 
The proton­consuming decarboxylation dependent on glutamate (GADR system) help 
some bacteria such as E. coli, L. monocytogenes, and lactic acid bacteria to resist acid 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
conditions. In this system, the amino acid decarboxylase will consume protons in the 
process of catalyzing amino acid decarboxylation [58]. Arginine is a kind of buffering 
compound produced by many Gram­positive bacteria, for example, E. coli, L. 
monocytogenes and L. lactis, which enhances its tolerance to acidic conditions [58]. 
Arginine is produced by the arginine deiminase (ADI) pathway [19]. Those two 
metabolic pathways are common mechanisms utilized by many microorganisms to 
resist acidic environments. However, C. glutamicum lacks the genes that encode 
proteins for neither GADR nor ADI system [16]. Perhaps we can use genetic 
engineering to introduce the enzymes and associated genes required for these two 
metabolic pathways into C. glutamicum, thereby further enhancing its tolerance to 
acidic environments. If it were possible to enhance acidic tolerance of C. glutamicum 
from pH 5.5 to 4.5, which corresponds to the natural pH of acid whey, it would be 
highly worthwhile to investigate.
