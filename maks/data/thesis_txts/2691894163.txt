Abstract 
Biofouling, i.e., the accumulation of marine organisms, is an economic and ecological problem in the 
maritime industries. A solution is antifouling coatings with antifouling activities derived from marine 
organisms in the form of natural products, bacterial extracts or even living bacteria. The genus of 
Pseudoalteromonas is interesting in this capacity due to its secondary metabolite production. 
However, in a study by Bernbom et al. (2013) Pseudoalteromonas strains on stainless steel surfaces 
were found to attract and induce growth of other bacteria in natural seawater instead of inhibit 
growth.  
Therefore, this thesis investigated the microbial community assembly on stainless steel surfaces in 
natural seawater in the presence and absence of Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio biofilm. By 
16S amplicon analysis and quantitative PCR, a higher abundance of bacteria was found in the 
presence of P. piscicida B39bio biofilm. Furthermore, the microbial communities in the presence 
and absence of P. piscicida B39bio biofilm were significantly different. In the presence of P. piscicida 
B39bio, the microbial communities were dominated by Pseudomonadaceae, and the 
Flammeovirgaceae and Kordiimonadaceae families were significantly more abundant. Whereas in 
the absence of P. piscicida B39bio, the abundance of Actinobacteria was significantly higher and the 
abundance of Pseudomonadaceae was significantly lower.

Resume 
Biofouling eller vækst af marine organismer på marin infrastruktur, er et økonomisk og miljømæssigt 
problem for søfartsindustrien. En løsning er at bruge begroningshindrende belægninger, som 
indeholder marine organismer, deres naturlige produkter eller bakterieekstrakter, der forhindrer 
vækst af marine mikro- og makroorganismer. I denne sammenhæng er Pseudoalteromonas slægten 
interessant, fordi den producerer mange sekundære metabolitter og da den ofte er isoleret i 
forbindelse med marine makroorganismer, som den beskytter mod uønskede mikroorganismer. Et 
studie af Bernbom et al. (2013) observerede dog Pseudoalteromonas stammer, der tiltrak og 
stimulerede væksten af andre bakterier i naturligt havvand i stedet for at inhibere vækst.  
Dette speciale undersøgte derfor sammensætningen af mikrobielle samfund, som opstod på rustfrie 
stålkuponer i naturligt havvand, hvor stålkuponerne forinden enten var sterile kontroller eller belagt 
med Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio biofilm. 16S amplikon analyse og kvantitativ PCR viste, at 
der groede flere bakterier på de B39bio biofilm belagte stålkuponer efter syv dage i havvand end på 
kontrollerne. Derudover var de mikrobielle samfund signifikant forskellige på kontrollerne og 
stålkuponerne med B39bio biofilm. På overfladerne med B39bio biofilm var de mikrobielle samfund 
domineret af Pseudomonadaceae familien og der var signifikant flere individer fra 
Flammeovirgaceae og Kordiimonadaceae familierne. På kontrollerne var der signifikant flere 
Aktinobakterier og signifikant færre fra Pseudomonadaceae familien.

Introduction 
Biofouling is an immense problem for the maritime industries such as aquaculture and maritime 
transportation [1–4]. Biofouling is the accumulation of marine organisms on marine infrastructures, 
such as vessels, nets, and cages [5]. The biofouling layer consists of microorganisms, i.e., bacteria 
and microalgae, but also larger marine organisms, such as macroalgae, bryozoans, mussels, 
seaweed etc. [5].  
Biofouling constitutes several different problems. In the maritime transportation industry, the 
biofouling results in increased frictional resistance, which is overcome by a higher fuel consumption 
[3,6]. This means that for a given ship, 40-80% of the fuel consumption is directly due to biofouling 
[7]. It makes biofouling a huge financial issue for the maritime transportation industry, especially 
considering that 80% of the global trade by volume is carried out by sea [8].  
The second problem caused by biofouling is the introduction of invasive species [9]. When a vessel 
travels from one port to another, it will inevitably bring marine organisms with it and thus introduce 
new species. This is a major threat to the world’s oceans and the prevention and control of 
biofouling is therefore very important [5]. The solution to biofouling is various types of antifouling 
(AF) coatings and paints. The most effective AF paint was the tributyl-tin self-polishing co-polymer 
(TBT-SPC) system, which targeted a wide range of fouling organisms and delayed dry-docking up to 
five years [10,11]. Unfortunately, TBT accumulated in sediments and non-target organisms and was 
therefore totally banned in 2003 [12,13]. Since then, many different types of AF coatings have been 
invented and experimented with, though none as effective as TBT-SPC [11].  
Some of the newer types of AF coatings contain natural products, bacterial extracts or even living 
bacteria [14,15]. These coatings have a huge potential and especially coatings containing pigmented 
Pseudoalteromonas species are of interest, because the genus has many antifouling, antibacterial 
and antialgal activities [16–18]. Though the species has potential, only one study has investigated 
the species antifouling properties in situ [17] and it more bacterial growth when the 
Pseudoalteromonas strain P. piscicida B39bio was present, than in its absence. The strain showed 
antibacterial activity and inhibited a monoculture of Pseudoalteromonas strain S91, however a 
coating of live P. piscicida B39bio seemed to induce growth of bacteria in natural seawater [17].  
Therefore, this thesis will investigate which marine bacteria that are attracted and enriched by P. 
piscicida B39bio, by looking into the microbial composition on stainless steel surface in natural 
seawater in the presence and absence of P. piscicida B39bio biofilm.

Biological fouling 
Biological fouling or biofouling is characterized by the International Maritime Organization (IMO) as 
the accumulation of marine organisms, including microorganisms, plants and animals, on surfaces 
and structures within or exposed to aquatic environments [5]. Within the first seconds to minutes 
of immersion in seawater, a surface will be covered in conditioning film, a layer of organic and 
inorganic molecules [19]. This first layer changes the physicochemical properties of the surface and 
it can affect the settlement of organisms to come [1]. After or parallel to the conditioning layer, 
microorganisms such as bacteria and diatoms (microalgae) will rapidly colonize the surface. Bacteria 
are the primary colonizers and they form biofilms and cell aggregates within hours to days of 
immersion of a surface [1]. The bacterial biofilm is a comprised of different bacteria encapsulated 
in extracellular polymeric substances such as glycoproteins, exopolymers, nucleic acids, proteins 
etc. The biofilm made by bacteria and the colonization of microalgae is characterized as microfouling 
[5]. Biofilms are highly active [20] and the composition of the microfouling layer affects the 
settlement of larger multicellular organisms. The settlement of larger multicellular organisms such 
as barnacles, tubeworms and macroalgae, that are visible to the naked eye, occurs within weeks of 
the immersion and is characterized as macrofouling [1,5]. The different layers and inhabitants of a 
biofouling layer is visualized in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: Temporal structure of settlement. Adapted from Abarzua et al. (1995) [21]. During the first minutes of exposure 
to seawater, an organic film (e.g., proteins) will settle on any given substrate. The next 12 hours, bacteria and diatoms 
such as Pseudomonas putrefaciens and Amphiprora paludosa, settle as the primary colonizers. Secondary colonizers, 
macroalgae and protozoa settle during the first week of exposure. Finally, the tertiary colonizers settle within 2-3 weeks. 
The tertiary colonizers are macrofoulers such as tunicate, polychaeta and bryozoa. [21]

Depending on the composition of the initial biofilm, some larger settlers are attracted, and others 
are repulsed. Mieszken et al. (2012) showed that biofilms from natural seawater enhanced the 
settlement of the green algae Ulva linza, whereas single-specie biofilms differed, some enhanced 
the settlement, others reduced or had no effect at all [22]. Another important point to make about 
biofilms is that even though an individual strain could inhibit e.g. larvae settlement alone, that strain 
might not dictate/control the larval response to the biofilm [20]. It is the overall structure of the 
community that influence the settlement of macro biofoulers [23], [24]. However, the biofouling 
communities varies due to seasonality, addition of new species, periods pf growth or regression, 
planktonic events, larval settlements, environmental conditions etc. [2,25,26]. 
The negative impact of biofouling 
All marine infrastructure, from permanent structures to nets and cages used in aquacultures and 
marine vessels will over time be exposed to biofouling organisms [1], [2], [3,4]. Biofouling on a ship’s 
hull creates a roughness that leads to an increased weight and frictional resistance [4]. Even a slime 
layer, a layer of bacteria which produce extracellular polymers, adds a significant increase in 
resistance and power necessary to maintain the same speed [3].  When it comes to heavy fouling, 
such as barnacle fouling, it is responsible for up to 93% of the increased frictional resistance at slow 
streaming speed, which is significantly less than maximal speed [27]. Another study showed that 
heavy calcareous fouling can lead to a powering penalty for up to 86% at cruising speed [3]. The 
power penalty is the extra power necessary to maintain a given speed. Whether the choice is to 
increase the power to maintain the speed or maintain the same power and thereby reducing the 
speed, the outcome is a significant economic penalty [28]. Worldwide this has a great impact, since 
80% of the global trade by volume and 70% of the global trade by value was by sea in 2018 [8]. 
United Nation Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) reported that the maritime trade 
reached a volume of 11.08 billion tons in 2019 and though the growth is the lowest in 10 years, the 
maritime trade is still growing [8]. The prospect of solving the biofouling issue in a sustainable 
manner, could save billions of dollars. A study by Schultz et al. (2011) estimated the cost associated 
with fouling, i.e., expenses for hull cleaning, painting and extra fuel, to be 56M dollars a year for a 
certain class, DDG51, of destroyers in the US Navy [6]. In 2011, the US Navy had 60 DDG51 
destroyers [29], each with a length of 150m, that is just under a million dollars per ship for just one 
year. There is no doubt that biofouling composes a huge issue for the maritime industry, not just in 
extra fuel but also in maintaining the ships. Micro biofouling on metal surfaces stimulates corrosion 
processes and thereby slowly degrades the ship [30].

Another important issue brought about by biofouling, is the introduction of invasive marine species. 
The introduction of non-indigenous and cryptogenic species has been identified as a major threat 
to the diversity of the world’s oceans [5,9]. Cryptogenic species cannot clearly be characterized as 
indigenous or non-indigenous, as they might have been introduced earlier due to human 
explorations and travels [31]. Non-indigenous species are found in the fouling directly on the hull, 
however, niches, such as ballast water [32] and sea-chests also carries a high diversity and 
abundances of marine organisms [9]. A study by Frey et al. (2014) showed 80% of the examined 
vessels had sea-chest macrofouling and 15-20% of those macro biofoulers were non-indigenous or 
cryptogenic. A study by Bouda et al. (2018) reported 41 known invasive species found along the 
Algerian cost line. These examples show, how widespread the introduction of invasive species have 
become. This is a serious issue, due to the significant influence non-indigenous species have on the 
local ecosystems. They compete with the native species for food and other resources and it leads to 
a reduction of the local abundance and diversity, which eventually results in global homogenization 
of the marine environments [33]. The problem stems from maritime transport, because the majority 
of invasive species are introduced by maritime vessels [33,34].  Biofouling and the introduction of 
invasive species are far from new problems and naturally there are numerous attempts and possible 
solutions to control and prevent biofouling and the spread of invasive species.  
To minimize the introduction of invasive aquatic species, the International Maritime Organization 
(IMO) provides a globally consistent approach to control and management of biofouling on ships. 
The Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) are behind the 2011 guidelines for the 
control and management of ships’ biofouling to minimize the transfer of invasive aquatic species 
[5].  
Prevention and control of biofouling 
Human measures to control and prevent biofouling have been taken for more than 2000 years [35]. 
Some of the first examples by ancient cultures are coatings of pitch, wax and tar [4], [11]. Moving 
to newer time, the first variants of antifouling (AF) paints were developed in the mid-1800s. The 
idea behind the AF paint was to continuously leak toxic molecules and thereby prevent the 
formation of biofilm [36]. The principle behind antifouling paints today remain the same [36]. The 
paints contained antifoulants such as copper oxide, arsenic and mercury oxide [4], [11]. The 
antifoulants were dissolved in turpentine oil, naphta or benzene and linseed oil varnish, tar or 
different types of resin were applied as binders [36]. These components with the addition of zinc 
oxide, zinc dust, mercury oxide, tar oils and rosin were the basis of antifouling paints until after the 
second world war where synthetic petroleum-based resins and organotins appeared [4,36]. The 
focus on safety and health management increased and with the introduction of the airless spraying,

it was the end of the organo-mercurials and organo-arsenicals in AF paints [4,36]. However, the 
lifetime of these paints was limited to around two years [37] because the release of biocides was 
uncontrolled and rapid in the beginning. Which meant that the effect weaned off, when the biocides 
had leaked out of the paint [4,36]. 
When Tributyl-Tin Self-Polishing Co-Polymers (TBT-SPC) was introduced in 1974 by Milne and Hails 
[38], it revolutionized AF paints completely. TBT-SPC paints consist of an acrylic polymer, such as 
methyl methacrylate, which is hydrophobic and stops water from penetrating the paint layer [4,10]. 
TBT groups are then bound to the backbone of the polymer through ester linkages [10]. When 
immersed into seawater, the pigment particles of the paint will start to dissolve and release TBT 
moieties, which are extremely active biocides, 10 times more active than copper [12,39]. TBT 
moieties interfere with the energy metabolism of chloroplasts and mitochondria as well as interact 
with proteins and membranes [40,41]. TBT therefore targets a wide selection of microfouling 
organisms. Tributyl-Tin and the Self-Polishing Co-polymer system is visualized in Figure 2. Less 
biocide is therefore necessary to keep fouling organisms away [12]. With time, more pigment 
particles are dissolved and the area, that leaches TBT moieties gets larger [42]. This exposes the 
polymer backbone which is partially reacted and can therefore easily be eroded by the sea, which 
exposes a less-reacted paint surface, that can release more TBT [42], [12]. Due to the self-polishing 
effect and the remarkable stable release of TBT of the lifetime of the paint [11], dry-docking, i.e. 
when ships are on land for maintenance, can be delayed up to five years [10]. The success of TBT-
SPC antifouling paints is evident from domination on the world market from its development in the 
mid-70s to several restrictions of the use of TBT based paints on smaller boats in the 80s and 90s, 
to the final ban on TBT based antifouling paints in January 2003 [13,43].

Several publications have shown the contamination of water and sediments by TBT [44–46]. The 
main problem with the use of TBT-SPC paints is the negative impact TBT have on non-target 
organisms [12]. Examples are the decline of the gastropod Nucella Lapillus during the late 80s due 
to imposex caused by TBT [47] and the almost collapse of French oyster culture farms due to the 
harmful effects of TBT on marine mollusks in the early 80s [48]. Imposex is a severe consequence of 
TBT accumulation, where TBT’s endocrine disruptive effects cause elevated testosterone levels 
[49,50], which lead to the imposing of male characteristics on the female genitalia and in severe 
cases cause sterility and local distinction the species [43]. The imposex of N. lapillus is even used as 
a biological indicator of TBT contamination [43]. Luckily, the different bans and restrictions on the 
use of TBT have had an effect. Birchenough et al. (2002) showed through measures of butyltin tissue 
burdens and imposex and intersex of neogastropods, that the TBT contamination in the North Sea 
was low already in 2002 before the final bans on TBT [43].  Already in 1982 when the French 
Government restricted the use of TBT based AF paints on vessels over 25m, the oyster culture farms 
were saved, though it also showed the damage caused by TBT paints [48]. Some places the TBT 
contamination is decreasing. However, Kim et al. (2014) identified hot spots in Korean ports and 
harbors with TBT levels exceeding environmental quality targets six years after the total ban on TBT 
[51].  
The development and use of tin-free AF paints started as early as the 1980s [11]. An example are 
the tin-free copper-based antifouling systems, which are composed of seawater-soluble matrices 
with biologically active ingredients such as a copper component and an organic biocide [11]. 
Examples of organic biocides are Irgarol 1051 and diuron, which work by blocking the electron 
transport at photosystem II and thereby inhibiting the photosynthesis in phototrophs such as algae 
[52].

However, the organic booster biocides accumulate and contaminate the oceans [46,51] and oyster 
have been shown to accumulate considerably amounts of copper [53]. Other examples of 
antifouling paints and coatings are the nanocomposite chitosan-zinc oxide coating with 
antimicrobial, antifungal and antialgal properties [54], pneumatic systems [55], paints with natural 
products [56] and so called ‘living’ paints [14]. These two last examples are somewhat natural 
systems and produces natural products, that have antifouling activities without being toxic or are 
toxic and biodegradable, so accumulation of toxic products is avoided [57]. The paints or coatings 
are either supplemented with secondary metabolites produced by microorganisms or the 
antifouling microbes themselves. The first successful attempt of a paint formulation with natural 
products incorporated was performed by Armstrong et al. in 2000 [57]. They incorporated 
supernatants and cell extracts from two bacterial strains, a Bacillus and a Pseudomonas, isolated 
from the surface of a seaweed and a nudibranch into a water-based paint. The antifouling effect of 
these paints were varying and far from the TBT and copper-based paints, however Armstrong et al. 
2000 showed the possibilities of natural products and bacteria in antifouling paints [57]. In the 
following years, there are several examples of natural products, cell extracts and dead bacteria 
incorporated into paints. Burgess et al. (2003) described the effect of marine bacterial extracts 
formulated into different water-based paints [14]. Many of the paints with bacterial extracts 
showed activity against biofouling bacteria, a few inhibited the settlement of barnacle larvae and 
algal spores and one paint with a Pseudomonas sp. strain NUDMB50-11 extract showed high activity 
against all assays [14]. Besides these two examples, there are several publications describing AF 
paints and coatings with natural products [58,59] or cell extracts [60,61], see Table 1.

The incorporation of living bacteria into the paint is categorized as a so called ‘living’ paint [15]. In 
‘living’ paints, the antifouling bacteria are immobilized in a polymer, where they release antifouling 
compounds. This type of coating will have an infinite lifespan if the bacteria can be kept alive. Most 
AF coatings and paints have relatively short lifespans and must be renewed when the supply of the 
biocide runs dry. So, the idea of an infinite supply from live bacteria tempting and there are several 
examples of bacteria immobilized in various polymer matrices [15,64,65], however, the major issue 
is the design of an appropriate polymer for immobilization [15]. Some polymers, like calcium 
alginate, degrade rapidly and others such as poly hydroxy-ethyl methacrylate, are toxic to the 
bacterium, in this case Pseudoalteromonas tunicata [66]. Yee et al (2007) showed that 
Pseudoalteromonas tunicata immobilized in κ-carrageenan beads survived under laboratory 
conditions up to twelve months and inhibited biofouling for up to seven weeks in the field [15]. The 
polymer used in this study is unlike other alternatives not toxic, it is a polysaccharide derived from 
the red algae Rhyodphyta and applied in both the food, pharmaceutical and cosmetic industries 
[67]. It is however, still a relatively short period of inhibition compared to the other types of 
antifouling paints, such as copper/biocide-based and TBT-based paints. Pseudoalteromonas 
tunicata and other species of the Pseudoalteromonas genus have been applied in experimental AF 
paints (see Table 1) due to their antibacterial, antialgal and antifouling activities [68]. 
The Pseudoalteromonas genus 
As seen in Table 1, there are many organisms with antifouling activities, especially marine bacteria 
[14,15,17,62–64]. Several of these belong to the Pseudoalteromonas genus of the 
Gammaproteobacteria class [17,63,69–72]. The genus consists of obligate marine bacteria, which 
are Gram-negative [73], non-sporeforming straight and curved rods [74] and was proposed in 1995 
by Gauthier et al. [75]. Currently, the genus contains 59 species [76] and constitutes on average 
2.6% of the global bacterioplankton population [77]. The Pseudoalteromonas species are often 
found in association with marine eukaryotic hosts and bacterial species belonging to the 
Alteromonas, Vibrio and Bacillus [78]. Many of the Pseudoalteromonas species produce interesting 
secondary metabolites, which can have multiple functions, including being antimicrobial, algicidal 
and antifungal [68,69].  
Secondary metabolites are unlike primary metabolites nonessential for growth, instead they confer 
an advantage for survival of the organism [79]. The secondary metabolites are a diverse group 
including nonribosomal peptides (NRPs), polyketides (PKS), phenazines, alkaloids, terpenes, etc. 
[79,80]. NRPs are short peptides produced by nonribosomal peptide synthases which are large 
multimodular enzymes that assemble peptides bypassing the transcription and translation 
processes [80]. Examples of NRPs are siderophores, pigments, toxins and antibiotics such as 
penicillin [81]. Polyketides are like NRPs synthesized by multimodular enzymes and produced in an

assembly line manner where similar units are connected and rearranged to form the final polyketide 
[79]. The polyketides includes metabolites such as the antibiotic erythromycin, the toxin aflatoxin 
and many more [82,83]. 
Paulsen et al. (2019) found that the pigmented Pseudoalteromonas devote up to 15% of their 
genomes to biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, whereas the non-pigmented only devote up to 
3% [84]. The phylogenetic diversity among the pigmented strains was also higher than among the 
non-pigmented [84]. The pigmented species include the dark green P. tunicata [85], the yellow P. 
piscicida [85], the red P. denitrificans [68] and P. rubra [86], the orange P. flavipulchra [68] etc., see 
Table 2.  
 
Several of the pigmented species have antifouling activity and the correlation between 
pigmentation and antifouling activity has been studies by several groups [16,84,87]. Egan et al. 
(2002) and Huang et al. (2011) performed similar mutagenesis experiments on P. tunicata D2 and 
Pseudoalteromonas sp. sf57, respectively [16,87]. P. tunicata D2 produces two pigments, a yellow 
of the tambjamine class [88] and a purple pigment, which results in dark green colonies, when both 
pigments are produced [87] and P. sp. sf57 produces a red pigment, a prodigiosin and an unknown 
yellow pigment [16]. After transposon mutagenesis, several mutants with altered pigmentation 
were isolated and tested for antifouling activities. The white and purple P. tunicata D2 mutants had 
lost some or all their ability to inhibit settlement of invertebrate larvae, algal spore germination, 
fungal growth, and bacterial growth, whereas the yellow mutants maintained their antifouling 
activities [87]. The white P. sp. sf57 mutants had lost their antifouling activity and even started 
inducing larval settlement, whereas the red and yellow mutants maintained their ability to deter 
larval settlement [16]. For P. tunicata D2 there is a clear correlation between loss of yellow 
pigmentation and loss of antifouling capabilities, which indicates that the yellow pigment is 
indirectly or directly important for the inhibition of larval settlement [87]. For P. sp. sf57, there

The antibacterial activities described, show that Pseudoalteromonas interact negatively with other 
bacteria. Tang et al. (2020) describes how Pseudoalteromonas sp. Strain CF6-2, isolated from 
sediment, can kill Gram-positive bacteria by producing and secreting the metalloprotease 
pseudoalterin, which degrades peptidoglycan chains. The degradation products of peptidoglycan 
induce the production of more pseudoalterin and the Pseudoalteromonas utilize the released 
nutrients for growth [93]. This is an example of an extracellular predator targeting a wide range of 
Gram-positive bacteria. Another example of Pseudoalteromonas bacterial interaction is reported by 
Desriac et al. (2020), who isolated Pseudoalteromonas strains from healthy oysters. These 
Pseudoalteromonas strains produces cyclolipopeptides called alterins, which kill Gram-negative 
bacteria by binding to lipopolysaccharides, provoking membrane depolarization and 
permeabilization eventually lysing the bacteria [94]. Richards et al. (2017) reported two mechanisms 
of killing and inhibition of competing bacteria by Pseudoalteromonas piscicida strains isolated from 
seawater. The three strains, that were investigated all inhibited and killed the Gram-negative 
bacteria; Vibrio vulnificus, Vibrio parahaemolyticus, Vibrio cholerae, Photobacterium damselae and 
Shewanella algae as well as the Gram-positive Staphylococcus aureus [91]. The inhibition may have

been related to secretion of cysteine proteases and electron microscopy revealed numerous vesicles

and caused cell lysis [91]. A study by Rao et al. (2005), showed a Pseudoalteromonas tunicata strain 
outcompete Cytophaga fucicola and Alteromonas in mixed species biofilms, however, 
Pseudoalteromonas gracilis and Roseobacter gallaciensis, co-existed and outcompeted P. tunicata,

suggested to be beneficial during competition with other bacteria. Only the species that were either 
insensitive to AlpP, P. gracilis, or produced inhibitory compounds itself, R. gallaciensis, were not 
outcompeted by P. tunicata [92].  
There are many accounts of Pseudoalteromonas species interacting negatively with other bacteria 
[91–94]. However, there are few studies describing Pseudoalteromonas co-occurring with other 
bacteria [78,92,95]. As mentioned above, P. tunicata co-exists with P. gracilis, because P. gracilis is 
insensitive to AlpP [92]. Balqadi et al. (2018) isolated the dominating strains from microfouling 
communities on nets in marine waters and found two Pseudoalteromonas species, P. shioyaskiensis, 
P. rubra, together with a Planctomicrobium and Vibrio harveyi [95]. Finally, Satheesh et al. (2016) 
found the following bacterial genera frequently dominating microbial communities associated with 
marine macroorganisms; Alteromonas, Pseudoalteromonas, Vibrio and Bacillus [78]. So it seems 
some Pseudoalteromonas species protect marine macroorganisms by producing antifouling 
compounds [69,78] and others are actually biofoulers themselves [95].  
Pseudoalteromonas species have been incorporated into paint as the antifouling component in 
several experiments [15,17,63]. Bernbom et al. (2013) performed several experiments with 
Pseudoalteromonas strains, some laboratory experiments and some were Pseudoalteromonas 
strains were incorporated directly into silicone-based fouling-release coatings. Under laboratory 
conditions, different Pseudoalteromonas strains were coated unto small stainless-steel coupons and 
exposed to monocultures of Pseudoalteromonas S91. The P. tunicata strains reduced the 
attachment of Pseudoalteromonas S91 slightly and even P. tunicata D2, which was included as an 
antifouling positive control, had very little effect on the attachment of strain S91. However, the P. 
piscicida strain B39bio significantly reduced the attachment of strain S91 after seven days and after 
53 days, no Pseudoalteromonas S91 were detected. These experiments suggested that P. piscicida 
B39bio could serve as an antifouling agent. Thus, coated B39bio coupons were exposed to natural 
seawater, and it was expected that there would be fewer cells on the coated coupons than on the 
non-coated control coupons. However, this was not the case. After seven days, the number of

Purpose of the study 
The purpose of this study is to investigate which bacterial genera that are attracted by pre-coated 
P. piscicida B39bio surfaces compared to non-treated control stainless steel coupons after seven 
days of incubation in natural seawater under laboratory conditions, similar to the experimental 
work by Bernbom et al. 2013.

Materials and methods 
Strains 
Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio was isolated from Danish coastal waters in 2009 [85]. 
Pseudoalteromonas tunicata D2 [17], Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 [17] and Phaeobacter inhibens 
DSM17935 [96] will be used as standards in the quantitative PCR.  
Experimental setup 
First, 2cm2 stainless steel coupons, circular racks, tweezers and metal stirrers were soaked overnight 
in 15% Deconex ® and subsequently rinsed and degreased in acetone following the protocol from 
Bernbom et al. (2009) [97] before autoclaving. Second, six circular racks with metal coupons were 
placed in 1L beakers with 250mL sterile Marine Broth (MB, Difco) and a metal stirrer. Three of the 
beakers were control beakers and nothing further was added for three days, where the beakers 
were left at 25oC and app. 150rpm. To the three remaining 1L beakers, 1mL Pseudoalteromonas 
piscicida B39bio culture, cultivated in triplicate in MB at 25oC and shaking conditions, was added 
and the bacteria were left to form biofilm on both sides of the metal coupons for three days at 25oC 
at app. 150rpm. After three days, several coupons were removed from the racks for further analysis. 
This sampling was denoted t0. From the control beakers, one coupon was removed from each 
beaker, functioning as a negative control for the biofilm formation by P. piscicida B39bio. From the 
beakers with P. piscicida B39bio, four coupons were removed from each beaker (4 replicates), used 
to determine the density of P. piscicida B39bio on the coupons (see procedure below).  
The racks with the remaining coupons were transferred to new 1L beakers, to which 250mL natural 
seawater was added. The seawater was collected from Bellevue beach on February 15th, 2021. 
Water temperature -0.4oC, salt concentration was 9.59 PSU, dissolved oxygen was 8.27 mg/L and 
conductivity was 8.70 mS/cm. The coupons were then left for seven days at 25oC and 150rpm, so 
fouling could take place on both sides of the coupons.  
Finally, five coupons from each beaker, after being submerged in natural seawater for seven days, 
were removed for further analyses. This sampling was denoted t7. The experimental setup is 
illustrated in Figure 5.

were PCR amplified using barcoded primers specific for the V3V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene 
(Fw_V3V4; CCTACGGGNGGCWGCAG, Rv_V3V4; GACTACHVGGGTATCTAATCC) [98]. For PCR

Removal of biofilm from coupons 
Coupons were removed from the beakers after three days in MB, at 25oC and app. 150rpm. All 
coupons were removed using sterile tweezers, any last drop of MB or seawater on the coupons were 
removed with paper towels and each coupon was transferred to a sonification tube containing 2mL 
3% Sigma Sea Saltwater (SSS). Adherent bacteria were removed from the surface of the coupons by 
ultra-sonification for 4min and vortexing for 15s at maximum speed.

Estimation of bacterial density using qPCR 
The bacterial density on the coupons was estimated by quantitative PCR of the 16s rRNA genes in 
all samples from coupons as well as the filtered seawater samples. Standards curves of Ct versus 
log(CFU/mL) were prepared using ON cultures in 5mL MB of Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio, 
Pseudoalteromonas tunicate D2, Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 and DNA samples of Phaeobacter 
inhibens DSM17395 [17,96]. Dilutions (10-2, 10-3, 10-4, 10-5, 10-6) were plated in triplicate on MA 
plates and colony counts were obtained (CFU/mL). 1mL of each dilution was centrifuged (15,000 x 
g, 3 min) and pellet was resuspended in 400µL sucrose lysis buffer and DNA was extracted as 
described above in DNA extraction and 16S rRNA gene amplification of seawater samples. A Master 
mix containing the PowerUpTM SYBRTM Green PCR Master mix and 16S rRNA primers, 338F 
(ACTCCTACGGGAGG CAGCAG), 518R (ATTACCGCGGCTGCTGG), was prepared. Fourteen microliter 
master mix was added to each well of a 96 PCR plate along with 1µL gDNA from each standard and 
sample. The PCR plate was covered with a sealing film and spun shortly on a mini plate spinner and 
transferred to the Stratagene Mx3005P Real-Time PCR system, following the qPCR protocol in B: 
PCR protocols Table 9. After the qPCR, the cell concentrations of the standards were converted to 
CFU/µL, then log transformed and used along with the Ct values to create a standard curve, see 
Appendix D: qPCR Ct standard curve. The qPCR efficiency of the four standards were 91%, 87%, 103% 
and 104% for P. piscicida B39bio, P. tunicata D2, V. anguillarum 90-11-297 and P. inhibens 
DSM17395, respectively. The standard curves were used to estimate the log(CFU/µL) of the 
samples, which were then converted to log(CFU/cm2). A Shapiro-Wilk normality test was run on the 
replicates from the control setup and on the replicates from the P. piscicida B39bio coated setup to 
confirm normal distribution before a Welch two sample t-test was performed.

Discussion 
The purpose of the thesis was to investigate the bacterial genera attracted to and enriched by P. 
piscicida B39bio coated surfaces after seven days in natural seawater. Therefore, the antimicrobial 
secondary metabolite potential of P. piscicida B39bio was investigated, the amount of surface 
attached bacteria was quantified with qPCR and the bacterial compositions were investigated by 
16S rRNA gene amplicon analysis and ANCOM-BC differential abundance analysis.  
An antiSMASH search of the assembled genome of P. piscicida B39bio revealed that the strain has 
fifteen potential BGCs. Paulsen et al. 2019 performed antiSMASH searches on several pigmented 
Pseudoalteromonas genomes and found 3-25 BGCs in the genomes and eighteen BGCs in the 
genome of  Pseudoalteromonas piscicida S2049 [84]. The number of BGCs in P. piscicida B39bio is 
consistent with the results from Paulsen et al. 2019. Furthermore, the P. piscicida B39bio genome 
harbors a biosynthetic gene cluster with 95% similarity to gene clusters producing alterochromides 
as well as their brominated and dibrominated analogues. Bromoalterochromides are yellow PKS-
NRPS metabolites, which have been shown to inhibit a Gram-positive bacterium, Bacillus, and a 
Gram-negative bacterium, Vibrio in a study by Suria et al. (2020) [117,121]. A study by Sobolevskaya 
et al. (2005) showed bromoalterochromide production in Pseudoalteromonas maricaloris KMM 636 
on nutrient poor media as well as nutrient rich, however the antimicrobial activity towards 
Staphylococcus aureus was lower in nutrient rich media [122]. Atencio et al. (2020) investigated 
several pigmented Pseudoalteromonas strains and found that most bromoalterochromides were 
produced in interaction against Candida albicans, Aspergillus fumigatus, Staphylococcus aureus and 
Escherichia coli, especially the newly discovered bromoalterochromide D series [123]. The 
antibacterial effect of bromoalterochromides have been observed in some Pseudoalteromonas 
species [117,122], and directly from purified bromoalterochromides [121], however the activity and 
mode of action of the bromoalterochromides have yet to be described. Since P. piscicida B39bio has 
the genetic potential for production of bromoalterochromides, and the B39bio biofilm was yellow, 
indicating that the bromoalterochromides were produced under the experimental laboratory 
conditions, the bromoalterochromides might have contributed to the antibacterial activity of the 
B39bio.  
Even though the B39bio strain has potential for antibacterial activity, the quantification of the 
surface attached bacteria revealed that the number of bacteria was significantly higher on the 
B39bio pre-coated coupons. This is not very promising for the use of Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
in AF coatings. This pattern was also observed by Bernbom et al. (2013), who estimated the total 
number of bacteria by 16S rRNA gene qPCR to 5.1 ± 0.3 log(cells/cm2) for the control and to 6.8 ± 
0.5 log(cells/cm2) on the coupons precoated with P. piscicida B39bio. They also estimated the cell

count with CFU counts on 50% MA to 4.8 ± 0.0 and 6.3 ± 0.1 log(CFU/cm2) for the controls and the 
P. piscicida B39bio coated coupons, respectively [17]. The total cell estimates in this thesis were 
more than 10-fold higher than in the Bernbom et al. study from 2013, however, the pattern is 
similar. The same is true for the CFU counts on MA plates. The CFU counts in this thesis were on 
100% MA, whereas the one in Bernbom et al. (2013) were on 50% MA. This might have influenced 
the CFU counts, however as mentioned, the qPCR results also showed higher cell numbers in this 
work.  
An important note is that the qPCR results are biased due to the varying 16S rRNA gene copy 
number. The correlation between a known concentration of cells in a solution and the number of 
16S rRNA sequences in the solution measured by qPCR is found. This correlation is then used to 
estimate the number of cells in a sample based on the number of 16S rRNA gene sequences in the 
sample. When the sample is a monoculture, it works, however it is a problem with environmental 
samples, because the number of 16S rRNA gene copies varies a lot between species [124]. The 16S 
gene copy number (GCN) vary in bacteria from one copy up to fifteen copies [124]. P. piscicida 
B39bio contain two copies, see Appendix H: ASVs belonging to the P. piscicida B39bio, whereas P. 
tunicata D2 (accession: NZ_CP031961) contains 10 copies, V. anguillarum (accession: NZ_CP031494) 
has two copies and P. inhibens (accession: NZ_CP010650) has four copies. This results in a bias 
towards bacteria with high 16S rRNA GCNs [125]. There are several practices when it comes 16S 
qPCR; no post bias correction but using several standards with different 16S GCNs like Bernbom et 
al. (2013) did and many different approaches of correcting for this bias [125]. However, a study by 
Louca et al. (2018) investigated three different bias correction tools and found that all tools had a 
low prediction accuracy when compared to completely sequenced genomes and there was a 
substantial disagreement between the tools. The study even recommend against bias correcting 
16S GCNs, unless the species are very closely related [125]. This recommendation is supported by a 
study by Starke et al. (2020), which compared mock community compositions to the predicted 
composition using 16S rRNA and 16S GCN bias correction. For most of the analyzed mock 
communities the bias correction failed to improve the prediction [126].  
This makes it difficult to compare to other studies with cell numbers obtained with other measures. 
In this work it might account for some of the differences in cell numbers between measures 
obtained with qPCR and SYBR gold staining and CFU plate counts, see Table 5.  
The total abundance might be different from the actual total abundance, however the bias 
introduced is very much the same as the bias introduced in the study by Bernbom et al. (2013). The 
16S rRNA primers and the protocols are the same, there is no correction of the 16S GCN bias and 
the standards are almost the same. Bernbom et al. (2013) used Phaeobacter strain 27-4, 
==PAGE:40==
Master Thesis (30 ECTS) 
Spring 2021 
 
Simone Rosen Balder, s163702 
 
40 
 
Pseudoalteromonas tunicata D2 and Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 as standards, whereas this thesis 
used Phaeobacter inhibens DSM17395, Pseudoalteromonas tunicata D2, Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-
287 and P. piscicida B39bio.  
The estimates of bacterial numbers obtained in this work and the study by Bernbom et al. (2013) 
are therefore comparable. The Bernbom et al. (2013) study determined the number of 
Pseudoalteromonas sp. organisms by 16S rRNA gene qPCR specific for the Pseudoalteromonas genus 
and found that the genus constituted app. 2.5% of the microbial composition [17]. The 
quantification of Pseudoalteromonas alone was not performed in this work. Therefore, it cannot be 
determined whether the higher absolute abundance in the P. piscicida B39bio coated setup is due 
to a high abundance of P. piscicida B39bio. The relative abundance analysis does not indicate that 
it is the case. The relative abundances of the Pseudoalteromonadaceae family ranges from 1.6-30% 
of the microbial abundance on the P. piscicida B39bio coated coupons after seven days in seawater 
and constitutes on average 9.9%. The P. piscicida B39bio coupons are more dominated by the 
Pseudomonadaceae family, which constitutes 43.8 ± 12.3% of the microbial communities precoated 
with P. piscicida B39bio vs 10.0 ± 3.7% in the control setup. The Pseudomonadaceae family is 
widespread in both soil and aquatic environments [127]. Of the nine genera, the Pseudomonas 
genus is the largest and most well-studied genus. Its members are highly diverse, they readily adapt 
to new environments [129] and they produce a wide range of secondary metabolites [76,127,128]. 
Many of the species are fluorescent and they produce pyoverdins (siderophores), phenanzines, such 
as pyocyanin, pyocins (bacteriocins) etc. [128]. All compounds that are toxic to other 
microorganisms or in other ways provide an advantage for the Pseudomonas [128]. Organisms that 
produce antimicrobials often have mechanisms which make them insensitive to the produced 
antimicrobials [130]. This is also the case for the Pseudomonas species which among other 
resistance mechanisms [131,132], have efflux pump systems, that pump different toxic compounds 
out of the cell [133–135]. The many resistance mechanisms along with the antimicrobial production, 
make the Pseudomonas species good competitors in microbial communities, which might be why 
they constitutes such a large portion of the microbial communities precoated with P. piscicida 
B39bio. 
According to the ANCOM-BC analysis, the Kordiimonadaceae and the Flammeovirgaceae families 
are also significantly more abundant in the microbial communities precoated with P. piscicida 
B39bio. They only constitute 2.1 ± 0.6% and 3.4 ± 1.8%, respectively. The Flammeovirgaceae family 
belongs to the Cytopha-Flavobacterium-Bacteroidetes phylum and of the eight genera belonging to 
the family, three genera, Roseivirga, Flexithrix and Reichenbachiella, are significantly differential 
abundant [76]. Some members of the Reichenbachiella genus produce flexirubin-type pigments 
==PAGE:41==
Master Thesis (30 ECTS) 
Spring 2021 
 
Simone Rosen Balder, s163702 
 
41 
 
[136], which have antimicrobial activities [137] and the Roseivirga produces unknown pigments, 
that give the colonies pink and orange colors [138]. The antimicrobial activity of some of these 
species might give them the advantage to compete with the Pseudoalteromonas. The 
Kordiimonadaceae family of the Alphaproteobacteria class only contain two genera, the 
Kordiimonas which currently include seven strains and the EIlatimonas [76]. The Kordiimonas are 
distributed globally in marine environments, where their relative abundance usually is less than 2% 
[139]. So, the relative abundance of the Kordiimonas is in the high end of what is usually observed. 
The Kordiimonas are motile [140] and motility can be an effective way to either avoid toxic 
compounds by moving away from the source or to fight the producer by covering the producing 
colonies [141]. If there are available surface spaces far enough away from antimicrobial producing 
colonies, that might be how the Kordiimonas have survived on the P. piscicida B39bio coated 
surfaces. However, it has been observed in several cases, that antibiotic sensitive strains have been 
isolated from the same biofilms as antibiotic producers [92,142,143]. Narisawa et al. (2008) showed 
the pyocyanin producing Pseudomonas aeruginosa strain P1 coexisting with a pyocyanin resistant 
strain from the genus Raoultella and a sensitive Brevibacillus strain. Mixed biofilms of the three 
strains showed that the resistant Raoultella strain formed a protective barrier between the sensitive 
Brevibacillus strain and the P. aeruginosa [144]. So even though some of the microorganisms 
present on the P. piscicida B39bio coated surface do not have any resistance mechanisms 
themselves, they might have indirectly via other microorganisms.  
Besides the families described above, the microbial communities in the presence of P. piscicida 
B39bio were dominated by Gammaproteobacteria followed by Beta- and Alphaproteobacteria. In 
comparison, the noncoated control stainless steel coupons were dominated by Betaproteobacteria 
followed by Gammaproteobacteria, Actinobacteria and Alphaproteobacteria. Furthermore, the 
control surfaces had a significantly higher alpha diversity. The two microbial compositions might 
seem similar on phylum level, however, the NMDS ordination, Figure 10, clearly shows three 
different clusters: the controls, the P. piscicida B39bio coated and the seawater samples. The 
seawater samples cluster separately from the control samples, they are more diverse with 
significantly higher alpha diversity estimates (Figure 6), and they have a higher relative abundance 
of Flavobacteria and Chloroplasts. This difference might be due to the new conditions, laboratory at 
25oC vs. sea at -0.4oC, and the fact that the microorganisms in the seawater samples are planktonic, 
whereas the microorganisms in the experimental work are surface attached. Furthermore, the 
controls at t0 showed some contamination, see Table 5, which might have influenced the microbial 
community assembly.

These general compositions are seen in other studies, such as Huggett et al. (2009), who observed 
a microbial composition mainly consisting of Betaproteobacteria, Alphaproteobacteria and 
Gammaproteobacteria after six days submersion of glass slides at Pearl Harbor during May. 
However, after ten days of submersion, the microbial composition was dominated by 
Alphaproteobacteria, the Cytophaga-Flavobacterium of the Bacteroidetes and Gamma-
proteobacteria [145]. Jones et al. (2007) studied biofilms on polycarbonate and stainless-steel 
surfaces submersed in the Delaware Bay during summer and fall and found that the biofilm 
communities were dominated by Alphaproteobacteria followed by Cytophaga-like bacteria and 
Gammaproteobacteria. A 16S clone library of the microbial communities after 10 days showed that 
47% of the 16S clone library based on the microbial communities belonged to the Rhodobacterales 
[146]. The Rhodobacterales are also well represented in this experimental work, where they take 
up app. 5%, 9% and 15% of the control setup, the P. piscicida B39bio coated setup and the seawater 
samples, respectively. So, the general microbial composition of the control samples resembles that 
of similar experimental setups.  
The abundance of Gram-positive bacteria, mostly Actinobacteria varies a lot in the three different 
sample types, and they are generally low abundant. In the seawater samples, where they are most 
abundant, they comprise 22.9% ± 2.2% of the microbial community. Are the Gram-positive bacteria 
underrepresented in the microbial communities of this work or are most marine bacteria Gram-
negative? Jensen et al. (1995) investigated the relative abundance of cultivable Gram-positive 
bacteria in tropical seawater samples taken close to the shore. They found that Gram-positive 
bacteria accounted for 14% of the cultivable bacteria [147]. A metagenomics study on tropical 
surface waters reported 8.1% Gram-positive bacteria, whereof 5.1% belonged to the Firmicutes and 
3.0% to the Actinobacteria [148], however another metagenomics study on seawater samples from 
the Baltic Sea, showed that the Actinobacteria dominated the surface samples by comprising up to 
app. 40% of the microbial communities [149]. The relative abundance of Actinobacteria seems to 
variate a lot and compared to the studies mentioned above, the Actinobacteria and Gram-positive 
bacteria are neither under nor overrepresented in this experimental work.  
The Actinobacteria take up 14.5% ± 3.5% of the microbial communities in the control setup, however 
in the P. piscicida B39bio coated setup, they only comprise 0.50% ± 0.98%. The ANCOM-BC analysis 
found a significant differential abundance of the Actinobacteria between the two setups, which 
might indicate, that the Actinobacteria are inhibited by the presence of P. piscicida B39bio biofilm. 
However, the Actinobacteria themselves have many BGCs and are known to produce thousands of 
biologically active compounds [150,151]. So, why are they inhibited and not more dominant like the 
Pseudomonadaceae? A study by Tang et al. (2020) investigated a Pseudoalteromonas sp. strain CF6-

antibacterial Pseudoalteromonas and Microbacterium are dominant [152].  
 
Future perspective 
Live coatings of Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio are not effective against microfouling, as they 
result in more microfouling than on noncoated surfaces. However, Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
B39bio and related species might still be promising for AF coatings in other ways. Kharchenko et al. 
(2020) showed some success with butanol extracts of P. piscicida 2202 [63]. More studies on 
Pseudoalteromonas piscicida and its antifouling activities, such as investigation of the 
bromoalterochromides’ mode of action and antimicrobial range, would therefore still be interesting 
and relevant for future work.  
Future work with respect to this project would be to determine whether the high total abundance 
of the t7 P. piscicida B39bio coated coupons was due mainly to a high abundance of P. piscicida 
B39bio. This could be accomplished by a more specific qPCR with primers targeting the species such 
as the one performed by Bernbom et al. (2013), where Pseudoalteromonas were quantified using 
primers targeting a Pseudoalteromonas specific region of the 16S rRNA gene [17,153].  
Finally, it would be interesting to include a non-pigmented Pseudoalteromonas strain in the 
experimental setup. Several studies [16,84,154] show a correlation between pigmentation and 
antifouling activity, so it would be interesting to investigate the differences in the microbial 
communities on surface precoated with a pigmented and a non-pigment Pseudoalteromonas, 
respectively.

Conclusion 
The experimental work and the qPCR quantification revealed significantly higher numbers of 
bacteria on surfaces precoated with Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio compared to noncoated 
surfaces, which points to the conclusion that coatings of live P. piscicida B39bio are not good 
antifouling alternatives.  
Though not a great antifouling agent, the biofilm of the B39bio strain do significantly influence the 
composition of the microbial communities on stainless steel in natural seawater. It results in 
microbial communities dominated by Gammaproteobacteria, mostly Pseudomonadaceae, followed 
by Alpha- and Betaproteobacteria.
