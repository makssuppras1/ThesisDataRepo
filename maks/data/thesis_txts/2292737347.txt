Abstract 
Many Danish SMEs see the possibility of expanding their business into emerging markets. However, 
this is associated with difficult challenges. Therefore, the objective of this report is to examine how 
SMEs can elicit user needs in emerging markets. Through the coures of this report, relevant methods 
for the elicitation of user needs within engineering design has been reviewed, together with being 
evaluated on their suitability for user needs elicitation by SMEs in emerging markets. Finally, 
methods are evaluatedm, and compared to two case companies. The methods have been evaluated on 
two sets of criteria, which are determined, based on factors that affect the choice of methods for use 
within SMEs and in emerging markets. For SMEs are the selected criteria; time, economy, expertise 
needed and quality of method. The selected critera for use in emerging markets are; culture, 
infrastructure and access to users. Through the evaluation an overall assessment of the usefulness of 
the methods for the elicitation of user needs is compared to two case companies. It was found that it 
was not possible to find one generic method, which was suited for use within SMEs. However, 
depending on the case type, different combinations of methods was found to be useful. It has been 
proved, that since the report focus is all emerging markets and not on any specific market. The 
evaluation that have been carried out are on a general level.

1.1 Problem definition 
Many Danish companies have realised the potential for sustainable growth in emerging markets such 
as China, India, Latin America, and Africa. However, many are deterrent from entering these markets. 
This is due to the major challenges connected with this (“Project description,” n.d.). Companies that 
have tried and failed to enter these markets, did not recognize the need to successfully adapt their 
products to the users’ needs (Deloitte Touche Tohmatsu, n.d.). Many companies use either old 
products or products with little variation when entering emerging markets, and are slow to recognize 
the needs of the users in these markets. In order to achieve sustainable growth (Deloitte Touche 
Tohmatsu, n.d., p. 1) notes that most major corporations have localised R&D facilities in emerging 
market, since it allows for greater access to local users. This however present a challenge for most 
SMEs, due to the economic burden of having localised R&D facilities. 
Emerging markets can be divided into three different market segments (Gadiesh et al., 2007); the 
premium, the middle and the low end market semegmet. The premium market segment is defined by 
the users’ willingness to purchase high-end product, with “state-of-the-art” features.  The middle 
market segment, consist of value seeking users with mid-level income. They are seen as very 
conscious when selecting products with a certain prices versus performance ratio, meaning that 
product developed for this market segment can be seen as “good enough” products. This is reflected 
through the diffraction of features from the premium market, which make products more cost-
efficient. The low-end market is defined as the users’ willingness or ability to pay for basic product 
features. Before entering an emerging market, companies do not only need to consider which market 
segment(s) they are aiming towards, they also need to consider which country they are entering since 
this can affect the product development process. In order to provide products that users want, 
companies have to understand the need of the user. This is however, at the moment difficult for SMEs, 
since existing methods, such as ethnography, are time-consuming and require specialized training 
together with being too expensive for SMEs to conduct. They are therefore not suited for use by 
SMEs to elicit user needs.

1.2 Research Question  
The main topic of the project is Adapting design specifications for emerging markets, but in order to 
get a clear understanding of how Danish SMEs can do this, the following research question has been 
formulated: 
 How can SMEs elicit user needs in emerging markets? 
To better understand how SMEs can elicit user needs in emerging markets, the research question 
where broken down into three sub-questions: 
 Which methods for the elicitation of user needs exist? 
 Which factors affect the choice of elicitation methods within SMEs? 
 Which factors affect the choice of elicitation methods used in emerging markets? 
The sub questions are used to break down the research question into researchable parts, the first sub-
question explores current methods for elicitation of user needs within engineering design. The second 
sub-question is used for determining criteria that affect the choice of elicitation methods used by 
SMEs. The third and last sub-question is used to determine criteria that can affect the choice method 
when eliciting user needs in emerging markets.

1.4 Research Limitations 
The primary focus of this is report is to understand how and by which criteria Danish SMEs choose 
methods for elicitation of user needs. The content of this report is a theoretical study, with a 
comparison to two small case studies. Methods found of interest for the elicitation of user needs 
within SMEs or in correlation to emerging markets are included in this report. Methods are primarily 
found within engineering design, however, some methods, which normally are used within social 
science and systems engineering, are also included. The selection of criteria for SMEs are based on 
two interviews and are anchored in the literature. The selection of criteria for the elicitation of user 
needs on emerging markets are based on information provided by the Danish Ministry of Foreign 
Affairs and Vaeksthus Hovedstadsregionen and supported by relevant literature. During the project 
there is not differentiated between the different types of users. The term “user” covers user, customer, 
consumer end-user, clients etc. Furthermore it will not be discussed how different user groups are 
identified.

2 Methodology 
The gathering of information for relevant methods for elicitation of user needs have been conducted 
through extensive literature research, within social science, systems engineering and engineering 
design. Information, regarding methods for elicitation of user needs, is mainly found through internet 
searches via web-portals, such as university databases and google scholar, but also through regular 
textbooks and academic books. The information found about methods during this research was used 
to firstly describe each method, and secondly for an evaluation of each individual method. The 
methods are evaluated in three parts; evaluation of methods by SME criteria, evaluation of methods 
by emerging markets criteria and an overall evaluation, where the two first parts of the evaluation are 
compared to each other. After the evaluation, the evaluated methods are compared to two case 
companies. Criteria for the evaluation of methods within SMEs a selected on the background of the 
two case companies. The selection of criteria for the use of methods in emerging are based on relevant 
literature, and conversations with the Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs’ The Trade Council and 
Vaeksthus Hovedstadsregionen. 
2.1 User needs in the requirement process 
In order to get an understanding of the importance of user needs within the design and development 
process, a short summery of the placement and importance of user needs in the requirement process 
have been done. User needs in the requirement process are based upon research mainly done by 
(Gershenson and Stauffer, 1999), (Li et al., n.d.) and (Jiao et al., 2006). 
2.2 Method review 
The method review has been conducted with the focus on the gathering and the identification of 
methods relevant for election of user need in engineering design, but methods used in systems 
engineering that have been found relevant are also considered and incorporated into the method 
review. Through the research several books, articles, thesis etc. have been used. It has therefore been 
important to remain critically of each source of information used in this report. It is important to be

3 User needs in the requirement process  
The design and development of new products are defined by how a market opportunity is transformed 
into physical products or services (Krishnan and Ulrich, 2001). The understanding of; user needs, 
competitive environment and nature of the market, are required in order for products to be 
successfully accepted by the market (Nelson and Paulson, 2006). The alignment of these three 
variables helps aim the product development process to better satisfy the users’ needs. Current little 
research has been done in the area of elicitation of user needs within emerging markets, however the 
increasing interest for companies, especially for SMEs, have shown a need for the understanding  how 
needs and requirements in emerging markets can be elicitated (Li et al., n.d.). One of the most critical 
factors within companies is time-to-market (Morris and Stauffer, 1994). In the concept design phase 
of the product development it is important to understand the users’ needs and requirements to better 
meet the needs of the user (Bossert, 1990).  
3.1 Design Requirement Sources 
(Gershenson and Stauffer, 1995) prose a set of four types of design requirement; End User 
Requirements, Corporate Requirements, Regulatory Requirements and Technical Requirements, 
Figure 3:1. By dividing requirements into four areas, makes it easier and more comprehensible for 
designers to focus on a specific requirement type. (Gershenson and Stauffer, 1995) and (Hauge and 
Stauffer, 1993) have made taxonomies for end users’ and corporate requirements, since technical and 
regulatory requirements both are content-depended an well documenten.  
 
Figure 3:1: Requirements cube showing the various types of requirements and how the information fits into the product 
definition process (Gershenson and Stauffer, 1999) 
Defining and expressing design requirements are usually the first steps in the development process. 
The definition of the requirements can be spilt into two group’s human and artefact, (Sudin et al., 
2010). Human requirement sources are expressed as; clients, end-user, market analysis, colleagues 
and the designers own requirements and expected solutions. Artefact requirement sources are; semi-
developed specifications, proposed solutions, existing products, previous products / projects, design 
and user guidelines.

3.1.1 Corporate requirements 
Design requirements spawned in the corporate category; designate the internal factor that concerns 
actives and human elements within the company, this can for example be department goals or 
individuals such as designers (Sudin et al., 2010). Requirements in the corporate category are ranked 
according to; safety issues, statutory regulation and customer requirements, (Li et al., n.d., p. 4). It is 
anticipated that the corporate category stay the same, when entering emerging markets, unless 
globalisation has an impact on the organisational structure.  
3.1.2 Technology requirements  
Technology requirements refers to engineering principles and scientific knowledge from e.g. 
experience and books.  Technology requirements are closely related to the professional expertise and 
knowledge acquiring ability (Li et al., n.d.).  
3.1.3 Social and Environmental requirements 
The seven factors that influence the external source of design requirements are; user, market 
competition, regional infrastructure, organisational infrastructure and regulation, only the user 
category will be described in this report.  
Users are defined as relevant individuals or groups of people who will buy or use the product (Li et 
al., n.d.). User influenced requirements covers requirements, which are influenced by end-users,

customers, consumers and clients. (Morkos et al., 2014), (Carreira et al., 2013) and (Chen et al., 2003) 
refers to user requirements as the most important sources of requirements, however user requirements 
are commonly unstructured and obscure, and need to analysed, this become increasingly difficult 
when attempting to enter new markets. The use of user requirements can spark conflicts of interest 
between other sources of requirements, e.g. regulation corporate requirements, it is therefore 
important to prioritise user requirements by making trade-offs. There exist several different methods 
of prioritising user requirements. Among them are Marlow’s hierarchy, which identify five levels of 
needs, Figure 3:3.  
 
Figure 3:3: Maslow’s hierarchy, (Veronika, 2013) 
Maslow’s hierarchy helps to identify the prioritization of the user needs, the higher levels only comes 
into play when the lower levels are satisfied. Other methods include the Kano model which prioritise 
three different types of needs (Shahin and Nekuie, 2011); Must be needs, One-dimensional needs and 
Attractive needs. Must be needs, are the basic needs, which need to be fulfilled otherwise users will 
be dissatisfied. However, if fulfilled satisfaction will not increase, e.g. users are dissatisfied if a milk 
container leaks, but if it do not leak this do not increase user satisfaction. One-dimensional needs are 
satisfied with a proportional level of fulfilment, e.g. a milk container that contains ten percent more 
milk for the same price will result in increased user satisfaction, but if it contains seven percent less 
milk, the user will feel misled and will lead to dissatisfaction. Fulfilment of Attractive needs will 
dramatically increase user satisfaction, e.g. a display on the milk container which show how much 
milk remain, but users will not be dissatisfy if there is no display, Figure 3:4.

Transforming customer needs into product specification is a time-consuming process, which must be 
managed throughout the entire product development process. Difficulties with managing the user 
requirements process stems from user needs are unstructured and imprecise. It is common for user 
requirements to be negotiable, but this can spark conflicts with non-negotiable requirements, such 
regulatory requirement. This means trade-offs are from time to time necessary in order to meet as 
many user needs as possible with interfering with non-negotiable requirements (Tseng and Jiao 1998).  
3.2.1 Elicitation of user requirements 
The elicitation of requirements from the user is the most crucial part in the requirement process (Cafer 
and Misra, 2009). When elicitation user requirements, several different methods exist. The most well-
known methods for elicitation user requirements are observations, structured, semi-structured and 
unstructured interviews, but these have their limitation, therefore other options have to be considered 
as well (Stanton et al., 2013). It is important to comprise the use of methods in such a way that they 
effectively captures as many user needs as possible (Yan et al., 2002).

3.2.2 Requirement analysis 
Data and information form the requirement elicitation process often come in a non-specific and 
unorganised form, therefore the main objective of the requirement analysis is to understand what the 
user want and determinine the conditions of new products or altered products (Jiao et al., 2006). 
During the requirement analysis, it is important for the designers to beware of conflicts within 
requirements from different stakeholders (Kotonya and Sommerville, 1998). Poor understanding of 
requirements can have a negative implication on the design and manufacturing process of the product, 
this can lead to increased manufacturing time, cost and loss in quality (Jiao et al., 2006). 
3.2.3 Requirement specification 
Requirement specification are highly dependable of the knowledge generated in the elicitation and 
analysis part of the requirement process (Jiao et al., 2006). Requirements specification is the creation 
of structural, concrete and precise product specification (Fung et al., 1998). In order to determine 
requirement specification, designers can make use of several different methods, such as the QFD, 
Quality Function Deployment, and Requirement Transformation models, which are used to capture 
the most essential part of the specification process (Otto and Wood, 1998).

4 Review of methods used to elicit user needs 
In this section, relevant methods for the elicitation of user needs in engineering design are briefly 
described, without going into an in-depth analysis of each individual method. Methods stated in this 
section do not give a complete overview of all available methods that can be used for elicitation of 
user needs. However, methods that are described are found relevant for both use within SMEs and in 
emerging markets. Through the review of methods, it is attempted to structure the section into 
manageable subsections (categories), such as interviewing methods, survey methods, observational 
methods, brainstorming methods and user feedback methods. Methods that are not divided into sub- 
categories are a combination different methods, which do not fit into one specific category.  
4.1 Interviews 
Interviews are a flexible way to gather large amount of specific information about users. This give 
designers the possibility of elicitating qualitative information and data about the users’ needs (Jordan, 
2002). Interviews are normally conducted by marketing and sales personnel and each interview 
typical lasts between one and two hours each (Ulrich and Eppinger, 2008). To strengthen the 
elicitation of user need in product development (Dieter and Schmidt, 2012) suggest that designers, 
engineers or other development team members conduct the interviews, preferably in the environment 
in which the product is going to be used, this will give designers a first-hand account of the users’ 
perception of the products strengths and weaknesses.  
There are three main types of generic interviewing methods: structured, semi-structured and 
unstructured interviews (Lindlof, 2002; Stanton et al., 2013). Among more specific interviewing 
techniques are the Critical Desicion method, Direct elicitation, Repertory grid and Laddering, these 
provides interviewers with unique probes to elicitation needs from users.  
(Lindlof, 2002) discusses what type of information designers can elicitate from interviews. The 
information provided by interviewing methods aid designers and engineers to understand usability 
and users perspectives through the retrieval of past experiences. Designers also use interviews to gain 
insight and information about the individual users; this is done through the obtainment of description 
of events that normally are unavailable through observational methods. Depending on the type of 
interview, it is easy to correct or alter questions during interviews, thereby making it a flexible method 
to elicit user needs (businesscom, n.d.). The use of interviews to elicit user needs can be very time-
consuming (Cohene and Easterbrook, 2005; “Techniques for Requirements Elicitation,” 2008), 
however, interviews can form the basis for the use of other methods, such as questionnaires, scenario 
building, observations, etc. (Soares et al., 2011). (Griffin and Hauser, 1993), studied the amount of 
interviews needed to uncover most of the user’s needs, 98 percent of user needs2 where revealed by 
nine customer interviews, compared to eight focus groups, which are equivalent 25 hours of data 
collection. As a rule of thumb for most product deployment cases, between 10 and 50 interviews are 
sufficient to gather the maximum amount of user needs (Ulrich and Eppinger, 2008).

4.1.1 Structured interviews 
Structured interviews are used as a method of gathering qualitative information, by using a set of 
predetermined questions, these are used to elicit specific information or data from each individual 
interviewee (Stanton et al., 2013). Structured interviews are usually used to gather data for statistical 
surveys (Lindlof, 2002; Ryan et al., 2009), e.g. public opinion or specific product attributes. 
Structured interviews are divided into two categories; face-to-face interviews and telephone 
interviews (Preface and Grosshans, 2010). Whereas telephone interviews are easy and less time-
consuming compared to face-to-face interviews. Face-to-face interviews provides the interviewer 
with the possibility of observing the interviewee during the interview, which allows the interviewer 
to observe facial expression and reaction to questions. Face-to-face interviews also allows for the use 
of more complex questioning, where illustrations and pictures are needed.   
The use of structured interviews aims to ensure each interviewee is presented with the same set of 
questions. This make it possible to compare the interviewees answers to one another (Patton, 2001). 
(Lindlof, 2002) describe how structured interviews are well suited for focus group studies, due to it 
is beneficial to compare each member of the focus groups answers. Structured interviews can to some 
extend be used as a substitute to questionnaires since it engages users directly, compared to 
questionnaires, e.g. mail and internet based, where users can either ignore certain questions or 
disregard questionnaires entirely (Preface and Grosshans, 2010).

attributes by personal associations, e.g. why is this important to you? (Reynolds and Gutman, 1988).

4.1.1.2 Repertory grid 
The Repertory grid is an interview technique, which is based on theory of psychology of personal 
constructs that breaks down complex personal views into meaningful sub-components (Kelly, 1963). 
A central part of the repertory grid is that each person has their own unique way of systemising, 
prioritization and interpreting the world (Fransella et al., 2003). Meaning that the each individual can 
rate the same “things” on different factors, e.g. a person might rate a place to live accordingly the 
distances to the nearest schools, whereas another might rate accordingly to the daily commute to 
work. 
The repertory grid consist of three phases for elicitation of user needs; Selection (Creation), Triading 
(Assessment) and Rating (Davis et al., 2006). In the first phase, the users choses around six to 30 
“elements”, which are products with in the same product category. In the second phase the users are 
asked to create “constructs”, e.g. good versus bad, fast versus slow, etc. In the third and final phase 
the users are asked to rate each element by all constructs on a scale of their choosing, e.g. if the 
construct focus on height, the users are asked to rate each element by height on a scale of e.g. one to 
nine, where one is lowest and nine highest (van Kleef et al., 2005).

4.1.2 Semi-structured interviews 
Semi-structured interviews make use of a predetermined interview guide that structures questions and 
the order in which they are asked, but unlike structured interviews, semi-structured interviews are 
flexible and  allow the interviewer to ask more open-ended questions (Stanton et al., 2013). Semi-
structured interviews also allow for spontaneous questions that give the interviewer the possibility of 
asking follow-up questions to get more in-depth responses form the interviewee (Baumbusch, 2010). 
The flexibility of semi-structured interview make it one of the most common forms of interview types 
(Soares et al., 2011). 
Semi-structured interviews provide rich and in-depth information about the users’ needs, however 
the interviewer can use leading questions, in which the interviewer can be influence the interviewee 
(“The use of semi-structured interviews in qualitative research,” n.d.). (Denscombe, 2014) discusses 
the “interviewer effect”, where the amount of information an interviewer can be obtain from the 
interviewee, depends on the difference3 between the interviewee and interviewer, in some cases this 
can be a hinderance during the interviewing session. The information gathered from semi-structured 
interviews provided reliable data, which to some extend can be used to compere answerers between 
interviewees (Lindlof, 2002). Semi-structured interviews is useful to form the basis for others 
methods such as questionnaires, scenario building, focus groups and observations.

4.1.2.2 Direct elicitation (Free elicitation) 
Direct elicitation, also called Free elicitation, is a semi-structured interviewing technique, which is 
based upon the memory schemata from (Collins and Loftus, 1975) theory of sematic processing. 
Direct elicitation focuses on the elicitation of product attributes, by using the users’ existing 
knowledge, by “recall” of memory (van Kleef et al., 2005). During a Direct elicitation session the 
interviewees are asked to come up with attributes they find most important when they are choosing a 
product or product category (Bech-Larsen and Nielsen, 1999). 
Using Direct elicitation to elicit user needs has the advantage of elicitating concrete product attributes 
directly from the users. However, in order to use Direct elicitation; it is recommended that 
interviewers predefine a list a product attributes (van Kleef et al., 2005). According to (Collins and 
Loftus, 1975) it is assumed that attributes mentioned by the interviewees first are the most important, 
since these are the most recognizable. 
4.1.3 Unstructured interviews 
Unstructured interviews are interviews where there are no formal structure or questions, meaning the 
interviewer is conducting the interview without knowing which direction interview will take, and 
leading the interview like a normal conversation (Stanton et al., 2013). Since unstructured interviews 
are informal interviews the information elicit from unstructured interviews provides valuable data 
about underlying attitudes, however the information extracted are difficult and time-consuming to 
analyse (Wispe and Thayer, 1954).   
(Lindlof, 2002) describe how informal interviews are useful to gather ethnographic information about 
users. The interviewer do not need any specific set of skills to conduct the unstructured interviews, 
however, compared to other interviewing techniques the interviewer has to be able shift the 
“conversation” without interrupting the interviewee strain of thought, in order to uncover relevant 
user needs. Unstructured interviews are a time-consuming method and requires the interviewer to 
able to quickly identify areas of interest.

4.2 Surveys 
In the development process surveys are used to evaluate concepts and products (Dieter and Schmidt, 
2012; Stanton et al., 2013). The applications of surveys covers the collection of quantitative data from  
large groups users, this makes it is possible to collect data regarding; usability, user satisfaction, 
errors, user opinions and attitudes (Colosi, n.d.).  When designing surveys it is important to keep the 
questions clear and understandable (Colosi, n.d.). Surveys are a useful data collecting method that 
can aid the design team in the elicitation of user needs, especially when it is combined with other 
methods, e.g. interviews, observations and user feedback methods. The advantages of using surveys 
as a method for eliciting user needs, is that it supplies a broad range of information and data that can 
be collected from users, together with the relative easy and inexpensive way distribution them, e.g. 
this can be done via email or internet. The disadvantages of using surveys are the reliability of the 
data that are gathered. The data depend on a large pool of users answering the surveys, if this is not 
the case it can become bias and therefore not represent the user group as a whole (Advantages and 
Disadvantages of Surveys n.d.).  
4.2.1 Property checklists 
As the name suggests the Property checklist is a simple method that in a structured way elicit user 
needs. In order to elicit user needs an interviewer go through a list of predefined properties, attributes 
and features, while the user answers whether or not the property is desired (Jordan, 2002). The 
Property checklist is an easy and quick way to elicit user needs, but the Property checklist is not useful 
for the generation of user needs.   
4.3 Scenario Building 
Scenario building offers a tool which both can be used to analyses user needs, but also elicit user 
needs. This is done by mimicking user-centred perception and taking the user’s point of view while 
keeping the scope of the product in a large context of use. Scenario building is a method, which is 
used by designers to create though up situations to simulate situations the users can experience 
(Hasdoǧan, 1996). There exist several different ways to build user scenarios, it can either be done 
“low” tech by the designer or engineer in their own imagination, or it can  done more elaborate with 
a more formal way of communicating, such as story building, conceptualization and simulating the 
product in use (Soares et al., 2011).  
(Campbell, 1992) has divided scenario building into four separate parts; scenarios to Illustrate the 
product, scenarios for evaluation, scenarios for design and scenarios to test theories. “Scenarios to 
illustrate the product” is used to illustrate how users can use a product. “Scenarios for evaluation” is 
used by designers to evaluate how a product preform in certain situations.  “Scenarios for design” is 
used to design product attributes or features. Lastly, “scenarios to test theories” are used to put 
theories and concepts to the test. By breaking down scenario building into four different parts, it is 
made easier for designers to focus their attention on particular areas, depending on the where in the 
development process they are. They can focus on overall design of concept or more detail on certain 
functions and attributes that can affect the user needs.

4.3.1 Immersion: experiencing user perspective  
When user needs are hard to elicit, especially when users’ are faraway, designers can “imitate” users 
and experience the challenges they face (Soares et al., 2011). In order for designers to experience user 
perspective, designers can put on special equipment that e.g. hinder movement or impair sight and 
hearing. (Soares et al., 2011) list an example of members of a design team designing a car, who wore 
special suits that added weight to imitate the feeling of being old and less mobile.  
(Langford and McDonagh, 2003) points out designers have to be aware of their limitations, and make 
clear distinctions between their targeted user groups. Immersion as a technique to elicit user needs 
can be extremely difficult to carry out especially when it comes to imitating users with special needs, 
e.g. special skills, gender or experience, these needs are difficult to simulate and can result in 
designers misconceive user’s needs.

guided by a moderator (Langford and McDonagh, 2003). The purpose of the focus group is to 
generate ideas and further build upon them, however focus groups can be used for the elicitation of

4.6 Brainstorming 
Brainstorming is a method used to generate concept, idea and identify needs (Maiden and Rugg, 
1996). One of the main rules in brainstorming is for the participants not to evaluate their ideas. The 
classic brainstorming method can be divided into to two types, group brainstorming and individual 
brainstorming (Lamm and Trommsdorff, 1973). As the name suggest group brainstorming, consist of 
groups of various size participating in a brainstorming session. (Lamm and Trommsdorff, 1973) have 
based on empirical data shown that brainstorming in small groups produce fewer ideas than the same 
number of topics in individual brainstorming, however it was not possible to measure the quality, 
uniqueness and variety of the ideas due to less clear evidence. The information generate from applying 
brainstorming methods to elicit user needs, aid the designers in generatin an overall concept of users 
perspective. Information generated by users in a brainstorm session can be unstructured, and hard to 
interpret. Most methods within brainstorming are generic methods, meaning they can be used as either 
group or individual brainstorming.  
In order to use brainstorming as a method to elicit user needs, it is suggested brainstorming, especially 
Concept mapping and brainsketching, is combined with other methods e.g. observational methods. 
Combing brainstorming with observational methods, allow designers to observe users in the creative 
process, giving them an insight on how users generate ideas, while having a minimum of influence 
on the users during the brainstorming session. The disadvantage of “only” observing users 
brainstorming can result in a stray of thought, meaning that users can move of topic. To get user back 
on topic can be time-consuming. Among methods that are useful to elicit user needs are; 
brainsketching (van der Lugt, 2002), Concept mapping (Wolf et al., 1991) and Appreciative Inquiry 
(Cooperrider et al., 2008).  
4.6.1 Brainskectching 
Brainsketching is a method based on brainwriting, which uses sketching as the primary means of 
documenting ideas. Brainsketching aims to provide designers with drawings of mental images of 
concepts and products (Purcell and Gero, 1998). During brainsketching participants sketch their idea 
on a large sheet of paper and post it on a wall, where each participants explain their idea, after they 
have presented their idea, the participants switch places and continue sketching. Users, who are 
involved in brainsketching sessions generate and build upon ideas individually, giving designers an 
insight on how the user “think”, e.g. what is important for the user.

4.6.3 Appreciative Inquiry 
Appreciative inquiry is a method that focuses on users’ positive experience instead of focusing on 
problems and issues (Gonzales and Leroy, 2011). Appreciative inquiries consist of the 4-D cycle; 
Discovery, Dream, Design and Destiny (Cooperrider et al., 2008), Figure 4:6. The four phases are 
designed in such a way that each phase works toward an affirmative theme, called the appreciative 
theme. A group of users can define the theme or it can be predefined by designers. In the discovery 
phase, a group of users are interviewed about high points in their life, by sharing positive stories the 
participants help define factors which contributes to success (Cooperrider et al., 2008). In the dream 
phase participants are asked to look in to the ideal future and look, and how they perceive the ideal 
product, without any constraints. The design phase takes the identified strengths of the ideal future 
and make it realistic. In the last phase, the destiny phase, participants select those needs and 
requirements that are feasible to implement into the product. 
 
Figure 4:6: The 4-D cycle of Appreciative Inquiry (Cooperrider et al., 2008, fig. 1.1) 
Appreciative inquiry promotes user involvement, thereby creating a sense of responsibility among 
the different user groups, making it more likely users will use the product after the development 
process have ended (Gonzales and Leroy, 2011). Depending on the sessions, appreciative inquiry is 
a relative fast method for elicit of user needs, a session can take between four and eight hours, but 
requires time to prepare (Rasmussen, 2011). 
4.7 Observations 
Observational methods are approaches where designers, engineers and other members of the 
development team learn about users by observing the users in different settings (Schwab et al., 2009). 
The dangers of using observational technique are that the collected information can be subjected to 
the observers bias opinion and can lead to misinterpretation, therefore it is recommend that the 
observer do not analysie the gathered information (“Using the Observation Technique for 
==PAGE:34==
 
Page 26 of 75 
 
 
Requirements Elicitation,” n.d.). Observational methods can be divided into two different categories; 
passive, e.g. shadowing (4.7.1), and active observation, e.g. empathic design (4.7.2) (Kosso, 2011). 
In passive observation the observer, does not interact with users in any way, but only take notes, it 
usual for the observer to ask follow-up question after they have finished observing. In active 
observation, the observer can ask questions during the observational session and can participate in 
the actual session. 
Observational methods are relative inexpensive methods, and data gather during the observational 
session can be viewed as reliable (“Using the Observation Technique for Requirements Elicitation,” 
n.d.). Problems with using observational methods are the amount of information which is generated, 
it is unstructured and unclear, and require further analysis, in order to elicit user needs (Stanton et al., 
2013). Furthermore observational methods a time-consuming and can be seen as one of the least 
effective methods.  
4.7.1 Shadowing 
Shadowing is an observational method where a person is accompanied by an observer, who does not 
interfere with the person who is being observed (“Focusing on methods,” n.d.). The benefit of using 
shadowing in eliciting of user needs is that it familiarize the observer with complexity information 
about users daily life (Czarniawska, 2014). Shadowing is an observational method it is a time-
consuming process, since it is only allowed to observed and not ask follow-up question during the 
session  (“Designing With People | Putting people at the heart of the design process,” n.d.). The large 
amount of information which are extracted require further interpretation in order to elicit user needs. 
4.7.2 Empathic design 
Empathic design is an observational method, in which users is being observed using products in their 
natural environment, such as their home or at their workplace. The idea behind Empathic design is 
that over time the designer will develop empathy for problems users face in their daily life (van Kleef 
et al., 2005). Empathic design is a method driven by the need of the user. Where the designer uncovers 
user needs by observing and getting a deeper understanding of their daily problems. Information 
gathered in empathic design are usually pictures or video, making the elicitation of user needs highly 
unstructured and requires the designer to interpret the data. The benefits of using Empathic design is 
that designers, not only see how products are being used, but also get an understanding the motivation 
behind using the product (Leonard and Rayport, 1997). The use of Empathic design as a method for 
elicitating user needs has the advantage that it do not take time from designers during the actual 
gathering of information. This is made possible through the option of having cameras installed in the 
users’ home and / or at their workplace. However, this have its limitations due to the cost of the 
installation of cameras and / or other technical equipment. The interpretation of the gathered data is 
a time-consuming process (van Kleef et al., 2005).

4.8 User feedback 
The use of user feedback for elicitation of user needs can be described through a number of 
approaches. These are usually used at a later stage in the product development process (Robert J. 
Dolan, 1993). Among methods and approaches that have been found relevant within user feedback 
for elicitation of user needs are; user testing, customer complaints and warranty data, Extremely 
Rapid, Experience Eampling Method and Image Based Experience Sampling. Together with methods 
such as Information Acceleration and Ranking which can be adopted for the early stages of the 
development procees in order to elicit user needs. These approaches are suitable when exists and 
similar products are on the market. In order for user feedback to effective, it is important to identify 
the right users and how they should be involved in feedback sessions (“Continuous Customer 
Feedback Programs, Part 1: Getting Started :: UXmatters,” n.d.). 
4.8.1 User testing 
When users are testing products, they can provide valuable feedback that either have been missed or 
overlooked by desginers during the early stages of the development process. User testing generally 
consist of users outside the design team, using and testing a mockup or prototypes (Soares et al., 
2011). There exist different methods of how to approach user testing; user testing can be long and 
rigorous that involves realistic prototypes, which are tested by large group of representative users. 
Other methods include Information Acceleration (Urban and Hausen, 1993) and Extremely Rapid 
Usability Testing (ERUT) (Pawson and Greenberg, n.d.). (Noorani, 2006) propose to build simple 
prototypes in order to test the usability at an early stage of the development process.  
Methods such as Information Acceleration and Extremely Rapid Usability Testing compared to other 
more rigorous user tests, enables designers with an “early” warning of problems with usability, and 
whether or not user needs are met, together with uncovering potential new user needs. (Soares et al., 
2011) propose to use user testing in general with other methods, such as focus groups and adapted 
ethnographic studies, section 4.11. User testing in focus groups give the design team the option of 
selecting users and different user groups to test the product in a controlled environment. User testing 
on one hand provide designers with real time knowledge about functionality and usability of products. 
However, it can be costly and time-consuming to develop prototypes, therefore the development of 
simple prototypes that are inexpensive and easy to make, are well suited for SMEs. 
4.8.1.1 Extremely Rapid Usability Testing (ERUT) 
Extremely Rapid Usability Testing is a method that can be used in a trade show setting, where 
designers have the ability to test the products with serval hundred people (Pawson and Greenberg, 
n.d.). Using ERUT at trade shows provide natural surroundings for the users and provide a natural 
discussion of product features, usefulness, and usability between the designers and users. The 
problem with ERUT lies within that the testing is not set in formal testing environment, thereby 
making it difficult to retain users for a fixed measure of time. User can use the product between one 
and 60 minutes, together with it being difficult to record and observe the user testing the product. A 
trade show offers many distractions that can influence the users in their test of products. ERUT can 
provide SMEs with the possibilities of accessing a large pool of users simultaneously at a relative low 
cost.

4.8.1.2 Information Acceleration 
Information Acceleration is a “virtual prototype” that used is as a concept testing method (van Kleef 
et al., 2005). Information Acceleration can be used through several multimedia devices, such as 
tablets and computers. Information Acceleration seeks to elicit user needs through the obtainment of 
user feedback. Information Acceleration is a relative inexpensive method to test new and unfamiliar 
products, e.g. new cars. (Urban et al., 1994) created a virtual environment where users virtual could 
buy an electric car and interact with sales personal. When users ended the session, the user needs are 
directly elicit by measuring users’ attitudes, preferences and intent to buy. Information Acceleration 
is useful method for the elicitation of user needs, when it comes to larger products, such as cars and 
similar. However, even though it is an inexpensive way to elicit needs compared to building a physical 
prototype. It is still a resource demanding method, this is due to the need for computer programing 
and designing. Information Acceleration do offer the possibility of for users fare away to test virtual 
products. 
4.8.2 Customer complaints and warranty data 
Customer complaints and warranty data contains valuable information about the needs of the users 
(Dieter and Schmidt, 2012). Customer complaints stems from a variety of sources, such as customer 
service departments, service centres, local stores, etc. The complaints are usually recorded by either 
telephone, letter or email (Pilgrim et al., 1997). Customer complaints give designers an insight to how 
users use products, in some cases products are used in an entirely different way then it was meant to. 
Information gathered from complaints can be a useful source of user needs for the development and 
designing of new products (“How To Turn Customer Complaints Into Innovation,” n.d.). Third-party 
internet companies, e.g. Trustpilot and Google Reviews, can provide designers with user information 
about both their own product, but also the competitions products. 
Warranty data provides information on existing product quality, which are used to statically identify 
design flaws and defects. However, this data can sometime be misleading due to product returns with 
no apparent defects. Return of products with no defects are a reflection of customer dissatisfaction 
(Dieter and Schmidt, 2012). 
4.8.3 Experience Sampling Method 
The Experience Sampling Method, also known as the daily diary method, is a method where users 
are asked to stop working at a predetermined time and take notes of their experience and what they 
are doing. The users can be asked specific question on devices they are using at the moment. (Larson 
and Csikszentmihalyi, 1983). Users who are participating in the Experience Eampling Method 
provide “real-time” information and data about their emotions, thereby capturing potential needs that 
could have been overlooked. However, the method is highly subjective and realis on users’ 
willingness, together with compliance to participate in the Experience Eampling Method.

4.8.3.1 Image Based Experience sampling (IBES) 
Image Based Experience Sampling is an approach that combines the Experience Sampling Method 
(4.8.3), image-based methodology and Conjoint Analysis (4.14.1) to elicit user preferences. Instead 
of interrupting the user at a predetermined time, monitoring equipment, e.g. video cameras and photo 
cameras, capture the users current activity. Thereby eliminating the disturbance of the activity (Intille 
et al., 2002). At later time, the users watch the images, which the triggers the users’ memory. This is 
then used as an input for the Conjoint Analysis for the elicitation of user needs. With the combination 
of Experience Eampling Method, image-based methodology and Conjoint Analysis, the designers are 
provided with rich information about the users’ preferences and needs; however, IBES is still a 
relative new method which has not been thoroughly tested. 
4.8.4 Ranking  
In the Ranking method users are asked to prioritize a set of products, in order to get a number of 
attributes based on the product deviation (Bech-Larsen and Nielsen, 1999). According to (Bech-
Larsen and Nielsen, 1999) ranking does not, compared to other techniques, generate a significant 
number of user needs. Ranking can be done either through one-on-one interview or through focus 
groups. The use of Ranking as tool to elicit user needs can be seen as problematic, due to the users 
are asked to compare existing product and focus on the product deviation, this presents a narrow 
minded perspective especially when it comes to new product development. However, Ranking can 
be beneficial when is comes to understanding users in a foreign market, since it makes it possible for 
designers to understand how users choose and prioritize products, attributes and features. 
4.9 Lead User Technique 
The Lead User Technique is also referred to as the Expert Source Technique (Soares et al., 2011). 
Lead users are a class of users, which have months or years of experience with in a field (Hippel, 
1988). They are therefore able to either identify or help identify new market trends. According to 
(Ulrich and Eppinger, 2008) the data lead users provide are useful for two underlying reasons. Firstly 
leader users often have the ability and are able to communicate their needs, this is due to they work 
with the product on a daily basis, and experience the shortcomings of the products more. Secondly, 
lead users can have modified existing products to their own specific needs (Belliveau et al., 2004).  
A study conducted by (Urban and Von Hippel, 1988), outline a hypothesis with a four step 
methodology for concept development and testing with lead users; Specify lead user Indicators, 
identify the lead user group, generate concept (product) with lead users and test the lead user concept. 
The first step is to specify lead users indicators; this means researchers have to define the parameters 
of what makes a user a lead user for the current product development, together with defining the 
measurement for potential benefits. Secondly, the lead user group is identified; this is done by a 
cluster analysis of surveys to identify the lead user group. The third step involves the development of 
concept with lead users, data and information are elicit form lead user in the form of novel attributes, 
product concept, personal experience and fully developed products. The last step in the methodology 
is the test of the lead user concept by non-lead users, since they represent a larger part of the market; 
this done by with methods that are more traditional.

The Lead User Technique is a useful technique to elicit user needs, since lead users are users who 
have unique insight into specific product categories. However, features and attributes that are 
preferred by lead users, do not automatically cover the need of non-lead users, e.g. looking at power 
tools, non-lead users would from time-to-time prefer an “all-in-one” tool, which combines multiple 
tools, compared to a  professional craftsman, who  would prefer specialized tools. Using the Lead 
User Technique combines a number of different method for gathering user needs, together with 
employing lead users who has expert knowledge with in a field. The cost of using the Lead User 
Technique is closely related to methods such as focus groups, interviews and questionnaires. 
4.10 Knowledge Recovery 
The elicitation of requirements have inherent difficulties, but by reusing knowledge from former 
development projects creates the possibility of identifying previous user needs and product designs 
that already have been accepted by users (Tseng and Jiao, 1998). The understanding and transfer of 
knowledge possessed by design engineers can have an impact on the product development process. 
During the development process large amounts of knowledge is generated, (Ahmed and Wallace, 
2004), for example the aerospace industry, the development of a single aero-engine generates around 
40.000 documents, some of this come in form of emails, sketches,  reports etc., (Marsh, 1997). 
Knowledge Recovery is a natural way of handling and facilitating user needs, and trade-offs among 
users, marketing and sales, and designers. The primary use of Knowledge Recovery is within 
companies who developed previous products similar to new to the product being developed, such as 
automotive or aerospace companies. The use of Knowledge Recovery is not a viable option, when 
companies have little to no experience in product development, since the preconditions is not 
available. Knowledge Recovery is primarily used to elicit user needs, but it can also be used to analyse 
and generate design requirements (Jiao et al., 2006). Knowledge Recovery enables designers to both 
extract previous product design requirements, for either directly use or modify them for the current 
development process.

4.12 Zaltman Metaphor elicitation technique (ZMET) 
Zaltman Metaphor elicitation technique, ZMET, is an interviewing method where users are to find 
images. The interviewer use the images a basis for the usage of probes in order to get a deeper 
understanding of users thoughts and feelings (Coulter et al., 2001). The use of images are meant as a 
stimulus for the interviewees. According to (Weisser, n.d.), when exploring user concepts, 
communication with pictures can be used as non-verbal entry points. ZMET is comprised of four 
steps; storytelling, missed images, Kelly Repertory Grid and Laddering, (van Kleef et al., 2005). In 
the first step, interviewees are asked to describe how each image is related to the value of the product. 
In the second step, interviewees are asked if there are any images that did not fit with in the value. In 
the third step, the interviewer randomly selects three images from the interviewees, and ask the 
interviewees how two of the images are similar, and how they differentiate from the third. ZMET 
collect the users’ mental perception of products, such as emotions, attitudes and feeling, which 
provide designers with a unique insight to the users’ perception of products. However, ZMET is an 
extremely time-consuming process that stretches over several weeks, the interviewees are required to 
spend up to two week collecting pictures and images for the interview (Coulter et al., 2001).   
4.13 Anticipated Experience Evaluation (AXE) 
Anticipated Experience Evaluation, AXE, is a method that both is used to elicit and evaluate user 
experience of early prototypes and concepts (“AXE (Anticipated eXperience Evaluation) « All About 
UX,” n.d.). AXE make use of one-on-one interviews with visual stimuli. AXE consist of three phase 
Figure 4:7; concept briefing, evaluation and analysis (Gegner and Runonen, 2012).  In the concept-
briefing phase the concept is presented to the users, the concept is typically presented through 
descriptions, scenarios or muck-ups. In concept evaluation the users are shown image-pairs to 
stimulate the users’ ability to reflect and express their attitudes and opinions towards the concept. In 
the second phase the designer, use the image pairs to form the basis further questioning and reasoning 
of the users’ selection. In the last phase of AXE, data analysis, the concept evaluation phase is 
transcribed and analysed, during the analysis the designer use an analytic framework adopted from 
(Hassenzahl, 2005).

4.14 Preference Analysis 
Preference Analysis is not a method generally used to elicit user needs, however, Preference Analysis 
form the basis for two elicitation methods; Conjoint Analysis and Category appraisal. The following 
section does not give a complete walkthrough, but gives a short overview of Preference Analysis. 
Preference Analysis is general a term for techniques that quantify, analyse and interpret information 
about users (van Kleef et al., 2006). Preference Analysis is divided into internal Preference Analysis 
and External Preference Analysis, Figure 4:9. Internal Preference Pnalysis use ranking of user 
references and uses perceptual information as a supplementary source information, whereas External 
Preference Pnalysis prioritize perceptual information by building product maps based on attribute 
ratings and incorporate user preferences at a later time. 
 
 
Figure 4:9: Methodological difference between Internal and External Preference Analysis (van Kleef et al., 2006) 
Internal Preference Analysis may be considered as a variant of principal component analysis, it takes 
individual users preferences rating for a number of products as inputs (S.W., 1998). External 
Preference Analysis, use the same data but in a different order (van Kleef et al., 2006).

4.14.1 Conjoint Analysis 
Conjoint Analysis is a method used to gather information about how users are influenced in their 
purchasing decisions, by different product attributes such as price, colour, ingredients, environmental 
impact, etc. When users are buying products, they are forced to make “trade-offs” between the 
different attributes, e.g. buying a car, if a customer want to buy a safe car, this usually means buying 
a big car, the trade-off values in this cases would price, fuel economy and environmental impact 
(Kuhfeld, 2011). In order determine how users are influenced by the product attributes, designers 
gather information directly form users, in form of questionnaires and surveys (Stanton et al., 2013).  
The use of Conjoint Analysis in product development helps designers evaluate trade-offs, users take 
into consideration when buying new products (van Kleef et al., 2005), together with providing a 
method to measure the preference of specific impacts for attributes and features (Wyner, 1992a). The 
limitations of using Conjoint Analysis come in form of the amount of information the method can 
handle. The more complex a product is, the more features and attributes are present, which therefore 
require a more detailed responses from users, making it increasingly time-consuming and complex to 
use Conjoint Analysis to elicit user needs (Wyner, 1992b). Conjoint Analysis is a costly and time-
consuming method to implement, especially in smaller companies (Turksen and Willson, 1993). 
4.14.2 Category appraisal 
Category appraisal is a technique that make use of a set of procedures to gather information from the 
users’ perspective. This enable designers to uncover product opportunities and attributes that are 
important for the users choice of product (van Kleef et al., 2005). Category appraisal combines 
Internal Preference Pnalysis (Coombs, 1960; Tucker, 1962), External Preference analysis (Green and 
Carmone, 1969) and Moskowitz’ Category appraisal (Moskowitz, 1986). Category appraisal is a 
“product-driven” method, which means it involves an evaluation of competing products by the users. 
The main reason for this is to gather information about the users’ perception of the competitive 
structure of availability of product categories in certain markets.  
In order to gather information users fills out a questionnaire, where they rank / rate products diversity 
among the attribute preferences. With the combination, of Internal Preference Analysis and External 
Preference Analysis and Moskowitz’ Category appraisal, the data that is extracted from 
questionnaires are mapped in a product map, where the attributes and preferences give designers an 
insight of opportunities for new products or ways to improve existing products (Urban and Hausen, 
1993).  
Category appraisal is method that is useful for designers to elicit and understand of the users’ 
preferences. However, the method requires specific knowledge by the designers, in order to properly 
construct the product map. This is due to Category appraisal combines several methods to both elicit 
data and construct the product map. It is therefore time-consuming and requires special skills to 
Category appraisal. Among other drawbacks are the need for the understanding other cultural factors 
especially if designers a developing products outside their home market (Thomas and McDonagh, 
2013).

5.1.1.2 Economy

5.1.2.1 Culture 
It has become apparent went entering new markets, especially on other continents, it is important for 
any company to consider both social and cultural differences when design product for new markets 
(“Designing for Emerging Markets | Experientia,” n.d.). Culture is an important aspect to consider 
for any company, e.g. (Letelier et al., 2003) points out the importance for companies to consider 
traditional values when accessing users in new markets, e.g. women living under purdah8. This affects 
how they dress, work and even how they talk to friends. This can therefore differ from western 
women. It is therefore important to understand the hesitation females in some emerging market would 
have talking with male researchers and vice versa.  When design products for emerging markets it is 
important for companies not to impose western culture and opinion upon them. Foreign cultures can 
offer a new knowledge base for the product development. (Prahalad and Hart, n.d.) explains: The 
Conservancy links these cultures together through Internet access and designates certain individuals 
to play the intermediary role with the outside world (while keeping the remainder of the inhabitants 
apart). (Prahalad and Hart, n.d.) points out that a commercialization of local knowledge both can be 
used as preservation of these cultures it is also beneficial for designers, when elicitating user needs, 
through it gives an understanding of local knowledge and culture.

The beginning of the product development process can be regarded as a cyclical and iterative process, 
in which cultural factors dominate the initial stage of  development process (Chen et al., 2003).

user follow a “lead” user group/individual or is the culture female/male dominated. (Devlin, 2001) 
addresses the issue of difficulties of cultural factors and conclude with successful cases, such as 
automotive and electronic manufactures in Japan, which revealed that companies to some extend were 
able to influence cultural beliefs of the users in potential markets, therefore companies have to focus 
on “how users do it” rather than “what users do”. Companies have to consider multicultural factors 
in order to capture user needs, if this is not the case, it can result in poor product conceptualisation 
and they might miss important preferences and interest of the different user groups (Chen et al., 2003).  
5.1.2.2 Infrastructure 
Emerging markets are rapidly changing, however, some areas still have relative poor infrastructure, 
especially in rural areas (“Developing winning products for emerging markets | McKinsey & 
Company,” n.d.). (accenture, n.d.) stresses the importance of tailor made solutions for the elicitation 
of the user needs. This means companies have to consider infrastructural factors which influence the 
choice of method, such as telecommunications, transport and electrical infrastructure. Emerging 
markets, such as China and Russia, have more internet users than most developed countries, but in 
developed countries a larger percentages of the population have access to computers and the internet 
(“Infrastructure | Data,” n.d., “World Internet Users Statistics and 2014 World Population Stats,” 
n.d.). Therefore when companies use internet and computer based elicitation methods, they have to 
consider in what part of the market segment their users are located, this means that depending on the 
users placement in market; designers cannot rely on users having access to the internet or computers. 
In some emerging market the reliability of the electrical and telecommunications infrastructure, also 
have to be considered, e.g.  a rainfall can throw the telephone infrastructure into chaos together with 
the stability of the electrical grid (Dr. N.Dawar and Chattopadhyay, 2002). Altogether, companies 
have to consider all aspect of infrastructural factors when choosing methods for elicitation of user 
needs.

5.1.2.3 Access to users 
In any product development process the access to users are important. Especially in emerging markets 
this can be challenging (Deloitte Touche Tohmatsu, n.d.). In order to overcome these challenges 
companies can apply “user segmentation” as a tool for establishing contact to users in emerging 
markets (accenture, n.d.). However, in some emerging markets there are a lack of access to reliable 
user data (Kiener, 2014). This means companies must make “user segmentation” as simple as 
possible, by using available demographic data such as age, domicile and household income. This can 
serve as proxies for selecting user groups that are relevant for the elicitation of user needs. (Yamazaki, 
1997) presents the “location” factor, which relates to the local availability of human resources, and 
the importance of developing products that satisfy the ‘‘happiness of customers’’, such as peace, 
independence and freedom. 
Another challenge companies face in emerging markets is the “access and trust” described by 
(Fletcher and Crawford, 2013). In emerging markets, especially those in Asia and Africa, it may be 
proven difficult for companies to access users due to the general mistrust of “outsiders” or strangers, 
together with discussing issues that may be perceived as personal or taboo. This is closely related to 
cultural factors. Lack of local cultural knowledge can have a negative effect on the willingness of the 
users, which comes to be shown in their willingness too fully, participate in user needs studies. The 
access to users also relies on the credibility of the elicitator and how they are perceived by users 
(Fletcher and Crawford, 2013). In emerging markets personal networks are critical, due to some 
extent the lack of government and other support programs. Informal social networks is an important 
aspect in business activities (Richard Fletcher and Tony Fang, 2006).

Throughout the evaluation of methods, three method stands out in the highest overall evaluation; 
Property checklists, Surveys and Ranking. Common for the three methods are they are quick to use 
and inexpensive, however they lack quality of needs elicitated, since all of the methods are 
quantitative methods where the user either rank or select needs from a list. The methods elicit user 
needs from a predefined list of attributes; they are good for reinforcing needs among a large group of 
users. However, they are not useful for “preliminary” elicitation and discovering of user needs. 
Quantitative methods such as surveys, Property checklist and ranking fit overall well into the user 
needs elicitation process within SMEs. A method that is well suited for the generation of needs within 
SMEs is the Lead User Technique. The Lead User Technique offers SMEs are well-balanced 
approach to generate user needs; this come down to the Lead User Technique offers to elicit structural 
needs form the lead user, together with being relative in-expensive. 
Generally, interviewing methods score high in the overall evaluation, with the expectation of 
unstructured interviews and repertory grid. The interviewing methods score mediocre on time, since 
the methods to some extend require preparation before conducting the actual interview. Depending 
on the type of interview, the interview session can last between one and two hours per interviewee. 
With the expecting of unstructured interviews which on the other hand do not necessarily require 
extensive preparation. However, the actual interviewing session can be very long, since the 
interviewer is not allowed to hinder the interviewee during the session. All of the interviewing 
methods are relative inexpensive to conduct since little cost is involved. The quality of interviewing 
methods score high for Critical Desicion method, Direct elicitation and Laddering because they make 
use of special probes to elicit user needs, these probes give the methods a structured approach where 
it is possible to capture certain user needs. Brainstorming methods score high in the overall 
evaluation, this is due the high quality of the output of the method. The needs elicitated from 
brainstorming methods, especially from Concept mapping, give the design team a unique insight into 
how users perceive products and their vision of future products. Focus groups get an overall mediocre 
score since it requires expertise to control the groups.  
The four lowest ranking methods in the overall evaluation are Ethnography, ZMET and observational 
methods, they all have in common that they are extremely time-consuming. Ethnography is in the 
absolute bottom of the evaluation, this is due to it is both are time-consuming, expensive and require 
highly specialized training. However, it score extremely well in quality, this is due to Ethnography 
captures unique and comprehensive knowledge about users and their needs, throughout the learning 
about users culture in their natural environment. Ethnography and ZMET are best suited for use 
within larger companies, which have resources to conduct large and time-consuming studies. 
Customer and warranty, and Knowledge Recovery are useful methods to obtain user needs, however 
it is preconditioned that companies have existing products on the market.

5.5.1 SumaCare Aps 
SumaCare have relied heavily on users to provide requirements. SumaCare, mainly Helle Thambo, 
have from the beginning of the development of Wear & Care12  involved users. This has been done 
by setting up several interviews with users of interest, but also by handing out questionnaires and by 
observing users in their daily work functions.  
We knew from the start what we would do, because I have a lot of knowledge 
about this error. We have defined it from the start and have had a dialog with the 
end users right from the beginning of this project. (Thambo, 2014) 
Before SumaCare started to interview the users, they predefined the overall requirements for Wear & 
Care and setup informal interviews (unstructured interview). Tthis gave SumaCare the freedom to 
explore new ideas and concepts, which had not yet been considered. The use of unstructured interview 
might not have been the best possible solution, since it require that the interviewer to be able to steer 
the conversation, together with it being a time-consuming method compared to the quality of 
information elicitated. Through the elicitation user of needs, SumCare also has used semi-structured 
interviews, questionnaires and observations.  
We made questionnaires and also used a lot of time at the institutions, by care 
homes and institutions with elderly people. We used a lot of time to go there and 
talk and interview the end users. (Thambo, 2014) 
SumaCare uses semi-structured interviews in the same way as unstructured interviews, but with the 
use of semi-structured interviews SumaCare has an interview guide, to ensure the interviewee does 
not stray off topic and make it possible to structure the information from the users. The use of 
questionnaires give SumaCare access to quantitative information. The information provided by 
questionnaires are usually useful and can give insight to a large pool of predetermined topics of 
interest. The use of questionnaires preconditions that SumaCare has sufficient information about the 
user in order to support the formulation of user need based questions. Questionnaires can impair the 
elicitation of user needs, by hindering of the discovery of new user needs, Since users’ needs normally 
are unstructured and vague. In SumaCare’s case, questionnaires should be used primarily to 
consolidate the importance of the user needs.  
As a final tool for elicitation of user needs used by SumaCare is observations. SumaCare gather raw 
data and information from users without restricting the users’ freedom of expression. However, this 
has its downside; since there are large amounts of information gathered which are unstructured and 
not always relevant. It is therefore time-consuming to both elicit and analyze data and information 
gathered from observations. By using observational methods in combination with other methods, such 
as interviews and questionnaires, SumaCare can gather a more complete picture of the users’ needs.

The methods used by SumaCare for elicitation of user needs are based on the most natural choice; 
since these are the best know methods for elicitation of user needs. Comparing SumaCares choice of 
methods to the evaluation of methods by SME criteria, it becomes clear that the use of observations 
and unstructured interviews do not matches well with the overall evaluation. For elicitation of user 
needs in their current market it would be recommendable to substituted; observations with the 
Experience Eampling Method and both unstructured and semi-structured with the Lead User 
Technique. By using the Experience Eampling Method instead of regular observations, SumaCare 
can free up precious resources and time to focus on other tasks. The use of the Lead User Technique 
instead of traditional interviews let SumaCare focus on a few lead users and thereby using them to 
generate needs for the “common” user. The needs generated by the use of the Lead User Technique 
can then be ranked by the common user. Either by the use of Property checklists or other types of 
surveys. 
At the current moment SumaCare is still in the early stages of entering emerging markets, it would 
therefore be recommendable for SumaCare to use a combination of the Lead User Technique, 
surveys, Property checklists, Immersion and Scenario Building.  The Lead User Technique should 
mainly be used for the generation of cultural and if possibly infrastructural needs from local lead 
users. After the generation of lead user needs, designers within SumaCare can use Immersion and 
Scenario Building to mimic the physical environment, thereby eliciting infrastructural user needs. 
The user needs elicitated by the use of the Lead User Technique, immersion and Scenario Building, 
should be used as a base for surveys, which are distributed among regular users. By combining these 
five methods, SumaCare have the possibility of elicitating both cultural and infrastructural user needs, 
together with supporting the findings among regular users, thereby getting a more clear understanding 
of the users’ needs. 
5.5.2 FOM Technologies Aps 
Since FOM Technologies develop and sale scientific equipment for the academic world, they custom 
produce each machine for each idividual user, they are therefore elicitating user needs for each 
project. According to Martin Kiener FOM Technologies, do use any specific approach for elicitation 
of user needs. 
We do not use any specific method of gathering design requirements (Kiener, 
2014) 
By having, no clear strategy of elicitation of user needs presents a challenge in itself; however, during 
the interview with FOM Technologies it became clear that FOM Technology uses a “fuzzy” or 
“chaotic” approach to elicit user needs. FOM Technologies use five different methods, user feedback, 
user testing, Ranking, Property checklists and Scenario Building, but without having any clear 
structure or approach. FOM Technologies use user feedback to elicit needs from existing users, 
thereby gaining insight into potential needs that might have been overlooked. FOM Technologies use 
the feedback to generate “feature” catalogues that then are presentet to new potential users, who then 
are able to choose desired features for their machine.

We draw a lot on the existing customer’s feedback, so in some case we will call 
some of the users who have the equipment saying we have this idea where we 
would it to do this, what do you think? And they come up with some needs and 
requirements. (Kiener, 2014) 
FOM Technologies also acquire user needs from a variant of user testing, where users are using the 
machine and modify them, by rebuilding or adding features and equipment to the machines. These 
types of user needs normally comes from users approaching FOM Technologies, rather than the other 
way around. This again aid FOM Technologies in the development of their “feature” catalogues. 
We have also reversed the process, where they (the users) approach us saying I 
did this in the laboratory and I build this rig and sits here and works with your 
equipment, would you like have that as a product. (Kiener, 2014) 
The use of scenario building within FOM Technologies is primarily focused on needs related to 
usability. The importance of usability can be portrayed as a general user need within FOM 
Technologies, since the machines need to be operated by scientist and not technicians. Normally 
technicians operates machines, however, FOM Technologies, want the actual user to be able to 
operate the machine. 
The second phase where we design towards what we think is the optimal way for 
user to run the machine, it have to very user-friendly. (Kiener, 2014)  
FOM Technologies uses scenario building to imitate users in their “natural” surroundings to insure 
machine meet the users usability needs.  
Due to each sold machine is specially developed for each user, makes it difficult for FOM 
Technologies to determine a set of general user needs. Therefore, FOM technologies must use a 
combination of Scenario Building and Immersion to imitate each individual needs for usability, 
together with user feedback / testing and ranking to generate and rank user needs. When comparing 
FOM Technologies choice of method to the evaluation of methods by SME criteria, both ranking and 
Property checklists score high, however since these methods are not suited for the generation of new 
needs, it would therefore be recommendable to supplement ranking and property checklsting with 
Concept mapping. By using Concept mapping as a supplement to elicit user needs, FOM 
Technologies have the possibility to access the user wishes for desired functions. Since the user 
segment is limited, the time consumption aspect by use of the method is irrelevant. Since Scenario 
Building, user feedback and user testing is the bottom half of the overall evaluation of methods by 
SME criteria, it would be recommendable for FOM Technologies to utilize Knowledge Recovery, 
since they have existing product already on the market, thereby having the possibility of reusing 
knowledge and experience from previous products.

6 Discussion 
During this report, relevant methods for the elicitation of user needs by SMEs in emerging markets 
for within engineering design have been reviewed and evaluated. Two case companies have been 
used in order to compare the theoretical aspect of this report to two real life cases. Throughout this 
report, it has become clear, that there has been little studies done on how SMEs adapt their product 
development to growing markets (Li et al., n.d.). Most studies that have been conducted have 
primarily focused on MNCs13 (Accenture, n.d.; Deloitte Touche Tohmatsu, n.d.; “Developing 
winning products for emerging markets | McKinsey & Company,” n.d., “Product Development In 
and For Emerging Markets - Accenture,” n.d.).  
Throughout this report the evaluation for methods for elicitation of user needs have been done on a 
general basis, this is due to the difficulties of specifying criteria to generic suite all emerging markets 
and SMEs. In order to generate specific criteria for the evaluation of the use of methods in emerging 
markets, it would therefore be natural to either divide the emerging markets according to their 
geographic location or identify criteria for each individual market. By dividing the criteria selection 
into geographical location of emerging markets, e.g. markets based in South America or South East 
Asia, make it possible to generate criteria that are more specific. Thereby by examining the 
commonalities within the cultural and infrastructural challenges, together with examining the degree 
of access to the users. Challenges associated with splitting the emerging markets in relation to their 
geographical placement, is that the determination of criteria for each market area, that each country 
in a "market" area may have different cultures and infrastructure. It would therefore be necessary to 
study each country in order to determine the commonalities of the infrastructural and cultural 
challenges. To get the best possible conditions for evaluation of the methods would be to determine 
a set of criteria for each specific country in emerging markets, however in order to do this a 
comprehensive studies of each factor that influence the choice of method, has to firstly be discovered 
and then analysed.   
The selection of criteria to evaluate the use of methods in SMEs, as mentioned earlier, are based on 
two case companies where the criteria are selected in a general analysis of the methods they use to 
elicitate user needs. In order to generate specific criteria for the evaluation of the usability of the 
methods, it is necessary to use more SMEs as case companies. The generalization of criteria for 
evaluating of methods suited for use within SMEs is coupled with the diversity of the two case 
companies, this is due to companies that develop and sell mass produced products needs to elicit user 
needs from a larger group of users, than companies that sell specialized products. SumaCare is 
developing a product that is intended for mass production, whereas FOM Technologies develop and 
sell specialized products for the academic world. However, problems associated with using SMEs, 
especially small and micro enterprises, are they do not have the ability to free up resources such as 
time, money and staff to participat in this type of project.

It is assumed in order for smaller companies to have an interest in participating in future studies, it

Through the evaluation of methods according to the selected criteria, it has been possible to determine

bear in mind that although these methods have received the highest overall score, it is necessary also 
to look at the individual score for each criteria in both evaluations14, before selecting methods to elicit 
user needs in emerging markets. Through the comparison with case companies has made it clear that 
it is not possible to determine one method that is suitable for all companies and emerging markets. It 
is therefore important to select methods that are best suited for companies’ expertise and market 
conditions. It is therefore possible to use the evaluations of the methods as a base for a future 
development of a framework for SMEs and possibly larger companies, which can be used to 
determine which methods, are best suited to elicit user needs. Through examining FOM Technologies 
case, it has been made evident there is a need to formalize the process of elicitating user needs. 
We do not use any specific method of gathering design requirements (Kiener, 
2014) 
By formalizing the process of elicitating user needs it is possible for FOM Technologies to optimize 
their development process. An optimization of the development process can ultimately result with an 
increase in turnover. In order to formalize the process of elicitating user needs, it requires a deeper 
analysis of FOM Technologies elicitation process. SumaCare uses familiar, but time-consuming

been possible to do in the course of this study, this is due there has been a tendency for restraintment 
among both case companies and other companies that have been asked about possible participation 
in this project. 
6.1 Future research  
For further research, it is suggested that more case companies are involved, to better be able to specify 
criteria for the evalaution of methods for elicitation of user needs. Inclusion of several case companies 
thus make it possible to make a statistical study of the methods they use, and what the cause of this 
is. It would also be useful to explore the possibilities and usefulness, of developing a framework for 
determining method for elicitation of user needs in emerging markets.

7 Conclusion 
This report has examined what methods can be used by SMEs to obtain user needs in emerging 
markets. This is done through a review of methods and an evaluation of these. In order to determine 
the usability, each method has been evaluated in three phases. In the first phase, methods have been 
evaluated according to the criteria for the use of methods within SMEs. In the second phase, the 
methods have been evaluated according to the criteria for use of methods in emerging markets. In the 
third and final phase, the methods have been compared in order to determine the usability of methods 
within SMEs and in emerging markets. The evaluation has shown that the methods that have 
performed best in the overall evaluation are those that are relatively inexpensive and do not require 
special expertise in order to conduct them. Furthermore, it has been made clear that it is necessary to 
consider not only the overall score but also the individual score. This means it is necessary to assess 
all the factors that influence the choice of method. 
In order to compare the theoretical aspect of the report with reality, the evaluated methods are 
compared with two case companies. In the comparison with the two case companies and evaluation 
of methods it has been found that, it has not been possible to determine "a" generic method for 
obtainment of user needs in emerging markets. It however, has, proved it was possible to use the 
evaluations of the methods to produce suggestions, for how the two companies can optimize the 
process of eliciting user needs, together with suggestion for possible methods for use in emerging 
markets. It isfound that SumaCare will benefit from using Experience Eampling Method and the Lead 
User Technique in their current market for elicitation of user needs. When SumaCare are to elicit user 
needs in emerging markets it is proposed to use a combination of the Lead User Technique, Property 
checklists, Immersion and Scenario Building. It is also proposed that FOM Technologies uses 
Knowledge Recovery, Concept mapping, user feedback / testing, and combination of Scenario 
Building and Immersion for the elicitation of user needs. For elicitation of user needs in emerging 
markets it is considered appropriate for FOM Technologies to use a formalized approach that consist 
of Scenario Building, Ranking and Property Checklist with the supplement of Concept mapping. 
Through the comparison of the two case studies, SumaCare and FOM Technologies, it has been made 
evident. There is a need to involve multiple companies in order to move the evaluation of methods 
from the general level to a more specific level.
