Abstract 
As the 3PL sector grows, the complexity for a company to manage 3PL along with other logistics 
operations also grows, leading to the outsourcing of the supply chain management to 4PL. To exploit 
the multiple benefits of cooperation among companies, 4PL can promote collaborations between 
different members of a supply chain. In the present thesis, the formation of collaborations and cost 
savings allocation was investigated from the 4PL perspective, for a 3-echelon 3-member per echelon 
supply chain.  
The study is divided in two parts. The first part has two subsections a) the creation of a simulation and 
optimization model to identify what collaboration types should be formed and between which supply 
chain members in order to minimize the total supply chain cost, picking among information, inventory, 
and transportation sharing, and b) the distribution of the generated cost saving among the supply 
chain members. The second part targeted the numerical example and demonstration of the developed 
tools. Two different algorithms were used for the collaboration’s formation, one allowed the 
participation of a supply chain member to only one collaboration while the other to multiple.  
The investigation showed that the most preferred type of collaboration formed in both cases, is 
inventory sharing. Moreover, it was found that the algorithm which allowed multiple collaborations 
per supply chain member led to the formation of 8 more collaborations on average and therefore to 
lower supply chain costs. Additionally, it was found that half of the first formed collaborations are 
responsible for more than 75% of the total reduction of the supply chain costs on average, for both 
cases.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
Supply chain is, by definition, a network between a firm and its suppliers for the production and 
distribution of a product to the final buyer. That network comprises of various and most commonly 
different activities, human and other types of resources (such as material), entities and information 
[1]. For managing its’ supply chain, a company will often use third parties in order to outsource parts 
of the distribution, packaging and warehousing, called third-party logistics (TPL or 3PL). The 
preference towards outsourcing led the 3PL global market to reach a value of 1.027 trillion USD in 
2019 and is expected to reach 1.7 trillion USD by 2027 [2]. However, the difficulty of one company 
managing all the collaborating 3PL, led to the creation of fourth-party logistics (4PL) which is the 
outsourcing of the management of all the 3PL activities to another party [3].  
It is often the case that companies who belong in the same supply chain/industry, collaborate in order 
to gain competitive advantage and offer higher value to their customers, or reduce their own costs. 
The collaboration of two or more autonomous firms involved in the same or relevant sectors, and who 
work together to plan and carry out various supply chain operations, can create notable benefits for 
the participants [4]. Supply chain collaboration has been extensively studied along with its’ benefits. 
The drivers and importance for collaboration in supply chain management are discussed by the OECD 
- International Transport Forum (ITF) [5], concluding that it is imperative for the members of a supply 
chain to collaborate among each other since that is a vital part for the improvement of their 
performance and competitiveness.  
The benefits and advantages born from the collaboration of companies in the supply chain are 
reported to be numerous. Myers reports in the Supply Chain Management Review [6] that 
collaboration can increase the total value provided to the customers and therefore gain higher market 
share. Long-term collaboration among buyers and suppliers can heavily reduce the costs and promote 
the generation of innovative ideas and high added value products. Moreover, it is mentioned that long 
term collaborations increase the chances of one company to picking up their partners joint venture 
collaborations.  
The advantages list is extended by taking into consideration that apart from cost benefits, supply chain 
collaboration reduces supply chain risk, improves customer satisfaction, increases supply chain 
flexibility [7], promotes fast decision making and reduces inventory and stock out levels [8]. All the 
aforementioned benefits had led several companies to collaborate, and as it was surveyed and 
reported from McKinsey, more than 80% of the companies participated in at least one collaboration 
to up to 10 collaboration initiatives [9].  
Two different types for supply chain collaboration have been identified so far, i.e. vertical and 
horizontal [10]. Vertical collaboration represents the relationship between companies which belong 
at different levels within the same supply chain [11], focusing on the synchronization of strategic, 
tactical and operational plans [10]. An example is the relation that exists between a manufacturer and 
a central distributor, or a distributor and a retailer.  
Horizontal collaboration is the relationship between competing or non-competing companies which 
belong within the same supply chain level [11]. For example, manufacturers who produce parts meant 
to be assembled into one item, or distributors responsible for distributing that item. Horizontal 
collaboration enables collective resource utilization and joint product innovation [12].

Horizontal and vertical collaboration can be implemented through different forms, the most common 
of which are:  
1) Information sharing. Information sharing can take place between different supply chain levels 
and can be either full or partial. The most common type is sharing the actual or expected 
demand to the upstream level (i.e. a retailer giving the expected demand to a manufacturer 
before placing an order) 
2) Resource sharing. Resource sharing occurs more often in horizontal collaboration, and 
includes, among others, sharing of production capacity, storage capacity, fleet and residual 
demand or supply. 
3) Decision sharing. Sharing decisions is a way to coordinate planning among companies of the 
same supply chain. An example is the collaboration of a retailer and a manufacturer in order 
to jointly plan the level of production depending on the retailer’s needs. 
All the previous three forms of collaboration affect each other and are usually mingled. For example, 
in order to jointly plan for a production level, one needs to share information for the expected 
demand. The review of the literature shows that, so far, each collaboration form has been researched 
separately.  
The present thesis aims to answer the question of how collaborations should be formed among the 
members of a supply chain network, and how should the generated cost savings be distributed, from 
the perspective of a 4PL centralized management. More specifically, it is taken into consideration that 
all the members of the supply chain network have formed a coalition and the task of the 4PL is to 
identify the collaboration types that should be formed and between which members. 
The study analyzes the effect of simultaneous application of different collaboration forms on the total 
cost of a supply chain network. The target is to develop a tool that identifies the most promising 
collaboration forms, the supply chain members that should form them, and construct the final supply 
chain configuration. The collaboration types considered are information sharing, resource sharing and 
decision sharing. The criteria for the identification of the optimal formation of collaborations is the 
minimization of total supply chain cost and maximization of supply chain cost savings. This is carried 
out through the simulation of a supply chain with multiple echelons and multiple members per 
echelon, to identify the interaction between the members depending on the existing collaborations 
and collaboration types. Additionally, the current thesis investigates the fair allocation of the 
generated savings among the supply chain members with an allocation method following the rules of 
transferable utility game theory.  
The structure of the thesis is described below. 
In chapter 2 (Literature Review), an extensive literature survey is given. The survey is focused on the 
various collaboration modes in a supply chain as well as the coalition formation and benefit 
distribution theories. The chapter closes underlining the novelty of the thesis based on the prior 
literature survey. 
The extensive description of the supply chain simulation model and the collaboration formation 
algorithms is provided in chapter 3 (Simulation and optimization model). First, the supply chain 
structure is presented along with the general simulation model structure which describes the 
processes of the model and components such as parameters and variables. It is followed by the 
interaction model of the supply chain members, mathematically describing the way each supply chain

Chapter 2. Literature Review 
The literature review is split into two parts. In the first part, the analysis of the different supply chain 
collaboration modes is carried out, targeting the collaboration types the present thesis will investigate. 
The focus is not only on the cases that are similar with the present thesis, but rather to analyze

review analyzes the coalition formation and benefit allocation models following a similar manner as 
the first part. Based on the literature review, the novel aspects of the present thesis are pointed out 
and further analyzed.  
 
 Supply chain collaboration 
In the following sections, information sharing, resource sharing and decision sharing are extensively 
analyzed, as they are the three types of collaboration this thesis investigates. Although the present 
thesis does not study the liabilities of a supply chain collaboration scheme, it is worth mentioning that 
despite the multiple benefits not all collaborations are viable or unaffected by various factors or risks. 
Some of the factors are commitment, trust, level of collaboration, interpersonal relationships while 
risks such as disruption in the collaborative supply chain are existent [13, 14].  
 
2.1.1 Information sharing 
Information sharing is one of the collaboration methods that have been extensively investigated, in 
the form of complete or partial exchange of information. In the following paragraphs an analysis is 
presented regarding the various aspects on the investigation of information sharing in supply chain.  
Research has been conducted in order to investigate the impact of information sharing in reducing 
the bullwhip effect. The bullwhip effect is a phenomenon observed in a supply chain when small 
fluctuations can often be amplified and result in exaggerated variations while going through the supply 
chain. Jeong and Hong [15] simulated a four -single agent- echelon supply chain, where the 
information shared was the customer demand forecast. The experiments run considering different 
rates of information shared (e.g. revealing half of the actual demand) and showed that the higher the 
information rate (i.e closer to 1) that is shared the lower the bullwhip effect is, and that the impact on 
bullwhip effect is different depending on the echelon it takes place. Jeong and Hong also observed 
that it is important to balance the ratio of information shared between each echelon because it may 
positively contribute to the bullwhip effect. 
Dominguez et al [16] studied the performance improvement of a four-echelon supply chain through 
information sharing of four heterogeneous retailers. The heterogeneity of the retailers occurs from 
the different operational factors, which are demand variance, forecasting period, average lead time 
and inventory policy. It was found that when the retailers share the same operational factors, they 
present equal potential value in the case of information sharing. On the opposite, when retailers are 
heterogeneous, the potential value from information sharing is different. The researchers found out 
that the performance is significantly affected by the number of retailers sharing information and by 
their configuration, and that high potential value retailers greatly contribute in achieving the majority 
of full information sharing benefits when they are partially sharing information

Information sharing has been analyzed in respect with cost sharing contracts and the trust that is 
embedded with them, by Han and Dong [17]. The investigation of a two-echelon supply chain was 
carried out, where soft-orders were placed by the retailer with the capacity to be cancelled before 
shipping, and trust (positive vs negative experiences) was the underlying factor which affected the 
supplier’s production. It was found that there is a threshold of negotiation power for each of the 
partners of the supply chain. That led to the conclusion that whoever exceeds that limit, faces a decline 
in the expected profit.  
The value of sharing disaggregated information in supply chains has also been studied by Kovtun et al 
[18]. The researchers studied a two-echelon supply chain represented by two consumer populations 
giving orders to a retailer. It was found that the optimal way for a retailer to place the order to its 
supplier is by calculating separately the order level for each customer segment, compared to the 
summation of all the customers demand. That way the value of information sharing from the retailer 
to the supplier is optimal. 
Kim et al [19] have studied how information sharing combined with innovativeness and strategic 
sourcing from the point of supplier, improved supply chain agility. The authors carried out a survey on 
over 270 suppliers in manufacturing industry and showed a positive correlation between the 
innovativeness of a supplier and information sharing, which in turn affects the supply chain agility by 
helping its members to react fast to market changes. It was also found that information sharing also 
promotes collaboration and joint planning. 
Information sharing also affects horizontal logistics collaboration as this was studied by Yuan et al [20]. 
The supply chain under consideration, had the necessity of multiple daily trips of low truck utilization 
in order to achieve a good service level. While analyzing the horizontal collaboration, the researchers 
have shown that a high frequency of information sharing can decrease the total travel distance and 
increase utilization level and supply chain performance. Furthermore, it was found that suppliers who 
had more orders would gain more from high frequency information sharing. 
In order to cover the case when information cannot be shared, several studies have been carried out. 
In a study, Ali et al. [21] analyzed a method where the downstream demand could be inferred when 
the situation did not allow for information sharing due to any number of constraints. It was mentioned 
that the proposed method was experimented on real data from a supermarket in central Europe. They 
concluded that supply chains are most effective when there is information sharing, no matter what 
kind of method is used for forecasting.  
Lai et al. [22] have investigated how information sharing influences the investment in maritime supply 
chain combined with risk behavior. In their study, the authors have analyzed a two-period model 
including a port and carrier in an uncertain environment, with variable risk behavior of the carrier in 
terms of making an investment to increase sustainability. It was found if the port shared the 
information of forecasted demand with the carrier, that led to increased profits for both parties and 
to the realization of the sustainability investment even in the scenario of the carrier being risk averse.  
The effect of information sharing on investment has been also studied in a different context. Raza [23] 
has investigated the effect of sharing partial demand and corporate social responsibility on the 
coordination of supply chain from a manufacturer perspective. The responsibility is manifested 
through an investment which impacts the market demand and the selling price of the retailers. It was 
demonstrated that the reduction of the effects from partial information of demand which is affected

by the corporate responsibility and the retailer’s prices, was possible through the use of a distribution-
free approach. 
Teunter et al [24] have studied the effect of the adaption speed of the retailers and the demand 
processes on the value generated by information sharing. It was supported by the authors that a slow 
adaptation speed is a more common reaction from the part of the retailer in actual situations. Based 
on that, it was found that when the retailer does not react fast to the demand change and the demand 
itself is random, then the information value can be negative.  
In a study carried out by Bian et al [25], two two-level competing supply chains were analyzed, in 
which there was partial information sharing on demand. The researchers found that the more 
competitive the supply chain is, the higher the expected value gained from information sharing. On 
top of that, it was found that if in one of the two competing supply chains there is information sharing 
while in the second there is not, the second’s expected value will also increase as a consequence of 
information sharing in the first one.  
Moreover, researchers have investigated different strategies by which partial information sharing 
could be implemented in non-homogeneous retailers. Dominguez et al. [26] studied the impact of the 
different partial information sharing strategies on the performance of four-level supply chain with four 
retailers. The authors reported that the strategy followed had significant impact on supply chain 
performance, with a difference between the best and worst strategy in performance that may reach 
27% for bullwhip effect and 7.5% for average inventory.  
In regard with managing the bullwhip effect, the role of information type (i.e. demand or supply line 
information) to be shared has been studied. Ojha et al [27] analyzed a multiple level supply chain. The 
researchers identified that in the cases where no kind of information is shared, the performance 
tradeoff is high in order to have high order fulfillment and low bullwhip effect. In addition, it was 
observed that the type of information leading to optimal order fulfillment also increased the negative 
contribution of bullwhip effect, leading to the conclusion that in cases of partial information sharing a 
company has to be careful as to what type of information is used for inventory level calculation.  
Furthermore, the effect that information sharing has on profit increase and inventory reduction of 
multi-supplier two-level supply chain has been investigated by Huang et al [28]. Additional to the 
calculation of demand, promotion activities had been taken in consideration regarding their influence 
on the formation of demand faced by the retailers. The researchers found that information sharing 
has greater impact on reducing the costs and inventory level when the demand faced by the retailers 
in consecutive periods has higher correlation coefficient. It was also proposed, that large-scale 
retailers, namely large supermarket companies, could benefit the most from information sharing with 
their multiple suppliers in such a manner.  
 
2.1.2 Resource sharing 
Horizontal resource sharing is another form of collaboration that has been extensively studied. Forms 
of resource sharing include, but are not limited to, the collaboration of shipping companies, sharing 
production capacity of manufacturers, raw material and finished products, and financial resources. 
Below, the literature survey on resource sharing is given, providing details on all the aforementioned 
aspects.  
==PAGE:14==
Chapter 2. Literature Review 
7 
 
In respect to resource sharing, Adenso-Diaz et al [29] have analyzed the collaboration and resource 
sharing of logistics companies in cross-supply chain cooperation. As mentioned from the authors, such 
a cooperation can reduce empty returns of trucks if the truck capacity is better utilized by carrying the 
collaborators items. The study used a metaheuristic in order to analyze the performance of the model 
and to calculate the cost reduction. It was found that depending on the case analyzed, the cost 
reduction would reach up to 40%. It was observed that the cost savings depended heavily on the 
discounts between the carriers for linking deliveries and on the geographical correlation of the 
deliveries. The greater the two parameters, the higher the interest in the cooperation. It was also 
found that as the number of the collaborating companies increased, the added savings increased with 
a decreasing rate. 
Another study performed by Cuervo et al [30], analyzed through a simulation model, the profit created 
from the collaboration of shipping companies in relation to the characteristics of the collaborating 
partners and the type of the operations each one focuses on. The researchers simulated shipping 
companies with different characteristics and an analysis on their complementarity was carried out. It 
was found that the characteristic which affects the most the coalition profits, is the average order size. 
This occurred because in large orders, there are plenty of trucks with unused capacity, which created 
the opportunity to collaborate with companies carrying small orders. Moreover, it was observed that 
the coalition is also impacted by the number of orders a company transports. The higher that number, 
the higher the potential of increasing coalition profits. The third and final characteristic which affects 
the coalitions profit, is the capacity to delay the delivery of the orders. That was found to provide the 
capacity to further fill the trucks of a stand-alone company with items from the coalition partner. 
The collaborative shipping by using the method of co-loading (resource sharing through utilizing the 
same vehicle to transport shipments), was studied by Tinoco et al [31], in terms of inventory sharing 
and cost and gains distribution. The target of the researchers was to determine the reduction of the 
costs and CO2 emissions through the reduction of the needed transports. They analyzed various 
methods in the cost and benefits redistribution including no redistribution or even sharing all the 
possible occurring costs due to the collaboration. The authors found that the agreement upon which 
costs are shared has the greatest effect on the stability and long-term viability of any collaboration. 
Moreover, the mechanism for distribution of costs and benefits plays a major role; a focus on the re-
distribution may not lead to a stable condition in which the participating companies gain from the 
collaboration. 
Yao et al. [32] focused on the developing economy of China and the resource-based supply chain by 
considering CO2 constraints in function of revenue distribution, in the context of green development. 
The authors used game theory to study the model of a centralized decision making, and decentralized 
decision-making equilibrium with three distinct market structures. It was found that the centralized 
decision making maximized the total benefits of the supply chain. It was observed that the effect of 
the customer preference on CO2 reduction, was positively correlated with the final resource prices. 
Additionally, the researchers found that by lowering the permitted emission limits, the expected 
supply chain profits initially present an increase and afterwards a decrease.  
Resource sharing and more specifically financial resource sharing was studied as risk management 
measure in supply chain disruption, by Cockx et al [33]. The researchers combine the Osotua-based 
resource sharing model in a model to propagate bankruptcy and showed that by sharing financial 
resources, the network’s overall robustness increased considerably. It was observed that a firm can 
==PAGE:15==
Chapter 2. Literature Review 
8 
 
be endangered in the financial viability by the avalanche of disruption in the supply chain even if that 
occurs at a firm on the same echelon. Additionally, the research reinforced the principle that resource 
sharing can successfully tackle issues created by disruptions in the supply chain. 
A study for resource sharing was carried out by Ma et al [34] in light of inter-organizational supply 
chain for the garment industry. The elaborated work focused on identifying whether sharing resources 
(and specifically capacity sharing) during the production phase of garment supply chain was feasible 
and specifying the most suitable type of garment to be considered for resource sharing. The authors 
used discrete-event simulation to compare 8 scenarios. The results showed that resource sharing 
positively contributed on the total yield and total profits. There were cases in which resource sharing 
had insignificant contribution on the production, but as rule it was shown that garment manufacturers 
could get great benefits by applying a resource sharing model.  
Resource sharing in garment supply chain was also studied by Wang et al. [35] with the use of dynamic 
simulation of an evolutionary game. The model analyzed the strategy of OEM manufacturers and 
garment brand on sharing production capacity and other hard resources and using shared production 
platforms. The results presented that the costs for entering a sharing platform are unbearable for 
small scale firms and they chose not to participate. On the other hand, medium and large OEM 
manufacturers and garment brands choose to participate in sharing resources. Moreover, the strategy 
of each supply chain member to gain stability was found to be positively correlated with the 
informationization level of the firm and negatively with the risk cost.  
Chen et al [36] studied inter-supply chain competition with a focus on parallel supply chains 
competition under constrained resources and disruption in the demand. The authors propose a model 
to study competing supply chain whose supply network is heterogenous, which is composed from 
inner and outer supply chain firms. The internal firms shared resources while the external did not.  The 
proposed model had two steps; in the first step the optimal strategy for allocating resources and the 
output of production was developed for each of the competing supply chains, while formulating the 
Cournot-Nash equilibrium as an inequality problem. In the second step, a demand disruption was 
introduced in the model and the supply chain members were required to maximize the revenues and 
minimize the costs for change. The model worked by assuming the information of the cost from other 
supply chains was available and demonstrated its positive results. 
Im et al. [37] conveyed a study based on data collected from almost 240 customers, in order to 
investigate the establishment of a long-term successful collaboration with a target to increase 
collaboration benefits. The researchers developed a model which explained how the alignment of 
governance mechanisms (incentives and goals) influenced knowledge sharing and how it affected the 
relationship benefits. The model was validated by the data collected and suggested that aligning 
ambidexterous governance and resource sharing mechanisms led to the generation of benefits from 
supply chain collaborations.  
Resource sharing and demand forecasting were studied as to their impact on developing new products 
in supply chain, especially in a volatile market. The authors Jha et al. [38] proposed a model which 
analyzed two companies with symbiotic capabilities (a technology development and a product 
development company) to a collaborative process for developing a new product. The analysis carried 
out had different scenarios with varying resources shared, technology type and level and forecast

sharing. The expected values and profits were calculated and the researchers supported that this 
model could be extended to multiple different supply chains.   
 
2.1.3 Decision sharing 
The third form of collaboration to be analyzed regards decision sharing, which is the combined effort 
of firms belonging to the same supply chain to take decisions that involve and affect all the supply 
chain members. 
As part of decision sharing, Sarkar et al. [39] proposed a model and studied the cooperative advertising 
of firms as a policy which could be followed from the perspective of centralized management in a 
three-level supply chain. The target was to analyze the boost in monetary gains for retailers, 
manufacturers and suppliers, when these were subject to variable demand. The demand was decided 
to be affected by the costs for advertisement and product pricing while the costs were modeled to 
follow the fuzzy set theory. By optimizing the model, it was observed that the results presented a 
growth in the profits through the supply chain. It was also observed that advertising affected the 
results depending on whether it was considered on a global or local scale. All in all, the authors 
concluded that the proposed model provided the necessary information so the decision makers would 
choose the appropriate values, prices and costs for the revenue maximization of the supply chain. 
Guler et al. [40] studied a decentralized and non-cooperative supply chain, analyzing the joint 
replenishment of multiple companies which utilize an Economic Order Quantity model. The target was 
to develop a mechanism which would specify the frequency of joint replenishment on the basis of the 
individual replenishment frequencies of the supply chain members. The researchers considered zero 
information sharing among the members of the supply chain and therefore information such as 
demand and inventory holding costs were unknown to other members, but can be observed. It was 
found that there was not a straightforward mechanism which can at the same time optimize 
efficiency, combine the rationale and budget of each individual company and compromise each 
member incentives. The researchers proposed a two-parameter mechanism with the common 
frequency for replenishment and the cost allocation to be the two parameters. It was proved that the 
proposed mechanism fared better than existing mechanisms in the literature in terms of efficiency 
and fairness. 
Decision making in collaborations is considered crucial for planning and was studied for sustainable 
supply chains. Allaoui et al. [10] proposed a novel framework to facilitate decision taking for 
collaborations within the perspective of planning sustainable supply chain, with the use of Information 
and communication technology platform. The focus was on the agricultural food supply chain where 
it was found in practice by the use of the proposed framework that opportunities for the reduction of 
the supply chain operation arose from cooperation and the implementation of multiple modes. It was 
also observed that actual case data would be a challenge to be collected and supported in their 
entirety from the proposed system. An additional constraint arose from the difference in sustainability 
approach from each country and therefore from different supply chain members around the globe.  
Mutual decisions have been under investigation regarding the effect on the supply chain co-ordination 
by Taghipour et al [41]. The authors studied the synchronization of a supply chain in terms of resource 
utilization in a dynamic environment by proposing a dynamic tool based on a programming approach 
of the most recent periods (rolling horizon). The supply chain was composed of two partners which

connected with exchange of material and non-strategic information. The researchers claim that their 
proposed model led to a successful strategy for both partners by improving the revenue sharing 
fairness when the results were compared to centralized supply chain planning.  
Marketing and operational decisions were studied by Avinadav [42] for a two-level supply chain which 
considers an economic order quantity inventory management model. The supply chain was composed 
of a manufacturer and a retailer which are bound by a contract for sharing revenues and deal with 
products of short-term expiration date. The author analyzed the performance of each party as well as 
of the whole supply chain by considering different decision takers in decision making regarding an 
investment in sales and cycle length. It was observed that when the decisions for cycle length are 
taken by one party then the conditions for the second party to increase its monetary gains are very 
specific. Moreover, it was found that if the party for investing in sales is the manufacturer then there 
are many cases where both supply chain members have gains. 
Supply chain coordination was explored and achieved by Shafiq et al [43] for a two echelon supply 
chain through the proposal of a model based on commitment and sharing revenues and including 
penalty. The supply chain was composed of a single retailer and manufacturer and the given demand 
was stochastic. The manufacturer was considered to need to procure capacity, and since the 
considered lead times were high, that procurement had to be done well before it was actually needed. 
Such an action is investment intensive and the manufacturer is the only one bearing the risk. The 
proposed model included a securing level of demand from the retailer with the inclusion of a penalty 
with flexible resolution in the case that demand was not achieved. It was shown that the retailer is led 
to make higher commitments compared to a typical contract with revenue sharing and a priori 
commitment. Moreover, it was observed that with the proposed coordination model can coordinate 
a supply chain with a product changing rapidly.  
Song et al. [44] investigated a three-level supply chain focusing on fresh and green products in terms 
of decision and coordination by considering the social, financial and environmental operation. The 
demand was considered to be two channel and dependable on the product quality. The authors 
explored four models, including decentralized and centralized supply chain management as well as 
contract-based management. The authors concluded that a decentralized management case was 
worse than a centralized one in terms of the performance in social, economic and environmental 
aspects. Moreover, it was found that the consumers preference towards a product with a better 
quality drove positively the overall supply chain performance.  
Decision knowledge sharing for the creation of seamless supply chains was studied by Saxena et al 
[45]. It was supported from the authors that the knowledge regarding which decision would have the 
highest impact in the financial performance of a flexible supply chain. Their work was based on 
simulating a model which applied a knowledge management system on seamless supply chain based 
on partial and full decision knowledge sharing and comparing the results with information sharing. 
The results showed that decision knowledge sharing (full or partial) played a more important role in 
the flexibility than information sharing. It was also observed that information sharing presented higher 
revenues at low levels of flexibility, while as the flexibility increased decision knowledge sharing was 
found to be more profitable.

 Coalition formation and benefit distribution 
2.2.1 Coalition formation and forms of collaboration 
The study of how coalitions should be formed and maintained in order to increase individual and 
whole supply chain benefits is closely connected with rules for benefit allocation. In the following 
literature analysis, the focus will be more on research carried out on coalition formation and stability. 
Coalition formation for the collaboration of logistics companies in forest transportation and spare 
parts transportation has been investigated by Guajardo et al. [47] by the use of operations research 
models. The authors targeted to find out which of the collaborations were qualified for formation and 
carried out the analysis by formulating a mixed integer model taking into consideration core stability 
and strong equilibrium. The collaboration which would minimize the total costs among the supply 
chain members would be the most promising one for formation. It was found that there were multiple 
coalitions which led to lower costs, but the best solution was to group supply chain members into 
multiple sub-coalitions instead of the grand coalition. Their model proved that the optimal cost of the 
strong equilibrium and the core stability was equal and therefore the solution to one is a solution to 
the other. 
Guajardo et al [48] have also studied the formation of overlapping coalitions for collaborations 
between transportation companies for forest biomass. Their approach was more general than the 
original approach where a company can participate in only one coalition. The authors adopted two 
approaches for deciding on the coalition formation, the first was the identification of the most proper 
coalitions per territory of delivery and the second incorporated the coalition formation within the 
transportation problem. Both approaches were modeled with the help of mixed integer linear 
programming models. It was found that the solutions extracted by the second approach provided 3% 
better savings compared to the first approach, and that the achieved savings of total 8.1% were 
comparable to the grand coalition (8.3% reduction).  
Tremewan et al [49] studied the dynamics of multilateral coalition formation in continuous time. The 
members of the supply chain, in sets of 3, would bargain regarding the allocation of profits at fixed 
intervals of time which would be paid only if two or more members agreed upon that. The authors 
explored the stability and frequency of the agreements along with the transition from one agreement 
to another. It was found that over 60% of the cases revolved around the splitting of benefits among 
two supply chain members while the third one remained without any gains. It was also observed that 
the coalition which proved to be the most stable one was the one which used an equal split among 
the 3 members. Furthermore, the researchers found that the transitions from one coalition to another 
were predominantly guided by short – term maximization of benefits which is considered myopic, and 
that they did not follow a Markovian chain.

Regarding the optimality of coalition formation and the distribution of the benefits, it was argued by 
Keyzer et al [50] that these were interconnected. It is mentioned that in a coalition formation game 
which is ruled by fuzzy theory, the supply chain members use a non-binary approach by varying the 
intensity of their participation. The optimum of such a game determines the optimal coalition as well 
as the distribution of profit among the coalition members. Moreover, the researchers observed that 
such an approach provided a framework which among others represented the core, the least core and 
the nucleolus theories. It was shown that the proposed formulation acted as an extension to existing 
production economies models. 
Fairness concerns in cooperative and non-cooperative game theory have been studied from Zheng et 
al [51] in a three-level supply chain composed of a retailer, a distribution center and a manufacturer 
with returns. The retailer declares concerns regarding the fairness in profit distribution. The study 
found that by taking into consideration the concerns expressed, the decentralized management model 
and the partial coalition model perform worse that the centralized one in respect to the allocation of 
gains generated. The authors proposed three mechanisms for the coordination under centralized 
management, using the Shapley value, the nucleolus and the equal satisfaction for benefit 
distribution, while incorporating the retailers concerns on fairness. The research concluded that such 
a model provided a solution which satisfied the rationality of each member separately and all 
members together and made the grand coalition to be stable. Moreover, it was found that each one 
of the mechanisms presented different advantages for each of the supply chain members. The 
distributor gained the highest profits under the Shapley value mechanism, the manufacturer had the 
highest profits under the nucleolus mechanisms, while the final mechanism provided equal 
satisfaction for each supply chain member. 
Horizontal coalition formation for cooperative replenishment of multiple companies from one supplier 
was investigated by Jouida et al. [52] under the assumption of existing vertical collaboration with 
contracts. The authors based their research on the de facto supply chain cost reduction from the 
formation of horizontal coalition and focused on the coalition subsets optimization for formation and 
creation of stable coalition. A methodology based on game theory was proposed called cooperative 
replenishment algorithm, which generated stable collaborations for reducing costs. Through this 
methodology, the authors confirmed that the formation of horizontal coalition between the 
companies to be supplied increased the benefits compared to no coalition formation. 
Horizontal collaboration was also studied by Mohebbi et al [53], focusing on the supply chain level of 
suppliers and utilizing the coalitional game theory. The authors developed a model in which the cost 
affects the coalition formation and constraints the size of the coalitions as well as the space of possible 
collaborations. The profit distribution followed a criterion based on the capacity shared from each 
supplier, and that criterion was used to allow each supplier to identify autonomously the best 
collaboration. The results were compared to the solutions occurring from Shapley value and 
proportional fairness and showed that a long-term collaboration resulted in an increased profit for 
each supplier in the supply chain.  
Coalition formation in assembly systems was investigated by Li et al [54], in terms of providing 
incentives for improving the product quality. The authors explored a system where a downstream 
manufacturing assembly is connected to multiple upstream suppliers complementary to each other. 
It was assumed that the demand was deterministic and was affected by the product quality and 
pricing. The model also allowed the suppliers to form coalition in order to coordinate their wholesale 
==PAGE:20==

pricing as well as their decision to improve quality. Three benefit allocation rules were analyzed for 
the suppliers, equal allocation and proportional allocation, and Shapley value. It was found that the 
first two allocation rules always led to stable grand coalition. However, the study showed that the 
coalition is stable based on Shapley value under the condition that the suppliers product qualities had 
low deviation.  
 
2.2.2 Distribution of benefits in coalition 
As presented in the previous section, there were various methodologies for coalition formation each 
one being affected differently by the methodology the benefits were distributed. As presented, the 
most commonly used tools include the Shapley value, the nucleolus, the equal and proportional 
method and the Core. In the following paragraphs, an analysis of different benefit allocation 
methodologies based on economic theories is carried out.  
The stability of coalition was studied for retailers which exchange inventory from Sosic [55], under a 
myopic and farsighted approach regarding the formation of collaborations. The model presents a 
stochastic demand which relies upon existing inventory in order to be covered. The remaining 
inventory was being transshipped to other retailers to cover any leftover demand. The analysis of the 
profit allocation showed that retailers which where only concerned about their immediate payoffs 
would often form subcoalitions and not form a grand coalition. On the other hand, the author proved 
that retailers who were concerned about the long-term benefits (farsighted) maximized their profit 
allocations by not defecting from the grand coalition.  
The case where the benefits generated were dominated by uncertainty was studied by Gao et al [56]. 
The authors, based on Shapley value, proposed a new method for profit allocation (uncertain Shapley 
value) which they also proved it was unique for a coalition formation game. This was also 
demonstrated through solving a benefit allocation problem in a coalition.  
Hernet et al. [57] investigated the fair sharing of the generated benefits during the shaping of a supply 
chain network. In the authors work, it was taken into consideration that the members of the optimal 
coalition share assets and resources, with the target to maximize the benefits. The target was to 
explore whether the benefits generated in a supply chain could be fairly distributed between 
participants while at the same time maintaining a stable coalition. It was found that a purely 
competitive mechanism for profit allocation could not handle the fair distribution of them.  
Profit distribution and two-echelon supply chain coordination based on altruistic principal was 
investigated from Gu et al. [58] while there is asymmetric information. The supply chain composed of 
a manufacturer and a retailer with production cost and selling cost respectively. A model was created 
with altruistic principal for the coordination of supply chain and an allocation rule for the created 
benefits. It was observed that the coordination was cost dependent as well as it was dependent on 
risk rates of both members, while the lower these two parameters were the better the supply chain 
was coordinated. Moreover, it was found that the cost of production, sales and price had positive 
correlation with the distortion of the quantities traded, while the market environment had negative. 
Tatarczak [59] also studied the profit allocation in supply chain coalitions of logistics companies, with 
the use of cooperative game theory and the development of a new tool based on ordering, holding 
and transportation costs. A one-commodity supply chain was considered with multiple retailers and 
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 2. Literature Review 
14 
 
one supplier. The author mentioned the use of the most common of the methodologies for benefits 
allocation of equal allocation, Shapley value, nucleolus and Gately point and the comparison of the 
proposed tool with them. The target was to produce guidelines for firms who collaborate in order to 
ensure the continuation of the coalition. It was found that the proposed method is efficient in terms 
of computational power needs and can also be applied in real life cases.  
Wang et al [60], studied a one-period supply chain network which operated under wholesale contract 
which deprived a system from coordination. The authors investigated a Pareto-equilibrium solution 
based on the power dependence theory for two separate cases. The first one represented a two-
member monopoly and the second one a supply chain with two retailers and a supplier. This study 
showed that the two cases had a unique power-balanced Pareto equilibrium which achieved 
coordination. 
Incomplete information was reported by Liu et al. [61] to negate the effectiveness of the most 
common profit allocation tools such as Shapley value and Banzhaf value. For that reason, the authors 
worked on proposing various mechanisms for profit allocation of logistics companies’ coalition when 
there is incomplete information. The proposed mechanisms depended on the individual gains as well 
as the contribution to the collaboration. Moreover, a variation of the mechanism included parameters 
such as the supply chain member’s efficiency, symmetry and anonymity as weights to separate the 
importance of each logistics firm. 
Transfer pricing and exchange rate was investigated by Huang et al [62] as mechanism for benefit 
distribution in an international supply network, focusing on global manufacturers. A mixed integer 
linear programming model was developed. The authors observed that transfer pricing, which is the 
price shared between within the subsidiaries of a global manufacturer, affected the overall benefits 
as well as the benefit allocation between subsidiaries. On top of that, it was found that exchange rate 
when considered as a parameter of production planning, affected the overall benefit generation and 
allocation in the global supply chain. 
Transfer prices for fair benefits allocation was also investigated by Liu et al [63] in supply chains with 
multiple levels. The authors proposed a mixed integer linear programming model to optimize the 
production of an active ingredient and its distribution. Regarding the fair profit allocation, three 
criteria were taken into consideration – proportional, minimum and maximum. It was found that the 
approach of total profit maximization gave worst results in fair profit allocation criteria than the 
approaches of Nash bargaining and lexicographic maximum. It is also supported that for large 
instances, the proposed mechanism on hierarchical approach provided adequately good results in fair 
profit distribution. 
 
 Novelty of thesis 
The work of the present thesis expands the existing literature on supply chain collaboration, inventory 
management models, and the methodologies for collaboration formation and distribution of 
collaboration deriving cost savings. 
With regard to supply chain collaborations, the literature so far focuses on the investigation of one 
type of collaboration applied on a supply chain at a time. The current thesis expands this approach by 
simultaneously applying multiple types of collaboration on a supply chain. More specifically, the 
==PAGE:22==
Chapter 2. Literature Review 
15 
 
collaborations taken into consideration include partial information sharing, resource sharing and 
decision sharing. The supply chain structure used to study the above-mentioned collaboration types 
has five-echelons and three-members per echelon, a structure quite more complex than what is 
typically met in the literature. However, the collaborations are formed for the three intermediate 
echelons.  
Another novelty point of the current thesis targets the collaboration formation and cost savings 
distribution within the supply chain. The most common methodologies which were analyzed in the 
literature survey may be too complex to handle large systems or take a lot of time to be calculated. 
An example is that Shapley value will have to examine approximately 24! (6.2∙1023) formations of 
collaborations in order to identify the most promising one for the proposed structure. For that reason, 
the thesis proposes a novel and fast method for collaboration formation and cost savings distribution 
in large systems, which can identify the most viable collaborations to be formed and distribute the 
cost savings among supply chain members with fairness. 
On top of main novel aspects, the current thesis examines the combination of periodic review as 
inventory management model and keeping backlog. To our knowledge has not been implemented 
before.

 Supply chain simulation model description 
The constructed supply chain simulation model, follows the structure given in the following figure 
(Figure 1) and it can be classified as a deterministic dynamic discrete event system.  
 
Figure 1: Supply chain structure to be modeled

The system is deterministic because its behavior can be precisely predicted every time the model runs 
with specific parameters and configuration, it is dynamic because the model variables change over 
time, and it is discrete because the model variables change at specific time points. In the present 
thesis, the model variables change every time period, where period can represent any time range i.e. 
day/week/month/year. 
The system is composed of 24 members (agents). More specifically 3 customer groups, 3 retailers, 3 
warehouses, 3 production plants, 3 suppliers and 9 transportation companies. It is considered that 
each material flow (transportation) is carried out by transportation companies which are modelled as 
a separate agent for each flow, and they have no interaction with the rest of the supply chain members 
other than transporting the orders. Moreover, it is considered that all transportation is done by trucks 
and no other means (e.g. aviation or ships). An exception is between retailers and the group of 
customers because it is assumed that the retailers do not ship to their customers but have a physical 
point for giving the order, leading to a total of 9 agents. For the material flow it is noted that a reverse 
supply chain was not included as part of the model, therefore no returns are taken in consideration.  
The customer base for each retailer was modelled as a group and therefore as one agent, because the 
model is a discrete-event simulation model and therefore the total customer demand is taken into 
consideration in the beginning of a period, regardless of how this demand will be distributed within 
this period. 
The warehouse functions as an intermediate between the manufacturer and the retailer, but it can be 
considered as autonomous part of the manufacturer. First, because in real world a manufacturer may 
have multiple warehouses in different locations than the production plant which can act 
independently in terms of managing the local demand. Second, by creating a separate agent for the 
warehouse, it can cover the situation where it may act as 3rd party distribution center.  
As far as the interaction of the agents is concerned, as is natural, orders are placed upstream while 
material flows downstream i.e. finished products, raw material. 
The model incorporates three different forms of collaboration; partial information sharing (providing 
details on the demand) from here on mentioned as information sharing, resource sharing, and 
decision sharing. Information sharing takes place from retailer to warehouse and from warehouse to 
production plant. Moreover, it includes resource sharing in six different cases: 
1) Finished products between retailers 
2) Finished products between warehouses  
3) Raw material between production plants 
4) Transportation between suppliers and production plants  
5) Transportation between production plants and warehouses 
6) Transportation between warehouses and retailers 
Decision sharing is reflected through the whole model, as it takes place in the form of the centralized 
management to decide which collaborations will be formed and how will the cost savings be 
distributed between the members of the supply chain.

3.1.1 Complete model  
The model is constructed around 4 main procedures which are built in a way that each procedure 
nests all the following ones (i.e. procedures 2, 3 and 4 are nested in procedure 1, procedure 2 embeds 
procedures 3 and 4, and procedure 3 nests procedure 4). Each one of the procedures has its own 
target, has specific tasks, may require different input from the rest of the procedures, and is designed 
in order to bear the required outputs. The first procedure (Procedure 1), fulfills the purpose to 
construct the configuration and output the final configuration (the collaborations which will be 
formed) along with the average cost of each supply chain member, as well as the whole supply chain. 
To perform that, it runs procedure 2 (which identifies the next collaboration to be formed) until there 
is no room to further reduce the total supply chain cost.  
Procedure 2, targets to extend the existing configuration by identifying the most suitable collaboration 
to be formed among all possible candidates. The most suitable is the one that will reduce the total 
supply chain costs the most. Procedure 2 therefore checks all possible candidates by calling procedure 
3, which is responsible to provide the average total cost of the so far constructed configuration (given 
in Procedure 1) with the addition of the collaboration which is being checked from procedure 2.  
To be able to produce the required output procedure 3, in turn, calls procedure 4 for a number of 
replications, feeding it different timeseries each time in order to acquire the average of the results 
from procedure 4. The role of procedure 4 is to simulate the interaction of the supply chain members 
over a number of periods and calculate the total costs of the supply chain and each member. The high-
level diagram of the 4 procedures is given in Figure 2. 
 
 
Figure 2: High level diagram of the procedures comprising the model

Procedure 3 to extract the average for all possible candidate collaborations dictated by Procedure 2,

calculation (seventh and eighth steps) and operates with the costs acquired from the supply chain. 
During this step, all possible collaborations are simulated one by one, and in the end of the algorithm 
(step 9) the most appropriate collaboration is chosen to be formed. That collaboration is fixed, and 
the collaboration formation algorithm runs again. More details on the collaboration formation

The seventh step is to simulate the supply chain network a certain number of replications in order to 
get representative results. This step is one of the most important ones, as the simulation data are 
created, based on the interaction of the supply chain members and therefore it is more extensively 
analyzed in section 3.2 Supply chain members interaction model. During this step and for the 
simulation purposes, the use of a warmup period is employed. The warmup period serves the purpose 
of tackling the difficulty created by the need to appointing appropriate initial values and contributes 
to stabilize the supply chain model.  
The eighth step follows the end of the simulation and involves the calculation of the supply chain 
costs. The costs that were taken into consideration are the following 
1. Inventory holding costs which depend on the number of items kept in inventory. These costs 
include among other labor costs, storage space and insurance. 
2. Fixed costs for placing orders, which include among other cost for the inspection of goods, 
and labor costs for processing the order. 
3. Fixed production costs for periods that there is production. This includes labor costs, setup 
costs, energy consumption for the facility. 
4. Variable production costs depending on the number of items produced, includes among other 
the materials used, the energy consumption for the machinery, maintenance and 
consumables. 
5. Shortage (backlog) costs for the number of items that cannot be filled in the order. This cost 
derives not only from missing a sale, but it also gets more salient the higher the shortage 
becomes since shortage may lead to the loss of a client. The backlog costs do not apply to the 
transportation companies as it is assumed that they have adequate fleet to cover the 
transportation in every time period 
6. Fixed shipping costs for the periods when there is transportation of items. These costs include 
among other personnel costs, truck license and insurance costs. 
7. Variable shipping costs depending on the amount of trucks used (with the capacity of different 
calculations based on the collaboration mode). The variable shipping costs depend on the 
distance between the start point and destination.  
8. Fixed and variable shipping costs for the cases of horizontal collaboration of resource sharing 
that involve the transportation of items from one supply chain member to another. These 
costs include the points 6 and 7. 
Step seven and eight are represented by the processes included in the green box in Figure 3. 
The first seven cost categories were chosen because they are also commonly used in the literature, 
while the 8th is a cost introduced in order to calculate the impact of a collaboration on the total value 
of a coalition [64, 65].  
These costs are combined in order to calculate the costs for each supply chain member. A further 
extensive analysis on the calculation of these costs is provided in section 3.3 Cost calculation. 
The ninth step relates to the sixth, since it represents the part of collaboration formation algorithm 
which checks which of the tested collaborations will be qualified to form. More details on the 
collaboration formation algorithm are given in section 3.4 Collaboration formation algorithm 
The tenth and final step is the extraction of the chosen results to a comma-separated values (csv) file, 
for further processing.

 Supply chain members interaction model 
The members interaction model is an iterative process which lasts as many periods as the demand set 
by the customer groups, as this is described in section 3.1.1 Complete model. During this iterative 
process, the model variables are defined based on the actions of each member of the supply chain. 
The interaction starts from the level closer to the customer groups and moves upstream towards the 
suppliers. More specifically the sequence of supply chain echelons which act is first retailer, second 
warehouse, third production plant and final the supplier.  
Every supply chain member does a sequence of actions before setting an order to the upstream level 
in order to be covered. In Figure 4, the interaction flowchart diagram is given, which depicts the 
actions carried out along with the sequence.

As it was mentioned in 3.1 Supply chain simulation model description, three different forms of 
collaboration were considered. During the members interaction model, any collaboration form that is 
active will have different effects in the supply chain, and these are: 
1) Information sharing. This type of collaboration is applied to retailers, warehouse and 
production plant. Information sharing takes place from downstream to upstream. The 
downstream shares the actual demand which faces with upstream. For example, if a retailer 
chooses to share info with a warehouse, then they share the amount of orders received from 
the customer group and the forecast related information of exponential factor alpha. Then 
this information is used from the warehouse to better forecast the orders that are expected 
to be set from the retailer in future periods. The same effect works between warehouse and 
production plant [16]  
2) Resource sharing for finished products or raw material. This type of collaboration is applied to 
retailers, warehouse and production plant. The raw material is used by the production plant, 
which converts them to finished products which are then used by warehouses and retailers. 
If two or more supply chain members choose to collaborate, then after filling the orders they 
receive, they may ship products to their partner in order to cover the other members backlog. 
The shipping of products of partner 1 to partner 2 is free of charge and aims to fill the whole 
backlog, but the collaboration is encumbered with the costs of shipping from one to another. 
The transfer of material in all the cases of limited inventory happens proportionally.  
3) Resource sharing for transportation. This type of collaboration is applied to transportation 
companies. If two or more transportation companies choose to collaborate, then they choose 
to ship together the products of the supply chain members they serve (function as a 
consolidation point) and therefore reducing the total cost by utilizing the trucks more. This 
function is modeled for the transportation of raw materials from suppliers to production 
plants, for transportation of manufactured products from production plants to warehouses, 
and from warehouses to retailers. The consolidation of the items is considered to take place 
in the middle of the distance between the supply chain members that are served while 
consolidating and splitting the goods. Therefore, such a collaboration induces costs for the 
transportation towards the cross-docking facility before consolidation and from the cross-
docking facility after separation.

are provided for each echelon where necessary. What is described is valid for all periods (p) except 
where it is explicitly stated otherwise. The sequence of actions for each supply chain member is

3.2.4 Forecasting 
In order to carry out the demand forecasting for each member of the supply chain, two methodologies

applying a positive adjustment for periods with product promotion.

showed that exponential smoothing performed better in terms of mean absolute deviation of the

3.2.5 Inventory control policy 
For the inventory management a periodic review (r, OUL) model was used, following a policy with 
fixed time between two orders and variable amount of order quantity in order to stock up to a certain 
level [67]. This inventory control policy was chosen for two reasons. First, it incorporates lead time 
and safety stock, a feat which was decided to be incorporated and studied in the current thesis. The 
second reason is that due to its discrete nature, it will facilitate modelling since it will not require too 
many periods to create enough time slots in order for the simulation to imitate (or try to imitate) a 
continuous review model. 
For the calculation of the quantity to order based on the periodic review policy, the order up to level 
(OUL) and the inventory position (IP) need to be calculated. Order up to level, represents the needed 
inventory quantity to cover demand for the period until the delivery of the next order and also keep 
a certain level of safety stock. The inventory position (IP) represents the total inventory level, by taking 
into consideration any future receipt of items for the same period as OUL. The formulas analyzed 
below are based on [68].

Delivery costs 
In the model description, it was mentioned that the transportation of items from one supply member 
to another was done by separate agents which have no interaction with the other members other 
than the transportation. These agents are defined from their transportation beginning and destination 
and from the echelon member they serve. Moreover, it is considered that these agents ship products 
with trucks without taking into consideration the completeness of the trucks used, as long as the 
necessary quantity is shipped in a period. The cost occurring to these agents are separated in variable

 Collaboration formation algorithms – optimization with greedy heuristic 
In the present thesis, two different collaboration formation algorithms were used. One algorithm 
allows the formation of multiple collaborations per supply chain member (overlapping), while the 
other allows only one collaboration per supply chain member (non-overlapping). Both algorithms 
were based on an optimization heuristic targeting to minimize the total supply chain cost. The total 
supply chain cost is used as the only variable in the objective function to be minimized for two reasons. 
First, it is calculated without requiring too much information from each of the supply chain members, 
and therefore facilitating the collection of data in the use of the model in real life cases. Second, by 
minimizing the total supply chain cost, it leads to always creating cost savings for the whole supply 
chain which can afterwards be redistributed among the supply chain members. For example, if the 
total supply chain cost is reduced by 2000 monetary units, and one of the supply chain members has 
increased its costs by 1000 due to the formation of a collaboration, this means that the total generated 
cost savings seen from the rest of the supply chain are 3000 which can be redistributed. For example, 
a supply chain with 3 members has the following costs without any collaborations:  
member 1 cost = 2000, member 2 cost= 2000, member 3 cost= 2000, Total supply chain cost= 6000 
Let’s assume that after forming a collaboration, the cost of each supply chain member is: 
member 1 cost = 3000, member 2 cost= 500, member 3 cost= 500, Total supply chain cost= 4000 
This shows that for the reduction of the total supply chain costs by 2000, when the cost of member 1 
increases by 1000, that means that the costs of the other two members 2 and 3 should show a 
combined reduction of 3000. Of course, the increase of member 1 should be covered by the cost 
savings allocation which will be presented in the following section 3.5 Cost savings allocation. 
The collaboration formation algorithms focus on the sequential insertion of bilateral collaborations 
and work by running the simulation with each one of the possible collaborations separately, and then 
selecting to form the one which provides the lowest total supply chain cost. After that the algorithm 
runs again to choose the second collaboration to form, and so on. Moreover, it is considered that once 
a collaboration is formed, it does not break, no matter the number of new collaborations formed after 
it. 
The number of possible collaborations among the supply chain members is 24, representing all 
collaboration types. For the algorithm to choose the first collaboration to be formed, the comparison 
must be made between the supply chain cost of any formed collaboration with the state of no 
collaborations at all. To facilitate that comparison, a “dummy” collaboration state is taken in

3.4.1 Pseudocode for single collaboration per supply chain member (non-overlapping 
collaborations) 
The algorithm starts by setting all collaboration formation variables to 0 which is non active (i.e. they 
have not been formed). Then the first iteration for the formed collaborations starts with no active 
collaborations (for k’ loop). Within the first iteration for the supply chain structure, the iterations to 
identify the most promising collaboration to be formed (for k loop) takes place. The algorithm 
compares the case with no formed collaborations with those when each one of all the possible 
collaborations is formed. It runs the supply chain member interaction model for 25 replications (each 
for the 25 demand realizations) and 400 periods for each one of them (see sections 3.2, 4.3, 4.4). The 
average total costs are calculated for each collaboration and then the one which provides the lowest 
of the total supply chain cost is qualified to be formed, and the respective collaboration formation 
variable is set to 1.

from the first iteration as formed. Then the iteration to identify the next most promising collaboration 
starts and investigates all the collaborations which do not have any common supply chain member 
with the already formed collaboration, by running the supply chain member interaction model. The 
average total costs are calculated for each collaboration and the one which provides the lowest total

 Cost savings allocation 
The cost savings allocation takes place after the simulation model has ended, the optimal sequence 
for collaborations formation has been defined, and the total supply chain cost as well as the individual 
costs for each supply chain member have been calculated. The cost savings allocation model was 
designed as to satisfy certain properties regarding the payoff.  
==PAGE:50==
Chapter 3. Simulation and optimization model 
43 
 
The payoff distribution x = (x1, x2,…xn) ∈ ℝn follows the transferable utility game (N, v) of a set of N 
players with v the worth of the cost savings, and therefore it has the following properties: 
- 
Efficient, which means that the total payoff is equal to the total cost savings generated from 
all the members, and therefore 𝑥(𝑁) = 𝑣(𝑁) [74] 
- 
Individually rational, meaning that the payoff to the supply chain member i has to be greater 
or equal to what that member generated alone, therefore 𝑥𝑖≥𝑣( {𝑖}) for all i∈N [74] 
- 
Coalitionally rational, leading to the payoff of a collaboration S to be greater or equal to the 
cost savings of that collaboration, therefore 𝑥(𝑆) ≥𝑣(𝑆) for all formed collaborations [74] 
 
The first property covers the fact that all the generated cost savings will be allocated to supply chain 
members. The second property allows no supply chain member having higher costs when a 
collaboration is formed regardless of that members participation in it, since the payoff is from the cost 
savings and equal to them. The third property ensures that the supply chain members which did not 
participate in any collaborations are not entitled to any cost savings. Moreover, supply chain members 
who participate in a collaboration are entitled only to the cost savings created by that specific 
collaboration. 
Additional to the allocation properties, the players of the coalition are already satisfied by their 
operational costs by participating in the current supply chain and will not act irrationally. That 
abolishes scenarios of emotional behavior such as getting jealous of other members cost savings from 
forming a collaboration and searching outside the studied supply chain to form profitable 
collaborations. 
For the successful completion of any payoffs it is considered that there will be a common pool (e.g. a 
bank account) for all the supply chain members which will facilitate the cost savings redistribution and 
will be managed from the centralized management. Any money that should be taken from a supply 
chain member will be deposited in that common pool and any money that should be paid to a supply 
chain member will be withdrawn from that pool.  
After the formation of a collaboration, the total cost of the supply chain is compared to the previous 
state without that collaboration. The difference is the cost savings created from the formation of the 
specific collaboration. That cost savings is equally distributed between the supply chain members who 
participate in the collaboration, because it is a fair method since it cannot be clearly and easily 
distinguished who contributed what for the creation of the cost savings. On top of that, the created 
cost savings would not be achieved if any of the two supply chain members did not participate.  
However, the generated cost savings is the summation of the effect that a formed collaboration has 
on the rest of supply chain members and it is usually met as a cost savings in some (cost reduction 
beyond the operational costs) and as deficit (cost increase beyond the operational costs) in other. To 
balance that, the members who have reduced costs compared to their prior situation, must pay the 
difference to the pool. From the pool, first the supply chain members which have increased costs are 
paid the difference in order to keep the same operational costs as prior to any collaboration formation, 
and then the supply chain members who participate in the formed collaborations allocate the 
remaining cost savings according to the allocation rules.

 Model calculations verification 
In order to verify the correctness of the model calculations, a series of tests were carried out with 
results extracted from the Julia model and cross-compared with calculations made at Microsoft Excel. 
A timeseries of 10 elements was chosen so that it would generate results which could be manageable 
for comparison. The tests covered every aspect of calculations made, a sample of which is presented 
below: 
1) Cross-check and verify the results from the simple functions for the calculation of inventory 
position, order up to level, quantity to order and inventory level. Specifically, the inventory 
level was tested for two instances, one after receiving items from an order set to an upstream 
echelon and one after filling an order set by a downstream echelon. 
2) Verify in the results that any unfilled order ends up recorded as backlog and that this backlog 
is taken into consideration as a part of the order to be filled for the next period 
3) Compare forecast technique results and alpha optimization results, by also taking into 
consideration the initial values 
4) Examine that the orders set from one echelon are the orders received by the echelon 
upstream 
5) Manually validate the results of the exponential smoothing and that the method developed 
to identify the optimal alpha factor works properly.  
6) Verify that each supply chain does the same calculations by simulating each of the three 
supply chains with the same demand and parameters and compare the results, which should 
be the same. 
7) For the resource sharing collaboration, check that the items sent from one supply chain 
member to another are equal to either the backlog of the one or the inventory level of the 
second 
8) Verify the cost calculations for different supply chain members 
9) Test that the model will indeed choose the collaboration which reduces the total costs of the 
supply chain and that it will input that collaboration as fixed.

 Simulation model benchmarking and optimization 
The run time for 200 replications and 500 periods was measured using Julia benchmarking tools to be 
1035.69 seconds or 17 minutes and 15 seconds. A simulation run was run again for 25 replications and 
500 periods each replication and the execution time was measured to be 139.14 seconds or 2 minutes 
20 seconds  
From the analysis of the execution time, it is observed that the running time is not proportional, so 
even by reducing the number of replications by 8 times, the running time did not reduce 8 times.  
Based on that observation, it is considered that even 25 replications with 500 periods each is going to 
be time consuming. Especially in the case that 25 possible collaborations will have to be tested in order 
to calculate the total costs of the supply chain and this kind of calculation to be run 25 times in order 
to create the sequence upon which the collaborations will form. It is estimated that each simulation 
run will take over one day (approximately 24 hours and 8 minutes). This running time, along with the 
fact that there are considered a lot of possible combinations for the sensitivity analysis makes the 
analysis non-viable for the scope of the present thesis. 
Therefore, tests were carried out in order to reduce the running time and the combination of following 
three actions was found to be the most important: 
1) Reduction of the number of files that are called or accessed during the execution of the script 
2) Optimization of the collaborations formation algorithm by ending it when the supply chain 
cost is not further reduced during a collaboration insertion iteration 
3) Since Julia is a language which is compiled at runtime (just in time), all the comments and 
unnecessary functions are deleted  
 
By implementing these changes and testing the code again it was found that for the complete model 
(including all the collaboration insertion) with 5 replications and 400 periods took 1562.60 seconds or 
26 minutes. Based on that, it is considered that a run of 25 replications and 400 periods for the 
collaboration insertion algorithm testing 25 collaborations would approximately require 2,5-3 hours.

 Experimental setup 
5.1.1 Assumptions and parameters 
For carrying out the computational study, it was necessary to assume certain parameters. The 
production capacity is assumed high enough, so that the warehouse demand set to the production 
plants is always covered. This reflects the normal operation of an already existing supply chain by 
avoiding high backlog buildups, and not being affected by any unforeseen production affecting events 
such as the COVID-19. 
The orders set by the customer group or any other supply chain member cannot be cancelled. 
Therefore, when an order delivery is delayed for multiple periods, that will result in accumulating 
backlog costs. 
The cost coefficients provided, are reduced costs in the form of a ratio with inventory holding cost 
coefficient, where Inv. Hol. cfij = 1. This was utilized for two reasons. The first reason is because there 
are no available data regarding actual costs from companies belonging to the same supply chain. In 
the literature, it was apparent that each industry had its own unique supply chain cost breakdown and 
even each member within that industry had a totally different supply chain cost breakdown depending 
if it is a transportation company, a distribution center or even a manufacturer. Moreover, to our 
knowledge, data regarding the ratio used between the actual costs of different supply members of 
the same supply chain are not available or accessible in order to be used. The second reason is because 
by using different ratios for the costs, the results can be scaled up to fit to multiple different case 
studies depending on the supply chain. 
An indication regarding the cost range was based on the most recent published breakdown that could 
be found from a consulting company, showing the distribution costs breakdown for various European 
industries. It was given that the total cost of transportation ranged from 60% to 88% of the total 
distribution costs. Similarly, the inventory costs would range from 2 to 29%. [77-79]. Given that, trials 
were carried out to properly adjust the transportation costs in order to fall within the given range, and 
therefore calculate the most appropriate parameters. To validate the choice of parameters, in 
Appendix B are given the calculated transportation cost percentage compared to the total supply 
chain cost from the experiments run in this chapter.  
The expected possibility of not stocking out is 95%, while the backlog cost was chosen to be from 
comparable to the rest of the costs up to many times higher as found in the literature [69, 72]. The 
turning point for the slope increase was assumed to be equal to 50% of the customer group demand 
average so that if 50% of the demand was left unsatisfied that would lead to more severe 
consequences. The rest of the cost parameters were assumed based on approximations and 
indications in order to complete the supply chain cost structure, while aiming the variable costs to be 
comparable with other variable costs and the fixed costs to be comparable among them.  
The distance between vertical supply chain member was considered 1000, 3000 or 5000 km in order 
to represent different distances for transportation within Europe or from Europe to Asia/Africa. i.e. 
~1000 km is Paris – Madrid or Copenhagen – Munich, 3000km is Copenhagen – Athens or Nantes -
Kiev or Sofia-Cairo, 5000 km is Moscow – Lisbon or Berlin – Astana. The distance between horizontal 
==PAGE:66==

supply chain members was assumed to be 100km (e.g. approximately the distance between 
Copenhagen – Kalundborg), as they can be located in bordering cities/ regions or industrialized areas.  
The lead time was chosen in such a way to imitate a fast, an intermediate or a slow delivery of orders, 
while the review period parameters were chosen to depict a fast or slow-paced check of the inventory.

the demand they face. That led to choosing either same average and coefficient of variation (CV), 
same average and different CV or different average value and same CV. Here it is mentioned that the 
CV is equal to standard deviation divided by the mean of demand. For a constant mean, high CV means 
a high standard deviation and low CV means low standard deviation.

The demand used is stochastically generated. Each customer group has a different set of timeseries 
realization, each set composed of 25 realizations (one for each replication that the simulation will run) 
with the same characteristics, following a normal distribution. The generation was made with the 
random function of Julia which uses the Mersenne Twister library. The demand timeseries set 
remained the same for each of the scenario in order to have comparable results, unless there is a 
change in the demand characteristics such as the average units or the standard deviation. Therefore, 
four timeseries sets were generated for each customer group. The model was created so when a data 
point was generated to be negative (because it is a normal distribution), it was replaced by zero 
instead. An example of three timeseries realizations is given in Figure 18, while more examples are

5.1.2 Experimental Matrix 
For the experimental matrix, two different cases are analyzed in terms of collaborations formation. 
The first one is the formation of non-overlapping collaborations (case 1) and the second one is the 
formation of overlapping collaborations (case 2). The source of this difference is the trust between 
the supply chain members. In case 1, each supply chain member will be able to participate in only one 
collaboration while considering that they do not trust that there will not be a leak of their information 
from their collaborator to another supply chain member. Moreover, this approach could provide the 
opportunity to more supply chain members participating in a collaboration and gain cost savings, while 
averting the collaborations formed to be dominated by few supply chain members. In case 2, each 
supply chain member will be able to participate in multiple collaborations while there is absolute trust 
that their information will not be transferred to other supply chain members through their partners.  
Within a designed scenario, all respective values are the same for every supply chain member unless 
stated otherwise. For example, the cost for placing an order is 2 monetary units per order for all 
retailers, warehouses and manufacturers. Respectively, if in a scenario the distance between two 
vertical supply chain members is 3000 km, then it is assumed that all of the supply chain members 
between two consecutive echelons are 3000 km apart.  
Based on the chosen range of the parameters, the following 14 scenarios were designed to be carried 
out and further analyzed for each of the two cases (non-overlapping and overlapping collaborations) 
as given in Table 5. The parameters which are not mentioned keep the same value as given in Table 4.  
Scenario 1 was chosen as a base scenario and therefore it is composed of the average value assumed 
for each of the parameter proposed, combined with homogenous demand faced by the retailers (same 
demand mean value and standard deviation). Each other scenario differs by the change of only one 
parameter which is set either higher or lower than the assumed average/basic scenario. For example, 
scenario 2 and 3 differ from the base scenario only by the change to the distance between vertical 
supply chain members, which decreases in the scenario 2 and increases in scenario 3.  
The parameters which are chosen to be studied on their effect on the collaboration formation are the 
distance between vertical supply chain members, the backlog costs, the lead time, the homogenous 
or heterogenous nature of customer groups demand, standard deviation of demand and the review 
period. The last two scenarios are dedicated in studying the lowest and highest values respectively of 
the parameters under investigation.  
Based on the parameter which is changed, an identifying code is given for each scenario in parenthesis 
bellow the scenario numbering. The codes are representative of the parameter, accompanied with a 
+ is the parameter increases and with – if it decreases compared to the base scenario. The generated 
coding is as follows. Distance: Dist, Backlog: Bcklg, Lead Time: LeadT, Coefficient of variation: CV, 
Heterogenous Demand: HetDem, Heterogenous coefficient of variation: HetCV, Review period: 
Review, All parameters: All. From here on, all the scenarios will be identified with the scenario number 
and the coding, e.g. 1: Base, 2: Dist -, 3: Dist + and so on.

5.2.1 Total supply chain cost 
After carrying out the computational study, the total supply chain costs are analyzed, as well as the 
way these change with the formation of collaborations.  
The total supply chain cost of each scenario without any collaborations formed is given in Figure 19. 
By comparing the base scenario 1 with scenarios 2 to 9 and 12 to 14, it is observed that when 
increasing one or more parameters from the ones presented in the experimental matrix, the total 
supply chain cost increases. The same trend is also observed with the reduction of the parameter’s 
values, which result in the reduction of the total supply chain costs.  
The concurrent trend observed for scenarios 2-9 and 12 - 14 (Increase or decrease of a parameter lead 
to the increase or decrease of total costs respectively), does not apply for scenarios 10: HetDem and 
11: HetCV. Both scenarios, present higher total costs compared to the base scenario, although the 
average value of the changed parameters are the same with the base scenario. For example, for HetCV 
the average standard deviation of the demand the 3 retailers observe is the same. This fact presents 
==PAGE:71==
Chapter 5. Computational study and discussion 
64 
 
that any type of heterogeneity in the demand faced from the retailers creates the conditions to 
increase the total supply chain costs. 
 
 
Figure 19: Comparison of total supply chain costs for different scenarios without any collaboration 
 
Placing in hierarchy the parameters studied, the one which affects the most the total supply chain cost 
is the transportation distance, followed by the review period. Next would be the lead time, CV and 
therefore standard deviation (since the mean demand is same for all retailers), heterogeneity in CV 
and then in mean demand and last of all the backlog costs.  
In Figure 20 below, the percent reduction in supply chain costs with the progressing formation of 
collaborations is presented. The figure depicts the results for non-overlapping collaborations on the 
top figure and the results for overlapping collaborations on the bottom figure. The figures do not 
present the no collaborations case because no cost reduction can be observed under that condition. 
In some cases, different point markers were used in order to better distinguish the related scenarios. 
All the points included in the region indicated as 1 refer to the 1st collaboration, in the region indicated 
as 2 refer to the 2nd collaboration, and so on. It is seen that not all scenarios form the same amount of 
collaborations. The respective monetary values are given in diagrams in Appendix D.

Based on the figures above, it is observed that the total cost reduction increases with every 
collaboration added, but each scenario tends to converge to a certain value, therefore creating a 
concaving curve which mimics a diminishing returns curve. That effect is found to be more distinct in

diminishing effect on the reduction of the total supply chain costs and creation of cost savings. 
Furthermore, it is observed that comparing the same scenarios between the non-overlapping and 
overlapping case, the latter one gives higher reduction on total supply chain cost. For example, 
comparing the scenario LeadT+ for overlapping and non-overlapping case, it is clearly shown that after 
the formation of the 3rd collaboration, the total reduction provided by the overlapping case is higher. 
That is similarly observed in all other scenarios (e.g. CV+, CV- or Bcklg+). This is considered to be based 
on the effect of the constraint to form only 1 collaboration per supply chain member for two reasons. 
First, for the number of total collaborations formed, where more collaborations led to greater 
reduction of the total cost. Second, for the type of collaborations formed where the difference in cost 
reduction appears for some scenarios (e.g. LeadT+, Bcklg+, HetDem, Base) within the number of the 
formed collaborations for the non-overlapping case i.e for Base on the 6th collaboration. 
Additionally, it is shown that most of the scenarios from non-overlapping collaborations case have a 
reduction of around 2%, while for overlapping case the reduction is 3% a fact which shows a relative 
consistency in the effectiveness of the forming collaborations. 
There are scenarios where the formation of collaborations has a greater effect than others and even 
though a scenario may begin with higher cost reduction than another, it may end up with lower overall 
cost reduction after the formation of all collaborations. Such two scenarios are Bcklg+ and HetDem, 
Base and Dist+, Bcklg- and CV- for both cases. This leads to the deduction that even though the added 
collaborations have a diminishing effect, this effect does not the affect every scenario the same. 
The scenarios given in the non-overlapping collaborations case, form from 3 to 7 collaborations 
depending on the scenario, and the overlapping collaborations case forms from 10 to 16 
collaborations. It is found that the overlapping case allows the formation of way more collaborations 
due to lifting the constraint which allows a supply chain member to participate in only one 
collaboration. More specifically, it allows the formation of 6 to 10 more collaborations depending on 
the scenario, and the total amount of collaborations formed is at least doubled for each scenario.

generation.

5.2.2 Formation of collaborations  
As was found in the previous section, the overlapping collaborations case allows the formation of 
more collaborations compared to the non-overlapping collaborations. As shown in Figure 21, the non-
overlapping case allows the formation of 79 collaborations throughout the studied scenarios, while 
the overlapping collaborations case allows the formation of 191 in total which is more than 200% 
increase. However, the type of the collaborations formed does not increase proportionally. In the non-
overlapping collaborations, the transportation sharing and inventory sharing collaborations dominate 
the types of the collaborations formed by presenting 32 and 38 occurrences respectively. The 
information sharing collaborations seem that they may not be that preferable with the formation of 
9 occurrences. Here it is noted that for the non-overlapping collaborations case in each scenario 3 
information sharing (1 in each supply chain), 3 inventory sharing (1 in each echelon) and 9 
transportation sharing collaborations can be formed at maximum. This fact indicates the preference 
of forming inventory sharing collaborations over information sharing.  
In the case of overlapping collaborations, the type which dominates the types of the formed 
collaborations is inventory sharing which covers 61% (116 occurrences) of the formed collaborations. 
That is followed by information sharing which presents a great increase reaching 22% while the 
transportation sharing falls to 17%. The observed redistribution is attributed to the constraint of 1 
collaboration per supply chain member. Therefore, if for example Warehouse 1 formed an information 
sharing collaboration with Retailer 1, that would constrain the formation of any other collaborations 
with Warehouse 1 or Retailer 1 which is 5 in total. However, that does not happen with transportation 
companies.

It is observed that in the cases of information sharing and inventory sharing, the collaborations which 
affect the retailer and warehouse echelon, are preferable than the ones in echelons upstream. For 
information sharing in both cases of non-overlapping and overlapping collaborations, there is a strong 
preference for forming collaborations between the Retaileri – Warehousei than Warehousei - 
Production planti. Specifically, in non-overlapping case, there are no collaborations at all formed for 
information sharing between Warehousei and Production planti, while in the overlapping case it is 
around 10 times more preferable to form a collaboration between Retailer-Warehouse than 
Warehouse-Production plant. That happens because forming a collaboration downstream has effects 
upstream, so the closer to the customer group it is the highest the impact. Information sharing 
between Retailer 1 and Warehouse 1 will also affect the orders set from the Production plant 1. On 
the contrary, information sharing between Warehouse 1 and Production plant 1, will not regulate the 
orders set for or from Retailer 1 but it can contribute to the reduction of possible backlog created.

Sharing inventory in the echelons of warehouse and retailers appears to be preferable than production 
plants, because it contributes more to the reduction of the total supply chain cost. That happens 
because sharing inventory reduces backlog and that affects the orders in two ways. First, by reducing 
backlog, the level of the order set from downstream to upstream is lower (since backlog is not taken 
into consideration for the OUL). Second, it provides the necessary number of items so a supply chain 
member can better fulfill the orders set from downstream. When the order set is closer to the 
==PAGE:77==

forecasted average, it does not create high fluctuation to the forecast of an upstream member, and 
when used for the calculation of the inventory position in high lead time periods it does not greatly 
affect the order quantity. Due to the aforementioned facts, inventory sharing contributes in the 
reduction of the bullwhip effect. In the case of non-overlapping collaborations, it is observed that 
collaboration between Retailer 1 – Retailer 2 does not appear while the rest appear numerous times. 
This is apt to the realizations of the timeseries and the constraint of 1 collaboration per supply chain 
member.  
Regarding transportation sharing, it is observed that collaborations between supply chain 1 and 2, 
dominate in both the non-overlapping and the overlapping case. There are two factors which affect 
this phenomenon. A factor is the number of products transferred through supply chain 1 and 2, and 
the complementarity between their timeseries realization. Another factor that affects that behavior 
is the better coordination between supply chain 3 from the insertion of inventory or information 
sharing collaborations, and therefore the number of items transported is less and consequently the 
cost savings margin to be gained is also less. 
It is noted that the trend regarding the preference of the general type of the collaborations and the 
echelon in which they are formed would remain the same, but the distribution between the different 
collaborations within an echelon (e.g. collaborations InvR1R2, InvR1R3, InvR2R3) might change in the 
case there was a different set of 25 timeseries realizations than the ones currently used.

warehouses and 3 of retailers while none on the production plants. That leads to the conclusion that 
in general the inventory sharing benefits mentioned previously for Figure 22 are more intensely

sharing in both non-overlapping and overlapping collaborations case. This shows that the high review

smooth the operation of the whole supply chain. Respectively for scenarios LeadT+ and CV+, 
information sharing appears 3 times in the first six collaborations formed for the overlapping 
collaborations, while in the non-overlapping case it appears only once because it is lower in the 
sequence for formation and its creation is constrained by the 1 collaboration per member rule.  
Moreover, it is found that in scenarios where the transportation distance is at the minimum value 
(Scenario Dist- and All-), no transportation collaborations are formed. This happens because after a 
certain ratio between the distances between vertical and horizontal supply chain members, the 
additional costs created do not surpass the cost savings and therefore such collaborations are not

profitable. In scenario Dist+, Bcklg-, LeadT- and CV- it is observed that transportation collaborations 
appear several times within the first 5 created and generally are among the first to be formed 
compared to the base scenario or scenarios Bcklg+, LeadT+, CV+ which have the higher values of the 
parameters changed in scenarios Bcklg-, LeadT-, CV-. It is observed that in scenarios Dist+, Bcklg-, 
LeadT-, CV- either the transportation distance is high or one of the lead time, standard deviation and 
backlog costs are low. Therefore, it can be assumed that in the cases where the supply chain 
parameters affecting inventory are low (i.e. lead time, backlog cost, standard deviation, review 
period), the transportation collaborations present higher cost savings margin than most inventory and 
information sharing collaborations. Likewise, when the conditions promote the formation of 
information/inventory sharing, the total amount of items to be transported is reduced and that makes 
transportation collaborations less preferable to be formed early in the sequence.  
In general, since the formation of a collaboration is affected by the previously formed ones and also 
affects the ones to follow, it is difficult near impossible to know in advance the exact sequence of the 
collaborations that will be formed. However, from the analysis carried above, the summary of the 
mechanisms that each collaboration type affects the supply chain costs and operation are given.  
Information sharing reduces the bullwhip effect and the fluctuation of the orders set. That not only 
affects inventory holding costs but also impacts the costs generated by transportation companies 
since there are fewer items to be transported.  
Inventory sharing also contributes to the reduction of the bullwhip effect as found previously in the 
same section. With the reduction of bullwhip effect, inventory holding, and transportation costs are 
also affected. But, on top of that, there are also savings from the exchange of items. For the supply 
chain member who sends products there are reduced inventory costs (since they hold fewer items) 
and for the supply chain member who receives there are reduced backlog costs. Moreover, sharing 
inventory on an echelon, also helps reduce the backlog that will be created in an echelon downstream 
because inventory sharing increases the availability of items to be shipped downstream. Inventory 
sharing collaborations are affected by the transportation distance between the horizontal supply 
chain members. If that distance is too great, there may be cases where the cost savings are lower. 
Transportation sharing does not directly affect the formation of other types of collaborations other 
than setting a target of low supply chain costs to be even more reduced. It is found that there is a 
point in the ratio of the distance between horizontal supply chain members to the distance of the 
vertical supply chain members, over which no transportation sharing collaboration is viable. That is 
because the added costs for transporting items between horizontal supply chain members is greater 
than the savings from filling one truck less which would go all the distance.

information will leak through their collaboration partners towards other supply members whom their 
partners collaborate with. That is represented as the difference in the lowest supply chain cost 
achieved between overlapping and non-overlapping collaborations algorithms. 
In Table 8, the cost of trust value is presented for every scenario both in monetary units and in

chain costs (without any collaborations). It is found that the highest % reduction would be achieved 
for Scenario LeadT+ reaching up to 2.07%, while the highest monetary reduction is presented for 
scenario All+ with a reduction of around 4.000 monetary units.  
Observing the values, it can be considered that when a supply chain member is in direct collaboration 
and exchange of data, this reduction is small if compared to the losses that might occur if the 
information passed along are not under careful control. However, this cost savings might be easier 
attenable if a 4PL party was involved managing the supply chain, the formed collaborations and the

5.2.5 Non interrupted model – alternative algorithm end condition  
The collaboration formation algorithm used is based on a greedy heuristic which stops inserting new 
collaborations when the total supply chain cost increases. With the slight alteration of the proposed 
algorithm, the rule to stop the insertion of new collaborations is lifted and further explored.  
The new algorithm is studied for overlapping collaborations because it is more flexible regarding the 
formation of new collaborations. The same parameters for scenario 1: Base are used, while in this case 
to save computational time, 1 replication and <5% error to the total supply chain cost are acceptable. 
In total 20 sets of timeseries realizations were tested (of 1 replication each). 
An interesting behavior was observed in some cases, similar to the one presented in Figure 25. While 
adding collaborations, the algorithm meets a local minimum total supply chain cost with the insertion 
of the 8th collaboration (noted with yellow label) and normally it would terminate. However, allowing 
the algorithm to continue initially increased the total supply chain costs but ultimately provided a case 
with an even lower total supply chain cost compared to the previous local minimum. That occurred 
with the insertion of the14th collaboration (noted with green label). 
 
==PAGE:84==
Chapter 5. Computational study and discussion 
77 
 
 
Figure 25: Demonstration of alternative end condition for collaboration formation algorithm 
 
This specific behavior of a reoccurring lower local minimum was observed to occur in 6 sets of 
timeseries out of the 20 tested. Although this phenomenon is observed, it cannot be proposed as an 
improvement to the current algorithm for a number of reasons explained below, but it can be used as 
an indicator that the optimization can be further studied.  
A reason is that this phenomenon may not occur often (25% of cases) and it will provide a slightly 
better solution, but by greatly increasing the model solving time in order to test all possible 
collaborations. Moreover, the tests were carried out with an error of around 5%, therefore that 
behavior has not been validated for a <1% error in order to be comparable to the scenarios tested in 
the present thesis. A test was carried out with 25 replications (for error <1%) which did not present 
that behavior under the altered end condition, but this can be entirely up to the 25 set of timeseries 
realizations.  
From the 4PL perspective, the changed optimization algorithm can be tested under the same 
assumptions and in case this phenomenon remains, keep the better solution. The cost savings 
allocation would then change and affect only the collaborations formed after finding the minimum 
the first time. It will actually redistribute only the cost savings created from that point and on. A 
complication which would occurs is the treatment of the collaborations which encumber the total 
supply chain costs, both during a transitional period and the final state.

 Summary of main findings 
Following, the highlights from the thesis findings are provided, and their implications or 
generalizations are discussed for the application in a real-life case. 
 
- 
Each additional collaboration has a diminishing effect on the reduction of the total supply 
chain costs 
This finding implies that in a case where a 4PL would charge management fees to each formed 
collaboration, there might not be worth to form all the proposed collaborations even though the total 
supply chain costs are reduced. To that end, two different aspects should be taken into consideration. 
First, the respective management costs should be assessed in comparison with the generated cost 
savings. Second, a further analysis and assessment should be carried out for the benefits which are 
not accounted for any of the collaboration forms. This analysis can be both quantitative and 
qualitative. For example, the creation of knowledge and innovation or the extension of one members 
current network through the collaboration has both monetary and operational benefits.  
- 
Inventory sharing appeared to be the most important of the collaboration forms  
Inventory sharing tends to dominate the type of collaborations formed since it provides numerous 
benefits. The timely recording of any backlog or extra inventory and the shipping to another member 
implies the need of a proper organization and tracking of inventory which may be facilitated from 
Industry 4.0 methodologies especially for more complex cases such as warehouses.  
Apart from the benefits, there may be suggested complications during its realization in an actual 
supply chain. As it is reported, for retailers inventory sharing may create a schism between the 
collaborating supply chain members as it can overcomplicate taxes, returns or even the used 
accounting system [80]. On top of that, it does not facilitate the reverse supply chain of the used 
commodities. One can understand that such problems can also arise between any other echelon such 
as warehouses and production plants. Therefore, it is obvious that although this collaboration type is 
the most preferable one, it may create problems in the supply chain when simultaneously applied in 
==PAGE:91==
Chapter 5. Computational study and discussion 
84 
 
many collaborations. These kind of complications can be also generalized to any other resource 
sharing collaboration type as for example transportation sharing.  
- 
Parameters which increase the bullwhip effect or affect the forecasting of demand, such as 
review period, lead time and coefficient of variability, provide the opportunity for greater cost 
reduction. 
A generalization of this finding occurs when there are events that may cause disruption to the supply 
chain increasing one of these parameters. Such is the case during the COVID-19 pandemic as reported 
by several consulting companies such as Delloite[81], McKinsey[82], PWC [83] and reputable 
newspapers [84]. In that case, supply chain coalitions which can use all of the studied forms of 
collaborations, can more actively respond to such events. Therefore, instead of resulting in an increase 
of the total supply chain costs due to the drawback, the coalition manages to keep the total costs at a 
lower level due to the higher reduction potential. 
- 
A supply chain member participating in a collaboration does not always receive money at the 
end of the calculation of the equilibrium payoff. Nevertheless, all members participate in a 
transaction. 
This finding leads to the implication that when applied in actual life, it will create a complex system of 
transactions which will have to be repeated every certain number of periods, from all supply chain 
members and towards all supply chain members. Moreover, it is based on the timely action and ethical 
behavior on the part of the participating companies in order to not withhold any money when asked 
to make a transfer. On top of that, is also requires the provision from the side of the companies to 
have that money available every certain period and not frozen or committed. These facts strengthen 
the necessity for a 4PL to coordinate the actions between the collaborating companies, and to carry 
out tasks that are time consuming for the supply chain members.

Chapter 6. Conclusions 
Nowadays, it is often the case that companies within the same supply chain cooperate to create 
benefits and be more competitive. With the growth of the supply chain and 3PL sector, a lot of 
research has been carried out regarding the types of collaborations to be formed among members of 
a supply chain and the allocation of created benefits. However, the existing studies primarily focus on 
studying the use of one type of collaboration in a supply chain and for limited supply chain networks. 
The present thesis investigates, with the use of a simulation and optimization model, the simultaneous 
application of multiple collaboration types (information, inventory, and transportation sharing) and 
the cost savings allocation from the perspective of a 4PL, for a 3-echelon 3-member per echelon supply 
chain. For the collaboration formation two cases were considered: a) each supply chain member 
participates in only one collaboration and b) no limit on the number of collaborations a supply chain 
member can take part in.  
It is found that increasing or decreasing the distance between vertical supply chain members, the 
backlog costs, the lead time, the coefficient of variation (CV) of the demand, and the review period 
increases or decreases the total supply chain costs respectively. Moreover, it is shown that 
heterogeneity to the demand faced by the retailers, either in the mean value or the CV, increases the 
total supply chain costs compared to homogeneity. 
Ranking the parameters from the one that affects the supply chain costs the most to least, the 
sequence is: transportation distance, review period, lead time, standard deviation, heterogeneity in 
standard deviation and then in mean demand and last the backlog costs. 
With the formation of collaborations, the supply chain costs are reduced. But with each collaboration 
that is formed, the effect on the total cost reduction is diminishing. The comparison of non-
overlapping and overlapping collaborations case for the same scenarios, showed that the latter one 
allowed the formation of 6-10 more collaborations and led to the greater reduction in the total supply 
chain cost. It is also noted that the non-overlapping collaborations case is more efficient than the 
overlapping collaborations case in terms of total supply chain cost reduction per collaboration formed, 
since the latter almost doubles the collaborations formed to achieve up to 3% greater reduction in the 
total supply chain costs. 
From the analysis of the generated cost savings for both cases, it is found that increasing the value of 
parameters which inherently lead to higher fluctuation in the orders set or in the forecasted demand 
from the supply chain members, consequently lead to higher margin for cost reduction by the 
formation of a collaboration which restricts that effect. These parameters were found to be the review 
period, lead time and CV.  
Regarding the collaboration types formed throughout all the scenarios, the non-overlapping 
collaborations case formed 79 collaborations which were dominated almost equally from inventory 
and transportation sharing with 48% and 41% respectively. The overlapping collaborations case led to 
the formation of 191 collaborations which were dominated by inventory sharing of 61%.  
Sharing inventory in the echelons of warehouse and retailers appears to be more preferable than 
production plants, because it contributes more to the reduction of the total supply chain cost. 
Similarly, sharing information is found to appear more frequently between retailers-warehouses 
==PAGE:93==
Chapter 6. Conclusions 
86 
 
instead of warehouses-production plants. The transportation sharing is found to promote the 
collaboration for items transportation to the members of supply chain 1 and 2, between all echelons. 
Regarding the effect of the collaboration’s type on the supply chain the following were found: 
- 
Information sharing reduces the bullwhip effect affecting inventory holding costs and 
transportation costs 
- 
Inventory sharing reduces bullwhip effect affecting inventory holding costs and transportation 
costs. Moreover, it contributes to creating savings from reducing backlog and inventory 
holding costs on the same echelon. Additionally, it reduces the backlog in downstream 
echelons by ensuring the necessary quantity of items.  
- 
Transportation sharing affects the formation of other collaborations by reducing the total 
supply chain costs, therefore narrowing down the possible candidates.  
The investigation on the contribution of each formed collaboration on the total reduction of supply 
chain costs indicated that in the majority of the scenarios, the first half collaborations formed are 
responsible for more than 70% of the generated cost savings. More specifically, for the non-
overlapping collaborations case, the formation of the first 3 collaborations comprised in average 75% 
of the costs savings. Likewise, for the overlapping collaborations case, the formation of the first 6 
collaborations, was on average, responsible for 80% of the overall cost reduction.  
Analyzing the cost of trust, it is found that the highest added value from overlapping collaborations 
case, improves the result of non- overlapping collaborations case by 2.17%, while the average is 0.95%.  
As far as the profit allocation model is concerned, the participation of a supply chain member in a 
collaboration leads to the reduction of that members total costs, but it does not necessarily lead to 
getting additional money during the balancing payoff. 
 
 Future work 
Based on the current thesis findings, as well as the authors aspirations, the future work can take 
multiple dimensions. The following ideas relate to the current thesis and are meant as a follow up 
which can have an impact. Some of the ideas refer to the possibilities of the simulation model and 
some to the collaboration’s formation and cost savings allocation algorithms.  
The comparison of the results from the current collaborations formation algorithm to the results of a 
non-linear mixed integer optimization problem can be a follow up, in order to benchmark the current 
solution, compare it with the optimal and identify the level of the occurring difference. That action 
can also include the study of collaboration algorithms with different variables in the objective function, 
such as the increase in the costs of supply chain members.  
Additionally, when choosing which collaboration is qualified to be formed, there may be cases where 
there are multiple ones, therefore picking to form the first one in ascending order. This can be further 
improved by investigating all possible candidates using a branching method (study all possible 
collaborations in turn (all branches)) and pick the one which provides the lowest total supply chain 
cost in the end. This will increase the complexity of the algorithm and the total execution time. 
==PAGE:94==
Chapter 6. Conclusions 
87 
 
Another possible variation is to study different cost savings allocation methods and compare them 
based on existing models such as the Nucleolus or the Shapley value. The target will be to study 
different scenarios which depend on rational thinking but also extend that to non-rational including 
the stochastic reaction of a supply chain member to the formation of a collaboration (i.e. vetoing the 
formation). 
Moreover, more variables which were found to affect greatly the supply chain costs could be modeled 
with stochasticity such as the lead time or the review period, accompanied with demand with higher 
standard deviation which may also follow different distributions other than normal. These changes 
which refer to the simulation model can be combined with a different and more detailed 
transportation model.
