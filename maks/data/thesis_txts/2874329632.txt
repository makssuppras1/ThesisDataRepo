Abstract
Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) impairs locomotor function and significantly reduces qual-
ity of life. While neuromodulation and rehabilitation offer promising strategies for
promoting recovery, quantitative tools to track motor impairments and guide thera-
peutic interventions remain limited. In this thesis, I developed a semi-supervised
Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) framework to extract low-dimensional
representations of mouse locomotor behaviour from high-dimensional kinematic
data, aiming to classify motor impairments and support adaptive close-loop stimu-
lation strategies in SCI models.
Training on both labelled and unlabelled data, the CVAE organized locomotor
patterns along a continuous severity axis. Clustering of the latent space revealed
five previously unlabelled intermediate subgroups, which were validated through
statistical comparisons of kinematic features, capturing biomechanically meaningful
differences such as side-specific impairments and trunk adaptations. A secondary
classifier trained on kinematic features achieved 97.4% accuracy and a macro F1
score of 0.97, enabling fast and robust severity classification.
The results highlight how latent behavioural modelling can support real-time
classification of motor impairment and inform adaptive neuromodulation strategies.
While developed for SCI, the approach is adaptable to other neurological conditions
where locomotor behaviour serves as a key indicator of functional status, providing
a foundation for data-driven neurorehabilitation in both preclinical and future
clinical applications.

Introduction
1.1 Background
Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) is a devastating condition that results in partial or complete
loss of sensory and motor function below the lesion site. SCI often leads to lifelong
disability and significantly reduced quality of life[1]. Globally, more than 15 million
people are estimated to live with SCI, with the majority of cases caused by traumatic
events such as traffic accidents, falls, or violence[2]. Although acute medical care
has improved, long-term neurological recovery remains limited[1], highlighting
the need for more effective and personalized therapeutic strategies.
Preclinical animal models are essential to understanding the pathophysiology of
SCI and testing potential treatments. Mice are widely used in this context due to
their genetic tractability, low cost, and the availability of transgenic lines that allow
for targeted manipulation of neural circuits[3–5]. In these models, standardized
behavioural scoring systems are used to assess motor recovery. The Basso, Beattie,
and Bresnahan (BBB) locomotor rating scale is commonly applied in rats[6], and
the Basso Mouse Scale (BMS) is the standard for mice[7]. These ordinal scales
assign scores based on observed hindlimb function and have been instrumental in
evaluating various interventions, including neuroprotective agents[8], stem cell
therapies[9], and neuromodulatory strategies[10].
1.2 Toward Objective and High-Resolution
Measures of Motor Impairment
Despite the widespread use of BMS and BBB, the scoring systems suffer from
several limitations. These observer-dependent scales are inherently subjective,
prone to inter-rater variability, and often lack the resolution required to detect
1
==PAGE:9==
subtle yet meaningful improvements or deteriorations in motor function[11, 12].
Such limitations become particularly problematic in studies aiming to investigate
recovery mechanisms or evaluate the efficacy of novel therapeutic strategies for
SCI.
To address these shortcomings, the field has increasingly turned to quantitative
motion analysis tools, such as marker-based tracking systems and, more recently,
markerless pose estimation software like DeepLabCut[13] and SLEAP[14]. These
approaches allow for detailed, non-invasive tracking of multiple body parts during
free movement. Several studies have shown that these features provide more
sensitive and reproducible measurements of locomotor recovery than traditional
scoring systems[15, 16].
Building on these advances, the Bellardita Laboratory developed KineMatrix,
a custom software tool designed to bridge the gap between pose tracking and
high-resolution behavioural phenotyping. KineMatrix processes pose-estimation
outputs and automatically extracts around 3,000 kinematic features per limb,
capturing spatial, temporal, and interlimb coordination metrics. These include
joint angle dynamics, stride and swing variability, trunk displacement, and gait
phase relationships. Unlike traditional tools that may focus on a narrow set of gait
metrics, KineMatrix enables a comprehensive analysis of locomotor behaviour. This
high-dimensional approach enhances the detection of nuanced motor impairments
and supports the development of objective, behavioural biomarkers. Additionally,
its compatibility with dimensionality reduction and machine learning pipelines
makes it a valuable tool for real-time monitoring, classification of injury severity,
and integration into closed-loop neurorehabilitation systems.
1.3 The Role of Machine Learning in
Adaptive Rehabilitation
The increasing availability of high-dimensional behavioural data has led to a
paradigm shift in how motor function is assessed following SCI. Traditional obser-
vational scoring systems, such as the BMS, are inherently limited by their ordinal
structure, low sensitivity, and susceptibility to inter-rater variability. In contrast,

automated video tracking systems paired with machine learning, offer the ability
to extract and interpret subtle, multidimensional patterns in gait dynamics. These
approaches allow for the estimation of an animal’s motor state and the classification
of injury severity without the need for manual annotation. This advancement not
only improves the resolution and objectivity of behavioural assessment but also
unlocks new possibilities for responsive, individualized rehabilitation strategies.
This approach is particularly relevant in the context of closed-loop neurorehabili-
tation systems, which aim to adjust stimulation parameters in real time based on
behavioural or neural feedback[17, 18]. These systems have emerged as powerful
tools for promoting functional recovery after SCI by synchronizing neuromodula-
tion with ongoing activity. For example, optogenetic stimulation enables precise
activation of spinal or supraspinal circuits involved in locomotion and has been
shown to enhance gait recovery in rodent models[10, 19]. However, despite their
potential, current protocols often rely on pre-defined, static stimulation parameters
that are applied uniformly across animals and sessions. This one-size-fits-all ap-
proach neglects individual variability in injury severity, behavioral phenotype, and
rate of recovery, which are all critical factors influencing therapeutic efficacy.
To optimize the application of closed-loop systems, there is a growing consensus
that neurorehabilitation must become more adaptive. This requires integrating
intelligent algorithms capable of interpreting real-time behavioural data and guid-
ing interventions accordingly. A robust system would allow for the assessment of
motor function and the dynamic modulation of therapy in response to ongoing
changes in motor function. In this context, scalable machine learning frameworks
offer a promising foundation for building adaptive, data-driven neurorehabilitation
systems.
To enable such adaptive and individualized rehabilitation, there is a critical need
for automated tools that can:
• Objectively estimate injury severity from raw kinematic data, eliminating
the variability and limitations of subjective scales.
• Monitor recovery continuously, thereby enabling longitudinal tracking of
functional improvement or decline over time.

• Dynamically adjust stimulation parameters, including frequency, intensity,
and timing, based on real-time estimates of the animal’s current motor state
and recovery trajectory.
Integrating these components could support the development of more personalized
closed-loop neurorehabilitation systems. Such frameworks may not only maximize
therapeutic outcomes through precise timing and targeting of stimulation but also
serve as research tools to uncover the causal relationships between neural circuit
engagement, behavioural improvement, and the principles of functional recovery.
Ultimately, the use of scalable, data driven behavioural estimation systems could
help bridge the gap between preclinical neurotechnology and clinical translation,
supporting the design of individualized treatments for impaired motor function
after SCI.
1.4 Objective
The objective of this thesis is to develop and validate a behavioural biomarker scale
that estimates the motor state of mice with SCI using high-dimensional kinematic
locomotor data and semi-supervised machine learning. This biologically meaningful
scale serves as a data-driven alternative to traditional, subjective scoring systems
such as the BMS, offering greater consistency, and automation.
The proposed scale is designed to function as a core component of an adaptive,
closed-loop neurorehabilitation framework, providing real-time, objective estimates
of motor function that can guide stimulation protocols. In the future, this frame-
work could be extended to investigate how specific neurorehabilitation parameters
such as stimulation frequency, intensity, or duration interact with the motor state,
enabling personalized treatment strategies tailored to individual locomotor impair-
ment severities.
Ultimately, this thesis contributes a scalable, automated solution to a central
challenge in SCI research: the need for reliable, real-time assessment of motor
impairment that supports individualized and dynamically adaptive therapeutic
interventions.

Methods
2.1 Study Population and Data Collection
95 female wild-type mice (8 weeks old; weight: 23 g ± 2) were used for this study.
Animals were housed five per cage in an environmentally controlled room (22°C,
12:12 light–dark cycle). All surgical procedures and experimental manipulations
were approved by the local and national ethical committee. Good handling practices
and animal welfare techniques were applied throughout the entire duration of the
project.
The walking sessions were held in a narrow corridor to encourage the mice to move
in a straight line, ensuring consistent and comparable data. Video recordings were
taken from the side as the mice walked in both directions (left to right and right to
left), with two to six recordings conducted per direction. This side-view approach
provided a clear perspective on their gait mechanics and locomotor patterns.
To model SCI, a thoracic contusion was induced in mice using a controlled impactor
device (IH-0400 Infinite Horizon Impactor, Precision Systems and Instrumentation,
U.S.A.) targeting the 10th thoracic segment of the spinal cord with 70 kilodynes and
1 cm displacement. This procedure results in a severe injury that lead to hindlimb
paralysis with some functional recovery in the weeks following the injury. This
method produces a reproducible blunt-force injury that mimics clinically relevant
SCI in humans[20]. Two weeks following the injury, 94 of the 95 mice were
recorded again to assess changes in locomotor function as a result of SCI. If any
mice stopped moving for too long during the recording, the session was discarded,
and the mice were guided to perform another walking trial. This experiment was
executed by two test performers.
The mice were divided into three groups based on injury severity: the Healthy
group (n = 95), which included all mice pre-injury; the Severe SCI group (n = 8),
5
==PAGE:13==
consisting of mice that experienced significant bilateral hindlimb paralysis; and the
Unlabeled group (n = 86), which contained post-injury mice whose impairment
severity was unknown. The Severe SCI group was assigned by the test performers
to include mice that were fully paralysed below the site of injury. This assignment
was later evaluated using the video recordings from the walking sessions conducted
two weeks after the SCI injury.
2.2 Data Preprocessing
The recordings before and after injury were first processed using DeepLabCut, a
markerless pose estimation tool that uses deep learning to automatically track body
parts in animal videos [21]. For each mouse, pose estimates were generated for a
set of anatomically defined key points, including the head, four spine points, base
of the tail, and five points along the tail. In addition, both forelimbs (shoulder,
elbow, wrist, forepaw) and hindlimbs (hip, knee, ankle, hindpaw, hindfingers) were
tracked on the right and left side of the mice. These pose coordinates were then
processed using KineMatix, a non-public software developed for high-dimensional
kinematic analysis. The software extracted a range of gait and posture-related
features, which were averaged across all runs for each individual mouse to get a
representative movement profile.
The kinematic features extracted for each mouse was 10731 and included metrics
such as mean step height, mean step length, mean velocity, and mean joint angles,
among others. These features were calculated across the entire gait cycle as
well as during the stance and swing phases. Circular data, such as angles, were
treated as continuous variables by applying sine and cosine transformations (i.e.,
sin(θ), cos(θ)) to the angular measurements. Prior to transformation, all angles
were converted from degrees to radians to ensure compatibility with the sine and
cosine calculations. This transformation made the angular features suitable for
standardization alongside other numerical features. As a result, the total number
of features increased from 10731 to 11596.
To handle inter-person variability from having two test performers, a person-specific
standardization was applied. In this approach, features were standardized within
each test performer group using the formula:

2.3 Conditional Variational Autoencoder
(CVAE)
To classify injury severity while leveraging both labelled and unlabelled data, a semi-
supervised Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) was implemented. The
model combines a CVAE structure with an auxiliary classifier trained on the latent
space. To guide the model away from grouping mice by injury laterality and instead
encourage a continuous severity spectrum, a one-hot encoded covariate indicating
injury side (left, right, or bilateral) was added to both the encoder and decoder
inputs. The architecture is designed to learn a low-dimensional representation
of high-dimensional kinematic features, structured by both reconstruction loss,
Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence loss and classification loss when ground truth
labels are available. The model architecture can be seen in Figure 2.2.

2.4 Latent Space Clustering Analysis
To investigate the structure of the learned latent space and explore whether the
CVAE captured injury-related variation beyond the supervised Healthy and Severe
labels, a density-based clustering approach was applied. The latent means (µ)
were extracted from the encoder for all training samples and projected into three
dimensions using Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP). UMAP
is a dimensionality reduction technique that preserves both local and global data
structure by projecting high-dimensional data onto a lower-dimensional mani-
fold[22]. Clustering was then performed using the Density-Based Spatial Clustering
of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) algorithm, which was chosen for its ability to
identify clusters of arbitrary shape and to separate noise points without requiring
the number of clusters to be predefined[23].
The DBSCAN parameters were set to ε = 0.75 and a minimum sample value of 4.
The ε parameter defines the maximum distance between two points for them to
be considered neighbors, and was empirically chosen to allow DBSCAN to form
clusters that broadly capture the known Healthy and Severe classes while still being
sensitive to the structure in the latent space. A minimum of 4 samples per cluster
was selected to ensure that each cluster had a small but meaningful level of internal
consistency.
To evaluate whether the discovered clusters reflected differences in motor behaviour,
statistical comparisons were conducted on the extracted kinematic features. For
each feature, a Kruskal–Wallis test was used to compare distributions across clusters,
as this non-parametric method does not require the assumption of normality.
Assumption checks using the Shapiro–Wilk test for normality and Levene’s test for
homogeneity of variance confirmed that parametric tests were inappropriate for
7824 out of 7891 features.
Multiple testing correction was applied using the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure to
control the False Discovery Rate (FDR). For features with significant FDR-adjusted
p-values (pFDR < 0.05), pairwise comparisons between clusters were performed
using Dunn’s post hoc test. The resulting p-values were again corrected for multiple
testing using the Benjamini–Hochberg method. Significant features were then

2.5 Classifier Training on CVAE-Derived
Labels
To assess whether the cluster assignments derived from the CVAE latent space could
be predicted directly from kinematic features, a secondary supervised classification
pipeline was implemented. Each mouse was assigned a cluster label based on
the DBSCAN clustering results. These assigned clusters served as class labels for
training the classifier.
All kinematic features were preprocessed similarly to the CVAE pipeline, including
sine and cosine transformation of angular features and person-specific standard-
ization. To reduce dimensionality and retain only the most informative features,
MI was computed between each feature and the assigned cluster labels. The 90
features with the highest MI scores were selected for model training. The result-
ing dataset was split into training and test sets using an 80/20 stratified split to
preserve class distribution. Four classifiers were trained and compared: Logistic
Regression (LogReg), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), and
a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP). All the models were trained with balanced class
weights or used Synthetic Minority Oversampling (SMOTE) to handle uneven class
distributions.
LogReg was implemented with L2 regularization, while the SVM used a polynomial
kernel of degree 2 to capture non-linear relationships. The RF model was imple-
mented with 300 trees, unrestricted depth, a minimum of five samples required
to split an internal node, and at least one sample per leaf. The MLP consisted of
two hidden layers with 256 and 128 units, ReLU activations, and early stopping to
prevent overfitting. It was trained using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of
1 × 10−4.
The classification models were selected to capture both linear and non-linear re-
lationships between kinematic features and injury severity. LogReg was included

3.1 CVAE Training and Loss Evaluation
To evaluate the training progress of the semi-supervised CVAE model, we mon-
itored the total loss over 148 epochs for both the training and test sets (Figure
3.1). The total loss comprised three components: the reconstruction loss, which
measures how accurately the model can reproduce the input kinematic data; the
KL divergence, which regularizes the latent space; and the supervised classification
loss, which penalizes incorrect predictions and encourages the model to structure
the latent space in a way that separates the two known injury severities (Healthy
and Severe).
Figure 3.1: Total training (blue) and test (orange) loss during training of the semi-
supervised CVAE model over 148 epochs. The loss includes contributions
from reconstruction loss, KL divergence, and supervised classification loss.

Discussion
4.1
A Data-Driven Approach to Injury
Severity Classification of SCI
This thesis introduces a scalable and biologically grounded framework to estimate
motor impairment severity following SCI in mice. By leveraging high-dimensional
kinematic features extracted with the KineMatrix software and combining it with
semi-supervised machine learning, we constructed a latent space that maps a
continuous spectrum of motor impairment. Traditional scoring systems like the BMS
or the BBB scale provide limb-focused assessments but fail to capture the complexity
of full locomotor assessments, subtle asymmetries, and dynamic locomotor changes.
In contrast, our approach uses high-dimensional kinematic data reflecting the
movements of all limbs, trunk, and postural adjustments, resulting in greater
objectivity and specificity.
Several recent studies highlight the value of whole-body kinematic analysis in
SCI research. For instance, Timotius et al. showed that motion capture allow
the extraction of spatiotemporal features that outperform traditional metrics in
identifying levels of SCI severity [24]. Similarly, the ALMA toolbox introduced by
Aljovic et al. employs over 40 spatiotemporal features including step height, paw
trajectory, knee joint flexion and knee joint extension to identify recovery stages
and classify motor impairments with high precision[25].
Building upon these efforts, our work uses around 3000 features per limb, cap-
turing both spatial and temporal gait parameters, interlimb symmetry, angular
joint excursion and trunk oscillations. This large and informative dataset is ideal
for integration with machine learning pipelines, allowing the discovery of latent
behavioural representations that reflects injury severity.
23
==PAGE:31==
By applying a semi-supervised approach, we demonstrated that even a small
amount of labelled data was sufficient to structure a latent space reflecting a
continuum of motor impairment. This approach supports classification of injury
severity beyond what ordinal scoring systems can offer and allows for objective
tracking of behavioural recovery over time. Additionally, the model highlights
subtle compensatory strategies that might be missed using standard assessment
scales such as the BMS.
4.2 Modeling Continuous Motor Impairment
with Latent Embeddings
Our approach of using a CVAE to extract latent representations of motor impair-
ment contributes to a growing body of research that uses latent variable models
to characterize rodent behaviour with greater objectivity and precision. Earlier
frameworks such as Motion Sequencing (MoSeq)[26] were among the first to apply
unsupervised algorithms for segmenting mouse behaviour into discrete motifs.
Specifically, it applies an AutoRegressive Hidden Markov Model (AR-HMM) to
video-derived pose data to discover and model behavioural syllables. More recently,
models like Variational Animal Motion Embedding (VAME)[27] and Poisson identi-
fiable Variational Autoencoder (pi-VAE)[28] have introduced latent representations
that capture smooth trajectories or interpretable components of motor sequences.
These methods demonstrate the potential of dimensionality reduction and deep
learning to uncover structure in high-dimensional behavioural data that traditional
scoring systems often overlook
Self-supervised frameworks like Contrastive Embedding for Behavioural and neural
Representation Analysis (CEBRA)[29] have also pushed the field forward by allow-
ing similar representations in latent space across subjects and conditions. Although
our current model does not introduce cross-subject consistency, future versions may
benefit from this.
Within this field, our CVAE offers a complementary approach focused on construct-
ing a latent space shaped by known extreme injury severity cases (Healthy and
Severe), while preserving the full spectrum of continuous behavioural variation.

In contrast to unsupervised models that focus on behavioural motif discovery, our
approach is designed to represent graded motor impairments associated with SCI,
supporting its potential as a quantitative biomarker for longitudinal monitoring
and closed-loop therapeutic strategies. Overall, our results highlight the potential
of latent variable modelling for addressing a central challenge in SCI research: the
need for reliable, automated, and scalable tools to estimate motor state and recov-
ery over time. By leveraging high-dimensional kinematic data, the semi-supervised
CVAE can allow real-time classification of impairment level, guiding therapeutic
decisions and contributing to more adaptive, individualized neurorehabilitation
protocols.
4.3 Evaluation of the Classification Pipeline
and Practical Integration
To address the issue of class imbalance in the training data, both the SMOTE and
balanced class weights were evaluated to determine the most suitable approach
for this specific framework. The performance of each method was tested across
multiple classifiers, with particular attention to accuracy scores and generalization
across all severity levels. Notably, the RF classifier performed equally well with
balanced weights and with SMOTE, achieving the highest test accuracy and macro
F1 scores. Given that SMOTE introduces synthetic samples that may not fully reflect
true biological variation, future iterations of this framework may favour the RF
model trained with balanced weights. This reduces the risk of introducing noise
from SMOTE sampling and ensures that classification decisions are based solely on
real, biologically observed kinematic patterns.
Furthermore, supervised classification was performed on the original kinematic
features extracted by the KineMatix software, rather than on the latent representa-
tions learned by the CVAE. This decision prioritized interpretability and practical
relevance: the features are directly linked to observable aspects of locomotion
and remain consistent across different recordings, making them more suitable for
biological and clinical interpretation. While the latent space captures underlying
structure in the data, it requires re-encoding new samples and lacks transparency.
In contrast, KineMatix-derived features are accessible within the existing workflow,

scalable integration into experimental and preclinical pipelines.
4.4 Scalability and Generalization of the
Framework
A key strength of this framework is its flexibility across many experimental and
clinical settings. Although it is currently applied to locomotor recovery in a mouse
model of SCI, the full process includes markerless tracking, extraction of kinematic
features, and semi-supervised learning of latent variables. This process can be
adapted to other conditions that affect movement, as long as suitable input data
and labels are available. For example, this approach could be applied to conditions
such as stroke [30] and Parkinson’s disease [31], where changes in motor behaviour
serve as important indicators of disease progression and therapeutic response. In
such contexts, there is a clear need for objective and high resolution analysis tools
that can support consistent and scalable assessment of motor function.
Importantly, similar computational approaches can also be applied to human data
captured through markerless motion capture or wearable sensors. Recent work in
brain–computer interface (BCI) has shown that structured latent representations
can support real time applications. For example, a study demonstrated that a voice
synthesis device could produce continuous speech from motor cortex activity by
learning a representation that links brain signals to patterns of articulation [32].
The framework presented in this thesis builds on similar ideas by using kinematic
data to learn a structured space of movement patterns, which allows quantitative
assessment of locomotor function.
Finally, the approach precented in this thesis offers a foundation for making closed-
loop neuromodulation systems adaptive. Real-time estimation of motor state from
behaviour or neural activity can inform adaptive stimulation protocols tailored
to individual recovery stages[17, 18]. When combined with techniques such
as deep brain stimulation, spinal cord stimulation, or optogenetics, this type of
feedback-based approach could contribute to more precise and effective rehabilita-
tion strategies.

4.5 Strengths and Limitations
A key strength of the framework is its reproducibility and accessibility. The pipeline
is built on open-source tools and standard hardware, allowing easy implementation
across labs. The structure of the pipeline allows for real time estimation of SCI
impairment severity, which can be incorporated into adaptive neurorehabilitation
systems. Notably, the CVAE model also revealed consistent biomechanical compen-
sation strategies, which supports the interpretability of the learned latent space. In
particular, the pattern between the forelimb and hindlimb on the injured side is
consistent with previous findings in rodent models of thoracic SCI[33], suggesting
that the model captures biologically meaningful features of locomotor adaptation.
Furthermore, the use of KineMatrix-derived features rather than latent variables
prioritized interpretability and ease of integration within the KineMatix software
pipeline.
Despite these strengths, several limitations remain. First, the number of labelled
Severe cases (n = 8) was limited, which may have affected the precision of
the latent space, and consequently the performance of the classification models.
Although the pseudo-labels assigned to intermediate severities were biologically
validated, they are still model-derived rather than true labels. Their reliability
depends on how well the latent space captures meaningful variation in motor
impairment. In addition, the dataset size (n = 189) is relatively small, which
may limit the stability and generalizability of the model. A larger dataset would
likely enhance the clarity of the latent structure and strengthen the reliability of
downstream classification.
Lastly, the CVAE model identified seven injury severity clusters, whereas the BMS
scale defines ten ordinal levels. While this discrepancy may reflect limitations in
the dataset, it also indicates that the clustering was shaped by patterns in the data
rather than predefined scoring criteria.

4.6 Future Perspective
This thesis highlights several directions to extending the current approach. One
key next step is to evaluate whether the model generalizes to the recovery phase
of SCI. Testing the current latent structure and classification models on data from
mice undergoing closed-loop optogenetic neurorehabilitation would determine if
recovery trajectories are appropriately captured within the learned severity scores
or if new clusters are needed to reflect functional recovery. Such experiments would
also clarify whether the model can serve as a reliable biomarker of therapeutic
progress.
In addition, while this approach must first be validated more thoroughly in mouse
models, the underlying principles of objective, high-resolution motor assessment
based on latent variable modelling, hold potential for clinical translation. Closed-
loop neurostimulation systems are already in use in clinical populations, particularly
in Parkinson’s disease, stroke rehabilitation, and SCI. However, these systems often
rely on broad scoring scales or indirect indicators of motor function. By integrating
wearable sensors or markerless video motion capture, a human-adapted version of
this pipeline could provide real-time, quantitative assessments of motor state to
guide neuromodulation strategies. This could support intelligent neurorehabilita-
tion systems by linking injury severity estimation directly to adaptive stimulation,
allowing for individualized interventions.

Conclusion
5.1 Conclusion
This thesis presents a data-driven approach for assessing motor impairment follow-
ing SCI, using a semi-supervised CVAE trained on high-dimensional kinematic data
from mouse locomotion. The model learned a structured latent space that reflects
a continuum of injury severity.
Through unsupervised clustering in the latent space using DBSCAN and statistical
validation, five previously unlabelled subgroups were identified, each characterized
by specific locomotor profiles, including side-specific impairments and compen-
satory adaptations. These findings not only enhance our understanding of locomo-
tor outcomes after SCI but also demonstrate how latent representations can inform
data-driven classification of motor function.
A supervised RF classification model trained on kinematic features achieved a
high accuracy score (97.4%) and generalization performance, showing that au-
tomated classification of injury severity is possible and reliable. This approach
lays the groundwork for integrating injury classification into adaptive closed-loop
neuromodulation protocols, where real-time classification can inform adaptive
stimulation strategies.
Overall, the methodology developed in this work offers a foundation for objective
assessment of motor impairment in the context of SCI rehabilitation. Its flexibility
and adaptability make it suitable for application to other neurological conditions
where changes in motor behaviour serves as an important indicator of disease
progression. This contributes to the development of more responsive and data-
informed neurorehabilitation strategies in both preclinical research and future
clinical use.
