Abstract 
α-alkylation of carbonyl compounds has been a conventional method of creation of c-c bonds, this 
method can produce dangerous and environmentally toxic waste. In recent decades, an increasing 
interest in more environmentally friendly reactions has been seen. This has lead to the development 
of methods where alcohols can be used directly for alkylation. Alcohols are relatively unreactive 
molecules and often need activation to participate in an alkylation reaction. In recent years some 
papers using benzyl alcohol radicals as alkylation agents have been published. This includes the 
discovery of Azizi and Madsen1 , a radical coupling between benzyl alcohol and N,N-
dimethylacetamide in the presence of KOtBu. 
This thesis aims to study the potential optimization of the reaction condition for the reaction 
described by Azizi and Madsen. The reaction is studied with experiments in which several factors 
from the original procedure have been changed. This includes reaction temperatures, different 
bases, and glymes as additives. Furthermore, N,N-dimethylacetamide have been substituted with 
several substrates to gain insights into the usability of the reaction. Among these a reaction between 
benzyl alcohol and β-bromostyrene in the presence of stoichiometric amounts of KOtBu.

Introduction 
Carbon-carbon bonds are a fundamental and essential part of organic chemistry. The formation of c-c 
bonds creates the backbone of organic molecules and the techniques developed to form c-c bonds 
highly dictate the complexity of possible synthetic molecules.2  
Nature can produce highly complex molecules, historically often more complex than what synthetic 
organic chemistry could offer. With modern developments a myriad of techniques has been 
developed to create highly complex molecules. Chemists today, given the enough resources and time 
can create almost all possible natural products in small quantities.3 
α-functionalization of carbonyl compounds is an important strategy in the creation of complex 
organic molecules, where α-alkylation is an important strategy in carbon-carbon bond formation. 
These strategies are important developments in creation of several molecules e.g. heterocycle 
compounds, pharmaceutical active compounds, and natural products.4 Several reactions to create α-
alkylation of carbonyl compounds have been discovered over the years, conventionally made using 
organohalides. However, organohalides are toxic reagents that are undesirable to use, furthermore, 
the reactions with organohalides create unwanted waste byproducts. 
However, with the increasing focus on efficiency and sustainable chemistry organohalides have been 
substituted with alcohols. Alcohols are comparatively a more environmentally viable reagent. 
Organohalides are often produced with alcohols as the starting material. Therefore, substituting 
organohalides with an alcohol a conversion step is spared. One downside to using alcohols as a 
starting material is they are relatively unreactive, one strategy to solve this is using a catalyst to 
convert the alcohol to a more reactive carbonyl compound.5 
In the following sections, the background of using alcohols as starting materials for α-alkylation of 
carbonyl compounds to create c-c bonds will be discussed. The starting point of this thesis is based 
on a paper by K. Azizi and R. Madsen in 2020 where a new carbon-carbon bond is created from 
benzyl alcohol and N,N-dimethyl acetamide to create N,N-dimethyl-3-phenylpropanamide.1 The 
mechanism is believed to be a radical mechanism. Focus on radical chemistry and c-c bond formation 
from radical reactions will therefore also be discussed.

Background 
In the following section three carbon-carbon bonds will be discussed, the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly 
reduction/Oppenauer oxidation (MPV-O), the Acceptorless dehydrogenation and lastly the Guerbet 
reaction. Hereafter, radical chemistry is being discussed followed by two examples of modern c-c 
bond formation using radical chemistry and benzyl alcohol as starting material. The first being 
Milstein et al.6 and the latter Azizi and Madsen.1 
Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction/Oppenauer oxidation 
The Oppenauer oxidation and Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction (MPV-O) is the reverse reactions 
of each other. The Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction is the reduction of an aldehyde or a ketone 
to the corresponding alcohol with an alcohol as a hydrogen donor. The Oppenauer oxidation is the 
oxidation of an alcohol to the corresponding carbonyl using a carbonyl as a hydrogen acceptor. The 
reactions are a base mediated hydride transfer catalyzed by aluminum- and alkoxides. Originally a 
stoichiometric amount was used, however, later transition metals have been shown to catalyze the 
reactions. In modern literature, it has been shown that aluminum- and alkoxides can be used in 
catalytic amounts. The oxidation/reduction is shown in Scheme 1. 7 
 
Scheme 1 Oppenauer oxidation/Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction 
Neither of the reactions in MPV-O forms a carbon-carbon bond. However, the reactions can be used 
to convert an alcohol to a carbonyl compound that can undergo an aldol condensation creating an 
α,β-unsaturated carbonyl. This will be reduced to the corresponding alcohol. One can use a 
inexpensive carbonyl to convert the starting material alcohol and create the c-c bond. 1 
The mechanism of the MPV-O reaction is believed to go through a 6 membered cyclic transition state 
shown in Scheme 2. This creates the hydride shift via deprotonation of the alcohol and transfers it to 
the carbonyl. In the transition state the carbonyl is activated by coordinating to the metal center.8,9 
 
Scheme 2 Transition state of Oppenauer oxidation/Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction8 
Acceptorless dehydrogenation 
A mechanistically different α-alkylation is the acceporless dehydrogenation or borrowing hydrogen 
principle, this creates similar products to the MPV-O reaction. Initially, a hydrogen autotransfer from 
an alcohol to a metal catalyst occurs, this activates the alcohol by oxidation to the corresponding 
carbonyl compound. Following this oxidation, a base mediated addition from a c-nucleophile, such as

Guerbet reaction 
Similarly, to the two previous reactions, the Guerbet reaction is an aldol condensation. The Guerbet 
reaction is a reaction between two alcohols that dimerize to produce longer β-alkylated alcohols with 
water as the byproduct.  
Initially, alcohol starting materials are activated by oxidation to the corresponding carbonyl 
compounds. Following this, a base-mediated aldol condensation takes place, dehydration occurs to 
create a α,β-unsaturated carbonyl. This is then reduced by the metal catalyst to finally yield the β-
alkylated alcohol. In Scheme 4 a generalized reaction scheme for a Guerbet reaction is shown. 13

The first step of the mechanism is the oxidation of the starting alcohols, this can be through a 
hydrogen borrowing mechanism or a MVP-O reaction depending on the catalyst. In the reaction both 
branched and unbranched alcohols can be used as starting materials. If two different alcohols are 
used as a starting material self- and cross-coupling is possible. 1 
The Guerbet reaction offers an efficient and capable approach to build larger and more complex 
alcohols from inexpensive small alcohols as starting materials. These smaller alcohols can be 
produced from renewable sources. 14

Radical chemistry 
Radical chemistry can be used to create carbon-carbon bonds, in this section the background of 
initiation of radical, stability of radicals and propagation of radicals is described briefly. Following 
this, two examples of radical c-c bond formation from modern literature being presenceed.  
Initiation of radicals 
Radicals are atoms or molecules with an unpaired electron. Radicals occur from homolysis or a single 
electron transfer. In some instances, the energy for homolysis can in some instances be thermal 
energy, and in other reactions, light provides the necessary energy.15 
Compounds that can easily undergo homolysis is often used as an initiator. The role of an initiator in 
a radical reaction is simply to create the radical. 15 Some examples of homolysis are shown in Scheme 
5.  
 
Scheme 5 Simple examples of homolysis 
Initiators for radical reactions are often stable at room temperature but break down by homolysis 
under mild conditions, making them ideal to create radicals. The initiators role is not to be part of the 
main reaction but to create a radical that can propagate into the reaction. Examples of radical 
initiators are peroxide- and diazo compounds, the weak O-O bond is readily broken by homolysis. 16 
Additionally, radicals can be created by reduction or a single electron transfer, taking place in some 
reactions with metals. A single electron transfer to a carbonyl would yield a carbon centered radical 
as shown in Scheme 6 16 
 
Scheme 6 Examples of single electron transfer 
Radical reactions and chain reactions 
Radicals are often highly reactive molecules, where different reactions can occur. They can react with 
a spin-paired molecule in either an abstraction or an addition reaction. In an abstraction reaction, a 
spin-paired molecule as well as a radical will be the product. In an addition a radical compound will 
be the product. Radicals can also decompose in a unimolecular reaction and make an elimination, 
this will yield a spin-paired molecule as well as a radical. All three examples are shown in Scheme 7. 16 
Often the initial radical compounds will make an abstraction from a reagent, in many instances a 
carbon centered radical will be created. The carbon centered radical will react further with other 
reagents e.g. in an addition reaction.

A radical chain reaction consists of three parts: initiation, propagation, and termination. The 
initiation is described in the previous section. The propagation step can consist of a series of the 
above-mentioned reactions. The propagation step is a cyclic series of reactions where a radical 
species is being recreated. Two radical compounds can react with each other and dimerize, this 
consumes both radicals and yields a spin-paired molecule and ends the chain reaction. 16 
Stability of carbon centered radicals 
The stability of any organic molecule can be assessed by using molecular orbital diagrams (MOs). The 
more stable a radical is, the easier it is formed. In the MO of a radical, the orbital containing the 
unpaired electron is called the singly occupied molecular orbital (SOMO). The lower the SOMO is in 
energy the more stable the radical. 
Neighboring groups will affect the stability of a radical, particularly electron withdrawing groups 
(EWG) and electron donating groups (EDG). With an EWG adjacent e.g. a carbonyl there will be a 
possibility for an overlap between the LUMO, a low energy π*, of the EWG and the SOMO of the 
radical. This creates an empty lower energy orbital that will be the new more stable SOMO, which 
will increase the stability of the radical. On the other hand, having an EDG adjacent will overlap the 
HOMO of the EDG, which will result in a higher energy SOMO decreasing the stability of the radical. 16 
 
Figure 1 Stability of carbon-centered radical and neighboring groups effect on stability16 
Hyperconjugation by adjacent alkyl groups will increase the stability of a radical. Due to overlapping 
with a σ-CH orbital, this can help delocalize the radical, therefore tertiary radicals tend to be more 
stable than secondary that tend to be more stable than primary radicals. Additionally, radicals will be 
stabilized by resonance.

The stability of a radical will influence the selectivity of the radial reactions. Lower energy radicals 
will be formed easier and will shape the selectivity. A radical with a neighboring EWG will be more 
electrophilic and will tend to react with nucleophiles such as enolates. For EDGs the radical will be 
more nucleophilic and will therefore participate in different reactions. Steric effects also affect the 
selectivity and reactivity of radicals. Sterically hindered radicals are less reactive and reactions with 
sterically hindered reagents can be difficult. 16

Milstein et al.: Radical coupling between benzyl alcohols and terminal alkynes 
Recently, Milstein et al. 6 described in 2019 in their paper a transition metal-free c-c bond formation 
starting from benzyl alcohols and terminal alkynes in the presence of a catalytic amount of potassium 
tert-butoxide. The reaction can synthesize α-alkylated ketones derived from benzyl alcohol. 
 
Scheme 8 Reaction observed by Milstein et al. 
In the paper they demonstrate reactions that produce a product class called dihydrogen chalcone, 
these are found in natural products such as aspalathin and various artificial sweeteners and 
antioxidants. A traditional method to produce these products would be using an enolate and an alkyl 
halide. However, this does need at least a stoichiometric amount of base. Furthermore, the 
traditional method produces unwanted salt wastes, where the reactions by Milstein et al. have no 
waste products, which make it a more sustainable alternative. These types of compounds can also be 
created by transition metal catalysis e.g. c-alkylation of a ketone. Again, this reaction can be a 
preferred alternative due to the lack of a need of transition metals. This make it an interesting, new 
and sustainable alternative. Additionally, it starts from an alcohol rather than a carbonyl, which 
possibly will make the starting materials more inexpensive. 6 
A mechanistic study was performed to determine the type of reaction involved in the c-c coupling. 
Different experiments were designed, the first experiments suggested that the mechanism did not 
proceed by a hydrogenation/dehydrogenation pathway. Next, a Wittig-type rearrangement was 
precluded as a mechanism together with the possibility of a Markovnikov addition of water. Leaving 
the possibility for a radical pathway. The addition of TEMPO decreased yields and indicated a radical 
mechanism. EPR experiments were performed to identify radicals and gain insight into the character 
of the radicals. All experiments indicated that the reaction proceeds through a radical mechanism 
with a carbon centered radical. 
A proposed mechanism is shown in Scheme 9. The first step is the initiation of the radical where the 
deprotonated benzyl alcohol makes a hydrogen atom transfer (HAT), which creates the propagating 
benzyl radical 1. Radical 1 reacts with phenylacetylene, this creates a c-c bond to create radical 2 and 
3. This can produce 4 in a hydrogen shift, which can then deprotonate benzyl alcohol and 
subsequently make a hydrogen atom transfer that creates the product 7 and recreates the 
propagating radical 1. 6

Azizi and Madsen: Radical coupling between benzyl alcohols and acetamides 
The point of departure for this thesis is the paper published by Azizi and Madsen in 2020 1, in 
particular, the experimental work is based hereon. In the paper, is a c-c bond described, with benzyl 
alcohol and an acetamide as the starting materials. This reaction was discovered upon studying 
manganese(III) porphyrincatalyzed alkylation of acetamides. When removing the catalyst the 
reaction proceeded as Scheme 10 where 2 was produced  without catalyst and 1 was produced with 
catalyst 
 
Scheme 10 Reaction observed by Azizi and Madsen1 
In the paper conditions for the reaction were optimized, with two equivalents of KOtBu refluxing in 
mesitylene for 6 hours 2 was formed in 85% yield. To study the mechanism of this reaction many 
experiments were performed. At first two labeling studies with PHCD2OH and PhCH2OD as reagents 
was performed.  
Yield from the first labeling study showed a combination of deuterium incorporation in α and β 
position or only in the β position. This indicates that the hydrogen transfer takes place at the α 
position of the alcohol. KIE has been measured for the two deuterium labeled substrates and was 
found to be around 1 in both cases. This shows that the hydrogen transfer in the mechanism is not 
the rate determining step. This discourages the mechanism from being a MPV-O mechanism. 
A radical mechanism was suspected and to probe whether or not it was right two radical scavengers 
were added to the reaction mixture. With a reaction time of 48 hours and the addition of cyclohexa-
1,4-diene or 4-methyl-2,4-diphenylpent-1-ene no product was formed, while the scavengers was fully 
consumed in the reaction. The main product of cyclohexa-1,4-diene was α-cyclohexenylbenzyl 
alcohol and 1,2-dimethyl-1,2-diphenylbutane was the product for reaction with 4-methyl-2,4-
diphenylpent-1-ene. This demonstrated that the radical formed could be trapped by a trapping agent 
and highly suggests a radical mechanism. The addition of TEMPO did lead to a lower yield of 50% 
while the addition of BHT did produce a 1:1 mixture of cinnamamide and proanamide. Again, 
suggesting a radical mechanism. 
A radical clock experiment was performed where benzyl alcohol was substituted with o-allylbenzyl 
alcohol. This did not produce any coupling with N,N-dimethylacetamide but did have a 50% 
conversion of o-allylbenzyl alcohol into 5:3:2 mixture of 2-methylindene, 2-methylindane and o-
propylbenzaldehyde. This demonstrates that the radical was created on benzyl alcohol since it 
participates in the intermolecular reaction with the allyl group.

EPR was used to determine if a radical was presence and if so the character of the radical. A singlet 
with a g-value of 2.0045 was observed in a mixture of benzyl alcohol, N,N-dimethylacetamide, and 
KOtBu. This value indicates that a carbon centered radical bonded to oxygen could be presence. With 
a mixture of benzyl alcohol and potassium tert-butoxide refluxing in mesitylene the signal was 
observed again, although weaker. All other mixtures of benzyl alcohol and acetamide or acetamide 
and base gave no signal, and does therefore not contain any radical species. This demonstrates that 
the radical occurs from potassium tert-butoxide and benzyl alcohol. This led to the proposed 
mechanism in Scheme 12.  
The first step in the mechanism is deprotonation of benzyl alcohol by potassium tertbutoxide to form 
an alkoxide. The initiation of the radical is likely to happen after a second deprotonation of the 
alkoxide yielding a dianion PhCHO2-. This dianion can serve as a strong single electron donor being 
able to donate a single electron to either acetamide, the solvent or the alcohol initiating the reaction.  
This radical can react with the enolate of the acetamide through transition state 2, This condensation 
is likely to be the rate-determining step. This creates an aminoketyl radical 3 that can act as a single 
electron donor donating to acetamide. Next, is the elimination yielding 5, this can undergo an 
addition reaction from the formerly created radical. This can finally react with benzyl alcohol creating 
the product 8.

Deprotonation of α-hydrogen 
An important step in the mechanism by Azizi and Madsen is the formation of the enolate of N,N-
dimethylacetamide. The α-proton of N,N-dimethylacetamide has a pKa value of 29.4 in water. In 
water, potassium tertbutoxide has a pKa value of 17. Close to no deprotonation of the acetamide 
would be expected to happen by potassium tert-butoxide. Since the reaction is performed in 
mesitylene, water might not be a good analog for pKa values. DMSO can be a better analogue for pKa 
values. N,N-dimethylacetamide has a pKa value of 35 and 29.4 for potassium tert-butoxide in DMSO. 
A smaller gap between pKa values would lead to more deprotonation, this is observed in DMSO. The 
values for mesitylene, xylene, or toluene are not known. Therefore, it is difficult to make accurate 
predictions of deprotonation. When using DMSO as an analog it appears like there is are smaller 
differences in organic solvents than in water. Other substrates with similarly acidic or more acidic α-
proton could possibly be used in the reaction. A couple of substrates with such acidic α-proton could 
be Tert-butyl acetate with a pKa of 30.3 in DMSO, dimethyl malonate with a pKa of 17.7 in DMSO, 
and acetate with a pKa of 33.5.17–19

Optimization with additives

Conclusion and future work 
Optimization of reaction conditions in the coupling between N,N-dimethylacetamide, and benzyl 
alcohol under basic conditions was attempted by varying solvent boiling points, by substitution of the 
base, and by the addition of glymes. The most significant change in yield derives from elevated 
reaction temperatures, secondly, an increase in yields has been observed with potassium bases 
compared to sodium bases.  
The addition of glymes did not have a significant impact on the reaction yield, nor did the base 
strength. This is by no means a complete study of all possible solvents, additives, and bases which 
could improve the reaction conditions. Further optimization is probably possible and additional 
experiments could give further insights into the.  
From the substrate scope containing, tert-butyl acetate, potassium acetate, disodium malonate, 
dimethyl malonate, potassium cinnamate, and β-bromostyrene only β-bromostyrene produced yield. 
The carbonyl compounds may have experiences challenges going into solution. For further studies, 
this is a factor that should be taken into consideration, by possibly changing solvents, the addition of 
additives, or a careful selection of reagents.  
Coupling between β-bromostyrene and benzyl alcohol was further studied with additional 
experiments. A nitro group in the para position of benzyl alcohol hampered the reaction and did not 
produce any yield. However, with a methoxy in the para position, the reaction did proceed. A radical 
clock experiment was performed, results from this indicates a radical mechanism and 
dehalogenation of p-chlorobenzyl alcohol seems to suggest the same thing.  
A proposed mechanism for the coupling of β-bromostyrene and benzyl alcohol was made but 
additional mechanistic studies would be needed to confirm this mechanism. As well as additional 
optimization studies of the reaction conditions should be perfomed to gain further insights into the 
reaction.
