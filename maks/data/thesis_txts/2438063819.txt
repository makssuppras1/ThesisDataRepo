Abstract
E-commerce and online sales has been growing the last decades and keep
on growing year on year. With the growing sales there is a growing demand
of items accessible through online sales sites, and thus e-commerce businesses
struggle with demand forecasting, inventory planning and delivery of products.
Alongside production technologies such as 3D printing has been developing
faster than ever making it possible soon to print end-products with only one
machine. The e-commerce giant Amazon has seen a penitential in combining
their e-commerce and 3D printing to make a service model, where 3D printers
are installed in their delivery vehicles, producing the products on the way
to the customer. This thesis presents research made on this new proposed
service model. The problem is described in detail and will be denoted the
MPRP - Mobile Production Routing Problem. A scenario has been set up, and
corresponding data instances has been generated. Instances have been solved
to optimality with a generic solver and a metaheuristic has been proposed
and implemented for optimizing the problem fast based on the Adaptive Large
Neighborhood Search ALNS framework. The heuristic has been tested on more
than 20 of the proposed data instances showing great results, with average
gaps around 5%. To check the viability of the model a comparison has been
made with stationary production hubs, showing that the mobile production
system has advantages. Lastly a dynamic update system for the metaheuristic
has been proposed, to make it ready for use in a dynamic market with new
incoming orders.

1| Background - Mobile Production Networks
1.1 The real life problem
A large part of goods bought by end-consumers today are bought via the internet
(e-commerce). As seen in ﬁgure 1 the percentage of internet driven sales are rapidly
increasing, and by Q4 2017 e-commerce in the US reached more than 10% of the
total quarterly retail sales [15]. These increasing trends are due to convenience and
lower pricing on goods bought via the internet.
Figure 1: U.S. Retail E-commerce sales as a percent of total quarterly retail sales.
The red graph is not adjusted for seasonal variation - the blue graph is adjusted for
seasonal variation. Source: U.S. Census Bureau News [15].
These Online stores do not have to have physical stores and a large staﬀto run
their business. In order to run a proﬁtable big scale e-commerce shop, the company
has to secure good end consumer pricing and a low lead time on orders (delivery
time). To be able to supply the right products at the right time, these companies
typically have to carry huge stocks of thousands of products. High stock levels are
equal to a large amount of liquidity bound in inventory, and this might be dangerous
when products are outdated or if too many products of a speciﬁc type are bought to
stock. These companies use sophisticated software to regulate their stock amounts,
but still no forecast in an open consumer driven market can be exact enough.

In order to overcome the big expenses with carrying huge stocks of items and do
inventory management, a solution is production to order (Make to Order MTO). In

1.2 New Possibilities with 3D Printing
3D Printing is developing faster than ever before [2]. New technologies are emerg-
ing every quarter and the technology is getting faster and better continuously. 3D
printing developers are looking further into combining materials, to make functional
products in one process. It is in 2018 possible to print in most common materials
(Metals, plastics, ﬂexible materials and ceramics), but also many interesting new
developments have emerged such as the integrated 3D circuit board printer devel-
oped by the company Nano Dimensions. With the possibility of printing electronics
directly into end products using only 3d printing technology, makes the portfolio of
products you can make with one machine a lot bigger.
1.2.1 Complexity is free
With 3D printing complexity of product design is free. This meaning, the complexity
of design and shape does not aﬀect the price of production of the 3D print itself.
For example printing a fully organic shaped product would cost the same as printing
a squared product of the same material and volume. This is the exact opposite of
traditional manufacturing methods, where products increase in manufacturing price
with the complexity of the design (see ﬁgure 3).

possibility to manufacture to order, will make new business models possible.
Huge companies like GE and HP have seen the possibilities with the technology and
are investing heavily in 3D Printing companies, and 3D technology development.
1.2.2 A New Business Model
Soon 3D printing technologies might be sophisticated enough for making relatively
low cost, multi-material production parts right out of the printer. This will make
it possible to make new interesting business models using MTO rather than having
huge inventories of thousands of products. A business model opportunity could be
closing down stocks and using 3D printers installed in delivery vehicles to produce
the purchased items while on the way to the customer. Not only would this give the
customer more ﬂexibility in the product designs they buy, but could also be a huge
advantage for the e-commerce companies due to the eﬀective new supply chain.
With 3D printers you eﬀectively decentralize production and eliminate transport
from production site to warehouse and warehouses can be closed (see ﬁgure 4).

1.4 Project Scope
This problem should in the end have a real-time engine being able to assign new
orders dynamically to the ﬂowing network of production vehicles. A real life working
model would need a signiﬁcant amount of data, and technologies well developed to
be able to function. The scope of this project will be the following:
• Develop a scenario and a mathematical formulation for the problem which will
be denoted the Mobile Production Routing Problem MPRP.
• Construction of relevant data instances based on the scenario and the MPRP
problem as described.
• Solve data instances to optimality through implementation in a generic solver
(GAMS CPLEX MIP).
• Propose, implement and test a metaheuristic for optimizing the MPRP.
• Propose a dynamic updating system which should work with the metaheuristic.
• Compare mobile production to solutions where production is static at produc-
tion hubs.
These areas will be covered in the following sections.

2| The Mobile Production Routing Problem
The Mobile Production Routing Problem (MPRP) will be the name describing a
problem type where delivery vehicles have production units installed, producing
MOD items on the way to the customers. Therefore a candidate solution to this
problem would both handle production scheduling for the machines installed in
the vehicles, alongside with the speciﬁc routing between customers for the delivery
vehicles. As this is not yet a system used in the industry, an imaginary scenario has
been set up to make basis for the solution approaches described later in the thesis.
2.1 Scenario
Since this is a complicated problem when applied to real life, a test-scenario has
been set up to work with during research in this project. This scenario is built to
replicate a real life possible situation, but simpliﬁed to some extent. Since the 3D
printing technologies are not developed enough to suit this application fully, and to
decrease the number of variables, it is deﬁned that only one type of 3D printer is
present in the vehicles, but this 3D printer can make all the purchasable products.
With this assumption only the printing time and capacity of the printers should be
considered in the production part of the model.
2.1.1 Production Vehicles
The production vehicles are considered autonomous cars driving from customer to
customer with a fully automated 3d printing production process installed in the cargo
space of the vehicle. This high level of automation is chosen because this will be a
very plausible scenario, when the 3D printing process is developed enough for this
type of service model. Furthermore it eliminates the need for incorporating manual
work labor and shifts into the model. The vehicle routing part of the problem, can
with these assumptions be made purely on transportation time between customers
and costs associated with traveling between customers.
2.1.2 3D Printers
The 3D printers used will be highly sophisticated systems, that can produce the
oﬀered MOD item fully. Therefore it is assumed that no pre- or postprocessing of
the items produced is needed. Hence the model can focus purely on the printing
time of the requested MOD items and the capacities of the machines available in the

2.1.3 Customers - Network
The network consists of a set of nodes representing customers, and a set of edges
representing roads between customers and distances (time between nodes).
All
customers in the network are associated with a given order. The order contains
information about: Order incoming time (when was the order put into the system),
order production time (the eﬀective machine time needed to produce the order)
and a soft deadline for when the customer expects to get the delivery of the MOD
item ordered. The deadline will not be modelled as a hard constraint, but as a
soft constraint favouring solutions where deadlines are met. Every order is seen as
a unique product so each spot in the production schedule will be labeled with a
speciﬁc customer.
2.1.4 Model inputs
Consolidating these sections, the inputs for the mathematical model becomes:
• A set of orders (with associated production times, delivery locations, order
time and deadlines)
• Vehicle starting locations
• Vehicle capacities (no. of 3D printers inside delivery Trucks)
Due to the slightly diﬀerent structure of this problem compared to normal VRP
or PRP problems, new datasets based on this scenario have been made.
A full
description on how the datasets have been constructed can be found in section 4.
2.1.5 Similarities to Other Problems
As brieﬂy mentioned the MPRP problem has similarities to both the vehicle routing
problem (VRP) and the expanded version the production routing problem (PRP).
Part of the MPRP is in essence a VRP in routing the vehicles between customers.
As described by Adulyasak [4] the production routing problem combines a VRP and
the well known lot-sizing problem (LSP). The main idea is to both optimize routing
of vehicles while also optimizing production and inventory at the production site,

3| Mathematical Model - Formulation
The mathematical model for this problem is in essence an extended version of a
vehicle routing problem (VRP) with added production scheduling in each vehicle
and a time aspect. The model is based on the scenario described above and is of
the MIP (mixed integer program) type. The proposed formulation for the mobile
production routing problem (MPRP) is deﬁned on an undirected graph G = (V, A)
where vehicle starting positions, customers and sink is represented by the nodes (V),
and the roads in between are represented by the edges (A). The sink node will be
denoted f and it is the last node of set V. Furthermore the following sets will be used:
3.1 Sets
• S is a subset of V referring to the starting positions of the vehicles
• N is a subset of V referring to all customers
• K is the set of vehicles.
• M is the set of machines available (3D printers).
In this formulation all vehicles will carry the same number of machines. The set of
nodes are arranged as follows: V = {1, ..., s, s + 1, ..., s + n, f}
3.2 Parameters
• tij - Transport time on edge i, j ∈A
• ai - Manufacturing time for order at customer i ∈N
• di - Soft deadline for delivery of order at customer i ∈N
• ei - First possible start of production - order incoming time for customer i ∈N.
• cij - Transportation cost on edge i, j ∈A.
• b - penalty cost for delivery after deadline (per time unit).

3.3 Decision Variables
• xijk - Binary variable: 1 if vehicle k travels from i to j, 0 otherwise.
• yijkm - Binary variable: 1 if order j is made on machine m in vehicle k after

3.4 Two Connected Problems
The formulation is made as a connection between a production planning problem
and vehicle routing problem. The vehicle routing problem making the route for the
vehicles and the production planning problem securing that products are produced
in the same vehicle before it arrives at the customer site.
Both problems have binary decision variables xijk and yijkm. xijk keeping track of
the speciﬁc route for vehicle k and yijkm keeping track of the production sequence
for each machine m in each vehicle k. For keeping track of time, both problems have
positive time variables qik and pikm. qik is the speciﬁc delivery time for customer
i ∈N by vehicle k ∈K. pikm is the speciﬁc starting time of production for customer
order i ∈N at machine m ∈M in vehicle k ∈K.
The vehicle and production link constraint (see section 3.6.3) links these two prob-
lems to secure that no product can be delivered before production is done for the
speciﬁc customer object.

4| Datasets
Because the MPRP is not directly comparable to other VRP problems, and because
it uses other parameters, new unique datasets have been constructed for the MPRP
problem. The datasets have been made to ﬁt the formulation described in section
3 made from the given scenario assumption (see section 2.1).A Java program has
been made to generate these randomized datasets for the MPRP to make it possible
to benchmark the heuristic on multiple data scenarios. With a series of inputs, the
program generates a dataset of the wanted size and with the number of vehicles and
machines given. The input parameters for the program are:
• number of vehicles
• number of machines in each vehicle
• number of customers
• hourly penalty cost
• Neighborhood size
• Order span
All the above parameters are given by the user and the four ﬁrst explain themselves.
The Neighborhood size, is also a parameter given by the user, determining the unit
scale size of the neighboorhood. If 10 is given as Neighborhood size, the customers
and vehicle starting locations, will be randomly distributed in a 10 by 10 length
x-y matrix. The unit itself does not represent a speciﬁc length, but they are a unit
of length measure (a unitless measure). The coordinates are used in the code for
calculating arc lengths as the euclidean distance between the points. The order span
determines in what interval the orders are going to be placed (ei ∈[0; orderspan]).
The order incoming time is also randomized within this interval.
From these parameters the program constructs the dataset as a .txt ﬁle. The gener-
ated dataset includes all parameters necessary for the model. The generated data is
formatted as seen in ﬁgure 6. This dataset is used by the meta heuristic. Further-
more the program will also construct another .txt ﬁle containing the same dataset
but formated for direct input in GAMS which will be used as the modelling program
for the generic solver to ﬁnd optimal solutions (see section 5).

The ﬁrst four data-points (vehicles, customers, machines and hourly penalty), are
all given user input data. The next 3xN matrix corresponds to customer, vehicle
and sink positions in the euclidean space with corresponding x and y coordinates.
x and y coordinates for the nodes are generated randomly as positive integers be-
tween 0 and the given neighborhood size. Transportation distances on all arcs are
calculated as the euclidean distance between nodes in the graph. These distances
are all listed in the tij matrix. By default the distance to the sink node is set to
0 as this node is an artiﬁcial node only used to ﬁnish the tour and production se-
quence. The production times ai for products that have to be delivered at customer
i are random integer printing times between 2 and 10 time units. The deadline for
delivery of item i is generated as ei + ai plus 2 to 7 time units of slack time. The
deadline is modelled in this way to symbolize diﬀerence in customer urgence. The
cost of traveling on an arc i, j has been made diﬀerent from the transpiration time
due to real life scenarios, where some roads might have a higher cost using either
due to diﬀerent road types or toll roads. The components of cij is made on basis of
tij multiplied by a random decimal factor. This factor is varying with datasets.
The names of datasets follow the following naming standard (AxBxC_Dlocp), where
A is number of customers, B is number of vehicles, C is number of machines in each
vehicle, D is an identiﬁer for same setup datasets. The suﬃxes mean the following:
c: increased cost on all edges - o: increased orderspan - l: larger distance in network
(map size) - p: no penalty for late delivery. Datasets can have any suﬃx mixtures.

6| Metaheuristic Implementation
As generic solvers such as CPLEX with GAMS as used in section 5 are typically
slow with bigger datasets and very expensive, a metaheuristic solution approach
will be used. A heuristic searches the neighborhood of potential better solutions
by generating feasible solutions and checking their objective impact. When a bet-
ter solution is found this solution will typically be the basis for further search. A
heuristic typically ﬁnds good solutions fast, but the global optimal solution is how-
ever not necessarily found. Because of the fast nature of heuristics they are a lot
more eﬃcient with larger instances and therefore a god ﬁt for problems where we
have a lot of variables and little time to ﬁnd a solution. This is also why a lot of
recent research for the VRP, VRPTW and similar high complexity problems have
been within metaheuristic solution approaches [14].
If the Amazon service model on which the MPRP problem is based should become
reality the characteristics of a metaheuristic is a good ﬁt. A lot of orders would
have to be handled, and vehicle routes and production schedules would have to be
updated fast and frequently. Calculation times of generic solvers as seen in table 3,
would simply not be feasible for a fully working service model. Therefore the model
has been implemented in a metaheuristic.
A lot of good metaheuristics have been proposed and yielded good results for similar
problems such as the production routing problem (PRP) as compared by adulyasak
[4]. The PRP is seen as the closest comparable to the MPRP as the PRP also
combines production and vehicle routing. Heuristic frameworks like GRASP show
good computation times for this problem, whereas the ALNS heuristic overall reaches
the best solutions of the Boudia et al. [3] benchmark instances for the PRP problem.
As the best possible solutions are desired the ALNS metaheuristic will be used to
optimize the MPRP problem.
6.1 ALNS
The chosen metaheuristic for implementation of the MPRP is Adaptive Large Neigh-
borhood Search (ALNS). ALNS was proposed by Røpke and Pisinger for variations
of the VRP problem [9] in 2005 and it has been used and researched widely since.
The ALNS framework is based upon large neighborhood search (LNS) proposed by
Shaw [13]. In an ALNS framework, the solution is iteratively destroyed to be re-

The ALNS framework shown above is the standard framework used for many types
of problems with speciﬁc customizations to ﬁt the problem. At the beginning of the
algorithm a feasible solution is constructed with a construction heuristic which in
many cases are greedy. On the basis of this feasible solution, the main optimization
while-loop will start. The stop criterion for the optimization can either be number
of optimization iterations or a set amount of time, whatever is more suitable for
the speciﬁc problem. In the optimization a new candidate solution is generated by
destroying the current accepted solution and repairing it again. Multiple destroy
and repair heuristics will be available for diversifying the search. For choosing the
speciﬁc destroy and repair heuristics used in a speciﬁc iteration, a roulette wheel
based selection is used. Each heuristic has an associated score or weight determin-
ing the probability of it getting chosen in the roulette selection. In this way good
heuristics can be given higher probability of getting chosen. During the iterations,
heuristics granting new better solutions will get their scores boosted and scores of
bad performing heuristics can be worsened. This adaptively changes the probability
of heuristics getting chosen based purely on their performance on the speciﬁc prob-
lem and dataset instance on hand.
After the destroy and repair heuristics have generated a new candidate solution,
simulated annealing (SA) is used to choose if the solution is approved or not. Simu-
lated annealing makes it possible to accept even worse solutions than the best found

6.3 Start Solution - Construction Heuristic
The construction heuristic greedyConstruction() is used before the main opti-
mization loop is started. This heuristic makes a feasible starting candidate solution
based on a greedy approach. As on time delivery is seen as an important objective
and because it is typically penalized quite heavily in the objective function, the
greedy construction heuristic is based on the customer order deadline.
Page 33 of 60
==PAGE:37==

DTU Management Engineering
DTU
Spring 2018
The heuristic runs in a while loop inserting the next customer with the highest
possible deadline.
The customer request in question is inserted in the cheapest
possible position (vehicle and production schedule) based on both delivery time and
edge cost. In the construction phase, customers can only be inserted in the end of
the schedule, whereas in the insertion heuristics (see section 6.5), insertions will be
evaluated on all available possibilities inside tours as well.
6.4 Removal Heuristics
This section will describe the destroy or removal heuristics used in this ALNS im-
plementation. The destroy heuristics are important because they are the creators of
new opportunity for the next candidate solution. Therefore three diﬀerent removal
heuristics have been set up, each with diﬀerent attributes and targets, to diversify
the search and to create new opportunities. The three removal heuristics chosen are
random removal, worst removal and Shaw removal.
All removal heuristics remove customers from their spot in the vehicle tour and the
corresponding production schedule spot so they are entirely taken out of the solu-
tion. The customer is then put into an array called requestBank. The requestBank
stores information about which customers have been taken out from the solution
and is later used by the insertion heuristics to know what customers to consider. To
diversify the search the number of customers removed from the solution is a random
number between 20% and 70% of the total number of customers in the solution.
The speciﬁc percentages were found during parameter tuning (see section 6.8). The
function numberOfRemoval() are used by all the removal heuristics and returns the
number they should remove.
A randomization parameter p has been incorporated to diversify the search even
further. The p parameter is used for the worst and shaw removal heuristics and
gives a possibility for them not to choose the worst or most related nodes - further
will be described in the worst removal and shaw removal sections. The inspiration
for this diversiﬁcation comes from Røpke and Pisinger [10].
6.4.1 Random Removal
The ﬁrst removal heuristic is a random removal heuristic (randomRemoval()). This
heuristic purely looks at how many customers it has to remove, and removes random
customers within the full range of customers. The random removal heuristic does not

random removal is good for diversifying the search and making it opportunistic. This
makes the neighborhood signiﬁcantly larger and the algorithm therefore searches
more of the solution space.
6.4.2 Worst Removal
The worst removal heuristic (worstRemoval()) considers the objective impact of
each customer in the current solution. The idea is to remove high impacting solutions
to ﬁnd better solutions. Objective impact can for the MPRP both be high insertion
cost due to the edge travelling cost or the delay of delivery. Both are considered
equally meaning the worst removal heuristic chooses from the overall worst objective
impacting customers. The objective impact is calculated as follows: Let s, t, u be
three customers in a part of the vehicle tour sequence (..s −→t −→u..). Edge costs
are given by cij, deadline by di, delivery time by αi and the pr. time unit penalty
for late arrival is b. Then the objective impact of customer t is calculated as follows:
(6.4.1)
impactt = (cs,t + ct,u −cs,u) + (αt −dt) · b
Note that impact can turn negative in this calculation, but this is on purpose. As
the delivery time and deadline are seen as important objectives, on time deliveries
are not as often removed with this heuristic. The ﬁrst part of the calculation corre-
sponds to the edge cost diﬀerence between the customer t being in the solution and
the customer not being in the solution.

6.4.3 Shaw Removal
The Shaw removal heuristic shawRemoval() considers relatedness of requests and
removes these accordingly. The Shaw removal heuristic was proposed by Shaw in
1998 [13] for optimizing vehicle routing problems with the LNS framework. The
Shaw removal is good due to a high likelihood of related customers beeing able to
be swapped, granting more possibilities for the insertion heuristics.
Relatedness can be measured on many diﬀerent parameters or can be a weighted
sum of the diﬀerent parameters. In the MPRP implementation, the relatedness is
measured purely on the production time of the two customers measured up against
each other. This is chosen due to the nature of the insertion heuristics described in
section 6.5, where insertions are primarily looking at when production time is avail-

relatednessi,r = |ai −ar|

6.5 Insertion Heuristics
The insertion heuristics of the ALNS framework rebuild the solution after the re-
moval heuristics has taken out requests from the solution (requestBank[]). The
insertion heuristics are therefore important because they are in charge of rebuilding
the solution to generate a potential better candidate solution. Again to diversify
the search two insertion heuristics has been implemented. These will be described
in the following sections.
6.5.1 Greedy Insertion
The greedy insertion heuristic (greedyInsertion()) is a purely greedy heuristic
choosing the next customer to insert based on the objective impact of the insertion,
choosing the lowest insertion impact request ﬁrst. This heuristic has a main while-
loop running until all requests from the request bank have been inserted into the
solution again. When calculating the insertion impact, the ﬁrst available production
spot in a each vehicle is found. after this, all possible insertions into the vTour
after the production is done are tested. When insertion is tested, the subsequent
customers in the vTour might also get later delivery - this is also a part of the impact
of the inserted request. So all in all the greedy insertion heuristic measures impact
on the total objective function impact an insertion will lead to. A pseudo code for
the greedy insertion heuristic can be seen in algorithm 5.

7| Comparison - Mobile vs. Stationary
To measure the eﬀectivity of having production units or 3D printers in the vehicles
producing on the way to the customers, a similar model has been set up where the
only diﬀerence is, that the printers or production units are installed stationary in
the starting nodes of the vehicles (Production Hubs).
To make a fair comparison between the two production possibilities (mobile and
stationary), It is assumed that all orders are given at time zero meaning ei = 0 ∀i ∈

to choose the cheapest route in the network without caring about production when
vehicles are on routes. The mobile version can start driving from time period 0 but
must be ready with production before delivery. The only new constraint needed

8| Discussion and Conclusion
Based on the Amazon patent [6], the MPRP mobile production routing problem has
been deﬁned and a likely scenario has been set up to describe it. As the technology
of today do not fully support a capable automatic production system in vehicles,
the scenario was built upon an expected scenario in 5-10 years.
As no research could be found on this speciﬁc topic new data instances has been
generated to ﬁt the scenario described with imaginary customers, networks and
production vehicles. All production and network related parameters are generated
within reason of what could be realistic.
The MPRP was implemented and solved with the the CPLEX MIP solver through
GAMS modelling program.
Multiple data instances generated with the dataset
maker program was tested in a variety of sizes. Unfortunately bigger problems were
unsolvable on the hardware available. Typically the hardware available ran out of
memory or the convergence was too slow. Optimal solutions were found to more
than 20 datasets which could then be used as benchmarks for the metaheuristic.
It was found, that the problem got hard to solve when number of customers were
high and when penalties for late delivery were high. Hence the larger datasets with
obtained optimal solutions were with no penalty for late delivery, making it more or
less a VRP problem. To obtain optimal solutions for larger datasets, decomposition
or better hardware should be used.
A metaheuristic with the ALNS framework has been proposed for optimizing and
ﬁnding fast good solutions for the MPRP has been proposed. With 3 removal heuris-
tics and 2 insertion heuristics in a simulated annealing framework, the metaheuristic
showed positive capabilities of ﬁnding good solutions fast for the MPRP. For many
of the smaller data instances, the optimal solutions could be obtained with the meta-
heuristic, and for larger problems the average gap was between 2 to 10 % from the
optimal solution found with the generic solver. On three data instances the average
gap was found to be more then 10% primarily due to a very small objective value
of the optimal solutions for these problems.
Furthermore an easy to implement change has been proposed to the metaheuristic
to make it able to dynamically update and optimize on an existing schedule with
new orders coming in. This would be done by simply locking orders that are un-
Page 52 of 60
==PAGE:56==
Master Thesis - Mobile Production Routing Problem
DTU Management Engineering
DTU
Spring 2018
der production or on the way to the customer at the time of update. Furthermore
requests that have already been delivered can be deleted from the solution. From
there the new order would be put into the request bank and the rest of the schedule
would act as a starting solution for the problem. The implemented ALNS MPRP
metaheuristic would then be used for optimizing the new situation with new cus-
tomers.
Lastly a brief comparable has been made between the mobile production routing
problem and a production ﬁrst problem. In the production ﬁrst problem, 3D print-
ers would be installed at hubs, producing all orders before the vehicles drive towards
the customers with their orders. With penalties for late deliveries the MPRP showed
far greater results than the production ﬁrst model. However a more fair compari-
son would be to implement a PRP like scenario, where vehicles can return to the
production hub to pick up new orders dynamically. This would yield better results,
but due to the time optimizing factor of producing on the way to the customer, the
MPRP would probably still yield the best results.

9| Future Work
During the work with the MPRP problem many interesting topics has been covered
with this project, but still many would be interesting to investigate further. It would
be desirable to work more with the data instances and get the generic solver to solve
larger data instances of 50, 100 and 200 customers with penalties for late deliveries.
In order to reach solutions for these sizes of data instances with delivery penalty
the MPRP should be solved on more powerful hardware, or decomposition methods
could be used.
Regarding the metaheuristic implementation, it would in line with the paragraph
above be interesting to test it with even bigger datasets. Furthermore testing other
metaheuristic frameworks like GRASP would be interesting. The GRASP meta-
heuristic framework has likewise showed great results on similar problems like the
VRP and the PRP problem.
From a business perspective it would furthermore be relevant to measure the MPRP
model up against a modiﬁed PRP problem, where inventories carry 3D printers to
produce speciﬁc MOD items on demand. This meaning that the vehicles can return
to the hub to pickup new orders.
From a mechanical and production viewpoint it would be interesting to research
what machines and technologies could be used in a mobile production network.
Both what 3D printers should be used, how the post-processing should be done and
how the packaging and delivery system within the vehicle should work.
Furthermore it would be interesting to expand the MPRP formulation and add other
things to make it even more realistic. This could be charging stations or material
gathering for the 3D printers. One should bear in mind that these add-ons would
make the model more complex and harder to solve.

C| Original Project plan and Description
3D Printing and transportation technology is under rapid development. Many see
the possibilities of 3D Printing being a new possibility for decentralizing production
and moving it closer to the end customer. Bigger companies like GE, HP and Ama-
zon are investing heavily in the technology and the development of the technology
is transforming fast.
in 2015 Amazon made a patent application on a service model using 3D Printers
in cargo trucks - 3D Printing the end product on the way to the customer. This
induces a combined vehicle routing problem and production problem, where each
truck has a 3D Printing capacity. The problem has similarities to know problems
with production hubs in some nodes of a network, but now the production capacity
is present in the trucks ﬂowing in the network.
This master thesis project will be based on this service model covering:
• Problem description
• Construction of simpliﬁed scenarios with relevant data instances for optimiza-
tion (network, capacities and trucks)
• A mathematical model for ﬁnding optimized routes and production schedules
for the system.
• An implementation of the problem using an exact approach ﬁnding optimal
solutions for benchmarking of heuristic approach
• An implementation of the problem with a relevant metaheuristic approach (for
fast rescheduling when new orders come in) (Java)
