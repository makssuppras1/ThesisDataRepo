Abstract
Purpose: To resolve pressure gradients across a 70% stenotic vessel both by
numerical simulation and experimentation. Especially with focus on catheter
sixe and entrance approach.
Method: A phantom representing 70% stenosed vessel was build and catheter
measurements were performed at 3 axial positions on the vessel; before, middle
and after the stenoses. An identical CAD ﬁle was constructed and subjeted to
Finite Element modeling in Comsol to obtain reference values for the catheter
measurements.
Results: It is concluded that entering the vessel with the ﬂow is the optimal
approach for accuracy when estimating the pressure drop.
The accuracy of
estimates obtained going against the ﬂow are highly dependent on the relation
between catheter and lumen size (RR). Further it is concluded that catheter
measurements in general are not ideal for estimating systolic pressure drops, at
lest not with the RR values investigated in this study.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Blood pressure (BP) measurement has been an important clinical diagnostic tool
for decades. But how best to estimate BP in-vivo? There is evidence that inva-
sive catheter measurements of BP are more accurate than noninvasive methods
such as oscillometry and tonometry [4], and that aortic BP is a better predictor
and indicator of cardiovascular pathologies than peripheal BP measurements
[5].
Catheter based BP measurements are particularly advantageous when assessing
diseased regions in the cardiac tree such as stenotic heart valves or atheroscle-
rotic plaques. The alternative when investigating diseased artery segments is
non-invasive angiography by ultrasound (US), CT or MR. Non-invasive angiog-
raphy of stenosed arteries presents several problems with respect to evaluating
the clinical signiﬁcance of the stenosis. This is due to the fact, that such inves-
tigations mainly build on assessing the diameter or area reduction of the artery,
which is associated with high interobserver variability, and that the inﬂuence
of diﬀuse or minor stenoses can be diﬃcult to quantify even though they might
have signiﬁcant hemodynamic eﬀects [6].
This project’s aim is to develop a catheterization lab to perform pressure mea-
surements on an artiﬁcial model of a stenosed blood vessel under both constant
and pulsating ﬂow conditions using a clinical ﬂuid ﬁlled pressure transducer
(PT). In addition, an identical experiment will be run using numerical simula-
tion.
When measuring body signals an important factor to account for is physiologic
reactance, which in short is deﬁned as: The eﬀect (usually negative) of a mea-
suring system on the phenomenon being measured. When measuring BP using

a catheter, the very presence of the catheter will aﬀect the ﬂow characteristics.
Several studies agree that an important parameter when assessing catheter in-
duced errors is the relation between vessel radius and catheter radius [7][8][9],
in the following denoted radis ratio (RR). Another point of interest is the radial
position of the catheter. During constant ﬂow, a catheter will fall to rest in some
arbitrary radial position which is more or less uncontrollable to the operator,
and during pulsatile ﬂow, a catheter may ﬂap around extensively. A mathe-
matical study, [10], concludes that radial position is signiﬁcant when measuring
pressure drop over an arbitrary stenosis if the catheter radius is large compared
to the vessel radius. But what constitutes a large relation?
BP recordings by ﬂuid ﬁlled catheter is an old technique and the theory behind
it is well documented and tested [3][2][11][12][13][14]. To begin with, this report
will brieﬂy outline the most relevant points of the theory before anextensive
analysis of the static and dynamic accuracy of the measuring system is presented
under the method chapter. This is done to ensure the credibility of the results
presented. As noted by [3], it is important to remember that one can not be
sure that the measuring system has adequate dynamic response just by looking
at the pressure waveform recorded.
It is known that noninvasively measured blood pressure underestimates ”true”
blood pressure measured invasively [4]. But one of the objectives of the project
at hand is to investigate the actual truth to the invasive measurements. In light
of this, the questions asked by this study can be summarized in the following
way: What is the scale of the pressure drops across a stenotic segment and how
precise can pressure drops be measure with a catheter? And more speciﬁcally,
what are the eﬀects of catheter size and catheter entrance approach, and are
these eﬀects signiﬁcant in relation to the ﬁrst question?

2.1.1
Pressure transducers
In general, pressure measurements can be divided into three categories:
Absolute: Pressure is measured in relation to vacuum.
Gauge: Pressure is measured relative to atmospheric pressure.
Diﬀerential pressure: Pressure is measured relative to a speciﬁc resting value.
The essential part of this project is to use a PT to measure the gauge pressure
within the phantom and the relate the pressure values to obtain a credible
estimate of the diﬀerential pressure across a stenotic segment of a tube. The
fundamental part of clinical PT is a Wheatstone bridge strain gauge. Figure 2.1
shows a simpliﬁed drawing of a ﬂuid ﬁlled pressure transducer for blood pressure
measurements. The diaphragm is much more compliant than the rest of the
system, and when the diaphragm deﬂects in response to a pressure change, the
displacement causes a change in impedance on the output of the bridge, which
can be measured and converted to a pressure drop.

2.1.2
The blood pressure pulse
The pressure pulse (PP) is a cyclic waveform and by Fourier theory, it can be
separated into multiplies of the fundamental frequency (harmonics), which will
have varying amplitude corresponding to the signal content at each frequency
(see ﬁgure 3.8). The fundamental frequency of the resting heart is around 1 Hz.
A ﬂuid ﬁlled PT measuring system will need to have a ﬂat frequency response at
the harmonics which contain majority the PP signal depending on the level of
precision required. A thorough study on harmonic analysis of PP [16], conclude
that all signiﬁcant information of the PP is contained from 0-20 Hz. Another
study [13], states that a measuring system should be able to reproduce the ﬁrst
6 −8 harmonics of the signal without distortion corresponding to fN = 12 −16
Hz for a 2 Hz cardiac output. In general, as long as the bandwidth is kept

when introducing the catheter against the ﬂow. When the catheter faces up-

ﬁrst term of (2.7). This means that the transducer will experience a higher
pressure than the true pressure in the vessel. When pressure is measured at

some of the blocked ﬂow will not manifest as a pressure increase but rather as

estimates the True pressure drop, just barely, within a standard deviation (see

5.3
Limitations and recommendations
5.3.1
Catheter positioning
It is very unlikely that the catheter is positioned perfectly concentric within
the vessel when recording pressure. The 3d model allows for the catheter to
be moved in the radial direction and thus placing the catheter in all sorts of
eccentric positions. One focus should be the after position of the catheter with
regards to the diﬀerence between laying in the the jet stream or being pushed out
in the calm region behind the narrowing. From the US it is virtually impossible
to asses the 3d position of the catheter hence at the moment, one can only guess
which of the 2 situations the catheter settles in. Also, the catheter shape could
==PAGE:64==
46
Discussion
be changed in Comsol to better imitate the actual shape of the catheters (see
ﬁgure 3.5) but the extra insight gained from this is questionable. The catheter
is harder to position during AF ﬂow, a statement which is boosted by the errors
presented in ﬁgures 5.1-5.5. The most robust way to secure the integrity of
axial position between measurements would be to use the GW every time the
catheter is repositioned. This method is very time consuming however, as the
transducer/catheter system needs to be disassembled and ﬂushed every time the
GW is reintroduced. Speak to any physician and she will agree, but for research
purposes it may be worth it to spend the extra time. Alternatively, place the
catheter at the three positions of interest with the GW and then clearly mark
them on the proximal end of the catheter before extruding the GW. But so far,
a suitable marker for this purpose has not emerged.
5.3.2
Potential errors
During US recordings of the phantom under pulsating ﬂow it became apparent
that the walls are not perfectly rigid, but will distend a bit under the the chang-
ing pressure conditions. This adds to the realism of the experiment in terms of
simulating in-vivo measurements, but weaken both the logic presented in (3.6)
and the governing Comsol model. As seen from ﬁgure 3.4 the PT is fastened
with tape to a wooden block positioned at the same height as the phantom. This
setup is not at all ideal. Because the focus of the study is diﬀerential pressure
measurements it is not overly important where exactly the PT is positioned as
long as it does not move during recordings. Elementary theory predicts that a
vertical displacement of ∆h = 1 mm will cause a pressure artifact
δp = ρg∆h = 10Pa.
(5.2)
The PT needs to be handled throughout a measurement session, e.g. when dur-
ing FF and RF, and the tape contraption unfortunately allows for the transducer
to wiggle around, most likely displacing the transducer several mm regardless
of the care taken by the operator. Ideally the PT should be screwed to the
wooden block or something similar. This would ease the data collection and
ensure that no pressure artifacts arise because of vertical displacements. There
exist an extra potential error associated with the static calibration, which has
not been accounted for. Ideally several data points should be estimated at each
step in ﬁgure 3.6 and preferably using a higher water column to thoroughly
assess the standard deviation on C. In conjunction with this, only superﬁcial
statistical methods has been applied for interpreting the results.
To further
illuminate the spread of the data, both with respect to equipment error and
between consecutive observations a thorough analysis of variance should be per-
formed. Especially if more data is recorded in the future. This study clearly

5.4
Clinical considerations
This study applies what is in the clinic normally referred to as the pull-back
method (PB) (for obvious reasons). Another common procedure in the clinic is
using a double lumen approach with two transducers to measure the pressure
simultaneous at two points (2T). An apparent advantage of the 2T method
is that one largely avoids the ﬂow induced δp when measuring AF. But this
approach puts even larger importance on RR because now the catheter is an
integrated part of the entire stenotic segment. As can be seen from ﬁgures 4.3-4.2
the physiological reactance is quite pronounced when inserting a 4F catheter,
thus a physician employing the 2T method must expect to over estimate the
pressure drop if RR ≥0.176 regardless of whether the entrance is WF or AF.
Figure 5.9 show the Comsol simulations for the 1.8F catheter at all positions.
It is evident that the catheter eﬀect is smaller, as expected, but from ﬁgure
ﬁgure 5.9 it is not entirely clear if the accuracy of the 1.8F measurements is
adequate in estimating the true pressure drop. An extensive analysis of the
two Comsol results would be needed to fully clarify if an RR = 0.075 manifests
as a signiﬁcant physiologic reactance when applying 2T method, but a study
investigating a similar matter [7] concludes that the systolic pressure will be
overestimated by as much as 20-30%.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Before/after pressure drops across a 70% stenotic segment exhibit magnitudes
481 (±3) Pa for constant ﬂow and MPD = −79 (±1) Pa, SPD = 299 (±2)
Pa during pulsating ﬂow when estimated with FEM modelling. Catheter size
proved most important when entering the vessel AF, where a radius ratio, RR =
0.167, signiﬁcantly underestimated the pressure drop whereas an RR = 0.075
eﬀectively reproduced the True pressure drop. When entering the vessel WF,
both simulation and experimentation showed that the diﬀerence in catheter size
is not important with regard to pressure drop estimation. From the Comsol
simulation it can be concluded that the radial point of measurements does not
aﬀect the pressure drop estimate in any signiﬁcant way.
The combined error estimate from all potential error sources throughout the
analysis has not been fully resolved. The pressure drops measured by catheter
are only compared to the simulation values in view of the error estimate of
the recording equipment. But the standard deviation attributed to the static
calibration factor and between repeated measurements were not included in
the analysis.
The ﬁrst is probably insigniﬁcant, but the latter could poten-
tially aﬀect the conclusion drawn by this study, especially during pulsating ﬂow
where signiﬁcant inter-observation variations are most likely to occur. More data
should be collected and a complete analysis of variance performed to illuminate
the full picture. And a complete frequency response test characterizing the sys-
tem transfer functions is needed to fully understand the dynamic accuracy of
the system
An overall advise from this study is that a WF pull back procedure is consistently
accurate when estimating MPD drops across a stenosis.
Additionally, if the
catheter lumen size is kept small; RR ≤0.075, the AF approach will accurately

the high gradients present in the ﬂow at this time instant.

Appendix B
Comsol speciﬁcations
All models was solved using a direct solver as oppose to an iterative solver.
Generally iterative solver will compute faster and use less memory than direct
solvers, but they are not as stable. It was found that for some catheter posi-
tions, iterative solvers would diverge and fail to ﬁnd a solution. Speciﬁcally the
parallel direct sparse solver (PARDISO) is used for all models in stead of the
default multifrontal massively parallel sparse direct solver (MUMPS), because
PARDISO was found to be a little faster. The default error tolerance of 10−3
was applied on all models.
Some models are solved on a ﬁne mesh and other on normal mesh. Fine mesh
is preferred in order to increase the resolution of the pressure curves applied in
the results section. Increasing the mesh resolution of course also increases the
memory load and can increase the computation time dramatically, which is why
a ﬁne mesh is not always feasible.
The larger resolution of the ﬁne mesh also improves the models performance
around the sharp edges of the catheter. Sharp edges are notorious FEM prob-
lems because the solution will tend to diverge because of the large gradients
associated with these. The Comsol function ﬁllet is used to round the edges of
the catheter. The actual catheter applied in experimentation has sharp edges
so using a ﬁllet is a necessary trade-oﬀbetween computational eﬀectiveness and
real life imitation. A ﬁllet radius of 0.5 mm is used on all 4F models. This is
the safe choice, some of the models described below will fail if a lower radius
is attempted.
For 1.8F models the safe choice is 0.3 mm, which is also the
maximum ﬁllet radius physically possible for such a small catheter.

against ﬂow
Having the catheter on the same side as the inlet presented signiﬁcant conver-
gence problems for all the solvers. Also, Study 2 was not able to solve the small
1.8F catheter against ﬂow model. The DD appeared to be able to solve the
models, but at an impractically slow convergence rate (computation time > 12
h). In stead a time-dependent solver was used to calculate a solution applying
the constant Newtonian iteration procedure with a damping factor 1. A ramp
function (location = 0, slope = 5, cutoﬀ= 1) was used to gradually apply the
inlet condition as also described in section 3.5.2. The model was solved from 0
to 1.2 s. To make sure that the solution has reached steady state at t = 1.2 s,
the model was initially set to run for a very long time period. It was found that
the model is already at steady state around t = 0.8 : 1 s. The constant new-
tonian procedure was used together with the Anderson Acceleration method,
which increases the eﬃciency of time stepping in ﬂuid dynamic problems [24].

Appendix C
Transducer preparation
The following is a step-by-step list on how the measuring system was prepared
prior to measuring.
1. Connect the unpressurized fenwel bag to the PT through the 3SC on the
back side of the PT.
2. With both the 3SC closed (always meaning closed to the exterior en-
vironment), the system is ﬁlled with water by pulling the FF tap. Be
advised that liquid will now drip from the distal end of the tubing. Filling
the system using gravity rather than the fenwell bag is recommended by
physicians at Rigshospitalet to minimize the initial buildup of air bubbles.
3. Pressurize the fenwel bag to 300 mmHg.
4. Always rinse the GW before inserting it into the catheter because of two
reasons: Mainly to rinse of dust etc.
which will otherwise accumulate
inside the catheter and can be propelled into the system or block the
catheter. Also, some GW are coated with hydrophilic material and will
slide much better through the catheter if wetted before use.
5. Insert the catheter/GW into the system through the water tight membrane
on the sheath and advance into the phantom.
Make sure to hold the
proximal end up high to avoid BMF to pour out of the system
6. When the catheter/GW is positioned satisfactorily, extract the GW from
the system and connect the proximal end of the catheter to the PT tubing.
Be aware that it is quite time consuming to reinsert the GW so be thorough
will it is there.
==PAGE:80==
62
Transducer preparation
7. Zero the transducer by closing the front 3SC to the ﬂow model, thus
exposing the PT to atmospheric pressure. Assure that the transducer is
positioned at the same height as the catheter tip. After zeroing, the 3SC
is turned back to the closed position.
8. A syringe with a mount that ﬁts ﬁrmly on the 3SC is supplied with most
PT sets. Fit the syringe to the valve of the front 3SC. Close the 3SC to
the transducer side. Use the syringe to suck out any air bubbles in the
catheter. Keep going until BMF is visible inside the syringe. After this,
close the 3SC and dismount the syringe.
9. Perform a Rocket Flush (RF) on the catheter dome. A study, [25], found
that performing initial RF before measurements with clinical PT results
in the best dynamic response of the system compared to regular FF. The
syringe is ﬁlled with demineralized water (care is taken to exclude air
bubbles) and attached to the back 3SC. The back 3SC is closed in the
Fenwell direction and while the FF tap is pulled, 5-10 mL of water are
emptied from the syringe in a powerful thrust, thus pushing all BMF and
any microbubbles present in the PT dome into the system.
10. If any air bubbles are still present; repeat.
11. Close both 3SC and dismount the syringe.
12. The system is now ready to record pressure. When doing long measure-
ments, perform regular FF to verify that the system ﬁdelity remains op-
timal.
The above list is formulated with assistance from clinical personnel at Rigshos-
pitalet as well as [18].
