Abstract
Recent studies have showed that polypehnol oxidases (PPOs) are capable of oxidizing
lignocellulose-derived phenolic compounds and lignin, furthermore it has also been showed
that PPO-activated lignin building blocks are capable of enhancing lytic polysaccharide
monooxygenase (LPMO)-driven cellulose degradation. However little work has been devoted
to study PPOs in the context of biomass conversion, as they are often associated with non-
desired post-harvest discoloration.
This thesis provides a framework for discovering and characterizing new PPOs, closely
related to the well studied MtPPO7 and investigates whether these enzymes are able to
prime LPMOs with electrons in a cascade reaction system for cellulose degradation.
In this thesis we successfully expressed and purified two PPOs from Myceliophthora ther-
mophila, namely MtPPO-010 and MtPPO-809. We compared their substrate specifity, using
UV-Vis activity assays, Oxytherm and HPLC-MS. Additionally we propose various struc-
tures for possible products of these enzymes on 4 different substares: Guaiacol, Catechol,
3-methoxycatechol and Ferulic acid.
Furthermore we present that the catalytic products of MtPPO-809 on Guaiacol can
enable NcAA9C activation and thus cellulose depolymerization, using MALDI-TOF.
Potentially, these results contribute to a better understanding of lignocellulose decon-
struction and supports the research of environmentally friendly biomass utilization in a
circular bioeconomy.

Introduction
3.1
Background
The valorization of lignocellulosic biomass has been gaining more and more attention
in recent years in the context of a circular bioeconomy.
Biomass is a highly abundant,
renewable resource, which can be converted into a wide range of valuable products, such
as chemicals, biofuels and other materials. Biorefineries and enzyme technology is the key
towards a technology transition, they are notable alternatives to the competing fossil-based
technologies and therefore help deliver the United Nation’s Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs).
Enzymatic degradation of polysaccharides is a crucial step in valorizing biomass through
industrial biorefining. It has been shown that polysaccharide mono-oxygenases (LPMOs) are
capable of disrupting the structure of recalcitrant cellulose, by this disintegration, they can
promote the further disruption of cellulose by other conventional hydrolases. These oxida-
tive enzymes require a reducing power and H2O2 to degrade polysaccharides, but how those
two components can be efficiently delivered to LPMOs remains challenging when they are
applied in biomass refining settings. Recent evidence shows that enzymes active on lignin
and biomass-derived phenolic compounds, such as polyphenol oxidases (PPOs) can boost
LPMO activity, suggesting a possible correlation between lignin modification and cellulose
degradation by fungi [1].
PPOs are not traditionally studied in the context of biomass conversion, as they are often
associated with non-desired post-harvest browning. However recent studies have showed
that PPOs are capable of oxidizing lignocellulosic-derived phenolic compounds and lignin,
this unraveled new possibilities for PPOs in biomass conversion application.
[1].
PPOs
can be divided into two distinct groups, so called "short" and "long" PPOs. MtPPO7 from
Myceliophthora thermophila belongs to the groups of "short" PPOs and shows an uncommon
feature when compared to mushroom tyrosinase (model "long" PPO), it shows preference
for methoxylated phenolic compounds [1]. Another short-type PPO from M. thermophila
has already been characterized [2] and shown to act on methoxylated phenolic compounds.
3.2
Thesis statement
Our hypothesis is that other short-type M. thermophila PPOs closely related to MtPPO7
also displays the ability to oxidize model biomass-derived methoxylated phenolic compounds
and to prime LPMO activity on cellulose in the presence of such phenolic compounds.

Objectives
3.3
Objectives
This project’s goal is to discover and characterize new enzymes that can fuel LPMOs
with electrons in a cascade reaction system for cellulose depolymerization.
Novel fungal PPOs predicted to be involved in the oxidation of lignocellulosic biomass-
derived phenolic compounds will be heterologously expressed and purified, which will then
be characterized individually and in coupled enzyme reactions with LPMOs for cellulose
degradation.
The 3 PPOs selected for this study belong to the "short-type" PPOs, closely-related to
MtPPO7, making them interesting targets to search for enzymes that might be active on
biomass-derived phenolic compounds and able to prime LPMOs.

Background literature
4.1
Lignocellulose for bioeconomy
4.1.1
Bioeconomy and circular economy
Bioeconomy is a term that refers to the sustainable use of biological resources and
technologies to produce goods and services, and it revolves around a technology transition,
especially biotechnology. It incorporates a wide range of sectors, such as agriculture, forestry,
fisheries, food, feed, and bio-based products. Bioeconomy aims to provide economic and
social benefits while minimizing the negative environmental impacts of resource use [3].
Circular economy can be defined as an economic model based on the principles of main-
taining the value of resources for as long as possible and minimizing waste and pollution [4].
This concept can be applied to bio-based production.
Bio-based residue products, such as lignocellulosic biomass represents an enormous
amount of waste on a global scale. This nutrient rich waste, however is often composted
or incinerated for the production of fertilizers or power. The effective valorization of these
organic residues by biotechnological processes is a key step towards a circular bioeconomy
[5].
4.1.2
Lignocellulose composition
Lignocellulose refers to plant dry matter, it is the most abundant organic material in
the biosphere. It is a complex organic material that is made up of three main polymers:
cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin (Figure 1).
Page 3 of 41
==PAGE:9==
4.1
Lignocellulose for bioeconomy
Figure 1: The structure of the three components of lignocellulosic biomass, namely cellulose,
hemicellulose and lignin [6].
Cellulose is a linear homopolymer made up of D-glucose monomers, linked via β-(1,4)
glycosidic bonds, with a degree of polymerization up to 15 000 [7]. In the cell wall of plants,
cellulose can be found in microfibrils with 5-50 nm diameter, this structure is stabilized by
intra- and inter-chain hydrogen bonds, making cellulose a stable polymer [7].
Hemicellulose is a more branched polysaccharide, made up of a variety of monosaccha-
rides, including D-xylose, D-mannose, D-galactose and L-arabinose. It forms a complex cell
wall matrix between the cellulose fibrils, by binding with lignin, thus helping to crosslink
and strengthen the cell wall. [8]
Lignin is a complex, highly-cross linked polymer that is rich in aromatic compounds,
it provides structural support to plants and gives them rigidity. The constructing aromatic
alcohols are polymerized via oxidative coupling steps. They differ in the degree of methoxyla-
tion, and depending on the plant species the composition of monolignols are greatly diverse.
Some of the most common monolignols include: coniferyl alcohol, sinapyl alcohol, and p-
coumaryl alcohol, can be seen in Figure 2 [9, 10].

4.1.3
Uses of lignocellulose
Lignocellulose has a wide range of uses, which includes more traditional industries, such
as animal feed for livestock, paper and packaging industry, and the food industry, where it
is used as food additives to improve texture. [12]
Lignocellulose, being a highly abundant, renewable raw material, has a huge potential
as feed stock for microbial fermentation processes, for the production of various chemicals.
In addition, it is also an important source of renewable energy, as it can be converted into
biofuels, this application of lignocellulose has been gaining more and more attention over
the past decades. [13]
As mentioned previously, lignin is a heterogeneous polymer, composed of a wide array
of aromatic compounds, which makes its valorization, where purity is often an important
factor, very challenging.
The hydrolysis process, in which biomass is turned into easily accessible substrates is
challenging for many reasons. The conventional process uses acids, high temperature and
high pressure, this is not just expensive, it is also not resource-efficient, which is an important
factor for circular bioeconomy [12].
A great alternative to the conventional process is lignocellulose hydrolysis by biorefiner-
ies, in the first step of which, the carbohydrate polymers need to be turned into fermentable
sugars. This hydrolysis step can be catalyzed by enzymes cocktails, including cellulases,
hemicellulases and other enzymes [14]. However, a cost effective enzyme technology to make
biorefineries widely available is still a work in process.
4.2
Polyphenol oxidases
4.2.1
Overview of PPOs
Polyphenol oxidases (PPOs) are copper containing enzymes, containing two copper ions
(CuA and CuB) each coordinated by three histidine residues. PPOs are widespread among
both prokaryotes and eukaryotes.
They can catalyze the oxidation of various phenolic compounds into ortho-quinones,
through two consecutive reactions: first, phenols are hydroxylated to form ortho-diphenols
Page 5 of 41
==PAGE:11==
4.2
Polyphenol oxidases
(monophenolase activity), then these are oxidized to ortho-quinones (diphenolase activity),
see Figure 3 [15][16].
The enzymes that can catalyze both of the previously mentioned
reactions are often called tyrosinases, whereas enzymes that only display diphenolase activity
are traditionally called catechol oxidases.
Figure 3: PPO reaction mechanism [17]. Starting from monophenols to diphenols and finally
ortho-quinones.
O-quinones are highly reactive molecules that can spontaneously form brown complex
polymers, called melanin [18]. Because of this browning, PPOs have been often associated
with non-desired post-harvest discoloration, tyrosinase inhibitors have been investigated aim-
ing to cope with the browning reaction which cause important economic losses in agriculture.
It has been found that PPOs in apples can be inhibited by ascorbic acid, citric acid and
sodium chloride [19], another study came to similar conclusions, where they discovered that
PPO activity can be significantly reduced by cysteine, ascorbic acid, citric acid and sodium
bisulfate [20].
In recent years their potential for different industrial uses have been widely studied.
PPOs remain active in a wide temperature and pH range, even when immobilized on dif-
ferent carriers, for this reason they are ideal for bioremediation for phenolic compounds in
industrial wastewater. However the high costs of this the production still limits the industrial
applicability of PPOs as remediation agents [21, 2].
Furthermore, a very important application of PPOs is as bio-catalyst for lignin utiliza-
tion. Min et al. explored the promiscuous activity of tyrosinase and found that it is capable
of cleaving different bonds in a dimeric lignin model. This suggests that PPOs have a huge
potential in utilizing lignocellulosic biomass and are potentially applicable for biocatalytic
delignification [22].
Within the scope of this thesis we are focusing on fungal PPOs. Agaricus bisporus
polyphenol oxidases (AbPPOs), often called mushroom tyrosinases, are the most well char-
acterized PPOs and frequently used as model PPOs for research [23], however the three
PPOs within the scope of this thesis originate from another fungi called Myceliophthora
thermophila.
4.2.2
Myceliophthora thermophila
Myceliophthora thermophila is a well-studied thermophilic fungi, with a growth-temperature
optimum around 45-55 °C. It possesses excellent lignocellulosic biomass degrading capabil-
ities, as it produces a wide range of thermostable enzymes, which have a role in breaking
down the heterogeneous (see Figure 1) structure of lignocellulose [24].

==PAGE:12==
4.2
Polyphenol oxidases
The genome sequencing of Myceliophthora thermophila has revealed a large number
of genes encoding for various carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZy), proteases, lipases and
oxido-reductases, and more than 200 gene sequences encoding for cell-wall-degrading en-
zymes have been identified [25].
4.2.3
Short and long PPOs
In 2010 two distinct groups of PPOs were identified by Gasparetti et al. differing mainly
in length, therefore called "short" and "long" tyrosinases [26]. The difference in length can be
attributed to the lack of C-terminal domain in the case of short PPOs. Another common trait
among short PPOs seem to be that they have activity on mono- and diphenolic compounds,
but no activity on tyrosine and L-DOPA [26]. A bioinformatics analysis of a selection of
fungal polyphenol oxidases sequences revealed two main PPO clades corresponding to the
so-called long and short PPOs [26], see Figure 4.

4.3
LPMO
Conserved unique peptide patterns (CUPP) is a peptide-based grouping of enzymes
at a lower level than protein families and/or subfamilies. Developed in 2019, it is used to
automatically annotate protein family, subfamily, and EC function of carbohydrate-active
enzymes (CAZys) [27].
MtPPO7 from Myceliophthora thermophila belongs to the group of short PPOs. It shows
activity on diphenolic compounds, but not on tyrosine and L-DOPA. In 2017 it has been
shown that MtPPO7 can hydroxylate methoxylated phenolic compounds while enhancing
the oxidative degradation of cellulose by MtLPMO9B [1]. The simultaneous lignin decon-
struction and enzymatic cellulose degradation is an important step for biomass utilization,
and for a circular bioeconomy.
During our investigation we have considered MtPPO7 as a standard when it comes to
enzyme activity, since the 3 enzymes within our research share a lot of similarities with
MtPPO7.
4.3
LPMO
4.3.1
Characteristics of LPMOs
Lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases (LPMOs) are copper dependent enzymes, that
catalyze the oxidative cleavage of β-1,4-glycosidic bonds in polysaccharides, with the help of
an external reducing agent [28].
The mechanism of action is proposed to be the following: during the priming reduction,
the copper ion in the active site of the LPMO is reduced from LPMO-Cu(II) to Cu(I) by
an external electron donor. Following this, the reduced enzyme reacts in a controlled and
substrate-associated manner with H2O2, which leads to the hydroxylation of the substrate,
consequent water release, then, the enzyme regenerates to LPMO-Cu(I) [29].
It has been shown that LPMOs, in the absence of substrate and presence of reduc-
ing agent, are capable of reducing oxygen to H2O2. This means that non-substrate-bound
LPMOs can generate H2O2, which could then drive the hydroxylation of the substrate by
substrate-bound LPMOs [29]. Bissaro et al showed that LPMO performance and stability
can be controlled by regulating the H2O2 supply [30], where the reducing agent is mostly
used for priming. This proposed mechanism can be seen in Figure 5.

It has been shown by Berka et al, that often the genomes of cellulolytic fungi, such as
Myceliophthora thermophila, encode more LPMOs than cellulases, which indicates that these
enzymes have a great role in the oxidative degradation of lignocellulose [32].
Furthermore, it has been recently discovered that LPMOs are capable of depolymerizing
cellulose, therefore boosting the hydrolysis by other cellulases, along with hydrolases [33].
4.3.2
Reducing partners and PPOs
As mentioned above, LPMOs require an external reducing partner for the oxidative
cleavage of glycosidic bonds. LPMOs can receive electrons from many different types of
reducing agents This electron donor could be ascorbic acid, reduced glutathione, phenolic
compounds, pigments or various enzymes [34].
Our hypothesis is that PPOs do not donate electrons LPMOs via direct electron transfer,
rather the catalytic products of PPOs that can donate the electrons to LPMOs.
As mentioned above that the enzymatic activity of LPMOs can be boosted by the
conversion of lignin-derived monophenols into diphenols by MtPPO7 [1]. They have shown
that genes encoding for MtPPO7-like enzymes and AA9 LPMOs are correlated in various
fungi species, therefore it is likely that LPMOs benefit from the activity of PPOs [1].
Based on this assumption, we would like to further investigate whether this boosting
effect on the activity of LPMOs can be achieved by other PPOs, closely related to MtPPO7.

5.1
Gene fragments and vector
The amino acid sequences of the proteins were retrieved from GenBank and the cDNA
sequences of the respective genes were codon optimized for Pichia pastoris synthesized by
GenScript (New Jersey, USA), and cloned into pPICZαA vector using EcoRI and XbaI re-
striction enzymes. This vector allows high-level, methanol inducible expression of the gene
of interest in various Pichia strains including X-33 [35]. The native signal peptides have
been replaced by the Saccharomyces cerevisiae alpha-factor secretion signal and ZeoR was
used as a selection marker. A stop codon was inserted at the C-terminus of the encoded
protein with the purpose of excluding the c-myc and His-tag present in the plasmid from the
protein, to avoid interference.

5.2
E. coli transformation
The following E. coli transformation steps were carried out in a laminar flow hood.
To begin with, 1 µL of the 200 ng/µL re-suspended gene fragments was added to 100
µL of thawed chemically competent E. coli DH5 α cells, mixed gently, then incubated for 10
minutes on ice. Following incubation, 600 µL of SOC medium (2 % tryptone, 0,5 % yeast
extract, 10 mM NaCl, 2,5 mM KCl, 10 mM MgCl2, 10 mM MgSO4 and 20 mM glucose )
was added to the 100 µL of competent cells, which was then incubated at 37 °C for 1 hour
in a shaker incubator.
After, 150 µL of the culture was plated on LB low salt agar plates (15 g/L agar, 0,5
g/L NaCl, 10 g/L tryptone and 5 g/L yeast extract ) containing 100 µg/ml Zeocin. The
remaining volume was centrifuged and 400 µL of the supernatant was discarded. The pellet
was resuspended, then spread and plated in the same way, as previously mentioned. The
cultures were incubated overnight at 37 °C.
Following overnight incubation, one isolated colony from the plate was transferred to 5
mL of LB low salt (0,5 g/L NaCl, 10 g/L tryptone and 5 g/L yeast extract) containing 25
µg/mL Zeocin. The cultures were incubated for 24 hours at 37 °C under agitation.
5.3
Plasmid purification, linearization and desalting
After overnight incubation at 37 °C, cells were subjected to Miniprep, following the
instruction guide by Thermo-Scientific [37]. All step were carried out at room temperature,
centrifugations were carried out in a microcentrifuge at 12 000 g.
Following plasmid DNA purification, the concentration of double stranded DNA was
quantified using Nanodrop.
Plasmids were linearized using MssI restriction enzyme. After 3 hours of incubation
, the enzymes were deactivated at 65 °C for 20 minutes. The volume of the samples was
reduced to about 10 µL by incubation with the lid open.
The linearized plasmid DNAs must be desalted prior to Pichia electroporation in order
to increase transformation efficiency and avoid arcing. For this, MF-Millipore 0.05 µm MCE
membranes were used in water.

5.4
Pichia transformation
5.4
Pichia transformation
Pichia pastoris electrocompetent cells were, first thawed on ice, then mixed with 10 µL
of the desalted DNA and incubated on ice. The full volume of this mixture was transferred
to a pre-chilled electroporation cuvette and incubated for an additional 10 minutes on ice
prior to electroporation using MicroPulser Electroporator (Bio-Rad, USA) .
Following electroporation, 1 mL of pre-cooled YPD (1 % yeast extract, 2 % peptone
and 2 % glucose) was added to the cells and this volume was incubated for 3 hours at 30
°C under agitation. The samples were washed once with cold YND (1,34 % yeast nitrogen
base and 1% glucose) and plated on YPD Zeocin plates (1% yeast extract, 2% peptone, 2%
glucose and 1,5 % agar), containing 100 µg/mL of Zeocin, then incubated for 2 days at 30
°C. X33 strain containing the empty vector was used as negative control.
5.5
Expression
After 2 days, one colony from each plate was innoculated in a liquid YPD and 100
µg/ml Zeocin pre-culture, to check if the transformants can grow overnight. Cryostock were
made from these pre-cultures by mixing 500 µL of pre-culture with 500 µL of 50% glycerol.
Following this, each Pichia transformant was innoculated in 50 mL of BMGY (1 % yeast
extract, 2% peptone, 100 mM potassium phosphate buffer pH 6,0, 1,34 % yeast nitrogen base,
4*10−5 % biotin and 1 % glycerol) and incubated for 24 hours.
Then, these pre-cultures were transfered to BMMY (1 % yeast extract, 2% peptone, 100
mM potassium phosphate pH 6,0 buffer, 1,34 % yeast nitrogen base, 4*10−5 % biotin and
0,5 % methanol) and grown at 20 °C for 5 days. For these 5 days, 250 µL of methanol was
added to the cultures every 24 hours to induce protein production.
Five days later the cultures were harvested, and the supernatant concentrated using
VIVASPIN 20 centrifuge tubes with 10,000 MWCO. Buffer exchange was done with 50 mM
sodium-phosphate pH 7 buffer.
The protein content of the samples were quantified by BCA, and the samples were stored
in -20 °C.
5.6
Copper saturation
PPOs are copper enzymes, thus additional copper saturation is necessary to achieve the
optimal activity, since the concentration of copper in the fermentation medium might not
be enough to achieve complete saturation.
This was done, using 6-fold copper-sulfate concentration to the protein concentration,
followed by 5 rounds of washing with 50 mM sodium-phosphate pH 7 buffer, by diafiltration
using VIVASPIN 20 centrifuge tubes with 10,000 MWCO, the dilution factor of this step
is 20. The free copper-ions, being a reactive metal, might be able to oxidize some phenolic
compounds, therefore we need to minimize the concentration.
The final concentration of free copper after diafiltration with MtPPO-594 sample as an
example, where the molar concentration of the enzyme was 291,8 µM, therefore the initial

5.7
SDS-PAGE
SDS-PAGE is commonly used to separate proteins based on their molecular weight,
without the influence of charge and structure. During sample preparation, 15 µL of the
samples are mixed with 5 µL of 4X Laemmli sample buffer (Biorad) with 50 mM DTT,
then heated on 95 °C for 5 minutes. The electrophoresis lasts about 35-45 minutes on 200 V.
After running the samples on the gel, the proteins were visualized by incubation in Coomassie
dye for 1 hour, which non-specifically binds to all proteins, followed by multiple rounds of
washing.
5.8
Protein purification
ÄKTA Purifier chromatography system (GE Healthcare Biosciences) was used to purify
the proteins of interest, their isoelectric points can be seen in Table 1.
Prior to chromatography, the samples were buffer exchanged to 10 mM sodium phos-
phate pH 7.5, which was also the buffer used in the column. In this buffer the proteins of
interest have a negative charge, therefore will be retained in the positively charged column.
2 mL of enzyme samples were loaded onto a HiTrap Q FF (5 mL) anion exchange
chromatography column (GE Healthcare Biosciences) pre-equilibrated in 10 mM sodium
phosphate buffer, pH 7.5. The unbound proteins were washed with 5 times column volume
of 10 mM sodium phosphate buffer, pH 7.5
Later, the charged molecules were eluted from the column with a linear gradient from 0
to 1.0 M NaCl in 10 mM sodium phosphate buffer, pH 7.5, comprising 20 column volumes,
at a flow rate of 5 mL/min. The elution was monitored at 280 nm.
Using SDS-PAGE we determined which fractions contained the enzymes of interest,
these fractions were later pooled, aliquoted and stored at -20 °C.

offset 500 V, nebulizer pressure at 3 bar, dry gas flow at 12 L/min with a temperature of

LPMO-PPO interaction assay
If we take a closer look at the specific m/z regions for DP4 (Figure 25) we can see that
both in the positive control (with ascorbic acid as reducing agent) and the LPMO + PPO +
Guaiacol + PASC + H2O2 reaction mixture the native form is present with a peak around
689 m/z and the -2 Da signal (compared to the native oligosaccharide) corresponds to the
4-ketoaldose form of oxidized oligosaccharide, which can be observed with a peak at about
687 m/z.
Looking at DP5 (Figure 26) at 851 m/z, once again we can see the native peaks in the
LPMO-PPO assay and in the positive control along with the oxidized product with an m/z
value of around 849.
For both regions a third, very distinct peak can be observed with an m/z value of +16
compared to the native oligosaccharide, see peak 705 m/z for DP4 and 867 m/z for DP5.
This peak corresponds to gemdiols, the hydrated forms of the 4-ketoaldoses.
As seen in Figure 24, in the MtPPO809 + Guaiacol + PASC + H2O2 control, mostly
small native oligosaccharides (DP2, DP3, DP4 and DP5) can be observed, this can be caused
by cellulases present in the MtPPO-809 sample, as this suggests, the removal of these en-
zymes from the background was unsuccessful during the purification process.
The fact that we can only observe the oligosaccharides with a smaller m/z value in the
control reactions where MtPPO-809 was added, is expected because of the above mentioned
cellulase background.
The peak-pattern for native and oxidized oligosaccharides are not present in the other
controls, suggesting that the catalytic products of PPOs can enhance the activity of LPMOs.

7
Conclusion
7.1
Thesis findings
The findings of this thesis are that two out of the three selected PPOs, namely MtPPO-
010 and MtPPO-809 were capable of oxidizing model biomass-derived methoxylated phenolic
compounds, such as Guaiacol, Catechol, 3-methoxycatechol, Syringol, and additionally Fer-
ulic acid for MtPPO-809 (Chapter 6.3). The products of these reactions have been further
investigated and various structures have been proposed, as seen in Chapter 6.5.
Furthermore, it has also been proven that enzyme MtPPO-809 has a boosting effect on
the oxidative degradation of cellulose by NcAA9C in the presence of Guaiacol and H2O2,
see in Chapter 6.6.
The framework considered in this thesis provides possibilities for future work.
7.2
Future work
We propose that the following points would be interesting to study for further work.
Conducting experiments to investigate the effect of pH and temperature on the activity
of MtPPO-010 and MtPPO-809, would allow us to test and compare the enzymes in their
optimal environment, providing a more accurate comparison.
Additionally, the determination of kinetic parameters of the selected recombinant en-
zymes could provide a further understanding and a better insight into the catalyzed reaction
mechanisms.
The boosting effect of the expressed PPOs on LPMO activity for cellulose degradation
could be further investigated on other substrates and using different LPMOs to further
support the findings of this thesis.
