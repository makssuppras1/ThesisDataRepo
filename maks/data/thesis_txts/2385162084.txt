Abstract
Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) are having an increasing presence in modern power
systems, and especially in distribution networks. Electric Vehicles (EVs), Heat Pumps (HPs),
Batteries, Photovoltaics (PVs), and small Wind Turbines (WTs) are DERs of which the pene-
tration is increasing. At the same time, Information and Communication Technology (ICT)
is advancing and its utilisation in the power sector is increasing. This leads to the situation
where power girds are becoming "smarter" and new possibilities emerge for a more eﬃcient,
more economical and safer operation of distribution networks.
The expected large penetration of DERs is alarming the Distribution System Operators
(DSOs), who are responsible for maintaining the distribution grid conditions within safe
operating limits. This includes line congestion and voltage level limits. The DSOs are looking
for ways to accommodate the integration of large shares of DERs in their networks without
compromising grid security and customer supply. This means that, the DSOs are investigating
ways to inﬂuence the operation of DERs in an economically optimal way, in order avoid
congestions. On the other hand, the concept of the Aggregator is gaining popularity as a
business model aiming to aggregate, represent and operate a large number of DERs. Hence, a
simple, straightforward and fair method of interaction between the DSO and Aggregators has
to be developed to allow the DERs to operate optimally within the network limits. In addition,
advanced ICT provides the ability to implement intelligent operation methods in distribution
systems with reliability, speed and in an automated manner.
The goal of this thesis is to investigate how a congestion management method can be
formulated. The approach that is followed includes optimal Day-Ahead (DA) scheduling
of the DERs and then, congestion handling in Real-Time (RT) operation. An interaction of
the Aggregators and the DSO is proposed, aiming at improving grid conditions, namely line
congestions and voltage limits violations. The suggested approach is a method based on the
Distribution Location Marginal Pricing (DLMP) concept, which this thesis expands by creating
an augmented DC OPF model which includes voltage levels. The proposed methods (DA
and RT) are solved in an iterative, distributed way, using the dual decomposition method,
hence the methods are called iDLMP methods. This way each method achieves minimum
exchange of information and small reliance on communication. To the best of the author’s
knowledge, this is the ﬁrst work on DC OPF-based iDLMPs that: i) optimises both generating
and consuming DERs, ii) considers voltage level limits iii) creates a generic method for optimal
real-time operation and iv) improves upon the convergence speed of the distributed solution.
A medium voltage reference grid is used to assess the DERs impact on the grid. Simulations
showed that, without controlling and optimising the operation of DERs the network constraints
are violated in the case of large shares of DERs in the distribution grid. Moreover, voltage
limits are still violated when one applies a DLMP method that considers only line limits. With
the proposed DA method, the DSO and the Aggregators converge to optimal DA schedules

that respect both line loading and voltage limits. The convergence of the DA iDLMP method
needs the same order of magnitude of iterations as previous works do, hence the method does
not suﬀer in applicability. The RT method successfully handles network limits violations in
real-time operation by optimally rescheduling the DERs. The RT method, also, converges fast
enough so its applicability is not hindered.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The modern power system is a complex interconnected network comprised of four basic
elements: Generation, Transmission and Sub-transmission, Distribution and Loads [1]. A con-
temporary observer might notice the presence of Information and Communication Technology
(ICT) in this network. Truly, the capabilities of modern ICT have been used in the past decades
to enhance power systems around the world. Furthermore, it is the increased penetration of
Renewable Energy Sources (RES) to the power system that makes these capabilities a necessity.
1.1
Motivation
The importance of increasing intelligence in Power Grids, both in Transmission and
Distribution Systems, has been stressed in the literature [2]. The distribution grid, however,
poses special challenges when it comes to its reliable and secure operation. Unlike transmission
grids, distribution grids have not been designed for bidirectional power ﬂow. Moreover,
measuring devices are scarce and usually the Distribution System Operators (DSOs) have
little to no observability and controllability. Furthermore, distribution grids have diﬀerent
physical properties than transmission grids, such as high R/X ratios and phase unbalances,
and therefore require diﬀerent control and operation methods.
One of the main problems associated with modern distribution systems is the possibility
of power line congestions and violation of voltage level limits due to the increasing presence
of Distributed Energy Resources (DERs). These DERs are typically, among others, Electric
Vehicles (EVs) and Photovoltaic panel installations (PVs). The charging of EVs can create extra
load to the buses they are connected (Device Connection Point or DCP), thus increasing the
power ﬂow to those buses. This can lead to violation of line loading limits or voltage level
limits, as they are deﬁned in Power Quality standards, such as EN50160 [3]. On the other hand,
PV production can create inverse power ﬂows in the distribution grid. This, in turn, can result
to overvoltages at the corresponding DCPs or even line limits violations. That is why the need
for congestion management is a much discussed topic in Smart Grids literature [4].
Considering the above case, many argue for the DSO’s role to be enhanced, so that the
operation and control of DERs in the distribution grid is part of the distribution network
enhancement strategies. Such a new approach on distribution grid operation will have to
compromise the objectives of the DSO and the objectives of the owners/operators of the DERs.
As a representative of the latter, the concept of the Aggregator is most commonly used as a for-
proﬁt entity that owns, manages or operates DERs [5]. The objective of the DSO is to maximise
==PAGE:20==
2
Introduction
social welfare by minimising total costs and at the same time respecting the operational
constraints of the distribution grid. The Aggregator’s objective is to maximise proﬁts and
minimise costs, constrained only by the operational limits of the devices he manages.
One of the suggested approaches, aiming to deﬁne the interaction between DSO and
Aggregator, is the deployment of ﬁnancial penalties or incentives through which the DSO can
inﬂuence the Aggregator’s behaviour. When it comes to penalties, the regulatory framework
is still not deﬁning such a possibility [6]. The remaining choices for the DSO, at least of the
present being, is either to provide ﬁnancial incentives or to formulate the Aggregator’s plan.
In either case, a certain interaction has to be established between DSO and Aggregators. This
brings the conversation on a second consideration that has to be taken into account. That is,
how much information and data can be shared by Aggregators with the DSO and/or other
Aggregators. This point raises questions on privacy of the end-users, fair ﬁnancial competition
and regulatory framework revisions. On this grounds, a control and operation approach that
relies on little or now information exchange among the interested parties could possibly ease
these concerns.
1.2
Scope
To this end, this project describes a control method for controlling DERs installed in the
distribution grid aiming to alleviate congestions and voltage limits violations with a ﬁnancially
optimal way. This method is deployed in two time horizons, one being the Day-Ahead (DA)
scheduling of the DERs and the other, the during-the-day or Real-Time (RT) operation of
the system. The real-time method’s purpose is to provide an optimal response to deviations
from the day-ahead schedule. The concept utilised by the two methods (Day-Ahead and
Real-Time) is that of Location Marginal Prices (LMPs) and more speciﬁcally, their distribution
grid counterparts, Distribution LMPs or DLMPs.
1.3
Objectives
The objectives of this thesis are, ﬁrstly, to create methods that alleviate line congestion and
voltage level violation problems, caused by DERs to the Distribution Network, in a cost-optimal
way. Secondly, the ﬁrst of the methods has to optimise the Aggregator’s portfolio in the DA
time frame and the second in the RT time frame. Thirdly, both methods have to be formed
as a distributed solution to ensure privacy and minimum exchange of information. All three
objective will be evaluated in the Conclusion of this thesis.
1.4
Outline
This master thesis is organised as follows: Chapter 2 will discuss the related literature on
the topic and outline the contribution of this thesis. Chapter 3 will introduce the mathematical
formulation of the optimal DA scheduling method using DLMPs. Chapter 4 describes the
mathematical formulation of the real-time method, relying on concepts described in Chapter 3.
Chapter 5 will present the test system used for the simulations part of this thesis, the patterns
and other data and a brief discussion on their properties. Chapter 6 will present the results of
the simulations performed to prove and demonstrate the value of the DA method and Chapter
7 does the same for the real-time method. Chapter 8 concludes this thesis and discusses future
work.

Chapter 2
Literature Review and
Contribution
In this Chapter, a literature review will be carried out, related to the topics of cost-optimal
control of DERs in distribution networks for Congestion Management. This will serve as the
basis for establishing the contribution of this thesis to the subject. The contribution of this

2.1
Literature Review
The discussion of the possible ways to optimise the operation of distribution grids in
order to accommodate a larger penetration of DERs is relatively new. However, a number of
interesting approaches have been suggested on tackling this particular problem. The most
promising of those categories of solutions will be brieﬂy discussed and explained. All of
the approaches serve as alternatives to grid reinforcements or upgrades, and the ﬁnancial
feasibility of any any approach is compared to them. All the approaches have one thing in
common, they use mathematical optimisation to model and solve the problem [7].
2.1.1
Direct Control Methods
Before we introduce indirect approaches like DLMPs, we should discuss methods that
favour top-down decision making. In such method the DSO directs the actions to be taken
by the Aggregators or other actions to be performed in the netwrork. One such method
is reconﬁguration of the distribution grid. This means open and close switches located
in the network aiming to rearrange the network in such a way that the network condition
improves. Approached such as [8], [9] and [10] are based on reconﬁguration. They use Linear
Programming (LP) or Mixed Integer LP (MILP) to solve the problem. The basic drawback of
such an approach is that of all centralised approaches, market interference, sharing of a lot of
indeterministic information and privacy issues.
Alternatively, the usage of reactive power control is proposed in [11] and [12]. This
reactive power can be delivered from ﬂexibel AC transmission systems (FACTS), static VAR
compensators (SVC) and DERs. Its value is greater in networks with voltage problems as
reactive power has the ability to contribute in the mitigation of voltage rises or drops along
lines.
==PAGE:22==
4
Literature Review and Contribution
The last option discussed here is the use of active power control. In such methods, DERs’
active power output/input is controlled to alleviate network problems in a cost-optimal way.
One can consider network losses, as in [13] when optimally scheduling the DERs. In [14] and
[15], weighting is used to assign value to changes in active power set-points of the customers.
In the latter, the weights are based on regulating market prices.
Other centralised approaches include the work in [16], where the method is based on
an EMS (Energy Management System). Demand side management and active distributed
generation are considered to participate in the electricity market.
2.1.2
Capacity Market
This approach is presented in [4], [17] and [18]. The Aggregators are allocated some of
the capacity of the grid each with an optimised price. In [4] and [18] the price is found with
an iterative process. A more recent approach is [17] where a distribution grid capacity market
scheme between the DSO and a Fleet Operator is used to solve the congestion problem.
2.1.3
Transactive Control
Alternatively, transactive control is proposed as way of managing DERs in distribution
Grids. It is an approach that utilises value as a way of controlling and operating resources to
balance supply and demand. PowerMatcher [19] is a "coordination mechanism designed to
integrate numerous small electricity consuming and producing devices in the operation of the
electricity infrastructure" [20]. One of the most interesting recent approaches is presented in
[21], where network-constrained transactive control is used and the problem is optimised in a
distributed manner.
Although a large number of works are concerned with energy management, cost eﬀective
operation and DER integration, the limitations that naturally the physical network imposes are
given little to no consideration. However, voltage level and line limits are naturally conﬁning
the capabilities of DER optimal operation and on many cases optimal operation directly causes
the violation of such limitations.
2.1.4
Dynamic Tariﬀs
In Dynamic Tariﬀschemes, the DER react to tariﬀs-prices. The DSO aims to ﬁnd those
tariﬀs that cause the alleviation of problems with the lowest possible overall cost [22]. The
DLMP based approaches [23] such as the one presented in this Thesis can be classiﬁed as
Dynamic Tariﬀmethods and will be discussed separately in this Chapter. In [4], a bi-level
optimisation approach was suggested, however bi-level optimisation problems are hard to
solve.
2.1.5
Distribution Location Marginal Prices
Location Marginal Pricing (sometimes referred to as Nodal Pricing) is a marginal-cost-
based mechanism for operating electricity markets [24, 25]. The concept of locational marginal
pricing is not new in the power systems as it has been widely implemented in Nodal Pricing
schemes for the US electricity markets [26]. In essence, it is an economically eﬃcient pricing
mechanism for short-term operation of transmission systems, more often associated with
congestions [27], [28]. In simpler terms, one can ﬁnd the Marginal Price at a location (Bus)
by calculating the cost of serving one more unit of load at that location; this can be called
the marginal cost of electricity at that bus. This marginal cost has normally three parts: a) a
system marginal price at the reference bus, b) a system loss part and, c) a line congestion part.
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Literature Review
5
The use of LMPs in distribution systems has started to be proposed in the literature during
the previous decade. A ﬁrst discussion of Nodal Pricing in distribution systems was performed
in [25], where only network losses were considered and the method was solved in a centralised
manner. Reference [29] proposes a DLMP method that uses both DC and AC OPF and includes
losses in the problem formulation. However, the non-convexity of the problem is not discussed
and the loss function is not presented. In [30], the authors presents a DLMP method that
considers losses and voltage constraints. However, the voltage related functions that constrain
the solution are not presented, hence the non-convexity in the formulation of the problem is
not addressed. In [31], a formulation of DLMPs is presented that includes line losses and line
congestions and aims at maximising production from renewable energy resources.
In power system analysis, Economic Dispatch (ED) is the solution of an optimisation
problem that ﬁnds the lowest cost for the dispatch of available generation constrained by
generator limits, with the objective of covering a given demand plus network losses. The
Optimal Power Flow (OPF) is a formulation of ED where power ﬂow calculations are also
considered. This means that ED and power ﬂow are solved at the same time. When the OPF
problem is formulated using a DC Power FLow model, the problem is called DC-OPF. When
AC power ﬂow is used the problem is called AC-OPF.
The DC-OPF does not consider network losses and voltage constraints and is lacking
accuracy compared to AC-OPF, therefore, it might not be a suitable model for the distribution
grid that the DLMP method can be based on. There has been attempts to obtain a convexiﬁed
AC-OPF formulation of the distribution grid that will guarantee solution and on the same
time allow for the problem to be solved in a distributed way. In [32], the author is employing
the Convexiﬁed AC-OPF formulated in [33] that is based on the branch-ﬂow model. The
convexiﬁed AC-OPF has been discussed and some questions on its exactness have been raised,
especially when voltage limit constraints are binding [34].
Given the complexity of the AC-OPF that is hindering its practical use, some of the literature
has turned to the DCOPF as the basis model for the implementation of the DLMPs concept. In
[23], the author is formulating the DSO problem as a cost minimisation problem constrained
by the line loading limits. From this constraint the DLMPs occur as a function of the Lagrange
Multipliers (LMs) of the optimisation problem. The Aggregators include the DLMPs to their
schedule, and the outcome is the desired by the DSO schedule that does not lead to any
congestions.
The merits of the method in [23] are elegance, simplicity and the lack of actual ﬁnancial
transactions between the DSO and the Aggregators. The DSO indirectly controls and optimises
the schedules of the Aggregators by communicating the DLMPs to them, without the Aggre-
gators having to consider the network constraints. One can observe that the DSO requires
a lot of information about the Aggregators clients in order to obtain the correct DLMPs, for
example: EV patterns, States of Charge (SOC), PV production, etc. This fact raises concerns
about privacy, conﬁdentiality and ﬁnancial competition issues.
Another issue regarding the work of [23], is that a linear objective function is used in both
the DSO and Aggregator cost minimisation problems. As proved in [35], the linear objective
function does not gurantee that the DSO and Aggregators’ decomposed problem solutions will
be the same, as linearity means that a range of solutions might be optimal. This problem was
addressed by [36], where a quadratic objective function was used that guarantees a unique
common solution of the two problems.
However, despite the elegance of the DLMP method, concerns about the exchange of data
and privacy remain. Thankfully, the convexity of the problem formulation in [36] means the
the problem can be decomposed using dual decomposition as described in [37] and solved
distributively. In [38], the authors implement a robust optimisation method where iDLMPs
are used, meaning DLMPs that where obtained through a distributed algorithm with the only

2.2
Contribution
This thesis seeks to introduce a number of novel elements to the concept of locational
marginal pricing for congestion management in distribution networks with the use of DLMPs
and DC OPF modelling.
Firstly, this thesis is the ﬁrst study on the aforementioned concept that, apart from ﬂexible
consumption, considers generation as a decision variable of the problem. Previous works have
focused on ﬂexible loads such as heat pumps [43, 38] and EVs [23, 36, 6]. However, we will
show that dispatachable and non-dispatchable distributed generation, such as PVs can also
be a cause of problems for the network. To solve these problems, we modelled distributed
generation as available power for curtailment and introduced this curtailment as extra decision
variables in our optimisation problem.
Secondly, this thesis is the ﬁrst work on DC OPF-based distributively solved DLMPs that
considers voltage level limits. A linear approximation of the voltage level on each bus is
used, which augments the traditional DC OPF modelling of the network. We will prove that
this approximation, which works better in predominantly resistive networks, can be used to
incorporate voltage levels into the optimisation problems. At the same time, we demonstrate
the formulation and handling of DLMPs, which are derived from more than one diﬀerent
constraints. Previous works, [43, 38, 6], consider only line limits, so only the corresponding
constraints contribute to the formulation of DLMPs. However, in our approach, voltage levels
are also considered.
Thirdly, this thesis introduces a Real-Time method that aims to solve network problems
during the day of operation and in real-time, so as to complement the DA method that aims
in optimising the next-day schedule. This method relies on some of the modelling of the DA
method but introduces new decision variables to solve the problem. These are the deviations
from the DA schedule and we describe how to formulate and solve the problem with these
new decision variables.
Finally, this thesis discusses sensitive aspects of the method and attempts to improve them.
One of the most important aspects is the price sensitivity parameter that will be introduced
in Chapter 3 as its value aﬀects the behaviour of the method. We ﬁnd the value used in
previous works [43, 38] to be large enough to dominate the results of the method. We bring
this parameter to more realistic levels and then solve the problem. In a distributed solution
with iDLMPs, this parameter aﬀects the convergence of the algorithm, so we introduce an
adaptive update step in our algorithm to speed up convergence.

Chapter 3
Day-Ahead scheduling
This Chapter will present the mathematical formulation of the method proposed in this
thesis for the optimal operation of DERs, namely EVs and PVs, on a Day-Ahead (DA) scheduling
horizon. This day-ahead market participation is the most common way an Aggregator will
participate in the electricity market and a schedule that does not create distribution network
problems is of the highest importance. The DA scheduling method for secure operation
proposed here includes cooperation between the DSO and the Aggregator but no exchange of
private information or sharing of information between Aggregators.
3.1
Aggregators optimisation problem
As already discussed, the Aggregator’s aim is to maximise his proﬁt or, equivalently,
minimise his cost. In this paradigm, the Aggregator manages EV charging and PV power
production. When he solves the problem for the DA scheduling he uses a prediction of the
DA market prices. Namely, these are the prices for which he will buy energy for charging the
EVs or at which he will sell the production of PV’s. However, the electricity prices depend on
the scheduled demand of the Aggregators. In addition, the relationship between Aggregator
schedule and spot price is not linear. To overcome this problem, a spot price prediction method
that relies on price sensitivity is proposed in [4]. In this paradigm, the electricity price that
the Aggregator will encounter depends on his demand, either in the form of demand of EVs or
in the form of curtailed PV production. Namely, for every time period t:
yt = ct + βpi,t
(3.1)
The price, is composed by the baseline electricity price ct and a sensitivity part βpi,t. Here,
pi,t, represents an active power demand variable, which will be used in this Chapter for the
decision variable correlated to the charging of EVs. As explained in [4], the electricity price is
linearised around a certain average price and the marginal cost functions of power plants is
used to estimate the sensitivity parameter β. This approximation allows for structuring the
Aggregator’s problem with a Quadratic Programming (QP) formulation where the impact
of the Aggregator’s behaviour is reﬂected and hence, unrealistic high peaks in demand are
avoided. In case the price was not sensitive to the Aggregators’ behaviour, all the charging of
EVs and curtailment of PVs will occur on the same time periods, namely the ones with the
lowest baseline price, thus creating high peaks in demand.

3.2
DSO’s network constrained optimisation problem
To explain the method, ﬁrst the optimisation problem of the DSO is introduced. The DSO
problem is that of maximising social welfare. In practice, this means minimising costs for all
Aggregators, while respecting their constraints and in addition, keeping the grid conditions
within limits. However, grid conditions introduce constraints that are more complicated and
are derived from power ﬂow equations
Finding an economically optimal solution for the power system is a well established
problem, called ACOPF. The objective function of the OPF is usually quadratic. In the OPF
problem, power ﬂow equations are the constraints. However, in ACOPF, power ﬂow equations
are quadratic and as a result the total OPF problem will be a quadratically constrained quadratic
program (QCQP). In the general case, this is a non-convex problem and hence, NP-hard [33].
A popular approximation of the problem is to use DC power ﬂow equations. This type of
the OPF is called DCOPF [39]. In DC OPF the power ﬂow constraints are aﬃne and hence
the problem becomes a quadratic programming (QP) problem, which is convex. In DCOPF,
power ﬂow equations can be approximated by linear equations on the condition that power
line losses are small, voltages are close to the nominal values and voltage angle diﬀerences
between neighbouring buses are small [40].
Typically, due to its robustness and speed, the DCOPF is used in LMP calculations in
research [41] and many commercial software tools for grid operation and planning [23]. For
the same reasons, in this thesis DCOPF will be used to formulate the DSO problem, when grid
constraints are considered. These constraints will therefore be linear and hence the problem
QP and convex.

3.3
DLMP for congestion management: Centralised solution
As already discussed in Chapter 4, DLMPs have been proposed as a means of controlling
DERs, EVs and other ﬂexible loads [23, 38]. The main idea is to use DLMPs as a signals that
carry information about congestion in distribution lines. This means that when the scheduled
production and consumption is causing violation of line limits a new price on each bus is
communicated to the Aggregators and they respond. If they Aggregators are rational, this

complexity might be diﬃcult to handle [43]. For this reason, a distributed approach might be
easier to adopt and implement. The augmented DCOPF problem and its properties, as described
in this section, will serve as the basis for the distributed method. Using dual decomposition, it

Chapter 4
Real-Time operation and control
This Chapter will present the mathematical formulation of the method proposed in this
thesis for the optimal Real-Time (RT) operation of DERs, namely EVs and PVs. By real-time, we
mean during-the-day or intra-day operation. This means situations where the DSO identiﬁes
problems that will occur in the next time period and aims in alleviating them. The length of
the time period deﬁnes the speed in which the DSO can react. For simplicity, the time period
is an hour in this thesis. It can, of course, be less than that, for example a time period of a
quarter of an hour is advised in real-life solutions. This part of the total approach aims to
handle deviations from the DA schedule in a cost optimal way, while respecting network
constraints and privacy of information, just like the DA method.
The RT method is similar to the DA method in that the Aggregator’s and DSO’s problems
are of a Quadratic Programming (QP) formulation and they are constrained by the same set
of equations. The only practical diﬀerence is that instead of trying to ﬁnd consensus on the
charging and curtailment power set-points, the do so for the deviations of the charging and
curtailment power setpoints.

5.2
The test system
The centralized and distributed DLMP methods described in Chapters 3 and 4 are evaluated
on the Baran-Wu 33 Bus Network [8], see Figure 5.1. It is a 12.66 kV, 2 feeder substation with
5 looping branches which in the simulations, are assumed to be open. The peak load is 3,715
kW. According to the authors [8], the network is not well-compensated and has a lot of losses.
Moreover, the voltage proﬁle is such that the 0.9 p.u. voltage limit is approached at the base
system conﬁguration.
Another observation is that the R/X ratio of the system is lower than 1 in all but the ﬁrst
11 branches, most likely because they may be overhead lines. However, in these simulations
a higher R/X ratio will be assumed to reﬂect the fact that in European countries most of the
urban distribution networks have underground cables. This ratio can be 2:1, or more depending
on the voltage level of the network or the type of the lines [49]. For equation (3.12) to stand
as a good approximation of the voltage level on each bus, the R/X ratio is very important, as
explained in section 3.2.
Equation’s (3.12) accuracy as an approximation is inversely dependant to the reactive
power that is injected on each bus. A Power Factor (PF) of 0.9 to 0.95 is typical for residential
loads. These values are within a range that does not make the approximation to fail.
To this end, the R/X ratio of the Bara-Wu system is adjusted to reﬂect a 3:1 R/X ratio and
a PF of 0.95 for every scenario other than the sensitivity analysis.

5.4
Discussion on price sensitivity parameter β
One of the most important issues that had to be investigate before assessing the validity
of the results is the price sensitivity parameter, β. In [43], a value of 100 €/MWh2 is used in
the simulations. When this value is used, the charging of EVs is spread out, meaning that the
behaviour of a single Aggregators has a very strong eﬀect on the resulting prices. This creates
unrealistic patterns for the EV charging. Therefore, a smaller value for β was sought after.
One has to be reminded that the price sensitivity factor is deﬁning how convex the
optimisation problem is. As a result, the smaller the β the less convex the problem, the slower
it is for the iterative method to converge to the solution. For extremely low values of β, for
example < 1 €/MWh2, the number of iterations needed is in the order of thousands. However,
such a value is too small, meaning that it has the opposite eﬀect of the 100 €/MWh2 value:
the price is not aﬀected by EV charging.
In this thesis we chose a value of 5 €/MWh2, which serves as a good trade oﬀbetween
the two extremes. The charging of EVs has a small eﬀect on the price and convergence is at
the hundreds of iterations. It is evident that research has to be conducted to deﬁne a realistic
value of β for every power system that can be used in future works.

6.1.3
Distributed Solution: iDLMP method
The distributed solution or iDLMP method relies on the same mathematical formulation
of the centralised solution. Namely, the dual decomposition of the DSO optimisation problem.
However, in the iDLMP method the LMs, that are used to derive the DLMPs, are calculated
iteratively. The end goal is for the LMs to converge to the optimal values which should be
the same as the ones calculated in the centralised solution. In this case, however, the LMs
are calculated in a distributed way, without information exchange between the DSO and the
Aggregators or among the Aggregators, apart from the signals from the DSP, described in
Chapter 3. The DSO is only responsible for calculating the update step of the iterative method
that will guarantee convergence, exactly as described in Chapter 3.
Figure 6.30: Sum of the objectives of the two aggregators as the iterations proceed, compared
to the same objective as calculated from the centralised solution.
First, the convergence of the objective is demonstrated in Figure 6.30. It can be observed
that the objective converges really fast, at the ﬁrst 180 iterations. This can be explained by the
fact that, the LMs associated with the upper line limit, converge fast, as well. The line upper
limit is not associated with the voltage issues as it refers to any violations of the line limits
caused by inverse power ﬂow, which in this study case are impossible to happen. The line upper
limit however is associated with the voltage lower limit. In simpler words, increased power
ﬂow for EV charging can create both line limit and voltage level limit violations.
Observing Figure 6.31, one can see how the LM ω−(17, 2) on Bus 17 increases and then
its increase slows down, at ﬁrst, and then it starts to decrease. At the same time ω−(18, 2)
increases continuously until it converges. This can be explained by the fact that at the ﬁrst
iterations some voltage limits are violated leading to the increase of their LMs. As the LM
on Bus 18 is increasing, it dominates the rest of the ω−LMs on that branch, making them
redundant. This meanes that a single LM on BUs can have the desired constrain eﬀect on EV
scheduling along the branch. That is why as ω−(18, 2) improves, ω−(16, 2) is reduced.
Unlike other works, in this thesis the problem converges at a little more than 1000 iterations,
namely 1251. This is more iterations than [38]. However, this is expected. Firstly, we use
a price sensitivity parameter β that is 5% of what is used in [43] and [38]. This means that

8.1
Proposed methods - a summary
This thesis presented two methods for optimal operation and control of DERs in the
Distribution Network aiming to avoid network problems. The ﬁrst method aims at the
optimal scheduling of DERs for the DA time frame. This method is useful to the DSO and the
Aggregators who want to participate in the day-ahead electricity market while making sure
the network’s line and voltage limits are not violated. The second method is created for the
RT operation time frame. This method is proposed in order to provide a tool for the DSO and
the Aggregators to solve network problems that occur at during-the-day operation, such as
unexpected events or forecasting errors that lead to network limits violations.
Both methods are built as a mathematical optimisation problem. To model the distribution
network, an augmented DC OPF model was used, that unlike traditional DC OPF, incorporates
voltage levels. As an objective function, a quadratic function was used that takes into account
the fact that the ﬁnal day-ahead electricity prices are sensitive to the resulting schedule of
the DERs. The outcome is a quadratic optimisation problem that is convex. As such, it is
proven that the problem can be decomposed through dual decomposition and can be solved
distributively. Both DA and RT problems are decomposed and the corresponding suggested
methods are solved distributively.
The proposed methods have the following desired traits: They optimise the Aggregators’
schedules in DA and RT time frames while respecting line congestion limits and voltage level
limits. Moreover, the DA method is complemented by the RT method, so any unexpected
events or deviations in system conditions can be handled in order to secure that network
problems will not occur. Thirdly, the problems are solved in a distributed manner, without the
exchange of sensitive information and without the DSO dictating the schedules.
Finally, the proposed method introduces, for the ﬁrst time, voltage level constraints to
the DC OPF-based, distributively-solved, DLMP method, securing safe network operation.
Moreover, this is the ﬁrst work that combined ﬂexible load with distributed generation to

8.2
Results discussion
This thesis presents a number of case studies that prove the validity of the methods. A
MV network was used as the basis for the simulations, the lines of which become severely
congested and its voltage level limits are severely violated in the case of large penetration
of DERs. This highlights the need for a method that optimally schedules the DERs while
respecting network constraints.
Firstly, the DA method was tested, solved both in a centralised and distributed manner.
The results showed that considering only line limits, as in previous works on the topic, will still
compromise the network’s operation, as voltage limits can still be severely violated. However,
with the proposed DA method, results showed that this problem can be solved, as the method
can produce a DA schedule for DERs that is free of line and voltage problems. Considering that
voltage limits are making the problem more complex to solve, however, results showed that this
increased complexity does not compromise the practical applications of the iDLMP approach.
The distributed method needs more iterations, meaning the number of iterations is in the order
of hundreds. However, such an increase is not large enough to make the method impractical
to use, nor is it signiﬁcantly larger than previous works. In addition, results conﬁrm that
optimising undispatchable with dispatchable DERs, like PVs with EVs, can facilitate renewable
energy integration and maximise social welfare.
Then, the RT method was tested on top of a case-study performed for the DA method.
The results showed that unexpected changes in the grid conditions, such as load changes, can
lead to network problems. These problems can be successfully handled by the RT method,
which reschedules the DERs in a real-time frame considering the new conditions. The end
result is that both line limits and voltage limits are respected while the problem is solved in a
distributed and cost-optimal way.
8.3
Final remarks and future work
This thesis is supporting our belief that the DLMPs are a promising way of dealing with
distribution network congestions. Using the proposed DLMP approach, we showed how some
of the most common drawbacks of the congestion management methods can be overcome.
We consider that a method for congestion management in distribution grids has to rely on i)
minimal sharing of data and information, achieved in this thesis by the distributed iterative
nature of the methods, and ii) speed, which is why an augmented DC OPF model for the
system is favoured over AC OPF approaches.
Future work can be carried out on a range of aspects of the suggested methods. Firstly,
diﬀerent DERs, such as heat pumps, wind power, batteries, etc., can be modelled and the
behaviour of the method can be tested against these new units. Then, the robust and stochastic
counterparts of the method can be investigated. The stochastic modelling can include EV
patterns, PV production, load, etc.
Then, research can be conducted on ﬁnding better approximations of the voltage levels
on each bus. If the the validity of the suggested approximation used in this thesis is found
to be insuﬃcient in some types of networks, diﬀerent approached can be investigated. A
lineariasation of the tradition power ﬂow equations around an operating point that has been
guessed using heuristic or machine learning methods can be tried.
Finally, a deeper analysis and investigation can be conducted on the price sensitivity
parameter. Reliable values for the parameter have to be obtained through statistical analysis.
On this context, work has to be done on the update step of the method, as it was observed
