Abstract 
Concern for food safety should always be of outmost concern. Risks for anaerobic bacteria to cause 
food poisoning outbreaks, in aquatic food, remain high because of few factors. Capture fisheries 
and aquaculture sectors are growing every year (FAO, 2018), while usage of modified atmosphere 
packaging (MAP) increases, as food industry seek to increase shelf life of products as much as 
possible. Anaerobic bacteria such as Clostridium botulinum type E is a constant treat. This dangerous 
pathogen can grow at low temperatures (as low as 3◦C) and is prevalent in aquatic environment. 
One of the concerns about food packed in MAP is that there are no sensory signs of spoilage as 
anaerobic conditions prevent growth of spoilage organisms and oxidation reactions, so Cl botulinum 
can grow and produce toxin unnoticed. Predictive food microbiology models for the effect of CO2 
on growth of Cl botulinum would reduce this risk, as time for safe consumption of aquatic food 
products in MAP would be predicted. Such a model was the focus of the present study. 
To create a predictive model for the effect of CO2 on growth of Cl. Botulinum, theoretical and 
methodical information was first collected and described. After that pre-test’s to determine most 
suitable materials and to test methods were conducted. Data for model development was collected 
at a temperature of 13.5◦C with standard deviation of 0.55 in packages with different initial carbon 
dioxide concentrations ranging from 0% to 100% (volume-to-volume ratio of gas in package). 
Concentrations of CO2 and pH were measured during each challenge test and chemical analysis was 
completed to determine environmental parameters for Cl botulinum type E. Experimentally 
determined growth data was fitted to a logistic model to develop primary models. Received data 
from primary models was used to obtain a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) value of 
minimum CO2 concentration at which growth of Cl. botulinum type E is inhibited. Using a CO2max  
value a secondary square root model was developed implementing the effect of CO2 on growth. The 
CO2 term was added to existing secondary model for Cl. botulinum type E with terns for effect of 
temperature, NaCl in water phase, pH, lactic, acetic, citric, sorbic and benzoic acids in water phases, 
and nitrite. The expanded secondary model for Cl. botulinum with the added CO2 term was 
evaluated in challenge tests. Data showed that for four different treatments observed growth was 
faster than the predicted growth, resulting in bias factor of 0.85 and accuracy factor of 1.34. 
Minimum amount of treatments for validation is recommended to be at least twenty. Conclusion 
from evaluation is that model is fail dangerous and too optimistic. Before usage, the model should 
be calibrated and evaluated again until the bias factor value is acceptable.

Introduction 
Concern for food safety should always be of outmost concern. Risks for anaerobic bacteria to cause 
food poisoning outbreaks, in aquatic food, remain high because of few factors. Capture fisheries 
and aquaculture sectors are growing early (FAO, 2018), while usage of modified atmosphere 
packaging (MAP) increases, as food industry seek to increase shelf life of products as much as 
possible. Anaerobic bacteria such as Clostridium botulinum type E is a constant treat. This dangerous 
pathogen can grow at low temperatures (as low as 3◦C) and is prevalent in aquatic environment. 
One of the concerns about food packed in MAP is that there are no sensory signs of spoilage as 
anaerobic conditions prevent growth of spoilage organisms and oxidation reactions, so Cl botulinum 
can grow and produce toxin unnoticed. Most common gasses in MAP are carbon dioxide and 
nitrogen. Nitrogen has no effect on bacteria growth; it is not reactive and has low solubility in water. 
It just replaces oxygen but does not work as preservative. As MAP creates good conditions for 
anaerobic bacteria like Cl. botulinum effect of carbon dioxide on such pathogens is important. 
Effect of CO2 on growth of Cl. Botulinum has been researched in various studies. Results vary a lot 
with conclusions sometimes being contrary. Results differ a lot based on research, some claim that 
carbon dioxide has no noticeable effect on Cl. Botulinum growth, others found out inhibiting effect. 
There is even statements that carbon dioxide increases spore germination (Eklund, 1982). Differing 
results like that proves that relation between CO2 and growth of Cl. Botulinum has to be researched 
further and preferably in repeatable way. 
Predictive food microbiology model for Cl botulinum, with multiple secondary models, that is 
capable of predicting growth of this pathogen in various conditions would give good guidelines for 
how long product can be safe to consume. With increasing acceptance of predictive food 
microbiology models in EU and USA, this would be useful tool in aquatic food production industry. 
The objective of this study was to develop secondary model with CO2 effect on Clostridium 
botulinum type E growth. To obtain data, required for model, challenge tests with determined 
parameters were conducted, data was fitted to primary models to obtain values defining growth. 
Received values were used to calculate minimum inhibitory concentration and MIC was used 
together with mathematical terms to create secondary model that was incorporated to tertiary 
model housing mathematical terms for other environmental conditions. Short, partial evaluation of 
final tertiary model was conducted.

Theory 
Clostridium botulinum 
Clostridium botulinum is anaerobic, motile, gram-positive, rod-shaped, spore forming pathogenic 
bacteria that can be found in soil, fresh and salt water. This pathogen is classified into seven 
serotypes (A-G) and two groups, proteolytic and non-proteolytic. It is well known for its neurotoxins 
potency, generally considered the most potent of all biological toxins (Gill, 1982; Lana, 1959). With 
estimated human median lethal dose (LD50) of 1.3–2.1 ng/kg intravenously or intramuscularly and 
10–13 ng/kg when inhaled it is the most acutely lethal toxin known (Arnon et al., 2001). That is 
reason enough to be worried about this pathogens prevalence in food products.  
Botulinum neurotoxin is produced by Clostridium botulinum in late logarithmic growth phase, but it 
can be inactivated by heat. Van Ermengun (1897) reported that toxin was rendered almost inert 
when heated at 80°C for 30 min and was completely destroyed at 100 ◦C. Dickson (1917).

Even with good manufacturing practices, hazard analysis control points, modern equipment and 
strict regulations outbreaks of clostridium botulinum still happen. Outbreaks of Clostridium

in Europe 2194 confirmed cases of listeriosis were reported, in Europa (ecdc.europa.eu). In 2014 
123 cases of botulism were reported and 91 confirmed in Europe alone (ecdc.europa.eu) some

Type E is predominant type of all Cl. Botulinum types in sub-arctic and temperate climatic zones and 
Scandinavia’s coastal waters have highest type E spore concentration in marine sediments

Bacterial growth 
Growth of bacterium is increase in population of bacteria. Bacteria reproduces by cell division 
process, usually if no mutation occurs its binary fission, meaning that two cells after division are 
same as first entity. Growth is affected by environmental parameters like nutrition concentration, 
temperature gaseous concentration, pH, ions and salt concentration, water activity. 
Bacterial growth is divided in three main sections: lag phase, log phase or exponential phase and 
stationary phase. After that follows death phase (figure 1). 
==PAGE:10==

Figure 1 Generalized curve, showing phases of bacterial growth. (www.britannica.com, 2018) 
During lag phase, individual bacteria are adapting to conditions and increase in their own size. 
Synthesis of RNA, enzymes and other molecules are happening in bacteria but cell division is no 
existent or minor. 
When bacteria adapts to environment and cell division starts bacteria population enters exponential 
or logarithmic growth phase in which cell numbers increase in a logarithmic fashion. The log phase 
continues until nutrients are depleted or toxic metabolic products accumulate. After log phase is 
finished growth rate and death rate become equal due to lack of nutrients or accumulated inhibitory 
metabolites. Death phase (decline phase) can be caused by lack of nutrients, or other injurious 
conditions, it results in reduction in bacterial population.

Modified atmosphere packaging 
Air normally consist of nitrogen (N2) 78.08% (volume per volume will be used throughout this 
chapter), oxygen (O2) 20.96% and carbon dioxide (CO2) 0.03%, together with variable 
concentrations of water vapour and traces of inert or noble gases (Mulan et al., 2011). Due to 
moisture loss or uptake, oxidation, growth of aerobic microorganisms (bacteria, moulds) foods spoil 
quickly in air. Common practice used in nowadays food production industry to increase shelf life is 
modified atmosphere packaging. Modiﬁed atmosphere packaging (MAP) is deﬁned as ‘the 
packaging of a perishable product in an atmosphere that has been modiﬁed so that its composition 
is other than that of air’(Hintlian &Hotchkiss,1986). 
Main idea is to improve shelf life of the product is to change concentration of gases in package so 
unwanted reactions (e.g. oxidation), microorganisms growth are slowed down or prevented. Gasses 
that are usually used in MAP are carbon dioxide (CO2), oxygen (O2), nitrogen (N2), carbon monoxide 
(CO) and noble gases (i.e. helium (He), argon (Ar), xenon (Xe) and neon (Ne)) (Mulan et al., 2011). 
==PAGE:11==
Janionis, 2018 
9 
 
Carbon dioxide and nitrogen are two most popular gases used in map for aquatic food products. 
Nitrogen is a relatively unreactive gas with no odour, taste or colour. Nitrogen inhibits the growth 
of aerobic spoilage but does not prevent the growth of anaerobic bacteria. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is 
a colourless gas with as light pungent odour at very high concentrations. CO2 dissolves in water 
(1.57 g/kg at 100 kPa, 20◦C) to produce carbonic acid (H2CO3) that increases the acidity of the 
solution and reduces the pH (Coles and Kirwan, 2011) . This gas is also soluble in lipids and some 
other organic compounds. The solubility of CO2 increases with decreasing temperature. For this 
reason, the antimicrobial activity of CO2 is markedly greater at temperatures below 10◦C than at 
15◦C or higher. The high solubility of CO2 can result in pack collapse due to the reduction of 
headspace volume (Coles and Kirwan, 2011). 
Having carbon dioxide and nitrogen, as only gases in package, aerobic microorganisms are inhibited 
and shelf life is extended but anaerobic bacteria can still grow. Because anaerobic pathogens as 
Clostridium botulinum have chance to grow and produce toxins and there are no signs of spoilage 
for consumer to reject product, food poisoning from anaerobic pathogens can occur. Importance of 
effect of carbon dioxides on anaerobic bacteria has to be discussed. 
Carbon dioxide known to have antibacterial properties (Valley & Rettger, 1927). In general, CO2 
increases the lag phase and growth rate of microorganisms. This effect is stronger at lower 
temperatures. There appears to be an array of antimicrobial mechanisms, including CO2 lowering 
pH, inhibition of succinic oxidase at CO2 concentrations greater than 10%, inhibition of certain 
decarboxylation enzymes and disruption of the cell membrane (Enfors & Molin, 1981). Carbon 
dioxide, particularly at low temperatures, is soluble in water and lipids, and adjustment for 
adsorption is required. A high concentration of CO2 can lead to defective changes of the product, 
e.g. increased drip in fresh meats, and to collapse of the package. Minimum of 20% CO2 
concentration is generally required to control the bacterial and mould growth. Optimal levels 
appear to be in the region of 20–30% (Coles and Kirwan, 2011). 
Predictive microbiology 
Predictive food microbiology is a field of study that combines elements of microbiology, 
mathematics, and statistics to develop models that describe and predict the growth or decline of 
microbes under specified environmental conditions. This area of food microbiology focuses on 
mathematical modelling of external environment and internal factors that affect growth of 
microorganism. Such effects include pH, temperature, water activity, effect of gases, acids and 
other. Lag time, growth rate and inactivation of the microorganisms can be modelled (Ratkowsky et 
al., 1991; Heitzer et al., 1991). Before modelling data has to be collected and growth curves should 
be determined in relation to different combinations of factors affecting growth (Dalgaard, 2009). 
Models are classified into three groups: primary, secondary and tertiary models (McMeekin and 
Ross, 2002). 
Primary model 
Primary models are least complex, they describe changes in microbial population numbers with time 
and describe kinetics of a process with least possible amount of parameters. Sigmoid curves are 
usually used to fit microbial growth data as plotting the (natural) logarithms of the bacterial 
numbers against time will typically result in a sigmoid curve. 
Specific growth rate µ(t) is an important dynamic variable that define sigmoid growth curve, which 
is defined as the (instantaneous) change of population per unit time divided by the number of 
microorganisms at that time. When the population size is expressed as the natural logarithm (ln) of 
the number of cells and this is plotted against time, the specific rate represents the (instantaneous)

growth rates than what is observed. If the Bias factor is >1 it is indicated that the model predicts 
higher growth rates than observed. A more graduated evaluation of the Bias factor is dependent on 
how growth of pathogens or spoilage organisms is predicted. For pathogens a Bias factor between 
0.95 and 1.11 is considered good, 0.87-0.95 or 1.11-1.43 is acceptable and <0.87 or >1.43 is 
unacceptable (Mejlholm et al., 2010). Preferably, a conservative approach, when predicted growth 
is higher then observed, should be applied in models predicting pathogen growth. Models predicting 
growth of spoilage organisms can be accepted within a broader range (Ross, 1999; Ross et al, 2000) 
Accuracy factor (eq 12) is another index used to describe spread of predicted results compared to 
observed data. The larger the value of Af the less accurate is the estimate. A value of one indicates 
perfect agreement between predicted and observed values (Ross, 1996).

Tertiary Models  
Tertiary models are the tools applying the developed models, they house one or multiple primary 
and secondary models and provide user interface. Primary and secondary models are compiled to 
provide prediction, connected with databases gathering the input parameters such as cardinal 
values, optimal growth rate etc., required to create simulation (Mcdonald and Sun 1999). Available 
and known tertiary models for growth predictions of various microorganisms are: ComBase 
Predictor (www.combase.cc), Pathogen Modelling Program, PMP 7.0 (www.ars.usda.gov) and Food 
Spoilage Safety Predictor, FSSP v 4,0 (fssp.food.dtu.dk). 
Literature and existing models 
Quite a few researches were done considering growth or toxigenesis of Clostridium botulinum in 
modified atmosphere packaging. Results and conclusions differ, depending on research conditions, 
products, and methods used (Baker and Genigeorgis, 1990; Post et al.l 1985; Reddy et al., 1996, 
1997, 1997, 1999). Some researchers even concluded that there was no significant effect by carbon. 
Eklund, 1982, suggested that spore germination of Cl. botulinum might be enhanced in a CO2 
enriched environment 
Few secondary models, that predict non-proteolytic Clostridium botulinum growth in food products, 
were created. Ann F. Graham, David R. Mason, Michael W. Peck at 1995 developed predictive model 
on growth of Clostridium botulinum incorporating effects of temperature pH and sodium chloride 
but no CO2 term. P.S. Fernandez, J. Baranyi and M.W. Peck at 2001 created a growth prediction 
model for non-proteolytic Clostridium botulinum in different CO2 concentrations. Concentrations 
that are included in the model range from 0 to 90% CO2. Gas percentage was measured volume to 
volume, in the beginning, in this research, so loss of gas through package was not taken into 
consideration. Even though secondary models on CO2 effect on growth of non-proteolytic Cl 
botulinum exist there are no freely available tertiary model with CO2 effect on clostridium botulinum 
growth.

HPLC 
5g of homogenized samples were weighted in centrifuge glasses. Extraction was done by using 20.0 
ml 0.6M perchloric acid that was cooled in ice. After perchloric acid was added samples were 
homogenized further (Ultra Turrax, IKA, Germany) for 1 minute in same centrifuge glasses as 
samples were weighted in. Neutralization of sample was done using 2.5M potassium hydrogen

centrifuge glass was washed with deionized water to transfer most of the sample to volumetric flask. 
Volumetric flask was filled to a volume of 50.0 ml with deionized water. Diluted sample was filtered 
to Erlenmeyer flasks, 30ml was sufficient to fill TVN cups. Neutralized filtered extracts were frozen 
for at least 24h at -20◦C. Before transferring 1ml of samples to HPLC vials they were defrosted at 5◦C 
and filtered trough 0.20µm filter (3d extraction method, modified by Ole Mejlholm, 2003). 
Standard solutions that were used in HPLC calibration were citric acid (Sigma C0909), Lactic acid 
(Sigma L1750), acetic acid (Sigma Aldrich 33209), and Benzoic acid (Sigma B3420). Two 
concentrations of standard solutions were made with differences in a factor of ten between them. 
Both solutions with two different concentrations, were made in three volumes each: 15μl, 30μl and 
45μl. After the calibration of HPLC 1ml of sample extracts were separated in HPLC column with 
length of 300mm and diameter 7.8mm (Aminex BIORAD HPX-87H, BIORAD, USA) and analysed using 
HPLC equipment (Hewlett Packard, 1100 series, USA). 0.008M H2SO4 was used as an eluent (made 
from 95-97% Sulfuric acid, Merck, Germany), Sulfuric acid was filtrated trough 0.45μm filter.

spatulas. Plating was done aerobically in aseptic conditions, after plating plates were moved to

Other methods 
Methods that are not included in identification and chemical method sections are described here. 
Gas composition measurement 
Gas composition measured using gas analyser (CheckMate 3, PBI dansensor, Denmark) capable of 
measuring percentage of oxygen and carbon dioxide. The place where needle was inserted for gas 
collection for analysis, the special sticky foam pad was placed to prevent gas from atmosphere 
coming into pack and altering measurements. 10ml of gas was used for analysis. 
Cooking 
Skinless cod was placed in sterile plastic bags (NEN 40/LLDPE 75), one fillet per bag, with low gas 
permeability and sealed using impulse sealer (Genpack sealer, Genpack, Denmark). All the bags with 
the fish were cooked in a water bath for at least 12 minutes at 90C in order to inactivate any 
vegetative cells and/or any spores of non-proteolytic C. botulinum present in the fish. The fish was 
left to cool down, enough to be handled, by leaving in room temperature, extra water is removed.  
Heat activation of spores 
The inoculated and vacuum-packed fish was heated in a water bath at 70◦C for 20 minutes in order 
to activate the spores and kill vegetative cells. Temperature reaches ~65◦C inside the fish muscles 
after 20min in water bath. The bags with fish is then placed to ice-cold water for 20 seconds to cool 
down and then are finally stored at 15◦C until further analysis. The temperature in the incubator is

Average CO2 v/v concentration during exponential growth 
To use maximum growth rate from obtained data for calculation of minimum inhibitory 
concentration, CO2 concentration had to be connected to µmax. For that equilibrium or average CO2 
v/v concentration during exponential growth was chosen. Exactly this value was chosen because it 
is after effect of gas transfer and solution and it could be calculated from initial concentration is 
parameters such as: gas permeability of package, package area, pressure of package, temperature 
etc. are known.  
Equilibrium CO2 concentration during exponential growth, was calculated by using figure from CO2 
measurements in packages during challenge tests. Section in which exponential growth occurred 
was marked and average of CO2 in that section was calculated 
For example samples, with 50% intended initial CO2 concentration, from first challenge test had 
growth from 45 to 190 hours. Green lines show start of exponential growth and CO2 concentration 
at that time, blue lines show end of exponential growth and CO2 concentration at that time (figure 
2). As decrease in CO2 concentration noticed to be more or less steady during exponential growth 
of microorganism, middle value between CO2 concentration at the start and at the end of 
exponential growth was taken, in this case 33.5 is average counted as average CO2 concentration 
during exponential growth. In most cases time when CO2 concentration was measured in packages 
did not align with start or end of exponential growth so standard deviation was not possible to 
calculate.

Tertiary model 
After term is chosen it was implicated into final predictive microbiology model for growth of non-
proteolytic Clostridium botulinum together with other terms. Addition of introduction term was also 
needed as it is used in psi value (Ψ) calculation.  
A Ψ value is determined from sets of environmental parameters and describes how far specific 
combinations of product characteristics and storage conditions are from predicted growth boundary 
(LeMarc et al., 2002). If Ψ is above 1 it indicates inhibition of growth by the combined effect of the 
environmental conditions. A Ψ value between 0.5 and 1 indicate some reduction of µmax, depending 
on the value of Ψ, whereas Ψ -values below 0.5 represent no reducing effect on µmax.

Proper evaluation of model to be used in food industry needs data from at least twenty different 
treatments. Challenge test that was conducted had four different treatments so this evaluation is 
just preliminary. 
In three from total of four cases predictions by model were underestimated and predicted maximum 
growth rate was lower than observed. Prediction for one code was conservative, model predicted 
slower growth than it was observed.

Discussion 
Few things from this study can be discussed. For example there is possibility of mistake in challenge 
test for validation. Code A1 in validation study had higher growth rate then B1 even though 
additional benzoic acid was added to code A1 which should equal to slower growth then without. 
This could be caused by some sort of error.  
Only four treatments used in validation, twenty treatments is the minimum number to properly 
validate the model. As it can be seen from differences of predicted versus observed maximum 
growth rates in each of four cases, more treatments could sway bias and accuracy factor closer to 
value of one. Proper validation with a lot more treatments should be performed. Bias factor that 
was calculated in evaluation of model can be used to adjust referential growth rate µref, it would be 
recommended to bring Bias factor to value of one, and reduce accuracy factor as much as possible. 
The media in which inoculated cultures were grown is very important. Some minimum inhibitory 
concentrations for other secondary models and terms were modelled based on data acquired from 
challenge tests in broth media. Variations between predicted and observed growth rates can be 
caused for this exact reason. 
For MIC of CO2, term equilibrium carbon dioxide concentration during exponential growth was used. 
Using the equilibrium concentration effects of gas transfer and solubility are taken out of account. 
Usually MAP composition is measured just after product is packaged in headspace and not trough 
out its shelf life. By implementing Henrys law and calculations of gas transfer from package, with 
known parameters, model could be used with initial CO2 concentration also.

Conclusion 
In this study three challenge tests were conducted to receive enough data for MIC of CO2. In 
temperatures from 13◦C to 14 ◦C and with 0.00, 1.50, 2.25, 19.50, 27.50, 33.50, 48.50, 48.00, 57.25 
percentage equilibrium CO2 concentration during exponential growth of bacteria (v/v). Slower 
growth pattern of Clostridium botulinum type E was noticed in increasing CO2 concentrations. 
Data was fitted using logistic model with delay to extract growth parameters from data. 
Minimum inhibitory concentration of CO2 on growth of Clostridium botulinum type E has been 
calculated with value of 270.95% and µref of 0.154. RSS and RMSE were 0.00776, 0.0333 respectively. 
And the equation that had best fit was mentioned RSS and RMSE values was (eq 10) 
Final tertiary model for Cl. Botulinum has been partially evaluated using data from small group of 
treatments (four treatments with different parameters) that was acquired by fourth challenge test. 
Preliminary evaluation showed that model predicts maximum growth rates with discrepancy 
compared to observed maximum growth rates. While proper evaluation could bring bias factor 
closer to one accuracy factor would still remain larger then one. This indicates that µref needs 
calibration to optimize performance of the model. 
In conclusion, model that has been created is working but based on accuracy and bias factors further 
adjustments have to be made. Final tertiary model should be optimized and properly validated 
before using it in food industry.
