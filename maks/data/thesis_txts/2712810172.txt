Abstract
This thesis is driven in collaboration with the Low-Tech Lab (LTL), a non profit organization
that seeks to develop and share low technologies. Low technologies are defined as useful,
accessible and sustainable systems. More than just a definition of a few technologies, it
is a real movement that questions the needs of our societies. The organization will lead
an experiment where two persons will try to be autonomous for a few months on the
Mexican coasts and with the help of several of those Low-Techs. The present document
will focus on the development of one of these which is a solar desalination system, more
commonly known as solar still. This technology seems crucial today as global warming
and international crisis are getting to the next level. Water accessibility, which is already
an issue for about a quarter of the world’s population, will keep worsening with, among
others, more frequent and intense droughts. The aim is to produce 40 L/day of drinkable
water in order to meet the two persons daily needs of water but also to grow their food.
To do so, a historic of water desalination is given as well as a state of the art of solar stills.
The starting point is then about modeling and caracterizing a previously tested prototype
of a still. It is based on M. Cyril Grandpierre’s work, Solar Drops [1]. A mathematical
model is proposed based on the thermal resistance model. A prototype has been built
and tested in laboratory with simulated sun and seawater. Once this is done, a second
prototype based on the research of Zhenyuan Xu and others [2] at the MIT is tested
to compare it with the first prototype and to asses if it is compatible with the Low-Tech
framework. This second prototype has been built and tested in the same conditions as
the former one. Some avenues for improvements for both prototypes and their robustness
are given. Thanks to the material selection software Granta Edupack, a sizing as well as
a material choice is done for both of those designs. Comparison of weight, cost, energy
consumption and CO2 footprint over the life cycle is also performed to chose which of
the two designs will be advised to be built. This is done by creating CAD model of both
prototypes under OnShape and by making Eco-Audits with the Granta Edupack software.
This work will be followed by the making of documentation to make the final chosen design
reproducible around the world via a tutorial on the Low-Tech Lab’s website.
The mathematical model turned out not to predict really well the behaviour of the former
prototype. However, further work to correct all the numerical values used in order to
better fit to reality could be done. The model might be useful to approximately predict the
overall production of such a still. Both prototypes worked well when tested and produced
a maximum of 0.57 L/h.m² of drinkable water for the former prototype and 0.79 L/h.m²
for the second one. However, the second prototype turned out to be more complex, less
viable, heavier and more expansive. It also has higher energy consumption and CO2
footprint at manufacturing and disposal stages. The former prototype is therefore the one
advised to be built. A mix of PC, Zinc, and wood seems to be one of the best solution
to built the system. The main improvements proposed in this work are to add reflectors
around the system, divide it into three sub-systems, use a thickness of 0.8-1mm of PC
for the top transparent layer, insulate the rest of the still, harvest the distillate water above
the seawater level and finally try to set a distillate water outlet by seawater inlet ratio of
about 2/3. Other minor improvements are made, mostly to make the design more robust.
Keywords: desalination, solar still, humidification dehumidification, Low-Tech, design,
arid areas, remote areas, passive technology

Introduction
1.1
The water accessibility issue
Thanks to the dedication of many scientists around the globe and the hard work of sev-
eral organization such as the IPCC, the environmental crisis is not debatable anymore.
Biodiversity is declining, a sixth planetary boundary has been crossed in 2022 [3] and
greenhouse gases emissions are constantly increasing resulting in diverse climate effects
such as an increase of the global temperature (fig. 1.1). In 2021, the ONU warned that
the world had already missed the Paris Agreements’ objective of an increase of +1.5°C
and that predictions argue for a catastrophic +2.7°C increase.
Figure 1.1: Global mean temperature difference from 1850 to 1900 [4]
This temperature increase and the perturbation of the water cycle lead to many extreme
events. The World Meteorological Organization (WMO) acknowledges that more than
50 million people were hit by disaster such as floods, droughts and storms in the world
in 2020 [5]. These disasters didn’t get better in 2021. Many regions of the world have
known an increase in the number of suffered droughts as seen on fig. 1.2. Among other,
subtropical South America suffered this kind of events for the second year in a row . It led
to ”significant agricultural losses”[4] and deeply disrupted citizens lives. Inequalities, bad
infrastructures or lack of financial means take the water accessibility issue up to the next
level: it is estimated that more than 2 billion of people, or a quarter of world’s population,
don’t have proper access to fresh water [6] [7].

1.2
What is Low-Tech?
Figure 1.4: Infography explaining the concept of Low-Tech by A. Keller and E. Bourningal
[12]
In this context of crisis, a way of rethinking technology and usage has made its appear-
ance. The Low-Tech movement, which seems currently to be more of a french concept,
seeks to enhance the development of solutions that meet basic needs such as the access
to water, food or energy by following three core principles: the solution must be useful,
accessible and sustainable [13]. The philosophy of the Low-Tech movement is ”to make
better, with less” [13] and is highly based on the Do It Yourself (DIY) mindset. Arthur Keller
and Emilien Bourningal tried to explain this concept with an infography shown above on
fig. 1.4. However, the concept still has blur boarders and may be considered as just a
new type of technology or as a complete political tool. Louis Lapaire and I proposed an
infography about Low-Techs to sensitize about the concept with an engineer point of view.
It is available in the appendix on fig. A.2.
Low-Tech actors are shyly taking a step forward on the international scene. The Low-Tech
Lab (LTL) is the precursor of the Low-Tech movement in France. Created by Corentin De
Chatelperron, this non-profit organization aims to discover and share all the Low-Tech
solutions around the world. A library of tutorials and documentation is available on their
website where several systems are already documented such as a solar oven, a passive
solar heating system or a wind turbine [13].
To find out all these technologies, to understand and test them, LTL’s team conducted
several projects. The main ones have been documented on different streaming platform
such as arte.tv. The Nomade des mers, le tour du monde de l’innovation (The sea nomad,
an innovation world tour) is a sail boat trip from 2016 to 2022 in order to meet inventors,
researchers and builders from around the world. It resulted into the still evolving library
on their website [13]. To test these solutions, the LTL’s founder did an experiment called
4 mois sur ma biosphère (4 months on my biosphere). With the help of about 30 low
technologies, he lived for 4 months in total autonomy on a 72 m² platform. Among the

1.3
A Low-Tech solution to water scarcity: the solar
desalinizer
96.5% of water on earth is available as seas and oceans [11] and it is estimated that dry
regions are surrounded by about 33 000 km of coast [14] which gives them easy access
to this salty water. It became obvious that using this resource could be an answer to the
aforementioned water access issue. Several industrial processes have been developed
such as reverse osmosis (RO), humidification and dehumidification (HDH) or mechanical
vapor compression (MVC) [14]. These technologies are now well known and have good
efficiency rates. However, they generally are complex in term of technology used and
consume high amounts of energy [14]. Passive SS or solar Humidifier Dehumidifer (HDH)
have thus been studied a lot to provide simple and affordable solution to water access, in
contrast to industrial systems. However, this technology generally runs with low efficiency
(about 35 %) [2] which is its biggest deficiency.
To tackle this efficiency issue and offer a useful, accessible and sustainable solution to
population in needs, many scientists have studied the SS. A team at the MIT [2], mister
Kumar [11] and others have dedicated years of work to improve this characteristic. In
France, a teacher from the Ecole Centrale de Nantes, Cyril Grandpierre, studied this kind
of system and tried to improve it as much as possible [1]. Starting from the Wilson’s
SS (presented in section 2.1), he added and modified different things, mostly inspired by
Maria Telkes, to improve the water yield and get to the final prototype he presents in the
document Solar Drops [1]. However, mister Grandpierre’s work was really experimental.
To be able to share the SS’s documentation on their website and to test mister Grand-
pierre’s last prototype, the LTL needs to get clearer and deeper insights on it. Indeed,
the experiment 4 mois sur ma biosphère will be renewed in Mexico in July 2022 and its
success relies a lot on getting the SS to work properly. Its production will not only provide
the water needed for survival but also water for cooking and for growing vegetables. A
total of 40 L/day is needed for two persons. This thesis aims to test the latest Cyril Grand-
pierre’s prototype and to characterize it. A second prototype that is supposed to get a lot
higher efficiency will be tested as well in the respect of the Low-Tech framework. Finally,
studies about the robustness and the sizing of a final prototype producing 40 L/day will be
provided. It is done in order to make technical documentation on how to build this system
and share it on the LTL’s website as an additional tutorial.

2
Water desalination: an overview
2.1
Water desalination history
Water desalination is firstly a process inspired by nature. For example, the different layers
of the earth’s surface act as filters that can sometimes make the seawater drinkable. The
cycle of water is also a huge HDH system. Water from seas and oceans evaporates
leaving the salt behind. When the air reaches the dew point (no more water can be
contained in a given volume of air), clouds are formed and it then rains potable water
which may be consumed and eventually ends up going back to its first place: seas and
oceans. Resulting developed technologies have then been used for more than 2000 of
years [15].
Inspired by this natural phenomenon, some people gave thoughts about it as early as dur-
ing the antiquity. Indeed, Plato and Aristotle tried to explain some desalination processes
and even made a few experimentations like filtering seawater with an earthly vessel. How-
ever, it remained unsuccessful [16] [14]. During the siege of Alexandria, Ptolemy cuts off
water supply to the city. Ceasar therefore decides to encourage his men to dig in the
ground to find potable water and manage to provide for his troupes [16]. The process
is then further studied to procure drinkable water to sailors on ships. Systems such as
wax filters or fleeces cover on the boats’ sides are tested [16]. One of the most devel-
oped strategy was for sailors to make seawater boil and to harvest the distilled water in a
sponge from which they could get the liquid [15]. Clay filters have also been used [17].
The middle age allows for the knowledge to spread from greek and latin worlds to arabic
and eastern worlds. The persian physician Abu Mansur Mwuaffaq is to be noticed for his
hard work aiming to explain the distillation process for water desalination [16]. Some other
methods are also further studied by Jhon Gaddesden such as the filtration of seawater
through a wax vessel or through dirt as well as distillation with the help of an alembic or by
boiling and condensing it [16]. However, distillation and solar distillation was mainly used
to prepare concentrated solution rather than to produce water at this time [14]. During
this period, more scientific basis for understanding these processes is given. It creates
a great starting point for the Renaissance and the modern times to start from and to dig
even deeper into those desalination solutions.
In 1560, a Spanish captain managed to provide about 40 large barrels of drinkable water
to a garrison left on an island with the help of distillation in alembics [16]. Several sci-
entists such as Francis Bacon or Giovan Battita Della Porta worked a lot on improving
the aforementioned technologies during the second half of the sixteenth century. Jean
Gautier then created in 1717 a still that has been used a lot on several ships for its great
efficiency due to a rotating part [16]. Finally, Luigi Ferdinando Marsilli tested out filtration
processes a lot. He also tried to remove the bitter taste of distilled water but it appeared
to be a really tough task [16].
Water desalination processes are then largely developed to higher scales during the nine-
teenth century and after thanks to steam propulsion [16] [15]. Charles Wilson developed
a 4460 m² desalination plant in 1872. It aimed to provide water to people and animals
working in a mine. He managed to produce 330 L/day of water [1]. In 1959 and in her
book Solar Still Construction, Maria Telkes made a great study of a flat SS with a much
lower thermal inertia than the Wilson’s SS [1]. During the same year, RO makes its ap-
pearance at the university of California and is industrially developed during the second

2.2
Desalination methods
In general, the different desalination methods can be classified in two groups: thermally
and electrically driven technologies. At the industrial scale, they are as followed [14]:
Thermally driven technologies
• Multiple Effect Distillation (MED) relies on several stages of distillation where water
is evaporated, leaving salt behind and then condensed on a cold surface and har-
vested. Generally, latent heat of condensing water is recycled and injected into the
seawater inlet [11].
• Multi Stage Flash (MSF) distillation is similar to MED. Seawater is heated and sent
to multiple chambers. The pressure in each room is lower than the precedent al-
lowing for water to be instantly vaporized, leaving the salt behind. The water is then
condensed on a cold surface and harvested [14]. It requires about 20 stages and a
temperature around 100 °C [11].
• Thermal Vapor Compression’s (TVC) principle dwells on compressing the water va-
por that has not been condensed with a steam thermo-compressor to heat up the
inlet seawater [14].
Electrically driven methods
• RO consists into applying pressure on seawater which will pass through a semiper-
meable membrane. The pressure applied is higher than osmotic pressure making
it impossible for the salt to also go through the membrane [14] [15] [11].
• Electrodialysis (ED) is similar to RO. However, it is not pressure that makes the
separation of salt from water possible. It is done by applying a difference of electrical
potential to the seawater. This will create a flow of the anions and cations that are
in the solution. Selective membranes will allow to filter those particles and therefore
to remove salt from the seawater which will then be harvested [15] [14].

• MVC is the same principle as TVC. However, the remaining vapor used to heat up
the seawater inlet is pressurized with the help of a mechanical compressor.
The industrial scale is mainly considering two of those desalination methods because of
their economical and efficiency properties. Those two methods are RO for about 60% of
world’s production with a rate of around 5 kWh/m3 and MED for around 27% of this same
production and with a rate of around 25 kWh/m3 [14]. These plants allow for a huge part of
the world’s population to have access to clean water. However, some are still working with
fossil fuels which makes them polluting. Moreover, desalination requires high amounts of
energy which generally makes the use of those technologies expansive [11].
Other methods exist for water desalination. The most famous are as following:
• Freezing is the process of bringing the water’s temperature down to the point where
it becomes ice. By doing so, salt will crystallize and will then be removed by washing
it from the ice. It will then be melted back to its liquid state [11].
• HDH is the basis for most of the thermal processes aforementioned. However, only
one stage of this process is not always sufficient to provide water at an industrial
scale. It relies on heating up water to vaporize it in a chamber and to condense it
on a cooler surface in an other chamber [14] [11].
• SS have been studied a lot and are already used in some regions. It is the same
principle as HDH but only one chamber is used both for evaporating water and
condensing it. The energy needed is directly harvested from solar rays [11].
The main default of these three last solutions is generally a lack of efficiency and explains
why it has not been developed to the industrial scale.
2.3
About solar stills
A SS is a desalination system based on the HDH principle. It is illustrated in the following
fig. 2.2. A SS consists of a closed volume called a greenhouse. A transparent layer at
the top allows for the sun’s energy to come in (a,b on fig. 2.2). A layer, called absorber,
will absorb this incoming energy to redistribute it to the dirty water inlet (2 on fig. 2.2.
This water will evaporate within the still, leaving impurities behind (3 on fig. 2.2). The
formed water vapor will then come into contact with a thermally conducting layer of the
still on which it will condense (4 on fig. 2.2). Water drops will then form on this surface
on which they will drip down to a collector (5 on fig. 2.2). In most cases, the transparent
layer is also the condensing surface. Water is generally contained into a basin within the
still. However, since desalination is a very old technology, many different prototypes have
been tested out. This section aims to list a few research made on SS.

Unconventional solar stills
In 1950, the scientist Maria Telkes studied a new type of flat SS [1] . In this type of still, no
water basin is required as the seawater inlet is channeled through a piece of fabric. By
Doing so, it is possible to orient the still in order to optimize its solar rays absorption. The
fabric will first absorb solar energy which it will give back to water flowing in it. It will also
retain water from flowing around and also from flowing too fast, increasing the chance of
evaporating it. This type of SS is the one that has been studied by a french teacher, Cyril
Grandpierre, in his work Solar Drops [1] and shown on fig. 2.7. In the Solar Drops paper,
a short history of SS is given. Some insights about what and how parameters improve
the efficiency of this type of still is given. Instructions of how to build such a SS are then
followed by a few experiments and comparisons of this still with a Wilson one. With this
new type of still, M. Grandpierre managed to obtain 3 times the production of a standard
SS. Cyril Grandpierre’s work will be the basis of the present master thesis project.

From that model, a few conclusions can be made, that will remain to be verified with a
real life situation (which is done later on in this work):
• It is possible to reach the distillate water flow obtained with the model.
• The still operates at a really high humidity ratio (around 95%).
• The production rate of the SS increases linearly with its area. Doubling the area

• The production rate of the SS increases linearly with the absorbed power. Doubling

• The production rate of the SS increases linearly with the temperature of the inlet

value defined by the ratio of the difference of the absorbed power and the losses

4.1
The method
Indoor experiments have been made with a 54x45cm artificial sun that simulates the sun’s
light spectrum and its radiative heat flux. This artificial sun consists into a huge lamp that
could produce a heat flux of from 750 W/m² to 1460 W/m². The presence of fans on this
same system allowed to increase the convection around the system. However, those were
”on-off” fans without the possibility to modulate their speed. Seawater was simulated with
tap water in which salt was added. The aimed salinity of this water was around 35 g/L
[45], which is the average salinity of seawater around the world.
As said before, the first iteration of the prototype, sometimes refered to as ”proto1”, has
been built upon Cyril Grandpierre’s instructions [1] and is presented in fig. 4.1. A detailed
illustration of the built prototype is given below in fig. 4.2. A few differences have to be
noted. The corners of the frames have been designed in a way to limit air flows and
therefore losses through them. The attempt to make one side of the fabric waterproof
was unsuccessful. A metal plate as been installed as an alternative to support that fabric
in order to keep it flat as well as to prevent leakages of seawater into the distilled water.
Finally, the water inlet as been set up as a pipe going through the system on its whole
width. Small holes were drilled in this pipe which was then surrounded by the fabric (see
fig. 4.2).

4.3
Summary of the former prototype experiments
The experiment as well as comparison with the model can lead to the following conclu-
sions:
• The mathematical model does not predict well the behaviour of the still. However,
it does not give a bad approximation of the produced distilled water rate and could
therefore be used for broad dimensioning of a SS similar to the one studied.
• The produced freshwater flow rate is linearly dependent on the input sun’s heat flux.
Doubling this input leads to doubling the produced freshwater flow rate. Both the
model and the experiment confirm this.
• Lowering the absorptivity of the fabric highly reduces the performances of the still.
Both the model and the experiment confirm this.
• Reducing the thermal resistance of the still (by changing the material or by increas-
ing convection) does not necessarily increases its performance. The model showed
an increase up to a maximum value which was not confirmed by the experiment. It
seemed that the optimum set up was when insulating the backplate (metal+wood)
and using a 3mm thick glass. But the optimum value of the glass layer’s thickness
might be around 4mm.

5.3
Robustness study
A robust design is a design that has been studied in a way to reduce variations and failures
and increase its usability [48]. In their article, Martin Ebro and Thomas J. Howard shed
light on multiple principles to increase the robustness of a design. A few of them seem
really appropriate to the SS studied before [48]:
• P1 - ”Parameter optimisation: If the Transfer Function (TF) is non-linear, the perfor-
mance variation can be reduced by adjusting the values of the design parameters
to fit with the ‘flat’ (non-sensitive) areas of the transfer curve”.
• P2 - ”Change of design principle: If the conceptual solution is changed, the TF and
the design parameters will also change. As part of the concept selection process,
the expected performance variation of each solution can be calculated and used as
input”.
• P3 - ”Uncoupling and decoupling: If multiple functions depend on the same design
parameter, the range of available values for a design parameter is reduced, because
two functional requirements have to be met simultaneously. As a consequence, the
design engineer may have to make a compromise and select design parameters
resulting in a mean functional performance that is different from the target value,
which in turn increases the performance variation”.

==PAGE:54==
• P4 - ”Redundancy: redundancy is typically used where the functional variation can
become so extensive that it turns into a failure with serious consequences. The
redundant functionality can be inactive during normal use, for example, a back-up
power supply for a nuclear power plant, or be an active part of normal use, for
example, multiple engines on an airplane.”

their capillary properties, pump the seawater from a reservoir, bring it inside the still and
conduct the remaining brine out. The fabric used is about 120 g/m² heavy wool textile.

7.1
Materials selection
The method of selection
To select materials for the final concept, the software Granta Edupack has been used.
The following criteria have been taken into account to make acceptable choices about the
different elements of the prototypes:
• The maximum service temperature: experiments showed that a maximum tem-
perature of about 100°C was reached. A safety coefficient of 1.25 has been used,
increasing the considered maximum service temperature to 125°C.
• The thermal conductivity: elements of the still must either thermally insulate or
conduct, considering their role in the system. Materials have therefore been con-
sidered being either ”Poor conductor, Good insulator” or ”Good conductor, Poor
insulator”.
• The UV durability: some elements are directly exposed to the sun on a daily basis,
it is therefore important to have good resistance against UV radiations.
• The water durability: some elements are directly exposed to water (either fresh or
salty), it is therefore crucial to have good resistance against this kind of environment
to limit variations and risks.
• The optical properties: the transparent layers in each prototype require specific
optical properties. For these elements, a criteria of transparency have been consid-
ered.
• The durability to marine atmosphere: the system is expected to be mostly used in
a marine atmosphere and therefore must be able to withstand this kind of conditions.
• The Young’s modulus: frames are the core elements that keep all the elements in
place. They also help to ensure to seal the condensing rooms and must therefore not
be too flexible. The material’s Young’s modulus is a good indicator of this behaviour.
A minimum limit of 8 GPa has been set based on average values of different types of
wood [49]. This criteria is experimental and further study about what is the optimal
Young’s modulus for material selection must be conducted.
Not all the elements of the two prototypes have to meet specific properties for those crite-
ria. Moreover, some of these criteria, when studied together for a same element, can be
too constraining leading to no existing material meeting the requirements. This problem is
dealt with by making compromises on one or several of the previously mentioned criteria.
Those criteria were applied as limits within the material selections software Granta Edu-
pack using the level 2 materials library. Those limits have been applied to three different
graphs comparing three materials’ properties in the following priority order : the CO2
footprint of their primary production, their recycling fraction and finally their price per kilo.
Indeed, those criteria seemed the more relevant for the Low-Tech framework the still is
designed within. Low prices help to meet its social requirements. CO2 footprint and re-
cycling fraction help to meet environmental expectations. They are also relevant for both

8
Conclusion
The global environmental crisis allied with social issues in many countries of the world lead
to high difficulties about water accessibility for a quarter of the world’s population [6] [7].
These crisis, which have been widely documented, are expected to become worst in the
coming decades [50] [8] [9]. In that context, the Low-Tech Lab seeks to develop and share
the use of low technologies, that is to say, technologies that are useful, accessible and
sustainable (see fig. 8.1), in order to question the relationship populations have with them
to reduce their impact on the environment [13]. Among those technologies, one could
be a solution to the water scarcity issue mentioned before in arid and remote areas: the
solar desalinizer, commonly known as solar still. This document goes through the design
process of such a still based on a prototype previously tested by M. Cyril Grandpierre and
inspired by the work of Maria Telkes and M. Wilson [1]. The production goal is of 40 L/day
for two persons.

nologies, mostly inspired by the natural water cycle on earth, are thousands of years old

be fulfilled [16]. For a long time, the efficiency of those technologies as well as the bitter

scaled technologies are well known and allow to provide 86.55 million m3 of freshwater
worldwide [11]. As said before, the one technology studied in this project is the solar still
which is not industrially scaled.
