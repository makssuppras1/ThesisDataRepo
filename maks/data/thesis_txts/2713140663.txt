Abstract
Background and Motivation: Multi-electrode arrays can help identify cardiotoxic drugs in pre-
clinical reducing drug development costs. The standard to produce mutli-electrode arrays is via clean-
room procedures. These are expansive, complicated multi-step procedures that require rigid substrates.
Objectives: develop a biocompatible conductive ink that can be used for direct ink writing 3D print-
ing. Design a muliti-electrode array for multi-material 3D printing and develop a printing procedure.
Conduct a proof of concept to validate the device’s design and biocompatible conductive ink.
Methods: the biocompatible conductive ink will be based on PEDOT:PSS. Different concentration of
it will be obtained via evaporation of the solvent and carbon based thixotropic agents will be added to
improve printability and viscosity. Qualitative, width and resistance analysis will be used to identify
the best ink formulation. SilidWorks will be used to create 3D model of the multi-electrode array pro-
totypes. These will be then 3D printed and inspected both visually and via a image analysis software.
A proof-of-concept recording will be conducted using a commercial multi-electrode array setup.
Key Results: the viscosity of water based PEDOT:PSS is mainly attributed to the PEDOT:PSS
concentration.
Printability is increased by the addition of a small amount of tixotropic agent; e.g.
(PEDOT:PSS) : carbon black=10:1. The biggest challenge in the design and printing procedure of the
milti-electrode array was the allignment of the printheads. The 3D printed devices, with 2D and 3D
electrodes, could successfully stimulate and record signals down to 1mV.
Conclusion: fully 3D printed multi-electrode arrays have the potential to increase accessibility of the
multi-electrode arrays and decrease drug development cost.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement
Only one out of 5-10 thousand drug candidates actually makes it to the final approval stage of the drug
development process [1]. Of these, 28% fail during the last two phases of clinical trials [2]; mostly due
to cardiovascular safety concerns [1]. This entails higher development costs and longer development
times. To reduce these, it is necessary to develop pre-clinical testing models that can better simulate
human in-vivo models [3]. Part of the issue is that current animal models have low aﬀinity to human
ones (rodent models have only a 43%)[1].
The use of human-induced pluripotent stem cells as well as 3D tissue models that help increase biolog-
ical resemblance can lead to greater aﬀinity. The use of human-induced pluripotent stem cells as well
as 3D tissue models that mimic the biological morphology increase the similarities with in-vivo organ
functioning[4][5]. This can help early detection of drug-induced cardiotoxicity (”toxicity that affects
the heart” [6]).
Traditionally cardiotoxicity is investigated via a patch-clamp essay. This technique, although capable
of detecting ionic currents from a single channel [7], lacks the ability to have a continuous and multi-
cellular overview of myocardial health [8]. The use of muti-electrode arrays (MEA) can make up for
what patch-clamp techniques lacks. Thus, MEA can increasing early detection of cardiotoxicity as well
as reducing development costs and development times.
Commercial MEA are usually produced via cleanroom procedures such as thin-film deposition, lithog-
raphy, and etching [9]. These multi-step fabrication procedures are complicated, expensive, and have
high energy consumption requirements [10].
Shifting MEA production to a multi-material 3D-printing based one could significantly decrease fabbri-
cation complexity and thus cost. This could potentially lead to a wider use of MEA for cardiotoxicity
screenings.
1.2
Project Goals
Here is a brief insight into the project goals and structure within this report. An overview of the project
will be given in section 3
1. Development of conductive DIW inks based on PEDOT:PSS and carbon particles
2. Design of a MEA device.
3. Procedure optimization for multi-material 3D printing of a MEA with custom inks - insulating
and conducting ones.
4. Proof-of-concept recording of electrical signals via the 3D-printed MEA device.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Cardiotoxicity
Cardiotoxicity occurs when a drug affects vital myocardial functions such as electrophysiology, contrac-
tility, mitochondrial vitality, and growth factor and cytokine regulation is threatened (see figure 2.1).
A brief introduction to the cardiac muscle and cardiomyocytes can be found in section A.1.
Figure 2.1: Main mechanisms for cardiotoxicity in red. Their effects on the heart in white..
For this report, only detection of electrophysiologic drug-induced cardiotoxicity will be further discussed.
When testing for electrophysiologic drug-induced cardiotoxicity a cell culture is exposed to the drug
and its electrical activity is monitored for arrhythmic episodes (see section A.2). There are two main
recording techniques to do so in invitro tissue: intracellular and extracellular recordings [11].
2.1.1
Intracellular Recordings
Intracellular recordings are usually manual and invasive procedures that can analyze the cell’s sub-
threshold ionic activity, meaning that excitatory, inhibitory, or endogenous AP events can be detected
[7] [12]. A pipette is used to gain contact with the cell membrane and monitor the voltage membrane
potential. The measurement can last up to a few hours [13] [14]. The pipette exhibits good coupling
with the membrane, this translates to having a high impedance (’gigaseal’) [8]. Ohms law (equation
2.1) explains why, even if the ionic currents (I) are small, changes in the voltage membrane potentials
(V) can be measured if the impedance (Z) is big.
V = I × Z
(2.1)
An illustration of a cell-attached patch-clamp can be seen in figure 2.2. The pipette, filled with physio-
logical solution, attaches to an ion channel via a gigaseal and can thus measure the small ionic currents.

2.1.2
Extracellular Recordings
Extracellular recordings are noninvasive voltage recordings performed via electrodes, these can detect
the electrical activity of cells without damaging or interfering with the cell’s activity. The coupling
between the cells and the electrodes is not eﬀicient, this implies that subthreshold ionic currents can
not be measured [8] [16]. Although extracellular recordings are not as sensitive as intracellular ones,
they can record signals for much longer, up to a few months [17]. For this report, only extracellular
recordings via multielectrode arrays will be further discussed in section 2.2.
MEA can detect local field potentials (LFPs); voltage potentials originating from a group of cells [18].
LFPs reflect the action potential (AP) activity of a limited tissue area, thus giving insight into its
duration and propagation within the tissue [19]. An overview of AP and LFPs is found in appendix
A.1.1.
2.2
Microelectrode Arrays MEA
MEA devices consist of a series of equally spaced electrodes located in a well plate designed for cell
culture[20].
In figure 2.3 a commercial MEA with all its main components can be seen. When cardiomyocytes are
located in the well their beating can be monitored thanks to the electrodes ( see figure 2.3.c). These
are connected to the contact pads that allow the signal to be transmitted to a digital interface that can
detect and record the LFPs activity [20]. Other key components of the MEA are the insulation layer
and the substrate. The first one insulates the wiring that connects electrodes and contact pads while
still allowing cell adhesion. The substrate, usually glass, needs to withstand the fabrication process and
dictate the stiffness of the final MEA.

2.2.1
Equivalent Circuit Model
Passive electrical circuit models are useful to understand the cell-electrode interface and optimize cell-
device electrical coupling. The cell’s membrane is divided into two components, a junction where the
membrane faces the electrode and a non-junction. A cleft is present between the cell and the electrode,
it is referred to as the seal resistance and it directly modulates the current that passes from the cell
to the electrode[22] [23]. Extracellular currents produced during AP are schematized as an alternating
current generator inside the cell.

etching techniques performed in a cleanroom. A cleanroom is an environment where air, temperature,
and humidity are constantly monitored.
Thin-film deposition is a process, usually performed under vacuum, that covers a substrate with a very
thin layer of material, conductive or semiconductor. There are different methods of thin-film deposition
that are commonly used such as vacuum evaporation, sputter deposition, and chemical vapor deposition.
A combination of methods can be used during the deposition of different layers that make up the MEA
[9].

2.3
Multi-Material 3D printing
Advances in 3D printing technologies have made it possible to increase printers’ sensitivity and precision.
The development of 3D-printable functional inks (e.g.: conductive, paramagnetic,..etc.) enabled the
creation of 3D-printed devices such as MEAs. This enabled scaling down complex electrical designs while
maintaining the advantages associated with 3D printing: fast prototyping with low cost, customizable
designs, and waste reduction by on-demand production.
Manufacturing devices via 3D-printing enables the use of flexible substrates; this is nearly impossible
with a cleanroom setup because of the high temperatures that the procedures implies. The possibility
of using different substrates allows for greater versatility and thus more accurate replications of in-vivo
conditions [12] [4][28]. Two of the most common 3D-printing techniques are inkjet and direct ink writing
(DIW).
2.3.1
InkJet Fabrication
Figure 2.5: Inkjet printing, on the left continu-
ous jet while on the right drop-on-
demand method. [29].
Inkjet print is a non-contact printing method that
uses droplets as building blocks to create pat-
terns and thanks to the use of functional inks also
electrical circuits.
There are two main types of
inkjet printers:
continuous jet (CIJ) and drop-
on-demand (DOD). The first one has a continu-
ous stream of droplets that, after having passed
through charging plates, are either directed to-
wards the substrate or collected and reused. DOD
printing methods only produce droplets when re-
quired, this may lead to clogging of the nozzle
but it decreases waist.
Thermal DOD produces
droplets thanks to a resistor that heats the ink
while piezo DOD utilizes a piezoelectric printhead
that can modulate ejection velocity and droplet
volume by changing actuating voltage, although
the droplet size is mainly determined by the nozzle
size.
Functional inks are made from mixing electronically relevant materials (conductors, semiconductors,
dielectric, and insulating) with a solvent, they must have rheological properties that allow for droplet
formation, extrusion, and deposition with high precision.
After droplet deposition and subsequent
“wetting” of the substrate is achieved the droplets need to attach and form a uniform and continuous
structure. After printing has terminated the electrical component sometimes requires further process-
ing/curing such as heat treatment to improve the end product [30].
In order to obtain droplet formation, viscosity and thus solid content of the functional material used
need to be relatively low. This, especially for conductive inks, can limit conductivity and thus sensitivity
to stimuli.
2.3.2
Direct Ink Writing
Instead of using droplets as building blocks, direct ink writing (DIW) 3D printers build patterns and
structures by depositing a filament in a layer-by-layer fashion. Enabling the creation of complex 3D
structures. Its functional components consist of a printhead, a dispenser, and a three-axis platform all
connected to a computer that regulates extrusion pressure and relative platform location. The print-
head is comprised of a cartridge that holds the ink and a nozzle. This last one is what mostly influences
the size of the filament [31].
==PAGE:14==
2.3 Multi-Material 3D printing

Filament extrusion is mediated by pneumatic pressure, piston, or by a screw mechanism. Printing
conditions, such as speed, pressure, and temperature only partially contribute to the effectiveness of
the printing result. Key to DIW success are the viscosity and shear-thinning properties of the ink used,
which ensures extrusion, filament deposition, and shape retention [31]. In this report, we will focus on
polymer-based bio-inks.
Figure 2.6: DIW extrusion modalities [31].
DIW ink Printability
”Printability is the capability to form and maintain
reproducible 3D scaffolds” [32]. Printability is in-
fluenced by the inks’ properties such as viscosity,
shear-thinning, and extrudability [31].
The viscosity of a fluid is its resistance to flow.
Higher viscosity leads to higher printing fidelity al-
though it requires higher extrusion force that can
sometimes damage the ink’s structure [31].
Shear-thinning is a particular flow behavior, that
entails a decrease of the viscosity when the ink is
flowing. The faster the ink flows the less viscose it
becomes; after which the ink recovers to its origi-
nal viscosity.
Extrudability is the ability of the ink of being ex-
truded through a nozzle. This is dependent on the

DIW Printing Procedure
To 3D print a device/object there are a few essential steps that need to be performed. First, the design
needs to be drawn on CAD (Computer Aided Design) software. If the design has some areas that need
to be completely covered by the ink the infill pattern needs to be determined. This can be adjusted
and modified according to the ink characteristics and the desired result.
Printing parameters such as printing height, feed rate, pressure, material thickness, strand start delay
(SSD), and the number of layers need to be determined and inserted in the CAD software. The required
parameters can vary according to the 3D printer used.
The CAD software can now generate the code that the 3D printer uses to print. This is called G-code
and is a computer numerical control programming language that tells the 3D printer where to print the
x-y-z plane.

2.4 PEDOT:PSS and DMSO
10
Poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT) is a partially oxidised conjugated polymer. It has a par-
tially mobile cationic charge that enables it to behave as a p-doped semiconductor.
Poly(styrenesulfonate) (PSS) is an anionic polymer that helps stabilizing the mobile cationic charge
originating from the PEDOT. When PEDOT and PSS electrostatically interact they form oligomeric
structures (”Oligomers are low-molecular-weight, cationic, organic molecules”,[40]).
Chains of PSS
entangles and π-π sacking between PEDOT leads to the formation of colloidal structures. When in
aqueous solutions, PEDOT:PSS (PDP) colloids have a hydrophobic core rich in PEDOT and a hy-
drophilic PSS-rich shell (see figure 2.11.A) [41].
Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) is a polar organic solvent commonly added that, after water evaporation,
brakes down PDP colloidal structures; facilitating oligomer unfolding and entanglement. DMSO reduces
electrostatic interactions between PEDOT and PSS and reduces the number of PSS interchain hydrogen
bonds.
This increases conductivity and modifies mechanical/ rheological properties [41][42].
Thus,
doping of PDP with DMSO (7-10 wt%) can be extremely beneficial for the fabrication of DIW inks
[42].
Annealing of PDP with temperatures up to 150°C has been found to further increase its conductivity.
This effect is however dependent on layer thickness [43][44].

CHAPTER 3
Project overview
The aims of the project will represent the major parts of the report.
1. Development of conductive DIW inks based on PEDOT:PSS and carbon particles
Taking inspiration from Yuk, Hyunwoo, et al. [38] a conductive DIW inks based on PDP will be devel-
oped. Instead of freeze-drying and redispersing the stock solution of PDP to increase its solid content;
water will be removed via evaporation and 10% v/v DMSO will be added [45]. Carbon particles (CB
and graphite) will be used as thixotropic agents to improve printability and hopefully increase conduc-
tivity. Studies will be based on qualitative and resistance analysis.
2. Design of an MEA device.
A commercially available MEA design will be modified to accommodate for spatial resolutions of custom
inks. The design will be further optimized for multimaterial 3D printing. Inspired by Schnitker, Jan,
et al. [37] and Yuk, Hyunwoo, et al. [38], four inks will be used.
• Biocompatible conductive ink: PDP based,
• Conductive path ink: silver based,
• Insulating ink: polystyrene (Ps) based,
• Well ink: glass ink
3. Procedure optimization for multi-material 3D printing of a MEA with custom inks; both conduct-
ing and insulating.
The 3D printing procedure of the designed MEA will be established and both 2D and 3D MEAs will
be printed. This will be optimized through an image analysis Matlab script.
4. Proof-of-concept recording of electrical signals via the 3D-printed MEA device.
Finally a proof-of-concept recording will be performed via a commercially available MEA recording
setup.

biocompatible conductive ink (Sigma PDP 4wt%:graphite=1:1 (D1S,GR), Sigma PDP 4wt%:graphite=1:2
(D2S,GR)), 15wt% Ps, silver ink, glass ink, rectangular substrate holder (made with the group), camera
(DinoLite microscope USB camera (S/N A8401220)), 3D printer, Solidwork, vacuum oven, microscope
Method:
Loading procedure: D1S,Gr or D2S,Gr , silver ink and Ps inks were prepared and inserted in the
cartridge, the nozzle (D1S,Gr and Ps inks: 60µm metal one, Silver ink: 150µm metal) was screwed on
and then loaded into the 3D printer. The substrate holder was mounted on the print plate and held in
place by 3 metal pins and vacuum; the glass substrate was then positioned in the substrate holder.
Calibration procedure: using the printer’s interface needle-length-measurement (NLM) and surface-
height-measurement (SHM) were performed. The print heads where lowered to position (X0.000 Y2.000
Z1.000), looking through the camera and using step movements of 0.100 and 0.010 the zero position
for the Z-axis was adjusted and verified. Print pressure was adjusted until the material extrusion was
uniform (this parameter was later further tuned in relation to the printing velocity). The procedure
was repeated for all print heads loaded.
Alignment procedure: after having loaded the printer with all the print heads, a trial print of the
final design was performed (printing pressure and velocity were adjusted if needed). Print alignment
was verified by positioning the substrate under the microscope. If misalignment was present between
the inks in X and/or Y-axis, the offset was corrected by modifying the settings. A second trial print
was performed to verify that offset had been corrected.
Nozzle purging: before every print, print heads nozzles were purged and cleaned by immersing them
in an appropriate solvent -water if water-based ink, acetone if organic-based ink- and some material
was expelled. During ink exhaustion the pressure was turned up and down until the nozzle appeared
clean. When nozzle appeared clean the exhaustion was terminated ad the solvent bath was removed.
This step is important to avoid nozzle clogging during the print and to avoid moving the nozzle thus
logins alignment between the print heads.
Printing: the desired design, drawn on Solidworks was exported to BioCAD where all necessary print-
ing parameters (initial height, feed rate, material thickness, SSD, number of layers) were assigned. Once
everything was ready, BioCAD generated the G-code that was loaded on the 3D printer’s interface and
the print was initiated. The device print order was the following: reference electrode (D1S,Gr ink),
electrodes (D1S,Gr,ink), silver lines (silver ink), silver reference (silver ink), silver pads (Silver ink),
insulation (15%Ps) and finally 2 layers of dots (D1S,Gr,ink). The G-codes can be found in appendx D
Annealing: the devices where annealed for 24h at 70°C.
Well print: after annealing the glass ink was prepared and loaded in the printer, a 400µm plastic
nozzle was used. NLM and SHM were conducted again to calibrate the printer. The desired design
was printed on BioCAD and the necessary printing parameters were assigned. G-code (found in section
D.5) was loaded in the printer’s interface and print executed. The devices were left one week to dry
(first 2 days under the fumehood).

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
6.1
Ink Development
Qualitative observations regarding Sigma PDP inks with increasing PDPwt% show that the viscosity
of the ink increases with the concentration of PDP. This result is in agreement with Yuk, Hyunwoo,
et al. [38]. This could mean that the preparation method of the ink does not significantly affect the
viscosity.
Width measurements of these inks show that width decrease with increasing concentration, this result
is in accordance with the definition of viscosity: resistance to flow. Width measurements are influenced
not only but the viscosity by also by the nature of the substrate and the ink nature. Being the main
solvent water and being the glass hydrophilic: this means the ink has high aﬀinity to the substrate and
will tend to expand on the surface. When trying to 3D print MEAs this characteristic leads to less
spatial resolution but allows for better infill of the inner reference. If a hydrophilic substrate were to
be used instead the spatial resolution would be increased but dewetting might occur.
The viscosity observations regarding PDP- CB and PDP- MFC (figure 5.4 ,5.7) indicate that the vis-
cosity of the ink is not significantly influenced by the thixotropic agent. It is important to note that
the solid content of CB/MFC introduced was very small compared to the PDP (less than 50%). This
result could have been further consolidated by showing photos of the PDP-graphite ink droplets.
Qualitative printing observation from tables 5.2, 5.3, and 5.4 highlight that adding a small amount of
thixotropic agent can improve shape fidelity and reduce nozzle clogging when using Sigma PDP. An
explanation to this phenomenon could be related to the thixotropic agent interactions with the PDP
colloidal structures, breaking them and thus improving sheer thinning and viscoelastic properties.
A printability improvement cannot be seen when adding graphite to Ph1000 PDP. Not much can be
concluded from this result since the study was limited to only two inks with graphite.
The same tables also show that, when too much thixotropic agent is used the benefits are lost. From the
microscope images (figure B.1, B.2, and B.3, B.4) it is clear that the addition of excessive thixotropic
agent promotes aggregate formation. This leads to nozzle occlusion and non-uniform shape fidelity. The
nature of the thixotropic agent can influence aggregate formation. Figure B.2 shows that when CB is
present with 10:2 ratio (or higher) blue aggregates can be seen. These could be PDP aggregates forming
due to the CB presence. These aggregates cannot be seen when using graphite. In literature PDP-CB
inks are sonicated(1-2h) before printing [47][45]. Doing so could help breaking down the aggregates and
form a more uniform ink.
The result of the conductivity measurement shows that CB decreases the conductivity. This result is
counter-intuitive and in contrast with what is found in the literature [48] [47]. A key difference between
the study conducted and the one in literature is that in the ones conducted the conductivity was mea-
sured before annealing. Another difference is that in literature [47] CB-PEDOT:PSS ink was sonicated
for 30 minutes and then spin-coated. In this project the ink was not sonicated, this could have led to
the formation of CB aggregates which could have negatively influenced the conductivity. Du, Yong, et
al. [45] demonstrate that conductivity drops when a 10:1 ratio (PDP:CB) is added, but the conductiv-
ity value is smaller by a factor of 10. Conductivity values are comparable with Yuk, Hyunwoo, et al. [38].
With the Silver oxidation study, it was verified that silver ink does not oxidize. Through the annealing

procedure study, we saw that the silver ink resistance, although affected by the different procedures
(figure 5.18), did not significantly increase compared to the Sigma PDP resistance values. This means
that the selection of the annealing procedure can be independent from the effect on silver ink.
It was demonstrated that annealing procedures did not influence the resistance of samples with Sigma
PDP 1.1wt%/2wt% lines and silver pads. This result is exciting since annealing at 70°C allows the use
of plastic-based flexible substrates that can not withstand high temperatures.
Studies on the interaction between Sigma PDP and silver ink were inconclusive. This interaction should
be further studied so that the design can be optimised by minimising or maximising the overlap area.
The width of the nozzle influences the effect of annealing on the resistance of the Sigma PDP 2wt%
traces. This is due to the layer height and it is in accordance with what is found in literature [43], [45].
Non the less the spatial resolution requirements of the MEA design demand the use of a 60µm nozzle.
The substrate-PDP and annealing-thickness study show that, when using a 60µm nozzle, the resistance
of the samples on the oxidized polystyrene is lower compared to those on glass. However, this is not
true when a bigger nozzle is used, making the study inconclusive.
Disclaimer: The width of the traces printed with all inks was highly dependent on the pressure and
feed rate used during printing. These parameters varied considerably from batch to batch and were
dependent on whether a Sigma PDP up concentration was used and when this was prepared. A more
rigorous protocol for ink preparation could result in better reproducibility of printing parameters and
width consistency.
6.2
Design and Multi-Material 3D Printing
A MEA design was successfully made and 3D printed. The most challenging and time-consuming aspect
of this process was the alignment between the different print heads. This was not expected since not
much is said in literature regarding alignment issues or procedures.
The image analysis Matlab script was effective in identifying the electrodes and calculating the mean
distances between them. This was helpful in validating the printing limitation of the 3D printer used.
The annealing process successfully renders PDP traces non-soluble in water but Sigma PDP and PH1000
do not adhere well to a glass substrate. Meaning the segments are dislocated after the water bath. This
is probably the cause of the electrodes breaking after the proof-of-concept recordings. The water used
to wash out the electrodes from the PBS after signal recording must have torn the 2D and 3D electrodes
out.
This problem could be solved by using a different substrate- e.g. oxidized polystyrene or a cell culture
well plate. Or by adding a binder that promoted glass adhesion to the ink.
Regardless of the number of layers and the geometry of the electrodes the 3D printed MEA managed to
detect a voltage stimulus down to 1mV. This result is a success. The readings from the commercial MEA
appear to be less sensitive but it is important to consider the size and surface area of the electrodes.
Commercial MEA has 2D electrodes with a diameter 30µm, the one layer no dots 3D printed MEA has
2D electrodes with a diameter of 79.23±11.40 (table 4.1), and one with 2 layers and dots has additional
2xπx[(79.23 ± 11.40)/2]2 surface area. This means that although the 3D printed MEA have greater
sensitivity it cannot be directly compared to the commercial one. In addition, the spatial resolution
and electrode density of the developed MEA is considerably less compared to the commercial one.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
During this project it was demonstrated that water-based PDP DIW ink can be developed without
freeze-drying. Thus the preparation complexity of the biocompatible conductive ink can be resuced and
it can be more easy to produce.
The viscosity of Sigma PDP inks was mostly affected by the PDP concentration. The printability these
inks improved when a small amount of thixotropic agents are added (10:1 CB, 1:1 graphite, 20:1 MFC).
The introduction of carbon particles reduced the resistance of segments printed with a 60µm nozzle but
also reduced the conductivity of the ink before annealing.
Using iterative prototyping a successful MEA design with both 2D and 3D electrodes was developed
using four inks: two conductive, one insulating, and one to build the glass well.
Optimization of printing procedure was achieved thanks to the creation of protocols such as printhead
alignment and nozzle purging. Image analysis help identify the printers printing limitations and verify
that the different printing procedure yielded the desired effects. The final spatial resolution of the MEA
was: electrode diameter 79.23±11.40µm and spaced 400µm cone from the other.
Finally a proof-of-concept experiment demonstrated that the 3D printed MEA -with both 2D and 3D
electrodes- could stimulate and record voltage signals down to 1mV. This result means that DIW MEA
that use water based PDP can successfully record myocardium membrane potentials. Although there is
still a lot of work that need to be done the fabrication method explored in this project has been proves
successfully and a viable solution for in-vitro drug testing.
7.1
Future Work
A lot need to be further studied and tested. A more rigorous preparation procedure should be devel-
oped to minimize batch-to-batch variations in the PDP ink. This would allow for more robust width
measurements and general reproducibility.
A conductivity measurement of Simga PDP 4wt% should be conducted after annealing. A study to
see how this varies with the addition of CB and graphite could also be beneficial to further understand
how the PDP interacts with the carbon particles. Furthermore, the up-concentrations of Ph1000 PDP
should also be investigated to see how the PDP concentrations influences the printability and viscosity.
Moreover, a deeper understanding of how the biocompatible conductive ink interacts with the Silver
and Ps ink could give an insight into how to further optimize the MEA design.
One of the most concerning issues that need to be further addressed is the PDP ink not adhering to
the glass substrate. This issue needs to be addressed before a cell culture can be put on the printed
MEA and tested.

APPENDIX A
Background
A.1
Heart, a brief overview
The Heart is situated in the mediastinal space of the intrathoracic cavity, wrapped in the pericardium.
Its main function is to move the blood through the circulatory system, enabling nutrients and oxygen to
reach the peripheral parts of the body, as well as eliminating cells’ metabolic by-products. Three tissue
layers compose the heart’s walls: the epicardium is the most external; the myocardium is the middle
muscle layer; finally, the endocardium is the most inner layer. Structurally the heart can be divided
into four sections: left/right atrium, characterized by thin walls, and left/right ventricle, characterized
by a thick myocardium layer. Two septa separate the left and right sides of the heart, prohibiting the
blood to flow from one ventricle/atrium to the other.
Figure A.1: The Myocardium periodically contracts acting as the pump for the blood circulation.
The Heart contracts 60-100 times per minute (BPM, beats per minute); electrical activity associ-
ated with each contraction can be revealed by performing an electrocardiogram (ECG). The ECG is
correlated with the membrane potentials of the different parts of the heart (figure A.4). To better un-
derstand how the cardiac muscle contracts it is important to have in mind how the cells that compose it
-cardiomyocytes function. When calcium is present in the cells, two myofilaments slide past each other
thus contracting the cell. Myofilaments are made of long proteins -Sarcomeres- that repeat themselves
and create rod-like structures called Myofibrils. Cardiomyocytes are linked through the Intercalated
disks, here the Gap Juncture is found. This element is responsible for the rapid transmission of ions from
cell to cell, effectively passing the electrical signal across the Myocardium generating a synchronized
contraction.

A.1.1
Action Potential and Local Field Potential
Gap Junction Channels allow for ionic currents to be transmitted from cardiomyocyte to cardiomyocyte,
initiating cascade events that depolarise and repolarise the cardiomyocytes membrane potential. This
cascade is called Action Potential (AP) and is comprised of 5 stages.
Phase 0 rapid depolarization, caused by the opening
of fast sodium channels.
This causes the
membrane potential to go from -80/-90mV
to around +30/+40 mV, for Atrial and Ven-
tricular Cardiomyocytes. [49]
Phase 1 initial partial repolarisation, generated once
the sodium channels are closed and voltage-
gated potassium channels (hERG) open and
potassium leaves the cell.
Phase 2 plateau, can be seen once voltage-gated cal-
cium channels open generating an inward cur-
rent. Potassium keeps leaving the cell help-
ing the plateau stabilization.
Phase 3 rapid repolarisation, caused by the closure of
calcium channels and predominance of potas-
sium efflux.
Phase 4 stabilization and return of resting potential
thanks to the Na-K ATPase.

tial of a cardiomyocyte during AP.
Cardiac contraction is initiated by pacemaker cells that spontaneously depolarise, determining the
heart rate. Pacemaker cells can be influenced by the automatic nervous system via the beta-1 adren-
ergic receptors. Via the same receptors, the automatic nervous system can also regulate the strength

of the contractions by altering the concentration of calcium released by the sarcoplasmic reticulum
(SR).[50][51][52]
Figure A.4: A) AP of the different part of the heart, propagation direction can be deducted by the
peak distributions. B) Ventricular hiPSC-CM AP and FP with relative ionic currents.

As mentioned in the previous section, myofilaments slide past each other shortening the sarcomere
and thus contracting the cell, this happens thanks to the presence of calcium. The concentration of
calcium in the cell is directly proportional to the strength of the contraction. During phase 2 influx of
calcium, by itself insuﬀicient, triggers the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) which diffuses more calcium in
the cell, thus initiating muscular contraction. This process is called calcium -triggered-calcium-released
and the concentration of extruded calcium concentration can be regulated by the automatic nervous
system. Contraction is terminated with a process called Lusitropy, in which CA-APPase (SARCA)
pumps calcium back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Thanks to this process the myocardium can relax,
and the atriums can fill with blood.
The local field potential (LFPs) is the summation of signals from a large number of cells, and it reflects
their AP [18]. Figure A.4.B shows the relationship between the AP of a ventricular myocyte and the
LFPs. It has been demonstrated that AP parameters, such as duration and rise time can be derived
from the LFPs [53]. From these fields, it is not possible to extract the absolute value of the properties/
currents. FPs are extremely useful when looking at the health and growth of cell cultures and drug-
induced pathophysiological alteration such as modifications in propagation and excitation as longer
polarisation or depolarization time and thus can be utilized in drug toxicity screenings.
A.2
Cardiac Arrhythmia
When abnormalities occur in the heart ether in rate, rhythm, conduction, or conduction pathway it is
defined as arrhythmia. In Fig. (A.5) a classification based on heart rate (HR) alteration is shown, if HR
is greater than 100 bpm tachycardia is occurring, while if the HR is less than 60bpm then bradycardia
is occurring. It is important to notice that not all arrhythmic episodes are pathological, the body can
==PAGE:63==
A.2 Cardiac Arrhythmia
4
increase or decrease its’ HR to maintain homeostasis. Example of events when physiological arrhythmic
occurs is during intense exercise, the heart needs to increase its HR and construction power to transport
suﬀicient oxygen to muscular cells. During sleep, the body lowers all its metabolic rate and thus needs
less oxygen and nutrients, this means the heart can lower its’ activity and save energy.
Pathological arrhythmias can be potentially very dangerous. There are four main causes for them to
occur: increase/decrease of automaticity, triggered activity, re-entrance, and connection block.
Automaticity is the autoregulation of the AP -this can be also influenced by the sympathetic and
parasympathetic nervous systems.
Triggered activity is usually caused by electrolyte imbalance. It consist in the early repolarization and
subsequent AP event before phase 2- It can also cause depolarisation and AP firing before repolarisation
is complete.
Re-entry occurs when the excitation follows a non-intrinsic pathway.
Finally, a conduction block happens when the atrioventricular node does not transmit or delays the
transmission of the electrical signal to the ventricles.

whole cell, and sharp electrode. Extracellular recordings monitor more cells at a time and are non-
invasive, their throughput is LFPs[8]. They are performed by MEAs, whose design allows monitoring

 Glass ink 
The developed glass ink has been developed to print stackable constructs. Examples of this ink used in 
practice can be found in Freja Høier’s (2020) and Simin Zhao’s (2021) respective Master Thesis’. 
The ink consists of a high solid content of surface treated hollow glass spheres and a combination of low 
molecular weight polystyrene and low polystyrene content SEBS with a combination of volatile Butyl 
Acetate and less volatile Hexyl Acetate. 
 
Glass ink surface treatment 
All of the entire procedure must be performed in fumehood with protective glass and proper gloves. I 
have used double gloves, changing the outer pair frequently during the entire process. 
Fill glass flask (200-500mL capacity) with 40g hollow glass spheres and add 100mL toluene and magnetic 
stirrer. Close the glass flask with a glass stopper and let it stir for 15minutes. 
Take a new glass flask of same size and add funnel+filter paper. Pour the toluene+glass beads into the 
filter and filter off toluene as good as possible. The mixture will still be wet with toluene but this is okay. 
Once the majority of toluene is filtered off transfer the washed glass beads back into the glass flask. 
Make 100mL of a 2v/v% solution of Hexyltrichlorosilane in Toluene and add this to the washed glass 
beads in the glass flask. Close with glass stopper and let stir overnight. 
Next day: 
Take a new glass flask+funnel+filter paper and pour the mixture into the filter paper. Let toluene 
solution drip off. Once dripped off pour 100mL toluene into the funnel and stir. 
Do this three times, this washing the glass beads in toluene 3 times, letting the toluene run off before 
the next round is initiated.  
Pour the accumulated toluene waste into a C waste box. 
Wash an additional three times with 96% ethanol.  
When the last round of ethanol has run off empty the ethanol in a C waste box. 
Transfer the glass beads (By know they should be very easy to remove from the filter paper) into 
suitable glass containers and put on a hot plate. Let the beads dry completely and stir frequently. After 
approximately 3 hours you will have a fine powder of glass beads. 
To quickly test if the surface treatment has worked add a small amount of water to a mixing cup 
(equivalent of a large droplet) and add a small amount of your treated glass spheres. Compared this to a 
mixing cup with water and non-treated glass beads. You should observe that the treated glass beads 
cover the droplets, whereas the non-treated beads integrate themselves with the water. If this drastic 
change in behavior is observed you have successfully changed the hydrophobicity of your glass beads.

➢ Wait period. The Wait parameter is the delay between switching the electrodes from
