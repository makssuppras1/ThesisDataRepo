Abstract
This thesis is concerned with the asymmetric lamination of tempered and annealed glass.
The project investigates the possibilities and limitations in the asymmetric lamination of
tempered glass with annealed glass. The interaction between the tempered glass and
more standard thicknesses of annealed glass is investigated to see how the residual
stress in the tempered glass affects the adjacent plies in case of partial or complete failure.
Experimental tests on five specimens with varying thicknesses of the annealed glass ply
are conducted at the DTU lab. Furthermore, numerical analysis using the Equivalent Tem┬¡
perature difference model was conducted. Finally, an FE model was created to investigate
a much broader variation to compare the experimental data to theoretical calculations.
The goal of the research work was to suggest proper thicknesses for the second annealed
glass ply, which could withstand the expansion stresses without a partial or complete fail┬¡
ure. The main finding in the research showcased that larger thicknesses of the annealed
glass plies could withstand this expansion, and the data was comparable to the theoretical
models made using ABAQUS.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The increasing use of glass in buildings has led to the need for laminated glass. This
type of glass is formed as a sandwich of two or more plies of glass as seen Figure 1.1in
below. A laminated glass product is a solid surface with a soft interlayer. It can be used
for various applications such as stairs, floors, and roofs. The plastic interlayer keeps the
glass fragments in place when laminated glass breaks, preventing the risk of splinters
caused by the fragments.
Tempered glass is a type of safety glass processed by controlled thermal or chemical
treatments to increase strength compared to normal annealed glass. Tempering puts the
outer surface into compression and the interior into tension. This type of stress distribution
causes the glass to shatter into small fragments when subjected to an impact, leading to
failure.
When tempered glass breaks, it shatters into small pieces due to the residual stresses
gained under the tempering process. Using glass as a structural material requires anal┬¡
ysis and prediction of its behavior in the pre┬¡failure state, during failure, and post┬¡failure
state. The post┬¡failure behavior of tempered glass is often considered unfavorable due
to the small fragments, which lead to a state where almost no bending stiffness is left.
Another effect of failure concerning tempered glass is the rapid in┬¡plane expansion of the
tempered glass fragments during the fracturing process. Under this process, each frag┬¡
ment expands and eventually results in a significant expansion of the fractured glass plate.

1.2
Objective and Method
Heat┬¡treated glass is widely used in civil engineering, automobile, space, and marine
applications due to its beneficial strength properties. This project investigates the possi┬¡
bilities and limitations in the asymmetric lamination of tempered glass with annealed or
other tempered glass. There will be a particular focus on the interaction between thin
glass and more standard thicknesses of glass and how the residual stress state in the
tempered glass affects the adjacent plies in case of partial failure.
In the case of laminated tempered glass with annealed glass, If the tempered glass ply
fails and fragmentizes, the ply will expand rapidly. The expansion stresses created due
to the failure will also cause the second layer of glass to expand. This thesis project will
aim to find and suggest proper thicknesses for the second annealed glass ply that can
withstand the expansion stresses without partial or complete failure. The project focuses
on theoretical and numerical work using FEM analysis. Further on, experimental tests
on laminated glass plates are conducted at the DTU laboratory. The test species are
provided and funded by a manufacturing company in Belgium ┬½AGC Glass Europe┬╗.

This thesis project investigates the possibilities and limitations in the asymmetric lami┬¡

scope is to investigate the interaction between the tempered glass and more standard
thicknesses of annealed glass and how the residual stress in the tempered glass will af┬¡

2.1
Glass Properties and Manufacturing Process
The first section of this chapter introduces the basic properties of glass. Further on, the
manufacturing process and post┬¡processing of float and tempered glass are described.
2.1.1
Properties
Amorphous solids, such as glass, are a material that is brittle and hard. It is also known
as a non┬¡crystalline solid. Unlike other materials, glass does not have a melting point. In┬¡
stead, it transforms when exposed to certain temperatures═¥ this makes it a highly durable
material. There are many types of glass with varying properties depending on the ap┬¡
plication of the glass. The most common type is soda┬¡lime or silica glass, which mainly
contains sand, cement, and ash [1]. As glass is an isotropic and brittle material at room
temperature, It is capable of deforming under stress but does not show plastic deforma┬¡
tion before failure. The compressive strength of glass is much higher than the tensile
strength [2] [3]. The tensile strength is lower mainly due to the properties of glass, which
are sensitive to micro┬¡crack and flaw development [4].
The strength of glass is time┬¡dependent, i.e., the strength depends on the duration of time
the glass has been subjected to a load, and it decreases with time. Glass is sensitive to
stress consecrations, and surface flaws can cause high┬¡stress concentration. As glass
is a brittle material with no plastic behavior, there is no stress redistribution as in other
materials like steel [2]. As glass is a linear elastic material, it can commonly be used in
various structural applications at temperatures above 100ÔùªC. Some of the most common
properties at room temperature are presented in Table 2.1 [5].

2.1.2
Manufacturing of Float Glass
The float process is the most common method for producing glass. It involves the melting
of raw materials at temperatures of 1500 C, and this process provides the panels with a
smooth surface. The glass is thereby cooled to a temperature of 600 C═¥ this step allows
the glass to cool down to reduce the residual stresses. Figure 2.1 displays the steps in
the manufacturing process of float glass. Due to the various steps involved in making
and handling glass, flaws can appear at any point on the surface. These flaws cause
the glass to lose its tensile strength. However, these flaws can be improved with other
methods such as acid treatment [5].

2.1.3
Manufacturing of Tempered glass
The tempering process starts with cutting and drilling the float glass before quenching.
This is done early in the process to avoid breakage due to the high level of residual stress,
which is created due to the tempering process. After the pretreatment, the glass is cleaned
and heated to above the transition temperature, Tg. Once the glass is uniformly heated
up above the temperature Tg, it is moved on rollers to the quench. In the quench, the
glass is cooled down rapidly by air jets while the glass is rolling back and forth to reduce
the isolating effect from the rollers [5]. As the glass is quickly cooled down by air jets,
the outer surface of the glass is cooled down much more rapidly than the center. When
the center cools, it tries to pull back from the outer surface. This process keeps the
center in tension while the outer surface goes into compression and is how the tempered
glass gets its strength ┬¡ glass in tension is about five times more breakable than glass in
compression [6]. The final product is often slightly curved due to the asymmetric cooling.
The rollers can produce roller waves in the glass, which can be seen as regular distortion
images reflected on the glass surface [5]. A Sketch of the process line for tempering float
glass is shown in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Sketch of the process line for tempering float glass. Extracted from [5].
In modern tempering facilities, the tempered glass is heat┬¡soaked. This is performed
because a certain amount of the tempered glass breaks spontaneously after tempering.
This breakage is due to the phase change in small nickel┬¡sulfide (NiS) particles. The NiS
particles expand during the phase transformation and cause breakage of the tempered
glass. In the heat┬¡soaking process, the tempered glass is heated to approximately 250ÔùªC,
which speeds up the phase changes of NiS, and thereby causes failure before the glass
unit is installed. This process reduces the problem, but not entirely, and therefore should
be considered under the design process [5].
2.2
Laminated Glass
Laminated glass is produced by combining two or more layers of float or tempered glass
with interlayers. The process continues with heating, pressing, and bonding the glass
plies together to create a flat or curved compound glass product. The most common
lamination process is autoclaving at approximately 140ÔùªC. The heat and the pressure of
14 bar ensure no air inclusions between the glass and the interlayer. Laminated glass has
good transparency, and its impact resistance is much higher than that of ordinary sheet
glass [7].

2.3
Structural Behaviour of Glass
Glass behaves considerably differently from other structural materials such as steel or
aluminum. Glass does not yield and therefore is a brittle material, and the fracture is
difficult to predict. The compressive strength of a 10 mm glass cube is approximately
1000 N/mm2. However, glass under compression also experiences tension, which in
theory is equal to excessive compression stress multiplied by the PoissonÔÇÖs ratio. The
tensile stress of tempered glass is much higher due to the residual compressive stress at
the surface, which is created due to the tempering process [9].
The modulus of elasticity of glass is about 70 GPa, which is around a third of steel. Glass
behaves linear elastic until a brittle failure occurs. As glass is a brittle material unable to
resist crack propagation, it does not physically strain and cannot dissipate imposed stress,
such as temperature shock. To avoid stress concentration points, direct contact between
glass, or other rigid structural materials should be avoided when designing. Further on,
glass is a non┬¡combustible material and does not contribute to the buildingÔÇÖs fire load.

However, glass can transfer heat, and under high temperatures, it can crack and break
down [10].
When structural engineers design steel structures, they typically concentrate on limiting
the stresses at places of maximum bending and shear. This is because steel can yield
when it is subjected to localized areas of concentrated stress, and localized yielding is
rarely considered during the design of steel elements. On the contrary, designers cannot
ignore stress concentrations and lack of fit when designing structural glass elements.
Therefore, connection design is a core component when designing structures made from

Structural Behaviour of Laminated Glass
The structural behavior of laminated glass depends on the glass type and the properties of

by both of the laminates acting compositely. Long┬¡term out┬¡of┬¡plane loads are considered
to be non┬¡compositely, with the loads being shared by each laminate in proportion to their
relative stiffnesses [11].

2.4
Post┬¡failure Behaviour
When glass breaks, the broken glass may still stay intact to its fixings. This depends on
the number of cracks/fragments, the pane size, fixing method, slope, and the cause of
the breakage. When designing glass, it is essential to consider the risk factor of falling
glass═¥ the glass pane should fall with an acceptable risk or remain in place until it can be
repaired. Monolithic glass tends to lack the redundancy of laminates flaws and is more
likely to fall from its support structure when it has failed [11].
2.4.1
Post┬¡failure behavior of laminated glass
Laminated glass tends to remain intact after a failure. This is due to the viscoelastic
interlayer between each ply of glass. The interlayer goes into tension and thereby keeps
the glass in place. Therefore, laminated glass is favored for glass panes that have a risk
of falling from heights. However, with laminated toughened glass with PVB interlayers,
the interlayer can tear up due to tearing from the fragments. This only occurs if all glass
plies in the laminated glass have failed, which results in a large amount of tension being
taken by the PVB [11]
Further on, the residual out┬¡of┬¡plane stiffness of the panel is significantly reduced when all
panes of the laminated toughened glass fail. The panel may fall, as excessive deflection
cause it to pull from a rebate or detach from its fixing. There are, however, two methods
to overcome this issue. One way is to make one of the glass plies of basic annealed or
heat strengthened glass, which will allow one of the glass plies to fail while the other ply
remains intact. This does in fact, mean that one of the plies is assumed to be ineffective in
the post┬¡failure condition and is therefore ignored. The second option is to use ionplast┬¡
based interlayers, which have a greater tensile capacity than PVB interlayers and are
much less likely to tear if all the plies in the laminated glass pane have failed [11].

Residual Stress
Residual stress is stress which remains in a stationary body, which is in equilibrium with
its surroundings. Residual stress can be a determinant factor in the performance and the
lifetime of a material or component. Residual stresses are more challenging to predict
than the in┬¡service stresses on which they superimpose. It is, therefore, essential to have
reliable methods for the measurement of these stresses and to understand the level of
information they can provide [13].
With modern computational techniques, it is often possible to predict the stresses an ele┬¡
ment is subjected to in serviceability. This is, however, not sufficient for a reliable predic┬¡
tion of the componentÔÇÖs performance. In many cases where failure has occurred, it has
been due to the presence of residual stress combined with the service stresses and short┬¡
ened the componentÔÇÖs lifespan. In other cases, compressive stresses have deliberately
been introduced to a component, e.g., in shot penning, which improves fatigue resistance.
Furthermore, in natural or artificial multi┬¡phase materials, residual stress is created from
differences in thermal expansivity, yield stress, or stiffness [13].
3.1
Residual Stress in Glass
One of the techniques to increase the strength of glass is by thermally toughening glass.
Detailed principles of this process are explained in subsection 2.1.3, this process can
increase the apparent strength of annealed glass more than five times. Generally, the
float glass is heated to a temperature near the glass transition temperature and then
quenched. The surface of the glass will contract and stiffen while the core will remain hot
and soft, unable to carry any stresses [14]. The transition line progress towards the center
plane of the glass, and stress build up in the outer layer of toughened glass is illustrated
in Figure 3.2
Figure 3.1: Overview of the toughening process. Extracted from [5].
Further on, the static loading performance of brittle material can be improved by using
residual stress. Rapid cooling of glass from high temperature generates surface stresses,
which are counterbalanced by tensile stresses in the interior [13]. A straightforward hy┬¡
pothesis for the apparent strength consists of the contribution from the residual stress and
the pure glass strength. However, it should be noticed that the pure glass strength is the
strength of annealed glass, and not float glass, without any residual stress [14].

3.2
Measuring Residual Stress
There are quite a few methods to measure residual stresses. The commonly used meth┬¡
ods are grouped as═¥ non┬¡destructive, semi┬¡destructive, and destructive or diffraction┬¡
based. Mostly all of the residual stress measurement methods are indirect, meaning the
residual stress is calculated or derived from a measured quantity such as elastic strain or
displacement [15].
Before selecting one method over another, it is essential to consider the sampling volume
characteristic of the technique and the types of stresses which may be of importance. In
many cases, much can be learned from the complementary use of more than one tech┬¡
nique [13]. One of the more common ways of measuring residual stress is the photoelastic
method, scattered light polariscope (SCALP). This method will be discussed more in detail
in subsection 3.2.1.

tribution, residual and loading stress in annealed, heat┬¡strengthened, and fully tempered
glass [16].
The instrument emits a polarized laser beam passed through the glass panels. A glass

4.2.2
Wheatstone DAQ┬¡System
An English physicist, Sir Charles Wheatstone, found a bridge circuit for measuring elec┬¡
trical resistance. In this bridge circuit, known today as the Wheatstone bridge circuit, the
unknown resistance is compared to well┬¡defined resistance. The Wheatstone bridge is
also well suited for measuring small changes of resistance and is, therefore, also suit┬¡
able for measuring resistance changes in strain gauges. Strain gauges transform applied
strain into a proportional change of resistance [17].
The application fields of strain gauges are for experimental stress analysis and the design
and manufacture of transducers. Depending on the measurement problem on hand, one
or more strain gauges are used at the actual or different location of the measuring points.
Such arrangements are often designated as ┬½full bridge┬╗, ┬½half bridge┬╗, ┬½quarter bridge┬╗,
although this is not actually correct. The circuits used for the measurement are always
complete and are either fully or partially formed by the strain gauges on the specimen and
are then completed by fixed resistors, which are incorporated in the instrument [17]. In
this test setup a ┬½quarter bridge┬╗ configuration was used as seen in Figure 4.5.

Numerical Model
Numerous methods have been derived to explain the behavior of glass and the different
stress states of annealed, heat┬¡strengthened, tempered, and laminated ┬¡glass. The use
of glass as a structural material relies on the accuracy of the analysis and prediction of
its behavior in the intact, during the fracture process, after the fracture process, and in
the post┬¡fracture limit state. In this present chapter, the simplified methods of deriving the
increase in the size of a glass pane made of thermally prestressed glass due to fracturing
by Nielsen et al. in [18] are utilized to estimate the extra load on intact plies in fractured
laminated glass panes.
In the post┬¡fracture limit state of laminated glass, a rapid in┬¡plane expansion of the tem┬¡
pered glass fragments occurs during the fracture process. This process releases a sig┬¡
nificant amount of strain energy. During this process, each fragment expands and results
in a global expansion of the fractured glass plate. To account for this effect, a simplified
model for computing the increase in the size of a glass pane made of thermally pre┬¡
stressed soda┬¡lime┬¡silica glass is developed within the paper ┬½The In┬¡plane Expansion of
Fractured Thermally Pre┬¡stressed Glass Panes┬╗ [18].

narios were investigated (i) Where the thickness of the float glass is kept constant, hf =

Finite Element Analysis
6.1
Finite Element Method
Many studies have been conducted for the impact analysis of laminated glass units. In
this chapter, a finite element method (FEM) based software, ABAQUS, is employed to
study how the residual stress in the tempered glass ply affects the annealed glass ply
when the tempered glass undergoes failure. Many of the physical problems in nature
may be represented by partial differential equations. Some of these equations may be
very complicated to solve using mathematical methods. FEM is a handy tool used to solve
these problems. The general concept of the FEM is to approximate the PDEs by trans┬¡
ferring the equations into discrete parts, and then by using numerical model equations,
approximations of the real solution of the PDEs are found.
6.2
Finite Element Model
In this FE model, a thermo┬¡mechanical approach is used to simulate the expansion cre┬¡
ated due to the fragmentation of the tempered glass ply. Principle approaches from the
ETD method described in [18], allow the use of both analytical and commercial software
to compute an estimate of the effects of the stress state in adjacent structural elements.
In this model, a predefined field on the tempered glass ply is created to apply temperature
to the top ply. The analysis is conducted as a sensitivity analysis, where the influence of
parameters, such as═¥ the thickness of the tempered glass ply, ht, the thickness of the
annealed glass ply, hf, and the stiffness of the tempered glass, Et, is investigated.

6.2.4
Element Type and Mesh
Generally, every finite element software is the discretization of a model in a number of
elements. A mesh is usually a network of lines connected with a certain number of nodes
used to solve a problem under external loading conditions. This gives an approximate
solution for internal forces, e.g., axial forces, bending, or shear forces. The accuracy of
the solution is highly linked to the number of nodes involved and the number of degrees
of freedom of each node.
Figure 6.4: Illustration of the applied mesh for the 2D glass plate model.
The laminated glass plate model is meshed using quadrilateral elements, namely CPE4, A
4┬¡node bilinear plane strain model with full integration. This can both be an advantage and
a disadvantage compared to a 4┬¡node bilinear plane strain model with reduced integration,
CPE4R, as a CPE4 element will increase the CPU power and duration of the analysis but
will calculate more accurate results in terms of stress and strains with a higher number
of integration points. A sensitivity analysis with both element types was conducted, and
the results were similar. Since the computational time for the CPE4 was not an issue, the
CPE4 element type was chosen.

7
Discussion
This thesis investigates the possibilities and limitations of asymmetric lamination of tem┬¡
pered glass with annealed glass. The projectÔÇÖs scope was to look at the interaction be┬¡
tween the tempered and more standard thicknesses of annealed glass and analyze how
the residual stress in the tempered glass would affect the adjacent plies in case of com┬¡
plete failure.
From the experimental tests presented in chapter 4, it was seen found that the highest
stress occurred in the smallest ply thicknesses. A tempered glass ply with residual surface
stress of ┬¡90 MPa, transferred a recorded 31.50 MPa tensile stress to the annealed ply
when it had failed. Further on, this value was decreased as the ply thickness increased for
the ply thicknesses of 1 mm, 2 mm, 3 mm, and 10 mm. For the annealed ply thickness of
15 mm, it was seen that that failure stress, ¤âf, had an increase. This was an unusual out┬¡
come, as the stress was initially assumed to decrease as the ply thickness was increased.
However, considering the bending of the plate, one can assume that the increase occurs
due to the center of rotation changing as the ply thickness increases, therefore increasing
the compressive stress.
Further on, analyzing the stress after the tempered ply has completely failed, it is seen
that the maximum stress occurs during the first oscillation after the failure. These results,
however, do not show a decrease in stress proportional to an increase in the ply thickness.
However, these results are oscillatory, and it seems like a much more dynamic problem.
This makes sense as the fracture velocity of tempered glass is as high as 1500 m/s.
Finally, the stress eventually stabilizes, and a much lower stress state is recorded at an
equilibrium state. It is interesting to see that only specimen 1A, with an annealed glass
ply thickness of 1 mm, is in tension, with ¤âeq = 10.15 MPa, and has the largest stress gap
at equilibrium state than specimen 2A and 3A.
Comparing the results to the peak stress from Figure 5.5, some significant differences
are seen. The peak stress values from the numerical model are almost twice as large
compared to the experimental values. However, the numerical model does not consider
the stiffness of the interlayer and the bending of the plies due to asymmetric failure.
Comparing the experimental results to the FE analysis presented in Figure 6.9a, it is seen
that for annealed ply thicknesses of 1 mm, 2mm, and 3 mm, the results are twice as large
as the FE analysis results, which is the same case as for the numerical model. Further on,
the experimental tests for specimens 10A and 15A are very similar when the estimated
stress values of the first oscillation after the complete failure of the tempered glass are
analyzed, ¤âmax.est. This is interesting, as these are the annealed plies that stayed intact
after the failure of the tempered glass ply.
There are some insufficiency and flaws in the presented models═¥ first of all, the numer┬¡
ical model does not consider the stiffness of the interlayer and bending of the plies due
to asymmetric failure. However, the fracture velocity of tempered flaws is as high as
1500 m/s, indicating a high loading rate of the interlayer and, therefore, a dynamic prob┬¡
lem. Further on, interlayers such as PVB show an increase in stiffness with the loading
rate, which can support the assumption of complete shear transfer between the plies [14].
There are similar flaws with the FE model, as the interlayer is modeled as an elastic ma┬¡
terial, whereas in reality, it has a viscous┬¡elastic behavior. In the case of the FE model, it
is therefore assumed complete shear transfer.

Finally, it should be mentioned that the crack propagation of the failed annealed plies
propagated from where the clamp was located on the plate. It is, therefore, reasonable
to assume that the crack might have propagated due to vibrations or bending around that
area. Therefore, different results may be achieved if the laminated glass ply is held at
different angles or from a different position. Further on, in this project, one specimen
from each series was tested due to the time limitation, this gave little information to create
sample statistics, and inconsistencies in the experimental results could not be detected.

8
Conclusion and Future Work
This thesis project aimed to find and suggest proper thicknesses for the second annealed
glass ply, which could withstand the expansion stresses without partial or complete failure.
The project focused on numerical work and FE analysis. Further on, experimental tests on
laminated glass plates were conducted at the DTU laboratory. The thesis investigated the
possibilities and limitations in the asymmetric lamination of tempered glass with annealed
glass.
Five laminated glass specimens with a constant thickness of the tempered glass ply of
15 mm were tested at the DTU lab═¥ the laminated glass had varying thicknesses of the
annealed glass ply of 1 mm, 2 mm, 3 mm, 10 mm, and 15 mm. The residual stress in
the tempered glass ply was measured on all five specimens, and an average value of ┬¡90
MPa on all specimens was calculated.
The experimental data found that a ply thickness of 10 mm and 15 mm was sufficient to
withstand the expansion stresses caused by the failure of the tempered glass ply. Fur┬¡
ther on, the FE analysis also showcased that annealed ply thicknesses of 7 mm and
larger could, in theory, withstand the expansion stresses. Comparing the data from the
specimens that could withstand the expansion stresses in the experimental tests to the
FE analysis, it was shown that the results were similar and that the FE analysis was a
reasonable estimate for the specimens where the annealed glass ply stayed intact.
Future work should gather more sample data from the experimental tests as only one
specimen from each series was tested in this research. Additional changes should be
made to the experimental tests to investigate the time of failure of the annealed glass
and the behavior by using Digital Image Correlation (DIC) programs or something similar.
Further on, the effects of the viscoelastic interlayer should also be considered in the FE
analysis, and new, more accurate estimates should be made which can be compared to
the experimental data.
