Abstract
This thesis explores the superdirective properties of an electric Hertzian dipole
and a co-located orthogonal magnetic Hertzian dipole simultaneously illuminating
a multilayered spherical particle. By exciting higher-order modes in the particle it
is possible to surpass the proposed directivity bounds. Vector spherical wave func-
tions are used to analytically describe the scattering problem, and relevant far-field
quantities are derived. Without loss of generality, the numerical implementation as-
sumes a +z-directed electric Hertzian dipole and a −y-directed magnetic Hertzian
dipole, both placed along the −x-axis. A genetic algorithm is used to determine
the source position; source excitation ratio; and the permittivity and radius of each
particle layer in order to maximize the directivity. Four specific source-particle con-
figurations all achieving superdirectivity are considered. Notably, a 5-layered and
10-layered particle both achieve a maximum directivity of 15 dB—surpassing the
theoretical directivity bounds by a factor of two or more. In both cases, the source
excitation is balanced, forming a Huygens source. A superdirective 3-layered parti-
cle is also shown. Whereas many particles need an impractical degree of precision
for the optimization parameters — upwards of 15 decimal points — in order to cor-
rectly recreate the results, the 3-layered particle sufficed with only four significant
digits. Lastly, excluding either the EHD or MHD led to a 2–3 dB drop in directivity
and a significantly worse front-to-back ratio.
Resumé
Denne afhandling undersøger de superdirektive egenskaber af en elektrisk Hertz-
dipol og en ortogonal magnetisk Hertzdipol, der simultant belyser en flerlags sfærisk
partikel. Ved at anslå højere-ordens tilstande i partiklen, er det muligt at overgå de

tet til den analytiske beskrivelse af spredningsproblemet, og relevante fjernfeltsstør-

en +z-rettet elektrisk Hertzdipol og en −y-rettet magnetisk Hertzdipol, der begge

Introduction
The interaction between electromagnetic fields and matter has been a topic of interest
for a very long time. Early developments can be seen in Lorenz’ [1] and Mie’s [2] sem-
inal works on scattering from a sphere illuminated by plane waves. In modern times,
a greater understanding on light-matter interaction has been achieved and more com-
plex techniques for describing the scattering mechanisms have been developed. These
developments have led to advancements in antenna theory, particularly for techniques to
obtain superdirectivity. The concept of superdirectivity was first discussed by Oseen [3],
in which higher-order multipoles at the same frequency were combined to obtain needle
radiation. Recently, Ziolkowski [4] has demonstrated this explicitly through a solution of
Maxwell’s equations, reinstating that the combination of higher-order multipoles leads to
superdirectivity.
Although superdirectivity has been discussed theoretically in a range of papers, e.g, [5]–
[7], superdirective antennas were not demonstrated in practice until recently. The su-
perdirective systems were deemed unfeasible due to major problems such as low radiation
resistance; low efficiencies; narrow bandwidths; and sensitivity to design parameters [7],
[8]. In recent years, practical superdirective systems have emerged in the literature in-
cluding [9]–[11]. With the advancements of superdirectivity and superdirective antennas,
considerations as to what bound is appropriate have been a topic of discussion. Some of
the most pertinent are Harrington’s [12] based on the maximum order of the spherical
wave functions, Kildal and Best’s [13] heuristic bound, and most recently Yaghjian’s [14]
bound.
With the continued advancement of wireless communication and low-power systems,
highly directivity antennas will continue to be of great critical importance for current and
future wireless applications and technologies. Superdirective antennas are particularly
beneficial in battery-powered biometric sensors; internet-of-things applications (IoT) [15];
and energy harvesting [16], [17].
The possibility for superdirectivity has previously been investigated for spherical par-
ticles, excited by either plane waves [18], [19] or an electric dipole [20], [21]. In these
works, the particles consist of multiple layers with varying material parameters. In this
way, it is possible to tailor the overlap in frequency of higher-order modes to enhance the
scattering properties. While resonance conditions can be used to determine material pa-
rameters [22], this is seemingly impractical for more complex particles with many layers.
Evolutionary algorithms have proved a worthy candidate in a wide variety of electromag-
netic and antenna problems. Examples of this can be seen in the design of a nonuniform
Luneburg lens [23], and of a corrugated horn antenna [24].
Given these challenges and growing interest in superdirective antennas, this thesis will
present a novel approach to enhancing superdirectivity. Specifically, the EHD source will
be accompanied by a magnetic Hertzian dipole (MHD), and the pair will illuminate a

The consequences of including a MHD are investigated by varying the excitation
ratio between the two sources. The theoretical developments using vector spherical wave
functions (VSWFs) will hold for the EHD and MHD arbitrarily placed and oriented in
relation to one another, however, they must be outside the particle. The main purpose

structure. To facilitate this, the material parameters, source position, and the excitation
ratio are optimized using a genetic algorithm (GA) to maximize the directivity and front-
to-back ratio (FTBR). The GA often yields optimization parameters with a precision up
to 16 decimal places, which is impractical for real-world fabrication. Thus, an additional
point of focus in this work is to identify solutions that remain effective when rounded off
significantly.
The thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 reviews existing theoretical and ex-
perimental work on superdirectivity and directivity bounds.
Chapter 3 presents the
theoretical framework of the scattering problem of a multilayered spherical particle ex-
cited by a combined EHD-MHD source using vector spherical wave functions. Chapter 4
discusses the numerical implementation of the problem using Python. Key assumptions
on the EHD-MHD source is made, and the GA is explained. Chapter 5 displays the
most relevant results, analyzing the impact of the source configuration and material pa-
rameters on directivity and FTBR. Finally, Chapter 6 recapitulates the crucial findings
and reflects upon potential future research directions. The appendices present additional
relevant information. A conference paper as a result of the present work can be read
in Appendix C.

Literature Survey
The literature survey serves as a review of the theoretical advancements of the upper
directivity bounds and the superdirectivity term is introduced. Academic contributions
to the excitation of higher-order modes (HOMs) in spherical particles in order to achieve
tailored scattering are explored. The highlighted structures involve exciting the HOMs
using a plane-wave and a electric dipole outside or inside the particle.
2.1
Superdirectivity and Directivity Bounds
A few attempts at defining directivity bounds have been made.
One of the earliest
attempts comes from Harrington [12]. In this paper, the bound is given in terms of the
highest order of spherical wave functions N as
DH−N = N 2 + 2N.
(2.1)
In the same article, Harrington shows that modes of order n ≤N = krmin — rmin being
the radius of the smallest sphere containing the antenna system — are not contributing
to the far-field radiation. With this addition, he then argues that a more appropriate
bound might be the ’normal’ directivity1, given by
DH−kr = (krmin)2 + 2(krmin).
(2.2)
In fact, this directivity bound is the usual benchmark for determining whether or not
superdirectivity has been achieved. A shortcoming of this method is, that for the limiting
case of an electric or magnetic Hertzian dipole, i.e., as krmin →0, Eq. (2.2) goes to 0
as well. This is not the case for these sources, which was also recognized by Kildal and
Best in [13]. Based on the directivity of a short dipole, be it a magnetic or electric one,
of Dshort dipole = 1.5 [25], they come up with the heuristic formula
DKB = (krmin)2 + 3.
(2.3)
This equation takes its form based on the directivity of a Huygens source, which is
DHS = 3, since this is the largest achievable directivity for a small antenna fed through
a single-port. This bound is once again mentioned in [26]. As illustrated by Fig. 2.1, the
bound was compared to some known antenna types. This shows great agreement between
the bound and the practical realizations of the antennas which thereby justifies the use
of Eq. (2.3) as a bound for maximum directivity. The heuristic bound is a combination

2.2.1
Plane Wave Excitation
Combinations of higher-order multipoles has been investigated for the case of a sphere
illuminated by a plane wave in a number of different studies. The difference in these con-
figurations is thus often how the spherical geometry is constructed in order to tailor the
directivity/scattered field to the needs of the specific study. It has been demonstrated,
that introducing a void in a single-material particle can change the frequency at which
certain higher-order modes get excited, and thereby it is possible to tailor the overlap of
higher-order modes [18], [29]. This is illustrated in Fig. 2.2. The introduction of a void
in Fig. 2.2b compared to Fig. 2.2a makes it clear, that the electric dipole mode has moved
up in frequency and now overlaps with the magnetic quadrupole mode. Consequently,
as shown in Fig. 2.2c, the backscattering coefficient grows by approximately a factor of
2 when the correct overlap happens. Another particle configuration often studied is the
so-called core-shell configuration which are two layered particles with different material
parameters. It has been possible to demonstrate, both at microwave frequencies [30] and
at optical frequencies [19], that higher-order modes can be combined to achieve enhanced
radiation characteristics.
Particularly in [19], it is shown that combining an electric
quadrupole with a magnetic quadrupole leads to a more directional main lobe, i.e., su-
perdirectivity is achieved. Additionally, sidelobe suppression by using mixed multipoles is
demonstrated. This may be seen in Fig. 2.3, where two different configurations and three
different combinations of multipoles are shown. For the first configuration of Fig. 2.3a,
an Ag core coated with a dielectric shell is considered. The modes are again tailored by
varying the silver core radius, first to excite a magnetic and electric dipole mode for a
Huygens source, and next to excite a magnetic and electric quadrupole mode for more
directive properties. The last figure shows a silver shell with a dielectric core. Here, the
strongest response comes from the hexapole modes, however, modes of lower order are
also excited which suppress the sidelobes.
2.2.2
Dipole Excitation
Controlling and tailoring the scattered electromagnetic field by manipulating higher-order
modes has also been studied for the case of a sphere illuminated by an ideal or practical
electric dipole. In [22], an arbitrarily placed and oriented EHD excites a two-layered
metamaterial sphere. As shown in Fig. 2.4, it is possible to greatly enhance the so-called
power ratio when combining magnetic and electric modes. The great enhancement is
achieved when using double positive (DPS) and double-negative (DNG) core-shell con-
figuration as an alternative to a DPS-DPS configurations. DNG materials refer to having
both negative permittivity and permeability, while DPS is positive for both. Addition-
ally, the power ratio is the ratio of the total radiated power to the power radiated by the
EHD alone when situated in free-space. Similarly to the case of plane wave excitation,
combining higher-order modes yield a larger scattering response as indicated by Fig. 2.4b,
which illustrates a quadrupolar structure. Here, the power ratio is obviously larger than
that of the dipolar structure of Fig. 2.4a. Many of the particles listed so far for both
excitation schemes have had a combination of single negative (SNG) or DNG materials
and DPS materials in order to achieve the desired scattering characteristics. It is inter-
esting, especially for optical purposes, to also use all-dielectric particles, since the losses
associated with metals are large in the optical regime. The possibility for superdirectivity

2.3
Summary
Existing theoretical and practical work on superdirectivity was presented. Initially, an
overview of some commonly used upper directivity bounds is given. Common to all is the
proportionality between directivity and the electrical radius of the minimum enclosing
sphere.
A brief survey of spherical particles was conducted. Particularly, the excitation of
HOMs by illuminating the particle with a plane wave or by using an electric dipole
source was shown. The superdirective properties of both homogeneous and multilayered
particles were presented, and the role of exciting specific HOMs was discussed.

3.3
Duality
Considering Maxwell’s equations in the form presented in Eq. (3.8), the pairwise similarity
of the equations are easily observed. In fact, as has already been done in previous sections,
splitting Maxwell’s equations into a set of equations with only electric sources, and a set
with only magnetic sources give rise to sets of dual equations. The meaning of having dual
equations is, that if the solution to one of the sets of equations is known, a systematic
change of variables will yield the solution of the other set of equations. Take for instance
the curl equations Eqs. (3.8a) and (3.8b), and let one pair have only electric sources,
signified with a subscript A as before,
EA = −jωµHA,
(3.23a)
HA = jωεEA + Je.
(3.23b)
Now, consider the same pair of equations with only magnetic sources:
EF = −jωµHF −Jm,
(3.24a)
HF = jωεEF.
(3.24b)
From these two sets of equations, it is obvious that when going from having only electric
sources to having only magnetic sources, one can substitute EA →HF →−EA, HA →
−EF →HA, Je →Jm →−Je, ε →µ →ε. This is the concept of duality, a very powerful
concept which instantly reveals the solution to the dual problem of having a magnetic
source instead of an electric, or vice versa [35], [39].

Summary
The theoretical framework of the scattering problem of an EHD-MHD source illuminating
a multilayered spherical particle is elucidated in this chapter.
Initially, the problem
geometry is shown, and the relation between the EHD and MHD current densities is
established. To this end, the excitation ratio is introduced to describe how balanced
the sources are in relation to each other. The introductory discussion concludes with
fundamental electromagnetic theory, and particularly the duality theorem, which is of
great importance to the present work.
The VSWE was briefly presented, and the expressions for the combined EHD-MHD
source was derived. By exploiting the Debye potentials and applying the electromagnetic
boundary conditions, the unknown expansion coefficients could be found through a simple
matrix equation. Ultimately, the total radiated power of the total field was derived which
directly led to the VSWF expression for the directivity.

Numerical Implementation
As indicated in Chapter 3, a great number of computations need to take place in order
to obtain the relevant results. The target is to obtain superdirectivity by finding the
optimal material parameters and radii of the individual layers that allow for this. The
computations are done using Python with relevant packages. The most important pack-
ages are: NumPy for fast computations of arrays [44]; SciPy for matrix inversion, special
functions, and interpolation [45]; Pymoo for the multi-objective optimization algorithm
NSGA-II [46]; and Matplotlib for plotting [47]. The main part of the code revolved around
the layered particle, which was constructed as a class taking in a range of different ar-
guments. When the class is instantiated, all the relevant computations will take place.
This includes the field computations, directivity, radiated power, front-to-back ratio and
side-lobe level. An additional thing to remember for the numerical implementation is the
fact that the summation in the VSWE cannot be infinite. Instead, it will be truncated to
some upper limit n = M, called the truncation number. The developed code can handle
field computations for all points, however, it has been decided for the report to restrict
it to the principal planes, i.e., the xy-plane, xz-plane, and yz-plane.
The fields, TRP, and directivity related to the specific configuration using the classical
approach can be found in Appendix B.

Genetic algorithms work based on principles adopted from biological evolution, where
the strongest and most fit population survives to the next generation where it evolves
slightly, to be an even better fit in the given environment [55]. With this common analogy,
the GA is most easily described. It starts out by initializing the first population randomly
based on the bounds of each optimization variable. A very basic outline of a GA can
be seen in Fig. 4.6. The initial population is a Npop × Nvar matrix, where Npop is the
==PAGE:46==
38
Chapter 4. Numerical Implementation
size of the population, and Nvar is the number of optimization variables. Each row of
the matrix corresponds to a single individual of the population. After the initialization,
the objective function is computed for each individual in the population. Next, the cost
of each objective function is sorted, and only the most fit individuals make it to the
mating pool, where the fittest individuals generate offspring for the next generation, also
known as crossover. The offspring generation is based on adding together two individuals
which each have been multiplied by some weight. Lastly, before a new evaluation of
objective functions is done on the new generation, mutation occurs, which introduces
some randomness to the newly created population. The reason for this is to avoid going
down a local minimum/maximum, i.e., to avoid the optimizer to converge before the
optimal solution is found.
4.3.2
The NSGA-II Algorithm
The algorithm used in this thesis is, as previously noted, NSGA-II [53]. This is a mulit-
objective GA which uses non-dominated sorting. Non-dominated sorting means that no
other individual in the population outperforms a specific individual, i.e., it is not possible
to increase the cost of one objective function without decreasing the cost of the other.
These specific individuals are the Pareto optimal solutions.
Based on this, a Pareto
front is constructed which gives all the Pareto optimal solutions for a given problem. An
example of a Pareto front can be seen in Fig. 4.7. The termination criterion was always a
maximum number of generations, Ngen, although other termination/convergence criteria
can be used. The NSGA-II, and in general GAs, allow for many options to be changed
including crossover and mutation probability, however, the default values used in the
implementation from [46] have been used in the present work. Thus, the population size
and number of generations are the only parameters that have been changed to obtain
better results. The reason being that the thesis is not a study on optimization but rather
on directive properties of N-layered particles illuminated by localized sources.
4.3.3
High-Performance Cluster and Parallelization
Initially, the optimization was done sequentially, i.e., each individual of a population was
calculated sequentially on a personal computer. This led to extremely large optimization
times for a relatively small number of computations. Therefore, the possibility of using
the high-performance cluster (HPC) available through DTU was explored. Furthermore,
the computations could be done in parallel which in itself reduces the time it takes to
perform all the calculations. These two things greatly enhanced the performance of the
optimization algorithm and allowed for larger population sizes and number of generations
to be explored. Thus, the results obtained were also consistently better in the sense
that a larger parameter space could be explored. Still, the optimizations took a long
time to complete especially with an increasing amount of layers. Had it been known at
an earlier stage in the thesis, the possibility for using graphics processing unit (GPU)
acceleration would have been investigated, as this is commonly known to speed up parallel
computations [56].

The numerical implementation of the VSWF approach to the scattering problem of a co-

Without loss of generality, it was decided to use a +z-directed EHD and an orthog-

However, the VSWF deviated greatly in the near-field when approaching the source due
to the finite truncation of the VSWE. Increasing the number of terms led to a better
correspondence between the two approaches. The VSWF approach was also compared to
commercial FEM software. Here, a 3-layered particle was illuminated by the EHD-MHD
source. Again, excellent correspondence was achieved. Lastly, the optimization problem
was introduced. The goal is to maximize the directivity and FTBR by optimizing the
parameters of the particles and sources. The NSGA-II algorithm was presented, and the
concept of the Pareto front briefly explained.

5.2
Population Size and Termination Criterion
Intuitively, having a larger initial population and more generations should yield better re-
sults due to the larger number of possible solutions being sought out. Generally speaking,
this is also the case. However, a number of times during the optimization, worse results
are achieved when increasing Ngen and Npop. The reason for this is that a very large num-
ber of local minima/maxima is present for the given problem. Thereby, if the optimizer
by chance is not close to the global maximum, it will be stuck in a local maximum and
converge to this value instead, even for large populations. This is especially a problem
as the number of layers grow in which case the number of optimization parameters also
grow, which leads to more possible values to check. In Fig. 5.2, two sets of Pareto fronts
can be seen. Both cases have an outer shell radius of rmax = 12 mm with εr ∈[1, 50];
rs ∈[rmax, 1.5rmax]; W ∈[0, 5]. Fig. 5.2a shows the case where Npop = 1200, Ngen = 700
while Fig. 5.2b shows the case where Npop = 2000, Ngen = 1200. Thus, in the second
case there should be a relatively large chance that the solution more easily converges to
a larger directivity due to the increase in population size alone. From Fig. 5.3, this is
also seen to be the case for N = 3, 8, however, when N = 5, the maximum achieved
directivity drops with more than 1 dB.
Another observation from Fig. 5.2 is that many solutions exceeding the superdirectiv-
ity requirement can be found. Evidently, this does not mean a global maximum is found,
since for the N = 5 the maximum directivity decreases when the population size and
number of generations increase. The initial population is, as mentioned earlier, randomly
generated within the specified bounds, and thereby it can get stuck in a local maximum
(of which there seem to be several).

5.5
Summary
This chapter presents the analysis of the outcomes of a variety of optimization setups
for a multilayered particle illuminated by a +z directed EHD and a −y directed MHD
located along the negative x-axis.
Initially, the proportionality between particle size
and directivity was verified. Larger particles produced larger directivities. Early on in
the chapter, Pareto fronts clearly demonstrate that superdirectivity and unidirectionality
can be achieved even for a relatively low population size and generation size of the GA.
Increasing the population size and number of generations generally led to better results,
however, it was also shown in for a few cases that worse solutions were found.
The Pareto fronts clearly illuminated that a plethora of solutions achieving superdirec-
tivity and unidirectionality were available to choose from. A few of these solutions were
chosen for further investigations. Firstly, a 10-layered and 5-layered case, both achieving
very similar far-field characteristics, were shown. The 10-layered case had a directivity
of 15.01 dB with a 16.77 dB FTBR, while the 5-layered case had a directivity of 14.92 dB
and a FTBR of 17.08 dB. Thus, even though the number of layers differed by a factor
of two, the directive properties were almost identical. These particles exceeded all the
directivity bounds by at least a factor of two.
The two formerly explained particles needed its optimization parameters to have at
least eight decimal places. Therefore, solutions more robust to rounding to four significant
digits were investigated. For this, a superdirective and highly unidirectional 3-layered
==PAGE:64==
56
Chapter 5. Results
particle was shown, which exhibited nearly identical characteristics when using rounded
off material parameters.
The 3-layered particle from the AP-S/URSI conference paper was shown. By not
exciting either the EHD or the MHD, the directivity decreased by at least 2 dB while the
FTBR dropped by at least a factor of eight. Thus, the necessity of exciting both was
demonstrated. Finally, a particle with radius λ was shown to also achieve superdirectiv-
ity. It was deemed unfeasible with the present algorithm to pursue configurations with
particles with a radius larger than λ.
==PAGE:65==
6
Conclusion
This thesis has demonstrated the interaction between a multilayered spherical particle
illuminated by a source consisting of a collocated electric Hertzian dipole (EHD) and an
orthogonal magnetic Hertzian dipole (MHD). In particular, the focus has been on exceed-
ing the directivity bounds to achieve superdirectivity, and to determine the consequence
of including a MHD as the source. A multi-objective genetic algorithm (GA) was used to
find the permittivity and radius of each layer, as well as the source position and ED : MD
ratio, that would maximize the directivity and front-to-back ratio.
Chapter 2 investigated the advancement of upper directivity bounds. Taking outset
in Harrington’s analytically derived upper bound based on the largest order of the spher-
ical wave function [12]. Harrington himself made a small correction to this expression
based on the fact that modes of order n ≤krmin do not contribute significantly. This
bound was deemed inappropriate for electrically small antennas.
Instead, Kildal and
Best [13] proposed a heuristic bound which modifies Harrington’s slightly. Most recently,
Yaghjian [14] proposed a bound similar to that of Kildal and Best. The main difference
was that he has different expressions for the directivity depending on the electrical radius
of the enclosing sphere.
Theoretical and practical spherical configurations for obtaining superdirectivity through
tailoring higher-order modes (HOMs) were also shown. For plane-wave excitation, intro-
ducing a void into a homogeneous dielectric particle proved as a viable method to obtain
more directive properties [18]. The excitation of electric and magnetic modes of equal or-
der was shown to provide the highest directivity. Unwanted sidelobes could be mitigated
at the expense of a slightly lower directivity by exciting electric and magnetic modes of
mixed order [19].
Similar studies were done with an electric dipole as the source. A strong scattering
response could be seen for a metamaterial inspired DPS-DNG core-shell sphere [22].
Furthermore, optimizing a 3-layered sphere using an evolutionary algorithm also resulted
in superdirectivity [21]. An experimental verification of superdirectivity for an electric
dipole illuminating a homogeneous sphere was demonstrated by comparing the measured
results with the Mie expansion coefficients [31].
Chapter 3 presented the theory that laid the basis for the numerical studies. Initially,
the configuration consisting of an EHD and MHD positioned and oriented arbitrarily out-
side a multilayered spherical particle situated in vacuum was shown in Fig. 3.1. Each
layer of the particle had a variable, but real and positive, permittivity and radius. The
relation between the magnetic and electric current distribution, Jm = WηJe, was es-
tablished. Here, W was a weighting factor determining the degree of imbalance w.r.t.
a Huygens source for which W = 1. To this end, the ED : MD = 1 : W ratio was
established.
A short remark on some basic electromagnetic theory was made and the duality con-
57
==PAGE:66==
58
Chapter 6. Conclusion
cept was established. This fundamental concept was the basis for deriving the fields from
a MHD, as the dual expressions for an EHD had been developed in previous literature.
The general concept of the vector spherical wave expansion (VSWE) was presented.
To this end, the material parameter-free vector spherical wave functions (VSWFs) were
presented. Expressions for an arbitrary electric and magnetic field were shown to be com-
posed of a TE and TM part, each having an appropriate expansion coefficient dependent
on the problem at hand. In this case, the problem at hand was first and foremost the
arbitrarily placed and oriented EHD and MHD fields. The EHD expressions were readily
available from the literature [20], while the MHD expressions were established through
duality. The superposition of these fields thus gave the total field of the combined EHD-
MHD source.
Subsequently, the scattered field from the multilayered particle was derived. An ap-
propriate way to express this is via the VSWE, and therefore the unknown fields in each
region was described in terms of their scalar TE and TM Debye potentials. By employ-
ing the electromagnetic boundary conditions, it became apparent that a matrix equation
could be used to determine the unknown expansion coefficients.
Far-field expression of the electric and magnetic fields were determined. Using these
far-field expressions, the total radiated power was derived using orthogonality relations
for the associated Legendre functions and the complex exponential. Lastly, the perhaps
most important parameter, namely the directivity, was introduced.
Chapter 4 demonstrated how the theory was implemented numerically. The most
important external Python packages were stated [44]–[47] and their uses explained. The
truncation number n = M was introduced as the computations had to have a finite
number of terms.
Key assumptions about the individual sources were made. It was determined to have
both sources collocated along the −x-axis and the sources were orthogonal to each other.
Without loss of generality, the EHD was +z-directed, while the MHD was −y-directed.
The VSWF approach was then compared to the classical approach in a number of
cases. Initially, the two approaches for the EHD-MHD source in free space was shown.
The directivity and total radiated power converged even for low M. The magnitude of the
electric and magnetic near-field also showed great agreement except when approaching
the source. Here, a large deviation due to the truncation of the VSWE can be seen.
As the truncation number grew, the VSWE approach was shown to follow the classical
approach more closely.
A finite-element method (FEM) comparison in COMSOL was also performed.
In
this case, the EHD-MHD source illuminated a 3-layered sphere, and the directivity using
the VSWF approach was compared to the result obtained using the FEM. Excellent
correspondence could be seen, and only small discrepancies were observed.
To round off the implementation, the optimization problem was described. The objec-
tive was to maximize the directivity in the +x-direction while simultaneously maximizing
the front-to-back ratio (FTBR). The optimization variables were the permittivity and ra-
dius of each individual layer, as well as the source position and ED : MD ratio. NSGA-II
was the preferred optimization algorithm due to its widespread popularity. The concept
of the Pareto front was described in this regard. Also, the need of parallelization and the
high-performance cluster in order to speed up the optimizations was discussed.
Lastly, Chapter 5 presented the outcome of the numerical studies performed. It was
quickly demonstrated, that superdirectivity and unidirectionality was easily achieved.
From the initial discussion of directivity bounds, the proportionality between the di-
==PAGE:67==
59
rectivity and particle size was shown numerically. The larger particles showed greater
directivity when compared to smaller particles.
By increasing the population size and number of generations for the GA, it was es-
tablished that the results would for the most part converge to larger directivities and
thereby better results. Exceptions to this fact was seen, however, which was attributed
to the great number of local optima. Decreasing the permittivity range was proposed as
an alternative as this limits the parameter space, and thereby supposedly gives an initial
population achieving better results.
A few highlighted solutions were presented. The first case was the 10-layered case
which obtained the largest directivity of all solutions at 15.01 dB with a 16.77 dB FTBR.
All the proposed directivity bounds were surpassed by at least a factor two. A second
case with very large directivity was shown. This was a 5-layered particle for which the
maximum directivity was 14.92 dB with a 17.08 dB. This was indeed a surprising result, as
it was to be expected, that a higher number of layers would lead to larger directivity [28].
This was most likely due to convergence issues of the GA. The high degree of similarity
of these cases was also reflected in the directivity, excited expansion coefficients, and
near-field plots.
The optimization variables often had upwards of 16 significant digits which in a prac-
tical setting is not feasible. Therefore, the possibility to truncate this to four significant
digits (two decimal places) was discovered. For the previously explained particles this re-
sulted in a 1-2 dB drop in directivity and at least a halving of the FTBR. Superdirectivity
and unidirectionality was still achieved, however.
A more reliant case regarding the rounding to four significant digits was a 3-layered
case with an extremely large FTBR of 118 dB and a 12 dB directivity.
Rounding to
four significant digits led to a large FTBR drop down to 81 dB, however, the directivity
remained unchanged. This also was the case for the related directivity pattern when
using the full precision and rounded parameters.
Penultimately, the ED : MD ratio was discussed. For the 3-layered particle ED : MD =
1 : 1.45, indicating that the imbalance in the excitation increases the FTBR. This was
supported for the 5- and 10-layered particles as well. The maximum directivity solutions
both had ED : MD ≈1 : 1, i.e., a perfectly balanced Huygens source. When investigat-
ing solutions with a large FTBR for these particles, the ratios were ED : MD = 1 : 0.80
and ED : MD = 1 : 0.91 for the 5-layered and 10-layered particle, respectively. It was
shown for the 3-layered AP-S/URSI configuration, that not exciting either the EHD or
MHD led to at least a 2 dB decrease in directivity and a decrease in FTBR by more than
a factor of eight.
Finally, it was deemed unfeasible to pursue superdirectivity for particles larger than
2λ with the current algorithm. Two examples of a particle of radius λ, both achieving
superdirectivity, were shown. Furthermore, their remarkable resilience to including only
four significant digits was mentioned.
Future Work
The most urgent extension to this work is a refinement of the optimization algorithm.
This could include varying other parameters in the GA instead of only the population
size and number of generations. Alternatively, it could be to employ some sort of ac-
celeration, possibly in the form of GPU acceleration, which could lead to a high degree
==PAGE:68==
60
Chapter 6. Conclusion
of parallelization and thereby a large increase in speed. Other evolutionary algorithms
could also be explored, as there exist a plethora of these [54].
If possible, finding specific resonance conditions for the N-layered particle could be
interesting.
This is done in [20], which seems to create a better way to ensure the
wanted modes are excited. This could possibly also be done by obtaining the expansion
coefficients through a recursive algorithm as done in [41].
Including a more complex material model would also be beneficiary. Introducing lossy
and dispersive materials would model the problem even more accurately. Extending upon
the material models, the inclusion of metamaterial inspired spheres might also lead to
interesting results. Coating a number of the layers with an impedance surface would also
have the potential to greatly alter the scattering properties of the structure. This has
previously been shown in, e.g., [57].
Finally, conducting simulations in commercial software using practical realizable sources
would reinforce the findings made in this thesis, as this could then lead to experimental
verification of a physically realizable model.

Multipole-based Superdirective Spherical Dielectric
Lens Antennas
Asbjørn T. Birch(1), Samel Arslanagi´c(1), and Richard W. Ziolkowski(2)
(1) Department of Space Research and Technology, Technical University of Denmark, Lyngby, Denmark,
(s184742@student.dtu.dk,saar@dtu.dk)
(2) Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, The University of Arizona, Tucson, AZ, USA
(ziolkows@arizona.edu)
Abstract—Advanced higher frequency wireless systems have
an intrinsic need for higher directivity antennas. Initial studies
of superdirective dielectric lenses are reported. The developed
devices are based on the higher-order transverse electric and
magnetic modes present in a multilayered sphere realized with
high permittivity dielectrics. The superdirective properties of
the lenses are empowered by exciting them with a basic uni-
directional mixed-multipole antenna. Analytical evaluations of
potential ideal configurations have been performed followed with
numerical simulations of more realistic versions. The simulated
outcomes of three-layer example systems are presented along
with comparisons with known directivity bounds to confirm their
superdirective performance characteristics.
I. INTRODUCTION
The concept of superdirectivity originated in the seminal
paper by Oseen [1] in which he suggested a classical (wave-
based) model of Einstein’s needle radiation, i.e., the quantum
mechanical photon. It was conceived as a superposition of
higher-order multipoles at the same frequency formed analyt-
ically as derivatives of the free-space Green’s function. This
fundamental concept of needle radiation through a solution of
Maxwell’s equations was demonstrated explicitly in [2].
There were many theoretical papers published in the last
century about superdirectivity and supergain, e.g., [3], [4],
[5], [6], [7]. However, the common consensus then was that
superdirective systems were not practical because of major
problems associated with them including low radiation resis-
tances; low efficiencies; narrow bandwidths; and sensitivities
to design parameters and, hence, fabrication tolerances, as
summarized succinctly, e.g., [5], [6]. A pivotal turning point in
superdirectivity was the demonstration in the beginning of this
century of practical electrical small (ES) two-element superdi-
rective monopole arrays, e.g., in [8]. In the past decade there
have been numerous examples of experimentally demonstrated
superdirective systems, e.g., in [9], [10].
To determine if an antenna system is superdirective or not,
the applied electromagnetics (EM) community has relied on
developed directivity bounds of radiating and receiving sys-
tems in linear, time-invariant (LTI) environments. Harrington
used spherical wave expansions of the EM fields to derive the
well-known maximum directivity bound DH−N = N 2 + 2N
for a system composed of electric and magnetic multipoles
whose largest order is N in [11]. Moreover, Harrington also
postulated in [11], [12] that multipoles of order N > ka cannot
be effectively used for engineering purposes for a system
whose electrically size is ka. Consequently, he suggested
the size-constrained maximum directivity bound DH−ka =
(ka)2 + 2ka. This has become a defacto comparison choice
for many endfire-radiating array articles. Nevertheless, it was
recognized by Kildal and Best in [13] that it inappropriately
goes to zero as ka does, i.e., the directivity of an ideal electric
or magnetic dipole is well-known [14] to be Ddipole = 1.5 =
1.76 dB. Consequently, the heuristic bound DKB = (ka)2 + 3
was suggested because an ideal Huygens dipole antenna
(HDA) has the maximum directivity DHDA = 3 = 4.77 dB.
The appropriateness of this bound was reinforced in [15].
Most recently, it has been recommended in [16] that for
realistic engineering purposes one should use DHY = 3 for
ka ≤1 and DHY = (ka)2 + 2ka for ka > 1, with HY
referring to Harrington-Yaghjian since this bound modifies
Harrington’s when ka < 1. Finally, it is also well-known
[14] that the maximum directivity of an aperture antenna/array
whose aperture area A is uniformly excited with a time-
harmonic signal whose wavelength is λ is DA = 4π A/λ2.
As emphasized, e.g., in [5], then a broadside-radiating system
is superdirective if its directivity exceeds this value. We

We report initial work on custom-designed multilayered
spheres formed with low and high permittivity dielectrics
and excited by a basic unidirectional mixed-multipole antenna
(UMMA) [17] to achieve superdirective unidirectional perfor-
mance. This work is a three-dimensional (3D) generalization
of the successful two-dimensional (2D) superdirective efforts
in [18]. The latter was based on an infinite magnetic line
source exciting positive and negative permittivity material lay-
ers and only transverse magnetic (TM) modes were available
since the configuration was 2D. The 3D configuration with
its low and high permittivity layers provides access to both
transverse electric (TE) and TM modes which can be excited
by both the electric (ED) and magnetic (MD) dipoles provided
by the UMMA, which is a variation of the many successful ES
HDAs [19]. Moreover, like the 2D physics, the UMMA excites
higher-order multipoles in the multilayered spheres that occur
near the same frequency. The consequent superposition of their
radiated fields leads to the superdirective outcome. While there
have been many works emphasizing similar multipole physics,
but with the excitation being a plane wave, e.g., [20], [21],
[22], [23], [24], or a simple electric dipole [25], [26], [27],
==PAGE:81==
[28], the simultaneous excitation with both ED and MD fields,
as well as them being unbalanced, is innovative. In fact, it
will be emphasized that the UMMA excitation can be tailored
to generally lead to a 2–3 dB enhancement in the maximum
directivity over a similar scatterer excited only by one of
them. Custom-designed three-layer dielectric lenses are used to
illustrate these concepts and their superdirctive outcomes along
with tailored large front-to-back ratios (FTBRs). Our work
has involved analytical studies, in-house developed codes, and
commercial software. All simulation results presented herein
were performed with ANSYS HFSS v21.

IV. NUMERICAL RESULTS
A 3-layer configuration custom-designed by trial and error
has the permittivities ε1 = 1.0, ε2 = 25.0, ε3 = 4.0; the radii
r1 = 2.0, r2 = 6.0, r3 = 8.0 mm; and the source distance
rs = 12.0 mm. The UMMA power ratio (PR), ED:MD, was
considered in four cases: A. only the ED is excited; B. only
the MD is excited; C. both ED and MD are excited equally,
and D. ED and MD were excited to achieve the best directivity
and FTBR outcomes. i.e., A. 1:0; B. 0:1; C. 1:1; and (for this
example) D. 2:1. The resulting maximum directivity and the
corresponding FTBR values for each of these four cases at the
peak directivity frequency of Case D, 9.94 GHz, were (in dB):
A. 6.47 and 6.24; B. 5.53 and 3.61; C. 8.92 and 17.70; and
D. 9.03 and 24.44.
Taken as a broadside-radiating circular aperture antenna, the
cross-sectional area of the UMMA-lens system at 9.94 GHz
with acirc = r3 yields kacirc = 1.58 and, hence, DA = 3.99
dB. Taken as a two-element (driven UMMA plus parasitic 3-
layer sphere) endfire array, the radius of the smallest enclosing
sphere is asystem = (8 + 12)/2 = 10.0 mm, which yields
kasystem = 1.98 and (in dB) DH−ka = 8.96; DKB = 8.40; and
DHY = 8.96. Consequently, while all cases exceed the DA
bound, only the tailored UMMA case is superdirective relative
to all of these bounds, while the HDA case is borderline. The
UMMA cases are essentially 2.45 dB greater than the ED or
MD only cases as anticipated.
As was noted earlier, a multi-variable GA-based optimizer
appropriate for our configuration is being developed and initial
results have been obtained. It yielded a 3-layer configuration
with the permittivities ε1 = 52.45, ε2 = 1.00, ε3 = 3.85; the
radii r1 = 2.11, r2 = 3.19, r3 = 10.0 mm; the source distance
rs = 15.5 mm; and the ED-MD source ratio 1.0:0.70. The
calculated maximum directivity at the design frequency, 10.0
GHz, was D = 11.19 dB with a FTBR = 19.18 dB.
The problem was then explored in HFSS. Note that these
model parameters were the values used in the HFSS model.
The actual analytically predicted parameters were given out to
16 decimal places. Consequently, the HFSS model with these
values having dramatically less decimal places (2) was used
as a gauge of the practical worth of the configuration.

V. CONCLUSIONS
This brief summary reported preliminary outcomes of the
investigations of superdirective spherical dielectric lenses com-
posed of low and high permittivity layers that were excited
with a basic ED and MD based UMMA. Both trial-and-error
and GA-predicted 3-layer cases demonstrated that superdirec-
tive properties with large FTBR values were obtained only
if the UMMA was employed either as an HDA or with a
tailored ED:MD power ratio. The development of an effective
GA algorithm for many-layered spherical dielectric lenses is
progressing nicely, as is our understanding of which TE and
TM modes effectively contribute to the far-field responses. We
expect to have several GA-designed many-layered lenses with
superdirective properties ready to present at the conference.
