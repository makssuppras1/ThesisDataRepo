Summary
This thesis further explores the modified Sokoban domain presented in the course 02285
- Artificial Intelligence and Multi-Agent Systems.
The main objective is to find out whether a dedicated planner is necessary to solve
the problems that arise in this domain, or if a general multi-purpose planner will suffice.
This is of particular interest as the domain corresponds directly to a lot of real-world
applications, such as robots in a packaging warehouse or in a hospital.
An AI to solve levels in the modified Sokoban domain has been designed and imple-
mented, and so has two additional extensions to the modified domain.
Comparisons have been made between this AI and a selected general planner to con-
clude whether a dedicated planner is necessary.
The conclusion is: Yes, a dedicated planner is necessary. The tested planner per-
formed admirably on very small problems but did not scale. Problems one would face in
the real world, are of considerable complexity, and would not be solved in a reasonable
timeframe by the tested general planner.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
“General-purpose planning techniques quickly become computationally in-
tractable as one tries to increase the scale of the problem from simple ex-
amples to real, useful applications. Practical solutions lie not so much in
enhancing general-purpose techniques but in finding ways to exploit the struc-
ture of specific problem spaces without compromising the goals of flexibility
and extensibility”1 - Theodore A. Linden
30 years later, and we still face the same reality.
Or do we?
Have general planners advanced far enough over these past 30 years?
Do we need a custom solution for each problem, or can existing software be the an-
swer?
We set out to test this.
We have previously explored a modified Sokoban domain first presented to us in
the course 02285 - Artificial Intelligence and Multi-Agent Systems. We continue our
exploration into this familiar domain. Sokoban is an interesting domain, as the issues
herein have a lot of similarities with issues you might find at a physical place of work,
such as a packaging warehouse or hospital, employing robots. The same question can
arise in both these domains:
How do we as efficiently as possible satisfy multiple simultaneous tasks requiring
objects of a specific kind to end up in a specific destination?
Our approach to finding this answer; let each of the two solutions compete. To accom-
plish this, we need a dedicated AI made for this purpose. The challenge is to design and
implement an AI for this modified Sokoban domain, attempting to exploit the structure
of the problems. Extensibility is also a relevant aspect, as the real-life scenario might
need more features than that of a simple video game. We design and implement two
extensions to this domain. One is an improved format of defining a level. This is already
more powerful in itself, but also eases the implementation of additional extensions. Sec-
ondly, we make a ’portal’ feature, meant to simulate a simple elevator, taking a robot
from one point in space to another.

On the other hand, we select a state-of-the-art general planner, and define our prob-
lem in a format it can use. Once we have our competitors in place, we let them fight
it out. They will compete on identical levels, meant to test different aspects of the

In the end, we will conclude: Is the dedicated AI necessary? We suspect so - but
will attempt to confirm or deny.

CHAPTER 2
Domain
We are exploring the Sokoban domain. An almost 40-year-old puzzle video game, where
one must push boxes around, for them to end up in specified storage locations. An
example of such a game can be seen in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: A Sokoban game[Unk].
More precisely, we are looking at the Sokoban-extension, Pukoban. Pukoban is the
same domain, where one also has the ability to pull boxes in addition to pushing them.
This is far more forgiving, as one can return to a previous position by reversing their
moves. We will be referring to the domain as Sokoban henceforth, as it is significantly
more well known than its successor.
==PAGE:14==
4
2 Domain
2.1
Practical Application
The domain is interesting. Not because we wish to perfect the solution of an old video
game but because of what the video game represents. Having a man move boxes around
to pre-specified spaces could very easily be compared to the job of a warehouse worker.
Which is the origin of the games name, Sokoban translated to English comes out as
warehouse keeper.
This means that a solution efficiently solving Sokoban could be applied in many real
world situations.
Another real-world application, presented to us in the course as described in sec-
tion 2.3, is that of a hospital. It could be robots moving beds around, delivering food,
medicine, or taking out the trash.
2.2
Multi-Agent
While the control of a single unit in the case of the video game can prove to be a great
challenge to solve, it does not necessarily always prove very useful in the real world. In a
large hospital or a big warehouse, one single robot might not be able to keep up with the
tasks one desires to be performed. In this case employing multiple robots is beneficial.
This introduces a lot of complexity. To be as efficient as possible, which robots are to
fulfill which tasks, in which order? In addition to this they may also have to assist each
other in the case of blocking objects, and ensure they are not themselves blocking each
other’s path.
In the case of a single person or robot, both of whom from this point forward shall
be referred to as agents, the branching factor is already considerable. T
he agent has the following actions:
• Move itself in all directions
• Push boxes towards multiple different directions
• Pull a box into its own space while moving to a new one.
The more boxes that are present for the agent to move, the larger the possible state
space also becomes.
As we introduce additional agents who can also perform all these actions, the branch-
ing factor rises exponentially. To efficiently solve this problem we employ heuristics.

2.4
Representation of domain
While this can be directly compared to a warehouse, hospital, or other institution in
need of pathfinding, it is modeled by the game of Sokoban. As we discuss different
problems in the future, it is important to understand what this entails. Namely how a
level is described, and which actions are available.
2.4.1
Level
A level describes the world of the agent. It is the hallways it can roam, the rooms it
can enter, the objects it can move. We describe these levels in a grid. Five things are
present in these levels:
Walls Walls are fields that the agents can not go into.
Available fields These fields are where the agents can go, assuming unoccupied.
This is everywhere there are no walls.
Agents This is the start position of the agents.
Boxes These are the objects the agents can move.
Goals This is where specific boxes must be placed to satisfy the level.
As a level is being solved, the walls, available fields, and goals, are all in static
positions, while the position of the agents and boxes are variable, the agents can move
themselves and they can move the boxes.
In a real-world situation, if a warehouse or hospital was described, the walls and avail-
able fields would be static between problems. Whereas the goals would be frequently
updated as the agents are to perform new tasks.
Figure 2.2 shows a simple level. The circles represent two different agents, the squares
represent two different boxes, and the colored fields represent goals. A box of the same
color must be on the field to satisfy the goal.

2.4.2
Actions
There is a set amount of actions an agent can take. Each action can only be taken if
their condition is met.
2.4.2.1
Move
The agents can move in any direction, that is up, down, left and right, in the grid.
Assuming the field they are moving into is not a wall, and that it is not occupied by
another agent or box. This is illustrated in Figure 2.3.
(a) All directions available
(b) 2 out of 4 directions unavailable
Figure 2.3: Drawings displaying the workings of an agent move action.

2.4.2.2
Push
An agent can push any box it is adjacent too, in any direction. That is, up, down, left
and right. Again, this is assuming the field the box is being pushed into, is available.
This means there is always at most three possible push actions between an agent and
a single box, as the box cannot be pushed into the field the agent itself is standing on.
Doing this action will leave the agent on the field that the box was previously occupying.
This is illustrated in Figure 2.4. The agent moves into the box’s previous space and
can choose to push the box in any of three directions. The result is shown for the case
where the box is pushed upwards, following the orange glowing arrow.
Figure 2.4: Illustrates how a box can be pushed, and the result of a push.
2.4.2.3
Pull
This action is the opposite of the push action. The agent can move in any direction,
assuming the desired space is available. This means the agent cannot move into the
field of the box being moved, as that would be a push action. As such, there is always
at most three available pull actions between an agent and a single box. As the agent
is moving away from the space it was occupying pre-move, the box it is pulling, will be
moved into that space.
This is illustrated in Figure 2.5. The agent pulls a box into the spot previously
occupied by the agent, and itself moves into one of three other available fields. In this
case, going downwards. This is very similar to the push action, acting as the inverse.
Figure 2.5: Illustrates how a box can be pulled, and the result of a pull.

CHAPTER 3
Extensions
Two extensions to the original domain have been implemented. First, we define a level
in a way completely different from the way it was done when we previously worked on
the domain. Secondly, we have made portals, so an agent can travel from one point in
a level to another.
3.1
Definition of a level
We chose to rework how we describe levels, to allow features that were previously im-
possible, and make the addition of new features a breeze – at least in regards to defining
them in a level.
3.1.1
Original format
Originally, as we worked on the same domain, we used the format shown in Listing 3.1.
The initial rows declare which agents and boxes are in the level and their respective
colors. Numbers represent agents, and letters represent boxes. An agent can only move
a box of the same color as itself. Each small letter represents a goal, which will only be
satisfied while a box with the same capital letter is placed upon it.

3.1.1.1
Advantages
This format has a very big advantage: Its simplicity. It is very easy to understand.
Describing a new level or changing an existing one, is a quick task due to this simplicity.
==PAGE:20==
10
3 Extensions
3.1.1.2
Disadvantages
However, the format also has disadvantages, such as:
• Multiple elements can not be placed on top of each other. Since each character
describes an entire field in the level, you cannot have two entities start on the same
field. In the original, that would be, an agent or a box can not start on a field
which is also a goal. We simply cannot represent this in this format.
• Limited amount of characters. We already use all the numbers and letters, making
extensions to the domain troublesome to describe in this existing format.
• An agent can only move a single type of box, limiting the possible levels one can
create. It is not possible to have a box that two individual agents can both move,
while one of the agents is the only one capable of moving a different box.
These disadvantages severely limited further ideas, which is why our first extension
to the domain lies in how we describe it.
3.1.2
New format
The disadvantages and limitations of the previous format were something we wished to
overcome, as such we choose to describe our levels in a different way, specifically by
describing them in a JSON format. This is merely a data structure, that can hold any
information we wish.
An example of a description is shown in Listing 3.2, the result of this definition is
shown in Figure 3.1.

As illustrated by Listing 3.2 and Figure 3.1, quite some information is required for even

==PAGE:22==
12
3 Extensions
Definition of walls
In levels with a lot of walls, we quickly discovered that typing
them out one by one in the new format was not an enjoyable experience. It both took
time to write, and it was also hard to imagine exactly how it would end up looking.
We ended up allowing one to define the walls in the old format, in a separate text file.
This allows for the design of more advanced levels, without heavily increasing the time
it takes to create. In the new format it is not necessary to define walls around the level.
If no walls are inside the dimensions of the level, no file is needed to describe the walls.
There is also no need to define spaces on rows, after the last wall on said row has been
placed. In the same vein, there is no need to define rows after the last wall in the level
has been placed.
3.1.2.2
Advantages
However, it is a more powerful descriptive way of defining our levels, allowing for designs
such as what is shown in Figure 3.2. Here a box is placed on a goal upon start, which
was previously not possible. In addition to this, one agent can move both boxes while
the other agent is only capable of moving one box. This was also previously not possible.
If we were to relate this to the real-life scenario again, one robot might be capable
of moving beds, and also deliver food. The smaller robot might only be able to deliver
food, but does not have the capability to move a bed, thus their abilities overlap, but
are not identical.
Figure 3.2: Illustrates a small level with multiple advantages over the original format.
And to reiterate, as this is simply a format storing any kind of data, we can add any
information we want. For example, if we wanted to give agents a name that was floating
around them, it would be effortless to add the data to a level definition.

3.2
Portals
The second extension made to the Sokoban domain is what is commonly referred to as
a portal. An example of a level with a portal is shown in Figure 3.3. For the extended
domain, it is a two-way portal, meaning a trip back is possible as well. One may wonder
how portals relate to the real world. The portal simulates the behaviour of an elevator.
Figure 3.3: A level where portals must be utilized.
In the extended Sokoban domain, any portal takes up one position of the level, and
is connected with one other portal. If an agent finds itself on a portal, it may expend
an action to take it, instantly appearing at the connected portal. Agents are also able
to push and pull boxes through portals. It takes two actions for both agent and box to
make it through a portal; one for the agent and one for the box.

4.1
Definition of levels
How we define levels have been described previously, in section 3.1.
In order to use the information stored in the level files, they must be loaded first.
The file name, of the file to be loaded, is specified so the level loader knows which JSON
and text file to look for. The levels are defined based on the information available in
the previously described format. All of this information is gathered in an object. This
is the state object that contains agent, box and goal objects among other things, like
the dimensions of the level. Agents, boxes, and goals receive an id based on the order
of which they appear in the JSON file, as well as the other information available there.
The walls are loaded from the text file of the same name and saved in a wall array.
4.2
High-Level Architecture
Our AI does decentralised planning, meaning that plans are made individually for the
agents. There is a focus on problem decomposition for this AI, as agents continuously
receive objectives they are supposed to complete, as the level is being solved. This is done
since the complexity of centralised planning increases exponentially with the number of
agents that need plans. A decentralised planner, on the other hand, only grows linearly,
by the amount of agents that need plans, but brings with it a host of problems that need
solving. One such problem stems from the fact that agents, that make individual plans,
do not know how the environment will change as they complete their plan. This may
cause conflicts, as different agents attempt to use the same resource at the same time.
Another problem is how to perform the objectives in the correct order. These problems
are addressed, by using a topological ordering, of a graph over the goals, as well as an
agent ranking system.
==PAGE:26==
16
4 Design
4.2.1
Online vs offline
Two paradigms exist, namely online and offline planning.
An offline planner creates the whole plan before any action is taken. In most real-
world examples, it is not feasible to do offline planning.
This is because an offline
planner assumes that the environment is deterministic, fully observable, as well as static.
If anything happens to interfere with the offline plan, before the goal is accomplished,
nothing can be done about it. An online planner, such as the one made in this project,
could stop and wait for the plan to be applicable again or even re-plan. This is because an
online planner is able to take action, without making one unchanging plan for completing
the entire level. This is useful for real-life scenarios where there is a risk of outside
interference. Since there is a focus on comparing AI’s for the sake of a real-life scenario,
it was deemed fitting to use online planning. To add to that, it seems rather difficult
for a decentralised planner, to make a working offline plan, since there is no room, for
even small errors, such as a conflict.
4.3
Pre-processing
Before we attempt to make a plan for a level, we process the level, thus pre-processing.
It is a set of actions we only perform a single time. The result of this continuously
help our agents throughout their attempt at solving the level. We have identified a few
elements in levels that are common across a majority of them, that it can be helpful to
pre-process.
4.3.1
All Pair Shortest Path
Something that can be pre-processed, is the shortest distance between any two positions
in the level. These distances will be used for the heuristic as well as creating objectives
for agents as discussed in the next sections. Creating these distances is called the all
pair shortest path problem and will be solved using the Floyd–Warshall algorithm, [H
C+10] which is shown in Algorithm 1. A lookup in a table is significantly faster than
calculating the shortest path continuously as the level is being solved, especially if you
want an accurate distance.
==PAGE:27==
4.3 Pre-processing
17
4.3.1.1
The Floyd–Warshall algorithm
If R is the amount of rows and C the amount of columns in the level, the result of the
algorithm is a 2D matrix containing R2C2 distances. For this project, the initial cost
between two positions is set to zero if they are the same. If they are adjacent, or if a
portal goes between the positions, while none of them contain walls, the cost is set to
one. Otherwise, the cost is set to RC. The algorithm compares each of these R2C2 costs
in the matrix with RC other sums of two other weights, in order to find the shortest
distances. If it is cheaper to get to destination B from start point A, by going through
C, then the cost of traversing from A to B is set to the cost of going from A to C to B.
The run-time of the algorithm is O(R3C3).

4:

5:

8: end for
4.3.1.2
Alternative Distance Measures
An all pair shortest path matrix contains the shortest possible distance between any two
positions, but it does not account for obstacles such as boxes and agents.
An alternative to measuring distances this way would be to instead use the Manhattan
distance. The advantage of doing this is that no pre-processing is needed, saving a run-
time of O(R3C3). This can be a huge gain in performance for very large levels. Fx. for
a level with R = C = 100, the Floyd–Warshall algorithm would need to do 1012 compar-
isons. To put that into perspective, if each comparison took only one nanosecond, the
total run-time would be about 17 minutes. The disadvantage of using the Manhattan
distance is that it does not take account of walls or portals. Because of this, the Man-
hattan distance is a bad fit for levels with portals and maze-like wall structures. This is
why it was not chosen as the distance measure for this project.
An alternative, that does take account of walls and portals, is the distance achieved,
using a search algorithm such as A*. In this case, pre-processing is optional. Using
A* would most likely be slower when used as pre-processing versus the Floyd–Warshall
algorithm and does not have any notable advantages. However, if it is used to calculate
the distance when needed, it has the advantage of taking agents and boxes into account.
In some cases, this can be a disadvantage, as the boxes and agents may move, causing
the calculated distance to become outdated. It has the disadvantage of not having a
constant time look-up, which the previously discussed methods do.
==PAGE:28==
18
4 Design
4.3.2
Geometric Analysis
Part of the pre-processing is identifying ’tunnels’ in the level.
Definition of a tunnel
A collection of fields is defined as a tunnel if it satisfies one
of the following conditions:
• It has two neighbouring walls in opposite directions
• It has three neighbouring walls
This idea of a tunnel is discussed in[JS01], specifically for the Sokoban domain. Tunnels
are important to consider for multiple reasons. They are places where live- and deadlocks
are prone to happen. Also, if a tunnel contains several goals, they must be completed
in the correct order.
Note that deadlocks cannot happen in our modified domain in a solvable level, as
all actions are undo-able. We can however run into deadlocks in the event of a faulty AI.
Another pre-processing task is finding positions that are good for stowing away boxes
or even agents in order to make room for another agent, and their plan. These storage
positions can not be part of a tunnel. Although it can be a good idea to stow away
items in a tunnel sometimes, it is easier for agents to retrieve boxes, or escape, from
non-tunnels. In addition, tunnels with two entrances, are not good for storage, since it
could be a bottleneck in the level. These storage positions are chosen so that they will
not block sections of the level, if they become occupied.
4.3.3
Goal Dependency Graph
The pre-processing also constructs a goal dependency graph, a form of partial-order
planning. The vertices of the graph are the goals. The edges are directed and represent
that the goal it originates from should be satisfied before the goal being pointed towards.
Since the graph is also acyclic, it follows a topological ordering. This is an order in which
goals can be completed. The goal dependency graph is created by considering different
features of the level. For example, a level such as the one shown in Figure 4.1 contains
a row of all the goals. Features such as this row are found and analyzed so that the
goals within the row are inserted correctly into the goal dependency graph. This is then
utilized to ensure that we satisfy the goals in the correct order.

4.4 Distribution of Objectives
19
Figure 4.1: A level where the goals need to be completed in a specific order.
4.4
Distribution of Objectives
Objectives are initially distributed to agents based on what boxes said agents can move,
and which goals those boxes can satisfy. Similarly, a list is constructed, over which goals
each agent can be responsible for satisfying. The distribution of objectives follows the
topological sorting of the Goal Dependency Graph. The box that is then chosen for such
an objective is the one closest to the goal, assuming the box is not already satisfying a
goal.
4.4.1
Path Clearing Objectives
If an agent attempts to create a plan based on its current objective but fails, it is assumed
that boxes or agents are currently in the way. In order to get rid of these obstacles a
relaxed plan is made to generate a path that must be cleared. This plan is created by
ignoring all the boxes and agents that could be on the path. In order to then clear
the path we identify the obstacles that must be removed. If boxes exist on the path,
objectives are created with the purpose of removing those boxes. This involves finding
agents that are capable of removing said boxes. If agents are on the path objectives
are created for them to move away from the path. The position these obstacles are to
be moved to is selected among several options. One such option is positions that are
especially good for stowing away agents or boxes. Another option is simply placing the
obstacle as far away as possible from the original position, avoiding position on the path.
==PAGE:30==
20
4 Design
4.5
Solving Objectives
To solve the objectives the agents create a plan. In order to then ensure that agents
don’t attempt to use the same resources simultaneously, a conflict avoidance system is
used.
4.5.1
Search Algorithm
Using A*, a plan is constructed for each agent. Part of the heuristic for this search
is the distance between the agent and the box, assuming the objective includes a box.
Another part is the distance between the targeted position and the box, or agent if no
box is specified. These parts are used to ensure that the search focuses on completing
the objective. Added to this is the count of goals, yet to be solved. This makes it so
that the search result is less likely to undo satisfied goals. The heuristic also adds a
punishment for moving boxes, that are not part of the objective. This is to make it so
the agent does not attempt to unnecessarily move boxes, possibly removing them from
optimal stowage fields. Another punishment is dealt for pulling boxes into a tunnel with
only one entrance. This makes it so an agent is less likely to end up in a situation where
it needs to undo the objective it has completed in order to get out again.
4.5.2
Avoiding conflicts
After a plan has successfully been created for an agent the positions included in that
that plan are saved. If multiple agents desire the same position, a ranking system helps
to resolve the conflict. The rank of an agent is initialised to its ID. If the agent just
created a plan with the purpose of helping another agent it receives a higher.
For an agent to use any position, it must out-rank all other agents who also desire the
same position. The agent with the most important goal is not necessarily the highest-
ranking agent. Specifically, an agent tasked with helping another agent with a clear
path objective, automatically outrank the agent it is helping.
If the agent is not allowed to use the position, it will wait until it is allowed. If the
agent can see field will never free it by itself, a new plan is constructed.
The avoidance conflict system is not perfect. In rare instances it can cause a deadlock.
To prevent this from making a solution impossible to obtain, we completely restart the
AI. The AI starts over, but with the current state as a starting point, keeping the re-
sult of pre-processing intact. While it is not an elegant patch, it does result in the AI
delivering solutions to some issues it would otherwise not deliver for.

5.1
PDDL
PDDL (Planning Domain Description Language) is a standard for describing domains,
that is used by the IPC (International Planning Competition). It is used to describe
problems for the general planners utilizing this format. Since the benchmarking involves
running a contender from the IPC it is necessary to describe the domain as well as the
levels in this format. The PDDL domain file can be seen in Appendix A. It is a heavily
modified version of the Sokoban domain from IPC 2018 [CCM18b].
The domain defines the predicates that can be used to check if a variable is true or
false. The variable can also be changed. This is relevant for the actions described below
the predicates. The precondition of the action must be met in order to apply the effect.
The precondition checks if the predicates hold true while the effect changes the values
of predicates. The predicates are used on the parameter variables of the action.
The level files contain a set of predicates that describe a state, as seen in Appendix B.
PDDL works under the closed-world assumption, meaning that if something is not de-
scribed, it is assumed to be false. That is why only true predicates show up in the level
files. The goal state is described in the bottom of the level files. They contain predicates
that must be satisfied. Only conjunctions between predicates are allowed in the goal
state.
==PAGE:32==
22
5 Benchmark
5.1.1
Temporal
The temporal tracks of IPC 2018 include the extensions of PDDL 2.1, which introduced
durative actions.These actions have a duration. A statement next to the predicates, in
condition and effect, describes at what time said predicate is used. The time at which
they are used is within a closed interval over the duration of the action. The over all
statement is the open time interval from the beginning of the action till the end [FL03].
The at start and at end statements respectively, cover the start and end of the closed
interval.
This extension of PDDL allows for simultaneous durative actions which is relevant for
many multi-agent domains. While it is possible to define shared actions in classic PDDL
such that multiple agents can take actions simultaneously there is a downside to it.
There needs to be a set of action schemas for each possible amount of agents. Since the
chosen domain has a variable amount of agents, using this extension allows us to define
only one set of action schemas.
5.1.2
The Domain
The domain written in PDDL works for levels with or without portals. One may notice
that there are two push actions and two pull actions. The reason this was deemed nec-
essary, is that there needs to be a distinction between satisfying a goal with the action
and not doing so. There can be multiple different ways of satisfying a single goal, which
this solution handles. This would not be necessary if disjunction was allowed in the goal
description. If disjunction was allowed, all goal locations could be checked, to see if they
were holding a box satisfying them.
A noteworthy predicate MATCHING-AGENT-BOX keeps track of what box each agent
can move. In the same vein MATCHING-GOAL-BOX keeps track of what goals, each
box can satisfy. The goals however, only show up as locations in this implementation.
Under some circumstances the MATCHING-GOAL-BOX predicate would be enough to
distinguish between the non-goal and goal actions. But as is the case with the chosen
IPC planner, no negation is allowed in the conditions. This is then handled by the
NON-MATCHING-GOAL-BOX predicate. This specifies for each box the location it
can not satisfy. This includes not only goal locations, but also all other locations. This
makes for large files in the case of large levels with many boxes.
The predicate satisfied is used to keep track of which goals that are satisfied as well as
those that are not. These are also used to describe the goal state in the level files.
MOVE-DIR is a predicate that tracks all possible movement between any two lo-
cations that are related to each other by one of the 5 direction variables. The final
direction is the portal direction, so the predicate can track what portals are connected
to each other.
A function is used to make it easy to define PDDL levels that fall under this domain.
The function turns a level state into a PDDL file. It is used to generate PDDL files for
benchmarking, since describing PDDL levels in hand for this domain is non-trivial.

5.5
Results summary
Some themes reoccur among the benchmarking results. The general planner is often
able to find better solutions on levels of smaller size, given extra time. As the levels
grow bigger in size, it finds solutions slower than the custom AI. The custom AI does get
slowed down by larger levels, mostly because of the all pair shortest path pre-processing,
but not at the rate of the general planner. The general planner may take several hours
to compute plans for bigger levels. If a hospital robot took this much time planning a
route, it would not be useful to the hospital.
When the general planner does find better solutions, it is often because of the custom
AI behaving sup-optimally, when it comes to avoiding conflicts. Other times, the cause
is how the problem gets decomposed. The agents focus on one goal at a time and the
makespan of the solution may suffer because of it.
When it comes to handling the extension of portals it would seem that the general
planner has the advantage. But combined with a large level, like a hospital floor, it
simply takes too long for it to find a solution. If a hospital had to choose a planner
between a generalised planner, like Optic, or a custom one, like ours, the custom one
would win. To model a real-life scenario in a hospital, with many agents and objects,
the general planner would have no chance of handling the combinatorial state explosion
==PAGE:53==
CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The main takeaway from our findings is that the general planner can not keep up with
our dedicated AI, as the problem size increases. This threshold is met very quickly. In
one case, the difference in computational time reached nearly 25000x, and this is only
on problems not near the size one could risk facing.
It is important to note that we did not manage to get our preferred general planner
to run, although we suspect it would have had the same limitations. If it were able to
solve levels significantly faster, it would have increased the threshold for which problem
size one would need to implement a dedicated AI for. We suspect that most problems
applicable in the real-life examples we have described, fall into this category. Especially
as we need to factor in outside influence. In this case, the ability to react and re-plan
is paramount, and here the general planner falls short. There is no plan correction, no
single agent that creates a new plan. The entire plan across all agents would have to be
recalculated in this event utilizing the general planner.
The centralized unit assigning objectives, but individual plans being decentralized,
works very well for this type of problem. Multiple agents might never interfere with each
other, and thus should not need to plan around them. In cases where they do have to
interact, a shared communication channel can alleviate the problems. Treating physical
space as a shared resource also eases the problem of agents with similar desires.
When the general planner does have solutions ready for us, they are often comparable
to our own, or better. However, we do not claim that our AI is optimized. In fact, it
has several issues.
Of course, other techniques could be created and used than just the ones we have uti-
lized, to even further advance the capabilities of the AI. Some issues with the techniques
we have used include:
All pair shortest path
If we were to run many issues on a level with static fields and
walls, there would be no contest here. But as we in our setup, testing many different
levels, have to fully populate our APSP matrix for every single solution, it can lead to
worsened computational times. In a 150x150 map, with a single box next to the agent
next to a goal, we would spend a significant amount of time populating this matrix,
although it would not be necessary.
Analysis of the level to determine whether the
matrix would be useful, could help alleviate this issue.
==PAGE:54==
44
6 Discussion
Benevolent Agents
Our system ranking agents to prioritize resources help ensure
that agents do not consistently block each other. However, the lower-ranked agents are
too patient. There is unnecessary wait time, in situations where the lower-ranked agent
could have crossed without actually interfering with the higher-ranked agent. Agents
with lesser patience would have lowered our makespan, but would also have increased
our computational time, at least slightly, as additional calculations would have been
necessary to lower the wait time.
Replan as a last resort
In our current implementation, agents only replan if neces-
sary. One could in some cases decrease the number of moves needed, by replanning. If
an obstacle disappears, taking a longer route because the obstacle was previously there,
does not give an optimal makespan. It does decrease computational time, so it can be
advantageous in some instances, but a situation where physical objects are being con-
trolled, is probably not one of them.
While the results we have gathered do speak for the necessity of a dedicated AI for
larger issues, it also speaks to the usefulness of general planners. In small problems,
they perform very well. And they have the huge advantage of being significantly easier
to extend. This is a very useful attribute for small but constantly changing problems.
Informing the planner of the new portal feature did not require us to inform it how to
solve such problems, unlike our own AI. We merely had to inform it, that the actions
were available.
The tests where we inserted portals into levels, also very clearly displayed the impact
the makespan of the solution has on the general planner. With no shortcut, computa-
tional times increased tenfold on the 8x8 level. This was further extended to a factor
of 300 times as fast with a portal, in the case where the general planner managed to
find a solution without dropping down to a best-first search. Smaller levels where it can
utilize its own optimizations is where it performs best, but whether it has to drop down
to best-first is very level-dependant.
Additional tests could further confirm our findings. Sadly, as described, running
these state-of-the-art planners proved difficult. Originally, we ran all our tests on one of
the 5 competition participants, that performed very poorly on our domain. So poorly
we could barely test anything, as levels such as those shown in this report would take
hours, if not more. As we were going back over our sources, we discovered Optic. The
time-constraints sadly limited our ability to do as thorough tests as we had initially
planned, with Optic. We still very much appreciate the find as it helped confirm that
general planners does have a use, and can compete up to a certain level, rather than a
conclusion of them being near useless for our domain.
There is no denying that the ability to utilize domain-specific knowledge, rather
than relying on general techniques, is very beneficial. Rather than trying all options,
being able to ensure we solve levels in the correct order in tunnels, for example, is very
powerful.
==PAGE:55==
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
An AI that quickly solves levels of appropriate difficulty for comparison against a general
planner has been designed and implemented. These levels are all within our modified
Sokoban domain.
Agent plans are decentralized, but objectives are delegated in a centralized fashion.
The AI analyzes geometric features of levels to prioritize goals, and ranks agents based
on the importance of their current objective. The result is a system that scales well in
regards to the amount of boxes, goals, and agents. This heavily trivializes planning for
tasks that do not interfere with each other, which is a huge advantage over a completely
centralized approach.
A general planner has been selected based on performance and possibility of use. We
did not manage to run the best possible planner, but still got useful results by testing
the available planner. Our domain and levels have been converted to PDDL, to allow
for the general planner to generate solutions for our problems.
The dedicated AI made for our specific domain and the general planner have com-
peted in solution efficiency and computational time on a selection of levels, to determine
the necessity of a dedicated planner. The result of this is: Yes, there is a need. The
general planner cannot handle the combinatorial state explosion of large levels, many
boxes and/or agents.
That is not to say there are no use cases for general planners. They have the advan-
tage of being flexible, and able to solve problems in a multitude of domains, compared
to just the single domain of the dedicated AI. Their computational time does however
not scale very well, so they are only well suited for small problems. It is much quicker
to describe the domain in PDDL, than to implement a dedicated solution for a small
problem. So with a small possibly dynamic domain a general planner is a good solution.
They do have the disadvantage that they do not work well with outside interference.
For our domain, the solutions generated by the general planner were useful. They
spent a sensible amount of moves, and had a fine makespan. However, increasing the
size of the problem rapidly increased the time it took to compute these solutions. Taking
over an hour to solve what would be considered very simple if it were to be applied to
a real-world scenario, is useless in the context of a ’high speed’ environment such as a
packaging warehouse or a hospital.
==PAGE:56==
46
7 Conclusion
Extensions have been made that confirm the flexibility of the planner. Simply inform-
ing it of the possibility of the new move allowed it to solve levels including an extension.
Whereas the dedicated solution needed additional code to handle extensions.
The general planner took very long to analyze states in some cases, but in cases
where it was able to solve it without dropping down into a best-first search, it explored
an impressively low amount of states. Almost as few states as moves were necessary
in some cases, speaking to the ability of the general planner. If looking for automatic
solutions, it is definitely a large step up from a simple breadth-first search.
The definite answer to the main objective of the present thesis: General planners can
not solve all problems. Large real-world problems such as our defined domain still need
dedicated solutions to efficiently solve the issues they face.
