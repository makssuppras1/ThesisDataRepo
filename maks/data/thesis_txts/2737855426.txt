Abstract
With road transportation responsible for two-thirds of all combustion engine emissions,
understanding vehicle contributions to greenhouse gas emissions in rural areas is cru-
cial. Traditional vehicle counting on highways and main roads is typically conducted for
traffic management purposes, but in rural secondary roads, this task often relies on rudi-
mentary methods like manual counting or automatic traffic counters. While effective on
individual roads, these methods lack a comprehensive overview, which is essential due
to the numerous forks and intersections in rural areas. They are time consuming and
produce discrete results both in time and space and are affected by human error.
This thesis leverages computer vision techniques to detect and count vehicles using im-
ages from two CCTV cameras mounted on a 58-meter-tall tower to estimate the traffic
intensity in a rural area next to Hove. Every 30 minutes, a sequence of three consecu-
tive images is captured from each camera, providing a full 360-degree view of the area.
By applying deep learning approaches, it is possible to accurately detect and count ve-
hicles. Various detection methods and model configurations were analyzed to determine
the most effective approach.
Findings indicate that analyzing smaller windows across the roads, rather than assessing
them individually, resulted in the most successful vehicle counts, accurately detecting
approximately two-thirds of the vehicles in the images. Statistical analyses reveal that the
busiest hour is from 14:00 to 15:00.

Introduction
The transportation sector in the European Union has witnessed a significant increase in
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, even as emissions from the energy sector have de-
creased. Including international shipping and aviation, transportation contributes about
one-third of the total emissions in the EU. In Denmark, for example, the transport sector
accounted for 44% of all CO2 emissions in 2019. The primary sources of these emissions
are the combustion engines in vehicles and vessels, with road transport alone responsi-
ble for 67% of these emissions and vessels 31%. This increase in road transportation
has directly resulted in higher GHG emissions, underlining the urgent need for effective
monitoring and management strategies.[1]
1.1
Study site description
For this thesis, the monitoring of vehicles has been possible thanks to a tall tower located
in Hove (55°42’59’’N,12°14’14’’E). Hove is a small village located near the town of Østrup
in the Region of Zealand, Denmark. Situated close to Hove Overdrev, it lies within the
boundaries of Egedal Municipality. The area is characterized by including agricultural
fields and green spaces. The villages, farms and other facilities are interconnected by a
rural road network, covering the entirety of the study area.
Establishing a traffic count in the Hove area complements and informs the flux collected
by the eddy covariance[2] tall tower. The latter is an atmospheric measurement station
that was implemented to monitor the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and sources in a
typical Danish agricultural area, such as Hove.
The tall tower is equipped with (among other) two gas analyzers and three sonic anemome-
ters that measure continuously and at high frequency, the fluxes of carbon dioxide (CO2),
methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O) and carbon monoxide(CO). This equipment helps to
monitor and quantify the gas exchanges in the atmosphere at the landsacpe scale.

1.2
Limitations of existing solutions
Several existing methods for vehicle detection and counting have significant limitations:
• Trafikinfo [4]. These systems typically provide traffic status on highways but not on
secondary or rural roads, leaving a significant gap in data coverage.
• Manual counting. In the UK, trained enumerators stand by the roadside for up to
12 hours a day to manually count vehicles. In hazardous locations, cameras are
installed, and vehicle counts are performed from video footage. Both methods are
labor-intensive and prone to human error.
• Automatic traffic counters. These devices offer narrow coverage and require in-
stallation on every road, making them impractical for widespread use in rural areas.
• Floating Car Data (FCD). This method involves vehicles sharing their location via
GPS to a control center. While useful for tracking vehicle fleets, it lacks precision
and relies on the presence of GPS devices in all vehicles.

Related work
Detecting vehicles from images is a crucial task in various applications, each with its
unique challenges and methodologies. Generally, there are two prominent approaches
to vehicle detection based on the use case and the type of images available.
Ground-Based Camera Detection
The first approach involves ground-based camera detection, commonly used for traffic
monitoring. Studies like [5] and [6] highlight the efficiency of cameras positioned close to
the ground. This proximity allows for clear and detailed images even with relatively low-
resolution cameras, reducing computational demands and making real-time processing
feasible and cost-effective. Additionally, [7] demonstrates the effectiveness of such setups
in urban environments, showcasing their ability to capture detailed images essential for
accurate vehicle detection and classification.
High-Altitude Camera Detection
In contrast, the second approach employs cameras mounted on tall towers or even satel-
lites, capturing images from a significant height. This method is less common for traffic
applications but can be crucial in other contexts, such as environmental monitoring or
urban planning. However, it introduces several challenges:
• Distance and Perspective: The increased distance between the camera and the
vehicles means that vehicles appear much smaller in the images, significantly re-
ducing the amount of detail available for detection algorithms.
• Image Resolution: To compensate for the greater distance, high-resolution cam-
eras are required. High-resolution images can capture finer details but also increase
the computational resources needed for processing.
• Field of View: Cameras mounted on tall structures often have a broader field of
view, covering larger areas. However, this also means that more objects (e.g., build-
ings, trees) may obstruct the view of the vehicles, adding to the detection complexity.
Research by Vohra et al. [8] and other studies [9] illustrate these challenges and the
potential applications of high-altitude imaging for traffic monitoring and other uses.
Advantages of High-Altitude Detection for Rural Areas
Despite the challenges, using a high-altitude camera in rural areas has distinct advan-
tages:
• Coverage: A single camera can cover a vast area, reducing the need for multiple
cameras and thereby lowering installation and maintenance costs.
• Environmental Impact: The ability to monitor vehicle presence and count can indi-
rectly assist in estimating greenhouse gas emissions. Although this thesis does not
perform the emissions calculations, the data collected can serve as a crucial input
for future environmental studies.
2.1
Small objects detection challenge
The main challenge in detecting small objects lies in the limited information available for
identifying and classifying objects. Small objects may offer only a minimal number of pix-

==PAGE:18==
els, reducing the information available. This scarcity of detail complicates the identification
of distinctive features that allow models to predict or classify correctly an object.
Objects are represented by pixels in images, each pixel containing information like color
and intensity. While larger objects offer a rich amount of details, small objects, with limited
pixel representation, often lack sufficient details, leading to scenarios where critical fea-
tures are barely discernible. The probability of the appearance of small objects is much
greater than others because their small size confuses the detector when detecting these
objects among many other objects that are nearby or of the same size or appearance.
There are two types of small object definitions now available: those based on relative
scales and those based on absolute scales. The definition based on relative scale consid-
ers the relative ratio of the object to the entire image. An object is considered small when
the width and height of the object bounding box to the width and height of the image are
less than 0.1, with the target vehicles in this thesis being approximately ten times smaller
[10]. The definition based on absolute scale considers the number of pixels occupied by
an object. One of the most common object detection datasets, the MS COCO dataset,
defines an object as small when the resolution is less than 32x32 pixels, a threshold gen-
erally accepted for datasets involving common objects [11]. In this thesis, the vehicles
being detected are approximately 25x12 pixels, categorizing them as small objects un-
der the MS COCO definition. This smaller size presents unique challenges for detection
algorithms, which are addressed in this research.
Work focused on small objects dataset creation is limited, leading to limited experience
and knowledge to engage in comprehensive research. Datasets may contain labeling
errors since, even for the human eye, it’s difficult to distinguish the key features to differ-
entiate one object from another with similar but not identical features.
Modern Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are optimized for larger bounding box
sizes [12][13][14]. This can explain the difficulties these models have in accurately rec-
ognizing smaller objects, as their limited pixel representation can diminish in significance
through the network’s layers. As a CNN gets deeper, it purposely reduces the size of the
image. This helps the network to focus on more important details and patterns rather than
every tiny pixel. By doing this, it can better understand and recognize the key features of
the input data, like shapes and objects.
However, this is beneficial when the objects are bigger since, after reducing their sizes,
there are still features or patterns that the model can learn from. When the objects are
smaller and the size gets reduced, the availability of information gets lower, effectively
causing them to vanish from the network’s detection capabilities.
Furthermore, the standard inclusion of pooling layers, designed to reduce the dimension-
ality of the data by consolidating information into a more manageable format, can also
contribute to this problem. However, this process can also cause problems by squeez-
ing important details of small objects into too few pixels, making it harder to detect and
recognize those objects accurately.
RetinaNet attempts to address some of these challenges through the use of a Feature
Pyramid Network (FPN) and a novel focal loss function to manage the class imbalance
between foreground and background objects [15]. Despite these innovations, RetinaNet
still faces difficulties when detecting very small objects due to the inherent limitations in
preserving fine details across network layers. This highlights the ongoing challenge in
optimizing CNN architectures for small object detection.

Initially, YOLO (You Only Look Once) was considered to serve as a baseline for com-
parison, with the expectation that RetinaNet would outperform it. YOLO is known for its
prediction capabilities and has been used extensively for its robustness in various detec-
tion scenarios. YOLO predicts bounding boxes and class probabilities directly from full
images in a single evaluation. However, during the research, it was found that YOLO of-
ten provided better performance compared to RetinaNet, particularly in terms of prediction
accuracy for small objects [16].
Ultimately, the EfficientNet classifier was adopted as the final solution due to its superior
performance in both accuracy and efficiency. EfficientNet scales up CNNs in a structured
manner, balancing network depth, width, and resolution. To address the challenge of
detecting small objects, EfficientNet was not used on the full image. Instead, a sliding
window technique was employed, where EfficientNet was run on cutouts from the full
image individually. This approach allowed for more precise detection of small objects by
preserving finer details throughout the network layers [17][18].

Car detection in aerial/satellite image data
Recently, low orbiting video satellites have gradually become a frontier technology in the
development of remote sensing because of their ability to quickly acquire high-resolution
video data over large areas.
In contrast to first-person images, aerial views have several shortcomings regarding as-
pects like: the objects have small dimensions, the position of an object is not fixed, the
image size might reach hundreds of megapixels and the availability of datasets is limited.
In contrast, real distances are always known, so it is easy to compute dimensions since
the observation angle is constant.

development of remote sensing because of their ability to quickly acquire high-resolution

aerial images appear much smaller compared to those in ground-based images, which
reduces the amount of detail available for detection algorithms. This limited pixel repre-

[19, 20]. Additionally, aerial images capture objects from various angles and heights,
introducing variability in perspective that can affect the consistency of the object detec-

require significant computational resources for processing [20]. Furthermore, there is a
scarcity of high-quality datasets specifically designed for vehicle detection in aerial im-
ages, limiting the range of scenarios covered [21, 20].
Despite these challenges, aerial imaging has the advantage of a constant observation
angle, allowing for easier computation of real-world dimensions from the images. This
aids in accurately determining the size and scale of detected vehicles [19].
Deep learning methods, especially convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have shown
significant promise in improving vehicle detection in aerial images. Techniques like Reti-
naNet and YOLO have been adapted to handle the specific challenges posed by aerial
imagery [20, 21]. Region-based detectors that combine region proposal networks with
CNNs have proven effective in identifying vehicles in high-resolution satellite images by
focusing on regions of interest within an image [20]. Advanced versions of YOLO, incor-
porate techniques like context-aware-based feature map fusion to handle complex scenes

Background
3.1
Neural Networks
A neural network is a method in a artificial intelligence that teaches computers to process
data in a way that is inspired by the human brain. It is a type of machine learning process,
called deep learning, that uses interconnected nodes or neurons in a layered structure
that resembles human brain. Neural networks are a means of doing machine learning, in
which a computer learns to perform some task by analyzing training examples. Usually,
the examples have been hand-labeled in advance. An object recognition system, might
be fed thousands of labeled images of cars, houses, coffee cups, etc and it would find
visual patterns in the images that consistently correlate with particular labels.
Modeled loosely on the human brain, a neural network consists of thousands or even
millions of simple processing nodes that are densely interconnected. Most of today’s
neural nets are organized into layers of nodes, and they are ’feed-forward’, meaning that
data moves in one direction through them. An individual node might be connected to
several nodes in the beneath layer from which it receives data and also connected to
several nodes in the layer above it, to which it sends data.
To each of its incoming connections, a node will assign a number known as ’weight’. When
the network is active, a node receives a different data item from each of their predecessor
nodes, which is then multiplied by the associated weight. It then combines the resulting
products together into a single number. When this number, the sum of the weighted inputs,
is higher than a threshold, the node sends it along all its ongoing connections. Otherwise,
this node will not send any data to the next layer.
When training a neural network, all of its weights and thresholds are initially set to random
values. Training data is fed to the bottom layer and it passes through the succeeding
layers where several different operations are applied to it until it reaches the output layer.
The status of the data is very modified and transformed in this process. The weights and
thresholds are continually adjusted during training until data with same labels consistently
produce similar outputs.
3.2
Addressing Small Object Detection Challenges with
RetinaNet
RetinaNet tackles two critical issues in object detection: class imbalance and scale invari-
ance, both of which are pivotal in improving the detection of small objects.
One of the challenges in detecting small objects is that they often appear less frequently
than larger objects in training datasets, leading to a significant class imbalance. This im-
balance can cause the model to prioritize the detection of more common, larger objects,
while overlooking the smaller, rarer ones. RetinaNet addresses this issue with a novel
focal loss function. The focal loss applies a modulating term to the cross-entropy loss,
which reduces the loss contribution from easy examples and increases the focus on hard,
misclassified examples. Since small objects are often harder to detect and classify, they
benefit from this increased focus, improving the model’s ability to learn from and accu-
rately detect these challenging cases.
Detecting objects of varying scales, especially small ones, requires the model to be able to
recognize features across different resolutions. RetinaNet incorporates a Feature Pyra-

By addressing class imbalance through focal loss and enhancing scale invariance with

the model’s performance in scenarios where small object detection is crucial, making
RetinaNet an effective solution for such tasks.
3.3
Introduction to YOLO and its Prediction Mechanism
YOLO (You Only Look Once) incorporates several key features that significantly enhance
its capability to detect small objects within images. One critical feature is its grid-based
prediction system, which divides the image into a grid of cells, each responsible for pre-
dicting bounding boxes and confidence scores. This dense grid structure increases the
likelihood of localizing small objects effectively within specific regions of the image. Addi-
tionally, YOLO employs multiscale training, where input image sizes are varied during the
training process. This technique allows the model to generalize across different scales,
which is particularly crucial for small object detection, as it ensures the model can recog-
nize objects at various resolutions.
Furthermore, YOLO utilizes predefined anchor boxes with diverse sizes and aspect ra-
tios, improving the initialization for bounding boxes of various shapes and sizes. This is
especially beneficial for small objects, as it enhances the accuracy of bounding box predic-
tions, ensuring even the smallest objects are detected precisely. Although YOLO’s single
forward pass is primarily designed for speed, it also preserves the context of the entire
image, which is essential for accurately detecting small objects without losing important
spatial information.
Moreover, the custom loss function in YOLO, which balances localization loss, confidence

3.4
EfficientNet Classifier
EfficientNet represents a groundbreaking series of deep neural network architectures,
setting new standards for efficiency and performance in image classification tasks. This
model family, from EfficientNet B0 to B7, exemplifies a novel approach to scaling and
optimizing neural networks.
The core principle of EfficientNet is compound scaling, a method that uniformly scales the
network’s depth, width, and resolution to maintain a harmonious balance among these
dimensions. By proportionally scaling each aspect, EfficientNet achieves remarkable ac-
curacy while minimizing parameters and computational resources (FLOPS).
EfficientNet offers a range of architectures, beginning with B0 as the baseline and extend-
ing through to B7. Each successive model in the series is progressively deeper, wider,
and has a higher resolution than the previous one, enabling incremental performance
enhancements.
The great efficiency and effectiveness of EfficientNet in image classification is mostly
thanks its fundamental building block, consisting of MBConv unit, Squeeze-and-Excitation
Optimization and ReLU6 and Swish.
The MBConv unit, derived from MobileNets, is the main building block of EfficientNet. It
combines depthwise and pointwise convolutions within an inverted residual structure. This
design enhances computational efficiency while enabling the extraction of more complex
features.
A key feature of EfficientNet is the use of the squeeze-and-excitation technique. This
method models channel dependencies to enhance the representational power of the net-
work. By learning interdependencies between channels and scaling channel-wise feature
maps, the network can emphasize relevant features and suppress less important ones.
This results in more discriminative feature learning and improved accuracy.
The employment of ReLU6, a variant of the standard ReLU function, which caps the ac-
tivation output between zero and six. This clipping mechanism is crucial in preventing
saturation issues in deep networks, thereby stabilizing the learning process. Additionally,
EfficientNet adopts the Swish activation function, a smooth, non-monotonic function that
has been shown to be more effective in deeper models.

4.1
3 classic computer vision methods
The three methods were employed as baselines for comparison against the deep learn-
ing approaches. These methods were background subtraction, frame differencing, and
optical flow. They all required tailored post-processing to achieve accurate results. Since
these are temporal-based methods, applying them without refinement could not guaran-
tee that the detected changes between frames were actually vehicles. For instance, a
tree moving in the wind or other objects could be mistaken for vehicles. The following
subsections provide a detailed description of each method.
4.1.1
Frame differencing
This method involves applying a simple difference between all the pixels from one image
to another. Ideally, on a non-windy day, since the camera is stationary and none of the
elements in the picture have moved except for the vehicles, each vehicle would cause a
detectable difference. In one frame, the vehicle would be in one position, and in the next
frame, it would be in a different position. However, in a non-ideal scenario, performing
frame differencing between two similar images results in significant differences because
even minor variations in light conditions can be detected. Therefore, post-processing
needs to be applied to the frame differences to filter out these unwanted variations.
PROCEDURE
First, a mask is applied to each image to retain only the parts of the images contain-
ing roads, ensuring that the algorithm finds moving objects specifically in the road areas
where vehicles should be. Next, a color transform converts each image to grayscale. Af-
ter that, frame differencing is performed, followed by applying a median blur filter, which
helps retain only the more significant differences corresponding to moving vehicles. Fol-
lowing this, an adaptive threshold is applied, and then several morphological operations
are performed: closing, dilating, and eroding. This sequence of steps produces a binary
mask that highlights the moving objects.
4.1.2
Optical flow
Optical flow is a computer vision technique used to detect and track the movement of
objects in a video. It works by analyzing the changes in the position of pixels across con-
secutive video frames to estimate the direction and speed of motion. Essentially, optical
flow compares two images taken at slightly different times and measures how the pixel
intensities shift from one image to the next. By calculating this shift, it determines the
velocity of every point in the image. This information can then be used for tasks such as
object tracking, motion detection, and video stabilization. Optical flow is particularly useful
because it provides detailed motion information, even for small and subtle movements.
4.1.3
Background subtraction
This method involves feeding the algorithm with a series of similar images, allowing it to
learn and identify the background from the static parts of the scene. Once the background

image is computed, subtracting it from an image containing a new object will ideally high-
light the moving object. Unlike the previous methods, this approach uses three consec-
utive images: the first two images help the algorithm learn the background, and the third
image is used for background subtraction to detect any new objects. The background
subtractor ’MOG2’[22] was utilized for this purpose. However, this method requires the

cleaner and more accurate detection of objects.

Training process
Training deep learning models is a very demanding process for most personal comput-

also makes it feasible to undertake more sophisticated and resource-intensive projects
that would be impractical with standard computing resources.
In this thesis, the High-Performance Computing (HPC) resources of the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark (DTU) were employed to train the deep learning models. The work-
flow involves submitting jobs to a queue system by creating and executing ’.sh’ script
files. These scripts define the computational tasks and resource requirements, which the
HPC scheduler then manages, ensuring efficient utilization of the available computing
resources.

Sliding window technique

size was chosen specifically to half the size of the window to avoid that a vehicle could not
be captured completely by the window. Once these parameters were carefully chosen,
by sliding the window across the polylines, cutouts from the original image were extracted
and stored in a folder.

Future work
This study represents an initial approach to vehicle detection and counting using images
captured by a CCTV camera mounted on a tall tower. The results indicate that classifiers
perform better than the predictors tested in this thesis. The discrete outcomes suggest
that either the data might not be the most appropriate, or the methodologies followed were
not the most effective.
Continuous training on new data would likely enhance the classifier’s robustness. Im-
plementing a script to filter images that are likely to contain vehicles and then manually
annotating them could be an effective strategy to obtain more training images. With this
script the camera could also capture images more frequently while discarding those with-
out vehicles, thus conserving storage space by avoiding empty images.
Another improvement could involve using higher-resolution cameras that perform better
at night and in daylight. This would make detecting and counting vehicles easier as the
objects would have more features to learn from, improving accuracy under various lighting
conditions. With more features available from a higher-resolution camera, the classifica-
tion of vehicles could also be achieved, adding another layer of analysis to the system.
Future work could also explore advanced machine learning techniques, such as semi-
supervised learning or transfer learning, to improve the model’s performance. Addition-
ally, integrating real-time processing capabilities and enhancing the system’s ability to
handle diverse environmental conditions could further refine vehicle detection and count-
ing accuracy.

7
Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis investigated vehicle detection and counting using images cap-
tured by a CCTV camera mounted on a tall tower. The aim of this project is to substitute
the current manual counting of vehicles with an automated system. Three classic com-
puter vision algorithms—background subtraction, optical flow, and frame differencing—
were initially tested, yielding high false positive rates. YOLOv8-based predictors were
then evaluated with various initializations: Kaiming initialization, pre-trained weights from
YOLOv8n fine-tuned on manually annotated images, a sliding window technique, and
temporal information from consecutive images. Among these, the sliding window tech-
nique achieved the highest performance with a 51% correct prediction rate.
A comparative analysis of EfficientNet classifier architectures revealed that EfficientNet-
b1 provided the most promising results, correctly predicting 73% of vehicles in the test
dataset while maintaining a good balance between true positives and false positives. Data
for the real-world application was collected during January (east view) and March (west
view), demonstrating that the busiest hour for vehicle detection was between 14:00 and
15:00.
Overall, this study highlights the potential of using deep learning classifiers for vehicle
detection and counting, with EfficientNet-b1 showing the most promise. These findings
can aid in accurately estimating GHG emissions from vehicle combustion engines, con-
tributing to better environmental monitoring and analysis.
