Summary
Nowadays, the trend of intelligent medical care is to hand over some routine diagno-
sis to artificial intelligence, because AI works more eﬀicient, more convenient, and
more personalized and they never get exhausted. In many medical fields, AI is more
accurate than doctors, for example, in diagnosing breast cancer from mammograms,
a study in the journal Nature suggests [1].
My project is an extension of the IoT device designed by Kalpit. The device designed
by Kalpit is able to monitor the heart beat of the patients precisely, and my duty
is creating binary classification machine learning models based on the ECG data
obtained from the equipment so that we can tell apart normal beats and the beats
with atrial fibrillation. Our expectation is to maximize both accuracy and university
of the model. Our final goal is saving time for cardiologist and comforting the people
who suffering from heart disease.

CHAPTER 1
Atrial fibrillation
Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common persistent cardiac arrhythmia in clinical
practice, with approximately 4% of people suffering from permanent or paroxysmal
atrial fibrillation. The prevalence of atrial fibrillation is increasing year by year, the
root cause is the increase in the average age of the population and the increase in the
number of patients with heart disease, especially the survival time of patients with
heart failure. While atrial fibrillation is often associated with atrial enlargement and
ventricular dysfunction of any cause, it can also occur in the absence of any struc-
tural heart disease. However, atrial fibrillation is clinically significant regardless of
the presence of heart disease, because of the increased mortality in affected patients,
and also due to the rapid and irregular heart rate, left atrial and left ventricular func-
tion following the onset of atrial fibrillation patients with atrial fibrillation are also
at increased risk of hemodynamic deterioration due to obstruction, as well as embolic
events caused by thrombus in the left atrium and left atrial appendage [2].
The frequency of atrial fibrillation in the population, the frequency of atrial fibril-
lation in people with and without heart disease, and the age-related correlation of
atrial fibrillation suggest that the etiology of atrial fibrillation is diverse, but that
atrial fibrillation may share common pathophysiological features. Atrial fibrillation
is associated with almost any underlying heart disease that causes changes in the
atrial myocardium, including cardiac dilation, inflammation, hypertrophy, ischemia,
and fibrosis. In addition, age-related normal myocardial changes, including amyloid
deposition and fibrosis, may be responsible for the increased incidence of AF in older
adults. Imbalances in input from the parasympathetic or sympathetic nervous sys-
tem also alter the electrophysiological properties of the atria and can cause atrial
fibrillation. Even in the absence of underlying atrial disease, systemic infection, pul-
monary disease and infection, pulmonary embolism, hyperthyroidism, and certain
toxins or metabolic abnormalities may contribute to the development of atrial fibril-
lation. In up to 15% of cases of atrial fibrillation, there is no structural heart disease
and no identifiable cause of the arrhythmia, which is called ‘autistic atrial fibrillation’.
Several autopsy studies have reported that the most common heart disease associated
with atrial fibrillation is coronary artery disease (CAD), with or without myocardial
infarction (MI), rheumatic heart disease, and hypertensive heart disease. However,
clinical observations highlight that left ventricular dysfunction or heart failure (HF)

1.1
Diagnosis of atrial fibrillation with ECG
There are many ways to diagnose atrial fibrillation, including blood tests, holter
monitor, event recorder, echocardiogram, stress test, chest X-ray... [3]. However the
most common and painless measurement should be electrocardiogram detection. So
how can we identify atrial fibrillation from an ECG, here are some key ECG features
of atrial fibrillation [4]:
• Irregular rhythm
• No P waves
• Absence of an isoelectric baseline
• Variable ventricular rate
• QRS complexes usually < 120ms, unless pre-existing bundle branch block, ac-
cessory pathway, or rate-related aberrant conduction
• Fibrillatory waves may be present and can be either fine (amplitude < 0.5mm)
or coarse (amplitude > 0.5mm)
• Fibrillatory waves may mimic P waves leading to misdiagnosis
Here are some atrial fibrillation examples to show for a more intuitive explanation.

1.2
ECG explanation
• P wave: Electrical activation of the normal heart begins at the sinoatrial node.
Since the sinus node is located at the junction of the right atrium and the
superior vena cava, the excitation of the sinoatrial node is first transmitted to
the right atrium and then to the left atrium through the interatrial bundle,
forming a P wave on the ECG. The P wave represents the excitation of the
atrium, the first half represents the right atrial excitation, and the second half
represents the left atrial excitation. The P wave duration is 0.12 seconds and
the height is 0.25mv. When the atrium is enlarged and the conduction between
the two chambers is abnormal, the P wave can be expressed as a high-pointed
or double-peaked P wave
• PR interval: The PR interval represents the time required for the excitation
generated by the sinoatrial node to reach the ventricle via the atrium, the atri-
oventricular junction and the atrioventricular bundle and cause the ventricular
==PAGE:16==
8
1 Atrial fibrillation
muscle to start excitation, so it is also called the atrioventricular conduction
time. The normal PR interval is 0.12 to 0.20 seconds. When the conduction
from the atrium to the ventricle is blocked, it is manifested as prolongation of
the PR interval or disappearance of the ventricular wave after the P wave.
• QRS complexe: The left and right ventricles are simultaneously excited to
form QRS complexes through the bundle of His and the left and right bun-
dle branches. The QRS complex represents the depolarization of the ventricle,
and the activation duration is less than 0.11 seconds. When there is conduc-
tion block, ventricular enlargement or hypertrophy of the left and right bundle
branches of the heart, the QRS complex is widened, deformed and the duration
is prolonged.
• ST segment: A period of time during which all depolarization of the ventricu-
lar myocardium has been completed and repolarization has not yet begun. At
this time, the ventricular myocardium at each site is in a depolarized state,
and there is no potential difference between cells. Therefore, the ST segment
should be on the equipotential line under normal circumstances. When there is
ischemia or necrosis in a certain part of the myocardium, there is still a poten-
tial difference in the ventricle after depolarization, which is manifested as ST
segment deviation on the ECG.
• T wave: The subsequent T wave represents the repolarization of the ventricle.
The T wave should be in the same direction as the QRS main in leads with
an upward main QRS complex. Changes in T waves on the ECG are affected
by many factors. For example, myocardial ischemia can be manifested as a low
level inversion of the T wave. The towering T wave can be seen in hyperkalemia,
hyperacute phase of acute myocardial infarction and so on.

2.1
Convolutional neural network
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a feed-forward neural network whose arti-
ficial neurons can respond to surrounding units within a certain coverage area, has
excellent performance for large-scale image processing [8]. Convolutional neural net-
work is constructed by imitating the visual perception mechanism of biology, and can
perform supervised learning and unsupervised learning. Small computational effort
to learn grid-like topology features such as pixels and audio, with stable effects and
no additional feature engineering requirements on the data [9].
There are several drawbacks about traditional fully connected neural networks,
and CNN is designed to deal with them:
• Unwrapping an image into a vector loses spatial information
• Too many parameters are ineﬀicient and diﬀicult to train
• A large number of parameters can also quickly lead to network over-fitting
Here is a simple CNN model as an example, which are designed to distinguish hand-
written numbers. This example also shows the general process of establishing a deep
learning model. The data set MNIST [10] is preloaded in the Keras library. The first

Here is an example in the working principle of CNN: Sliding the filters on the input
feature, stop at every possible locations and perform dot product for the filter and the
input at that location. This performance can be shown in the Figure 2.5, the symbol
‘*’ represents convolution operation. The part enclosed by the green box performs

is also in green box. This is the so-called convolution operation. In every possible
location of the 6×6 input matrix, the dot product is carried out with the filter, finally

After the convolution layers, there are max pooling layers in the example model.
Max pooling is a pooling operation that selects the largest element from the area of
the feature map covered by the filter. Therefore, the output after the max pooling
layer will be a feature map containing the most salient features of the previous feature
map. The necessity of the pooling layer lies in these two points:

• The pooling layer summarizes the features present in the region of the feature

K-nearest neighbors
K-nearest neighbor algorithm, that is, given a training data set, for a new input
sample, find the K samples that are closest to the sample in the training data set,
and most of these K samples belong to a certain class. The sample will be classified
into that class.
Figure 2.9. Example of KNN classification [14]
As the Figure 2.9 shows, there are two different types of sample data, which are rep-
resented by blue squares and red triangles, and the data marked by the green circle
in the middle of the figure is the data to be classified. The KNN algorithm will be
used to classify this green dot:
• If K = 3, the nearest 3 points of the green dot are 2 red triangles and 1 blue
square, and the minority belongs to the majority. Based on statistical methods,
it is determined that the green dot to be classified as the same type of red
triangles.
• If K = 5, the nearest 5 points of the green dot are 2 red triangles and 3 blue
square, and the minority belongs to the majority. Based on statistical methods,
it is determined that the green dot to be classified as the same type of blue
square.
It’s very important to select a proper K value. If the K is too small, then it means that
our overall model will become complex and prone to overfitting because the model
would learn much noise and outliers. As Figure 2.10 shows, the pentagon should
belongs to the blue squares. When K = 1, which is too small, the pentagon will be
classified into black dots. If the K is too large, it is equivalent to using training data
in a larger neighborhood to make predictions. At this time, training samples that
are far away from the input sample will also play a role in the prediction, making the
prediction wrong. The increase of the K value means that the overall model becomes

Random Forest
To understand random forests, we first need to understand what a decision tree is.
A decision tree is a tree structure. A decision tree is constructed by dividing the
data into subsets with similar values to build a complete tree. Each non-leaf node
on the decision tree is a decision node. After each decision node, multiple branches
will be generated, and each branch is an output subset of a certain value range. Each
leaf node on the decision tree is continuous or discrete data expressing the output
result [16].
Random forest is a more advanced algorithm based on decision trees. Like decision
trees, random forests can be used for both regression and classification. As can be
seen from the name, a random forest is a forest constructed in a random way, and
this forest is composed of many unrelated decision trees. Real-time random forests
are essentially a very important branch of machine learning called ensemble learning.
Ensemble learning solves a single prediction problem by building a combination of
several models. It works by generating multiple classifiers or models that each learn
==PAGE:25==
2.3 Random Forest
17
Figure 2.11. An example for decision trees [17]
and make predictions independently.
These predictions are finally combined into
a single prediction, thus outperforming any single-class prediction. The process of
random forest:
1. Randomly select k features (columns) from the dataset, with a total of K fea-
tures (where k is less than or equal to K). Then build a decision tree based on
these k features.
2. Repeated n times, these n features build n decision trees based on randomly-
selected m samples (totally M samples, m is less than or equal to M, this is
so-called bagging).
3. Store all predicted results (targets), you can get n kinds of results from n
decision trees.
4. The predicted target with a highest number of votes is used as the final predic-
tion of the random forest algorithm.
Advantages of random forest:
• Can be used to solve both classification and regression problems.
Random
forests can handle both categorical and numerical features.
• Anti-overfitting ability.
Reduce the risk of overfitting by averaging decision
trees.

3.1.1
Overview of the training data
The data set used in this project is from Kaggle ECG Heartbeat Categorization
Dataset [19].
The data source of this data set is the MIT-BIH arrhythmia data
set [13]. The ECG Heartbeat Categorization Dataset is big enough to train a neural
network. The signals correspond to electrocardiogram (ECG) shapes of heartbeats
for the normal case and the cases affected by different arrhythmia and myocardial
infarction. These signals are pre-processed and segmented, with each segment corre-
sponding to a heartbeat and last for about one second [19]. Some basic facts about
the data set:
• Number of Samples: 87554
• Sampling Frequency: 125Hz
• Number of Categories: 5
• Missing values: 0
• Classes: [‘N’: 0, ‘S’: 1, ‘V’: 2, ‘F’: 3, ‘Q’: 4]
– ‘N’: Non-ecotic beats (normal beat)
– ‘S’: Supraventricular ectopic beats
– ‘V’: Ventricular ectopic beats
– ‘F’: Fusion Beats
– ‘Q’: Unknown Beats

Data balancing
In machine learning, we often encounter the problem of unbalanced distribution of
class data, that is, there is a lot of data in one class, while the amount of data in
other classes is very small, just as the Figure 3.7 shows. In this case, the prediction
models developed using traditional machine learning algorithms may be biased and
inaccurate. The reason for the above is that traditional algorithm models improve
accuracy by reducing loss, and they do not take into account the uneven classification
of categories. . Therefore, machine learning algorithms often produce sub-optimal
classifiers when faced with imbalanced data-sets.
Standard classifier algorithms such as decision trees and logistic regression tend to
predict only majority class data. Characteristics of minority groups are seen as noise
and are often ignored. Therefore, the minority class has a high probability of being
mis-classified compared to the majority class. [20] For example, if we want to filter
1000 emails, 990 of them are useful, and 10 are advertising emails, which need to
be filtered. This is a classifier we trained with an accuracy of 99.0. It looks very
accurate, but it is useless. This classifier may consider all 1000 letters to be useful,
and in this case, it does not play a role in filtering.
In our situation, I perform the random under-sampling for the 2 classes in our training
data set, which is randomly deleting events from the majority class to make sure the
2 classes in the training data set are balanced.

Activation Function
The activation function is to introduce nonlinear factors into the neural network.
Through the activation function, the neural network can fit various curves. The acti-
vation function is necessary because the input and output of each layer in the neural
network is a linear summation process, and the output of the next layer is just a
linear transformation of the input function of the previous layer, so if there is no
activation function, then no matter how complex the neural network and how many
layers you construct is, the final output is a linear combination of the input, pure
linear combination cannot solve more complex problems.
In the CNN layers, Relu is used as activation function:
Relu(x) = max(0, x)
(3.1)

Figure 3.9. Relu
Relu is the most generic activation function and used in almost every convolutional
neural networks because of these two reasons:
• Relu is easy to calculate, so it has a lower computational cost.
• Relu has a derivative of either 0 or 1 depending on the value is positive or nega-
tive, which can prevent ‘vanishing gradient’ problems. The ‘vanishing gradient’
refers to the tendency for the gradient is approaching to 0 when the input is
really high or really low.
