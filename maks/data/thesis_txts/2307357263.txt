Abstract
The objective of this thesis is to study the local buckling behavior under uni-axial com-
pressive loads for steel members classiﬁed as class 4 cross-sections by EN1993-1-1. The
research is carried out by performing a parametric investigation in FEM software Abaqus
where the primary focus is upon the accuracy and validity of semi-empirical analytical
methods recommended by European standards: EN1993-1-3 and EN1993-1-5, methods
which are used to estimate the capacity of such elements.
The structural elements analyzed here are threated as internal parts/sub-panels that may
be comprised in a proﬁle (for example the web of an I proﬁle or the ﬂange of trapezoidal
sheeting). The buckling response of internal elements subjected to uni-axial compression
is investigated for two types of cross-section. Firstly, a simply supported rectangular plate
with two diﬀerent strain condition at the edges and secondly an internal element with one
intermediate stiﬀener.
A close look will be taken at the elastic buckling response by performing a Linear Buckling
Analysis in order to determine how the aspect ratio, loading type and also the cross-section
inﬂuence the critical buckling stress, mode, and length.
Lastly, a considerable section is reserved to the non-linear response of imperfect elements
under compressive loads. The domain where geometric imperfections hold great impact on
the plate post-buckling capacity has been studied for a wide range of magnitudes.Further,
the assumptions used by the eﬀective cross-section method were discussed and backed to
some degree by the results from Finite Element simulations. The results from numerical
simulations are also used to quantify the post-buckling capacity of class 4 elements by
establishing buckling curves for both plate and stiﬀener type element based upon non-
dimensional factors.
Keywords:
Post-buckling, intermediate stiﬀener, distorsional buckling, local buckling, imperfections,
cold formed, eﬀective width method

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, structural engineers are confronted with the task to design slender but eﬃcient
load bearing steel structures. Due to the cost and weight of steel material, there is a high
interest for both manufactures and designers, to form the cross-section of steel proﬁles
based upon the optimal stress distribution. Thus, steel members are usually slender and
therefore sensible to buckling phenomenon.
Buckling behavior develop under compressive load and is classiﬁed in two main categories:
global buckling and local buckling. While global buckling inﬂuence the capacity of whole
member directly, local buckling aﬀects the individual parts of a proﬁle.
The focus of this thesis is upon the local and distorsional buckling response of ﬂat plates
respectively plate type elements with intermediate stiﬀener. The local buckling capacity
is governed largely by the width-to-thickness ratio of the plate and the support condi-
tion. Local or plate buckling is associated with post-buckling behavior and due to its
non-linear response, the real capacity is rather complicated to estimate. In consequence,
semi-empirical analytical methods were developed to estimate their capacity. One of them
is the eﬀective cross-section which is described in the European standard for steel struc-
tures: EC3: Design of steel structures - Part 1-3: Supplementary rules for cold-formed
members and sheeting and in Part 1-5: Plated structural elements.
1.1
Purpose
The eﬀective cross-section method, uses reduced (eﬀective) areas for the panels compris-
ing an element where the full strength of the material is applied to calculate its capacity.
The magnitude with which the cross-section area reduces, is determined by the reduction
factors quantiﬁed in buckling curves.
The main aim in the present paper is to investigate and analyze the accuracy of the
analytical methods prescribed by the above mentioned codes by using Finite Element
approach.

2.1.1
Elastic plate buckling
The elastic plate buckling theory is based on the same concept as used for column buck-
ling, developed by Euler in 1744. This implies that the plate will behave elastically and
that the plate is considered to be perfectly ﬂat prior to its loading (without any imper-
fections). P. Bulsen deﬁnes the critical load as the edge in-plane load at which instability
occurs due to the fact that the energy of the plate in a ﬂat position equals the energy of
the plate in a bend position. This point is deﬁned as the ”bifurcation point” and is the
point where a state of neutral equilibrium exist [2].
It should be mention that the critical buckling load deﬁned in this section is in fact a
linear elastic buckling load. The linear buckling load is determined by assuming small
deﬂections, this means that it does not take into consideration post inelastic buckling
which involves membrane actions.

chosen in such a way that it would ﬁt the boundary conditions and loading scheme used
in FE - modeling at a later stage. Hereafter the equilibrium of a rectangular plate that is

2.1.2
Post-buckling plate behavior
Compared to columns, plate elements develop higher buckling capacity than the value
prescribed by the critical buckling stress in Equation (2.6). In order to illustrate better
the post-buckling behavior, the buckling shape of the simply supported rectangular plate
is plotted. Let us now introduce a grid system, where each grid can be assumed to be a
longitudinal, respectively a transverse strut. If the longitudinal struts are considered as
individual columns subjected to end loads, they would simply collapse when the critical
buckling load is reached. However, in the grid model the collapse of longitudinal strips
are prevented by the transverse strips which are anchored to the edge.
In the same manner as the grid model, the rectangular plate would buckle at the critical
buckling load (σcr), but it would still be able to carry more load as it deﬂects, due
to membrane actions in the perpendicular direction to loading. The plate post-buckling
behavior is in contrast with the column buckling which experience low or no post-buckling
capacity and it collapses once the critical load is reached [11].
Figure 2.3: Buckling shape of the rectangular plate and grid the model
When the compressive stress σx equals σcr the plate buckles and it enters in a post-
buckling stage. If until this point the compressive stresses could be considered uniform
along the width of the plate, from this point on, the stress distribution becomes non-
uniform. As the plate deﬂects out-of-plane, the central part of the plate is carrying less
and less stress than the outer part. This is due to the fact that longitudinal edges can not
deform out-of-plane, as a result the edge strips can carry more stresses and help support
the central part of the plate through membrane forces.

Internal elements with one intermediate stiﬀener
In this section the analytical model used by the most design codes to determine the ca-
pacity of an internal panel stiﬀened with one longitudinal stiﬀener will be presented. The
method is based upon the eﬀective width approach described in the previous section.
Meaning that the main assumption used, is that: strain distribution throughout the cross
section is linear. Thus, the bearing capacity is approximated by determining an eﬀective
cross section.
By providing the panel with intermediate stiﬀeners the capacity of the element can be
substantially improved. In the case where a very rigid stiﬀener is assumed (meaning that
it does not buckle at all), the stiﬀener can be considered as a simply support for the two
adjacent plates comprising the panel. However, this behavior is rarely the case due to the
fact that is hard to form a fully eﬀective stiﬀener on a slender panel. As a result, most of
the stiﬀeners tend to buckle in a distorsional mode [10].
The method, deals with this behavior by considering a reduced compressive stress in the
stiﬀener. The factor with which the stress is reduced depends on the critical buckling load
of the stiﬀener. An advantage of this calculation procedure, is that the critical buckling
load can be determined in a simply manner .

Chapter 3
FEM set-up
This chapter will provide insights on how diﬀerent models were set-up in ﬁnite element
modeling software Abaqus 6.14. Drawings and detail information about each of the steps
used to deﬁne the model‘s geometry, material, boundary condition, constraints, applied
displacement and others.
It is desired in this paper to do a parametric study meaning that a large number of
parameters will be iterated (eg. initial geometric imperfection, slenderness ratio, diﬀerent
models). Due to this fact, the models and the analysis are created and carried out in
Abaqus by running Python scripts. By scripting the models, diﬀerent parameters can be
iterated if a vector with the desired values for that parameter is deﬁned, vector which is
run in a loop by Python‘s code. After the models and jobs are completed, yet another
python script is run in order to access the database of the analysis, from which the desired
results are saved to a text ﬁle.

3.1 Description of the models
FEM set-up
Each numerical simulation consists of two sub-models from which two analysis are per-
formed. One model, used for running a Linear Buckling Analysis (LBA) and another one
for performing a Geometrical and Materially Non-linear Imperfect Analysis (GMNIA).
Linear Buckling Analysis is performed in order to determine the most critical buckling
modes. This analysis will provide the most gravely shape for imperfections in the element.
The post-buckling behavior of the imperfect element is determined by performing the sec-
ond analysis, Geometrical and Materially Non-linear Imperfect Analysis. The shape of
lowest local and distorsional buckling mode (in the case of elements with stiﬀeners) is
imposed on the element in order to account for initial imperfections. The two analysis
are further explained in section (3.4) and (3.4).
The ﬂow chart in ﬁgure 3.1, illustrates schematically the order in which the models are
deﬁned and executed by the Python script in Abaqus. Firstly, the LBA model is created
and diﬀerent components are deﬁned. At component ”Steps” the model is copy together
with the initial condition and renamed as GMNIA model. At this stage the two models
have exactly the same features. Subsequently, in each model a second step is created, a
Linear Perturbation step for the ﬁrst model respectively a Static Riks step for the second
one were the load are applied in the form of displacements. The type of second step will
deﬁne in turn the analysis preformed. Before creating the jobs, the keywords in both of
the models are edited to account for several thing, as it will be explained later on in this
section.
3.1
Description of the models
In this section the two types of elements analyzed in this thesis will be presented. The
ﬁrst model is a simply supported rectangular plate, so called Plate model, whereas the
second model comprises of an internal panel with one intermediate stiﬀener. It shall be
noted that the two models are diﬀerent, not only in terms of geometry but also on how
the load and boundary conditions are applied to the model, as it will be illustrated later.
All of the models presented in this paper are modeled by using 3D conventional shell
elements. These elements can be used for thick or thin shells and can use full or reduced
numerical integration to form the stiﬀness matrix. Shell elements are characterized by
the number of nodes per element and the integration type (S3, S4, S4R, S8R) [7]. In
order to chose the proper element type needed for our analysis, several simulations were
performed. Based on reasons of accuracy and time cost the element type was chosen (see
section 3.3).

1. Plate with unstiﬀened edges
In practice, this type of model corresponds either to a simple stand alone plate or

can be considered to have unstiﬀened edges, only if the stiﬀness provided by the

2. Plate with stiﬀened edges
Opposite to the ﬁrst case, the plate with stiﬀened edges corresponds to a panel
which is part of a proﬁle with very stiﬀﬂanges.
It shall be noted that the models analyzed here consist of simple plates (not proﬁles). In
order to assume that the plate model will behave the same as if part of a proﬁle with
stiﬀﬂanges, the following condition had to be imposed: the deformations in longitudinal
direction along the edge must be linear, or in other words the strains must be constant
along the edge. This state of constant strain along the edge is satisﬁed, if during uni-axial
loading the longitudinal edge deformations are forced to behave linearly.
Even-though both models may be considered as part of a proﬁle, the reader should be
aware that modeling individual parts of a proﬁle separately implies that 3 assumptions
had to be made (Note: which may not always hold true in reality):
- The ﬂanges act as a support and they can maintain the longitudinal edges of the
plate straight
- Rotational stiﬀness that may be provided by the ﬂanges is not considered
- The interaction between ﬂange and web buckling is omitted

observed that by doing so, failure by local crushing at the loading edges could be avoided.

1. Uniform compression load

P is determined by taking the sum of node reactions. The magnitude of the displacement
is chosen in such a way that for a General Static analysis the material would reach its
yield strength everywhere (from this the so called ”yield displacement” term was chosen).
It should be mentioned that in this case the same magnitude of displacement will be
applied to both Linear Perturbation and Static Riks step.

In this section only element type S4, S4R and S8R is investigated. Element S4 has 4 nodes
doubly curved with ﬁnite membrane strains where full integration is used to determine

A GMNIA analysis is run for each of the three element type in order to determine and

plates. S8R on the other hand prove to be very computation costly, without much gain
in accuracy.

3.4
Linear Buckling Analysis
In this section the purpose and how the Linear Buckling Analysis (LBA) is implemented
in Abaqus is discussed.
As seen in the ﬂow chart, at ﬁrst a model is set up for the
LBA, where a ”Buckle” step is created and by using a Linear Perturbation procedure the
eigenvalue problem is solved. The Linear Perturbation step uses by deﬁnition a linear
procedure to determine the response, meaning that only the elastic part of the material
behavior will be used. In section 6.2.3. of the Abaqus manual it is recommended to use
Subspace iteration method, when a low number of eigenmodes (less than 20) is required,
as it is faster than the Lanczos method. In general, the number of eigenvalues requested
is determined such that the ﬁrst distortional and local buckling modes are comprised in
this number.
The mentioned analysis is used in general for two things: to estimate the critical (bifurca-
tion) buckling load through eigenvalues and/or for the investigation of imperfect elements
by superimposing the eigenmodes. The purpose of this paper is to investigate the response
of class 4 elements, therefore the Linear Buckling Analysis will be used as an initial analysis
from which the critical buckling mode will serve as the imperfect shape of the element for
GMNIA. Hereafter, the eigenvalues are used throughout this thesis either to calculate the
34

critical buckling stress or to determine which eigenmode is the critical one. Displacement
controlled analysis is used and as mentioned in section (3.1.1.2) the magnitude of the ap-
plied reference displacements are chosen proportionally to the element‘s yielding strength.
The LBA provides a good indication upon the possible failure shape of the element through
it‘s eigenmodes, but it does not provide any indication upon the real deﬂections (only the
normalized deﬂections are saved by this analysis). It should be mentioned, that not always
the ﬁrst eigenvalue/eigenmode is the critical one, this is the case whenever the applied
load has a stress ratio less than -1 (ψ < −1). This is due to the fact that even-though
the force was applied correct, the program interprets that the lowest force that makes the
element buckle should act in the opposite direction, ending up with negative eigenvalue
for the ﬁrst mode. For this reason, the critical buckling mode is determined by the lowest
positive eigenvalue.
In reality the elements can not be manufactured perfectly straight, as a result they are
imperfect. The shape of these imperfections may vary from element to element and it can
not be apply as a general rule to all. Therefore it is conservative to assume the shape of
the imperfect element as the shape of the critical buckling mode. For that, the coordi-
nates of each node from the buckling modes are saved to a ﬁle by editing the keywords.

3.5
GMNIA
A Geometrical and Material Non-linearity Imperfection Analysis is performed in order
to determined the unstable post-buckling response. As the name suggest in this analysis
the geometrical and material non-linearities are accounted for, boundary conditions and
material plastic behavior are considered.
This analysis is implemented by deﬁning a Static Riks step in GMNIA model. In Abaqus
Static Riks analysis, the magnitude of the load is unknown, the load (in our case displace-
ments) is being applied incrementally and static equilibrium load-displacement paths are
solved by using Newton‘s algorithm to solve nonlinear equations (for more details consult
Section 6.2.4. in Abaqus manual). In order to quantify the progress of the solution Riks
method uses arc lengths along the equilibrium paths. As mentioned, load-displacements
curves are determined from which the maximum capacity of the element can be deter-
mined. Furthermore this analysis is also used to investigate the stress distribution along
several paths in the element at diﬀerent load frames.
The type of incrementation in Static Riks step can be set either to automatic or ﬁxed by

the user. Fixed type uses a ﬁxed arc length increment throughout the simulation which
may pose problems of not ﬁnding equilibrium if the increment size is large or it may
provide inaccurate results for the position of maximum capacity. As a result, an auto-
matic type incrementation is used instead, where the arc length increment is controlled
by the program. However, a set of parameters can also be deﬁned by the user to control
the incrementation process, parameters like the number of increments, the interval and
the initial arc length. The interval is deﬁned by inputing a maximum and a minimum
desired arc length from which the program will chose the maximum value which provides
equilibrium. It should be noted that the maximum arc length increment deﬁnes how
smooth the load-displacement curve is (smaller the value smoother the curve is, which in
turn provides better accuracy on ﬁnding the position of maximum capacity). The number
of increments is also of great importance on ﬁnding the maximum capacity, for that an
appropriate number is found by trial were it has been seen that the load-displacement
curve had at least one peak for the plate model and two peaks for elements with stiﬀeners.
The step can be ended prematurely (before the prescribed number of increments is
reached) by specifying a value, either for load proportionality factor or a maximum dis-
placement at a certain node. After performing a couple of analysis for diﬀerent slenderness
and analyzing the maximum out-of-plane displacement, the incrementation‘s stop param-
eter was chosen to be the displacement at node with the maximum deformation.

The eﬀective with method uses the assumption that plane section remain plane. When a
plate buckles it exhibit high strains at the edges and low strain in the central part of the

true, two strips (here called eﬀective widths) near the edges of the plate are considered
where the strains are constant and equal to the edge strain. The goal here is to determine
the width of these two strips based on the edge strain.

From the analysis several conclusions can be highlighted. Firstly, it was observed that
the magnitude of imperfection have a great importance not only on the ultimate capacity
of the plate but also on the magnitude of the edge strains at which the ultimate load
occur. In other words, plates with higher imperfections develop their ultimate capacity
at much greater deﬂections, whereas plates with small imperfections develop them close
to the point of initial yielding. This is in important factor which should be consider if
one has to verify an element with large imperfections at serviceability limit state.
In general, plates with large amplitudes of imperfection tend to increase their capacity
(eﬀective width), once the material yield strength is reached at the edge.
Furthermore, plates with very small imperfections can be considered almost as fully ef-
fective for small values of edge stress, but as σe increases the eﬀective width experience a
sudden drop. Not the same thing can be said about plates with big imperfections where
the eﬀective section is relatively constant throughout loading, a plateau can be observed
and no matter the magnitude of the edge stress the eﬀective width will nearly stay the
same.
Yet another interesting fact to observe, is the magnitude of the post-buckling capacity
compared to the elastic buckling plate capacity. A slender plate (b/t=200), exhibit a
post-buckling capacity of 5.5 times higher then its elastic buckling capacity, whereas a
medium plate 1.5 times. On the other hand, a stocky plate (b/t=38.25) will reach its
post-buckling capacity ﬁrst, meaning that the critical buckling load will never be attain.
It is generally thought that for plates with high relative slenderness (¯λp > 3) the mag-
nitude of imperfection do not inﬂuence the post-buckling response.

5.1
Introduction
In this section the buckling behavior of internal elements with one intermediate stiﬀener
will be investigated and analyzed based upon the results from FEM simulations. In order
to observe the interaction between local and distorsional buckling modes, a Linear Buck-
ling Analysis is performed at ﬁrst, where the elastic buckling stress is determined as a
function of member length.
At ﬁrst the critical buckling length is approximated using the approach described by the
analytical model (see equation 2.19). The length of the model is than determined in such
a way that one full wave should ﬁt the length of the member (a = 2xLs). Subsequently, the
LBA analysis is performed and the lowest local and distorsional buckling modes are saved
to be used in GMNIA, where the eigenmodes are imposed as the imperfect shape scaled
with the equivalent geometrical imperfection. From Static Riks analysis, the membrane
stress distribution is extracted at certain paths and compared with the assumptions used
by the analytical model presented in section (2.2).
The models investigated here, were chosen based upon their relative plate and distorsional
slenderness. For that matter, the task was to deﬁne the models in such a way that they
would ﬁll out as much as possible of the ¯λp - ¯λd space. The main stiﬀeners investigated in
this thesis are ”U” and ”V” shape, due to the fact that these types are the most used in
cold formed members, based upon which the analytical model was made. Nonetheless, ”I”
type stiﬀeners were also investigated shortly, as they present some modeling advantages
compared to ”V” or ”U” shaped.
The variable of interest from each GMNIA simulation is the ultimate load (Pu) that the
model can withstand. Load from which the reduction factor for distorsional (ﬂexural)
buckling of the intermediate stiﬀener will be determined and scattered in a 3D space.
The space is deﬁned by the reduction factor, relative slenderness of the plate and relative
slenderness of the stiﬀener. The results are then compared with the distorsional buckling
curve prescribed by EN1993-1-3 (see eq.2.20) .

5.2
LBA - Local and distorsional buckling
The eﬀects of both local and distorsional buckling has to be taken into account when the
capacity of slender members is of interest. As previously stated, the buckling capacity
of an element is dependent on the critical buckling length, either of the plate (lb) or of
the stiﬀener (Ls). In order to observe how the length of a member inﬂuence the critical
buckling stress in terms of local and distorsional buckling, 2 models with diﬀerent cross-
section are chosen to be given as an example.
It shall be mention that the relative slenderness is the factor which determines whether
local or distorsional buckling mode is the leading mode. This means, that if for example
¯λd is greater then ¯λp, then the leading mode is the distorsional one, as it yields the lowest
buckling stress. It should be noted that ¯λp and ¯λd are calculated based upon the analyt-
ical model, therefore in reality the two slenderness factors can be slightly diﬀerent. This
is due to the fact that the analytical model does not account to what degree are the two
adjacent plates clamped into the stiﬀener, for the calculation of ¯λp (see table 5.1).

Chapter 6
Conclusion and further research
The buckling behavior of rectangular simply supported plates and internal elements with
one intermediate stiﬀener has been investigated by performing a parametric study using
Finite Element approach. The results were quantiﬁed by using non-dimensional param-
eters to determine the buckling curves. Additionally, the eﬀective cross-section method
prescribed by the European standard for steel was reviewed and the veracity was analyzed
by analogy with the results from numerical simulations.
In the case of simply supported rectangular plate, it was found out that for the whole
domain of stress ratio (1 < ψ < −1) and plate slenderness (0.673 < ¯λp < 4.5), the reduction
factors prescribed by EN1993-1-5 yield conservative yet accurate results when the eﬀective
widths are determined at ultimate capacity (Pu).
However, for cases where the magnitude of eﬀective widths at lower loads is of interest
(when σcom occurs), larger deviations between the numerical and analytical determined
curves are observed. Un-safe results for the design are seen at values where σcom/fy < 0.3,
but the interval can be even greater in some cases. The EC3 approach to determine the
reduction factor for σcom by using ¯λp.red, prove to be a simple way that yield roughly
good results. Nonetheless, from the structural point of view the method is to some degree
ambiguous due to the fact that it relies on the assumption that plate slenderness changes
under loading. Further, it was observed that the magnitude of imperfection has great
inﬂuence on eﬀective widths even for very slender plates.
Further research could be carried out by re-doing the FE simulation, where both me-
chanical and geometrical imperfection (as 80% of the tolerances) could be used to obtain
more accurate results of the eﬀective width when σcom occurs. Yet other areas include:
the degree in which the inﬂuence of the rotational stiﬀness provided by the ﬂange in-
crease the eﬀective width of the web, as well as, the interaction between the two parts. It
is assumed that the ﬂanges provide a simply support to the web and that they are able
to keep the longitudinal edges straight, however this condition may not hold true in reality.
In the second part, the analytical method described by EN1993-1-3, is used to determine
an eﬀective cross-section from which the desired load-bearing capacity of a member is
calculated. The ultimate compressive capacity of internal elements with one intermediate

Conclusion and further research
”U” and ”I” shaped stiﬀener is determined using the analytical and also from FE simula-
tions. It was noted that, overall the analytical method provides good approximations, yet
there are areas where the method yield either untrustworthy or too conservative results.
A distorsional buckling curve based upon the interaction of column and plate buckling
curve could improve the results and reproduce the behavior of a larger spectrum of stiﬀ-
eners.
An initial study has been carried out also on ”I” type stiﬀeners were some general con-
clusions have been drawn: much easier to model (due to the applied constrains), they
are symmetric and therefore buckle in perfectly symmetric waves, can occupy more of
the ¯λp-¯λd space, the column-beam method provide high accuracy in estimating the crit-
ical buckling stress for this type of stiﬀeners (almost identical with the results from LBA).
Despite the advantages mentioned above, ”I” shaped stiﬀeners possess some downfalls in
terms of quantifying the results accurate. In some cases it was observed that the stiﬀener
provides high rotational stiﬀness and therefore the assumption that the two adjacent
plates are simply supported by the stiﬀener is wrong and may alter the results of ﬁnding
a correct distorsional buckling curve. Furthermore, as it is seen in the distorsional buckling
curves in Appendix (B.2) elements with exactly the same ¯λp and ¯λd may develop diﬀerent
distorsional behavior. This is due to the fact that the stiﬀener does not fail by distorsional
buckling rather by torsional buckling. This phenomenon is depended exclusively by the
torsional stiﬀness of the stiﬀener and is not cover by the analytical method.
Further
research could be carried out on this type of stiﬀener in order to improve the analytical
method.
Above that, a more eﬃcient design could be obtain because the failure by
torsional buckling of the stiﬀener seem to provide greater overall capacity as it provides
larger eﬀective widths.
