Abstract
In this thesis we study two magnetic layered materials CrCl2(pyz)2, Na2Co2TeO6 and focus on
their magnetic excitation properties. For CrCl2(pyz)2, the inelastic neutron scattering experi-
ments were conducted by Dr Helen C Walker at MERLIN@ISIS and LET@ISIS where the sample
is deuterated to get rid of the incoherent scattering from hydrogens. For Na2Co2TeO6 these
were done by Dr Jonathan Taylor and Dr Ursula Bengaard at MARI@ISIS. After analysing the
INS spectra, we are able to identify their magnetic excitations and with the help of spinw which
is a MATLAB package utilising linear spin wave theory, we can construct the spin Hamiltonians
for them. By ﬁtting the experimental spectra, we are able to extract the best-ﬁtting exchange
couplings.
For CrCl2(pyz)2, we ﬁnd a J0–J1–Jlayer Heisenberg Hamiltonian indeed can reproduce the exci-
tations at the low (Q, E) range, where J0 and J1 are the NN, NNN interactions respectively and
Jlayer is the interlayer coupling. But it fails to give the right Curie-Weiss temperature where the
calculated result is 10.46 K whereas the measured one is 140.6 K.
For Na2Co2TeO6, we found that the minimal model is a J1–J2–J3–K Heisenberg-Kitaev Hamil-
tonian and there are two sets of exchange couplings that can give rise to the experimental INS
spectrum. One with AFM type J1 and the other is FM type. Although we can not distinguish
these two from the powder spectrum level but our results indicate the potential of Na2Co2TeO6
to be the ﬁrst d7 Kitaev material.

1.5
From another perspective, Kitaev honeycomb model can be regarded as a frus-
trated system which arises from the direction-dependent exchange couplings in-
stead of the common geometry frustration. In a honeycomb lattice, there are
three neighbours for each spin and the NN couplings tend to polarise the spin
either along x, y or z directions dependent on the bond type. Therefore, the di-
rection of each spin can not be determined on a classical level and frustration
arises. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

Chapter 1
Background
The following sections will provide relevant concepts for the study of magnetism in transition
metal compounds, the corresponding microscopic Hamiltonian and a basic theory of neutron
scattering.
1.1
Elements of Magnetism in Transition Metals
Transition metal (TM) compounds present a unique class of solids, where there are extremely
rich physics. Among them materials can range from good metals to large gap insulators, or have
metal-insulator transitions. They also serve as an important platform for the study of mag-
netism. Given different TM ions and chemical environment within a lattice dispersive mag-
netic properties can occur. In the following section, we will introduce the basic building blocks
governing their magnetic properties.
1.1.1
Mott and charge transfer insulator
From the standard condensed matter theory [7], we know the classiﬁcation of a solid as a metal
or an insulator depends on whether the Fermi level lies inside or outside of the electronic band
gap, which is solely determined by the number of electrons within the system. However, when
we take the onsite Coulomb repulsion U and the charge transfer gap ¢ into account, the previ-
ous physical picture changes dramatically [8]. Since for most TM compounds, other ions and
orbitals are involved besides the TM ions and their d orbitals, for example TM oxides where
oxygens connect neighbouring TM ions and their p orbitals also contribute to the formation of
electronic bands.
In Figure 1.1 we show such Metal-Oxygen-Metal (M-O-M) structure to illustrate the basic phys-
ical principle. Here we consider a simple case where one electron on the d orbital of TM ion
and two electrons on the p orbital of oxygen since in most TM oxides the oxidation state of oxy-
gen is -2. The electron on the left d orbital can jump onto the right d orbital by the following
two hopping processes governed by the kinetic hopping t: (1) one electron transfers from the p
orbital to the right d orbital, (2) the left electron hops from the d orbital to the oxygen p orbital.
Moreover, besides t there are two additional energy scales involved, one is the onsite Coulomb
repulsion of d orbitals U and the other is the energy difference between the p orbital and the
d orbital, which we call the charge transfer gap ¢. If t ¿ U and ¢, electrons can move freely

1.1.2
Hund’s rules
Typically there are more than one electrons in atoms and ions, hence it is an important task
to classify the resulting ground state of a many-electron system by determining the ﬁlling of
corresponding energy levels. Without going into details, we formulate here only the most im-
portant conclusions for isolated atoms and ions, serving as the starting point when discussing
what modiﬁcations would appear for ions in solids in next section.
• Rule I: The electrons ﬁll the levels so as to make the largest possible total spins S.
• Rule II: Among different possible conﬁgurations with maximal S, the ground state is the
state with maximal possible total orbital angular momentum L.
• Rule III: Due to the spin orbital coupling, ~L and ~S will mix and produce a total angular
momentum ~J =~L +~S characterised by the quantum number J, which takes values from
|L ° S| to |L + S|. For less-than-half-ﬁled shells, the lowest level is the one with smallest
possible value of J, and the energies of multiplet levels increase with increasing J. For
more-than half-ﬁlled shells, the lowest level is the one with maximum value of J and the
order of energies of multiplet levels is inverted.

1.3
Neutron Scattering As a Probe For Condensed Matter
Neutron scattering serves as one of the most powerful tools in condensed matter physics to
understand material properties. In this section we will ﬁrst discuss the property of neutron
and introduce the magnetic cross section both for a single crystal and a powder. Afterwards a
standard beam line conﬁguration of a time of ﬂight (TOF) spectrometer will be given and the
corresponding kinematic constraints will be derived.
1.3.1
Properties of the neutron
Neutron is a spin 1/2 particle with a mass approximately identical to the proton. Since it doesn’t
carry any charge, there is no interaction between an electron and a neutron by the Coulomb in-
teraction, instead it directly interacts with nucleus of atoms which is a strong nuclear interac-
tion and of short range. In addition, due to neutron carries magnetic moment ~µ, it can couple
to a magnetic ﬁeld ~H via the Zeeman interaction °~µ· ~H. Here the magnetic ﬁeld can either be
external or generated by the moving unpaired electrons in magnetic compounds. Therefore,
it is ideal to use neutron to study magnetic systems by doing scattering experiments and the
understanding of the corresponding cross section becomes a priority task.
Neutrons for scattering experiments are produced either at a nuclear reactor or at a spallation
source. This project’s experiment was performed at a facility employing a spallation source.
Here pulses of protons are accelerated onto a heavy metal target where spallation process takes
place. The resulting neutrons are slowed down in a moderator and guided to the instruments.
Since the interaction between a neutron and a matter is relatively weak, special detectors such
as He3 are required where they work with neutrons resulting in charge signals that can be am-
pliﬁed and detected by standard means.

Spin Wave Theory
In this chapter we will talk about spin waves in magnetic ordered system. For simplicity, one
can consider a Heisenberg Hamiltonian and write down the equation of motions (EOM) for
spins at different sites, for detail derivations one can ﬁnd in the lecture note [14]. Here we sum-

precesses on a cone with respect to a certain direction ~n and the collective motion propagates
like a wave. Similar to acoustic waves in solids are called phonons, in the language of second
quantisation, here we call these spin waves magnons.

3.3
Spin wave analysis
So far we have identiﬁed possible magnetic excitations in CrCl2(pyz)2 from the INS powder
spectra by inspection of the data in the Figure 3.6aª3.6f . In order to quantitatively describe
these excitations, we ﬁrst analyse their peak positions and then introduce a phenomenological
2D model based on LSWT. For more accurate descriptions, we use the spinw Matlab package
introduced in Chapter 2 to extend the pure 2D model to include intra-layer and nest nearest
neighbour interactions. By ﬁtting the experimental spectrum measured at Ei = 3.6 meV with
the simulation result, we can extract values for exchange couplings.

3.4. SUMMARY
3.4
Summary
In this chapter, we ﬁrst identiﬁed the magnetic excitations in CrCl2(pyz)2, then based on LSWT
we constructed a Heisenberg Hamiltonian by including the NN, NNN and interlayer couplings
to explain the observed INS spectrum, which indeed give a good description for the excitations
below 1 meV. However, the comparison between the experimental and the simulated spec-
tra shows that the width-broadening effect can not be explained by this model. One possible
explanation could be there is phonon coexistence at the same energy scale which is hard to
distinguish in the INS powder spectra. Moreover the huge difference between the theoretical
Curie-Weiss temperature and the measured value indicates this model is not valid and require
us to have more understanding of this material, such as how the electron residing on the pyz
ring affects the magnetic properties.
Besides that, there are some additional puzzles. One is that since the Curie-Weiss temperature
is so large that we should see high energy excitations in the INS powder spectra but there is
nothing based on the data we had. Another is since this is a conductor at room temperature,
it is natural to guess probably a metal description is more suitable then the spin should be
itinerant instead of localised at each site. However, the conductivity measurement and the op-
tical band-gap data reveal that there is a small gap in this material, while the DFT result shows
that without including the onsite Coulomb repulsion U this material is half-metallic. Hence it
should be still within the Mott-insulator picture and assign S=3/2 to Cr(III) is reasonable. But
during the powder diffraction experiment at FRM II, we did not see any magnetic Bragg peaks
and the intensity of the nuclear Bragg peaks changes very little when temperature decreases
below ordering temperature, which is counter-intuitive as we expect strong magnetic signals.
In general, more experiments are required to study this material. For the INS experiment, more
counting hours is necessary to identify high energy excitations due to their signals are relatively
weak. Moreover mapping the phonon cross section is also helpful since it can help us to dis-
tinguish magnetic excitations and phonons at low Q, which is a difﬁcult task to separate them
directly from the measured spectra.

In the last decade, Kitaev materials and the related physics draw much attention from con-
densed matter communities. Follow the initial suggestion proposed by Jackeli, Khaliullin and
coworkers [1, 11, 12], so far efforts are mainly focused on d5 metal ion based materials, such
as Æ-RuCl3 and Ir compounds. A recent proposal made by Liu and Khaliullin [5] suggest that
instead of d5 metal ion compounds, ions with d7 conﬁguration such as Co(II) may also realise
Kitaev bond-dependent interactions and existing d7 layered hexagonal materials, for exam-
ple Na2Co2TeO6 and Na3Co2SbO6, may not be accidental to share the similarity that builds up
zigzag AFM type order at low temperature. Follow this idea, in this chapter we apply INS ex-
periments on Na2Co2TeO6 powder sample to get access to its excitation spectrum and examine
the possibility of it being another type of Kitaev material.

4.1. CRYSTAL STRUCTURE AND MAGNETIC PROPERTY OF NA2CO2TEO6
Previous studies [3, 19] shows that Na2Co2TeO6 is a layered hexagonal material where CoO6
and TeO6 octahedrons form the ab plane as shown in Figure 4.1. The trigonally distorted (ﬂat-
tened along the c axis) CoO6 octahedra connects to each other forming a honeycomb unit by
sharing an edge and Te atom sits in the unit centre. All six bonds within the honeycomb unit
are equivalent and it is expected that they have same the exchange interaction strengths. Along
the c axis, Na ions distribute between neighbouring honeycomb layers disorderedly and have
three different sites resulting in a limited short-range magnetic correlation length along the c
axis which can only be probed when the temperature is down to 1.8 K [3].
A long-range antiferromagnetic order has been found below TN ª 24.8 K [3]. The magnetic
ground state is determined to be zigzag antiferromagnetic (see section 4.3.1) within the hon-
eycomb lattice layer and characterised by a propagation vector~k=(1/2 0 0) by neutron scatter-
ing [3], see Figure 4.2. Also the reduced magnetic moments for Co ions in two sublattices are
mCo1 = 2.77(3)µB/Co2+ and mCo2 = 2.45(2)µB/Co2+ respectively [3], signiﬁcantly reduced from
the expected spin only ordered moment value of 3µB for Co2+ due to the quantum ﬂuctuation.
The magnetic susceptibility measurement gives the Curie-Weiss temperature which is around
TCW º °20.9 K and the effective moment is estimated to be µeff = 5.55µB/Co2+ [3, 19], which
is larger than the spin-only moment (3.87µB) for S=3/2 Co2+ and close to what would be ex-
pected for the presence of a full orbital contribution (6.63µB). In addition, even above the Neel
temperature, there still exists short-range magnetic correlation within the honeycomb lattice
plane whose correlation length is estimated to be ª 12 Å at 25 K [3].

4.3
Spin wave analysis
So far we have identiﬁed all the magnetic signals from the measured INS spectra, now we try
to construct a spin Hamiltonian for Na2Co2TeO6 to explain all these excitations. Normally this
is not trivial and requires additional information besides the powder spectra. Hence before
presenting any speciﬁc models, we summarise some of the key features which are useful for
the determination of the Hamiltonian.
• Each CoO6 octahedron is trigonal distorted but all six bonds are equivalent for the hon-
eycomb unit.
• According to the susceptibly, the Curie-Weiss temperature is around -20.9 K and the ef-
fective moment is estimated to be 5.55µB [3].
• Within the honeycomb lattice plane, spins are pointing along the crystallographic b axis
and exhibit zigzag AFM order at low temperature [3]. Along the crystallographic c axis,
neighbouring spins point along opposite directions and the interlayer coupling is there-
fore AFM.
• Based on the present INS data, we can not infer whether the low energy excitations are
gapped or not. But if there is a gap it should be less than around 0.5 meV according to
the Figure 4.3 (notice this observation is instrumental resolution limited and the current
resolution is around 0.3 meV). The low energy excitations come out of the momentum
transfers 0.79 Å°1, 1.94 Å°1, 2.50 Å°1 and 3.08 Å°1 respectively.
• There are two types of excitations. One is related to the zigzag AFM ordered state and
their excitation energies h!1,2,3 (here 1, 2, 3 represent different modes) are around 6.46
meV, 7.17 meV and 2.77 meV respectively, in particular the mode at 2.77 meV connects to
the low erngy dispersive mode at 0.79 Å°1. The other mode sits between 20 meV and 30
meV which is a broad continuum and persists even across the Neel temperature.
In following sections, the main analysis method is LSWT which only accounts for those excita-
tions related to the ordered ground state and the magnetic signals between 20 meV and 30 meV
cannot be explained within this formalism. Hence here we give two possible origins for these
temperature-robust excitations. One possible interpretation is that the two excitations corre-
spond to the ﬁrst two excited single-ion levels. Like what we discussed in Chapter 1, due to the
spin-orbital coupling and the octahedral crystal ﬁeld the HS Co (II) levels will split into multiple
levels and we call these levels the single-ion levels. If the crystal ﬁeld is of cubic symmetry, then
the resulting energy levels can be characterised by the total angular momentum j with values
1/2, 3/2 and 5/2. However if the octahedron is trigonally distorted, the higher energy levels will
further split. For Co (II) which is characterised by quantum numbers (Lz = 3, Sz = 3/2), there
are in total (2Lz +1)£(2Sz +1) = 28 manifolds for the diagonalization of its Hamiltonian so the
ﬁnal multiple levels may be 14 Kramers doublets which allows the low temperature or ground
state description of Co (II) to be an effective spin 1/2 particle [9]. If this is the case, then the
large effective moment can be explained by the single-ion model which naturally gives an high
effective moment (ª 6µB) [9], but the interpretation of Curie-Weiss temperature needs to be
careful since at around 200 K (ª 20 meV) the higher levels are already been populated. Inspired
by Æ–RuCl3 [6], another possibility is that the whole broad continuum excitation may involve

the excitation of Majorana fermion and gauge ﬂux which will disappear if the temperature is
further increased and goes beyond the Neel temperature. For Æ–RuCl3, this happens around

In following analysis, we will treat those high energy modes as single-ion level excitations and
only focus on the low energy part of the INS spectrum up to ﬂ! = 10 meV. At low temperature,

Chapter 5
Conclusions
In this thesis we studied two magnetic layered materials CrCl2(pyz)2 and Na2Co2TeO6. After
identifying their magnetic excitations, the minimum spin Hamiltonians were constructed with
the help of spinw which is a MATLAB package based on the linear spin wave theory. In addi-
tion, we ﬁtted the simulated results to the experimental spectra and were able to extract the
exchanges couplings.
For CrCl2(pyz)2, the minimum Hamiltonian J0–J1–Jlayer we created can capture the excitations
spreading at the low (Q, E) range but the width-broadening effect it fails to reproduce. More-
over, the calculated Curie-Weiss temperature 10.43 K is much smaller than the measured value
140.6 K, indicating this model is not valid. Due to the high TCW , we expect to see high energy
excitations in the powder spectra, however this is contrary to our data. Considering the scat-
tering time is limited to 4 hours, probably the magnetic signals are too weak to be observed.
Therefore in the future, long scattering time is necessary for INS experiments. Furthermore, we
need more understanding of this material and the most important question is how the electron
residing on each pyz ring affects its magnetic properties.
For Na2Co2TeO6, we found two types of excitations, one is related to the AFM zigzag order
(below 10 meV) which vanishes after the temperature is higher than Neel temperature. While
the other (locate between 20 meV and 30 meV) is a broad continuum which is robust against
temperature. In the thesis we attribute them to the single-ion level excitations. For its spin
Hamiltonian, the conventional Heisenberg Hamiltonian fails to reproduce the powder spec-
trum, especially the high energy part (between 6 meV and 8 meV) where the observed two-
peaks feature is not reproduced. Following a theoretical prediction, we ﬁnd the J1–J2–J3–K
Heisenberg-Kitaev Hamiltonian is the minimum model that can reproduce the low energy ex-
citations. However this requires us to set the spin ordered direction along one of the cubic axis
which is 35.62 ° away from the experimental result. For this minimum model two parameter
sets are found, one with AFM type J1 and the other is FM type. However we can not distinguish
these two since they offer almost the same spectra which have the high similarity to the experi-
mental one. For the future study, we ﬁrst need to study the temperature dynamics of the broad
continuum excitation, which can gives us the information about the origin of them. Further-
more, single crystals are necessary since there are two things we want to study in details. One is
the spin ordered direction since one can not distinguish the cubic axis direction and the crys-
tallographic b axis direction fromm the powder diffraction spectrum. Another is to probe the
magnon dispersion ﬂ!(~Q) and compare with our model. At the moment, based on the powder
spectra all we can conclude is that it may be another type of Kitaev material.
