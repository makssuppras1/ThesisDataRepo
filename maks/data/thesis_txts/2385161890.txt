Abstract
This thesis explores the possibility of predicting the backﬁlling time scale for wave to
wave induced scour beneath submarine pipelines. A series of experiments are conducted
in a ﬂume with an established sand pit, a constant water depth, a constant sediment
grain size and a hydraulic wave piston to generate waves. The wave piston is connected
to an Active Wave Absorption Control System (AWACS) and controlled with DHI Wave
Synthesizer software. Diﬀerent sizes of pipeline models in solid PVC are placed on top of
the sea bed and the experiments are recorded with video, the surface elevation measured
with wave gauges and the orbital velocities measured with Laser Doppler Anemometry
(LDA) equipment. All the raw data is afterwards processed in the MATLAB environment
where the large amount of data is boiled down to manageable and clear results.
The experiment are designed to resemble previous experiments from Fredsøe et al.
(1992) and Bayraktar et al. (2016) and numerical simulations from Fuhrman et al. (2014)
for easier comparison and yet still produce new results that expands the ﬁeld of study.
Several phenomenon regarding the scour and backﬁlling processes have been observed
and documented. None of critical signiﬁcance to the results but useful knowledge for
further similar experiments in the future.
The data results are merged with results from Fredsøe et al. (1992) and from this,
three diﬀerent empirical expressions are established to predict the backﬁlling time scale.
Two of them include both the Shields parameter and the scour depth or Keulegan-
Carpenter number prior to and after a change in wave climate from violent and stormy
to calmer conditions. They both predict the backﬁlling time scale better than the last
expression of the three but none of them are dominating in all scenarios. One of the two
has limitations in its range of initial and ﬁnal scour depth but predicts the backﬁlling time
scale better within its range. The other one is only limited by the range of parameters in
these experiments but are often inferior to the before mentioned expression. One uniﬁed
expression was not established in this study and further experimentation is most likely
needed to achieve this.

Introduction
Submarine pipelines is a very common oﬀshore construction in civil engineering as they
are designed to transport water, waste water, natural gas, oil etc. Designing these are
not always easy with regard to the seabed evolution for the pipelines entire lifespan. It
is important to understand how the seabed evolves in a given environment as it locally
can create large holes beneath the pipeline. In a worst case scenario these can cause the
pipeline to break under its own weight resulting in hazardous consequences. While the
scour process of the seabed evolution has been well studied, the backﬁlling process has not
as much. Backﬁlling of a scour hole beneath a submarine pipeline happens if the weather
conditions and thereby the wave climate, changes to lighter conditions - e.g. especially
after a storm. The change in scour depth is known but how long time the backﬁlling takes
is uncertain. Despite it has been investigated with experiments no one has yet come up
with an empirical expression for the backﬁlling time scale. At least not for wave-induced
scour followed by wave-induced backﬁlling. Experiments on this matter has been done
at DTU by Fredsøe et al. (1992) where it was concluded the non-dimensional backﬁlling
time scale is controlled by The Shields parameter, the Keulegan-Carpenter number or
the scour depth prior the a change in wave climate and the Keulegan-Carpenter number
or the scour depth following a change in wave climate. The Shields parameter which
determines whether or not sediment is transported and how much, was proven a direct
and critical parameter regarding both the scour and the backﬁlling time scale in the same
article.
Numerical simulations with a hydrodynamic model is based on Reynolds-averaged
Navier–Stokes (RANS)equations, coupled with a two-equation k - ω turbulence model
was carried out by Fuhrman et al. (2014). Here the backﬁlling time scale was studied and
the simulations support the conclusion from Fredsøe et al. (1992) that the scour depth
and seabed proﬁle is mainly governed by the new wave climate. An expression to predict
the backﬁlling time scale was not established in this article but it lays the foundation for
further research.
An experimental study of current to wave-induced scour and backﬁlling was conducted
in 2016 by Bayraktar et al. (2016).
Conditions similar to Fredsøe et al. (1992) and
Fuhrman et al. (2014) was chosen and an expression for the non-dimensional backﬁlling
time scale was established. This expression excluded one of the three before mentioned
variables as the initial scour depth was neglected based upon the uniformity of steady
current scour. This was to simplify the situation and the expression was concluded valid
for strictly current to wave scenarios where the normalized initial scour depth from a
steady current does not exceed 0.6-0.8.
The purpose of this study is to establish an expression for prediction of the non-
dimensional backﬁlling time scale based on all three variables. The basis for this will be
a line of experiments carried out in a ﬂume in building 114 at the Technical University

Experimental set-up
2.1
The set-up in general
The experiments are conducted in a 22 m long and 0.595 m wide ﬂume in building 114
at the Technical University of Denmark. Waves are generated with a hydraulic wave
piston connected to an Active Wave Absorption Control System (AWACS). Active wave
absorption for the reﬂected waves is deactivated to generate the largest waves possible
for the set-up. If activated there is a chance that the hydraulic wave piston generates a
wave with maximum stroke at the same as it compensates for a reﬂected wave, resulting
in the piston moving further than the physical stroke limit. The result of this scenario
would be a sudden stop in wave generation and thereby also the experiment. For smaller
waves the wave absorption is also deactivated to create as uniform conditions as possible.
Three pipelines with diﬀerent diameters is utilized in the experiments - 3 cm, 5 cm and
7 cm. Each pipeline is a solid PVC rod produced speciﬁcally to these experiments. The
3 cm pipe is chosen to compare result with previous experiment performed by Bayraktar
et al. (2016) and Fredsøe et al. (1992). This pipe also allows a larger span of data in
terms of the KC-numbers because the Keulegan-Carpenter number KC goes up when the
pipeline diameter D goes down. A smaller pipeline diameter would make measurements
diﬃcult and too inaccurate but a larger pipeline diameter increases accuracy at the cost
of data span - meaning a smaller range of KC. The KC range for larger pipelines lie
in the lower end of the KC scale and because they provide better accuracy, this is an
easy way to generate as wide spread data as possible. Varying the pipeline diameters is a
way to vary the KC-number and the scour depths S while keeping all others parameters
constant.
The pipeline models is placed on top of a sand pit with a length of 8.38 m and a width
of 0.595 m. Both ends of the pit is stabilized by a wall of bricks with the same height
as the sand pit. One end has a slope made of crushed stones and sand, where the other
end joins with an artiﬁcial shore slope or wave breaker. The wave breaker is designed to
both resemble a shore and to absorb as much of the waves as possible. The sand pit has
a height of 11 cm which is chosen to ﬁt the bricks at the ends and also provide enough
sand to make sure the bottom of the sour hole does not collide with the bottom of the
ﬂume.
The water depth is kept constant at h = 40 cm above the sand pit section for all
experiments, which is the same depth as in Bayraktar et al. (2016). This and everything
described above is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1 and 2.2.

The wave gauge data is collected with the software DataAssemble and before each
experiment the wave gauges are calibrated. While the water surface is completely still
the surface level is measured at three diﬀerent vertical positions for the wave gauges. The
distance between each position is 8 cm and this is matched with the change in volt from
each measurement. With the polyﬁt function in MATLAB a linear expression is created
for the calibration which afterwards is used to calibrate the actual data from the given
experiment.
The scour and the backﬁlling process is monitored by a Nikon D5600 from one end of
the pipeline, as seen in ﬁgure 2.2. The camera is positioned perpendicular to the end of
the pipeline and the video is recorded in 1080p with atleast 50 fps. The scour process is
recorded with video and the backﬁlling process is recorded with a time-lapse function in
the camera. This takes a high resolution picture every second and compiles them all into
a video. This is to ensure the high picture quality when analysing the video in MATLAB
and a better accuracy in the measurements. The backﬁlling process takes several hours
and the cameras capacity for high resolution video is 30 minutes. Even if the resolutions
and fps was lowered the camera cannot record for a whole backﬁlling period in one take
which is also why the time-lapse function was utilized. How often the camera takes a
picture can be changed and one picture per second is the lowest interval which is chosen
to have as detailed data as possible. A recording of the backﬁlling process with the time-
lapse function still produces a smaller video ﬁle than a recording of the scour process
with HD video.

The velocity at the middle of the pipe is measured with Laser Doppler Anemometry
(LDA) equipment to ensure the actual velocities, and not theoretical velocities, are uti-
lized in further calculations. The equipment is a DANTEC DopplerLite DPSS laser and
a DANTEC FiberFlow to split up the laser into two beams. A probe is placed at the
opposite end of the pipeline than the camera and it both shoots out the laser beams and
picks up beams reﬂected on moving particles. LDA measurements are performed indi-
vidually without the pipeline in place as measurements at the middle of the pipeline else
would be impossible. Figure 2.4 shows an illustration of the principle of the equipment.

2.2
Preparing the experiments
The sand pit contains plywood boards with the same upright standing height as the sand
pit and spanning from the brick sections at each end of the pit to approximately 0.5 m
from the pipe. These serve the purpose of easing the surface process of the sand pit. An
aluminium board is utilized the drag across the sand pit which removes all sand above
the plywood boards. At the middle section where the pipeline is placed later on, the sand
is levelled with a plywood construction resting upon the ends of the plywood boards in
the sand. This makes it possible to level oﬀthe sand evenly with satisfying precision
even though there are no plywood boards in the middle section. If the boards in the sand
continued beneath the pipeline area they would disturb the scour process and perhaps
result in incorrect scour depths.
The ﬂume is ﬁlled with water until the water depth is 0.4 m above the sand and
afterwards the DHI Wave Synthesizer is calibrated according to the manual from (DHI,
2002).
The pipeline is placed after the ﬂume is ﬁlled with water to avoid any scour
process from happening due to smaller streams and waves caused by the ﬁlling process.
Wave gauges is mounted and and calibrated as described above in section 2.1 as the
last thing before the experiment is started.
During the scour part of the experiment with the initial waves the scour process is
watched to see if anything goes wrong and make sure the best possible conditions is
present for the backﬁlling part of the experiment.

3.4
Scour depth measurement
As mentioned previously the experiments consists of a part with scour induced by an ini-
tial wave spectrum, followed by a part with backﬁlling induced by a ﬁnal wave spectrum.
The initial waves were maintained until the expected scour depth S0 was reached which
happened within 5-15 minutes. Since the scour process was not always evenly distributed
along the pipeline at the beginning of the scour process, the waves were maintained until
the expected scour depth was constant along the entire length of the pipeline.
The same process was executed with the ﬁnal wave spectrum but for a longer time as
backﬁlling is a slower process than scour. This part was maintained for enough time to
make sure the scour depth had reached a new equilibrium, based on theoretical expres-
sions described in the next section.
The recorded video data is analysed in MATLAB with the VideoReader function and
looping through the video in approximately 60 steps of 2-5 minutes depending on the
total time of the backﬁlling. The bottom of the pipe is marked with the ginput function
as a base point to subtract with each individual scour measurement. If this point is called
p0 and the individual measurement of the scour depth is pi the scour measurement can
be expressed as:
Si = |p0 −pi|
(3.25)

Discussion
4.1
Alternative wave spectrum
The wave spectrum generated by the DHI Wave Synthesizer consist of a large amount
of diﬀerent waves ﬁtting the JONSWAP spectrum, as seen in ﬁgure 3.1 and 3.2. The
more waves in the spectrum the larger the span from small to large wave and hereby the
span in wave heights and periods. Even though the waves together makes up the wanted
signiﬁcant wave heigh and wave period there are still some very large and some very
small waves. To see if this had any inﬂuence on the scour and backﬁlling evolution and
the equilibrium scour proﬁles, a new wave spectrum with a lesser amount of waves was
generated in MATLAB. The idea was that the larger waves maybe scoured too much and
the smaller waves backﬁlled during the scour part of the experiments. When changing to
the large 7 cm diameter pipeline, deposition of sediment below the center of the pipeline
was observed. Changing to a spectrum with fever waves means less variation in wave
height and period and will therefore determine if the number of waves in the spectrum has
any inﬂuence and if it causes deposition of sediment beneath the center of the pipeline.
Five waves were chosen to prevent a situation with a standing wave due to the inevitable
wave reﬂection. This was applied for experiment 15, 16, 17, 18, 19 and 20 and the results
was scour proﬁles very similar to those from the original wave spectrum but the scour
process was easier controlled.
Since the scour proﬁles did not change the deposition
phenomenon, that is most likely caused by something else and this is discussed further
in the next section.
The reason why the scour process was easier controlled with this wave spectrum
is because the waves has to be a certain size to initialize the scour process beneath
the pipeline.
Creating an actual hole below the pipeline take long time with small
waves of which there are a larger amount of in the spectrum generated by the DHI
Wave Synthesizer. Once there is a hole below the pipeline the scour process speeds up
because even the small waves generate small velocities at the sea bed which is accelerated
signiﬁcantly below the pipeline due to the tiny gab between pipeline and seabed.
Experiment 21, 22 and 23 was changed back to the DHI wave spectrum as the spec-
trum with ﬁve waves started to become a bit too unreliable. The measured signiﬁcant
wave height Hs, peak wave period Tp and normalized scour depth S/D sometimes de-
viated too much from the theoretically calculated values of which each experiment was
planned from.
4.2
Scour proﬁles
With the 7 cm diameter pipeline unsymmetrical scour proﬁles was observed as the scour
hole was deepest towards the front of the pipeline. Still below the pipeline but more to the

opposite direction of which the waves were propagating. Though the exact reason for this
was not determined, there is some potential reasons which could be due to streaming or
non-linearity in the waves. Streaming is known to create an onshore-directed ﬂow and net
transport of sediment. As the waves in these experiments are propagating in one direction
towards an artiﬁcial shore, it is possible that a small streaming eﬀect could interfere with
the sediment transport below the pipeline. If so sediment would be transported from
right the left and this could maybe result in a deeper scour hole to the right. Figure

and 20 minutes are plotted on top of each other to illustrate the evolution of the sea

happens mostly within 5-15 minutes of the scour process. This could be because the
wave generated orbital velocities dominates the scour process until a certain depth from
where the eﬀect causing this becomes dominant.
A third reason could also be the presence of a minor stream near the seabed in the
opposite direction of the wave propagation. Because the waves do propagate in a direction
away from the piston, some amount of water is transported in the same direction and
as it cannot escape at the end of the ﬂume it has to go somewhere else. If so this water
will escape near the bottom of the ﬂume and this would create a stream near the seabed
which could be responsible for this phenomenon.

4.4
Time series
The time series in ﬁgure 3.4 generally looks as expected with a fast scour period and a
slower backﬁlling period. The scour period is stretched on the x-axis to better illustrate
the scour evolution as it would look almost linear if not stretched. The scour happens very
rapidly at the beginning as soon as passage beneath the pipeline is established. It then
gradually slows down as the free span beneath the pipeline grows and this happens until
equilibrium is reached. This is the case for many of the experiments like experiment
8 and 17. In other cases like experiment 7 and 13 the plot does not clearly indicate
equilibrium is reached and this is because the initial waves was stopped as soon as the
scour depth was near the expected scour depth. The reason for this procedure is the
==PAGE:42==
CHAPTER 4. DISCUSSION
32
initial waves in some cases tend to backﬁll the scour hole after equilibrium is reached
and sometimes even before that. This is the phenomenon discussed ealier in chapter 3 |
Results. Experiment 1, 2 and 3 are good examples of this and the reason might be the
presence of bed forms. According to Hedegaard (1985) the velocity above a ripple crest is
1.5 - 1.9 times the free stream velocity. The velocity increases the Shields parameter and
thereby also the sediment transport. During a half wave period when the ﬂow moves in
one direction, the sediment moves over the wave crest. Some of the sediment settles into
a vortex on the lee side of the ripple and the remaining sediment is moved even further
and settles beyond the vortex. This situation is illustrated in Figure 4.4 which originally
is from Hedegaard (1985).
Figure 4.4: Illustration of the ﬂow and sediment transport along a ripple formations. Figure is originally
from Hedegaard (1985).
The vortex can extend up to a half ripple length λ and sediment settling beyond this
are caught in the bed load transport moving up the slope of the neighbour ripple. Some
sediment might even not settle completely before passing by the next ripple crest. In the
other half of the wave period the same happens in the opposite direction - presuming the
waves in the experiment are symmetric. They are not completely symmetric but that
is probably because of a certain amount of reﬂected waves and how much they interfere
with the symmetry of the waves has not been documented in this project. This is not
the point here though but the increased velocity over the ripple crests results in more
sediment transport at the ripple crests. There are no ripples beneath the pipeline but
there are right next to it on both sides.
It is possible that this increasing sediment
transport when the ripples develop, is responsible for some backﬁlling during the scour
part of the experiment prior to the change in wave climate. Of course the ripples has to
be somewhat large compared to the pipeline for this eﬀect to overrun the scour process.
If not it would also be observed in experiments with smaller waves and it would increase
the backﬁlling prior the change in the wave climate.
In some experiments, like experiment 4 and 10, nothing happens at the ﬁrst 5-10
minutes of the scour process. The explanation is the scour process only started at one
end of the pipeline in these experiments - the opposite end of the camera. This happened
when the pipeline bottom was placed some mm into the seabed at the end near the
camera. This was not deliberate and when determined this was the cause, it was carefully
prevented afterwards. As a results the scour process only initiated at the other end of the
pipeline as that was not embedded but just resting upon the seabed. The scour process
would then slowly expand along the pipeline until it reached the other end and covered
the whole length of the pipeline. It did not aﬀect the equilibrium scour depth but it
aﬀected the process until that point. At the end where the camera was recording the
pipeline would actually self bury and when the scour hole expansion reached this area,
the scour process would happen slightly faster than normal.

to the ﬁnal wave conditions. The ﬁrst three did not fully reach the expected equilibrium

4.6
Experimental comparison
Basically there are two types of experiments to compare with: the similar wave to wave
induced scour experiments by Fredsøe et al. (1992) and the current to wave-induced scour
experiments by Bayraktar et al. (2016). Both utilized a D = 3 cm diameter pipeline like
some of the experiments in this study and they utilized approximately the same sediment
grain size, except for a few experiments in Fredsøe et al. (1992). Bayraktar et al. (2016)
is also conducted in a similar ﬂume with the same water depth.
==PAGE:52==
CHAPTER 4. DISCUSSION
42
Regarding the experiments from Fredsøe et al. (1992) the scatter matches quite good
in both the S/D vs. KC plot and the T ∗
b vs. θ plot.
Regarding the experiments from Bayraktar et al. (2016) similar behaviour in the scour
and backﬁlling time series are observed. Behaviour where the scour depth ﬂuctuate up
and down when equilibrium is reached. This probably happens because the bed forms
are not stationary. Another reason could be the irregular waves them self as the large
waves in the spectrum removes a lot of sediment and sometimes when a smaller wave
follows, it backﬁlls a bit as it has a smaller equilibrium scour depth. In the recorded
videos during experiments the bed is in fact observed to be equally dynamic but it can
not be the only reason since this also happens with regular waves in Bayraktar et al.
(2016).
In general the backﬁlling time scales are bigger for the current to wave experiments
which makes sense as the scour hole caused by a steady current is larger than one caused
by a wave. Naturally it will take longer time to backﬁll a larger volume as meantioned
several times in this chapter.
4.7
Simulation comparison
Numerical simulations for wave to wave-induced scour and backﬁlling is carried out in
Fuhrman et al. (2014) with a ﬁxed pipeline diameter D = 3 cm and a ﬁxed sediment
grain size of d = 0.19 mm.
Just like the experiments ﬂuctuations in the scour and backﬁlling time series are
observed. Especially when the scour or backﬁlling evolution has converged towards an
equilibrium. When the change in KC number is large the backﬁlling happens in two
stages or phases in the simulations. The ﬁrst phase is very fast and is described as a
redistribution of sediment near the pipeline. The second phase is described as a slower
backﬁlling towards a new equilibrium. This is also observed in some cases in the experi-
ments in this study but not all of them and not only those with a big change in the KC
number. Experiment 16 e.g. looks similar to this and here the ∆KC = 5.8 and ∆S/D =
0.10. Other cases follows the exponential time function quite good like experiment 7 and
11. The numerical simulations with a smaller change in KC takes longer time to backﬁll
and the backﬁlling is not as rapid as the cases described before. The two examples of
this in Fuhrman et al. (2014) are not comparable to any time series in the experiments
from this study. Perhaps the time series with the smallest change in KC but those it
resembles a little bit are experiments with a much smaller change in KC. The degree of
asymmetry in the experiments and the numerical simulations are reasonable similar. A
larger ripple or shoulder tend to form on one side and is often balanced by two smaller
ripples or shoulders on the other side of the pipeline. What determines which side grows
into one big or two smaller ripples has not been detected in this study. It seem that
sometimes even the smallest inconsistencies in the levelling oﬀthe seabed can aﬀect the
formation of the ripples. Even if this has any eﬀect it is considered small and since this
was the same in the numerical simulations, where the sea bed is completely ﬂat at the
beginning, there must be another reason. Whatever it is, it is not considered important
for the scour and backﬁlling process in waves.
One more similarity is the deposition phenomenon beneath the center of the pipeline
as described in 4.2 Scour Proﬁles. In general there are many similarities between the
numerical simulations and the experiments, though they are not completely identical.

Conclusion
5.1
Conclusion to this study
The present thesis has experimentally investigated wave-induced scour and wave-induced
backﬁlling beneath submarine pipelines. Experiments was carried out in a wave generat-
ing ﬂume with three individual pipelines varying in diameter. One pipeline at the time
was placed on top of a ﬂattened sand pit for a total of 23 experiments. An initial wave
climate was implemented to simulate storm conditions and create a scour hole beneath
the pipeline. The wave climate was afterwards changed to calmer conditions to initiate
a backﬁlling process. The experiments were designed to be comparable with similar ex-
periments performed by Fredsøe et al. (1992) and Bayraktar et al. (2016) and numerical
simulations carried out by Fuhrman et al. (2014).
Problems with backﬁlling during the scour process occurred and to resolve it diﬀerent
changes was applied. The accuracy of the sand levelling was increased to make sure the
pipeline was placed on top of the sand with contact along the whole length. At the same
time to make sure the pipeline was not buried into the sand - not even a few mm, as this
in some cases resulted in self-burying. The scour process was stopped as soon the scour
depth was approximately constant along the whole pipeline and close to the theoretical
expected scour depth. The waves were generated with DHI Wave Synthesizer software
and the wave spectrum consisted of a large amount of diﬀerent waves which in total
generated a wanted signiﬁcant wave height and peak wave period. This wave spectrum
was replaced with an alternative created in MATLAB consisting of only ﬁve waves for some
of the experiments. Five waves was chosen instead of one, to prevent a scenario with a
standing wave. This turned out not to solve the problem but stopping the experiment
as soon as the scour depth was near the expected depth did. This meant bed forms was
not fully grown yet which probably caused the backﬁlling during the scour process.
During the scour process with the larger pipelines a tendency was observed where the
scour hole was deeper at the front of the pipeline. The reason for this was not determined
in this study but it could be caused either by a streaming eﬀect, asymmetry in the waves
or a small current at the bottom. Streaming and wave asymmetry is known to generate
onshore net transport of sediment and this could maybe erode extra sediment at spot
below the front of the pipeline. A small current at the bottom is also possible as the
waves propagate in the direction from the wave piston and towards the artiﬁcial shore.
This moves an amount of water in that direction and as it can not get out at the end
of the ﬂume it must go backwards again. This happens as a small current at the seabed
which perhaps erode extra sediment at the discussed spot. It is probably not caused by
a combination of all three suggestions as they generate sediment transport in opposite
directions. As this was not observed with the smallest pipeline it indicates this also has
44
==PAGE:55==

45
something to do with the pipeline diameter though it is possible it was too small to detect
with this pipeline.
During both the scour and the backﬁlling process a phenomenon was detected where a
tiny top or ripple formed right beneath the center of the pipeline. A similar phenomenon
was observed in Fuhrman et al. (2014) where a top, larger than the one from these
experiments, formed beneath the center of the pipeline when suspended sediment was
turned oﬀin the simulation. This was explained as converging-diverging eﬀects in the
ﬂow beneath the pipeline driving bed load towards the center of the pipeline. Suspended
sediment is present in the experiments but it could be its ability to remove sediment is
weakened with larger pipelines. Perhaps when the grain diameter to pipeline diameter
ratio d/D increases.
The velocities were measured with LDA equipment and when compared with veloc-
ities calculated with linear theory based on the measured signiﬁcant wave height and
peak wave period, they turned out smaller in general. This is because the waves utilized
in the experiments are not applicable with linear wave theory. LDA measurements are
therefore necessary to achieve accurate and correct results in these kinds of experiments.
The experimental results was combined in diﬀerent ways to formulate an empirical
expression to predict the non-dimensional backﬁlling scour depth. Bayraktar et al. (2016)
formulated an expression for this in current to wave scenarios involving the Shields pa-
rameter but not the scour depth. The expression was similar to the one from Fredsøe et
al. (1992) and was suggested to be taken as an upper limit for more general wave-induced
backﬁlling situations. This holds up reasonably with the results from this study as the
non-dimensional backﬁlling time scales are mostly below what the expression predicts.
Some results are above it but the expression is based on scattered data and should be
seen more as a line with an error above and below it or an area. A regression line based
on the data from this study and the data from Fredsøe et al. (1992) was established and
from this a similar expression which is below the expression from Bayraktar et al. (2016).
This expression is T ∗
b = 0.2θ−5/3 and has an error of 8.39.
As this expression only relies on the Shields parameter another approach to for-
mulate an expression to predict the backﬁlling time scale is attempted. A regression
line based on the scattered data in a T ∗
b /θ−5/3 vs.
∆S/D plot yields the expression
T ∗
b = 0.8(∆S/D)0.64θ−5/3 with an error of 7.27. A smaller error than the ﬁrst expression
and involving both the Shields Parameter, the initial and the ﬁnal scour depth.
Yet a third approach is attempted based on a 3D plot of the three parameters
T ∗
b /θ−5/3, S0/D and Sf/D. From a regression plane a third expression was formulated
T ∗
b = (0.31 + 3.72S0/D −5.74Sf/D)θ−5/3 with an error of 7.34. Despite the slightly
larger error compared to the second expression this is better at predicting the backﬁlling
time scale than the second expression. Except for cases with small changes in the scour
depth ∆S/D = 0.04 to 0.07 for initial scour depths S0/D ≥0.26. In these cases the third
expression predicts negative backﬁlling time scales which is useless and here the second
expression is the better choice. The third expression could maybe work if based on more
data.
In general the results are reasonably similar to prior experiments by Fredsøe et al.
(1992) and Bayraktar et al. (2016) which improves the validity. Within the limits of the
experiments the second and third expression can be a reasonable approximation of the
backﬁlling time scale - if the velocity measurements are validated.

5.2
Future work
More similar experiments
More experiments in the lower range of the KC number would hopefully ﬁx the problem
with the third expression where it turns out negative backﬁlling time scales.
Varying water depth and grain size
To completely unify the second and third expression into one reliable empirical expression
that can predict the non-dimensional backﬁlling scour depth, more experimental data is
needed. If extra experiments are conducted a way to expand the range of the results
could be by varying other parameters like the water depth of the sediment grain size.
This would either change the initial and ﬁnal wave climates of the Shields parameter.
Validating the LDA velocity measurements
Even though the velocity measurements seemed correct a validation is still required if
the expressions is to be used in any way. As earlier mentioned a way to do this could
be by creating a steady current in the ﬂume. If the discharge in each end of the ﬂume
is known the velocity can be calculated and compared to the velocity measured with the
LDA equipment. If these are close enough to validate the settings of the LDA equipment
it also means the velocity measurements utilized in this study is validated.
