Abstract
This thesis evaluated deep learning based model for segmenting stroke lesions us-
ing 3D Diffusion Weighted Imaging (DWI), Fluid Attenuated Inversion Recovery
(FLAIR), and Apparent Diffusion Coeﬀicient (ADC) volumes and was successfully
utilized for investigate the possibility of estimating model performance on Out-Of-
Distribution (OOD) datasets without voxel-wise annotations.
The results demonstrate successful and reliable performance estimation for the
segmentation model using Average thresholded confidence and deep supervision
mismatch based methods, and showed that feature distance based method could
not produce reliable performance estimations. Class specific variants of the perfor-
mance estimations marginally improved the reliability of performance estimations.
The segmentation model achieves a Dice Similarity Coeﬀicient (DSC) score of 0.67
on an In Distribution test set and generalized well an OOD dataset with a DSC
score of 0.64. Blob-level evaluations show good generalizability with a recall of 0.78
and precision of 0.35. Voxel-level segmentation does not exhibit significant over or
under segmentation, however, precision suffers on OOD data.
Confidence calibration techniques successfully improved calibration errors of
which Platt scaling showed the best performance, however, did not necessarily
result in better performance estimations. Ensemble models was shown to improve
calibration as well but did not improve the perofrmance estimation capabilities of
deep learning based segmentation models.
Dataset-level performance estimation proves valuable in understanding how the
model performs on data from new sites. ATC-based methods exhibit reliable esti-
mation without significant variance.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Stroke is the second most frequent cause of death globally[1] and has other severe
chronic implications such as dysarthria, paralysis, and amnesia[2]. Particularly in
the early stages of stroke, quantification of the stroke volume plays a critical role
in determining the clinical actions to be taken[3,4,5,6].
Stroke lesions can be detected by segmentation, i.e. delineation and identifica-
tion of stroke lesion volume in 3D medical imaging of the brain, for which purpose
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) have shown higher sensitivity for acute stroke
lesions[7,8] compared to other imaging modalities. However, manual detection and
segmentation of stroke volumes in 3D MRI is a time-consuming process that is
also prone to significant variability between different observers[9]. Therefore, the
utilization of fast automatic methods for stroke detection and volume quantifica-
tion holds great value in clinical routines, as timely treatment has been strongly
associated with favorable outcomes[6].
Deep learning models have demonstrated state-of-art performance for various
automatic segmentation tasks in medical imaging, however, for these models to be
reliably used in clinical settings, it is crucial to undergo thorough clinical validation.
The evaluation of these models should not only be limited to unseen data within
the In Distribution (ID), but also extended to Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) settings.
This is particularly important for deep learning models as they can struggle to
generalize to data from different domains and distributions than the training set[10],
resulting in well documented degradation in performance[11,12,13].
Thus clinical evaluation in OOD settings is especially relevant in MRI applica-
tions, as models trained on certain MRI protocols often struggle on images from
different imaging protocols, scanner manufactures and acquisition sites[14]. This
sensitivity to domain shift is particularly critical to evaluate for MRI, where vari-
ations in imaging protocol are common[15]. It is thus essential to evaluate models
in OOD scenarios that reflect clinical practice to help address reduced performance
caused by distribution shifts and over-fitting[16,17] to ensure reliable performance.
However, evaluation of segmentation performance requires voxel-wise annota-
tions curated by medical experts, which is tedious and time-consuming to acquire
and therefore often not available. Thus knowledge about segmentation performance
in absence of voxel-wise labels in OOD settings is of great value prior to clinical
deployment, while also bringing information about model reliability at test time
for more trustworthy clincal usage. Knowledge of segmentation performance also
provides useful information about how models may perform when deployed in new
settings and can serve as a tool to evaluate if large clinical validations are necessary
on new MRI acquisition sites.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Stroke imaging
The progression of stroke from the onset may be categorized into 4 phases[18] each
representing distinct physiological conditions. The first phase is the hyperacute
phase, which is defined as stroke within the first 24 hours of onset, followed by
the acute phase which lasts up to 7 days. From 7 days to 6 months is defined as
the sub-acute phase of the stroke, while strokes after 6 months are defined as the
chronic phase.
Stroke detection and volume quantification can be effectively performed using
3D medical imaging of the brain.
Among medical imaging modalities, MRI is
the preferred imaging modality due to its superior brain tissue contrast[19] and
higher sensitivity for all stages of acute stroke lesions[7,8] compared to other imag-
ing modalities. For stroke detection and treatment planning, the MRI sequences
DWI[20,4,21,22], FLAIR[23] and ADC[5] provides particularly useful information
in the acute stages of stroke.
DWI is a highly effective method for identifying
diffusion restriction caused by acute stroke minutes after its onset and is regarded
as the preferred modality for stroke core detection[20]. The presence of restricted
diffusion can be observed in the hyperacute phase of stroke through elevated DWI
signals and reduced ADC signals[5], that being bright areas in DWI images and
dark areas in ADC images compared to the rest of the brain. FLAIR imaging
demonstrates high sensitivity to acute stroke lesions [24]; however, it is typically
not suitable for detecting stroke lesions within the initial few hours[25,26]. On
FLAIR images stroke lesions appear hyperintense compared to other brain tissues
similar to DWI. Leveraging the information provided by all three sequences proves
valuable in the detection and staging of strokes, however, the focus of this thesis
revolves around the binary detection of any stroke type versus the absence of stroke.
A typical acute and hyper-acute stroke lesion is visualized in Figure 2.1 and 2.2 as
2D slices of brain volume from each of the three MRI sequences.

2.2
Segmentation of stroke
Stroke volume may be quantified by dividing the MRI volume of interest into mul-
tiple distinct regions or segments. By representing an MRI volume as V ∈RW,H,D
with width W, height H and depth D as a multidimensional array, segmentation
of the MRI volume can be achieved by classifying each element, e.i. voxels in V ,
as being a part of the stroke lesion or not. The objective then is to assign a stroke
lesion or non-stroke lesion class label to all voxels within the volume based on its
visual characteristics and properties.
The segmentation accuracy of voxels in V can be enhanced by including in-
formation from multiple MRI volumes from the same patient. The segmentation
problem can thus be expressed as:

based Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)[27,28] due to their superior perfor-
mance and feature representations[29] compared to other classical approaches. Among
the neural network architectures the U-net[30] have shown state-of-the-art perfor-

2.3
U-net
The U-net is a supervised CNN architecture, being a neural network architecture
designed to produce outputs of similar dimension as the input, originally developed
for end-to-end biomedical image segmentation with few training samples. The U-
net architecture comprises an encoding path responsible for feature encoding of
the input and a symmetric decoding path that facilitates the precise localization
of relevant features specific to the task at hand.
The encoding path utilizes convolutional layers to extract features from the
input image.
Convolutions are executed using convolution kernels, also known
as filters, which traverse the entire input to generate feature maps. Each filter
produces a feature map, and these maps are stacked to form the channels of the
subsequent layer input. By utilizing convolutions with filters, the U-net achieves
weight sharing among input nodes in the neural network, as the same kernel is
applied to all spatial positions in the input. This approach acts as a regularization
technique and is motivated by the fact that similar spatial features, such as edges,
are significant across the entire input image. The convolution operation is inher-
ently linear, thus the outputs of convolutional layers are always pushed through a
non-linear function to be able to learn non-linear patterns in the input.
To expand the receptive field of the input image, the U-net incorporates down-
sampling of the inputs, reducing the resolution of the feature maps each time and
creates different depths within the architecture. Each depth represents a feature
map with a particular resolution. For all networks trained in this work, the down-
sampling is achieved by strided convolutions.
Multiple convolutional layers are
added at each depth before further downsampling, and the downsampling of fea-
ture maps enable more feature maps to fit in memory at each depth. A schematic
of the U-net architecture is shown in Figure 2.4 where the height and width of each
colored box represents the resolution of the feature maps. The thickness represents
the number of feature channels present at each depth.
The encoding path results in a lower-dimensional feature encoding of the in-
put, which the decoding path utilizes to generate a segmentation output of the
same size as the original input. The decoding path involves upsampling the feature
encoding, convolutional layers, and concatenation of features from the correspond-
ing encoding path at corresponding depths. These concatenated features, known
as skip connections, play a critical role in reconstructing relevant spatial features
during the decoding process. By incorporating skip connections, the U-net retains
high-resolution information from feature maps in the encoding path, allowing the
decoder to access detailed spatial information, while also improving gradient flows
during training.
Upsampling is performed using transposed convolutions in all
trained networks.

2.5.2
Optimization
In practice the weights of the network are optimized with respect to the loss function
using a stochastic gradient descent algorithm with gradients estimated on a subset
of the data. For the segmentation problem, this means that the gradients used
for optimization of the network weights are estimated based on B volumes rather
than all N volumes in the training dataset. In practice, gradients are computed by
backpropagation[34] and most commonly the Adam optimizer[35] is used for neural
networks, which also incorporates momentum and adaptive learning rates.
To
address the vanishing gradient problem intermediate layers of the network should
include weight normalization, of which the instance normalization technique[36]
proven to work well across many medical image segmentation tasks[37].
To further guide the features learned in the decoding path, the U-nets trained
in this work are enforced with the deep supervision mechanism, in which downsam-
pled versions of the binary segmentation mask is attempted to be reconstructed at
each depth of the network. This effectively means that each depth of the network
attempts to generate a low resolution segmentation prediction to match the corre-
spondingly downsampled ground truth segmentation mask. Segmentation predic-
tions is compared with the ground truth dowsampled segmentation mask at every
depth with a loss function, adding a supervision signal to the optimization of the
network at multiple resolutions.
To avoid overfitting of the trained U-net models parameter weights are trained
with L2 regularization and incorporation of dropout[38] layers after convolutional
layers of the decoding path of the network.

2.5.3
Data augmentation
Neural networks require a large amount of data to train effectively and avoid over-
fitting to the training data. In low data regimes, data augmentation can artificially
increase the size of the training dataset by generating new images with variations
in position, orientation, and appearance to help prevent overfitting and improve
robustness to differences in the input volumes resulting in models with improved
generalization performance. This is especially relevant for MRI data, which exhibits
significant variations in appearance due to differences in acquisition protocols, imag-
ing sequences, scanner artifacts, patient movement or patient populations. Many
data augmentation techniques exists for MRI applications, but largely falls into 2
groups. Within the first group of augmentations the overall shape of the structures
in the same position, but voxel intensities are changed. Data augmentations tech-
niques within this group are: Intensity Inhomogeneity Distortion that simulates
magnetic field strength inhomogeneities in MRI scans, contrast adjustments such
as gamma transforms of voxel intensities, additive or multiplicative noise, ghosting
and ringing artifact simulations. The other group of data augmentations changes
the geometry of the MRI, which also necessitates a similar transformation on the
voxel-wise annotations. During transformation, linear interpolation is performed
for the MRI voxel intensities and nearest neighbor interpolation for the segmen-
tation.
Data augmentation techniques within this group includes, mirroring of
the volume, rotation of the volumes simulating differences in how the patient may
have been placed in the scanner and elastic deformations to simulate differences in
brain shape. All the above mentioned methods have shown to improve segmenta-
tion performance for MRI applications[39] and will be tested for the binary stroke
segmentation problem.
2.6
Uncertainty quantification
Uncertainty quantification in neural network segmentation provides a measure of
confidence or reliability associated with the segmentation predictions, helping clin-
icians assess the quality and robustness of the output. This is essential for clinical
decision-making and may contribute to more trustworthy usage in clinical settings.
One straightforward method of quantifying the predictive uncertainty of neu-
ral networks is to utilize the readily available voxel-wise probabilities produced
by the softmax layer in the U-net[40]. However, softmax output probabilities of
neural networks tend to be overconfident[41,42], leading to unreliable predictions
especially on out-of-distribution data including for segmentation tasks[43]. This
means that neural network models produces confident misclassifications of voxels,
and the probabilities do not represent the actual confidence of the network in the
predictions[44].
In other words, neural networks are often miscalibrated in the sense that model
confidence does not reflect the model’s accuracy and likelihood of correctly classi-
fying voxels.

2.6.2
Bayesian methods
Estimating uncertainty based on the model confidence from the Unet outputs, how-
ever only captures aleatoric uncertainty even when probabilities are calibrated.
That is, the uncertainty information captured mainly reflects the inherent noise in
the data. This type of uncertainty may arise from noise in inter-annotator vari-
ability and measurement noise due diagnostically ambiguous voxels. As such, the
variation represented by aleatoric uncertainty is irreducible and can not be dimin-
ished by more data[50], while epistemic uncertainty captures the models lack of
knowledge about its weights and data, and is reducible. The epistemic uncertainty
may be reduced by addition of more data which contributes to more certainty in
the underlying model of the data. One way to capture epistemic uncertainty is
by introducing Bayesian neural networks in which parameters of the network is
modeled as random variables rather than scalar parameters.
Given the data Bayesian methods assumes a prior distribution over the param-
eters and uses the likelihood of the data to compute a posterior distribution over
the parameters. This directly allows for uncertainty quantification in the model
parameters based on the data, which at inference time can be used to reason
about the predictions as well as predictive uncertainty. However, exact inference
for Bayesian neural networks is computationally intractable, thus many methods
to perform approximate inference have been proposed, which includes Laplace ap-
proximations[51], Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods[52] and variational
inference methods[53,54]. However, these methods require large modifications to
the training pipeline. To this end, two popular sample based methods have been
proposed namely the Monte Carlo dropout[55] method, which uses dropout at test
time as a variational inference approximation, and deep ensemble[56], which trains a
collection of the neural network models with randomly initialized weights. In prac-
tice the deep ensemble have been shown to improve calibration and performance
within neural networks[56,57] and outperform Monte Carlo dropout methods.

2.6.3
Image level uncertainty
The above metioned methods produces voxel-wise uncertainty estimates by dis-
criminative modelling of the classification in each voxel, however, for performance
estimation the predictive uncertainty of neural network is only needed on a sam-
ple level. Predictive uncertainty on a sample level can serve as a proxy for model
reliability and certainty in its predictions for each particular sample, which in turn
can be used to reason about the performance drop on new data.
Sample level predictive uncertainty is an inherent aspect of out-of-distribution
detection, quantifying the likelihood of an unseen sample being outside the train-
ing distribution, often measured by the distance between new samples and in-
distribution samples in a relevant feature space. Some argue that such a distance
metric encodes epistemic uncertainty of the neural network[60,61].
Recently, another sample level predictive uncertainty measure was enabled by
the introduction of deep supervision in segmentation models, in which the disagree-
ment between intermediate deep supervision predictions was leveraged to quantify
the models uncertainty on a particular sample[62].
2.7
Performance estimation
The performance and generalization of segmentation models must be evaluated on
new unseen data to access its reliability on data that it was not trained as perfor-
mance of deep learning models deployed in OOD settings tend to degrade[63,11,12,13].
However, the evaluation necessitates voxel-wise annotations, which is highly tedius
and time consuming to acquire. Thus quantification of the performance drop on
OOD data in absence of labeled data is highly relevant to quickly access the gener-
alization of a segmentation model and concurrently provide a measure of reliability
of the model on the OOD data.
Estimating the performance of segmentation models is closely related to quan-
tifying uncertainty, as models tend to generate less reliable predictions for samples
that exhibit higher uncertainty. Numerous methods have been proposed to address
this objective; however, they can generally be classified into two primary groups;
The first line of research utilizes confidence scores produced by the network,
in which performance is expected to drop for samples that the model is less con-
fident about. These methods are mainly concerned with proper calibration of the

network probabilities and finding a suitable strategy to map model confidences to
performance predictions.
The second line of research focuses on designing a distance metric between the
training data and unlabelled OOD data in some latent feature space. The intuition
is that models are more uncertain about features of OOD samples far away from
the training data, which in turn leads to lower performance.
The introduction of deep supervision in U-net segmentation models have in-
spired a another way to quantify image level uncertainty and therefore performance
by investigating segmentation predictions from available at all depths of the U-net.
This work specifically targets methods that provide performance estimations
at the sample level, either through direct estimation or by aggregating voxel-level
uncertainty without the need for auxiliary model training. Furthermore, methods

2.7.2
Feature distance
Performance estimation is closely related to the field of OOD detection, which
focuses on accurately determining whether new, unseen samples exhibit significant
domain shift from the ID data and are therefore considered OOD. The fields are
related as the degree of domain shift plays a crucial role in determining the model’s
uncertainty regarding a sample, which subsequently affects performance.
Thus, inspired by remarkable achievements in OOD detection for segmentation
problems using distance metrics that measure dissimilarity between training sam-
ples and OOD samples in a relevant feature space, this study also aims to explore
whether similar distance metrics can effectively predict performance on OOD data.
Model performance is expected to degrade on new features that significantly
differ from those encountered during training[68]. We thus assume that images
with similar features will exhibit a smaller distance in embedding space, whereas
images comprised of distinct features will display a larger distance. This assump-
tion is reasonable considering that feature representations learned by CNNs have
previously shown to contain semantic meaning, i.e. elements in close proximity
tend to visually similar[69]. Consequently, a considerable distance in embedding
space from previous training instances serves as an indication that the model has
not encountered those activation patterns before, and is unlikely to produce reli-
able predictions. Thus we can leverage feature distance metrics between training
samples and OOD samples as a surrogate for image-level uncertainty to predict the
performance.

The brain MRI data used throughout this thesis is acquired from 2 distinct sites;

Model architecture
The neural network trained for the segmentation task is a U-net with the architec-

and is implemented in Tensorflow 2.9.0. The main model architectural choices are
outlined in the listings below:
• Depth: 4
• Max pooling with stride 2x2x2 and kernel size 2x2x2 is used to downsample
features from one depth to the next.
• 96 Filters for convolutional layers in the most shallow depth. For each con-
secutive depth the number of filters are doubled.
• For 3D segmentation problems the convolutional layers perform 3D convo-

channel dimension.
• All convlutional filter kernels have size 3x3x3
• Networks are trained on patches of the input MRI volumes of shape 64x64x64,
which was the largest patch size possible with the above configurations, while
still maintaining a batch size bigger than 2 as recommended in [37].
• The within network non-linear activation function is leaky Relu with slope
10−2
• With inclusion of deep supervision mechanism a contribution factor of 1/2d
is applied on depth d = 0..4.

4.3
Out-of-distribution performance estimation
Using the final model presented above the DSC performance of each sample in the
OOD set was estimated using three types of performance estimation techniques.
The three methods is comprised of image level aggregates of voxel confidence pre-
sented in Section 4.3.1, DSM based methods in Section 4.3.2 and feature distance
based methods in Section 4.3.3. The same OOD performance estimation techniques
is applied to an ensemble model in Section 4.3.4.
The reliability of the performance estimation techniques is evaluated by MAE
and Pearson correlation between the observed ground truth DSC and the predicted
DSC on the OOD samples. For each method a Bayesian linear regression was fitted
to the observed ground truth DSC and predicted DSC scores. The Bayesian linear
regression have a zero mean Gaussian prior, Gaussian likelihood and posterior, and
prior precision of regression weights and data noise is estimated by maximizing the
marginal likelihood. This regression allows for accurate estimation of uncertainty
on the regression slope and noise level. 95 % prediction interval (PI) are computed
by 1.96σP I where σP I is the predictive uncertainty measured by the standard
deviation.
Confidence intervals on the regression slope is likewise computed as
1.96σslope where σslope is the uncertainty on the slope parameter of the linear
regression measured by the standard deviation.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This thesis aimed to train and evaluate generalizable deep learning models that seg-
ment stroke lesions using 3D Diffusion Weighted Imaging (DWI) Fluid Attenuated
Inversion Recovery (FLAIR) and Apparent Diffusion Coeﬀicient (ADC) volumes
and investigate the possibility of estimating segmentation model performance on
Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) datasets in the absence of voxel-wise annotations.
The results show a segmentation model that generalizes well to unseen In Dis-
tribution (ID) data evaluated on a independent test set with a DSC score of 0.64
compared to 0.67 DSC on the validation set used for model selection.
Using the segmentation model as a diagnostic tool for image level stroke detec-
tion, where the segmentation can be viewed as a tool to explain the reasoning of
the model to have chosen to classify the particular patient as stroke or non-stroke,
the model achieves an accuracy of 80% and a perfect recall of 1 on the independent
test set. The perfect recall does however come with the image level precision of
0.627. Hence the model detects all patients with stroke in the test set, however,
achieves this by making a significant amount of false positives.
The image level performance metrics do however not capture if the segmenta-
tions are accurate, and to quantify this the blob level test performance also show
generalizability to the test set with a recall and precision of 0.78 and 0.35, com-
pared to 0.83 and 0.33 on the validation set. This metric is of particular interest, as
individual stroke lesion in the brain might have different onsets and can be viewed
as separate segmentation problems. The blob recall remains high, indicating that
the model does capture most of the individual stroke lesions present, however, the
models tendency to generate false positives is aggravated on these metrics. This
further highlights the model’s bias towards predicting image patterns that resemble
stroke more often than not.
Voxel level recall and precision do not show significant over- or under segmenta-
tion, i.e. a systematic bias towards segmentations that are bigger or smaller than
the ground truth lesion volume on the ID test set, as systematic over-segmentation
would show up as low precision due to extensive false positives and under-segmentation
as low recall with many false negatives.
However, when the model is evaluated on the OOD data the voxel level preci-
sion suffer significantly. The general tendency of generating many false positives
on image and blob level is further aggravated, where all metrics suffer a drop in
performance. Note particularly that the image level specificity is 0, i.e. the model
did not produce any true negatives. This behavior might however be explained by
the low number of normal samples, i.e. healthy controls of which there are only
10 in the OOD set. This may also explain the not so dramatic drop in image level
precision as the amount of false positives is limited by the number of negative cases.
==PAGE:64==
54
5 Discussion
Visual inspection of the segmentations produced by the neural network model
shows accurate stroke lesion segmentations that capture the general shape of both
small and big lesions well. However, the segmentations tend to be slightly smaller
than the annotated stroke lesion. Through visual inspections we also see that most
of the false positive stroke lesion predictions are caused by the model segmenting
various artifacts of the MRI data acquisition that result in bright regions as seen
for stroke. The significant drop in precision is, in part, caused by the segmenta-
tion predictions of the geometric distortions of the DWI data, which is segmented
as stroke in many OOD cases, as seen in the visual inspections, and ignored for
similar geometric distortions in MRI data from the test data, as seen in Appendix
A.2.2. This may be caused by how the geometric distortions appear in the ID data
compared to the OOD data.
The model experiments conducted to find the optimal model architecture and
training configuration showed that among the tested loss functions, the focal loss
was the best performing loss with β = 0.25. The focal loss outperforms CE, WCE,
BCE indicating that the modulating factor γ have successfully down-weighed easy
examples such that the network focuses on the hard examples, and dealt with the
class imbalance problem inherent in segmentation problems.
Most data augmentation techniques either improved performance or made the
performance marginally lower, however, kept in the model due to the model would
generalize better and be robust to such changes in the OOD data. The only data
augmentation not used for training the final model was the rotation augmentation,
as it decreased model performance to a great extent.
This behavior might be
explained by the fact that most brain volumes were well registered without much
variation in rotation, and that the interpolation applied during rotation added
additional undesired noise in the ground truth label map and input volume.
The fact that the model performance increased drastically when the number
of filters in the initial layer was increased to 128 filters indicates that the model
performance is limited by how expressive the model is, as more filters allows for
more features to be learned.
With the relevant model extensions of the model architecture and training con-
figuration found by model experiments the model showed an increase in OOD DSC
performance from the baseline of 0.44 to 0.64.
The model experiments conducted does however have a major limitation in that
models were only trained once to determine its impact on the segmentation perfor-
mance. This might have caused some extensions to some superior performance by
chance of a good local minima found during optimization. Other significant limi-
tations include the significant downsampling of the input data to 2mm isotropic
resolution in the scan plane, which simultaneously results in upsampling with in-
terpolation of the last dimension. Lastly the segmentation problem was simplified
to a stroke segmentation problem, all other pathologies which might also cause
diffusion restriction in the DWI is labeled as healthy tissue.
In general the results do however show that the model generalizes well to the
independent ID test set, however, with a performance drop on the OOD dataset.

5.1 Calibration

5.1
Calibration
All calibration techniques examined improved calibration, but the results unam-
biguously show that the Platt scaling results is the best calibration of probabilities
for the final stroke segmentation model.
The transformation applied to the probabilities ˆpi for the Platt scaling with
the fitted parameters is visualized in Appendix A.3.1. Notice that the point at
which the sigmoid curve surpasses ˆqi = 0.6 is not 0.5, such that any voxel that
was classified as stroke with probability 0.5 < ˆpi < 0.6 is now classified as the
background class. That being said the prediction confidence of the background
class, i.e.
ˆpi < 0.6 is generally increased as ˆpi represents the probability of the
stroke class. For ˆpi > 0.6 the confidence of the stroke class is also increased. A
similar pattern is seen for the isotonic regression, however most ˆpi are given a lower
probability of the stroke class all the way up to ˆpi = 0.9.
Surprisingly the added flexibility of the isotonic regression did not improve
calibration errors more than the Platt scaling, and the single parameter TS gen-
erally results in the least calibrated models. This suggests that transformations
that are more flexible than a single parameter, but not as flexible as the isotonic
regression are optimal. The isotonic regression might have slightly overfitted to
the validation data, which is prevented for simpler calibration techniques that are
more regularized. This is in line with previous studies exploring model uncertainty,
which showed that good calibration on the validation data does not necessarily
mean better calibration on OOD data [78].
The TS parameter T is estimated as 0.461, i.e. below 1, which effectively means
that confidence of all predictions independent of class prediction is increased, while
the class-specific TS parameters T0 and T1 are 0.448 and 1.29, respectively. This
means that confidence of the voxels predicted as the background class is increased,
while confidence of the stroke class is decreased. Refer to Appendix A.3.2 for a
showcase of how probabilities of the temperature scaling methods are transformed.
Notice that the class agnostic T is remarkably close to T0, most likely due to
the class imbalance problem resulting in a large contribution from the abundant
background class voxels in the data.
This most likely also explains the better
calibration performance of the class-specific TS.
The class-specific TS calibration results are very similar to calibration errors of
the Platt scaling both with better calibration than the TS, implying that calibration
techniques with >1 parameters might be suitable for calibration of softmax based
probabilities in the stroke segmentation problem.

Performance estimation
5.2.1
Confidence based methods
Although the Platt calibration showed superior calibration error it does not trans-
late into the best performing AC method on the downstream task of performance
estimation.
==PAGE:66==
56
5 Discussion
All AC based methods seem to consistently overestimate the performance on
each sample, indicated by most of the points being above the y = x line, which
can be attributed to the well known property of neural networks being overconfi-
dent[41,42]. The isotonic regression based AC method shows the lowest MAE of
0.152 among the tested methods, indicating that on average the DSC prediction
is off by 0.152 from the observed DSC. This may be attributed to the isotonic
regression performing a less dramatic transformation, i.e. a less dramatic increase
in confidences ci.
ATC based methods outperform the AC based counterparts and the Class-
Specific version outperform the rest of the methods in line with the ATC based
performance estimation techniques used for segmentation problems in the literature
[49]. Inspecting the ATC thresholds applied for on the raw softmax probabilities
and CS-ATC thresholds the CS-ATC threshold is slightly higher, indicating that a
higher confidence is needed for a stroke classification of a voxel.
Notice that the ATC results of the ATC based method on the raw softmax
probabilities, Platt scaled and TS are identical. Conceptually, the ATC threshold
the cut-off applied to ci such that the number of voxels above the threshold matches
the number of correctly classified voxel level. As such both Platt scaling and TS are
monotonically increasing functions that when applied to the probabilities simply
correspond to a monotonically increasing transform applied to the ATC threshold.
Refer to Appendix A.3.1.1 and A.3.2.1 for derivations of the transforms applied to
the ATC thresholds. We may view the ATC threshold as a calibrated threshold to
produce a stroke label on each voxel for the segmentation problem.
Although the calibration techniques did not improve the ATC performance,
they are however expected to improve for other downstream tasks which relies
on the uncertainty available at the voxel level, such as active learning based on
uncertainty sampling. Furthermore, the TS method is expected to improve when
applied to multi-class problems as have been shown in previous literature[67,49].
The performance estimation reliability is however expected to decrease for larger
distributional shifts of the OOD data, as have previously been shown in the case
of uncertainty quantification methods for classification problems [78].
5.2.2
Deep supervision mismatch
Deep supervision mismatch results generally a under-estimation of the actual un-
certainties on a image level. This may be caused by the interpolation performed
to match the sizes of the predictions at different depths, as this makes smooth esti-
mates of segmentations. This generally results in a DSC score computed between
the depths with very smooth segmentations and well defined shapes of the stroke
segmentations from the most shallow depth. The inclusion of more information
about lower resolution segmentation produced at deeper levels of the network did
not result in more robust performance estimations.
5.2.3
Feature distance
Performance estimation based on feature distances showed very poor results as
increasing feature distances was not correlated with lower performance. The low
==PAGE:67==

R-squared indicates that the linear regression model very poorly fits the data and
has limited ability to explain the variability in the DSC score based on the feature
distances, which shows in the performance estimations. The feature distances do
however seem to work somewhat for OOD detection, as the validation data is
significantly closer to each of the feature spaces compared to the OOD data. This
is somewhat indicated by the mean distance to the validation data being smaller
than that of the OOD feature distances. For OOD detection the cosine similarity
seem to separate the ID validation from the OOD data the best.
5.3
Ensemble
Segmentation model performance was significantly increased on ID data for the
ensemble model, however, did not show increased performance on the OOD set.
The ensemble model does however in general improve the precision compared to
the final model, i.e. producing less false positives on image, blob and voxel level.
It increases the precision of the model by drowning spurious predictions of single
members of the ensemble as was seen in the visual inspections of the ensemble in
Section 4.2.2.2.
The ensemble model was, in general, better calibrated than the single segmen-
tation model, which is also shown in previous studies [57]. Better calibration of
the segmentation model resulted in ATC thresholds of the ensemble closer to 0.5,
meaning the segmentation predictions are more reliably found by thresholding the
probability of stroke class with 0.5. We expect the calibration error to decrease by
addition of more ensembles as seen in [59].
5.4
Dataset level performance estimation
Sample level performance estimation may prove useful for trustworthy use of seg-
mentation models in clinical settings, however, dataset level performance estima-
tions can work as a powerful tool to know how a model performs on data from a
new site. This is particularly interesting as we expect a drop in performance for
OOD data, however, can not in advance know the degree of the performance drop.
This problem was investigated through dataset level performance estimation by the
mean DSC score of the sample level performance estimations of the most promising
methods investigated. The dataset level results show that the ATC based methods
most reliably estimate the average DSC on the OOD set without significant vari-
ance on the estimate. The results presented in this thesis is however based on tests
on only one OOD dataset, and should be tested on other OOD settings as well.
