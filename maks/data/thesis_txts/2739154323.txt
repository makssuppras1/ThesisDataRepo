Abstract
Contamination of soil and groundwater is a widespread problem that poses a risk to both the environment
and human health. Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) are a contaminant group that has received
increasing concern. It is a group of highly persistent synthetic chemicals used across various industries.
The PFAS quality criteria have been tightened in recent years, both in Denmark and the EU. PFAS are
surface-active compounds, which make their behaviour in the environment unique. Unlike other con-
taminants, PFAS have the ability to adsorb at the air-water interface. Retention of the compounds in the
subsurface is therefore dependent on both solid-phase sorption and adsorption to the air-water interface.
It is crucial to understand the transport behaviour of PFAS when assessing the risk at contaminated sites.
The geology in Denmark is dominated by glacial deposits. This makes the subsurface unique compared to
other countries and is expected to impact PFAS transport significantly. International studies may therefore
not directly apply to Danish conditions. PFAS transport behaviour in Danish conditions has not been well
studied. This project has investigated how PFAS is transported in the subsurface and identifies the most
important parameters for the retention of PFAS. This is achieved through laboratory transport column
experiments, which before this study have not been conducted with Danish sediments.
Column experiments were conducted with sand from a former air base located in Værløse, Denmark. The
experiments were representative of a sandy porous media with properties typical for Danish aquifers.
A PFAS transport model, coupled with saturated and unsaturated column experiments, was successfully
used to derive the two adsorption coefficients for solid-phase sorption (Kd) and air-water interfacial
sorption (Kai). The focus has been on the six PFAS; PFPeA, PFBS, PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA and PFOS.
Findings show that sorption of PFAS increases with chain length to both solid-phase and the air-water
interface. Based on prior determination and calibration of van Genuchten parameters, porosity, grain
size, hydraulic conductivity, dispersivity and air-water interfacial area the transport model was adjusted to
represent the columns. The magnitude of the air-water interfacial area was estimated based on the porous
medium and the water saturation. It was found to directly govern the retention in the unsaturated zone,
illustrating the need for robust estimation methods. Key findings have shown that sorption to the air-
water interface predominantly mediates the retention of the long-chained compounds in the unsaturated
zone. The fraction associated with air-water interfacial adsorption constituted more than 90% of the
total retention for PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA, and PFOS in the column experiments. In contrast, the shorter-
chained compounds were found to be mobile both in the unsaturated and saturated zone. These results
highlight that the unsaturated zone significantly contributes to the retention of especially longer-chained
PFAS in the subsurface. The magnitude and significance of the two retention processes depend on the
individual properties and site-specific conditions including the porous medium, water saturation, PFAS
concentration, organic matter, and aqueous chemistry.
Model simulations of the site have shown that low-permeable layers highly impact the air-water inter-
facial area. This indicates that clay or silt lenses have the potential to increase the retention of PFAS in
the unsaturated zone tremendously. PFOS was found to be retained in the unsaturated zone at the study
site. This agrees with findings from transport experiments showing significant retention due to air-water
interfacial adsorption. The shorter-chained compounds are likely depleted from the source area.
This project has contributed to an enhanced understanding of PFAS transport in the subsurface. This
includes the determination of important PFAS transport parameters. This knowledge provides valuable
insights for future risk assessments, particularly within the context of Danish geology.

Sammenfatning
Forurening af jord og grundvand er et udbredt problem med risiko for at påvirke både miljøet og men-
neskers sundhed. Per- og polyfluorerede alkylstoffer (PFAS) er en gruppe af syntetiske og meget persis-
tente forureningsstoffer, der har været anvendt i mange brancher. I de senere år har der været et øget
fokus på PFAS, og kvalitetskriterierne er blevet skærpet både i Danmark og i EU. PFAS er overfladeaktive
stoffer, hvilket giver dem unikke egenskaber i miljøet. I modsætning til mange andre forureningsstoffer,
kan PFAS sorbere til grænsefladen mellem luft og vand. Tilbageholdelse af stofferne i jord og grundvand
afhænger derfor både af sorption til jord og sorption til luft-vand grænsefladen.
Det nødvendigt at forstå stoffernes transportegenskaber for at kunne foretage en risikovurdering ved
PFAS-forurenede grunde. Geologien i Danmark er domineret af glaciale aflejringer, hvilket betyder, at
Danmark er speciel set i en international sammenhæng. Disse særlige geologiske forhold forventes at
være afgørende for PFAS-transport i jord og grundvand. Erfaringer fra internationale studier kan derfor
ikke nødvendigvis overføres direkte til danske forhold. Ydermere er PFAS-transport i jord og grundvand
kun undersøgt i et begrænset omgang for danske forhold. Projektet fokuserer på dette videnshul og har
til formål at undersøge, hvordan PFAS transporteres i jord og grundvand med fokus på en dansk lokalitet.
Herunder at bestemme de styrende processer og vigtige parametre for tilbageholdelsen af PFAS. Denne
viden er opnået gennem kolonneforsøg i laboratoriet, hvilket ikke tidligere har været udført med danske
jorde.
Kolonneforsøgene blev udført med sand fra en tidligere flyvestation i Værløse (Danmark) med henblik
på at repræsentere et porøst medie typisk for danske forhold.
En PFAS transportmodel, koblet med
mættede og umættede kolonneforsøg, kunne effektivt benyttes til at estimere adsorptionskoefficien-
terne for sorption til jord (Kd) og sorption til luft-vand grænsefladen (Kai).
Fokus har været på de
seks PFAS forbindelser: PFPeA, PFBS, PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA og PFOS. Forsøgene viste stigende sorption
med kædelængde til både jord-vand og luft-vand grænsefladen. Modellen blev indledningsvist tilpas-
set kolonneforsøgene gennem bestemmelse og kalibrering af van Genuchten parametre, porøsitet, dis-
persivitet, kornstørrelsesfordeling og det specifikke luft-vand grænsefladeareal.
Resultaterne viste at
luft-vand grænsefladearealet er direkte styrende for tilbageholdelsen af PFAS i den umættede zone og
afhænger af vandmætningen samt det porøse medie. Det er fundet at adsorption til luft-vand grænse-
fladen overvejende er styrende for tilbageholdelse af de langkædede PFAS-forbindelser i den umættede
zone. Mere end 90% af den samlede tilbageholdelse for PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA og PFOS kunne tildeles
sorption til luft-vand grænsefladen i kolonneforsøgene. Derimod blev de kortkædede PFAS-forbindelser
fundet til at være mere mobile i både den umættede og mættede zone. Disse resultater understreger
vigtigheden af den umættede zone for tilbageholdelsen af PFAS særligt for de langkædede forbindelser.
Tilbageholdelsen afhænger af stoffernes individuelle egenskaber samt de stedsspecifikke forhold såsom
sedimenttype, vandmætning, PFAS-koncentration, organisk stof samt grundvandskemien.
Modelsimuleringer af den umættede zone viste, at lavpermeable lag har stor betydning for det specifikke
grænsefladeareal mellem luft og vand. Dette indikerer, at ler- og siltlinser har et stort potentiale for at øge
tilbageholdelsen af PFAS i den umættede zone. Ved den undersøgte lokalitet, blev PFOS fundet at være
tilbageholdt i den umættede zone mens de kortkædede og mere mobile PFAS sandsynligvis er udvasket
fra kildeområdet. Dette er i overensstemmelse med resultater fra kolonneforsøgene.
Dette projekt har bidraget til forståelsen af PFAS-transport i jord og grundvand. Dette inkluderer bestem-
melsen af betydende parametre for PFAS-transport.
Denne viden er værdifuld for fremtidige under-
søgelser og risikovurderinger ved PFAS-forurenede grunde i Danmark.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Water Framework Directive requires a sustainable management of the water bodies within EU to
ensure good chemical and ecological quality. This includes protection and restoration to prevent dete-
rioration of the surface water and groundwater bodies (European Commission, 2024). Groundwater is
a valuable and finite resource, providing 65% of the drinking water quantity within the EU and 99% of
the quantity in Denmark (EEA, 2022; Miljøstyrelsen, 2023). However, groundwater is under increasing
pressure, due to contamination from industries, agriculture, and effects of climate change (EEA, 2022).
PFAS is one group out of many chemicals that threaten the environment, including groundwater and sur-
face water. PFAS is abbreviated for Per- and PolyFluoroAlkyl Substances, which is a group of thousands
of fluorinated man-made chemicals. The compounds contain the extremely strong and stable C-F bond
and can be either poly- or perfluorinated. Polyfluorinated PFAS, typically referred to as precursors, have
the ability to transform in the environment to perfluorinated PFAS, also named terminal or "dead-end"
PFAS, such as PFOA and PFOS. Terminal PFAS are highly persistent in the environment and have even
been found in remote regions (ITRC, 2022). Furthermore, PFAS contain a fluorinated hydrophobic and
lipophobic alkyl chain and a hydrophilic end group (Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). Due to this chemical structure,
PFAS have unique surface-active properties. As a result, PFAS have been widely used in many applications
such as coatings for textiles, food packaging, and household products. In the industry, PFAS have been
used in firefighting foams, galvanisation, and paints among others (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016c). The presence
of PFAS in the environment originates from environmental releases from consumer products and indus-
trial raw materials, which induce sources of exposure. The most important exposure pathways of PFAS
are namely via direct intake, in for instance food and water and exposure to dust (Miljøstyrelsen, 2015).
Until now the main regulatory focus has been on the subgroup PFAAs (perfluoroalkyl acids and their salts)
which includes the two long-chained compounds PFOS and PFOA. These two substances are ubiquitous
in the environment as they are both highly persistent and toxic at low levels with the possibility to
bioaccumulate (Buck et al., 2011; Miljøstyrelsen, 2016c). Some of the toxic effects towards humans are
carcinogenic and endocrine disrupting effects, such as being harmful for reproduction (ECHA, 2024a).
PFAS poses a greater and more complex risk to the environment than other contaminants, partly because
they behave differently and spread over greater distances (Danske Regioner, 2022). In Denmark, PFAS
were initially detected in the environment in the early 2000s (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016b). In 2009 PFOS was
listed under the Stockholm Convention, and in 2020 and 2022 PFOA and PFHxS were also listed. In
2021 the Danish EPA sharpened the quality criteria for PFAS to 2 ng/l for the sum of 4 PFAS compounds,
and to 100 ng/l for the sum of 22 PFAS. This has since led to growing concern, and enhanced efforts to
investigate PFAS-transport and fate at contaminated sites. Focus has mainly been on firefighting training
sites, but PFAS have been used within many industries. It has been estimated that 15,000 sites in Denmark
are potentially contaminated with PFAS (Danske Regioner, 2024).

saturated and saturated zone (Arcadis, 2023). Due to their surface-active properties, transport in the
unsaturated zone is both governed by solid-phase sorption and adsorption to the air-water interface. The
fraction of total retention associated with air-water interfacial adsorption cause that PFAS behaves dif-

ferently compared to other contaminants usually studied at contaminated sites (ITRC, 2022). Therefore,

for Danish conditions. Finally, laboratory experiments and model findings should be compared to field
measurements from PFAS contaminated sites (Arcadis, 2023; Baun et al., 2023).
This thesis is based on a specific PFAS contaminated site in Denmark, which will be used to characterize
PFAS-transport in the subsurface and to address some of the knowledge gaps mentioned. The site is a
former military air base located close to the town Værløse, Denmark. It was operable between 1920 and
2004, where some of the activities at the location include stock of chemicals, maintenance of aircrafts,
and weapons (DMR, 2023). Prior to this study, multiple investigations have been conducted to assess the
risks related to the contamination at the site. Measurements in the upper groundwater have detected up
to 4100 ng/l for the sum of 4 PFAS compounds (PFHxS, PFOA, PFOS, PFNA) which is 2000 times above
the Danish quality criteria. In addition, the hydrogeology of the upper aquifer is well characterized (DMR,
2023). Notable for this site is that the unsaturated zone is shallow, with a groundwater table located 3-
4 m below ground level. This enables that sampling and characterization of both the unsaturated and
saturated zone is accessible. In addition, the unsaturated zone primarily consists of sand, which simplifies
the study of PFAS-transport in the subsurface and characterization of relevant transport parameters.
Aim
The overall aim of this thesis is to assess the transport of PFAS based on improved understanding of their
behaviour in the subsurface and how they interact at the interfaces in the unsaturated and saturated
zone of a sandy aquifer at a former air base. To fulfil the aim, the following specific objectives are to be
reached:
• Review of the physico-chemical properties of PFAS, with focus on the dominating PFAS compounds
at a former air base.
• Determine PFAS-transport parameters through unsaturated and saturated column experiments and
distribution tests in the laboratory.
• Evaluate and interpret laboratory results to adjust a transport model with the obtained parameters
and compare to field measurements.
• Assess the sources, transport and distribution of PFAS compounds to evaluate the fate of PFAS in
the upper aquifer at the site.

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances
2.1
PFAS in the environment
PFAS are a group of thousands of synthetic compounds widely used due to their unique and desirable
properties such as water, stain and soil repellence, friction reduction, and thermal stability (ITRC, 2022).
For instance, PFAS are used in textiles, household products, firefighting foams, as surfactants for water,
and grease repellents and much more (ECHA, 2024a). PFAS are defined as fluorinated compounds that
contain at least one fully fluorinated methyl (CF3) or methylene (CF2) carbon atom (ECHA, 2024b).
The carbon-fluor bond is one of the strongest chemical bonds in nature, resisting both abiotic and biotic
degradation. PFAS are released to the environment from direct and indirect sources such as industrial
facilities, consumer products and food contact materials. Many PFAS are considered persistent and mobile
in the environment, and worldwide, they contaminate groundwater and drinking water. During the last
decades, PFAS have been recognised as harmful compounds with effects on both human health and
the environment. Some of the toxic effects towards humans are carcinogenic and endocrine-disrupting
effects, such as reduced fertility (ECHA, 2024a).
2.2
Regulations
The early stage of PFAS production is estimated to have begun around the year 1949. In 1966-1990
production increased and in the years 1990-2000 PFAS were produced in great amounts. Since the first
findings of PFOS and PFOA in the environment in the early 2000s (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016b) several regula-
tions have been enforced to eliminate the use of certain PFAS compounds. In 2006, PFOS was regulated
in firefighting foams by the EU (EU, 2006) and in 2009 PFOS, its salts, and related compounds were
included in the Stockholm Convention under the EU’s Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) Regulation
under Annex B (restricted use) (UN Stockholm convention, 2024). In 2015 the first regulations in Den-
mark regarding PFAS were enforced in drinking water, soils and groundwater. It was proposed by the
Danish Ministry of the Environment based on a health hazard analysis regarding PFAS exposure. The
regulation included a quality criterion for the sum of 12 PFAS compounds of 100 ng/l in water and 400
µg/kg DW in soils (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016c, 2015). In 2020 PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid), its salt, and
related compounds were banned under the POPs Regulation, and in 2022 the same applied for PFHxS
(Annex A) (UN Stockholm convention, 2024).
In 2020 EU adopted a requirement for all member states to measure for 20 PFAS compounds by 2026 at
the latest and to comply with a quality criterion of 100 ng/l for the sum of 20 PFAS, and 500 ng/l for
"total PFAS" (directive 2020/2184) (European Union Council, 2020). In 2021 the Danish EPA sharpened
the quality limits for the sum of 4 PFAS substances (PFOA, PFOS, PFNA, PFHxS) to 2 ng/l in groundwater
and 10 µg/kg DW in soils (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024a). At the same time, the quality criteria for the 12 PFAS
substances was extended to cover 22 PFAS substances. An overview of the current quality criteria for
PFAS in Denmark is provided in Table 2.1.

2.3
Chemical structure of PFAS
PFAS are aliphatic compounds containing a fluorinated hydrophobic and lipophobic chain of carbon
atoms linked to a hydrophilic functional group (Figure 2.1) (ITRC, 2022). They can be divided into
per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, depending on the degree of fluorination. Perfluorinated substances
contain the perfluoroalkyl moiety CnF2n+1 −R which is fully fluorinated besides the functional group.
Polyfluorinated substances contain at least one perfluoroalkyl moiety besides one or multiple C-atoms
which are not or only partly fluorinated besides a functional group, such as 6:2 FTS (Figure 2.1) (Buck
et al., 2011). Polyfluorinated substances are also referred to as precursors because they have the ability
to abiotically or biotically transform in the environment into perfluoroalkyl substances, typically PFAAs
(Buck et al., 2011).
Figure 2.1: Example of a per- and polyfluorinated PFAS.
PFAS can be divided into long-chained and short-chained compounds. The number of C-atoms that define
if it is a long-chained compound differs depending on the PFAS of interest. PFCAs require at least 7 C-
atoms, whereas PFSAs require only 6 C-atoms to be defined as a long-chained compound (Buck et al.,
2011).
Production of perfluoroalkyl substances occurs mainly through two principal processes; Electrochemical
fluorination and telomerization (Buck et al., 2011). Electrochemical fluorination is a technology where
all H atoms of the alkyl chain are replaced by F atoms by electrolysis. This process results in both linear
and branched isomers, but linear isomers dominate (Buck et al., 2011; Arcadis, 2023). Telomerization is a
process where the alkyl chain is built from building blocks of two carbon atoms at a time. Therefore, PFAS
manufactured through this process, consists of only linear alkyl chains with an even amount of carbon
atoms. In addition, this method can produce both per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (Miljøstyrelsen,
2016b). Electrochemical fluorination was the most commonly used technology until 2002. Since then,
telomerization has become the only process used for the production of PFAS (Kato et al., 2011). There-
fore, sites contaminated with both linear and branched PFAS originate from before the year 2002.

2.4
PFAS family
The PFAS family can be divided into two overall classes; polymers and non-polymers (Buck et al., 2011).
The non-polymer based PFAS can be divided into two subclasses; Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances. The
perfluoroalkyl substances contain the group of PFAAs. The PFAAs and their precursors are considered
to be most relevant for soils and groundwater (Arcadis, 2023), and the focus is on these compounds
throughout the rest of this thesis. Figure 2.2 shows the PFAS family and selected subgroups.
Figure 2.2: The PFAS family and selected subgroups.
2.4.1
PFAAs
PFAAs are a group of PFAS that contains several subgroups including carboxylic acids (PFCAs), sulfonic
acids (PFSAs), sulfinic acids (PFSiAs), phosphonic acids (PFPAs), and phosphinic acids (PFPiAs) (Arcadis,
2023; ITRC, 2022). Perfluoroalkyl carboxylic acids (PFCAs) have a carboxylic acid as a functional group
attached to the fluorinated alkyl chain. The general chemical formula is CnF2n+1COOH (Buck et al.,
2011). Perfluoroalkane sulfonic acids (PFSAs) have the general chemical formula CnF2n+1SO3H with a
sulfonic acid functional group attached to the chain (Buck et al., 2011). The most well-known PFCA is
PFOA while PFOS is the most well-known PFSA. PFAAs are commonly found in the environment, either
due to direct emissions, use of the compounds or their salts in various applications, or from the degrada-
tion of precursors (Buck et al., 2011). They are highly persistent substances and sometimes referred to
as "terminal PFAS" (Buck et al., 2011; ITRC, 2022). Many of the long-chained PFAAs are gradually being
phased out, which has resulted in increased use of the shorter chained compounds (Arcadis, 2023).
2.4.2
PFAA precursors
Precursors are defined as chemical compounds that can transform into other chemical compounds. PFAA
precursors contain a polyfluorinated backbone or side chain and can be both larger functional derivatives
of non-polymers or polymers (Buck et al., 2011). The C-H bond makes them less stable and more sus-
ceptible to degradation. This ultimately results in the formation of shorter-chained perfluorinated PFAAs,
also referred to as terminal PFAS. The properties of the precursors might be different from their trans-
formation products, and thus they may have a different environmental behaviour. For example, cationic
or zwitterionic precursors may be susceptible to ion exchange and bind to clay minerals (Pancras et al.,
2016). However, there is in general much less knowledge available for the precursors compared to the
PFCAs and PFSAs (Pancras et al., 2016).

Transformation of precursors can occur either abiotically or biotically under industrial, environmental,
or metabolic conditions ITRC (2022); Buck et al. (2011). Abiotic transformation under ambient environ-
mental conditions includes hydrolysis, photolysis and oxidation of precursors which can occur in natural
waters and soils. These transformations may not immediately form PFAAs, but instead various intermedi-
ate compounds that eventually form PFAAs. One example is the hydrolysis of some PFAA precursors that
followed by biotransformation can produce PFSAs as PFOS. Another example is oxidation with hydroxyl
radicals that can lead to the formation of terminal PFAAs (ITRC, 2022).
The polyfluorinated precursors have shown the potential to biotransform under aerobic conditions, but
anaerobic biotransformation has also been reported for some PFAA precursors. The biotransformation
of precursors can occur via multiple intermediates (ITRC, 2022). As a result of biotransformation of
precursors in soils, terminal PFAAs may leach to the groundwater or enter surface waters through runoff
and thereby precursors can act as a secondary long-term source of PFAS contamination (Arcadis, 2023;
ITRC, 2022). When PFAS are released to the environment, either to air or water, the precursors serve
as an important source for findings of terminal PFAS in the environment and in remote regions (ITRC,
2022). Throughout the rest of this thesis, PFAAs will be referred to as PFAS unless anything else is clearly
stated.
2.5
Physico-chemical properties
The physico-chemical properties of PFAS are important for their behaviour in the environment. When
looking into the physicochemical properties of PFAS, it is important to distinguish between the protonated
and anionic forms of the compound, which is dependent on their acid strength (pKa). This is due to the
varying properties, which results in different behaviours in the environment (Buck et al., 2011; Pancras
et al., 2016). For instance, the neutral form of PFOA has a low water solubility and a substantial vapour
pressure, while the anionic form of PFOA has a high water solubility and a low vapour pressure (Buck
et al., 2011). At a normal environmental pH (5-9) most PFAS are found in the anionic form. The anionic
form is typically highly water-soluble and with reduced sorption to soils and sediments compared to the
neutral form. Due to the high solubility, it is uncommon that PFAS can occur as a separate phase, such
as in solid form or as NAPL (ITRC, 2022). The terminal PFAS are very stable and both abiotic and biotic
degradation are unlikely (Arcadis, 2023).
In general, PFAS are not volatile due to a low vapour pressure. Some PFAS precursors such as FTOHs are
more volatile and therefore can be found in the gas phase.
The physico-chemical properties may vary depending on the isomer (Buck et al., 2011; Wang et al.,
2011). A study by Wang et al. (2011) looked into the differences between branched and linear isomers of
C4 −C8 PFCAs. They found that the branched isomers overall had a lower KOW and KOA and a higher
water solubility compared to the linear isomer. However, the variability between the two types of isomers
was small, and it was concluded they are likely to show the same behaviour in the environment.
The hydrophobic/lipophobic and hydrophilic properties give PFAS a tendency to accumulate and aggre-
gate at interfaces, such as air-water and soil-water interfaces (Figure 2.3) (ITRC, 2022). The hydropho-
bicity of PFAS tends to increase with the alkyl chain length (Pancras et al., 2016). Furthermore, a high
degree of fluorination implies additional hydrophobicity to the compounds. Therefore, a strong affinity
for interfaces is observed for longer-chained PFAS (ITRC, 2022).

One of the interfaces at which PFAS accumulate at is the fluid-fluid interface (e.g. air-water or NAPL-
water interface). In the air-water interface, the hydrophilic head is in the water phase while the hy-
drophobic tail is in the air, forming a monolayer (Figure 2.3) (Krafft and Riess, 2015). This interface is
also referred to as the surface microlayer (SML) which is a thin layer (around 50 µm) that is in contact
with the ambient air (ITRC, 2022). However, PFAS behave differently depending on the concentration.
When high concentrations are present, PFAS are no longer freely dissolved but instead tend to aggregate
into micelles where the hydrophobic tails interact with each other and the hydrophilic head interacts
with the water. This formation of spheres is driven by hydrophobicity and occurs when PFAS are present
above the critical micelle concentration (CMC) (ITRC, 2022; Krafft and Riess, 2015). The CMC decreases
exponentially with increasing chain length (Krafft and Riess, 2015). Other types of aggregates known as
hemi-micelles or bilayer structures can also be formed (Figure 2.3) (ITRC, 2022). Hemi-micelles can for
example be formed near a surface, which can result in an increased concentration close to that surface.
A similar principle can occur at the SML (ITRC, 2022; Lei et al., 2023).

Dispersion
Spreading of contaminants in the subsurface is caused by diffusion and variations in velocity due to
sediment grains that force the groundwater to flow in flowlines along and around grains. This spreading
of contaminants is referred to as the hydrodynamic dispersion. The hydrodynamic dispersion is described
by:
DL = αL · vp + D∗

Dispersivity is often described in three directions; longitudinal, αL, transverse, αT, and vertical, αV.
The longitudinal dispersion governs spreading along the flowline whereas the transverse and vertical
dispersion governs spreading across the flowline (Appelo and Postma, 2005; Cook, 2022):
The effect of the longitudinal dispersivity is greater for a pulse release, while a continuous release is only
affected at the fringe of the plume. In both cases, it influences the arrival time at the point of compliance.
The longitudinal dispersivity is significantly larger than the transverse and vertical dispersivities which
results in long and narrow plumes (Appelo and Postma, 2005; Cook, 2022).

3.3
Retardation
Another relevant process for the transport of contaminants is sorption. Sorption causes partitioning from
one phase to another, such as solid, liquid, and air. The dimensionless retardation factor, R, describes the
retardation of a compound due to sorption. This factor describes the ratio between the total contaminant
mass in a system and the dissolved contaminant mass in the pore space. The retardation factor thus
describes the delay of contaminants compared to water or a tracer (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2000).

Understanding the transport behaviour of PFAS is crucial when assessing the risks at a PFAS-contaminated

3.4
Solid-phase sorption (Kd)
Sorption to solid-phase components in the soil is a major mechanism for retaining PFAS in the subsurface
before it reaches the groundwater (Brusseau, 2023b). Sorption is described through the soil-specific
distribution coefficient Kd (Pancras et al., 2016).
Overall, sorption of PFAS is quantified through two main mechanisms; hydrophobic sorption to naturally
occurring solid organic particles, and surface sorption to charged particles and minerals also known as
electrostatic interactions (Pancras et al., 2016; Adamson et al., 2020). Figure 3.3 shows the different
types of interactions.
Figure 3.3: Adsorption of PFAS on adsorbent by hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions (Lei et al., 2023)
Hydrophobic sorption describes the interaction between a nonpolar hydrophobic compound and its affin-
ity to attach to hydrophobic surfaces, such as soil organic carbon (Pancras et al., 2016; Lei et al., 2023).
The degree at which PFAS sorb to organic carbon can be estimated through the method using the linear
relationship between Koc and foc, using equation 3.8 (Brusseau, 2023b):
Kd = Koc · foc
(3.8)
where Koc is the organic carbon normalised sorption coefficient and foc is the fraction of organic carbon.
Another approach is to estimate Kd experimentally through laboratory batch equilibrium experiments
(OECD test 106) (OECD, 2000).
When Kd is estimated from Koc and foc it is assumed that sorption is based solely on the fraction of organic
carbon governed by the hydrophobic interaction mechanism (Brusseau, 2023b). This is an approach that
has been questioned for PFAS due to their surface-active properties.
The sorption mechanisms for the different PFAS compounds are primarily dependent on their chain
length. Long-chained PFAS have a greater hydrophobicity than the short-chained PFAS and sorption
is primarily mediated by the fraction of organic carbon (Pancras et al., 2016). Therefore, solid-phase
sorption of the long-chained PFAS are primarily driven by hydrophobicity, which can overcome the elec-
trostatic repulsion between the negatively charged anion and the negatively charged soil particle. On

contribute to sorption of the short-chained PFAS (Brusseau, 2023b; Kang et al., 2023). Therefore, the
short-chained PFAS are more sensitive towards deviations in inorganic constituents and clay content. The
mechanism includes electrostatic repulsion and attraction. Electrostatic attraction can occur between the
anionic functional group of PFAS and a positively charged soil particle or inorganic constituents (Pancras

negative soil particle, resulting in greater sorption of PFAS to the soil organic matter (Lei et al., 2023). To

mineralogy and groundwater geochemistry, such as mineral content, is needed (Pancras et al., 2016). A
study by Wang et al. (2021), suggests that interactions with inorganic constituents are rapid compared
to soil organic matter. In aquifers where the organic content usually is low, interactions with inorganic
constituents (electrostatic interactions) may play a key role in the retardation of especially the short-
chained PFAS (Lyu et al., 2023). Electrostatic interactions are also important when PFAS are present as
zwitterions or cations. These PFAS compounds can sorb to anionic soils through electrostatic interactions
(Arcadis, 2023). Zwitterionic and cationic PFAS may also bind to clay minerals through ion exchange
(Pancras et al., 2016).

3.5
Air-water interfacial adsorption
Besides solid-phase sorption, partitioning of PFAS to the air-water interface is also important for the
retention of PFAS in the subsurface (Adamson et al., 2020). The sorption mechanism depends on the
adsorption coefficient Kai and the interfacial area between air and water Aai. These two parameters are
described in the following.
3.5.1
Air-water interfacial adsorption coefficient (Kai)
The interface between air and water is important for many environmental processes such as adsorption
and retention of matter, fluid distributions, and flow (Brusseau, 2023a).
Especially the retention of
matter due to adsorption at the air-water interface has received attention when studying the transport
of PFAS due to their interfacially active properties (Brusseau, 2023a; Lyu et al., 2018). It has been
anticipated that the air-water interface has a significant influence on the retention of PFAS in unsaturated
soils. A study by Lyu et al. (2022) found that air-water interfacial adsorption accounted for more than
63% of the retention for the long-chained PFCAs. However, the air-water interface has also been shown to
influence the transport of other solutes such as hydrocarbon surfactants, hydrophobic organic compounds
and pharmaceuticals (Brusseau, 2023a). To characterise and model the transport of PFAS it is crucial
to determine the air-water interfacial adsorption coefficient, Kai, and how it varies with concentration
(Brusseau, 2021). Kai can be described by the surface excess, Γ, which is the amount of a substance
adsorbed to the air-water interface, and the aqueous-phase concentration, Cw (Brusseau, 2021; Lyu
et al., 2018):
Kai = Γ
Cw
(3.9)
Kai can be estimated by measuring the surface tension as a function of the aqueous concentration and
applied to the Gibbs adsorption equation (Brusseau, 2021). This method has been tested for a wide range
of conditions and has been demonstrated to be representative of transport conditions in the mg/l range

tions usually found in the environment (Lyu et al., 2018; Brusseau, 2021).
Kai can also be estimated from column experiments with a tracer and the substance of concern by mea-
suring the retardation factor and rearranging equation 3.7. Column experiments conducted by Lyu et al.
(2018) found that Kai increased with decreasing concentrations. Another study by Lyu et al. (2022)
found greater adsorption to the air-water interface of the long-chained PFCAs compared to the shorter-
chained PFCAs. In addition, the experiments showed that interfacial adsorption dominated the retention
of long-chained PFCAs while the retention of the shorter-chained PFCAs was dominated by solid-phase
adsorption. This illustrates that Kai is dependent on the concentration, substance, soil type, and environ-
mental conditions(Brusseau, 2023a; Lyu et al., 2022, 2018).
In the literature, there is a discussion about which sorption isotherm best describes Kai in relation to
concentration. Studies by Brusseau (2021), Lyu et al. (2022) and Silva et al. (2021) suggest that the
Langmuir model can be used to predict interfacial adsorption. In that case, Kai approaches a maximum
constant value at low concentrations (Figure 3.4a) where surface tension measurements are inappropri-
ate. However, a study by Schaefer et al. (2019) illustrates that for dilute concentrations a Langmuir-
Freundlich (LF) isotherm where Kai increase with decreasing concentrations may be more appropriate.
Such isotherm predicts up to orders of magnitude greater Kai values compared to the Langmuir model,
as shown in Figure 3.4b. The LF equation is a hybrid between the Langmuir and Freundlich isotherm,
providing the benefits of Langmuir at high concentrations, and Freundlich isotherm at dilute concentra-
tions.

3.5.2
Air-water interfacial area (Aai)
The specific air-water interfacial area (cm2/cm3) is an important parameter for PFAS retention, and
depends on the specific porous media. Column experiments conducted by Lyu et al. (2018) with two
different sands have demonstrated that adsorption to the air-water interface increases with decreasing
water saturation and grain diameter, implying a greater retardation of the substance. It can be determined
by multiple approaches, e.g. measured values and empirical estimations. However, different methods for
determining the interfacial area obtain different results for the same medium, which may impact the
retention significantly and lead to different predictions of the travel time (Brusseau, 2023a). Brusseau
(2023a) found that this difference primarily is caused by contributions of solid-surface roughness, which
is relevant when wetting films are formed at a low water saturation.
Brusseau (2023a) has developed different estimation methods to determine the air-water interfacial. It
should be noted that they are based on three data sets from ideal homogeneous sand.
The first empirical correlation is based on the NBET (Nitrogen-Brunauer-Emmett-Teller) solid surface
area (NBSSA) and the water saturation.
NBSSA can be measured at some commercial laboratories
and is a common method to calculate the specific surface area, usually given in the unit g/cm2. The
NBSSA incorporates solid-surface roughness and is therefore a good estimation method for PFAS and
other interfacially active solutes compared to other estimation methods relying on the specific smooth
solid surface area (Brusseau, 2023a). When using NBSSA in the empirical correlation, the unit should
be cm−1.
The empirical correlation determines the maximum air-water interfacial area from NBET
(761 · logNBSSA −2025), adjusted by the water saturation, Sw:
Aai = (0.83(1 −SW )2 + 0.16(1 −SW )) · (761 · log NBSSA −2025)
(3.11)
It is found that NBSSA increases with decreasing median grain diameter (Bigller et al., 2024). Therefore,
if the NBSSA is unknown, another estimation method using the median grain diameter can be applied
(Brusseau, 2023a):
Aai = (−2.85 · SW + 3.6) · ((1 −SW ) · 3.9 · d−1.2

(3.12)
Interfacial areas can also be measured experimentally. One method which was determined to accurately
represent the air-water interfacial adsorption of PFAS is column experiments where Kai is known and Aai
is the only unknown parameter (Brusseau, 2023a; Brusseau et al., 2020).
Brusseau (2021) found Aai values in the range of 70 to 100 cm−1 for sand with a mean grain diameter
of 0.35 mm and for water saturations around 65%.

Site description
One site which is contaminated with PFAS is the former military air base named Værløse air base located
in Værløse, Denmark. This site will be used throughout this thesis to study the transport of PFAS in the
subsurface.
4.1
The site at Værløse former air base
Værløse air base is a former military air base located in an area of specific interest for drinking water
(NIRAS, 2023). The site has been used as a military camp for The Danish Defence from 1910-1920s. In
the 1920s it became an airfield and the runway was established in 1952 (DMR, 2023). The air base was
active in operation for military purposes until 2004 (DMR, 2023).
4.1.1
Former activities
Some of the activities at Værløse air base include stock of chemicals and fuel, workshops, and firefighting
training sites. These activities have caused widespread contamination of soil and groundwater in the area
(NIRAS, 2023; WSP, 2023).
A former hangar is located in the eastern part of the area (Figure 4.1 and 4.2), which is the area this
thesis will focus on. It was built in 1936 and used for workshops, operation, and service of equipment.
Furthermore, the hangar has also been used for storage of e.g. oil products, lubricants and degreasers.
Appendix A.1 provides a detailed description of the activities. Firefighting training has taken place at
other locations at Værløse air base, but probably not at this site (DMR, 2024).
North-west of the hangar, a paved area is anticipated to be the main source of PFAS contamination at the
site. Figure 4.1A shows a dark area on the pavement, which might indicate fire-fighting activity or spill
of chemicals. Figure 4.1B shows a car ramp located south of the paved area, where chemicals and great
amounts of water presumably have been used. In 1988 (Figure 4.1C) the area was cleared.

The air base is located in the Søndersø Valley (Søndersødalen), which is a 3-4 km wide and 20-40 m deep

4.4
Potential uses of PFAS
PFAS have been used in many products e.g. in firefighting foams, for galvanisation, and in hydraulic
liquids. To assess the uses and possible sources associated with a former military air base the following
is used: a guide for PFAS source differentiation at airports (Anderson et al., 2023), a report mapping
uses of PFAS in different industries (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016b) and results from screenings conducted at sites
owned by the Danish Defence (Forsvarsministeriets Ejendomsstyrelse, 2016).
According to Anderson et al. (2023), the major sources of PFAS contamination at airports are associated
with the use of Aqueous film-forming foams (AFFFs). Activities typically related to the use of AFFFs
include firefighting training activities and fire incident responses such as fuel fires. Furthermore, PFAS
have also been present in some fire suppression systems in hangars where it has been used in emergencies,
tests or accidental activation. PFAS contributions from fire suppression systems can enter the environment
if the release of AFFF infiltrates the ground, enter surface waters through runoff or end up in the pipeline
network.
Releases of PFAS from other activities than AFFF can be associated with aircraft maintenance facilities,
aeronautical manufacturing facilities or hydraulic fluid leaks. Sources from maintenance could include
plating and surface coatings. However, PFAS releases from these sources are typically associated with
much smaller quantities due to typically lower concentrations of PFAS in these products and due to fewer
direct pathways for PFAS to enter the environment compared to the use of AFFF (Anderson et al., 2023).
Miljøstyrelsen (2016b) has looked into reported products used within different industries. It has been
reported that PFOS has been used by the Defence as part of firefighting and surface treatment products
such as metal surface treatment and corrosion inhibitors.
Screenings of PFAS at sites owned by the Danish Ministry of Defence show different sources and activities
that have led to PFAS contamination in soil and groundwater. Most of the screened activities have led to
contamination of the groundwater, while only a few have led to contamination in soil above the guide-
line criteria (Forsvarsministeriets Ejendomsstyrelse, 2016). Based on the screening, it was found that
the highest PFAS contaminations originated from firefighting training sites, which is in accordance with
findings from Anderson et al. (2023). Other activities associated with PFAS contamination include stock
of foam liquids, metal coating workshops, hydraulic oils, painting of aircrafts, waxing/polishing/washing
of aircrafts and weapon workshops (Forsvarsministeriets Ejendomsstyrelse, 2016). It was concluded that
waxing, polishing and washing of aircrafts is a considerable source and up to 800 ng/l was found in the
groundwater downstream to these types of activities.
Data from the U.S. show that PFOS, carboxylic acids (PFCAs) with eight or fewer fluorinated carbon
chains and 6:2 FTS are relatively frequently detected at airports, while the longer chained PFAAs are
less abundant. The most frequently detected compounds and in the highest concentrations at airports
were PFBA, PFBS, PFPeA, PFPeS, PFHxA, PFHxS, PFHpA, PFOA, PFOS, 6:2 FTS (Anderson et al., 2023).
However, it should be noted that these conclusions are based on typical products and procedures in the
U.S., which cannot necessarily be directly applied to Danish cases. A screening of the commonly detected
PFAS at Danish military sites has been conducted by Forsvarsministeriets Ejendomsstyrelse (2016). This

Design and setup of the column experi-
ment
PFAS transport in the unsaturated and saturated zones can be assessed and quantified through model
simulations. To assess the transport of PFAS at the site, transport parameters of the aquifer material
and the specific compounds are of interest. One way to obtain estimates of these parameters is through
laboratory column experiments. Figure 6.1 illustrates the aim of the project, which is to use column
experiments with sand from the site to derive transport parameters using model simulations.
Figure 6.1: Sketch of the project aim; To obtain estimates of parameters to assess the transport of PFAS in the
subsurface through column experiments and model simulations.
Page 28 of 132
==PAGE:40==
Master Thesis
August 2024
When conducting the column experiments, the conditions should be as close to real conditions as pos-
sible to obtain realistic parameter values. However, a completely identical reproduction of the natural
conditions such as geology and differences in beds, geochemistry, flow patterns, infiltration rate, and
ranges, cannot all be met in the laboratory. Furthermore, considerations of the column size, flow rate,
injection concentration, time and chemical composition are also important factors for a realistic and
reproducible experiment. The limitations and deviations from the natural conditions are crucial to be
aware of. This section addresses these limitations together with theoretical and practical challenges for
obtaining reliable results when conducting a column experiment.
Column dimensions
The dimensions of the columns should be chosen mainly for practical purposes, such as fitting the flow
rate so that the differences in the retention time of the compounds are captured while still avoiding an
inordinate experimental period. Additionally, the Peclet number is highly dependent on the length of the
column and the dispersivity. It is desired to obtain a high Peclets number, to assure that the breakthrough
occurs after 1 pore volume. Furthermore, a relatively large cross-sectional area is preferred to avoid
preferential flow along the edge of the column (Miljøstyrelsen, 2003).
The expected breakthrough time, t, of a contaminant can be calculated according to the equation:
t = R · θw · L
I
(6.1)
where R is the retardation factor, θw is the water-filled porosity, L is the length of the column, and I is the
infiltration.
Sediment
The sampling of sediment from the subsurface and the selection of specific sediment types for the ex-
periment should be assessed. This includes considerations regarding the sediments dominating at the
site of concern. To establish reproducible experiments, the column sediment should be homogeneous.
Homogeneity can be established through packing and by selecting a narrow range of grain sizes (Oliviera
et al., 1996). However, when repacking soil in a column, the original structure will be destroyed. By
using an intact core sample, this effect is minimised, however that also comes with a cost as homogeneity
cannot be assured.
Infiltration
For determination of the infiltration rate, the flow capability of the sediment should be evaluated. Addi-
tionally, it is a balance between meeting site-realistic values and obtaining a realistic time frame for the
experiment.
At Zealand, Denmark, the annual average net infiltration is around 200 mm/yr (GEUS, 2021). In a sandy
column experiment with a porosity of 33%, this corresponds to 60 cm/yr, thus a very long experimental
period. Considering the retardation of PFAS, this will result in an unrealistic experimental timeframe.
The advantages would possibly be outweighed by the many practical issues and errors related to such
an experiment. The inflow rate should therefore be feasible within the timeframe of the project. In the
literature, column experiments are reported with a pore water velocity of 25-50 cm/hr (Table 3.1 and
3.3), which is considered representative for transport conditions (Lyu et al., 2022).
Uniform flow
A uniform flow of water is crucial at the inlet of the column. Here, a reliable pump compatible with low
flow rates is necessary. Furthermore, the columns should be properly packed to minimise preferential flow
paths. At the top of the column, a layer of coarse material can be packed to ensure evenly distribution of
Page 29 of 132
==PAGE:41==
Master Thesis
August 2024
the water across the cross-sectional area. Such a layer can also be applied at the bottom of the column
right before the outlet to prevent potential clogging.
The flow direction should also be considered. An upward flow direction is preferred for a saturated
experiment, as it minimises the impact of density-driven effects. However, this is not possible for an
unsaturated experimental setup, and therefore a downward flow direction is nessecary.
Temperature and light
In a natural Danish aquifer the temperature is around 10 ◦C. For column experiments where degradation
either biotically or abiotically can influence the contaminants of interest, it is crucial to conduct the
experiment in 10 ◦C and in darkness or by using light-impenetrable columns. However, considering
the persistence of PFAS, neither temperature nor light is expected to influence the composition of the
compounds.
However, depending on the room temperature, evaporation of water from the sampling vials should be
considered and measured. Evaporation is expected to increase with temperature and the cross-sectional
area of the tip of the sampling vials. Since most PFAS are not volatile, it may potentially result in an
up-concentration if the sampling vials are left open for a longer period of time. To minimise this, small
vial tips are preferred.
Laboratory equipment
The laboratory equipment used in the experiment such as column material, tubes, pumps and vials should
be considered as they can both sorb and release contaminants. Sorption of PFAS to laboratory equipment
may vary depending on the PFAS compound and the matrix used (e.g. distilled water, tap water, sedi-
ments) (Lath et al., 2019).
Multiple column experiments are reported in the literature, where acrylic columns, flow distributors, glass
beads and polypropylene sampling vials have been used. In these experiments, the materials have shown
to have no interferences with PFAS determination (Brusseau et al., 2019; Brusseau, 2018; Brusseau et al.,
2020; Yan et al., 2020; Lyu et al., 2022).
A study by Lath et al. (2019) examined sorption losses from aqueous PFOA solutions in contact with
laboratory tubes and filters of glass and different plastics. They found substantial sorption of PFOA to
polypropylene (up to 45%) and glass (up to 24%). However, the article is inconclusive about which
material is best to use to avoid sorption of PFAS as it might vary widely depending on the conditions,
different suppliers and batches.
Another study conducted by Miljøstyrelsen (2024b) investigated traces of PFAS from different sampling
equipment. The report concludes that polypropylene, rilsan bags and nitrile gloves among other things
are found to be free from PFAS, whereas for example, glass might contain PFAS.
Therefore, it is suggested to test the different laboratory equipment that is in direct contact with PFAS
before the experiment is conducted.
Pulse vs continuous injection
Whether the solution should be injected as a pulse or a continuous injection should be considered when
designing the column experiment. A continuous injection enables the possibility of injecting low con-
centrations close to field levels, as the outlet concentration over time will equal the inlet concentration
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2003). However, depending on the flow rate and experimental timeframe, it requires a
significant amount of stock solution. A pulse injection, on the other hand, requires higher concentrations
as it should still be detectable due to dispersion and retardation. However, it requires a much smaller
Page 30 of 132
==PAGE:42==
Master Thesis
August 2024
amount of stock solution. A pulse injection enables the possibility to investigate mass loss to the column
materials such as acrylic, glass beads etc. Additionally, the dispersion is easier to determine using a pulse
injection, as it affects both the arrival and elution limb of the curve.

6.2
Adjusting a one-dimensional vertical transport model to the
column experiment
Model simulations were used to assess and quantify PFAS transport in the column experiments. The
model used in this project is a modified version of the vertical PFAS transport model developed by Møn-
sted and Haugsted (2024) based on a model by Guo et al. (2020) that runs in COMSOL Multiphysics
version 6.2. The model is modified to fit with the column dimensions and consists of two parts; a flow
model and a transport model. The flow model uses Richards’ equation (COMSOL’s Richards’ equation
interface) to model vertical flow and saturation distribution in the unsaturated zone. The equation de-
scribes the change in water distribution and fluxes based on van Genuchten parameters and hydraulic
conductivity.
A constant water saturation throughout the column length is desired for simplification when conducting
a column experiment to study PFAS transport. To include this in the model, two domains are applied in
the flow model; a column domain and a bottom domain (Figure 6.5). By fixing the head at the bottom
boundary of the bottom domain (Dirichlet boundary condition), the water content remains uniform in
the column domain.
When simulating unsaturated conditions, H0 is set to zero, resulting in a head located at the bottom. This
results in uniform water saturation in the column domain, while the water saturation approaches close
to saturated conditions in the bottom domain. When simulating saturated conditions, the boundary head
is set to H0 = Lcolumn domain + Lbottom domain, which results in a head at the top of the domain (fully
saturated conditions). Both domains consist of one sand layer. At the top of the column domain, a fixed
flux equal to the infiltration rate of the experiment is applied in the flow model. No flow boundaries
(Neumann) at the side ensure that only vertical flow is modelled. At the bottom, a Dirichlet boundary
condition with a fixed head is applied. The flow model runs until steady state (constant water saturation
and hydraulic conductivity), whereafter the last time step is used as an input to the transport model.
For the transport, the two types of retention processes relevant for PFAS (solid-phase and air-water in-
terfacial adsorption) are included. Furthermore, the model includes dispersion and diffusion. This is
modelled through the transport equation:

Discussion of transport parameters
In the following section, the important transport parameters are discussed and related to the presented
results from the column experiments.
7.3.1
Solid-phase sorption (Kd)
Kd values have been derived for the six PFAS compounds based on the saturated column experiment. It
is observed that the Kd value increases with chain length, which is in accordance with observations in
the literature (Lyu et al., 2023). Overall, the solid-phase retention for all compounds is relatively low
for the sand from the site. In the literature, column experiments with a 0.35 mm sand low in organic
carbon (0.04%), also obtain low Kd values (see Table 3.1) (Lyu et al., 2018, 2023; Li et al., 2021; Wang
et al., 2021). The Kd value for PFOA determined in this thesis from the column experiment is found to be
0.06 l/kg, which is within the range of what is reported in literature column experiments (0.01 to 0.08
l/kg). However, the other PFAS compounds have Kd values approximately one order of magnitude lower
compared to what is found from column experiments in the literature (see Table 3.1).
In this project, two approaches have been used to determine solid-phase sorption; column experiments
and laboratory batch experiments. Laboratory batch experiments have been conducted on two types
of sand from the site; the column sand (mixture of sand from 0.5-6 mbgl), and some expected clean
sand collected 2 mbgl. Table 7.8 lists the results. The Kd values are based on linear regression sorption
isotherms between the measured aqueous concentrations and calculated sorbed concentrations at equi-
librium. Appendix C provides a detailed description and discussion of the results. Furthermore, Appendix
H.4 shows all calculations and sorption isotherms for laboratory batch experiment.

A comparison of the two methods for determination of Kd (transport column experiment and laboratory
batch experiment) illustrates that the methods result in different estimations of Kd values for the six
tested compounds. Since Kd laboratory batch experiments have been conducted with the column sand,
it can be excluded that the difference in Kd values is due to different soil properties, e.g. foc, CEC and
clay content. Possible reasons for lower Kd values determined in the column experiment are explained
in the following.
Inspection of the breakthrough curves shows that all PFAS compounds are asymmetric, with extensive
concentration tailing for especially PFHxS, PFOA and PFOS (Figure 7.5). The tailing of the PFAS com-
pounds is much greater compared to the tracer. However, considering the high detection limit of the
tracer, tailing cannot be neglected. The relative difference (∼7%) between the effective water-filled
porosity and the total porosity indicates that immobile water is present in the column, and tailing of the
tracer can be due to diffusion into the immobile zones, even though it cannot be clearly observed. Tailing
can also occur if the Peclet number is low due to a high dispersivity coefficient, resulting in tailing and
early breakthrough of both the tracer and the compounds (Bjerg and Kjeldsen, 2002). This will lead to an
underestimation of Kd. Figure 7.13 shows simulations of bromide and PFOA using different dispersivity
coefficients. As seen in Figure 7.13b, a dispersivity coefficient of 0.5 cm fits the PFOA measurements,
but not the bromide measurements as shown in Figure 7.13a. The dispersivity coefficient is equal for all
compounds in the column and should be calibrated based on the tracer measurements. Therefore, tailing
and early breakthrough of the longer-chained PFAS cannot be explained by a high dispersivity coefficient.
This is also in accordance with the calculated Peclet number (Table 6.2) which is relatively high for the
experiments. Tailing of the compounds may therefore be explained by other mechanisms.

Besides immobile water and a low Peclet number, rate-limited sorption/desorption may also result in
tailing of the compounds. Rate-limited sorption can occur because sorption is slow compared to the flow
velocity, which results in an early breakthrough and/or tailing of the compounds (Fesch et al., 1998). A
relatively sharp front is observed for the breakthrough curves, while the elution limb shows tailing. The
sharp front can be explained by a high flow velocity in the column, resulting in non-equilibrium sorption
to soil organic matter. Tailing is a result of the slow desorption process, which due to a high flow rate
results in non-equilibrium desorption. Rate-limited sorption may therefore underestimate the Kd value
of the compounds (Fesch et al., 1998). This might explain the difference in the Kd values obtained from
the column experiment, which is lower compared to the Kd values determined from the laboratory batch
experiment. Due to a high soil solution ratio in some of the vials in the batch experiment, the samples
were shaken once a day. When shaking the samples, there is a risk of soil abrasion, leading to exposure
of additional sites, which can result in an overestimation of the Kd value in batch experiments, compared
to column experiments (Van Glubt et al., 2021). Another issue with batch experiments, when measuring
sorption of PFAS, is if air is present in the sample vial. As discussed, PFAS can bind to the interface
between air and water, which might result in an overestimation of Kd, due to lower measured water
concentrations. Furthermore, accessibility to sorption sites in column experiments might be lower due
to dense packing (Fesch et al., 1998). A study by Van Glubt et al. (2021) investigated differences in the
sorption isotherms determined from batch and column experiments. The study concludes that the two
methods provide remarkably consistent sorption isotherms with small variability. The same trend is not
fully observed in this thesis.
7.3.2
Interfacial area (Aai)
The air-water interfacial area depends on the porous medium. In the column experiments, the air-water
interfacial area has been determined from the empirical correlation (eq. 3.12) based on the water satu-
ration and the median grain diameter. Both a lower water saturation and lower median grain diameter
results in a higher interfacial area.
A study by El Ouni et al. (2021) determined Aai for sand with a median grain diameter of 0.23 mm
in the range of 60-70 cm−1 for a water saturation of 80%. Overall, these properties are similar to the
conducted column experiment in this thesis, with a water saturation of 70-80% and a median grain
diameter of 0.197 mm. When compared to the column experiment with a similar water saturation (C2,
SW=0.8), the interfacial area is determined to 114 cm−1. Considering the smaller grain size in the
conducted column experiment, the determined interfacial area seems reasonable. Furthermore, since the
sand in the column experiment is relatively uniform (low uniformity coefficient, U=3), it is evaluated that
the empirical correlation gives a reasonable estimate of the interfacial area in the column experiment.
However, if less uniform sand is to be used, it should be considered if another method for determining the
interfacial area provides a more accurate estimate that relies on d10 and d60 rather than a median grain
diameter. In this study, Kai is determined based on a fixed Aai. Considering varying grain sizes, it should
be considered if a more accurate estimate can be obtained using a fixed Kai value from the literature and
use model simulations to calibrate Aai.
One way to obtain a more precise estimate of the air-water interfacial area is to estimate it with NBET
solid surface area (NBSSA). Brusseau (2023a) reports the empirical correlation (eq. 3.11) which uses
NBSSA and the water saturation. The advantage of this method is that it directly measures the specific
surface area of the sediment and thereby takes varying grain sizes and surface roughness into account. A
study by Fiandaca et al. (2018) has measured grain size distribution and NBET from different sandy soils
from the Western part of Denmark (Grindsted Landfill). Table 7.9 lists some parameters for three selected
soil samples from that site, with calculated interfacial area based on the two empirical correlations (eq.
3.11 and 3.12). NBET is multiplied with the dry particle density to obtain the unit cm−1 used in the
equation. The table shows that "sand no. 1" and "sand no. 3" have approximately the same median grain
diameter as the column sand. However, they obtain very different NBET estimates, and thereby also Aai

7.3.3
Air-water interfacial adsorption (Kai)
Based on the unsaturated column experiments, Kai values were derived for the six PFAS compounds.
The Kai values vary with multiple orders of magnitude between the compounds. In addition, it has been
observed that the Kai values increase with increasing chain length, which is in agreement with multiple
studies reported in the literature (Silva et al., 2021; Lyu et al., 2022; Brusseau, 2019). Furthermore, the
individual compounds have Kai values in the same order of magnitude as what is suggested by literature
from column experiments and surface tension measurements (see Table 3.3 and 3.4).
One topic which has been discussed in the literature is which sorption isotherm that provides the best
estimate of Kai in relation to concentration. Schaefer et al. (2019) found that a Freundlich isotherm
where Kai is increasing with decreasing concentrations might be more appropriate at low concentrations
compared to a Langmuir isotherm where Kai approaches a constant value at low concentrations (Figure
3.4). The derived Kai values from the experiments are within similar ranges as Kai values found in similar
column experiments in the literature, even though the experiments in this thesis are conducted at lower
concentrations. This is supporting the theory of Langmuir sorption isotherm. However, the determined
air-water interfacial area is higher in the column experiments compared to the literature, which was
explained by a smaller median grain diameter. However, if Aai is overestimated, the Kai values should be
greater and a Freundlich sorption isotherm would be a possibility.
Another issue addressed in the literature is competitive sorption to the air-water interface (Silva et al.,
2021). At high concentrations, competitive sorption has an impact, resulting in reduced adsorption of the
less surface-active components. However, for PFAS with chain length <8 C, competitive sorption is not
considered relevant for concentrations in the µg/l range (Silva et al., 2021). Since the total concentration
of PFAS injected in the column is approximately 350 µg/l, competitive sorption is considered negligible.
Another issue that literature addresses to be of concern is the ionic strength that influences the air-
water interfacial adsorption (Brusseau and Van Glubt, 2021; Le et al., 2022). The ionic strength of the
water used in these experiments is around 12 mM (Appendix B.4). The tap water used was tapped
continuously, and small variations in the ionic strength are therefore expected. However, a study by
Brusseau and Van Glubt (2021) demonstrated that changes in the ionic strength within environmentally
relevant ranges had minimal impact on the air-water interfacial adsorption. It is therefore not expected
to have influenced the derived Kai values. However, many Kai values reported in the literature are based

on high ionic strengths or deionized water. These conditions are unrealistic for typical environmental
conditions. In such cases, Kai is impacted by the ionic strength.

7.3.4
Fraction associated with air-water interfacial adsorption (fAWIA)
The fraction of total retention, associated with air-water interfacial adsorption, is quantified through
fAWIA (Table 7.7). Overall, PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA and PFOS are highly affected by air-water interfacial
adsorption as fAWIA is above 90%. The short-chained compounds PFPeA and PFBS are not affected by
the air-water interface, while the effect increases for the longer-chained compounds. This tendency is
Page 54 of 132
==PAGE:66==
Master Thesis
August 2024
in accordance with Lyu et al. (2022) who found that an increase in fAWIA was observed with increasing
fluorinated chain length.
Based on the conducted experiments, sorption to the air-water interface seems to be even more relevant
than first anticipated in the literature. In previous sections exact determination of Kai and Aai have
been discussed, however the exact values do not affect the fraction of retention associated with air-water
interfacial adsorption since the two parameters are multiplied.
The derived Kd values from the saturated column experiment were slightly lower compared to findings
from the literature, except for PFOA. A higher Kd value would result in a lower fAWIA. However, the
saturated breakthrough curves seem reliable, and thus it cannot be excluded that air-water interfacial
adsorption accounts for more than 90% of the retention for the longer-chained PFAS studied in this
experiment.
This means that air-water interfacial adsorption plays a key role in aquifers where solid-phase sorption
is generally low, either due to low organic content, clay minerals or inorganic constituents. For soils
(e.g. mull or clay) with naturally higher organic carbon content, the fAWIA is expected to be lower as
solid-phase sorption has a relatively higher impact on the total retention. From the laboratory batch
experiments a Kd value for PFOA for the column sand was determined to be 0.16 l/kg (Table 7.8). Using
this Kd value and the obtained Kai and Aai from the column experiment, a fAWIA of 79% is obtained.
This illustrates that the fAWIA is highly dependent on both the magnitude of Kd and Kai multiplied by
Aai.
7.4
Learnings from column experiments
This thesis shows that column experiments with Danish sediments prove to be a successful method for
determining relevant PFAS transport parameters. The following section presents practical insights gained
from the column experiments that can be applied in future work.
The column experiments were performed as three separate experiments, where two were unsaturated
with two different water saturations and one was saturated. Due to differences in the experimental
setup, the three experiments had different pore water velocities, as listed in Table 6.2. All experiments
were conducted at much higher velocities compared to environmentally realistic infiltration rates due to
practicalities in the laboratory and the experimental timeframe. However, column experiments reported
in the literature, considered to produce results that are representative of transport conditions, are per-
formed at significantly higher pore water velocities (in the range 25 to 50 cm/hr) (Lyu et al., 2022)
compared to what was used in these experiments.
In the unsaturated column experiments, PFOS was never detected due to the large retention associated
with the air-water interface. To be able to detect PFOS in future column experiments and determine its
Kai value, a higher flow velocity or shorter column should be considered to obtain a faster breakthrough
while still obtaining a high Peclet number. However, a higher flow velocity also results in a higher water
saturation, unless a vacuum pump is used. This can be compensated by using a homogeneous sediment
with a larger median grain diameter.
Results from the column experiments showed that the expected saturated column was not saturated in
the beginning due to the presence of air. To avoid this, the column should be left saturated for some time
to ensure that all air have escaped and the column is fully saturated before the experiment is initiated.
One way to check if the column is fully saturated is by weighing the column and calculating the volume
of sediment and water using a mass balance (as done in Appendix E.4). Another approach could be to
pack the column wet. However, this makes it more difficult to determine the porosity of the column.
Furthermore, it is recommended to perform the saturated experiment with an inflow from the bottom
and an outflow from the top. Thereby, a water table at the top of the column is avoided.

The water saturation was investigated after the experiment was conducted by opening the column and
weighing the sand in six fractions throughout the column length. A recommendation for future column
experiments is to build a column comprised of segmented rings, which ensures that the volume of sand
in each fraction is uniform for a more precise estimate of the water saturation as described in Bigler et al.
(2024). This also enables a precise estimate of the porosity of the sediment in each fraction as the volume
of each segmented ring is known.
During the experiment, pH and temperature were measured. pH was measured from the inlet water to
7.8 which is in accordance with what is reported from the local water work (Appendix B.4). A minor
increase was measured in the effluent samples (pH ≈8.3) likely due to evaporation of CO2. Since PFAS
have very low pKa values they remain anionic within this pH range. The temperature was measured to
21 ± 2◦C. Due to the use of a sampling carrousel where samples were left without a cap for multiple
hours. It has been speculated whether evaporation of water from the samples has occurred which could
potentially influence the measurements. The naturally present chloride in the tap water has been used as
a tracer and no clear trend is observed between the chloride concentration and the time each vial was left
without a cap (see Appendix E.8). It is therefore concluded that evaporation has not affected the results.
This is also in accordance with already presented results which seem reliable. However, to minimise
evaporation in future column experiments, the experiments could be conducted at lower temperatures.

added to the top and bottom of the column for further distribution of the water.

8.1
Distribution in pore water
Pore water measurements show high concentrations at PV203 at the northern part of the paved area. The
composition is dominated by PFOS (340 ng/l out of sum 22 of 360 ng/l). At the other pore water sample
locations, multiple PFAS compounds are detected in low concentrations. From the column experiments,
it was concluded that air-water interfacial adsorption has a great impact on the retention of the longer-
chained PFAS in the unsaturated zone, especially PFOS. High sorption of PFOS to the air-water interface
might explain why high concentrations of PFOS is measured in the pore water at the site.
Adsorption in the unsaturated zone is highly dependent on the water saturation. At the site, the wa-
ter saturation fluctuates due to seasonal variations. During the summer, it is expected that the water
saturation decreases in the unsaturated zone resulting in higher retardation of the compounds, while it
increases during winter. The travel time of the compounds in the unsaturated zone is therefore expected
to be highly dependent on seasonal variations. Appendix G.1 shows the measured water-filled porosity
in the two soil cores.

The flow direction was found to be south-southeast, which is in accordance with previous investigations
(DMR, 2024). The mean gradient was found to be 0.0078 m/m based on well soundings from 02-02-

8.5
Assessment of PFAS transport at the site
To assess the determined sorption parameters; Kd and Kai, travel times of PFAS are calculated and
compared to the measurements from the site. Based on Figure 8.6 and 8.7 the suggested source area is
located at the northern part of the paved area. Combined with the historical aerial photos, the spill is
estimated to have occurred between the 1970s and 1990s. Therefore, a spill is simulated and the spatial
distribution of the compounds in the subsurface is assessed after 30 and 50 year.
Transport of the compounds can be divided into vertical transport in the unsaturated zone and horizontal
transport in the saturated zone. For simplification, the geology is initially assumed to consist of one
uniform sand layer. This assumption produces faster travel times than what is expected at the site as the
geology in reality is heterogeneous with less permeable lenses of silt and clay. Furthermore, solid-phase
sorption of PFAS is greater in such layers. Appendix G.2 provides an overview of the model setup and
parameters. Appendix H.8 provides all model simulations and calculations for the study site.
8.5.1
Vertical transport in the unsaturated zone
Transport mechanisms in the unsaturated zone are complex due to air-water interfacial adsorption and
varying water saturation, which affects the interfacial area. Assessment of PFAS transport in the unsatu-
rated zone at the site is therefore quantified through model simulations with the PFAS transport model.
To obtain a realistic estimate of the travel time, it is important to be aware of the different parameter
values for field conditions compared to the column experiments, such as variations in geology and net
infiltration.
For the simulations, it is assumed that the median grain diameter and van Genuchten parameters are the
same as those determined in the column experiment. Furthermore, the dispersivity coefficient remains
unchanged, even though it is expected that the longitudinal dispersivity coefficient is larger for field
conditions (up to orders of magnitude higher (Appelo and Postma, 2005)), compared to the column ex-
periments. However, this does not affect the travel time. The dispersivity and van Genuchten parameters
depend on the geology and sediment packing. Furthermore, it is known that the median grain diameter
varies in space. However, to avoid adjusting too many parameters, it is assumed that they are similar to
the parameters determined in the column experiment.
The model is adjusted to match the depth of the unsaturated zone, which was measured to 3 m at
the time the field work was conducted. Fluctuations in the water table is expected to fluctuate due to
seasonalities and 3 meters represents a relatively high water table at the site. Therefore, an assumption of
an unsaturated zone of 3 m result in PFAS reaching the saturated zone earlier. The empirical correlation
(eq. 3.12) is used to determine the air-water interfacial area, using the median grain diameter and the
water saturation. It is assumed that it provides a reasonable estimate when assuming that the unsaturated
zone consists of homogeneous sand. Lastly, the Kai and Kd values determined in the column experiment
are used (Table 7.7).
The infiltration rate differs for field conditions compared to the column experiment. Net infiltration
varies in time and space and depends on the geology and ground cover. To obtain an accurate estimate
of the net infiltration, fluctuations of the groundwater table should be monitored to analyse the impacts
of seasonalities and vegetation. Frederiksen et al. (2023) determined a net infiltration of 120 mm/yr
at a site also located in the northern part of Zealand, Denmark, where the upper geology is dominated
by clayey till. At the site in Værløse, sand dominates in the unsaturated zone, and the net infiltration is
presumably higher. Both the net infiltration and the hydraulic conductivity affect the water content in the
unsaturated zone. Larsen (2024) suggests a net infiltration of 200 mm/yr for an area located close to the
site with low vegetation. Previous investigations at Værløse air base conducted by Grathwohl et al. (2003)

Chapter 9
General conceptual understanding of PFAS
transport in the subsurface
This thesis clarifies the important processes that drive the transport of PFAS in the subsurface, both in the
unsaturated and saturated zone. In the unsaturated zone, retention of PFAS is driven by both solid-phase
sorption and air-water interfacial adsorption while in the saturated zone, retention is only driven by
solid-phase sorption. The unsaturated zone plays a crucial role for the understanding of PFAS transport
due to the contribution from these two processes which is unique for PFAS compared to the usual studied
contaminants.
The shorter-chained PFAS are found to be less impacted by both solid-phase sorption and air-water inter-
facial adsorption compared to the longer-chained PFAS. These significant differences in PFAS properties
likely result in plumes dominated by the shorter-chained compounds at the fringe, while the longer-
chained PFAS are heavily retained in the unsaturated zone. With time, this may result in very long
plumes of PFAS, where the short-chained compounds dominate at the plume fringe and the long-chained
compounds dominate closer to the source area. This is especially important when assessing the contami-
nant flux. An estimation of the flux should be conducted for each compound instead of the sum of PFAS
due to the very different transport properties of the compounds, especially when a deep unsaturated zone
is present.
It is important to note that the retention of all PFAS depends on site-specific conditions, including geology,
water saturation, and the depth of the unsaturated zone. At sites with a very deep unsaturated zone,
longer-chained PFAS can potentially be retained for many decades. However, once the longer-chained
PFAS reach the saturated zone, they become relatively mobile and can be transported rapidly with the
groundwater flow.
Adsorption to the air-water interface is highly dependent on the porous media and its water saturation.
The water saturation of the unsaturated zone is governed by the infiltration rate, which varies in time
and space. During the winter, where the net infiltration is generally high, the water saturation increases
resulting in a smaller air-water interfacial area and therefore fast transport of PFAS towards the saturated
zone. On the other hand, during summer, where the water saturation is generally low, PFAS is highly
retained in the unsaturated zone due to a large air-water interfacial area.
The specific porous media is also crucial when assessing the air-water interfacial adsorption. Finer sedi-
ments, such as clay or silt, significantly increase the air-water interfacial area leading to greater retention
of PFAS in the unsaturated zone. Consequently, the presence of clay and silt lenses in the unsaturated
zone can result in prolonged PFAS retention, whereas in a homogeneous sandy unsaturated zone, PFAS
may be transported more rapidly toward the groundwater.
In Denmark, clayey till is commonly present in the subsurface. It is well known that clayey till is hetero-
geneous and often fractured. Fractures often have a high water saturation and thereby a low air-water
interfacial area (Zeng and Guo, 2023). Therefore, fractures may act as preferential flow paths. In terms
of PFAS, this means that fractures have the potential to accelerate the transport of PFAS toward the

Chapter 10
Future perspectives
When assessing the risk at a PFAS-contaminated site, several factors are crucial to determine. The un-
saturated zone proved to significantly impact the total retention of the compounds. The shorter-chained
compounds shows to be more mobile and may spread over larger distances, while the longer-chained
compounds are strongly retained in the unsaturated zone (up to decades). The unsaturated zone can
therefore serve as a long-term source of PFAS contamination to the underlying groundwater. Under-
standing the transport mechanisms of PFAS in the subsurface therefore is crucial when assessing the risk
and evaluating future remedial actions at PFAS-contaminated sites. The following present recommenda-
tions for future investigations needed for a sufficient risk assessment.
To obtain reliable estimates of the transport time, a collection of Kai and Kd values shall be prepared
based on experiments and literature findings. However, only a few Kd experiments have been conducted
on typical Danish soils, which likely result in different estimates due to different geological and chemical
compositions. Therefore, it is suggested to derive relevant Kd values which can be applied when assessing
transport at Danish sites.
Kai and how depends on concentration has been greatly discussed in the literature. Assuming a Langmuir
sorption isotherm, Kai approaches a constant maximum value at low concentrations. For Danish cases
where PFAS are found in ng/l or µg/l ranges, it is therefore reasonable to assume a constant Kai value. It
was found that small deviations in Kai result in significant changes in the travel time. Thus, a robust es-
timation is needed. Column experiments have shown to produce estimates of Kai at low concentrations,
where surface tension measurements are questionable. However, only a few column experiments at low
concentrations are reported in the literature. Therefore, to extend the robustness of Kai values at envi-
ronmentally relevant concentrations, multiple column experiments at low input concentrations should be
performed. Furthermore, for Danish cases where Kai can be assumed constant, column experiments with
different types of sediment can provide valuable information on how Aai varies.
Determination of Aai is crucial when assessing the fate of PFAS. Aai is highly dependent on the water
saturation, the soil texture, and grain size distribution. Determination of the air-water interfacial area
and how it varies with water saturation and geological beds is crucial, since the magnitude of Aai directly
governs adsorption to the air-water interface and thus retention of the compounds. One approach, relying
on an empirical correlation, has been deemed to produce reasonable estimates of Aai for homogeneous
sandy materials based on the water saturation and median grain diameter of the sediment. However, the
empirical correlation does not account for heterogeneities. Therefore, it is suggested to further research
the variations in Aai depending on heterogeneities. Furthermore, more research is needed to assess if
empirical correlations based on NBET or the uniformity coefficient can provide more robust estimates of
the interfacial area.
Model simulations can provide valuable information regarding PFAS transport. Transport of PFAS is
highly dependent on the water saturation, which is described by the van Genuchten parameters. van
Genuchten parameters may be obtained through soil water retention curves (SWRC). Therefore, ex-
periments are needed to produce SWRC and to determine van Genuchten parameters for Danish soils.
Furthermore, studies have also shown that Aai can be estimated from SWRC (Silva et al., 2022).
To summarize, it is recommended that research institutes obtain knowledge and collect the following

data to provide parameter estimates that can be used when evaluating the fate and risk of PFAS at a
contaminated site:
• Create a collection of Kd values for different Danish soils.
• Create a collection of Kai values at environmentally low concentrations.
• Determination of SWRC for different soils to obtain van Genuchten parameters (α and n).
• Establish empirical correlations for Aai relevant for Danish soils.
The above-mentioned knowledge should be assembled for field-scale application when assessing the risk
at a specific PFAS-contaminated site. Furthermore, other parameters mentioned in the following should
be determined based on the site-specific physical and chemical conditions as part of a risk assessment.
It is general practice to characterise the geology when investigating a contaminated site. This knowledge
can be used to determine relevant Kd values. Additionally, it is recommended to determine the fraction
of organic carbon for a more precise estimate of Kd values. Furthermore, it is suggested to collect soil
samples for determination of water content and grain size analysis for application of empirical correla-
tions for Aai estimation. Samples for determination of Kai are not necessary since it does not dependent
on the geology, but on the concentration. As mentioned, for Danish cases, Kai is assumed to be constant.
Thus, determined values from laboratory experiments can be applied directly to evaluate transport times
at field scale, depending on the compounds of interest.
Besides these mentioned parameters, fluctuations in the groundwater table should be considered. Fluctu-
ations in the groundwater might mobilise/retain PFAS in the unsaturated zone during periods with heavy
precipitation or draught, which impacts their leaching time to the groundwater.
In Denmark, the subsurface is heterogeneous. It is well known that subsurface heterogeneities can cause
preferential flow paths due to the presence of macropores and fractures in clayey till. It is hypothesised
that fractures can accelerate the leaching of PFAS from the unsaturated zone. Fractures usually have
a higher water saturation and low air-water interfacial area, which reduces the air-water interfacial
adsorption. In that case, the long-chained PFAS might have a short retention time compared to a sandy
unsaturated zone, where the compounds can be retained for many decades.
To assess the transport time of PFAS in the subsurface, several physical parameters must be determined
and evaluated.
Firstly, measuring the depth of the unsaturated zone is crucial, as this zone retains
the longer-chained PFAS significantly.
Additionally, physical parameters such as the infiltration rate,
hydraulic gradient and hydraulic conductivity can be used to assess the transport time in the unsaturated
and saturated zones.
Based on this, it is recommended to measure and assess the following parameters and conditions as part
of field investigations used for PFAS risk assessment:
• Assess the depth of the unsaturated zone
• Measure the water content in the unsaturated zone
• Conduct grain size analysis from soil samples
• Assess the heterogeneity
• Assess infiltration rate, hydraulic conductivity (slug test or permeameter test) and hydraulic gradi-
ent
• Select relevant Kd and Kai values based on site-specific conditions
• Determine Aai based on empirical correlations using geological characterisation or NBET measure-
ments

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This project has aimed to assess the transport of PFAS based on an improved understanding of their be-
haviour in the unsaturated and saturated zone at a former military air base. Laboratory results have been
conducted to evaluate and assess the most influential parameters for PFAS transport. This project has
contributed to improved knowledge and understanding of PFAS transport and parameter determination,
which can be applied in future risk assessments at PFAS-contaminated sites.
PFAS are surface-active compounds. Their ability for solid-phase and air-water interfacial adsorption
increases with chain length. Adsorption to the air-water interface consists of a chemically dependent
contribution (Kai) and a component depending on the physical field conditions (Aai). The magnitude of
Kai is increasing with decreasing concentrations, approaching a plateau at low concentrations.
This study has investigated PFAS transport in column experiments at unsaturated and saturated condi-
tions with a focus on the six PFAS compounds; PFPeA, PFBS, PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA and PFOS. Model
simulations using a PFAS transport model have been shown to effectively predict transport parameters
for the column experiments. Kd values determined from the saturated column experiment were overall
low, ranging from 0 to 0.057 l/kg. From this, Kai has successfully been determined for the compounds.
Findings agree with reported values in the literature. Moreover, this study supports that solid-phase
sorption and air-water interfacial adsorption increase with increasing chain length. This shows that col-
umn experiments are an effective and reliable method for generating transport parameters relevant for
PFAS-contaminated sites.
Column experiments showed that short-chained PFAS experience minor retention during transport both in
the unsaturated and saturated zone. For the longer-chained compounds, air-water interfacial adsorption
is the predominant contributor to retention in the unsaturated zone. This agrees with findings reported
in the literature. The fraction of total retention due to air-water interfacial adsorption is found to be
more than 90% for PFHxA, PFHxS, PFOA and PFOS in the unsaturated column experiments with fine
sand. This illustrates that retention in the unsaturated zone might be even more important than first
anticipated. Furthermore, it highlights that the relative contributor of air-water interfacial adsorption
compared to solid phase sorption (fAWIA) depends upon the porous medium. In an aquifer with glacial
sand deposits and low organic carbon content, the retention due to the air-water interface is expected
to be high. Solid-phase sorption impacts the retention relatively more for soils with a naturally higher
organic carbon content such as mull or clay. This has significant implications when assessing the transport
and leaching of PFAS in the unsaturated zone.
The specific air-water interfacial area is found to highly impact the estimation of transport time in the
unsaturated zone and a more precise estimation is therefore crucial. It is found that the interfacial area
depends on the grain size distribution of the porous medium and the water saturation. It is believed
that the interfacial area increases with decreasing median grain diameter or greater heterogeneity. More
research is needed to quantify how the interfacial area depends on heterogeneity and to develop new
estimation methods for practical implications.
At the former air base, the composition of PFAS in the shallow groundwater is dominated by PFHxS,
PFHpS, PFOA, and PFOS. Results from pore water samples show that PFOS is the dominating compound

Simulations of transport in the unsaturated zone showed that minor deviations in Kai have a major impact

This indicates that clay or silt lenses consisting of very fine sediments have the potential to increase the

the determination of Aai for multiple soil types and heterogeneous sediments.
This project has contributed to extended knowledge on PFAS transport in the unsaturated and saturated
zone and the determination of important PFAS transport parameters. This provides valuable insights for
future field investigations and risk assessments, particularly in a Danish context.

B.2
Grain size distribution - procedure
Grain size analyses have been conducted on the sand from the site used for column experiments and soil
extracted from the soil cores.
For the grain size analysis, 80-120 g dry sample was sampled into a 250 ml plastic bottle and 100-150
ml water was added. The plastic bottle were clamped to a rotating apparatus for 18 hours to disintegrate
strongly aggregated particles. Afterwards, the samples were sieved through a 0.063 mm sieve. The sieve
was placed in a funnel on a glass bottle and water was added until clear water came through the sieve.
The coarse fraction was transferred to a metal tray and dried at 60 ◦C for 3 days. The 0.063 mm sieve
was thoroughly cleaned between each sample.
The grain size distribution of the coarser fraction is determined from 9 sieves with the following mesh
sizes: 2.0-1.0-0.6-0.355-0.250-0.180-0.125-0.090-0.063 mm. Each sieve was weighted. The oven-dried
coarse fraction was weighted and placed on the upper 2 mm sieve. The sieves were placed on a sieving
machine, the cover was fastened, and the machine was switched on for 15 minutes at amplitude 1.50.
Afterwards, each sieve with sample was weighted again. The fraction <0.063 mm was added to the
suspension in the glass bottle.
The suspension with the fraction <0.063 mm was mixed, and a few drops were added to a laser diffrac-
tometer to measure the clay and silt content.

Appendix C
Kd laboratory batch experiments
This appendix provides information regarding the laboratory batch experiments, including determined Kd
values, background concentrations of the soil, and Kd values determined by Ghazaleh (2024) on some other
Danish soils.
C.1
Kd from sorption isotherms
Kd laboratory batch experiments have been conducted with the three dominant soil types at the site;
topsoil (mull), sand and clayey till. Besides these soils, the column sand was also used in the batch
experiment to enable comparison with the column experiments. Before the batch experiments were
conducted, the soils were analysed for total concentration of PFAS, the results are shown in Appendix C.2.
Furthermore, experiments have been conducted with the soil and clean water to examine the desorbed
concentrations. These experiments are used to account for potential background concentration of PFAS
in the samples. All calculations and regressions can be seen in Appendix H.4.
The sorption isotherms are determined from a linear regression between the measured aqueous concen-
tration at equilibrium (after 7 days). The results are shown in Table C.1.

compound to provide representative Kd values. However, the short-chained PFAS are not expected to

have a removal percent between 20-80% except for PFOA in sand (removal <20%) and PFOS in clayey
till for the high soil solution ratio (removal> 80%).

Overall, Table C.1 illustrates that the Kd values increase with the chain length, as expected from the
literature. Furthermore, the Kd value is generally highest for mull and clayey till, which have a higher
organic content compared to the sand. This is very pronounced for PFOS, due to its higher hydrophobicity
compared to the shorter chained compounds. The linear regressions shows overall a good fit between the
sorbed and aqueous concentration of each individual compound.
The Kd value determined from the low soil solution ratio is systematically higher than when Kd are
determined from the high soil solution ratio, except for PFOA in sand and PFOS in mull and clayey till.
The explanation might be that not all grains in the high soil solution ratio have been exposed to the PFAS
solution, which results in a lower Kd value. During the experiment, it was observed that the soil in the
samples with a high soil solution ratio were not fully suspended. Therefore, the vials were additionally
shaken manually once a day. For future experiments, it is suggested to use another rotator to mix the vials
or use a lower soil solution ratio to ensure that the soil is fully suspended throughout the experimental
period.
The Kd values determined from the sand and column sand is overall in accordance with reportings from
the literature.
For instance, Nguyen et al. (2020) report Kd values for PFPeA in sand of 0.04 l/kg,
compared to 0.041-0.15 l/kg determined in these experiments (Table 3.2).
The Kd value for PFOA in sand in these experiments have been determined to 0.045-0.16 l/kg, which
is lower than what is reported by Nguyen et al. (2020) (Table 3.2) of 0.53 l/kg. A study by (Ghazaleh,
2024) determined Kd values on Danish soils, and determined a Kd value for PFOA in sand of 0.04-0.4
l/kg (listed in Appendix C.3), which is comparable to what was determined in these experiments.
For clayey till, the lowest determined Kd value for all compounds is comparable to what is reported in
the literature for loam which has a similar organic carbon content (Nguyen et al., 2020) (Table 3.2). For
instance, PFHxS have been determined to 0.48-1.4 l/kg, which is reported to 0.45 l/kg by Nguyen et al.
(2020). For PFOS the Kd value for loam is reported to 4.2 l/kg, and is in these experiments determined
to 5.4-11.9 l/kg. Ghazaleh (2024) determined a Kd value for PFOS in clayey till from a Danish site of
3-6.3 l/kg, which is similar to the lowest determined Kd value from these experiments.
For mull, Ghazaleh (2024) have determined some Kd values of mull from a Danish site. Compared to
these Kd values, the Kd values determined in these experiments are overall higher. For instance, the Kd
value for PFPeA have been reported to 0.10-0.12 l/kg, which is these experiments have been determined

Appendix D
Analytical description of the chemical
analyses
Samples collected by DMR (2024) have been analysed at a commercial accredit laboratory. Samples collected
as part of this thesis have been analysed by the laboratory at the Technical University of Denmark. The
description of the analyses have been provided by Olsson (2024) and can be seen below:
D.1
PFAS soil samples
5 g sample were extracted by 40 ml Methanol overnight at room temperature. The extracts were evapo-
rated to dryness using a gentle stream of Nitrogen and resuspended in 1 ml Ammonium acetate:Methanol
(50:50).
D.2
PFAS water samples
The PFAS were analyzed by High Performance liquid Chromatography – Triple Quadrupole Mass Spec-
trometry (1290 Infinity II- 6470 LC-QQQ, Agilent Technologies).
Chromatographic separation was achieved on a 2.1 x 50 mm, 1.8 micron reversed phase column (Zorbax
EclipsePlus C18 RR, Agilent Technologies), with 10 mM Ammonium acetate pH 4 as aqueous phase (A)
and 95 % Methanol with 10 mM Ammonium acetate pH 4 (B) using the following gradient: 90% A for
0.5 min, then up to 30% B in 1.5 min, then up to 100 % B in 12.5 min and held for 3.5 min with a column
regeneration time of 5 min. The flow is kept at 0.5ml/min and the column temperature at 50 °C.
Detection was achieved in ESI- mode using Dynamic Multiple Reaction Monitoring (dMRM) with the
following transitions: The source temperature was kept at 230 °C with 4 l/min nebulizer gas flow and
the sheath gas at 360 °C at 12 l/min. The collision energy and fragmentor voltage were optimized for
each compound.

Principle 
 
Anions in aqueous samples are analyzed by ion-chromatography (IC) – suppressed conductivity detection using 
and anion-exchange column under isocratic conditions with a carbonate/bicarbonate eluents system. No pre- 
treatment of samples other than filtration are needed. Anions included in the analysis are 
 
Chloride (Cl-) 
Bromide (Br-) 
Fluoride (F-) 
Nitrate (NO3 ) 
Nitrite (NO2 ) 
Phosphate (PO4   ) 
3- 
 
Sulfate (SO4   ) 
2- 
 
 
And the quantification limit is 0.5mg/l. 
 
 
Chromatography 
 
The anions are separated on an anion-exchange column  equipped with a guard column (IonPac AS22, 4 x 250 
mm, Ionpac AS22 Guard column, 4 x 50 mm, Thermo Scientific). Separation is achieved at isocratic conditions 
with 4.5 mM bicarbonate/ 1.4 mM carbonate as eluant. The flow is kept constant at 1,2 ml/min, the column 
compartment at 20 degrees and the total analysis time is 13 min per sample (Appendix A). A 200 µl sample loop 
is used, enabling an injection volume up to 100 µl. 
 
Suppressed conductivity detection 
 
In order to remove background conductivity from eluent and sample counterions the eluent is converted tio a 
weakly dissociated form by the suppressor. The Dionex AERS 500 is an electrolytically regenerated suppressor 
enabeling it to operated continuously with a water source as a regenarent. In the present method the suppressed 
eluent is directed back into the suppressor and used as the regenerant water source making the operation of the 
suppressor effortless (auto suppression recycle mode). It should be noted however that the recycle mode is 
recommended for matrices containing little or no precipitating ions such as transition ions, calcium or magnesium. 
If any of these contaminants are present an external water mode should be used where the regenerant water source 
is added externally using a peristaltic pump. See the Dionex ERS 500 Suppressor manual. 
 
The suppressor (Dionex AERS 500 for Carbonate, 4 mm, ThermoScientific) is operated at 41 mA when using the 
chromatographic conditions above with the compartment held at 20 °C. Detection is achieved by a conductivity 
detector (ICS-5000 Conductivity detector, ThermoScientific) held at 20 °C.

E.2.1
Packing techniques
The packing technique is important to consider when conducting column experiments to avoid air pockets
and preferential flow paths. Before the columns were packed with sand from the site, different packing
techniques were tested with some test sand. Initially, test sand was poured into the column while gen-
tly shaking the column. This resulted in big air/water pockets when applying flow. To avoid the big
air/water pockets, another packing technique was tested. Sand was poured into the column in layers of
approximately 5 cm in thickness and stamped to compress each layer. This resulted in an uneven distri-
bution of water in the column (see Figure E.3). From the distribution of water in the column, it seems
like stamping of thick layers of sand results in the upper part of each layer being more densely packed.
This will result in smaller pore volumes and thus a higher capillary pressure of the upper part of each
layer. A high capillary pressure is able to hold more water, which can explain the stripes of water (Figure
E.3). To avoid this problem, the columns were packed in thin layers (0.5 cm in thickness) as suggested
in Oliviera et al. (1996). The finial packing technique is described in section E.2.2. The even distribution
of water when saturating the columns can be seen in section E.2.3.

F.5
Simulation of PFPeA breakthrough for column C2
For the unsaturated column C1, the total effective porosity and effective water-filled porosity is known
from weighing the column and from the tracer breakthrough curve. However, for the unsaturated column
C2, the tracer breakthrough was not measured due to a faster transport than initially expected. The
effective water-filled porosity is therefore unknown.
However, because PFPeA and bromide has approximately the same breakthrough time in column C1 and
a low retardation in both the saturated (C2) and unsaturated (C1) experiment, it is assumed that PFPeA
can be used to simulate the tracer breakthrough. Therefore, PFPeA is used to calibrate the effective
water-filled porosity for column C2 as shown in Figure F.12 until a reasonable fit is observed between the
measured values and the model simulation.
First, the breakthrough was modelled with a total effective porosity equal to the measured water content
of the column (0.254). With a porosity of 0.254 the model calculates an effective water-filled porosity of
0.234. However, the model predicts that the breakthrough occurs later than measured, which indicates
that there are some immobile zones of water, which contributes to the overall water content, but does
not contribute to transport of the compounds. The best fit was observed with an effective water-filled
porosity of 0.216, as shown in Figure F.12.
