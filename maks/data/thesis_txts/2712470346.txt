2. Executive Summary  
 
This project focuses on two important for our environment technologies: a technology that transforms 
renewable energy from solar or wind energy parks into other forms of power. The so-called Power-to-X 
(PtX) is about a tremendous resource for the continuous green transition of our global community, for 
instance in the form of green hydrogen or green methanol, that can substitute the fossil-based fuels. At the 
core of most Power-to-X principles is the use of renewable power for the water electrolysis to produce 
hydrogen. The above hydrogen is used directly as a final energy carrier or transformed into methanol, 
synthesis gas, liquid fuels or chemicals, for instance.  
The second technology presented in this study is the wastewater treatment technology and especially its 
aeration process, the biological decomposition of the organic components, which is achieved in the aeration 
tank. The most common method of biological treatment of liquid waste is the method of activated sludge. 
As air is drained into the aeration tank, the microorganisms present in the effluent consume the organic 
matter, and then agglomerate to produce an active mass of agglomerates, called active sludge. Air is 
introduced into the tank usually using diffusers. The use of the organic fraction of waste as food of 
microorganisms results in the conversion of a part into simple final products - such as water and carbon 
dioxide - while the rest is converted into cellular material of organisms. This not only renews the population 
of microorganisms, but also achieves the biological degradation of organic components. 
The location which was taken as reference for the above two technologies to be implemented, is the island 
of Bornholm, in Denmark. Since Bornholm is now getting an electricity hub which will use electrolyzer-
based PtX facilities to enable its green transition, the purpose of this project is to examine the viability of 
integrating the oxygen byproduct of a large-scale electrolyzer into local industrial facilities and processes, 
like wastewater treatment. To enable a quantitative evaluation of its techno-economic viability, a 
mathematical model was implemented. 
The calculations of this project are formed as a continuation of the white paper of DTU “Optimal placement 
of P2X facility in conjunction with Bornholm energy island”[1] . Bornholm's electricity generation utilizes 
37 MW of onshore wind turbines. The process of electrolysis takes place in a 27.6 MW electrolyzer, which 
is being used in the mathematical model’s calculation in order to retrieve its annual oxygen production 
(23.123 tonnes). Given the yearly oxygen demand (1.733,750 tonnes) for the aeration process inside a 
50.000 PE wastewater treatment plant [2], it is concluded that the integration of the PtX’s produced oxygen 
has an economical benefit, compared to a scenario in which oxygen is not being used. The energy savings 
correspond to 282,375 MWh/year, translated to economical savings of 28.237 EUR/year.

3. Introduction 
 
Green technologies such as Power-to-X are critical in the development of a carbon free energy system. 
Wind and solar power can be turned into hydrogen or hydrogen-based chemicals such as ammonia, 
methanol, and methane and used as green fuels in vessels, aviation, vehicles, and heavy industry using this 
technology. 
This enables Power-to-X to contribute to resolving our climate concerns. Below an illustration (Figure 1) 
of how Power-to-X can fit into a smart energy system is being presented.

Power-to-X plays a key role in the future energy system, supplying sustainable fuels to the transportation 
sector while also assuring that energy can be stored for later utilization. This indicates that the energy 
system will succeed only if Power-to-X technology works. 
PtX is based on electrolysis, splitting water into oxygen and hydrogen using electricity. At the moment, 
Denmark's hydrogen usage is quite low and mostly confined to refineries where it is utilized to manufacture 
fossil fuels. Additionally, hydrogen can be utilized as a key component to produce a variety of other fuels 
and goods.[3]

3.1 Water Electrolysis 
 
The fundamental principle of electrolysis is the use of electricity to separate water into oxygen and 
hydrogen. In the electrolysis cell, the splitting occurs through two partial reactions at the cathode (-) and 
anode (+) electrodes. In practice, electrolyzers are composed of a number of linked electrolysis cells, also 
known as stacks. When voltage applied, the cathode produces hydrogen and the anode produces oxygen.[4] 
In between the two partial reactions, charge equalization occurs via ion conduction through an electrolyte, 
an electrically conductive substance. A membrane is also required to spatially separate the two reactions 
and avert the produced gases from mixing. In various electrolysis technologies, both the ion charge and the 
type of electrolyte vary [4]. 
Nowadays, a fundamental distinction between alkaline electrolysis (AEL), proton exchange membrane 
(PEM) electrolysis, and solid oxide electrolysis (SOEL) can be made. AEL and PEM electrolysis occur at 
low temperatures, whereas SOEL operates at high temperatures[4] 
The electrolyzer that is considered in this project is an alkaline electrolyzer of 27.6 MW. [1] The liquid 
electrolyte utilized by AEL is potassium hydroxide. The electrodes are constructed from metal. Between 
the two electrodes is an impermeable diaphragm to both hydrogen and oxygen. 
On average, AEL achieves a 70 percent efficiency rate, with an expected 80 percent efficiency rate in the 
long run. Since 1927, AEL has been deployed on a large scale, has lower investment costs and a longer 
lifetime than the other two technologies, and achieves the highest nominal outputs (>100MW) without 
requiring critical raw materials. 
However, AELs lack the durability of PEM electrolyzers. As gases dissolved in the electrolyte remain in 
the cycle, they become more sensitive to impurities in the product gas. In addition, the AEL has a lengthy 
50-minute cold start time[4]

3.3.1 Aerobic Wastewater Treatment: Pure oxygen utilization for aeration 
 
Aeration is the most significant factor in aerobic wastewater treatment. It provides dissolved oxygen to 
microorganisms, keeps particles suspended, and prevents fouling in membrane bioreactors. However, 
conventional activated sludge treatment is limited to low strength wastewaters, as greater loadings require 
both increased biomass and increased dissolved oxygen concentrations. By substituting pure oxygen for 
air, oxygen transfer rates are increased at lower flowrates. Increased loadings can be treated with pure 
oxygen without impacting effluent quality. Due to their greater contact area, fine bubbles are more efficient 
at oxygen transport. However, pure oxygen is not always required, and it is recommended that it be used 
only in situations where air is insufficient [9] 
The rate of oxygen transfer is dependent on both the driving force and the liquid side mass transfer 
coefficient (kLa) (Stenstrom and Rosso, 2010; Gostick et al., 1992). To be utilized by the cells during 
aerobic treatment, oxygen molecules must overcome a sequence of transport resistances, as seen in Figure 
6. These include the following: 1. Transfer from the bubble's interior to the gas–liquid interface, 2. The 
passage of gas across the liquid–gas boundary, 3. Diffusion across the bubble's relatively stationary liquid 
film 4. Transport by bulk liquid 5. Diffusion across the somewhat impermeable liquid coating that surrounds 
the cells 6. Movement across the junction between the liquid cell and the solid cell, 7. Diffusion through 
the solid to the individual cell, if the cells are in a floc, clump, or solid particle (this phase is not necessary 
for individual cells); and 8. Transport via the cytoplasm to the reaction site. (2012) (Doran)

The features of the biomass and the design of the aeration system influence oxygen transport in wastewater. 
Aeration is related to the three characteristics that define biomass, namely particle concentration, particle 
size, and viscosity. The degree of aeration influences the particle size and viscosity. Any increase in 
viscosity has a detrimental effect on oxygen transmission, which is modified by the solids’ concentration. 
Oxygen transport is also influenced by particle size and concentration, which have a mutually reinforcing 
impact. Lastly, the form of the aeration system influences oxygen transfer, with the factor being the primary 
parameter determining the aeration capacity of the system. α-Factor quantifies the effect of wastewater on 
oxygen transport and changes according to wastewater quality, MLSS content, and mixing or applied 
turbulence strength. Both the β-factor, which takes into consideration the influence of salts and particles in 
wastewater on oxygen transfer, and the temperature factor have a negligible effect on mass transfer and can 
thus be neglected (Rodrguez et al., 2014, 2012b, 2011, 2010; Germain et al., 2007). 
 
Under conditions of increased organic loading, PO results in increased OTEs at lower flowrates. The 
pushing force for mass transfer increases as the partial pressure of oxygen in the gas phase increases. As a 
result, PO has a greater gas phase oxygen concentration than air, enabling for more rapid therapy at higher 
MLSS concentrations and shorter HRTs (Zhuang et al., 2016a; Rodriguez et al., 2013). However, in covered 
POAS systems, the CO2 produced may impair oxygen transfer because each time an oxygen bubble is put 
into the mixed liquor, CO2 is introduced, lowering the oxygen partial pressure and DO saturation 
concentration (Gostick et al., 1992). 
 
The amount of oxygen transmitted is proportional to the flowrate applied – the greater the flowrate, the 
greater the oxygen transfer. When air and PO flow at the same rate, the amount of oxygen transferred is 
greater with PO due to its greater driving power. The saturation concentration of DO in PO is up to five 
times that of air at the same flowrate (Zhuang et al., 2016a; Rodriguez et al., 2013). Lee and Kim (2013)

discovered that when the PO flowrate was adjusted from 0.0125 L min-1 to 0.025 L min-1 in a tank with a

increase progressively at the remaining flowrates. Similarly, Zhuang et al. (2016a) observed that when the 
PO flowrate was increased from 0.5 L h-1 to 1.5 L h-1, the DO concentration at a specific time of 300 s

was five times that of a corresponding air flowrate, and that there was a point beyond which any additional 
rise in the PO flowrate would have no effect on the OTE. 
Further, Rodrguez et al. (2014) observed that in an MBR supplied with either PO or air and operated at a 
constant HRT, the α-factor increased as MLSS concentrations dropped, but PO resulted in greater α-factors 
for the same MLSS concentration. Similarly, Rodrguez et al. (2011) demonstrated that increasing the MLSS 
content from 3420 mg L-1 to 12600 mg L-1 in an MBR employing PO aeration decreased the α-factor from 
0.426 to 0.022 at constant HRT (and SRT). However, the clearance efficiency of organic matter remained 
high, indicating that even at high MLSS concentrations, PO maintained the aerobic conditions required. 
In principle, the influence of MLSS concentrations must be evaluated in conjunction with the applied HRT, 
as HRTs control the amount of oxygen in contact with the liquid. Rodrguez et al. (2012b) demonstrated 
that both MLSS and HRT concentrations influenced the α-factor, but the MLSS concentration had a 
stronger correlation with it. However, data from a single case study cannot be directly compared to other 
data, because the α-factor is strongly dependent on the bubble size, the geometry of the system, and the 
concentrations of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) and soluble microbial products (SMP), although 
the EPS and SMP have a lesser effect. Additionally, when considering full-scale applications, α-factors that 
can sustain aerobic conditions at the bench scale must be used with caution (Rodrguez et al., 2012b). 
Finally, Rodrguez et al. (2013) demonstrated that less energy was utilized in PO circumstances, specifically 
0.62 kg O2 h-1 kW-1, compared to 3.31 kgO2 h-1 kW-1 for air. Oackley (1997) previously stated that PO 
aeration has a 25% reduced power cost. Nonetheless, producing oxygen incurs a cost.[9] 
 
3.3.2 Pure Oxygen Aeration and Temperature 
 
Temperature is a major operating aspect that influences bacterial metabolic activity, and thus has a greater 
impact on biomass than the type of aeration — any temperature changes can result in poor sludge settling, 
increased turbidity in the final effluent, and other undesirable effects (Rodrguez et al., 2014; Bernat et al., 
2017). However, Rodrguez et al. (2011) showed that when an MBR was fed with PO and operated at an 
SRT of 39.91 days, the 0.4 gSS g-1 COD of sludge produced was comparable to those reported in earlier 
research using air but was obtained at a lower temperature in their study. Since temperature influences 
sludge production, with the latter reducing as the former increases, the increased value was linked to the 
usage of PO, which kept sludge production low.  
However, one process that is particularly interesting is the aerobic thermophilic process, which occurs at 
temperatures greater than 45 °C. During aerobic sludge digestion in the thermophilic range, a constant 
deficit of DO is detected in excess sludge, as both the potential for oxygen absorption and the oxygen 
demand are increased due to the increased rate of sludge digestion. According to Zupani and Ro (2008), 
sludge treated with PO declined between 22 and 50 degrees Celsius, but sludge treated with air degraded 
between 32 and 65 degrees Celsius. When PO was used, no sludge digestion occurred beyond 50 °C – such 
high DO concentrations do not occur naturally, and bacteria were unlikely to withstand them. PO performed 
better in the mesophilic range. Zupani and Ro (2008) demonstrated that both methods of aeration had their 
advantages and downsides, with temperature taking precedence over the type of aeration. High temperatures

favoured air digestion, which was impossible for the PO, which operated better at lower temperatures. 
Finally, Collivignarelli et al. (2015) demonstrated that by performing ammonia utilization rate tests at 49 °C

be noted that Cohen (1977) discovered that heterotrophic mesophilic bacteria could achieve a substantial 
biomass reduction in an uncovered POAS system. In this regard, PO aeration appears to be more suited to 
lower temperatures[9] 
 
3.3.3 Oxygen Requirements for Wastewater Treatment 
 
The oxygen requirements for municipal wastewater treatment can be accurately calculated using basic 
laboratory analysis techniques such as the BOD test (special requirements exist for some industrial 
wastewater). The BOD test is used to determine the biological oxygen demand of wastewater and serves as 
an ideal basis for design, assuming the following [10]: 
I. 
Wastewater is already accessible for testing, indicating that this is not a new initiative. 
II. 
Wastewater is indicative of the full scale. 
For wastewater, the field or actual oxygen requirement is proportional to the length of the treatment 
procedure. Generally, oxygen demand is divided into carbonaceous and nitrogenous demands: oxygen 
necessary to stabilize carbon in waste and oxygen required to stabilize nitrogen in the wastewater, 
respectively. Nitrogen is typically found in waste as ammonia - NH or NH4. 
 
▪ 
Carbonaceous BOD 
Carbonaceous demand must be evaluated in two components for many treatment plants: 
 
Oxygen to feed the active sludge with sufficient oxygen to extract soluble carbon (BOD) from solution and 
generate further sludge. This carbon to cell conversion is the synthesis reaction, which requires 
approximately 0.5 to 0.6 kg O2/kg BOD.  
In the case of the prolongation of the process, a second oxygen demand is created for oxidizing and 
digesting the cells (stabilizing them). Endogenous respiration is the second step and requires an additional 
0.8 to 0.9 kg O2/kg BOD. Total oxygen required (in kg) for carbonaceous BOD elimination can range from 
0.7 kg/kg BOD up to 1.5 kg/kg for activated sludge with a short detention time for biomass (low sludge 
age). BOD for extended aeration combined with a long retention of biomass (MLS, i.e., a long sludge age) 
in the biosystem.[10] 
It should also be stated that the addition of nitrogen oxygen demand must generally take place for lengthy 
aeration because nitrification is expected in most long sludge age projects operating at temperatures greater 
than 55-10°C.

▪ 
Nitrogenous BOD  
Nitrogenous BOD requires 4.6 kg O2/kg BOD removed oxygen at the demand floor. To convert ammonia 
to nitrate via nitrogenous conversion, a prolonged aeration time with a low food to microbe ratio is required, 
i.e., a large amount of sludge MLS (M) with a low food supply (F). This scenario results in a prolonged

▪ 
Removal of Biological Nutrients (BNR for Nitrogen) 
Denitrification may be essential for advanced waste treatment methods. Denitrification requires the 
effective removal of both carbonaceous and nitrogenous compounds. Nitrogen is removed from the system 
through the conversion of nitrate (NO3) to nitrogen gas. Denitrification has the unintended consequence 
and advantage of recovering oxygen and lowering overall oxygen demand. Oxygen recovery is roughly 
2.86 kilograms of oxygen for every kilogram (kg/kg) of NO3 decreased and eliminated.[10] 
 
▪ 
Oxygen Requirements in Total 
The following equation can be used to calculate the field oxygen requirements for BOD D removal: 
Oxygen Requirements in Total = Requirement for carbonaceous BOD + Nitrogen demand appropriate 
for oxygen recovered during denitrification[10] 
To establish field oxygen requirements, each treatment process and waste must be analysed. 
 
 
 
 3.3.4 Oxygen Transport in the WWTP 
 
Oxygen transport, the process by which oxygen is transported from the gas to the liquid phase, is an 
important part of many wastewater treatment processes. The functionality of the aerobic process, such as 
activated sludge, biological filtration and aerobic digestion, depends on the availability of a sufficient 
amount of oxygen.[11] 
The most common application of oxygen transport is the biological treatment of wastewater. Due to the 
low solubility of oxygen and the corresponding low oxygen transfer rate, not enough oxygen is introduced 
into the water to aerobically treat the waste through the normal water-air interface. Thus, to transport the 
large amount of oxygen required, additional interfaces must be formed. Air or oxygen can be introduced 
into the liquid or the liquid in the form of droplets may be exposed to the atmosphere. Oxygen can be 
supplied by air or pure oxygen bubbles introduced into the water to create additional fluid-gas interfaces. 
[11] 
In wastewater treatment plants, bubble aeration is achieved by diffusion of air bubbles into the liquid, at a 
depth up to 10m. Depth over 30m has been used in some plants to create small bubbles by impact flow of

introduced under the centre of the engine. These mixers are designed to mix the liquid in the tank and to 
expose the liquid to the atmosphere in the form of small droplets [11] 
In a given volume of aerated water, the ventilation devices are calculated on the basis of the amount of 
oxygen transferred per unit of air introduced into the water for equivalent conditions (temperature and

3.3.6 Oxygen Requirement in the WWTP 
 
The nutrients (N, P) and the oxygen requirements in a Wastewater Treatment Plant are important factors 
for growth, because the growth of microbes doesn’t only depend on the organics, energy etc, but also on 
nutrients. Nitrate and Phosphorus are needed in order some of the molecules (for instance proteins, which 
create enzymes) to be built.  The typical nutrient requirements are based on the following equations:  
 
NR = 0.086 Yobs       [gun/gCOD] 
PR = 0.017 Yobs         [gP/gCOD], 
 
Where:  NR: the nitrate requirement per amount of COD biomass produced 
             PR: the phosphorus requirement per amount of COD biomass produced 
             Yobs: the observed yield 
 
The conversion factors: 0.086 gN/gCOD and 0.017 gP/gCOD are considered as typical biomass 
composition.[12] 
The typical formula used for bacterial biomass is: C5H7O2N. Based on this formula, the amount of Nitrate 
(N) per COD can be estimated. Regarding Phosphorus (P), a more complicated formula is being considered.  
The nutrients requirements are used by looking into them and making sure that enough ammonia to sustain 
growth does exist. For example, if the COD of the wastewater is very high, and the nitrate content is very 
low, in a sense that gN/gCOD is below 0.086, there might be problems in carbon removal. However, this 
is not often the case for municipal wastewater; municipal wastewater has plenty of Nitrate and Phosphorus, 
so the bacteria can grow in there. On the other hand, the ratio gN/gCOD is an important factor to consider 
when the wastewater is industrial (for instance wastewater created in oil platforms), as it is very poor in 
nutrients and the and the value of gN/gCOD is usually lower than 0.086, so microbes cannot grow properly.   
Nutrients also play a crucial role in the design of the aeration process. This is because the nitrate that has 
been assimilated, doesn’t have to be nitrified, so there may be configuration when more nitrate assimilation 
is being promoted, and therefore there is less need for nitrification.

6. Conclusion – Further Recommendations 
 
Oxygen is a byproduct of the electrolysis process, and its value depends on its application. As mentioned 
previously, the purpose of this study was to investigate the application of utilizing the oxygen byproduct of 
an electrolyzer in a PtX facility. Considering the results presented in the previous chapter of this project, it 
is possible to conclude that replacing the air in a wastewater treatment plant's aeration process with oxygen 
is economically advantageous. 
On the island of Bornholm, 37 MW of onshore wind parks are operational, and it was assumed that a 27.6 
MW alkaline electrolyzer facility was being powered by them. The electrolyzer is located near one of the 
island's Wastewater Treatment Plant. 
The key findings of this study indicate that the amount of money that could be saved if oxygen from an 
electrolyzer replaced air in the aeration process of wastewater treatment is 28.237 Euros. These cost savings 
translate to 282,3 MWh of energy, which is equivalent to a year's worth of energy savings at a Bornholm 
wastewater treatment plant with a capacity of 50.000 PE. 
In addition, it should be noted that this study was based solely on the replacement of air with oxygen and 
not on additional recovery methods. Standard conditions were considered without any intermediate 
considerations. 
This study aimed to provide a preliminary overview of the replacement of air capture with oxygen from the 
P2X facility for delivery to the aeration tank. Impacts on the environment and society were outside the 
scope of this study. To evaluate the overall feasibility of replacing air with oxygen, however, an 
investigation of the environmental and societal impact is required. 
Furthermore, the connection between a wastewater treatment facility and an electrolyzer – the 
transportation of oxygen into the aeration tank and its costs – could be investigated further. For this type of 
application, oxygen must be packaged and transported to the appropriate location. However, in order to 
optimize the transportation costs, the site's proximity to a wastewater treatment plant should be investigated 
further. 
A further recommendation would be to analyze under which pressure and temperature the losses during the 
entry of oxygen could be reduced and, using a sensitivity analysis, to determine the optimal conditions 
(oxygen pressure and temperature). 
Finally, as pure O2 reduces the volume of air blown into the basins by 80%, 1 m3 of O2 is equivalent to 5 
m3 of air[17] . Electrical ventilators are typically used to aerate the sludge treatment basins by blowing air 
into them. The use of pure O2 instead of air will reduce the volume of air blown into the basins by 80 
percent, thereby reducing aeration costs. The estimated value of electricity in Euros per megawatt-hour 
could also be posed as a separate research question.
