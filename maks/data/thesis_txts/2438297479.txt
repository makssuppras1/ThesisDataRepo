Summary
In this thesis, we present novel physical brain games for cognitive training. The physical
brain games are implemented by the MOTO tiles, which is a typical modular playware
product. The playware is a relatively new field of research, which can be defined as
intelligent hardware and software that creates play and playful experiences for users of
all ages. The original motivation for designing the MOTO tile was to provide physical
exercise while enjoying games (i.e., exergaming). Such feature allows us to implement
brain games which can also be beneficial to physical health.
A literature review of the playware and brain games reveals that playing the MOTO
tiles could produce potential positive effects on cognitive ability. The use of brain games
as a means to enhance performance on other tasks is referred as ”transfer”. The distance
of transfer is usually related to the possibility that a brain game can be effective to other
tasks. Moreover, before the start of this project, a pilot study was conducted and the
results show that reaction times of several cognitive tasks were enhanced after a short
MOTO tile training. Based upon these theories and findings, a MOTO tile training
program was designed.
The designed MOTO tile training program was evaluated by a controlled trial, which
was formed on the basis of the latest gold standard of cognitive study. The trial included
unique measures of expectation of the training, which could ensure that the outcome
measures are unaffected by placebo effect.
The trial was conducted in Shanghai. Totally 22 older adults were enrolled and an-
alyzed after two weeks training. Results of statistical analyses reveal that the training
yielded significant improvement in the tests visual search and tandem walk. The for-
mer implies benefit to attention, scanning, visualization abilities, and the latter implies
benefit to balance ability.
Since the sample size of this trial was small, there could be more positive effects
of the proposed MOTO tile training that was not found by the analyses. The thesis
also presented future possibilities on the basis of reflection of this trial. Due to the
high variance of functional abilities of the elderly, subjects would be categorized into
subgroups, and the intervention and outcome measures would be designed depending on
the subgroups in future works.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis covers my work on my master project: Physical Brain Games. The scope of
this project is to design and test novel brain games which can be played in a physical
interactive manner. The games are implemented by a typical modular playware: MOTO
tiles. In the rest of this chapter, we will introduce the concept of playware, historical
researches related to brain games, and potential effect of exercise on cognition.
1.1
Playware and MOTO Tiles
The concept of playware was proposed by Prof. Henrik Hautop Lund in 2005 [1]. Broadly
defined, playware can be considered as any intelligent hardware and software that creates
play and playful experiences for users of all ages [1]. Such concept was initially inspired
from the concern about the decrease of children’s outdoor activity due to increasing
use of electronic and digital product, which could lead to rapid growth of obesity [2,
3]. In order to prevent this trend, Lund suggested to use intelligent technology solution
to create playful experience for children’s physical activities, and then the idea was
expanded to become a new research area [1]. Recently Lund proposed a novel playware
ABC concept [4], which is formed by:
• A: Anybody, Anywhere, Anytime
• B: Building Bodies and Brains
• C: Construct, Combine, Create
The item A suggests that playware research focuses on creating technology solution for
anybody, anywhere, and anytime. The item B involves two aspects. First it implies
the use of embodied artificial intelligence, which has an intimate connection with body
(hardware) and brain (software). The implementation of the embodied artificial intel-
ligence is often achieved by user-configurable modular system [5]. On the other hand,
such modular design allows users to solve cognitive challenges by physical actions, which
means playware can also build bodies and brains of users. Lastly, regarding the item
C, it indicates that users are able to create their own solutions by constructing and
combining with the modules.
The MOTO tiles are a typical playware product, which was developed and updated
from the first digital playground for children [1]. The latest version of MOTO tiles can

are shaped as 30 × 30 cm puzzle pieces so that users can easily combine and construct
their layout. Each tile has a binary pressure sensor, and eight light-emitting diodes

The LEDs can be lighted up in any 24-bit true color, but for the current

network operating in the 2.4 GHz ISM band.

Since the tiles are connected to the tablet wirelessly, the tiles do not have to be
always combined together. Therefore, there can be infinite possible layouts for the tiles.
With various patterns and colors of the LEDs, MOTO tiles can provide a great number
of games with different modes (e.g., single-player and multi-player) that users can play.
Although the MOTO tiles were emerged from the digital playground for children,
they can be played by people from almost all age group. Since 2014, Lund and Jessen
conducted a series of trials regarding the effect on physical ability of the elderly [6–
10]. From their randomized controlled trials (RCT), the results show that there was
significant improvement in the test “Chair Stand” and ”Line Walk”, but insignificant in
the ”Timed Up and Go” test.
Since the physical effect of MOTO tiles has been investigated by the comprehensive
study mentioned above, in this thesis, the main objective will be the cognitive effect

1.2
Brain Games and Their Effect on
Cognition
The name of the area of ”brain games” might be sometimes confusing, as it can also
be named as ”brain training”, ”cognitive training”, or other similar terms. The term
“brain training” is used mostly by companies marketing cognitive interventions, whilst
the term ”cognitive training” is more common to be used by researchers as cognitive
interventions are normally focused on cognitive abilities rather than neural functioning
[11]. However, because the topic of this thesis is highly related to playware, in the rest
of this thesis, we will mostly use the term ”brain games”, but it can also be replaced by
its synonyms.
Broadly defined, brain games refers to the engagement in a specific program or
activity that is able to enhance a cognitive skill or general cognitive ability [12, 13].
The rapid growth of research and market of brain games started in the early 2000s.
Nintendo’s Brain Age which was launched in 2005 was regarded as the first brain game
product marketed on a massive scale. However, the advertising claims of Brain Age
and its sequel, Brain Age 2, cannot promise any measurable and quantitative benefits
[11]. Lumos Labs’ product Lumosity is another widespread brain game product. Lumos
Labs’s website states that they work with over 100 researchers worldwide and their games
were developed as cognitive or psychological tasks, but the original researches cannot
be found from there. Unlike Brain Age and Lumosity, Posit Science’s BrainHQ is a
brain game product which provides relatively credible evidence. For example, there are
peer-reviewed paper regarding the effect on processing speed [14–16], memory [17–19],
and attention [20–23].
The earliest and also the largest brain game intervention up to now was the Advanced
Cognitive Training for Independent and Vital Elderly (ACTIVE) trial conducted by Ball
et al. [24]. The trial was started in 1998 [25]. It had three different cognitive interven-
tions (i.e., reasoning training, memory training, and speed-of-processing training) and
a passive control group. Totally 2832 persons aged 65 to 94 years old (mean age = 74
years) participated the trial. Participants in treatment groups received 10 sessions of
training. Each session lasted for around 60 to 75 minutes. The time frame of the 10
sessions varied between 5 to 6 weeks. Sixty percent of the participants who attended
more than 80% of the initial training sessions were offered 4-session booster training 11
months later. After the initial training, a number of follow-up tests (1 year, 2 years, 5
years, and 10 years) were conducted in order to exam the maintenance of the cognitive
improvement [24, 26, 27].
Generally, the results of ACTIVE trial show that all the three intervention groups

improved in the cognitive ability corresponding to the intervention immediately after
the training, but improvement in other cognitive ability was not observed. For example,
memory intervention improved memory ability but not reasoning. At the 5-year follow-
up, effects produced by all the three interventions remained more or less, but at the
10-year follow-up, the memory effect were almost lost, whereas the speed and reasoning
effects still remained. Note that as the participants were the elderly, who are expected to
get worse cognitive ability as age grow, the positive effects produced by the interventions
de facto indicate less declines in their follow-up tests.
Clinical Trial NCT00430560 (registered at clinicaltrial.gov) is another intervention
that led to multiple papers [28–31].
Totally 145 participants with schizophrenia or
schizoaffective disorder were enrolled in this trial. The cognitive intervention consisting
of attention, memory, and executive functioning, was developed by Scientific Learning
Corporation and CogRehab.
Subjects’ cognitive abilities were measured by 15 stan-
dardized neuropsychological tasks. In addition to the cognitive intervention, subjects
also received social therapy and work therapy. The intervention lasted for 6 months and
there was also a follow-up test 12 months after the training began. There was significant
improvement in three out of five cognitive tests.
Apart from ACTIVE and NCT00430560 trial, there were many other trials with
passive control group but smaller scale (i.e., small sample size) compared with ACTIVE
and NCT00430560 [32–36]. Generally their results showed similar feature that cognitive
tasks similar to the training tasks can be improved, but such effect cannot be transferred
to other cognitive tasks.
The trials mentioned above (e.g., ACTIVE trial) are all with passive control groups.
However, considering potential placebo effect, these studies may not be very credible. In
comparison, trials with active control group have better reliability, but the number of
such studies are much less than those with passive control groups. Some famous trials
with active control groups are: Iowa Healthy and Active Minds Study (IHAMS) [37, 38],
Staying Keen in Later Life (SKILL) [39–41], Improvement in Memory with Plasticity-
based Adaptive Cognitive Training (IMPACT) [18, 19], and Clinical Trial NCT00312962
[42].
The IHAMS study was conducted in order to overcome the shortcomings of the
ACTIVE trial. Its sample size is therefore similar to the ACTIVE trial, but only focused
on speed-of-processing training. The control condition of IHAMS was crossword puzzles.
The results showed that the intervention produced more improvement in Useful Field
of View Test (UFOV) comparing with the control group [37]. In the follow-up test one
year later, the improvement remained [38].
The SKILL study enrolled 895 community-dwelling adults aged over 60 years for the
original baseline study. The intervention of the SKILL study was speed-of-processing
training, while the control group was taught how to use internet. The training included
ten one-hour sessions. The results indicated that the improvement of the intervention
group in UFOV and instrumental activities of daily living (IADL) tasks was greater than

that of the control group.
The IMPACT study enrolled 487 participants who aged 65 years and older. It utilized
40 hours (1 hour per day, 5 days per week, for 8 weeks) of Posit Science Brain fitness
auditory training as intervention, whereas the control condition was an educational
training program, which consisted of watching educational DVD programs on different
subjects, and answering written quizzes that were based on the DVD programs. Right
after the training, test results showed that the intervention group had more improvement
in Repeatable Battery for the Assessment of Neuropsychological Status (RBANS) for
the use the auditory modality, and a number of secondary measures of memory and
attention than the control group [19]. A follow-up test was conducted 3 month after the
training. In most cognitive tests of the follow-up, the improvement were still significant,
but slightly less than the original post-test [18].
Clinical trial NCT00312962 enrolled 80 participants with schizophrenia. The inter-
vention was 50 hours of cognitive training from Posit Science. Meanwhile the control
group was assigned to play a series of computer games (e.g., visuospatial puzzle games,
clue-gathering mystery games, pinball-style games) for the same time period as the
intervention group [42].
Hooker et al.
[43] applied a functional magnetic resonance
imaging (fMRI) task to analyze the training outcome. The fMRI results showed signifi-
cant difference in bilateral amygdala, right putamen, and right medial prefrontal cortex
which can be corresponded to happy, surprise, and fear expressions respectively. Mayer–
Salovey–Caruso Emotional Intelligence Test also showed significant difference between
the intervention group and the control group.
All the studies mentioned above had interventions which were designed specifically for
training cognitive ability. Additionally, there were also studies investigating the effects
of other types of video games (e.g., action game and strategy game). Li et al. [44, 45]
firstly compared the performance of action game players and non-action game players.
Then in a second experiment, the non-action game players were randomly assigned to
an action game training group and a non-action game control group. The action game
intervention was Unreal Tournament 2004 and Call of Duty 2, while the control condition
was The Sims 2. Their results from both the two experiments showed that the action
games can produce positive effect on visual skills measured by lateral masking task.
Anguera et al. [46] designed a video racing game called NeuroRacer. By playing
its multitasking mode for one month, participants reduced their reaction time (RT) in
delayed-recognition working memory task and test of variables of attention. A six-month
follow up showed that the effects remained for this period.
Kühn et al. [47] investigated how a classic video game Super Mario can affect human
brain.
Forty-eight participants were enrolled and randomly assigned to a treatment
group and a passive control group. The intervention was playing Super Mario 64 30
minutes per day for two months. Participants’ brains were scanned before and after
the training. The results revealed increase of grey matter volume in right dorsolateral
prefrontal cortex, right hippocampal formation and bilateral cerebellum. These changes

Basak et al. [48] conducted a study where a real-time strategy (RTS) game Rise of

were 40 older adults who had not played any video game for two years before the study.
The training program consisted of 15 1.5-hour training sessions. The results indicated

Although there is a vast number of studies investigating the effect of brain games, Si-
mons et al. [11] commented that the evidence is still not compelling as most studies had
more or less different kinds of critical limitations. Common defects include passive con-
trol group or inappropriate active control group, unmatched baseline, and uncorrected
multiple comparisons.
1.3
Effect of Aerobic Exercise on Cognition
Considering the MOTO tiles game is normally played by stepping on the tiles, which is
similar to walking or jogging, it is necessary to review if aerobic exercise can improve
cognitive ability. The earliest investigation of such effect was conducted in 1966 [49].
Over the past 50 years, there were over 200 articles discussing similar topic. Hence, a
number of meta-analyses were conducted in order to summarize the effects of exercise
[50–55].
The latest efforts on the meta-analysis were made by Barha et al.
[53] in
2017, but their work focused on sex difference of this effect. In the same year, Northey
et al. [54] published a paper focusing on older people and in 2016, Groot et al. [55]
discussed the effect in dementia patients.
The overall results revealed that aerobic
exercise (e.g., walking) improved several cognitive abilities such as executive control
function, processing speed, memory, and spatial abilities.
However, similar to the limitation of studies of brain game interventions, few stud-
ies of aerobic exercise interventions controlled placebo effects of treatment group and
control group to be the same [56]. Fortunately, Stothart et al. [57] presented evidence
that expectations did not differ between aerobic and non-aerobic interventions in 2014.
In their survey, totally 657 participants were enrolled via Amazon Mechanical Turk.
Participants read descriptions of cognitive tests in combination with an aerobic training
program or a non-aerobic training program, depending on which group they were as-
signed to. Afterwards, their expectation of the aerobic or non-aerobic training for each
cognitive test was measured by answering a questionnaire. The results of the question-
naire implied comparable expectation from the aerobic and the non-aerobic group. Thus,
it could be concluded that the evidences about aerobic exercise from previous studies
were not resulted from placebo effects.

1.4
Main Contributions
This thesis presents the first systematic investigation of effect of the MOTO tiles on
cognitive abilities. Furthermore, in order to justify observed training outcome was not
resulted from placebo effect, we applied a measure of expectation of intervention and
control condition. The measure of expectation would made the benefits found from the
trial especially credible.
The thesis investigate both cognitive and physical abilities produced by the MOTO
tiles.
For the cognitive abilities, we found significant improvement in attention and
visualization after two weeks training. For the physical abilities, we found faster speed
and increased steps in tandem walking test. Those results also verify previous theories
of cognition transfer and evidence of the effects of the MOTO tiles on balance ability.
In addition to the benefit of MOTO tile training, the thesis also summarize the pros
and cons of our trial design. We point out some games and outcome measures which
are not suitable for the elderly and therefore should be carefully used in future study.
Other future possibilities are also proposed in this thesis.

Thesis Overview

Chapter 1: This chapter.

2.1
Communication between Tiles and Tablet
As we have introduced before, each set of the MOTO tiles consists of a number of
tiles and an Android tablet. The tiles are controlled by the tablet via ANT protocol.
The ANT protocol allows devices to send lightweight messages with very limited energy
consumption. ANT can also be configured to switch to a sleep mode with very low
power, and wake up in a very short period. Such feature enabled the tiles to work for
up to 20 hours without charging.
A typical ANT message consists of 8 bytes.
ANT accommodates three types of
messaging: broadcast, acknowledged, and burst. Broadcast is the most lightweight type
and is used by the MOTO tiles. A broadcast message can be received by any ANT
device within communication range (30 m), so it is necessary to define the information
of sender and receiver so that only correct receiver can react to the message. Although
the tiles look the same, in fact an ID is assigned to each tile so that they can differ from
each other. In our ANT message, the first byte is the ID of the receiver and the last
byte is the ID of the sender. Specifically, when the tablet send message to a tile, the
first byte is the tile ID and the last byte is the tablet ID, and vice versa. The reason
why tablet ID is also included in the message is that there can be circumstance that
two or more sets of MOTO tiles are played simultaneously at the same place. Thus, it
is important to contain the tablet ID in order to prevent interference between different
sets.
When the tablet sends message to the tiles, the second byte is used for commands,
such as set color, spinning, fading, etc. The remaining bytes are used for supplementary
information of a command. For example, when the command is to set color, the third
byte indicates the color to be set, and the forth byte indicates how many LEDs should
be lighted up.
The message sent from the tiles to the tablet is simpler. There are only two possible

pressure sensor detects a press or a press is released. In addition, the forth byte of the

The Tiles
Each MOTO tile has a binary pressure sensor and 8 LEDs, which are controlled by a

The firmware includes many handy LED functions so that tablet does not need to

color, spin, fade, blink, and countdown.
Theoretically, the LEDs can be lighted up in any 24-bit true color. In the firmware,
the colors are defined by ordinary RGB color code, namely three bytes of integer. For
convenience, we pre-defined 8 rainbow colors so that the color can be indicated by a
single byte of an ANT message.

A pilot test was conducted as part of a special course ”Playware Software and Games”

The pilot test was conducted at the Playware Lab at DTU. Totally 20 students

The cognitive tests for measuring training outcome were four tests from an Android

the original stimulus is.
Choice Reaction Time: The test CRT is a color discrimination task. Each

Sternberg Scanning: In the test SS, a group of boxes with different colors are
displayed in each stimulus. Then after a short pause, a color appears on the
screen and the subjects determine if the color is one of the colors from the
stimulus.

4.1
Theories behind Brain Games
The historical researches of brain games have been reviewed in Chapter 1. Most of them
focused on if a brain game can enhance the performance of other cognitive tasks rather
than the performance of the game itself. That is, ”transfer” from a training task, to
other different untrained cognitive task. The history of transfer can date back to two
theories one century ago.
The first theory is ”formal discipline theory”. In this theory, our mind can be regarded
as a combination of different capacities (e.g., concentration, reasoning ability, memory)
[11]. Training of a task involving a particular capacity will enhance this capacity, and
thus enhance the performance of other tasks involving this capacity. However, other
capacities will not be improved by this training.
The formal discipline theory was firstly introduced by William James [58] in 1890.
He and four of his students made an experiment where they measured their memory
speed of a poem, and after many days they measured the memory speed of another
poem. Two of them had faster memory speed for the second poem.
Later in 1906, Edward Thorndike tested if training of estimating rectangle area can
enhance the estimation of other shapes [59].
Unfortunately, the transfer effect was
not significant.
He concluded that cognitive training involves countless independent
capacities and habits, which tightly depend on the concrete data entailed. The transfer
from one task to another task will only be possible if they possess common elements.
This idea was afterwards named as ”transfer by identical elements”, which is the second
theory commonly used in the area of transfer.
Today Thorndike’s transfer by identical elements theory is more broadly accepted by
researchers, despite there are more empirical evidence supporting the formal discipline
theory [11]. In fact there is no clear definition which can distinguish the capacity from
the formal discipline theory and the element from the transfer by identical elements
theory. The field of transfer still needs a more rigorous theory to illustrate the conflicted
==PAGE:30==
18

facts of transfer [60].
In the contemporary field of transfer, researchers usually use near or far to define
the similarity of two tasks involved in transfer. However, similar to the vague difference
between capacity and element, near transfer and far transfer do not have an operational
definition either. Perhaps the best attempts to solve this issue were applying taxonomy
to transfer [60–62], although the concept is still somewhat based on relative similarity.
Carroll’s three-stratum model [62] is one of the well accepted descriptive taxonomy for
transfer. A schematic diagram of the model is shown in Figure 4.1. The model divides
human cognition into three levels: one general intelligence (stratum III), nine broad
abilities (stratum II), and 63 narrow abilities (stratum I). Nowadays the three-stratum
model has become a part of the Cattell–Horn–Carroll model (CHC) [63].

5.1
Choice of the cognitive tests
Recently in 2016, Simons et al. [11] suggested that outcome measures of a cognitive
training should meet the following criteria:
• Reliability: the outcome measures should be reported reliable. Unreliable measures
may be affected by factors unrelated to the intervention.
• Multiple measures: Each outcome construct should be assessed by multiple mea-
sures. Single measures may fail to capture all aspects of an outcome construct.
• Separately reporting outcomes: If a trial involves multiple cognitive abilities, the
training outcome of them should be reported separately.
where the first two criteria are related to the choice of cognitive tests, whilst the third
criterion is about reporting and documentation.
In Section 4.2, it has been mentioned that the chosen MOTO tile games will entail
three broad abilities: visual processing, processing speed, and short-term memory. Three
games are chosen for training visual processing and processing speed, and two games
are chosen for short-term memory. Since the abilities visual processing and processing
speed are trained together, they are regarded as a combined outcome construct when
applying the multiple measures rule. Clearly, more measures of a construct will result in
more accurate results. However, due to the fact that the subjects of this trial are elderly
people, and it is not feasible to ask them perform too many tests in a short time, we
selected only two cognitive tests for each outcome construct. For visual processing and
processing speed, CRT and VS will be used to evaluate the outcome. For short-term
memory, n-back test and MTS will be applied. These tests and the tests used in the
pilot experiment will be introduced in the following sections.

5.2
Choice Reaction Time
In the CRT task, subjects are required to determine which of two possible stimuli is
presented. The earliest prototype of CRT perhaps was Galton’s work in 1890 [64]. His
instrument was a long box. One end had a horizontal slot to look in, and at the other
end there were two square windows for presenting two possible stimuli. The RT was
measured by an oscillating pendulum along a chord.
There are many variations of the choices in CRT task, such as four-choice [65] and
three-dimensional objects [66]. However, previous research showed that those variations
will not affect the RT, provided that subjects have sufficient practice [67]. Thus, the
basic color discrimination CRT (blue/red) was used in the trial.
In 1960, Bertelson and Boons [68] found that the RT can be influenced by the uncer-
tainty of the time intervals between stimuli. Apparently, when the interval is a constant,
it is possible to anticipate the timing of the coming stimulus, and thus get better prepa-
ration to the stimulus. Therefore, in this study, the time interval was a random number
varying between one to two seconds.
Compared with simple reaction time test where subjects simply need to react to a
repeated stimuli, CRT also requires the decision making and processing abilities [68,
69].
In the case of color discrimination, it indicates the utilization of Visualization,
Perceptual Speed, and Rate-of-Test-Taking, which is well matched to the MOTO tile
games for visual processing and processing speed.
5.3
Visual Search
In a VS task, typically a number of items are displayed to subjects. Then subjects
determine if a specific target is presented or not among irrelevant non-targets. The VS
task has been a critical tasks in visual attention study for over 40 years [70]. Generally
VS tasks can differ in two aspects: Number of displayed items (set size), and how target
can be discriminated from non-targets (type of VS). Regarding the set size, researches
showed that increased set size leads to slower and less accurate reaction [71, 72]. The
types of VS can be divided into six categories [70]:
Feature search: The target is defined by a particular feature, such as a specific
letter or color.
Hard feature search: Similar to feature search, but the feature is a unique
orientation.
Conjunction search: The target is defined by conjunctions of two different
features, such as a particular letter with a particular color.
Within-dimension conjunction search: Similar to conjunction search, but the
two features for the target are within the same dimension. For example, the
==PAGE:37==
5.4 N-back Test
25
target is combined shape colored in red and green, and the non-targets are
red and blue, or green and blue.
Spatial-configuration search: In spatial-configuration searches, targets are
defined by spatial arrangement of line segments.
Other search: VS tasks that do not belong to and of the five categories above,
such as searches whose targets are defined by three features.
According to Wolfe’s meta-analysis [70], conjunction search and feature search are
most popular types of VS, as around 50% historical experiments are conjunction searches,
20% are feature searches, and none of the remained types accounted for over 10%. There-
fore, feature search and conjunction search were applied to our trial.
In the pilot study, there were 24 stimuli for feature search and 24 for conjunction
search. The set sizes were 5, 15, and 30, each of which had 8 stimuli. The performance
of feature search and conjunction search was recorded as a whole. For the feature search,
targets were letter S, and non-targets were letter X and T. For the conjunction search,
targets were green T, and non-targets were green X and brown T.
In this trial, the number of stimuli was reduced to 18 for the two VS tasks. The set
sizes were 5, 10, and 15, which were also reduced from the pilot study, in order to fit
the cognitive abilities of the elderly, and also match most of previous VS studies [70].
Considering that the subjects would be older Chinese adults, some of whom may not
have a good reaction to Latin script, the items of the VS task were changed to shapes
instead of letters. For the feature search, targets were a cross ”×”, and non-targets were
circle ”O” and plus sign ”+”. For the conjunction search, targets were red ”+” or blue
”O”, and non-targets were blue ”+” and red ”O”.
5.4
N-back Test
N-back test was originally developed by W. K. Kichner in 1958 [73], and it has become
a widely used paradigm for measures of short-term memory in recent decades [74], or
so-called working memory coined by Pribram et al. [75]. In n-back tests, subjects are
required to respond to a series of stimuli, and judge if the current stimuli is the same as
the one presented n steps before. In most studies of n-back tests, n varied from 0 to 3.
When n equals to 0, the task is actually to respond if a predefined target is presented.
In addition to being a typical measure of working memory, n-back test and its varia-
tions are also widely used as cognitive training tools. Specifically, adaptive dual n-back
training was reported that it could improve fluid reasoning ability [76–78], which is
defined as the ability to solve new problems using unfamiliar information or novel pro-
cedures from CHC model[63, 79].
The design of a basic n-back test can be divided into four aspects: type of the
stimuli (letter, number, shape, etc.), number of possible stimuli, time duration of each
==PAGE:38==
26
5 Cognitive Tests
stimulus, and the variable ”n” itself. In this study, in order to match the abilities of the
elderly, the test was designed on the basis of a previous n-back test for mild cognitive
impairment and elderly depression [80]. A 1-back test was applied. The possible stimuli
were numbers 1, 2, 3, and 4. Each stimuli lasted for 1.8 s. Totally 20 stimuli were
displayed and subjects had to react to the last 19 stimuli.
In most n-back studies, RT and accuracy were recorded for analysis. However, al-
though n-back has been used so frequently, few studies clearly reported how accuracy
was defined. Even for those where the accuracy were defined, their definitions conflicted
against each other [81]. This confusing phenomenon can be attributed to the complexity
of the possible responses of n-back tests. Firstly, for the first n stimuli in an n-back test,
subjects do not need to have any response since these stimuli do not have n antecedents.
Then when subjects press a button, which indicates that they believe the current stim-
ulus is a target, it is debatable that if this should be counted as a wrong press, or the
press can be simply neglected. After the first n stimuli, there are four possibilities of
a response to a stimulus: target with a press, target without a press, non-target with
a press, and non-target without a press. Clearly, target with a press is correct press,
and non-target with a press is wrong press. However, the rest two cases are ambiguous.
Missing a target might be counted as a wrong response, but could also be neglected
when calculating the accuracy. Similarly, no press to a non-target might be a correct re-
sponse, but could also be neglected since subjects may fail to press because they cannot
remember the stimulus n steps before, instead of knowing it is a non-target.
According to those studies that distinguished miss and wrong press, [82, 83]. The
two types of false responses actually did not correlate with each other, but missing
rate can be correlated to RT. Another feature was that most errors were missing, while
wrong pressed were rare. However, due to the fact that there were only 20 stimuli in
our 1-back test, it would be unstable to measure each kind of error individually, as the
samples would be too few. Thus, in this study, correct responses were defined as target
with a press and non-target without a press. Wrong responses were defined as target
without a press and non-target with a press. Press on the first stimulus was not counted
for neither correct nor wrong response.
5.5
Match to Sample
In the MTS tests, a stimulus is presented to subjects. After a delay, two or more options
are presented. Subjects have to determine which of them matches the original stimulus.
The MTS test was firstly introduced in 1950s by multiple researchers [84–86]. The
initial version of MTS was designed for animal experiments, as the tests are very easy
to be understood and implemented. Over the past half century, In addition to the eval-
uation of human cognitive, the MTS tests were also applied for pigeon [84–87], monkeys
[88, 89], rats [90, 91], sea lions [92, 93], and dolphins [94, 95]. Apart from general in-
telligence and cognition, MTS is also widely used in the field of neuroscience and brain

27
image [96, 97].
The difficulty of a MTS test can be manipulated from five aspects: stimulus complex-
ity, time duration of stimuli, delay between the stimuli and display of options, similarity
between targets and distractors, and the number of options. When applied to measure
short-term memory of human, MTS was usually designed to be square matrix whose cells
were filled by two colors. Such kind of MTS test is a perfect measure of the outcome
of the Pattern Memory game applied in this study, since they are very similar based
on the transfer theories mentioned in Section 4.1. In 1974, W. A. Phillips conducted a
comprehensive study which analyzed how the matrix size and delay time affected the
performance on MTS [98]. In his study, three different matrix sizes (4 × 4, 6 × 6, and
8 × 8) were tested. Subjects were students and staff of the University of Sussex. Since
the elderly in our study have cognitive abilities worse than university students and staff,
the 4 × 4 size was chosen for our study. In Phillips’ experiment, stimuli were presented
for 1 s. Then response accuracy of four different delay (0.02, 1, 3 and 9 s) were measured.
The results showed that when the delay was 0.02 s, the accuracy was almost perfect at
96.7%. Then the figure dropped to 92.8%, 85%, 73.3% for delay of 1, 3, 9 s respectively.
It seems that 3 s is a suitable delay as it allows subjects to have some room to improve
their performance, and also not too difficult for elderly people. Hence, in our study, the
MTS test was 4 × 4 matrix filled by blue and red, 1 s stimuli, 3 s delay, and two options
for choice. Totally 15 stimuli were presented to each subject.
5.6
Sternberg Scanning
The SS task is a memory scanning task proposed by Saul Sternberg in 1966 [99]. In
his original work, subjects saw a sequence of numbers and they had to remember these
numbers. After a short delay, a test number was shown and the subjects were required
to determine if the test number was one of the numbers shown previously. Initially, the
purpose of his study was to examine how our mental operations accessed our short-term
memory, and his results showed that the RT was linearly correlated to the length of
the sequence, with a slope about 40 ms per item. Based on the experiment results,
he suggested that information is stored and read in our brain serially. However, there
were also other researchers argued that the processing should be parallel or a mixture
or parallel and serial process [100–102].
In the pilot study, SS tests were conducted in order to measure visual memory. Thus,
a color scanning variation, instead of the original digit scanning was utilized. In the color
scanning test, s sequence of squares with different colors was presented. Subjects were
asked to remember the colors and after a short pause, answer if a color was shown in
the previous sequence. Such SS test has two defects. First, the subjects should not have
color blindness. Second, the theories behind the test is still debatable, which does not
meet the criteria for choosing outcome measures as mentioned in Section 5.1. Hence,
the SS test was not used in this project.

6.1
The Gold-Standard Design of Cognitive
Interventions
For most trials in medicine, psychology, and many other fields, the gold standard of the
trial design is a double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT [11]. Double-blind indicates that
both participants and testers do not know if they are in the intervention or the control
group. Blinding and the control of placebo effects eliminate the effects of expectation
which might be different between the intervention and control group. RCT requires
participants randomly assigned to an intervention group or a control group, which can
avoid bias due to selection and baseline difference.
In most medical trials (e.g., drug study), blinding is a fairly easy task as participants
and tester cannot distinguish the established drug and placebo. However, in cognitive
trials, participants have considerable chance to figure out which group they are assigned
to. For example, many action video game interventions use Tetris as a control condition.
Some participants may guess that the Tetris is a control condition because it is an
old fashion game compared with the action game intervention.
Even if participants
are perfectly blinded, so that they believe that they receive the true intervention, the
different essence of the intervention and the control condition could lead to different
expectations (i.e., placebo effect) [103].
Assume participants take a fast paced task
as an outcome measure (e.g., CRT). The action game group would likely expect more
improvement in the task than the control group who play Tetris.
In order to eliminate the potential bias due to different placebo effects, in addition
to choosing adequate control condition, it is necessary to measure the expectation of
the intervention and control condition. Ideally, such expectation measures should be
conducted for each individual outcome measure for cognitive abilities, since participants
==PAGE:44==
32
6 Experiment Procedure
could have different expectations of different cognitive tasks.
For cognitive interventions, preregistration is a critical process which can help re-
searchers to avoid potential false positive results.
Besides, it can also enhance the
transparency and avoid ambiguity of studies.
Randomization of samples prevents differences between baselines and conditions of
intervention group and control group. Effective randomization requires large samples,
which can be calculated by statistical power from pilot study. If small sample size must
be used, a compromised solution is to randomly assign participants to control condition,
while keep other factors (e.g., age, gender, etc.) to be comparable. Moreover, results
of pre-tests should also be compared so that the training process of the two groups
start at the same place [104]. If applicable, randomization can be after pre-test in order
to create matched groups. The matched randomization can be implemented by two
methods. The first one is Addelman’s block design [105]. Participants are firstly divided
into a number of subgroups based on the factors that may relate to the training (e.g.,
age and intelligence). Then in each subgroup, subjects are equally randomly assigned to
the intervention group and the control group. The second method is Feldy’s pair design
[106]. The pair design is similar to the block design, but instead of dividing subjects
into blocks (subgroups), the pair design pairs subjects based on the same factors, so in
fact the pair design can be regarded as a special case of the block design where the block
size is two.
Outcome measure is a crucial process which can significantly affect experiment re-
sults. As described in Chapter 5, multiple reliable outcome measures should be used.
According to existed evidence of transfer effect, the outcome measures should be similar
to the cognitive interventions. There is little chance to observe training outcomes from
inadequate measures.
After collecting necessary data, training outcomes are examined by statistical analy-
sis. A typical method for direct comparison is analysis of variance (ANOVA), while more
advanced approach can be multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) or analysis of
covariance (ANCOVA), which can take account of the interaction among the outcome
measures and the effects of continuous conditions (e.g., age).
When documenting cognitive trials, a Consolidated Standards Of Reporting Trials
(CONSORT) diagram should be included in order to depict the sampling process [107].
The results of all outcome measures should be documented in a table, which includes
but not limited to means, standard deviations, confidence intervals, and sample sizes.

As suggested in the last section, the sample size of the trial can be determined by

with significant difference between the intervention and control group in terms of the

the three figures indicates roughly 400 subjects would be required. However it was not
feasible to recruit so many participants in a short time. As a matter of fact, since the age
group of the subjects in the pilot study is quite different from that in this project, the

The inclusion criteria of the recruitment were:
• Older residents aged over 55, which is the standard retirement age of Chinese

• Have an contract with izhaohu for at least one month, such that subjects can

The exclusion criteria were:
• People with serious dementia and cannot understand and perform the cognitive

bad short-term memory but can easily talk with others and understand everything well.
Another mild dementia patients may still have a fairly good short-term memory, but
become unstable and irritable during communication. Although both the two example
patients have mild dementia, the former is eligible for our criteria but the latter is not.

Ideal cognitive trial should be double-blind, and subjects are randomly assigned to an
intervention group and a control group. However, when subjects could communicate
with each other, they might be able to figure out which group they were assigned to.
Thus, in the initial protocol, the trial was designed to be conducted at two nursing
homes: izhaohu Wanli (IWL) and izhaohu Ouyang (IOY). Both the two nursing homes
are located in Shanghai. IWL would be for the intervention group while IOY would
be for the control group. Such design could allow participants to be blinded well, but
the trial was not randomized. Thus, the baseline (e.g., performance in pre-test) of the

6.2.5
Outcome Measures
As described in Chapter 5, the outcome measures of cognitive abilities consisted of four
tests: CRT, n-back, VS, and MTS.
In addition to the cognitive tests, in order to collect supplementary evidence of the
training effects on physical abilities, a set of physical tests was also conducted. The
physical tests are Timed Up and Go Test (TUG), Chair Stand Test (CS), and Tandem
Walk Test (TW).
The TUG test was originally developed to evaluate mobility and fall risk [108, 109].
In the beginning, subjects sit on a standard arm chair. After receiving a start signal
from tester, subjects stand up and walk to a mark which is 3 meters away from the chair.
Then, turn walk back and sit down to the chair. The time that it takes for the whole
process is measured in seconds.
The CS test measures lower limb strength, range of motion, balance, and endurance
[110]. Subjects are asked to stand up from a chair and then sit down. The process need
to be repeated for five times. Total time that was spent is measured.
The main goal of the TW test is to measure balance ability and fall risk [111]. Sub-
jects are required to walk heel to toe for ten steps. The time that is spent is recorded.
If subjects lose balance during the test, the number of steps before losing balance is
recorded.

Analyses Method
After collecting all necessary data, the distribution of the data was firstly analyzed. Since
the trial was not fully randomized, the baselines of the intervention group and the control
group should be compared. If the data had been normal distributed and the baselines of
the two groups had not been comparable, ANCOVA would have been applied in order
to compensate the mismatch of baselines. Matched baselines and normal distribution
would allow us to apply ANOVA for the analyses. Lastly if the data was not normal
distributed, non-parametric tests (e.g., Mann-Whitney U test) would be applied.

n-back. However, since the baselines of the accuracy of n-back of the intervention group
and the control group did not match each other as mentioned in Section 7.2, it is not
credible to conclude that the intervention outperformed the control condition in terms
of the n-back test.
In Table 7.4, we can see that the changes of the performance in the accuracy of the
n-back test of both the intervention group and the control group fit normal distribution.
Hence, ANCOVA was applied to compensate the mismatch of the baselines. The results
of regression part of ANCOVA is shown in Table 7.6, where the adjusted means of the
samples can be found. The ANOVA part of the ANCOVA showed that the difference
between the intervention group and the control group was not significant after controlling
for the pre-test performance (α = 0.05, p = 0.63).

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
In this chapter, the trial design will be reviewed and discussed based on the implemen-
tation. Also the experiment results will be discussed in relation to the previous studies
and theories presented in Chapter 4 and 5.
8.1
Refection on the Trial Design
Although the study protocol was designed following existed theories and evidence, there
were still some drawbacks emerged during the trial. Hence, in this section, the pros and
cons of the trial design will be discussed.
8.1.1
Intervention
Generally, most participants and caregivers were happy with the MOTO tiles. They
commented that the MOTO tiles made their routine walking exercise interesting. By
contrast, traditional training (e.g., parallel walking bars) is usually boring. There was
one older resident (did not be analyzed because of dementia, but participated some
MOTO games) at ILF who had never smiled before, but smiled during her first play of
the MOTO tiles. The expert of izhaohu working with us evaluated the MOTO tiles as
follows (translated from original Chinese text):
The MOTO tiles are entertaining. It requires certain level of standing and
cognitive abilities (below mild dementia).
Thus, it is more suitable for
community-dwelling and day-care elderly. The modularity and vast number
of games allow caregivers and therapists to create more interesting games
and training methods. To some extent, the essence of entertainment is able
to improve users’ sense and motivation of participation. Furthermore, it can
also increase social intercourse indirectly.
Nevertheless, there were still some negative feedback and observations regarding the
MOTO tiles training. Our training was originally designed for seniors with relatively
normal state without any cognitive challenge apart from normal age-related decline.
However, due to the Chinese tradition of filial piety, most family only send their seniors to
elderly care facilities when they have relatively serious aging symptoms (e.g., Alzheimer’s
==PAGE:62==
50
8 Discussion
disease).
This phenomenon made the population of the trial include some samples
with different extent of cognitive impairment. A common consequence of it was that
the elderly sometimes had difficulty to switch between different games. For example,
after playing the Special Pattern game, when switching to the Final Countdown, some
of them might still look for a tile with four LEDs on, even though they were told
that this was a new game and the rule of the game was clearly illustrated. Besides,
some participants could hardly understand the memory games Simon Says and Pattern
Memory. Normally the two games lasted for about 5 minutes. They may spend 3 to
4 minutes understanding the games, and left only 1 minute to play. This would made
the training frustrating for both the elderly and caregivers. Therefore, future training
program could be designed based on the cognitive ability of target users. For those who
have relatively good cognitive ability, multiple games including memory games can be
assigned. For those whose cognitive ability has declined, they could simply play some
intuitive games such as Color Race and Special One. The games could also be repeated
for a number of times rather than switching between different games.
The training session in this trial consisted of five games. Typical time to complete
one session was 20-30 minutes. In practical, the older adults usually got tired in 10-15
minutes, and thus needed a long break at that moment. Future training session can be
reduced to a short session like 15 minutes, but with a higher frequency of sessions, such
as twice a day.
There was one participant complaining that he was not comfortable to use eyes
intensively. In addition, a professional staff member of izhaohu reminded that there
were some older adults could not look at something too bright. Consequently, we can
consider to make the brightness of the LEDs on the tile adjustable, so that every elderly
people can play the MOTO tiles comfortably.
8.1.2
Cognitive Tests
In this study, four cognitive tests (CRT, VS, n-back, and MTS) were applied to measure
training outcome. The tests were implemented on Android tablets. The tablet tests
went smoothly in the pilot tests where subjects were students from DTU. However,
when the elderly took the test, a few of them need quite a long time to understand how
to manipulate tablets, even though they simply needed to touch left or right side of
screens. Another issue of the tablet tests was that the elderly generally have significant
decline in their eye sight. Thus the small and bright screen prevented some older adults
to take the tests. One possible solution can be projecting the tests on a bigger screen,
and connecting physical buttons for answering the tests.
Similar to the MOTO tiles games, a few subjects had difficulty in switching between
different cognitive tests, especially when switching from VS feature to VS conjunction.
Moreover, n-back seemed to be the most difficult test to be understood, even though
it was the basic 1-back version that was applied. Simpler tests should be considered in
future research.
==PAGE:63==
8.1 Refection on the Trial Design
51
Although the number of stimuli had been significantly reduced from the pilot study,
still some subjects felt that the tests were too long. Since less stimuli could lead to
unstable test result, future study may provide very limited number of stimuli, but repeat
the test session a number of times to evaluate cognitive abilities. For example, design a
10-minutes test session and conduct the session once a day in three consecutive days.
8.1.3
Physical Tests
TUG, CS, and TW tests were conducted in this trial. The measures and analyses of the
TUG and CS tests went smoothly. However, in the TW test, considerable inter- and
intra-group variance was observed. Many subjects actually could not walk for a single
tandem step. The measure of the TW test also left a lot of uncertainty, as judgment
of error step was very tricky and unreliable. Consequently, future research could apply
relatively stable tests, such as tandem stance and four square step test [116].
8.1.4
Measure of Expectation
The measure of expectation was a unique part of this study. However, the measures were
simplified to the expectation to overall cognitive ability instead of individual cognitive
task due to declined cognitive functioning of the elderly. In Table 7.2 and B.1 it can be
seen that the average expectation was close to 1 (no effect). Many subjects answered
that they did not believe there could be positive effect because they knew aging and
related disease (e.g., Alzheimer’s disease) were irreversible.
In order to investigate the relationship between the expectation measures and the
placebo effects, the training outcome and the expectation were presented in a scatter plot
shown in Figure 8.1. The rate of change was calculated as the average of the percentage
changes of all the RTs and accuracy of the five cognitive tests. Since most subjects
answered no effects as their expectation, it is difficult to observe significant correlation
between the expectation and the training outcome. In the future, if sufficient samples can
be obtained, a model would be constructed so that the placebo effects can be eliminated
from the outcome measures.
8.1.5
Randomization and Blinding
The trial was originally designed to be conducted at two places for blinding purpose.
However, due to the lack of participants, the trial was modified to be partially random-
ized, but some subjects from the control group were able to communicate with subjects
from the intervention group.
In the last section, we found that actually most participants did not expect that the
training or control condition could bring benefit to their cognitive abilities. The average
of their expectation were close to no effect. Consequently, isolating participants might
not be too necessary in future study where target participants are older adults. Rather,

8.2
Training Outcomes
The results of the trial has been presented in Chapter 7. Here we will discuss the effect
of the intervention based on these results.
8.2.1
Effects on Cognitive Abilities
The statistical analyses of the cognitive tests revealed that the intervention group out-
performed the control group in the RTs of VS feature and VS conjunction.
Due to
mismatched baseline in the pre-test of the accuracy of the n-back test, it was reana-
lyzed by ANCOVA which took the pre-test performance into account. The result of
ANCOVA indicated that there was no significant difference though direct comparison
showed significant difference. ANCOVA used linear regression to eliminate the effect of
covariates, but how pre-test correlated to the changes of performance was not clear, so
there still can be small possibility that there was significant difference in the accuracy
of the n-back test. However, since the analysis should be rigorous, we must regard it
insignificant based on current evidence.
In this trial, the intervention included only 10 sessions of 30 minutes training, which
lasted for about two weeks. From the review in Section 1.2, we can see the time frame
==PAGE:65==
8.2 Training Outcomes
53
of most large-scale cognitive studies varied from 5 weeks to 6 months. The shortest
training was the RTS game intervention conducted by Bask et al. [48], which consisted
of 22.5 hours training in total (15 sessions of 1.5 hour). In contrast, we had only 5 hours
training in total. Therefore, we could expect that if a longer length training is conducted
in the future, more remarkable outcome will be observed from the results.
The results of the trial somewhat matched the transfer theories described in Chapter
4.
Since many subjects did not understand the memory games very well, we could
estimate that their improvement in the memory games was little, and consequently
there should be even less transfer effects on the memory tests. By contrast, most subjects
enjoyed the attention games (e.g., special one), which resulted in significant transfer to
the VS task whose objective is similar to the attention games (i.e., searching for a special
target).
However, note that previous studies proved that aerobic exercises could produce
positive effects on cognitive abilities as reviewed in Section 1.3, which leads to a question:
was the improvement resulted from the games themselves, or the fact that the play of
MOTO tiles is a kind of aerobic exercise? In some recent reviews [54, 55], aerobic exercise
could be beneficial to the overall cognitive abilities, including memory, processing speed,
etc. Thus, we could infer that the effect of aerobic exercise was insignificant as the
improvement in memory tasks was not observed. The inconsistency to the previous
theories might be due to the short time frame of the training. Among those studies
where aerobic exercises were effective, the minimal training duration was 9 weeks [117],
whereas in this study the duration was only about 2 weeks. Further research may conduct
a longer trial in order to obtain the effects of aerobic exercise.
8.2.2
Physical Abilities
Physical ability tests were also conducted in order to verify the results of previous
studies. In this study, the intervention group did not outperform the control group in
the TUG and CS tests in terms of the changes of performance during the training. Direct
comparison of the two groups of the TW test was not possible due to lack of samples,
but the intervention group improved significantly in terms of the time.
In Jessen’s previous study [10], the analysis method was different from this study.
In this study, the changes of performance of the intervention group and the control
group were compared, whereas in Jessen’s study, the performance of pre- and post-test
were compared.
We argue that the comparison of changes should me more credible
since the placebo effect can be eliminated by such comparisons. By contrast, the signif-
icant improvement from pre-test to post-test might contain more or less placebo effect.
Moreover, Comparison of changes also takes possible decline of the elderly into account.
However, since Jessen’s study only focused on physical abilities, which are not easy to
be influenced by psychological effects comparing with cognitive abilities, their results
can still be regarded as reliable evidence.
==PAGE:66==
54
8 Discussion
The analyses results of Jessen’s study revealed that there were significant improve-
ment in the CS and TW tests, but the improvement in the TUG test was not significant.
The outcomes of TW and TUG matched the results of our study, but in our study
the intervention group did not perform better in the CS test. The mismatch could be
attributed to the small sample size and short training period in this trial. There were
totally 24 training sessions over a span of 12 weeks in Jessen’s study, and 30 subjects
were enrolled for each group. Note that their training was quite moderated (roughly 2
times a week), but in our study it was an intensive training (roughly once a day and
totally 10 sessions). Thus, it would also be worthwhile to investigate whether intensive
and moderated training produce different effects in future research.
8.2.3
Statistical Power
In addition to hypothesis tests, statistical power is another vital factor that is worth
noticing. In Table 7.5, it can been seen that for most cases where there were no significant
difference, their statistical power were pretty small, except for the CS test whose power
was 0.746. Such values indicated that there was a high probability that there actually
was significant difference but the statistical tests did not find it. Apparently this is due
to insufficient sample size. In further researches, ample participants should be recruited
if available.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have presented novel physical brain games implemented by the MOTO
tiles. The games were tested and evaluated by a controlled trial conducted in Shanghai.
In the introduction chapter, we firstly reviewed theories and applications about play-
ware and the MOTO tiles. Then, we looked at previous evidence of effects of brain
games and aerobic exercise. The review of previous studies implied that the MOTO
tiles game could be beneficial to cognitive abilities.
Before this master project, a pilot study was conducted in order to collected prelimi-
nary information for the design of the brain games. In the pilot study, 20 student from
DTU were enrolled. The results showed that even after a very brief MOTO tile training,
subjects can still get improvement in reaction time of certain cognitive tasks.
Most previous studies of brain games concluded the same fact that brain game train-
ing can only produce effects on cognitive tasks that are similar to the training (i.e., near
transfer). However, how to define the similarity is still debatable. We implemented the
CHC model in order to define the similarity, which is a relatively widely accepted and
operational theory in the field of cognition. Based on the CHC model, five MOTO tile
games were selected and designed for the training of different cognitive abilities. After-
wards, four cognitive tests were chosen as outcome measures because they were similar
to the games.
The trial protocol was designed following the gold standard of cognitive intervention
proposed by Simons et al. [11]. A unique feature of this trial is the measure of expec-
tation, which allowed us to eliminate placebo effects in analyses. Unfortunately, due to
practical issues and overestimation of cognitive abilities of the elderly, the sample size
and the training sessions were reduced a lot from the original plan.
Despite the large modification of the trial, some positive effects were still be found. In
the cognitive tests, the RTs of VS feature and VS conjunction were improved significantly
in the intervention group, which implied the designed MOTO tile training is of benefit to
attention and scanning abilities. In the physical tests, the improvement in TW revealed
positive effect on balance ability.
The limitation of this study and possible future research were discussed in Chapter
8. The main shortcoming was the small sample size, which lead to low statistical power
of analyses. The low statistical power meant there could be some significant differences
that were not found by our analyses. The training period was also relatively short due to
the time limit of the master project. A long-term intervention with abundant subjects

may lead to more positive findings.
In the review of outcome measures and interventions, we have discussed that tablets
may not be an appropriate tool to evaluate the cognitive ability of the elderly. Physical
controller and bigger screen would be a better choice for this purpose. Some tests and
games were too difficult for some elderly adults, so it would be necessary to catego-
rize subjects into subgroups based on their abilities, and then design tests and games
accordingly.
