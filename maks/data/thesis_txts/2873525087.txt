Abstract
The transition towards a lowยญcarbon energy system in France and Europe is driving signifยญ
icant investments in hydrogen infrastructure, particularly to support renewable energy inยญ
tegration and decarbonize industrial and mobility sectors. Hydrogen infrastructure develยญ

seeking to formulate tailored strategies. This study examines the relevance of establishยญ
ing a hydrogen transmission network in Franceโs GrandยญOuest region (Brittany and Pays
de la Loire), characterised by strong renewable energy potential. It investigates the facยญ
tors influencing spatial configuration, production location, and regional integration within
the broader European hydrogen economy.
Using the openยญsource energy system model PyPSAยญEur, this research employs scenarioยญ
based analysis to assess six alternative futures for 2050, varying policy constraints, techยญ
nology costs, renewable deployment patterns, and hydrogen demand assumptions. The
model covers France at NUTS1 resolution and interconnected European countries at naยญ
tional level, with hourly or two hourly temporal granularity to capture renewable variability
and system interactions. Emphasis is placed on creating a replicable methodology that
can be applied to other regions in France and Europe, offering strategic insights for poliยญ
cymakers and industry stakeholders.
The findings demonstrate that hydrogen network development in GrandยญOuest is conยญ

resilience to demand variations compared to continental averages.
The analysis reveals that GrandยญOuest exhibits both convergent and divergent responses
relative to the continental system, demonstrating functional adaptability that enables tranยญ

regionโs coastal location, renewable resources, and strategic connectivity create persisยญ

These results provide evidenceยญbased support for hydrogen infrastructure investment in

Background
The integration of renewable energy into Europeโs energy system is essential to achievยญ

a strategic energy vector, enabling the decarbonisation of โhardยญtoยญabateโ sectors, (e.g.

In France, the Grand Ouest region ยญwhich includes Brittany and Pays de la Loire ยญ holds
significant potential for offshore wind development due to favourable wind conditions and
proximity to coastal areas [3], and a politically supported deployment trajectory. Recent
national planning documents have updated offshore wind targets to as much as 25 GW
across these two regions, highlighting their strategic role in the national and European enยญ

solar and onshore wind. However, fully exploiting this potential remains challenging due
to the inherent intermittency of renewable energy sources. While recent policy develยญ

actual role of the Grand Ouest in the future French hydrogen system remains uncertain.
The regionโs localisation (quite isolated, especially for Brittany), distance from major deยญ

to the local use or transport of renewable electricity.
Existing studies have assessed hydrogen infrastructure needs at national or European
scales [5, 6] , yet few have focused on the specific conditions of the Grand Ouest, nor
tested how different policy and technoยญeconomic parameters might create configurations
in which regional hydrogen production becomes advantageous.
The development of

future hydrogen applications, excluding power generation, require a consistent and reliยญ

network. Additionally, its widespread adoption remains uncertain. While some scenarios
envisage high hydrogen demand, justifying rapid investment in transport and storage inยญ

9]. Furthermore, alternative solutions of importing hydrogen derivative such as ammonia

Energy system modelling tool
Energy models simulate and optimize energy systems, allowing researchers to quantify
the impacts of decisions such as infrastructure investments, operational strategies, and
renewable integration. They are essential for addressing temporal variability, spatial dyยญ

requirements: it must enable a comprehensive representation of hydrogen transport netยญ

teractions with existing energy infrastructures. The framework should support a temporal
resolution fine enough to capture the variability of renewable energy generation and deยญ

days throughout the year. Additionally, the framework should be robust, validated, and
actively maintained, with frequent applications documented in contemporary research and
be openยญsource to facilitate transparency, adaptability and collaboration,
To identify the most suitable tool for this study, comparative analysis between several
openยญsource energy system modelling frameworks were reviewed [11, 12, 13, 14]. These
frameworks vary in terms of spatial and temporal resolution, hydrogen transport modelling

3.1.1
PyPSA Framework
Python for Power System Analysis (PyPSA) is an openยญsource framework developed to
model and optimise integrated energy systems over multiple temporal and spatial scales.
It is designed to support both operational dispatch and longยญterm investment planning,
using linear or mixedยญinteger linear programming formulations to achieve computational
tractability even in largeยญscale applications [40].
PyPSA is inherently multiยญenergyยญcarrier, enabling the representation of sector coupling
across electricity, hydrogen, gas, heat, transport, agriculture and industry, while integratยญ
ing conversion processes such as electrolysis, methanation or fuel synthesis, as well as
various storage technologies.
In this way, the framework provides a detailed representation of production, storage, transยญ
port and consumption infrastructures. Thanks to full parametrisation (yields, costs, availยญ
ability profiles, emission factors), it offers a high degree of adaptability. Its integration with
the broader Python ecosystem facilitates interoperability with other tools for data processยญ
ing, visualisation, and geospatial analysis.
3.1.2
PyPSAยญeur Model
PyPSAยญEur is a sectorยญcoupled model of the European energy system, built upon the
PyPSA framework. It provides a detailed representation of electricity, hydrogen, and heat
systems across Europe, with input data derived from geospatial, technoยญeconomic, and
meteorological sources. Indeed, it already provides readyยญtoยญuse open dataset that inยญ
cludes Europeโs highยญvoltage transmission network, weatherยญbased renewable potentials,
technology costs, and sectoral energy demands [22]. In this study, PyPSAยญEur serves as
the foundation for simulating the French energy system, with adaptations to focus on
hydrogen production and transport in the Grand Ouest region under various policy and
technological scenarios.
3.1.3
Data Sources and Input Datasets
PyPSAยญeur includes several openยญsources datasets to represent the European energy
system with spatial and technological granularity. The meteorological inputs for renewยญ
able energy generation are derived from a combination of ERA5 reanalysis, data set on
climate and weather, and SARAHยญ3 solar radiation datasets, processed via the atlite tool
to produce siteยญspecific generation profiles [41]. Technological assumptions ยญ including
costs and efficiencies ยญ are based on the PyPSA technology database [42] and the Danยญ
ish Energy Agencyโs energy storage technology catalogue [43]. The highยญvoltage electricยญ
ity transmission grid is reconstructed from OpenStreetMap data using a prebuilt network
[44]. The distribution of demand for industry uses the geographical data from the hotmaps
industrial database [45]. Underground storage potentials for hydrogen in European salt
caverns is acquired from Caglayan et al [46]. Additional spatial data from the PyPSAยญEur
data bundle provides constraints on land use, population distribution, and growth domesยญ
tic product [47].

Optimisation
The model coยญoptimises the investment and operation of generation, storage, conversion
and transmission infrastructure for the leastยญcost outcome in a single linear optimisation
problem.
Network
PyPSAยญeur simplifies energy exchange representation by excluding distribution networks
for electricity and hydrogen. Only the total power exchange capacity between transmisยญ
sion and distribution level is coยญoptimised [5]. The radial paths of the network, i.e. nodes
with only one connection, are eliminated by connecting remote resources to adjacent
nodes and transforming the network to a uniform voltage level of 380 kV. Each region
is modelled as a single aggregate node, balancing hydrogen and electricity production
and consumption. Hydrogen pipelines are represented using a marketยญbased approach,
without hydraulic considerations or compression systems. Energy flows are constrained
by interยญregional exchange capacities and limits on the hourly rate of flow variations.
Demand
The distribution of demand for industry uses the geographical data from the hotmaps inยญ
dustrial database [45]. The remaining electricity for households and services is distributed
inside each country in function of the population and the growth domestic product (GDP).
The default is set to 40% according to population and 60% according to GDP.
Energy production
All major generation technologies can be included, such as wind (onshore/offshore), soยญ
lar PV, hydroelectricity, nuclear, and gas turbines. Renewable generation is based on
hourly weather data and spatial potentials, while fossil generation includes capacity, effiยญ
ciency, and emission constraints. Renewable energy capacities (e.g., wind onshore/offยญ
shore, solar PV) are geographically distributed according to resource availability, landยญuse
constraints, and predefined potential maps. For instance, wind and solar potentials are
computed using historical weather data (from reanalysis datasets such as ERA5) comยญ
bined with land eligibility filters (e.g., protected areas, slope, population density). These
potentials define the maximum installable capacity per node. Fossil and nuclear generaยญ
tion is distributed based on existing infrastructure, reported capacities in open databases
(e.g., ENTSOยญE, OPSD), and future expansion plans when applicable. In optimisation
runs, the model allocates new generation capacity across the network nodes based on
economic efficiency (LCOE, transmission constraints, CO2 costs) while respecting techniยญ
cal and geographical constraints. This results in spatially optimized capacity layouts, with
renewables concentrated in highยญresource areas and flexible generation placed closer to
demand centres or storage hubs.
Energy carriers such as hydrogen and synthetic fuels can be produced from electricity
using electrolysers and methanation, with their own conversion efficiencies. Flexibility
in energy production is introduced via battery storage, hydrogen storage, demandยญside
management (e.g., EVs, heat), and interconnections. The model also accounts for CO2
pricing and carbon budgets to steer generation choices in line with decarbonization tarยญ
gets.
3.1.5
Imports
In PyPSA, the European Union is assumed to be fully autonomous in the production of key
industrial commodities such as steel, and aluminium. This implies that no imports of these
goods are considered, and all domestic demand must be met by internal EU production
capacities. Imports for energy commodities (gas, hydrogen, oil, ammonia and methanol)
is an option.

Liquid hydrocarbure
PyPSA treats each liquid hydrocarbure (oil and methanol) as a single energy vector, with
all production inputs (e.g. oil refining, FischerโTropsch synthesis and eยญbiofuels) feeding
into one aggregated node (EU oil or EU methanol). This creates a pooled system in which
total production is collected and then redistributed. For example, it simplifies the Fischerยญ
Tropsch process, which produces a mixture of products (approximately half kerosene,
one quarter diesel and one quarter naphtha) by modelling it as producing only generic
โoilโ rather than specific fuel types. It has also for consequences to not consider transport
of these hydrocarbure (i.e, the supply can feed any demand in EU, disregarding their
location). However, this approach prevents decarbonisation from being directly linked
to specific sectors, such as aviation. The transportation of liquid hydrocarbons (e.g. oil
and methanol) is not considered a limiting factor from a technical standpoint, and the
associated cost being low, it is deemed negligible, i.e. production and consumption does
not consider geographical constraint.

6.4
Study limitations and future research directions
This study presents several methodological and structural limitations that should be conยญ

6.4.1

While the study adopts a regional (NUTS1) resolution for France and a national resolution

subยญregional dynamics within the Grand Ouest. In particular, the localisation of hydrogen
demand and production sitesโsuch as coastal electrolysis units or industrial offยญtakersโ

6.4.2
Weather data
The modelling relies on weather data from the year 2023 to derive renewable generaยญ
tion profiles using reanalysis datasets. While this approach ensures internal consistency
across scenarios, it does not account for potential changes in climate patterns by 2050.
Consequently, interยญannual variability and longยญterm climate evolutionโsuch as shifts in
wind regimes, solar irradiance, or extreme weather eventsโare not captured. This limiยญ
tation may affect the robustness of results, particularly regarding the availability and reliยญ

6.4.3
Cost assumptions
Cost assumptions were based on the 2020 version of the technology_data_for _el_and_dh
dataset [43]. A more recent update (2025) is available and would likely provide more acยญ
curate technoยญeconomic parameters. Even though the majority of the data has not change
significantly, and the major differences for offshore wind have been manually corrected,
as cost assumptions have a direct influence on technology deployment decisions, the use
of outdated data introduces uncertainty in the economic viability of hydrogen infrastructure
deployment.
6.4.4
Wind turbines and capacity factors
A further limitation concerns the renewable generation profiles. Capacity factors for wind
and solar were computed using the Atlite tool, which applies a single wind turbine type for
each category (onshore and offshore). This simplification does not reflect the technologยญ

bine types were manually selected from the Atlite catalogue to better match the DEA

A
PyPSA general hypothesis modelling
A.1
Industry
In PyPSAโs industrial modelling, most of the decarbonisation trajectories are represented
through technological evolution and the gradual substitution of conventional processes
by lowยญcarbon alternatives in several key sectors. In the steel industry, primary steel
production rises from 60% in 2020 to 30% in 2050, reflecting an increase in recycling. By
2050, 100% of primary steel production will be based on the direct reduction of ore (DRI)
process. In aluminium production, the share of primary metal will gradually decrease to
20% in 2050, also illustrating a strengthened circular dynamic.
Basic chemistry is modelled without including ammonia and with a particular focus on amยญ
monia and valuable hydrocarbons (HVCs), chlorine or methanol. HVC production gradยญ
ually moves from a high dependence on virgin materials in 2020 to 40% in 2050, with
a combined rise in mechanical (30%) and chemical (20%) recycling and 10% of plastics
being reused (modelled by a fall in demand for HVC). Ammonia, which is modelled sepยญ
arately, can be produced by steam reforming of methane or by electrolysis. The cracking
of ammonia, used to transport hydrogen, is modelled.
A.2
Heat
Heat demand is modelled with a focus on progressive electrification for the residential
and tertiary sectors. Space and water heating are supplied by resistive heaters, electric
boilers, and largeยญscale heat pumps (especially in district heating systems), while there
is a gradual heat demand reduction (ยญ29% by 2050) thanks to the increase of insulation.
The model also incorporates thermal energy storage (TES) and allows for oversizing of
heating generators to better meet peak demand and both urban central and decentral
district heating networks are enabled. Additionally, the model considers waste heat reยญ
covery, including from electrolysers, fuel cells, and other conversion processes, which
can contribute to district heating supply where available.
