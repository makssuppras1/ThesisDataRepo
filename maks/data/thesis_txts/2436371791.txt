Abstract 
 
Shale is a fine-grained sedimentary rock, representing a big part of the stratigraphic column of the Earth’s 
crust. Although it commonly acts like a source rock by releasing hydrocarbons into reservoir rocks, recent 
research has indicated that shale might act as an unconventional reservoir, behaving simultaneously as a 
source and a reservoir rock. Throughout this project, a petrophysical analysis of a high porosity shale 
formation, located in the Danish North Sea, was conducted. The objective was to investigate the potential 
presence of hydrocarbons and, gas, in particular. The shale of this study, consists part of a specific mudstone 
formation, extending over the central and northern North Sea, referred to as the Lark Formation. 
In order to evaluate the entire formation with respect to the different types of sedimentary rocks, Gamma 
Ray, Density-Neutron porosity, Caliper, Spectral GR, Sonic and Resistivity depth plots were used, along 
with 25 core samples from various depths of the well. The depth interval of the investigation ranged from 
1200 m to 2500 m. Specifically, 1270-1711 m were identified as shale, 1712-1985 m as shale with slightly 
different properties and 1986-2500 m as limestone. Within the shale layer of interest (1270-1711m), grain 
density was estimated according to the mineralogical composition of the core samples that were available. 
The cementation exponent 𝑚 and saturation exponent 𝑛 were assessed for the entire shale interval, by 
creating a physical model with respect to the Neutron Log. In addition, the resistivity of the water in the 
undisturbed zone was assessed according to the Pickett Plot, while porosity and water saturation were 
extensively studied. Brittleness modelling was used in order to evaluate the stiffness of the formation.  
Grain density was found to vary from 2.485 g/cm3 to 2.856 g/cm3. Results based on the cementation 
exponent 𝑚 and saturation exponent 𝑛 indicated 4 areas with distinct parameters: 1264-1388 m with 𝑚 = 
2.5, 𝑛 = 3; 1389-1500 m with 𝑚 = 2.15, 𝑛 = 2.15; 1501-1638 m with 𝑚 = 1.75, 𝑛 = 1.75; 1639-1711 m with 
𝑚 = 1.45, 𝑛 = 1.45. The resistivity of the water in the undisturbed zone, 𝑅𝑤, was estimated to be 0.03 ohm 
m. Results from porosity evaluation displayed particularly high values, varying from 20 to 40%, indicating 
a high porosity area. Combining the above and assessing the water saturation, the presence of high 
hydrocarbon levels was verified, indicating a gas reservoir within the shale. To estimate the gas saturation, 
three grain densities were examined, a minimum of 2.485 g/cm3, an average of 2.626 g/cm3 and a maximum 
of 2.856 g/cm3, designating high gas saturations, ranging from 20 to 60%. Brittleness modelling of the 
formation revealed a combination of soft solid and stiff fluid. Although that would indicate a water-wet 
reservoir and thus, a producible gas, the softness of the solid could prevent the development and 
preservation of hydraulic fracturing, which is a highly preferred technique, used to increase the production 
of a well.

CHAPTER ONE – OVERVIEW 
 
1.1 Introduction 
 
One of the most abundant sedimentary rocks representing a big part of the Earth’s stratigraphic column is 
shale. Shale is a fine-grained sedimentary rock that consists of clay-sized mineral particles and its properties 
define it as one of the most important energy resources, since it often acts as a source rock for hydrocarbon 
release. Conventional reservoirs commonly constitute of a source and a reservoir rock. During migration, 
hydrocarbons are released from the fine grained, low permeable source rock and are transported into the 
porous, permeable reservoir rock. Typically, shale behaves as a source rock, while sandstones and 
limestones act as reservoir rocks. However, recent research has revealed that shales might as well contain 
significant amounts of hydrocarbons and have the potential to be economically viable reservoirs. They are 
often referred to as unconventional reservoirs, since the same rock acts simultaneously as a source and 
reservoir rock (Jadoon et al., 2016; Aguilera, 2016).  
 
                                            
Figure 1: Shale gas resources around the globe (Boyer et al., 2011) 
 
Production of hydrocarbons from shale deposits began with the Barnett Shale in Texas, where the first well 
for the exclusive production of gas from shale was drilled. Since then, many regions with shale gas potential 
have been studied (Boyer et al., 2011). The United States energy Information Administration (EIA) has 
assessed reservoirs that are located around the world, which are displayed in Figure 1.

1.2.2 Lark Formation 
 
The depth of investigation was between 1200 and 2500 m with a particular interest at the shale interval of 
1270-1711 m. The specific location is part of a specific formation, referred to as the Lark Formation, with 
a starting depth of 1300 m (TVDSS) and an ending depth of 1650 m (TVDSS). The Lark is a subsurface 
formation located in the Danish and the UK section of the North Sea, above the Horda Formation. It extends 
over the central and northern North Sea and it reaches a maximum thickness of 1194 m on Siri-3 well. 
It is characterized by brown-grey mudstone, partly silty, grading into sandstones as moving upwards to the 
Skade Formation. It is divided into two layers with distinct features, the upper Lark and the lower Lark. Its 
base is dominated by a downward change from brown-grey mudstone into darker, greenish grey mudstone 
and silty mudstone. The boundary with the Horda Formation is marked by a downhole decrease in the 
Gamma Ray Log. The upper Lark is characterized by pale to dark brown-grey mudstone with intervals of 
green-grey mudstone in its lower parts (Aabø, 2016). 
 
1.2.3 Objective 
 
The objective of the specific project was to complete a petrophysical analysis of the Lark Formation, by 
investigating the shale rock of the 1200-1700 m interval, in search of the potential existence of 
hydrocarbons and gas, in particular. The study includes: 
 Cross-Plotting for quality check of data 
 Well Logging Interpretation for the identification of the layers with distinct properties 
 Formation pressure and temperature evaluation 
 Grain density definition from mineralogical composition 
 Determination of the water resistivity in the undisturbed zone (Rw)  
 Estimation of water saturation (Sw) and porosity (φ) 
 Comparison of water saturation and porosity with respect to varying grain densities 
 Assessment of cementation exponent 𝑚 and saturation exponent 𝑛 by modelling the Neutron Log 
 Evaluation of solid and formation fluid stiffness

CHAPTER TWO – THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
 
2.1 Well Logging 
 
Well logging, also referred to as borehole logging, is a practice that is used in order to record a formation´s 
properties and is aiming at exploring the subsurface. It can be described as a technique that performs a 
continuous, detailed recording of the geophysical and petrophysical parameters of the formation all along 
a borehole and is a necessary tool for the Oil and Gas Industry. 
There are mainly two different types of well logs, including Wireline Logging and Logging while drilling 
(LWD). The operational method is the same for both of them. A string or logging tool attached at the end 
of a wireline cable is lowered inside the wellbore and takes a variety of measurements by using different 
kinds of sensors. The data can either be transferred at an electronic device in the surface or be recorded and 
kept in the logging tool until the wireline is removed from the well. Well Logging can only be performed 
when the well has reached its total depth as the measurements are taken from the bottom of the hole and 
upwards. On the contrary, LWD can be used during the drilling process and provide measurements at the 
same time while the well is being drilled. 
 
2.1.1 Gamma Ray and Spectral Gamma Ray Log 
 
The GR Logging is a technique for measuring the natural emission of radioactivity of a formation. In nearly 
all the cases, all the gamma radiation that is present in the earth is emitted by the radioactive Potassium 
isotope of atomic weight 40 (K40), while other radioactive elements are Uranium and Thorium (Fabricius, 
2003). A combination of those three element’s radioactivity is the result of the simple GR Log, measured 
in API (American Petroleum Institute) units. 
The GR Log is considered to be a good shale indicator, as shales and clays have by far the strongest natural 
radioactivity (Figure 4). Cleaner formations such as sandstones and limestones have usually a very low 
radioactivity and for this reason the GR Log can also be referred to as the “Shale Log”. However, not every 
shale is radioactive and not every formation with high radioactivity is shale.

Figure 4: Radioactivity level for various rock types (Bigelow, 1992) 
Regarding its purpose and uses, qualitatively, the log can be used for lithology identification as it represents 
a good shale indicator and it can provide information about the depths that shale might be located. 
Quantitatively, is principally used to estimate the shale volume. 
The Spectral Gamma Ray Log indicates the amount of each of the most common components of naturally 
occurring radiation in rocks (Potassium, Thorium, Uranium-K, Th, and U, respectively) that contribute to 
the total radioactivity of the formation. The total gamma ray spectra measured, is resolved into those three 
elements. Therefore, the gamma ray emission of the elements is measured separately, and three different 
depth plots can be presented. The applications of spectral-gamma ray logs focus on the relationships 
between the three elemental concentrations. In particular, Th/K and Th/U are often used for petrophysical 
interpretation and log correlation. 
 
2.1.2 Caliper Log 
 
The Caliper Log is a tool for measuring the diameter, size and shape of a borehole during the drilling process 
of a well. It is used as an indicator of the condition of the wellbore, therefore its main application is the 
quality check of the hole and to what extent the drilling mud has managed to maintain the stability of the 
well. The measurements are taken by a tool with 2, 4, or more extendable arms pushed against the borehole 
wall and linked into a cursor. Any movement of the arms is translated into movement of the cursor and is 
converted into electrical signals which are then transformed into diameter variations after calibration  
The recorded data are of great importance for the inspection of the well and produce useful and reliable 
results when combing with other logs. In many cases there are deviations between the bit diameter and the 
diameter measured by the caliper tool. These deviations can be either positive (larger diameter than the bit 
size) or negative (smaller diameter than the bit size). Larger measured diameters than the bit size is an 
indication of cavings or washed out holes. The former happens when the rock layer where drilling takes 
place is weak and pieces of rock from the walls break into the hole, while the latter happens when the 
==PAGE:21==

borehole walls are eroded by the circulating drilling mud. Other possibilities of presence of cavings are 
because of the bouncing of the bit or they preexisted in the formation. 
 
2.1.3 Resistivity Log 
 
The Resistivity Log measures the resistivity of a formation and is expressed in ohm-m. The majority of 
rock materials are insulators and they present no resistance to the passage of electrical currents. Hence, a 
porous formation, containing salty water would present a low resistivity. Opposing to that, hydrocarbons 
present a high resistance to electrical currents, therefore high resistivity responses might indicate 
hydrocarbon- bearing formations. Typically, porous rocks containing hydrocarbons produce very different 
responses than rocks filled with formation water. Therefore, the Resistivity Log is of great value in 
formation evaluation as it is a good indicator of possible productive horizons. Overall, it can be mainly used 
to identify hydrocarbon versus water-bearing formations and determine a crucial petrophysical parameter, 
the water saturation Sw. 
However, in the case of shaly formations it is not always easy to distinguish the hydrocarbon and water-
bearing zones, since clay minerals are present. Clay minerals, as well as other minerals contained in shale, 
like pyrite, conduct electricity and have been identified as a main cause for resistivity decrease, even in 
small amounts. The resistivity decrease is mostly attributed to the existence of bound-water contained 
within the clay microporosity, but also to the surface conductivity of the clays themselves. The resistivity 
reduction, due to the clay content, might cause an overestimation of the water saturation which will have a 
negative effect on the estimation of the hydrocarbons in place (HCIIP). Therefore, a precise evaluation of 
the composition of the shale and the estimated clay is necessary in every petrophysical and log 
interpretation. 
 
2.1.4 Sonic Log 
 
The Sonic or Acoustic Log is a measurement of the time that a pulse of sound (elastic wave) needs to travel 
from a transmitter to a receiver through a formation and is based on the theory of wave propagation. The 
main characteristic that permits wave propagation through a formation is its elastic nature. Various types 
of waves exist which are distinguished according to the arrival time from the transmitter to the receiver. 
For borehole logging, the most significant are the compressional (P-wave) and the shear (S-wave). 
Compressional waves are usually the fastest and have a small amplitude, while shear are slower but usually 
with a higher amplitude. Sonic values are commonly presented in microseconds per foot (µs/ft), which 
represents the interval transit time of the wave. This value can be converted into velocity and thus, be 
translated into m/s. The acoustic wave velocity is strongly affected by the lithology, the clay content, the 
pore fluid stiffness and composition, the pressure and temperature of the formation.

The Sonic Log can be used quantitatively in order to estimate porosity, through Wyllie’s relationship 
(Wyllie et al., 1956), results, however, are usually imprecise and correction coefficients are often used when

2.2 Mineral Composition 
 
Shale is a fine-grained sedimentary rock that is mainly composed of clay-size mineral grains. The basic 
mechanism that results in its formation is the compaction of these silt and clay-size particles, typically in 
marine environments. It is considered as the most abundant sedimentary rock, representing around ¾ of the 
stratigraphic column of the Earth’s crust. Clay minerals such as illite, smectite and kaolinite, account for 
about 50-60 wt% of a common shale. Other tiny grains that are usually present in shales are silicates like 
quartz and feldspar, carbonates like calcilte as well as pyrite and organic matter. The variety and conciseness 
of minerals are often defined by the shale’s environment of deposition.  
Identifying the mineralogical composition is of great significance for any reservoir and especially for gas 
shales that are particularly complicated, yet of great importance for the increasing global demands for 
energy resources. The mineral identification contributes to the quantification of each mineral’s volume, 
which is crucial for defining the brittleness/stiffness of the rock, allowing to predict the reaction of the 
formation when drilled, fractured and during production initiation. Moreover, it is necessary to make more 
efficient and accurate evaluations of porosity and gas saturation. 
A well-known technique for mineralogical analysis is the X-Ray Powder Diffraction (XRD). XRD analysis 
on core or cutting samples is a rapid, simple and accurate method of analyzing fine grained crystalline 
structures (Jadoon et al., 2016). It is particularly useful when applying in tiny sized mixtures as it can 
identify the fine grained minerals that other techniques fail to analyze. 
 
2.3 Formation Pressure and Temperature  
 
2.3.1 Formation Pressure 
 
The term formation pressure is referred to the pressure of the fluid that is located inside the pore spaces of 
a rock. In the Oil and Gas industry it is considered to be a critical parameter, especially during the drilling 
operations and the hydrocarbon production. It is a crucial factor as it can affect the reservoir properties, the 
fluid phase behavior and overall, the condition of the well and the quality of production. Formation pressure 
==PAGE:23==
Petrophysical Interpretation of the Lark Formation  
Technical University of Denmark 
23 | P a g e  
 
is usually affected and defined by the overburden which is the weight of the overlying rocks above the 
formation. 
The pressure that is equal to the hydrostatic pressure of the fluid that extends from the surface to the 
subsurface formation is characterized as normal and is only dependent on the density of the fluid inside the 
pores and the corresponding depth. However, there are pressures that can be found to lie above or below 
the normal pressure.  
A condition in which the pore pressure of a formation exceeds the expected hydrostatic pressure is referred 
to as abnormal pressure or overpressure. It is a common state, when compaction takes place rapidly, 
especially in impermeable formations like shale, and the fluids contained in the pores cannot escape. The 
pore fluids are then forced to support the overlying rocks, leading to a high pressure. Although high pressure 
zones are considered potential hydrocarbon reservoirs, they can affect the balance of the borehole, causing 
blowouts and hydrocarbon release.  
On the other hand, a condition in which the pore pressure of the formation is less than the hydrostatic, is 
referred to as subnormal pressure or underpressure. Underpressure zones are expected to be found in areas 
where hydrocarbon production took place. 
 
2.3.2 Formation Temperature 
 
Reservoir temperature can be proved useful as it is a good indicator about other formation and wellbore 
conditions like pressure and flow rate (Duru et al., 2010). A precise knowledge of borehole temperatures is 
also significant for detecting fluid movement and plays an important role for a successful log evaluation. 
 
2.4 Grain Density  
 
Grain density is defined as the density of a rock or mineral with zero porosity in a formation or core sample. 
It describes exclusively the density of the solid material and is commonly measured in g/cm3. It is 
considered a significant parameter inside a reservoir, since it affects many variables and particularly, 
porosity. In homogeneous formations, grain density is usually known and relatively stable within a depth 
interval. However, in complicated formations with mixtures of different minerals, the evaluation of grain 
density might prove challenging. The measurement techniques that can be used vary. Typically, grain 
density can be measured experimentally, through saturation and Boyle’s law, indirectly from logging data 
and mathematically from identifying the mineralogical composition.

2.5 Water Resistivity in the Undisturbed Zone, Rw – Pickett Plot 
 
Archie’s formula is well known for its wide application on the estimation of water saturation from resistivity 
measurements for a particular formation or type of rock. In order to accurately interpret a Resistivity Log, 
it is essential to know two basic parameters: the porosity, φ and the resistivity of the water in the 
undisturbed zone, Rw. A practical method for establishing the Rw is the Pickett Plot. Pickett Plots have 
been recognized as a useful tool in the petrophysical interpretation of reservoirs (Aguilera, 2001). Although 
they provide accurate and reliable results, they are primarily used for conventional reservoirs and not so 
often for shale oil and shale gas reservoirs (Yu and Aguilera, 2011). They are mainly used for the estimation 
of particular parameters such as the water saturation and resistivity of the formation water. The common 
method is to create a log-log plot that includes true resistivity of the formation in the x-axis and porosity in 
the y-axis. Sw and Rw can be calculated directly from the plot for given petrophysical parameters a, m and 
n. The basic Equation that is followed through this procedure is the Archie’s Equation (El-Khadragy et al., 
2014).  
 
2.6 Water Saturation 
 
Water saturation (Sw) can be defined as the ratio of water volume to the total pore volume of a formation 
and is usually expressed in percentage or fraction. It is one the most important, complex and challenging 
petrophysical calculations and its determination is necessary in order to quantify a more significant 
parameter, which is the hydrocarbon saturation (Shc). An accurate determination of water saturation will 
result in an efficient reservoir characterization, a good estimation of the hydrocarbon volume and 
potentially, an optimized production. 
Different methods are available for the calculation of water saturation, including laboratory experiments on 
cores or crushed samples, logging interpretation, petrophysical modelling and capillary pressure 
measurements (Al-Bulushi et al., 2009). Amongst them, the most common and frequently used method is 
based on petrophysics and combines porosity and resistivity values from logging data. This method was 
first proposed in 1942 by Gus Archie (Zhang et al., 2016) and since then, it is widely used for determining 
the water saturation in reservoirs all over the world. According to Archie’s formula, it is essential to know 
two basic parameters, the porosity φ and the resistivity of the water, Rw, in the undisturbed zone. Apart 
from that, another important parameter for an efficient use of the Archie Equation, is the specification of 
the petrophysical properties of the rock. The petrophysical or electrical properties are terms that are used in 
the formula in order to calibrate the water saturation according to the petrophysical profile of the formation. 
These parameters are: 
Cementation exponent m: It expresses the increase of the resistivity from the pore network, assuming that 
the rock is non-conductive. Its values range regarding the porosity type. Generally, for unconsolidated sands 
==PAGE:25==
Petrophysical Interpretation of the Lark Formation  
Technical University of Denmark

m = 1.3, for sandstones with diagenetic or detrital clay in pores m = 1.7 −1.8, for formations with clean, 
macro to micro sized pore throat m = 2 and for formations with vuggy porosity m = 2.2 −3.0. 
Saturation exponent n: It expresses the effect of the presence of non-conductive fluid in the pore space. 
Although in complex rocks it can change with water saturation, a typical value is n = 2. 
Tortuosity exponent a: It expresses the variations in compaction and pore structure and is related to the 
pathway of the fluid flow. Its values range between 0.5 and 1.5. 
 
2.7 Porosity 
 
Porosity can be defined as a measure of the void space of a rock and it represents the storage capacity of a 
reservoir that contains fluids (Figure 5). It can be expressed as the volume of the void space divided by the 
total volume and is usually indicated as a fraction (ranging between 0 and 1) or as a percentage (ranging 
between 0% and 100%). The pore space can either contain water, oil or gas, or a combination of the three 
and it represents only a small part of the whole rock volume (Hu et al., 2017). High porosity is an indication 
of high capacity of a formation to contain water and/or hydrocarbons, while low porosity is an indication 
of low capacity, respectively.  
 
Figure 5: Schematic representation of a clay-rich, organic-rich shale formation (Passey et al., 
2010) 
Porosity is considered to be one of the most significant characteristics that define a hydrocarbon reservoir, 
therefore, a realistic estimation of porosity is fundamental and a crucial factor for the petrophysical 
interpretation of any reservoir. In a shale gas reservoir in particular, it is an important parameter as it can 
be used as a variable that affects the free and the absorbed gas (Yu et al., 2018). Moreover, it is necessary 
for the estimation of the potential volume of hydrocarbons contained in a formation and is frequently, 
qualitatively and quantitatively used for the assessment of the reservoir.

2.8 The Neutron Log Model 
 
The Neutron Log is a useful tool for lithology identification. Although quantitatively it is used to provide 
measurements of porosity, it is widely used geologically in order to identify lithology (Rider, 1986). The 
main function of the neutron tool is to estimate the hydrogen index/amount in the formation and relate it to 
porosity. 
The neutron logging tool consists of a chemical source that produces and emits high-energy neutrons. The 
neutrons collide with the formation having a specific energy and velocity. Most of this energy is lost after 
the collision with the hydrogen atoms present in the formation and the neutrons will gradually slow down. 
The neutrons have already lost part of their energy, microseconds after being released, and have entered the 
thermal state. In that state they are captured by other atoms, which start emitting gamma rays (Lyons et al., 
2011). The above procedure can be evaluated by detecting and capturing the emitted gamma rays or by 
measuring the slowing down of neutrons. 
As indicated above, the neutron log measures the hydrogen index. Sedimentary rocks like sandstones and 
limestones do not contain hydrogen in their mineralogical compositions and the only hydrogen present is 
in the formation water and the hydrocarbons (if present). Therefore, the neutron log responds to the volume 
of water that fills the pores and represents the actual porosity of the formation (Liu, 2017). On the contrary, 
shale formations contain hydrogen in their composition, which results in overestimated hydrogen index and 
thus, overestimated porosity. Moreover, considering that the log is sensitive to all forms of hydrogen, it 
detects both free and bound water. Bound water can be found within the clays in shaly formations and 
disrupts the neutron porosity values. Consequently, the neutron log cannot be used to estimate the porosity 
in shales without corrections. 
 
2.9 Wave Propagation and Elastic Parameters-Stiffness Evaluation 
 
The stiffness properties of soils and rocks have been widely investigated and tested by the geotechnical 
field using a variety of techniques. Acoustic logging is extensively used in order to understand the 
mechanical properties of the rocks and in many cases, to predict the formation behavior under a variety of 
conditions. From both a safety and an economical aspect, it is essential for the oil industry to foresee and 
evaluate the reaction of the formation during the drilling operations and the hydrocarbon production 
process. Amongst the major concerns, are the possibility of the formation collapsing during the production 
stage, the likelihood of the borehole collapsing during drilling and the formation behavior under applying 
excess pressure during hydraulic fracturing (Ellis et al., 2007). 
Rock physics is necessary for understanding the properties of a formation by interpreting already known 
rock characteristics and combining them with logging and seismic data. The aim of a rock model is to 
combine the knowledge of the elastic features of the constituents (solid, fluid) of the rock, in order to obtain 
the elastic properties of a fluid saturated formation (Josh et al., 2012). This process is required for estimating 
==PAGE:27==
Petrophysical Interpretation of the Lark Formation  
Technical University of Denmark 
27 | P a g e  
 
the brittleness/stiffness of the rock, approximating the reaction of the formation when fractured, planning 
the hydraulic fracturing and finally, evaluating the producibility of the hydrocarbons within it. 
An efficient method of modelling the elastic stiffness of a shale formation is through the Voigt, Reuss and 
Hashin-Shtrikman bounds. The saturated shale formation is considered as a binary mixture and the bounds 
are estimated with respect to the soft and the stiff components.  
In general, in order to predict the elastic moduli of a mixture of grains and pores, three basic parameters 
need to be specified: the elastic moduli of each of the components, their volume fractions and their 
geometrical arrangement. However, in many cases, only the moduli and the volume fractions are known 
and therefore, the upper and lower bounds can be approximated by using the Reuss and Voigt formulas. 
The Reuss mixing rule typically underestimates the mixture stiffness and suggests a loose lower bound for 
the effective moduli, whereas the Voigt mixing rule overestimates the mixture stiffness and suggests a loose 
upper bound for the effective moduli (Swan et al., 1997). At any given mixture, the elastic moduli will be 
located somewhere between the bounds. An intermediate bound is often calculated along with the upper 
and lower bounds and is referred to as the Voigt-Reuss-Hill (VRH) average. The Hashin-Shtrikman bounds 
are specified in the same way, providing the narrowest possible range, however, they are efficient when the 
mixture constitutes of only two components.

Samples were tested aiming at identifying the mineralogy of the shale interval. In total, 25 samples from

Diffraction (XRD). XRD is a technique mostly used in to identify the phases of an unknown crystalline

are diffracted and Bragg’s Law is applied in order to calculate the d-spacings. The set of d-spacings 
generates a unique signal of the minerals present in the sample, which when properly interpreted, allows 
the identification of the minerals. XRD experiments were not conducted in the specific study. The entire

CHAPTER FIVE – DISCUSSION 
 
5.1 Well Logging 
 
From cross-plotting and assessing the given logging data, no unreliable or inconsistent values were 
observed. Some slightly abnormally high Uranium and bulk density values were isolated but, overall, data 
appeared consistent and no corrections were considered necessary.  
Gamma Ray, Density-Neutron porosity, Caliper, Spectral GR, Sonic and Resistivity depth plots of the 
whole interval (Figure 12) were used for the recognition of the layers. The available logging data indicated 
three areas with distinguished characteristics that could be considered as different layers: 
 1270-1711 m: Shale layer 
 1712-1985 m: Shale layer with different properties 
 1986-2500 m: Chalk layer 
 
5.2 Mineral Composition 
 
XRD analysis showed that, amongst the clays, Smectite was the most abundant with values ranging between 
16-42 wt% of the entire rock sample. Illite and Kaolinite ranged between 6-25 wt% and 2-10 wt% 
respectively. Smectite is considered to cause osmotic swelling which results to permeability reduction and 
therefore, it can affect negatively the reservoir (Zhou, 1996). Overall, the total content of clay was moderate 
to high and varied between 30-68 wt%. Non-clay content mostly consisted of Quartz (15-48 wt%) and 
Calcite (2-40 wt%), which are identified as the most abundant minerals in a shale reservoir (Zhao, 2015). 
Although clay-abundant shale layers are considered to have a great gas storage capacity, mainly because 
the clay minerals have big surface areas, the presence of large amounts of tiny particles, causes a decreased 
reservoir quality as these particles prevent the contacts between Quartz molecules (Zhang, 2017). In 
general, reservoir capacity is strongly affected by minerals, and especially clay minerals, due to the fact 
that their grain size is relatively small and may result in reducing the effective porosity and permeability of 
a sedimentary rock (Jiang, 2012).  
The gas storage mechanism differs between a conventional hydrocarbon reservoir and a shale gas reservoir. 
The organic matter of shales and in certain cases, the present clay minerals, are known to absorb natural 
gas, resulting in a rich gas reservoir (Wilson, 2017). However, the swelling tendency of clays blocks the 
pores, causes formation damage and is recognized as one of the reasons of reservoir deterioration. Thus, 
high clay content might affect negatively the reservoir quality.

5.3 Formation Pressure and Temperature 
 
5.3.1 Formation Pressure 
 
The pressure within a reservoir is commonly expressed by the density of the drilling fluid that is necessary 
to circulate inside the wellbore in order to balance the pressure within the formation pores. At the depth of 
1266.6 m, the density of the drilling fluid changed from 8.66 ppg to 11 ppg, indicating the depth of the 
overpressure. Overpressure is described as the condition in which the pore pressure of the formation 
exceeds the expected hydrostatic and is a common state, when compaction takes place rapidly, especially 
in impermeable formations like shale. During compaction, the fluids contained in the pores cannot escape 
and are forced to support the overlying rocks, resulting into a high pressure. Although overpressure zones 
can affect negatively the balance of the borehole, causing blowouts and hydrocarbon release, they are 
indicators of potential hydrocarbon reservoirs. A normal pressure can be defined at around 9 ppg, thus the 
reservoir is characterized as a normal pressure reservoir. 
 
5.3.2 Formation Temperature 
 
Overall, the average thermal gradient was estimated at 30.8oC/km for the total depth, which is defined as a 
normal gradient for a reservoir. It should be noted that the temperature measured in the borehole does not 
represent the true formation temperature, but the temperature of the mud that circulates inside it. The drilling 
mud is cooler than the formation and can be representative of the real formation temperature only when 
equilibrium between them is achieved, which is a process that is very slow. Thus, the estimated 
temperatures for each depth could be lower than the actual formation temperatures. 
 
5.4 Grain Density 
 
The average grain density estimated from the mineral composition was 2.626 g/cm3, while an average of 
2.643 g/cm3 was obtained from Helium measurements. Overall, no specific conclusion could be derived, 
since in some depths, mineral density appeared higher than helium, while in others appeared lower. 
Although no specific trend was followed, it could be stated than in the majority of the samples, mineral 
density displayed slightly lower values than helium. Even though, Helium-porosimetry is considered as an 
accurate method, Ferjá, 2017 mentions that, during the experimental procedure, due to the small size of 
Helium atoms, they could have entered the sample’s structure, lowering the measured pressure and thus, 
resulting in higher grain densities.

5.5 Water Resistivity in the Undisturbed Zone, Rw – Pickett Plot 
 
The first plot In Figure 17, represents the entire interval of investigation (1200-2500 m), divided into the 
two layers, shale (1270-1700 m) and chalk (2000-2500 m). Although petrophysical parameters 𝑚 and 𝑛 
varied within the shale, an overall slope of −𝑚= −2 was used as an initial approximation to determine the 
water resistivity. The selected cementation exponent, 𝑚= 2, (considering that the rock type is shale) can 
be verified from the trend that the data follow both for the shale and the chalk interval. Therefore, it was 
assessed as insignificant to consider a different value for the chalk and 𝑚= 2 was kept constant. 
The resistivity of the water was estimated to be Rw = 0.03 ohm m. However, it is observed that the specific 
water resistivity does not entirely match the chalk layer, as a remarkable proportion of its water saturation 
is above 100%. Considering that the interval of interest is the shale layer, no correction was made.  
Four additional plots for the intervals with the specified petrophysical parameters 𝑚 and 𝑛 were created, in 
order to precisely indicate the water saturation. Figure 18 demonstrates the shallowest part of the shale, 
corresponding to a depth of 1264-1388 m, with petrophysical parameters 𝑚= 2.5 and 𝑛= 3. The water 
saturation for the majority of the data varied between 41-71%, allowing a 30-60% hydrocarbon saturation 
to be located inside the pore space of the shale reservoir. Figure 19 indicates the depth of 1388-1500 m 
with petrophysical parameters 𝑚= 2.15 and 𝑛= 2.15. The water saturation varied between 50-71%, 
which allows a 30-50% hydrocarbon saturation. Figure 20 presents the depth of 1500-1638 m with 
petrophysical parameters 𝑚= 1.75 and 𝑛= 1.75. The water saturation ranged between 50-71%, which 
indicates a 30-50% hydrocarbon saturation within the pores. Figure 21 demonstrates the deepest part of the 
shale, corresponding to a depth of 1638-1711 m with petrophysical parameters 𝑚= 1.45 and 𝑛= 1.45. 
The water saturation varied between 50-71%, allowing a 30-50% hydrocarbon saturation. Overall, the 
interval of interest (1200-1700 m) exhibits a water saturation of 40-90% with an average of Sw = 64%. 
This could be interpreted as a potentially gas saturated shale formation with Sg = 10 −60%.  
Although, the Pickett Plot is a good indicator for the water saturation within the formation, its main use was 
for the evaluation of the water resistivity in the undisturbed zone. Further discussion and analysis of the 
water saturation follows in Chapter 5.6. 
 
5.6 Water Saturation 
 
Archie’s formula was used in order to calculate the water saturation with respect to porosity and true 
formation resistivity for various 𝑚 and 𝑛 parameters. For the depths that core data were available, grain 
densities for each depth were used, while for an overall assessment an average grain density of ρgrain =
2.626 g/cm3 for the shale was used.

For the shale interval (1200-1700 m), that core data were available (25 depths), the average water saturation 
was 61% with the lowest value (Sw = 45%) observed at 1640 m and the highest (Sw = 77%) at 1300 m 
(Figure 24). 
For the same interval, but for an average grain density of ρgrain = 2.626 g/cm3 (Figure 25), the average 
water saturation was estimated to be Sw = 64%. Overall a reduction was observed as depth increased, with 
the lowest values being noticed between 1500-1700 m. This could be interpreted as a potentially gas 
saturated shale formation with Sg = 30 −60%. For the entire shale interval, (1270-1711 m) the expected 
gas saturation remains high, between 10 −60%.

was made between the minimum (ρgrain = 2.485 g/cm3) and maximum (ρgrain = 2.856 g/cm3) grain 
densities for the shale layer (Figure 26). Results for the chalk layer remain the same, since the grain density 
was kept constant at ρgrain = 2.7 g/cm3 for the specific layer. The average water saturation for the area of 
interest (1200-1700 m) considering a minimum grain density of ρgrain = 2.485 g/cm3 is Sw = 74%. For 
a maximum grain density of ρgrain = 2.856 g/cm3 the average water saturation corresponds to Sw = 54%. 
Lower grain densities result in higher water saturation and therefore, decreased gas saturation. On the 
contrary, higher grain densities affect positively the reservoir, reducing the water saturation and thus, 
enhancing the presence of hydrocarbons. It could be stated that an optimistic assumption of an average 
grain density of ρgrain = 2.856 g/cm3 would result in particularly high gas saturations with an average of 
46% for the entire shale interval, while a maximum of 50-60% is observed at depths between 1500 and 
1700 m. Overall, the interval 1500-1700 m was proved to be particularly interesting as it indicated the 
highest gas saturations that ranged between 20-60% for varying grain densities (2.485-2.856 g/cm3).  
Consequently, grain density is an important parameter that directly affects the water and hence, the gas 
saturation and should be carefully evaluated. 
The amount of Gas in Place (GIP) in a reservoir indicates the sum of free, solution and sorbed gas. Free gas 
is defined as the gaseous phase present in a reservoir (typically forms a gas cap), solution gas is the dissolved 
gas in a mixture of reservoir fluids and sorbed gas, is the gas attached on the surface of a solid material 
such as grains (Sang et al., 2016). The estimated gas saturation (10-60%) designates the amount of free gas, 
as a separate gaseous phase was taken into consideration when calculating the density of the fluid. 
Consequently, more gas could be present in the form of solution or sorbed gas. 
Archie’s formula is an efficient method to estimate the water saturation in the pore space of a rock and is 
widely used in the oil and gas industry. However, it was developed to produce results when used in clean 
sandstone formations and is not performing the same way when shale is present (Zhang et al., 2016). The 
main restriction is that it is based on the assumption that the only conductive material in the formation is 
brine. On the contrary, in a shaly formation, clay-bound water produces additional conductivity to the 
formation water (Al-Bulushi et al., 2009)

In general, shales are not always characterized as conventional reservoirs and can create complex problems 
during their petrophysical and geophysical analysis. An important factor that should be taken into 
consideration when investigating a shale reservoir, is the high clay content. Clay content was discovered at 
an average of 50% of the entire rock mineralogy at the specific shale formation. Clay minerals are 
responsible for increasing the conductivity of the formation, especially at low water saturations, due to the 
fact that they attract and absorb more water, which is collectively referred to as “clay-bound water” (Passey 
et al., 2010). This gives rise to an excess conductivity that might hide the high resistivity of hydrocarbons. 
Since the Archie Eq. assumes that the only conductive material is the formation water, the presence of 
another conductive material (shale) might lead to potentially hydrocarbon bearing zones being missed (Alao 
et al., 2013). Overall, water saturations obtained from Archie’s Eq. have a tendency to overestimate the 
water in shaly formations and thus, the obtained 𝑆𝑤 values might be higher than in reality. 
 
5.7 Porosity 
 
Although shale reservoirs are commonly accompanied with porosities lower than 10% (Bust et al., 2013; 
Kuila and Prasad, 2013; Zhu et al., 2018), it was observed that the specific porosity values indicate a high 
porosity formation (Figure 29). Porosity displayed particularly high values, between 20 and 40% for the 
shale interval. Overall, a reduction was observed as depth increased, but the average porosity remained high 
at 31%. High porosity areas mean that there is a high fluid storage capacity within the pores and therefore, 
there is enough space between the solid grains, for hydrocarbons to be trapped. 
Neutron Porosity is plotted along with density porosity for the entire interval. Although it exhibits higher 
values for the shale interval, it is an outcome that it is expected since the Neutron does not indicate real 
porosity for lithologies other than clean limestones, without conversion factors. On the contrary, it 
represents real porosity for the chalk interval. Since chalk displays no deviation between neutron and 
estimated porosity, the consistency of the model could be verified. 
High clay content characterizes the specific shale reservoir. Clay-rich rocks and mudstones contain very 
small particles and pores within their matrix, which makes the determination of their porosity particularly 
challenging (Passey et al., 2010). Shale porosity varies a lot with mineralogy, but, as a general rule, clay 
rich shales, like the examined reservoir, present higher porosity than silica-rich or carbonate-rich shales. 
However, detrital quartz shales have higher porosity than biogenic quartz shales, therefore, the mineralogy 
might not be reliable or indicative for estimating the porosity (Bustin et al., 2008).  
Shales and mudrocks contain a microporosity which is related with clay minerals and which might increase 
the total porosity of the rock, yet it is a parameter that is rarely considered mainly because clays are 
considered responsible for the opposite effect on porosity. As stated before, due to their tiny grain size, clay 
minerals may block the pore space of a rock, reducing the porosity of the formation and affecting its 
hydrocarbon storage capacity.

The importance of grain density was highlighted in Figure 30. For an average minimum grain density of 
ρgrain = 2.485 g/cm3 porosity varied between 15 to 35% for the shale layer (1200-1770 m), while for an 
average maximum grain density of ρgrain = 2.856 g/cm3 porosity ranged between 25 to 45%. Higher grain 
densities result in higher porosity, which is a desirable characteristic in every reservoir, as more space 
between the pores results in potentially higher concentrations of hydrocarbons. 
 
5.8 The Neutron Log Model 
 
Neutron porosity is not representative for shaly formations as it overestimates the hydrogen content. Hence, 
the estimated modelled porosity of Table 16 does not signify the true formation porosity. The aim of 
modelling the Neutron Log was to define the petrophysical exponents 𝑚, 𝑛 with a relative validity in order 
to accurately interpret the reservoir and evaluate the water saturation. As it was proved, proper estimation 
of the parameters has a great impact on the estimation of water and hence, gas saturation, as they are directly 
correlated according to the Archie Eq. (Shedid et al., 2017). 
 
5.9 Wave Propagation and Elastic Parameters-Stiffness Evaluation 
 
The stiffness of the formation was evaluated with respect to the solid phase and the fluid phase. Regarding 
Figure 33, although, the upper bounds of the soft solid model (Model 1) appeared closer to the formation’s 
shear modulus values (𝐺𝐿𝑜𝑔, estimated from the Sonic Log, see Chapter 3.9.3), no conclusion could be 
derived for the stiffness of the solid from the above figure. As it was proved, the lower bounds were zero 
for all the models. Considering that the formation’s shear values were particularly low (≈1 𝐺𝑃𝑎), and thus 
closer to the lower bounds, it appeared that they were more realistic and should be considered as more 
accurate throughout the stiffness interpretation of the formation. 
With respect to Figures 34-42, it was noticed that the formation’s bulk modulus values (𝐾𝐿𝑜𝑔, estimated 
from the Sonic Log), were located between the upper and lower bounds in the majority of the models. 
Although, the models indicated very distinct results, some conclusions could be derived regarding the 
stiffness of the solid and the fluid. Emphasis was given at Models 3, 6 and 9, as their lower bounds were 
more similar to the formation’s bulk values. Combining the three models, it was noted that in every case, 
the fluid that was used was characterized as stiff. A stiff reservoir fluid can give an estimation about its 
wettability. Specifically, a brittle/stiff fluid indicates a rather water-wet reservoir, since the gas would be 
present in the form of bubbles within the fluid. Water-wettability is a preferable condition as it is an 
indication that the existent hydrocarbons are usually producible. 
Considering the stiffness of the solid phase, the upper bounds of Models 1, 2 and 3 appeared relatively close 
to the measured bulk modulus values (𝐾𝐿𝑜𝑔). That would indicate a soft solid. Brittleness of gas shales is 
an important parameter that needs to be well defined in order to understand whether the formation would

in micro-fractures which could increase the reservoir potential (Ding et al., 2012).

CHAPTER SIX – CONCLUSIONS 
 
Gamma Ray, Density-Neutron porosity, Caliper, Spectral GR, Sonic and Resistivity depth plots were used 
in order to evaluate the entire formation with respect to the different types of sedimentary rocks. The interval 
of 1270-2500 m was examined and divided into 3 layers: 
 1270-1711 m: Shale layer 
 1712-1985 m: Shale layer with different properties 
 1986-2500 m: Chalk layer 
The shallow shale layer (1270-1700 m) was of particular interest and was examined in more detail. 
Grain density was calculated for the 25 depths that core samples were available within the shale layer, 
according to the mineralogical composition. The density ranged between 2.485 g/cm3 and 2.856 g/cm3 with 
an average value of 2.626 g/cm3. A comparison was made with grain densities obtained experimentally 
with Helium measurements from Ferjá, 2017, which exhibited an average of 2.643 g/cm3. Overall, no 
specific conclusion could be derived, since in some depths, mineral density appeared higher than helium, 
while in others appeared lower. Taking into consideration that the results displayed mainly small differences 
and the average values were particularly close, it could be stated that the densities obtained from the 
mineralogy were relatively accurate. 
The resistivity of the water in the undisturbed zone, was estimated to be 𝑅𝑤= 0.03 ohm m according to the 
Pickett Plot.  
The cementation exponent 𝑚 and saturation exponent 𝑛 were assessed by modelling the Neutron Log. The 
Neutron Porosity was modelled with respect to the Slowing Power of the mixture (solid-water-
hydrocarbons) for each depth that core samples were available. The results of the modelled porosity were 
plotted along with the Neutron Porosity, obtained from the logging data. The petrophysical parameters 𝑚 
and 𝑛 were specifically defined for each depth so that the modelled porosity would match the Log. The 
entire shale interval was then divided into 4 areas with distinct 𝑚 and 𝑛 parameters: 
 1264-1388 m: 𝑚 = 2.5, 𝑛 = 3 
 1389-1500 m: 𝑚 = 2.15, 𝑛 = 2.15 
 1501-1638 m: 𝑚 = 1.75, 𝑛 = 1.75 
 1639-1711 m: 𝑚 = 1.45, 𝑛 = 1.45 
A precise evaluation of the parameters was proved critical, since it had a great impact on the estimation of 
the water, hence, the gas saturation and the porosity. 
Archie’s formula was used in order to define the water saturation for the shale interval (1200-1700 m), with 
respect to porosity and true formation resistivity for the various 𝑚 and 𝑛 parameters. For an average grain

observed as depth increased, but the average porosity remained high at an average of 31%, indicating a high 
porosity reservoir.

of their effective elastic moduli (bulk and shear). 9 models were suggested regarding the elastic bulk 
modulus (K) and 3 regarding the elastic shear modulus (G). A combination of the models indicated a stiff 
fluid, which would designate a water-wet reservoir. Water-wettability signifies that the existent gas is 
producible. The solid was characterized as soft, which could probably affect negatively the brittleness of 
the formation, since brittle rocks allow the development and maintenance of hydraulic fractures and are 
commonly preferred.

FUTURE INVESTIGATION 
 
Throughout this project, the gas saturation within the formation’s pore space, was examined. During the 
evaluation of gas saturation, the gaseous phase was considered as a separate phase, when estimating the 
density of the fluid, which in reality, resulted into calculating the free gas, present in the reservoir. Further 
analysis could be made in order to assess whether adsorbed gas could be detected. Sorbed gas differs from 
free and is considered an important aspect within the shale gas, with particular interest. The adsorption 
capacity depends highly on the specific surface area of the minerals and primarily, of clays. Taking into 
consideration that a high clay content exists within the shale, the sorbed gas assessment would result in a 
better understanding of the hydrocarbon saturation within the specific reservoir and would contribute into 
a thorough overview of the existent gas.
