Summary
This thesis report examines neural networks and its application in financial prediction
for stocks listed on the Oslo Stock exchange. The goal of the project was to explore
the efficiency of recurrent neural networks applied on technical and fundamental data.
Besides, a recurrent neural network was implemented in a reinforcement learning
system to utilize autonomous stock trading.
The findings suggest that fundamental data is superior to technical data when
using recurrent neural networks for financial predictions. The reason is that a recur-
rent neural network using fundamental data appears to learn better. In the case of
using technical data, a recurrent neural network does not achieve good results in this
report.

Terms
• Stock: Represents a particular part of a corporation’s assets and earnings where
an investor can buy parts of the company by acquiring shares.
• Stock exchange: A marketplace where the general public can buy shares of
companies.
• Stock index: A collection of stocks which total value is reflected in the index
price.
• Indicator: A relative number which is calculated by multiple financial num-
bers.
• Short: The investment strategy where an investor bets that the value of a stock
will decrease.
• Long: The investment strategy where an investor bets that the value of a stock
will rise in value.
• Hold: The investment strategy where an investor does not buy or sell any
shares in the stock market.
• Machine learning: A branch of artificial intelligence where learning algo-
rithms are used to learn from past experience. The objective of these learning
algorithms is to learn patterns from data and be able to estimate the outcome
of previously unseen data accurately.
• Dataset: Data that a learning algorithm observes and learns from. A dataset
is divided into input data, X, and target data, y.
• Supervised learning: A machine learning task that a learning algorithm tries
to solve. The goal is for the learning algorithm to estimate y, given X as input
properly.
The contrary is unsupervised learning, where the algorithms only
observe from X, without measuring its performance against y.
• Reinforcement learning: A set of components where an agent interacts with
an environment. The agent is rewarded/penalized with rewards upon perform-
ing actions in the environment.

• Artifical Neural Network: A class of learning algorithms that attempts to
solve supervised learning tasks where the target data is non-linearly separated.
• Recurrent Neural Network: In recurrent neural networks, the data is strictly
ordered by time. This allows these types of neural networks to potentially derive
time-dependent information from the input dataset.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
There is no secret that the rapid development of new technologies has enabled inno-
vations in ways that were unimaginable only a few decades ago. In fact, computers
today can provide drastically higher computing power to a much lower cost than for
just a few years ago. An uttermost example is that the computing power in a recently
released iPhone would have cost 1 billion dollars in 1970 [10].
The financial sector is one that has taken advantage of computing power to lower
prices. One example is that financial institutions and banks today can provide histor-
ical and current financial data instantly to their customers. As these data are more
accessible today than ever before, it is now possible to take advantage of the data for
machine learning purposes. One such example is to use the financial data to predict
whether the development of a stock price in the future.

2.1
About Stocks and the Stock Market
The first stock, The East Indian Company, issued its first shares to the public in 1602
[1]. The reason for the listing was merely for the reason that no single person was
willing to put all of his money into boats owned by the company. Therefore, a group
of investors came together as co-owners of the ships.
As investors saw that similar co-ownerships could be beneficial for other compa-
nies, shares for other companies also became available in the coffee shops in England.
Furthermore, many more companies issued shares that could be traded between in-
vestors. As a result of this, the need for a stock exchange was necessary for investors
to be able to trade stocks more efficiently. The first stock exchange that worked in
a similar way as the stock exchanges we know today was created in New York in 1602.
An important point to notice within finance is that the terms stocks and stock
market are used interchangeably by investors and the media. One crucial point here
is that these words are used in different settings. A stock represents a particular part
of a corporation’s assets and earnings where an investor can buy parts of the company
by acquiring shares. Typically, an investor that purchase shares in a company be-
lieves that the company will do well in the future, and hence that the company shares
will increase in value. The stock market, on the other hand, is a collection of stocks.
When people refer to the stock market, they typically refer to a number of the largest
companies in a country. In Denmark, the stock market index, OMX Copenhagen 20,
applies to the twenty largest stocks in Denmark. I.e., if OMX Copenhagen 20 goes
up on a specific day, the average stock price of the 20 largest companies in Denmark
increase in value.
As some information now has been provided, it makes sense to cover the two ways
investors typically approach a stock trade. That is, investors either bet that a stock

Buying a stock is known as long, in which an investor believes that the stock will

3.2
Artificial Neural Networks: Key Topics
Artificial neural networks is a class of machine learning algorithms which intends to
solve supervised learning tasks where the target data is non-linearly separated. The
objective of neural networks is to propagate input data (observations in X) through
the network and produce outputs (estimations as ˆy) that ideally are as close as pos-
sible to the true target data, y.
Neural networks are trained over the course of
epochs, which describes how many times all observations in X has been propagated
through the network. Each epoch entails of a specific amount of forward pass and
back-propagation phases, according to a chosen batch size (amount of observations in
X observed by the network simultaneously). Forward pass propagates the observa-
tion batches as signals through the network, and stores the state of each intermediate
neuron (network node). Eventually, the forward pass phase reaches the output layer,
which produces estimations, ˆy. Back-propagation uses the states of each neuron, and
intends to optimize their individual contribution to the deviance between ˆy and y [19].

3.2.1
Organization and Components
3.2.1.1
Layered Architecture
Neural networks are comprised of three layers; input, hidden and output. On a high
level, they are described as follows:
• The input layer simply passes observations from X as signals to the first hidden
layer.
• The hidden layer capture non-linear patterns, that in many cases are unfeasible
for humans to derive. A neural network can consist of multiple layers which
facilitates for more patterns to be recognized.
• The output layer produces the estimations, ˆy, to be compared against the true
target data, y.
3.2.1.2
Data Format
As indicated in the start of this section, a neural network takes input data, X, pro-
duces estimations, ˆy, and compares them to the true target data, y. Consequently,
the neural network must be designed according to the shape of X and y, which is as
follows:
• X has a shape of [rowsX, columnsX]. rowsX describes how many observations
that the input dataset contains, while columnsX describes how many features
each observation has.
• Similarly, y has a shape of [rowsy, columnsy]. rowsy must be equal to rowsX, i.e.
every target must have a corresponding input. Consequently, the same follows
that the shape of ˆy must be the same as the shape of y.
3.2.1.3
Neurons, Connections and Flow
Each layer contains a finite amount of neurons, which are responsible for forwarding
signals to the next layer. While the amount of neurons can be flexible in the hidden
layers, the amount in the input and output layers are determined by X and y, respec-
tively. The hidden and output neurons are connected to neurons in their preceding
layers with the help of synapses. Each synapse is linked to a weight, and signals
travelling over a synapse are multiplied with the associated weight. Intuitively, each
result represents a signal’s strength, i.e. how much it will account for in the final
output estimations.

3.2.2
Training the Network
Training neural networks are done over the course of two phases in a single itera-
tion. First, the forward pass phase (subsubsection 3.2.2.2) is executed, in which the
intermediate signal results at each neuron is calculated. All these results are stored,
so that the computer can retrieve them for when the back-propagation phase (sub-
subsection 3.2.2.3) is executed afterwards. The back-propagation phase updates the
network’s weights, which is where the actual learning takes place.
3.2.2.1
Batch Size

amount of iterations are executed per epoch. The amount of iterations are determined
by the batch size chosen for the network. Batch size states how many rows of input
data to be fed into the algorithm. Consequently, the propagation of data throughout
the network is implemented using matrices. The examples provided in the forward
pass and back-propagation phases assumes a single row of input, for the sake of
simplicity.

3.3
Recurrent Neural Networks
Recurrent neural networks (RNN) deal with problems related to sequence information,
i.e. strictly ordered data. This could be time series data, sentences, audio, and car
trajectories. As opposed to feedforward neural networks (FFNN) that have no notion
of time, RNNs attempts to overcome this limitation.
3.3.1
Neurons
As can be related from the preceding section, a basic neuron computes its output
signal by applying the transfer function on the weighted output signals from the neu-
rons in the preceding layer.
In contrast to the basic neuron, recurrent neurons considers data to be ordered
as sequences. A recurrent neuron computes hidden states (an output associated to a
time step in the sequences). The hidden state is distributed both as an output of the
recurrent neuron at time step t −1, as well as an input to the same neuron at time
step t:3:

3.3.3
Back-propagation Through Time
When a layer is comprised of recurrent units, back-propagation through time (BPTT)
is used. The sole difference from normal back-propagation is that BPTT calculates
the gradient for the input-to-hidden weights by accumulating the error contributions
at each time step. This is simply because the input-to-hidden weights in the recurrent
unit are shared across all time steps [4].
3.3.4
Long-short Term Memory Unit
A common problem in recurrent neural networks is that the gradient for input-to-
hidden weights can vanish or explode.
If a weight w has a value that deviates from 1, then the gradient tends to exponen-
tially increase (if w > 1) or exponentially decrease (if 0 < w < 1). This is especially
a risk when the amount of time steps is large. Consequently, as the time steps are
increased, the harder it will be for a recurrent neuron to capture long-term dependen-
cies.
One way to deal with exploding and vanishing gradients in recurrent networks is
to use more complex neurons in the network. One of these neurons, known as the
Long-short Term Memory unit, can account for this.

3.4 Reinforcement Learning

3.4
Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is an area of machine learning where an agent obtains
rewards from an environment by performing actions. RL deviates from supervised
learning, in the sense that the agent is not explicitly presented with labels in a su-
pervised dataset upon performing actions. Instead, the agent attempts to collectively
maximize rewards obtained from an environment.
3.4.1
Components and Interactions
3.4.1.1
Agent
The agent is the program / bot that can receive outputs (states/observations and
rewards) from the environment, and use these as inputs to determine which action
to perform on the environment. In order for the agent to do intelligent decisions, it
usually adopts an algorithm of some sort that can facilitate for this.
3.4.1.2
Environment
The environment is in essence the simulation of the problem domain, which the agent
interacts with and attempts to optimize its performance.
The simulation can be
of a computer or board game, balancing a stick, or, in case of this report, of a
stock exchange. The environment can in many cases be restricted in contrast to its
corresponding real problem domain, in the sense that it can be otherwise hard to
accurately mimic a real environment.
3.4.1.3
State
The environment transitions between states. Usually, the new state s′ depends on
the action performed in the previous state, s.
The term observation is also used
interchangeably.
3.4.1.4
Action
The environment accepts a particular set of actions that the agent can perform. The
actions can be of either a continuous or discrete space, e.g. buy/sell a particular
amount of shares, or just perform a long/short/hold operation to reflect the status of
the shares. Performing an action a in state s, returns the immediate reward R(s, a),
and transitions the environment into the new state s →s′.
3.4.1.5
Reward
The reward measures how well the agent’s chosen action performed. A negative value
indicates bad performance, while positive indicates good performance.

Initially, the agent has not obtained any state from the environment, i.e. it has no
knowledge of the environment. Consequently, the agent starts by obtaining a state
from the environment. The state is (along with a fixed amount of preceding states) im-
mediately realized as input to the network, in which the forward pass phase is started.
As the state is transformed into signals, and the signals are being propagated
through the network, they eventually reach the output layer. The output layer uses
softmax as activation function, according to the discrete action space of n actions
that the environment supports. Since softmax produces a probability distribution on
the actions, the agent chooses the action with the highest probability. This concludes
the forward pass phase.
Before adjusting the weights, the agent must obtain the reward corresponding to
the selected action. Consequently, the agent interacts with the environment and per-
forms the selected action. As the reward has been extracted, the back-propagation
phase is initiated. It operates just the same way as a normal feed-forward network,
however, the objective of the gradient descent algorithm is to maximize the reward
obtained in the environment.

4.1
Financial Forecasting using Technical Data
As research related to technical data were explored, one common tendency was that
stock indices, rather than stock by stock were analyzed. One report that analyzes
stock indices with use of technical data was released by Yao, Tan, and Poh in 1998 [21].
They use data from the Kuala Lumpur Composite Index (KLCI), calculated from 86
major Malaysian stocks - 1911 rows of data from 3. January 1984 to 16. October 1991.
The report uses the following features as input to the network: previous close (Ct−1),
current close (Ct), and seven technical indicators. Ultimately, their objective is to
estimate the next close, Ct+1. After estimating the next close prices, they computed
the return between every trailing estimation and the current price (Ct+1/Ct). What
they concluded that the data is very noisy which makes forecasting very difficult [21].
One reasonable argument pointed out in Yao, Tan and Poh’s research is related
to its applicability in the real world. The reason is that when investing money in
an index, it is complicated to manage how much money should be placed into the
different companies that the index constitutes. Mostly, it is a more straightforward
process to buy or sell one single stock, than an index containing multiple stocks.
Mann, Gorse and Denise [14] released one paper that looks only at the OHLCV
features. Here, they alter the OHLCV features to achieve higher accuracy. In contrast
to the prediction of future closing prices, the mid-price feature is compressed and more
compact. The mid-price is calculated by the average of how a stock moves in a period.

Fundamental Data and Recurrent Neural Networks
Finally, only a few papers use a neural network with fundamental data to predict
future closing prices. In these reports, the features are extracted from quarterly or
annual company reports.
Vanstone, Finnie, and Tan released one report that explores the use of funda-
mental data in financial prediction in 2004. By using some fundamental features,
they concluded that a neural network in more than 70 percent of cases could predict
whether a stock will rise with more than 10 percent [17].
A different report by Milosevic uses 11 features from financial reports to predict
whether a stock will increase more or less than 10 percent. By using 1739 stocks from
different indexes, Milosevic achieved 76.5 percent accuracy using a neural network
[15]. The few research related to fundamental data and neural networks, therefore,
provide promising results.

4.3
Evaluation of Related Work
After having explored related work, a few things are worth mentioning. First, there is
more technical data available than fundamental data, and consequently, more research
has been done regarding technical analysis. Second, the research done in the field of
technical analysis tends to use index data instead of individual company stocks.
Index prediction appears to provide better results, which is evident due to smaller
volatility. However, the papers concerning technical analysis as the data source for
single stock price predictions does not reach similar performance. The reports dis-
cussed using fundamental data appears to have more promising results for machine
learning applications.
Finally, a comment on using index data in reinforcement learning applications is
briefly mentioned. The problem of using index data in the context of reinforcement
learning is that the profit is measured by the return on investment computed from
the index data. In the real world, index trades are managed by index funds, in which
a fund manager must decide which companies comprised by the index that should
be invested in.
Consequently, there is no guarantee to reach the exact return on
investment dictated by the index data.

6.1.2
Environment: Stock Exchange
In contrast to a real stock exchange, the modeled environment is a simplified stock
exchange. The implementation assumes no charges when shorting or longing stocks.
In a real situation, shorting stocks is usually expensive, and the rent fee increases
over time, in which it should be profitable for the trader whom loans off the stock
out as well. Longing stocks come at a lower fee, but it is still at a rate where a
positive accumulated return on investment could be negative if the accumulation is
small enough.
6.1.2.1
State and Reward
The environment stores all observations in an ordinary dataset (i.e., each row pertains
to an OHLCV tuple), strictly ordered by time.
The state of the environment is comprised of three components, which is the
following:
• The observation at timestep t: OHLCVt.
• The stock state as described earlier in subsubsection 6.1.1.1.
• When the action buy long (1) or sell short (3) is executed, the environment will

current if action hold (0) is executed.

6.1.3
Agent: Trader
The trader is the central component of the system, that glues everything together. It
retrieves the algorithm’s belief and converts it into an action according to the belief
and stock state. It retrieves the states (observation state, stock state and hold state)
and rewards from the stock exchange by performing the actions shown in table 6.4.
The trader collects each observation state in sequences. That is if there are 2 time
steps per sequence, and the environment’s (observation) state is OHLCV1 (t = 1),
the trader has the following sequence:
seq1 = [OHLCV0, OHLCV1]
seq1, the last (and only) sequence, is in this case used as input to the algorithm
to obtain the belief of which direction the next closing price will be (t = 2), and map
it into the appropriate action. By executing the action, the next state is obtained,
and a new sequence is realized:
seq1 = [OHLCV0, OHLCV1]
seq2 = [OHLCV1, OHLCV2]
6.1.4
Algorithm: Recurrent Policy Gradient
The algorithm adopts a recurrent policy gradient implementation, briefly covered in
subsubsection 3.4.2.2. It samples the next belief, given the latest sequence that the
trader holds, performs actions, and the trader calculates the discounted reward for
the latest sequence. When the training period ends, the gradients are calculated for
all sequences with their respective discounted rewards, and updated accordingly.
The train and test period of the dataset in the environment is divided by 90%/10%.
This implies that during the test period, the weights will not be updated, and the
algorithm will sample its belief entirely based on the weights of the network updated
from the training period.

8.1
Technical Analysis
As discovered in this report, technical analysis does not appear promising for financial
prediction. This statement is related to both the findings in this report as well as
similar research.
Firstly, this report finds that technical analysis does not get an accuracy of much
higher than average. This is however similar to other reports, where the accuracy
ranges from around 50 percent to a maximum of 70 percent. While 70 percent may
seem promising, it should be noted that this is the extreme case. Because of this,
it seems that buying a stock, even with an accuracy of close to 70 percent does not
make much sense in the real world. The reason for this is that costs associated with
trading may be up to 20 percent.
The second point is that there may not be a logical reason why technical analysis
should work with stock price prediction. Even though some may believe that this is
an exaggeration, the research does not provide evident proof that technical analysis
works over time. If this is the case, however, past stock information does not imply
future stock prices.
In this report, historical financial data used is going as far as 20 years back in
time. One point here is that the data basis in this report used data from both the
financial crisis in 2000 and 2007/2008. This is different from other reports, where
financial data from only a few years back in time are used. As the data in this report
include data from significant market movements, the network may be had a hard time
learning based on previous data.
Finally, it should be noted that more company data could be included in the
analysis. However, as the networks implemented does not show promising results,

8.2
Fundamental Analysis
While researching the use of fundamental analysis and stock price prediction, two
interesting findings were discovered. The first discovery shows that analyzing one
company at a time does not provide excellent accuracy. This as the network tends
to classify all cases in the validation and test set to either positive price direction (1)
or negative price direction (0). As data for more companies were used for training,
the network does, however, seem to catch up on some trends. This is seen as the
validation accuracy tends to provide an accuracy on higher than average.
A second observation was that the validation set does not learn much over the
epochs. Even though this is the case, it seems that the accuracy of the oil drilling
sector does learn during the final epochs. The limited learning for the validation set
is further observed for the test set. Here, the test set is not able to predict future
stock prices on more than average.
The reason why fundamental data appear more promising is however based on
one clear finding. While training on one company at a time, the network classifies all
cases to one class. However, as more data is used for training, the network can predict
in both directions. Therefore, it seems that by adding more data, the accuracy for
using fundamental data with a neural network could provide better results.
Finally, it should be noted that external factors may not be analyzed by the
network. Such factors may include that the oil price mostly affects the development
of an oil drilling company. A thought here, however, is that the development of the
oil price, in general, should be reflected in the earnings for an oil drilling company.
The use of external factors was outside of the scope of the research conducted in this
report.
8.3
Future Work
As the research finds fundamental analysis as the most promising, the suggestion is
that future work will be related to this area. While this report used limited data, the
suggestion is that future research boost the amount of historical data. The belief is
that a higher amount of data will enhance the accuracy of stock price prediction.
Also, external happenings that are not directly related to a company could also
be used as inputs to the networks and potentially provide better results. For instance,
for the companies in the oil drilling sector, the oil price development could be added
as an additional input feature of the neural networks. This type development does
most likely have a relation to the stock price.

is not able to predict future stock prices on much higher than average.
• To train on one or many companies does not make a difference for technical
data.
• Even though reinforcement learning provides some promising results using tech-
nical data, it is not clear whether such either of the three proposed investment
strategies would be profitable in real-world applications.
Fundamental Analysis and RNN conclusion:
• Fundamental data appear more promising for stock price prediction than tech-
nical analysis using a recurrent neural network.
• To use historical data for one company at a time does not provide accurate re-
sults as all cases for a company is generally classified to either positive directions
(1) or negative directions (0).
• To use all data for companies within a sector to predict future returns for one
company provides validation accuracy of respectively 65 percent for the heavy
asset sector and 70 percent for the oil sector.
Therefore it seems that the
accuracy of fundamental data is higher than for technical data.
• The accuracy of the test set using all data is lower than 50 percent for both
the oil drilling industry and heavy asset industry. This is a result of that the
network does not learn much over the epochs. This may be caused that the test
set includes unique cases which are not represented in the historical data.

Study papers regarding existing research on the matter, as well as theory and

• Data Processing
The data processing phase focuses on obtaining and preparing financial data to
fit machine learning problems.
– Technical data gathering (Feb 07-Feb-15)
Technical data for a given company and interval (day, week or month) is
usually described as an OHLCV tuple (open, high, low, close, volume).
That is the opening price, the highest and lowest price in the given period,
the closing price, and the frequency of purchases and sales in the inter-
val. The data is available publicly (for instance Yahoo Finance or Google
Finance), and is estimated to take at most one week.
– Technical data preprocessing (Feb 15-Feb 22)
Processing technical data into supervised sequence learning problems (for
neural networks), and into reinforcement learning problems.
– Fundamental data gathering (Feb 22-Mar 31)
Fundamental data is data about the companies that could have a certain
correlation to stock prices. The data is not publicly available, in which
some manual labor must be performed. Consequently, some extra time
estimation has been allocated for this task.
– Fundamental data preprocessing (Mar 31-Apr 6)
Processing the fundamental data to fit the preprocessed technical data.
