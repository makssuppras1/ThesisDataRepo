Abstract
Plastic recycling remains a global challenge, particularly for polymers
like polyurethane (PUR). Enzymatic degradation is a promising alter-
native, but characterized PUR-degrading enzymes are limited.
Four
enzymes — Pam85, PbrevLIP, PbrevAM1, and PbrevEST — were iden-
tified from fungal secretomes capable of clearing Impranil DAH. These
enzymes were expressed in Pichia pastoris and screened against five
PUR-like substrates.
Pam85 showed the greatest potential as an industrial candidate, ex-
hibiting enzymatic activity across all the tested substrates and high-
est unfolding temperature (Tm). A deglycosylated crystal structure of
Pam85 was resolved at 2.8 ˚A, revealing an amidase signature featuring
a catalytic triad analogous to that of the recently published UMG-SP3.
However, the active site is displaced by up to 60 residues away from
the N-terminus, and a threonine residue replaces the stabilizing serine
observed in UMG-SP3. A novel glycan-mediated dimer domain swap
at glycosite N192, stabilized by a NAG–Y434 hydrogen bond and hy-
drophobic interactions, was observed and may contribute to Endo H
resistance.
Overall, Pam85 represents a promising biocatalyst for polyurethane
degradation, with further investigation and optimization required be-
fore industrial application.

Introduction
Plastics are versatile, durable and indispensable
materials used extensively in both industrial ap-
plications and everyday life. In 2017, global plas-
tic production reached approximately 350 million
tonnes[1, 2].
A rise in public interest has led to
recent plastic legislative policies such as the ’3R
policy’ (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle) whilst the Euro-
pean Union target to reuse and recycle (end-life-
stage) 100% of all plastic by 2030 [1, 3]. Current
plastic recycling methods have significant draw-
backs, as they are often complex, costly, and in-
complete[3]. An example of incomplete recycling is
observed in both primary and secondary recycling
processes, which involve converting plastics into the
same or similar products. These methods lead to
degradation of the polymer structure, resulting in
a loss of material integrity that restricts plastics
to only one or a limited number of recycling cy-
cles.
An example of costly recycling is observed
in tertiary recycling, also refereed to as chemical
recycling, which typically includes routes such as
solvolysis e.g.
hydrolysis or thermochemical e.g.
pyrolysis[4, 5]. Due to the various types of plas-
tic a ’one-size fits all’ chemical recycling method
is not viable whilst current methods might both
be harsh and costly in energy e.g hydrogenolysis
which can hydrolyse polyolefins such as Polyethy-
lene (PE) at 300 C◦and need H2 to maintain a
reductive environment [6]. The drawbacks of pri-
mary, secondary and tertiary recycling, combined
with the recent successful engineering of a cutinase
into a cost-effective industrial PETase—a polyethy-
lene terephthalate (PET)-degrading enzyme, have
further accelerated research into plastic depolymer-
ization for the production of virgin-grade plastic.
Enzymatic recycling of terephthalic acid (TPA),
one of the two monomeric building blocks within
PET, is estimated to entail a more environmen-
tally sustainable supply chain, compared to vir-
gin production of the same monomeric building
block. This environmental gain is claimed to ap-
ply broadly for polymeric systems in which enzy-
matic de-polymerization can be used[7]. These ad-
vances has enabled the development of newly engi-
neered polyurethanases (PURase) within the field
of biocatalytic plastic recycling [4, 8]. Biocatalytic
plastic recycling of polyurethane (PUR) not only
achieves reducing operational costs and avoiding
harsh and polluting chemicals, compared to chemi-
cal recycling, it also enables mixed PUR waste to be
reused as building blocks in new polymer synthesis,
due to the high specificity of enzymes. However, the
current limitation to this field is the low amount of
naturally occurring enzymes that can degrade PUR
and the subsequent lack of knowledge surrounding
such enzymes[9, 10].
In the search for an industrially applicable
PURase, two key factors must be considered: the
complex chemical structure of PUR and the cat-
alytic requirements of the enzyme, which includes
optimal temperature, pH, stability, re-usability,
and substrate specificity[10]. The complex struc-
ture of PUR that makes it difficult to recycle or
degrade comprises of various stable bonds like ure-
thane, ether, urea, and ester, along with crosslink-
ing networks.
This limits enzymatic accessibility
and requires tailored degradation strategies utiliz-
ing different enzyme classes, namely urethanases,
amidases, proteases, ureases, esterases, cutinases
and lipases, shown in Figure 1 [11].

To evaluate the potential of enzymes to hy-
drolyze PUR, a range of PUR-analogous model
substrates are normally tested.
These included
urethane-like compounds such as ethyl carbamate
derivatives (e.g., ENPC, DUE-MDA, elastane, and
Impranil DLN-SD), as well as chromogenic p-
nitrophenyl esters, such as p-nitrophenyl butyrate
(pNBP). The substrates examined in this study are

Several enzymes have previously been identified
as capable of hydrolyzing PUR-like substrates con-
taining urethane bonds, including UMG-SP1-3 and
Rhodococcus erythropolis MP50. Enzymes known
to act on ester bonds within PUR-like substrates
include PudA, CE Ubrb, and PueA-B from Pseu-
domonas protegens and from Pseudomonas chloro-
raphis.
However, the number of available crystal
structures for these enzymes remains limited [11].
To date, no fungal-derived urethanases have been
reported to possess the amidase signature catalytic
triad KSS[11, 12].
Most known urethane- and ester bond–degrading
enzymes exhibit low or no glycosylation and are not
secreted, primarily due to the use of bacterial ex-
pression systems. Glycosylation has been shown to
improve thermal stability and occasionally activity,
though the mechanisms remain unclear [13]. Pichia
Pastoris offers key advantages as a heterologous
host by enabling post-translational modifications
such as glycosylation and supporting protein se-
cretion, which facilitates proper folding and easy
purification - the latter being beneficial from a bio-
economic standpoint[14, 15].
In this study, four recombinant enzymes ex-
pressed in Pichia Pastoris — Pam85, PbrevLIP,
PbrevAM1 and PbrevEST evaluated in a stepwise
screening process to identify the candidate with
the highest potential for industrially relevant PUR-
degradation. A more thorough explanation of the
backgournd can be seen in Figure 2. The enzyme
sequences were selected based on the secretomes of
Penicillium Crustosum and Penicillium Brevicom-
pactum, followed by a sequence homology search
targeting the highest identity to the known PUR-
degrading enzyme UMG-SP3. The candidate en-
zymes were tested for hydrolytic activity against
pNBP and ENPC, as well as for their thermal un-
folding temperatures.
Among the four enzymes,
Pam85—originating from the P. crustosum secre-
tome—demonstrated the most promising activity
and thermal stability, and was therefore selected
for further characterization.
Subsequent assays were conducted to assess
Pam85’s activity on additional PUR-like sub-
strates, including DUE-MDA, and elastane. Sta-
bility studies were also performed, and structural
insights were obtained through the successful de-
termination of a 2.8 ˚A resolution crystal structure.
An overview of the experimental workflow is pro-
vided in Figure 3.

2.1
Heterologous
Transformation
Of
The AOX1 Gene Into P. pastoris
Prior to the present project, Pam85, PbrevAM1,
PbrevLIP, and PbrevEST (Table 1) were recom-
binantly expressed in P. pastoris by Rune Rahbek
Østergaard.
2.2
Growth Conditions for P. pastoris
All incubation were conducted at 30◦C, 180 rpm,
in Shaking Incubator, New Brunswich Scientific-
Innova 4000.
Proteins were grown in two batch
sizes,
125 mL for all four proteins and up-
scaled to 500 mL for Pam85.
For the 125
mL batch size; recombinant colonies were inocu-
lated in 20 mL BMGY [thermofisher˙easyselect]
media and incubated for O/N. Following O/N
growth, cells were placed into 100 mL BMMY
[thermofisher˙easyselect] media to growth for a
total of 48 hours as to induce protein expression.
After 24 hours, 1.2 mL 100% methanol was added
for a final concentration of 1% as to sustain induc-
tion of protein expression.
Up-scaling to 500mL
batch size was performed under same conditions.
2.3
Purification of Proteins
Cells were removed by centrifuge and filter. The
125 mL batch size were centrifuged for 2 min at
1000xg at 4◦C in Sigma, Centrifuge 3K10 and sub-
sequently filtered manually with handheld pipette
through Q-Max syringe filter, PES 0.22 μM result-
ing in cell-free extrat (CFE). The 500mL batch size
were centrifuged for 10 min at 10 000xg at 4◦C in
Sorvall LYNX 6000 Superspeed Centrifuge and sub-
sequently filtered by vacuum pump through Sarto-
rius 25007-47N Polyamide Membrane Filter, pore
size 0.2 μM.
A 5 mL Ni-NTA coloumn (HisTrap, Cytiva) was
equilibrated with Buffer A (Table S1) and CFE
was loaded, at a 4 mL/min flow at 4◦C using Gilson
Minipuls 3 peristaltic pump.
Previously to load,
pH of CFE was equilibrated to a pH of 7-8. Af-
ter CFE loading, column was washed with Buffer
A (Table S1) until absorbance at 280 nm reached
baseline again. Elution was achieved with Buffer B
(Table S1) in a linear gradient (10mM-300mM).
Samples of pre-filtering (0.2 μM), post-filtering (0.2
μM), Flow-through of CFE and wash (7 coloumn
volumes/35mL) as well as elution fractions were
collected and analyzed in sodium dodecyl sulfate-
polyacrylamide gel (SDS-PAGE) and subsequently
pooled and up-concentrated with Amicon® Ultra-
15 centrifugal filters at 4000xg, 4◦C (30 kDa molec-
ular weight cutoff, Utlracel®-30K; Merck-Millipore
Ltd., Cork, Ireland) down to a 1.5 mL volume. Us-
ing Storage Buffer A(Table S1) and the same Am-
icon® Ultra-15 centrifugal filters, buffers was ex-
changed. For the Pam85 500 mL batches further
purification was achieved with Hiload Superdex 200
pg 10/600 and up-concentrated again with new Am-
icon® Ultra-15 centrifugal filters to 12 mg/ml.

Protein
Concentration
Measure-
ments
Protein concentration was determined measuring

pH Optimum Assay Of Pam85
The reaction conditions were the same as for the
initial activity assay 2.5.1, however, ENPC was

Tris-HCl was used as buffer from pH 7-9, whereas

as the substrate. The same reaction conditions as
described in Section 2.5.1 were applied.

sites were investigated by identifying N-linked gly-
cosylation motifs and evaluating their surface ex-
posure and structural compatibility. Glycan struc-
tures as well as c-terminus 𝛼-helix, were analyzed
for dimer domain-swap.

Pam85 eluted with retention volumes (𝑉𝑒) between

Kinetic Stability Of Pam85
Glycosylated Pam85 showed higher kinetic stabil-
ity, exhibiting 33.5% higher activity on pNBP af-

Pam85.
Glycosylated and deglycosylated Pam85
retained 50% of their activity after 30 and 27 min-

observed in the kinetic stability assays can be at-

whereas the assays at 60 ◦C and 70 ◦C were per-

3.6.1
Pam85 Showed Similar Amidase Sig-
nature As sRO15 And UMG-SP3
Pam85 was selected from a secretome capable of
clearing Impranil DAH and shares approximately
20% sequence identity with sRO proteins — a
group of amidase signature enzymes currently un-
der investigation by the Preben Morth group. The
sROs were developed from UMG-SP1–3 proteins
by Preben Morth’s group. Of these, sRO15 shows
the highest urethanase activity on ENPC, with a
crystal structure determined (pending publication).
UMG-SP3 (PDB: 9FVF) is a recently published
urethanase with resolved crystal structures both in
the apo form and in complex with ligands such as
DUE-MDA [23].
Structural camparison of Pam85 with sRO15
and UMG-SP3 shows that Pam85 has a catalytic
triad within the Amidase Signature class (See Fig-
ure 9).
However, several key differences distin-
guish Pam85 from the other structures. In Pam85,
the catalytic triad residues are located up to 60
positions further away from the N-terminus com-
pared to UMG-SP3 and sRO15.
Pam85 uses a
threonine (T236) to stabilize the CisS217 conforma-
tion, whereas the corresponding residue in sRO15
and UMG-SP3 is a serine.
Interestingly, Pam85
also exhibits the longest distance between Ser241
and CisSer217 at 3.4 ˚A, compared to 3.0–3.1 ˚A in
sRO15 and UMG-SP3. This residue is considered
the initiating nucleophile of the catalytic triad, and
in UMG-SP3 complexed with 4,4′-MDA, this dis-
tance is further reduced to 2.6 ˚A [23]. The increased
spacing between Ser241 and the substrate in Pam85
may contribute to its comparatively lower catalytic
activity, as suggested by structural data.

3.6.2
Dimer Domain Swap Involving the 𝛼-
Helix and N192 Glycosylation Site:
Glycan-Mediated
Swapping
Stabi-
lized by Hydrogen Bonding Between
NAG and Y434 in Pam85
The mature Pam85 protein sequence was fused
downstream of the 𝛼-factor pre-pro leader for ex-
pression. Consistent with this, the crystal structure
revealed a truncation of 23 amino acids at the N-
terminus, corresponding to the cleaved signal pep-
tide region.
Pam85 forms a dimer exhibiting domain swap-
ping at the truncated N-terminal 𝛼-helix (see Fig-
ure S10), alongside a hydrogen bond between Loop
2 residue Y434 and the second NAG glycan of the
N192 glycosylation on Loop 1 (see Figure 10)
Domain swapping of secondary structural ele-
ments has been reported to confer advantages in
catalytic properties, including enhanced catalytic
rates and the alleviation of substrate inhibition;
however, these effects have been claimed to be ex-
clusively in vivo [24].
Xie et al.
(2021) demon-
strated that optimizing domain swapping between
𝛼-helices in myoglobin leads to improved protein
stability [25]. Based on these findings, it is reason-
able to hypothesize that this truncated N-terminus
𝛼-helix dimer domain swapping contributes to in-
creased stabilization.
To further validate this
hypothesis, generating knock-out mutants at two
main hydrogen binding residues L26, L114, or both
could serve as an effective proof-of-concept.
Pam85 contains six identified N-glycosylation
sites:
N208,
N192,
N301,
N321,
N365,
and
N393. Upon Endo H treatment, all glycosylation
sites—except N192 and N208 — were trimmed to
a single N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc1), consistent
with cleavage between GlcNAc1 and GlcNAc2. No
found prior studies have reported glycan-mediated
dimer domain swapping in amidases.
This phe-
nomenon may therefore represent a novel obser-
vation.
The resistance of these regions to Endo
H cleavage suggests that the glycosylation swap-
ping represent a preferred conformational state of
Pam85. It can be hypothesized that this glycosyla-
tion swapping confers protection against Endo H-
mediated cleavage at the N192 glycosylation site.
The
N192
glycosylation
site,
located
on
Loop 1 (sequence:
LSEWADMRSNNYSEGFSA)
of chain A, carries a glycan composed of a
core trisaccharide—NAG-NAG-BMA. The second
sugar, NAG, forms a hydrogen bond with Y434
on Loop 2 (sequence: DNYGSEGGLPGIHPAFGS-
GQD) of chain B. The BMA residue is further
linked to one mannose (MAN) at one site and
three mannose residues at another, constituting the
branching structure of the glycan (See Figure 10
A,B and E). To further investigate the role of the
glycan-mediated dimer domain swap, generating
knock-out mutants at the N192 glycosylation site
or the Y434 hydrogen-bonding residue, followed by
thermal stability (Tm) measurements, would pro-
vide valuable insights.
N208 is placed in a cavity of the protein, which
might be the reason for a lack of a GlcNAc1-
GlcNAc2 cleavage. This resistance to Endo H cleav-
age may contribute to the preservation of structural
stability following deglycosylation, as reflected in
the minimal thermal stability difference (Δ𝑇𝑚=
2.4 ◦C) between glycosylated and deglycosylated
Pam85 (see Figure S9).

Conclusion
Four
proteins—Pam85,
PbrevLIP,
PbrevAM1,
and
PbrevEST—isolated
from
the
Impranil
DAH-clearing
fungal
se-
cretome and exhibiting approximately 20% sequence similarity to
sROs, were expressed in P. pastoris and investigated for their unfolding
temperature and enzymatic activity against PUR-like substrates
including pNBP, ENPC, DUE-MDA, and Elastane.
Pam85 demon-
strated the greatest potential as an industrially viable candidate for
polyurethane degradation, exhibiting the highest thermal stability
(Tm) and enzymatic activity across all four tested substrates. Pam85
had different expression rates and size-variance for different growth
conditions and batch size (120 mL/500 mL). Furthermore, the pH
optimum and kinetic stability of Pam85 were assessed.
The crystal
structure of deglycosylated Pam85 were determined (resolution: 2.8
˚A, R-work:0.2315 R-free:
0.2661) and key structural features such
as active site and domain swapping was analyzed. Pam85 exhibited
amidase signature with a catalytic triad similar to those of sRO15
and UMG-SP3. Its catalytic residues were located up to 60 positions
further from the N-terminus. Additionally, the serine residue known
to stabilize the catalytic conformation in UMG-SP3 was replaced
by a threonine in Pam85.
The glycan-mediated dimer domain swap
at glycosite N192, stabilized by a NAG–Y434 hydrogen bond and
hydrophobic interactions, represents a novel structural mechanism. To
determine its impact on stability and activity, generating a knock-out
mutant at N192 or Y434 is recommended.
Similarly, the functional
role of the 𝛼-helix domain swap could be explored through knock-out
mutants targeting residues L26 and L114. Further investigation and
characterization, followed by optimization, are essential for Pam85
before it can be developed into a viable industrial PURase.
