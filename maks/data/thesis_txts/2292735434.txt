1 Abstract 
The following thesis investigates the Dynamic Patient Admission problem as described by Demeester 
et al. The problem is describe and a simplified mathematical model presented. The paper details the 
construction of an algorithm based on the tabu search method aimed at finding the best possible 
solutions for the problem. The quality of the end result is tested against already existing instances 
provided by Demeester et al. and a critical evaluation of the results follows. This paper concludes 
that the tabu search algorithm developed shows promise but highlights the issue of implementing 
non improving neighbourhood searches in the tabu search framework. For the instance set for which 
there was ample time to tune and run tests on the algorithm performs fairly well and even detects 
an improved solution over those already found by Demeester et al.

2 Introduction 
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate different ways of solving the Patient Admission Scheduling 
(PAS) problem as described by Demeester et al. The intention will be to develop a metaheuristic 
based around the tabu search algorithm and gauge its performance against already available metrics 
in an attempt at improving already existing algorithms. 
This work will begin by introducing and defining the scope and characteristics of the PAS. It will then 
run through the already available literature and present the approach taken in this case. The 
developed algorithm will be presented and the tuning process explained. The developed algorithm 
will be run against instances created by Demeester et al. and its efficiency measured. Finally, a 
discussion of potential extensions and improvements will be developed and a conclusion drawn. 
3 Problem Definition 
The PSA problem is a classic scheduling problem where the input data is a series of patients, along 
with their required treatment, their expected arrival and departure times (expected stay) and list of 
preferred room features. These patients then need to be assigned to beds in hospital rooms such 
that they receive their required treatment and as many as possible of their requirements are met. To 
better understand the format of the problem, the following key terms need to be defined. 
3.1 Key Terms 
i. 
Patient – He/she is a person in need of a particular medical treatment. A patient 
contains the following information: 
a. Name – In the format “Pat_X” where X is an integer (e.g. “Pat_1”). 
b. Age. 
c. Gender – Male or Female. 
d. Registration – Day when the system becomes aware of the patient. 
e. Admission – Day when the patient expects to be admitted to hospital. 
f. Discharge – Day when the patient expects to be discharged. Note that for this 
project, the discharge date is the first day when the patient is no longer present. 
g. Variability – The number of days past the discharge date that a patient may need to 
stay. 
h. Max_admission – The latest day on which the patient can be admitted. 
i. 
Treatment. 
j. 
Preffered_capacity – The patient can express a preferred room capacity. 
k. Room_property_list – A list of preferred and required room properties. 
ii. 
Room – Each room has various properties, as follows: 
a. Name. 
b. Capacity. 
c. Dep_index – an indication of the department to which the room belongs. 
d. Gender_policy – Each room has one of 3 gender policies: 
i. SG – The room can have either male or female occupants as long as, on any 
given day, all occupants are of the same gender. 
ii. Ma – The room can only have male occupants. 
iii. Fe – The room can have only female occupants. 
iv. All – Any patients, of any gender, can be present in the room at any point in 
time.

e. Features_list – A list of features the room has, corresponding to the patient’s 
room_property_list. 
iii. 
Department – Each hospital contains a set number of departments. These contain the 
following information: 
a. Name. 
b. Age_constraint – Some departments will only accept patients in a specific age group. 
c. Main_specialism_list – This is a list of treatments for which the department is best 
suited to treat. 
d. Aux_specialism_list – This is a list of treatements for which the department is 
capable of treating, but not ideally. 
iv. 
Transfer – A patient assigned to a particular room can be transferred during his/her stay. 
This incurs a penalty. 
v. 
Delay – A patient can be delayed admission until his/her max admission day. This incurs 
a penalty. 
vi. 
Overcrowded Risk – A penalty is incurred when patients with a variability greater than 0 
are assigned to rooms that are full on the days after their expected discharge.

It is worth noting here that the room preference constraint can be interpreted in two, equally valid 
ways. A room preference of say, less than or equal to 2 may mean that the patient does not want to 
be in a room with 3 or more beds or that he/she does not want to be in a room with 3 or more 
people at any time. The distinction can result in very different optimal solutions. For the purpose of 
this project, in order to maintain consistency with Demeester et al. the constraint is taken to refer to 
the number of beds in a room and not the number of patients. The distinction may be worth 
considering at a later stage. 
4 Literature Review 
This problem has been studied before both from an integer programming perspective as well as a 
metaheuristic approach. The following section will briefly describe some of the literature’s attempts 
and findings. 
4.1 Modelling and solving the dynamic patient admission scheduling patient 
admission scheduling problem under uncertainty 
This article by Sara Ceschia et al. investigates both the static and dynamic verisions of the problem 
(predictive and daily versions respectively). The authors propose a metaheuristic approach based 
around simulated annealing. Their research indicates that an exact value for the problem only yields 
results for the small and medium instances (up to 250 patients)  but that their metaheuristic’s 
results are at the same level of quality. 
Their approach structure the moves into two distinct types – moves which assign patients to rooms, 
either by swapping patients or by changing rooms and moves which assign a delay to each patient. 
Their initial solution is a random one and, because they are using simulating annealing, they 
assigning a very high original temperature (essentially forcing the algorithm to act like a hill climber). 
As time moves on the temperature drops making it more likely for a non improving solution will be 
allowed. This gives them the opportunity to explore more of the neighbourhood around the local 
optimals. 
It is important to note that not only did Ceschia et al. discover that the instances above the medium 
short cannot be solved to optimality in reasonable time, because of the way the mathematical 
model was implemented, on many occasions,  the heuristic performed better than the integer 
program. 
4.2 A hybrid tabu search algorithm for automatically assigning patients to beds 
This article by Demeester et al. looks at the possibility of applying the tabu search algorithm to solve 
the problem. As with Sara Ceschia et al. they attempt an MIP solution which does not find a feasible 
solution within one hour. Based on this, they decide to focus exclusively on the metaheuristic 
approach. Their approach looks at a patient-day-bed combination, giving a very large neighbourhood 
by allowing the movement of patients from one bed to another in the same room. They define three 
different neighbourhoods. The first (and smallest) allows only for swapping patient days to different 
beds in the same department. This ensure that the treatment and age constraint is never violated. 
The second allows for the moving of patients to a different department and the third moves patients 
to the same department if there is space. According to the authors, this approach provides 
acceptable results which are far superior to any MIP solution they attempted, the best of which took 
3 hours for the first feasible solution to be obtained. 
==PAGE:8==
 
7 | P a g e  
 
4.3 A column generation approach to solving the patient admission scheduling 
problem 
Range et al have introduced a column generation approach to solving the problem. Their method 
splits the original large problem into a master and pricing problem. The aim is to minimise the 
number of variables in the original problem and dynamically create variables to add to the original 
problem, tightening the bounds with every iteration, producing an LP solution that ultimately via a 
branch and bound algorithm will produce an IP solution. Their method has been tested on a limited 
number of instanced but so far has yielded the best results so far, having found better solutions that 
those already found in 6 out of the 12 instances tested. Range et al do admit that their method has 
problems finding solutions for instances with more than 14 day planning horizons. 
 
Overall the literature shows a clear preference for metaheuristic approaches, the problem being 
considered too complex to be solved to optimality with current technology.

problem.

The above code works in the following way: 
1. Create a new solution element and clone the elements from the input solution into this new 
solution element. This will be used to perform any operations without manipulating the 
original solution. Note that the clone function performs a deep clone. Without it, solution 
would have been copied by reference instead of by value, meaning that essentially, the

solutions to change with the swap and so, the function always returned the swapped 
solution, regardless of the objective value.  
2. Pick a random patient using C#’s inbuilt random number generator. The function’s 
constructor takes the current date and time as a seed, ensuring that the same results will 
never be repeated. 
3. If the patient’s canSwap list is not empty a random patient is selected from there and the 
two patients are swapped. 
4. The objective values are computed for the two solutions and the lower value is returned.

6.6 Hill Climber 
To best combine the two properties of the two swapping functions, the are each assigned a 
probability of 50% and combined to produce a hill climber that works as follows: 
1. Pick a number between 0 and 1 
a. If 0 – swap patients 
b. If 1 – change room 
2. Perform move 
3. Repeat if stopping criteria not met. 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 | P a g e  
 
 
Figure 14 - Hill Climber 
As is, this algorithm performs as a simple hill climber. It looks for a better solution until some 
stopping criteria is met but will always get stuck in a local optimal as soon as one is found. This limits 
the neighbourhood being searched. As such, the hill climber needs to be able to get out of a local 
optimal. For this, a series of un-improving swaps need to be allowed. 
 Diversification 
6.6.1
In this case, in order to explore the neighbourhood more thoroughly, the diversification strategy will 
use the same strategy as the solution search except it will return any solution, not just an improving 
one. How we can decide that a local optimal is reached in the first place and how many swaps need 
to be done in order to ensure that we do not return to the same solution will be discussed in the 
tuning section on page 31. 
6.7 Tabu Search 
The problem with the hill climber approach is a fairly obvious one – there is nothing stopping the 
algorithm from attempting to repeat swaps already made. The tabu search algorithm incorporates a 
degree of memory by “tabuing” moves which will produce a worse solution. This requires the 
implementation of a tabu list which will contain a list of n illegal moves. The most obvious way of 
creating this list would be, at least as far as the patient swap is concerned, to tabu particular swaps. 
For example, if patient 3 has just been swapped with patient 5, in order to avoid repeating moves, 
those two patients can be forbidden from swapping. The problem with this approach however is 
that it cannot be applied to both types of swaps and more importantly, it can in principle ban 
improving moves very quickly. For example, if patient 3 is swapped with patient 5 and then patient 5 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 | P a g e  
 
is swapped with patient 4 (who is in a third room) then banning the swap between patient 3 and 5 
no longer makes sense as you are now banning a solution that has never been visited.  To 
accommodate for this, the tabu list was implemented to contain a patient – room pair. This means 
that, regardless of what kind of swap was performed, what will be added to the tabu list will be the 
patient name and the room from which he/she was taken. The only difference between the two 
swaps in the way in which they implement the tabu list is that the room swap removes one element

7.5 Tuning 
The full tabu list program, which incorporates both swap methods as well as a tabu list contains the 
following variables: 
1. Tabu size 
a. The length of the tabu list is very important. If the tabu list is too short the program 
acts essentially as a simple hill climber. If the tabu list is too long, potentially 
improving moves may be forbidden and the result obtained will be too far from 
optimal. 
 
2. Number of diversifications 
a. In theory, the more diversifications done, the more likely a better result is found. 
Increasing the number of diversifications too much however will result in the 
algorithm taking too much time to complete. If the best solution is usually found 
withing 3 diversification attempts, it is redundant to force the algorithm to diversify 
10 times. 
 
3. Number of non-improving iterations 
a. Before diversifying, it is important to ascertain that you are really in a local optimal 
and no improving swaps exist. If you diversify too early, you risk leaving a region 
before finding the local optimal and if you diversify too late you waste a lot of time. 
 
4. Number of diversifying iterations 
a. After a local optimal is found, it is important that the right number of iterations are 
made to leave the local optimal. If too few iterations are made, the diversification 
has no effect – chances are you return to the same local optimal. If too many are 
made, you again waste a lot of time. 
All of these parameters need to be tuned for each individual type of instance. Due to time 
constraints, it is not possible to tune as excessively as would be desired. It has been done however 
on the small_short, small_mid and small_large instances. These contain the following information:

result was usually obtained within the first 4 diversification attempts. As each of the small_short

appropriate to use 7 as the tuning parameter.

tuning for the medium and large instances was done with a 10 minute time constraint per test, run 4 
times per instance over 2 instances. This means that 80minutes are used for each data point on the 
tuning. This has also severely minimised the number of data points which could be collected

7.7 Evaluation & Future Improvements 
It is clear from the above results that the algorithm performs as expected. It is however, far from 
perfect. There are many things to consider and improve in future.  
 Redundancies 
7.7.1
As it is constructed now, the tabu list method does not save as much time as it could. Currently it 
works by picking a random patient and a random room or swappable patient as may be the case and 
checks if the particular pair is present in the tabu list. If it is, it does not attempt to swap and returns 
the initial solution. This still means that an iteration is wasted picking a random pair and checking it 
against the tabu list. The data then becomes riddled with data points which are the same not 
through virtue of having been deemed better than the random swap but due to no change having 
happened at all. Ideally, the algorithm would be implemented in such a way as to not allow for the 
picking of members in it in the first place. This would save considerable resources. These 
redundancies play a huge role in the time delay associated with the algorithm as it works now. In 
fact, one of the main resource drains according to Visual Studio’s diagnostics tool is the objective 
value calculation which happens twice each swap. This could be addressed by considering only the 
change in cost associated with the swap as opposed to recalculating the cost for each patient. 
Another possibility for future development would be to make use of GPUs instead of CPUs. This 
would allow for many more calculations to be done in parallel, saving a lot of time.

 Delta Calculation 
7.7.2
In order to streamline the process and ensure that improving moves are made more often, it would 
be useful to have an easily calculated delta values for certain moves. Consider the scenario in which 
Patient 2 is picked for a patient swap. At this point his canSwap list contains 5 other patients. As the 
algorithm stands now, a random patient is picked. It would improve the algorithm drastically if these 
5 patients were ordered in terms of the likelihood of a swap providing an improving solution. This 
could be done by associating some fixed costs to each patient-room pair. If a particular patient swap 
will ensure that both patients will now meet all of their room preference, room capacity and 
treatment requirements there is a good chance that the swap is preferred, regardless of the added 
potential costs from gender and overcrowd risk penalties which may be incurred. If these “fixed 
costs” are calculated in advance for each patient and each room, one can easily calculate a delta – 
the difference in cost associated with the swap. This would allow for a ranking of potentially 
improving swaps from which to pick prior to any swap being made. This would in theory restrict the 
search space but if implemented correctly it could ensure that fewer non improving moves are made 
in the search for a local optimal.  
 Initial Solution 
7.7.3
As it is now, the algorithm’s initial solution is a greedy heuristic which does not spread out patients 
evenly across the rooms. The only element of improvement present is the initial ranking of patients 
in order of the number of rooms they can occupy. This means that, regardless of the seed, the 
algorithm will always begin at the same solution. This could be addressed by implementing an 
element of randomness in the construction of the initial solution. This could be achieved by first 
constructing for each patient a list of rooms in which they can fit, then picking a random patient and 
a random room from their list and assigning them there if they can fit. This would create possibility 
of a different initial solution being constructed each time. This has two drawbacks. Firstly, it does not 
ensure that all patients get a room to begin with. This can be addressed in one of two ways: 
1. Implement a ranking system as done in the current algorithm ensuring that the patients with 
the fewest feasible rooms get picked first. 
2. Create a “virtual” room in which remaining unassigned patients are placed and assign any 
patients in that room a high penalty. Start the algorithm and fairly quickly the patients who 
were originally unassigned will be redistributed amongst the real rooms as long as the 
penalty for being in the room is high enough. 
Secondly, this method of creating the initial solution does not allow for a very good starting solution. 
It would make sense to try and construct an initial solution as close to a local as possible. This could 
be done by calculating the “fixed costs” mentioned in the previous section and assigning patients to 
their “best” rooms from the very beginning. 
Finally, an issue has been discovered with the way the solution is implemented – despite the ranking 
of patients by number of available rooms, there is still at least one instance for which the initial 
solution cannot be feasible. 
 Diversification 
7.7.4
As it stands, the algorithm requires tuning to ensure with each diversification, the local optimal is 
actually left. This is because he way a restart is achieved is by leaving the local optimal via a series of 
swaps. As mentioned above, if too few swaps are made, the algorithm doesn’t leave the local 
optimal and simply returns there. At the same time, these particular swaps each take time. A better 
solution would be to produce more “initial solutions” at the beginning of the algorithm and 
randomly pick one of them with each restart. This ensures that a restart leaves the current 
==PAGE:42==
 
41 | P a g e  
 
neighbourhood in only one iteration. Not only does this make the diversification strategy more 
efficient but also saves valuable time. 
 Delay 
7.7.5
The biggest drawback of the algorithm described is that it does not allow for delaying patients. One 
of the main reasons for this is the fact that implementing a delay element is fairly counter intuitive in 
a tabu framework. In any scenario, simply adding a delay on a patient will always make a solution 
worse. The only scenario in which delaying patients make sense within this framework would be as 
part of the diversification. This however adds a new dimension to the search space making it 
exponentially bigger. This makes it much harder to explore a significant proportion of it. Here we see 
one of the drawbacks of a tabu search algorithm in general – it does not allow for non improving 
movements. Adding them can be done, but it is artificial and hard to implement.  In order to best 
implement a delay element, a different type of algorithm would likely have been more appropriate – 
ALNS and Simulated Annealing are two possibilities which are more suited for the possibility of a non 
improving move. 
 Transfer 
7.7.6
The penalty for transferring a patient during their stay is the largest possible and it was considered 
that the likelihood of a transfer being more efficient than a swap in terms of improving a solution is 
too low to be worth the added complexity. That being said, implementing the possibility for a 
transfer would  entail splitting up every patient into n independent (but consecutive) elements 
where n is the number of days the patient is expected to be in the hospital for. This would again 
increase the size of the search space exponentially and provide little in the way of potential 
improvement. 
 Tuning 
7.7.7
The quality of the results can be greatly improved with more extensive tuning – by tuning over more 
instances the algorithm can be tailored much better to the situation at hand. Unfortunately time was 
such that this was not possible.  
8 Conclusion 
This thesis has investigated the Dynamic Patient Admissions problem and developed a tabu search 
algorithm to solve the problem. The results obtained are encouraging – The algorithm works as 
expected and, given the time constraints and the algorithm’s relative inefficiency with respect to 
time taken for a solution it can be said that the algorithm is worth considering as an efficient 
method. As already discussed however, its main drawback is the fact that it does not easily allow for 
non improving moves. This makes implementing patient delays a problem – one which is not 
unsurmountable. 
With the suggested improvements, this algorithm can produce efficiently good results that would 
rival those already found.
