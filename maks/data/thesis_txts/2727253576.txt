Abstract
Children visiting the emergency department often experience anxiety and stress due to
the unfamiliar environment and medical procedures. In Danish Pediatric Emergency De-
partments, effectively managing pain and anxiety in children remains a significant chal-
lenge. Current non-pharmacological interventions, particularly those that equip children
with age-appropriate medical knowledge, fall short due to their time-consuming nature
and lack of variability. Hence, there is an unmet need for evidence-based, accessible, non-
pharmacological solutions that can work in conjunction with existing pharmacological and
distraction options to reduce anxiety in children during their hospital experience. Digital
interventions, especially those involving serious games and extended reality (XR) tech-
nologies, have shown significant potential to fill this void and positively affect children’s
health behaviors and hospital experiences.
The objective of this thesis is to design an evidence-based digital intervention that aids chil-
dren in preparing for their medical procedures in pediatric emergency departments, with a
particular focus on reducing anticipatory anxiety. The intervention aims to leverage seri-
ous games and augmented reality (AR) technology to deliver age-appropriate medical in-
formation, thereby enhancing children’s comprehension and fostering coping mechanisms.
This thesis is guided by the Double Diamond Framework and deploys a mixed-methods
approach. The overall process encompasses a literature review, primary research at the
Herlev Hospital, and the iterative process of ideation, development, and evaluation of
the digital intervention, infused throughout with a participatory design approach. The
findings from the literature review and primary research inform the ideation and imple-
mentation of digital intervention. Usability, effectiveness, and satisfaction among children
and caregivers are evaluated through usability testing, questionnaires, and data analysis.
The digital intervention designed in this thesis consists of a serious game featuring ani-
mated LEGO characters and augmented reality (AR) cards, providing children with in-
teractive and tangible experiences. The intervention incorporates evidence-based learning
objectives, game mechanics, and user-centric interface design, addressing key aspects iden-
tified through healthcare professional interviews and scientific literature.
The results of the study demonstrate the feasibility and potential effectiveness of the
developed digital intervention in the PED setting.
Healthcare professionals expressed
enthusiasm for the concept, considering it a potentially useful tool for both nurses and
children within the context of procedural preparation. Both children and parents conveyed
positive perceptions regarding the utility of the intervention and signaled an openness
to using comparable interventions in the future. Children were observed to be actively
engaged while interacting with the intervention, and they typically found the game easy
to use and were satisfied with the characters. These findings underscore the potential of
digital interventions, leveraging game-based and AR technologies, to prepare children for
medical procedures and reduce anxiety in pediatric emergency settings.

1.1
Context and Motivation
Visiting an emergency department can be a stressful experience for anyone, but for chil-
dren, the challenges become even more pronounced. After triage, children and their care-
givers enter the pediatric emergency department (PED) in a state of uncertainty, often
enduring prolonged waiting periods in anticipation of treatment and reassurance [1, 2,
3]. The unfamiliar situation, presence of medical equipment, and uncertain procedures
can provoke negative feelings among young children [4, 5], as visually demonstrated in
Figure 1.1. Anxiety among children is one of the most commonly reported emotional fac-
tors that may lead to increased pain perception and reduced cooperation during medical
procedures [6]. Insuﬀicient pain and anxiety management during a child’s PED stay and
subsequent medical procedures can adversely affect their overall experience [2, 7] and may
have long-term detrimental effects on the child’s well-being and development [5].
Figure 1.1: Images sourced online showing children exhibiting anxiety during medical
procedures in PEDs [8, 9].
To manage the anxiety of young patients, preparing them for upcoming medical treatments
is an important element of the pediatric emergency care setting that requires multiple
interventions and the involvement of different roles in the PED environment. Adequate
preparation has been shown to decrease anxiety and increase a child’s coping before a
medical procedure or surgery [2]. The waiting time during their stay at the PED presents
a unique opportunity to introduce interventions specifically designed to assess and reduce
children’s anxiety. This can include educating and supporting children and their caregivers
during the waiting period, providing information about the procedures, offering distraction
techniques, or creating a child-friendly environment [10].
The motivation for this thesis stems from a research project conducted in collaboration
with Herlev Hospital, which addresses the urgent need to assess and reduce anxiety through
effective and comprehensive approaches among children during their PED visits.

1.2
Background
1.2.1
Anxiety in children within PEDs
Children’s anxiety within the PED is often signaled by subjective feelings of worry, ten-
sion, nervousness, and apprehension. The manifestation of this emotional condition varies
considerably among young children, who often express their feelings through their actions
rather than their words. The presence of anxiety in children is linked to alterations in
behavior, including uncooperativeness, aggression, social withdrawal, and emotional re-
gression. Factors such as age, temperament, and parental anxiety have been identified as
predictors of these behavioral changes. The management of child anxiety presents unique
challenges due to differences in pain and anxiety perception compared to adults, along
with their limited ability to communicate effectively and their insuﬀicient understanding
of medical procedures [10].
1.2.2
Approaches to Managing Anxiety
Extensive literature reveals a wide range of interventions that have been employed to
alleviate anxiety among children in healthcare settings [10], as illustrated in Figure 1.2.

divided into pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions [12].
While phar-

delayed discharge [13]. In turn, healthcare professionals (HCPs) are cautious not to over-

1.2.3
Limitations of Non-Pharmacological Interventions in PEDs
Despite the recognition of the importance of non-pharmacological interventions, their ap-
plication in the PED setting is limited. In Danish PEDs, there is a notable inconsistency
in the application of national guidelines for pain and anxiety management in children. Key
elements such as pain and anxiety assessment and non-pharmacological methods are not
fully integrated into many EDs’ guidelines. Despite awareness of these guidelines, their
use in practice is not consistent among HCPs [5]. These findings underscore the need for
more uniform implementation of the national guideline across Danish EDs, mirroring the
limitations of non-pharmacological interventions at the Pediatric Emergency Department
of Herlev Hospital in Denmark.
Distraction techniques, such as using clowns, bubbles, toys, movies, or video games, are
commonly employed in the PED to transiently reduce anxiety.
However, while these
techniques provide temporary relief, they fall short in terms of comprehensive information
provision, modeling, and coping skills, which are essential for adequately preparing children
for medical procedures. This gap becomes more evident when considering the challenges
faced by caregivers and HCPs in determining what information to convey, how to convey
it effectively, and when to provide it to the child [17].
1.2.4
Promising Use of Digital Interventions
The advent of digital technologies has greatly influenced many sectors, including health-
care, offering opportunities for innovative approaches to care.
Children, in particular,
have shown a strong aﬀinity for digital technologies, including mobile phones and tablets,
which are often used for educational and entertainment purposes.
In the context of pediatric healthcare, digital interventions offer a unique opportunity to
positively influence children’s experiences. Studies suggest that such interventions can
enhance children’s understanding of medical procedures, reduce anxiety, and improve co-
operation during these procedures[14, 18, 13, 19]. However, current applications of digital
interventions in PEDs predominantly focus on distraction, often overlooking the valuable
educational component necessary for eliciting behavior change[14]. This indicates a gap in
realizing the full potential of these interventions to prepare children for medical procedures
within emergency settings.
Considering the promising advantages of digital technology, children’s inherent aﬀinity for
it, and the capability to develop a tangible prototype, this thesis suggests employing a
digital intervention to eﬀiciently prepare children for medical procedures, a process that
could potentially result in reduced anxiety.
1.3
Problem Statement and Research Questions
HCPs at PED of Herlev Hospital prioritize the well-being of children during the wait-
ing period by implementing various non-pharmacological interventions to reduce their
anxiety. These include engaging services such as clown performers, providing toys, and
offering video games and movies, among other interventions. However, the current ap-
proach at the hospital still lacks standardized strategies for adequately preparing children
for medical procedures in advance. There is a noticeable absence of comprehensive infor-
mation provision that delivers accurate and age-appropriate medical information, as well
as the necessary coping skills to prepare children before undergoing procedures in Herlev
Hospital’s emergency settings.

Related Work
This chapter provides an overview of the related work in pediatric procedural prepara-
tion including theoretical models, the content of preparation information, and cognitive-
developmental considerations in Section 2.1. It also explores the categorization of prepa-
ration interventions in Section 2.2. Additionally, Section 2.3 addresses challenges specific

2.1
Overview of Procedural Preparation Strategy
Procedural preparation strategy in pediatric healthcare refers to a range of techniques and
interventions aimed at preparing children for medical procedures by providing them with
essential information, support, and tools to reduce anxiety and enhance their comprehen-
sion. It not only provides general procedural information but also delivers accurate and
specific information about realistic expectations, and pain management as well as teaches
effective coping strategies [20]. Effective explanations enable children to process informa-
tion and allocate attention appropriately, fostering trust, reducing uncertainty, enhancing
their belief in their ability to cope, and optimizing treatment outcomes [21]. Studies have
shown that better-informed children generally experience lower anxiety levels and exhibit
better adjustment during and after medical procedures [14, 22]. Furthermore, providing
accurate and timely information to children can ease the burden on caregivers, empower
them to support their children, and reduce parental anxiety[17].
2.1.1
A Theoretical Model of Information Provision
In recent years, two theoretical frameworks, namely self-regulation theory and schema/script
theories, have emerged to elucidate the processes related to information provision before
medical procedures [17]. Figure 2.2 shows the processes involved in the provision of prepa-
ration information for medical procedures.
The self-regulation theory emphasizes the dynamic process of regulating behavior based
on expectations and experiences. By providing accurate preparatory information, such
as descriptions of expected sensations during a medical procedure, incongruence between
expected and actual experiences can be minimized [17]. This theory highlights the impor-
tance of predictability and cognitive abilities in evaluating one’s perceived ability to cope
with a forthcoming event [23].
In contrast, schema and script theories focus on the role of mental representations and
memory in shaping individuals’ expectations and interpretations of events [24]. Schemata
are formed based on past experiences and information obtained from various sources.
Preparatory information activates schematic memories, and providing accurate informa-
tion helps individuals generate, modify, or refine specific and appropriate schemata [25].
Given the constructive nature of memory and children’s susceptibility to memory dis-
tortion, it is imperative to accurately and positively reframe medical experiences. This
reframing plays a vital role in mitigating anticipatory anxiety and promoting a more pre-
cise comprehension of the procedures [17].
To integrate these theoretical approaches, an Information Provision Model has been pro-
posed by Jaaniste. T and colleagues [17]. This model emphasizes the use of prepara-
tory information by individuals facing medical procedures. It considers individual experi-
ences, such as previous medical experiences and individual difference factors, in shaping

as distress levels and perceived pain intensity.

2.1.2
Content of Preparation Information
The content of preparation information in pediatric healthcare plays a crucial role in shap-
ing children’s expectations and coping behavior during medical procedures. To ensure that
information effectively influences a child’s expectations and coping behavior, it is vital that
the content is comprehensible and engaging for the child. This is particularly important
for preschool children who have limited cognitive abilities to envision future states beyond
their immediate experiences, such as hunger, thirst, or tiredness [27]. Given this develop-
mental characteristic, it is essential to provide very specific and concrete information that
helps shape a child’s expectations and prepares them for the upcoming procedure [17, 28].
The available evidence suggests that informing a child about a forthcoming medical pro-
cedure should include the following aspects:
• Procedural information: how long the procedure will take, who will participate,
what tools will be used, how will the procedure be performed, and why the procedure
is necessary [17, 28, 29];
• Sensory-based information: physical and emotional sensations children may ex-
perience during the procedure, including pain [17, 30];
• Coping strategies: such as distraction and deep breathing exercises [31, 32].
Nevertheless, the potential risks and pitfalls associated with information provision should
also be considered. The provision of sensory information alone about what an individual
might expect may exacerbate the distress of highly anxious individuals [17]. In particular,
children who have had a previous negative medical experience may be sensitized by infor-
mation about a forthcoming procedure. Therefore, it is important to provide these children
with not only information about the procedure but also advice on coping strategies [33].

Notably, reassurance statements (e.g., “It will be OK,” “It will be over in no time”), often

2.1.3
Cognitive-developmental considerations
When providing information to children, it is crucial to consider their cognitive develop-
ment and adapt the content accordingly [15, 6]. According to Piaget’s theory of cognitive
development, children in the preoperational stage (2 to 6 years old), benefit from focus-
ing on concrete external events [36]. For this age group, preparatory information should
include procedural descriptions and sensory details that help them understand and en-
gage with the medical procedure.
In addition to simple explanations, children in this
age range require more tangible and concrete means of information provision [28]. As
children enter the concrete operational stage (7 to 12 years old), their cognitive abilities
progress, allowing them to grasp more complex concepts. Preparatory information for
this age group may involve explaining how symptoms relate to the procedure, providing
information about duration, and using analogies to aid understanding. For adolescents
in the formal operational stage, typically 13 years old and older, a more advanced level
of preparatory information is necessary. Providing detailed information about internal
body structures and processes can help them develop a comprehensive understanding of
the procedure. Moreover, explaining how the results of the procedure will be used in the
future can contribute to their engagement and cooperation [17, 28].
Stage-based approaches to cognitive development provide valuable insights but have limi-
tations, as they may overlook domain-specific knowledge and individual experiences, and
lack clear-cut transitions [17]. Nonetheless, considering cognitive development can inform
age-appropriate preparatory information for children undergoing medical procedures.
2.2
Categorization of Different Forms of Preparation
Interventions
Preparation interventions for children regarding a forthcoming medical procedure may
take many forms, including oral or written summaries, visual aids, hospital tours, medical
play, modeling, interactive media as well as combinations of the above. More detailed
descriptions of each category are provided below:
Oral or written summaries: They are the most commonly employed approach for
providing information in healthcare settings. Healthcare professionals can verbally explain
the procedure to the child in simple, age-appropriate language. They can discuss what
will happen, the purpose of the procedure, and what the child can expect to experience.
Written materials, such as brochures or pamphlets [37, 18], can be provided to the child and
their family to increase their understanding of medical procedures and help to clarify their
misconceptions [4]. These materials may include step-by-step instructions, explanations
of medical terms, and tips for coping with anxiety or discomfort during the procedure.
Visual Aids: Visual aids, such as pictures, illustrations, or videos, can be used to sup-
plement verbal and written explanations and help children visualize the procedure. Visual
aids, particularly videos, can make complex medical concepts more accessible and easier
to comprehend when explaining medical procedures [17, 38, 39]. However, it is important
to note that videos should not be seen as a cure-all solution in information provision.
Consideration should be given to the content, timing, and format of video presentations

[28]. For children who are unfamiliar with a forthcoming medical procedure, studies have
shown that the most effective video preparations feature a peer model who not only goes
through the procedure but also shares their feelings and provides strategies to minimize
the impact of the event [40]. Video presentations may be less effective for children with a
history of significant prior medical events [41].
Hospital tours: Hospital tours provide young patients with the opportunity to explore
the hospital settings themselves prior to the actual procedure [22]. The tour allows children
to familiarize themselves with the hospital environment, including the waiting areas, single
wards, examination rooms, and procedure rooms. Hospital tours may be accompanied by
explanations from healthcare professionals or child life specialists who can address any
questions or concerns. While effective in helping children become gradually accustomed
to fear-inducing stimuli, it should be acknowledged that such training sessions can be
demanding in terms of time and resources.
Medical play: Medical play involves the use of toys or real medical equipment (e.g.
stethoscope, syringe without needles, bandages, medical cup, gloves, mask, nurse’s cap,
dressing pack, etc.), dolls, or models to simulate medical procedures, providing children
with an opportunity for imaginative play and self-expression. Through engaging in play
activities, children can explore their feelings, familiarize themselves with the medical equip-
ment and medical procedures, and develop a sense of control and understanding [4]. Med-
ical play creates a safe and empowering environment, allowing children to gain knowledge
about the procedure, the roles of healthcare professionals, and the sequence of events
involved. It offers a hands-on approach to preparation, enabling children to actively par-
ticipate in the process and alleviate anxiety through play-based experiences.
Modeling: Modeling involves healthcare professionals or trained individuals demonstrat-
ing the medical procedure step-by-step.
This technique utilizes procedure-specific (eg,
lumbar puncture, sutures, and intravenous start) teaching kits to provide a clear vi-
sual demonstration of the procedure, explaining each action and component involved [15].
Modeling aims to enhance children’s understanding of the process, the roles of healthcare
providers, and the specific steps of the procedure. Modeling complements verbal explana-
tions and provides a visual representation of the procedure, promoting comprehension and
familiarity. Peer models often play a role in providing information about hospitalization
and common sensations experienced during medical procedures [42].
Interactive Media: Interactive media refers to digital platforms, including websites,
apps, virtual reality (VR), and augmented reality (AR) tools, that provide children with
interactive and immersive learning experiences within the healthcare context [43]. These
media can offer dynamic and engaging content that can be customized to match the child’s
age, developmental level, and specific educational objectives. By incorporating multimedia
elements such as videos, animations, games, quizzes, and interactive simulations, inter-
active media in pediatric healthcare aim to enhance the child’s understanding of medical
procedures, conditions, and health-related topics [14]. Compared to passive screen-based
activities like watching videos, interactive activities like video games, which require user
input for gameplay, offer a higher level of engagement, potentially resulting in greater
user immersion. Theoretically, increased attention invested in the intervention may re-
duce the availability of cognitive resources for pain perception, potentially leading to a
greater reduction in pain [7].

2.2.1
Aligning Preparation Formats with Cognitive Development
Stages
Drawing on Piaget’s theory of cognitive development and the available intervention meth-
ods, a targeted mapping of preparation formats for different age groups is proposed. This
mapping offers age and cognitively-appropriate intervention forms, taking into account
individual differences and experiences for effective preparation.
Children in the preoperational stage (2 to 6 years old) often respond well to concrete,
tangible, interactive, and simple illustrative formats.
They could greatly benefit from
visual aids such as storybooks or cartoon videos, medical play, modeling, and interactive
media. Children in the concrete operational stage (7 to 12 years old) are able to understand
more detailed and complex ideas. Therefore, hospital tours, medical play, visual aids,
explanatory videos, and immersive games through interactive media would be suitable.
Adolescents in the formal operational stage can handle advanced formats such as in-depth
videos, reading materials, and interactive digital content. It’s worth noting that visual
aids and interactive media can be beneficial to all age groups.

2.3
Preparation Strategies in PEDs
In PEDs, children commonly undergo a variety of medical procedures, including needle-
related procedures, general check-ups, and imaging examinations. Needle-related proce-
dures, such as venipuncture, IV catheter insertion, and blood draw, are prevalent sources
of pain and discomfort among children in both primary and tertiary pediatric care settings
[44]. These procedures, along with imaging examinations, present specific challenges re-
lated to pain management and anxiety reduction. Children experiencing their first needle-
related procedure and those who may have had fearful experiences with prior procedures
may demonstrate heightened fear and anxiety [45]. During imaging procedures, children
and caregivers may be unprepared for the specific requirements, leading to increased anx-
iety and non-compliance.
Factors such as large and noisy machines, the need for IV
cannulations, and unfamiliar environments contribute to children’s reluctance, potentially
resulting in delays, cancellations, or the need for sedation [19]. These challenges in PED
not only impact the well-being of the children but also increase risks, prolong procedure
times, and incur additional costs.

However, compared to preparation for hospitalization and surgery, there has been more
empirical attention given to those procedures than to shorter, primary care procedures

medical procedures remains an area of limited knowledge, especially in emergency settings.
There is still a need for further research to determine the ideal timeframe for delivering
preparatory information to children [17].

The thesis project adopts the Double Diamond Framework, developed by The Design
Council, as its overall approach [52]. This framework employs two diamonds to represent

clear objectives. The second diamond encompasses “Develop” and “Deliver”, emphasizing
focused action and convergent thinking to develop and implement effective solutions [52].
The framework provides a structured approach for progressing through the Research,
Synthesis, Ideation, and Implementation phases.

Children’s journey in the PED
In Denmark, access to EDs, including PEDs, generally requires a prior referral from the
healthcare system. Referrals can be obtained by calling the GP cooperatives, the health-
care service number 1813, or the emergency number 1-1-2.
Citizens can also present
themselves directly to the emergency departments, where they are advised to call one of
the out-of-hours healthcare services [66].
Upon arriving at the PED, the child’s initial step is to register at the reception, which is
then followed by a rapid assessment known as triage. The triage process determines the
urgency of the child’s condition (see the triage system in Appendix B.4). After triage, the
child and caregivers may need to wait in the waiting area, with the duration of the wait
varying based on the severity of the condition and the PED’s workload. Nurses in the
PED have indicated that the wait time in the waiting room can range from a few minutes
to as long as three hours.

4.2.2
Factors Contributing to Children’s Anxiety and Uncooperative
Behaviors
A combination of content analysis and thematic analysis was used to extract insights
from the qualitative data gathered through observations and interviews [67]. The content
analysis was used to systematically categorize the observed behaviors and interview re-
sponses, transforming the substantial data into an organized framework. Subsequently,
thematic analysis was used to delve deeper into these categories and identify themes. By
iterating through this process, five main factors contributing to children’s anxiety and
uncooperative behaviors were discerned and elaborated upon in the following subsections.
Developmental stage and understanding
Children’s developmental stage and understanding play a significant role in their behav-
ior and cooperation levels during their time in PEDs. School-aged children (8-12 years
old) often exhibit calmer behavior and show increased independence from their caregivers
while waiting and undergoing procedures. They can express their concerns, understand
explanations provided by nurses, and are generally more cooperative throughout the pro-
cess. Preschoolers (3-7 years old) display a wide range of behaviors and responses. Some
preschoolers can engage directly with nurses, comprehend basic explanations, and coop-
erate to a certain extent. While some presented limited comprehension and struggle to
articulate their fears or concerns. Toddlers (1-3 years old) require constant attention and
may experience diﬀiculty in maintaining focus or being easily distracted. They heavily
rely on their caregivers for comfort and support during their time in the PED and find it
more challenging to cope due to their limited understanding. Infants (under 1 year old)
rely primarily on their caregivers for comfort and soothing. Their responses are largely
driven by their sensory experiences, such as being held or fed.
Fear of the unknown
The uncertainty of waiting for a long time without knowing the procedure can lead to
impatience and anxiety among children. The environment in the triage and examination
rooms, with healthcare professionals and unfamiliar medical tools, can also contribute to
higher anxiety levels for some children due to fear or unknown experiences. Young children
who are unfamiliar with tools like the pulse oximeter in the triage, and although it is not
painful, they were still reluctant to be assessed with it. Similarly, the use of a mask for
gas administration, which does not cause pain, can still evoke fear and increased anxiety
in children who are unfamiliar with it, leading to their reluctance to wear it as instructed
by the nurses. Some commonly used medical tools are shown in Figure 4.6.
Previous negative experiences
Negative experiences from previous medical procedures, particularly those involving painful
procedures like needle insertion, can significantly influence children’s anxiety levels and
cooperative behavior. Even when children are aware that they will undergo a similar pro-
cedure, they may still exhibit fear and apprehension. For instance, a 6-year-old boy who
had previously experienced a painful blood test demonstrated initial cooperation with the
nurse but continued to cry throughout the procedure, indicating the lingering impact of
his negative experience.
Pain or discomfort
Pain and discomfort significantly contribute to children’s emotional state and anxiety
levels. When children are in pain or experience discomfort, they often withdraw from
exploration and play, preferring to stay close to their caregivers and remain silent. They
may also exhibit startle responses to healthcare professionals. Additionally, children in
pain or discomfort are more likely to display uncooperative behavior, such as crying or
pushing others away, both before and during procedures. For example, a 4-year-old child
with fever, virus infection, and limb pain exhibited significant discomfort and fatigue.

While waiting with her mother, she remained quiet. However, during assessments in the

Lack of control or autonomy
As shared by Gitte (see Appendix C.1.1 for detailed information), children’s characteristics

4.2.3
Methods of Preparation in the PED
This subsection presents an analysis of the common preparation methods utilized in the
PED, focusing on oral explanations and written materials, to provide information and
alleviate anxiety among children and their caregivers.
Oral explanation
Nurses primarily rely on oral explanations as a technique for providing information about
the procedure and preparing children and caregivers. They verbally explain the procedure,
its purpose, and what to expect during and after the medical intervention to children and
caregivers. Gitte emphasized that it can be overwhelming for children to comprehend and
remember all the procedures at once. To alleviate this, a step-by-step approach is adopted,
wherein children are informed only about the immediate procedure or the “next thing”
that will occur. The duration of each procedure should also be communicated, as it helps
provide children with a sense of understanding and expectation. For young children, it
is important to utilize age-appropriate terminology, and concise and straightforward lan-
guage to explain the procedures (see Appendix C.1.1). During the shadowing of nurses,
effective communication with caregivers was highlighted as crucial by two nurses. It allows
for a better understanding of children’s experiences and individual characteristics, facili-
tating tailored strategies for their specific needs. Honesty was also deemed important by
nurses. They found that when children have a clear understanding of what to expect, they
are more likely to cooperate during the procedure. Nurses also address any concerns or
questions children may have, providing reassurance and accurate information to alleviate
anxiety and build trust with them (see Appendix B.3).

4.3.2
Target Audience and Their Unmet Needs
The impact of cognitive abilities on children’s pre-treatment anxiety levels, information
processing capacity, and their level of cooperation during treatments are apparent, as ev-
idenced by the primary and secondary research outlined in Sections 2.1.3 and 4.2.2. To
better comprehend the dynamics across various age groups, an age matrix was developed
(documented in Appendix D.2). This matrix assesses parameters such as cognitive abil-
ities, comprehension of information, the content of information, and the effectiveness of
exposure preparation techniques across distinct age categories, including infants, toddlers,
preschoolers, school-aged children, and adolescents.
An analysis of the matrix revealed a specific target audience for this project: preschool
children aged between 3 and 7 years, who are scheduled for procedures at the PED. The
assessment showed that school-aged children and adolescents generally possess a more
developed ability to comprehend and cope, whereas infants and toddlers, being at an
early cognitive developmental stage, have a limited capacity to understand information.
Preschool-aged children present especially challenges when it comes to comprehending
medical procedures and participating in preparatory activities due to the following char-
acteristics: need for support and information, need for control, capacity to understand
age-appropriate information, lack of communication and coping skills, and limited reading
ability. Children in this age range exhibit a sense of autonomy coupled with a desire to
actively understand their healthcare experiences. Even though they are at a cognitive
and emotional developmental stage where they can start comprehending the concept of
preparation, it is pivotal to equip them with age-appropriate resources that align with
their developing understanding. The inclusion of strategies like imaginative play and role-
playing can prove exceptionally beneficial. These activities allow children to familiarize
themselves with medical procedures in an interactive and engaging manner, thereby help-
ing reduce fear and anxiety. With these considerations in mind, the project decided to
focus on children aged between 3 to 7 years as the target demographic.
The observational and interview data underscored that current procedural preparation
strategies heavily depend on written and oral explanations. These conventional methods,
while informative, lack suﬀicient interactivity, visual aids, and personalization to effectively
engage preschool children, aged between 3 and 7 years. This demographic, with its specific
cognitive and emotional developmental needs, engages better with learning resources that
are interactive, visually stimulating, and customizable. Traditional preparation methods
tend to overlook these aspects, leading to a gap in meeting the children’s learning needs
effectively. The research thus emphasized the critical need for tailored, visually engaging
resources that align with the developmental stage of these children, offering them more

Strengths and Weaknesses
One of the key strengths of preparation interventions is their potential to improve the
overall PED experience for children. By providing age-appropriate information and ex-
planations, tailored to the child’s comprehension level, preparation interventions can help
alleviate fear and anxiety. Effective communication techniques, such as using child-friendly
language and visuals, can enhance understanding and cooperation. Additionally, involv-
ing caregivers in the preparation process can provide comfort and support to children.
This engagement can foster a shared understanding and knowledge between caregivers
and healthcare professionals, creating a more supportive environment for the child.
However, one potential weakness of preparation interventions is the variation in children’s
responses and coping mechanisms. While some children may significantly benefit from the
interventions and exhibit reduced anxiety, others may still experience high levels of fear and
distress. Individual differences in temperament, past experiences, and coping styles can
impact the effectiveness of the interventions. Moreover, the time and resources required
for thorough preparation can pose challenges, particularly in urgent medical situations
where timely interventions are necessary.
Hanne emphasized the strength and opportunity of engaging caregivers in the prepara-
tion process.
By involving caregivers, the effectiveness of interventions can be further
enhanced, as it provides an opportunity for caregivers to gain a better understanding of
the procedures and contribute to creating a supportive environment for their child. At
the same time, Hanne acknowledged the challenges associated with individual differences
and resource limitations (timing for preparation), which can impact the effectiveness of
preparation interventions.

In comparison to VR, AR provides significant advantages

real world, promoting contextual learning and easier application to real-world procedures.
Given that AR can be implemented on commonly used devices like smartphones and
tablets, it is more accessible and cost-effective than VR. Moreover, AR is safer as it
doesn’t disconnect users from their surroundings completely. All these factors make AR
a more suitable choice for preparation interventions in a medical context.

The Information-Motivation-Behavioural (IMB) Skills Model
The Information-Motivation-Behavioral (IMB) Skills model is a social psychological con-
ceptualization that aims to understand and promote health-related behaviors [72]. This
model presents a holistic view of information, motivation, and behavioral skills factors.
The IMB model identifies causal relationships among these constructs and offers a set
of operations for translating its approach into health promotion interventions [72]. This
model highlights the importance of providing relevant and structured information to indi-
viduals, enabling them to develop a clear understanding of the procedures and effectively
regulate their emotions and behaviors.

5.4.2
Serious Game
Serious games have gained increasing interest as instructional tools that leverage the ap-
peal of games and the effectiveness of information and communication technologies in
various domains, including pediatric healthcare [73]. They can turn passive learning into
an active exploration and increase users’ engagement and persistence [74]. Serious games
aim to combine educational objectives with engaging and entertaining gameplay, ensuring
a balance between fun and learning [73]. In the context of pediatric healthcare, serious
games have shown the potential in addressing various challenges including reducing anx-
iety and increasing understanding of medical procedures among children. By integrating
game mechanics and educational content, serious games have showns effectiveness in pro-
viding an interactive and immersive experience for children, facilitating their learning,
engagement, and emotional well-being before and during medical procedures [14].
Balancing Fun and Learning through Serious Game Mechanics
The core characteristic of a serious game is its capacity to maintain a balance between fun
(being a game) and facilitating the transfer of learning (being serious). This equilibrium
should be targeted from the design phase throughout the development of the game. The
Serious Game Mechanics (SGM) model, proposed by Sylvester Arnab et al., introduces
the concept of game components that translate a pedagogical practice into concrete game
mechanics that are directly perceivable by a player [73]. SGMs are the game components

a player’s actions [73]. The Learning Mechanics and Game Mechanics (LM-GM) model,
a core part of the SGM model, guides the design of the intervention. This model enables
serious game mapping that emphasizes the principal learning and game mechanics involved
in each game situation, thereby supporting the identification and analysis of emerging
SGMs.

Learning Mechanics–Game Mechanics (LM-GM) Model
Designing effective serious games for pediatric healthcare requires meticulous consideration
of several elements. Game mechanics, which are the rules or methods dictating the flow and
interactions of a game, describe interactions, game conditions, and triggers in an abstract
fashion [14]. The LM-GM model ensures that the chosen game mechanics contribute to the
desired learning outcomes. For example, learning mechanics like exploration, repetition,
and planning can be mapped to game mechanics such as storyline, sequential information
delivery, and strategic planning [73]. Utilizing the LM-GM model to guide the design of
a pedagogy-driven serious game ensures that game mechanics are chosen based on their
ability to contribute to the intended outcomes. In the context of the solution at hand,
the model was used to map game mechanics with determinants (factors contributing to
children’s anxiety before procedures) and the desired outcomes defined in the research
phase.
5.4.3
Augmented Reality for Motivated Learning
Augmented Reality (AR) is a pioneering technology that superimposes computer-generated
virtual imagery onto a live, real-world environment in real-time [75]. Its unique character-
istics of immediacy and interactivity facilitate innovative modes of human-computer inter-
action [74]. These distinctive qualities have propelled AR into a range of applications, with
a particularly significant impact in the field of education [76]. By fostering an immersive
and interactive learning environment, AR engages multiple senses, making the learning
experience more meaningful and effective [76]. AR-based learning techniques stimulate
children’s motivation and enhance their understanding of complex concepts [77]. This
multi-sensory engagement is especially crucial for children, as visualization significantly
bolsters their comprehension and problem-solving abilities. Recognizing this potential, we
propose a novel learning system that leverages AR technology to refine children’s learning
skills. This system enables children to interact with virtually displayed content via smart-
phones, thus merging education and entertainment in a way that piques their interest
and enthusiasm for learning. AR, with its capability to generate interactive 3D virtual

tion that combines digital technology and physical elements. By utilizing a combination
of engaging gameplay, informative content, and interactive experiences, the intervention
aims to educate and empower children, reducing their anxiety and increasing their pre-
paredness for medical procedures. In alignment with insights from healthcare professional
interviews (see Appendix E.4.1), four common procedures frequently encountered by chil-
dren in the PED have been selected for design and development: Triage, Blood Test,
X-Ray, and Ultrasound. The initiative aims to temper anticipatory fear and anxiety in
children prior to these procedures and enhance their coping abilities during the procedures.

Children’s current level of anxiety before intervention
To assess children’s current level of anxiety, the Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) measure was

bottom representing 0, indicating the lowest level of anxiety, and at the top representing

the intervention. Additionally, the Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (mYPAS)
was utilized as an observer-rated scale to assess anxiety in children. The mYPAS consists
of 27 items categorized into five domains: activity, vocalizations, emotional expressivity,
state of arousal, and use of parent. These domains reflect behaviors indicative of anxiety
in children.

Results
The data showed a strong positive correlation between children’s self-reported anxiety and
the observer’s assessment, suggesting the reliability of self-reported measures in determin-
ing children’s anxiety levels. Observational data indicated that older children (5-7 years)

engage with the intervention, suggesting that age is not the sole determinant of indepen-

mobile game observations revealed that children were more engaged in active simulation
games compared to passive video explanations.
As for the AR prototype, it elicited initial curiosity and intrigue due to its novelty. How-
ever, some usability issues were noted, including challenges in capturing images which
might be due to the unfamiliarity with the AR technology and an overall lack of interac-
tion with informational elements. The novelty also seemed to wear off quickly, as evidenced
by the children not scanning all the cards. The mobile game was generally found to be
more interesting than the AR game, possibly due to its higher degree of interactivity.
Children’s responses to the questionnaire suggested moderate enjoyment of the interven-
tion, indicating that both the AR intervention and the mobile game could be improved
in terms of their ability to engage children, particularly older males (6-7 years), who gave
lower ratings, perhaps due to the use of non-neutral, more feminine colors in the blood

Limitations
Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, strict inclusion criteria,
combined with a seasonal decrease in the number of children requiring the interventions
in question (blood tests, x-rays, and ultrasound procedures), significantly reduced the po-
tential participant pool. The evaluation period, constrained to two weeks, further limited
the feasibility of a more comprehensive clinical control study that could have compared
outcomes across multiple time points. As a result, children who did not immediately need
the procedures featured in the intervention, but met other inclusion criteria, were also
selected. This selection process might have resulted in relatively lower helpfulness ratings
for the intervention.
Inherent variability in children’s characteristics and experiences may have also influenced
the study’s results. Some children had prior experience with the procedures, which could
have impacted their engagement and perception of the intervention. The hospital envi-
ronment and children’s varied physical and emotional states could have further affected
their attitudes toward the intervention.
The presence of observers during the study could have potentially introduced the ”ob-
server effect”, altering children’s behavior and responses. This effect may have resulted in
children interacting with the intervention differently under observation than they would
in a natural setting.
The design of the intervention may not have been suﬀiciently neutral, leading to some
children disliking the intervention. For instance, color choices in the game scene (blood
test) might have been perceived as too ”cute” or non-neutral by older male children (6-7
years), possibly leading to lower engagement scores from this group.
Finally, language barriers impeded the observation of children’s verbal behaviors, limiting
the depth and breadth of the study. The inability to capture children’s spontaneous verbal
reactions and feedback could have affected the richness of the qualitative data collected
during the study.
Future work
Future improvements for the intervention should focus on three key areas:
• Usability: To reduce errors, it will be beneficial to enhance the visibility and intu-
itiveness of interactive elements in both the mobile game and AR intervention. En-
hancements should involve amplifying gamification, devising engaging storylines, and
incorporating more common medical procedures. Moreover, efforts should be made
to make the character design and theme more gender-neutral to increase acceptabil-
ity among different genders. To enhance the game’s utility, it may be beneficial to
incorporate more common procedures in the Pediatric Emergency Department. The
real voice-over should be considered in the audio and video.
• Technological Refinements: It is critical to rectifying technical issues within the
current design, such as audio playback problems, and consider alternative platforms
that offer improved functionality. The AR prototype could be refined for more robust

Research questions and results
This section revisits the research questions, reflecting on how each question was addressed

What essential medical information could potentially be included in the in-
tervention to enhance children’s understanding of their medical experiences
and which learning objectives should be considered in the design of the digital
intervention?
Based on scientific research and theoretical analysis, essential medical information includes
procedural details such as duration, participants, tools used, process, and necessity of the
procedure. It also involves sensory-based information about physical and emotional sen-
sations, including pain, that children may experience. Moreover, it’s crucial to provide
coping strategies like distraction techniques and deep breathing exercises. However, pro-
viding sensory information alone may increase distress in highly anxious individuals, so
the information should be accompanied by coping advice. Furthermore, certain reassur-
ance statements, often used by adults, should be used cautiously as they can inadvertently
heighten fear (refer to 2.1.2).
As for the learning objectives, the digital intervention should aim to reduce children’s fear
of the unknown, enhance their sense of control and autonomy, shift their focus away from
pain, and help develop communication skills. Learning objectives should allow children
to understand the PED environment, identify types of medical staff and their roles, and
explain medical tools and procedures in age-appropriate terms. Children should also be
able to recall the sequence of events for the upcoming procedure, develop and practice
coping strategies, and explore potential stress points within the procedure beforehand.
Additionally, the intervention could help enhance children’s communication skills, allowing
them to use age-appropriate terminology, describe their pain or anxiety, and formulate
worries (refer to Section 5.5.1).
How can the digital intervention be designed to transfer knowledge and edu-
cate children in an informative and engaging manner?
Visual aids such as illustrations, diagrams, and animations should be utilized to sim-
plify complex medical concepts and make them more accessible to children. This visual
representation can enhance understanding and engagement (referenced in Section 2.2).
Healthcare professionals (referenced in Section 4.2.4) further reinforce the value of visual
presentations and customizable elements in the preparation process. Incorporating med-
ical play into the digital intervention can create a playful environment where children
can familiarize themselves with medical procedures. This hands-on approach helps reduce
anxiety and provides a practical understanding of the healthcare process.
Additionally, leveraging interactive media, serious games, and extended reality technolo-
gies can significantly enhance children’s medical education. By integrating game elements

increasing children’s willingness to participate and learn about their healthcare experi-

Which specific age group of children should be targeted for the digital inter-
vention?
Children aged 3-7 years old. Referencing Section 4.3.2, the choice of target demographic
was informed by comprehensive research on cognitive abilities’ influence on children’s pre-
treatment anxiety, information processing capacity, and cooperation during treatments
(Sections 2.1.3 and 4.2.2). The data pointed to preschool children, aged between 3 and 7
years, as an especially vulnerable group with a pressing need for specialized preparatory
activities before medical procedures. Preschool-aged children, with their evolving cognitive
and emotional capacities, need support, comprehensible information, a sense of control,
and tools to develop communication and coping skills. However, the existing preparatory
strategies in the PED of Herlev Hospital heavily rely on written and oral explanations.
These conventional methods, although informative, lack suﬀicient visual aids and inter-
active elements necessary for this age group. This deficiency hinders their capacity to
maintain interest and engagement, thereby limiting the effectiveness of such strategies.
Considering these factors, the research identified the need for age-appropriate resources
incorporating interactive and visually engaging elements tailored to the cognitive and emo-
tional developmental stage of the target age group. By focusing on this demographic, the
project aimed to fill the existing gap in procedural preparation strategies, emphasizing
the need for digital interventions that align with the children’s developmental context and
promote understanding and cooperation during medical procedures.
Can a digital intervention that incorporates augmented reality (AR) aid in
delivering information and gaining acceptance from children aged 3-7 years in

Yes, a digital intervention that incorporates augmented reality (AR) can aid in delivering
information and gaining acceptance from children aged 3-7 years in a pediatric emergency
department setting.
Based on the secondary studies (refer to5.4.3), it has been found that Augmented Reality
(AR) offers significant advantages in delivering information and educating children. It
overlays digital information onto the real world, providing contextual learning and easier
application to real-world procedures. AR engages multiple senses, making the learning
experience more meaningful and effective.
It enhances children’s interest, information
retention, and problem-solving abilities. AR is accessible and cost-effective, as it can be
implemented on commonly used devices like smartphones and tablets. It also allows users
to maintain control over their environment, reducing anxiety and enhancing comfort.
In an evaluation with clinical children (refer to Section 6.5.3), the AR prototype suc-

However, some usability issues were noted. These were mainly due to children’s unfa-

on-screen tutorial may be necessary to guide them through this process in the future.
Despite these issues, the children generally enjoyed the intervention, indicating a positive
reception overall.

7.2
Limitations
The following limitations were identified during the study, affecting the process at various
stages:
• Child Participatory Design: Given restricted access to Danish children, it was
challenging to engage them directly as design partners or informants throughout
the design process. Instead, insights about children’s needs were primarily drawn
from healthcare professionals, caregivers, and subjective observations. This approach
could introduce biases and potentially miss some nuances of the target user group’s
perspectives and preferences.
• Cultural Factors: The task of designing a game for Danish children, being an indi-
vidual unfamiliar with Danish culture, introduced challenges in aligning the game’s
context with local cultural norms and the specific preferences of Danish children.
Understanding and adapting to a different user population, with its own unique
cultural characteristics, necessitated careful planning and consideration.
• Qualitative Data Collection: The study encountered diﬀiculties in gathering
adequate qualitative data during the child evaluations. Language barriers precluded
the use of think-aloud techniques, which could have offered deeper insights into
children’s experiences.
Therefore, data collection leaned heavily on observations
and simple closed-ended questionnaires, which might not fully capture the range of
children’s perspectives and reactions.
• Evaluation Limitations: Due to constraints such as a stringent recruitment cri-
terion, limited sample size, and seasonal variations, the study could not perform
a formal summative evaluation.
As such, a definitive understanding of the digi-
tal intervention’s effectiveness remains elusive. The evaluation conducted primarily
centered on gauging the feasibility of the intervention and assessing the attitudes
and perceived opinions of children and caregivers about its effectiveness. Therefore,
although the preliminary results provide significant observations, a more rigorous
approach would be required to establish the true impact of the intervention on
children’s anxiety levels and experiences. Future studies could employ a pre-post
experimental design with a control group with a larger sample size to measure the
direct effects of the intervention.
• Timing of Intervention Delivery: The ideal timing to introduce the intervention
remains unclear. While offering it before a child’s arrival at the PED affords ample
time for familiarization, it might lack specificity to the child’s actual procedures.
In contrast, delivering it during the PED visit, when the upcoming procedures are
known, provides a more targeted approach, but may not allow enough time for
effective engagement. Hence, striking the right balance between suﬀicient exposure
and immediate relevance is an area that warrants further exploration.

Conclusion
This master’s thesis investigated the topic of reducing anxiety in children during their
emergency department visits. The research aimed to develop a digital intervention that
can enhance children’s understanding of medical experiences, promote behavior change,
and potentially reduce anxiety.
The literature review emphasized the importance of age-appropriate information provision
and explored various forms of preparation interventions, highlighting the value of interac-
tive and engaging digital interventions such as serious games and XR technology. These
findings provide opportunities for further research and development.
The primary research findings revealed the limitations of current preparation strategies and
interventions utilized by healthcare professionals and caregivers. Specifically, the study
identified unmet needs among preschool-aged children (3-7 years old) and recognized the
potential of digital interventions, particularly those incorporating augmented reality (AR),
in effectively preparing children for medical procedures and alleviating anxiety.
Based on the identified determinants and learning objectives, the intervention was designed
to improve children’s comprehension of medical experiences and impart coping skills. This
approach, validated by healthcare professionals and grounded in the Serious Game Me-
chanic, is aimed at fostering behavioral changes and reducing anxiety. The intervention
consisted of a serious game featuring animated LEGO characters and augmented reality
(AR) cards, providing interactive and tangible experiences. The implementation phase
involved multiple sprints, with the final sprint evaluating the usability, satisfaction, and
initial clinical findings associated with the digital intervention.
The results demonstrated that children in the pediatric emergency department of Herlev
Hospital found the intervention easy to use, although they exhibited some unfamiliarity
with AR technology. Children generally enjoyed the intervention, with the mobile game
being preferred over the AR game. Active engagement with the mobile game and AR in-
tervention showed higher levels of engagement compared to passive video watching. Both
children and caregivers expressed positive attitudes towards the usefulness of the inter-
vention, with 75% of children expressing a desire to use it in the future and all caregivers
willing to use similar interventions. However, the clinical evaluation had limitations due
to a small sample size and the lack of a rigorous effectiveness study.
While the research and development process significantly contributes to comprehending
preparation interventions for pediatric anxiety and promising indicators of the digital in-
tervention’s potential, several limitations were identified, including challenges in involving
children in the design process, considering cultural factors, gathering qualitative data, and
conducting a formal summative evaluation. Further research is needed to refine the inter-
vention. Future work should focus on improving the intervention’s usability and technical
aspects, conducting a more rigorous evaluation to measure its effectiveness in reducing
anxiety, and expanding the age range to include older children.

B.1
Environment Observation
The environmental observation of the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) reveals a
well-designed and accommodating space for pediatric patients and their families. The spa-
cious and well-lit waiting hall, equipped with comfortable seating and engaging elements
such as fish tanks and a foosball table, aims to provide a relaxing atmosphere. The triage
area, located conveniently near the reception room, offers the space for initial assessments
and discussions, is supported by distraction tools like bubbles and toys, and has some
brochures for parents. The single ward provides necessary medical equipment for patient
care, along with a computer, no written information is provided inside the ward, while
there is a shelf of informational materials in the corridor outside.
Despite the positive aspects of the environment, it is evident that the current approach to
providing preparatory information for children in the PED is inadequate. The written
materials available, such as brochures and leaflets, predominantly target caregivers rather
than directly addressing the informational needs of children. Although distraction tools,
such as toys and entertainment options, are used as strategies to reduce children’s anxiety,
there is a notable absence of comprehensive and age-appropriate preparatory information
tailored specifically for pediatric patients.

B.3
Shadowing of nurses
The shadowing period, lasting approximately five hours, focused on observing three specific
nurses in various settings including the nurses’ oﬀices, single wards, and examination
rooms. The key objectives of this observational period were to:
• Understand the nurses’ roles, duties, and workflow within the PED.
• Observe strategies employed by nurses to reduce children’s anxiety before and during
procedures.
• Observe the use of preparation strategies for children, which included the timing and
delivery of preparatory information.
• Observe interactions among children, nurses, and caregivers.
Records were documented using written notes, and relevant information was captured
through the use of a mobile camera with the mutual consent of all parties involved. The
interactions and care provided by three nurses were observed and recorded, involving
three children: a nine-year-old girl, a six-year-old boy, and an eleven-year-old girl. Due to
the predominantly Danish language used in communication between nurses, children, and
parents, the analysis relied on observing behaviors, expressions, and subsequent interviews
conducted with both nurses and parents. These sources of raw data formed the foundation
for further analysis and interpretation.

B.3.4
Outcome
1. Nurses’ roles, responsibilities, and workflow within the PED:
In the PED, nurses have specific roles, responsibilities, and workflows. Their shifts typi-
cally last 8 hours each day. The charge nurse in the PED has the responsibility of super-
vising the entire department and providing guidance to other nurses. Several experienced
nurses are assigned to triage, where they organize and prioritize multiple cases, supervise
nurses during their shifts, and provide guidance or direct treatment to patients when nec-
essary. The rest of the nurses carry out various interventions for the patients they are
assigned to. These interventions may include administering medications, monitoring vital
signs, providing treatments, and performing procedures such as IV insertions, blood tests,
and wound dressings. Additionally, nurses in PED need to support the emotional and
psychological needs of children and their families during their hospitalization. They rely
on computer systems to maintain comprehensive and up-to-date records, which facilitate
the delivery of optimal care. Every weekday at 4 pm, nurses in the PED communicate
with the healthcare team, including physicians and other nurses, to discuss the progress
of the children, address any concerns, and make necessary adjustments to the care plan.
This collaboration ensures that the care provided to each child is continuously evaluated
and updated as needed.
2. Strategies employed by the nurses in reducing children’s anxiety before and
during medical procedures:

In order to reduce children’s anxiety before and during medical procedures, nurses employ
several strategies in the pediatric setting:
• Clear Explanations: Nurses provide clear explanations about the procedure to
children, including its purpose, duration, and any sensations they might experience.
This helps children understand what will happen, reducing their fear of the unknown.
Additionally, nurses may explain the application of numbing cream or other pain
management techniques to alleviate discomfort during the procedure.
• Distraction Techniques: Nurses utilize various distraction techniques to divert
children’s attention and alleviate anxiety during procedures. This may involve pro-
viding snacks, toys, or engaging in conversation with the child.
By focusing on
something other than the procedure, children can feel more relaxed and less anx-
ious.
• Clown Services: Some hospitals have clown services or trained volunteers who
specialize in entertaining and comforting children during medical procedures. The
presence of these individuals can help reduce anxiety and create a more positive and
relaxed environment for the child.
• Play Therapy:
Nurses may incorporate play therapy techniques to help chil-
dren cope with anxiety before and during procedures. This can involve using age-
appropriate toys, games, or role-playing to familiarize the child with medical equip-
ment or simulate the procedure in a less intimidating way.
• Emotional Support: Nurses provide emotional support by being present, empa-
thetic, and responsive to the child’s needs. They offer reassurance, comfort, and
encouragement, helping the child feel safe and supported throughout the procedure.
3. Implementation of preparation strategies for children:
In the implementation of preparation strategies for children in the pediatric setting, the
following approaches are observed:
• Oral Explanation: Nurses primarily rely on oral explanations as a technique for
providing information about the procedure and preparing children and parents. They
verbally explain the procedure, its purpose, and what to expect during and after the
medical intervention.
• Timing and Delivery: The timing and delivery of preparation information may
vary among nurses.
Some nurses inform children and parents shortly before the
procedure, while others seek parental preference before sharing information. This
flexibility ensures that the information is provided at a suitable time based on indi-
vidual circumstances.
• Age-Appropriate Strategies: The preparation strategy employed may vary depending
on the child’s age. Older children, typically of school age, can better reason and
directly express their concerns. They demonstrate a better understanding with the
explanation provided. On the other hand, younger children pose more challenges due
to their limited cognitive abilities and shorter attention spans. Nurses may adjust
their approach accordingly to effectively communicate with younger children.
• Honesty and Addressing Concerns: Nurses emphasize the importance of being
honest with children about the procedure. They address any concerns or questions
the child may have, providing reassurance and accurate information to alleviate
anxiety and build trust.

C.1.1
Interview with Gitte
Gitte Maria Flyger Würtz, is a researcher and clinical nursing specialist at the Department
of Children and Adolescents of Herlev og Gentofte Hospital. She has continually shared
valuable insights and professional knowledge during numerous interview sessions.
The
following is the documentation of all conducted interviews:
What is the process that children and their caregivers go through when they
arrive at Herlev PED?
The first step is for parents to either call acute medical 113 or their doctor for an appoint-
ment. Once they arrive at the PED, they need to check in at the reception desk. The
waiting time in the common area is typically around 10 minutes. From there, we
take them to the triage area where one of our nurses will conduct various assessments using
different devices. We measure their pulse, heart rate, respiration, temperature, and
blood oxygen levels, and also access their pain levels. Based on this assessment,
the nurse determines whether the child should receive a brief examination by a doctor and
then be discharged, wait in the general waiting room, be admitted for further evaluation,
or wait in the larger waiting area. The wait time in the waiting area can vary, but it
can take up to 3 hours. Some additional procedures that may be mentioned during the
assessment include blood tests, urine tests, and abdominal palpation.
What are the symptoms that you commonly see in children at Herlev PED?
We frequently encounter children who are experiencing symptoms such as fever, respi-
ratory issues, stomach problems, and broken limbs. Now, it’s important to note
that children aged 0 to 2 years are directed to the specialized Child and Youth Reception.
On the other hand, children between the ages of 2 and 12 are referred to the dedicated
Emergency Department. We ensure that each child receives the appropriate care based
on their age and specific medical needs.
Can you provide an overview of the medical procedures that children typically
undergo at your hospital?
Children may undergo various medical procedures depending on their condition. Some
common procedures include blood tests applied on the arm or finger, urine tests, x-ray
and ultrasound examinations, although these are typically conducted in a separate de-
partment, and evaluations by surgeons to assess abdominal conditions such as appendicitis
or other gastrointestinal issues. We also perform essential measurements, such as taking
the child’s temperature using a thermometer placed in the ear, assessing their pulse, mea-
suring blood pressure, checking oxygen saturation levels, and occasionally administering
medication rectally. In certain cases, children present with severe stomach pain due to
obstipation, and we may need to provide them with a rectal laxative.
Are there any patterns or trends you have observed in the number of children
visiting the PED?
On weekdays, our busiest time is usually after 4 PM, as many children are out of school
and their parents are available to bring them in for medical attention.
On weekends,
we tend to see a higher number of children, and they often arrive earlier, around 1 PM.
Additionally, during vacation periods, we also experience an increase in the number of
Enhancing user experience for children in emergency departments: A game-based preparation
intervention aimed at reducing anxiety
83
==PAGE:91==
children coming to the PED. These patterns help us anticipate and manage the patient
flow more effectively.
How do you assist children and parents in managing their anxiety during the
waiting period?
We have several strategies in place to help alleviate anxiety in children and parents during
their wait.
We offer various entertainment options to keep children engaged, such
as TV programs on tablets and a selection of toys. We have knitted toys that children
can squeeze, which can provide a sense of comfort and reduce anxiety. Additionally, we
engage in conversations with them, discussing topics of interest to make them feel
more at ease. We also have clowns available who can provide some distraction. In certain
cases, we may utilize pharmacological interventions like laughing gas, oral sedation,
and numbing cream to help alleviate anxiety when appropriate. Furthermore, effective
communication is crucial in our approach. We have developed a set of recommended
language guidelines for both staff and parents when interacting with children.
Is oral sedation safe for children?
Yes, it is generally safe for children. It is a commonly used method to help reduce their
anxiety. However, it’s important to note that oral sedation takes a longer time for children
to digest, so we usually ask them to stay longer in the wards until they are fully recovered.
We would choose to use distraction methods first, and let children connect with something
else.
Varying characteristics of children.
Children’s characteristics can vary greatly, and some children may be very insistent
on taking control of their own bodies.
In such cases, distraction techniques and
pharmacological methods may not be effective. I remember a particular experience with
a little girl who was in a high state of anxiety. Despite our attempts to distract her, she
couldn’t be calmed down. We decided to try using laughing gas, but she strongly resisted
and refused to inhale it because she wanted to maintain full control over her body. At
that point, we had to stop and explore other methods to help her feel more at ease. It
just goes to show that every child is unique, and we need to be adaptable and find the
approach that works best for them.
How do nurses prepare children before conducting the procedures?
We employ a combination of oral explanations provided by nurses and written
descriptions given to parents directly by the nurses. However, we understand that
it can be overwhelming for children to comprehend and remember all the procedures at
once. To address this, we take a step-by-step approach and only inform them about the
immediate procedure or the ”next thing” that will occur. Additionally, we emphasize the
