Abstract
This thesis continues the ongoing research of biopolymer concrete and multi­axial 3D
printing at DTU. As the ongoing research was mainly focused on the material properties,
this thesis focuses on the structural design possibilities through the finite element analysis
of the structures using previously researched material properties.
The first part of the thesis describes a review of the current state of the art of biopolymer
concrete and 3D constructional printing. It includes a description of material properties
and printing setup. The material properties missing to make a sufficient numerical model
are listed and, in the final part, found through destructive testing.
The second part evaluates and describes the structural design possibilities to find the
most suitable structure for printing try­out. It includes a description of topology­optimised
structures and a description of the existing 3D­printed structures. Finally, the dome is
chosen as an optimal structure mainly because of its good compression properties and
ability to showcase angled printing.
Next, the numerical model for finite element analysis is made in Grasshopper with the
help of two different finite element analysis software. The numerical model build starts
with describing and defining finite elements and boundary conditions, as well as choosing
the optimal software tool for the problem. The analysis is first performed for the small
wall specimen and later for the dome. Material properties are corrected based on the test
results, with which the finite element analysis results are compared and evaluated.
In parallel with the making of the numerical model, the manufacturing of the small wall
specimens and dome took place. The small wall specimens are tested on the 3­point
bending test.
In the end, after the complications with multi­axial robots, the dome is
printed. It is, as initially planned, printed using the multi­axial to showcase the material
ability of angled printing. The observation of the whole process is extensively described.
Finally, the findings of the thesis are concluded in the last chapter, including the description
of the enriched table of material properties. Further possible areas of development are
discussed.

The thesis idea started with a visit to the Designmuseum Denmark, where the 3D printed
structures made from biopolymer concrete were exhibited. After contacting Professor
Holger Koss, he was keen to introduce me to the project and give me an opportunity to
help further develop sustainable concrete solutions for the future. For this reason, I would
like to deeply thank Holger Koss for always standing by my side, giving me insightful
thoughts and ideas, and guiding me in the right direction. Furthermore, special thanks
are given to Julian Christ and his resourceful PhD Thesis research and help with his
knowledge about the material and KUKA robot, even though the latter at the end didn’t
work.
Additionally, I would like to thank the people of the DTU Byg laboratory, especially Ian
Rasmussen, Troels V. Kristensen and Christian P. Rasmussen, for always helping me
solve all the small issues while testing and making the printing process work. After all,
thanks to PhD student Marco Brander for taking his time to help and make the objective

the project better together. With you, the process was more fun.
Lastly, I would like to thank my family for always supporting and encouraging me through­
out my study in Denmark. Special thanks to my co­workers for their help and patience
while working remotely these two years.

Introduction
1.1
Research Motivation
A great effort is put towards making all aspects of our lives more sustainable and envi­
ronmentally friendly, which follows the mission of 17 sustainability goals presented by the
United Nations [1]. In the construction sector, the short­term goal is to reach net­zero car­
bon emissions by 2030. To reach it, it was estimated that carbon emissions need to lower
by 6 % yearly or 50 % by the year 2030 [2]. Furthermore, the long­term sustainability
goal is placed for zero carbon footprint of construction projects and business operations
by 2050 [3].
In 2022, the built environment represented 40 % of gross worldwide annual carbon emis­
sions, 27% of which are contributed from building operations and an additional 13% from
materials and construction [4], [5]. In new building construction, embodied carbon repre­
sents 57% of total carbon emissions, while the other 47 % are operational carbon emis­
sions [4]. Additionally, 23% of total global CO2 emissions represent just the 3 most widely
used construction materials, concrete, steel, and aluminium. Concrete is the most signif­
icant contributor out of the three with 11% [4]. Furthermore, as reported by Architecture
2023, the global floor area of the built environment is expected to double by 2060 [4], so
it is important to lower carbon emissions as soon as possible.
To reduce the environmental impact of new constructions and the amount of embodied
carbon, the following measurements can be taken: reuse and reduce. To lower carbon
emissions, this study focuses on the reduction, as it aims to reduce the use of concrete,
especially cement while reducing the need for labour and framework with the use of 3D
printing technology. To reduce cement use in concrete, broad research was already per­
formed on replacing it with more environmentally friendly alternatives. Where the most
known are silica fume and fly ash. Later studies came to the point of using biologically
based binding materials.
The research in a PhD project conducted at DTU described and tested possible binders in
more detail, where bone glue (BG) from mammal gelatine (MG) was described as a most
perspective binder for use in 3D printing [6]. In addition to replacing cement, less concrete
can be used with precise additive material placement with 3D printing technology [7]. This
can be achieved with topology optimization of structures, which will be described in more
detail in Section 3.
Furthermore, the 3D construction printing (3DCP) process contributes an additional part to
lowering carbon emissions, as it doesn’t require a framework and less labour force. Both
contribute to saving up to 50 % of the cost compared to cast­in concrete construction. [7],
[8].
The ongoing research tackles all parts of the above­mentioned possibilities, where most
research has been done on material rheology. For this reason, this thesis focuses on
structural possibilities using concrete with BG as a binder and multi­axial 3D printer, solv­
ing problems by using less and more sustainable material while avoiding the use frame­
work. Figure 1.1 presents such a multi­axial printer.

Project Objectives
Project objectives follow the recent research at DTU on biopolymer concrete and multi­
axial robot, which aims to replace cement in concrete for 3DCP. As the material has
already been researched in previous years, it is described in Section 2. The main focus
of this thesis is thus to use those findings and research BPC from the structural point of
view.
The main objective of this study is to explore how well a 3DCP structure or structural ele­
ment using BPC can be analysed analytically and numerically. There are several particu­
larities in printed structures, such as the layer orientation and interlayer bonding strength,
that possibly imply a difference in the structural analysis compared to casted composites.
The capabilities of standard finite element (FE) models with respect to their applicability
to printed structures will be investigated.

research provides some of the material properties required for structural analysis. Miss­

2.1
Biopolymer Concrete
Biopolymer concrete follows recent worldwide research push towards finding sustainable
solutions in the construction sector. The topic of partly or fully replacing cementitious
materials in construction is highly important as cement production is one of the biggest
carbon emitters in the world. In 2019, it was estimated that in producing 1 ton of cement,
1 ton of CO2 is emitted [3]. Considering the annual worldwide production of cement, this
accounts for 3 to 4 gigatonnes of carbon emissions [3]. Another point worth mentioning
is that construction is not expected to slow down in the coming years but to double by
2060 [3]. These are some of the many reasons why innovation and improvement have
to occur in the construction sector to lower CO2 emissions. In recent years, studies have
researched the possibilities of using bio­based or recycled materials to replace cement.
Implementing bio­based materials first started with using natural fibres to reinforce ce­
mentitious materials [9]. Bio­based materials are defined as biodegradable materials and
don’t necessarily need to be of natural origin [15].
Clays are the first bio­based materials that were researched and could be used in 3D
printing. One of the examples is a project called TECLA, aiming to make 3D­printed
structures from raw earth materials, in this case, clay. An important point in raw earth ma­
terial 3D printing is that it makes construction nearly zero­emission [16]. Examples of such
structures are presented in Figure 2.9. The research at DTU took a step forward, where
the potential of 6 natural polymers for use in 3D printing construction was researched
[6]. That includes κ­carrageenan, sodium alginate, agar, chitosan, mammal gelatine and
cold water fish gelatine. Six polymers were evaluated and tested regarding their material
and rheological properties, where 4 of them weren’t showing promising results and were
discarded from further research. Where mammal gelatine (MG) or bone glue (BG) was
found as the most suitable. Besides showing similar material properties to cement­based
concrete, bio­based materials are less energy intensive than cement.[6]
Additionally, mammal gelatine, known as the world’s oldest known adhesive, is produced
from structural protein collagen, specifically from animal bones through Gelatinization,
making it widely available and inexpensive [17] [7]. It provides an option to be produced
on the spot, in remote environments, possibly a solution that could be developed and
used for space exploration. On the other hand, initial research has shown that bio­based
materials are sensitive to environmental conditions [10], where more research must be
done on their fire resistance and waterproofing. Research on pores in BPC has shown a
positive direction in good water resistance [11].

Materials
Biopolymer concrete is a mixture of mineral aggregates, biobased binder and water. It
has a visual appearance of mortar because of the fine sand aggregates. Nonetheless,
initial research showed similar material properties to cementitious concrete mixtures for
3D printing, which makes it a potentially suitable replacement [6].
Silica sand aggregates nr.13 with a maximum grain size of 0.18 mm were supplied from
Dansand A/S [18]. However, in the first mixes performed through this research, a mixture
with a maximum sand grain of 0.25 mm didn’t give the right consistency of the BPC. The
following is because not enough importance was given to the grain size, while it is crucial
as sand with a smaller maximum grain size has a bigger surface that can absorb water,
making the final mixture less liquid.
Bio­based hydrogel binder is a mixture of MG and water. The MG was supplied from
DICTUM (Art. Nr.: 450141) in a granular form [6]. The granulates size is 2­7 mm and has
a density of 1.3 g/cm3 [19]. Figure 2.1 shows bone glue granulates.
Figure 2.1: Bone glue granulate
BG granulates soaked in water create hydrogel, mixed with sand and work as a binder,
making biopolymer concrete. In addition, the dissolving process of BG in water can be
influenced by temperature, where it is estimated that better dissolving can be achieved
with a temperature higher than 20/30 °C [9].

Material preparation process
The optimal material preparation process follows the method described by [18]. The opti­
mal procedure was followed multiple times through the beginning phase of this study until
the final procedure was established. Initial experiments gave unsatisfactory mixes re­
garding buildability, as most were too liquid. At last, besides figuring out the right system,
it was discovered that the sand grain size is critical in making this composite material.
To begin with, the right mixtures were first calculated following the described nomenclature
above. Then, mammal gelatine granulate was soaked in water for 30 minutes and mixed
under the lowest speed and without heating in the Kenwood KCC9060S cooking chef
machine, which enables simultaneous stirring and heating. At the same time, sand was
put in the oven and preheated to 50 °C. After 30 minutes, the hydrogel was kept mixing for
additional 10 minutes and heated to 65 °C. After the elapsed time, sand was added to the
hydrogel, mixing speed and temperature stayed unchanged and kept mixing for 20 more
minutes. Finally, the process was paused at the midpoint of the last phase, and the bottom
of the mixer was scrubbed with a spatula to ensure all the material was homogeneously
mixed. The mixing process inside the cooking mixer is presented in Figure 2.2. After
mixing, the spatula test was used as the initial consistency test of the mixture. The test
showed if the mix was too liquid or too dense [7]. For the approximate assessment o the
consistency, the mixture should stay on the spatula for a few seconds until dropping off.

Complex modulus
Complex modulus (G*) presents the modulus of elasticity of viscoelastic (VE) fluids and
VE solids. Viscoelastic material behaviour shows a time­dependent response to applied
stresses [20] [13]. It is described as a vector sum of G’, properties of the elastic part of
VE material or storage modulus, and G” or loss modulus, properties of viscous part. [17]
[7] [13]. For example, if G’> G”, the material will have properties more on the side of the
solid [13]. The complex modulus of the BPC was determined through oscillatory shear
tests [17]. Equation 2.2 presents the derivation of G’ and G” from G* regarding the loss
factor [21].
G′ = G∗cos(δ)
G” = G∗sin(δ)
(2.2)
In tests performed by [17], complex modulus increased over time, showing the thixotropic
behaviour of the composite material. Thixotropic behaviour means that the viscosity of
the material decreases with time and increases shear strain [22].
The complex modulus of the mixture MG80B40, closest to the final one chosen in the
previous chapter, is between 25 kPa and 200 kPa [17]. Complex modulus decreases
with the increase of shear strain. At the same time, loss factor tan(δ) increased from 1
to 3. A loss factor greater than 1 defines a solid, while a smaller than 1 defines s liquid.
Tests were performed at 3 Hz frequency and 50°C. Additional measurements were taken
taking time into account, where the temperature was decreased from 50 to 20 °C. At that
temperature, G* was 0.5 GPa, a large increase from the previous measurement and loss
factor of around 0.15. [17]
Regarding storage and loss modulus, complex modulus can be calculated following Equa­
tion 2.3 below. From the equation E’ or G’, the real part of the complex modulus can be
derived using Equation 2.3 [21].
E∗= G∗= G′ + iG” = |G∗| cos(δ) + i |G∗| sin(δ)
(2.3)
In this case, E’ is 0.494 GPa. Furthermore, elastic modulus can now be calculated follow­

Rheological Properties
Rheology generally describes how the material reacts to applied stresses [25]. Factors af­
fecting the rheology of usual cementitious concrete are particle packing, aggregate shape
and grading, water/cement ratio, mineral admixtures, and chemical additives [7]. Some
are also relative for composite mixtures, especially particle packing, aggregate shape,
grading, and water/binder ratio. Three main properties should be evaluated and described
as rheological properties of 3D­printed concrete: extrudability, buildability and printability.
Extrudability describes the material’s ability to extrude through the nozzle and is closely
Optimised Design of Structural Components for multi­axial 3D Printing
11
==PAGE:24==
correlated with workability [26]. For good printing, the extrusion has to be consistent [7].
However, for good extrudability, the maximum size of the aggregate’s grains is recom­
mended to be smaller than 1/10 of the diameter of the extruder nozzle [27]. Viscosity is
also closely related to extrudability, meaning that material should have too high or too low
viscosity to be extrudable.
Buildability is the ability of the material to print layers on top of each other without exces­
sive deformations of the below layers [28] [7]. In research from [7] and [17], buildability
was measured when printing a simple wall of 4 layers. The height of the layers was set
to 10 mm, making an ideal total of 40 mm. Buildability was then evaluated based on the
height of the printed wall. It was defined to be 5 mm below or above the ideal height.
Staying within this boundary means that the material is buildable. This aspect was con­
sidered in the later stages of this thesis, as a simple wall was printed in a similar form
[7].
An essential aspect of buildability is layer time or open time, which is the time that allows
the material to develop sufficient yield strength so that the layer below the printing layer
doesn’t settle too much [7]. For example, a layer time of 90 s was optimal for BPC printing
[17]. Printability is defined based on the before mentioned parameters, workability, ex­
trudability and buildability [7]. As already mentioned in previous chapters, in making the
final mix for this thesis, problems occurred with the printability of the mixture, which was
eventually solved with the use of finer aggregates. Therefore, the table below is presented

2.2.1
Existing Technologies
Regarding 3D printing, there exist many definitions and categorisations of it, such as
additive manufacturing, digital fabrication, or rapid prototyping [33]. Digital fabrication with
concrete (DFC) or digital concrete gives possibilities for improved shapes of structures,
shapes that follow forces, also known as topology optimisation. An area of DFC used
throughout this thesis is additive manufacturing (AM), where structures are printed on
an additive layer­per­layer principle. [7] Additionally, AM can be categorised into several
subcategories. One of them is following The American Society for Testing and Materials
(ASTM) [35] and [7], which divides AM into 7 categories:
• Material extrusion: It is described as an additive manufacturing process (AMP) in
which material is, in layers, extruded through the nozzle [36]. It is most widely used
and is most known for plastic material fused deposition modelling (FDM), which has,
in recent years, advanced to concrete use. [7]
• Binder jetting: It is described as AMP in which liquid binders are deposited to join
powder materials [36].
• Powder jetting: It is described as AMP in which a heat source, usually a laser, fuses
layers of powder bed [36].
• Direct energy deposition: It is similar to powder jetting, which uses a heat source to
heat the powder. In this, the difference is that the powder is melted simultaneously
as it is deposited[36].
• Material jetting: It is similar to a material extrusion process, with the difference that
in material jetting, droplets are deposited layer­by­layer and solidified under UV light
[36] [7].
• Photopolymerisation: It is described as AMP in which a liquid photopolymer is pre­
cisely solidified using UV light [7].
• Sheet lamination: It stacks thin material layers, usually metal, together, cut in the
right shape and thermally bonded together after positioning [36] [7].
As mentioned before, this thesis focuses on material extrusion, described in more detail
in the following section.

2.2.3
Advantages and Constraints
3D printing of concrete has multiple advantages over traditional in­situ concrete construc­
tion, which were already briefly mentioned. One of them is reduced cost and efficient con­
struction. More efficient construction with the implementation of digital fabrication means
less energy spent and less material used [33][7]. Furthermore, as the 3D printed model
is self­standing, there is no need for formwork, which is a major contributor to the overall
construction cost. Based on the research from [41], formwork labour and material cost can
take up to 66 % of the whole typical concrete construction. This way, having no formwork
can reduce total costs significantly.
On the other hand, the shape of the 3D printed structure without the framework is fully
defined by the rheological properties of the concrete. The material requires sufficient yield
strength throughout the printing process to retain its shape without major deformations.
Increasing cement content or using large amounts of additives is required, making the
concrete less environmentally friendly. Nevertheless, fabrication of overhangs or open­
ings is still impossible with normal concrete, and printing has been only achieved to the
maximum angle of 45°, which leads to architectural limitations [42][33][7].
To achieve the construction of more significant overhangs, the return of framework support
is imminent, and layers would have to be offset or cantilevered, compromising surface
connection [43][44][12]. In Figure 2.12, 3 approaches to slicing, consequently printing,
are presented. On the left figure are illustrated horizontal printing and the staircase effect.
The middle figure presents printing with an increasing angle, which is the case in BPC.
Once the maximum angle is reached, a technique of constant angle, presented in the right
figure, can be used.

Extruder setup and improvements
For the extruder, an automated caulking gun DEWALT DCE581E with a 600ml tube ca­
pacity was used [18]. The aluminium tube cartridge is 37 cm long and has a diameter
of 5.5 cm [18]. A caulking gun should push material out at a 40 mm/min speed, which
varied during our testing because of the friction between the tube and the gun head. This
is one thing that had to be considered when testing the printing process, as the extrusion
wasn’t continuous all the time. A plastic 13 mm round nozzle was mounted at the end of
the tube. Round nozzle prints round layer, while if the nozzle were square, it would have
printed more straight­walled layers, making for better surface adhesion.
As mentioned in the material preparation description, the material must be heated to have
suitable rheological properties and be printable. For this reason, the extruder tube is
wrapped in a heating coil with a spacing of 1.5 cm and covered with insulating foil and
foam [18]. Between the coil, a temperature sensor was also installed and connected to
the temperature switch, from which the heating coil was operated. This was done to keep
the temperature in the tube stable and at the desired value. The temperature required for
the heating must be around 60 °C.
Furthermore, an external switch is used to remotely control the on/off function of the caulk­
ing gun, which was most beneficial for multi­axial robot, as everything has to be done
remotely [18]. For moving the extruder gun horizontally, it is installed on the steel frame
on the horizontally movable wooden sled with a whole to put the extruder tube through
[7]. The height is adjustable using the lab holder and retort stand. Figure 2.15 presents
the old version of the extruder setup and sled with the new improvements.
After some tests with the extruder gun and biopolymer concrete at the beginning of this
research, it has been noticed that the setup requires some improvements. The main prob­
lem was the inaccurate temperature reading and unstable extruder mounting. First was
required as BPC should be printed at the exact temperature to ensure the right rheolog­
ical properties. The second problem occurred when adjusting the height of the extruder
after a layer was printed, as the next position would be a bit off­centre. Consequently, the
following layer would be eccentric as well.

inner extruder tube (IET), was found to fit in the outer tube, named the outer extruder tube

cartridge can be filled with plastic bags in advance and changed in the extruder. The OET

Arduino board, which was beforehand coded to read and display the input temperatures.
This was mainly done for more accurate temperature readings than the previous setup.

In previous research, a KUKA multi­axial robot was used for more complex prints, which

3.1
Topology optimisation
One of the structural possibilities following the advancement of 3DCP are topology­optimised
structures. The main benefit of topology optimisation is the reduction of material consump­
tion by using computer algorithms. The topology optimisation algorithm is a computer al­
gorithm that takes the structural model and its loading and optimises the structure through
an iterative process. Such structure follows the flow of the forces, meaning more material
is placed where higher forces occur. Force flow in structures usually occurs in complex
shapes, meaning that the final design of the optimised structure is also complex, making
it difficult to manufacture. Constructing it with conventional cast­in concrete would require
an extensive framework, increasing the construction cost and time. [9]
Through the advancement of 3DCP and the processing power of computers, topology
optimisation has gained more popularity [53][54] [9]. 3DCP enables the manufacture of
complex designs without or with significantly less framework, making the whole topology
optimisation more feasible and justifiable.
There are already many known examples of topology­optimised 3D printed concrete struc­
tures, which cannot all be mentioned and included in this research. However, some of the
most picturesque and interesting are presented. The topology­optimised structures de­
scribed below are divided into two categories, the first being 3D printed topology­optimised
structures and the second being cast concrete structures inside the 3D printed framework.
The latter are irrelevant to this research but are still worth mentioning because of their
possibilities. The focus is on unreinforced structures, mainly working in compression.
All fundamental structural elements regarding printed topology­optimised structures have
been researched to some extent in the past. Figure 3.1 presents an example of the
topology­optimised beam. On the left figure, half of the cantilever beam with a down­
ward load at the free end is presented. In the right figure can be seen how the forces
are transferred through the material that is left after topology optimisation, where blue
presents tension and red compression stresses.

Numerical model
One of the central thesis objectives is to make a numerical model (NM) for finite ele­
ment analysis (FEA) that reflects the test results of the printed structures described in the
previous chapter. It should be a reliable first step in predicting the material and struc­
tural response under loading and a base for further research and making more complex
structures. Calculating 3D printed structures may require a special numerical model to
reflect the layered composition of the structural element. The numerical model derived
through this research would need to be improved as the material research and printing
progresses. The approaches to making a numerical model follow current recommenda­
tions in the literature, which are all described in this chapter. It is therefore divided into 5
subsections. In Section 4.1, FEA is described to understand better the process of FEA
and modelling in software for structural analysis. There are many possibilities regarding
which structural analysis software to use. They are evaluated in Section 4.2, where the
most suitable for the FEA of BPC structures is chosen. As the modelling process itself
is still largely independent of the choice of the software, the boundary conditions of mod­
elled structures are described in Section 4.3. Finally, the structural analysis procedures
of structures, the simple wall and the dome, are described in Sections 4.4.1 and 4.4.2,
including the evaluation of the results in Section 4.4.3.
4.1
Finite element analysis
With the evolution of computers and software, all complex structural analyses are nowa­
days performed using FE software. There is a wide variety of tools to choose from, but
regardless of the choice, the modelling process and the FEM are independent of the
choice of the tool.
The finite element method (FEM) is a numerical method for finding a solution from a sys­
tem of equations expressed in partial differential equations [64]. Through FEM, a complex
structure is divided into smaller elements called finite elements, which are connected in
nodes. The partial differential equations calculate displacements and forces in all nodes.
The accuracy of the FEA solution is closely related to the number of finite elements or the
number of interpolation functions. An additional influence on the accuracy of the solution
is the choice of the type and shape of the finite elements, described below. [65]
A simplified FEM process starts by defining a structural model of an initially stated problem
structure. The structural model is then divided into finite elements. As the complex model
is divided into smaller elements, the structure is approximated. The approximation is,
therefore, the main emphasis of structural analysis. Finally, the division of the model is
performed into the mesh, which is thus described in more detail. [64]
Meshes should be finer or denser around corners, cut­outs, supports and loads. Those
are the points where higher stresses are expected, as the equation for stress is a force
on area, meaning a smaller area gives greater stresses. Figure 4.1 compares the mesh
density, where the finer mesh gives more accurate stress gradients. [64]

Another aspect of meshing that shouldn’t be neglected is that all mesh endpoints should
be connected. This means that one shouldn’t just stop in the middle, except the external
line of points. Mesh requires boundary conditions such as limited movement of nodes in
order to support the mesh to prevent the moving as a rigid body. Usually, FEA software
automatically warns about the problem of the rigid body.
Regarding loads, finite element mesh can only understand and transfer the external loads
through nodes of the finite elements. For this reason, the external load must be covered
to an equivalent point load in the nodes. For example, line load should be converted to
equal point load over a specific line. It is an approximation where the denser mesh gives
more accurate results.

4.2
Software tools
The software tools were evaluated based on multiple criteria. The first one was the sim­
plicity of use and user­friendly interface. The second criterion was the ability of parametric
design as 3D printed structures allow for more design freedom. The final criterion is the
software option for model improvement and the addition of more features as the research
evolves. This section evaluates different software, while the research on the layering and
its influence on the numerical model is described in Sectios 4.3.2 and 4.3.3.
The conventional method for modelling follows the graphic (manual) definition of geomet­
ric and structural elements. This means that for any change in the design, all intercon­
nected geometric elements need to be changed, which can be time­consuming, especially
when finding the correct geometry or modelling complex, parametric structures. On the
other hand, parametric modelling generates a model based on the input parameters that
determine the geometry and all other parameters of the model. These parameters work
as sliders that can be manipulated to change the properties of the model. The process
allows workflow and repetitive tasks optimisation, faster change of the design shape op­
timisation, and handling complex geometries. [66]
Additionally, as the 3D printed structures allow for more design freedom and paramet­
ric design, software that enables the integration of parametric modelling is compulsory.
Each element is defined by essential variables or parameters connected in clusters, which
determine the final model. These models can easily be updated based on the parame­
ter values, allowing for seamless iterations. This design process in engineering is also
referred to as Value Engineering. [66]
Complex structures that are difficult to model with conventional tools are now possible
using parametric tools. The most popular and widely used one is Rhinoceros with a
Grasshopper (GH) plugin. The latter works in Rhinoceros as a plugin, where the para­
metrical coding, also called visual coding, is performed, followed by the graphical pre­
sentation in Rhinoceros. An additional benefit is the possibility of multiple plugins inside
the GH environment, which allows for more advanced mathematical algorithms, structural
analysis, or the export of the model to other FEM software. Therefore, the following steps
of the evaluations follow the implementation capabilities of FEA tools with GH. Figure 4.3

Material Properties and Boundary conditions
Determination of material properties and boundary conditions is the first step to making an
accurate numerical model. Therefore, they are defined in this section. The findings are
generally valid for both structures modelled in this thesis, as well as for other structures
with the same principle of construction and material used. They are based on the research
of structural analysis of 3D printed concrete structures and glulam wood structures, as the
structures work similarly to BPC 3D printed structures. As shown later, these are valid no
matter the FEA software used. Deviations from the below­mentioned will be described
in the following sections, where modelling procedures in Karamba3D and Sofistik are
described.

4.3.2
Interface
The 3D­printed BPC structure is made of layers, so interface behaviour is important in
making an accurate numerical model. The question is whether the structure under loading
breaks at the interface or inside the layer and whether the layering has a substantial
influence.
As mentioned in Section 2.1.2, when describing tensile bonding strength, approximately
40% of the 7 days old tested specimens under tensile force broke at the layer, while
60% broke at the interface. The number was 30% and 70% for 28­day­old specimens.
Furthermore, as the area at the interface is smaller, stresses there are more significant,
which means that the percentage of failures having one homogeneous specimen would
be expected to be higher there. Therefore, a 60/40% ratio can be assumed to be 50/50
%, while the ratio of 28­day­old specimens could be slightly higher. This means that the
layer interface plays a role in the structure’s behaviour but can be assumed to 50/50% for
initial simplified structural analysis.
Additionally, modelling the layers themselves and assigning the material properties to the
interface joint may not make much sense. This is because making an interface joint in a
numerical model requires a complex numerical model and additional material properties,
in this case, transitional and rotational spring stiffness, which wasn’t researched previ­
ously. Transitional spring stiffness, in simple, can be derived by pulling two joined layers
apart longitudinally with prescribed displacement. At the same time the latter is applied,
force is measured. The relation between the force and displacement would give transi­
tional spring stiffness. Similarly, rotational spring stiffness can be derived, with the only
difference being that rotational displacement is prescribed instead of tension force.

4.3.4
Supports
As mentioned in Section 4.1, the FEA model understands loads and supports only through
nodes of the finite element mesh. For this reason, supports in both structures modelled
in this study are assigned only in points.
The simple wall is assigned to the final nodes, as the structural system is a simply sup­
ported beam. Each support has 6 degrees of freedom, which include 3 translations (UX,
UY, UZ) and 3 rotations (RX, RY, RZ). The first support only allows rotations RY around
the y­axis, while the second allows translation in UX, x­direction and rotations RY.
On the other hand, supports are assigned to all points at the bottom boundary line to
support a dome. Like the wall, supports of the dome include 3 translations (UX, UY, UZ)
and 3 rotations (RX, RY, RZ). They act as fixed support, where all degrees of freedom are
prevented. A more detailed explanation follows in the section describing the modelling
itself.
4.3.5
Loads
Both structures have two loads applied inside the numerical model. First is gravity, which
is based on the material’s specific weight. The second is external load, which is similar to
the supports applied only in points. In the case of the simple wall, it is applied only at the
top midpoint, as that is where the force is applied in the 3­point bending test. In the case
of the dome, the load is applied at all points of the top boundary of the dome, acting as a
line load.

Dome
A similar process, as described in the section on modelling the wall, is used to model the
dome. However, first, initial analysis is again performed in Karamba3D, evaluated with
the tests and finally analysed in Sofistik.
The dome functions as a shell, which is how it is also modelled in the FEA. It is an open­
top dome where the loading in the testing is applied as a line load on the top boundary.
The structure is optimised to act well in compression. The forces are transferred through
so­called meridional forces, which are in compression and hoop forces which may act
either in compression or tension. Usually, in the top half of the dome, compressive hoop
forces are expected, while in the bottom, tensile hoop forces are expected. This is why
it is known that the dome is spreading or broadening at the bottom. Where tension or
compressive hoop forces depend on the load type. The principle is presented in Figure
4.15 below, which is also how the forces and stresses are expected to be distributed in the
numerical model [72]. Additionally, between the hoops regions occurs so­called neutral
hoops, which can also be seen in the illustration of the results.[73]

5.1.2
Testing
[In collaboration with Aline Verwilghen [14]]
Test theory
The simple wall structure is tested for bending in a three­point bend test, which could
also be a four­point bend test. Three­point was chosen because of the relatively short
span, where there wouldn’t be enough space for 2 supports on each side. Generally, the
four­point bend test is better than the three­point, giving a constant moment distribution
between the loading two points.
In the test, the load is applied to the beam at the top middle point, while the beam is
supported at two bottom ends. In such a simply supported beam under vertical loading,
compression occurs on the upper side of the beam, while tension occurs on the bottom
side. This is explained in Figure 4.8 in Section 4.4, where red represents compression
and blue tension.
Normal cementitious concrete as a material is well known to be good in carrying com­
pression forces and less suitable for carrying tensile forces. Therefore, considering the
occurrence of the tensile stresses at the bottom, the failure of the beam is expected to
be at the midspan at the bottom of the cross­section. The same is expected for simple
biopolymer concrete wall specimens.
Test setup
The test setup generally follows the test description in Eurocode 12390­5 for unreinforced
specimens [74]. Testing was performed on the INSTRON 5500R machine with a hydraulic
cylinder capacity of 50 kN. The test setup consists of the hydraulic press machine, two
supporting rollers acting as supports, and one upper roller attached to the press head,
which applies force to the specimen. The length between the supports and applied force is
half of the span length, in this case, half of either 13 or 15 cm. These lengths were decided
based on the initial decision of having a 15 cm long testing setup. For the described test
setup to work, at least 1 cm of the specimen should be over the support, requiring 2 cm
longer specimens. Because some specimens were already printed to 15 cm, the decision
was made to have some of 13 cm length between the supports and the other 15 cm. This
also allows us to test different span lengths and compare their influence on the results.
Additionally, an electronic deflection gauge was mounted at midspan below the beam
to measure the deflection. Both deflection and applied loading are documented through
computer software, through which the machine is also operated. First, the run programme
is defined, with the loading speed, which was set to 10 N/s and the limit deflection, as a
safety mechanism to prevent damage to the machine. After defining the programme, it
runs automatically. The same setup and program were used for all tests conducted in
this study. The programme to control the INSTRON machine was saved and can be used
for additional tests in the future. Figure 5.7 presents a standard three­point bending test
setup diagram, where the lengths between the supports based on the specimen height are
defined. Furthermore, Figure 5.8 shows the actual testing setup with a 4­layer specimen.

Bending tensile strength
One of the material properties required for finite element analysis is tensile strength.
As the material is weaker under tension, tensile strength is smaller than compression
strength. Therefore, failure occurs on the tensile side of the cross­section. More accu­
rately, at the bottom of the midspan. The material’s tensile capacity at that point is called
bending tensile strength, which is not the same as tensile strength. The tensile strength
refers to the overall strength of the beam under tensile stress, while the bending tensile
strength refers to the strength of the concrete at the bottom of the cross­section, where the
beam is most likely to fail due to bending stresses. In the numerical model, the utilisation
of the cross­section, which predicts the failure, is checked. It means the bending stress at
the bottom should be compared to the bending tensile strength. Therefore, bending ten­

Material preparation

Slicing
The other part of the preparation process was making the robot work by providing it with a
print code. The work was done with the student working on this project and PhD student
who was previously working with the ABB robot. This procedure doesn’t follow the Python
code made by [12], as that was made specifically for the KUKA robot and KRL language.
Additional code transformation would be required to make it for the ABB robot, which uses
RAPID language. For this reason, a different procedure was followed, which is described
in Figure 5.20.
Figure 5.20: Slicing workflow diagram
First, the dome surface was made in Grasshopper, which is the same surface that was
input for the numerical model. It is beneficial to use GH to define this surface, as it was
already used for the numerical model, so the surface needs to be defined only once.
Next, the surface in GH was sliced into 1 cm high layers, making a continuous print path.
Every layer was then divided into 100 points with position and angle coordinates. The
RobotStudio, an ABB robot software, uses RAPID language, which uses quaternion points
and angles [75]. Quaternion angles are defined as angles about the rotational axis, which
give the fourth values besides the x,y, and z position coordinates. Coordinates from GH
had to be converted, which was done using MATLAB script with the help of a PhD student
working with the robot. Then, the converted points were imported into the robot software
and transformed into RAPID language code. Afterwards, the printing parameters and
speed were added to the robot code command. Finally, the size of the extruder had to be
implemented to have the right printing point and to check for any clashes.

Observations
This section describes all observations and improvements in the printing process. Soon
after starting printing, it was noticed that the material use was higher than first expected.
This is because a lot more material went to waste than expected when starting and stop­
ping the print. Additionally, due to printing failure, some layers from initial tests were
thrown away. Furthermore, it was noticed that the material at the end of the bag didn’t
have satisfactory consistency and was, therefore, not used. Initially, it was estimated that
30 bags should be enough for two 60 cm diameter domes, while it took around 25 bags
for one. It was also noticed that, for some reason, the material indeed shouldn’t be heated
in the oven for more than 2­3 hours, following suggestions of [9]. We were unable to ex­
trude the material that was in the oven for over 3 hours. Approximately 6 minutes were
required to refill the tube, and around 4 minutes was layering time with the fan. It could be
optimised to 3 minutes, but 90s was too low in our case. In the end, visual assessment
of layers was proven to be more important than time.
The printing table where the dome was printed was a normal pallet, which wasn’t fixed
to the ground. An improvement would be a fixed printing table to eliminate the chance of
hitting and moving it, as then it is hard to find the right initial position.
While printing, it was noticed that at approximately half the length of the layer, the robot
turns in a way that makes the printing inconsistent at that place. It makes the nozzle turn,
which in some cases makes the material unstable at that place. The final printing angle
was around 65° for the second half dome and slightly less, about 60° for the first half.
As mentioned, the layer height in this project was set to 10 mm. It should be lowered to 7
or 8 mm to improve layer adhesion. Because the nozzle is 13 mm in diameter and 10 mm
in height, there is not enough height difference for the material to be pushed down and
flattened. Additional few millimetres would give just the right layer push, consequently,
76
Optimised Design of Structural Components for multi­axial 3D Printing
==PAGE:89==
better adhesion and width. It was also noticed that, at some point, layers would be pulled
off while printing, as the material has some elasticity. This could be due to the print speed,
meaning that the print speed was too fast compared to the extrusion speed and should
be adjusted, in this case, lowered.
Additionally, layer height could be detected using height sensors, meaning the slicing
would be performed in real time based on the height of the previous layer, as some layers
are printed higher than others. This way, the only input would be the surface that the robot
must follow and the height measurement, based on which the slicing would be performed.
All described follows the future idea of excluding as many human factors as possible from
the material preparation and printing process.

Conclusion
6.1
Conclusion and Findings
The overall aim of this research was to continue the ongoing research at the DTU about
the BPC and focus on the structural possibilities of building with such material. The ob­
jectives from the beginning were to first gather and evaluate all the information about the
material as such, to provide sufficient material properties for further investigation, more
specifically, a finite element analysis model. Chapter 2 discusses the material proper­
ties, the multi­axial robot and the printer setup. In the next chapter, structural design and
possibilities of implementing BPC and 3DCP were researched and evaluated, and the
final complex structure was chosen for printing to showcase the material’s capabilities.
Chapter 4 discusses the finite element analysis, where the influence of the layer interface
and orientation are elaborated. The finite element analysis of the simple wall structure
in Karamba3D and Sofistik FEA software followed that. Finally, the dome was analysed,
and the results were evaluated. The final chapter presents the work done in the lab. It
started with testing the material and finding suitable properties, which took longer than ex­
pected. The simple wall specimens were printed using the horizontal printing setup and
tested after curing for 28 days. Finally, after all the complications with the KUKA robot,
the dome was printed using ABB multi­axial robot.
Following the thesis objectives, the following section elaborates on the main conclusions
drawn from this study.
• Mechanical and rheological properties of the BPC were researched, and based on
this, the final mix of MG80B35 was chosen. All material properties from previous
research and those that were added to this research are presented in Table 5.8,
which also includes the accuracy estimates for those properties.
• The printing setup was described and improved. The improvements include a new
extruder design, which simplifies the cartridge change. Temperature sensors were
also added to the extruder for more accurate temperature measurements. A new
extruder mount for the multi­axial printer and the horizontal printer setup was de­
signed and made for a more stable mounting. Finally, another multi­axial robot was
used, with another type of software and slicing, which opens more possibilities for
printing with different robots in the future.
• The structural design and possibilities were evaluated, based on which the dome
was chosen as the most appropriate complex structure to showcase the BPC capa­
bilities.
• The influence of the layer orientation and interface was discussed, where it was
assumed that the effect shouldn’t make a noticeable difference in the numerical
model. The assumptions mainly follow the tensile strength research, where it was
concluded that there is almost a 50/50 failure distribution in either the layer itself or
the interface.
• The initial accurate numerical model was made for different variants of simple walls
and domes through an iterative process compared with the test results. The work­
flow from GH, Karamba3D and Sofistik is added to the appendix to be used in the
future for other structures. The FEA results are compared and evaluated with the
test results and between the software.
Optimised Design of Structural Components for multi­axial 3D Printing

==PAGE:92==
• Different material mixtures were mixed and tested until the final mix was chosen.
After that, simple wall specimens were printed and tested in a 3­point bending test.
From the tests, it was also observed that BPC has good capabilities as a bonding
material.
• Finally, the dome was printed from two halves using ABB multi­axial robot. It was
made from two parts, as that was the only possibility with the mentioned robot. Other
observations are also presented.
6.2
Recommendations for future work
Even though extensive research has already been performed on the BPC, it is still a
novel material, and further investigation is still necessary before it could potentially be
used in real civil engineering projects. Step forward is research performed throughout
this research, where some of the questions were researched and answered. On the other
hand, some new questions arise and could be a part of further investigation. The main
recommendations for future works are described in the points below:
• Even though the printing setup was improved, it could additionally be improved in the
future. A continuous feeding system should be added. An automated stopping of
the material extrusion should be implemented in the nozzle setup, with the addition
of the rectangular nozzle. Mixing and heating of the material should be automated
to avoid anomalies and have material consistency.
• More research should still be done on material behaviour and properties. Transi­
tional and rotational spring stiffness could still be found with testing. Additionally, the
influence of water on the BPC could be researched, as it was found that the material
melts while cutting with the water­cooled saw. The right saw should be found, as
well as the solution to keep the material safe from water when it would be potentially
exposed to the weather.
• Once transitional and rotational spring stiffness are found through testing, numerical
models could be improved by adding layers. They could be added directly in GH for
both Karamba3D and Sofistik.
• The tests performed on simple wall specimens could be repeated following the pro­
cedures described in this thesis to get a broader range of tests to derive more ac­
curate results and conclusions. Tests of the simple wall specimens with vertical lay­
ers should be repeated, and outcomes evaluated. Additionally, by printing another
dome, it could be tested and evaluated with the numerical model.
• Elastic modulus should be researched regarding time dependence and how much it
changes over time. For now, the initial and final elastic modules are derived, while
the values in between could be found by testing the simple wall specimens curd
from a week to 28 days.
• While testing the simple wall specimens, the bounce effect was observed. It is hard
