Abstract
This thesis focuses on developing and evaluating a planning-and-guidance framework for
an Autonomous Underwater Vehicle (AUV) to intercept a moving, potentially intelligent
intruder in a partially known, dynamic underwater environment.
The primary goal is to integrate sampling-based path planners with target motion predic-
tion and guidance laws to produce time-efficient interception trajectories. The framework
is designed to be resilient to map inaccuracies and dynamic obstacles by supporting real-
time re-planning and local tree updates. The performance is quantified in terms of inter-
ception time, path quality (cost and length), and robustness across a set of representative
scenarios implemented in a physics-faithful simulator using ROS2 and Gazebo. The pro-
posed Reactive Anytime RRT* (RARRT*) architecture combines anytime/asymptotically-
optimal sampling-based planning, lightweight kinematic target prediction, and layered
guidance (lookahead and pure-pursuit).
The thesis addresses the challenges of intercepting a moving target with complex control
behavior, navigating in partially-known and time-varying environments, ensuring safety-
aware path generation, and balancing real-time responsiveness with path quality.

1
Introduction
The seabed has become a strategic environment hosting an expanding and heteroge-
neous set of infrastructure. Submarine communication cables carry the vast majority of
international data traffic, while pipelines, offshore wind installations and military assets are
increasingly critical to national economies and security. These installations are exposed
to a growing spectrum of threats: deliberate interference from hostile underwater vehicles,
unauthorized drone activity, and accidental damage resulting from maritime traffic.
Protecting these assets presents several distinct challenges. First, the areas of inter-
est are often vast and remote, spanning tens to thousands of kilometres and including
deep or hazardous waters, which makes persistent human-led surveillance and interven-
tion operationally costly. Second, the underwater domain is inherently opaque: acoustic
and sonar sensing have limited resolution and range while line-of-sight is even more con-
strained than at the surface, and intruding vehicles may exploit this to remain stealthy.
Third, any active interception system must respect a dual safety imperative: it must re-
liably interdict malicious actors while avoiding harm to legitimate marine users (vessels,
divers) and to the environment.
Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) offer a promising approach to these challenges.
Recent progress in sampling-based, asymptotically optimal planners combined with im-
proved vehicle miniaturization, power efficiency and sensors make deployable AUV-based
monitoring and interception increasingly feasible. These advances motivate the present
work: to combine state-of-the-art path-planning, target prediction and guidance methods
with realistic vehicle and simulation models to improve autonomous interception capabil-
ities in constrained, partially known underwater environments.
1.1
Goal
The primary goal of this thesis is to develop and evaluate a planning and guidance frame-
work that enables an AUV to intercept a moving, potentially intelligent intruder in a partially-
known, dynamic underwater environment while respecting operational safety constraints.
Concretely, the thesis aims to:
• Integrate sampling-based path planners with target motion prediction and guidance
laws to produce time-efficient interception trajectories.
• Ensure the solution is resilient to map inaccuracies and dynamic obstacles by sup-
porting real-time re-planning and local tree updates.
• Quantify performance in terms of interception time, path quality (cost and length)
and robustness across a set of representative scenarios implemented in a physics-
faithful simulator.
1.2
Scope
This work focuses on the planning, prediction and guidance stages of the interception
process. Detection and sensor data fusion (object detection, classification, multi-sensor
tracking) are assumed to be available as input, i.e., the AUV receives pose and velocity
estimates for the target at a given update rate. The vehicle and simulator models used are
intended to be realistic, but hardware-in-the-loop and full-system field tests are beyond
the scope of this thesis.

The intruder considered in this study is assumed to be intelligent in the sense that it
can follow complex control trajectories (not necessarily simple polynomial paths), which
motivates the use of robust, yet simple, predictive planners rather than methods tailored
to limited motion models.
1.3
Problem statement
Interception in the underwater domain is challenging because the planner must trade off
speed, safety, and robustness in the face of uncertainty. The concrete research problems
addressed in this thesis are:
1. Interception of a moving target with complex control behaviour. How can the
planner and predictor be combined so that the AUV intercepts targets whose trajec-
tories do not obey simple models, while keeping computational demands suitable
for real-time operation?
2. Navigation in partially-known and time-varying environments. How can the
planner remain effective when maps are partially correct or when dynamic obstacles
(e.g., other vessels, debris, new infrastructure) appear, without restarting planning
from scratch?
3. Safety-aware path generation. How can the system guarantee (or at least priori-
tise) the safety of third parties and the protected infrastructure by incorporating ex-
clusion zones, wall-buffering, and collision-avoidance into planning and guidance?
4. Real-time responsiveness vs. path quality. How to balance producing quick,
feasible interception trajectories (so the vehicle departs promptly) with the desire to
refine the path towards more optimal, lower-risk solutions while en route?

2
Literature review
The task of intercepting an intruder autonomously can be separated into multiple phases.
• Detecting where the target is.
• Estimating where the target will be.
• Finding and maintaining a trajectory to intercept it.
• Command the drone to follow the trajectory.
• Control the actuators to execute the commands.
For this master thesis, we decided to assume the detection is a given and we are focusing
on the 3 middle phases, as detection and autopilots could deserve a master thesis of their
own. Thus the literature review is oriented towards:
• Target path prediction.
• Path finding in partially known and changing environment.
• AUV robust trajectory following.
2.1
Target path prediction
Articles like Harbour protection: moving invasion target interception for multi-AUV based
on prediction planning interception method [1] use linear extrapolation to predict the future
position of the target and use it as the interception goal instead of the raw position of the
target, which reminds greatly of proportional navigation [2].
In Improved RRT Algorithm for AUV Target Search in Unknown 3D Environment [3] the
algorithm performs interception in two phases. First the exploration through ergodicity
reduction and target probability regions. Then, once the target is detected, the future pose
and speed of the target is estimated through three-point numerical differential method, a
lightweight alternative to higher-order interpolation. The author also discusses the two
and five point numerical differential method but discards them. The former because it is
less accurate and the latter because it requires more processing power.
In A Fusion Designed Improved Elastic Potential Field Method in AUV Underwater Target
Interception [4], when the target is far, a least square method is used to fit a polynomial
function on the target trajectory to estimate its future positions. When the target is close
enough (below a threshold distance), elastic predictive interception is used to finalize the
interception.
2.2
Path planning in partially known and changing
environment
A lot of different algorithms are presented in Path planning techniques for mobile robots:
Review and prospect [5], sampling based methods like RRT, graph search algorithms like
Dijkstra or A*, artificial potential fields, or even bionic algorithms, like ant-colony optimiza-
tion or bacterial foraging optimization.
We are choosing to study sampling based methods, in particular RRT* variants as they
present key benefits compared to the other types of algorithms.

Anytime RRT* [6] is a modification of RRT* that can provide a path whenever the vehicle

path is committed and the rest of the path continues to be improved. With ARRT*, you
can tailor the rules of the tree expansion to make up for the vehicle limitation, like the
turning radius, as you would do with a classic RRT.

ning in partially known environments. First, it generates an initial path using a backward-
expanding RRT* (rooted at the goal) to create a state tree with stored cost-to-goal metrics.
Upon detecting new obstacles, it:
• Reconnects orphaned nodes (deleting colliding branches and reassigning parents
to maintain tree validity),
• Re-plans locally by selecting the best alternative node from candidates within the
robot’s detection range, using Pareto dominance to balance path length and steer-

Cascaded PID
A Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller is a control loop feedback mechanism
used to regulate a process variable by adjusting the control output based on the error
between a desired setpoint and the actual measured value.
The PID controller consists of three main components: the proportional term (P), the
integral term (I), and the derivative term (D). The proportional term responds to the current
error, the integral term eliminates steady-state errors by accumulating past errors, and the
derivative term anticipates future error trends to improve stability.
The output of a PID controller is given by:

In a cascaded PID architecture, multiple PID controllers are used hierarchically, with the
outer loop controlling position and the inner loop controlling velocity, allowing for more
precise and robust control. This allow more modularity as we can change the content of
the inner loop, whether we want to control over velocity, position or both.
More advance control exist like Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR), Model Predictive Con-
trol (MPC), Sliding Mode Control (SMC) or even H infinity. In our case, the control was
out of the scope of this project and cascaded PID proved to be satisfactory, simpler to
implement while using less processing power than these other more advanced technics.
In fact in most industrial cases, PIDs are used for these same reasons.
3.1.4
Lookahead guidance
Lookahead guidance is a path-following strategy that determines a target point along the
reference path at a predetermined distance ahead of the vehicle’s current position. This
target point serves as the setpoint for the control system, effectively guiding the vehicle
along the desired trajectory.
The key parameter in this guidance approach is the lookahead distance, which signif-
icantly influences the vehicle’s path-following behaviour. A smaller lookahead distance
results in tighter path following but may limit the vehicle’s maximum speed when operating
under position control. Conversely, a larger lookahead distance allows for higher speeds
but may result in less precise path following.
Unlike line-of-sight (LOS) based guidance methods (such as LOS, ILOS, or ALOS) that
are typically optimized for velocity and heading control, lookahead guidance is particularly
well-suited for position control systems.

Based on Rapidly-exploring Random Trees (RRT) [18], RRT* [19] has been introduced in
2010 by Karaman and Frazzoli, and improves it in being asymptotically optimal.
The idea behind RRT is to pick a random point in the exploration space, steer towards it
according to the step distance and limitation of the vehicle, and to connect it to the nearest
connectible node to expand the tree. The random strategy of the exploration make it very
fast at covering a large and complex area.

3.2.2
Anytime RRT*
Anytime RRT* (ARRT*) was introduced in Anytime Motion Planning using the RRT* [6]
in 2011 by Karaman & al., the creators of the RRT* algorithm. This algorithm aim at
improving the applicability to real system by taking into consideration the refining time to
converge to an optimal solution.
Indeed, RRT* is asymptotically optimal thus it needs time to converge. So, with little time
the path is suboptimal or even not finding a solution, and with too much time we are
wasting time waiting for convergence. Thus anytime RRT* provides quickly a suboptimal
path for the vehicle to start moving towards its goal, but then improve the path while the
vehicle is moving to converge to an optimal solution. This is illustrated in figure 3.7.
Figure 3.7: Four runs of the Anytime RRT* on a robot forklift. From Anytime Motion
Planning using the RRT* [6].
The most important change to RRT* is the committed path. When a path is requested, the
algorithm will commit a part of the path and continue improving the tree from the last node
committed. This way, most of the tree can be saved and improved while the autonomous
vehicle go along the committed section.
ARRT* also introduces the branch-and-bound feature. By introducing an heuristic to as-
sess the cost-to-go, meaning an estimation of the cost to the goal, we can assess what
branch have a chance to be better than the active best branch. This cost-to-go function
needs to provide a lower-bound on the optimal cost to reach the goal, so we are sure the
branch cannot do better than this lower bound. The euclidean distance to the goal fulfil
this criteria. This way, once we have a path reaching our goal, the algorithm will remove
at a certain frequency, branches that cannot improve our path.

points, while otherwise, peripheral branches that cannot improve the path, would have

4.1.1
Sensors
As the sensing in general was outside the scope of this project, an important assumptions
was made, the sensors are perfect, suffer no noise nor lag and are always available.
This is an strong assumption as sensor integration and filtering is usually a very important
part of any autonomous system. Though, as our project revolves around the path planning
we decided to put our focus and time on challenges closer to its scope. Moreover, the
path planning, as it is a lower frequency function, suffers less directly of the sensor noise
that is more a problem for mapping or control and guidance.
Here is a summary of the different sensors we are using and the assumption made about
their data:
• Drone localisation and odometry: exact ground truth at 20 Hz.
• Target localisation and velocity: exact ground truth at 1Hz.
• Sonar: replaced by a perfect LiDAR with 300m range, assuming sonar treatment
would provide us a point cloud of our surrounding.
The sonar range has been chosen to be close to what a real sonar range could be. It was
assessed over the reviewing of commercially available sonars (Edgetech, DeepVision,
...) that range from 1000 to 10000 euros. Their range varies depending of the emitted fre-
quency, the resolution of the resulting image and several other factors, thus we estimated
a plausible equivalent range to be 300m.

Lookahead guidance
The guidance algorithm depends on which phase of the interception we are at. To ap-

interception (see section 4.4).
As presented in the methodology chapter, section 3.1.4, the lookahead guidance needs
to find a point over the path given by the path planning.

1. Find the minimal distance from the robot to each segments of the path.

4.1.4
Pure pursuit guidance
The final part of the interception is done with pure pursuit (PP) guidance.
With the knowledge of the target position, the guidance is done by setting the path to be
a straight line between our position and the target’s one.
This guidance was favoured over proportional navigation (PN) because of development
time constraint and because the controller is set to position control while PN works better
with speed and heading control.
To prevent collisions in this close range part of the interception, the line of sight is checked
regularly and in case of an obstacle detection, RARRT* takes over and resume the looka-
head guidance.

To intercept a target, most paper rely on a method to predict the position of the target. In
most interception cases the final goal or the path of the target are unknown. Moreover
information like position and velocity are not necessarily reliable, especially in early stage
of the interception.
In our case we consider the target’s position and velocity to be known at a decent fre-
quency and wherever the target is on the map. Once again, the goal being to study the
path planning strategy to intercept the target, it is interesting to have to deal with a varying
target position prediction but the rest is out of the scope of this master thesis.

4.3
Path planning
The ARRT* algorithm allows to improve a path while driving towards an objective. As we
presented it in section 3.2.2, though it was neither studied to reach a moving target, nor
it was developed to handle changing environment.
Thus, this section will present the integration of ARRT* to the path planning and the mod-
ification made to adapt it to our problematic. First by presenting how it handles new
obstacles and then how it avoids crossing user defined zones.
4.3.1
Continuous path improvement
ARRT* is a tree based path planning algorithm. Thus the first things to implement is the
tree with its nodes and vertices.
Architecture
Multiple architecture were possible, list of nodes and vertices, list of nodes referencing
their direct parent, individual cost to vertices or sum at the node, ... etc. A few were
experimented and eventually the following architecture was chosen:
Path Planning for Interception of an Autonomous Underwater Vehicle
25
==PAGE:40==
• The nodes: memory structure to keep the locations and the connections of the
nodes.
– Location: (x,y,z) coordinates, also used as the key to search a point in the tree
(dictionary).
– Parent: (x,y,z) coordinates of the parent node, meaning a vertices links the
parent node to the current node.
– Cost: The sum of the costs from the original root1 to this node.
– Distance: The sum of the distances from the original root1 to this node.
• The tree: Structure to keep the nodes and the functions to use it.
– Nodes dictionary: Keep nodes indexed based on their coordinates.
– Root: Keep reference to root node as we often need to use the root node.
– Spatial index: Improves distance search speed through grid bucketing.
– Functions: Tree editing and search functions, to keep coherence in the nodes,
avoid loops, fetch a branch or a path to the root, ... etc
• The spatial index: Grid based bucketing index to improve distance search speed
with large tree.
The code initialize a tree on the position of the drone when a goal is given to the path
planning.
Then most of the processing time is allocated to expand the tree according to the ARRT*
algorithm presented in section 3.2.2.
The rest of the processing time is allocated to handle path requests, clean the tree through
branch and bound, update the tree according to map update information or display func-
tions when debugging.

Path request
When a path is requested, the algorithm:
• Searches for the most optimal node.
• Get the path from this node to the root.
• Commit a part of the path.
• Publishes the path to the guidance module.
• Update the tree root to the end of the committed path.
• Remove all the branches that are not descending from the new root.
Then the algorithm continue to expand the tree until the next path request.

Find optimal node
To find the most optimal node we need to consider multiple scenarios.
First, one node only is in range of the goal. Then we select the one node as the optimal
node.
Second, multiple nodes are in range of the goal. Then we select the one with the lowest
total cost as the optimal node. The total cost is the sum of the cost of the node and the
cost to goal (weighted line from the node to the goal). This way a node in range of the
goal but that cannot reach it because of an obstacle will have an infinite cost and not be
selected over a node that has a line of sight to the goal.
Then, if no nodes has been found, we try to find a node with direct line of sight on the
goal. To do so, we double the goal range, then triple, and so on, to find nodes and test
whether they have a direct line of sight on the goal, if so, the lowest cost one is selected.
This is done until one node is found or a timeout is reach, as otherwise this can take a
very long time to test all the nodes.
Finally, if we could still not find any node, either we accept that the path planning needs
more time to find a feasible path to the goal, or we try to find a non-optimal node to start
moving.
To find a non-optimal node, we select the closest node to the goal. This is usually not
perfect but it allows us to start going in the general direction of the goal. Though it some-
times can backfire in complex environment like a maze or a harbour, and we will test such

==PAGE:43==
a scenario to consider whether it is acceptable or not.
If the node (in range or not) has a line of sight to the goal, then we save the cost and
distance to goal sum, as the bound for the branch and bound algorithm. Otherwise we
deactivate the branch and bound algorithm as we still need to expand the tree to find a
real reaching node.
Branch and bound
The branch and bound algorithm, presented in section 3.2.2, uses the cost and distance
to goal sum of the last published optimal node, to remove all the nodes whose cost and
distance to goal sum are higher. This way we free space in the tree to only keep the
branches that can improve the path.
Here we do not use the total cost, as a node that does not have a line of sight to the goal,
would have an infinite estimated cost to goal (and not distance to goal) and be removed.
Thus the euclidean distance provides a reliable lower bound to the estimate which is
enough for the heuristic function of this estimation.
Figure 4.9: Illustration of branch and bound effect. Left: Before a feasible path is found
the tree expand to explore all the space. Right: Once a feasible path is found, the tree is
limited by the cost bound.
Goal update
As the target is moving the goal of the pathfinding must be updated. Not only the coor-
dinates need to be adjusted but also the branch and bound maximum cost value. This
way when the target position changes from the last known position, the cost of movement
between the last known position and the new one is calculated and added to the bound
cost value as this additional movement represents the worst case scenario in terms of
cost to reach the new position of the target from the last known best path.
4.3.2
Obstacle handling
As the tree is being refined while moving towards the target, new obstacles can be de-
tected and have to be dealt with.
The easiest solution would be to restart the tree from zero to consider the new environ-
ment. Though as the environment can continuously change, this would take too much
resources, and probably never converge to a satisfying result.
On the other hand, the tree structure of the algorithm allows some flexibility in keeping al-
ternative routes alive, and thus resilience to environment changes. Moreover some algo-
rithm like MOD-RRT offer ways to deal with updating the tree upon environment changes.

Detecting impacted nodes
Whenever we receive a new observation map on the dedicated topic /bluerov2_heavy/obs_map,
we calculate the difference with the previous observation map. If any cell changed, we
create a mask out of the changing cells and an inflated version of this mask. The first one
will determine, which nodes are to remove and the second, which one are to update.
The inflated mask is a faster way to detect what vertices have to be rewired. As the max-
imum distance between two connected node is known to be the rewiring distance, then a
vertex can only be crossing the newly detected obstacle, if the two nodes it connects, are
both within the rewiring distance of the new obstacle.
This way we can go through all our nodes and create a list of nodes to remove, and a list
to rewire.
To increase the chances of reconnection, the 2nd generation descendants of the nodes
to remove are also added to the nodes to rewire (if they are not already in the remove list)
to increase the chances of the rewiring process.

Updating the tree
Once the list are ready we start the rewiring process.
It is sensibly the same process as the rewiring used in RRT* with the difference that it does
not consider the nodes that are in either list (remove or rewire) or that does not have a
path to the root. This avoids connecting to a part of the tree that will later be disconnected
because it does not have a path to the root.
When rewiring a node, we update the costs and distances of all the descendants with the
delta between the new cost and the original cost of the rewired node.
We go through all the rewiring list over and over again, until there is either no more nodes
to rewire or that no node was rewired through a whole passage through the list. This is
unfortunately a weakness of this algorithm as it yields a O(n!) worst case complexity, in
the case of only one node being rewired at every passage.
After the rewiring is done, all the nodes that should have been rewired but could not, are
added to the list of nodes to remove and then are all discarded with their descendants.

4.3.3
Zone of exclusion
To inform the drone on positions it should avoid, we are using an occupancy grid with
weights and connection costs architecture. This was presented in section 3.2.3.
Occupancy grids
There are two occupancy grids, the observation one, representing the physical obstacle
that the drone cannot physically go through, and the zone one, defined by the user as
zones to avoid, with a certain degree of severity.
The observation grid is both predefined according to a priori knowledge of the area and
is updated during the course of the drone with the observations given by the sonar or any
other observation sensor. In the simulation, this would be done via the LiDAR emulating
the sonar.
The zone grid is only predefined or updated by the user during the course of the drone,
but could as well be updated if the drone is capable of semantic interpretation of its sur-
rounding. The value are set from 0 to 100, representing the severity of the zones.
All these functions are located in the auv_mapping node, and maps are saved as png, then
converted to a 1 cell/meter resolution occupancy grids. They are respectively published
on /bluerov2_heavy/obs_map and /bluerov2_heavy/zone_map at a settable frequency.
The frequency has been set to 1 Hz as mapping is usually a low frequency process.
Figure 4.14: Occupancy grids and their merging. Top-left: zones to avoid. Bottom-left:

Occupancy grid value
The value used for the occupancy grid are arbitrary, but it would be interesting to consider
them as a ratio to the resolution and thus, as a virtual extra distance added to a path going
through a cell.
For example, a 10m horizontal segment going through a zone with cells having a value
of 3 and the resolution being 2m/cells, then would cross 6 cells resulting in a cost of 28.
The ratio would then be 1.5 and the cost estimation is close to be 1 + 1.5 = 2.5 times the
distance.

Cost and distance calculation
The cost of a movement has to be calculated to provide a metric to the path planning to
compare path. Thus this function is essential to the good working of the algorithm.
Moreover this function has to be running fast as it is used at every expansion and rewiring
multiple time.
The Bresenham algorithm presented in section 3.2.3, algorithm 2, efficiently provides
coordinates of traversed cells of an occupancy grid.
The distance is calculated as the euclidean distance between the points and is used to
initialize the value of the cost. This way we ensure that the lower bound of the cost of a
path is the total distance of it.
Then the cost is calculated by summing the value in every cell traversed by the segment
linking the two points. This represents the cost of going through a zone to avoid, and
additionally reflecting the severity of its crossing. It also represents the cost of going
through a part of the observation grid where the chances of finding an obstacle is higher.
To prevent the crossing of an unbreachable cell, which should not happen, the cost of
such a segment is set to be infinite. That way, if a cell value in the observability map is
higher than a threshold (set to 50%) then the cost of the segment is returned as infinite
and the path planning will know that this connection is not possible.

5.1.3
RRT* implementation
To compare our algorithm RARRT* we set up a RRT* algorithm in the same environment.
For the comparison to be relevant, the number of variations between the two path planning
method should be reduced only to the fundamentals of their functioning. This way we
adapted our algorithm by neutralizing certain features depending on the test scenario.
Static RRT*
In the case of the static goal, the RRT* algorithm is the same algorithm as ours, but the
anytime function is neutralized to only provide a path after a set processing time.
This way the weights on the occupancy grids are handled the same, as well as the opti-
misation of the calculations. The only difference is then in the continuous improvement,
as opposed to a longer initial processing time.
42
Path Planning for Interception of an Autonomous Underwater Vehicle
==PAGE:57==
Interception RRT*
On the other hand to be able to intercept a moving target, some development had to be
made to make a RRT* capable of guiding our drone.
Like for the static RRT*, we customized RARRT* to limit the optimization differences with
our algorithm. Thus, the interception RRT* has a set time to compute a path.
Then, contrary to the static one, it checks at a set frequency whether the goal has gotten
too far from the path. If so, it then recalculate a new path while moving along the outdated
path.
Once the new path is found, a quick optimization is done to adapt the newly found path to
the movement that happened while it was computed. This is done by checking if connect-
ing directly to the next few nodes, would reduce the overall cost of the path. If so, then we
remove the first few nodes of the path to directly reach the later ones. If no optimisation
is possible then we just use the result of the RRT* as is.
Another change was done to reduce the processing time when we approach the target
close enough. In the first few test of this interception RRT*, the algorithm proved to be
taking too much time to process a new path when in mid-range of the target and letting
the target run away while not knowing what to do. This way when in the transition to pure
pursuit (see figure 4.17), the processing time is divided by 3.
Branch and bound
An option was added to activate or deactivate the branch and bound, as it is technically
not part of the official RRT* algorithm, it was interesting to compare the results with and
without, as it also highlight its importance for the RARRT* algorithm.
5.1.4
Intruder
The intruder, presented as a target we are predicting from in section 4.2, is set to follow
a given path at a constant speed with a lookahead guidance. The speed here was set to
0.5 m/s, so our drone going at 0.6 m/s can intercept it even when following it.

Scenarios and metrics
The nature of the task being complex by essence, finding scenarios and metrics to com-
pare multiple solutions is a complicated endeavour. In this section we present the sce-
narios, the features they aim at testing and the metrics we use to measure the efficiency
of these.
5.2.1
Metrics
Path quality
The quality of a path is a qualitative metric, but quantitative metrics can help us assess
the quality of a path.
We chose to consider 3 metrics, the length of the path, the cost of the path and the time
to reach the target.
The length of the path is probably the most obvious metric when trying to reach a point,
but it can also be miss-leading in our case. A short path usually means less travel time
and less energy spent. Though, a short path can also be a path that goes through zones
to avoid, close to walls, or a path that took a long time to process.
This is the reason why we consider the cost of the path as our second metric. This way
if a path goes through zones that should have been avoided, close to walls or zones with
a risk of an obstacle, then the cost will be higher. The cost can be associated to the risk
taken by the path.

path is not visible in the length metric. This matters a lot in our case, as a major factor of
improvement from RRT* to ARRT* is the fact that the vehicle starts moving while refining
a path.
It is also important to note that in our case the drone is slow, a velocity of about 0.6 m/s,
but in the case of a faster drone the difference between the length of the path and the
time to reach the target will be even bigger.

getting too close to the corner.

zone. This way the drone has to find the path of ”lesser evil” and minimize its time in
a zone. A zone with lower cost is to the left, but the shortest path is a fast crossing
through the high cost zone and going around the rest of the zones.
• Scenario 3: A point in a narrow corridor with no direct line of sight from the open. This
scenario aims at tricking the algorithm with following a suboptimal path that would

6
Discussion
This chapter interprets the experimental results presented in Chapter 5, relates them to
existing literature, discusses limitations and threats to validity, and proposes concrete
directions for future work.
6.1
Overview of main findings
The experiments reported in this thesis produced several consistent findings:
• Sampling-based path planning is suitable for interception tasks. The sampling-
and-tree paradigm proved adequate for generating feasible interception trajectories
in the simulated underwater scenarios.
• Tree-based planners are resilient to target motion. The tree structure (incre-
mental expansion and local rewiring) maintained useful connectivity as the target
moved, enabling the vehicle to adapt without re-planning from scratch.
• The anytime property combined with RRT* improves performance. The reac-
tive anytime RRT* implementation (RARRT*) improved time-to-interception, path
length and constraint satisfaction compared to non-anytime baselines. The size of
these gains was larger than initially expected and would probably be even larger
with a faster interceptor.
• Local tree updates upon obstacle detection save computation time. Updating
parts of the tree after detecting obstacles considerably reduced processing time
compared to re-generating the tree from scratch; observed savings reached up to
80% in some scenarios despite the implementation complexity.
• Simple target prediction worked satisfactorily. Despite the prediction method’s
relative simplicity compared to advanced predictors in the literature, it produced
behaviour adequate for interception in our scenarios.
The combination of these findings suggests that a carefully engineered sampling-based
planner with anytime improvements and local tree maintenance can produce an effective
interception capability for AUVs operating in partially-known, dynamic underwater envi-
ronments.
6.2
Interpretation and relation to prior work
Many of the observations agree with theoretical expectations for sampling-based and any-
time planners, but there are important nuances worth highlighting.
6.2.1
Sampling-based planning for interception
That sampling-based planners are suitable for interception is an expected outcome: the
stochastic exploration of configuration space naturally deals with complex obstacle ge-
ometries. What was less documented in the literature, and what our work highlights, is
the applicability of these methods to moving targets. A survey of related work yielded few
direct comparisons applying anytime RRT variants to interception of moving agents, so
our experiments fill an empirical gap and suggest this combination is promising.
Path Planning for Interception of an Autonomous Underwater Vehicle
61
==PAGE:76==
6.2.2
Anytime RRT* behaviour
The RARRT* variant behaved consistently with the properties described in the original
work on anytime asymptotically optimal planning [6]: given time it refines solutions to-
ward lower-cost trajectories while remaining feasible. The magnitude of the observed
improvement relative to static RRT was larger than anticipated in some scenarios. It is
important to note that absolute processing times in our experiments were affected by im-
plementation and language choices (Python prototype), so the processing times reported
should be interpreted as upper bounds on what a well-optimised implementation could
achieve. Crucially, the implementation inefficiencies affected both RARRT* and RRT*
similarly, preserving the relative comparison between the two methods.
6.2.3
Tree updates for obstacle handling
The idea of locally updating a search tree after changes in the environment appears in
several algorithmic variants (e.g. MOD-RRT [7]) but published quantitative comparisons
are scarce. Our implementation shows that local rewiring can yield substantial savings
in processing time versus full re-planning, but these gains come at algorithmic and engi-
neering cost. The observed low performance in worst cases stems from high algorithmic
complexity in the rewiring strategy, indicating room for optimisation in both the detection
of affected nodes to reduce the number of nodes to process and in the rewiring process
itself. That the approach nevertheless produced up to 75% savings in some scenarios is
encouraging: it demonstrates practical value even for imperfect implementations.
6.2.4
Target prediction
The simple prediction scheme used in this work provided robust, usable forecasts of the
target’s short-term motion despite being far less sophisticated than many methods found
in the interception literature. Papers such as [1] and [4] propose more elaborate fusion
and prediction techniques for AUV interception, but direct numerical comparison is diffi-
cult because those works do not report quantitative results for their estimation process.
Qualitatively, the interception behaviour driven by our predictor is similar to those reports,
indicating that for the class of scenarios studied here a lightweight predictor may be suf-
ficient when combined with an adaptive planning layer.
6.3
Limitations and threats to validity
No experimental study is without caveats. The principal limitations of this thesis are:
Platform speed and timing.
The AUV model used in the experiments is relatively slow. This increases the available
time for anytime refinements and therefore may exaggerate RARRT*’s performances.
Though, a faster AUV using RARRT* would have an even bigger overall time advantage
over planners that compute a full trajectory before departure. That said, many AUVs
used for infrastructure protection are also relatively low-speed platforms, so the conclu-
sions remain relevant for typical applications. Nevertheless, results may change for faster
vehicles (e.g. high-speed gliders or torpedo-like platforms).
Dimensionality (2D vs 3D).
The control and planning experiments were carried out in 2D. While the algorithms (sam-
pling, tree rewiring, voxel-based occupancy tests) are conceptually portable to 3D, the
computational cost and sampling density requirements will increase. Extending the frame-
work to 3D is feasible and remains an important future step.
Obstacle-handling assumptions and refresh rates.
Tree-update performance was not evaluated under frequent map refreshes or highly dy-
namic obstacles. In the worst case, naive rewiring can be computationally expensive;

frequent changes may amplify this effect. Mitigation strategies (improved impacted-node
detection, prioritized rewiring) are discussed in Section 6.4.
Sensor and perception assumptions.
The current experiments assume perfectly accurate target pose and velocity estimates.
Real sensor noise, false detections, and missed detections will make prediction and
planning more difficult. This would likely degrade interception metrics unless addressed
through sensor filtering, robust estimation, or reduced prediction horizons.
Implementation and computational constraints.
The prototype is implemented in Python and is not optimised for deployment on embedded
platforms. Real-time execution on resource-constrained hardware (e.g. Jetson-class de-
vices) will require algorithmic and software optimisation, or hardware acceleration (GPU).
The observed processing times thus reflect proof-of-concept performance rather than
production-ready timings.
Environmental effects.
Ocean currents and complex hydrodynamics were not fully modelled. Currents will affect
both guidance performance and the intruder’s motion, so including them in future simu-
lations (and field trials) is important to validate the generalisation of the present results.
More advanced guidance controllers (e.g. ALOS or ILOS) could mitigate current-induced
errors [10].
Scenario diversity and statistical coverage.
While a range of scenarios was evaluated, additional scenario diversity (different intruder
strategies, obstacle layouts, sensor update rates) and larger sample sizes would strengthen
statistical claims.
6.4
Future work
Based on the lessons learned, the following topics are what we foreseen as interesting
direction for further developments:
6.4.1
Algorithmic and implementation improvements
• Performance optimisation. Re-implement the core planner in C++, profile the
code, and rework the overall structure of the code to reduce the absolute processing
times.
• Sampling strategy enhancements. Explore mixed sampling pools (goal biasing,
free-space sampling, general region sampling) to improve sampling strategy effi-
ciency and resilience to complex environments.
• Backward and bidirectional trees. Study backward propagation from the pre-
dicted interception region or bidirectional approaches (e.g.
RRT-Connect) as it
might be more resilient to obstacles but not necessarily working well with anytime
path requests.
6.4.2
Obstacle handling
• Efficient impacted-node detection. Design fast and conservative detection func-
tion to reduce the set of nodes that need rewiring when an obstacle appears or
changes.
• Prioritised rewiring heuristics. Rewire nodes in order of increasing cost-to-root
or other priority metrics so that early rewires are more likely to reconnect large parts
of the tree efficiently.

• Dynamic obstacle testing. Evaluate the enhanced rewiring scheme under fre-
quent map updates and dynamic obstacles to measure robustness under realistic
refresh rates.
6.4.3
Guidance, prediction and environmental realism
• Advanced guidance and current compensation. Replace simple guidance laws
with ALOS/ILOS variants or comparable controllers to improve robustness in the
presence of currents [10].
• Improved prediction models. Investigate learning-based or game-theoretic target
predictors for adversarial intruders; quantify the trade-off between prediction com-
plexity and planning responsiveness.
• 3D extension and high-fidelity hydrodynamics. Extend the planning framework
to 3D and include realistic current and acoustic propagation models to move closer
to field conditions.
6.5
Concluding remarks
This thesis demonstrates that sampling-based, anytime planning combined with lightweight
prediction and local tree maintenance provides a practical and effective foundation for
AUV interception tasks in partially-known underwater environments. The experimental
results show strong relative gains for RARRT* and large runtime savings when using
local tree updates for obstacle handling, even when implemented in a non-optimised pro-
totype. At the same time, the study highlights important engineering and modelling gaps,
notably computation, sensing, dynamic obstacles and environmental forces, that must be
addressed before field deployment. Addressing those gaps is a clear next step toward
robust, deployable autonomous interception systems.

7
Conclusion
This master’s thesis successfully developed and evaluated a robust path planning for in-
tercepting moving targets in complex underwater environments using a 6-DOF AUV. The
Reactive Anytime RRT* (RARRT*) algorithm, combining ARRT* with kinematic target pre-
diction and dynamic obstacle handling, demonstrated superior performance over classical
RRT* in terms of interception time, path quality, and respect of safety constraints.
The RARRT* ability to adaptively replan in response to dynamic obstacles and chang-
ing environments addresses critical challenges in underwater interception missions. The
integration with ROS2 and Gazebo provided a realistic simulation platform that closely ap-
proximates real-world conditions, including hydrodynamic effects, to allow future project
to reuse this work both to develop other functions and to deploy it on a real hardware more
easily.
The results highlight the importance of real-time path refinement and constraint handling,
especially in scenarios with moving targets and complex environments. The modular
design of the framework allows for future extensions, such as integrating advanced control
strategies or sensor fusion techniques, to further develop towards a fully autonomous
interception system.
This work contributes to the growing field of autonomous underwater vehicle interception
by providing a practical, efficient, and safe path planning solution that balances computa-
tional feasibility with operational effectiveness in dynamic, partially known environments.
