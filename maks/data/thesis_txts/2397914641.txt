Abstract 
Metabolomic profiling is used to analyze a group of metabolites in the metabolism of a biological 
sample. To get more information about the metabolism in a cell, a snapshot of the metabolic 
activity is needed to obtain a metabolic fingerprint. One of the main analytical techniques used to 
detect metabolites is NMR. The aim of this study was to establish a robust and reproducible 
extract protocol for analysis metabolic tracer experiments of human cell models with dDNP-NMR. 
A prostate cancer cell line DU-145 was used to find the most optimal extraction method. The 
extraction was incubated with PCA and MeOH to compare the methods. Here, it was found that 
PCA extraction was the most optimal extraction method to develop metabolic fingerprints of DU-
145. The PCA extraction method was used to evaluate the effect of nutrition changes. For this, two 
cell lines DU-145 and PNT-1A were grown with high and low glucose concentration and acetate. 
Here, it was found that there was no significant difference in growth between high and low 
concentration of glucose medium. Additionally, acetate did not show any change in the metabolic 
fingerprint, which indicates it is not a good biomarker. However, the cell lines can still grow on 
acetate medium, but further experiments are needed to investigate the role of acetate with a 
glucose biomarker.  
Resumé 
Metabolisk profilering bliver brugt til at analysere en gruppe af metabolitter i metabolismen i en 
biologisk prøve. For at få mere information om metabolismen i en celle, er der brug for et 
øjebliksbillede af den metaboliske aktivitet for at opnå et metabolisk fingeraftryk. En af de 
vigtigste analytiske teknikker, der anvendes til at detektere metabolitter, er NMR. Formålet med 
dette projekt var at etablere en robust og reproducerbar ekstraktionsprotokol til analyse af 
metaboliske sporings forsøg af humane cellemodeller med dDNP-NMR. Til dette blev DU-145 
cellelinje brugt til at finde den mest optimale ekstraktionsmetode. Ekstraktionen blev udført med 
PCA og MeOH for at sammenligne metoderne. Her blev det konstateret, at PCA ekstraktionen var 
den mest optimale ekstraktionsmetode til at opnå metaboliske fingeraftryk af DU-145. PCA 
ekstraktionsmetoden blev brugt til at evaluere effekten af ændrede næringsforhold. Til dette blev 
to cellelinjer DU-145 og PNT-1A groet med høj og lav glukose koncentration og acetat. Her blev 
det konstateret, at der ikke var nogen signifikant forskel på det metaboliske fingeraftryk mellem 
høj og lav glukose koncentration. Desuden, viste acetat ikke nogen ændring i det metaboliske 
fingeraftryk, hvilket tyder på, at det ikke er en god biomarkør. Cellelinjerne kan dog stadig vokse 
på acetatmedium, men yderligere eksperimenter er nødvendige for at undersøge acetats rolle 
med en glukose biomarkør.

1. 
Purpose and aims 
1.1 Aim 
The aim of this project is to investigate a metabolite extraction method to find the most optimal 
method to obtain metabolic fingerprints of prostate cancer cells. Along with the metabolite 
extraction method, observe metabolic fingerprints based on different nutrition’s. The quantitative 
data from detected metabolites will be obtained by analysis of dissolution DNP-NMR (dDNP-NMR).  
 
1.2 The objectives 
To establish a robust extract protocol for analysis metabolic tracer experiments of human cell 
models with dDNP-NMR: 
The main objective is to investigate which method is the most optimal extraction method for 
investigation of metabolic fingerprints of prostate cancer cells. The metabolic output is performed 
with an 800 MHz NMR spectrometer for quantification and evaluating the method performance. 
Use the established method to produce dDNP-NMR metabolic maps of prostate cancer cells to 
obtain.  
The extraction methods used in this study: 
 Perchloric acid. 
 Methanol. 
 
To evaluate the effect of nutrition changes using dDNP-NMR metabolic maps obtained with the 
established method: 
The established method will be used to investigate the effect of a change in nutrients to analyze a 
growth response or cell type response to a changed metabolic fingerprint using dDNP-NMR.  Two 
different cell lines will be exposed to different nutrition supply.  
The nutrition supply used for the experiments: 
 High and low glucose concentration medium 
 High glucose concentration and acetate medium

2. 
Background 
2.1 Metabolomic profiling 
Metabolomics is a comprehensive study of metabolites and metabolism in a certain biosample 
which provides a snapshot of the metabolic activity in the extracted sample. Metabolomic 
profiling is the quantification of the complete complement of low-molecular-weight molecules 
generated within a given biological sample, such as amino acids, lipids and carbohydrates[1][2]. 
One key aim of metabolic profiling is to obtain as much information as possible of intermediates 
(metabolites) levels from biochemical pathways included in the glycolysis, the citric acid cycle etc. 
in e.g. cancerous cells that are associated with a biological sample, which is the main focus in this 
project. The information of the biosample can give a measure of the relative abundance of the 
substrates and products of a specific reaction and this will give an opportunity to obtain indirect 
insights of the activity of a given enzyme or metabolic circuitry[2].  
To detect metabolites in a certain sample, a metabolic profiling tool needs to be used. The main 
analytical techniques that have been developed to detect metabolites are based on mass 
spectrometry (MS) and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). Both of the analytical methods are 
capable of profiling the full complement of the analysts in a sample to generate a spectrum that 
can give further information about the components[1]. Both MS and NMR are suitable techniques 
for metabolomic analysis, but the different techniques have different analytical strengths and 
weaknesses[3]. MS has a pre-separation step of the metabolic components which include either 
gas chromatography after chemical derivatization, or liquid chromatography[3]. The efficiency of 
chromatographic separation is related to the solvent systems, column type with separation of 
(hydrophobic/hydrophilic) components and column pressure. This provides a semi-quantitative 
information with very high sensitivity and is more able to detect small chemicals in a sample[1][4]. 
The main details of MS will not be further described because the aim of this study will be on 
detection of metabolites in cancerous cells by NMR.  
NMR spectroscopy is a robust quantitative technique for metabolite profiling. The detection of 
diverse metabolites provides a snapshot of a specific time point[3]. However, 1H NMR (proton 
NMR) spectrum is insufficient on its own to provide the full information that characterizes the 
metabolite. The peaks in 1H NMR can overlap easily, which makes it difficult to detect the 
compound. Alternatively, 13C NMR can be used, but it has relatively low sensitivity and is limited in 
detecting metabolites[5]. 1H NMR is generating a spectrum of the biological sample, which 
represents peaks from the different metabolites. The peaks represent a proton in a different 
chemical environment and the peak area is directly proportional to the concentration. A biological 
sample will typically generate a spectrum displaying crowded and overlapping peaks[1]. One 
limitation of NMR is its low sensitivity and thus higher limits of detection for metabolites. This can 
be solved by using hyperpolarized DNP, which can lead to improved sensitivity by dissolution 
==PAGE:14==
Page 13 of 75 
 
Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (dDNP)-NMR[6]. The details of dDNP-NMR will be discussed later in 
section 2.5 and will be the analytical tool used in this project. 
 
2.2 Metabolomic analysis of cancer cells 
The metabolomic analysis is a used term for analysis of a large number of metabolites in a certain 
biological sample; in this case prostate cancerous cell. The analysis of metabolic fingerprints of 
biological systems can give important information on their biochemical phenotypes and on the 
metabolic changes due to stimuli of different nutrients or drugs[7]. Sample preparation is an 
important source of variability in the analysis because it is difficult to quantify for cells. Therefore, 
it is not possible to establish a general method for sample preparation in metabolome analysis[5].  
To detect metabolites and changes in metabolism, it is important to develop a method that is 
sensitive, accurate and reproducible. To develop the most optimized sample preparation method, 
it is important to investigate the various steps in sample preparation for analysis of mammalian 
cell culture to detect the highest amount of metabolites[7][8]. Also, the developed sample 
preparation method should fit the analytical method for detection, which will be dDNP-NMR in 
this project.

Figure 1 summarizes the main steps in sample preparation for analysis of metabolites in extracts 
to maintain valuable information of the cancer cells by the analytical technique, dDNP-NMR. The 
metabolite levels reflect the response of a biological system e.g. glycolysis and tricarboxylic acid

metabolite levels within the response of biological systems, the first step in sample preparation is 
a rapid quenching of all biochemical processes after sample harvesting.  
 
2.2.1 Quenching method 
The quenching procedure step is performed to stop the cellular enzymatic activity of the 
metabolism and prevent the turnover of metabolites[9][8]. The metabolite turnover will mainly 
depend on the metabolite species (e.g. primary or secondary metabolites), and the localization 
(e.g. intra- or extracellular). The intracellular turnover of metabolites is much faster than the 
turnover for the extracellular medium and the intracellular metabolite concentration is usually 
much higher than the extracellular concentration. In addition, most of the primary metabolites 
have an intracellular half-life in order of seconds or less and metabolites formed via secondary 
metabolism are usually accumulated in the cell or are secreted to the extracellular medium, which 
has a much slower turnover. Thus, the primary metabolic reactions are the most critical part of 
quenching cells and the primary metabolites are involved in most of the biochemical reactions and 
have an important role in cellular function. Therefore, most metabolome studies are focused only 
on intracellular metabolites. On the basis of this information, quenching metabolism is a very 
important step to consider during the establishment of a sample preparation method for 
metabolome analysis[5]. Basically, a metabolome analysis represents a snapshot of the in vivo 
metabolic state of a cell in a specific developmental stage and environmental condition[5].  
To inactivate metabolism, there are two general strategies; one of the strategies is quenching and 
extraction of intracellular metabolites that are combined. Typically the quenching results in partial 
extraction of the intracellular metabolites. In this case, intracellular and extracellular metabolites 
will be analyzed together. The second strategy will be quenching followed by separation of the 
biomass containing intracellular metabolites from the extracellular medium. This strategy 
eliminates the extracellular compounds and ends up with analysis of only the intracellular 
metabolites[5].  
In this project, the method will be based on the first strategy where the analysis will be performed 
with intracellular and extracellular metabolites in an extract sample. Therefore, no separation will 
happen between quenching and extraction. Also to mention, in this project, quenching and 
extraction are performed in the same step, but both steps are described to point out the 
differences in quenching and extraction methods.  
==PAGE:16==
Page 15 of 75 
 
The metabolic outcome is dependent on the quenching procedure but also the solutions that are 
used for the quenching. Several quenching methods with different solutions have been used for 
quenching cell culture[7][10][9]. A stop of metabolism is mostly achieved by a fast change in the 
pH value (acidic or alkaline) or temperature(ice cold or hot)[8]. The most commonly used 
quenching method for cell culture samples are based on solutions such as Perchloric Acid (PCA), 
but studies have shown that aqueous solutions containing an organic solvent like methanol 
(MeOH) are a better quencher than PCA[5]. 
For quenching cell activity, there are two procedures to choose between. One of the procedures is 
quenching the cells directly in the cell flask without detaching the cells with trypsinization. The 
other procedure is handled in an Eppendorf tube, where cells need to be detached from the cell 
flask by trypsinization before quenching.   
The issue with harvesting cells by the use of trypsin to detach cells is due to the physiological state 
of the cells, that interacts with membrane proteins[9]. During the trypsinization, there are no 
nutrients and supplements available in the solution, and multiple steps are necessary before cell 
activity is finally quenched. This can cause a small loss of intracellular metabolites, due to the 
different osmotic strength of the applied solution, their small molecular size and their fast 
turnover rate[9][5]. To prevent metabolite losses during trypsinization, several studies have 
investigated the use of a fast direct quenching method with the use of cold MeOH that does not 
contain a separation step of intracellular and extracellular metabolites[9]. Q. Teng et al. 2009[9] 
investigated the method to avoid cellular environment changes caused by trypsin or other foreign 
material and the time-consuming steps using trypsin in PCA method. In addition, the study showed 
that the direct quenching method with cold MeOH showed greater metabolite yield compared to 
the conventional method that uses trypsinization and PCA.  
In this project, the second procedure is used because the small loss of metabolites during 
trypsinization is not an issue. The trypsinization is only done for few minutes during sample 
preparation. The cells will immediately after trypsinization, be provided with nutrient media.   
 
2.2.2 Extraction method  
A typical biological sample contains three general classes of metabolites: water-soluble 
metabolites or polar compounds, water-insoluble metabolites or non-polar compounds, and 
volatile organic metabolites. All types of metabolites can be found intra- or extracellularly, but 
there is no single method to extract all of them, so a specific technique needs to be used to ensure 
minimal loss of metabolites[5]. The next section is about how to obtain the maximum number of 
metabolites by extraction.  
The extraction procedure is performed to complete leakage of the intracellular metabolites in the 
aqueous suspension. Cell extraction makes it possible for the metabolites to be accessible to the 
==PAGE:17==
Page 16 of 75 
 
analytical method, in this project, dDNP-NMR. During extraction, it is not possible to avoid losses 
due to diverse chemical and physical properties of the metabolome.  
Acidic and alkaline extraction is commonly used for the extraction of intracellular metabolites[8]. 
PCA is a polar acid that is commonly used for extraction of nucleotides and other water-soluble 
metabolites, but mainly polar and acid-stable metabolites[11][5]. The procedure is performed on 
ice to keep the temperature low, to avoid degradation of thermo-labile compounds. The PCA will 
react with the cell wall by denaturing most proteins, saponification of lipids in the cell membrane 
and hydrolysis the macromolecular polymer networks. Unfortunately, the extremely low pH is a 
bad condition for the cells, because several metabolites are not stable under this condition. Some 
of the primary metabolites (amino acids and components from the glycolysis and tricarboxylic acid 
cycle (TCA)) may be lost during the extraction, which would like to be avoided[12][5]. After adding 
the PCA, the cells organic waste product is removed from the liquid suspension by centrifugation. 
Thereafter the pH of the liquid suspension is neutralized with potassium hydroxide (KOH), which is 
a polar alkali compound that will stabilize the metabolites. During the pH neutralization process of 
PCA by KOH, salts (KCIO4) are precipitated, which is also removed by centrifugation. It is possible 
that co-precipitation of metabolites can take place during this process. Note: the acid and 
potassium salt of the PCA are both strong oxidizers. With the use of KOH for neutralization, some 
of the nucleotides, sugars and sugar phosphates, basic amino acids and most of the organic acids 
involved in the glycolysis and TCA cycle may be lost. This is caused by the chemical change in the 
suspension or may adsorb to the heavy KCIO4 precipitate[8][11].  
Freeze-drying is used to concentrate, remove water and avoid thermal degradation of the 
metabolites in the extracted sample[8]. 
A study done by Martineau et al. 2011[7] proposed a strategy for choosing extraction methods for 
an NMR-based metabolomic study of mammalian cells. They investigated extraction methods to 
compare the efficiency of different methods and the recovery yield of intracellular metabolites. 
One of the methods was to use 100 % MeOH, which is a weak polar alcohol that extracts polar and 
weak polar metabolites[13] and another one was to use PCA. The result of the study showed that 
there was a difference between MeOH and PCA extraction due to the recovery of metabolites. The 
recovery yield was higher for MeOH extraction than for PCA extraction. In addition, the study also 
indicated that the pH of the extraction solution influenced the extraction of acidic and basic 
metabolites[7]. In fact, the solubility of the metabolites in the extraction solution is related to their 
polarity. If the extraction media is more polar it will dissolve more polar compounds and vice 
versa[8].  
Furthermore, it is difficult to find and make a precise metabolic analysis/extraction protocol 
because it depends on which metabolite or part of the metabolism you want to investigate. Also, 
the extraction of metabolites depends on the analytical tool. Figure 2 below summarizes the 
comparison of gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) analyzed metabolite profiles in 
==PAGE:18==
Page 17 of 75 
 
extracts obtained using five different extraction methods. It is clear that all extraction methods 
have their strengths and limitations for detection of a certain metabolite. Lactate as an example 
varies between the different extraction methods. Water extraction detects the highest amount of 
lactate than the other methods. This indicates it is very important to make an extraction method 
that can extract the metabolites you want to investigate along with the analytical tool.

 Evaluate the main steps in sample preparation for metabolic analysis of prostate cancer cells to

studies.

2.3 Metabolic transformation of prostate cells 
Prostate cancer is the most common type of cancer in males and the second leading cause of 
death[14]. The prostate cancer is developed in the prostate gland[15]. The prostate gland is 
divided into three major glandular regions, the peripheral zone, the central zone and the transition 
zone. The central zone is relatively resistant to carcinoma and other diseases, but the two other 
regions can develop cancer[16]. 80 % of the cancer lesions can be developed in the periphery 
zone, which is the most common and the remaining lesions are found in the transition zone, which 
is located in the periurethral region. The prostate has a unique metabolism that changes during 
tumor onset and progression from the prostate intraepithelial neoplasia to metastasis[15].  
The main function of the prostate gland is to produce and secrete a large amount of citrate that 
will be secreted from the epithelial cells into the lumen, which can be seen in figure 3. The citrate 
is produced by a specific type of glandular epithelial cells in the prostate and it is not observable in 
any other kind of cells. The prostate cells modify the epithelial cells by a transformation of the 
energy metabolism[15].  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The normal prostate cells inhibit the oxidative phosphorylation and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) 
cycle for releasing a large amount of citrate, which can be seen in figure 3 and 4. The prostate cells 
consume the glucose and catalyze it through the glycolysis to obtain pyruvate. The produced 
pyruvate from the glycolysis is decarboxylated to TCA enzymes and to acetyl co-enzyme A (acetyl-
CoA) in the TCA cycle under anaerobic conditions[4], which is combined with the oxaloacetate to 
produce citrate. The citrate is then oxidized through TCA cycle and ends up as an end product 
instead of intermediate after leaving the TCA cycle, see figure 4.  Despite a large amount of citrate 
in the cell, the zinc level in normal prostate cells is very high. The function of zinc accumulation in 
the mitochondria of the normal prostate cells is to truncate the TCA cycle by inhibiting m-

aconitase, which is the enzyme that is responsible for the citrate oxidation, and it is also the first

The early development of prostate cancer cells makes the cells more energy efficient by modifying 
the metabolism. The epithelial cells are transforming to develop cancer cells. The prostate cancer 
cells corrupt associated fibroblasts (CAFS) to activate the Warburg effect that leads to a secretion 
of lactate instead of citrate that is used by cancer cells for anabolic (synthesis) and catabolic 
(breakdown) processes[15]. The Warburg effect (aerobic glycolysis) increases the flux of glucose 
through the glycolysis and down-regulate the flux through the TCA cycle in cancer cells[4]. The 
production and secretion of lactate to tumor microenvironment via MCT4 transporters by the 
glycolysis is known to increase tumor invasion, but it can also be an alternative carbon source for 
the surrounding oxygenated cells[17][18]. The high rate of lactate leads to the acidification of the 
tumor environment[14]. Thus, it is possible that the cancer cells lipid synthesis may function as a 
carbon sink to hide or isolate excess pyruvate and avoid lactate production to equilibrate the 
intracellular pH[14]. The lactate can be transported to other neighboring cancer cells and taken up 
by MCT1 importers where it is used for other metabolic pathways in tumors[18] e.g. lactate helps 
to regenerate NADH and accelerate glycolysis[15]; this facilitates the survival of cancer cells in 
harsh conditions[18]. The CAFS fibroblasts induce the phosphorylation in cancer cells to oxidize

2.4 Nuclear magnetic Resonance

local magnetic fields around atomic nuclei. NMR investigates the molecular structure, which gives

provide better diagnostic information compared to a similar technique called magnetic resonance 
imaging (MRI) used at the hospital[21].

2.4.3 Precession 
Nuclei spin around their own axis, but when the magnetic field is applied they start changing 
direction. The nuclei will align with the magnetic field and start wobble around the axis of the 
applied magnetic field. The wobbling motion is called precession. The magnetic moment of a 
spinning nucleus also precesses with an angular frequency ω, called the Larmor frequency, which 
is a function of γ and B0. The frequency is given by: ω = γ*B0[21]. This can be seen in figure 7 
below. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
When a nucleus in the lower spin state absorbs a photon, which is a radio frequency (rf) wave, it 
will afterward be transformed into a nucleus in the higher-energy spin state. For this to occur, the 
energy gap between these two spin states must equal the energy of the absorbed photon for 
absorption of radiation, where more particles must be in the lower-energy state than in the higher 
one. If these two populations happen to be equal, the condition is saturated, where a transition 
from the upper state to the lower state likely to occur as absorption. If a systems energy levels at 
thermal equilibrium, there will always be more particles in the lower state than the upper 
state[21]. This condition is known as the Boltzmann distribution:

2.4.4 Relaxation 
When a Boltzmann distribution of nuclei is precessing in a magnetic field it changes direction, and 
start precessing around an external magnetic field seen in figure 8 (a). When the radiation rf (radio 
frequency pulse) starts, the nuclei become phase coherent. It tracks the oscillating magnetic field 
of the radiation and precesses around the z-axis with the Larmor frequency, see figure 8 (b). So, 
the M vector in x,y-plane determines the net magnetization and the measured signal[21]. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The magnetization of M in x,y-plane is determined by the tipping angle α, which occurs with the z-
axis, see figure 8 (b). The precessing of the nuclei in x,y-plane and back to equilibrium generates an 
oscillating magnetic field. This can be registered by a receiver coil, which induces an alternating 
current within the wire. This alternating current is the precessional frequency of the nuclei. This 
signal needs to be amplified and plotted as a function of irradiation frequency, which is resulting in 
a free induction decay (FID) to generate an NMR spectrum. When the radio frequency pulse is off, 
the system can turn back to the Boltzmann distribution along the z-axis and lose their phase 
coherence, which is governed by the longitudinal relaxation time T1. This relaxation causes decay 
of the net magnetization on the x,y-plane controlled by the spin-spin relaxation time T2. The value 
of T1 ranges broadly, depending on the type of nucleus and the nucleus location within a molecule. 
The larger the value of T1 is, the longer a collection of nuclei takes to reach or return to thermal 
equilibrium. The value of T1 determines the acquisition parameters when making an NMR 
spectrum[21].

2.4.6 Spin-spin coupling 
When a 13C NMR spectrum or FID signal is examined, it is the splitting that arises from the protons 
that are directly attached to the carbon that is studied. This is a one-bond coupling. It is rare to 
observe homonuclear (carbon-carbon) spin-spin splitting patterns where the interaction occurs 
between two 13C atoms because of its low natural abundance. However, the use of an isotopically 
labeled 13C atom makes it possible to obtain a 13C-13C split pattern, see figure 9 below. The spins of 
protons attached directly to 13C atoms do interact with the spin of carbon and cause the carbon 
signal to be split. This is called heteronuclear (carbon-hydrogen) coupling involving two different 
types of atoms[20]. 
When a 13C atom is attached to three protons it splits into a quartet, and each spin combination 
interacts with 13C carbon to give a different peak of the multiplet. The splitting can be predicted by 
the N+1 rule, N = number of protons. For each carbon, the multiplicity of the signal depends upon 
how many protons are attached to it. Example; one carbon shows a singlet, one carbon + 
hydrogen shows a doublet etc[20]. 
==PAGE:27==

The case before with the 13C atom attached to three protons will have a split of a quartet by (n + 1 
= 3 + 1 = 4). Since the hydrogen’s are directly attached to the 13C carbon, the coupling constants (J 
values) for the interaction are quite large. The coupling constant is the distance between the lines 
of a multiplet, measured in Hz[20].  
A 13C NMR spectrum will not show the protons on the spectrum, but observing the effect of the 
protons on 13C carbon atoms. Spectra that show spin-spin splitting or coupling, between 13C 
carbon and protons directly attached to it are called proton-coupled or sometimes nondecoupled. 
Using proton-decoupled spectra techniques simplifies the spectrum and avoids overlapping 
multiplets because it removes all interactions between protons and 13C nuclei, therefore only 
singlets will be observable[20]. The protons are removed by a decoupler. Irradiation causes the 
protons to become saturated, and undergo rapid upward and downward transitions, among all 
their possible spin states. This will decouple any spin-spin interactions between hydrogen’s and 13C 
nuclei that are observed. This technique has a disadvantaged because the information on attached 
hydrogen’s is lost[20]. Due to 13C labeling, it is possible to observe Jc-c coupling on the spectrum. It 
will show a doublet with a coupling constant around 35 Hz (C-C) or 55 Hz (C-CO)[23].

2.5.1 Dissolution DNP 
dDNP-NMR is a method to develop hyperpolarization in a liquid state by preserving its nuclear 
polarization after the sample has been cooled down to about 1 K and then brought into a warm 
solution that is transferred to a high-resolution NMR spectrometer for detection[24][6]. The 
polarization of a sample is performed in a DNP polarizer that consists of a magnet (3.35 T) and a 
sample space with cooled liquid-helium less than 2 K (-271.15 oC). The sample is placed in a sample 
cup, which is inserted in the DNP polarizer with a loading stick. Inside the polarizer, a microwave 
source is used to transfer electron spin resonance to the 13C spins in the sample, which take 1.5-2 
hours. For preserving the nuclear polarization in the sample, the sample is dissolved by an 
injection system inside the DNP magnet. The sample is then transferred to an NMR tube and then 
to the high-resolution NMR spectrometer[6]. This method is described in Ardenkjær-Larsen et al. 
(2003)[6].

3.1 Cell lines 
The cell lines used in this project are DU-145 and PNT-1A. The DU-145 cell line was isolated from a 
brain lesion of metastatic prostate carcinoma. The metastases from the central nervous system 
was isolated by Kenneth R. Stone et al. 1978[27] from a patient with metastatic carcinoma of 
prostate. The patient had a three-year period of lymphocytic leukemia. DU-145 is not hormone-
sensitive, which means it is androgen independent. The cell line is very aggressive and is widely 
used in experiments to investigate different aspects of prostate cancer. The PNT-1A cell line is 
derived from normal prostatic epithelial cells in the beginning of cancer development. The cell line 
have been proven to be a good model for analysis cellular processes as the prostatic epithelium 
proliferation in response to androgens[28]. Both of the cell lines are immortalized and biosafety 
classified at level 1.   
 
3.2 Cell culture and sub culturing 
The cells are cultured in T75 flasks containing RPMI 1640 medium mixed with fetal bovine serum 
(FBS) and pen-strep (penicillin and streptomycin) at 37 oC in a humidified atmosphere of 5 % CO2. 
The RPMI 1640 medium contains 2 g/L Sodium Bicarbonate, 0.3 g/L L-Glutamine and 2 g/L D-
Glucose[29]. The cell culture is started from a frozen vial and cultured in a T75 flask. If more cells 
are needed, the cells will be transferred to a T175. For harvesting, the cells need to be above 75 % 
confluent for splitting, but for further studies the cells need to be at a higher degree of confluence 
approx. 90-100 %.   
For sub culturing/splitting the cells, it starts by discarding the medium, and then rinsing the cells 
twice with phosphate buffer (PBS). Afterwards, the cells are harvested by trypsination. Trypsin is 
added for detaching the cells from the surface of the cell flask and incubated for 2-3 min at 37 oC. 
After incubation, fresh medium is added for stopping the cell protease activity. The cells are then 
centrifuged for 3 min at 200 rpm. After centrifugation, the cells are re-suspended in an 
appropriate volume of fresh media. Before splitting the cells into new T75 culture flask, cell 
counting is needed. 40 µL of cell suspension is transferred to an Eppendorf tube and 30 µL cell 
suspension is mixed with 30 µL of trypan blue (Sigma Aldrich). After mixing, it is transferred to the 
cell counting plate and the plate is then placed in a Hemocytometer for counting. The viability 
should be between 90-100 %. After cell counting, the appropriate volume of the cell suspension is 
transferred to new T75 cell culture flasks and incubated for 2-3 days at 37 oC.

3.3 Freeze culture  
Freeze cultures are made for DU-145 cell line by the following procedure. First the cells are grown 
to confluence in a T75 flask and then treated by the harvest procedure. The medium from the T75 
flask are discarded and the cells are rinsed twice with PBS. 5 mL trypsin is added to the T75 flask 
for detaching the cells from the bottom of the flask and placed for incubation for 3 min at 37 oC. 
For neutralizing the cell protease activity, fresh medium is added to the T75 flask. The cell 
suspension is transferred to a Falcon 50 mL tube for centrifuging. The cells are centrifuged at 200 
rpm for 3 min. The supernatant is discarded. 5 mL PBS are added for rinsing the cells again. The 
cells are again centrifuged at 200 rpm for 3 min. The supernatant is discarded. 1 mL DMSO is 
added together with 9 mL serum free medium and added to the cell pellet. Before 20 min is 
passed, 1 mL containing approx. 2-3 million cells is transferred to 10 Nunc CryoTubes. The 
CryoTubes are transferred to a Cryofreezing container and placed in a – 80 oC freezer. For longer 
storage the Cryotubes are transferred to a liquid nitrogen tank.  
 
3.4 Preparation of extracts 
3.4.1 PCA quenching and extraction 
Extracts are performed on DU-145 cell line with same amounts of cells and incubation time. The 
cells are grown to confluence approx. 90-100 % and harvested for counting on Hemocytometer. 
The cells are centrifuged at 200 rpm for 3 min and dissolved in 500 µL 40 mM phosphate buffer 
with 10 million cells. The 500 µL phosphate buffer with cells is transferred to a 1.5 mL Eppendorf 
tube and placed on a Shaking thermostat at 37 oC. After 3 min 100 µL 120 mM U-[13C, D] glucose is 
added to the cell solution and incubated for 30 min at 37 oC. After 30 min the cells are quenched 
with 400 µL 2.2 M ice cold PCA. The sample is placed on ice for 10 min and then centrifuged for 10 
min at 10.000 rpm and 4oC. The supernatant is transferred to a new 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube and 60 
µL universal indicator is added to the sample. For neutralizing the sample, 10 M KOH is added to 
get a green color. After neutralizing, the sample is put on ice for 10 min and again centrifuged for 
10 min at 10.000 rpm and 4oC. Finally, the supernatant is transferred to a new Eppendorf tube 
with holes in the lids made with a needle. The sample is dipped in liquid nitrogen for 5 min and 
then it is freeze-dried at 0.06 mbar and -80 oC.  
3.4.2 MeOH quenching and extraction 
After harvesting and counting cells, the cells are incubated in 30 min with 120 mM U-[13C, D] 
glucose. After incubation with glucose the cells are quenched with MeOH. For quenching, 1 mL 
cold 100 % MeOH is added to the sample. The sample is then dipped in liquid nitrogen 3X and 
warmed in hands between the dips. The sample is centrifuged for 10 min at 10.000 rpm and 4oC. 
The supernatant is transferred to a new 2 mL Eppendorf tube® and dried on SpeedVac at 60 oC. A 
detailed figure with PCA and MeOH extraction can be seen in section 9.4 in appendix.

3.7 Thermal NMR detection 
After freeze-drying the extract consists of a concentrated powder or by drying on SpeedVac the 
metabolites are attached to the tube surface. For preparing the sample, 500 µL 40 mM phosphate 
buffer pH = 7.3 is added to the tube containing the metabolites. Then 75 µL D2O, 15 µL (0.5

3.8 DNP NMR detection 
After freeze-drying the extract consists of a concentrated powder or by drying on SpeedVac the 
metabolites are attached to the tube surface. For hyperpolarization, the extract was dissolved in 
150 µL polarization medium (section 9.1 in appendix) and 5 µL 50 mM HPO1 as reference. The 
content is mixed together by spinning it down, using a vortex and an ultrasonic bath and then 
transferred to a sample cup. The DNP was performed at 3.35 T in HyperSense DNP polarizer. The 
sample were dissolved with 5 mL 40 mM phosphate buffer with a pH = 7.3 (section 9.1 in 
appendix) after heating to 175 oC. The optimal polarization time is 1.5 under reasonableness 
conditions, decided in [22]. After heating and dissolution, the sample is transferred to a 5 mm 
NMR tube and into the 9.4 T NMR spectrometer instrument. Transfer and injection time is 
between 12-15 sec and the pulse was adjusted to 70 degrees.  
 
3.9 Data analysis 
3.9.1 Mnova  
When the spectra have been acquired, the fid-file is loaded into program MestReNova (Mnova) 
version 11.01-17801. The data is processed in this program to analyze the data properly. When the 
fid-file is loaded, phase correction and baseline correction (equal to 0) were performed. 
Apodization along t1 is also applied to the data for attenuate the noise and causing an increase of 
the signal-to-noise ration. The exponential apodization frequency was set to 3 Hz. The internal 
reference (HP01) was set to 23.7 ppm and the peaks on the spectra were manually integrated 
relative to the internal reference.   
 
3.9.2 Statistic 
The recovery was handled on Microsoft Excel. For this purpose, the average and the standard 
deviation are calculated for further analysis. This is done using the internal standard (HP01) peak 
on the spectra. The internal standard is used because the amount of HP01 added to the sample is 
known. The only variation that may come depends on the polarization on the hypersense. To find 
the recovery values, the integral of the peak area from a metabolite is divided by the integral area 
of the HP01 peak.  
                          
          
 
Further analysis of the recovery, will be done by calculating the average and standard deviation 
(average ± sd) for quantification.

4.2 Recovery of spiked metabolites in dry samples 
A number of experiments were conducted to find a solution to recover pyruvate by MeOH 
extraction. Pyruvate is an important metabolite, because it is a part of the central carbon 
metabolism in cells. Table 7 summarizes the drying procedure experiments that were performed 
to detect pyruvate. The experiments were performed with varying volume, temperature and 
MeOH % concentration. Respective spectra and quantification of metabolites can be found in 
section 9.5.5 in appendix.  
The four different drying experiments performed in this experiment are; dry air, SpeedVac, 
Rotavapor and freeze-dry. Dry air is a method where the sample is drying under a stream of air at 
room temperature. The problem with this method is the air pressure. You have to be careful 
because the air pressure can be too high and it will blow sample out of the tube. This would cause 
a loss of sample.  
SpeedVac is used to concentrate small volume samples under vacuum. The vacuum removes easily 
the solvent and the sample with metabolites will remain in the solid phase. In addition, the 
temperature can be controlled by choice and dry the sample fast.  
Rotavapor is a method where a sample is evaporated under reduced pressure by a vacuum system 
and a heated fluid bath. A condenser with a coil surrounded by ice is used to cool down the steam 
from the liquid sample. The idea is to lower the boiling point for a solvent. The temperature, 
vacuum pressure, and rotational speed can be controlled. A disadvantage in rotavapor is some 
samples types can bump, e.g. water, which can result in loss of the sample[30].  
Freeze-dry is a method that reduces the surrounding pressure of a frozen sample to let the frozen 
water go from the solid phase to the gas phase. The only problem with freeze-drying with MeOH 
in high concentrations is due to its low concentration of water.

As mentioned in cell line section, DU-145 cells are in the metastatic state and are very aggressive. 
PNT-1A cells are in the early state of cancer development and are not aggressive as DU-145. The 
cell lines derived from different parts of the human body. The cell lines show different 
characteristic and behavior, according to other scientist and studies investigated on these cell 
lines. When the cells proliferate it is due to the nutrient supply which affects the growth response 
and which metabolites the cells produce. Thus, to observe a cell growth response in a cell line, it is

changed nutrient. The experiment was made to grow DU-145 and PNT-1A cells under certain

5. 
Discussion 
5.1 Extraction method  
The results from quenching prostate cancer cells (DU-145) with cold MeOH solution did not tend 
to show higher amount of metabolites or new metabolites compared to perchloric acid as 
described in the literature[9][7]. There was a higher increase in the number and concentration of 
observable peaks in the spectrum when perchloric acid was used to extract metabolites compared 
to MeOH. This result was not surprising since the solubility of metabolites is related to their 
polarity. The intensities of several signals corresponding to organic acids as pyruvate, acetate etc. 
are increasing in the spectra obtained after the use of perchloric acid. This indicates that the pH 
value of a solvent influences the extraction of acidic and basic metabolites. 
The results from the total metabolite extraction with cold MeOH showed extraction of 
intracellular metabolites. This indicates the cancer cells were sensitive to the cold MeOH, which 
was expected due to the literature. However, there was hardly any detection of pyruvate in most 
of the experiment done with MeOH. Additionally, it was difficult or not possible to recover spiked 
pyruvate in the drying experiments. It was difficult to recover pyruvate even when the MeOH 
concentrations were changed from low to high concentrations. During the drying experiments, the 
pH value of the mixed metabolite stock solutions was changed from an acidic pH value to a neutral 
pH value. This was done to observe any changes of the discovery of pyruvate and other 
metabolites. It did have a small impact on recovery of metabolites, but still no detection of 
pyruvate. The temperature of drying the extract samples did not have any impact on the 
recovering of metabolites. Furthermore, a small sample volume with high concentration of MeOH 
is better to obtain detection of metabolites.  
All these experiments can indicate that pyruvate might be unstable during extraction or it 
disappears in contact with cold MeOH. This was the same result for S. G. Villas-Boâs et al. 2005[8] 
that experienced no detection of pyruvate after quenching yeast cells.  
Pyruvate is an important metabolite in this analysis of prostate cancer cells because it is a main 
part of the glycolytic metabolism. Pyruvate is produced by the glycolysis which in the end of the 
glycolysis is converted to lactate. Then the lactate is transported out of the cells. This is done to 
facilitates the survival of cancer cells in a harsh environment[18].  
The bad recovery and detection of metabolites with cold MeOH solution does not exclude the use 
of this method in metabolome analysis for mammalian cells. It depends on what type of 
metabolites (acidic or basic) that are investigated in the metabolism of the certain cell. However, 
this method with cold MeOH seems not to be suitable for precise analysis of metabolites in 
prostate cancer cells due to this experiment. Table 16 below shows the strengths and limitations 
for choosing the MeOH method versus perchloric acid method for mammalian cells.

5.2 Nutritional supply 
5.2.1 Metabolic fingerprints of glucose growth  
The results obtained from the experiment with metabolic fingerprints of DU-145 cells and PNT-1A 
cells grown with high and low glucose concentrations can be seen in figure 22 with bar charts of 
produced metabolites from PNT-1A and DU-145. From these results, it is evident that the 
metabolic fingerprints of PNT-1A cells and DU-145 cells are not sensitive to different glucose 
concentration. However, there is a difference in the production of metabolites between the cell 
lines. This might due to the cell type and the cancer stage they are developed from. 
These results do not correlate with the growth curve of glucose experiment in figure 19.   
As shown in figure 22 PNT-1A cells produce more glutamate and alanine compared to DU-145 cells 
even when it is grown on high or low glucose concentrations. This might indicate that more of the 
pyruvate for PNT-1A cells is transferred to the amino acid synthesis and the glutaminolysis through 
the TCA cycle compared to DU-145 cells. DU-145 cells produce more lactate than PNT-1A cells 
which indicate more of the produced pyruvate for DU-145 cells is directly converted to lactate and 
transported out of the cells. DU-145 cells are metastatic cells and metastatic cells shut down the 
oxidative phosphorylation and TCA cycle, which means the citrate, is not oxidized further in the 
TCA cycle. A large amount of lactate is correlated to the shutdown of the oxidative 
phosphorylation and TCA cycle. DU-145 cells still produce a small amount of glutamate which can 
correlate to the growth medium which contains glutamine that is converted to glutamate in the 
glutaminolysis in the cell.  
As mention in the theory part section 2.3 of metabolic changes of prostate cancer cells, a large 
production and secretion of lactate to tumor microenvironment by the glycolysis is known to 
increase tumor invasion. Hereafter, the lactate can be transported to other cancer cells and used 
for other metabolic pathways in a tumor. This can help the cancer cells to survive under harsh 
conditions. This information fits the results obtained for DU-145 cells, because it is a very 
aggressive cancer cell line [22-23]. PNT-1A cells are not aggressive like DU-145 cells because the 
PNT-1A cells are in the early stage of developing cancer and that might be the cause of more 
production of alanine and glutamate and less production of lactate. This observation was in line 
with the theory of prostate cancer stages in section 3.1.  
When normal prostate cells are transforming into cancer cells they start the oxidative 
phosphorylation and TCA cycle. This means the citrate is oxidized further in the TCA cycle to 
produce more energy in the form of ATP.  The TCA cycle is combined to the glutaminolysis which is 
essential for the production of glutamate.  This information can correlates to the larger production 
of glutamate for PNT-1A cells because they are in the newly developed stage of cancer cells.

5.2.2 Metabolic fingerprints of high glucose and acetate growth 
The results obtained from growth with high glucose and acetate medium of DU-145 cells and PNT-
1A cells indicated that there were no big differences in the metabolic fingerprint and the peak 
pattern on the spectra.  
The growth on acetate medium showed proliferation and this observation can tell us that DU-145 
cells and PNT-1A cells can grow and proliferate in an environment of acetate medium along with 
glutamine because the cells did not die. It might be the same for PNT-1A since it grows very well 
on acetate and glutamine medium see figure 23. This correlates to the growth curve of DU-145 
and PNT-1A grown on acetate medium see figure 23. 
Acetate can be consumed by the lipid synthesis[14] and converted to the intermediate 
metabolites Acetyl-CoA and acetyl-carnitine. It is expected to observe peaks in the metabolic 
fingerprints of Acetyl-CoA and acetyl-carnitine in the spectra. Unfortunately, there was no 
detection of the intermediate metabolites on the spectra for DU-145 cells and PNT-1A cells. No 
detection can be a sign of acetate did not convert into the lipid synthesis in the two cell lines. This 
can be correlated to the acetate consumption figure 26, which showed high acetate concentration 
for both cell lines. Another reason can be correlated to the glutamine in the growth medium. 
Maybe the cell lines prefer using glutamine instead of acetate for survival. A study on glutamine 
dependence showed that DU-145 was strongly glutamine dependent[32]. 
Finally, there was no observation of metabolic fingerprints that could be correlated to the 
biomarker acetate. To observe any changes in the metabolic fingerprints of the cell lines grown on 
acetate medium, it would be better to use a glucose biomarker.

6. 
Conclusion 
This work aimed to evaluate two commonly used sample preparation methods in the metabolic 
field of prostate cancer cells by the analytical tool dDNP-NMR. For instance, the use of MeOH in 
this study was not sufficient compared to the method with perchloric which is already used in this 
laboratory. It was clear from the recoveries of metabolites obtained from different experiments 
that MeOH was not an adequate cell extraction method to obtain a good metabolic fingerprint of 
prostate cancer cells in this study. MeOH could not detect and recover pyruvate, which is a very 
important metabolite in the metabolism of a cell. However, the aim of the study was not only to 
detect pyruvate but to analyze the metabolic fingerprint of DU-145 cell line. This indicates that 
perchloric acid as an extraction method for prostate cancer cells is the adequate extraction 
method to obtain a good metabolic fingerprint.  
It was shown that a change in nutritional supply had a very small effect on the metabolic 
fingerprints of DU-145 cells and PNT-1A cells. There was nearly any change of the metabolic 
fingerprint of DU-145 and PNT-1A grown on high and low glucose concentrations. However, there 
was a significant difference in the concentration of the metabolite outcome between the cell lines. 
This is due to the different metabolic stages of the cell lines.  
Cells grown on acetate medium did not show any metabolic fingerprints that were different from 
the acetate reference. There were no peaks of acetyl-CoA and acetyl-carnitine on the spectra. 
However, the cell lines DU-145 and PNT-1A can still grow with acetate medium along with 
glutamine. To see any metabolic fingerprints of the cell lines DU-145 and PNT-1A, another 
biomarker must be used e.g. glucose. This indicates that acetate is not a good biomarker because 
there was no change in the peak patterns of the metabolic fingerprints in the different cell lines 
and growth media with high glucose concentration and acetate compared to the acetate 
reference.

7. 
Perspective 
Based on the results from DU-145 cells and PNT-1A cells grown on the high and low glucose 
concentrations, more experiments are needed. Especially, for the growth with high glucose 
concentration with PNT-1A which only had one sample to compare with two samples from low 
glucose concentration. It could be good to see if new experiments can show similar metabolic 
fingerprints as the previous experiments. Additionally, if it will show the difference in metabolic 
fingerprints grown on high and low glucose concentrations as the growth experiment did in figure 
19.  
In future research, it could be useful to grow the cells in acetate medium with glutamine and use 
glucose as a biomarker to observe any metabolic fingerprints that might show a different 
metabolic pattern. Additionally, grow cells only on acetate medium without glutamine to observe 
if the cells consume acetate for survival.  
Finally, it might be interesting to make growth studies based on different fatty acids to investigate 
the metabolic fingerprints of the lipid synthesis and how prostate cancer cells in different cancer 
stages take advantageous of fatty acids to survive in a certain environment.

9.2 Appendix 2 
Various media: 
The different medias are stored in the fridge, and penstrep and trypsin are stored at -20 oC and are thawed 
to room temperature or 37 oC before use.  
RPMI medium: 
RPMI medium – 1640 medium (R8758 – 500 mL) with 2 g/L D-Glucose and 0.3 g/L L-Glutamine. 
Description of RPMI medium can be read on the page from Sigma Aldrich®[29].  
Minimal medium: 
Minimal medium is used for neutralization of trypsination and dilute counted cells for extraction. 
For this, DMEM Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium base without D-glucose, L-glutamine, Phenol 
red and sodium pyruvate (D5030 – 500 mL) is used. For 250 mL neutral medium:  
2.5 mL (2 mM) L-glutamine is mixed with 2.5 mL penstrep, 25 mL FBS and 220 mL DMEM medium.  
Low and high glucose medium: 
The low and high glucose medium is made with DMEM Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium base 
without D-glucose, L-glutamine, Phenol red and sodium pyruvate (D5030 – 500 mL). In the low 
glucose medium the concentration of D-glucose is 2 mM and the concentration of D-glucose in the 
==PAGE:66==
Page 65 of 75 
 
high glucose medium is 10 mM.  
A 50 mL mixture of minimal medium for two flask: 
2.5 mL D-glucose (2 mM or 10 mM) is mixed with 0.5 mL (2 mM) L-glutamine, 0.5 mL penstrep, 5 
mL FBS and 41.5 mL DMEM. The concentrations are the final concentration in the medium.  
Acetate medium: 
The acetate medium is made with DMEM Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium base without D-
glucose, L-glutamine, Phenol red and sodium pyruvate (D5030 – 500 mL). A 50 mL mixture of 
minimal medium for two flask: 
0.5 mL acetate (2 mM) is mixed with 0.5 mL (2 mM) L-glutamine, 0.5 mL penstrep, 5 mL FBS and 
43.5 mL DMEM. The concentrations are the final concentration in the medium. 
Antibiotics – Penstrep: 
Penicillin – Streptomycin (P4333 – 100 mL). The penicillin and streptomycin is 10 000 U/mL regard 
to penicillin and 10 mg/mL regard to streptomycin. Sterile filtered.  
Trypsin: 
Trypsin-EDTA Solution 0.5 % (10X). 1 mL of trypsin is mixed with 9 mL PBS without Ca2+ and Mg2+ 
and then added to a 15 mL Falcon tube. The trypsin-EDTA solution (0.02 – 0.05 %) is needed to 
detach adherent cells on the cell flask surface within 5 minutes. 
Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS): 
Fetal Bovine Serum (F7525 – 500 mL) sterile filteret. The serum is thawed and divided onto 10 X 50 
mL Falcon tubes. One tube with 50 mL FBS is mixed with 500 mL medium, which makes the 
medium 10 % with regard to serum.
