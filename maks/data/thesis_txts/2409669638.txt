Executive Summary
The literature has mostly focused on the technical side of project cost, investigating material
and manufacturing costs. However, a signiﬁcant cost factor of engineering projects are
engineering efforts. Since engineering efforts are determined by various factors that may
change over the project, it is essential to incorporate, simulate, and analyze these uncer-
tainties. This paper provides practitioners with a framework that can help them to select
the best suited cost estimation technique, reﬂect on major cost drivers and uncertainty
factors, simulate uncertainties, and ﬁnally, determine project schedule and costs.
Addressing the research gap and assisting practitioners
There is numerous research on cost estimation, focusing on the technical side but
limited research on how to estimate engineering efforts. Further, there a various
factors that are afﬁliated with uncertainties and hence, inﬂuence engineering
efforts. Therefore, the framework takes a holistic approach on estimating engi-
neering efforts by determining inﬂuential factors and incorporating uncertainties.
Exploratory case study and structured literature review
The main cost estimation techniques, generic cost driver and uncertainty factors
were determined by a structured literature review. It further led to the proposed
framework including a simulation to incorporate and analyze uncertainties. An
exploratory case study was conducted in order to obtain input from practitioners
and prove the real-world applicability of the framework. To perform the valida-
tion, a tool was developed that is based on the principles of the framework.
Framework
The proposed framework guides practitioners from the selection of the most
suited cost estimation technique to the project schedule and costs. It contains
generic cost drivers and uncertainty factors that have an inﬂuential impact on
engineering efforts. Part of the framework is a Monte Carlo simulation and a
sensitivity analysis in order to incorporate, simulate, and analyze uncertainties.
By choosing a conﬁdence-/risk-proﬁle for the project, a project schedule and costs
can be determined by the framework.
Findings and conclusion
The framework offers a novel approach on project cost estimation and is appli-
cable for any company with system engineering efforts. Since the framework
is dynamic, the company can grow with the framework. The simulation of the
impact of uncertainties and sensitivity analysis on input variables allows project
managers to assess and evaluate on the risks of cost overruns. Lastly, deploying
the framework to the case ﬁrm, showed a signiﬁcant improvement of cost estima-
tion accuracy. The accuracy could be improved from a initial 20% underestimation
to an 8% overestimation by the framework.

Abstract
Purpose — Project cost estimation are mostly concerned about incurred cost, such
as material and manufacturing. It often focuses only on the technical side of
project costs, whereas all too often engineering efforts are underestimated and
thus, leading to inaccurate estimates. The difﬁculty about estimating engineer-
ing efforts is the complexity of inﬂuential factors, resulting in a high degree of
uncertainty. Therefore, the purpose of this paper is to identify cost drivers and
uncertainty factors for engineering projects, and methods to incorporate, simulate,
and analyze uncertainties.
Design/methodology/approach — The research for this paper was conducted,
using a structured literature review and an exploratory case study. The literature
review aimed to identify a structured process that is applicable for practitioners
to estimate engineering efforts in a repeatable way, incorporating uncertainties.
Whereas the case study helped to focus on both real-world applicability and the
prove of concept.
Findings — The paper proposes a framework that allows companies to select the
most suitable cost estimation technique, including the identiﬁcation of major cost
drivers and uncertainty factors, and consequently, incorporate uncertainties to
their estimates.
Research limitations/implications — The provided theoretical framework is holis-
tic which makes it applicable to any ﬁrm, dealing with engineering projects. Even
though the framework is dynamic and the company can grow with the framework,
it requires project-speciﬁc adaptations.
Practical implications — The overview of cost estimation techniques, catego-
rized cost driver, and uncertainty factors along the project life cycle constitute a
decision-making framework for practitioners. Using the framework, practitioners
can assess the most suitable cost estimation approach for their projects.
Originality/value — Most papers, concerning estimation of project costs, address
technical-related costs. There is limited research on estimating engineering ef-
forts, especially on incorporating, simulating, and analyzing uncertainties. Cost
drivers and uncertainty factors are either industry speciﬁc or not speciﬁed at
all. Therefore, this paper contributes to ﬁlling the gap by examining generic cost
drivers, uncertainty factors, and simulating uncertainties with a novel, theoretical
framework.
Keywords Engineering efforts; cost estimation techniques; cost driver; uncertainty
factor; Monte Carlo simulation; sensitivity analysis; project schedule and costs;
COSYSMO

Introduction
The increased complexity of product structures together with developing and
managing engineering projects under time and resource constraints, are today’s
challenges for engineers and managers (Shi et al., 2015). In addition, globalization
has increased competition on engineering projects being put up for bidding (Yan
et al., 2004). This has forced companies to make quotes rapid and as accurate as
possible with limited information available in order to win the bid (Dan and Zhi-
guo, 2012). On one hand, an underestimation of project costs results in ﬁnancial
losses for the company and potentially for its stakeholders, and might prolong
the completion time. On the other hand, an overestimation of project costs might
lead to lose customers to competitors (Caputo and Pelagagge, 2008; Hooshmand,
Köhler, and Korff-Krumm, 2016). Thus, it is essential for companies to estimate
their project costs as accurate as possible under consideration and incorporation
of uncertainties since both over- and underestimation have strong consequences
(Muench, 2016).
There is numerous research on project cost estimation, focusing more on the tech-
nical side where costs or time are afﬁliated to single parts or components along the
work breakdown structure (WBS) (not exhaustive; Caputo and Pelagagge, 2008;
Tu, Xie, and Fung, 2007; Wang et al., 2010; Wei and Wang, 2009). However, there
is limited research on management and engineering costs of projects (Dillibabu
and Krishnaiah, 2005; Valerdi, 2005). Further, the literature speaks about cost
drivers (CDs) and uncertainty factors (UFs), and about their inﬂuence on costs
but they are either not speciﬁed at all, only applicable to one project/industry
or doesn’t connect CDs to UFs (not exhaustive; Dillibabu and Krishnaiah, 2005;
Kwok, Anderson, and Ng, 2010; Marzouk and Ahmed, 2011; Membah and Asa,
2015; Valerdi, 2005). Although an overview of the main cost estimation techniques
(CET) has been made by Niazi et al., 2006, there is no research that summarizes
the required input data for each technique. In addition, there is no general link
from CET to Monte Carlo simulation in order to model and analyze the effect of
uncertainties on engineering efforts. Research has been done on the single pieces
of the puzzle, but there is no framework that connects the single pieces in order
to provide guidance to practitioners.
Consequently, the focus of this paper will be to establish a framework that guides
practitioners from the selection of the right CET, including CDs and UFs, to
simulating the project schedule and costs for engineering efforts along the project
life cycle (PLC). Therefore, a case study will be conducted where the developed
tool, based on the principles of the framework, will be deployed in order to
prove real-world applicability of the framework. Hence, a case ﬁrm was chosen
operating within the healthcare solution industry (HSI). The motivation to take a

Therefore, this paper will address the following two research questions (Re’s):

Methodology
2.1
Case Study
As the research is exploratory, a case study will be conducted in order to explore
the situation, test the proposed framework, and provide evidence for real-world
deployment, based on concrete data from qualitative and quantitative research
(Vissak, 2010; Yin, 2003).
Case study research is a robust methodology in order to explore and understand
complex issues on a real-world basis where a holistic view is required to assess
the problem (Vissak, 2010; Zainal, 2007). It can be reasoned that a case study is
a valid methodology - be it exploratory, explanatory or descriptive - because it
combines theory with practical data and thus, will contribute to the literature
(Yin, 2003). An exploratory case study addresses the exploration of a current
business situation, dives deeper into the observed phenomena, and establishes a
framework (Yin, 2003).
Using an exploratory case study as a research method allows the researcher to
fully explore the given situation, based on a real case. The investigation and the
conducted interviews enable the researcher to establish a framework. Further, to
prove the validity of the framework and its real-world applicability, the framework
needs to be applied to a case ﬁrm (Voss, Tsikriktsis, and Frohlich, 2002; Wacker,
1998). Vissak, 2010 claims that the downsides of case studies are the singularity
and individuality of the case’s situations which might be not adoptable to other
cases due to the lack of generalization. Therefore, it is essential that the researcher
uses the information, gathered through the case study, only as an additional source.
Combining the theory with the case study overcomes the drawbacks of singularity.
The case ﬁrm was chosen because it matches the relevant criteria for the case study.
For the following reasons the company was selected: (1) the healthcare solution
industry is in particular need to deliver its projects on-time and on-budget because
municipal public hospitals are scarce on monetary resources and exceeding the
deadline might have impact on patients’ treatment; (2) the case company deals
with complex engineering projects; (3) they have issues with both accurate cost
estimates and incorporating uncertainties; (4) the projects’ investment are of high
value, i.e. inaccurate cost estimates have strong ﬁnancial impact on stakeholders
(7 digit investment); (5) the company results from a merger of 5 independent
companies, i.e. the case study can draw from a larger sample size and there-
fore, standardize the process within the organization and create a generalized
framework.

2.2
Case Firm
For the sake of conﬁdentiality the company and the investigated product will
be referred as HealthCareSolution and PharmaSol, respectively, operating in the
healthcare solution industry (HSI). The company is headquartered in Canada and
has another ofﬁce in the United States of America. HealthCareSolution offers
complex, customized solutions for hospitals and pharmacies, including both
hardware and software in order to improve safety, productivity, and efﬁciency.
The offering reaches from drug order to delivery-to-prescription-to-patient for
both Intravenous (IV) and solid doses. The production is outsourced to various
suppliers which adds a different angle of complexity and uncertainties to projects.
Project duration for the product PharmaSol lasts from 6 months for an off-the-shelf
product up to 1.5 years for a highly customized product. Lastly, the portfolio of
HealthCareSolution contains of 100 widgets and 5 software platforms.
2.3
Structured Literature Review
In order to review the literature effectively, a structured approach was deployed.
On one hand, this approach is suitable at the beginning of an exploratory research
in a way that the researcher acquires a ﬁrst understanding of theory and terminol-
ogy, known as briefsearch (Rowley and Slack, 2004). Based on that the researcher
has collected related keywords, terminologies, and authors which enables him
or her to sift through the full topic. This is known as Pearl Growing effect (Boell
and Cecez-Kecmanovic, 2010). But on the other hand, the subject can be reﬁned
systematically and boiled down to speciﬁc inquires in order to determine the
research gap, where the researcher’s work can contribute, namely successive
fractions (Rowley and Slack, 2004). By applying this method, the review process
is rigour, systematic, and transparent (Tranﬁeld, Denyer, and Smart, 2003).
The structured literature review was conducted in two main stages - exploratory
and reﬁned - with three successive reviewing sessions (Table 1). At the beginning,
the literature research approach was exploratory to get an overview of existing
cost estimation models and techniques for engineering projects. After exploring
the topic, a ﬁrst reﬁned research session was conducted based on reﬁned termi-
nologies such as cost drivers, uncertainty factors, Monte Carlo simulation, and
sensitivity analysis. Beside those listed in Table 1, other speciﬁc inquires were
done to get clariﬁcation, i.e. COCOMO, COSYSMO, parametric, and so forth. In
a second reﬁned research session, the uniqueness of the study was addressed
and therefore, the validity of the contribution to the literature since none of the
combinations of the research topic brought up relevant or similar search results.
For the structured literature review, both the database DTU FindIt by the Technical

2.4
Data Collection
Qualitative and quantitative data were gathered from the case company via in-
ternal documents, online interviews, online meetings, data extraction, and email
conversations. The data collection was done over a period of ﬁve months in order
to get a sound understanding of their business processes and needs. Using both
qualitative and quantitative approaches, provide the best outcome in order to
answer thoroughly the research questions because they are complementary to each
others (Creswell, 2013; Krathwohl, 1993; Mackenzie and Knipe, 2006; Thomas,
2003).
As the research is exploratory, the ﬁrst meetings aimed to get ﬁrst insights into the
company and its processes. This was done with open (or unstructured) questions
to get a broad overview, allowing the respondent to express his view (“Intro-
duction to Qualitative Research Methods”). After having a broad picture about
key issues, the researcher moved on to semi-structured questions which concern
mainly open-ended questions in order to obtain information within a concrete
topic (“Introduction to Qualitative Research Methods”). To clarify and validate
certain assumptions as well as the proposed framework, the researcher made
use of close-ended questions (“Introduction to Qualitative Research Methods”).
Table 2 depicts only the online meetings together with the gathered information.
For most meetings/interviews the respondent from HealthCareSolution was the
Executive Vice President of Quality, Process Improvement & Strategic Planning,
short Exec. VP QPISP.
The gathered qualitative data offer valuable information to understand the ﬁrm’s
processes and thus, establish a framework. However, to validate the framework’s
applicability, it requires quantitative data since the numerical form allows to
compare previous estimates with new estimates, based on the framework (Hart,
1987). The quantitative data were gathered through data extractions, internal
documents, and online sessions.
Quantitative data collection contains spread sheets about current cost calculations
and logged project costs in order to both understand the case ﬁrm’s procedure
and calculate the actual deviance between estimated and accounted cost as a
reference point. Through a 2.5 hours online workshop with the Exec. VP QPISP
and the Director of Special Projects, the input data for the tool were collected in
order to validate the proposed framework.

2.4.2
Validity of collected data
To ensure validity of data, collected through interviews, multiple sources of evi-
dence were used. Yin, 2003 speaks of a chain of evidence, where different angles
from where to look at the phenomenon are key for the validity of information.
Even though the data gathering comprises only three different employees of the
case ﬁrm, the informants hold in key positions for the project. Further, the infor-
mants are from different management levels, CEO, Executive Vice President of
Quality, Process Improvement Strategic Planning, and Director of Special Projects
which ensures different viewpoints on the topic.
Since the new estimate by the developed tool were done on a complete project,
it was important to keep bias of the estimators as low as possible in order to
guarantee a high level of validity. Therefore, the researcher aimed to interview at
least two employees who haven’t worked on or were only partly involved in the
determined project. The interviewees were the Executive Vice President of Quality,
Process Improvement Strategic Planning, who wasn’t involved in the project
at all, and the Director of Special Projects, who was only partly involved. The
Executive Vice President and the researcher worked as facilitators and questioned
the responses from the Director of Special Projects. The following questions were
asked in order to keep bias as low as possible:
1. Would you have been came up with the same estimate if you haven’t been
involved into the project at all?
2. Is this estimate based on your general knowledge or is it biased by the project?
3. Try to reconstruct the information you had at the point when you did the initial
estimate.
4. What would you have done differently today with the current information
about the project? ("Subtracting" the gained information leads the estimator to
the information available at Day 0 of the project)
Further, the quantitative data validity is ensured by extractions from the com-
pany’s information system. Since it is data that is internally used, the validity can
be considered as high.

3.1
Project cost estimation techniques
Fundamentally, project cost estimates (PCE) can be distinguished in a priori and
a posteriori, or non-algorithmic and algorithmic, respectively (Al-Sarayreh and
Meridji, 2013; Attarzadeh and Ow, 2010). Williamson, 2013 deﬁnes a priori as
a way of knowledge if it is independent of experience, whereas it is a posteriori
if and only if it is dependent on experience. Many cost estimation models are
built on past experience and historical data, hence, in a a posteriori context but
are used in a a priori context since PCE are usually made in an early stage of the
project (Al-Sarayreh and Meridji, 2013). Further, Al-Sarayreh and Meridji, 2013
claim that even though historical data as input are measured precisely, there is no
guarantee that the outcome of the estimates has the same certainty level. Thus,
a few independent studies have low accuracy performance on their a posteriori
estimates since the uncertainties are not reﬂected in the data. However, most
PCE models are based on experience and historical data since it brings higher
repeatability as well as reproducability and refers to experience than relying
on a priori knowledge, namely gut feeling (Al-Sarayreh and Meridji, 2013; Kim,
Seo, and Hyun, 2012; Malone and Wolfarth, 2013; Rakovi´c, 2004). Therefore, by
combining the advantages of both a priori and a posteriori PCE, the model should
be based on experience, i.e. historical data, but also allow the ﬂexibility of a priori
judgment about uncertainties.
Another commonly used differentiation of cost estimation techniques is into qual-
itative and quantitative. Qualitative estimation refers to the analysis of a new
product/project compared to previous products/projects, whereas quantitative
estimation relies on analysis of detailed product/project design, its elements, and
processes (Duchi et al., 2014; Hooshmand, Köhler, and Korff-Krumm, 2016; Niazi
et al., 2006). Qualitative estimation are split up further into Intuitive, based on

3.2
Cost drivers and uncertainty factors of project life cycle
To classify the CDs in terms of occurrence and guarantee generic project applica-
bility - no matter of industry sector or project complexity - the ISO 21500 standard
on project management processes will be deployed. The International Standard
categorizes project management processes of PLC into ﬁve project processes:
Initiating - Planning - Implementing - Controlling - Closing (Danish-Standard-
Organization, 2012).
Initiating
This initial phase - some literature refers to Conceptualization - is the start point
of a project where the objectives and needs of the project are deﬁned (Danish-
Standard-Organization, 2012; Pinto and Slevin, 1988; Watt, 2013). This includes a
feasibility study whether the objectives can be met (Watt, 2013).
Planning
In the planning phase, a detailed plan will be developed in order to manage
the implementation and measure the project performance against the baseline
(Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012). The developed project plan outlines the
tasks, activities, and responsibilities to be undertaken in order to meet the objec-
tives (Watt, 2013).
Implementing
Implementation or execution describes the phase where the project’s tasks and
activities are performed according to the project plan in order to meet the deliver-
ables (Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012; Watt, 2013).
Controlling
Controlling is the continuously process of monitoring and measuring the project
performance against the project plan (Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012; Watt,
2013). Controlling is an ongoing process and it should be incorporated into the
implementation (Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012). Hence, it won’t be consid-

Closing
The ﬁnal phase of a project is the closure of a project that terminates the project.
An important step here is to review the project and establish lessons-learned in
order to close the feedback-loop (Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012; Watt, 2013).
This will be added to the knowledge pool or data base of previous projects in
order to guide estimators with future projects (Watt, 2013).
Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012 collects the standard processes, occurred in

good guideline to group the CDs. The subject groups are: Integration, Stakeholder,
Scope, Resource, Time, Cost, Risk, Quality, Procurement, and Communication
(Danish-Standard-Organization, 2012). Deﬁnition and description of each subject
group can be read up in DS/ISO 21500.

3.2.2
Uncertainty factors
This paper uses the terminology uncertainty factor (UF) for all factors, associated
either with risks for projects or uncertainties. Therefore, the deﬁnition of UF
comprises all factors where the information is unknown or not clear (Ivanenko
and Pasichnichenko, 2014). Hence, all relevant factors that might have a nega-
tive/unknown impact on the outcome of projects will be gathered in Table 6. It is
important to reﬂect on UFs since they are present in most projects - no matter of
size, location or industry. Thus, it is necessary to evaluate their negative impact on
projects’ outcome and incorporate them into the cost estimates (Oyewobi, Ibrahim,
and Ganiyu, 2012). However, some cost drivers are also UFs since the evaluation
is associated with uncertainty. Table 7 groups the main UF along the project life
cycle and can be used by practitioners as an uncertainty portfolio for project cost
estimation. While considering diverse cost centres together with potential cost
drivers, managers can reﬂect on relevant UFs (Chang et al., 2017). An useful
tool for managers in order to analyze and map project cost, cost drivers and
uncertainty factors is the tree model, known from Risk Management (Kansala,
1997). Again, this listing doesn’t claim to consider the full portfolio of UF rather
that it accounts for 80 % of uncertainties by 20 % of UFs. The basic cost calculation
can be summarized in Equation 1:
C = CC ∗CDs ∗UFs

Due to the unknown/unclear nature of uncertainties, the estimation is, instead of
one particular number, rather a range of potential estimates (Chang et al., 2017).
Thus, the estimation is rather a probability distribution (Oyewobi, Ibrahim, and
Ganiyu, 2012). In order to reﬂect on estimate distributions due to uncertainties, a
simulation combined with the cost estimation techniques is needed (Asiedu and

Monte Carlo simulation & sensitivity analysis
In order to incorporate and model explicitly uncertainties and its range of poten-
tial values, probabilistic methods are commonly used (Touran, 2006). Instead of
presenting one point estimate, probabilistic methods offer a more realistic repre-
sentation of project costs/duration, using a range of estimates together with a
desired conﬁdence level. Probabilistic methods can be divided into Non-Simulation
Methods and Simulation Methods (Bakhshi and Touran, 2014). In order to get a more
realistic representation, this paper will only focus on the probabilistic simulation
method, namely Monte Carlo (MC) Simulation. MC simulation is widely used
to incorporate uncertainties into estimates (Bakhshi and Touran, 2014; Kwak and
Ingall, 2007). MC simulation speaks for the concept of repeating random samples
of model input variables, namely the value range of uncertainty factors, over multi-
ple iterations. Thus, the model creates and simulates randomly selected scenarios
out of the data range in order to calculate an output distribution (Sokolowski,
2010). The distribution on potential outcomes together with their likelihood of
occurrence allows project managers to better reﬂect and communicate on project
costs/duration.
Although MC simulation incorporates uncertainties, it doesn’t provide insights
on which input variable drives the outcome to what extent. Therefore, sensitivity
analysis are another aspect for project managers to evaluate the inﬂuential factors
of changes in input parameters (Abdel-Khalik, Bang, and Wang, 2013; Lambropou-
los, Manolopoulos, and Pantouvakis, 1996; Zhang et al., 2017). Sensitivity analysis
can be classiﬁed in two categories, namely local and global or derivative-based
and importance-based, respectively (Abdel-Khalik, Bang, and Wang, 2013; Morio,
2011). Local techniques focus only on one change of one certain variable. As the
name indicates, the local impact of that one certain parameter is evaluated on
the model’s outcome while the other variables are held constant (Morio, 2011;
Van Griensven et al., 2006). Global techniques sample in a random or systematic
way the whole range of parameter variables and analyzes how the input variability
inﬂuences the range of output. It determines which part of the outcome distri-
bution is impacted by the different input variables (Morio, 2011; Van Griensven
et al., 2006; Wainwright et al., 2014). The sampled parameter sets indicate project
managers which parameter drives the model the most (Abdel-Khalik, Bang, and
Wang, 2013; Van Griensven et al., 2006). Since project cost estimations may infer
multiple input parameters and these parameters may come with uncertainties,
local techniques are not sophisticated enough to reﬂect on the interplay and
thus, the impact of multiple parameters on the outcome. Therefore, this paper
recommends to deploy global techniques. There are various methods, such as
Sobol indice, Kolmogorov - Smirnov (K-S) test, and Morris OAT, that deploys

Selection of cost estimation approach is the starting point of the framework.
To provide practitioners guidance on the selection process, Table 3 summarizes
pros and cons of each technique. Further, the framework includes a matrix with
key decision points to determine the best suited approach for each company’s
individual situation. The questions to ask in order to determine the best suitable
approach, are the following: (1) does the estimation needs to be done in the early project

phase, namely Initiating/Conceptualization?, (2) are only high level (no detailed) data
available?, (3) are there no data at all available?, and (4) is this approach applicable for
simulation? Answering these questions with YES, shows practitioners the appli-
cable approaches. Intuitive and Parametric approach fulﬁll the same key decision
points. Parametric models such as COSYSMO are industry-speciﬁc and the de-
rived coefﬁcients might not reﬂect the exact values for different industries outside
of aerospace and defense. However, it can be a good guideline and indicator
for estimators, whereas Intuitive approaches rely solely on Expert Judgement. In
order to overcome the weaknesses of both approaches, this framework combines
the beneﬁts of both. Those are good guidance, high repeatability and robustness
with the ﬂexibility of expert judgement. Therefore, the researcher recommends,
based on ﬁndings, to decide in favor of the Parametric approach. Same applies to
Analogical versus Intuitive.
Identify cost centres, cost drivers, and uncertainty factors is the next step. This
step varies from one approach to another, therefore, each approach requires its
own explanation.
For the Intuitive approach, it is important to reﬂect for the determined level of
detail on relevant cost centres, such as activities, cost drivers (Table 5), and uncer-
tainty factors (Table 7) along the project life cycle. An useful tool might be a tree
model, known from risk management, where for each CC the relevant CDs and
UFs are listed. This is helpful for the following quantiﬁcation. The product of CC,
CDs and UFs gives a cost range with best and worst case. The sum of all costs
and their ranges can be simulated with Monte Carlo simulation in order to get a
ﬁnal cost/time distribution.
The Analogical approach is only applicable in case of historical data. Hence, one
will derive the cost function from the determined cost drivers through (multiple)
linear regressions. In order to grant ﬂexibility in case of new processes, machine
equipment, and so forth, this framework includes expert judgement that allows
adjustment of the cost function. Uncertainties can be expressed either in a range
of cost function coefﬁcients or in a range of input variables which can then be
simulated with Monte Carlo simulation.
The Parametric approach takes in this framework the structure of COSYSMO
model. In case of no data, the framework recommends to use the derived co-
efﬁcients from COSYSMO, and incorporates expert judgement to adjust these
coefﬁcients on a weighted average. In case of the availability of historical data, the
company can tailor the coefﬁcients of the model to its situation. Determination of
coefﬁcients is based on (multiple) linear regressions. The more previous projects
and data the company possesses, the rather it can increase the weighted average
of the coefﬁcients to a fully tailored model. This allows companies to grow with
the framework since it is dynamic and thus, applicable to company’s individual

==PAGE:33==

July 4, 2017
situations. Uncertainties are incorporated and can be modeled by the range for
each size driver. In COSYSMO there are four size drivers that reﬂect the functional
size of the system (Valerdi, 2005).
Since the Analytical approach is only applicable when detailed information are
available, consequently, uncertainties will be low. However, uncertainties can be
still expressed in a range of time/costs per activity. The sum of all costs, including
their uncertainties, can be simulated with Monte Carlo simulation.
The underlying principle for the framework asks to Quantify Cost Centres. De-
pendent on the cost estimation approach which has been selected by the estimator,
one need to quantify the following variables, such as CC, CD, UF, size drivers
and/or coefﬁcients. Hence, the degree of uncertainty is captured since the estima-
tor can insert a range of numbers in case she is uncertain.
Next step is to Run Monte Carlo simulation. Since the estimator has expressed
her uncertainties by ranges of CC, CD, UF, size drivers or coefﬁcients, the simula-
tion generates various scenarios within the given range by a certain number of
iteration. The outcome of the simulation will be, according to input, in Person
Months or Person Weeks. Since the simulation computes various potential project
duration, both the likelihood of occurrence and the distribution density (normal
distribution) can be determined. This allows estimators to set their conﬁdence
level/risk proﬁle which again reﬂects the uncertainty level of the estimator. In
addition, in terms of a ﬁxed due date of the project that will be earlier than
the computed ideal schedule, this can be reﬂected by an compressed schedule
with increased labor allocation. From this schedule, the ﬁnal project costs can
be determined. Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis will be conducted in order to
determine the input parameters that drive the model’s outcome the most. Table
8 summarizes the main method for sensitivity analysis with their limitations to
certain functions. It is essential for engineers and managers to investigate which
parameters are predominant to the project duration/costs in order to constantly
assess and govern those parameters.

Validation
The proposed framework was deployed to a complete project, named for conﬁ-
dentiality reasons GH17, of the case ﬁrm’s product line PharmaSol, in order to
prove real-world applicability. The logged costs of GH17 are $3,695,212.00, the
estimation was $2,970,406.72 and the deviance is $724,805.27. In order to obtain
results for comparison, a built-in Excel simulation, called COSYSMO-Xpert, is
supposed to prove the validity of the framework.
5.1
Tool
Starting out with the selection of the cost estimation approach, the only two
suitable approaches are Intuitive and Parametric, since HealthCareSolution does
its project cost/schedule estimation in the early stage with only few information
available and without historical data available. As described in section 4, it is
recommended to decide in favor for the Parametric approach. Therefore, the simu-
lation was built on a parametric approach, combining variances from COCOMO
and COSYSMO with the input from the case company. COSYSMO is built on
the idea of COCOMO, where size drivers capture the functional size and cost
drivers reﬂect on multiplicative coefﬁcients (Trendowicz and Jeffery, 2014; Valerdi,
2005). The size drivers for the tool are used from COSYSMO and the idea of
incorporating uncertainties at the size drivers is from COSYSMO-R. COSYSMO-R
reﬂects also on uncertainties at the cost drivers where the estimator can select
a range of ﬁxed effort multipliers. However, the steps within the given rating
scale for each CD are large and thus, have strong impact on the estimates. For
instance, rating the CD Requirements Understanding from Low to Very Low will
increase the multiplicative factor from 1.36 up to 1.85. Instead of selecting a range,
the researcher incorporated the possibility to allow expert judgement about the
multiplicative factor together with a conﬁdence level. According to the conﬁdence
level, a weighted average for the cost driver will be computed. Furthermore,
COSYSMO generates only a one-point estimate, whereas COSYSMO-R computes
various estimates for a given set of conﬁdence-/risk-proﬁles. In order to have a
seamless set of estimates for each possible conﬁdence-/risk-proﬁle, the researcher
incorporated a Monte Carlo simulation that computes 1000 different scenarios.
By calculating the mean, standard deviation and then, the normal distribution,
the estimator can select her individual conﬁdence-/risk-proﬁle without being
constrained to given proﬁles. In addition, COSYSMO-Xpert contains a sensitivity
analysis in order to determine the predominant input parameters that drive the
model’s outcome. The impact of each input variable are computed as absolute

and relative value together with the standard deviation. Parameters with high
impact on the outcome and a high standard deviation, should be managed closely
by the project team.

Sensitivity analysis
The integrated sensitivity analysis in COSYSMO-Xpert is depicted in the appendix,
Figure 10, and is based on the following principle. Monte Carlo simulation is
used as a sampling method with 1000 iterations. One input parameter at a time
was held constant, while the other parameters were kept loosely, sampling their
full range of uncertainty. For the constant input parameter, it was used both
the lowest and the highest number of input variables, best case and worst case,
respectively, and ran 1000 iterations. The mean of each of the 1000 iterations for
best and worst case was taken. The deviance between mean worst and mean
best case of the outcome was divided by the deviance between the highest and
lowest input variable of the constant parameter. This resulted in a mean sensitivity
coefﬁcient that is interpreted as the following. A coefﬁcient for the input parame-
ter Numbers of System Requirements of 0.9 means that an increase of one System
Requirement leads to an increase of 0.9 Person Months of system engineering
efforts. In addition, an extreme sensitivity coefﬁcient was generated in order to
reﬂect on the full impact of uncertainties. This was done by taking the lowest
possible outcome of the 1000 scenarios of the best case and the highest possible
outcome of the worst case. The deviation is divided again by the deviation of
the input variable. Providing these two coefﬁcients to the project team offers
them a valuable information on the impact of each parameter on the outcome.

approach was approved and it was conﬁrmed that it is essential to determine key
drivers of the outcome in order to make informed decisions.

Implications on theory and practice
6.1
Implication on theory
The key contribution to theory of this paper is the establishment of a framework
that allows companies to select the best suited cost estimation technique for their
individual situation. Further, it is a dynamic framework, thus, it allows companies
to grow with the framework. The framework contains a decision matrix at the
beginning with key questions that enables practitioners to ﬁnd the best suited
estimation approach for their situation. For a more detailed investigation about
the right technique within the estimation approaches, Table 3 was composed with
pros and cons of each cost estimation technique together with the required input
data. Pros and cons for each technique has been investigated earlier by other
researchers, but it was never emphasized what kind of data, whether high-level
data, historical data, and so forth, are needed to make the technique work.
Furthermore, the investigation showed that there are cost drivers and uncertainty
factors that have an inﬂuential impact on system engineering efforts. This paper
offers not only a portfolio of the predominant CDs and UFs where practitioners
can reﬂect on, but also an allocation of these factors along the project life cycle
in terms of processes and subject groups. The aim of this allocation was to give
practitioners further guidance while reﬂecting these factors.
In addition, the framework contains a Monte Carlo simulation that addresses
uncertainties within the input variables and enables the computation of a prelimi-
nary project schedule and system engineering costs. The framework ends with a
sensitivity analysis of the input variables in order to determine which variables
drive the model’s outcome.
6.2
Implication on practice
Implications on practice of this work are the ability to incorporate uncertainties to
the estimates. Thanks to the Monte Carlo simulation, the estimator can simulate
different scenarios according to her conﬁdence-/risk-proﬁle. Combining this with
the sensitivity analysis, shows the estimator which variable drives the outcome
the most. Thus, the project team can either investigate further the predominant
variable in order to mitigate uncertainties or emphasize the governance on the par-
ticular variable. Further, due to the different scenarios and the possible outcomes,
the project manager can communicate the possible costs to her stakeholders and
client. For example, it can be thought of that the price for the client will be
communicated as a range between the conﬁdence level of 75% and 95%. In case
of less incurred costs, there will be a price reduction for the client.

Lastly, the real-world applicability of the framework was proven by deploying the
framework to the case company. The result of the validation has shown that there
was a signiﬁcant improvement in accuracy of the estimate on system engineering

There are also limitations of this work. Since the parametric approach of the
framework and consequently, COSYSMO-Xpert generates the costs for system

the same or at least similar processes. Therefore, it is important for companies
to compare their processes with the one of the EIA 632 standard. If they deviate

the company has the same processes as EIA 632 standard, but it has an additional
process due to industry speciﬁc requirements, the estimator needs to separately
assess this process and add the efforts to the generated system engineering efforts,
obtained by the framework.
Another limitation of especially parametric models are that the parameters need to
be fully understood. Therefore, counting rules of size drivers need to be deﬁned
for each organization individually in order to obtain a robust and repeatable
technique.

Conclusion
This study has identiﬁed main cost estimation techniques together with their pros
and cons and the required input data for each technique. In addition, main cost
drivers and uncertainty factors were determined that have an inﬂuential impact
on system engineering efforts. In order to guide practitioners when reﬂecting on
CDs and UFs, these inﬂuential drivers were categorized along the processes and
subject groups of the project life cycle. Furthermore, methods were researched that
are able to incorporate, simulate, and analyze uncertainties with cost estimates. A
holistic framework was developed which guides practitioners from the selection
of the most suitable cost estimation technique to the ﬁnal project schedule and
costs. The provided framework is a novel approach to estimate system engineer-
ing efforts under uncertainties. It is readily applicable and can be used by all
practitioners to estimate system engineering efforts. The deployment of the frame-
work to the case ﬁrm showed signiﬁcant improvements in accuracy of the cost
estimates, namely from an initial 20% underestimation to a 8% overestimation by
the developed tool COSYSMO-Xpert. Further, the integrated sensitivity analysis
was proven as an essential tool for practitioners to make informed decisions about
project costs.
The developed framework is limited to guide practitioners from the right cost
estimation technique to the estimated project costs. It may require additional case
studies to deﬁne more detailed, generic processes of adaption and implemen-
tation of the framework. The case study addressed the parametric approach of
the framework and showed signiﬁcant improvements, however, validations for

Intention/Scope of COSYSMO 
 
COSYSMO was derived from COCOMO and intent to calculate the systems engineering efforts, in terms 
of Person Months, that are required for the successful conceptualization, development, and testing of 
large-scale systems. System engineering is defined by INCOSE as: 
Systems Engineering is an interdisciplinary approach and means to enable the realization of 
successful systems. It focuses on defining customer needs and required functionality early in 
the development cycle, documenting requirements, then proceeding with design synthesis and 
system validation while considering the complete problem. 
EIA 632 Standard describes the general processes for System Engineering as followed: 
 
Acquisition and Supply 
o 
Supply Process 
o 
Acquisition Process 
 
Technical Management 
o 
Planning Process 
o 
Assessment Process 
o 
Control Process 
 
System Design 
o 
Requirements Definition Process 
o 
Solution Definition Process 
 
Product Realization 
o 
Implementation Process 
o 
Transition to Use Process 
 
Technical Evaluation 
o 
Systems Analysis Process 
o 
Requirements Validation Process 
o 
System Verification Process 
o 
End Products Validation Process 
This implies that COSYSMO estimates only the efforts and costs for the above mentioned processes. It 
ends with the delivery of the system to designated operating, customer, or storage sites, install these 
at designated operating sites or into designated platforms, and conduct an end product validation. 
However, it does NOT include the following cost point (list is not exhaustive):  
- 
Material costs

Travel expenses

STEP 3 Sensitivity Analysis 
 
In order to get an idea to which extent one size driver drives the outcome, the sensitivity coefficients 
in the last two columns are calculated. For this, 1000 scenarios are generated by deploying the outer 
boundaries of one size driver to the COSYSMO formula and keeping the other size drivers lose (using 
their whole range). For example, 1000 iterations were ran by taking the lowest number from the range 
of # of requirements but use the full range for the other size drivers. The same was done with the 
highest number of the range. Then, the mean and the standard deviation were calculated for the 1000 
scenarios. In addition, the lowest and the highest outcome of the 1000 scenarios for minimum input 
and the maximum input, respectively, were determined, called extreme. After, the deviance for both 
the mean of min. and max scenario, and the deviance for both extreme min. and max scenario was 
taken. The deviance of the scenarios divided by deviance of the range of input parameters, presents 
the sensitivity coefficients. This means, that for an increase of one input parameter by one (one 
additional count), the outcome will increase by 0.9 PM for the # of requirements.
