Big Data in Venture Capital:  
Theory and Practice of Web-Based Data Mining Approaches in Indicating and 
Predicting Early-Stage Venture Success 
 
Timo Hermans 
==PAGE:2==
 
Abstract 
 
 Identifying promising investment opportunities is an effortful and to this date mostly 
manual conducted process by venture capital (VC) practitioners. To improve efficiency, 
Venture capitalists (VCs) show increasing interest in the application of data-based investment 
processes. VCs collect information for automated investment processes from few dominating 
venture data platforms like Crunchbase. Scarce data availability for startup ventures in the realm 
of startup prediction causes a critical problem that limits automated deal-sourcing. To mitigate 
the problem of meaningful data in early-stage ventures, this thesis elaborates practical 
approaches to collect publicly available venture data on a large scale. First, success measures 
reaching beyond traditional approaches were identified leading to implications that detailed 
human capital information on founders and novel criteria such as sentiment may indicate 
venture success. As granular founder information and novel, less explored metrics are not 
provided by VC databases, this thesis focuses on the elaboration of approaches to collect 
lacking and potentially innovative meaningful factors on a large scale. The thesis thereby 
elaborates a variety of automated data-mining approaches which show that data can be collected 
on a large scale. To explore the usability of the developed approaches, they are tested by 
collecting data on 992 early-stage ventures from public platforms, such as Twitter, LinkedIn, 
Google and News platforms. The web-scraping approaches revealed that meaningful data 
collection is possible on a large scale and delivers a vast number of novel indicators. 
Furthermore, a detailed data exploration indicates measures, such as news sentiment, twitter 
popularity and ecosystem rankings related to funding success. As the data analysis is limited 
within the scope of this paper, more research is required to further explore the causality and 
value of web-scraped indicators. The data analysis also revealed that information can only be 
found on a restricted number of ventures, indicating that a lack of data availability calls for 
more advanced web-scraping approaches.

1. Introduction 
 Research background and motivation 
The “Age of Data” (Mikalef, Boura, Lekakos, & Krogstie, 2019, p. 261) has brought 
growing awareness for the usage and exploitation of data. The phrase “Data is the new oil” 
(Parkins, 2017; Schwab, Marcus, Oyola, Hoffman, & Luzi, 2011) corresponds to the ever-
growing business opportunities evolving as a by-product of technological development 
(McAfee, Brynjolfsson, Davenport, Patil, & Barton, 2012). Sector-independent, data 
exploitation has become a powerful value driver (Farboodi, Mihet, Philippon, & Veldkamp, 
2019) and data analytic approaches have been found to generally correlate positively with the 
performance of a company (Mikalef et al., 2019). The value propositions of data-based 
approaches also promise tremendous opportunities in the venture capital (VC) industry (Ferrati 
& Muffatto, 2021). From different perspectives, the complexity and processes of venture 
capitalists (VCs) ignite theoretical, empirical, media and political interest as they tackle the 
extremes of challenges in corporate finance: “uncertainty, information asymmetry and asset 
intangibility” (S. Kaplan & Lerner, 2016, p. 3). In this term, the flourishing three Vs of Big 
Data – volume, variety, and velocity – also call for application in the venture capital (VC) 
industry as a “tool” to mitigate information and automatization challenges. While the VC 
industry shows increasing interest in the application of quantitative approaches particularly in 
pre-investment screening (Arroyo, Corea, Jimenez-Diaz, & Recio-Garcia, 2019; Gompers, 
Gornall, Kaplan, & Strebulaev, 2020) practical approaches have hardly investigated especially 
in terms of valid data channels to supplement established data sources. While first practitioners 
appear as pioneers on the market, data collection methods to retrieve a better information 
foundation of quality measures on startups are hardly explored (Sharchilev et al., 2018) in terms 
newly evolving startups, although they may be of essential value for VCs automatization 
approaches. As the VC’s automatization spurs competitive, efficiency and yet undiscovered 
innovative opportunities, also the focus in VC research becomes increasingly data oriented 
(Arroyo et al., 2019). However, while many researchers refer to VC databases as primary data 
source (Ferrati & Muffatto, 2021), there is little research about opportunities and practical 
approaches that may mitigate information asymmetries through further data. Particularly web-
based data generation empowered by Big Data mining approaches lack of research in context 
of startup deal sourcing and evaluation. This implies the relevance of exploring opportunities 
and practical approaches in identifying valuable investment criteria.

 Problem statement  
Facing environmental uncertainty, VCs tend to occupy an adaptive and flexible business 
attitude (Matusik & Fitza, 2012, pp. 407, 423). As a result, VCs set up diversified portfolios 
with varying firm age, industry, and geography to reduce the systematic investment risk. An 
increasingly diverse investment philosophy and low comparability between investments creates 
a growing challenge for VCs to manually evaluate the diverse investment candidates in an 
efficient manner (Felske, 2020, p. 3). According to Gompers et al. (2020, p. 2), considering the 
average VC’s work allocation, VCs spend an average of 22 hours (40 percent) a week on 
sourcing deals. This indicates the potential and need to create more efficiency in deal sourcing 
through the support of modern data driven approaches. As screening process underlies high 
pressure and time constraints (Zacharakis & Meyer, 1998; Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2001) 
decisions in screening selection are increasingly urged to be improved to mitigate opportunity 
costs. However, these processes are hardly improvable and scalable due to their manual nature 
(Retterath, 2020b, p. 7).  
Furthermore, considering an increasing VC market concentration and competition 
(Hong, Serfes, & Thiele, 2020), the willingness to invest in non-mature ventures has increased 
significantly over the past years (Kim & Wagman, 2016, p. 12). The average investment size 
in ventures prior to Series A between the years 2010 and 2018 has multiplied by 4.3 (Loizos, 
2019). This intensifies the requirements to identify startups earlier to detect startups “under the 
radar” with low visibility (Bengtsson & Hsu, 2010, p. 6).  
However, deciding which startups are promising investment candidates requires a good 
information base. Unless investment opportunities evolve from the VCs internal network, VCs 
rely heavily on information from VC databases to increase their scope for promising investment 
opportunities. While many VCs approach the sourcing process yet rather manual (Gompers et 
al., 2020), automated approaches based on data providers may suffer from the limited data 
points provided (e.g. Retterath, 2020a; Ünal, 2019).  
Besides traditional investment strategies, automated deal-sourcing has shown academic 
and practical interest to improve the investment selection process (Gompers et al., 2020; 
Retterath, 2020b). Quantitative deal-sourcing is the process of identifying deals through 
quantitative data from multiple sources to recognize promising investments with high exit 
profitability (Gompers et al., 2020, p. 7). However, data quality and meaningful investment 
criteria are crucial for the performance and usefulness of such data automation tools. While VC 
databases like Pitchbook and particularly Crunchbase are the dominating sources for 
quantitative investment approaches (Ferrati & Muffatto, 2021, p. 14; Retterath & Braun, 2020),

these databases provide often only limited and basic information on ventures. Hence, 
identifying startups based on external data bears tremendous difficulties due to the lack of 
information in public databases. This contributes to a mainly manual and subjective investment 
selection and evaluation process. In order to increase the performance and efficiency in VC 
investment processes, it is critical to identify new ways to collect and retrieve supplementary 
metrics.   
 Research relevance and research questions 
Concerning the above issues, the digitalization and automatization of the VC investment 
process provides both, value propositions, and application related challenges in the VC 
universe. Data mining techniques, such as machine learning (ML), web-crawling and text 
analytics could assist the quality assessment and performance predictions of startups on a large 
scale. As Gartner predicts, data-science application in VC as well as Private Equity (PE) 
industry will be a common practice by the year 2025. It will shift “[…] the early-stage venture 
investing strategy away from gut feel and qualitative decision making to a more modern 
platform-based quantitative process” (Gartner, 2021). 
However, as data-based approaches often source information from the main established 
VC databases, public data-based automatization raises both potentially not explored challenges 
and more importantly, opportunities and requires exploration. In this context, previous research 
in data-based success prediction and on VC databases calls for collecting variables beyond 
traditional sources and traditional information and ask for innovative ways to collect data and 
add novel variables (e.g. Retterath, 2020a, p. 144).  
In this context, this thesis attempts to explore new approaches forrelevant private 
company data collection by exploring and developingdata-driven techniques with a particular 
focus on web-scraping and data mining. In this regard, it is evaluated how and what variables 
can be collected and processed to retrieve meaningful data on private companies and to support 
research in the VC industry and the practical application of data-based venture identification. 
Based on an explorative, inductive Big Data approach, the following research questions will be 
tackled throughout the study.   
Research Question 1:  Which (non-)financial and novel soft factors could serve as a proxy 
for venture success prediction?  
Research Question 2:  How can practical data-mining approaches be developed and 
implemented in an automated scale to identify and collect venture 
success signals based on publicly available online sources? 
==PAGE:12==

Research Question 3:  What informational value and data variety can be achieved based on 
the novel collected in order to supplement data in indicating and 
predicting early-stage venture success? 
 Research contribution 
Elaborating on these research questions, the thesis attempts to contribute on the research 
gap of collecting novel and meaningful venture data by providing implications that could 
support and refine data-based VC investment processes from both a practical and research 
perspective. In this context, this thesis develops a theoretical background, discussing less 
finance based soft factors apart from traditional venture success factors and further elaborates 
approaches how data can be collected automatically.  
VCs and private company research can benefit from the created insights on new avenues 
for data generation. VCs can enhance the quality and reliability of their pre-investment 
decisions by considering more diverse and novel information sets (Felske, 2020, p. 4) and by 
applying automated and modern approaches of data collection on investment opportunities. 
This thesis suggests broadening the scope of consideration in possibly influencing parameters 
in venture success and involving more new and less investigated variables in context of (early-
stage) venture success. Also, the presented programming approaches could provide inspiration 
and methods that help future research to collect web-based information on a large and 
automated scale. 
 Structure  
First, the existing problem of information asymmetry between VCs and startups will be 
discussed, as it comprises the lack of data and information for startup identification. In this 
context, Chapter 2 provided an overview of VC investment processes and the bottlenecks of 
data-based approaches in terms of data quality.  
The following Chapter 3 elaborates background that provides answers to research 
question 1. The Chapter serves the goal to identify criteria that may be meaningful in context 
of venture success prediction. After deriving success indicators and quality measures for 
startups in Chapter 3, a summary of the data collection strategy and research design of the thesis 
will be presented in Chapter 4. A data sample from Crunchbase will be retrieved in order to 
implement and test data collection approaches in the following Chapter 5.  
Chapter 5 tackles research question 2 by discovering how web scraping as a method for 
big data collection can be used as an automated tool for data collection on ventures with a focus

venture data set, approaches will be elaborated and implemented to detect how and which

2. Theoretical Background 
This chapter gives an overview of the VC industry and its practice. It serves as a 
fundament of critical factors and processes in VC practitioners’ investment decisions with a 
particular focus on the most relevant steps for automatization application and respectively 
protruding bottlenecks in data-based approaches. 
 VC’s and startup’s economic impetus  
For a consistent understanding of the term VC in this thesis, following will serve as the 
general definition for VCs. Similar to Felske (2020, p. 5) a VC will be defined as: “A ‘VC’ is 
an investor who provides capital to early-stage ventures which exhibit high growth potential in 
exchange for an equity stake.”  
VC plays an important role in the economy and are a central driver of industry 
development. Thus, VC’s function and current industry perspective will be briefly discussed. 
Entrepreneurship perspective 
Besides financial capital, strategic advisory, and monitoring, VCs function as important 
value drivers to enable startups to professionalize (Hellmann & Puri, 2002b, p. 183) and 
increase success (Hellmann & Puri, 2002a, p. 20). VCs have tremendous influence on the 
survival of private companies as the mentioned functions lead to an increased preservation and 
promotion of startups, fueling entrepreneurship and innovation. As a result, VC supported 
startups shape solutions of nascent and prevalent problems leading to industrial transformation. 
The establishment of innovative startups spurs innovation and drives economic growth 
providing impetus on the economy, making VC an important driver for economic growth (Jeng 
& Wells, 2000). Furthermore, rapidly growing ventures function as a disproportionately large 
contributor to net new jobs (Henrekson & Johansson, 2010; Schreyer, 2000).  
VC perspective 
VC investments have been continuously increasing over the last decade.  They recently 
have reached an all-time high in the second quarter of 2021 with $157.1 billion raised across 
7,687 deals (KPMG, 2021), representing a year on year increase of 94 percent (Teare, 2021). 
Along with the funding growth, VC’s interest in early-stage ventures has increased over the last 
decade leading to a new investment record in 2021 (Teare, 2021). VCs are seeking for the most 
promising investments and are raising interest in research on innovative and effective 
approaches for identifying early-stage ventures. With significantly growing capital inflow, 
==PAGE:15==
 
7 
competition among VCs to identify promising investment opportunities has further amplified 
(Ang, Chia, & Saghafian, 2020; Retterath, 2020b, p. 23).  
In return for the resources and investments VCs spend to promote the firm growth, they 
expect returns exceeding the invested sum several times. The high return requirements are based 
on the significant risk each investment bears as a venture’s development can be highly 
uncertain. As 90 percent of startups go bankrupt within their first five years of existence 
(Żbikowski & Antosiuk, 2021, p. 2), investors expect most investments to be a loss (L. Huang 
& Pearce, 2015, p. 9). Nonetheless, the successful ten percent involve startups with vast growth 
potential including future unicorns1 with extraordinary financial returns. To mitigate risk and 
to maximize their portfolio performance, VCs continuously seek for the most promising 
investments.  
To keep up with competition and industry transformation, VCs are obliged to further 
develop their startup screening and evaluation approaches. A prerequisite of long-term 
competitiveness is the awareness of new technologies. Hence, the automatization of the VC 
industry plays a significant role in the performance of VCs and has impact on the startup 
landscape and consequently the economic health and growth. 
 VC investment decision making 
To lead through venture investment approaches, critical factors and measures that are 
relevant in a VC’s venture selection and evaluation process will be explained below. Taking 
the classic approaches and measures as a basis, the thesis will further narrow down to factors 
and criteria that are publicly accessible online. The process of how this data can be gathered 
and why it plays a role in the venture valuation process will be further detailed. 
 Information asymmetry 
In ideal circumstances, a decision is made based on a holistic and sufficiently well-
known information basis (Kahneman, 2012). In contrast, early-stage venture investments take 
place on a very limited set of known factors bearing high uncertainty (Kirsch, Goldfarb, & 
Gera, 2009, p. 489) also stated as “investing in the unknown and unknowable” (Diebold, 
Doherty, & Herring, 2010, p. 313). Early-stage venture investors have to make decisions under 
high information asymmetry (Andranik Tumasjan, Braun, & Stolz, 2021, p. 1) with a hardly 
predictable risk qualification (L. Huang & Pearce, 2015, p. 2).  The uncertainty is caused firstly 
by the dynamic environment of a startup. In the early stages of a venture development process,

entrepreneurs face many challenges including the planning and implementation of product 
development, team management, customer relationship management, sales channel building, 
and production processes. As these are challenging attributes for the entrepreneurs themselves, 
they are even less calculable for outside stakeholders leading to the fact that VCs must mitigate 
these uncertainties before taking investment decisions.  
However, taking measurable factors into account is challenging since early-stage 
startups provide only a short operating and mostly insignificant financial track record 
(Sørensen, 2007, p. 2726; Andranik Tumasjan et al., 2021, p. 2). Accordingly, a significant 
number of ventures is pre-revenue at time of their first funding, e.g. 69 percent in a study by 
Wong (2002, p. 11) and 38,5 percent in a study by S. N. Kaplan and Strömberg (2003, p. 285). 
Because of their short track records, VCs cannot build on the performance history of the venture 
(Andranik Tumasjan et al., 2021, p. 2) in contrast to investors and private equity firms in later 
stage ventures.  
The accountability of information provided by startups is aggravated by the principal 
agent problem. In the relationship between the principal (entrepreneur) and the agent (VC) 
entrepreneurs have naturally incentives to act on their behalf. As a result, entrepreneurs tend to 
overstate own reports on their venture’s performance, such as future growth and earnings, to 
rise the interest of investors (Jin, Wu, & Hitt, 2017, p. 2). This leads to the conflict that the 
information by entrepreneurs’ lack credibility from the VC’s perspective. Predictions provided 
by the ventures themselves are thus given little attention during the valuation process. 
Particularly early-stage ventures suffer from that conflict, as can provide even less tangible 
information due to their novelty. (Aldrich & Fiol, 1994, p. 646 f.). According to Gompers et al. 
(2020, p. 3), nearly a third of early stage investors and 20 percent of all VCs do not forecast 
cashflows before their investment decision.  
Thus, approaches based on tangible factors such as cashflows and other accounting 
information find little relevance in early stage venture investing (Felske, 2020, p. 20). 
Conversely, VCs try to mitigate this conflict by collecting soft factors that are less measurable 
and rely more of more subjective matter. Consequently, decision making of VCs relies to a 
significant extent on the investor’s intuition and gut feeling (Hisrich & Jankowicz, 1990, p. 50; 
L. Huang & Pearce, 2015, p. 6). This makes venture selection a manual and biased process. The 
low objectivization of variables thereby creates high efforts for the VCs in terms of manual deal 
selection and evaluation.

 Investment selection and evaluation process 
The investment process follows a funnel from a broad deal-sourcing to a potential deal-
closing. Evaluation processes in the literature can be distinguished by the number of process 
stages, e.g. six stages (Fried & Hisrich, 1994), five stages (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984) and two 
stages (Festel, Wuermseher, & Cattaneo, 2013, p. 213). However, all approaches follow a 
common funnel, starting with sourcing and screening potential deals and proceed toward 
closing a deal and post-investment activities.  
The main task of a VC is illustrated in Figure 1. The investment process consists of 
sourcing investment opportunities, screening, and eventually evaluating the non-rejected 
ventures. In case the venture is not rejected in the previous stages a final valuation and 
investment process will be considered. The investment relationship may be continued by 
follow-on investments. Eventually the VC exits and closes the deal in an Initial Public Offering 
(IPO) or Merger & Acquisitions (M&A) process (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984).  
Figure 1: VC investment process and thesis focus 
The figure shows a combination of the VC investment process as illustrated by Tyebjee and Bruno (1984) and Fried and Hisrich 
(1994). The color bar illustrates the focus weight of this thesis. 
 
As the figure illustrates, quantitative and automated tools, such as data-mining, Machine 
Learning (ML) and Artificial Intelligence (AI) are especially relevant within the first three 
stages (Retterath, 2020a; Schmidt, 2019). As the color bar in the Figure 1 reflects, this thesis 
elaborates on data-based approaches across these three stages. The focus is on the identification 
and pre-selection of promising ventures with relevance from deal origination to evaluation. The 
characteristics of the first three stages are discussed below. 
Deal origination/sourcing 
Deal origination is an essential step in the pre-investment process, where an initial pool 
of potential candidates is created. In this regards a wide channel is a prerequisite to achieve 
exceptional investments and therefore fund performance (Sahlman, 1990) implying “the more 
options the better”. This makes it crucial for investors to retrieve a variety of startups to avoid 
excluding potentially promising investment opportunities. From a value creation perspective, 
the deal-sourcing process is of substantial importance as it naturally serves as the initial source

more to investment return (60 percent) than the later value added by the VC (40 percent). This 
also reflects the time spent in these processes. According to (Gompers et al., 2020, p. 2), VCs

Screening 
Screening describes the process where the “wheat [separates] from the chaff” (Retterath, 
2020a, p. 101). This step involves the thereafter following process of pre-selecting companies 
with a promising profile in terms of success probability. Due to time constraints, only a fracture 
of the initial company set originated in the sourcing step will be further processed through the 
investment funnel. Thus, VCs assess venture profiles considering different indicators of 
success. The screening process underlies high pressure (Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2001) because 
of the large amount of possible opportunities. According to Hartmann, Zaki, Feldmann, and 
Neely (2016), deal sourcing together with deal selection results in 67 percent of the VC’s value 
added.  
(E)valuation 
After having identified possible candidates for further evaluation, the following raises 
the question of an appropriate valuation approach. Valuation refers to the financial assessment 
of company value and determines a price the VC is willing or asked to pay as return for agreed 
equity shares (Miloud, Aspelund, & Cabrol, 2012). Valuation can be separated into quantitative 
and qualitative methods. While qualitative methods play a significant role particularly for early-
stage ventures due to their lack of tangible and financial information, quantitative valuation 
methods become relevant towards later stages of venture maturity (e.g. Ang et al., 2020, p. 5). 
Commonly used methods include the pre- and post-money valuation. Pre-money calculates a 
company’s value before an investment and post-money refers to the valuation after funding 
(Frei & Leleux, 2004). A method established in the financial industry is the discounted-
cashflow (DCF) method (Dittmann, Maug, & Kemper, 2004; Festel et al., 2013). Other methods 
include the Book Value method, the application of scorecards, and the Risk Factor Summation 
method (Nasser, 2017).  
As the thesis focuses on the pre-investment process of ventures the actual financial 
valuation plays a less significant role. In these lines, the following part discusses previous 
research in terms of status-quo and trends focused on automatization in VC investment 
processes.

 Manual and quantitative sourcing approaches 
As described, the sourcing and initial identification of potential investment opportunities 
builds an important set of potential investment prospects. According to Gompers et al. (2020, 
p. 7f.), only one percent of early-stage venture deals are generated exclusively through 
quantitative deal-sourcing. The largest share of closed deals initially origins from the VCs 
network and personal communication efforts. The critical share of VC deal flow (around 58 
percent) is generated from the VCs network (referred by other investors, inbound from 
management, or self-generated). Nevertheless, Gompers et al. (2020, p. 7f.) presume an 
increasing use of quantitative sourcing tools and predict increased automatization as upcoming 
trend.  
The channels of finding investment opportunities can be classified into inbound and 
outbound channels (Retterath, 2020a, p. 64). Inbound channels describe when a startup itself 
approaches VCs e.g., by e-mail or direct connections. Conversely, outbound channels describe 
when the investor actively tries to explore and source investment opportunities. This includes 
the usage of match making platforms, suggesting pre-tailored investment opportunities, the 
usage of accelerator websites, and VC databases. According to (Retterath, 2020a, p. 64), VC 
databases embody the by far most important (outhouse) outbound source as these databases 
provide a high company coverage and largest scope of potential investment opportunities. This 
puts importance on the dependency of VCs on the information of VC databases. The relevance 
and bottlenecks of outbound data based automatization approaches will be discussed below.  
 VC outbound data sources 
As mentioned, automatization (e.g. AI and ML) in VC finds application in stages of 
sourcing, screening and evaluation (Schmidt, 2019). In this regard, VCs are strongly dependent 
on the data variety, quantity and quality of external venture databases. According to a 
benchmarking study by Retterath (2020a, pp. 54-92), Crunchbase and Pitchbook are the most 
used databases by VC practitioners with Crunchbase having the highest dominance (32 percent) 
and Pitchbook the second highest (21 percent). Data from Crunchbase and Pitchbook build the 
center of data retrieval for private company data sets. Although further data providers such as 
Angellist, Traxcn operate on the market, they have little practical relevance (see Figure 2).

 Bottlenecks of VC databases in academics and practice  
As previously discussed, little extensive attempts in using alternative sources exist to 
generate novel data points as supporting input variables. This may be especially due to the lack 
of insights into obtaining new data sources on a large scale and little awareness for further 
meaningful variables besides the provided variables of e.g., Crunchbase. Because of the 
monotonous data variety, investigated variables and features for startup success prediction 
overlap largely. Some attempts fail especially with regards to early-stage startups based on the 
data provided by Crunchbase (Ang et al., 2020, p. 24). This prevailing data scarcity for startup 
companies in the realm of startup prediction is a recognized problem (Ünal, 2019, p. 41). 
Authors mark the limitations of the usage of databases like Crunchbase (Banerji & Reimer, 
2019, p. 51; Ünal, 2019, p. 41) and underline the relevance of new data identification 
approaches (Felske, 2020, p. 160; Retterath, 2020a, p. 145; Xiang et al., 2012). Along with the 
issue of data scarcity, Arroyo et al. (2019, p. 124242) calls for the consideration and elaboration 
of further variables. Also Retterath (2020a, p. 145), who conducted a large-scale study on the 
application of ML on venture decision support and calls to elaborate and investigate new 
methods to collect conventional and unconventional venture variables.  
Based on a survey, Retterath (2020a, p. 119) reveals, only 10 percent of VCs were 
confident to make decisions based on screening criteria that only include general company 
information. In this context, adding information on the startup’s team alone would increase the 
percentage of confident investors to 53.8 percent. This implies the relevance of adding 
additional information to the general information on the team and potentially other factors. 
Consequently, research as well as databased VC decisions depend critically on the data variety 
from VC databases. Furthermore, Big Data literature reaching out to unconventional platforms 
and data that supplement Crunchbase is scarce and calls for novel approaches.

3. Venture Success Signals and Investment Criteria 
Considering the data scarcity on private ventures, it is unrealistic to reach a perfect 
information basis. Nonetheless, as discussed, the information density has potential to be 
improved through Big Data approaches. Before deciding which supplementary variables can be 
collected, literature is screened for criteria that can be associated with promising ventures from 
a VC’s perspective. This question is tackled by reviewing signals that are influential on venture 
performance based on academic research.  
In depth company related success factors 
On a holistic view many variables need to be considered. Critical venture success factors 
include the business model (Zott & Amit, 2008), the product’s potential, e.g. in terms of novelty 
(Hofer & Sandberg, 1987) and differentiation (Hofer & Sandberg, 1987; Miloud et al., 2012) 
and product timing (Di Benedetto, 1999). From a market perspective, market and industry 
characteristics are important dimensions (MacMillan, Siegel, & Narasimha, 1985; Zacharakis 
& Meyer, 2000). Furthermore a VC’s considerations in terms of IPO (Black & Gilson, 1999) 
and M&A exit possibilities (Smith, Pedace, & Sathe, 2011) are determining investment 
decisions. In this regard, the literature provides a large body of factors that could be considered 
as influential factors indicating the development and success of startups. 
However, as described in Chapter 2.2, the central scope of the thesis are investment 
stages that are focal in a data-based automatization context. Thus, while the above-mentioned 
factors are naturally of importance in an VC’s eventual investment decision, they require 
detailed internal company information and individual market assessment. Consequently, they 
are less quantifiable in automated identification and screening approaches of investment 
opportunities. A detailed review of these factors is thus beyond the scope of the thesis. 
 (Traditional) success measures 
Before the focus is set on soft factors, e.g. human capital, institutional factors and social 
media, Chapter 3.1 takes a brief look at (traditional) metrics to measure success. 
IPO and Merger & Acquisitions  
A common measure for the success of a portfolio company is an initial public offering 
(IPO) or the event of an acquisition by a third party (Nahata, 2008, p. 130). Mostly, this also 
implies a VC’s exit, selling the previously acquired equity stakes. Accordingly, scholars define 
successful companies as companies that achieved an IPO or have been acquired and 
unsuccessful companies as companies that did not reach such status (e.g. Gompers & Lerner, 
==PAGE:23==
 
15 
2000; Nahata, 2008). It is necessary to mention that not every M&A transaction is per se up to 
the VC’s return expectations, which mitigates M&A success measure function (Gompers et al., 
2020, p. 18). 
Funding  
Another commonly used funding status related measure found in previous studies is the 
fact whether a startup received funding and total funding amount. Accordingly, an investors 
willingness to invest in a company signals the quality of the company. Greater VC funding 
indicates greater quality of the company (e.g. Nahata, 2008, p. 134 f.). Thus, funding is an 
indicator of startup success whereas funded companies are reportedly more successful than non-
funded companies.  
Accounting based success measures 
 Less event related measures are equity and accounting-based measures. Financial 
performance metrics, (e.g., revenue, EBITDA2, and cashflows) embody fundamental and 
accurate indicators to evaluate previous company performance and predict future venture 
success. As they build the fundament of traditional company valuation, accounting based 
metrics naturally represent an intuitive success measure. If these information are provided, the 
meaningfulness of financial statements is hardly unsurpassable in venture valuation processes 
since venture’s financial statements and KPIs are an accurate and realistic measure of 
performance (e.g. Tian, Yu, & Guo, 2015).  
 Non-financial success criteria and soft factors 
While Chapter 3.1 discussed mostly financial success measures, the following 
subchapters discuss non-financial investment criteria. The presented criteria involve a diverse 
and less finance related scope. The presented factors serve the identification and inspiration for 
less conventional, yet relevant criteria based on previous research. This could indicate new and 
relevant factors to be collected for startup evaluation. 
 Human capital and team 
Gompers et al. (2020, p. 9), surveyed 885 VCs regarding their focal points in investment 
decisions and perceived success criteria. 96 percent of the VCs mentioned the 
management/founding team as a crucial factor in their decision-making process. This weighted 
importance from a VC’s perspective is consistent with previous studies classifying human

capital as key signals of startup success (e.g. Baum & Silverman, 2004; Chatterjee, Lubatkin, 
Schweiger, & Weber, 1992). Following a definition by Pennings, Lee, and Witteloostuijn 
(1998, p. 426) a firm’s human capital is defined as “the knowledge and skills of its professionals 
that can be used to produce professional services”. Accordingly, human capital is an important 
proxy to determine the success potential of early-stage ventures and deserves further attention. 
In this context, it needs to be defined how human capital can be measured. Becker (2002) 
determines two fundamental measures for human capital as knowledge and experience. Both 
were previously investigated in literature in context with entrepreneurship (Marvel & Lumpkin, 
2007) and will be discussed below.  
 Experience   
Various literature streams indicate that labor market experience, management 
experience and previous entrepreneurial experience influence venture outcomes in a positive 
way (Dahlqvist, Davidsson, & Wiklund, 2001; Gastaud, Carniel, & Dalle, 2019, p. e.g. ). 
Research emphasizes the meaningfulness of social and human capital (Plummer, 
Allison, & Connelly, 2016, p. 1589ff.) and in particular the relevance of founder’s experience 
in venture success. Zhang and Wiersema (2009) found for example a signaling function of 
managerial experience on stock markets. According to Ucbasaran, Lockett, Wright, and 
Westhead (2003) higher managerial experience is positively correlated with the manager’s 
ability in opportunity recognition. Further research states that the more diverse a person’s 
experience, the higher is the likelihood, the person is self-employed. Accordingly, founder 
experience is another decisive factor measured as relevant variable as success proxy for startup 
funding and respectively a success indicator. 
Another statement from literature is that serial founders are more successful than novel 
founders. This has been found in different studies regarding venture performance (Nahata, 
2019; Paik, 2014; Toft-Kehler, Wennberg, & Kim, 2014).  Gastaud et al. (2019, p. 9) measure 
founder experience as number of previously founded companies and highlights the 
meaningfulness in regards of venture success. Thus, it could be valuable to access information 
on the number of companies the founders of prospect ventures previously founded.  
 Education  
Analogue to experience, education suggested as a performance indicator. In this context, 
previous studies give evidence that better educated founders perform better (Backes-Gellner & 
Werner, 2007, p. e.g.; Gounopoulos, Loukopoulos, & Loukopoulos, 2021). Accordingly, 
==PAGE:25==
 
17 
startups by founders with a higher academic degree establish more innovative startups (Backes-
Gellner & Werner, 2007). Similar consensus has been found by Gounopoulos et al. (2021) who 
states that a venture’s IPO success is related to higher educated CEOs. Education related 
success is thereby attributed to higher intellect, more talented and pressure resistance of 
founders (Miller, Xu, & Mehrotra, 2015). Furthermore, the quality of the academic institution 
has been claimed to be a value driver. For example reputable universities provide a better social 
network and study quality (Colombo, Meoli, & Vismara, 2019).  
From a VC’s perspective, there exist arguments that VCs weigh their personal similarity 
to the founders’ profile as important. This suggestion has its theoretical foundation in sociology, 
restored in the term homophily. Homophily describes the tendency of individuals being 
attracted more by individuals with common characteristics, e.g. gender, age, values or education 
(McPherson, Smith-Lovin, & Cook, 2001; Rogers & Bhowmik, 1970, p. 416). In this context, 
Bengtsson and Hsu (2010, p. 4) state that the prevalence of similarities between founder and 
VC partner decreases information costs. VCs identifying similarities among founders reduce 
their matching costs for investors due to lower screening efforts (Bengtsson & Hsu, 2010, p. 
3f.). Accordingly, a match is more likely when both founder and VC graduated at top ranked 
university.  
 Team size and human resource development  
In accordance with previous research, Gastaud et al. (2019) find that team size has 
significant impact on the growth probability of startups and thus influences the attractiveness 
towards investors.  A larger team is more capable in work distribution and allocation along team 
members (Eisenhardt & Schoonhoven, 1990, p. 510), marketing and sales and external relation 
processes, such as acquiring key outside advisers (Kazanjian, 1988, p. 273).  
Consequently, human resources activities are a mandatory and decisive step as the firm 
growths and presumably a necessary step to drive firm growth itself. This raises interest in 
monitoring the firm’s activities in human resources and puts focus on whether this could be 
collected as a potential indicator for firm performance. 
In context of the above-mentioned team size and employment activity, also the 
headcount (HC) growth of a venture has been previously found as an important proxy for 
venture success (Caliendo & Kritikos, 2008; Davila, Foster, & Gupta, 2003; Hall, 1987). HC 
growth further has been found to be related to a startup’s valuation as Davila et al. (2003, p. 
700f.) explore.  
==PAGE:26==

Accordingly, the exact team size, a startup’s human resources development and the team 
growth in terms of headcount suggest important indicators for company success and thus 
investment decisions.  
 Startup ecosystem 
Geographic specifics have been previously found to be influential on venture 
performance. VCs are more likely to invest in “geographically close ventures (Lerner, 1995; 
Sørensen, 2007, p. 2727). This may be caused for example by geographical proximity effects 
that cause knowledge (e.g. technology and policy related) spillover effects according to OECD 
country studies (Schreyer, 2000, p. 23f.). As a result, an outstanding number of high-growth 
firms is established in a small number of regions. The OECD dataset shows e.g. France, Spain 
and Germany as countries with centered high-growth venture hotspots. On a closer regional 
scope, share of high-growth ventures is centered in single urban hotspots with high economic 
activity. This is consistent with a recent ML based study indicating that geographical location 
is an important success factorb by Ang et al. (2020). The authors find that regions with a higher 
centralization of economic acitivities locate more investors in that specific region and origin 
more succesful startups. They find that specific locations, e.g. the San Francisco Bay Area and 
London place more operational startups in their dataset. Furthermore, they find that these 
hotspots place startups with the highest success rate as measured in the number of IPOs in their 
dataset.  
 Web attention and social media signals 
Social media plays a mediating role in the information exchange and may serve as a 
solution to the lack of information that market participants are exposed to. Particularly social 
media platforms and news publications could function as venture performance signal in case 
they can be retrieved to a sufficient degree for early-stage ventures.   
 Social media signals 
In previous studies the consensus has amplified that social media attention and activities 
play a meaningful role in context with a venture’s fundraising outcomes. The attempt to connect 
media signals with venture prediction and performance has also found interest in Big Data 
related studies. Few research projects have thereby taken different media signals as a variable. 
Ren, Raghupathi, and Raghupathi (2020) recently strengthened the conception that web and 
media attention plays an influencing role in venture performance as measured in funding. A 
==PAGE:27==
 
19 
startup’s appearance on social media increases its visibility to market participants. This 
enhances the probability to achieve the awareness and get in an investor’s sights. As startups 
use social media as communication and marketing channel, their social media appearance may 
serve as company specific information channel an additional information channel for outsiders 
(Weibl & Hess, 2019).  The published content and receiving users’ reactions can be transformed 
into useful indicator and shed light on company online prestige and digital footstep (Jin et al., 
2017, p. 2).  
In this regard assessing sentiment scores of published contents, e.g. a positive or 
negative mood has been applied to measure emotional states of texts (e.g. positive or negative 
mood) (e.g. Andranik Tumasjan et al., 2021, p. 2). The sentiment analysis, also referred to 
opinion mining, is a widely spread approach in context of natural language processing research. 
It represents a valuable approach to computationally assess the subjectivity of opinions, 
emotions and attitudes in a written text or paragraph (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014, p. 219). Sentiment 
analyses have found application in various research contexts.  
For example, a research by A. Tumasjan, Sprenger, Sandner, and Welpe (2010) shows 
the value of sentiment in context of how politicians’ public social media perception relates to 
election outcomes. In the financial sector, Albrecht, Lutz, and Neumann (2019 investigate the 
impact of social media sentiment on financial performance by measuring the signaling role of 
sentiment on the outcome of initial coin offerings (ICOs) of blockchain ventures. They find that 
positive language use is positively correlated to the ICO performance. Furthermore, pioneer 
studies have investigated the role of twitter sentiment as a soft signal in VC financing (Andranik 
Tumasjan et al., 2021). Thus it is important to evaluate whether social media data can also be 
found and used for early-stage startup evaluation. Furthermore as studies primarily focussed on 
the sentiment it could be valuable to identify whether further social media related KPIs can be 
measured and potentially deliver a novel signal for venture valuation.  
 News 
News and media articles belong along with other documents including business plans 
and financial forecasts to the documents often provided by founders when they disclose initial 
information towards VCs in fundraising proposals (Aggarwal & Singh, 2013; Felske, 2020; A. 
G. Huang & Mamo, 2016). This implies that information on a venture’s media appearance may 
be of substantial interest in the pre-investment screening process. The media thereby may build 
a foundation to grasp a first impression on a venture’s popularity in terms of media and 
journalism interest (Xiang et al., 2012). A few studies have yet implemented computed 
==PAGE:28==
 
20 
sentiment for the analysis for news articles (Agarwal, 2020; Fong, Zhuang, Li, & Khoury, 2013; 
Raina, 2013; Urologin, 2018). Capital market focused studies have found evidence that the tone 
of news influences the investor’s perception and a more positive sentiment is suggested to 
influence the investor’s willingness to invest in a company (A. G. Huang & Mamo, 2016, p. 
5f.). In this context, various parameters were found to be influential on equity investor behavior. 
Although previous literature predominantly focusses on the capital market, the usability of news 
sentiment for startup success prediction could be also valuable in case information can be 
collected on early stage-ventures.  
 Summary and research gap 
Unless investors do possess fundamental information provided from internal 
information sources, they may have to look at less financially related signals and take them into 
account as a basis for the first decision processes (Andranik Tumasjan et al., 2021). In this term 
general factors such as the ecosystem quality may deliver first insights on the potential of a 
venture. Furthermore, the founder’s human capital and team related factors are important 
factors in venture evaluation. Detailed founder information on founders’ experience and 
academic profile are suggested as key signals of startup potential. Additionally, previous 
research suggests that soft signals provide an increasingly meaningful information channel for 
early-stage ventures, which implies a higher relevance of the inclusion of novel and non-
financial criteria. Signals, such as the social media sentiment of ventures’ online posts can be 
valuable for an investor's decision-making process (Albrecht, Lutz, & Neumann, 2019; 
Andranik Tumasjan et al., 2021, p. 12). Also, news related factors may provide valuable insights 
on the venture’s previous viral footstep and public acknowledgment (Jin et al., 2017). Soft 
factors in this context may add supplementary information to mitigate the information 
asymmetry and scarcity of early-stage ventures.  
However, even though research suggests these signals as success measures, the 
difficulty in accessing and assessing these variables efficiently on a large scale remains in force. 
First, the automatic collection of variables in a structured manner needs to be solved to empower 
quantitative deal-sourcing approaches. This puts importance on identifying methods to retrieve 
detailed background information of startups and founders. Second, the value of information 
retrieved by automatic data mining needs to be explored. In this sense, the next chapters 
elaborate on research questions 2 and 3, focusing on how and to what extent these signals can 
be retrieved. Building on the provided information of existing data providers, it will be analyzed 
how innovative approaches can supplement a lack of information in regards of the elaborated

web-based channels.

4. Research Methodology and Data Collection Strategy   
 Research Design  
This chapter introduces the research design, data collection methods, and data collection 
strategy. 
 Research methodology   
While deductive reasoning focuses on “testing a single theory for empirical adequacy” 
(Ketokivi & Mantere, 2010, p. 318), this research applies an inductive research approach not 
intending to test theory-driven hypotheses (Woo, O'Boyle, & Spector, 2017, p. 5). In this sense, 
this thesis is focusing on developing and applying data mining methods in order to draw 
inferences about explored patterns in the data as suggested in inductive Big Data research 
(McAbee, Landis, & Burke, 2017).  
Following inductive Big Data research disciplines, this thesis relies on an explanatory 
data analysis approach, which allows to force the data analyses in discovery and exploration of 
phenomena in data (Jebb, Parrigon, & Woo, 2017). This approach opens the spectrum of various 
data analysis and data visualization opportunities, which was a prerequisite in this thesis to 
avoid restricting the range of approachable data sources and respectively to expand the 
interpretative freedom of the data variety collected based on the thesis’ research questions. This 
approach is also referring to the goal of retrieving a high data scope in terms of volume and 
variety as suggested by George, Osinga, Lavie, and Scott (2016, p. 1495), suggesting that 
researchers yet need to commit to new questions based on a high data scope and granularity. 
Along these lines, Big Data methods and analytics embody a new and innovative approach to 
embrace inductive organizational and management research (McAbee et al., 2017, pp. 2, 11). 
To discuss research question 2, data mining approaches will be developed. The focus 
on presenting results in terms of their accessibility, collection, storing, and the applicability of 
the developed algorithms correspond with the central ideas of knowledge discovery in databases 
(KDD) (Fayyad, Piatetsky-Shapiro, & Smyth, 1996, p. 39f.) and the CRISP-DM framework 
(Saltz, 2020), which are the most popular data mining process frameworks.  
“KDD focuses on the overall process of knowledge discovery from data, including 
how the data are stored and accessed, how algorithms can be scaled to massive data 
sets and still run efficiently, how results can be interpreted and visualized, and how 
the overall man-machine interaction can usefully be modeled and supported.”

The thereby developed and implemented Big Data mining approaches results to the 
collection of large volume dataset with high variety. After the data have been preprocessed, 
exploratory analyses approaches will be applied to answer research question 3 and identify 
meaningful content and patterns in the data.  
 Web scraping as data collection approach 
One of the key challenges for research is “to think outside the box” to reveal new 
valuable insights (George et al., 2016, p. 1496). This philosophy encourages the commitment 
to innovative and less established research approaches, such as attending to unveil valuable 
insights from large dataset approaches. In terms of data collection, George et al. (2016) suggests 
web-scraping as innovative method to exploit available data and overcome challenges of data 
collection. As technological advancements opened new opportunities in data collection, the 
“researcher’s imagination” may be more limiting than the technological possibilities. Web-
scraping thereby allows to expand the horizon of retrievable information and allows the 
researcher to achieve a new perspective on problems and raising new questions as the data 
granularity and the data scope increases. The web-scraping approach within this thesis 
addresses rather the spectrum of high scope and high granularity (George et al., 2016). 
 Python as programming language for web-scraping 
Web scraping describes the process of saving data, mostly in textual and numeric form 
from websites. Web-scraping describes “the construction of an agent to download, parse, and 
organize data from the web in an automated manner” (Broucke & Baesens, 2018, p. 3). A main 
value proposition of web-scraping embodies its automatization function whereby a machine or 
program is automating the manual efforts that would be necessary to retrieve data from the 
internet. From a practical standpoint, web-scraping revealed significant economic value and is 
meanwhile an established data acquisition approach used by businesses. In the thesis’ scope, 
web-scraping fits the purpose of automatization of VC data collection processes and delivers a 
valuable method to exploit possibilities of advanced IT technologies.  
As web-scraping is the pursued data collection method, a reliable and universally 
established programming language needs to be chosen to ensure the generalizability and 
usability of developed programs. In the programming area, Python is a popular object-oriented 
programming language valued for its readability and general-purpose applicability. It is ranked 
among the ten most popular programming languages since 2003 according to the TIOBE 
==PAGE:32==
 
24 
Programming Community Index (TIOBE, 2021)3. Python offers a versatile and established 
programming environment and represents a powerful tool for web-scraping and large scale data 
analysis (e.g. Broucke & Baesens, 2018, p. XV). Therefore, Python was used as the 
fundamental programming language in context of this thesis. 
 Data mining and evaluation workflow 
Each data collection approach follows the framework of CRISP-DM as defined below.  
CRISP-DM stands for “Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining” (Wirth & 
Hipp, 2000, p. 1). The framework became a leading approach among data miners and data 
scientists (Saltz, 2020). It represents a general process of data-mining projects independent of 
the industry and technology applied (Wirth & Hipp, 2000, pp. 1-2). The CRISP-DM process 
guides the general data processing and data handling approach in this thesis, whereas the 
following steps build the most relevant.  
Step (1) Identify the business problem. The business problem refers to the goal to be 
achieved. Regarding the thesis this step refers to research question 2 and 3, the collection and 
analysis of signals that may support the prediction of startup success.  
Step (2) Identify data sources: Following, relevant data sources that are in line with the 
overall business problem solution need to be identified. Along with research question 1, signals 
were derived from theory in Chapter 3 to answer the most sufficient data sources.  
The following Chapter 5 involves particular the following Step (3) and Step (4).  
Step (3) Select the data. Despite the saying, “the more data the better”, irrelevant data 
collection should be omitted. Accordingly, relevant data needs to be collected and stored.  
Step (4) Clean the data: Data cleaning includes data preprocessing where 
inconsistencies, duplicates and missing values are handled.  
Building on Chapter 5, Chapter 6 involves Step (5) and Step (6).  
Step (5) Transform the data:  Data transformation into appropriate data types is essential 
for further processing, e.g. coding textual strings to numeric data.  
Step (6) Analyze the data. After conducting all previous steps, the actual data exploration 
and analysis can be implemented, which leads to new insights.  
The last following and last step is particularly relevant when a developed application is 
launched on the market or integrated in practice in a user-friendly way, which is not included 
due to the limited scope of the thesis.

relevance for VCs, to also analyze whether information on startups can be retrieved efficiently.

 Web scraping specifics and selection of target measures 
 Web-scraping  
Libraries 
Programming efficiency can be drastically improved by using libraries. Libraries can be 
explained by a collection of modules that can be installed including pre-defined executable 
statements. For web-scraping two established Python libraries are of main relevance in this 
context. Beautiful Soup and Selenium. Both are established and popular libraries in web-
scraping application (Broucke & Baesens, 2018). As of limited scope of the thesis on technical 
explanations of established software, the libraries are only briefly discussed below. For a 
comprehensive discussion of Selenium and Beautiful Soup, it is referred to Broucke and 
Baesens (2018). 
Beautiful Soup. The purpose of Beautiful Soup is to organize complex website content 
“it helps to parse, structure and organize the oftentimes very messy web by fixing bad HTML 
and presenting us with an easy-to-work-with Python structure.” (Broucke & Baesens, 2018, p. 
61). With Beautiful Soup almost any website can be opened, and the raw text information can 
be stored. After storing, the text can be then scanned and cleaned for relevant information.  
Selenium. The second library is called “Selenium” (Selenium, 2021). Selenium’s main 
purpose is to automate browsers (Selenium, 2021) and was built with the purpose of automated 
website testing. Selenium requires the integration of an external “WebDriver”, such as Firefox, 
or Google Chrome (Broucke & Baesens, 2018, p. 134). The WebDriver represents the actual 
interface for automating the actions that would usually be done by a human user. Selenium can 
perform valuable actions such as clicking buttons or scrolling down on a website. This 
automation capability is valuable for web-scraping as it avoids often necessary processes, such 
as logging in and entering the required log-in data on the platform. 
 
==PAGE:37==
 
29 
Challenges in web-scraping processes 
An important issue to mention in the application of web-scraping is the dynamic nature 
of websites. As noted above, websites are built on basis of HTML code. The named scraping 
tools are directly accessing the HTML code. However, as platform providers ongoingly change 
the strcuture of HTML codes, e.g. as a consequence of content updates and optimizations, the 
initially prevalent HTML can change. Thus, it is necessary to reanalyze the website structure 
and update the code respectively. Consequently, all codes and algortihms extracting 
infromation from HTML must be constantly updated and approved for correctness.  
Another important challenge to mention is that many platforms try to prevent scraping 
processes by implementing request limits. A high frequent number of requests often results in 
server errors and restricted server access. This requires work around solutions, which can be 
only identified in an interative coding and error decoding process. This was especially necessary 
for some processes in LinkedIn, and Google searches since no APIs for public usage are 
provided as discussed in Chapter 5.2. To reduce conflicts, all of the scraping approaches in this 
thesis prevent exceeding request rates, which required the integration of a “sleep timer”, 
meaning the request frequency “lags” to simulate human interaction. 
APIs 
A valuable method to avoid the above-mentioned challenges are provided by 
Application Programming Interfaces (APIs). APIs enable controlled access to selected 
resources provided by the hosting organizations themselves. Generally, the application of APIs 
is more preferable than above mentioned web-scraping methods because APIs do not cause any 
of the above mentioned conflicts. Another important advantage is that APIs allow a more 
structured access on the server provided data, which means data preprocessing and cleaning 
processes can be mitigated. Thus, an API access would be always preferrably. However,  within 
this thesis, twitter’s API accesable with the library “Tweepy”4 was the only applicaple API.

 Passive LinkedIn data access  
As previously mentioned, headcount growth suggests a valuable success measure and

websites with current information. This suggested an attractive approach to circumvent a log-

considerable as convenient channel to passively access LinkedIn data. The approach revealed 
two relevant institutional measures of interest: the number of employees and number of a 
venture’s followers.  
Google specific keywords techniques offer a way to increase the density relevant search 
results. The right combination of keywords and operators is a matter of trial and error. In terms 
of LinkedIn search, the following request turned out to reveal the most constant and accurate 
results: 
URL = (1)http://google.com/search?q= +(2) allintext +(3)companyname[i] +(4)”view all employees” 
The first part(1)is the basic Google search URL, which can be seen in any Google 
request. The Google operator allintext limits the search results exclusively to the websites 
where the entered keyword is included. The (3)companyname[i] is the variable that will be

 Twitter and news sentiment analysis  
As previously discussed, the online generated content could give information on how a 
venture is perceived (in case of news) or how the venture communicates towards their followers 
(in case of twitter tweets). A useful method may be in both cases a sentiment analysis. 
Before the sentiment could be analyzed, the following steps of text normalization and 
extraction of stop words were relevant for both Twitter tweets and news, as both channels will 
be analyzed for their sentiment. 
Text normalization 
A common step of text preprocessing and normalization in the process of sentiment 
analyses is to exclude unnecessary text fractures and expressions (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014). This 
includes the following steps: Text Cleaning and preprocessing i.e., special character cleaning, 
abbreviation replacement, html format and lower case transformation. 
To increase the relevance of the assessed texts it is beneficial to exclude words and 
expressions that have a high textual contribution, however, are of little value for the eventual 
sentiment analysis. These are also described with stop words and were excluded from the 
eventual texts. As there is no exhaustive stop word list, an easily accessible and popularly used 
stop words dictionary was imported. 
Sentiment analysis 
After the tweets were cleaned with the previous steps, they were analyzed using the 
VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary for Sentiment Reasoning) sentiment analysis. VADER is 
a model that computes sentiment scores of given textual input and is valuable for any sort of 
research problems involving textual data. This was the reason why it was also applied in context 
of the social media and news analysis. As both channels (Twitter, and News) are analyzed for 
their sentiment, both approaches follow the same process.

7. Discussion and Implications  
In the following chapter a concluding discussion of the previous data exploration will 
be presented. 
 Implications from data exploration 
Sentiment 
Twitter sentiment. In regards of Twitter, strong negative sentiment scores were 
reached. Manual checks revealed that negative scores were sometimes caused by company 
unrelated negative sentences and expressions. This shows the limitations of sentiment analyses. 
This means the sensitivity of the sentiment scores can cause ambiguous results and lead to 
wrong implications. Also, the relatively low correlation scores show yet low signaling power 
as stand-alone parameter.  
News. News sentiment reaches comparably more positive scores than twitter sentiment. 
This may be because news potentially have a more formal and positive language in general. In 
regards of correlation analysis, a significant correlation between sentiment and funding was 
measured. This has implications that news sentiment could potentially be a meaningful 
indicator of company success. However, these results must be considered carefully as there may 
be many factors that could bias the correlation e.g. the rather small dataset size.  
Generally, the applicability of sentiment scores in ML models could create more fruitful 
results in future venture prediction approaches. This is true for all collected factors. The 
adoption of all the within this thesis collected variables in a ML program could generate a 
unprecedented powerful tool to predict venture success. 
The correlation analysis further provides insights on the circumstance that number of 
mentions is associated with tangible financial outcomes. However, this does not provide an 
answer to the question whether more mentions influence the funding performance or vice versa 
as the analysis was merely correlational. For future research it would be valuable to conduct 
longitudinal data analysis of the collected variables. 
The study also discussed the meaningfulness of KPIs on correlation analyses. The 
analyses showed that data scraped from public websites can be associated with how much 
money a venture received. Funding is often used as an indicator of success, which shows the 
applicability of the signals as success indicators. The significant correlations strengthen insights 
from previous studies that considered weak factors, such as social network indicators may be 
valuable signals to venture performance.  
==PAGE:66==
 
58 
New and innovative parameters, such as human resources development in terms of the 
number of job postings could thereby serve for VC practitioners as an indicator for previous 
venture performance. Measuring of job postings could also give insight on the venture’s growth 
activities in terms of human capital.  
 Limitations and avenues for future research 
Limitations 
Using Crunchbase as exclusive sample venture dataset may limit the initial data density 
on the included ventures. A combination of venture databases could increase the density of 
funding or general business information.  
Also, a broader scope of possible platforms could be approached as there may exist far 
more publicly available platforms that could include valuable information and success criteria 
on ventures. E.g., the competitive level could be measured by trying to find similarities among 
the business descriptions via further textual evaluation of business descriptions, e.g., available 
on the venture’s website. 
A holistic and in-depth data exploitation was beyond the scope of the thesis but rather 
the demonstration of possibilities to extract a large data variety through web-scraping. Thus, 
the correlation analysis considered only an extraction of the total number the retrieved 
information. The collected data may provide far more quantifiable information and more 
extractable variables. For example, the quantification of soft skills, such as language types and 
language levels could be investigated in relationship to venture performance.  
In context of correlations considering funding amount the issue of reverse causality 
should be critically challenged. Because the Crunchbase dataset naturally provides funding 
information from the past, correlations between collected signals and funding are exposed to a 
reverse causality. This is because funding data is in the past, and most of the collected data like 
employee growth reflect the present. Correlation coefficients with funding could bias 
correlations as better financed companies e.g., may attract more twitter attention or involve 
more popular tweet authors.  
A deeper analysis of the importance and meaningfulness of variables is necessary. The 
explorative approach of this thesis is not sufficient to provide sufficient evidence of the 
meaningfulness of the measured criteria. Regression models, ML algorithms could further 
validate the usability and value of web-scraped company information. 
 
 
==PAGE:67==
 
59 
 Future research 
The issue of reverse causality could be mitigated by measuring the performance on a 
longer time span and including also ongoing funding events. As the news web-scraping method 
showed also funding events could be recorded and filtered, which could be applied as an up-to-
date funding-radar. 
Furthermore, the application of ML approaches could offer a large set of further insights 
and could empower particularly in the prediction of venture success. A combination of 
advanced web-scraping approaches and ML methods could create an extraordinary powerful 
tool for researchers and especially practitioners in context of venture success prediction. Less 
established concepts, such as Named Entity Recognition Techniques (Sharchilev et al., 2018, 
p. 9) could create a set of new opportunities in analyzing general text from websites and 
assessing the relatedness of each startup. 
Also, more subjective screening levels could become more relevant in investment 
screening. For example, data on personality characteristics of founders could be involved. An 
evolving research stream has put focus on the influence of character traits and funding success 
by using text analysis approaches of founders funding pitches.  
A longitudinal study of variables and a larger base data set is a promising approach for 
further insightful research. Future research can investigate on a greater dimension the signaling 
power of various variables that can be collected from publicly available websites on future firm 
development. By observing larger firm data sets with ongoing funding events and continually 
updating company profiles, more detailed analyses and interpretations could be built. 
Longitudinal monitoring of firms beginning from their first VC database entry until their first 
funding events on a Big Data scale could be of tremendous value for understanding what online 
profiles belong to successful companies.  
More research on the interpretability of sentiment scores and the meaningfulness of the 
discussed and scraped variables would shed more light into a possibly new are of venture 
evaluation parameters. While each parameter has already a qualitative meaningfulness, a deeper 
analysis of each parameter individually but especially of the parameters in combination could 
bring valuable performance enhancement in private company evaluation.  
Future Research could also use similar approaches and merge data from several 
databases such as Pitchbook, Dealroom, Venture Source, Angel List to get a higher sample data 
density. The consideration of further signals could also add more valuable variables to be 
collected through web scraping approaches. For example, VC reputation of previous investors 
could be also included as a success indicator and be added to a large-scale database (Ang et al., 
==PAGE:68==
 
60 
2020, p. 23). Similarly to ecosystem and university ranking, an automatic approach of retrieving 
information to take the ranking and popularity of previous investors into account could be 
developed. This may introduce another valuable indicator. 
Larger scaled base data set could reveal new information on the scalability of such web 
scraping approaches.  
 Ethical thoughts  
Eventually, the ethical perspective is important to mention. In the presence of the 
increasing importance of data protection, also the legal perspective of web-scraping deserves 
further sensitive attention. As web-scraping approaches mostly benefit from the “grey zone” 
legal situation, it must be critically evaluated how far web-scraping can be used in terms of 
personal data extraction in the future and where to put the boarders among economic value 
versus ethical and moral thresholds.

8. Conclusion 
 Taking a perspective on the VC industry practice, quantitative deal sourcing is an 
upcoming trend. The increasing size of VC investment rounds and an increasing number of 
deals that must be evaluated puts focus on tools to make VC investment processes less manual 
and call for efficient approaches in the deal sourcing and investment due diligence process. 
Intelligent data analytics solutions in research give proof on the potential of data usage in 
investors’ pre-investment and investment decisions. However, after taking a perspective on the 
used data in research a strong monotony of used databases and the retrieved variables sticks 
out. Data quality and data dimensions are a critical in this regard as discussed above. This issue 
is noticeable considering various requests in literature to find more ways and sources to 
complement existing databases.  
Besides the inconsistent information of data providers, the amount of meaningful 
information on early-stage ventures is a critical problem because of the lack of tangible 
measurables, especially for early-stage ventures. Due to the early development stage, valuation 
of these ventures based on tangibles is not possible. This means VCs are unable to assess early-
stage ventures based on hard factors. An opportunity to compensate the information challenge 
in the venture evaluation process is the consideration of less financial indicators and signals.  
It turns out that factors could be of various types, as literature shows. Some factors may 
be on an institutional level, e.g., the growth of a company. Besides, less quantifiable factors, 
such as human capital, including founder education, founder experience and diversity play are 
crucial criteria. Furthermore, less obvious factors, such as the social media appearance and web 
signals may deliver a relevant signal to evaluate a ventures identity and performance and could 
be supportive in the venture assessment. Accordingly, especially team related information and 
web-based signals were found to provide valuable implications on a venture’s success potential.  
However, these factors represent a critical challenge in the deal automatization 
processes, as existing databases do not cover the necessary spectrum and detail of variables that 
could be valuable and VCs trying to automate outbound processes must rely on the basic 
information given by existing venture data providers. This puts interest in the applicability of 
data mining solutions to increase the information density needed to improve databased 
automatization processes. 
The methods have shown that it is possible to retrieve data on private companies through 
web scraping approaches. By taking advantage of Python as a programming language to extract 
textual and numeric data from websites, a multitude of new variables could be identified that 
may be useful for early-stage venture identification. Big Data processing shows that large sized 
==PAGE:70==
 
62 
datasets can be analyzed and filtered for valuable information. VC platforms provide an initial 
basis of information on a firm’s existence and previous operating performance. Data retrieved 
from platforms and social network allow a deeper analysis of the firm’s behavior and activities 
in social network. For one the social network growth can be measured and can be used as an 
indicator to assess a startups growth curve and digital communication behavior. The application 
of advanced data analysis approaches, such as sentiment analyses allows a complex evaluation 
of the social media content provided by startups. The discussed data collection and analysis 
methods may provide avenues for the further automatization of VC processes and may 
eventually lead to higher efficiencies in the pre-investment selection of early-stage ventures. 
The extraction of news articles from various platforms allows a better understanding of the 
public acknowledgement of the respective firm. A higher amount of news could be indicative 
for more attention by journalists and public media. Besides the quantitative analysis, also in 
context of news analysis can be developed as a beneficial tool for the evaluation process of 
early-stage ventures.
