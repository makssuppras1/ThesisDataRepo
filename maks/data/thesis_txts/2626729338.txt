Abstract
District heating (DH) providers can produce heat and power simultaneously. They can therefore decrease
their operational costs by not only providing heat but also entering the day-ahead electricity market by selling
electricity as a bi-product. However, in order to be competitive on the day-ahead market the DH providers
need an optimization model to generate optimal block bid placements and block bid-prices. The bidding
process takes place under great uncertainty due to high ﬂuctuations in the market’s electricity price and the
amount of heat generated from renewable energy sources.
The aim of this thesis is to investigate how the uncertainty for the DH companies can be handled with the
use of metaheuristics to improve the block bidding process. The data from three Danish DH companies
is available in the form of electricity prices for the DK1 market, the heat demand and the renewable heat
production.
From the area of operations research a genetic algorithm is developed to handle the uncertainty of the bidding
strategy. The methodology of uncertainty handling focuses on Monte Carlo sampling from historical data and
Monte Carlo sampling from Time Series Models. Eight diﬀerent sampling approaches were tested, e.g. re-
sampling, varying sample sizes and sample average approximation. Furthermore, ﬁnancial risk management
was incorporated in order to represent the block bidding process as portfolio optimization.
The genetic algorithm proved to be capable of ﬁnding block bids in the DH system of Ejby, however, failed
to place bids in more complex systems such as that of Middelfart and Hvide Sande. This observation held
true for both the historical and non-historical data samples. The price of using the combined heat and power
producing units could not be matched by the electricity price of the market. The CHP units had diﬃculties
in competing with other cheaper heat production options such as wood pellets, wood chip boilers and solar
panels.
Furthermore, it proved diﬃcult to generate exact bid prices for DH systems such as Hvide Sande which
rely on renewable energy sources. This resulted in and increase of the production cost due to low bid price
estimation.
The use of metaheuristics to optimize the block bidding process of the DH providers is therefore not recom-
mended. The metaheuristic performed well for simple systems. However, the uncertainty handling methods
could not generate block bids for more complex systems. Future improvements may be possible by handling
the uncertainty outside of the metaheuristic and thereby taking advantage of the computational speed by
running multiple experiments in parallel.

Introduction
Private companies, public institutions and governmental organizations constantly make strategic and eco-
nomic decisions on a large range of topics which impact performance and revenue both in the short and
long term. Whereas some decisions are simple and straightforward, others are highly complex with many
inﬂuencing factors that need to be considered simultaneously. Boards and committees want to be able to
consider a range of scenarios to also be able to include organization-speciﬁc visions and goals as well as
stakeholder demands in this process. Getting it wrong, will potentially have major consequences for the
economic revenue, staﬀsatisfaction and retention and, ultimately, survival of the organization.
Making decisions under uncertainty not only requires a clear target, it should if possible be based on repro-
ducible data and predictions. The enormous technological development over the past decades has greatly
improved the capture of real-time data and their storage and programming facilities can now handle large
data sets from a variety of sources for development of descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics.
From predictive and prescriptive analytics statistical and mathematical models can be developed which take
uncertainty into account. Such models can be used to adjust long-term decisions.
By continuous collection of data and optimization, improvements can be made in order to react or adjust
to realisations of uncertainty. Thus, the use of optimization algorithms has become increasingly popular, as
they mathematically model the highly complex decisions of increasing proﬁt or eﬃciency, lowering costs or
manpower as well as increase stability and sustainability for optimal resource exploitation.
Optimization algorithms such as Linear Programming (LP) and mixed linear integer programming (MILP)
cannot directly take uncertainty into account and require decisions to be described as deterministic assuming
no randomness in the system. To analyse the system behaviour taking diﬀerent scenarios into account would
require to solve the LP or MILP repetitive times. This is usually not computationally feasible. Therefore
specialized techniques such as robust optimization algorithms or stochastic optimization algorithms have been
developed which are resilient to changes in uncertainty and thus remove some of the risk for the decision
maker [1]. Robust optimization is used when the decision maker is very risk averse as it attempts to protect
the decision maker from risk [2], whereas stochastic programming attempts to ﬁnd the best decision to make
here and now by analysing the expected performance in future states. Stochastic programming is based on
estimation of probabilities for future scenarios to predict the best performance .
Solving these specialized optimization algorithms can however in some cases become too computationally
expensive.
Thus, other techniques such as metaheuristics are used in circumstances where more precise
methods fail [3], [4]. Metaheuristics is usually applied for problems which are assumed to be deterministic
in nature and not focused on uncertainty. This thesis aims at exploring possibilities of handling uncertainty
in metaheuristics using two approaches based on Monte Carlo sampling: sampling from historical data and
sampling for error distributions of forecasting models. Behaviour and performance will be assessed for each
technique. For each technique behaviour and performance is assessed. The experimental set-up involves e.g.
cross-over and cost functions as well as sampling techniques. To achieve these goals this thesis incorporates
data from the district heating (DH) companies in Denmark
The DH companies are responsible for generating heat and hot water for consumers in the surrounding area
and employ many diﬀerent heat producing units. These consist of boilers which heat water using either
natural gas, wood chips, wood pellets or energy from renewable sources. However, many DH companies also
have the possibility of producing heat and hot water using a combined heat and power producing unit (CHP).
By using the CHP heat is produced with electricity as a bi-product. CHP units enable the DH companies to
increase revenue and reduce the overall operational cost. This is achieved by selling the bi-product of power
on the electricity market by placing block bids on the day-ahead market.
The day-ahead market is highly competitive as the power producing companies can place block bids at any
hour of the day and with variable length. In addition, the block bids must be at least three hours long and
5
==PAGE:7==
can last a total of 24 hours [5]. To be proﬁtable, such bids need to consider uncertainties with respect to
the electricity prices on the day-ahead market, as well as when and how to produce heat from natural gas,
biomass or renewable energy sources. These uncertainties aﬀect at what time the block bids can be placed
in the day-ahead market. Due to the number of combinations available to generate heat and power the DH
companies face a signiﬁcant challenge as to how to determine the most proﬁtable hours to produce electricity
while still fulﬁlling the required heat demand. This challenge is a data set for which metaheuristics may be
an optimal solution to place competitive block bids on the day-ahead electricity market under uncertainties
[6].
3.1
Problem statement
How can uncertainty handling approaches be integrated in a metaheuristic, when optimising the block bidding
process of the day-ahead energy market for district heating providers?
3.2
Learning objectives
This thesis has the following objectives:
• Survey the academic literature and summarise the state-of-the-art methods for handling uncertainty in
meta-heuristics
• Describe the bidding process for the district heating providers.
• Identify, collect and analyse relevant data provided by the district heating providers.
• Design a mat/meta-heuristic for the electric power market bidding process of district heating providers
• Implement the designed heuristic in Julia or other relevant programming language
• Identify and implement diﬀerent approaches to handle uncertainty in a heuristic
• Assess the diﬀerent uncertainty handling approaches for the electric power market bidding process of
district heating providers
• Evaluate and discuss the performance of the implemented optimisation methods.
• Communicate and mediate the methods and ﬁndings of the project both orally and in a written report
The placement of block bids on the electricity market can be represented as a two-stage stochastic problem.
The ﬁrst stage decisions involve the optimisation of hourly location of block bids on the day-ahead market,
as well as estimating their respective bid-price. The second stage contains the heat production optimization
given the accepted or rejected block bids for diﬀerent realisations of uncertainty. The goal is to ﬁnd the
best placement of the block to maximise the return from the market based on samples of uncertainty and
subsequently to optimize the heat production in the second stage.
The ﬁrst stage is optimised using a Genetic Algorithm (GA), while the second stage is optimised using a
linear program (LP). Uncertainty occurs in tandem with the ﬁrst and second stage decisions. In the ﬁrst stage
the uncertainty in electricity prices aﬀects where the blocks must be placed and at what price. The second
stage decision is aﬀected if the blocks are accepted or rejected on the market. The rejection or acceptance of
the block bids aﬀects the optimal heat production schedule and therefore the total operating cost.
The thesis follows the outline depicted in the Flow Chart of ﬁgure 1. The model presented in this thesis is
not case speciﬁc, but can be applied for all district heating companies with a similar set-up.

The ﬁrst step of the thesis will involve an exploratory literature review aimed at analysing state of the
art methods in handling uncertainties in metaheurisitcs, block bidding strategies, forecasting techniques
and ﬁnancial risk management. The review will also analyse how GA has been used in similar optimization
problems. To ensure problem speciﬁc knowledge is utilized a further investigation of the day-ahead electricity
market and its mechanism is done. An investigation into how DH companies operate on a daily basis is
performed along with a speciﬁc descriptive analysis for the case of Danish DH companies.
The next step is the design and building phase of the model and its building blocks. Two methods have
been used to account for uncertainty - simple Monte Carlo sampling of historical data (MC) and scenario
generation by Monte Carlo sampling from Time Series models (TS). For each of these models diﬀerent
uncertainty handling approaches are tested. The MC and TS methods are used to account for the uncertainty
in electricity prices. To account for uncertainties in renewable energy production the heat produced by solar
panels is predicted using simple multiple linear regression. The model can hereafter be sampled from again
using Monte Carlo sampling. As an evaluation criteria the total operational cost is used. However, as an
extra uncertainty handling procedure a concept from ﬁnancial risk management is used. The Sharpe Ratio
(SR) is mostly used to evaluate the performance of a stock portfolio. A collection of blocks bids has a similar
structure as a stock portfolio, and has to our knowledge not been used before as a method for evaluating
metaheuristics in the bidding market. These methods are used in the GA which optimises the block bid
location and estimates the bid price, while the remaining heat production is optimised using an LP model.
In the last step experiments are conducted starting with a model comparison and selection. Since GAs can
vary in design due to problem speciﬁc constraints several diﬀerent set-ups of the GA are tested. The GAs will
be parameter tuned through the design phase. A numerical study of diﬀerent uncertainty set-ups is tested
and evaluated to provide the DH companies with proﬁtable block bids. Overall eight diﬀerent set-ups are
tested and presented.

Literature Review
This section explores the literature related to application of metaheuristics for handling uncertainty in decision
making and modelling. The key ﬁndings and methodology of articles cited in our reference list have been
used as inspiration to develop solutions for our speciﬁc study area of bidding strategies in DH companies.
An exhaustive literature search for uncertainty handling in metaheuristics indicated a sparse amount of data
published.
To our knowledge, no previous research group has addressed the speciﬁc context of District
Heating (DH) companies for developing bidding strategies in the electricity market using metaheuristics
under uncertainty.
This prompted us to broaden our literature search to cover both major elements of
metaheuristics as a technique for handling uncertainty in general, speciﬁc aspects of uncertainty related to
the heating and electricity markets and the bidding strategies for DH companies. As this thesis includes a
novel component – risk management - we also explored literature related to ﬁnancial risk management. As
we apply genetic algorithms for optimization of the bidding process, literature regarding construction and
optimization was additionally reviewed.
There are three main areas of uncertainty in the operation schedule for DH companies: heat demand, elec-
tricity prices and production level of renewable energy sources. In this thesis heat demand is assumed to be
deterministic, i.e. it is assumed that the heat demand will be known with certainty in the genetic algorithm.
Electricity prices for the day ahead market depend on the supply (producer) and demand (consumer) both of
which can exhibit considerable variation. In the example of the German electricity market the introduction
of renewable energy sources increased the variability of electricity prices to an extreme degree [7]. This was
largely due to political strategies that were put into place which ﬁnancially favoured renewable energy over
conventional energy sources. On the one hand this reduced consumers’ costs, but on the other hand it also
increased the volatility of electricity prices making predictions more diﬃcult. The Danish market is subject
to similar prioritizations, which explains the need for mathematical models which can handle uncertainty.
The high volatility of electricity prices aﬀects all stake holders. Thus, diﬀerent forecasting techniques have
been studied to reduce uncertainty, thereby reducing costs and increasing proﬁt. Autoregression with In-
tegrated Moving Average (ARIMA) techniques were developed for the Spanish and Californian market [8].
They appeared reasonably reliable in predicting day-ahead prices and were thus used as an inspiration in
this thesis. Although time series models like ARIMA do not entirely capture all variation of future electricity
prices, they can generate price scenario trends for decision making by using reduction techniques such as
the Probability Distance Based Backward approach [9]. This reduces samples size and eﬀectively describes
the nature of the electricity prices. Thus, reduction techniques were applied in this thesis to feed accurate
representations of future price scenarios into the ﬁnal model.
Due to the high degree of complexity of the electricity prices Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN) models have
been developed to ﬁt non-linear models to the forecasting [10].
Their performance was superior to the
ARIMA model. However, additional development of combinatorial ANNs further increased predictability by
cascading neural networks extract features from one layer into another [11]. This provides a high degree of
predictability of electricity prices albeit at the expense of excessive parameter tuning.
Another approach is to create several models for comparison by using historical data. A previous study
fed historical daily pattern data into Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH),
ARIMA and ANN models.
Thereafter the predictions were fed to classiﬁcation models such as Support
Vector classiﬁcation to identify the best predictions for the future electricity prices [12].
Although this
showed promising results, the approach was computationally too demanding.
As previously discussed reduction and clustering techniques of observations are used in scenario generation.
However, by using reduction and clustering techniques extreme observations are removed . Such extreme
values are needed to create robust and reliable models.
The authors presented methods which included
extreme points [13]. Benchmarking the models, test for validity and predictability showed that including
9
==PAGE:11==
extreme points for the forecasting models generally yielded a better result.
The Danish market for electricity brieﬂy favoured CHP units and wind energy prior to 2005, but since then
they compete on equal conditions with other energy sources. This created the need to develop optimal and
ﬂexible bidding strategies. One model applied a linear program to optimize the estimation of future electricity
prices, however, it lacked start - stop criteria due to the dimensionality increase of a Mixed Integer Linear
Program (MILP) and the lack of necessary computational power [14]. GAs have also been used to create
bidding strategies for the American energy market. Using Game Theory and GA it was possible to mimic
trading agents on the electricity market [15], thus creating a model which could formulate bidding strategies.
The algorithm proved very useful in generating bids which were accepted thus providing support in how GA
could be utilized in bidding strategies.
Using a two-step Particle Swarm Otimization (PSO) considerably improved the bidding strategy in compar-
ison to models bidding at the marginal cost [16]. Such two-stage approaches can be used in other algorithms
as well. Other groups have successfully placed block bids on maximizing social welfare between consumers
and producers using genetic algorithms [17].
Stochastic optimization models have been developed to generate bids for the day-ahead market [18]. The
optimization algorithm relied on utilizing scenarios generated by a SARIMA model which predicted the
electricity prices. The model considered several days ahead when placing the bids on the electricity market.
The increased costs of producing heat with the CHP units was ensured to be matched by the revenue from
the electricity market. Two stage stochastic programs have been presented as a viable option when bidding
on the day-ahead market [6] incorporating uncertainties in the form of scenarios for electricity prices, solar
heat generation and wind power generation. From Monte Carlo sampling of forecasted values and K-medoids
clustering scenarios were generated to represent the future electricity prices.
Robust optimisation algorithms which only create block bids when the cost of doing so cannot exceed the nor-
mal operational costs have proven to be advantageous for DH companies [19]. This is achieved by iteratively
generating bids based on unit replacement costs and the resulting Heat Unit Replacement Bidding (HURB)
algorithm could generate safe bids for the market. MILP was employed to optimize the heat production in
each time period. By iteratively replacing heat producing units the bidding prices and power production
amounts could be estimated. Both [6] and [19] present state of the art methods for bidding strategies under
uncertainty.
There has been an increasing focus on how metaheuristics can be used to eﬃciently solve stochastic combina-
torial optimization problems especially under uncertainty. According to [20] Monte Carlo sampling has been
used in several diﬀerent metaheuristics with promising results. Among the most interesting methods was the
GA approach when optimizing electricity network expansion under demand uncertainty. The incorporation of
mathematical models such as LP and MILP models in metaheuristics for handling uncertainty has also been
studied in detail. Describing problems as two-stage stochastic optimization problems where a metaheuristic
solves the ﬁrst stage problems and a MILP solves the second stage problem has shown promising results.
Financial risk management can be applied to the day-ahead market for energy trading using the sharpe ratio
as a gold standard [21]. The electricity market resembles the stock market as energy sources such as natural
gas, wind power, crude oil, electricity and more can be sold on the stock market.
Methods for handling uncertainty in metaheuristics have been studied by other research groups, i.e. com-
puting expected values in relation to the ﬁtness function. By sampling and resampling from data a better
representation of the current solution can be achieved. One proposed method was an extension of the original
PSO [22]. To increase performance of the PSO the algorithm re-evaluated both the personal best solution
found for one particle and the overall best solution found for all particles. By monitoring population statis-
tics the re-sampling technique reduced blindness and deception and the ﬁtness function resulted in a better
overall accuracy. This approach will also be used in the thesis.
In Norway, a study analysed how the ﬂeet vessel size could be optimized when performing maintenance on
10
==PAGE:12==
wind mills [23]. Uncertainty regarding weather conditions when performing maintenance on wind mills proved
to be the main challenge to handle. Through the greedy randomized adaptive search procedure (GRASP)
metaheuristic and scenarios generated from historical data they showed how the metaheuristic could ﬁnd the
same solutions as a stochastic MILP could, however much faster.
A survey on how metaheuristics can solve Stochastic Combinatorial Optimization Problems (SCOP). [24]
described how to implement two-stage and multi-stage stochastic problems by the use of metaheuristics
under uncertainty. By creating metaheuristics the SCOPs could be solved faster than the exact methods.
The highlighted metaheuristics consisted of; Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), Particle Swarm Optimization
(PSO) and Evolutionary Computation (EC). Approaches such as sampling and reevaluation proved to be a
repeating pattern in the published literature. As a subsection to evolutionary computation GA was perfom-
red well when applying several diﬀerent samples. By incorporating problem speciﬁc knowledge along with
sampling of data, the GA proved to be very robust in ﬁnding solutions.

Background Knowledge
The aim of this section is to provide a general understanding of the district heating companies and how
they interact with the electricity market to explain the system-speciﬁc operators used in the metaheuristic.
Furthermore, in order to test and evaluate diﬀerent strategies of handling uncertainty in a metaheuristic,
three district heating systems are selected as test cases.
5.1
District heating companies
A DH company is responsible for the production of heat used for heating of houses and apartments as well
as tap water [25]. More than 370 DH companies exist in Denmark and fulﬁl the demand of more than 1.7
mio. households [26]. The DH companies play an essential role in reducing the CO2 emission of Denmark
and globally [27]. District heating uses many diﬀerent sources of energy - utilisation of excess heat from
e.g. industry, solar power, electricity, wood chips, wood pellets and natural gas resulting in 60 % of all heat
production in Denmark being produced using sustainable natural sources [26]. On an international level only
12% of heat is produced using district heating, however, the European Union has announced that 50% of the
total heat production in the EU should be produced by district heating companies in 2050 [28].
DH companies have expanded their operations to enter the electricity supply market with the aim of increasing
their revenue and ultimately lower the cost for their consumers. In addition, this development generates
electricity from more sustainable sources compared to traditional electricity supplies from fossil fuels. The
sole responsibility of the DH companies is to fulﬁl the heat demand, and extra revenue earned on the electricity
market is a bonus. The key element making it possible for the DH companies to be present on the electricity
market is a production unit named Combined Heat and Power unit (CHP). However, some DH companies can
also oﬀer electricity produced from wind energy. In this thesis the main focus is on the CHP unit. The CHP
can produce electricity as well as heat, but is more costly than the standard production units. It is therefore
not an optimal solution to use the CHP at all times, which underlines the relevance of an optimization method
to handle the CHP schedule. In the following sections a further explanation of the current bidding process
is presented and the district heating systems used to test the designed and implemented algorithm and its
inﬂuence on the diﬀerent uncertainty approaches.
5.1.1
District heating companies in the electricity market
The DH companies have multiple ways of interacting with the energy market, as the market contains several
sub markets. The sub market in scope for this thesis is the day-ahead market using regular block orders, also
called block bids. The block bid must contain a number of consecutive hours at a certain price per hour. The
11
==PAGE:13==
block bid is either fully rejected or fully accepted, meaning the bid covers all hours and the speciﬁed volume
[5]. A block bid is accepted if the average electricity price for the given duration of the block is higher than
the block bid price provided by the DH company and otherwise rejected. When placing a block bid the DH
company participates in the day-ahead market, meaning that the block bids are submitted the day before
delivering the electricity. Based on all bids submitted and the demand for heat, an aggregated demand and
supply curve is created for every hour. The equilibrium of the two curves provides the electricity price for
that given hour. Based on these prices the bid is either rejected or accepted [29]. The bidding process is
presented in ﬁgure 2.

A DH company has an advantage in that they can place bids without any direct risks. If their bids are
rejected the DH companies can always restructure their production schedule by replacing the CHP with a
cheaper production unit. If the block is accepted the district heating company will be paid a price from the
market, which is either enough to cover the extra cost of using the CHP or greater than the cost of using the
CHP.
Current practice it to look at prognoses of the electricity price and at the weather forecast to predict whether
the next day is expected to have high electricity prices. Based on this limited information the DH company
decides to place the bids in the hours with the highest electricity price. However, electricity prices are highly
volatile making it almost impossible to predict. It is therefore relevant to investigate how to handle this
uncertainty while optimising the CHP block bids.
District heating companies have always been looking for better practices in producing heat. In the years
prior to 2005 the DH companies were supported by the government to supply heat using the CHP units in
order to facilitate more sustainable energy production. However after 2005 these units were added to the
deregulated market and had to compete alongside all other units [14]. Since then many DH companies have
been looking into alternatives using cheaper production units and renewable energy to facilitate the shift to a
more sustainable solution. This has resulted in CHP units having to compete with highly eﬃcient equipment
and heat produced by entirely renewable sources.

(combined network of Ejby and ˚Aby) and Hvide Sande. Each system presents diﬀerent levels of complexity.

Descriptive Statistics
To understand how uncertainty can be incorporated into a metaheuristic initially the sources of uncertainty
must be investigated. For the DH companies the sources of uncertainty come from the amount of heat that
they must supply to the customers, the electricity spot prices on the market and the production of heat using
renewable energy. The following section will focus on analysing the available data to give insights into how
the system could be aﬀected by the ﬂuctuations in demand, price and production. The available data set for
this project has been provided by the DH companies and cover diﬀerent time periods. For the Hvide Sande
System the available data is from 2017 until 2018 whereas the data from Ejby and ˚Aby (Middelfart) covers
March 2019 until March 2020.

Forecasting Techniques
6.1
Monte Carlo Sampling
The Monte Carlo (MC) method is useful when dealing with uncertainty of large complex systems as it
allows the representation of the range of outcomes and solutions when the future is diﬃcult or impossible
to predict. By assigning variables at random and running the model an outcome becomes available. This
outcome is stored where after the process is repeated. The range of outcomes enables the decision maker
to understand the various components included in the model and to make an informed and better decision
despite uncertainties [32].
Investigating how to handle uncertainty in metaheuristics, applying the MC method may yield good results.
The metahaeuristic is able to assign values to uncertainties and will attempt to ﬁnd near optimal solution
structures for the problem at hand. Furthermore the metaheuristic can assign diﬀerent values during itera-
tions to improve the solutions. By generating new random variables the search space of the metaheuristic
can change into another representation of possible futures. The MC method approach in creating a bidding
strategy for the DH companies by sampling from historical data. As described in 5.2 the uncertain variables
for DH companies show large variation across seasons, days and hours. Therefore the data will be aggregated
for each season, after which the MC method will sample on an hourly basis. The data will thus represent
possible outcomes that the metaheuristic could encounter in real world scenarios on the day-ahead market.
The MC method provides solutions in a suitably short period of time and is capable of generating possible
outcomes which reﬂect the variability in e.g. electricity prices.
However, it could also prove advantageous to apply the MC method on data which is not historically gen-
erated. The MC method can also be applied to models which can predict electricity prices or solar heat
production. By sampling from error terms several diﬀerent future scenarios can be generated for the uncer-
tainties.
6.2
Linear Time Series Modelling
To allow the metaheuristic to take uncertainty into account and to use Monte Carlo sampling for non-historical
data a model for predicting the data must be created. The main source of uncertainty for the DH companies
comes from the highly volatile electricity prices. From section 5.2 it can be seen, that the electricity prices
have a speciﬁc structure to them with a seasonal and daily pattern. Using time series the structure could be
captured and utilized in order to create better representation of future electricity prices [33].
Time series modelling can generally be used when the variable of interest changes over time e.g. temperature,
stock prices or interest rates. In the following linear time series models will be used to predict the future
electricity prices based on current and previously observed data points. When ﬁtting time series model to
the data of electricity prices the Box-Jenkins approach is used. This approach is an iterative approach to
ﬁnding a suitable model. An illustration of the model ﬁtting process can be seen in ﬁgure 16.

When the time series is stationary, model parameters can be estimated. The autoregressive terms are found

preceding values are needed to predict future values. The moving average (MA) terms are found by analysing

terms for the immediate preceding observations. From the AR and MA terms the initial model can be found.
Furthermore it is possible to see if seasonality aﬀects the time series. If seasonality is present, the original
diﬀerencing will not be enough and a further seasonal diﬀerencing must be added.
Once the initial model is found the parameters are estimated using a Maximum Likelihood approach and
residuals are checked with histograms, qq-plots and the residuals are checked for normal distribution. The
autocorrelation and partial correlation plots are controlled for signiﬁcant lags to control if parameters could
be missing from the model estimation. This iterative process is depicted in ﬁgure 16.

represent a season i.e. winter, spring, summer and autumn. Therefore the data set is split into season ﬁrst,
then into a training and a test set. The training set for each season is used to estimate the model parameters
and the model is hereafter evaluated for its performance in the test set. The models’ performance is visually
inspected to see if the assumptions are held. Hereafter the diﬀerent models are compared using the Akaike
Information Criteria (AIC). The AIC is calculated using equation 2 where k is the number of estimated

6.2.2
K-Medoids Clustering
The predictions generated using the time series models have to be reduced into a manageable size. This is
done using clustering. It would be common practice to use the k-means algorithm to cluster the observations.
However, due to the possibility of having extreme outliers created by the time series model the K-Medoids
/ Partitioning Around Medoids (PAM) algorithm is chosen. The K-medoids algorithm is more robust in
clustering data as it assumes that the centre of a cluster is an actual observation in the data set. Using this
algorithm it is possible to limit the number of scenarios in the future by clustering the observations together.
After the clustering has been performed the probabilities for each cluster/scenario can be calculated.
The k-medoids algorithm divides the predictions into clusters minimising the dissimilarity of the observations
in each cluster. Initially k number of medoids / scenarios are speciﬁed. The medoids are deﬁned at random
from the observed data, resulting in k medoids. The remaining observations are categorised to belong to the
medoids which minimises the total dissimilarity in each cluster. Then for each medoid a diﬀerent observation
is chosen and the remaining observations are reassigned to the medoids that minimises the total dissimilarity.
If the new solution presents a better solution the observations are chosen as the new medoids. This is repeated
until no further improvements are achieved [36].

6.2.4
Out of Sample Generation
Evaluation of the metaheuristic is performed by using an out of sample test. From the metaheuristics the
best solution is taken after which it is tested against a new sample. The samples that are used for testing the
solution performance will consist of 50% Monte Carlo samples from historical data and 50% from sampling
using Time Series modelling technique. This distribution ensures that the models and diﬀerent techniques
for handling uncertainty can be compared. The models using MC sampling from historical data will also be
evaluated on samples from non-historical data from the time series models and vice versa. Each solution will
have a potential of performing very well with the sample which shares its methodology and performs worse
on the opposite methodology. The samples are generated for each season using the diﬀerent methodologies

The objective is to minimise the expected total cost by calculating the heat production cost and subtracting
the revenue gained from the electricity market through the block bids. The production schedule is dependent
on the electricity price scenarios, and the uncertainty is represented with a probability π for each scenario
set ω. The ﬁrst constraint (20) forces the CHP to produce at its maximum heat production capacity within
the given block bid hours. The model assumes that the CHP is either oﬀor producing at its maximum
capacity for the sake of simplicity. The next constraint (21), states that the electricity production of the
CHP is forced to produce at the maximum capacity for the given accepted block bids. The third constraint
(22) ensures that the heat production capacity is not exceeded for all units. Constraint (23) is a demand
constraint ensuring demand is fulﬁlled by either the production, storage or a combination. Only heat, not
electricity, can be stored. The next four constraints are storage constraints. Constraint (24) and (25) are
storage ﬂow-constraints where the ﬁrst one initiates the storage level in the ﬁrst time period (t=1), while the
next constraint ensures the storage level for all other time periods. Constraint (26) ensures that the ﬂow of
heat from unit u is received in its respective storage unit. The last storage constraint (27) sets a maximum
limit for the storage level at all time periods. Constraint (28) ensures that all heat produced by renewable
energy is accounted for, it is assumed that if heat is generated by a renewable energy source then all the heat
must be included in the production, as DH can’t turn of a renewable energy source. The last constraint (29)
calculates the cost of a heat unit which is connected to the electricity grid. The cost of this unit is therefore
a the operational cost plus the electrical cost of running the unit.

Metaheuristic
This section will describe in detail how the meta-heuristic is designed to handle the uncertainty and how it is
adapted to the study case of district heating. The method of metaheuristic does not have a single deﬁnition,
but can be described as ”solution methods that orchestrate an interaction between local improvement pro-
cedures and higher level strategies to create a process capable of escaping from local optima and performing
a robust search of a solution space” [4]. As with any optimisation method, the goal is to either ﬁnd the
global minimum (for minimisation problems) or a global maximum (for maximisation problems). However,
a metaheuristic cannot ensure this will be accomplished and may therefore ﬁnd itself a local minimum or
maximum. A metaheuristic will investigate the search space by trying numbers of combinations in the search
for the global optimum, and depending on the metaheuristic this is conducted in multiple ways. The two
most common approaches are single solution and population-based. For a single solution approach, the search
space is investigated using a single candidate solution, while the population-based approach investigates a
40
==PAGE:42==
collection of solutions in parallel. In this thesis a population-based method is used, called Genetic Algorithm
(GA). Other methods were also considered; however, the binary solution structure of a GA is suitable for this
problem. Handling uncertainty with a GA seemed plausible as it can take the form of a two-stage stochastic
programming model [24]. The ﬁrst stage is conducted using the operators, while the second stage is handled
in the ﬁtness function. Thereby the uncertainty is handled within the algorithm.
8.1
Genetic Algorithm
A GA is a population-based optimisation algorithm, which is inspired by principles of biological evolution, in-
cluding the ideas of Darwinism. The algorithm uses the advantage of exploring several individuals (solutions)
in parallel, where the entire group of individuals are referred to as a population. The population evolves
in each generation (iteration), until a stop criterion is met. The solution representation for an individual is
called a genome and typically consist of binary values, but it is also possible to have a solution based on
integer or continuous values. The genome structure is problem speciﬁc, but for the problem at hand a binary
representation of the solution is used [4]. In Figure 27 the general steps in a genetic algorithm are presented.

The initial step is to create a population. This is usually done at random to ensure diversity in the population.
Diversity is important as it increases the chances of investigating a greater search space in order to locate the
global optimum or minimum. If the population is too similar the population will converge too quickly and
the chance of reaching the global minimum or maximum is lower. The individuals in the initial population
are evaluated based on their ﬁtness function, which is a measure of the overall performance of the individual.
The objective is to either maximise or minimise. The performance of an individual is crucial, as it determines
whether it should be used for the reproduction stage.
With respect to the reproduction stage, parents are chosen based on how strong their respective genes are.
When individuals are selected to be parents the reproduction is performed using a method called crossover,
where genes are mixed and transferred to the oﬀspring. The oﬀspring then experiences a mutation of their
genes to bring randomness into the population, which ensures investigation of new areas of the search space.
The ﬁtness of the oﬀspring is evaluated and the entire generation is compared, at which point the principle
of ”survival of the ﬁttest” comes into play. The population must be reduced to its original population size,
which is often done by selecting the ﬁttest i.e. the individual that performs best.

8.2
Model Design
This section describes the general ideas behind the algorithm design and presents the iterative approach of
building the algorithm. The design phase is focused on how to make the GA problem speciﬁc and how to
handle uncertainty. With respect to the problem speciﬁc aspect the focus has mainly been on how to adapt
the GA operators to resemble reality and ensure feasible solutions, while uncertainty handling focuses on
how the GA can represent a two-stage stochastic problem.
Inspiration from existing research was a signiﬁcant contributor to developing the algorithm. To our knowledge,
no other group has published the use of metaheuristic to solve this speciﬁc problem.
Thus, inspiration
was drawn from similar optimisation problems, or cases where uncertainty handling was included in the
metaheuristic for other applications. [6], [19] and [14] were used to gain the problem speciﬁc knowledge of
district heating in Denmark and provided information on how to formulate a two-stage stochastic model for
this exact problem. Many articles cover the bidding strategy optimization on the electricity market, although
it is often not from the perspective of a district heating. Simple GA operators such as uniform crossover and
mutation operators were used in [17]. These are very generic operators and will also be used in this thesis.
Furthermore, problem speciﬁc knowledge was added to the model in order optimize the system.
The design of the metaheuristic must represent the actual system shown in ﬁgure 2. The algorithm must ﬁrst
create block bids, then estimate their individual bid-price after which the electricity prices are published and
the DH companies are informed which block bids have been accepted. With this information the production
schedule needs to be optimised. The ﬁrst stage is the creation of block bid as well as estimating the bid prices.
This stage will be conducted by the operators within the reproductive stage of the GA. The second stage
will then take place within the ﬁtness function, i.e. the uncertainty handling within the algorithm. Based on
diﬀerent electricity price scenarios the block bids are accepted or rejected and the production is optimised
accordingly. The ﬁtness function is thereby the minimisation of total production cost and the maximising
of revenue. The total cost will be calculated as the expected cost across multiple electricity price scenarios.
These scenarios are either generated using the Monte Carlo sampling from historical data, also referred to as
MC, or by the Time Series model, which will be referred to as TS. The design of the metaheuristic maintains a
two-stage stochastic programming model as the ﬁrst stage does not include uncertainty, however, the second
stage does.
8.2.1
Operators
As shown in ﬁgure 27 there are 6 main steps when executing one iteration of the algorithm - initialising the
population, parent selection, crossover, mutation, ﬁtness evaluation and survival selection. This subsection
will focus on the operators and does not include the ﬁtness evaluation. Depending on the problem at hand
these operators can be altered to ensure problem speciﬁc behaviour. This subsection will therefore present
how each operator is functioning and address how it has been altered to accommodate the block bidding
process.
Initial population
The initial population contains N individuals where all individuals have a binary string
(genome) for each of the CHP’s with a length of x hours, here chosen to be 24 hours. The binary string is 1
if the CHP is on and 0 if it is oﬀ. If the binary string has a consecutive number of hours where the CHP is
on, this is referred to as a block bid. The block bid must be at least 3 hours long, but there is no upper limit
other than the total number of hours. The genome is created at random for all CHP’s for each individual in
the attempt of creating a diverse population without any prior patterns. This is crucial as it can prematurely
narrow the search space. In ﬁgure 28 an example of a genome string is shown.

Parent Selection
The selection of parents for reproduction can be performed in multiple ways. The general
idea is that well performing individuals are chosen to reproduce and pass their features onto the oﬀspring. The
parent selection method used in this thesis is a combination of tournament and elitist selection. Tournament
selection is used to choose half of the population for reproduction. The method selects two individuals at
random and from them the best performing individual. This is repeated several times. This method allows
poor performing individuals to take part in the reproduction, as it may not only be the best individuals which
are closest to the optimal solution in future. The poorer solutions therefore help to investigate a greater area
of the search space [4].
After identifying half of the population for reproduction the individuals are paired. The pairing is inspired
by the elitist principle, i.e. the two best individuals are paired and then the next two best etc. [4]. Using the
elitist principle ensures that the good features of the best individuals are passed on to the next generation.
Together with the tournament selection it is not only the top half of the population which is mixed.
Initially, the parents were selected solely on the elitist principle, but this inﬂuences the model to converge
immediately. The inclusion of the tournament selection has to some extent prevented this behaviour. The
parents’ pairs are now ready to reproduce which is conducted by the use of the crossover operators.
Crossover
The aim of the crossover function is to recombine the genes of two parents, in order to form
oﬀspring with new variations of the hereditary. The crossover method is where the algorithm learns which are
preferable patterns and passes some of them on to the new oﬀspring [4]. It is therefore also highly inﬂuential
in respect to the convergence of the solution. The more similar the population gets, the more similar oﬀspring
it creates. As mentioned, two crossover functions are used. Uniform crossover is more random as it selects
at random which gene should be taken from which parent whereas the two-fold crossover takes larger parts
of a parent and transfers it to the oﬀspring.
Each of the parents are copied to create two oﬀspring. If the genes are diﬀerent, a random number of 1 or
0 is drawn for each oﬀspring. If the number is 1 for oﬀspring 1, then the gene will change to that gene of
parent 2, and if the number is 1 for oﬀspring 2 the gene will be changed to that gene of parent 1. If it is 0,
then no change happens. This procedure is shown below in Figure 29.

Mutation
The mutation operator reﬂects the mutation happening in every evolving population. The aim
with the operator is to introduce randomness to the solutions and to enable the population to explore new
areas of the search space [4]. In this thesis the mutation operator has been extended to adapt to problem
speciﬁc operations. Therefore, 3 types of mutations are used; bit-ﬂip mutation, extending/reducing existing
block bids and creating/removing block bids. Not all oﬀspring must be mutated, as it is not preferable to
introduce too much randomness into the model, and the mutation rate of genes should be around 0.05-0.1
[37]. The selected oﬀspring will only experience one type of mutation. However, all oﬀspring will subject to
a feasibility mutation operator to ensure feasible blocks.
The bit-ﬂip mutation is much used when working with a binary representation of the solution. The idea is
that a random gene is selected and will ﬂip to the opposite value, i.e. a 1 will become a 0 and vice versa.
The number of genes ﬂipped depends on the selected mutation rate.
The expanding and reducing operators will iterate over all blocks and at random select whether the block
should not be mutated, be expanded or reduced. It is possible for a block not to be reduced as it may
introduce too much randomness, and the GA should be able to identify patterns and not just perform a
random walk. Furthermore, the block will only be expanded or reduced by one gene at a time, but this can
happen in the beginning, the end or in the middle of the block. This is also to ensure that not too many
genes are randomly switched.
The creation or removing of block bids also iterates over the CHP strings. For each string it is decided at
random whether a block should be created or removed. The location of where the block bid should be placed
and its length is decided by drawing a random number between the plausible hours It is also decided at
random which of the blocks in the gene string should be removed.
The mutation operator checks the length of a block. If the length is more than 3 hours no actions are taken.
If the block is 2 hours, the pattern from the crossover has been passed on and it is preferable to have a block
present in the given location. It is a random draw, to which side the gene is ﬂipped, but ultimately the block
will be 3 hours long. If a block is 1 hour long, it is a random draw whether the gene is completely removed
or whether two bits are ﬂipped to become 3 hours long. This feasibility check is conducted on all oﬀspring
after the general mutation operators have been run.
Initially, only the bit-ﬂip mutation operator was used. However, this did not have the desired eﬀect as it
created many small blocks of 1-2-hours. Combining this with the feasibility mutation operator often resulted
44
==PAGE:46==
in too many blocks being deleted. The two other mutation operators have enabled the investigation of new
blocks in the search space.
Survival selection
The survival selection determines which individuals are kept alive for the next gener-
ation [4]. At this point in the algorithm the population consists of the entire ﬁrst generation and the newly
generated oﬀspring. The population has therefore doubled in size and it needs to be reduced to the original
size. The question is therefore which individuals are allowed to continue to the next generation. There are
diﬀerent ways of selecting, but the most common is based on ”survival of the ﬁttest” i.e. the best half of
population based on the ﬁtness function are brought to the next generation. The elitist principle is used once
again to select the better half.
Hamming’s Distance
This is not a generic GA operator, but it is often used to avoid premature conver-
gence of the GA algorithm. The concept of Hamming’s Distance is to measure the diﬀerence in the population:
the higher the distance, the more diverse, and the lower the distance, the more similar. Hamming’s Distance
is well suited to use in a GA as it is applied for categorical variables. It will compare two gene strings and
count every time whether there is a diﬀerence between the two, this is extremely diﬃcult for e.g. continuous
solution spaces [38].
Hamming’s Distance is tested in this thesis due to rapid convergence of the model. If the model experiences
a Hamming’s Distance below a certain threshold it will ﬁnd the most similar individuals and replace half of
them with new individuals. The aim it to re-start the exploration of new areas of the search space.
8.2.2
Bid-price Estimation
The purpose of bid-price estimation is to ﬁnd the price for each of the block bids. In this thesis the block bids
are placed independently, i.e. the price and volume is ﬁxed for each of the blocks, regardless of how many
are accepted or rejected. This section will describe the general concept of how the bid-price is estimated and
the process of identifying the selected bid-price method. In total, 3 methods were tested, two of which work
through approximation and the last is an exact method using linear programming (LP).
All three methods are to some extent inspired by the Unit Replacement Cost (URC) method presented in
[19] and [14]. The general idea of URC is to ﬁnd the cost equilibrium between two units, which both can be
used. The cost equilibrium represents the revenue needed to cover the extra costs of using the more expensive
CHP rather than a cheaper heat-only unit. Thereby, the DH companies will not experience an increase in
cost.
The process of adapting the unit replacement cost method to the GA required several attempts. Initially,
the aim was to approximate a bid-price using a similar logic as in [19] and [14] ensuring a low impact on
the running time. This resulted in the two-approximation methods and unlike [19] the replacement cost was
based on a weighted average. As the GA ﬁnds the block placement, this method only aﬀects the respective
hours. The procedure is as follows: each block is examined independently for the duration of the block to
establish how the production cost would be if the cheaper heat-only unit were producing. It is therefore
calculated how many hours and volume of heat are needed to fulﬁl the demand in the block hours using heat-
only units. This calculation is conducted by inserting units at increasing costs. For the ﬁrst approximation
method a weighted average is calculated based on the number of hours that each of the heat-only units are
on. For the second method the weighted average is based on the production volume. These two methods
are approximations as they do not take storage into account. They are therefore very conservative bids. The

8.2.3
Fitness Function
The ﬁtness function measures the performance of an individual. The ﬁtness function is the driver for the
algorithm and inﬂuences which individuals survive in each iteration. Two ﬁtness functions are used in this
thesis - total cost and Sharpe Ratio. Having total cost as a ﬁtness measure is the most obvious choice, as
it is always easy to measure in monetary values. In order to introduce a new approach, the Sharpe Ratio is
used as it is a ﬁnancial risk measure for evaluating investment choices in respect to stock trading.
The ﬁtness function is the second stage decision, i.e the optimisation of the production. As mentioned earlier,
this is the part which has to handle the uncertainty of the model. The ﬁtness is evaluated per electricity price
scenario, i.e. when having a scenario set of 10 electricity price scenarios of 24 hours each, the individuals are
ﬁrst evaluated on the ﬁrst scenario, then second and so forth. Using this approach, the ﬁtness evaluation
becomes computationally heavy, however, it imitates reality well.
This mirrors the process of electricity
prices being published and DH companies being informed which block bids are accepted. The production is
then only optimised for the accepted block bids using the LP models. Executing this process, a total cost is
found for each scenario and an average is then calculated when using electricity prices generated by MC and
a weighted average when using prices generated by TS.
9
Uncertainty Approaches
Once the GA has been developed several diﬀerent techniques for handling uncertainty in the metaheuristic
are applied to better understand how the uncertainty will aﬀect the performance of the GA.
47
==PAGE:49==
9.1
Baseline methods
Initially, models are created in order to generate a baseline for the metaheuristic function. This is done by
giving the metaheuristic a static sample of the historical and non-historical data. The algorithm will thereby
search for the placement of the optimal block bids and their respective bid-prices. The models will be used as
a baseline for more advanced uncertainty methods. The baseline will also function as an assessment for model
performance. If the model converges for diﬀerent runs towards the same optimal value it can be concluded,
that the model is consistent. The baseline models are run for a static sample size of 10, 50 or 100 each.
9.2
Re-sampling methods
Re-sampling techniques were incorporated into the metaheuristic. By re-sampling historical and non-historical
data the search space changes. The re-sampling technique is incorporated in order to allow the metaheuristic
to converge towards robust solutions by assessing more possible representations of the solution space under
uncertainty. It is assumed that the genome structure of the individuals in the GA will hold information from
the previous iterations which will allow it to converge faster and more consistently for the new data. The
sample sizes change to be either 10,50 or 100 as in the baseline models. It was also tested if varying the
number of samples between 10 and 100 would increase the performance of the GA.
9.3
Sample Average Approximation
A common method for handling uncertainty in stochastic problems is the sample average approximation
(SAA). The general procedure is to create scenarios/ samples representative for the search space. Thereafter,
the problem is solved for each individual scenario/sample and the results are recorded. From this an average
performance of the model can be presented. This approach is also applicable to metaheuristic. It is possible
to run the metaheuristic as a deterministic problem, after which it is solved again for another sample [39].
However, this results in the uncertainty being handled outside of the metaheuristic, which is not in the scope
of this thesis.
The method of SAA was applied in a modiﬁed version for the entire model in both the ﬁrst and second stage
by evaluating each individual of the population for each sample/scenario and identify which blocks win for
a given scenario and thereafter optimizing the production. The expected cost is thereby the average of the
cost found for each scenario.
10
Financial Risk Management
We aimed at including a measure of ﬁnancial risk into the metaheuristic to improve the applicability of the
model. Although the total operating cost was already included as a ﬁtness function in the GA, an additional
measure of the actual risk of putting a block bid on the market was potentially valuable for the DH companies.
By including a risk measure in the ﬁtness value the model gets penalized for creating poor bids.
To this end, concepts from ﬁnancial risk management were investigated as the electricity market has many
similarities with the stock market despite the obvious diﬀerence in using short-term prognoses in the former
and long-term prognoses in the latter. The two markets share the challenge of bidding under uncertainty.
Several diﬀerent metrics in ﬁnancial risk management will aim at ensuring the highest possible return and
simultaneously reducing risk. One of these metrics is the Sharpe Ratio (SR) which is presented in equation
45. Rp is the expected return of the portfolio, Rf is the expected return of risk free asset and σp is the
standard deviation of the portfolio. To calculate the SR the expected return of the risk free asset must be
subtracted from the expected return of the portfolio after which it is divided by the standard deviation of
48
==PAGE:50==
the portfolio. The risk free asset could be e.g. government bonds. Therefore the expected return is always
in relation to a safe option. For the bidding strategy of the DH companies this value is assumed to be zero
as they have the possibility not to bid.
Sharpe ratio = Rp −Rf
σp
(45)
The SR aims at ﬁnding the optimal portfolio of stocks which has the highest return in relation to its risk.
When optimising a portfolio of stocks diﬀerent combinations of stocks are compared. Usually, a low standard
deviation (risk) will result in a low return and vice versa. The SR can be maximised by either increasing the
return or by lowering the standard deviation (risk).
Using the SR as a ﬁtness function for the GA will allow the metaheuristic to estimate which individuals
are risky and which are not. Blocks which have a high return could be very risky, and blocks with a low
risk could be less proﬁtable. It is expected that the overall cost of the system may not be reduced to the
same level as when using only the total operational cost. However, it is expected that the SR could generate
solutions which are more robust and/or have more bids in general. Furthermore, if the metaheuristic would
be used as a rolling horizon the SR may improve the performance due to the problem becoming similar to
that of the stock market.

Experimental Study
This section will present experiments for model selection and uncertainty approaches, introducing ﬁrst the
development process and evaluation of the model selection, and then a small parameter tuning of the mutation
rate (MR). Based on the results the selected model is used for all future experiments.
A preliminary experimental study is then conducted, exploring 8 diﬀerent uncertainty methods including three
baseline methods, 4 re-sampling methods and the sample average approximation method. The preliminary
analysis is conducted for two diﬀerent approaches for estimating the electricity prices. The ﬁrst method is
generating electricity scenarios using MC simulation, which samples electricity prices based on historical data.
The second method is using the forecasting methods based on a SARIMA model which generates scenario
using MC and clustering. This model is referred to as TS.
Based on the results three uncertainty approaches are selected and used for additional experimental testing
in a more relaxed environment of DH. This is expected in part, to give the metaheuristic and the uncertainty
approaches more room to optimize within.
These experiments include removing initial storage from the
model and therefore release extra capacity for the CHP to produce. Furthermore, a diﬀerent angle of the
bidding strategy is tested using the three selected uncertainty methods. Using the SR as a ﬁtness function
the aim is to remove the focus from total cost and focus on the proﬁtability of a block bid. By introducing
risk versus reward into the block bid, the uncertainty handling is extended to be incorporated within the
ﬁtness function.

GA is developed using the generic operators. Then the operators are extended to become more problem
speciﬁc. This refers to the mutation operators which expand/reduce (second model) or create/remove blocks
(third model), as explained in Section 8.2.1.

11.2.5
Conclusions of Preliminary Results
The preliminary results shows that Ejby has great potential in winning blocks, and the Base models seems
to be suﬃcient in capturing the volatility to still place proﬁtable bids. The most preferred methods when
generating price scenarios using MC is base 50, whereas when using TS Re-sampling 10 and Base 10 performs
very well. The performance of Ejby is also reﬂected in the out of sample data, as the price level for the MC
and TS out of sample data is diﬀerent, creating a variance in the result. One needs to be aware of this when
evaluating the model.
Middelfart is experiencing some accepted block bids in autumn, but the remaining seasons do not perform
very well. The optimised system with the pipeline between ˚Aby and Ejby makes it very diﬃcult to create
proﬁtable bid-prices. In general the performance of the uncertainty methods is very similar, but in general the
re-sampling methods and vary samples shows a very small increase in performance. Middelfart is experiencing
the same inﬂuence from the out of sample data as Ejby, as the data is exactly the same.
Hvide Sande is not performing well. Even the months without a high expected solar level show no tendency
for proﬁtable bids. The methods showing some indication of trying to place bids are Re-sampling 10 and
Vary Samples.
Based on these results three uncertainty handling methods are chosen for further analysis. As it is already
proven that Ejby performs well, the further analysis will focus on Middelfart and Hvide Sande. The three
uncertainty methods in focus are Re-sampling 10, Vary Samples and SAA as they showed a slight improvement
in performance for DH Hvide Sande and Middelfart.

11.4
Sharpe Ratio
The aim of using Sharpe Ratio (SR) as the ﬁtness function is to remove the focus from the total cost of the
system to a focus on the proﬁtability of the block bids. When using total cost as the ﬁtness function the
performance of the individuals is always compared to the optimal cost for a system without CHP production.
By removing this constraint the model is not limited by how much the CHP is allowed to produce. The sole
purpose is to identify bids and evaluate the bids based on the risk and reward. It is therefore also a risk
that the total cost will increase which for a test setup of 24 hours may be a disadvantage. However, if future
studies were to use a rolling horizon the excess of heat produced by the CHP for a given day can be stored
and used the next day. The tests conducted with SR are for Ejby, Middelfart and Hvide Sande using Ejby
as a baseline model, as we have conﬁrmed that this model works well for applying total cost as the ﬁtness.
Middelfart and Hvide Sande are used to explore if SR could be the solution for creating bids. For all tests
the initial storage level is included.

12
Discussion
This thesis has combined a metaheuristic with linear programming, forecasting methods and ﬁnancial risk
management in order to asses how uncertainty can be handled within a metaheuristic.
The uncertainty
handling has been applied to a block bidding optimisation problem for district heating providers in the day-
ahead electricity market. The stochastic element in the model is the volatility of the electricity prices, making
them diﬃcult to predict. Furthermore, one of the DH case studies adds an extra stochastic element by using
solar heat production, which is highly dependent on the weather. This section will discuss the suitability of
the algorithm to handle uncertainty and how the design could be improved. Furthermore, the design of the
experimental study is addressed with respect to its limitations and simplicity. Lastly, the results from the
experimental study will be discussed in relation to system ’bottlenecks’ and the performance of the diﬀerent
uncertainty handling approaches.
12.1
Choice of design of the optimisation algorithm
When developing the metaheuristic a literature review indicated that the best suited metaheuristic was a
genetic algorithm (GA) as the solution could be represented as a binary vector [3], [4]. The literature search
showed that a GA has been successful in placing bids in the electricity market: [15] successfully used Game
Theory and GA to model the trading agents and generated bids to the market and [17] tried to use a GA
when maximising social welfare while bidding into the day-ahead market. Using a GA as an adaptation of
a two-stage stochastic programming model has also been shown to be successful, i.e. the hybrid method for
portfolio optimization with uncertain asset prices in [40]. However, other approaches could also have yielded
good results. As the literature review states other methods such as PSO could also have been used. Several
diﬀerent options were evaluated as well e.g. memetic algorithms and local search algorithms, however GA
was chosen based in the research available on the topic.
The GA designed in this thesis follows the same design structure as a stochastic mixed integer linear op-
timization problem, using the GA to conduct the ﬁrst stage decision and the ﬁtness evaluation with linear
programming to conduct the second-stage decision. The resulting GA approximated a two-stage stochastic
67
==PAGE:69==
program. It was decided that the uncertainty should be handled internally in the algorithm. The algorithm
would be presented with new data during the search, resulting in a reset of the search space. By re-sampling
it was expected that the solution representation would be stable as the electricity prices had a general struc-
ture. Results indicate however that the GA struggled with optimizing the block bidding procedure due to
the changing search space.
In incorporating the uncertainty in the metaheuristic several forecasting techniques were used. Generally, the
performance of the SARIMA models seemed reasonable. However, as the literature review states capturing
the electricity prices using time series models can in some cases be diﬃcult due to the volatile nature of
electricity prices. The GA is directly aﬀected by the estimated electricity prices. Thus, a best possible ﬁt for
the SARIMA models was prioritized, assuming that the number of parameters needed between days of the
seasons could have adversely aﬀected the estimation. The linear regression model for solar heat production
was carefully chosen. The resulting linear model only included all critical parameters for prediction of the
solar heat production, except for the winter season. It was, however, hypothesized that the same model could
be used for all four seasons. This may have adversely aﬀected the predictions and thereby the GA.
Initially, the bid-price for the CHPs were estimated using a weighted cost. However, extensive testing showed
that the bid-price estimation consistently overestimated the necessary electricity price needed to run the
CHP. Therefore it was decided to calculate the bid-price exactly using an LP which considerably increased
the algorithm run time. Each block had to have its price estimated in relation to the remaining production
of heat. Using the exact bid-price estimation allowed the GA to place bids on the day-ahead market which
were capable of winning. This was especially true for small systems such as DH Ejby.
Utilizing the diﬀerent system complexities of DH companies such as Ejby, Middelfart and Hvide Sande
provided the necessary testing environment for the GA. For small system such as the DH Ejby without
renewable energy, the GA is capable of placing block bids in the day-ahead market resulting in a lower total
operating cost. However, for systems with greater complexity such as Middelfart and Hvide Sande the CHP
units have diﬃculties in competing with the cheaper biomass boilers and the renewable energy sources. For
DH systems such as Hvide Sande with renewable energy sources an exact bid-price estimation could not be
performed. For this CHP the bid-price needed to be calculated as a weighted average of the electricity prices
for the electric boiler and the solar heat production. The results however indicated that the bid-price for
the DH system was too optimistic and therefore underbid the market. The GA does not yield the expected
return from the market. The successful bids incur at a higher cost for the individual in the population, than
that of running the system with the expected operational costs. Individuals not placing any bids therefore
have an advantage since they do not underbid the market, resulting in no blocks being placed for the CHP in
Hvide Sande. Additionally, the cost equilibrium between the CHP and the replaced units increases drastically
when storage is available and solar heat production is available, resulting in the DH system not being able
to proﬁt from the CHP units.
12.2
Choice of testing setup
Speciﬁc favourable days were chosen to test the GA for performance in placing block bids on the day-ahead
market. Two days were chosen in each season to represent the diﬀerent levels of heat demand for the DH
companies. The days were speciﬁcally chosen to have either a high heat demand or a medium heat demand
dependent on the seasons normal level of demand. This was done to ensure that the likelihood of having
to produce heat would be greater, thus lowering the bid-price for the CHP. Ensuring that the demand was
high would increase the likelihood that the GA would be capable of placing block bids, therefore allowing
model performance to be investigated. However, the heat demand is not high enough during spring and
summer seasons. The GA is not capable of placing bids at a favourable price even when there is no heat
storage. The choice of days will have an inﬂuence on how the model performance can be investigated. Due
to the low number of days tested because of time constraints the model’s performance may not be accurately
68
==PAGE:70==
represented. The performance of the GA depends highly on the season. Since favourable days were chosen,
it may not be representative for all other days of the season.
The results indicate that the models perform very diﬀerently in the out of sample test depending on whether
data comes from Monte Carlo sampling of historical data or Monte Carlo sampling for a time series forecast.
The TS models overall perform best in the out of sample test in comparison to the MC models. The variation
in solution costs for the individual models is also dependent on season. This underlines that great care must
be taken to ensure that the data is representative of the real world scenario. In some cases the historical
data gives rise to increased variance in solution costs compared to the time series data and vice versa.
It was also chosen not to test the GA for more than one day at a time in order to simulate the process of
bidding on the day-ahead market. The results indicate that the models were capable of placing block bids
in seasons with high heat demand such as the autumn and winter months, both with and without having
heat stored prior to the day. It would, however be a beneﬁt for the GA if there was a possibility to take into
account several days of demand in the future. By having a rolling horizon of heat demand the placement
of block bids could become more proﬁtable. Producing heat today and storing it until tomorrow could be
ﬁnancially beneﬁcial for the DH companies. However, a rolling horizon was not implemented in this thesis
due to time constraints.
12.3
Assessment of the results
Literature suggested that a stochastic two-stage optimisation model using GA could potentially be successful
for our problem. The performance of the model varied considerably between the three DH companies. Ejby
showed potential with savings per day up to 5000 DKK, but as the complexity increases with Middelfart and
Hvide Sande, the performance dropped. This tendency is consistent in all test set-ups - with initial storage
level, without initial storage level and when using Sharpe Ratio as a ﬁtness function.
The biggest challenge for modelling success is the bid-price estimation, as it is highly inﬂuenced by the cost
of the heat-only unit and the heat-demand level. The electricity prices need to be at a certain level. If a
season has low electricity prices, the model cannot perform well. For Middelfart, the wood chip and the wood
pellet boiler are very cheap compared to the CHP. It therefore makes sense that bids are only accepted in
seasons with high demand, and almost no bids are accepted in low demand seasons. For winter, the model
does not perform well, as this speciﬁc season that year has exceptionally low electricity prices. Such unusual
ﬂuctuations need to be taken into account when creating the out of sample set.
In seasons with low heat demand the model does not place any bids. If the model were to handle a rolling
horizon in future, this may change. Letting the model transfer the heat to the next day will decrease the
total cost and thereby the bid-price. A rolling horizon is expected to improve the overall result for Middelfart
and Hvide Sande for all seasons, whereas Ejby is expected to beneﬁt mainly in the low demand seasons.
In summary, all uncertainty methods showed a very similar performance. It is a concern that the rapid
convergence in the model has limited the inﬂuence by the diﬀerent uncertainty methods, i.e. the true inﬂuence
of the handling methods has not been realised. This need to be considered in future models. The results
did indicate that for small systems such as Ejby, the Base methods were suﬃcient in identifying acceptable
block bids. However, with increased complexity the need for more advanced sampling is needed. The choice
of continuing with Re-sample 10, Vary Sample and SAA is based on their performance for Middelfart and
Hvide Sande, as these were the systems that needed extra care to create acceptable bids. As the performance
between the uncertainty handling methods were very close one could for future work investigate the inﬂuence
of the other methods. Though since the the performance of the model does not seem to be highly inﬂuences
by the uncertainty handling, the focus should be on restructuring the GA or trying to make it more problem
speciﬁc and thereby avoid the premature convergence.
Switching the ﬁtness function to Sharpe Ratio the performance of the uncertainty methods were unchanged.
69
==PAGE:71==
As the performance of the bidding strategy did not increase using Sharpe Ratio, it is expected that the
performance of Middelfart and Hvide Sande were also unchanged.
The performance of Sharpe Ratio is
inﬂuenced by the constrained way of estimating the bid-price. If a rolling horizon were to be used the bid-
price level would decrease and the heat produced by the CHP during long block bids could be transferred to
the next day. A exploration of this could be valuable for further analysis.
13
Conclusion
This thesis is using a Genetic Algorithm to investigate how uncertainty can be handled within a metaheuristic.
The application area is the optimisation of block bidding strategy for electricity in the day-ahead market
for district heating providers, as they are challenged with the uncertainty of the highly volatile electricity
prices. It is extremely diﬃcult to get a good prediction of electricity prices, ultimately making it a challenge
to place the block bids in the most suitable hours. The problem is solved by imitating a two-stage stochastic
problem using a GA combined with linear programming. The ﬁrst stage is placing block bids at an estimated
bid-price, this is conducted using the operators of the GA and the second stage is the optimisation of the
heat production dependent on whether or not block bids were accepted, this is conducted using an LP.
The scenario generation of electricity prices are conducted using two methods - Monte Carlo sampling from
historical data and a SARIMA model using Monte Carlo sampling.
Despite a combination of multiple uncertainty handling approaches we did not achieve a model with a robust
bidding strategy that can be directly applied in diﬀerent district heating systems. Thus, in its current form it
cannot be used by Danish DH companies to securely improve their revenue through block bidding on the day-
ahead electricity market. A potential improvement may be possible if a rolling horizon method is adopted,
which will ultimately reduce the bid-price of the block bids and increase the acceptance rate. Furthermore,
future improvements could be explored by handling the uncertainty outside of the metaheuristic and thereby
taking advantage of the computational speed by running multiple experiments in parallel.
