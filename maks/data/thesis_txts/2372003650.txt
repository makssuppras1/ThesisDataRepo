Abstract
In this thesis a Finite Element (FE) model has been constructed for modelling of
transverse tunnel cracking in polymer matrix composite.
This issue is of great interest since tunnel cracking occurs in non-crimp fabric used
e.g. as the load carrying laminates in wind turbine blades and in pre-pregs used in
aerospaces.
The experimental analysis of this fatigue initiation process is rather time expensive
due to the severity to measure the size of the cracks for carbon ﬁbres. A precise
crack-tip ﬁnite element simulation for an oﬀaxis tunnel crack is made in order to
couple the crack-growth measurements with the actually crack-tip loading.
The analysis of the tunnel crack in a polymer matrix composite is divided in three
steps. The ﬁrst step is a parametric/convergence study for the length, width, thick-
ness and mesh of the model. The goal of this step is to get a precise model to
calculate the energy release rate as the total work release per unit length of crack
propagation. The results of this analysis was validated against the path independent
contour J integral, where it was observed that these corresponds to each other. The
laminate thickness study revealed that the ratio between the thickness of the whole
laminate and the oﬀaxis layer have a large inﬂuence on the energy release rate when
the ratio is small.
The second step is a parametric study of the angle of the oﬀaxis layer / tunnel
crack for two glass ﬁbres and two carbon ﬁbres. The goal of this study was to see
the inﬂuence of the angle and how the material properties inﬂuence the normalized
energy release rate. The tunnel crack angle study revealed that the material param-
eters for the glass ﬁbres does not inﬂuence the energy release rate that much. For
the carbon ﬁbres does it have an inﬂuence of the energy release rate.
The third step is a parametric study of the length between multiple parallel cracks
with uniform spacing. The goal for this study was to see how close multiple cracks
==PAGE:4==
could be to each other without interfering with each other. The multiple tunnel
crack study revealed that a FE-model for one tunnel crack halfway across the width
could be used for multiple cracks when the length between the cracks l/h is larger
than 10, with a relative error below 1%

Preface
This master thesis was written at the departments of DTU Wind Energy and Me-
chanical Engineering at Technical University of Denmark in the period of January
2017 to June 2017. This thesis "Finite element simulations of oﬀ-axis tunnel crack-
ing in layered composite materials" documents the work carried out in a 30 ECTS
points project.
The aim of the master thesis was to develop a FE-model for tunnel crack-tip simu-
lation to quantify experimental measurements.
I would like to thank my main supervisor Lars Pilgaard Mikkelsen for great support,
inspiration and guidance throughout the project and second supervisor Christian
Frithiof Niordson for the guidance in Fracture Mechanics.

1
Introduction
The typically fatigue damage evolution in composites are initiated in polymer matrix
composite by transverse tunnel cracking. These tunnel cracks can occur between
the transverse backing bundles of ﬁbres that holds the unidirectional (UD) ﬁbres
in place in a non-crimp fabric used e.g. as the load carrying laminates in the wind
turbine blades. These tunnel cracks can also occur in pre-pregs used in the aerospace
industry.
Non crimp fabrics are a composite with multiple layers with diﬀerent orientation to
get multi directional material properties.
Pre-pregs are reinforced fabric which has been pre-impregnated with resin. The
pre-preg is then ready to lay into a mold and add pressure and heat.
This fatigue initiation process can experimentally be investigated and quantiﬁed by
using a non crimp fabric with a [0/θ/0/−θ]s laminate material layup subjected to a
remote tensile stress. By application of this layup it is possible to keep track of the
initiation and growth of the oﬀ-axis tunnel cracks occurring in the oﬀaxis layers.
The experimental analysis of this fatigue initiation process is rather time expensive
due to the severity to measure the size of the cracks for carbon ﬁbres. The method
used to measure the size and number of the cracks is by lighting through the laminate
and taking a picture. Due to that the carbon ﬁbres is not transparent, this method
for measuring the crack length and spacing can not be used.
To quantify these experimental measurements, an accurate crack-tip ﬁnite element
simulation is necessary, in order of coupling the crack-growth measurements with
the actually crack-tip loading.
1.1
Background
Fracture (crack growth) occurs when the energy near the crack tip is large enough
to overcome the resistance of the material, causing an increase in the crack length.
During growth of a crack, energy is released as deformation and a new crack surface
area. This energy is called the energy release rate (strain energy release rate): G
and is the rate of change in potential energy of a newly created fracture surface area,
for a linear elastic material[1, p. 13].

1.1
Background
The energy release rate was described by J. L Beuth (1992), as the total amount
work released per unit length of crack propagation [3]. This was done for a crack
in a thin bonded ﬁlm in residual tension. The energy release rate was calculated
for pure Mode I loading. A mode I loading is where the principal load is applied
normal to the crack plane.
S. Ho and Z. Suo [5] (1993) analysed Beuth’s method and modiﬁed it to describe
the energy release rate of a tunnel crack in a brittle layer between two tougher sub-
strates for mode I.
N. Lucato [7] (2012) then took S. Ho and Z. Suo’s method and modiﬁed it to handle
oﬀ-axis tunnel cracks. This was done by adding the total work release per unit
length of crack propagation in the crack direction that occurs from the shear stress
(Mode II), to the energy release rate from the transverse stress (Mode I). Addition-
ally he also investigated the inﬂuence of crack tip shapes and came to the conclusion,
that the energy release rate calculated with a J-integral is not aﬀected by this shape.
F. Bertuol [2] (2014) made a parametric study of the angle of the oﬀ-axis laminae
layer and tunnel crack. He compared his results with N. Lucato and M. Quaresimin
[8], and found an oﬀset/deviation between his and N. Lucato and M. Quaresimin
results. He came to the conclusion, that the variation in the ply thickness aﬀects
the variation in the energy release rate.
J. W. Hutchinson and Z. Suo [6] (1993) pursued the approach of multiple tun-
nel cracks in a thin brittle layer between two tougher substrates with pure Mode I
loading and described two diﬀerent cases. The ﬁrst one is where each crack is with
uniform spacing and equally extended as seen later in ﬁgure 7.8. The second one
is where every other crack is tunnelled across the whole layer, and the cracks in
between are in the process of tunnelling across the layer as seen later in ﬁgure 7.9.
They concluded that there is a variation in the energy release rate between the two
cases, when the cracks gets closer to each other. The energy release rate gets smaller
for the case where every other crack is tunnelled across the whole layer, because of
lower tensile stress in the cracked layer when every other crack have tunnelled across
the whole layer.

1.2
Thesis Objectives
The main goal of this thesis is to construct an accurate tunnel crack-tip model using
a commercial FE-program such as Abaqus. The FE-model should be validated for
diﬀerent parameters to see which parameters inﬂuence the energy release rate. A
validated FEM model with a maximum error on 1% should be made.
During the thesis work, following task will be addressed.
• The energy release rate as the total work release per unit length of crack
propagation should be compared with the path independent J-integral at the
tunnel crack tip.
• A Parameter study on which inﬂuence the thickness has on the energy release
rate of the tunnel crack tip, for the oﬀ-axis layers compared to the unidirec-
tional layers (UD).
• Comparison of the energy release rate for a crack tunnelled halfway across the
laminate, compared to a crack that is fully tunnelled across the laminate.
• An investigation of what inﬂuence material properties for diﬀerent materials
has on the resistance against tunnelling of the crack.
• Convergence of the numerical predictions as a function of crack spacing.

Numerical Modelling
This section starts by giving a brief introduction to energy release rate G, since this
is essential for the understanding the concept of crack propagation. Next, is the
path independent J-integral described ﬁrst as non-linear elastic energy release rate,
there after as an energy domain integral used for the numerical model.
The energy release rate is then described for a tunnel crack in thin, brittle layer
between two tougher substrates. Additionally, is the normalization of the energy
release rate, G, shown to make a generalized parameter that is independent the
parts geometry.
The analysis of the energy release rate for an oﬀaxis tunnel crack is based on a
integral of the the total work release per unit length of crack propagation proposed
by Bueth[3], S. Ho and Z. Suo[5]. This is compared with the path independent J
contour integral by Rice[9].

8
Future Work
This section contains suggestions to extensions of the ﬁnite element program and the
parameter study to get a better understanding on what diﬀerent parameters does
to the energy release rate. This section also contains suggestions how to minimize
the error between numerical and experimental results. Convergence study for the
other materials.
In this study, was it concluded that small values of the hight of the UD layer com-
pared with the oﬀ-axis layer had a huge inﬂuence in the energy release rate of the
tunnel crack. For future studies diﬀerent values of the UD layer hight could be
studied because that in e.g. load carrying laminates in windmills, are the oﬀaxis
layers used as backing, really small compared to the UD layers.
In the study of the inﬂuence of the material parameters, four diﬀerent materials were
used. These materials were diﬀerent in all the parameters, so is was not easy to make
a conclusion on which parameter that made a change in the energy release rate. A
new study then needs to be made, where only one material parameter changes at
the time.
In the same study, was it concluded that for an oﬀ-axis layer and tunnel crack with
a low angle, with negative transverse stress, would the laminate close itself and the
crack opening would be negative. The FE-model have to be modiﬁed with boundary
conditions in the crack that takes this into account. An implementation of friction
between the crack faces can also be a future study.
The numerically calculated energy release rate can be compared with real experi-
mental tension tests. Using UD laminates as load carrying material in windmills,
these layers are held together with oﬀaxis ﬁbres. These ﬁbres are in bundles, so it
can be assumed that there is a "crack" in between these bundles. The comparison
between the numerical model and experimental test, can be done until the length
between the "cracks" reach a limit on l/h = 10.
When producing ﬁbre composites, a combination of pressure and heat is necessary
to get the laminate to cure. A future study is to implement thermal strains to the
FE-model.

Conclusion
A precise tunnel crack tip model in the ﬁnite element code Abaqus was modelled.
This was used to determine the energy release rate of this tunnel crack. Converge
studies for the length, width and mesh size for the model was made so the deviation
was under 1% for a fully converged model.
A parametric study was made for the relation between the thickness of the whole
laminate and the oﬀaxis layer: H/h to see how this had an inﬂuence on the energy
release rate. This study, showed that the thickness of the oﬀaxis layer compared
to the whole thickness of the laminate had a large inﬂuence in the energy release
rate, because of the increase in the stiﬀness of the UD layer compared with the oﬀ
axis layer when the ratio H/h gets larger. When the ratio H/h = 30 it starts to
converge against a constant value.
The method on calculating the energy release rate as the total work release per unit
length of crack propagation was validated against the path independent J-integral.
It was found out that for the modiﬁed energy release rate with mixed modes corre-
sponds with Abaqus’ three dimensional J domain integral with a slightly little error.
A parameter study was made for the inﬂuence of the energy release rate by changing
the material properties. This study, showed that the energy release rate for mixed
loading modes does not inﬂuence the results for the tested glass ﬁbres that much,
even though the change in the material parameters. However for carbon ﬁbres, there
are a small variation between the two materials for high values of the tunnel crack
and oﬀaxis layer angle, and a large inﬂuence for low values of the angle.
The study also showed that with a tunnel crack and oﬀaxis layer angle on 0 −45◦
for carbon ﬁbres and angles on 0 −25◦for glass ﬁbres, the crack would try to close
itself, and the crack faces crosses over each other and gave a negative crack opening.
This gave a positive GI even though it should be zero.
It was assumed that the normal tension only gave normal crack opening, Mode I,
and the Mode II contribution from normal tension is very close to zero, if no friction
is taken into account.
60
==PAGE:77==
A parameter study were made for the inﬂuence of the energy release rate for both
Mode I and Mode II, by changing the length between multiple cracks in the oﬀaxis
layer. This was done for two cases, one where the cracks are parallel, with uniform
spacing, l and equally extended, and the second one where every other crack with
the distance, 2l is fully extended across the whole width of the layer, and where a
new set of cracks occurs in between these.
This study showed that the energy release rate Gss calculated as the total work
release per unit length of crack propagation, long from the crack tip and laminate
edge, did not take into account that the tensile stress in the cracked layer becomes
a little smaller when ﬁrst set of cracks with a distance 2l are tunnelled across the
whole layer for the second case. However the J integral did take this into account.
It also showed that the FE-model for one tunnel crack halfway across the width
could be used for multiple cracks for both cases when the length between the cracks
l/h is larger than 10, with a relative error below 1%.
