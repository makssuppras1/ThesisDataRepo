Spatial Matched Filter Focusing in Ultrasound
by Mikkel SCHOU
This project investigates the feasibility of combining Spatial Match Filtering (SMF) with Syn-
thetic Aperture Sequential Beamforming (SASB). The main objective was to develop a new al-
gorithm and asses the performance in an attempt to answer the hypothesis: Can Spatial Match
Filtering enhance the resolution and/or contrast of SASB?
The resolution of SASB have shown to increase with a decreasing F#, but at the cost of higher
side- and grating lobe artefacts. SASB is ideal for use in a handheld device and therefore have
great potential for commercial use.
The developed algorithm substitutes the second stage of SASB with a Spatial matched ﬁltering
approach. Field II is used to generate expected responses of the ﬁrst stage beamformer for each
image point. The expected responses are then matched with the responses from the ﬁrst stage
providing a low resolution image. Multiple low resolution images are then summed to output
a high resolution image.
Multiple simulations were performed to asses the performance of the SMF-algorithm. A BK
9040 convex transducer was used as a model for the simulations. A small parameter study in-
vestigated the effect of two parameters: the F#, and the number of ﬁrst stage lines used in the
second stage. The resolution and contrast were quantiﬁed with the Full-Width-Half-Max, Cys-
tic Resolution and Contrast-to-Noise ratio, and compared across different parameter settings.
A few experimental phantom measurements were performed and the beamformed images
were presented. This showed that the algorithm could be translated from simulations to ex-
perimental data successfully.
Results showed a similar resolution and contrast for both SMF and SASB. The side lobes were
not lowered with SMF, but in fact enhanced. Images showed the spatial spread, and energy
of the grating lobes to be lower with SMF than SASB. A potential application area for this
algorithm is to compensate SASB in times when the given parameter settings are restricted.
The study concludes the effects of the combined algorithm to be minor. The hypothesis is not
entirely rejected but deemed improbable with the given form of the algorithm.

Resume
Dette projekt undersøger muligheden for at kombinere Spatial Match Filtering (SMF) med Syn-
thetic Aperture Sequantial Beamforming (SASB). Hovedmålet med projektet var at udvikle en ny
algoritme og bedømme billedkvaliteten af outputtet i et forsøg på at besvare hypotetesen: Kan
SMF forbedre opløsningen og/eller kontrasten af SASB? Det er før blevet bevist at opløsning
af SASB bliver bedre ved at sænke F#, men på bekostning af højere side lobes og grating
lobes-artefakter. SASB er ideelt til brug i en trådløs enhed og har derfor et stort kommercielt
potentiale. Den udviklede algoritme skifter anden stadie af SASB ud med SMF. Field II bruges
til at generere forventede responser fra første stadies stråledanner fra ethvert billedpunkt. De
forventede responser matches til linjerne fra første stadie og giver herved et lavopløsnings-
billede.
Summeres mange lavopløsningsbilleder fremkommer et højopløsningsbillede som
output fra algoritmen. Flere simuleringer blev benyttet til at bedømme billedkvailteten af SMF-
algoritmen. En BK 9040 konvex transducer probe blev brugt som model til simuleringerne. Et
lille parameterstudie blev udført hvor to parametre blev sat i fokus: F# og antallet af linjer fra
første stadies. Opløsningen og kontrasten blev målt med hhv. Full-Width-Half-Max, den cys-
tiske opløsning og kontrast-til-støj forholdet. Målene blev herefter sammenholdt på tværs af
forskellige indstillinger. Desuden blev et par eksperimentielle fantom målinger også udført og
de færdige billeder vist. Dette blev gjort for at bevise at algoritmen kunne overføres fra simu-
leringer til eksperimentielle målinger. Resultaterne viste tilnærmelsesvis samme kontrast og
opløsning for både SMF og SASB. Simuleringner viste at side-lobes-artefakterne ikke dæmpet
men derimod forstærket. Billederne viste at den spatialle fordeling og energien i grating lobes-
artefakterne var lavere med SMF end SASB. Et potentielt anvendelseområde af algoritmen, er
derfor til tider hvor grating lobes opstår i SASB. Dette sker hvis parameterindstillingerne for
SASB skal begrænses. Projektet konkluderer at effekten af SMF-algoritmen er lille. Hypotesen
er ikke forkastet, men kendt usandsynlig med den givne udviklede algoritme.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Purpose
The purpose of this project is to investigate the effect of Spatial Match Filtering (SMF) applied
to Synthetic Aperture Sequential Beamforming (SASB). The tested hypothesis is: Can SMF im-
prove the resolution and/or contrast in SASB? An algorithm is develop and tested with simula-
tions from Field II. The image quality will be assessed with different quantiﬁcation techniques.
Both pros and cons of the algorithm will be discussed and results will be presented.
1.2
Motivation
The purpose of this report is to describe the work done in the project Spatial Matched Filter
Focusing in ultrasound. It involved implementing a combined algorithm comprised of Spatial
Matched ﬁltering (SMF) and Synthetic Aperture Sequential Beamforming (SASB). SASB is a
two stage approach where the ﬁrst stage only requires a simple transducer set up and the
second stage can be handled in post processing. SASB is ideal when it comes to low trans-
ducer complexity, since the complex focus operations are moved from the transducer to post
processing later on. The post processing is quite extensive but a real time implementation is
possible with the use of computer clusters. The transducer itself is therefore easy and cheap
to produce. This could have a huge impact on the clinic today making Ultrasound Imaging
techniques available at any time. Lightweight solutions up until today have involved Dynamic
Receive Focusing (DRF), where the focusing is performed by electronics thereby requiring a
high production cost and complexity. Studies have shown that SASB can be used as a medical
ultrasound imaging technique and that there is a 70 % probability that a doctor will prefer the
SASB image compared to the conventional image [1]. SASB is, as of today, not commercially
implemented in the clinic. In this project it is investigated whether the image quality of SASB
can be improved. SASB can achieve an even better resolution than DRF with a low F#. The low
F# have drawbacks; one being the high grating and side lobes, which inﬂuence the contrast
negatively, this phenomena is observed both by [2] and [3]. In the past apodization techniques
and a high number of emission lines have been used to suppress these artefacts. The Time-of-
ﬂight (TOF) approach that these techniques use ([3], [2]) assume the transducer elements to be
point sources. This assumption may not hold for areas close to the transducer [4] since the spa-
tial spread of the transducer element surfaces are not considered. SMF is therefore introduced.
SMF techniques have proven to lower the sidelobes of an ultrasound system as it compensate
for the change in spatial impulse response of the system [4]. It has never been combined with
SASB before, and in this project a new algorithm combining the second stage of SASB with
SMF is attempted. The project have focused on developing the algorithm, and it is tested using
simulation with Field II [5], [6] and later measurements from phantoms are shown. Both pros
and cons of the SMF-algorithm will also be discussed later.

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Basic ultrasound imaging concepts
In the following sections the theory of ultrasound will be described. This includes transducer
geometry and beamforming techniques such as DRF, SASB, SMF, and others. Resources for the
this section include: [7], [8].
2.1.1
Transducers geometry
A modern ultrasound transducer consists of an array of piezoelectric crystals that can con-
vert electrical energy into mechanical energy, and mechanical energy back to electrical energy.
A transducer crystal element can be used as both a transmitter and a receiver of ultrasound
pressure waves. Transducer elements are aligned in an 1D array. The geometry of a standard
ultrasound transducer array can be divided into two main groups; a Convex Array and Linear
Array. Linear arrays are usually used as a ﬁrst step, when designing new ultrasound focusing
techniques due to the low complexity of the geometry. The Linear array does however have
a narrow ﬁeld-of-view (FOV) compared to the Convex array. Therefore the Convex array de-
sign is the transducer geometry most often used by clinicians. The larger FOV of the convex
array is ideal when examining tissue structures. The techniques used in this project can be
implemented regardless of transducer geometry. A convex array transducer is chosen as the
geometry used in this project due to its commercial potential. For focusing purposes modern
ultrasound scanners are often phased arrays. With this approach the beam steering and fo-
cusing is made without any physical parts moving. This is crucial when implementing time
dependent ﬁltration. An important thing to notice is the line density of the two transducer ge-
ometries. In ﬁgure 2.1 the beam direction and elements placement are plotted for a convex and

2.1.2
Focusing and beamforming in ultrasound imaging
Focusing with a phased array is done by applying a separate time delay to each of the crystals
in the transducer. Beamforming is the term for delaying the time signals in order to shape the
pressure wave emitted from the transducer surface. Usually beamformation is divided into
transmit and receive beamforming. This means that the delay proﬁle can be different in the
two steps. In ﬁgure 2.2 the transmit beamforming and receive beamforming is visualized with
a timing scheme. In transmit a set of weighting coefﬁcients (wj) of the individual channels
electrical energy applied together with a transmit time-delay proﬁle given by τtj. The electrical
signals are then converted to mechanical energy by the array elements. The waves emitted
from the array elements will converge with their wavefronts at one point due to the time delay
proﬁle and the weighting. This is referred to as the focal point or transmit focus.

2.4
Quantifying an Ultrasound Imaging system
Evaluating an ultrasound imaging system involves quantifying aspects of the ﬁnal images. Im-
age quality is often assessed like a Linear Time Invariant (LTI) system by looking at the impulse
response of a system i.e. the point spread function (PSF) of the image [8]. Imaging a true point
source and afterwards quantifying it provide a measure for how well an imaging system per-
forms. The PSF is often evaluated based on the Full-Width-Half-Max (FWHM) and the Cystic
Resolution (CR). A Third measure also often used is the Contrast-to-Noise ratio which helps
quantify the noise induced by the system. All three measures will be used throughout the
report and here follows brief descriptions of them.
Full-Width-Half-Max (FWHM)
The FWHM is a standard way of quantifying the resolution of an ultrasound image. The mea-
sure itself is calculated by looking at the 1D proﬁle curve of the PSF. A point less than the
expected resolution is imaged. The 1D proﬁle can then be extracted either in the lateral or axial
(depth) direction. The appearance of this will be a bell shape. On the bell shaped curve half of
the maximum (or at -6dB assuming max is 0) will occur on both side of the peak. The width
between these two points is the FWHM [8]. The unit is in meters but often mm is used for
practical reasons. The lateral FWHM is used in this report and is the size of the FWHM in mm
in lateral spread. The FWHM can be approximated with the use of the F#, as [12]:
FWHM ≈λ · F#
(2.13)
where λ is the wave length of the emitted pressure wave. This is only valid right in the focal
point for FRF.

This algorithm was developed with the assumption that the spatial impulse response changes
as function of space, and that the TOF calculation used in standard SASB does not account for
this change. SMF were applied on the FRF ﬁrst stage beamformed data, to ensure only one
signal is passed from the ﬁrst to the second stage per emission. Therefore SMF was performed
on the LRLs after the ﬁrst stage beamformer. This way the second stage of the SASB was
substituted with the SMF-algorithm.

assumed that the insoniﬁed area is the same and only points inside this area should be ﬁltered.

4.4
Discussion
The following section is divided into three parts. Part one discusses the performance of the
SMF-algorithm compared to SASB and DRF. Part two discusses the potential causes of the arte-
facts and investigate the method in details. Part three discusses some implementation issues.
4.4.1
Performance
When the project hypothesis was formed, it was suspected that the side lobe artefacts often
seen in SASB could be removed (see ﬁgure 2.9). SASB with the current TOF calculation does
not consider the spatial change of the spatial impulse. The wanted effect of removing the side
lobes, was not seen in the results from the current form of the SMF-algorithm. The side lobes
were in fact doubled in size for SMF compared to SASB, and mirrored around a vertical line of
the PSF. These artefacts were present in all SMF image. This can be seen in ﬁgures 4.9-4.10 for
a selected set of parameter settings. But they persist in all tested parameter settings which is
seen in appendix A. The side lobes artefacts spread or shape did not change if the number of
LRLs were changed. They only change with a changing F#. The spatial spread of the grating
lobes were smaller with SMF than with SASB in most of the images, revealing a potential use
for SMF. The cause of the side lobes and grating lobes will be investigated in details later.
The pattern observed by Kortbek [3] of increasing resolution with decreasing F# is reproduced
here. This is visible in ﬁgure 4.11 and 4.12, notice how the number of LRLs does not affect the
FWHM or CR6dB metrics. Suggesting these were only inﬂuenced by the F#. A general trend
across all parameters is that SMF was a bit higher than SASB except at CR20dB and CR40dB for
192 emissions. The higher metrics can be caused by the extra spatial extent of the side lobes. It
could also be caused by the ﬁltration operation. The ﬁlter operation expand the effective length
of a event in the response [17],[19]. This causes a loss in resolution in beam direction which in-
ﬂuences both the lateral FWHM and the axial FWHM with a convex transducer. The loss of
axial resolution (gain in FWHM) when applying SMF has been observed in other studies and
is thought to be caused by the ﬁltration operation [19], [20]. Usually the gain in axial FWHM
is accompanied by a gain in contrast compared to Delay-and-Sum techniques similar to DRF
[19], [4]. The same improvement in contrast is not seen in the results here.
The improved contrast for SMF over SASB was only seen with a few of the tested parame-
ter settings. Comparing the images to the CR40dB metrics suggested the improved contrast
of SMF only occurs in times when the grating lobes peak energy level were around 50-60 dB
in SASB. This is visible in the CR40dB with 192 LRLs and F# of 1.5 in ﬁgure 4.10. Here the
grating lobes are caught inside the area where the metrics is calculated. This caused the metric
for SASB to be larger than SMF in depths below 60 mm. Notice the focal length for F# 1.5
was approximately 30 mm and account for the drop in SASB at this depth. The CR40dB sug-
gest that SMF would have a higher contrast. Looking at the proﬁle curves in ﬁgure 4.14 shows
a similar trend. The SMF-algorithm effect on the grating lobes artefacts are small. The SMF
seemed to lower the overall energy away from the main lobe. The SMF-algorithm pushes the
grating lobes below the dynamic range limit of -60 dB, in situations where the grating lobes are
around that level. This can be seen for F# of 0.5 and 384 emissions in ﬁgure 4.9. This is also
visible in the M2Gp metrics shown in ﬁgure 4.13. The lowered energy could in principle pro-
vide a higher contrast in SMF compared to SASB. If the success criteria of the algorithm were
to attenuate the grating lobes with more than 60 dB then this only happens for a few settings
according to the M2Gp measure. DRF still out performs the two other methods in the CR40dB

This limited the area used to estimate the metrics, since the grating lobes should be excluded.
The CR40dB metrics suffer the most. The maximum energy is limited by the ± 10 mm given

SMF enhanced the difference, in times when the grating lobes peak energy level were attenu-
ated at about 50-60 dB. Kortbek proved that grating lobes artefact is avoided in SASB with with
the use of more LRLs or by changing the pitch of the transducer [3]. The M2Gp for λ/2-pitch
compared to λ-pitch with F# did not match the ﬁndings made by Kortbek. This may have
been a result of using a convex transducer array. The spatial sampling changes as a function
of depth for a convex array. When no grating lobes where present the function would select
the next highest energy level which were the side lobes of the 1D proﬁle. The values for these
settings should be disregarded in ﬁgure 4.13 since M2Gp was meant to quantify the effect of
the grating lobes. The settings which should be disregarded included: F# 1.5 with 384 and 256
LRLs. F# of 1 and 384 LRLs.
To remove the grating lobe artefacts for low F#s a higher number of emissions (LRLs) can be
used, this is seen in the images 4.9 and 4.10 and was also proved by Kortbek ([3]). A conse-
quence of using many LRLs is the high number of emissions needed. This limits the frame rate
of a video, since each transmission and reception requires some time. The acquisition of a full
HRI is limited by the pulse repetition frequency and the ultrasound wave physics in general
[12]. SMF could help lower the acquisition time since it compensate for the lower number of
LRLs. Looking at the contour plots in 4.9 and 4.10 showed the spatial extend of the grating
lobes to be smaller with SMF than SASB.
SMF applied after the ﬁrst stage did not show a huge improvement in performance compared
to normal SASB. The side lobes artefacts were simply to high. A potential use of SMF is to
compensate SASB in times when number of LRLs are limited, or if the F# setting is pushed to
the limit of SASB.

4.4.2
Investigating the artefacts
The idea of an ultrasound imaging technique, that took advantage of the gain in resolution
with a low F# combined with a wireless handheld device, sounded promising. SMF used in
other ultrasound imaging techniques have previously shown to lower unwanted artefacts [20],
[19]. It appears as though this was not the case when comparing with SASB in the current
form of the SMF-algorithm, since not only are the side lobes still present they were also mir-
rored in a horizontal line going through the PSF. Kim et al. (2006) proved that applying SMF
after beamformation would result in a resolution similar to a synthetic aperture approach [19].
Kim also theoretically showed that applying SMF after summation would result in relatively
higher-amplitude side lobes compared to doing it before summation (beamformation). The
SMF-algorithm applies SMF after beamformation and therefore higher energy side lobes are
reasonable. SMF would have to be implemented before the beamformation to remove the side
lobes [19]. To do so all channel data for each emissions should be available for the ﬁltration op-
eration, making it impossible to implement on the transducer level and the combination with
SASB is redundant. The side lobes are therefore investigated in detail.
Expected responses for selected image points were simulated to understand why the side lobe
artefacts have the relative high energy and why they are mirrored. A point scatter placed in
[0,0,50] mm was the object being imaged. In ﬁgure 4.16 the selected image points are plotted
and labelled with a number.

Discussion
The images in ﬁgure 5.2 and ﬁgure 5.3 show the algorithm has been successfully translated
from simulation to real measurements. They highlight some of the difference between the
two methods, especially in ﬁgure 5.2 where each wire appears bigger in SMF for SASB. This
indicates a lower resolution of SMF than SASB. The area surrounding the wires was darker in
SMF suggesting that a the overall energy is lowered in these areas. This supports the ﬁndings
in section 4.4.1. As expected there were not seen a huge improvement in image quality, but a
small improvement in contrast is observed in ﬁgure 5.3. Where in SMF the largest cyst, with
center in [25, 0, 15] mm, is somewhat visible in SMF compare to SASB. The F# setting of 1.5 is
low [3], which is assumed to generate higher grating lobes in SASB compared to SMF. It was
later discovered that the transducer model had some wrong parameters that did not match the
transducers real parameters. This could have had a negative consequence on the quality of the
ﬁnal images. A comparison of the output can however still be done since both SASB and SMF
were imaged with the wrong parameters.
A last thing to consider is the direct comparison between the model and measured data, where
it is assumed that the linear model that Field II utilizes (in eq. (2.8)). In reality a model would
not be able to account for everything. The SMF-algorithm uses an impulse response in its
model which was recorded with the BK 9040 transducer. In the model it is also assumed that
the speed of sound is constant. This assumption may only be valid for one type medium. With
the given results it is not possible to asses the importance of this assumption. It can only be
said that ﬁndings of the measurements appears similar to the simulations.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The project was divided into three main objectives. 1) Develop the SMF algorithm which ap-
plies SMF on beamformed data. 2) Test the algorithm with simulations. 3) Translating the
algorithm from simulations to experimental phantom measurements. The project was of an
exploratory nature, which caused a limited parameter space due to time constraints.
1) Implementation of the algorithm was described for a convex transducer. The output from
the ﬁrst stage beamformer i.e. LRLs were transformed to one LRI and summed to reveal a
high resolution image. The main assumption of this method was to assume the spatial im-
pulse response of the system to be varying as a function of space. By matching a model for
each image point to the beamformed LRLs it was attempted to compensate for the change in
the spatial impulse response. Implementation of the algorithm involved testing each operation
multiple times which was very time consuming.
2) Assessing the performance of the SMF-algorithm was performed with Field II simulations.
The two tested parameters were: F# and number of LRLs used in the second stage. The re-
sults showed that SMF followed normal SASB in almost all measured metrics. The resolution
for both increased with a decreasing F# across the FWHM and CR6dB. Both techniques proved
to outperform DRF in these two metrics at an F# ≤1. The other metrics proved no real differ-
ence between the two methods, only in some parameter settings was SMF useful to remove the
grating lobes artefacts. The SMF did not remove the side lobes artefact as hoped. The sidelobes
were in fact doubled in size for all parameter settings, suggesting that the origin of the high
level side lobes cannot be removed with the current SMF-algorithm. It is hypothesised that the
side lobes is a product of the FRF as they are present in both SASB and SMF. A potential use of
the algorithm revealed itself when the number of LRLs were restricted. Simulations showed
that SMF lowers the overall energy. This attenuated the grating lobes below -60dB in level
with some parameter settings. A simulation proved to enhance the CNR 10 times for SMF over
SASB with an F# of 0.5 and with 256 emissions. The sources of the grating lobes artefacts in
SMF proved to be undersampling of space as with SASB, whereas the side lobes were assumed
to originate from summing all responses together in the ﬁrst stage. A lag-dependent pattern of
the ﬁltered LRLs was discovered and assumed to be the main cause of the side lobes artefacts.
3) The experimental phantom measurements proved that the algorithm could be used on ex-
perimental data. The images showed similar results as in simulations. Only a few images were
processed to enhance the difference between the two.
==PAGE:68==
54
Chapter 6. Conclusion
The hypothesis stated in the introduction was: Can SMF improve the resolution and/or con-
trast of SASB?
The hypothesis is not rejected but deemed highly improbable with the current form of the SMF-
algorithm. The enhanced side lobes were too dominant to prefer the SMF-algorithm over SASB.
The shown lag-dependent pattern of the ﬁltered LRLs may in future work be compensated
for. The SMF-algorithm developed here has proven to compensate the contrast loss of SASB
in times when parameters settings are limited. The gain in contrast with the SMF-algorithm
over SASB should be weighed against the extra processing time and loss of resolution. Further
investigation are needed to fully cover the application area of the current SMF-algorithm.
