Summary
In this thesis the fluid dynamics of the sea sponge Halichondria panicea are explored
to estimate the exhalant jet velocity under varying ambient velocities in vitro. 10 ex-
periments were performed on six sponge specimens. Measurement techniques involve
photographing naturally present particles illuminated by a thin laser sheet and using
two-dimensional particle image velocimetry (PIV) and particle tracking velocimetry
(PTV) to estimate velocities in the flow.
A method involving momentum conservation is attempted to estimate the jet exit
velocity but ultimately turns out unsuccessful. Three theoretical absolute velocity
profiles are fitted to measured velocity profiles. Reciprocal functions are fitted to the
high points of these to estimate momentum flux in the jet while momentum flux is
estimated directly from one of them. The momentum flux and osculum area is then
used to estimate jet exit velocity and Reynolds number in two ways. Exponential
functions are fitted to the three decays as well. Additionally, an exponential function
is fitted to velocities of individual particles.
Estimates made making use of reciprocal fits are higher than estimates using expo-
nential fit. One sponge explant is observed to cease all activity during a particularly
high velocity experiment, possibly due to debris in the seawater. No conclusions are
definitely made on how changes in ambient velocity affect jet exit velocity. Possi-
ble reasons for the discrepancy are discussed as well as Reynolds number range, and
future work proposed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and purpose
Sponges are multi-cellular filter-feeding organisms found throughout the earth’s oceans,
rivers and lakes. They have been studied extensively throughout the years and one of
the areas of interest is how they behave in, and react to, ambient flow. A particular
area of interest is how much water they filter under various ambient flow speeds, how
fast they filter it and what benefits they might experience from reacting to the changes.
The obvious property to investigate is the flow speed of the jets expelled from the
sponge’s body. To explore this, a flow channel was designed and built in 2017 [1]
to measure the fluid dynamics of small sea sponges using particle image velocimetry
(PIV) in vitro. The idea is to place sponge explants in the channel, pump seawater
through it at controlled speeds and observe the flow through the channel’s transpar-
ent walls. Detailed methods had not been developed, few measurements taken and
no conclusion reached other than a ”proof of concept”. The purpose of this master’s
project is to explore measurement techniques for jet exit velocity and hopefully reach
some conclusion on how the sponges behave in ambient stream and, in the long run,
help to shed more light on the natural history of sponges.
This project is a part of a larger interinstitutional interdisciplinary research project
on sponges named Early evolution of multicellular sponges - a bioenergetic and bio-
fluid mechanical approach for understanding evolutionary adaption to animal filter-
feeding in the sea. It involves participants from University of the Southern Denmark,
Aalborg University, Aarhus University, Copenhagen University, Technical University
of Denmark, Natural History Museum of London, Paleontological Museum in Munich
and Ruppin Academic Center, and is funded by Villum fonden [2].
1.2
The sponges
The sponges to be examined were seawater sponges of the species Halichondria pan-
icea.
Like all sponges, they draw water in through their ostia due to a pressure
difference caused by the flailing flagellum of their choanocyte cells, before pumping it
through its internal channel system and exiting through one or more oscula. In the
process, food particles are filtered out by the choanocyte cells, providing nutrition
for the sponge. Usually, multiple ostia lead to a single osculum (though one sponge

might feature multiple oscula) and the filtered water exits in a palpable jet [3]. H.
panicea has the leuconoid body type, featuring multiple internal channels and cham-
bers lined with choanocyte cells pumping water from ostia to osculum [4]. A diagram
of leuconoid body structure can be seen in Figure 1.1. It has been hypothesized that
the purpose of the jet is to carry the filtered water far away from the sponge or sponge
colony, minimizing the odds of re-filtration, and mix the water around the sponge [5].
It has also been hypothesized that sponges utilize ambient streams to assist in the
pumping but research into this matter has not reached a definite conclusion on and
it even appears that in some cases, some species prefer to inhibit the flow rather than
utilize it [6][7][8]. These are just some of the fields of interest for sponges in ambient
streams.
Figure 1.1: Body structure of a single-osculum leuconoid sponge with cell types.
Multiple ostia and choanocyte chambers are visible with intricate con-
nections. Yellow: pinacocytes, Red: choanocytes, grey: mesohyl, light
blue: water flow [9].
Measuring fluid velocities around sponges is not a trivial matter, though. As the
jet is devoid of measurable particles due to the sponge’s filtration it is impossible
to measure the jet exit velocity directly close to the osculum and center of the jet.
Indirect methods making use of individual particles entrained in the jet further down-
stream of the osculum will therefore be necessary to estimate jet exit velocity. Earlier
methods include fitting exponential functions to the velocity decay of particles in the
fluid and filtration measurements over comparatively long time [10][11][12].
New
methods were developed for this thesis, some successfully, others not, and compared
with results from previous experiments.

2.3 Measurement techniques

2.3
Measurement techniques
2.3.1
Particle image velocimetry (PIV)
Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) is a method of observing velocities in a moving
fluid [13][14]. A series of photographs were shot of vertical planes in the fluid illu-
minated by the laser sheet which in turn highlights particles in the in the fluid. An
example of such an image can be seen in Figure 2.5. Software is then used to compute
velocities from the differing positions of the particles between the images, resulting in
a velocity vector field in the observed plane. The software used for these experiments
was Dantec DynamicStudio 6.0.
No particles were added to the stream for these
experiments as the particles naturally present in the seawater were enough to get rea-
sonable results. By moving the laser along the z-axis (per coordinate system in Figure
2.1), multiple parallel planes could be illuminated and photographed, resulting in a
good overview of the flow in the vicinity of the sponge. As only one camera is used,
the output can only be a two-dimensional vector field. This is considered adequate
enough as it is assumed that the z-velocity component is small in comparison with
the other two.
Figure 2.5: An example of a photograph used for PIV. Some of the particles used in
the PIV computations are visible in the free stream. A sponge specimen
is clearly visible at the bottom of the channel.
==PAGE:22==
8

The camera was focused on the particles and 100 images shot over a period of 5 s
(at 20 Hz) for each plane measured with an exposure time of 5000 µs for each image.
Additionally, a picture was taken of a 40 mm ruler placed in each plane for calibration.
The images were then processed in DynamicStudio. First, the minimum value of each
pixel in a 100-image series was subtracted from all images in said series to decrease
effects of noise and light reflection off the tunnel’s surfaces. Then Adaptive PIV was
performed on the series using a grid step size of 16×16 pixels with interrogation area
between 32 × 32 and 64 × 64 pixels. No window or filter functions were implemented
nor any peak validation. Universal outlier detection was set to 5 × 5 pixel with min.
normalization of 0.1 and acceptance limit 1.50. Adaptivity algorithm were not used.
This would yield 99 vector maps from which the mean could be taken, resulting in
one final vector map illustrating velocities in the plane illuminated by the laser. This
protocol was performed on each plane in each experiment using the same settings.
2.3.2
Particle tracking velocimetry (PTV)
DynamicStudio can also perform Particle Tracking Velocimetry (PTV) from the same
raw images. This results in a map of the location and velocities of individual particles
between the individual photographs shot during the experiment which can be used
for further analysis [15].

of the osculum, D0, can be measured using ImageJ, an open-source image processing
and analysis software [16]. The osculum is assumed to be circular. The osculum area

2.3.4
Measurement uncertainties
In any experiment, it is important to estimate measurement uncertainties. Errors in
particle displacement can be assumed to be around 0.1 pixels under good conditions
[17] while the maximum magnification measured in any plane in any experiment
was 20.5. With a pixel pitch of 4.8 µm × 4.8 µm the displacement error is within
0.1 · 20.5 · 4.8 µm ≈10 µm. The images were shot at 20 Hz so velocity measurement
error is within 10 µm · 20 Hz = 0.2 mm/s.
To calibrate DynamicStudio a 40 mm ruler was mas measured in the plane defined
by laser sheet in pixels and the magnification computed using these distances and pixel
pitch. The measurements should be accurate within 2 pixels, including proper mea-
surement errors and inaccuracies in placing the ruler. With a maximum magnification
of 20.5, the measurement error of dimensions should be within 2·4.8 µm·20.5 ≈0.2 mm
over 40 mm, or 0.5 %.
For bulk flow estimation the vessel volume Vvessel and filling time t is estimated
to be within 2 %. Channel depth h tended to fluctuate slightly, especially at higher
flow rates, but can be safely estimated to be within 5 % as well while error of width
w is negligible in comparison. With Equation 2.1 the error for Ubulk should be within
√
2 · (2%)2 + (5%)2 ≈3.6% [18].
Measuring D0 could sometimes be difficult as the osculum edge was sometimes
blurry or unclear. To account for this and possible fluctuations during experiments,
osculum area A0 was only estimated to one significant figure.
Errors otherwise not specifically mentioned here are assumed to be negligible.
2.4
Physical assumptions
2.4.1
Seawater
Neither salinity, temperature, density or viscosity of the seawater were directly mea-
sured so estimates need to be made. The seawater is assumed to have a constant
kinematic viscosity of ν = 1.373 mm2/s and density of ρ = 1027 kg/m3, which are
typical properties of seawater at 10 ◦C [19]. These properties might vary between ex-
periments (for example due to variations in salinity or temperature) but are assumed
to be constant. It is furthermore assumed that the particles naturally present in the
fluid are neutrally buoyant, do not have any effect on the flow and follow it perfectly.
The bulk flow is assumed to be fully developed and fairly uniform in the measurement
chamber.

2.4.2
Sponges
The sponges are living organisms that respond to their environment.
They have
interindividual differences in characteristics such as size, shape and A0.
Further-

experiments [3]. Despite this, it’s necessary to assume that the specimens have a
constant pumping rate and osculum area during the experiment. To make sure that
this assumption is valid, the size of the osculum was measured before and after each
experiment to make sure that it would remain constant.
2.5
Jets
The fluid in the jet extending through the osculum is same as the fluid it extends
into. Physical properties such as buoyancy and viscosity difference can therefore be
neglected and what’s left is a case of a round and viscous submerged jet in an ambient
and identical fluid.

2.5.2
Instability
Low to intermediate values of Re usually indicate that the flow is laminar while higher
values of Re usually indicate that it is turbulent [24]. However, jets have a high ve-
locity gradient at it boundaries, and thereby shear stress, resulting in transition to
turbulence starting to occurring at fairly low Re, around 30 [20]. One experiment
suggests that early instabilities occur in the range of 10 < Re < 30 and sinus undu-
lations start to appear when Re ≈100 [25], while another suggests that jets can be
marginally unstable albeit laminar close to the orifice at Re = 680 but have become
unstable and turbulent at Re = 1030 [26]. A third experiment suggests that a jet

2.6

PIV is ineffective to measure velocities within the jet exiting through the osculum

the Osculum. Despite of this, the mean jet velocity might theoretically be found by
utilizing one of the governing principles in fluid mechanics: Conservation of Momen-
tum [34]. Assuming that the flow is steady, fully developed and without pressure
gradients, and by ignoring body forces (such as gravity), the momentum balance for
a 3-dimentional control volume (CV) in the free stream should be

A

the ”invisible” jet being mixed into the surrounding flow within the control volume.
The momentum balance in the CV then becomes

2.8
Exponential fitting
2.8.1
Exponential fitting to individual particles
Goldstein et al. (2018) [10] and Keal (2018) [11] use a method of tracking individual
particles accelerated by the jet in still water to estimate the jet exit velocity. This
is done by fitting an exponential function to the absolute velocity U as a function of
the distance ζ between said particle and osculum on the form
U = C1 · exp(−C2 · ζ)
(2.37)
MATLAB’s curve fitting tool is used to find constants C1 and C2. C1 is the value
where the profile intercepts the y-axis and indicates what the velocity is at the osculum
even though there are no data points available where ζ = 0. In other words, the
estimated jet exit velocity is
U0,exp = C1
(2.38)
This method was simulated here using PTV measurements from DynamicStudio with
the individual particle positions manually tracked.
2.8.2
Exponential fitting to velocity decay
A last estimate of jet exit velocity can be made by fitting the exponential functions to
the velocity decay described in subsections 2.7.2 to 2.7.5, that is the maximum value
of the modified Schlichting velocity profiles, 13-point moving averages and smoothing
splines. This can be done analogously to the method described in subsection 2.8.1.
That is, fitting
Uc = C1 · exp(−C2 · xγ)
(2.39)
to the decays with the two fitting parameters C1 and C2, and finding the jet exit
velocity as
U0,e2 = C1
(2.40)
This assumes that U →0 when xγ →∞, which is not true when Ubulk is high. To
account for this, the function
Uc = C1 · exp(−C2 · xγ) + C3
(2.41)
can be fitted to the decay for the three fitting parameters C1, C2 and C3 and the jet
exit velocity estimated as the value of U when xγ = 0, that is
U0,e3 = C1 + C3
(2.42)

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
4.1
Conservation of momentum method
The conservation of momentum method could not give any results though the good
analytical fits described in Appendix B suggest that there might be possible to predict
the discrepancy in force on the control volume.
The cause of this discrepancy is
probably due to a pressure gradient or that the flow isn’t fully developed in the
channel.
This might possibly be fixed with a longer observation chamber, giving
the flow a chance to develop, but that would probably cause the it to become less
uniform, which could have other effects on the sponges and their jets. In any case, it
was decided not to pursue this method further.
4.2
Validity of profile fitting
The four methods generally give velocity estimates of a similar order of magnitude
but do not concur on exact values. This begs the question of which methods assesses
the jet exit velocity most accurately. Looking at Figure 2.15, it’s pretty clear that
the worst method is probably to find K from the modified Schlichting profile with the
highest R2 where profile seems to fit badly with the data at higher Ubulk and when
asymmetry sets in due to the wake of the sponge. Additionally, the profile with the
highest R2 does not necessarily mean that it has the best fit as variance in the data
also affects R2. An example of this can be seen in Figures 3.9 and 3.10. Figure 3.9
displays the fitted velocity profile with the highest R2 for that particular experiment
but it’s also visible that the data follows it very well. Figure 3.10 displays a velocity
profile slightly closer to the osculum with its own fit in red and a theoretical profile
using the same k as in Figure 3.9 in yellow. The fitted profile seems to underestimate
the velocity near the center of the jet due to a few slow outliers near the jet center
while the theoretical one seems to overestimate as almost all points near the center are
below it. An overestimation of the centerline velocity means that k is underestimated
(as Ucenter ∝1/
√
k) and that seems to be the case for this method in most of the
experiments. The other three methods likely have the same or similar level of accuracy
as they do not show significant variation in their results for any experiment. Due to
this similarity, the conclusions can be simplified as the mean of these three methods.

4.3
Reynolds number
Excluding the best R2 method, the Reynolds number of the jet ranges between 8.2
and 58.6, making the tube flow within the sponge laminar [28]. For the sponges ex-
amined, the round pipe hydrodynamic entry length Lh (see Equation 2.10) would be
less than approximately 2 mm. Though it is difficult to assess exactly how long the
internal channel is before the osculum or how straight it is, it’s probably not much
shorter than that, making it possible that the pipe flow manages to fully develop
before exiting the sponge. This short entry length means that the velocity profile in
the pipe flow within the sponge is more likely to be fully or close to fully developed
than uniform, making the estimates for U0,dev and Redev more likely to be correct
than estimates for U0,uni and Reuni.
Comparing to [25], [26] and [27], early instabilities have started to form in the
jets but they are still laminar in this Reynolds number range. Sinusoidal undulations
appear when Re ≈100, making it possible that the sponges have evolved so that these
undulations would not appear, as they might cause stress and wear on the sponges.
4.4
Inconclusive experiments
Some of the experiments were inconclusive. In those cases, the jets were either po-
sitioned in a cross-flow and diffused to fast to be fitted with the models, or were
to weak to measure. All of these sponges could probably have been assessed in still
water but not in bulk flow. The jets in counter flow could probably never have been
assessed using the methods above but the weak jets could maybe have been explored
better if higher resolution of the images was available, which would have led to a more
accurate assessment of the position and velocity of the particles. Better resolution

4.7
Sponge inactivity in high Ubulk
In the last experiment, 02 MAY: 4, the sponge shut down. This might have been
due to the debris observed in that experiment triggering the closure as a protection
mechanism. A similar response was described by Ludeman et al. (2017) [8] for Cliona
delitrix and Callyspongia vaginalis, which slowed their pumping rate when ambient
flow was very high, and by Leys et al. (2011) [7] for glass sponges which ”...appear
to be able to control their flow and arrest pumping despite high ambient velocities, a
mechanism which may allow them to protect themselves from damage by brief periods
of high particulate (i.e.,suspended sediment) loads.”.

5.1
Conclusion
The conclusion of this thesis, in short, are:
• A method of using conservation of momentum over a control volume to estimate
jet exit velocity was attempted but could ultimately not be implemented due
to accumulative discrepancies in computed force on the CV.
• Four similar methods were used in 10 experiments to estimate the jet exit
velocity utilizing velocity profiles of the jet, one of which seemed to be less
accurate than the others.
• An exponential function was fitted to the velocity decay of individual particles
in one experiment to estimate the jet exit velocity. This estimate was much
lower than what the other methods for the same experiment suggested. This
might simply be due to different assumptions being made regarding velocity
decay of the particles.
• Exponential functions were also fitted to the centerline velocity decay of three
of the four fitted velocity profiles. These estimated jet exit velocities were all
much lower than the estimates found before.
• The Reynolds number range was discussed and hypothesized that the upper
limit might be due to prevent jet instability effects causing stress and wear on
the sponge.
• The results for jet exit velocity estimates using reciprocal fits to velocity decay
were compared to former work by other researchers and they seemed to be
generally higher. This might be due to them fitting exponential decay functions
which seem to underestimate the jet exit velocity in comparison with the other
methods used here.
• One sponge was observed to turn inactive in a high-velocity experiment, possibly
due to the high amount of heavier-than-water debris in the flow.
==PAGE:66==
52
5 Conclusion and future work
5.2
Future work
Methods have been developed to measure jet exit velocity but since only 10 experi-
ments were performed on six sponges, definitive conclusions regarding U0 under dif-
ferent ambient velocities can not be reached. The logical next step would therefore be
to perform more experiments on more sponge explants and either confirm or deny one
or more of the statements made in Section 4.8. To begin with, it would be advisable
to examine medium to large explants before smaller ones as their weak jet appears
to be difficult to measure with the current setup. It might become easier by moving
the camera closer to the sponge or use a higher definition camera. Furthermore, it
would be advisable to examine sponges in co-flow rather than counter-flow as trends
are less obvious when the jet diffuses quickly.
It would also be of interest to replicate the fitting method with the fitting param-
eter ξ added to find a virtual origin x0 as discussed in Section 4.9 and see how that
would fit with existing literature and whether the jet exit velocity estimates would
be more accurate.
In analyzing results of further experiments, it would be of interest to compute the
Reynolds number and explore within which limit it is, what trends appear, and how
the jet would behave if Re would appear outside these limits or trends. It might also
be advisable to run the experiments for longer to get more measurements to fit decay
to.
Though it would be interesting to continue development on the conservation of
momentum method, it probably won’t be practical to investigate further. It is im-
practical, if not impossible, to create a completely uniform flow with no velocity or
pressure gradients that fulfill the assumptions made for the theory of this method.

A.10
2 May: experiment 4
Fourth of four experiments performed on sponge # 4 and the highest Ubulk tried
on any sponge. The flow was not constant though and it became difficult to keep
the water level in the channel steady. Furthermore, debris entered the channel with
the stream and the individual pieces seemed to be larger than the particles observed
hitherto, while not large enough to get caught by the upstream filters. The debris
also seemed to be heavier than water, accumulating at the bottom of the channel
and the surface of the sponge. The cause of this debris is unknown but it might be
accumulation of natural particles within the pumping system, shaken loose by the
high flow rate.
When the jet was to be measured the sponge had become inactive. The previously
visible osculum had closed and no jet could be seen. Jet velocity and Reynolds number
are therefore 0 for this sponge.
