Abstract
This thesis deals with the synthesis and implementation of a distributed range based localisation algorithm
based on ultra-wideband radio transceivers. The thesis addresses the problem of localizing a small amount
of “nodes” in an environment where no absolute positioning system, such as a GPS or motion capture
systems, are available. By equipping each node with ultra-wideband transceivers, they are capable of not only
measuring distance to each other, but also use this interface for communication. During the thesis different
models for node dynamic are proposed, such as stationary nodes used to provide a reference coordinate
system, nodes free to move in a 2D space with inertial sensors aiding estimation and nodes with no inertial
sensors, relying on a random walk velocity model.
A hardware platform is developed, allowing validation of the devised algorithm, as well as enabling
future research in the area of, possibly distributed, range based localisation. Each hardware “nodes” is
equipped with an inertial sensor, barometric pressure sensor, a magnetometer, and two ultra-wideband
transceivers providing antenna diversity. The hardware platform stores sensor readings and manages
transmitter scheduling of the transceivers, storing the data to an SD card, allowing ofﬂine processing.
A through investigation of the utilised DWM1000 ultra-wideband transceivers is made, including a
description of algorithms for estimating range based on transmitter and receiver time stamps. The effects
of obstructions in the signal path and orientation of antennas are also discussed, further the discovery of a
periodic perturbation in the time stamping engine of the DWM1000 is discussed.
A distributed localisation algorithm based on diffusion Kalman ﬁltering with the novel aspect, that nodes
share information about only the part of their states pace relating to local node dynamics is developed. The
algorithm handles nonlinearities in node dynamics and measurement functions by linearization around the
current estimate. To ensure a minimal representation of estimation error states, the methodologies of the
indirect, or error state Kalman ﬁlter is adopted.
The thesis demonstrates, how ultra-wideband technology can be for localization in different scenarios;
experiments are carried out both with data collected by the developed hardware, and synthetic data sets.
Experiments are carried out on localisation of a robot driving in a rectangular pattern, node localisation in a

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
There is no doubt that localization is an important topic, especially to the ﬁeld of robotics and control
theory in a world with an increasing focus on autonomy; we want self driving cars, robotic vacuum
cleaners and grass mowers, and a vast number of applications for unmanned aerial vehicles are continuously
uncovered. A central challenge to autonomous robots is self-localization. After all steering towards a speciﬁc
goal is difﬁcult if no knowledge of your current position is known. Compare for example early robotic
vacuum cleaners randomly traversing the house, changing direction only on physical impact with obstacles,
hoping that in time they would cover the entire ﬂoor plan. To recent iterations employing localization
algorithms to optimize their performance. Carefully, the modern robot vacuum maps out the entire house
using e.g. a camera or laser scanner; by knowing where the robot is, it can then ﬁnd the optimal route
through the house.
In outdoor environments, the obvious choice is a Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) such as the
Global Positioning System (GPS), or derivatives there of, such as differential-GPS. In indoor environments
or situations where a relative accuracy better than that provided by GNSS is necessary, different approaches
must be utilised. Centralised localization algorithms based on visual tracking of a set of identiﬁers, such as
Vicon or OptiTrack have existed for a while but are expensive. For vehicle positioning, visual odometry or
odometry based on wheel rotation can be used for dead reckoning, but does not give any information about
absolute position.
Recently, systems using Ultra-Wideband (UWB) radio technology have been proposed. These radios
can be used to measure distance with centimeter precision. By mounting anchor transmitters in a known
conﬁguration a GPS-like system can be recreated, tracking a moving receiver based on the distance to the
anchors. This technology has been shown to successfully replace visual tracking of miniature drones, as
seen in the works by [Greiff, 2017]
Another signiﬁcant trend in robotics research is cooperative and distributed systems, where multiple
robots together accomplish what none of them could alone. Recent examples includes cooperative lifting of
objects with multi-rotor drones [Wu and Sreenath, 2014], cooperative architectural construction [Augugliaro
et al., 2014] and cooperative quadrocopter ball throwing and catching [Ritz et al., 2012].
The vision of this project is to bridge the aspects of cooperative robotics with those of range-based
localization; developing a cooperative, distributed localization system. Cooperative in the sense that the
availability of inter-node range measurements should increase performance with the same amount of anchor;
leading to better performance than a system utilizing only node-anchor range measurements. Distributed
in the sense that information is not gathered and processed at one single node, but rather the estimation
process is spread over the entire network both computation and communication wise. This facilitates easy
removal and insertion of new nodes, as well as providing robustness against single-node failure. UWB radio

transceivers can serve both as a communications medium and provide a measure of inter-node range. As
such they seem an obvious candidate technology for implementation of a distributed localization system.
The thesis deals with the development and implementation of a distributed localization algorithm, based
on the Decawave DW1000 UWB transceivers. To facilitate validation on real data, hardware with the
necessary capabilities was constructed. In view of the limited resources available both monetarily, but more
signiﬁcantly due to time constrains, the hardware itself does not allow real-time evaluation of the localization
algorithm; it solely gathers data for ofﬂine computation. Ofﬂine, the sampled data can be played back in the
same order as it was recorded and the algorithm can be tested as if it was running in real-time.
1.1
Thesis Goals
The main goal of this thesis is to document the development and validation of a distributed localization
algorithm, capable of localizing an amount of moving nodes based on inter-node distances, as well as
distance to a few ﬁxed nodes, so called anchors. The algorithm takes into account the constrains posed by
the somewhat limited communications bandwidth of the utilised UWB radios. The algorithms is designed
under the assumption that there is a direct line of sight available between nodes that communicate.
During the development the following sub-goals were deﬁned, to guide and structure the project.
• Design and assemble hardware for interfacing with the DWM1000 radios and other sensors.
• Perform a survey of existing algorithms for distributed, range based localization.
• Characterise the UWB radios, with respect to their utilization as range sensors.
• Propose an algorithm to calculate range, based on receive and transmit timestamps from a series of
UWB radios.
• Propose distributed algorithm(s) to localize nodes, under the assumption that range is available as
a measurement. The algorithm must take into consideration the bandwidth limitations of the UWB
radios, such that communication can be done through the UWB network.
• Experimentally validate the devised estimator.
1.2
Thesis Outline
Chapter 2 describes the hardware platform in detail. An overview of the important components is
given, design choices are explained and instructions for use of the developed ﬁrmware is given; including

Chapter 3 presents the DWM1000 ultra-wideband radio transceiver module that the project uses for

coupled with doing so - such as clock skew, multipath-reﬂections and phenomena related to the speciﬁc
radio modules used. The algorithm used for calculating inter-node distances developed in [Rajan, 2016] is
presented. Methods for synchronising the network of nodes globally, to allow message scheduling and avoid
on-air collision of messages are presented, but not implemented due to lack of time. A simpler master-slave
approach was adopted and is described brieﬂy.
Chapter 4 deals with the development of the localization algorithm. Previous works in the area of
distributed range-based localization and probabilistic distributed estimation are presented and related to the

2.1
Schematic Overview and Design Choices
This section details the core components of the hardware, their functionality, and reasons as to why they
were chosen. Fig. 2.1 shows a a picture of the hardware, annotated with core component locations. Fig. 2.2
shows the top level schematic, detailing interconnections between different parts of the hardware.
As the goal of the project is to perform distributed localization, naturally multiple copies of the hardware
platform had to be manufactured. It was chosen that at least ﬁve identical boards should be produced; this

both to reduce cost, and development time.
The main components of the hardware are
• DWM1000 UWB transceiver radio module from Decawave [Dec, 2016a,c]. The module includes a
monopole antenna, the DW1000 chipset responsible for transmitting and decoding radio signals and
support circuitry such as a crystal oscillator and voltage regulators for the DW1000. This module
is, as far as the author knows, the only UWB radio transceiver module both affordable (8 e to 14 e
depending on quantity) and readily available.
Two transceivers modules are used per unit, with their axis of antenna polarization offset by 90°. This
is done so that no matter the orientation of two units, reception will be good for at least one antenna
pair. This is further discussed in Section 3.2.2
• MPU9250 Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) from InvenSense [Inv, 2016]. The MPU9250 includes a
MicroElectroMechanical Systems (MEMS) 3-axis accelerometer, a 3-axis MEMS rate gyroscope and
a 3-axis hall-effect magnetometer. The accelerometer has a full-scale range of ±157 m/s and can be
sampled at up to 4 kHz. The gyroscope has a full-scale range of ±34.9 rad/s and can be sampled at
up to 32 kHz, although in this project the sample rate is ﬁxed to 8 kHz. Both sensors have a resolution
of 16 bit over the full range. An analysis of noise intensities is carried out in Section 4.4.5.

2.2.1
Data Acquisition
The ﬁrmware program is split into the following sections:
• Reset: After power on or reset the device awaits user input and does nothing.
• Set node index: Pressing the user switch will increment node index by one, waiting for 2 s will accept
the setting and go to the next step. Current index is displayed on LEDs 1-3 in binary. The admissible
range is [0; 7].
• Set total number of nodes: Pressing the user switch will increment node count by one, waiting for 2 s
will accept the setting and go to the next step. Count starts at 1. Current count minus one is displayed
on LEDs 1-3 in binary. Admissible range is [1; 8]. If the speciﬁed node index is larger than, or equal
to the speciﬁed node count the ERR LED is lit.
• Sensors and radio modules are initialised. If any error occurs the ERR LED is lit.
• Data acquisition starts and data is logged to the SD card. During this phase LED 3 blinks to indicate
successful writes to the SD card.
• End of acquisition: Press the user switch to stop data acquisition. This is an important step, as the SD
card cannot be powered off while data is being written to it, without risk of data corruption.
In ﬁgure 2.3 examples of user LED outputs throughout initialization and use are illustrated. In addition to
the user LEDs, a handful of debug oriented LEDs are connected directly to each of the DWM1000 modules.

2.2.2
SD Card and Raw Logs
During operation the hardware logs sensor readings and time stamps from the UWB radios. The log
consists of “lines” or frames, each frame corresponding to a measurement from the IMU i.e. 125 µs, as the
==PAGE:31==
2.2. USER INSTRUCTIONS
9
gyro sample rate is 8 kHz. The line may also include data logged from the magnetometer, barometer and
UWB radios if new information was gathered during that frame. A swing buffer is used to store data and
transmit it to the SD card. While data is being ﬂushed to the SD card from one swing set, data is moved
from sensors into the other. It should be noted at this point that the SD card does not contain a ﬁle system,
but is to be interpreted as one contiguous ﬁle. The ﬁrst 512 bytes of the log is not part of acquired sensor
readings, but rather a heading containing information allowing the unpackaging software to detect the SD
card when inserted into a PC, as well as information on node ID and count.
For each message received over the UWB radio interface, the time stamps at time of arrival are stored, as
well as the ID of the transmitter and a “message number” generated by the transmitter which serves to align
the message stream when processing the data log ofﬂine. Each time the hardware device transmits a message,
the time of transmittance time stamp is logged, as well as the reception time for the non-transmitting radio
module. Message number and transmitter ID is logged as well.
To preserve space, only bytes carrying information are stored; that is a sensor reading taking up one
half-word (two 8-bit bytes) is stored as two bytes, a radio time stamp will occupy 5 bytes, as that is the
resolution of the time stamp register in the DW1000, and so forth. For some sensor readings a dummy byte
is included in the beginning. This is an unfortunate side effect of utilizing Direct Memory Access (DMA), a
hardware component in the micro controller allowing parallelization of memory operations, to move sensor
readings directly from peripheral memory into the swing buffer.
Endinaness of all peripheral sensors (i.e. order of most and least signiﬁcant byte) is not equal for all
sensors, and due to the nature of the DMA operations are not swapped to mach each other before storage to
the SD card. Table 2.1 documents the position of sensor readings in the 44 byte long frame, their endinaness,
and whether values are to be interpreted as two’s-complement signed integers, or unsigned integers.
SD card speed requirements
Modern Secure Digital High Capacity (SDHC) storage cards are commonly found in speed classes (4)
(6) and (10), corresponding to an average write speed of 4 MB/s, 6 MB/s and 10 MB/s respectively. SD
card speed class is speciﬁed as the average write speed over a memory area called an Allocation Unit (AU)
[SD , 2017]. For SDHC cards in the size 1 GiB to 32 GiB the maximal AU size is 4 MiB, this means that
during a 4 MiB data transfer a class (4) card may indicate busy for at most 1 s, where as a class (10) card
indicate busy for at most 400 ms.
With a frame length of 44 bytes, and a sample rate of 8 kHz of the IMU, a total of 352 kB/s or
360.4 KiB/s of data must be logged to the SD card. This indicates that with a speed class (10) card, each
set in the swing buffer must be at least 140.8 kB or 137.5 KiB to store enough sensor readings, so that no
measurements are lost during the longest allowable busy time of the SD card. Unfortunately the SRAM
memory (memory for variables) of the micro controller is limited to 128 KiB. Taking into account that
some memory must be allocated for stack usage and static variables, the size of each swing buffer set is set
to 55 KiB, equivalent to 1280 frames. This allows the SD card to block for up to 160 ms, without losing
samples.
While the SD standard speciﬁes a lower bound on real-time performance for a given speed class, in
reality SD cards will often have much better worst-case than the guaranteed minimum. It was found that in
particular the Kingston microSD Action Camera UHS-I U3 and SanDisk Ultra cards perform well for tasks
requiring low busy times. Should a user wish to use a different brand of SD cards, it is recommended that
the SD card be thoroughly tested. If the SD card ever indicates busy for too long, code execution will stop
and the ERR LED will light up. It should be noted that the write performance of some SD cards has been
observed to degrade after extensive use of the SD card.

CHAPTER 3
Ultra-Wideband Transceiver
This chapter describes the UWB radio transceiver modules from Decawave, whic are used both as
communication devices and as range sensing devices. The chapter attempts to describe how ultra-wideband
technology can be utilised to distinguish the line of sight signal from reﬂections, and thus accurately
determine arrival time of such signals. It is explained how a bidirectional communication link between two
radios can be used to estimate inter-radio distance. The chapter shall also give an overview of some of the
most prominent factors inﬂuencing the quality of the estimate, including antenna orientation, clock drift and
phenomena speciﬁcally related to the Decawave DW1000 chipset.
It should be noted that while other UWB chipsets exist, all observations and models presented are to be
assumed only valid for the DW1000 chipset and DWM1000 module.
3.1
UWB Theory of Operation
Many different narrow band communication schemes exist, though the two most commonly preferred
are Amplitude Modulation (AM) and Frequency Modulation (FM). Common for these methods are that
a carrier frequency is modulated either in amplitude or frequency by a slowly varying modulation signal
carrying data.
The DW1000 is an IEEE 802.15.4-2011 compliant ultra-wideband radio transceiver. In contrast to narrow
band radio, the DW1000 sends out small burst of energy. While these burst are generated by modulation of a
carrier, the bandwidth of each pulse is extremely wide, leading to the name of this type of communication,
also sometimes dubbed impulse radio. By transmitting bursts with either positive or negative amplitude,
binary signals can be encoded and transmitted. In Fig. 3.1 an example of classical narrow band modulation
is illustrated, whereas in Fig. 3.2 the modulation scheme of the UWB transmitter is visualised. Due to the
proprietary nature of the DW1000, the visualisation is of a ﬁctive UWB transmitter with properties similar
to the DW1000.
The wide bandwidth of the DW1000 ensures that transmitted pulses are very narrow, this allows
distinction of signals traveling along different paths, such as separating the line-of-sight signal from
reﬂections. This phenomena is illustrated intuitively in Fig. 3.3 where the modulation signal from a
pulse radio with a bandwidth of 1.3 GHz is compared to that of a pulse with a bandwidth of 133 MHz when
both the direct signal and a reﬂection delayed by 5 ns, or approximately 150 cm, are received.
Messages transmitted by the DW1000 can be split into four sections, a preamble, a frame start delimiter,
a header with meta-data about the message and the actual payload. Of greatest importance is the preamble,
that allows accurate determination of the time of arrival of the transmitted signal and the data payload, that
allows data exchanges. The four sections are described brieﬂy:

• Preamble: The preamble consists of a sequence of pulses with a periodic perfect autocorrelation
function. This in effect allows the DW1000 to calculate the impulse response from transmitter to
receiver, the so-called Channel Impulse Response (CIR). This allows the receiver to utilise energy
from reﬂections when decoding the payload, it also has the added side effect that time of arrival of the
incoming signal can be accurately determined.
• Start Frame Delimiter: A special sequence that marks the end of the preamble and the beginning of the
payload. This is the point in the message that the [Ele, 2011] standard deﬁnes as where timestamping
happens.
• Physical Layer Header: Header describing data rate, payload length, preamble duration and a few
other bits.
• Data payload: Data package, up to 127 bytes if a device compliant with the IEEE standard is desired,
otherwise can be up to 1023 bytes. It should be noted that a longer data package increases the chance
of not receiving all data correctly. The last two bytes are reserved for a Cyclic Redundancy Check
(CRC) value, used to detect erroneous reception.
3.2
UWB Challenges
On paper the UWB technology may sound like the perfect candidate for a distributed localization system,
allowing accurate timestamping of message transmit and receive times for use in range calculation, and at
the same time providing a data exchange network. In reality performance is severely impacted by a handful
of natural phenomena, such as transmitter and receiver not sharing a common deﬁnition of time; physical
objects obstructing the direct signal path, and phenomena relating speciﬁcally to the implementation in the
DW1000.

3.2.2
Reﬂections and Obstructions
A sometimes overlooked assumption, when dealing with estimation based on ranges acquired through
time of ﬂight measurements, is that there has to be a known relation between the signal time of ﬂight and the
direct distance between transmitter and receiver. This assumption is fulﬁlled if the detected signal travels
along the direct path, and the speed at which the signal travels is known along all parts of the way.
Under normal circumstances this assumption will be true; UWB technology increases the certainty of
detecting the direct path, as the high bandwidth separates the direct impulse from reﬂections, so the detected
signal can be assumed to be that of the direct path. If there are no obstructions between transmitter and
receiver the signal will traverse a constant length of antenna with one speed, then through air at (almost)
the speed of light. The effect of the delay through the antenna can be compensated for through calibration,
and the speed of light through air is constant. Thus the detected signal will be that of the direct path and the
relation between time of ﬂight and distance is known, allowing the distance to be calculated accurately.
In reality these assumptions do not always hold. In Fig. 3.5 the amplitude and timing of the signal along
the direct is compared to that of a reﬂection. Fig. 3.5a illustrates the nominal case, where the direct path is
unobstructed. Fig. 3.5b illustrates a setup, where the direct signal is delayed by traveling through a wall.
Fig. 3.5c illustrates a setup, where the direct signal propagates through a medium with high absorption, such
as a human, and the direct signal is not detected. Fig. 3.5d shows a setup where due to antenna positioning
the direct signal is attenuated below the trigger threshold. Fig. 3.5e shows a setup, where reﬂections from a
surface close to the direct path creates destructive interference and the direct signal is not detected.
Different approaches exist to deal with occlusion and multi-path reﬂections, but the algorithms often
require knowledge of the area in which the system operates, as well as high amounts of communication.
Following will be a review of previous works dealing with UWB localization under non-ideal circumstances.
It should be noted that neither of these are based on the DW1000 chipset.
In the paper [Dardari et al., 2008] a centralised cooperative localization strategy is investigated that takes
onto account the effects of signal propagation delays caused by occlusion. Two solutions are proposed:
One where a map of the environment is known. An iterative algorithms is deployed and based on range
measurements nodes are localized, and the number of walls crossed by the signal is estimated based on the
map, the resultant range bias is subtracted and node positions are estimated. This continues until convergence.

pair will have their direction of polarization being non-orthogonal.
3.2.3
Timestamp Noise
To utilise UWB technology in a stochastically optimized estimator, a noise model of the device is needed.
During experiments designed to quantify noise variance and whiteness properties, it was discovered that
the receiver timestamp is, on top of white noise, corrupted by a periodic signal. This section will deal with
describing the nature of, well as reasoning about probable causes of this disturbance. Finally the solution to
this problem will be discussed.
To illustrate the phenomena the following experimental setup is made: A single device is set up to gather
data and to perform range estimation between the two onboard radio modules. As the two radio modules are
located on the same circuit, the inter-radio distance can be assumed to be constant. Observing the transmit
timestamp of one transceiver and comparing it to the receive timestamp of the other transceiver the following
relation arises:
Trx,k = Ttx,k + τ + ek
(3.13)
Where Trx,k and Ttx,k are the receiver and transmitter timestamps, τ is the constant time of ﬂight (including
antenna delay) and ek accounts for measurements noise, clock drift and the periodic disturbance. It is
assumed that transmitter times are accurate, and the timing of the transmitter is used as a reference. Sampling
is done at 250 Hz.
Numerically differentiating both sides, the time of ﬂight vanishes and noise terms can be isolated

(3.14)

Possible solutions
The presence of none-white measurement noise is usually an unwanted behaviour, it would therefore be
valuable if a method for removing the effects of this periodic disturbance can be found. One can think of a
few ways to do so:
• Model Distortion Shape: If the wave shape of the induced distortion can be found, and is relatively
constant, it can be removed by subtraction. That is assuming the error is based on how far the
interpolated point is from it’s source point, and no other parameters inﬂuences the error; a compensation
function based on the distance from the source point can be created.
Experimental data does not indicate that this method is valid. A 2-D histogram of E′
k as a function of
the interpolation index (distance from source samples) over a 10 min window shows a pattern, but not
one easily used for correction. Further the pattern changes over time. The histograms are included in
Fig. 3.10.
• Adaptive Filter: Since the frequency of the periodic is known, or rather can be estimated through
clock drift estimation, it should be possible to construct a notch ﬁlter that tracks and ﬁlters out the
disturbance.

3.3
Clock Synchronisation and Ranging
As touched upon in Section 3.2.1, ranging with the DWM1000 modules can not be accomplished without
some way of synchronising the timebase of the involved transceivers. Synchronisation is also important to
allow scheduling of which transmitter should transmit when, such that on-air collision does not happen and
messages are lost simply because they interfere with each other. The requirements for these two types of
synchronisation differ slightly:
• Synchronisation for Ranging: To obtain an accurate estimate of the distance between two nodes a
and b, sub-nano-second-accurate clock synchronisation is a must as reasoned in Section 3.2.1. As we
shall see in Section 3.3.1 both range and clock parameters can be observed solely based on a ﬁnite
amount of messages between node a and b; while the inclusion of communication with a third node c
might improve accuracy and all ranges can be estimated jointly, information from c does not change
the ability to observe range for the pair {a, b}.
This sub-nano-second-accurate clock synchronisation is thus only needs to be performed pairwise for
nodes, and global synchronisation of the entire network is not a requirement.
• Synchronisation for Scheduling: To allow allocation of time-slots for each transceiver to be transmitting
in, so that no two transceivers are transmitting at the same time, a common timebase for the entire
network must be established. The on-air-time is dominated by the length of transmitted messages as
they are in the range of hundreds of microseconds to a few milliseconds depending on bandwidth
and payload size. The rate at which communication can be performed is bounded by the message
length. Inserting a small period of time, where no transmitter is active, does not signiﬁcantly impact
communication rate, but signiﬁcantly increases robustness as local time bases can drift slightly from
the common timebase, without having messages collide.
The conclusion is that synchronisation for transmit scheduling has different requirements than that for

3.3.1
Pairwise Synchronization and Ranging
In his dissertation Relative Space-Time Kinematics of an Anchorless Network [Rajan, 2016] Rajan intro-
duces two novel algorithms for joint calculation of clock parameters and inter-node ranges for localization
in time of ﬂight systems. One algorithm for pairwise estimation of parameters, and an extension of the
algorithm, that allows estimation of all clock parameters and ranges in a network of transceivers. Unless
otherwise stated, derivations and equations in this subsection originate from [Rajan, 2016], with the main
part concerning the pairwise algorithm based on [Rajan, 2016, chap. 4.3].
The scope of [Rajan, 2016] is to facilitate localization in a network of satellites orbiting the Earth,
and as such many aspects differ from the works of this Thesis: UWB radio technology is not speciﬁcally
addressed, local clocks considered in [Rajan, 2016] are vastly more accurate than the crystal oscillators
of the DWM1000 (as the budget for satellites are less restrictive, than for this project), and distances that
ranging is performed over are several orders of magnitude larger. The main problem, however, remains the
same: Based on timestamps of transmissions and receptions that are corrupted by small amounts of noise
(relative to the total time of ﬂight) collected in different timebases; estimate clock parameters and time of
ﬂight, such that inter-transceiver ranges can be computed.
Clock model
The clock model in Pairwise Synchronization and Ranging is based on the works of David Allan [Allan,
1987], to better understand the formulation, ﬁrst the introduction of instantaneous frequency and accumulated
phased is needed.
When talking about clocks and timekeeping, one often assumes the existence of a device that oscillates
with a known frequency; the number of periods elapsed since some deﬁned starting point, then becomes
ones observation of “time”; This could be counting the number of times a pendulum crosses it’s resting
position, as in a classical watch; counting output pulses from an oscillating quarts crystal, such as in the
clocks commonly used for timekeeping in computers; or it could be a device based on the natural oscillations
of atoms, such as used in atomic clocks.

Implementation
The Pairwise Synchronization and Ranging is implemented in C++, so that it can be utilised with data
gathered by the hardware (Chapter 2). Pairwise calculation of clock parameters and range is performed, as
this keeps the noise in ˆdij,k independent with respect to i and j. This is a useful property when the result

To perform pairwise ranging between two transceivers i and j, both transmit timestamps and receive

message sequence: {i →j, j →i, i →j, j →i} The transmit timestamps can be embedded in the transmit-

node j will keep track of received messages and transmit this information as well. That way node i receives
Tji,1 in message 2 and Tji,3 in message 4.
Bandwidth usage
Timestamps on the DW1000 are stored as a 5 B variable with a resolution of 15.65 ps.
To facilitate pairwise ranging the DW1000 must transmit the transmission timestamp, as well as a receiver
timestamp for each transceiver that should be capable of ranging with it. If each device is equipped with two
transceivers the required bandwidth is (10M + 5)B per message, where M is the number of devices to be
ranging with.

3.3.2
Message Scheduling
Scheduling which device should transmits when is important, as no two transmitters can be simultane-
ously transmitting, if messages are to be reliably received. To facilitate message scheduling, nodes must
agree on a common timebase and a method for dividing intervals and timeslots, for each device to be
operating within. The task of obtaining a common basis for timekeeping in a distributed system is a well
debated topic; most famous is probably the Network Time Protocol (NTP) [Mills, 1991] used for clock
synchronisation of desktop and server computers. Recently algorithms tailored for deployment in small
network with better knowledge of message propagation delays (as opposed to the somewhat unpredictable
timing of the Internet) have been developed, no doubt motivated by the popularisation of “Internet of Things”
(IoT) devices. The interested reader is redirected towards survey paper [Sundararaman et al., 2005], which
provides an excellent overview of clock synchronisation algorithms for use in distributed sensor networks-
As such the task of “coarse” clock synchronisation (in contrast to the nano-second accuracy required for
range estimation) is regarded as a solved problem.
Unfortunately due to the limited time allocated for the project, and technical issues relating to the fact
that multiple clock domains exists on each device, no network-wide synchronisation protocol is implemented
Instead a much simper master-slave algorithm was implemented. While objectives such as robustness
towards single node failure, and optimal performance in case of message loss are not fulﬁlled; the algorithm
performs sufﬁciently for gathering data for veriﬁcation of the developed localization algorithm.
Master-slave message scheduling
The master-slave algorithm works by deﬁning a “master” node, that transmits at regular intervals,
alternating between it’s two transceivers. Each “slave” node has an identiﬁcation number, ranging from 1 to
M, M being the total count if nodes. Based on their ID and the detected arrival time of a master message,
slaves respond. This ensures that no message collision happens, as all transmits are aligned to a common
timebase (that of the master). The drawback is that if, for a given slave-node, communication from the
master-node does not arrive; that node will not transmit. To add some robustness, a slave is allowed to trigger
off an “old” master message if no master message was received, but only a certain number of times.
The algorithm is described as following Given a network of size M ∈N>0, deﬁne a message transmit
interval Ts > Tmsg, Tmsg being the worst case on-air-time of a message (message length plus time of ﬂight).
For each node Di, assigned unique identiﬁcation number di = i ∈N, 0 ≤i < M; for each transceiver pair:
Xi,0 and Xi,1 assign unique identiﬁcation numbers xi,0 = 2i, xi,1 = 2i + 1. Finally deﬁne Nm ∈N≥0 as
the number of times a slave may transmit, if no master massage was received. Algorithm 1 describes the ﬂow
of the algorithm. In Fig. 3.12 the transmit sequence for at network of size M = 3 and Nm = 1 is illustrated.

CHAPTER 4
Distributed Localization
This chapter addresses the design and development of a distributed localization algorithm, based on
range measurements from UWB transceivers. A thorough review of previous work is performed, including
references to centralised and distributed localization algorithms exploiting various technologies, but mostly
focused on range based localization. The topic of general distributed estimation, focused on linear stochastic
(sub-)optimal estimation is also covered. It is reasoned why UWB technology is a good candidate for
research in distributed localization and the localization problem based on UWB range is cast in a linear
estimation framework, where cooperation is achieved through the use of consensus algorithms.
The chapter includes a short introduction to the theoretical tools used for solving the problem; such as
the Kalman ﬁlter, covariance intersection, diffusion Kalman ﬁlter, and Error State Kalman Filter. It is shown
how theses theories are combined to form a ﬁlter useful for distributed localization.
4.1
Previous Work
Before blindly developing a new algorithm, reinventing the wheel, it is always important to form an
overview about how other people have solved this, or similar problems. Not unlike visiting a fair or a
workshop, one browses the available tools and inspects solutions to similar problems proposed by others,
hoping to be inspired. Being inspired either to solve the problem using a different tool-set, be that theoretical
or technology wise, improving upon existing solutions or combine techniques and tools from different
solution in a novel way.
This section aims at giving a broad overview of some previous solutions, including tools for localization
and distributed estimation. Paper reviews are grouped in three categories: Localization with centralised
algorithm, localization in a distributed setting and general distributed estimation techniques.
4.1.1
Centralised Localization
The dissertation Sensor Fusion and Calibration of Inertial Sensors, Vision, Ultra-Wideband and GPS
[Hol, 2011] gives an in-depth overview of sensor fusion with IMUs and a series of possible absolute
localization systems. In this work both visual tracking, localization based on UWB beacons and GPS is
considered and compared. The most relevant contributions are: a novel routine for calibrating UWB anchor
locations, and a novel noise model for UWB range measurements. The proposed noise model assumes
measurement noise to be a sum of Gaussian noise and exponentially distributed noise. The Gaussian noise
originates from leading edge detection, whereas the exponentially distributed noise accounts for errors
introduced by multi-path reﬂections and occlusion - both of which can introduce a positive bias. The work

demonstrates how UWB technology can, in a centralised setup, provide results of comparable quality to
those of optical tracking.
The paper Fusing ultra-wideband range measurements with accelerometers and rate gyroscopes for
quadrocopter state estimation [Mueller, Hamer, and D’Andrea, 2015] describes how ultra-wideband technol-
ogy can be used for robot localization, speciﬁcally to localize an airborne drone. Inertial measurements, such
as acceleration and angular velocity is used to propagate state estimates in an iterative linear estimator, the
Kalman ﬁlter. In a round-robin fashion the drone communicates with stationary UWB anchors, calculating
distance to them and using this information to update the state estimate.
The paper A Robot Self-Localization System using One-Way Ultra-Wideband Communication [Led-
ergerber, Hamer, and D’Andrea, 2015] presents an algorithm in which a robot equipped with a UWB
receiver localizes based on stationary UWB transmitters. The work emphasizes, how anchor placement
impacts performance. During evaluation of the algorithm the Decawave DWM1000 models are used, and
the estimator output is used for closed loop control of an airborne drone in an indoor environment. The work
demonstrates that even cheap UWB solutions, such as the DWM1000, can provide localization estimates
accurate enough to allow closed loop drone control.
These works mostly highlight the use of UWB technology for localization, surely other technologies
exist; but most of these are quite mature compared to UWB, and thus more extensive research has been
made in these areas. For completeness a few of these “standard” solutions are mentioned here. Generally
localization technologies can be divided in two groups: external localization, in which absolute positioning
is provided by stationary base stations, and internal localization in which the device to be localized carries
senors for localizing based on features in the surrounding environment, often resulting in only relative pose
information. Among external localization methods, not including GPS and derivatives, the most common is
camera based MOtion CAPture systems (MOCAP) in which visual markers are tracked by stationary cameras.
By viewing the same markers with many cameras their absolute location in the room can be calculated. Well
known companies producing such systems include Vicon [Ltd, 2017] and OptiTrack [NaturalPoint, 2017],
the topic is also covered in [Hol, 2011] and [Greiff, 2017]. Systems based on ultrasonic transducers also
exists; much like UWB these systems work by measuring the time of ﬂight between one or more moving
nodes and stationary anchors; in this case however, sound waves are utilised instead of radio signals making
propagation times much longer, thus lowering requirements for accurate timing. Interested readers are
referred to the survey paper [Ijaz et al., 2013]. The technology never really took off for indoor use, but
acoustic localization is used in underwater operations as sound is one of the few mediums that are available
for underwater communication. Internal localization methods rely on detecting features in the environment
and, by tracking the movement of these features, a robot can estimate its own movement (egomotion).
Common approaches rely either on visual information [Mourikis and Roumeliotis, 2007, Mur-Artal et al.,
2015, Mur-Artal and Tardos, 2016, Klein and Murray, 2007, Engel et al., 2014] or information from laser
scanners, so called Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR) sensors [Thrun et al., 2005].
As evident in [Hol, 2011, Mueller et al., 2015, Ledergerber et al., 2015] UWB technology is a promising
candidate for external localization in GPS denied environments, and with the recent recent arrival of the
Decawave DWM1000 in 2014 the technology has become available at an accessible price point (≈20 e).
Further, as UWB technology not only allows range estimation, but also communication, it is a good candidate
technology for research in distributed localization.

Distributed (range based) Localization
The dissertation Models and Algorithms for Ultra-Wideband Localization in Single- and Multi-Robot
Systems [Prorok, 2013] deals with the implementation of a localization system, combining absolute posi-

tioning supplied by UWB beacons with cooperate sensing based on infrared LEDs. Cooperation between
robots allows building a map of UWB range error distributions as a function of location. In doing so,
some of the issues regarding occlusion and multi-path reﬂections are alleviated. The project demonstrates
how cooperation between multiple robots can improve localization accuracy, and deals with some of the

The paper Robust Distributed Network Localization with Noisy Range Measurements [Moore, Leonard,
Rus, and Teller, 2004] deals with distributed localization in a network of static nodes using arbitrary range

when so called ﬂip ambiguities exists is addressed. Flip ambiguities can be be described as the possibility
of different node locations to result in the same range measurement. Especially with noisy measurements
determining the correct node conﬁguration can be difﬁcult. In the paper the solution is to cluster nodes in so
called “robust quads”, clusters of four nodes that all communicate, and are spaced, so that noise does not
induce ambiguity. This work illustrates some of the inherent limitations of range based localization,
The paper Consensus-based Algorithms for Distributed Network-State Estimation and Localization
[Soatti, Nicoli, Savazzi, and Spagnolini, 2017] describes a method for localizing in non line of sight condi-
tions, when utilising a measured Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI). A novel model is proposed,
in which both the mean of measured RSSI and variance are included, as both change during occlusion.
Localization is performed as solution to a non-linear least-squares problem, the novelty lies with demon-
strating how this can be accomplished in a distributed fashion by interleaving Gauss-Newton iterations with
consensus on neighbouring node parameter estimates (parameters being all node x and y coordinates).
The paper Particle Swarm Optimization-Based Particle Filter for Cooperative Localization in Wireless
Networks [Li, Wu, Wang, Shi, Yuan, and Kuang, 2013] tackles range based localization in a sub-optimal
stochastic setting. Node dynamics are modeled as constant velocity driven by Gaussian white noise, range
measurements also corrupted by Gaussian white noise. A few anchors of known position are assumed present
as well. Each node uses a particle ﬁlter representation of its local position and velocity, to conserve bandwidth
only a Gaussian approximation of the particle ﬁlter is transmitted. When integrating new measurements a
node will then aggregating measurement noise with the Gaussian approximation of estimation error from
the other involved node. This work demonstrates that range based localization algorithms can perform
adequately, even when nodes do not transmit information about the full state space, but only local node
coordinates.
The papers Consensus-Based Cooperative Source Localization of Multi-Agent Systems with Sampled
Range Measurements [Chai, Lin, Lin, and Zhang, 2014] and Distributed Source Localization of Multi-Agent
Systems With Bearing Angle Measurements [Lin, Lin, Zheng, Yan, and Mao, 2016] tackle the localization
problem in reverse attempting to, for each node, estimate the position of a stationary beacon in local
coordinates - thus localizing relative to it. Based on local velocity estimates an either range or bearing
measurements a nonlinear observer that is asymptotically stable given a connected network and sufﬁcient
node motion is derived. The work illustrates how knowledge of node dynamics can assist in localization,
allowing observation of node positions in systems that would not be possible with static nodes.

Extensions and open issues
It should be noted that there are a handful of open questions regarding covariance intersection and
especially its use in optimal recursive estimation. When integrating measurements through covariance
intersection in a recursive estimator, a value for κ(k) must be chosen in each iteration. This is usually
done by minimizing the trace or the determinant of either the inﬂated error covariance matrix P (k) or
the posterior covariance matrix P +(k) (Table 4.2); however the κ(k) optimal at time step k might not be
optimal for the fusion at a future point. One could wish that κ(k) could be chosen, such that the trace or
determinant of a future P (k + n) is minimized. In [Ajgl et al., 2014] a method is adopted, solving this issue
by storing a window of previous measurements and state estimates; each time a new measurement arrives,
the state estimate is reset back to the begging of the window, κ’s for the entire window are calculated based
on minimizing P(k) at the end of the window and measurements are re-applied.
Covariance intersection is only the optimal linear estimator when fusing two estimates, when fusing
multiple sets of data with unknown cross-covariance (either contiguously or as part of a recursive estimator)
CI tends to yield estimates where P (k) is much too conservative as demonstrated in [Reinhardt et al., 2015].
The paper [Noack et al., 2017] details methods for improving accuracy, when fusing multiple datasets
with correlated errors due to data sharing in distributed estimation, exploiting structures in the correlation to
improve upon the CI algorithm and provide a conservative estimate of P (k) that is tighter than the P (k))
provided by CI. The paper was unfortunately not discovered in due time to have its ﬁndings included in the
implementation.

Error State Kalman Filter
The idea behind the ESKF as described in [Solà, 2015] is very similar to the EKF, in the sense that errors
are propagated based on a linearization of the system equations; however the difference lies in how the
estimation error is deﬁned. In the EKF, the true state and the estimate are related by a sum x = ˆx + ˜x; in
the ESKF the true state dynamics x are separated into a nominal part x and an error state δx related by the
composition
x = x ⊕δx.
(4.89)
During the prediction step the estimate is regarded as a known signal, propagated by known system dynamics
and inputs of the nominal dynamics; the estimation error is regarded as a stochastic signal and propagated
separately, collecting noise terms and linearization errors. The error is assumed to be small enough, that it
can accurately be represented by a Gaussian distribution and as the error is small, linearization of system
equation f(·) is assumed to be accurate. Once a measurement becomes available, it is used to create an
estimate of the error state and through the composition x = x ⊕δx the estimate is updated ˆx ←ˆx ⊕ˆ
δx.

4.4
Range Based Distributed Localization with Partial State Sharing
Having presented all relevant theory, the algorithm can now be described. As earlier mentioned, only
motion in a 2D plane is considered, but the algorithm can be extended to 3D without signiﬁcant changes.
The estimator is constructed by letting every node estimate the state (location, orientation, sensor biases
etc.) of all nodes. This is done within the ESKF framework. Nodes equipped with, and utilising inertial
sensors will use these to propagate their estimate locally, but will not transmit these measurements; other
nodes are forced to update the velocity and orientation of these nodes with increased uncertainty. When
nodes transmit UWB messages, they embed the estimate of their local state-space, and error covariance
matrix. When nodes i receives a UWB messages from node j, node i updates its estimate of node j based on
this new information and then applies the range measurement.
Each node has a local Error State Kalman Filter, tracking the states of all nodes Di, 1 ≤i ≤M, with M
being the total amount of nodes. Let ˆxi,j(k) be the estimate of local state xj(k)) of node Dj as estimated
by node Di at time step k, then the entire state-space estimate for node Di is

• Fixed on Line: Anchor-like node that is known to be positioned on a straight line, but its exact position

• Stationary: Anchor-like node, known to be stationary, but its exact location is not known. As
time passes and the stationary nodes estimate of its position becomes more certain, its functionality
approaches that of the base node.
• Inertial Aided: Node dynamics are that of a rigid body constrained to a horizontal plane. The node is
equipped with inertial sensors measuring linear acceleration and angular velocity, used for propagating
the state. The inertial measurements are not transmitted to other nodes, and so they use a white noise
model for acceleration and angular velocity.
• Inertial Aided, Plane Aligned: Node dynamics are that of a 2D rigid body, free to move and rotate in
the plane. Nodes are equipped with inertial sensors, where local z-axis is assumed aligned with global
z-axis. Like the unaligned inertial aided node, the node does not communicate inertial measurements
and other nodes assume a white noise model for acceleration and angular velocity.
• Random Walk Velocity: Nodes not equipped with inertial sensors, or where inertial sensors are not
used. Nodes of this type do not estimate their orientation and assume that their velocity is some-what
constant, in the sense that velocity is modelled as a random walk function corresponding to a white
noise acceleration model.

4.4.7

As different node types have different number of states, the bandwidth usage for sharing local estimates
of local states and corresponding error covariance matrices also differs from node type to node type. It is

can be done using a single precision ﬂoating point representation, 4 B per number. It should also be noted
that covariance matrices are symmetric, as such only diagonal and upper triangular parts need be transmitted.

As noted in Section 3.3.1, the UWB transceivers must also return the timestamps for received messages,

number of nodes to range with. Note that Mn may differ from the total amount of other nodes M −1, as
nodes may chose not to perform ranging with all other nodes, to conserve bandwidth. Nodes may even chose
which nodes to range with adaptively to conserve bandwidth while at the same time having good estimation
accuracy, however, such adaptation is outside the scope of this work.
Keeping the maximum standard payload size of 127 B in mind, for nodes with a random walk velocity
model, a total number of 6 nodes can perform range measuring and distributed estimation, as the total
payload size would then be 111 B. For nodes with higher order dynamics a different solution must be
chosen. One could choose to increase payload size as the DW1000 does allow payloads up to 1023 B, this
will come at the cost of less frequent updates as the on air time would increase to approximately 10 ms. A
different solution is to spread the data sharing over multiple messages (as range calculation requires at least
three messages anyway), using both transceivers and assuming that two transmissions from each is used for
each range measurement, increasing the payload size to 175 B would allow inertial aided node dynamics.
This does, however, introduce complications as the shared data will be slightly delayed, compared to the
measurement. Robustness to message loss is also decreased, as cooperative fusion can not be performed if
even a single message is lost.
In the current implementation, with off-line computation, there is no limit to the amount of data that can
be shared, as communication does not happen in real time, but is simulated on a desktop.

Results
This chapter details the experimental validation of the developed algorithm. Several cases are presented,
each showcasing an attractive aspect of the algorithms. Some experiments are carried out in real life, and
the algorithm is applied to the gathered data. Other experiments are carried out on synthetic data alone.
Experiments with synthetic data allows comparison of true and estimated states, and enables experiments
with a larger amount of nodes, than physically available. The cases considered are:
• A two-wheeled balancing robot, with a node rigidly attached to its body traces a rectangular shape.
Stationary nodes are placed in the four corners of the rectangle. The algorithm is run, comparing
different node dynamics for the moving node.
• A moving node traces a ﬁgure eight, with three stationary nodes on a line next to the ﬁgure. A
secondary moving node moves in a linear pattern above the line of anchors. With out cooperation
between the two moving nodes, the one tracing a ﬁgure eight cannot accurately localize, as node
position will at times be on the line of the anchors.
• Eight stationary nodes are positioned in a grid, where nodes communicate only in a pattern resembling
a truss structure. This experiment is carried out in simulation alone, as no more than 5 nodes were
constructed. The experiment demonstrates how even nodes with no direct communication to a base
node can localize.

Experiment 1: Inertial Aided Node - Centralised

Note that despite no use of a magnetometer or any other absolute heading reference, the node is capable of
estimating its orientation. This is due to the relatively large acceleration in the corners; as the acceleration
is measured in local coordinates the estimator is capable of, based on range measurements, inferring the
orientation.
Note how the rectangle is not tracked exactly along all edges, as the estimated shape is taller than the
true rectangle, with a deviation of approximately 20 cm. The reason could be that the UWB range bias, as
described in Section 4.4.5, is not chosen correctly; the effect of signal strength on bias is not modeled and
thus could be the cause; or manual placement of anchor nodes may be slightly inaccurately. This is a rather
common pattern to see in UWB based localization; the estimated path overlaps with it self, such that ex.
multiple laps around the rectangular shape are estimated to have the same shape, but this shape does not
match the actual path exactly. A similar pattern in the estimation error can be seen in [Mueller et al., 2015,
Grob and Stalder, 2015].
In Fig. 5.4 the result of running the experiment with synthetic data is plotted, demonstrating that if range
and inertial measurements correspond to actual distances and accelerations/angular velocities, the position
of the moving node is estimated accurately.

Experiment 3: Random Walk Velocity Node - Cooperative
This experiment aims to demonstrate the viability of using a random walk velocity model for node
dynamics of moving nodes. Although this is a slight model-plant mismatch in the sense that the actual
velocity of the moving node is not a random walk, but consists of sections with constant velocity (the sides),
and sudden changes in velocity (the corners). The random walk velocity motion model also has the added
beneﬁt of not needing information about node orientation to properly propagate the node state estimate.

on correlating the second derivative of the measured motion with measured acceleration. Ina centralised
setting this can be done, but in the distributed estimator too much information is lost during covariance
intersection - or alt least that is one interpretation of the result.
The experiment is carried out twice, with two different intensities for the model of the driving noise
standard deviation ¯σan = 1 and ¯σan = 5 to demonstrate, how this affects performance.

Figure Eight Path
This experiment demonstrates, how two moving nodes can cooperate to alleviate some of the problems,
range based localization generally faces. Three anchors are placed on a straight line, separated from each
other by 1 m, above them a node moves back and forth; below them a moving node makes a ﬁgure eight
pattern, so that the shape touches the line of anchors (Fig. 5.9). If no prior information about node positions
(and velocities) was available, and moving nodes did not communicate, the position of the moving nodes
would be ambiguous; as mirroring the position of one node through the line of anchors yields identical
range measurements. The moving node tracing a ﬁgure eight comes close to this line, and without the other
moving node to give information about which side of the line it belongs to; the estimate may "ﬂip" over to
the wrong side.
In Fig. 5.9 this experiment is carried out in simulation. The estimated node trajectories with, and without
communication between the two nodes are illustrated. It is obvious that without cooperation, estimation in
this case would yield erroneous results.

5.4
Future Work
While this work demonstrates the feasibility of utilising UWB technology in pursuit of a fully de-
centralised, range based localization algorithm; many possibilities for improving upon the methodology
exits.
• Real time implementation. The current implementation relies on gathering data and storing it for
playback later. This allows algorithm development to progress relatively painlessly, as one does not
have to worry about resource optimization. This approach does, however, not allow deployment in an
actual robotics system, as localization is not performed in real-time. A natural continuation of the
work would thus be to implement the algorithm on some sort of embedded device, especially since the
hardware has connections for both a USB interface and a serial connection.
• Nonlinearities and ﬂip ambiguities. The current state of the algorithm does not take into account
the "hard" nonlinearities that are present in a range based localization system, resulting in so called
"ﬂip ambiguities", where different node positions result in the same, or similar range measurements,
as covered in [Moore et al., 2004]. To resolve this issue, while staying in the framework of (extended)
Kalman ﬁltering one could adapt the solution proposed in [Johansen and Fossen, 2017], the eXogenious
Kalman Filter (XKF) in which a (semi-)globally stable nonlinear estimator is used to determine the
linearization point for use in EKF or ESKF; such an estimator could be based on [Moore et al., 2004].
• Improvements to CI. As mentioned in Section 4.3.2 covariance intersection is only optimal, when
fusing two data sets with unknown cross covariance, but under iterative fusion of data from multiple
sources, or from the same source multiple times; the CI approach is too conservative. for example
the paper [Noack et al., 2017] presents a method, that improves upon the CI algorithm by utilising
the correlation structure that a diffusion Kalman ﬁlter imposes. It would be very interesting if this
approach could be extended to the case, where only part of the state-space is shared.
• Improvements to transmit scheduling. As mentioned in Section 3.3.1 the bandwidth required to
obtain range estimates based on the algorithm proposed in [Rajan, 2016] scales linearly with the
number of nodes; this is obviously a hindrance, if the algorithms is to be deployed in larger networks.
As future work an algorithm that adaptively selects, which nodes should perform ranging with which
nodes is suggested, alleviating this issue. One may also look into utilising different transmitter center
frequencies on the UWB transceivers, so that multiple networks can operate in the same area.
5.5
Conclusion
During the project both hardware and software has been developed to facilitate distributed localization
based on the Decawave DW1000 ultra-wideband transceivers.
The hardware allows gathering of inertial data at rate of 8 kHz, as well as barometric pressure and a
measure of magnetic ﬁeld strength. The hardware also encompasses two Decawave DWM1000 transceiver
modules, and manages communication between multiple hardware units, so that range can be estimated.
While the hardware unit was used during this project to test a speciﬁc algorithm, in can also be used -
together with the developed software - as a platform to test and evaluate future algorithms in range based
localization.
A distributed range based localization algorithm has been implemented, based on a diffusion Kalman
ﬁlter with the novel aspect, that nodes share only their estimate of local states. This ensures that the
==PAGE:107==
5.5. CONCLUSION
85
requirements for communication bandwidth remain low enough, so that all communication can piggyback
on the transmissions used for ranging.
Through experiments carried out both on simulated data and data gathered by the hardware it has been
demonstrated, that the algorithm can accurately track the motion of a robot moving in a rectangular pattern,
when using different models for the motion of the node. Naturally a moving node with access to inertial
measurements performs better, but adequate results can also be obtained with a random walk velocity model.
It has been demonstrated that nodes located in a truss-structure-like grid can estimate their position, even
when only communicating with immediate neighbours. Further, through simulation it has been demonstrated,
how node cooperation can improve robustness to ﬂip ambiguity, in setting where anchor positioning is not
optimal.
