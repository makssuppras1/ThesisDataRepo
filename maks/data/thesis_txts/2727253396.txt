Abstract 
The purpose of this article is to present a framework to address the need for a combined 
assessment of absolute sustainability and damage, for both approaches to be simultaneously 
considered by decision-makers. This will enable them to determine the current and future 
sustainability status situation, and areas to prioritize. 
 
Using Impact World+ as LCIA, an Environmentally Extended Multi-Regional Input-Output 
Model, and sustainable boundaries aligned with the LCIA metrics, this framework proposes to 
globally determine damage and absolute sustainability status for specific impact categories in 
the human health (HH) and ecosystem quality (EQ) areas of protection (AoP). Furthermore, to 
allow detailed disaggregation of a specific impact category behavior, the framework performs 
three additional approaches based on the damage´s source (elementary flows), where the 
damage is generated and to whom affect (inducer and receptor perspective), and by economics’ 
source (by sector). The framework used 2019 as the reference year for the case study and 
considered ten impact categories for HH AoP, and eight for EQ. The detailed analysis was 
applied to the climate change impact category.  
 
Based on the results 50% and 62,5% of the HH and EQ impact categories are exceeding global 
sustainable levels. All the elementary flows, continental regions, and economic sectors are 
surpassing the climate change sustainable boundaries. Furthermore, the exceedance factor does 
not directly relate to the damage per se, reason why these are not equivalent indicators, and 
therefore the prioritization of a sector or category will vary according to the parameter is based 
on.

1. 
Introduction 
 
Starting 
in 
the 
sixties 
with 
the 
environmental assessment of products, Life 
Cycle Assessment (LCA) has and continues 
to evolve, being the nineties the decade in 
which the current LCA framework was set 
(Hauschild, Rosenbaum, & Olsen, 2018). 
Two 
decades 
later, 
in 
2009, 
the 
Environmental Limits framework was 
popularized by Rockström et al. with the 
Planetary Boundaries concept, though 
different approaches to it exist (Heide, 
Hauschild, & Ryberg, 2023). Nowadays 
traditional LCA tools can be used under the 
Environmental 
Limits 
framework 
to 
determine if a product or system´s impact is 
aligned with the environmental limitations 
(Stranddorf, et al, 2023; Li, et al., 2021), 
being these assessments known as Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessments 
(AESA) (Heide, Hauschild, & Ryberg, 
2023).  
 
Both 
frameworks 
continue 
evolving, 
though the interaction among them is 
limited, despite having the AESA an LCA 
basis (Bjørn, et al., 2020). AESA sets the 
relevance of an impact category depending 
of its status against the Safe Operation 
Space (SoS), and the traditional LCA 
approach does it based on damage.1 AESA 
focuses on a long-term vision, while LCA 
does it on the disease or pollution burden. 
Nevertheless, 
both 
frameworks 
are 
complementary 
and 
non-mutually 
exclusive. As mentioned by Rockström et 
al. (2023) & Li et al. (2021), the stability of 
the Earth system itself and human well-
being are connected, reason why the 
proposed framework wants to link the 
AESA with the damage generated in 
selected impact categories for decision-
makers to consider the implications of the 
current scenario for the short- and long-term 
and the burden it generates on current and 
future society.   
 
1 When considering the end point category for 
assessment.  
This paper aims to create a novel framework 
to be used by decision-makers and other 
stakeholders 
to simultaneously assess 
absolute sustainability and damage. The 
framework relates the exceedance status of 
different environmental boundaries in 
absolute terms and links it to the endpoint 
damage that a system generates for two 
LCA´s Areas of Protection (AoP). More 
specifically, it addresses the need to: a) 
Define absolute sustainability boundaries 
compatible with a Life Cycle Impact 
Assessment (LCIA) method; b) Develope a 
representation to visualize both exceedance 
of sustainable boundaries and damages on 
human health (HH) and ecosystem quality 
(EQ); c) Apply it to a global consumption 
scenario as determined by an Input-Output 
model; 
and, 
lastly, 
d) 
Differentiate 
exceedance and damage for the climate 
change impact category by elementary 
flow, by inducer and receptor approach, and 
by industry sectors.  
 
Section 2 details the methodological set-up 
and the study case used to exemplify the 
framework. Results and discussions are 
merged in Section 3, where the chosen set-
up and the multiple case studies approaches 
are analyzed. Limitations of the study are in 
Section 3 too. Lastly, Section 4 presents the 
conclusions.  
 
2. 
Method 
 
The 
methodology 
for 
the 
combined 
assessment consists of six steps and five 
external inputs summarized in Figure 1, and 
described as follows. The methodology is 
iterative as it allows for results to guide 
improvements. 
 
2.1. LCIA method 
 
The 
LCIA 
method 
transforms 
the 
elementary flows inventory into impacts or 
damages of the product or system of 
analysis and multiple alternatives of it exist

3
(Frontera, Salieri, & Righi, 2020). From the 
existing methods, Impact World+ was 
selected as the LCIA for this study, because 
it has characterization factors at midpoint 
and endpoint damage, granting flexibility to 
the user in the set-up of the sustainability 
boundaries, and because it is built on the 
knowledge from previous LCIA´s. 
 
Impact World+ was designed as an update 
of existing LCIA methods (Impact 2002+, 
LUCAS and EDIP), and as a global level 
coverage 
method. 
It 
has 
specific 
characterization 
factors 
on 
different 
resolutions that allow for spatial variability, 
and damage quantification for each AoP 
disaggregated 
by 
impact 
categories– 
including a temporal disaggregation of the 
damage for specific impact categories 
(Bulle, et al., 2019).

2.3. Damage assessment environment  
 
The Environmentally Extended Multi-
Regional Input-Output Model (EE-MRIO) 
is used to obtain the damage results for a 
specific annual consumption. The EE-
MRIO is a top-down approach based on 
monetary 
flows 
that 
determines 
the 
environmental implications of a specific 
product or system, as well as of a sector, 
country or region of study. Therefore, it is 
useful to analyze the environmental 
implications on a large-scale level (Jolliet, 
Saadé-Sbeih, Shaked, Jolliet, & Crettaz, 
2015). 
For 
it, 
two 
parallel 
yet 
complementary elements are need it: i) the 
Input-Output (IO) database, and ii) a script.  
 
The IO database refers to the database that 
details the expenses per economic sector 
and country in which the EE-MRIO bases 
it’s monetary to damage conversion (Jolliet, 
Saadé-Sbeih, Shaked, Jolliet, & Crettaz, 
2015). Having determined the LCIA to use, 
the IO database selected was Exiobase, 
(version 3.8.2), as the LCIA method already 
has publicly available the characterization 
factors matrix for it.3  
 
Exiobase -in its third version- focuses on 
european countries and major economies. It 
differentiates 
between 
164 
sectors 
(including final demand) and has 49 
specific geographical areas, from which 44

2.4. Case study 
 
The case study assessed all objectives for 
the global economy using the consumption-
based approach. By using this approach, the 
damage is linked to the country inducing it 
and not to the country producing it. 
Furthermore, the reference year is 2019 to 
have 
an 
environmental 
performance 
scenario pre-COVID-19 pandemic. 
 
The case study is divided into two parts: i) 
global consumption damage across impact 
categories, and ii) multiple approaches for 
climate change impact category (per 
elementary flow, inducer and receptor per 
continental regions, and by industry 
sectors). Part I allows to have an overview 
of the global consumption damage across 
all impact categories to base the decision of 
what impacts to focus on. Part II allows 
having a better understanding of a single 
category based on multiple approaches to 
comprehend the situation from multiple 
perspectives. Together, both parts explain 
the overall situation and allow for a deeper 
understanding of the impact category.  
Part I, and inducer and receptor approach in 
part II use equal per capita as sharing 
==PAGE:10==
 
5

approach uses two different SP to determine 
the 
sustainable 
damage: 
Economic 
allocation and grandfathering. Both cases 
use 2019 as reference year for the allocation 
principle and are applied to short term 
climate change impact category. Economic 
allocation sets the SoS based on the final 
demand monetary output associated to the 
element of analysis, while grandfathering 
does it based on historical environmental 
pollution (Hjalsted, et al., 2021) 
2.5. Robustness check 
 
Damage results are compared against 
results from other two studies: i) Bulle et al. 
(2019), and ii) Lund (2018), to find 
consistency among categories. Despite the 
three studies use the same LCIA method, as 
they 
consider 
different 
years 
and 
inventories, the damages obtained are not to 
be the same, but just a reference.  
 
For the environmental categories for which 
inconsistency among the results was 
detected, a comparison of the damage by 
elementary flows of this study and the one 
of Lund (2018) was performed to detect the 
cause of it and corrected if needed.4

2.6.2. Approaches for climate change 
impact category 
 
To understand better the behavior of an 
impact category, three different global 
approaches were applied to a single 
category (i.e., climate change), where each 
of them has a specific procedure.  
 
Analysis per elementary flows 
The analysis per elementary flow zooms in 
into a category by quantifying the damage 
and exceedance factor relevance for each of 
the elementary flows of the category. This 
allows to understand in what flows the 
highest impacts are being generated and 
therefore where the biggest mitigation 
opportunities should be focused on.  
 
As the climate change boundary is set up 
with a specific GHG composition, each 
elementary flow is compared against its 
own gas boundary. From the seven GHG 
gases in Exiobase, only four -that represent 
98,7% of the Exiobase climate emissions- 
align with the characterized composition 
and are therefore considered in the 
analysis8. 
 
Formula (1) estimates the sustainable 
damage for all flows based on the GHG 
composition. 
 
Analysis per inducer and receptor approach 
This analysis determines the damage 
generated per continental region and the 
damage received by each of them. This 
approach can be applied to any impact 
category with a global damage effect (e.g. 
Ozone depletion).  
 
Three elements are needed for this analysis: 
i) damage per country using Impact World+ 
and Exiobase (i.e. results from case study 
part I), ii) the damage distribution on global 
level, and iii) countries´ population. Firstly,

Analysis per sector approach 
Multiple alternatives and combinations for 
the application of the SP exist and the 
sustainable damage assigned to a sector will 
be determined by the SP applied (Bjørn, et 
al., 2020), as these distribute the boundary 
from global to sectoral level. As mentioned 
by Ryberg et. al  (2020) and Andersen et al. 
(2020), the application of the SP will 
determine the exceedance factor status. For 
this study, the global sustainable damage is 
set across sectors using two parallel SP: 
grandfathering and economic allocation.  
 
In grandfathering allocation the SoS 
assignation is based on the damage emitted 
by the different sectors, as seen in formula 
(6); while in economic allocation is based 
on the monetary output of each sector 
(formula (7)) (Bjørn, et al., 2020). For 
economic allocation and disregard of the 
sector, -unlike in the grandfathering- the 
sustainable damage per monetary output is 
the same across sectors. This means that, for 
example, one monetary output unit from the 
construction sector has the same right to 
damage as one of the educational one.  
Three different scenarios were set for this 
approach: i) individual sectoral results 
ranked by damage generated per final 
demand output; ii) sectors´ categories 10 
ranked by absolute damage value, and iii) 
sectors´ categories ranked by damage 
generated per final demand output. The last 
one was selected for the analysis as it 
facilitates SP comparison on an aggregated 
level and alignment with the previously 
presented analysis. Results for the two other 
scenarios are available in SM4 and SM5. 
 
2.7. Assessment of Abs. boundaries 
 
The assessment of the absolute boundary is 
a comparison between the current or future 
damage and the sustainable damage. For all 
impact categories and approaches for 
climate change the absolute status is 
determined using the exceedance factor 
(formula (8)).

of damage is higher than the accepted 
threshold, and therefore the level of 
consumption is not sustainable. If it is d1 
the consumption is sustainable as the 
damage is within the threshold.

by 
the 
grandfathering approach.  
 
Disregard of the SP to use, both SP are 
aligned in their results. The current analysis 
allows to simultaneously identify the 
sectors generating the biggest damage, both 
in terms of exceedance and damage itself, 
while providing flexibility in the SP to 
apply.  
 
3.5. 
Study limitations 
 
The following study limitations and 
recommendations to overcome them were 
identified: 
• The sustainable boundary for cancer and 
non-cancer sustainable damages are set 
using the one of PM as a reference to 
facilitate comparison across human 
health categories, which might not be 
accurate in public health terms. To 
improve the accuracy of the sustainable 
damage of these categories, a specific 
cancer 
and 
non-cancer 
sustainable 
boundary from WHO or other public 
health authority should be used.  
• From the 22 GHG considered in the 
climate change boundary setup, only 
==PAGE:23==
 
18
four were considered for the elementary 
flow analysis as the CF for the remaining 
18 were not available in the used 
Exiobase version. Hence, the damage 
and exceedance status for 82% of the 
GHG, representing less than 0,2% of the 
allowed emission, was not quantified. To 
avoid this situation, CF from different 
sources and same disaggregation level 
should be incorporated into the study to 
fill the CF gaps. 
• The climate change damage distribution 
under the receptor perspective approach 
is based on a 2008 study that considered 
the implication of malnutrition, malaria, 
diarrhea, heat stress, and flooding with a 
0.68 
Celsius 
increase. 
For 
future 
implementation of this framework, it is 
recommended to update the damage 
distribution and temperature increase 
reference to use as the one used is 15 
years old. For example, the HH damage 
factors by Rupcic et al. (2022) can be an 
alternative to consider for this update.  
• The study does not consider a sensitivity 
analysis around the decisions taken 
within and their relevance to the results. 
To 
improve 
the 
results, 
it 
is 
recommended to perform a sensitivity 
study regarding the assumptions used 
(e.g. sustainable boundaries, LCIA 
method, and SP) to determine the 
relevance of those decisions on the 
findings. 
• Aligned with the previous limitation, a 
one-step SP assignation approach was 
applied to the climate change by sector´s 
categories analysis. It would be of 
interest to determine the implications for 
the results if a two-step SP assignation is 
used, to be fully aligned with the 
recommendations by other authors when 
performing a sectoral analysis.  
• The framework allows the user to 
understand where to act – as it shows 
where the damage and exceedance are 
located- but not what to do (the 
mitigation measures). For the latter, a 
second study is necessary. 
Though is not a limitation per se, but more 
of an aspect to be aware of, the framework 
requires incorporating external inputs. In 
case these inputs are erroneous or uncertain, 
the 
errors 
and 
uncertainty 
will 
be 
incorporated into the framework. Therefore, 
it is important to use trustful sources (e.g. 
WHO, World Bank or other peer-reviewed 
articles) and to clearly state what are the 
external inputs being used and what for. In 
that way, chances for addition of wrong 
information are minimized and the results 
become more transparent and trustworthy 
for people to use. 
Furthermore, be aware that whatever focus 
are proposed based on the results from case 
study part II and future studies, those 
changes have an implication on the other 
impact categories as no impact category is 
isolated from the rest. Thus, any proposed 
scenario needs to be modelled to avoid 
burden 
shifting 
across 
categories. 
Nevertheless, the definition of those 
changes, the damage and status implications 
of the new scenario are out of the scope of 
this study. 
 
4. 
Conclusions 
 
The 
study 
introduced 
a 
combined 
framework 
to 
simultaneously 
assess 
absolute sustainability and damage to bring 
the two approaches forward for decision-
makers to consider. The proposal works as 
a macro-level trend tool that allows to 
pinpoint the areas with high reduction 
requirements both in terms of exceedance 
and damage, and the results´ dissimilarity in 
the approaches. The framework fulfilled the 
established objectives, stated the relevance 
of the different analysis and evidentiated the 
prioritization difference across the two 
approaches. 
 
The framework allows to have an overall 
perspective status and to focus on those 
impacts 
of 
interest 
using 
multiple 
approaches. As shown, the framework is 
flexible and adapts to the user needs by 
adding or removing impact categories, 
setting sustainable boundaries in different 
terms (emission, concentration or damage), 
==PAGE:24==
 
19
and by incorporating different data sources 
according to available information. 
 
From the 18 impact categories considered 
for both AoP on the global consumption 
level, 11 need to be reduced for humanity to 
live within sustainability levels. Regarding 
the damage, four impact categories across 
the AoP represent at least 70% of the 
individual damage generated in each AoP. 
Specifically, the climate change detailed 
analysis results show that CO2 is the 
elementary flow to prioritize, that the 
consumption standard from citizens in the 
developed countries need to be reduced, and 
that by focusing on one third of the sectors, 
about 70% of the damage for both AoP can 
be reduced. 
 
As the prioritization based on exceedance 
level and on damage are different, the 
framework highlights both situations for 
governments and other stakeholders to 
consider 
in 
their 
decision-making 
processes, and that is where the value added 
of the framework lays. Building on the 
results from this study, more specific 
studies can be carried out on a national or 
sectoral level to identify concrete actions to 
tackle current impact levels (reduce damage 
or exceedance factor).
