Abstract
This thesis regards designing compliant mechanisms using topology optimization as a
synthesis tool. The mechanism is for a charging station for hearing aids, for the company
Oticon Denmark A/S. Compliant mechanisms and their main features, drawbacks, and
advantages is presented as well as introducing the main parts of the density distribution
approach for topology optimization. A code has been developed from the TopOpt 88-line
Matlab code for topology optimization and includes implementing the robust formulation.
A choice of material for the mechanism is made on the basis of availability, re-usability, and
production method. Here Polyoxymethylene (POM) was chosen as it excelled strength,
heat resistance, and fatigue resistance. Optimization was done in 2d, where it was split
in to three parts: A model working in the vertical direction where the goal is to reach a
deﬂection of 10mm, a model working in the vertical and horizontal direction where the
goal is 10mm and 2mm respectively, and ﬁnally, that same model with an additional load
case with the same goals.

1.1
The problem
In past few years battery charging technology has made huge leaps in development and
the ability to recharge batteries by inductive charging, or wireless charging, has become
a reality in many ways. For people with a need for hearing aids, see Figure 1.1a, this
technology can be an ease to the every day life where it would otherwise be necessary
to recharge these by wire or by replacing the battery.
To accommodate this Oticon
is developing a recharging station for hearing aids, in which a cartridge containing the
hearing aids is inserted into the device to charge. It is is done by two copper wire coils
on each side of the battery that need to be coaxial with them-selves and the battery. A
cross section of the charging station can be seen in Figure 1.1b where the copper wires are
visible. If the user needs to change their type of hearing aid to one of a diﬀerent size it
would be beneﬁcial if the coils were able to move to the correct position accordingly. To
do this a compliant mechanism (CM, see Section 1.2 for introduction on this) capable of
positioning the coils correctly has to be designed using topology optimization. It should
be a monolithic mechanism made of polymeric material and be able to be manufactured
by injection molding. The objectives of the mechanism are:
• Vertical deﬂection of +/ −5mm
• Horizontal deﬂection of +/ −1mm
• To be made of recyclable polymeric material
• To be produced by injection molding

1.2
Compliant mechanisms
A compliant mechanism is a type of mechanism that gains some or all of its mobility from
the ﬂexibility of its members. Conversely rigid body mechanisms use pins, hinges, and
bearings to gain mobility while the connection of these are rigid. If assuming minimal
friction in the moving parts it is safe to say that energy is conserved between input and
output here. However this also gives rise to more extensive assembly and manufacturing
and to general wear and tear between parts in contact.
Where rigid body mechanisms lack, compliant mechanisms excels. Their main advan-
tage is: minimized backlash since there is no part-in-part movement, ease of manufac-
turing and assembly due to a low part count, and built-in restoring force (Howell and
Midha 1994). This last point gives rise to the main disadvantage: input/output energy is
not conserved, as part of the input energy is stored as deformation energy in the ﬂexible
members. Further, since they undergo regular deformation they are also prone to fatigue.
An example of a compliant mechanism can be seen in Figure 1.2.
CMs are extensively used in micro electro-mechanical systems, which are sub-millimeter
mechanisms where a conventional rigid-body mechanism is impractical or impossible to
manufacture to scale in a reliable way. There exists a number of ways to synthesize com-
pliant mechanisms, for example by the method presented in (Howell and Midha 1994),
where a rigid body mechanism is converted to a pseudo-compliant mechanism, which is
analyzed and optimized, until the desired behavior is obtained and then converted to a
fully compliant mechanism. This thesis uses the topology optimization method for syn-
thesizing the CM.
Topology optimization (TO) is a method for ﬁnding the optimal distribution of mate-
rial within a given domain, minimizing some cost function and being subjected to some
constraints. It is an iterative process, where the current design is evaluated by numerical
methods, usually the ﬁnite element method (FEM) due to its high versatility, i.e. it is
easy to implement a multitude of physics.
TO has its origin from (Martin Philip Bendsøe and Kikuchi 1988) in which the ho-
mogenization approach was presented which uses concepts from composite materials to

This thesis uses the density distribution approach (Martin P Bendsøe 1989), (Zhou
and Rozvany 1991) for topology optimization which is a gradient based approach where
the variable is the density of each ﬁnite element. TO generally has the advantage that one
only has to know the general conditions of the problem e.g. load, boundary conditions, and
possibly area to be left with or without material. The TO program takes over from there
and ﬁnds the optimal structure with respect to the cost function, conditions, and possible
constraints. It has been used extensively as a synthesis tool for compliant mechanisms,
where it was ﬁrst introduced in (Ananthasuresh, Sridhar Kota, and Gianchandani 1994),
though the approach used in this thesis was introduced in (Sigmund 1997), where the input
and output deﬂection is controlled by an artiﬁcial spring inserted in the input/output
degrees of freedom (DOF).

2.2
Topology optimization
This section presents the concepts of topology optimization as it is used in the thesis. Ma-
terial interpolation, sensitivity analysis, ﬁltering and threshold projecting is introduced,
as well as the robust formulation.
2.2.1
Stiﬀness matrix and material interpolation law
The goal of a topology optimization scheme is to select which combination of solid elements
that maximizes/minimizes the given cost function. This formulation is a discretely deﬁned
problem, meaning the elements are either solid ’1’ or void ’0’. This is a diﬃcult formulation
to solve since each element is a variable to optimize. Instead the problem is relaxed so the
elements can take intermediate values making it a continuous problem where the stiﬀness
matrix is made dependent on the design variable ρ. This is done by means of the Solid
Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP) approach deﬁned as
Ei(¯ρ) = Emin + ¯ρp

2.2.2
Filtering
To avoid the well know problems in TO of checker boarding and mesh dependency the
design variable is ﬁltered.
Checker boarding is an artifact that stems from poor numerical modeling, where the
material is distributed in alternating solid and void pattern so the solid elements is only
connected by one node. Though the optimizer interprets this as optimal it is realistically
unfeasible from a manufacturing stand point, as well as generating huge stress concentra-
tions around these nodes.
Mesh dependency is the addition of ﬁner details to the optimized solution with a greater
mesh resolution. It stem from the non-existence of solution for numerical discretization of
topology optimization problems (Martin Philip Bendsøe and Sigmund 2004). The desired
eﬀect of greater mesh reﬁnement is instead the same as with any ﬁnite element modelling;
better physical representation of the problem.
To alleviate these problems one can use several techniques described in (Martin Philip
Bendsøe and Sigmund 2004). These are: adding additional constraints, directly limiting
the parameter space, or ﬁltering, The latter of which is used in this thesis. It is a tech-
nique where each element density is made dependent on the surrounding ones making it
impossible for the optimizer to make a checkerboard pattern.

2.3
Robust formulation
The robust formulation for topology optimization was introduced in (Sigmund 2009) and
extended in (Wang, Lazarov, and Sigmund 2011), and it is a method for producing opti-
mized topologies that are robust towards manufacturing error and with controllable min-
imum length-scale. This method resolves known problems such as one-node connected
hinges from compliant mechanisms and seen in (Sigmund 1997), and multi-physics prob-
lems where gray transition areas and small gaps poorly describe the underlying physics,
an example of which is electrostatic mechanisms as studied in (Qian and Sigmund 2013).
One-node connected hinges, and its related cousin, low density hinges, is presented in
Figure 2.4. It is a phenomenon where the optimizer exploits the checkerboard artifact to
produce hinges connected only by one node, as the name suggests, which can transmit
high forces, but oﬀer no bending resistance (Sigmund 2007), while the low density hinges
is what happens to the one-node hinge, when the density ﬁlter is applied. These are
both poor representation of physics, as well as extremely sensitive towards manufacturing
errors, where the mechanisms would clearly become disjointed if they for instance were
under-etched.

2.4
The method of moving asymptotes and optimiza-
tion ﬂow chart
The optimization method used here is the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) devel-
oped by Krister Svanberg (Svanberg 1987). It is a method for solving non-linear optimiza-
tion problems by generating strictly convex subproblems of the objective and constraint
functions, which in turn can be solved by some method, like a dual method (Martin Philip
Bendsøe and Sigmund 2004). The objective and constrains subproblems are bounded by
asymptotes that gives name to the method. It is gradient based and thus needs to be
fed the sensitivities as well as previous iteration history. It is highly capable of solving
structural optimization problems with a large number of variables and constraints.
A ﬂowchart of the topology optimization algorithm is as follows:

material distribution of ¯ρ.
2. while |ρi−1 −ρi| > 0.01
• Update loop counter: loop = loop + 1
• Perform ﬁnite element analysis
• Determine f(¯ρ)e,i,d and gi and their respective sensitivities
• Filter sensitivities according to the chain rule
• update design variables using MMA

Multi dof compliant mechanisms in topology op-

The idea of using TO to obtain multi-input/multi-output compliant mechanisms is not
new, examples can be found in: (Larsen, Signund, and Bouwsta 1997; B. Z. ˜Q. C. ˜M. J. ˜X.
Zhang 2018; Benliang Zhu & Qi Chen 2019), as well as (Zhan and X. Zhang 2010) with
the ground structure approach. All of these examples present mechanisms that take one
or more inputs and can move in the plane, i.e. an x-y compliant mechanism. In (Sigmund
2001a; Sigmund 2001b) the same was found, though the actuation was produced by
thermal expansion induced by an electrical current as opposed to a input force. These
same papers also produced thermal actuators with three outputs; a gripper that can
move in the plane as well.
(Alonso, Ansola, and Querin 2014) produced mechanisms
with separate coaxial outputs for several diﬀerent inputs. The compliant parallel grippers
shown in (Sigmund 1997) are also similar to the problem in this thesis as it takes an input
and moves a platform with two output dofs. In (Frecker, Kikuchi, and Kota 1999; Saxena
2005) mechanisms with a single input and multiple outputs were presented for truss
structures. Clearly then, using topology optimization to obtain compliant mechanisms

This chapter serves as a precursor for the results of the 2d optimization. In here the

3.1
Positioning of the mechanism in the charging sta-
tion
An overview of the dimensions for the domain can be seen in Figure 3.1a, as well as a
picture of the domain in the charging station in Figure 3.1b. The ellipsoid indicates where
the mechanism should go, while the red arrow roughly indicates the point of actuation.
The white area on the left is the carrier of the coil, where the mechanisms should be
attached. Figure 3.1c shows a diagram of how the mechanism is actuated. The cartridge
containing the hearing aid is inserted into the charging station (1), which forces the coil
carriers to move back, giving rise to the actuation (2). A premise for the mechanism is
an alteration to the chassis of the charging station, so the mechanisms can be actuated
from the chosen position. Besides this, great consideration should be made towards how
the mechanism attaches to the coil. As it is presently, the coil carriers also have to be
modiﬁed with an extension so it is in contact with the mechanism. An alternative method
could be to do it magnetically instead so the mechanism carries a magnet that moves the
coil, though great care has to taken with this method, so the magnet does not interfere
with the inducted current for the battery. A position that was considered was inside the
carrier for the coil. This would make the actual actuation easier, as the coil would be
directly above the mechanism. However space limitation and method of production makes
this a diﬃcult position, thus leading to the chosen position. In principle the mechanism
could be used inside the coil carrier where it would simply be ﬂipped and the actuation
could come from an indent in the wall. Though the carrier would have to be modiﬁed
and the mechanism would likely be retroﬁtted.
Currently the chosen position is also occupied by a spring that re-positions the coil
carrier when the hearing aid is pulled out. However, as mentioned in Section 1.2 a nice
property of compliant mechanisms are the built-in restoring force, meaning the mecha-
nisms can replace the spring if this restoring force is high enough.

Chapter 4
Results
This chapter presents results for the choice of material, and the results of the optimization
in 2d. The optimization is done in stages, so a model working exclusively in the vertical
direction is found to start, then a model with a DOF added in the horizontal direction is
optimized, and ﬁnally this model is introduced with two load cases is optimized.
4.1
Material Choice
As presented in Section 1.1, a choice of material should be made for the mechanisms,
which have to be able to be recyclable, as well as be able to be injection molded. At
Oticon there is the possibility of using several types of polymers that have been recycled
by being ground up and reused. The materials are: Polyamide (Pa), Polypropylen (PP),
and Polyoxymethylen (POM). Any of these materials would be appropriate for the mech-
anisms, though POM excels due to its high mechanical abilities, namely both stiﬀness
and hardness, easy manufacturing, and temperature resistance, which could very well be
necessary since the inductive charging produce heat (Cardarelli 2018, p724). Besides this
it has good fatigue resistance making it the best candidate for compliant mechanisms.
The material properties are provided by Oticon as: E = 2GPa and ν = 0.4. For the
optimization scheme Young’s modulus is kept at E = 1, though this should not aﬀect the
usability of the mechanisms since linearity is assumed, though Poisson’s ratio is kept at
ν = 0.4.
4.2
Optimization of more advanced cases
With the conﬁrmation of multi input/multi output mechanisms being possible to obtain
using the code as shown in Figure 2.8, a more complex model resembling what could be
used in the charging station is investigated. This is done in several stages. To begin with
the most simple case where a single input force is applied to the mechanism with two
output DOFs in the vertical direction. Afterwards, as the mechanism should also be able
to move horizontally, an output DOF is added in this direction. Finally, several load cases
are applied to simulate hearing aids of diﬀerent sizes being inserted.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The model to be optimized, as described in Section 3.1, was chosen over a model
residing in the coil carries, primarily due to the limit space with in it. As mentioned the
10mm is a large deformation for the small mechanism, so it was believed that it needed
as much space as possible. Thus the most spacious location was chosen. This location of
course brings the added problem of attaching the mechanism to the pin that the coil is
wrapped about, but it was thought possible, or an alternative method like magnets could
be used. This is still believed to be possible, however the alteration for the chassis could
be extended so the lower part of the wall was removed as well and the actuation point
was moved downward with it. From the optimization it is clear that this space is unused
so it would not interfere with current results. This would yield greater distance from the
support to the force, which could help achieve greater output magnitudes.
It should also be said that the author has some doubt to whether the 10mm are even
necessary as the total height of the coil carrier are 15mm and the front face where the
coil is inserted into is 10mm. Though this could simply mean that a redesign of the coil
carries should be considered if the 10mm deﬂection is strictly necessary. If this is so, it
would be much easier to implement a way to mount the compliant mechanism and achieve
the desired output as mechanism and carrier could be designed in tandem.
Choice of material
Originally, the choice of material was thought more comprehensive
than it showed to be. This was reduced i great part due to the availability of recycled
polymers at Oticon. The mechanism has to be made of polymeric material as this ensures
that it can be produced in-house. This gives the excellent possibility to both have the
raw material, and the production in house.
General remarks about the current code
The biggest drawback in the currently
designed 2D optimized structures is the lack of inclusion of non-linear ﬁnite element anal-
ysis. As it is, the output deﬂection is very large, which can easily lead to highly deformed
elements, as well as the assumption that equilibrium is maintained being violated, leading
to the stiﬀness matrix becoming dependent on the deﬂections. Non-linear mechanisms
have been used extensively in topology optimization (Pedersen, Buhl, and Sigmund 2001),
where it was used for large deﬂection force inverters, as well as implementing path gen-
eration, where it was demonstrated that a mechanism could be obtained so it followed a
speciﬁc path. A more recent paper (Silva, Beck, and Sigmund 2020) included both the
robust formulation, geometric non-linearity, path-generation, as well as it being stress
constrained (See (De Leon et al. 2015)). (Silva, Beck, and Sigmund 2020) also showed
an interesting feature, where the path generation of the mechanisms is devised in such
a manner that a speciﬁc force yielded a speciﬁc output, meaning it did not necessarily
need to be a linear path. This is especially interesting for the given case where it could
be implemented in such a way that the force introduced by inputting a speciﬁc hearing
aid is tailored to a speciﬁc output deﬂection.
29
==PAGE:42==
Input deﬂection
As is stated in Section 1.2 input and output energy is not conserved
for compliant mechanisms. This means that the input deﬂection is most likely higher than
the output deﬂection. When observing the Figures 4.1, 4.5, and 4.6, it is clearly seen that
the only support for the mechanism is right next to the point of actuation. With an input
deﬂection > 10mm this would give rise to the material yielding. This would of course be
unacceptable as this essentially means that the mechanism can only be used once, if even
then, as the material would behave non-linearly. A redesign of the domain as mentioned
previously could help alleviate this problem since the point of actuation and the support
would be far apart.

Discussing the obtained results
The results from the vertical model
The intermediate results of the vertical model
does indeed present deﬂections that is of the desired magnitude. However as is clear,
the diﬀerence in the deﬂection between the two output ports is of a size that should
be considered. Though some attempt was made to alleviate this problem it was only
partly successful as the average diﬀerence went from being 31.17% to 15.9%. However
this average hides the fact that the constraint yielded greater diﬀerence of the output
ports in the intermediate design but lower in the eroded and dilated.
Most, if not all, of the diﬀerence in two output ports should be alleviated for the ﬁnal

The horizontal model error
The greatest obvious drawback of the code is the error
introduced by the third DOF that was added in Section 4.2.2. As mentioned there, the

even is a bug. The low values was found to be stemming from void elements in the model.
Since the deformation is very large compared to the size of the structure, it could be that

model in Table 4.3 is of a similar size as the vertical model in Table 4.1.

The second coil
So far the results only concern one coil. This is due to the fact that
the 2d model is only a conceptual model which should serve as a precursor to the 3d
model. This should of course also be modeled since co-axiality is of great importance for
the battery charging to work.
The second coil is the diﬃcult of the two to model. Unlike the coil that is modeled it
does not have the advantage of extra space behind it so it would have to reside in the coil
carrier, which is the only place with the space for it. This then brings back the discussion
of the diﬃculty of alternating the coil carriers and the lack of space there in.
The method of actuation should also be considered greatly. The ﬁrst coil uses the
backward motion of the coil carries as the method of actuation, but the other carrier is
stationary so the actuation has to come from another method. The best solution here is
in all likelihood the cartridge being inserted, which can give vertical actuation. It would
be more diﬃcult to reach the targeted output, but a great advantage would be for the
ability to use the same mechanism in the two carriers.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to design compliant mechanisms using topology optimiza-
tion, for use in a charging station for hearing aids. To start, a code for obtaining compliant
mechanisms was developed from TopOpts 88-line Matlab code for compliance optimiza-
tion, which was done by following the ﬁrst half of the course ’Topology Optimization:
Theory, Methods and Applications’. The code was validated by a problem from the lit-
erature, and was found to be accurate. A conceptual problem of a double force inverter
was introduced and optimized using the code, to verify that it was possible to ﬁnd mech-
anisms with multiple inputs and multiple outputs. The mechanism was indeed capable
of producing a mechanism that had this.
A parameter study for the 2d models was made. A volume fraction of V ∗= 0.07 was
chosen, and input and output spring stiﬀnesses was: kin = 1·10−4, kout[1,2] = 1·10−4. The
spring stiﬀness for the third DOF was kout,3 = 1 · 10−4. The addition of the third output
lowered the deﬂection of the ﬁrst two, so kout[1,2] was lowered to 1 · 10−5. A constraint
to reduce the diﬀerence in output deﬂection between the output port one and two was
implemented though to minor success, and an estimation for the length scale was made.
The choice of material for the mechanism was investigated. This was based on poly-
mers that is possible to be recycled at Oticon, where the mechanism is also to be produced.
All the available materials was a viable option. The options are polyamide (Pa), poly-
oxymethylene (POM), or polypropylene (PP). Here POM excelled due to its high strength,
heat resistance, and fatigue tolerance.
The optimization of the 2d model, resembling what could be used in the charging
station, was done in three parts: ﬁrstly a model working in the vertical direction was
found, with two outputs, where the outputs of a model with, and with out an equality
constraint was found. Over all the constrained model had the best results. A target of
10mm vertical deﬂection was set, and for the horizontal model a deﬂection of −2mm in
this direction was set.
For the results of the vertical mechanism only the intermediate realization reached the
desired output deﬂection, and there only one of the output ports did that. The model
tested without the equality constraint had less discrepancy in the intermediate design,
but much greater in the eroded and dilated ones, so the constraint was kept.
The horizontal model with the spring stiﬀnesses altered for the ﬁrst two outputs ex-
ceeded the 10mm, one of them greatly, but the other realizations did not. The output in
the horizontal direction reached magnitudes twice the wanted −2mm in the intermediate
and dilated design but not in the eroded.
The horizontal model for two load cases yielded ﬁne results, greatly resembling the
ones found with one load case. This was believed to be do to the greater of the forced
dominating the sensitivities.

6.1
Future work
There is a number of points to ﬁnish for the mechanism. Firstly, it should be investigated
if geometrically non-linear models should be implemented in the optimization code, as
the relative deﬂections is very high. Further, the deﬂection of the ﬁrst two output ports
should be leveled by some method, possibly some constraint.
From there a mechanism in 3d should be developed, as it is indeed a 3d problem.
Beyond this there are several interesting subjects that could be investigated along the
given ones.
Since the wireless charging produce heat, it could be interesting to implement multi-
physics ﬁnite elements to investigate the eﬀects that this has on the mechanism.
As mentioned in Chapter 3, the mechanism sits in a position which is currently occu-
pied by a spring. It is possible for the mechanism to replace the spring, if the restoring
force is high enough, though if it is not it could be interesting to combine the complaint
mechanism optimization with compliance optimization so it is ensured that the mecha-
nism serves the purpose of the spring. This could for instance be done by constraining the
compliance of the mechanism. This is though a diﬃcult problem as compliant mechanisms
optimization and compliance minimization is inherently opposite problems.
As the mechanism can be under load for longer periods of time, creep should be
investigated as this could be an issue.
