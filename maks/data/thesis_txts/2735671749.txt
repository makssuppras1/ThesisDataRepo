Abstract
Using unmanned vehicles as a primary sensor platform for site surveying and monitoring
rivers and streams has gained increasing prominence across various scientific and civil
applications, with implications for environmental monitoring and resource management.
This thesis addresses the unexploited potential of small high-frequency MIMO radars for
mapping. This thesis proposes a novel method combining a Texas Instrument 77 GHz
MIMO radar with an industrial-grade DJI M600 Pro to map and detect the river eleva-
tion and river banks accurately. The approach employs binary phase modulation (BPM)
with a radar system consisting of two transmitters and four receivers to create a synthetic
array of eight receivers for uniform linear array signal processing.
The application of
standard direction-of-arrival techniques in conjunction with the drone platform is demon-
strated to achieve accurate stream bank detection. Furthermore, leveraging drone position
and attitude data, we transform radar image pixels into earth-centred and geodetic coordi-
nates. The findings demonstrate significant stream mapping capabilities and the proposed
method’s effectiveness in detecting stream banks. These results underscore the potential
of radar imaging techniques beyond a mere altimeter. Future research directions include
optimising radar imaging techniques for diverse environmental conditions, better data
synchronisation between the drone platform and radar sensor, and new experiments with
optimised radar parameters and RTK implementation to achieve better position accuracy.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The radar’s technological journey began in 1886 when German physicist Heinrich Hertz
demonstrated how radio waves reflected off solid objects. At the beginning of the 19th
century, several patents and inventions spurred the progenitors of the modern radar that
could detect objects at a distance. Before and during World War 2, radar technology be-
came a crucial and standard technology all modern military forces needed since its ability
to detect the range and bearing of far-off objects was second to none. The capabilities
of the radar are further expanded by modulating the frequencies of transmitted waves;
this modulation gave measurements not only in range but also in the velocity of what-
ever the radar was looking at. In newer times, the application of high-frequency bands
is becoming the preferred frequency spectrum in the communications industry, mainly
because high frequency means a smaller form factor in antenna design, and it is easier to
obtain high bandwidth. The 5G generation operates at 24.25 GHz to 52.6 GHz and has a
wavelength in mm scale, rightfully naming the band between 30–300 GHz: the mm band,
EHF (Extremely high frequency) or M-band (NATO).
The newer generation of vehicles incorporates sensors in a larger sense for developing
”smart cars”. The sensors are cameras, GPS, and radars to scan and sense the environment
around the car.
The sensor data are then fed to algorithms for autonomous driving,
collision avoidance by object detection, and range and velocity determination. The mm-
wave radar was a perfect vehicle choice because of its high reflection and small form
factors.
Mm-wave type radars are primarily designed as Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave
(FMCW) radar, where the range and velocity information of a target lies in the beat
frequency [1, 2]. A one- or two-dimensional array of receivers can be constructed using
multiple receivers and transmitters. Different spatial signal techniques can be applied to
obtain signals from specific angles. Other examples where mm radars have been used are
human motion behaviour detection [3], vital signs monitoring [4], SAR application [5] and
altimetry [6].
Denmark’s streams and rivers are vital freshwater sources, crucial to the nation’s infras-
tructure. They have garnered public attention due to their susceptibility to agricultural
pollution [7, 8]. Beyond their role in supplying freshwater, these waterways provide habi-
tats for various aquatic life. The ecological balance is delicate and requires diligent mon-
itoring to ensure drinking water quality and biodiversity. This fulfils the sixth, 13th and
14th sustainable development goals.
Aerial mapping has been conventionally measured using photogrammetry and LiDAR for
area measurement [9]. Radar altimetry for water level measurement has also been a subject
of interest [10]. The use of MIMO radar technology to actively map the water level and
the surrounding stream- and river banks is an endeavour not yet to be explored.

Project Objective
This thesis will explore three main areas
• Theoretical consideration of a mm-wave MIMO radar capabilities
• Integration and implementation of a radar-drone configuration
• Mapping capabilities
The first part delves into the theoretical foundation of radar technology, emphasising the
workings of the radar system, signal processing techniques, and sensing limitations. It then

photogrammetry, which can generate depth maps and a stitched image of the complete

and statistically.

Pulse Radar
The pulse radar is the simplest and most widely used radar type worldwide. The radar
system consists of a transmitter and receiver, where the transmitter sends a pulse in a pre-
determined direction. The pulse travels with the speed of light until it hits an object that
reflects a portion of the wave back to the receiver. When the reflected wave gets recorded
by the receiver, the range of the object can be determined from the time the transmitted
pulse was sent out till it was received and is the principle of radar measurements [11], see
eq. (2.1).

5.2
Radar Drone Configuration
Correct synchronisation between the drone position data and radar data is crucial to the
georeference of the radar result on a map. The drone is connected to a GPS receiver,
while the radar time information is determined from the chirps eq. (3.11) and frame
configurations from mm-wave studios. Elapsed time from start measurement is: Nframe ·
Tframe, and then a timestamp can be estimated when the start time is known. In this
manner, the Frames are then synchronised, to the best extent, to the drone position data.
The data from the drone is outputted at 10 Hz while the radar operates at 1/Tframe Hz,
which is usually a higher value. The drone data is interpolated between points to match
the number of frames for synchronisation. The Intel NUC clock operates in UTC. As of
today, there is a time difference of 18 seconds between UTC and GNSS time. This is
because UTC has added leap seconds, but GNSS does not. The leap seconds are added
so that the UTC syncs with Earth’s rotations [32].

instructions and save data via the mm-Wave studio. Power management was complex
since the radar, PC, and Gimbal required different voltages and amperes. The system

Many of the pixels in the radar images are either noise or artefacts from the spectral
estimators, i.e. sidelobes. The Constant-False-Alarm-Rate (CFAR) algorithm is applied
Development of MIMO Radar System for Drone Mapping Application

to extract significant image pixels. The CFAR algorithm is a statistical test that compares
a pixel or cell’s noise level with surrounding cells. This is coined as the ”cell under test”
or CUT. Most of the time, cells are surrounded by cells with equal or similar values. Cells
lagging and leading from the CUT are called guard cells and will be ignored in the test.
The cells that are tested against are the training cells and are the cells leading and lagging
from CUT and guard cells. See fig. 5.3 for example. There will be no thorough description
of the CFAR algorithm, but information can be found from [34].

5.6
Photogrammetry
A precise landscape model was created using photogrammetry methods. The model is
meant to be used as a high-resolution image of the scene to compare with the radar
data. It was also made to create a Digital Elevation Model (DEM). The DEM will be
used to compare height estimations of the radar point cloud. Another drone was used
for the photogrammetry operations. The drone used was the DJI Phantom 4 and had an
inbuilt Real-Time Kinematic Positioning (RTK) module, achieving cm-level positioning
data. The drone flies in a grid-like pattern above the area and takes images. The capturing
rate is scheduled so images have areas that overlap each other. This is important for the
underlying algorithm to generate the Orthomosaic and DEM, and the overlap percentage
increases precision, data size and processing time. The images have embedded geodetic
metadata, which is then input into the Agisoft Metashape Pro software, which applies
standard photogrammetry methods to create a DEM and an Orthomosaic image. The
underlying algorithm in Agisoft software is not known to the public for competitive reasons,

The first step is to load all the images into the software and filter unnecessary photos.
The software then aligns the images. This is most likely done by finding tie points in the

creation of a dense point cloud of the scene is made. Then follows a process of creating
a polygon mesh of the points, applying texture in the polygons and from here to build
the DEM. The Orthomosaic is then made by projecting the images onto the DEM; this

orthomosaic is a single large image of the area formed by stitching the images together.

the MVDR method creates more localised areas. The traditional Bartlett beamforming
was calculated using velocity-filtered data, which was done by setting all values outside

again to do the DOA estimation. This was done so the estimation would not consider

The MUSIC algorithm fails. The explanation might result from many correlated signals in
the scene, which can make DOA estimation fail [37]. It could also be that the number of
targets exceeds the number of arrays, which would give the correlation matrix rank( ¯R) =
NV x. Then, the noise subspace will not exist to take advantage of [17, p.159]. See figs. A.8
and A.9 for MUSIC estimation using 5 and 1 noise eigenvectors.
The CFAR algorithm was applied to the MVDR image using a 7x7 guard cell grid, and a
5x5 training cell was used. The size choice comes from the fact that there are large clutter
areas that we wish to classify as target pixels, and this configuration gave the best results.
The search spectrum is also defined and was chosen from -44 to 44 degrees, ranging from
9 to 18 meters. The searching width corresponds closely to the 3 dB beam directivity but
was also selected because, looking at the many (R,θ) - maps, it was noted that the object
did not occur outside this range. The search spectrum for the range was chosen with the
same considerations. The false alarm probability was selected from 10e-7. This choice
comes from trial and error. This value would not overfit or underfit the pixel detections.
It can be seen from fig. 6.5 that the CFAR finds and classifies pixels from the water and
the two clutter areas to the left and right.
The MVDR takes much computation time compared to the Bartlett beamformer, so much
that a single raw .bin could take up to 20-30 minutes to generate the (R, θ) estimations.

6.1.5
Nadir Summary
The (R, vr) - maps strongly aligned with the dynamical considerations before the flight.
Since much of the velocity information was noise, the PRF could be lowered in future
flights.
The (R, θ) - maps showed great results where the surface reflection was easily distin-
guished. MVDR proved to be the best DOA estimator, while the MUSIC showed very
poor results. For future flights, only MVDR will be used. The CFAR algorithm did not de-

detected sidelobes from strong scatterers.
The mapping results are very similar to the visual observation from the photogrammetry
drone. The generated point cloud showcases more dispersed points in areas where the
reeds are in the water, and in the regions that show clear terrain, the point cloud is more
concentrated. The georeferenced radar pixels have a time and a spatial offset, which could
come from unsatisfactory time synchronisation or low position accuracy from the M600
drone.

Chapter 7
Perspective & Discussion
This chapter discusses and provides perspective on the methods and results. Emphasis is
placed on optimising the methods, and a list of error sources is listed and explained.
The main objective of this thesis was to explore the mapping possibilities of Texas In-
strument’s IWR1843 MIMO radar and how, in tandem with a drone, it could provide a
different alternative from traditional mapping sensors, i.e., LiDAR and photogrammetry.
Using DOA techniques coupled with the drone position, velocity, and attitude, a novel
way of detecting the brinks of a water stream was achieved with relatively great success!
The resulting point clouds and georeferenced radar points agreed well with the terrain
and curvature of the stream. The banks were influenced heavily by tall reeds, and the soil
distinguished the banks in some places.
Since the MUSIC algorithm failed, the MVDR technique was the primary DOA technique.
The problem was thought to come from the number of targets exceeding the number of
receiver elements and highly correlated signals[38]. There exist methods to overcome this,
ex., by use of spatial smoothing or improved MUSIC algorithms [29, 39].
Sidelobes from high-intensity scatterers - water surface and reflectors - were inadvertently
classified in the CFAR algorithm. This was seen in radar point (R, θ) - maps. Implement-
ing either point cloud segmentation techniques or filtering by amplitude removed these
artefacts.
The CFAR algorithm proved exceptionally helpful in extracting the correct
pixels. This technique was not extensively researched and was mainly based on trial and
error. The pixel extraction can be optimised for future use by investigating the method
further.
During post-processing, several error sources create high uncertainty in the georeferencing
scheme. The M600 drone possessed relatively poor GNSS capabilities, evident from the
position biasses. An unsatisfactory time synchronisation between the radar and the drone
created a complex matching that was only partially solved by trial and error with manual
position and time offsets. The perfect solution would be to use a Precision Time Protocol
(PTP) to synchronise all the sensors and vehicles.
Another source of errors was the gimbal on the drone. It was observed that when the
drone changed its yaw/heading, the gimbal did not turn with it, but there was a slight
delay. This is critical since the drone’s yaw is essential for correctly georeferencing. If only
the nadir scheme had been used, the gimbal would not have been necessary. Instead, a
stiff platform on the drone would be best. This way, the drone’s yaw, pitch and roll values
can be used to estimate radar orientation better. A separate IMU could be placed on the
radar to measure the radar’s attitude directly.
The point cloud analysis showed that the point cloud from the banks showed consistency
throughout the flight and was close to a normal distribution.
The height differences
showed bimodal distribution for both banks; the left bank showed higher bimodality than
Development of MIMO Radar System for Drone Mapping Application

==PAGE:92==
the right. The errors mentioned above would add to the high biases, but there was also
the problem of the bank heights measuring the soil rather than vegetation. This would
add a constant bias since the DEM do measure the reed heights. This leads to the next
problem. The interaction of the radar with the environment surrounding the water stream
is unclear. This is where experiments exploring relationships would be appropriate. A way
to test this would be to place reflectors along stream banks. The location of the reflectors
would be known, and relating this to the range and angle estimation would provide better
knowledge about the sensing of the environment.
When the georeferenced pixels were plotted on the map, it was clear that some pixels
represented objects further ahead or behind the radar.
This was especially noted on
the bridge part figs. 6.19 and A.17. This ambiguity presents wrong estimations and will
create the wrong estimation when the pixels are georeferenced. The IWR1843 did possess
a third transmitter, which would have developed capabilities for making DOA estimations
in elevation. In hindsight, it was wrong not to utilise this capability, as this would have
eliminated the ambiguities.
The radar configuration in the mm-wave studio could have been set more effectively to
better accommodate the height and velocity. The nadir measurement in the second flight
had more inactive time where no pulses were sent from the radar. This should have been
changed so the number of chirps corresponded better with the frame time. Increasing the
number of chirps would then increase the accuracy of the DOA estimation scheme. From
tables 5.3 and 5.4, the system range resolution was higher than the radar range resolution.
This could have been adjusted by raising the number of fast-time samples to accommodate
this. The large tunable radar parameter matrix should be optimised for future use.
The usage of the radar can be extended further than mapping purposes. The motivation
for the thesis was to research the capabilities of using the IWR1843 radar to keep the
drone along the water stream’s central axis. From the results, this could be possible. By
applying the techniques used in real-time for every frame, it would be likely to determine
the drone’s position relative to the stream and feed that data into the flight computer for
automatic corrections. Further techniques should be applied to the data, such as creating
a classification of pixels. A possible solution could be to apply Deep Learning (DL) or
Machine Learning (ML) algorithm to either the (R, vr) - mapping, (R, θ) - mapping or
even the 3D point clouds to extract the correct points. Using DL in radar images and
point clouds for classification has been used in many cases [40, 41].

Chapter 8
Conclusion
From theory and implementation to the results, this thesis has shown a novel method for
extracting water stream banks using the industrial radar IWR1843 produced by Texas
Instruments. It was possible to create an orthomosaic image for visual comparison and
generate a DEM for height comparisons using aerial data gathered from a photogramme-
try drone in situ. Using the position metadata from a DJI M600 Pro, a frame conversion
from radar frame to ECEF frame and geodetic coordinates was possible. The georefer-
enced radar point cloud matched the visual structure of the stream from the orthomosaic
image exceptionally well. Artefacts such as the bridge, reflector, and tree branches were
distinguishable in the point cloud in terms of both height and amplitude values. Point
cloud segmentation algorithms helped separate the banks from the water but failed when
there was too much overlap. Using amplitude values as a filtering tool, it was possible to
almost identify and separate the water from the banks.
The georeferencing was prone to many error sources, namely poor time synchronisation,
high GNSS uncertainty for the M600 drone and poor attitude determination of the radar
platform. Using a PTP, RTK, and a more stable platform with an IMU for the radar
would reduce these errors.
The height differences between the radar points and the DEM showed bimodality in dis-
tribution, which is thought to stem from - but not exclusively - the georeferencing errors.
It was also demonstrated that the bank heights were very similar to the water heights, and
this could mean the radar mainly measured the soil and not the reed vegetation. Since
the DEM did measure the reed vegetation heights, this would lead to the high biasses.

Chapter 9
Future Work
The project showed an exciting use of the MIMO radar. There are many prospects on
which this technology could be applied or improved upon.
The IWR1843 relied on the mm-wave software to set up the measurement scheme. The
software provides many tuning parameters for chirps, frames, and sampling. The huge
tunable parameter matrix in mm-wave studio can be optimised to suit the drone’s height
and velocity better. For example, it was seen in the (R, vr) - maps (figs. 6.1 and 6.18)
that the data was relatively sparse in the velocity spectrum. Thoroughly optimising all
the parameters to suit the situation could create higher-quality data.
This thesis was built on a request from DTU Sustain for precise navigation along a river or
stream, and this exciting problem is still open to being solved. The proposed methods from
the thesis data gathered could be applied to different control algorithms to manoeuvre the
drone correctly. Using sensors to guide vehicles is not new and has been implemented by
various sensors and algorithms [42, 43, 44].
An exciting improvement would be incorporating cameras with radar for precise classifi-
cation and target recognition. The radar sensor is excellent for correctly detecting targets
and their location. The area scan camera provides much visual information about the
environment. It provides information about shape, colour, and texture, which in turn
can be used in many different ways to identify and correctly classify objects in an im-
age using ML or DL [45, 46]. Radar can provide initial detection and tracking of objects,
while cameras can provide detailed visual information for precise classification. This could
help filter out wrongly detected pixels in the radar and give more details for surveying
purposes. Regarding navigation, the use of radar and image processing is widely used in
the automotive industry and has also been proven to be an effective system for vehicle
guidance [47, 48].

A.1
Data Capturing with mm-Wave studio
Before capturing data, several radar parameters must be programmed. The IWR1843 is
advertised as having a low, medium, and long-range configuration [49]. In this context, the
short-range would be 5–10 meters, and the long range would be 100–150 meters. From
eq. (3.21), it can be seen that the max range can be extended by increasing sampling
frequency fs, and vice versa, it can be reduced by increasing the linear modulation slope
S. Ti, which stands for the intermediate period, is the time between pulses. The time
pulse length or the time pulse is ”ramping” (increases frequency), which is called Tr and
is called the ramping time. For a MIMO radar, this means the Pulse Repetition Period
(PRP) is then:
PRP = NTxTr + NTxTi =⇒PRF =
1
PRP
(A.1)
As mentioned, the measurements are divided into frames, and each frame period is also
predetermined in the radar configuration step in the mm-Wave studio.
Setting up the radar and the DCA1000 with mm-Wave studios will not be shown here but
at [50]. A short overview of the GUI and the relevant parameters that should be changed
will be made. In the sensor configuration tab, see fig. A.1, to set the PRF, the ramp time
and intermediate time values are changed accordingly, and the PRF is as eq. (A.1). Chirp
IDs are set in the ”Chirp” segment to set the MIMO mode. For TDM, check only the box
”Tx0” and set the ”Start Chirp for Cfg” and ”End Chirp for Cfg” to 0. Press ”set”;
next, check only ”Tx2” and set ”Start Chirp for Cfg” and ”End Chirp for Cfg” to 1.
This creates two chirps from Tx0 or Tx2. In the frame segment, set the ”Start Chirp
TX” to 0 and the ”End Chirp TX” to 1. The number of frames can be set from the
”No. of Frames” option. If set to 0, the system will keep measuring infinitely until the
”Stop Frame” button is pushed. ”No. of chirp loops” are not the number of chirps
but the number of times the start and end chirp will be executed for each frame. When
everything has been set, press the ”DCA1000 ARM” and the ”Trigger Frame” to
begin data collection. The data will be stored in a .bin file and can be read in Python,
Matlab, or other software. If the ”No of Frames” was set to 0, then a .bin file with
suﬀix _0 will be made, which will have a max storage capacity of around 1 GB. It will
subsequently create more .bin files and increment the suﬀix for each new file created.
