Abstract
Bound States in the Continuum (BICs) and Superscattering phenomena, enabled by elec-
tromagnetic Metasurface (MS) cavities, are explored both analytically and numerically. A
comprehensive methodology to describe these effects emerging from MS cavities char-
acterized by surface electric and magnetic responses is presented, examining planar,
cylindrical, and spherical geometries. Exact conditions are established for cavities of any
size. The analytical results are confirmed through numerical simulations conducted with
COMSOL Multiphysics. The simulations involved ideal MSs defined through the under-
lying boundary problem, as well as homogenized MS models used for validation at both
optical and microwave frequencies.
The findings of this work could be applicable to a wide range of practical applications, such
as lasing, sensing, filters, antennas, nonlinear systems, bioimaging, and optoelectronics.

Introduction
Metamaterials and their two-dimensional counterparts, metasurfaces (MSs), are artificial
structures that enable the engineering of unconventional behaviors of the electromagnetic
waves. For this reason, they have received immense attention from the research com-
munity in the recent decades. Among all the interesting and useful effects they enables,
there are also some exotic phenomena. It is the case of Bound States in the Continuumn
(BICs) [1], standing waves which remain trapped in open metastructures while all the sur-
rounding energy is radiated away by propagating waves. Superscattering is another class
of these exotic phenomena: the energy of an incoming wave is captured and redirected by
a metastructure in a manner that significantly enhances its measurable quantities, such
as the scattering cross-section [2].
BICs have been widely studied in both the optical and microwave regimes, and more re-
cently, superscattering has also attracted considerable attention from researchers. How-
ever, many unexplored areas remain for both phenomena, and investigating them could
enhance our understanding of the fundamental physics involved while offering new, valu-
able tools for addressing future engineering challenges.
This work continues the exploration of BICs and superscattering, investigating MS cavities
characterized by both electric and magnetic responses. Planar, cylindrical, and spheri-
cal cavities characterized by an electric surface impedance Ze and a magnetic surface
impedance Zm are analytically investigated. A rigorous methodology to derive exact solu-
tions for Ze and Zm, enabling the identification of passive MS cavities capable of sustaining
BICs and active gain MS cavities that exhibit superscattering with infinite cross sections
is introduced. It is remarkable that, as seen in [3], combining both electric and magnetic
responses in the same MS enables the determination of exact conditions for any cavity
size. To validate the proposed methodology, numerical simulations are performed with
COMSOL Multiphysics. For passive MS cavities, the simulations involve realistic MS de-
signs based on well-established models, such as the particle model [4], an all-dielectric
MS model previously applied in [3] for optical purposes, and a bi-layer Huygens MS used
for the demonstration at microwave frequencies. For active gain MS, numerical simula-
tions employing ideal MSs are presented to validate the analytical results, and a potential
pathway for implementation using physical MSs is proposed. Much attention is placed on
the discussed phenomena in subwavelegth and deeply subwavelegth cavities.
This report is divided into 6 chapters. In Chapter 2 MSs, BICs and superscattering are
introduced along with some of the recent studies in the corresponding fields. Chapter
3 addresses the analytical characterization of the MS cavities in planar, cylindrical and
spherical configurations characterized by electric and magnetic responses presenting the
exact solutions to realize BICs and superscattering. Chapter 4 describes the MS mod-
els along with some design practices to implement the discussed cavities, and Chapter
5 presents the numerical results. Eventually, Chapter 6 summarizes topics and findings
of the present study. Appendix A provides some important definitions and derivations
related to Bessel and Legendre functions, which are necessary to support the analytical
characterization presented in Chapter 3. Importantly, this study is a continuation of a
project course and has led to a journal paper [3] and a conference paper. Both publica-
tions are included in Appendix B.
This work takes the reader on a journey through the realm of MS cavities, exploring some
of the exotic wave effects that are achievable through their precise engineering. It intro-
duces simple yet powerful analytical tools and showcases effective designs that validate

2
Exotic Wave Phenomena: Bound States
in the Continuum and Superscattering
Electromagnetic waves in precisely engineered artificial structures can exhibit unconven-
tional behaviors, resulting in exotic wave phenomena. This chapter presents the concept
of MS and explores some of the fascinating wave phenomena enabled by their proper
application. Specifically, it focuses on Bound States in the Continuum (BICs) and super-
scattering, presenting some of the studies in these areas.
2.1
Electromagnetic Metasurfaces
Metamaterials are artificial materials that, thanks to the inclusion of subwavelength com-
ponents, exhibit unconventional electromagnetic properties. They are characterized by
homogenized material parameters. Their surface equivalents are the MSs, which, con-
versely, are characterized in terms of homogenized boundary conditions. The concept
of MSs is to create thin, planar arrays of subwavelength resonators that induce abrupt
modifications in the amplitude, phase, and/or polarization of an incoming wave. MSs are
usually described by a transmittance and a reflectance, which capture their transmissive
and reflective properties. Alternatively, they can be described using their electric and
magnetic polarizabilities, ps
e,m, which represent the effective polarizabilities per unit area.
Each resonator is characterized by an electric and a magnetic polarizability, pe,m, defined
as the ratio of the dipole moment to the local field [5]. Multiple resonators closely spaced
on the MS allow to define the MS polarizabilities, which account for coupling effects [5, 6].
As detailed in the next chapter, from the polarizabilities it is possible to define the electric
and magnetic surface currents to be used in the homogenized boundary problem. By
carefully adjusting the array’s properties, including its material composition, dimensions
and shapes of the resonators, and lattice constant, one can modulate polarizabilities and,
consequently, the surface currents that govern the boundary conditions. This control al-
lows for precise tuning of the reflective and transmissive properties of the MS, enabling
advanced wavefront manipulation. Thus, MSs that control both reflection and transmis-
sion through the engineering of electric and magnetic polarizabilities are called Huygens’
MSs, inspired by Huygens’ principle, which states that ”each point on a primary wavefront
acts as a source of secondary spherical waves, with the secondary wavefront formed as
their envelope.”
Furthermore, recent advancements in MSs that incorporate active components providing
additional energy to the system have opened new possibilities in wave manipulation. By
integrating components with intrinsic gain properties, these MSs can mitigate and over-
come traditional limitations, such as losses, and actively enhance wave transmission or
reflection [7, 8].
2.2
Bound States in the Continuum
BICs are intriguing, highly resonant states localized in structures open to incoming waves
[9, 1, 10, 11, 12]. They possess a non-decaying nature described by an infinite qual-
ity factor (Q-factor), sparking great interest from both physicists and industry innovators.
First, BICs were predicted by Neumann and Wigner in quantum systems [13], and since
then they have been extensively studied across various wave domains, including acous-
tics [14], hydrodynamics [15], and electromagnetics [9]. They are found to occur when
zeros and poles of a Hermitian system overlap [16]. In the real world, a system designed
Resonant States in Passive and Active Metasurface Cavities
3
==PAGE:10==
to sustain BICs will always be subject to some degree of unavoidable losses, whereas
a minimal coupling with the external fields, achieved by slightly detuning the system, is
necessary to excite the resonance. As a consequence, the Q-factor becomes finite, and
in practice, one will always encounter quasi-BICs.
We are here concerned with (quasi-) BICs in electromagnetic systems, which are typically
observed in photonic (periodic) and plasmonic configurations, as well as in structures with
extreme material properties, such as epsilon-near-zero (ENZ) materials and perfect elec-
tric conductors [17, 18, 19, 20, 21].
BICs are typically classified based on their underlying interference mechanisms, leading to
the so-called symmetry-protected and accidental BICs. Symmetry-protected BICs arise
when the structure’s symmetry enforces destructive interference, effectively preventing
radiation. In contrast, accidental BICs occur when the coupling with the radiation waves
vanish due to the continuous tuning of one or more system parameters. To enable the
emergence of quasi-BICs, a slight coupling with radiation channels must be introduced,
by subtly breaking the structure’s symmetry for symmetry-protected BICs or by slightly
adjusting the parameters governing the BICs in the case of accidental BICs.
2.2.1
All-dielectric Metasurfaces
Arrays, such as MSs, have been mostly investigated in optics because the Q-factor scales
with the total number of elements, making them impractical for low-frequency applications.
In optics, ohmic losses make all-dielectric materials the preferred choice, leading to an
extensive body of literature on all-dielectric MSs used as BIC systems, which are applied
in various important fields such as lasing, sensing, and wavefront manipulation [12, 22,
23]. All-dielectric MSs enable extreme Q-factors (see, for example, [24], where a Q-factor
of ∼106 was observed), but, in many instances, the underlying (quasi-) BICs are spatially
extended, often leading to a low local density of states [18].

Plasmonic Metasurfaces and Single Resonators
At the same time, recent studies investigated the application of BIC theory in hybrid
dielectric-metallic [20, 26, 27] and in purely plasmonic structures [28, 29, 30], culminating
in a work that revealed the physics underlying high-Q plasmonic structures by tailoring
the dissipative properties of resonant modes during the transition between local and non-
local regimes in plasmonic MSs [30]. Note that “local” refers to quasi-isolated site reso-
nances with localized fields and minimal interaction between meta-atoms, while “nonlocal”
describes collective resonances with significantly extended fields and strong interactions
among the meta-atoms. Fig. 2.2 shows a plasmonic MS made of split-ring resonators that
supports dark and bright localized surface plasmon resonances along with the radiation
patterns for different heights of the meta-atoms, illustrating the transition from nonlocal to
local regimes.

2.2.3
Metasurface Cavities
Another class of BIC systems that has recently attracted the interest of researchers is the
MS cavity [35]. The typical MS cavity is a dielectric or empty cavity which, according to
the geometry, is bounded or enclosed by two or one MS. Considering a planar geome-
try, the MS cavity is composed of two MSs arranged to form a slab, whereas considering
a cylindrical or spherical geometry, the MS cavity consists of a single MS enclosing the
cavity. The concept behind such a BIC system is to utilize the MSs to realize cavities
that sustain highly resonant modes, similar to those in conventional closed cavities, while
allowing external excitation and thus resulting in quasi-BICs. In fact, the main limitation
of regular cavities is that they are either fully closed, like metallic cavities at microwave
frequencies, or partially open but with poor efficiency, like optical Fabry-Pérot interferom-
eters. MS cavities designed to sustain quasi-BICs solve both problems, allowing external
excitation of high-Q resonant states. The conventional class of MSs used in this context
is the impedance surface, defined by incorporating only the electric response. Impedance
surfaces have been studied at microwave frequencies in both planar and cylindrical ge-
ometries, demonstrating their ability to support quasi-BICs [36, 37]. However, relying
solely on the electric response imposes specific size constraints on the cavities: in the
planar geometry, the two MSs must be separated by a distance equal to a multiple of λ/2
[36], where λ is the wavelength, while in the cylindrical cavity, the resonance conditions

2.3
Superscattering
Another fascinating phenomenon which has been attracting great interest from researchers
is superscattering [2]. It is easy to see why finding effective methods to maximize mea-
surable quantities such as scattering cross sections is critically important for a vast array
of applications, spanning from fundamental physics to numerous engineering challenges.
The concept of superscattering was introduced in 2010 by Ruan and Fan as a 2D prob-
lem [38] and then extended to 3D scatterers [39]. They wanted to overcome the limi-
tation of scattering cross sections that, for subwavelength scatterers in free-space, are
bounded by a maximum of 2λ/π and 3λ2/2π for 2D and 3D configurations, respectively.
The power scattered by an arbitrary scatterer can be defined as Psca = P
m |Γm|2, where
Γm is the scattering coefficient associated to the m-th channel (or mode). Lossy sys-
tems are bounded by the so-called single channel limit |Γm| < 1. However, an arbitrarily
large cross section can, in theory, be achieved by optimizing the contributions from a suf-
ficiently large number of channels. In practical terms, this means designing a scatterer
capable of exciting multiple radiative modes that collectively exceed the single-channel
limit. This novel approach ignited the interest of the global scientific community, leading
to a surge of research on superscattering. Researchers began exploring the directional-
ity of superscattering [40], leading to phenomena like superbackscattering [41, 42, 43],
and superscattering was found to significantly enhance other distinctive scattering phe-

and cloaking [46, 47]. The phenomenon of multifrequency superscattering has been un-
covered, where superscattering occurs simultaneously across multiple frequency regimes
in hyperbolic media [48]. Furthermore, the scattering cross section was shown to be en-
hanced using ENZ environments [49], active gain MSs [7], and BICs [50], and the concept
of superscattering was extended to sound [51] and water waves [52]. Fig. 2.5 illustrates

perscattering emerges. Furthermore, for both polarizations, it can be analytically shown
that in the limit x0 →∞, Re(Ze/η0) →−1/2, Im(Ze/η0) →0, Re(Ymη0) →−1/2, and
Im(Ymη0) →0, consistent with the constant solutions for planar cavities (3.17). As already
highlighted, these superscattering solutions are independent from the dielectric inside the
cavity, depending only on the cavity size (radius) and on the host medium.
Fig. 3.6 shows solutions (3.29) as function of rc/λ0 for the first 4 modes. The selec-
tion of the order is clearly relevant only for subwavelength cavities. As the cavity size
increases, the solutions approach the previously discussed limits, effectively rendering
the achievement of single-mode superscattering unfeasible. Furthermore, in the case of
deeply subwavelength cavities, the impedances reach extreme values, either zero or in-
finity. Therefore, realizing single-mode superscattering necessitates precise engineering,
balancing the cavity size with the ability of the physical MS to precisely exhibit the desired
gain and reactive behavior.

4
Metasurface Design
Until this point the MS was assumed to be homogeneous, infinitely thin and lossless. The
next step involves investigating surface designs that can achieve the required impedances
to excite quasi-BICs and superscattering. Periodic structures, such as frequency selec-
tive surfaces (FSS) and MSs, can be modeled as homogenized impedance sheets [37,
55, 56]. While many of these surfaces have corresponding analytical models, they often
account only for the electric response.
In the passive scenario, we introduce a straightforward all-dielectric MS model [4] de-
signed to achieve homogenization for operation at optical frequencies. This model de-
rives expressions for the effective surface impedances and admittances of an array of
spherical particles. Additionally, to extend applicability to the microwave range, a bi-layer
Huygens MS is proposed. In this case an analytical expression is not available, and its
response will have to be tailored through shape optimization. Along with these models,
relevant phenomena potentially affecting the design are discussed, including angular dis-
persion, homogeneity and losses. Finally, a methodology for implementing active gain
MSs is proposed, potentially enabling the superscattering phenomenon discussed in the
previous chapter.

4.2
Fabrication Techniques for All-Dielectric Metasurfaces
The particle model’s strength lies in its simple analytical formulation for surface impedance
and admittance, making it a valid tool for demonstrating the potential of the proposed ana-
lytical approach. However, implementing the spherical particle model as a MS on a cavity,
particularly when curved, as in cylindrical and spherical cavities, may present significant
challenges. Despite this, suitable impedance/admittance combinations can be probably
achieved using other types of shapes such as rectangular and cylindrical forms [57], for
which are not available straightforward analytical models but can be engineered through
parameter optimization. To achieve a physical design it is possible either to implement
a MS based on a 2-Dimensional lattice made of dielectric resonators with low aspect-
ratio, such as the particle model, or a 1-Dimensional lattice made of resonators with high
aspect-ratio, such as cylinders or pillars. An example of a low aspect-ratio MS (spher-
ical particles) in a cylindrical configuration is shown on the left of Fig. 4.2, while a high
aspect-ratio MS (cylinders) in the same configuration is depicted on the right. According
to the chosen strategy, recent advancements in meta-device fabrication provide several
techniques that can be utilized [58]. Considering a 2-D lattice, electron beam lithogra-
phy combined with chemical etching has proven to be an effective method for fabricating
dielectric nanoparticles with various shapes and highly defined, sharp edges [59]. Shift-
ing to spherical shapes, femtosecond laser printing can be used to produce spherical
silicon nanoparticles, with the possibility of patterning these nanoparticles onto any sub-
strate [60]. Self-assembled all-dielectric MS are also a promising technique and can be

4.4
Mode Selection
The case of passive and active cylindrical and spherical cavities, as already outlined,
involves a single-mode scenario for both BICs and superscattering (except for specific
cases, which will be discussed in the next chapter, where a dual-mode quasi-BIC can
be excited). This means that, when designing the MS, a mode from which the desired
phenomenon arises must be selected. Theoretically, any mode can work. In practice,
higher-order modes become increasingly sensitive to structural imperfections, making
them impractical. A more detailed analysis of the relationship between Q-factors in pas-
sive structures and scattering efficiency in active structures, with respect to mode order,
is presented in the next chapter.
4.5
Angular Dispersion
When homogenizing the MS using a physical model, it is reasonable to expect that dif-
ferent incidence angles will introduce angular dispersion. This can cause local variations
in the MS impedance and admittance, ultimately degrading the Q-factor in passive struc-
tures and the scattering efficiency in active structures. In a planar configuration, this
effect is solely related to variations in the angle of incidence. When Monti et al. proposed
the particle model [4], they analyzed the surface impedance curves for various incidence
angles on planar MSs, deriving them analytically and validating their findings through full-
wave simulations. Their analysis revealed that, as the incidence angle increases, the
resonances experience a redshift. However, impedance curves evaluated away from the
resonances exhibit less sensitivity to changes in the incidence angle. This implies that, as
long as the cavity size does not demand extreme surface impedances and admittances
to sustain the (quasi-)BICs, the impact of angular dispersion will remain minimal. On the
other hand, in the case of curved MSs, such as those enclosing cylindrical and spherical
cavities, local variations of the incidence angle on the surface are inevitable. For cylin-
drical configurations, Padooru et al. [68] previously addressed this issue using full-wave
simulations to examine how curvature affects the local impedance in MSs composed of
sub-wavelength printed and slotted arrays. They found that impedance expressions de-
rived for planar cases remain valid, provided the element dimensions and periods are
subwavelength and smaller than the local curvature.
In our context, considering these findings and designing the MS accordingly should ef-
fectively mitigate angular dispersion effects, preventing them from significantly degrading
the system’s performance.

4.6
Homogeneity
Homogeneity is a crucial factor in the design of physical MSs. It is well-known that the
more homogeneous the MS, the closer it nears the ideal behavior, a fact that has also
been shown in cylindrical configurations [68].
When characterizing a MS analytically, the assumption of an infinitely extended surface is
valid for a planar configuration as long as the number of unit cells is sufficiently large. Un-
der this assumption, homogeneity issues may arise if the analytical model accounts only
for certain contributions, such as dipole interactions. If the unit cell is too large, homo-
geneity is compromised, leading to the emergence of higher-order modes that influence
the scattering mechanism and cause deviations from the analytical model. As previously
discussed, this is the case with the particle model [4].
When moving to curved finite MSs, the situation becomes more complicated. Cylindrical
cavities are infinitely extended in one dimension, while their circumference is, of course,
finite. Their analytical characterization holds only for high aspect-ratio MS cavities. Spher-
ical cavities are completely finite. The finite nature of these problems limits the number of
unit cells in a MS based on their size, which can significantly impact homogeneity. It can
happen, for instance, that a design which theoretically achieves the exact impedances
required for a given task may, in practice, experience significant detuning due to high in-
homogeneity caused by the limited number of unit cells that fit within the configuration,
resulting in a poor performance. This is the case of small cavities that require extremely
small or large impedances, meaning that a MS with strong interaction with the incident
wave is needed. The approach to address this issue is to explore designs with higher
permittivity of the dielectric incorporated in the unit cell, which reduces the constraints on
the size improving homogeneity. However, this involves a delicate trade-off, and finding
satisfactory solutions may be challenging, particularly for deeply subwavelength cavities
where extreme values of impedances are required, and even small structural deviations
can significantly impact the performance.
A detailed analysis of how homogeneity influences the Q-factor of cylindrical passive MS
cavities will be presented in the next chapter.
4.7
Lossy Cavities
As demonstrated by the results in the previous chapter (see Table 3.1), the active MS
solutions for impedances leading to superscattering are independent of the dielectric in-
side the cavity. In contrast, the passive MS solutions that define the conditions for BICs
are inherently dependent on the dielectric properties. Up to this point, cavities have been
assumed to be lossless; however, a discerning reader may question how the previously
derived solutions are affected when losses within the cavity are considered. This is a
crucial aspect in the design of MS cavities. The impedance curves that establish BIC
conditions in the previous chapter (see for example Fig. 3.3) were derived assuming
empty and lossless cavities. Understanding the necessary conditions when losses are
introduced is essential for designing cavities that can support quasi-BICs. Considering
a generic dielectric inside the cavity with a relative permittivity εr, and remembering the
adopted convention ejωt, the losses can be described using the complex refractive index,

planar MS cavity in the MS-MS configuration. The cavity is composed of a lossy dielectric,

4.8
Gain Metasurfaces
Active gain MSs (from now on referred to simply as ”gain MSs”) represent an emerg-
ing research field, trying to achieve full control over the resistive behavior, in addition
to the reactive one. The goal is to develop effective methods for compensating losses,
which remain a major source of performance degradation. In our case, we focus on gain
MSs to achieve the superscattering conditions outlined in the previous chapter. However,
emerging gain MS models are often tunable [7, 8], suggesting that a MS capable of be-
ing adjusted to achieve both superscattering and quasi-BIC excitation—while potentially
actively compensating for losses within the cavity (see the previous section 4.7)—may
be feasible. This section outlines the operating principles of existing gain MS models
for microwave applications, explores potential approaches for implementation at optical
frequencies, and presents an equivalent circuit model for a MS capable of exhibiting the
necessary behavior to achieve superscattering.

4.8.1
Microwave Applications
The idea behind gain MSs for microwave applications is to realize a negative conductivity
that satisfies |σ(ω)/ω| > |ε′′(ω)|, with ε′′(ω) > 0. In this scenario, the negative conduc-
tivity brings a gain to the system which effectively cancels and exceeds the losses. This
was demonstrated to work when incorporating active gain elements on the MS [7, 8].
The conductivity considered so far is referred to a microscopic scale. When moving to
the macroscopic world, it is associated with the differential resistance RL, the real part of
the complex impedance ZL [8]. It is possible to use the classical transmission line the-
ory to characterize this problem from a scattering viewpoint. We know that the reflection
coefficient ΓL that characterizes a boundary, defined as the ratio between the reflected
and the transmitted wave, can be written as ΓL = (ZL −Z0)/(ZL + Z0), where Z0 repre-
sent the characteristic impedance (typically 50 [Ω] in transmission line theory but Z0 = η0
in our context). Traditional systems present losses and are characterized by a positive
differential resistance, being constrained to |ΓL| < 1. However, a sufficiently negative
differential resistance can cause the denominator absolute value |ZL + Z0| to be smaller
than the numerator absolute value |ZL −Z0|, resulting in |ΓL| > 1 and operating as a gain
system. In simpler terms, adequate negative differential resistances, related to negative
conductivities in the field micro analysis, can introduce gain in the MS. This was proven
to be possible using tunnel diodes (TDs) [7, 8], a type of diode with an very thin depletion
region that enables quantum tunneling at small forward biases, allowing them to reach
states where they exhibit effective negative resistance. On the left of Fig 4.7 a sketch of
a possible gain MS unit cell is shown, along with its equivalent circuit model in the center.
As one can observe on the right of Fig. 4.7, the I-V curve of a TD has a region where as
the driving voltage (V ) increases the current flowing through the diode (I) decreases. By
defining the resistance of the TD as Rn = dV /dI (small signal resistance), it is clear that
this results in a negative value.
So far in this section, we have assumed only one generic complex impedance ZL. How-
ever, to achieve the necessary condition for the superscattering, we need a precise con-
trol over two different impedances, Ze, associated with the electric field, and Zm = 1/Ym,
associated with the magnetic field. Although such a design is theoretically possible, its
implementation in practice poses a significant challenge. It requires extremely precise
control over the active and reactive effects of the MS, ensuring this control applies to two
distinct responses. Employing transmission line theory with equivalent circuits and fully
separating the two effects provides a useful starting point for addressing resistive and
reactive responses. However, in practice, the two circuit models will inevitably be cou-
pled, necessitating the use of advanced full-wave simulations to achieve an accurate and
effective design.
4.8.2
Optical Applications
At optical frequencies the situation becomes even more challenging. Due to the reduced
sizes, achieving such a precise control of both resistive (with gain) and reactive behaviors
on a physical MS appears as a formidable challenge. However, it is important to note that
the superscattering solutions for the MS impedances are independent of the cavity inter-
nal contents, relying solely on the host medium and the cavity geometry and size. We are
essentially free to utilize the cavity for design purposes, which could also be beneficial
when working in the microwave range. The only concern when implementing a physical
design is that the superscattering mechanism, which cause a great field enhancement in-

phonons, enables energy transfer to a specific optical signal.

utilizing nonlinear interactions would be precisely characterizing ε′′(ω). While analytical

the specific task that we are facing. Furthermore, achieving the required combination of
Ze and Ym necessitates precise tuning of their reactive components. This involves pat-

effects operate in perfect synergy with the gain system. Without the aid of precise numer-
ical simulations, designing such a configuration is nearly impossible. For gain provided
by nonlinear effects, this could be a significant technological limitation. However, with fur-
ther advancements in the theory and the application of gain MSs at optical frequencies,
a realistic implementation could become achievable.

Numerical Results
To validate the theory presented in Chapter 3, results obtained from full-wave simulations
are here presented. Specifically, quasi-BICs in planar MS cavities are examined, con-
sidering configurations based on the particle model in both the MS-MS and the MS-PEC
configurations and on the the bi-layer Huygens MS model in the MS-PEC configuration,
with particular emphasis on deeply subwavelength cavities in the latter case. In addition,
cylindrical MS cavities are simulated using the particle model, focusing on the selection of
the mode from which the quasi-BIC emerges and the relationship between homogeneity
and the Q factor. Moreover, a cylindrical cavity that supports a dual-mode BIC, with the
poles of the internal field arising from two different modes simultaneously, is discussed.
Finally, superscattering is explored in a cylindrical MS cavity characterized by ideal sur-
face currents. The full-wave simulations were performed using COMSOL Multiphysics.
5.1
COMSOL Models
The simulations were conducted using the following approach: first, the MS was char-
acterized in terms of its surface electric impedance and magnetic admittance using the
COMSOL Frequency Domain Solver. Once the desired values—determined by a specific
configuration of the MS design parameters—were achieved, the MS model was incorpo-
rated into the cavity model. The system was then analyzed using the COMSOL Eigen-
frequency Solver to identify resonances and compute the numerical Q-factor. Finally, the
COMSOL Frequency Domain Solver was employed to simulate the field patterns associ-
ated with the targeted quasi-BICs. In the case of superscattering, the MS was considered
ideal and modeled using surface electric and magnetic currents as boundary conditions.
These currents were directly defined from the tangential fields and from the impedance
and admittance values (as detailed in Chapter 3), which were manually set as parame-
ters.
To characterize the MS response, a single unit cell was placed at the center of a rectan-
gular cuboid of free space with a length of λ0, as shown on the left of Fig. 5.1. By applying
Floquet boundary conditions [69], which enforce periodicity over a selected surface (red
arrows on the left of Fig. 5.1), the MS was effectively modeled as infinitely extended. The
reflection (S11) and transmission (S21) coefficients were then obtained by defining Port
1 and Port 2, where the excitation was defined at Port 1 as a plane wave propagating
along x with a y- (z-) polarized electric (magnetic) field, resulting in a normal incidence on
the unit cell. Ze and Ym were eventually calculated from the reflection and transmission
coefficients using (4.3).
For planar cavities, the cavity simulations followed a similar approach, modeling the cav-
ity as a single-unit-cell MS and applying periodic boundary conditions. However, this
approach was not feasible for cylindrical cavities. Instead, symmetry was utilized to sim-
plify the problem to a low-aspect-ratio semi-cylinder. By applying perfect electric con-
ductor (PEC) and perfect magnetic conductor (PMC) boundary conditions and exploiting
image theory, the problem was reduced to half of the cylinder’s circumference and to a
height equal to half the lattice constant of the unit cell. Consequently, two concentric
cylinders with a low aspect ratio and ideal PEC/PMC walls on their planar surfaces were
implemented. A perfectly matched layer (PML) was placed on the outer curved surface
to prevent reflections and ensure that outgoing waves were not scattered back into the
structure. The MS was positioned on the boundary of the inner half-cylinder, forming the
MS cavity. This setup is shown on the right of Fig. 5.1.

To guarantee that the solutions converges, areas with curvatures, edges, and corners
require a fine meshing to accurately capture smooth and abrupt transitions. However, in
regions without curved geometries or sharp corners, a coarser mesh is sufficient. The
memory usage is directly affected by the number of mesh elements, so minimizing un-
necessary refinement allows simulations to run efficiently, particularly on less powerful
computers. Note that, when using periodic boundary conditions, surfaces parallel to each
other and joined by the periodic condition must have identical mesh to avoid artifacts
at the periodic interfaces. Finally, to verify that the mesh is suitable and ensures result
convergence, it is essential to analyze how a key parameter, particularly relevant to the
problem, varies as a function of another parameter that controls the overall mesh fine-
ness. For each simulated model, this assessment helps determine the optimal mesh
resolution. When the key parameter remains constant despite further mesh refinement,
it indicates that the mesh is sufficiently fine to produce accurate results. Fig. 5.2 illus-

results and is directly related to the mesh refinement and to the memory usage [70]. It
is evident that at some point the results stabilize, indicating the simulation’s convergence

Dual-Mode BIC
As previously mentioned, the BICs identified through the proposed methodology are pri-
marily single-mode, with the total field inside the cavity being forced to zero by the ZeYm =
1/4 condition, and the pole order n arbitrarily selected in (3.28). However, in certain cases,
for specific cavity dimensions and modes, two distinct poles arising from two different
modes can coincide, effectively forming a dual-mode BIC. This is the case of an empty
cylindrical cavity of radius rc = 0.564λ0, where the impedance/admittance solutions de-
rived from (3.28) for mode n = 0 and n = 3 overlap, as can be seen on the left of Fig.
5.12. Recalling that lower-order modes exhibit broader responses, this holds true in the
present case with the excitation of the n = 0 mode. In particular, this characteristic is also
transferred to the n = 3 mode, resulting in a reduced sensitivity to structural imperfections
for that mode. This behavior is illustrated on the right side of Fig. 5.12, where the local
field variation at (x, y) = (rc/6, 0) as a function of rc/λ0 is identical for both n = 0 and
n = 3, as expected. Regarding the field distribution, the dual-mode nature leads to the
simultaneous excitation and spatial overlap of the standing wave patterns associated with
modes n = 0, n = 3 and n = −3 (because of the symmetry of the cylindrical harmonics
expansion) within the cavity. This results in a (quasi-)BIC with a more uniform spatial
profile, which may be particularly relevant for applications such as sensing devices. This
effect is depicted in Fig. 5.13, where the left and center panels show the field distributions
in the xy-plane for the n = 0 mode and the superposition of the n = −3 and n = 3 modes.
The right panel presents the total magnetic field intensity distribution, where, as expected,
the overlapping patterns lead to a combined distribution in which only points that are null
in both individual patterns remain null.
We expect similar overlapping modes in spherical MS cavities as well.

Summary and Conclusions
This study examined metasurface (MS) cavities with planar, cylindrical, and spherical
geometries, characterized by surface electric impedance Ze and surface magnetic admit-
tance Ym. The focus was on leveraging these surface responses to achieve exotic wave
phenomena, specifically Bound States in the Continuum (BICs) and superscattering. After
a rigorous analytical determination of the necessary surface impedances/admittances to
enable these phenomena in the aforementioned cavities, full-wave simulations were per-
formed using COMSOL Multiphysics with both ideal and homogenized MS models. These
simulations validated the analytical characterization and highlighted potential effective de-
signs for a future experimental validation in both the optical and microwave ranges.
Chapter 2 introduced the concepts of electromagnetic MSs, BICs, and superscattering,
reviewing some of the recent studies in the relative fields.
Chapter 3 presented a thorough analytical characterization of MS cavities, deriving the
exact solutions for Ze and Ym that enable BICs and superscattering for the considered
geometries. The solutions for BICs correspond to passive MSs that rely solely on reac-
tive effects, whereas those for superscattering involve MSs that supply additional power
(gain MSs).
Subsequently, Chapter 4 explored two homogenized passive MS models that incorporate
both Ze and Ym, specifically an all-dielectric MS model for optical applications (particle
model) and a bi-layer Huygens MS for microwave applications. Additionally, an analysis
of the mode selection, MS homogeneity, angular dispersion and the losses within the cav-
ities was presented, followed by a discussion on gain MSs for superscattering, proposing
potential methodologies for their future implementation.
Chapter 5 presented the numerical results from COMSOL full-wave simulations. Quasi-
BICs were demonstrated in planar and cylindrical cavities, where the MSs were homoge-
nized using the models described in Chapter 4. An analysis on the relationship between
MS homogeneity and the cavity Q-factor was conducted, highlighting that inhomogeneous
MSs exhibit lower performance. Additionally, a dual-BIC configuration for cylindrical cavi-
ties was discussed. Finally, a numerical simulation using an ideal gain MS was performed,
showcasing the superscattering effect emerging from a cylindrical cavity.
Importantly, this work was built upon a previous project course, resulting in one journal
publication [3] and one conference paper. Both articles are included in Appendix B.
This contribution represents an endeavor in characterizing the discussed wave phenom-
ena in the framework of MS cavities, aiming to enhance the understanding of these phe-
nomena and providing simple yet effective tools for their application in practical scenar-
ios. The results of this work may be relevant to a broad array of practical applications,
including lasing, sensing, filters, antennas, nonlinear systems, bioimaging, and optoelec-
tronics. The natural progression of this research will focus on the physical design aspects
to achieve an experimental validation. Regarding superscattering, while realizing the pro-
posed cavities with gain MSs presents a significant challenge, we are confident that, due
to recent advancements and efforts in the engineering of such MSs, it will become feasible
in the near future.

Bound States in the Continuum in Cylindrical All-Dielectric Metasurface Cavities
Pietro Brugnolo, Samel Arslanagi´c and Rasmus E. Jacobsen
Department of Space Research and Technology,
Technical University of Denmark,
Lyngby DK-2800, Denmark
Bound states in the continuum are presently demonstrated in all-dielectric, metasurface-based
cavities of circularly cylindrical shapes. While their cross-sectional sizes may be arbitrary, emphasis
is put on the much-needed excitation of these intriguing states in sub-wavelength cavities. The em-
ployed metasurfaces are characterized by effective electric and magnetic surface impedances, allowing
for a swift analytical analysis of the underlying problem. The inclusion of magnetic responses, in
addition to the electric one, enhances the versatility of the proposed cavities, significantly extending
their application, particularly in optical contexts where magnetism is absent. To demonstrate the
potential of the proposed method, an all-dielectric metasurface composed of appropriately arranged
silicon particles is designed and investigated in the optical frequency range, resulting in a quality
factor of ∼1.7 × 104.
Bound states in the continuum (BICs) are highly res-
onant states localized in structures open to incoming
waves [1–5]. Theoretically, BICs possess infinite quality
factors (Q-factors), producing near field enhancements of
non-decaying nature, making them interesting in terms of
fundamental physics and for many industrial applications
[6, 7]. They are found to occur when zeros and poles of
a Hermitian system coincide [8]. Originally predicted in
quantum systems [9], they have since then been observed
in a range of wave settings, including acoustics [10], hy-
drodynamics [11] and electromagnetics [1]. Focusing on
the latter case, they are typically observed in photonic
(periodic) and plasmonic configurations, and in struc-
tures with extreme material parameters, such as epsilon-
near-zero (ENZ) materials and perfect electric conduc-
tors [12–16].
For practical implementations, a reason-
able coupling with external fields is necessary to excite
the resonance, resulting in quasi-BICs. This is achieved
by slightly detuning the system away from the ideal BIC
condition. Including ineluctable structural irregularities
and material losses, their Q-factors become finite.
BICs in arrays, such as metasurfaces (MSs), have
mostly been studied in optics. This is because the Q-
factor scales with the total number of array elements, es-
sentially making them impractical for low-frequency ap-
plications. Low-loss dielectrics are the preferred mate-
rial choice in optics due to severe metal losses at these
frequencies. Several all-dielectric MSs have been inves-
tigated for various applications including lasing, sensing
and wavefront manipulations [5, 17, 18].
Extreme Q-
factors have been demonstrated in all-dielectric MSs [5],
reaching values as high as ∼106 [19]. However, the un-
derlying (quasi-) BICs are in many cases spatially ex-
tended, often leading to a low local density of states [13].
At the same time, recent studies explored the use of the
BIC theory in plasmonic structures [15, 20–24], culmi-
nating in a comprehensive strategy that, exploiting the
balance of local and non-local responses of the MS, tailors
the dissipative behaviour of the resonant modes attaining
high Q-factors [24]. Furthermore, quasi-BICs in single
metallic and dielectric resonators exhibit significant field
localization with small modal volumes [16, 25–28].
Considering regular cavities, they can be fully closed,
like metallic microwave cavities that support high-Q
modes but block external excitation, or partially open,
like optical Fabry-P´erot interferometers that suffer from
phase lag and low efficiency.
Recently, reflecting MSs
were shown to form cavities that support modes similar
to regular cavities, but allowing external excitation and
presenting high Q-factors, resulting in quasi-BICs [29–
31].
At microwave frequencies, cylindrical and planar
cavities formed by one and two MSs, respectively, have
been investigated [30, 31]. Both geometries rely on MSs
with effective surface impedances close to zero, resulting
in quasi-perfect electric conductors. These resonant MSs
have specific size requirements: in the planar case, the
two MSs must be spaced by a multiple of λ/2, with λ
being the wavelength, whereas for the cylindrical cavity,
the resonance conditions are the roots of the cylindri-
cal Bessel functions. In both the planar and cylindrical
cases, the effective surface impedance of the MSs origi-
nate merely from electric dipole excitations, and thus no
magnetic effects were considered. In addition, these MSs
utilize substrates with metallic patterns and metallic par-
ticles at microwave and optical frequencies, respectively.
In this work, we demonstrate that BICs can be formed
in cavities of any size by utilizing all-dielectric MSs with
both electric and magnetic surface impedances.
The
magnetic response, on top of the electric one, expands
the versatility of the proposed cavities and goes beyond
the application potential of electric-only configurations,
specifically at optics where magnetic materials are ab-
sent.
The theoretical foundation is presented together
with the analytical solutions for the required electric and
magnetic surface impedance. All-dielectric MS designs
are subsequently explored for potential optical applica-
tions. The synthesized MSs consist of spherical dielectric
particles, and their effective surface impedances are de-
termined using the model reported in [32]. This enables
an analytical approach, and we compare the analytical re-
sults for selected MSs with the full-wave simulations con-
firming the existence of the quasi-BICs, with a Q-factor

finitely thin, lossless, and homogeneous. The next step is
to determine a surface design that exhibits the required
impedances to excite the quasi-BIC. Periodic structures
like frequency selective surfaces (FSS) and MSs can be
treated as homogenized impedance sheets [31, 37–39].
Many of these surfaces have associated analytical mod-
els, but they often only include the electric response. In
contrast, the spherical particle model accounts for both
electric and magnetic dipoles, providing expressions for
effective surface impedances for an array of spherical par-
ticles [32]. In the planar case, this model consists of an
infinite grid of spherical particles with radius rs and lat-
tice constant d. Particles are made of a non-magnetic
material with relative permittivity εr,s, and the grid is
embedded in a non-magnetic host medium with relative
permittivity εr,h. To ensure the dipole model holds, pre-
venting higher-order modes from affecting the scattering
mechanism, rs must be reasonably smaller than the wave-
length in the host medium, whereas d should be smaller
than the wavelength to avoid diffraction.

In this work, BICs were demonstrated in cylindrical
MS cavities exhibiting effective electric and magnetic sur-
face impedances.
The analytical solutions for the re-
quired surface impedances were derived and used to show
that quasi-BICs can be induced in associated cylindrical
cavities of arbitrary sizes. As a proof-of-concept configu-
ration, a MS composed of spherical silicon particles was
designed using established MS homogenization models,
and its ability to support the excitation of quasi-BICs
was shown and further verified through full-wave analy-
sis of the actual cylindrical MS. It should be noted that
other MS designs, utilizing different materials and ele-
ment shapes, can be utilized as long as they exhibit the
required surface impedances [38, 39, 43]. Moreover, we
expect the presence of BICs in other geometries, like pla-
nar slabs and spheres, exhibiting similar properties, al-
though the required surface impedances will differ. Im-
portantly, as can be seen from the boundary conditions
(2), the condition ZeYm = 1/4 (corresponding to a zero in
the internal field) is general and will still apply. Despite
the demonstration at optical frequencies, the proposed
MS cavity design may also be of potential interest in the
design of filters, antennas, sensors, and nonlinear systems
at frequencies ranging from radio to mm-waves.

S4.2
Homogeneity
In the previous subsection we highlighted the need to improve homogeneity in order to
enhance poor Q-factors. Considering the particle model, a MS design that sustains a
quasi-BIC can be found by precisely tailoring the particle size, the lattice constant and
the particle permittivity. Small cavities require extemely small or large impedances,
meaning that a MS with strong interaction with the incident wave is needed. To achieve
this, we can increase the particle size, the particle permittivity, or both. However, it
is important to note that increasing the particle size can significantly affect the ho-
mogeneity, limiting the number of particles that can fit on the circumference and/or
reducing the unit cell. As a result, this configuration may lead to a significant detuning
from the required impedances, and thus to a poor Q-factor. The approach to address
this issue is to explore designs with higher particle permittivity, which reduces the
constraints on particle size improving homogeneity. However, this involves a delicate
trade-off, and finding satisfactory solutions may be challenging, particularly for highly
sub-wavelength cavities where extreme values of impedances are required, and even
small structural deviations can significantly impact the Q-factor. It is well-known that
the more homogeneous the MS, the closer it nears the ideal behavior, a fact that has
also been shown in cylindrical configurations [S8]. To have an idea of how this affects
our configuration, we performed some additional simulations. Considering the same
cavity as described in the article, i.e. an empty cavity with a radius of rc = 0.7878λ0,
we simulated various MSs that theoretically share the same impedances, supporting

S4.3
Angular Dispersion
While the analytical approach used to derive (S2.1) and (S2.2), based on the cylindrical
harmonics analysis, already accounts for the curvature of the MS and ensures solutions
constant across the curvature, when homogenizing the MS using the particle model it is
reasonable to imagine that different incidence angles will introduce angular dispersion.
This could lead to a local variation of the MS impedances, degrading the Q-factor.
For cylindrical configurations, Padooru et al. [S8] previously addressed this issue us-
ing full-wave simulations to examine how curvature affects the local impedance in MSs
composed of sub-wavelength printed and slotted arrays. They found that impedance
expressions derived for planar cases remain valid, provided the element dimensions and
periods are sub-wavelength and smaller than the local curvature. Furthermore, Monti
et al., when they first introduced the particle model [S7], analyzed surface impedance
curves for various angles of incidence on planar MSs, deriving them analytically and
validating through full-wave simulations. As the incidence angle increases, the reso-
nances undergo a redshift. However, when examining the impedance curves away from
the resonances, they appear to be less sensitive to changes in the angle of incidence.
This suggests that, if we model cavities with sizes that do not require extreme surface
impedances to sustain the quasi-BICs, angular dispersion will have a minimal impact.
This is the case of the MS made of silicon particles proposed in the article, as one can
observe from Fig. 2 and Fig. 4 in the manuscript. Considering our work, given that
a high numerical Q-factor derived through full-wave simulation (such as the last two
reported in Tab. S 2) indicates that surface impedances are nearly constant across the
entire MS (otherwise, for certain angles, the zero-pole condition describing the BIC
would become further detuned, resulting in an additional radiative loss), we believe the
considerations made thus far are reasonable for describing the general mechanism of an-
gular dispersion. Considering highly sub-wavelength cavities, this effect, combined with
the homogeneity issue and the high sensitivity to structural deviations, could negatively
affect the Q-factor.

S5
Practical Implementation
The strength of the particle model lies in its simple analytical formulation for surface
impedances, making it an effective tool to showcase the potential of the proposed ana-
lytical approach. On the other hand, applying the spherical particle model as a MS on
a cylindrical cavity could present significant challenges. However, we believe that suit-
able impedance/admittance combinations can be achieved using other types of shapes
such as rectangular and cylindrical forms [S6], for which are not available straightfor-
ward analytical models but can be engineered through parameter optimization. To
achieve a physical design we can either implement a MS based on a 2-Dimensional lat-
tice made of dielectric resonators with low aspect-ratio (such as the particle model) or
a 1-Dimensional lattice made of resonators with high aspect-ratio (such as cylinders or
pillars) and main axes aligned with the cavity axis (z-axis in our convention). According
to the chosen strategy, recent advancements in meta-device fabrication provide several
techniques that can be utilized [S13]. Considering a 2-D lattice, electron beam lithogra-
phy combined with chemical etching has proven to be an effective method for fabricat-
ing dielectric nanoparticles with various shapes and highly defined, sharp edges [S10].
Shifting to spherical shapes, femtosecond laser printing can be used to produce spheri-
cal silicon nanoparticles, with the possibility of patterning these nanoparticles onto any
substrate [S15]. Self-assembled all-dielectric MS are also a promising technique and
can be adapted to soft substrates [S2]. Although these techniques are typically used for
planar metasurfaces, recent approaches for nanopatterning on non-planar/cylindrical
surfaces have emerged [S14] [S4], suggesting that such an implementation could be fea-
sible in our context. Alternatively, to fabricate 1-D lattices made of resonators with
high aspect ratio requires advanced manufacturing techniques such as combining ther-
mal scanning probe lithography and dry etching [S5], technologies based on top-down
highly directive etching [S12] and nanoimprint lithography [S3]. We are confident that
a practical implementation is achievable with the right approach; however, this requires
further investigations in future works.

Pietro Brugnolo, Samel Arslanagi´c, Rasmus E. Jacobsen,

pietrobrugnolo.pk@gmail.com; saar@dtu.dk; rajac@dtu.dk
Abstract—Bound states in the continuum enable extreme con-
finement of electromagnetic energy in structures otherwise open
to incoming waves. In recent years, they have been demonstrated
in various photonic systems and single resonators. Presently, we
investigate Fabry-P´erot resonances in a planar cavity consisting of
two all-dielectric metasurfaces. The metasurfaces are character-
ized by their effective magnetic and electric surface impedances
enabling an analytical approach. The resonance conditions are
derived and two cavity designs comprised of metasurfaces of all-
dielectric spheres are investigated. The analytical and numerical
results obtained for the two designs are compared showing
excellent agreement, with quality factors of ∼1700 and ∼6500,
respectively. Furthermore, we show that the proposed approach
can be extended to other geometries, presenting a design for
a cylindrical cavity enclosed in a concentric metasurface with
quality factor ∼17000. The proposed metasurface cavity may
be of interest for the design of filters, antennas, sensors, and
nonlinear systems.
Index Terms—Bound states in the continuum, embbeded eigen-
states, cavity resonator, field localization.
I. INTRODUCTION
The ability to confine fields within cavities plays a funda-
mental role in many electromagnetic systems, ranging from
optical to microwave and radio frequencies. Usually, fully
or partially closed cavities are driven in resonant states with
high quality (Q) factors, presenting low energy dissipation. At
microwave frequencies, the typical device consists of a fully
closed cavity formed by metallic walls, enabling resonances
with high Q-factors and closed to external excitation. Alterna-
tively, optical Fabry-P´erot interferometers consist of partially
closed mirrors that confine the field between them through
wave interference. However, these interferometers are prone
to phase lag and exhibit a low overall efficiency.
An intriguing alternative to these classical devices is repre-
sented by open structures that support quasi-bound states in the
continuum (quasi-BICs). BICs are highly resonant states which
lie inside the continuum but remain localized with no radiation
[1]. Theoretically, BICs exhibit infinite Q-factors, resulting
in non-decaying near-field enhancements. Practically, due to
unavoidable structural losses, Q-factors are finite. Moreover, to
achieve their excitation with external waves, it is necessary to
ensure an adequate coupling with the external fields, resulting
in quasi-BICs. This is achieved by slightly detuning the system
away from the resonance. Recently, open structures supporting
these fascinating states received high interest [2, 3, 4, 5]. BICs
typically occur in periodic photonic structures, as well as in
systems with extreme material properties, such as epsilon-
near-zero (ENZ) materials [6, 7]. Furthermore, quasi-BICs
have been demonstrated in cylindrical and planar cavities
consiting of one and two metasurfaces (MSs), respectively
[8, 9]. Both geometries rely on MSs with effective surface
impedances close to zero, resulting in quasi-perfect electric
conductors. The main limitation of these configurations is that
they present strict size requirements to support the formation
of quasi-BICs. In the planar configuration, the two MSs must
be spaced by a multiple of λ/2, with λ being the wavelength,
whereas in the cylindrical one the BIC conditions are the roots
of the cylindrical Bessel functions. In the modelling of these
cavities, only MSs with electric responses were considered,
ignoring any magnetic effect.
Presently, we analyze quasi-BICs formed in dielectric planar
cavities enclosed in two identical MSs which exhibit electric
and magnetic surface impedances, Ze and Zm, respectively.
We show how the presence of both magnetic and electric
effects enable the existence of quasi-BICs within cavities of
arbitrary size, thus overcoming the size constraints on the
spacing between the two MSs, which previously needed to be a
multiple of λ/2. Moreover, as a proof-of-concept, we propose
two different designs using an all-dielectric MS model [12].
The analytical results are compared with full-wave simulations
showing an excellent agreement. The two designs resulted in
Q-factors of ∼1700 and ∼6500.

The configuration of interest is shown in Fig. 1, where a

IV. CONCLUSIONS
In this work, planar cavities supporting quasi-BICs were
examined analytically and numerically. The results demon-
strate that, with adequate MSs exhibiting electric and magnetic
surface impedances, quasi-BICs can be excited in cavities of
any size (avoiding extreme impedance values). Two cavity
designs with different dimension based on an all-dielectric
homogenized MS were proposed and numerically simulated
using COMSOL Multiphysics, demonstrating the existence
and excitation of quasi-BICs. The Q-factors for the two
cavities were ∼1700 and ∼6500, respectively. Furthermore,
to show that the proposed approach can be extended to other
geometries, we developed a design for a cylindrical cavity
enclosed in a concentric MS, supporting a quasi-BIC with a
Q-factor of ∼17000. The derivation and formulation of the
expansion field coefficients, along with the MS electric and
magnetic impedances necessary for exciting the quasi-BIC in
the cylindrical cavity, will be presented at the conference.
