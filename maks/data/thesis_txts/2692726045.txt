Executive Summary
As the production of cereal crops heavily relies on services provided by the ecosphere,
they seem sensitive to them. In recent times, the yield of wheat has stagnated, while
production has increased. The main cause has been identiﬁed to correlate with climate
change and the increase of extreme weather conditions. This could indicate that new
forms of agricultural techniques needs to be investigated.
In this Life Cycle Assessment, two agricultural farming techniques of wheat are mod-
elled. The study is conducted as an attempt to investigate how a novel, alternative farm-
ing technique would compete with the current conventional practice in Denmark. These
are evaluated by their environmental performance. The alternative system investigated
is that of indoor vertical farmed wheat using hydroponics. All inputs for the vertical sys-
tem are anthropogenic. Both systems are modelled as cradle-to-gate and include three
life stages: preparation, growth, and harvest.
The functional unit is deﬁned as:
Produce 1,000 kg of wheat grain in Denmark
The reference ﬂow is the ground area needed for each system to produce the functional
unit, and the time needed to produce it. The conventional system needs 0.137 ha and one
season equals one year in Denmark to fulﬁll the functional unit. For the vertical system,
the area needed is 0.027 ha and 70 days. The growth area for both systems is 0.137 ha.
The methodology of Environmental Footprint 3.0 is used for the LCIA including results
from 16 midpoint impact categories, whereof seven of agricultural importance are in-
cluded in the main report. For the conventional system, the foreground processes in-
cludes data from relevant literature of high data speciﬁcity. For the vertical system, the
foreground processes are built from relevant literature, assumptions, and estimates.
The conventional system is outperforming the vertical system by a fair margin across all
impact categories. It is indicated that the electricity consumption in the lighting process
is a main barrier for the vertical system. This is true for multiple different scenarios.
This suggests that with current lighting technology and limited options to generate clean
electricity, the vertical system can not compete with the current practice of conventional
farming of producing a 1:1 product in respect to the environmental load. It is recom-
mended to gather better and more primary data for the indoor system, in order to inves-
tigate different system setups in Denmark containing less uncertainty, and comparing
the results to the conventional system.

BACKGROUND
1
Background
1.1
Introduction
With a current world population of 7.5 billion people and counting, the demand for food
and food security is a central priority. Coupled with land becoming more scarce, alter-
native ways of producing food need to be investigated (1). Wheat is one of the most
cultivated crops in the world, and almost one eighth of the world’s calorie consumption
comes from wheat (2). A linear increase in grain yield has been observed since the 1960’s
up until the early 2000’s. Then, a stagnation and decline was observed even as produc-
tion continued to increase, and a correlation with climate changes and increasing extreme
weather conditions was identiﬁed (3). This gives rise to concern about the robustness of
the supply from current wheat production, as the yield is heavily relying on inputs from
the natural ecosphere, sunlight, water, nutrients etc., but also sensitive to extreme cli-
mate. Apart from climate change affecting the yield efﬁciency, the current agricultural
sector has been subject to much criticism in respect to the environmental impacts that the
farming techniques have on the planet (4), indicating a positive feedback loop.
A way to combat the negative environmental impacts followed by farming wheat while
creating a steady secure supply of the crop, unattached from the uncertainty that follows
the inputs from the ecosphere and year round production, is controlled-environment
agriculture (CEA). This technique of farming has proven very effective in producing leafy
greens, herbs and even strawberries indoors at multiple ﬂoors (5), (6). This indoor, verti-
cal novel farming technique has the potential to steady the supply of wheat, by simulat-
ing all the correct conditions for all life stages to reach optimal yield (7). This is achieved
by closely monitoring the crop in a closed environment, optimizing the growth potential
by controlling the nutritional uptake in each growth phase, provide favorable conditions
regarding lighting and temperature while also protecting the crop from disease and pests
(7). This technology allow farmers to optimize yield of a given crop compared with the
time spent optimizing every resource in the process.
There are different types of CEA depending on the product produced and the technology
applied. This study focuses on an multiple ﬂoor solution of hydroponics, where the
lighting is supplied by artiﬁcial LED full spectrum light.

1.2
Method
This study aims to map the environmental impact of introducing an indoor vertical farm-
ing system for wheat production in Denmark. This is investigated using a Life Cycle
Assessment (LCA) approach which quantiﬁes the environmental performance presented
in midpoint impact categories, using the methodology provided by the Product and Or-
ganisation Environmental Footprint called Environmental Footprint 3.0 (EF 3.0). The
guidelines from the International Life Cycle Data system (ILCD) and to some extent the

2
Goal Deﬁnition
The goal deﬁnition determines the purpose of the LCA and how the results are meant
to be used. It is the ﬁrst phase of the study. In this section, the methodology is deﬁned
along with who the LCA is directed at, and what limitations can be expected in respect
to the results.
2.1
Intended Application
The indented application of this study is to inform the agricultural sector of alternative
farming techniques. The technique under investigation is the vertical farming of wheat,
which will be compared to the conventional farming of the same crop, both systems lo-
cated in Denmark. Hot spots will be identiﬁed for life cycle stages and processes. Further
scenarios regarding possible upcoming technology trends are investigated to study the
environmental impact of these. The toxicity proﬁle of the conventional system is exam-
ined as a toxicity assessment of the pesticide used. This study can be used as documen-
tation for the environmental performance of the two agricultural systems producing the
same crop at the same location.
2.2
Limitations Due to Methodological Choices
Seven impact categories of high agricultural relevance are presented in the main report,
while the rest is presented in the appendix. Included in the main report are: climate
change, fresh water and marine eutrophication, fresh water ecotoxicity, water and land
use and fossil resource use (11). The study does not analyse the economical or social as-
pects of the two farming systems, only the environmental performance.
The two base systems are modelled with the same potential output, as newer data re-
garding optimized and modiﬁed wheat improving the yield in vertical farming are still
under investigation. The use of the end-product is not included in this study, as the ﬁnal
life stage is harvesting, hence focusing on cradle-to-gate. Furthermore, the texture, size,
nutritional value and taste are not differentiated, as both systems uses the same wheat
variety. After production, the wheat straw is not accounted for or credited by in any of
the systems.
The conventional system is modelled with average Danish data. This includes the soil
type, no additional ﬁeld watering, pesticide usage, sowing and harvesting method. Hence,
the results may vary if compared to a speciﬁc location in Denmark. For the vertical sys-
tem, the equipment is highly inﬂuenced by estimates and assumptions as no full scale
system is currently in operation which focus on indoor wheat production. It is not pos-
sible to do a Monte Carlo simulation as the software used (SimaPro) does not contain
that function in the available version. Considering the toxic impact of the conventional
farming system, the Ecoinvent database is underestimating the current impact. A newer

2.3
Reasons for Carrying Out the LCA Study and Decision Context
In order to react to the negative environmental impact that the agricultural industry has
on the planet (4), alternatives or ”improved” systems need to be investigated and imple-
mented with the ultimate goal of having zero environmental impact. This study inves-
tigates wheat production as wheat is the top cereal crop produced in Denmark, hence
assumed of having a high environmental load (9). The general decision basis is relying
on information from studies and investigations such as this to evaluate how an alterna-
tive agricultural method performs versus the traditional practice, thereby expanding the
knowledge base. This study evaluates the environmental performance of conventional
farming versus vertical farming by comparing midway impact scores using the Environ-
mental Footprint (EF) 3.0 method. No such study has been made in Denmark, which
gives basis for conducting this LCA. Due to the setting of this study, it is considered a
situation A micro-scale decision context, since the alternative system is not implemented
in Denmark and will not affect the current line of production of wheat (12).
2.4
Target Audience
This study is being conducted as a master thesis at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU), therefore DTU becomes the main audience. Besides the main audience, this re-
port targets the Danish agricultural stakeholders and agents within the sector, to create
awareness of alternatives such as vertical farming.
2.5
Commissioner and Other Inﬂuential Actors
As this is a master thesis conducted at DTU, DTU becomes the commissioner and the
reviewing part. No ﬁnancial aid has been involved in the making of this report. No data
has been provided. All data has been found through literature and databases of as high
relevance as possible.

3.3
Life Cycle Inventory Modelling Framework
This study is deﬁned as a situation A. Thus, according to the ILCD guidelines, a frame-
work can be identiﬁed which can be applied to the LCI (12), (13). The guidelines rec-
ommend using an attributional approach for this situation. Average processes are used
when modelling the background processes. When handling multi-functional processes,
subdivision is recommended, following the hierarchy of: subdivision →system expan-
sion →substitution →allocation. Since the main database Ecoinvent 3 does not con-
tain a speciﬁc subdatabase for subdivision, this study uses the consequential database of
Ecoinvent 3. This means using system expansion for handling multi-functional processes
unless stated otherwise.
The database of Ecoinvent 3 - consequential, has mainly been used to model both the
vertical- and conventional system. By using the attributional database, the model uses
average processes and follows the ILCD guidelines when handling multi-functional pro-
cesses (12). The method used to obtain midpoint characterisation factors is EF 3.0, which
has build-in normalized and weighted sets.

4.2.1
Conventional System
Preparation
The processes within this life stage has been divided into four main processes: fertilizers,
ﬁeld operations, seeds and transport of seeds. As the ﬁeld is considered of average Dan-
ish soil, the fertilizers used are those of nitrogen as N, potassium as K2O and phosphate
as P2O5. Beyond these, calcium is being added to adjust the pH of the soil to optimize
plant growth. While the fertilizers are modelled by global processes, the data is gathered
from a Danish study (10). Field operations are that of ploughing and sowing by tractor
with adequate machinery both modelled after system processes from Switzerland, which
include fuel consumption, maintenance, shed, emissions, transfer to ﬁeld (1 km). The in-
put seeds are standard winter seeds assumed to have a 30 km transport associated with

the process.
Growth
Not much attention is needed at this stage as nutrients, water and energy all come from
the natural ecosphere. The processes included are: pesticide, spread of pesticide and lu-
bricating oil for the machinery. A roundup pesticide is used, with the active ingredient
being glyphosate. The application method used for the pesticide is by broadcaster, tak-

lubricating oil.
Harvest
The ﬁnal stage includes the processes: harvesting, drying and transport. For the harvest

ploughing processes. If the water content within the grain is higher than 15 %, the need
for drying applies (10). The transportation covers from ﬁeld to farm, and is modelled
using a tractor with trailer.
4.2.2
Vertical System
As this is a novel technology, this system has been subject to estimates and assumptions.
Preparation
The processes included: Building and equipment, fertilizers, seeds and transport, sow-
ing and water. The building uses an average process for a steel constructed hall, which
supports the area of growth but at one ﬁfth of the area used in the conventional system,
as the wheat is being grown at ﬁve ﬂoors. Included are infrastructure and equipment
space. The equipment included all the practical elements for the system to operate, plas-
tics, metal, pumps, LED’s and growth media. Some equipment data have been supplied
by Nordic Harvest for another master thesis from 2019 (18), these are conﬁrmed by mail
to still be relevant.
For the fertilizers, all macro and micro nutrients have been added following the guide-
lines from Yara, including nitrogen, potassium, phosphate, magnesium, calcium, zinc,
sulfur, manganese, copper, boric acid, iron (17). As a results of the fertilizers only being
in contact with water and wheat, a reduction in emissions by 70% have been introduced
to the model. Seeds and transport include the same assumptions as for the conventional
system. Sowing has been modiﬁed to ﬁt that of the vertical system, using an electric mo-
tor to power the system. Finally the water input is calculated as water uptake for wheat.
Which is roughly 15% of the total output of grain and straw, plus an additional 10% due
to loss and evaporation not captured by the emission ﬁlter.

hence this life stage is modelled with: A/C, irrigation and plumbing, lighting and emis-

ventional system, drying is a process added in this life stage with similar assumptions.

Assumptions

The following assumptions affecting the conventional system: a) the grain has a higher

4.4.1
Signiﬁcant Difference
When comparing the environmental performance of two systems, it is important to know
the breaking points of signiﬁcant difference in each impact category. This is a widely
debated topic, and often experts decide the estimations (21). For climate change the
estimate is generally 35%, meaning if the difference is less than a factor of 1.35, the results
are considered inconclusive (22). For the freshwater ecotoxicity category, the uncertainty
factor is very high, around a factor of 100-10,000 (22). This is due to the nature of how
toxicity is measured, with many conversion factors affecting the results. In this study for
the toxicity impact category the factor of 100 was chosen to reﬂect signiﬁcant difference.
For the remaining impact categories, a factor of 5 have been applied (21).
4.4.2
Uncertainty Analysis
Acknowledging uncertainty within the model is important to fully understanding the
ﬁnal results. This section will explain where the uncertainty is located by looking at the
systems at a system process level using the pedigree matrix approach, ﬁrst suggested by
Weidema in 1996 (23). This method is used to score data quality in ﬁve different indica-
tors by adding an associated score from the best 1) to the worst 5), for every process. See
appendix E for the pedigree matrix applied.
The ﬁrst data quality indicator is data Reliability, which is used to rate the ﬂow of the
system by looking at whether data is estimated, calculated or measured and the veriﬁca-
tion of these. This is covering the methodological approach and consistency.
Next is data Completeness check. This indicator focuses on the input and output of the
system, in accordance with the system boundaries. Where the input includes natural
resources, product inputs, capital inputs and service inputs, the outputs focus on the
product, emission and waste. The higher the detail for each process, the better the score.
The data correlation is divided into three indicators. The Temporal Correlation is check-
ing the relevance for the data in respect to how new or old the data is. The Geographical
Correlation evaluates the data on the geographical area, hence how relevant the data is
to this study depending on the geographical differences. The ﬁnal indicator is that of

6.1
Signiﬁcant Issues
Identifying the potential issues which can lead to a distorted LCA result is important,
thus these issues need to be found and checked. The method of EF 3.0 is applied when
ﬁnding the impact scores for the midpoint characterizing impact categories in this study,
hence the choice for this method needs to be tested against another method. Checking
for methodology robustness is not enough, as the results need to be evaluated to external
studies with similar setup and output to verify the validation and correct order of mag-
nitude of the results beyond the study. Lastly the identiﬁcation of sensitivity of the most
contributing process for each of the impact categories in focus needs to be analysed to
understand the potential uncertainty such processes inﬂict.

CONCLUSION AND LIMITATIONS
8
Conclusion and Limitations
When evaluating the environmental performance of two systems with the same function,
service or product, the LCA framework offers a valuable insight into the impact of the
systems which is presented by the method used. Relying on the method of EF 3.0 the
conventional farming system seems to have the lowest impact compared to the vertical
farming system, when producing 1,000 kg of wheat grain, across all midpoint impact
categories presented.
The biggest difference was observed in the impact category of land use, where the con-
ventional system outperformed the vertical by a factor of 227. The second highest was
for fossil resource use by a factor of 101 and the third was for fresh water eutrophication
with a factor of 60. The closest impact category for the two systems was that of water
use, with a factor of 19, which is still signiﬁcant.
A hot spot analysis was conducted and it was found that the life stage for the vertical
system contributing the most to the high environmental impact was the growth stage.
Within this stage the process of lighting had by far the highest contribution, which was
identiﬁed by a process contribution analysis. It was found that the lighting process was
accountable for more than 90% of the total impact across all impact categories for the full
vertical system. In the conventional system, the most contributing life stage seemed to
be that of preparation, with fertilizer as the most contributing process.
An uncertainty analysis was conducted using the pedigree matrix approach. This iden-
tiﬁed that the vertical system was highly inﬂuenced by uncertainty across all life stages
due to data quality, where the conventional system had mostly basic data quality. This
information was paired with a sensitivity check on a system process level, which was
performed on the highest contribution processes to indicate if the processes were sen-
sitive to uncertainty. It was found that for the vertical system the lighting process was
sensitive to uncertainty in all impact categories. For the conventional system, the process
concerning the fertilizers were sensitive to ﬁve out of seven impact categories, and the
seeds were sensitive in the land use category.
The lighting process had by far the highest inﬂuence on the results, hence scenarios con-
cerning this process were evaluated for the vertical system. It was found that in 2030
the potential of the vertical system increased by improving the LED efﬁciency and ad-
justing the electricity mix to reﬂect 2030. Further was the energy sources evaluated by
testing onshore wind power, offshore wind power and solar power. While all signiﬁ-
cantly improved the system, the best over all option seemed to be onshore wind power.
Combining the ﬁndings in these scenarios led to the development of the best case sce-
42
==PAGE:51==

nario, improved LED efﬁciency and electricity generated by onshore wind power. This
signiﬁcantly improved the overall performance of the vertical system, but not enough to
compete with the conventional system. The closest impact category was in fresh water
ecotoxicity, with a factor of 2, and the worst was fresh water eutrophication with a factor
of 6.
From worst impact category of land use in the baseline vertical system to the best case
scenario was a factor of 55 improvement, which was the biggest improvement observed.
The reason was due to the massive electrical demand by the vertical system in the light-
ing process, where wood chips from various tree types as part of the input for the elec-
tricity generation resulted in the big difference for the two baseline systems. However,
this was changed when changing the electricity mix, and moving away from burning
wood chips which resulted in the big improvement.
Overall this suggests that farming wheat in a indoor vertical hydroponic system, is not
feasible with current technology with the electricity consumption in the lighting process
being the main barrier, as wheat is a very energy demanding crop. However, the uncer-
tainty found both by the pedigree matrix and in the sensitivity analysis suggest that the
results does not come without a healthy portion of uncertainty for the vertical system.
The two baseline systems are well within signiﬁcant differences and this is somewhat
true for the best case scenario. However, not having fully quantiﬁed the uncertainty
and the difference is calculated between a factor of 2 and 6 for the best case vertical sce-
nario compared to the conventional system, no deﬁnite signiﬁcance can be stated without
knowing the uncertainty. Though, the uncertainty needs to be around 600% for the best
case vertical system to perform equally or better than the conventional system.
A toxicity analysis was conducted on the active ingredient in the pesticide used by the
conventional system. It was identiﬁed that glyphosate had the tendency to stay in the
soil, while roughly 20% migrated to the fresh water network, over a period of 9 days
after application. The highest environmental potential impact was from the fresh water
to the fresh water ecosystem. For human exposure, the highest potential pathway was
air, but this had little consequence as it showed glyphosates residence time is very short
in the air compartment.
8.1
Recommendations
For future work it is recommended:
• that the uncertainty for the vertical system is quantiﬁed at a more detailed level,
especially the electricity consumption, to provide a better comparison of the two
systems. This could be done by running a Monte Carlo simulation using different
software, such as EASETECH or openLCA that support such feature

• to investigate the efﬁciency of a lab experiment growing wheat under CEA con-
ditions to reduce overall uncertainty thus give a more accurate emphasize to the
differences between the vertical and conventional system with higher data speci-

• to include the social aspect of why vertical farming have a future in the agricultural
sector, as the potential to produce year round and at greater quantities is signiﬁcant
compared to the conventional practices
• to model hybrid CEA systems, where the sun light is utilized in combination with
