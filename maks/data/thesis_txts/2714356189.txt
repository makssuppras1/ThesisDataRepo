Abstract
This thesis explore and investigate a new hybrid robotics concept called QUADRU-
COPTERPED (Quadrucopterped), and the feasibility of using it to explore and in-
vestigate places where robots have limited access. This robotics concept combines a
quadruped robot’s four-legged locomotion and a quadcopter’s flying ability. The main
focus of the thesis is to test the robot in Gazebo [24] simulation and observe the behavior.
Moreover, this thesis will investigate the challenges and feasibility of implementing the
proposed concept.
The proposed robot system will expand the range of motion in the physical world
using legged motion and flying capability. Moreover, it will provide flexible operation
for the complex task where other robots have diﬀicult access, such as inspecting places
like caves and underground areas. Also, this thesis will discuss the approaches taken to
design the proposed concept, importing the design to Gazebo [24] simulation, integra-
tion of Robot Operating System (ROS) [37] control frameworks for flying and walking
locomotion.
Combining both locomotions using a hybrid state machine, testing the
performance, and finally, testing the hybrid state machine in various scenarios.
The results of this thesis showcase that the proposed concept is feasible and prac-
tical. Moreover, the robot was tested in various conditions with the help of Gazebo
[24] software simulation and ROS [37] software framework. The control framework for
legged locomotion and flight maneuver has been integrated with the system and tested.
A hybrid state machine has been implemented to manage the movement depending on
the various scenarios and states. Various conditions have been created in Gazebo [24]
software simulation and studied the system’s behavior. The proposed hybrid system has
tested different setups. This includes testing the robot in repetitive take-off and land-
ing, testing on an inclined plane, and finally testing using external range finder sensors
mounted on the robot. The testing of Quadrucopterped using Gazebo [24] simulation
has successfully been conducted and proven the feasibility of further development.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Legged and flying robots are an active field of robotics research and are used in many
applications. Legged robots have a tremendous advantage in exploring uneven terrain
because of the low contact area made by the legs and the ground. A quadruped robot
can mimic animal walking and perform diﬀicult walking on highly rough surfaces. This
feature favors inspecting rough terrain and extreme places humans cannot access. In
addition, drones have superior air support, enabling them to move fast from one point
to another. Combining these technologies and creating a hybrid robotics system can
solve the fast agility and inspection demand required in many places like caves, disaster-
affected areas, radio-active areas, and many more.
1.1
Motivation
The motivation of this project is to push the hybrid robotics development by extending
the robot’s maneuverability by combining legged locomotion and flying ability. Legged
robots have a better advantage due to low contact area with the ground than wheel
robots, and this enables legged robots to explore discontinuous terrain very effectively.
In addition, autonomous aerial vehicles have been used extensively in many applications,
and tremendous research is happening in this domain. The main advantage of drones is
their air superiority, enabling them to travel considerable distances quickly. Combining
the ability to walk in terrain with four legs and flying will give a substantial advantage
in exploring rugged landscapes.
This idea of combining legged locomotion and flight maneuver can be aligned with
flying organisms in nature. Most flying organisms have legs to stand on the surface to
perform specific tasks, such as birds fetching food on the floor, bees collecting honey
from the flower, etc. Legs enable them to stand on a suitable surface and perform the
desired task. Meanwhile, they can let their wings take a rest and regain energy for
performing the next flight. Adopting this agility from nature can unlock the robotics
system’s potential and eliminate the risk of failure due to limitations in locomotion.
1.2
Problem Statement
Combine legged locomotion and flying capability to a single robotics system called
Quadrucopterped and test the feasibility and challenges of the system using software

Objectives
The main intention of the thesis is to solve the limitation of locomotion by developing
a hybrid robotics concept called Quadrucopterped combining legged locomotion and
flying capability. The project’s desired outcome is the simulation of the robot in the
Gazebo [24] simulator using the Robot Operating System (ROS) [37] software framework.
The result obtained from the Gazebo [24] simulation will be observed, and study the
feasibility of the robot for further improvement.
The Key Performance Indicator (KPI) of the project will be measured by validating
whether the robot is achieved the combined walking and flying maneuverability.
In
addition, the hybrid state machine which controls the flying and walking maneuverability
will be tested, and the system’s performance will be measured using different scenarios
in simulation.The methods for testing and measuring the performance of hybrid state
machines include
⇒Repetitive take-off and landing of robot.
⇒Testing robot with the inclined surface.
⇒Testing the hybrid state machine using an external sensor.

Software Control Frameworks
One of the critical performance indicators 1.3 of the thesis is to perform simple walk-

control framework. To establish the smooth and desired control of the system, various
control frameworks implemented using the ROS [37] library have been explored. This

multi-rotor aerial vehicles in ROS [37].
2.4.1
Legged-Robot Control Frameworks
Once the design and URDF development is accomplished, the next step towards achiev-
ing the key performance indicator 1.3 is to perform simple legged locomotion in Gazebo
==PAGE:25==
2.4 Software Control Frameworks
11
[24] simulation using ROS [37] control framework. The background study regarding the
available ROS [37] control framework for legged locomotion is carried out in this section.
Control framework called CHAMP [17] is widely used for controlling legged robots
using ROS [37] platform. The fundamental method of this control framework is based
on the hierarchical control algorithm for quadrupedal locomotion proposed by Jongwoo
Lee et al.[25]. This research address the main challenges for developing the application,
like running at high speed and maneuvering in specific mode like throttling, galloping,
etc. The control framework CHAMP [17] is mainly derived from the main focus of this
research of controlling ground reaction force on the toes of each leg. In addition, four
legs’ coordination is critical while performing stable locomotion.
robotoc [19]- eﬀicient robot Optimal Control solvers developed by Katayama S et al.
consists of various control approach for legged robots. One of the practical approaches to
controlling the legged robot using robotoc [19] control framework is implemented based
on the research done by Katayama S et al.[21]. This work studied how to model the
quadruped into a switched system and use the Newton-type method [29] to optimize the
control under a given set of sequence modes. The study formulates a nonlinear program
(NLP), which will compute the local convergence of a Newton-type method [29].
robotoc [19] consists of another control framework based on the proposed by Katayama
S et al. [20]. This work focuses on the method for improving the convergence properties
of Newton-type methods [29] proposed by the previous work [21]. It is an improved
method of the prior way [21] of using the nonlinear program by computing local con-
vergence of a Newton-type method [29]. This method has reduced computational cost
compared to the previous Newton-type method [29] proposed [21].
Reinforcement learning-based control frameworks are a good call for controlling the
legged system effectively. The recent research by Zipeng Fu et al.[10] uses a control
and optimization method using model-free reinforcement learning as the optimizer for

3.3.1
Analysis of Quadruped control frameworks
CHAMP [17] control framework for legged robot is the implementation of Hierarchical
control system proposed by Jongwoo Lee et al [26] in Robot Operating System (ROS)
[37]. This is one of the control systems implemented during the early stages of MIT
Cheetah [22] development. Approaching the system from lower to higher levels of com-
ponents is a widely accepted and simple control strategy. It requires less computation
power and does not require many optimizations; the control method tears down the
control problem into three major contexts.
• Stabilization of the entire robot body.
• Control of ground reaction force exerted on each leg.
• Gait pattern to maintain stability.
Solving each of the mentioned challenges in control with a specific approach is an eﬀicient
methodology, Which is a straightforward way of dealing with a sophisticated system
like Quadruped eﬀiciently. Since the control methodology only cares about these three
factors, the computation cost is meager, and the control system is very energy eﬀicient.
robotoc [19]- eﬀicient ROBOT Optimal Control solvers developed by Katayama S et
al. are an excellent choice for controlling legged robots. However, the proposed method,
especially the improved Newton-type methods proposed by Katayama S et al. [20] is
based on the formulated non-linear program that will compute the local convergence
of Newtons methods still requires a good amount of computation power.
Since the
computational cost and energy budget is critical for the system during its development,
it is not an ideal candidate for system design. Moreover, the methodology of the system
follows a simple design rather than a sophisticated approach.

The control optimization method proposed by Zipeng Fu et al. [10] using model-

4.1
Study of four-legged animal walking
The movement of quadruped robots is inspired by observing the leg dynamics of four-
legged animals. They achieve motion by successively lifting their legs and swig, then
land on the ground. There are four types of movement performed by four-legged animals,
which are simple walking, trotting, canting, and gallop.
The project’s primary focus will be achieving simple walking on the Quadrucopterped.
As part of studying the simple walking conducted by four-legged animals, the work
done by photographer Eadweard Muybridge [15] has been referred. According to the
observation, simple walking is achieved by periodic and repetitive execution of swing and
stance movement in a sequence. The entire series of one cycle from stance and swing is
called stride, also called the gait cycle [39].
4.2

As described in the section 4.1, a detailed study of four-legged animals walking is the

This section describes a detailed

The walking part of the Quadrucopterped system consists of four legs, each with three
joints. The joints on each leg are called the hip joint, upper leg joint, and lower leg joint.
The hip joint connects the robot torso and the leg. The upper leg joint connects the hip
link with the upper leg link. The lower leg joint connects the upper leg link to the lower
leg. The four-legged mechanism has 12 degrees of freedom. Figure 4.1 illustrates the
simple isometric view of the legged system that will be used to develop Quadrucopterped.

4.3.2.2
Synchronization using feedback
It is essential to synchronize the gait pattern generated by the modulator with the
corresponding environment of the swing and stance phase based on the touch-down
event. Therefore precision in measuring the touch-down and lift-off event is critical. In
most legged robot research, force sensory feedback is used for measuring these events.
However, the control system proposed by Jongwoo Lee et al. [26] uses low mechanical
impedance characteristics of the leg to detect the touch-down event. This method doesn’t
require any additional force sensor. When the leg touches the ground, the radial force
towards the outward direction is applied by impedance control.
This will delay the
sudden flexion of the leg due to the ground reaction force due to impact. This value
is higher than the command during the swing phase. Deploying a limit switch that
monitors this radial force threshold can easily measure the touch-down event.
4.3.3
Leg Trajectory Generator
The phase signal generated by the gait pattern modulator of CHAMP [17] control frame-
work will feed to the leg trajectory generator and produce desired trajectory output for
each leg to perform the required locomotion. The reference work of this control frame-
work Jongwoo Lee et al. [25] states that trajectories for each swing phase and stance
phase are designed separately to enable the smooth and continuous transition.
The swing phase trajectories are designed using Bézier curve [25] using 12 control
points. Moreover, the stance phase trajectories are intended as a sinusoidal wave with a
period of twice the length of stroke length. The critical element used while designing the
trajectory for the swing phase is to elongate the leg to achieve enough ground clearance
to avoid obstacles. While integrating the CHAMP [17] framework with created URDF,
this factor will be important when tuning the control. The detailed discussion regarding
trajectory generators is beyond the scope of this thesis. Since the project uses the built-in
trajectory generator for legs from the CHAMP [17] library, there is no need to design the
trajectory generator separately. The research was done by Jongwoo Lee et al. [25] can
be referred to understand more detailed design aspects of the leg trajectory generator.

Leg control
Finally, the leg trajectories are sent to the legs to perform desired locomotion. The
first step is converting the trajectory position to corresponding joint angles. The inverse
kinematics using analytical method discussed in the section 4.3.4.1.2 has been used to
convert from the desired position [x,y,z] to angle for each joint [θ1, θ2, θ3]. The CHAMP

publisher module in ROS [37].
The joint state publisher will give the current state
of the joint and will be used as feedback to control the joint while performing legged
locomotion. The overall leg control has been given in figure 4.6. As illustrated in the
figure, the input position [x,y,z] from the leg trajectory will be given to the inverse
kinematics block. The output of the inverse kinematics block will be joint angles [θ1,
θ2, θ3] and feed to the Proportional–integral–derivative controller (PID) [1] of leg joints.
Finally, the leg joint motors will receive the signals, and the desired actuation will be
performed. The joint state publisher will be sending the current joint of the legs. These
readings will be given to the forward kinematics block and converted into [x,y,z] positions,
this will be used as the feedback for the leg control, and the corresponding correction

4.3.4.2
Stabilization of the robot
One of the significant and critical challenges regarding controlling the robot is the stabi-
lization of the torso; to address this challenge, an IMU based correction is implemented
in CHAMP [17] control framework. While discussing this simulation aspect of robot
stabilization, the IMU sensor plugin is used with robot URDF using Gazebo [24] to
stream the data and calculate odometry. This stabilization approach’s primary focus
is to ensure that the robot does not lose the projected center of mass outside of the
polygon support made by the legs. The fundamental working of stabilization using IMU
is by calculating the position and orientation of the center of the body using forward
kinematics and transformation matrix. That value will be compared with IMU measure-
ment and estimate the error. An Extended Kalman Filter-based Odometry estimation
node is used to avoid error accumulation due to continuous IMU measurement. The
detailed discussion regarding this system is beyond the scope of the thesis. For more
details, please refer to the code base of CHAMP [17].

URDF design
To simulate the Quadrucopterped in Gazebo [24], a Universal Robot Description Format

the Quadrucopterped is developed by collecting all the information from the literature
review and theoretical aspect. The major design choice for developing the URDF is
that the URDF should be compatible with CHAMP [17] control framework for legged
locomotion and RotorS-MAV Gazebo[11] framework for the flight control simulation
using ROS [37].
Figure 5.8 illustrate how the joints and links are connected in URDF. Moreover,
figure 5.8 explains how one single leg unit is constructed from the base link using joints
and connections. For simplicity and easiness of understanding, the figure shows the
kinematic chain of only one leg. (All four legs have the same kinematics chain). The
main difference between the four legs is that they are attached to the torso in different

positions to support the body. The torso of the robot is defined as the base_link of the
Quadrucopterped. Moreover, the centroid of the torso is specified as the origin of the
base_link. The remaining links and joints are attached to the origin of the base_link
with specific coordinates measured from the CAD model using Solid Works software.
The URDF of the Quadrucopterped is divided mainly into two parts, legged loco-
motion and flight maneuvering. The legged locomotion part consists of four legs, each
identified with a prefix representing the leg’s position.
The kinematic chain of the leg consists of four joints and four links. Each of the joints
and link names consists of a prefix representing the leg’s position for identification. The
HIP_JOINT connects between base_link and HIP link of the leg, and this joint provides
coupling between torso and starting joint of leg. The parent link of HIP_JOINT is
base_link, and the child link is HIP link. The THIGH_JOINT connects between the
HIP link and THIGH_LINK, the parent link of THIGH_JOINT is the HIP link, and
the child link is THIGH_LINK.
The THIGH_FOOT_JOINT is connecting between the THIGH link and the FOOT
_LINK, the parent link of THIGH_FOOT_JOINT is the THIGH link, and the child
link is FOOT_LINK.
Finally, a fixed joint called the TOE joint connects the FOOT and TOE links. The
TOE link is added for the experiment with friction value and foot contact.
The URDF of the flight maneuvering part consists of four rotors attached to base_link.
Each rotor link is connected with base_link using a joint named rotor_joint. The parent
link of rotor_joint is base_link, and the child link is rotor link with prefix. The rotor
joint is a continuous type joint, and the prefix of joint and child link is based on the cor-
responding position with respect to the torso(lh_rotor,lf_rotor,rh_rotor,rf_rotor). The
link name will be mapped to the numbering convention for the RotorS-MAV Gazebo
[24] control framework[11] to enable the framework for identifying the rotor for flight
maneuver.

control. However, the CHAMP [17] control framework focuses on supporting the
main body while performing legged locomotion that will prevent the quadruped

5.6
Hybrid System Design
Since Quadrucopterped is a hybrid system equipped with both Quadruped and Quad-
copter maneuvering capability, it needs a system capable of handling the sudden tran-
sition from one type of maneuverability to another. The system can be simplified and
broken down into a Hybrid dynamic system mentioned in the section 4.3.1 developed by
Thomas A Henzinger. [14].
Figure 5.10: The hybrid system of proposed hybrid robot control.
The proposed hybrid system consists of Guardwalk to fly, Guardfly to walk and the action
called Transition map figure 5.10.When the system is in the condition Guardfly to walk ,
the transition from flying to walking will occur after the safe landing of the robot. The
rotors will be powered off, and the system will be ready to perform walking. During this
transition of state, the transition map will switch to 0 to 1 and the CHAMP [17] control
framework and associated ROS [37] nodes will activate. The Guardwalk to fly consists of
switching condition between walking to flying, and the hybrid control will change to
the flight control state, and the MAV control framework [11] will activate. During this
transition,the transition map will switch to 1 to 0, the legs will stop moving, the rotors
will power up,and the system will be ready to take-off.
The landing event is verified by the touch-down event of all four legs using the
CHAMP [17] control nodes for four-legged locomotion mentioned in section 2.4. The
transition map will verify the leg touch-down events. The system will send zero velocity
commands to stop the rotors when the proper touch-down is triggered. In addition to
that, the odometry value will use to verify the adequate landing occurred at this stage.
Figure 5.11 illustrates the proposed hybrid system layer. As illustrated in the figure

5.11 the key observation made by the transition map is from odometry data, data from

By observing foot contact, the transition map will verify that the robot stays on
the ground or is flying and send the corresponding switching phase signal for choosing
the corresponding control framework to achieve the desired maneuver. Observing only
the foot contact is not a suitable validation method to understand the current state of
the robot. In some cases, there is a probability that the robot will be sliding through
the inclined surface due to low friction, then causing the robot to plummet. During
such cases, it is crucial to observe the odometry data and switch to flight control rather
than entirely relying on foot contact.

Universal Robot Description Format

Universal Robot Description Format (URDF) [38] is a standard XML format to represent
a robot model in terms of all details, including joints, links, and related properties.
Especially for the application for simulation, analysis, and control design tasks for robot
models URDF is widely used. Moreover ROS [37] support URDF [38] format and it is
easy to use it for simulation experiments in Gazebo [24].
The URDF[38] of the Quadrucopterped is generated using solid-works software and
optimized with the help of Gazebo [24] simulation environment. To generate URDF of
the robot, joints and links discussed in the section 4.2 has been used. The prefix of the
joints and links are based on the position of the legs discussed in the section 4.2 : [LH-Left
Hind, LF-Left Front, RH-Right Hind, RF-Right Front]. This naming is chosen to enable
easy identification of links and joints while debugging and troubleshooting the URDF
file. Moreover, the CHAMP [17] framework is also following a similar naming convention.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
In this chapter, the primary discussion will be observations noticed during the testing
and result analysis and sharing information regarding the limitation of the current im-
plementation of the project. At the end of the chapter, future work and methods of
improving the system will be discussed.
The project’s major accomplishment is solving limitation of locomotion by developing
hybrid robotics concept called Quadrucopterped by combining legged locomotion and
flying capability. The final 1.3 objective of the thesis is achieved by producing simulation
of the Quadrucopterped robot in the Gazebo [24] simulator using ROS [37] software
framework. The result obtained from the Gazebo [24] simulation is observed and the
result assuring that the feasibility of Quadrucopterped is possible.
The URDF of the Quadrucopterped is created and integrated with the CHAMP [17]
control framework for legged locomotion and RotorS - MAV control framework for flight
maneuver. A hybrid state machine that will manage control and maneuverability based
on the system state has been implemented and tested. Further development like robust
control system, integration of external sensors, design changes, and implementation of
navigation algorithms needed for the further stage of developing hardware and testing it
in real-world scenarios. One key drawback of the system observed is that the proposed
hybrid system is not ready to handle complex maneuvering, and it needs more testing
using sensors like Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) or depth cameras. Also, the
touch-down event of legs, measured using the CHAMP [17] control framework, gives
some noise. This will affect the performance of the transition map that enables the
transition from flight maneuvering [MAV [11] control] to legged locomotion [CHAMP
[17] control]. A more accurate method of measuring the touch-down event of the legs is
essential for robust hybrid control system development.
Furthermore, since the system is lightweight and the swing height of the leg is min-
imal, the robot might not be able to handle very complex uneven terrain using legs.
Hence the navigation algorithm or planning algorithm which will be developed in the
future should be focused on strategic decisions based on the limitation of the Quadru-
copterped. For example, more giant robots with bigger legs can tackle more significant
obstacles, perform better walking, and spend more time on ground surfaces rather than
flying in a dynamic environment. The flight control looks pretty robust and reliable
for this robot. However, if computational cost constraints are not a significant demand,
more sophisticated control system methods can be tested with the system and compare
the performance.

9 Discussion
9.1
Limitations of the System
There are a few limitations observed while testing the Quadrucopterped in simulation.
Because the robot is still in the proof of development stage, there will be many challenges
while deploying into actual hardware. Since the gap between the simulation and the
natural world is vast, research and development need to be done for further hardware
development.More study regarding design changes will be essential to accommodate the
actual weight of the system with the legs.
An analysis regarding disturbance and strategies to minimize the disturbance of the
system is lacking in the project. For example, the legs will be folded towards the body
to reduce the disturbance during flight maneuvers and increase aerodynamics, and this
behavior is naturally observable in birds. Such strategies and control approaches are not
included in the system.
The hybrid state machine, which manages the transition between legged locomotion
and flight maneuver, needs to be tested with more sensors and scenarios and redesign the
transition and control methodology. Primarily since the system is targeted to deploy in
places like caves, subterranean areas, and other dynamic environments, the system with
quick response, which has robustness, is essential for the deployment. The system needs
to be equipped with a navigation planner to make decisions based on a map created by
sensors and perform an energy-eﬀicient maneuver, saving time. One of the experiments
on testing proximity sensors with the proposed transition map was unsuccessful, mainly
because the proximity sensor was not enough to understand the complex environment
and detect the object in front of the robot. More sophisticated sensors and approaches
are needed to verify this system.
9.2
Future Work
The major factors limiting the mentioned development are the lack of resources. The
primary focus of the future work would be developing hardware and testing the prototype.
Further, more development of more robust control strategies by analyzing and modeling
the system and implementation of a navigation algorithm is required. Testing with the
various sensor can be executed in the future to understand which sensor would be best
suited for the system.
As part of developing the robust navigation algorithms, decision layers like Markov
decision process (MDP) [2] can be explored in the future. The decision-making algo-
rithms are essential for this kind of hybrid robot system to make energy-optimized path
planning algorithms. There are many other approaches like reinforcement learning [18]
where the robot model will be trained using data from sensors, and a robust navigation
strategy can be deployable. Moreover, the other mentioned control methodologies in
the literature review section need to be explored in the future.
Once the system is complete in hardware, it should be tested in a natural world.

and more optimization and agility should be implemented. This fundamental research
and further development require significant resources, time, and funding.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The Key Performance Indicator of the thesis is achieved, and the expected outcome is
validated with KPI metrics. The hybrid robotics system, Quadrucopterped, capable of
performing combined legged motion and flight maneuvers, has been developed and tested
in Gazebo [24] simulation environment. The thesis has delivered the project’s expected
outcomes. Including fully functional CAD design of Quadrucopterped, Universal Robot
Description Format (URDF) of the Quadrucopterped, ROS [37] compatible simulation
framework, achieved walking and flight maneuver, hybrid state machine which enables
the switching between the legged locomotion and flight maneuver, results of hybrid state
machine tested in specific scenarios, and final analysis of the result. The simulation is
tested with various scenarios involving combined walking and flying maneuvers, stabiliz-
ing the system when it slips off from an inclined surface, and safe landing and take-off
based on the touch-down events from each leg. The results and observations have proven
that the concept is feasible and requires more robust control and navigation research.
The systems need further design improvements to reduce the weight and accommodate
more sensors and power-full computers for navigation. The various response has been
analyzed and studied from leg contact and rotors dynamics to understand the further
changes required for the proposed hybrid system.
This hybrid robot is an excellent
promise to push the limits of robotics and exploration of places that were dangerous
to humans and not accessible. Many applications which involve flight and walking ma-
neuvers can be explored and solved using this robot in the future. Since many space
and research organizations have extensively explored the opportunity to deploy flying
helicopters and legged robots on places like mars, caves, and other terrains, which have
diﬀicult access, The Quadrucopterped would be a small contribution to such robotics
exploration.
