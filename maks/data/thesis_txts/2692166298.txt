Abstract
As offshore wind energy is developing rapidly, the complex substructures and metocean
conditions challenge the controlling of ﬂoating offshore wind turbines. In order to avoid the
well-known negative damping problem, the simplest method is to reduce the bandwidth
of the basic onshore PI controller with the expense of rotor speed overshoot and poor
power track. This thesis aims to develop a generic controller to improve the stability of the
platform pitch that can be widely applied to different ﬂoaters. First, a selection of control
methodologies for ﬂoating wind turbines in recent research is reviewed. Next, a simpliﬁed
linear ﬂoating wind turbine model has been developed, which allows the control design
and parameter calculation. The open-loop and closed-loop models based on the simpliﬁed
linear ﬂoating wind turbine model are validated on the IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine
supported by the WindCrete spar buoy ﬂoater and the UMaine semisubmersible ﬂoater.
Later on, the conventional onshore tuning of DTU Wind Energy Controller is investigated
and modiﬁed for ﬂoating wind turbines. An enhanced controller with torque compensator
is proved to effectively solve the right-half-plane-zero problem and stabilize the system
above rated wind speeds. The application of gain scheduling and ﬁlters on the two ﬂoaters
is discussed in a detailed manner. Finally, the proposed control strategy is evaluated
by simulating two selected design loads cases with HAWC2. A comparison of motions,
loads and power is carried out between different controllers and ﬂoaters. The enhanced
controller with torque compensator results in notable improvements especially in platform
pitch and rotor speed, regardless of the ﬂoater type and loads conditions.

1
Introduction
As the concern for climate change and carbon emission rises in the recent decades,
renewable resources provide a trustful path to a sustainable development since they are
free and inexhaustible. Among various means of renewable energy, wind energy is a
competitive and fast-maturing technology with great potential. A few decades ago, United
Kingdom, Denmark, Germany, Sweden, and Netherlands were the pioneers on wind energy
generation. Nowadays, more countries have realized the beneﬁts and played an active role
in the market.
Fig. 1.1.: New wind power installations outlook 2020-2025 (GW) [7].
The 93 GW of new installations in 2020 brings global cumulative wind power capacity up to
743 GW. And not only that, GWEC Market Intelligence forecasts that through 2030, more
than 205 GW of new offshore wind capacity will be added globally, including at least 6.2
GW of ﬂoating offshore wind [7]. Under the shadow of the coronavirus pandemic, offshore
wind is being seen as a major contributor to a global economic recovery, since it has
been less impacted by the pandemic compared to other energy sectors while continuously
growing. Figure 1.1 shows the expected growth of wind power installations.
1.1 Floating oﬀshore wind energy
Floating Offshore Wind (FOW) holds the key to an inexhaustible resource potential in
Europe. 80% of all the offshore wind resource is located in waters depth of 60 m and
7
==PAGE:17==
deeper in European seas, where traditional bottom-ﬁxed offshore wind (BFOW) is not
economically attractive [27]. Besides, wind turbines in far seas have less interfere with
life and activities on land, but steadier atmosphere and higher mean wind velocity. The
industry is moving fast to investigate and develop ﬂoating offshore wind turbines (FOWT).
The ﬁrst full-scale ﬂoating wind turbine Hywind Demo, which has a 2.3 MW turbine and 85
meters blades, is able to operate in harsh environment and become a successful precedent
for further research since 2009. In 2017, the world’s ﬁrst ﬂoating wind farm, the 30 MW
Hywind Scotland pilot park installed at Buchan Deep in the North Sea, began to supply
clean power and veriﬁed the concepts of ﬂoating wind farms.
FOW is the future and next stage of wind industry, although traditional wind farms
remain their importance. With the industrialization and investment of manufacture and
construction, the cost of ﬂoating wind will be greatly cut down, thus promoting the energy
transition. It is exciting to see more players widening the context and capital. Various
stakeholders have established projects to participate in the game, including lead wind
companies like Siemens and oil majors like Ideol, etc.
1.1.1 Floater concepts and types
Floating wind turbines differ from conventional bottom-ﬁxed turbines mostly in the
substructure. Instead of mounting a ﬁxed foundation such as monopiles and jackets into
the seabed, a ﬂoater is designed to keep turbines upright, maintain turbine stability and
connected with a mooring system. In most cases, FOWTs share the same rotor-nacelle
system with onshore and offshore applications. But they have different tower properties to
ensure the structural response of the platform and the disposition of the reinforcement
steel and the post-tensioning [5].
Floaters can be categorized into four common types: spar-buoy, semi-submersible, barge
and tension-leg platform (TLP). The ﬁrst one, spar-buoy, usually appears as a long slender
cylinder with large ballast at the bottom in order to make the centre of gravity lower than
the centre of buoyancy. The bottom ballast helps stabilizing the system when the platform
tilts. As for the semi-sub, it is generally shaped as three or four short cylinders connected
by braces. The stability is achieved by using a large water-plane area. Similarly to a ballast,
heavy plates can be applied at the bottom to increase damping.
However, the barge usually has a plane and rectangular body. Through a large water plane
area such as a damping pool, it achieves stability using distributed buoyancy and creates
a restoring moment in case of rotational displacement [3]. For station keeping, these
three types are loosely moored to the seabed, allowing for easier installation, while the
tension-leg platform is more ﬁrmly connected to the seabed thus stablizing by mooring
line tension [15]. Though the catenary mooring system causes complex dynamics and
platform motion, TLP shows noticeable advantages: inherent platform stability, minimal
wave loading and simpliﬁed dynamics, but contrarily results in expensive anchors and

1.2 Wind turbine control
Control systems are essential for wind turbines to produce power efﬁciently as well as
withstand different operational conditions. The main objective of wind turbine control
is the compromise between maximizing power production and reducing loads, whilst
ensuring stable grid integration. Depending on actual operational conditions and owners’
requirements, more comprehensive needs can be considered in control strategies.
To approach the aforementioned objectives, variable-speed wind turbines as the most
dominant type will be outlined in this section with focus on the generator torque controller
and pitch controller.
1.2.1 Conventional control
The conventional control of variable-speed wind turbines is reviewed, and the following
formulas and theory has been introduced in [20][10]. The operating regions can be
divided into three typical regions, shown in Figure 1.3. When below cut-in or above
cut-out wind speed, the turbine is at standstill so there is no power production. Between
cut-in and cut-out(region 1, 2 and 3), the rotor rotates and actively generates the power.

However, when linearizing the equation for drivetrain motion at the operating point, the

characteristics (poles) of the closed-loop system maintain the same regardless of wind

its rated value but the power is below the rated power. Therefore, the objective is speed
regulation to regulate the rated rotor speed by using torque controller, whilst the turbine
cannot operate at optimal Cp. In some cases, region 2 is relatively narrow and negligible.
1.2.2 Challenges for ﬂoating wind turbines
Much attention has been drawn to control strategies for FOWTs. Unlike onshore and
bottom-ﬁxed offshore structures, ﬂoating wind turbines feature tower designs and more
complex structural dynamics in response to both wind and waves. Prior research has proven
that the utilization of a conventional onshore controller can cause negative aerodynamic
damping, especially at above rated wind speeds. A consequence of conventional pitch-
to-feather control of wind turbines is pointed out in [13] that steady-state rotor thrust is
reduced with increasing wind speed above rated, introducing instability in platform pitch
motion.

As the tower and platform are moving in normal operation, when the tower-top is moving
in the upwind direction in region 3, the relative wind speed met by the rotor increases.
Accordingly, the onshore controller will increase the blade pitch angle to limit power
output and thereby reduce thrust, as shown in Figure 1.5. Hence, the tower-top moves
more forwards in response to a decreasing aerodynamic force. The occurrence of negative
damping is more problematic for FOWTs, since the lowest natural frequency of the platform
mode is normally 10−1 times less than the lowest tower mode. But for bottom-ﬁxed wind
turbines, the problem can be reduced by selecting controller bandwidth below the ﬁrst
bending frequency of the tower and still ensure a good performance [24].
Fig. 1.5.: Thrust and power curve for the IEA 15 MW Offshore Reference Wind Turbine [5].
From the perspective of control theory, the problem can be described as a right-half-plane-
zero (RHPZ) problem [26]. Taking the system transfer function in Laplace domain, poles
are the roots of the denominator whilst zeros are the roots of the numerator. A linear
system is stable under the condition that the characteristic equation has all its roots in the
left-half plane. For complex-conjugate poles σ ± jω, the corresponding natural modes are
eσ sin ωt and eσ cos ωt. If poles occur on the right-half plane with positive σ, the system
response does not decay but grows.
Moreover, zeros at the right-half plane set bounds for the control bandwidth. Even in the
left half plane, their position determines the amount of fore-aft oscillation that must be
tolerated in order to achieve a given performance level of generator speed tracking [26].
In general, the closed-loop poles move towards the open-loop zeros when increasing the
feedback gain. Hence, the RHPZ problem is ampliﬁed when closing the loop using an
onshore controller with a relatively high gain.
1.2.3 Existing control methods
Several modiﬁcations on conventional control methodologies have been proposed to solve
the negative damping and improve the platform instability. One straightforward method
is to reduce the bandwidth by detuning the gains of the collective pitch controller based
on the pole placement. The eigenfrequency of the closed-loop is lower than that of the

the platform instability and rotor speed variation reﬂecting higher blade pitch activity,

maximum generator torque and therefore increased fatigue of the shaft torsion.

More advanced and complex control methods have been employed to overcome the

1.3 Thesis objectives
The objective of this study is to develop a controller for ﬂoating wind turbines to improve
the platform stability focused on the above rated wind speed region, without adding
controller complexity. The case study uses the International Energy Agency (IEA) 15 MW
offshore reference wind turbine [5] with two different ﬂoater types: the WindCrete spar
buoy [18] and the UMaine semisubmersible[2], respectively. The idea is to investigate the
practicability and adaptability of the designed controller tuning and gain selecting applied
on generic ﬂoaters.
As the ﬁrst step, a simpliﬁed linear FOWT model is derived and validated with a fully
aero-servo-hydro-elastic model HAWC2 [14]. Next, the selected control strategy is further
extended and a controller is designed based on the linear model. Simulations in two
representative loads cases are carried out to evaluate the controller performance in wind
and waves. Finally, a comparison of platform motions, loads and power is drawn against
the current detuning strategy of the conventional DTU Wind Energy controller.
1.4 Thesis structure
The report is divided into ﬁve parts.
• Chapter 2 presents the theoretical knowledge and assumptions for the linear model
derivation, including aerodynamics and hydrodynamics, taking the spar type as a
starting point. A simpliﬁed linear state-space model is established in the form of
symbolic matrices.
• Chapter 3 presents the process of obtaining matrices values from the HAWC2 model,
and then validates the linear model in MATLAB. Both open-loop and closed-loop are
compared, showing good match with HAWC2.
• Chapter 4 explores the control design that can be implemented to two different
ﬂoaters. The onshore tuning is examined and improved for FOWTs based on the
linear model. More importantly, the gain scheduling and ﬁlters are also discussed.

2.5 Aerodynamic linearization
Aerodynamic thrust and torque contribute signiﬁcant nonlinearity to the ﬂoating wind
turbine system. The thrust can be calculated by a thrust coefﬁcient depending on the
tip speed ratio and blade pitch, that is to say, the aerodynamic terms can be written as a
funtion of the wind speed relative to the rotor-plane, the rotor speed ˙φ and the blade pitch
angle β.
The linearization is applied at an operating point ( op). Assuming small deviations from
the operating point and applying Taylor series expansion, the ﬁrst order terms are kept,
while the higher order terms are omitted:
Fwind(evop −˙xt,op + ∆ev −∆˙xt, ˙φop + ∆˙φ, βop + ∆β) ≈Fwind,op + CF,v(∆ev −∆˙xt)
+ CF,ω∆˙φ + CF,β∆β
(2.36)

+ CQ,ω∆˙φ + CQ,β∆β
(2.37)
where ev is the wind speed. CF,v, CF,ω and CF,β are the derivatives of the thrust with respect
to wind speed, rotor speed and blade pitch angle. CQ,v, CQ,ω and CQ,β are similarly the
derivatives of the aerodynamic torque. Notice that these aerodynamic gradients indicate
the aerodynamic sensitivity of operating parameters, which place importance on the system
response and stability. For an onshore turbine, the values can be generated by HAWCstab2
with good accuracy. However, for a ﬂoating wind turbine, it is more difﬁcult to obtain
reliable aerodynamic gradients, which could be a problem for the succeeding controller
design. The computation approach is detailed in section 3.2.4.

3.2.4 Aerodynamic gradients
The six aerodynamic gradients(CF,v, CF,β, CF,ω, CQ,v, CQ,β and CQ,ω) are required to com-
plete the A and B matrices. They reﬂect the sensitivity of aerodynamic thrust and torque
with respect to the wind speed, blade pitch angle and rotor speed, which characterizes the
turbine performance and is important for the controller gain scheduling.
As for the IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine, the aerodynamic gradients are easily com-
puted by HAWCStab2, which is a frequency-domain aeroservoelastic code for steady states
computation and stability analysis. Unfortunately, the ﬂoating wind turbine models have
not been adequately performed in HAWCStab2. Since the negative damping phenomenon
is closely related to the aerodynamic characteristics, in order to provide accurate analysis,
the gradients are calculated manually near the operational point.
Taking CF,v = δF

shown in Table 3.5. The difference between HAWCStab2 and the calculation method is
signiﬁcant for all the gradients, which implies that the distinctive motion of the ﬂoating
wind turbine affects its aerodynamic sensitivity and thus raises a question towards the
tuning method of the basic onshore controller. Consequently, an improved gain scheduling
considering more aerodynamic gradients is suggested in section 4.2 to obtain the desired
controller frequency for ﬂoating wind turbines.

4
Control design
To start with, the main control objective is emphasized that is to reduce the platform pitch
instability and fatigue loads in above rated wind speeds. For the purpose of platform
stability, earlier work has shown the effectiveness of additional feedback loop either
to the pitch controller or the torque controller, as a modiﬁcation on the conventional
PI controller. However, prior studies have mostly validated the strategy on single type
of ﬂoating platforms, and such loads cases do not account for severe wind and wave
conditions. In this work, the implement of two methods are further investigated for both
the spar buoy and semisub ﬂoater, including the need of additional gain scheduling and
ﬁlters. Moreover, the linear model serves to place the poles and determine the extra gain.
Finally, the performance and utility of the approaches are compared and demonstrated.
4.1 Baseline controller
The available basic controller designed for the IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine is the
DTU Wind Energy Controller, which contains basic control features such as collective pitch,
generator torque proportional-integral-derivative (PID) controller and advanced control
features [19]. The minimum rotor speed is set as 5 rpm to avoid possible 3P ( three times
per revolution) resonance with the tower natural frequency, and thus at low wind speeds,
the turbine pitches the blades to track maximum power while maintaining the minimum
rotor speed. As the rotor speed increases with the wind speed, the turbine operates at
the optimal tip speed ratio at the optimal pitch angle 0◦. In above-rated wind speeds, the
rotor speed is regulated at its rated value 7.55 rpm, while the proportional-integral (PI)
controller keeps the rated power by pitching blades collectively.
For an onshore wind turbine, the poles of the closed-loop drivetrain system is usually set to
be 0.05 Hz and damping ratio of 0.7, with a traditional assumption of constant power [5].
However, when implementing to the ﬂoating wind turbine, the basic onshore controller
needs to be detuned regarding the negative damping problem. Meanwhile, the constant
torque strategy is adopted for ﬂoating systems with signiﬁcant advantages on the platform
motion. The methodology of detuning the basic controller parameters is to reduce the
bandwidth below the platform pitch mode, but to keep it as high as possible to ensure
control performance.
From a control perspective, the pole-zero map can easily demonstrate the effect of the
baseline controller frequency to the system stability. In order to do so, the linear model,
which has been developed and validated in the previous chapter, is used to ﬁnd the poles
47
==PAGE:57==
and zeros of the transfer function from blade pitch to rotor speed. Due to adequate
hydrodynamic damping, the open-loop poles are located in the left-half plane, as shown in
Figure 4.1. However, with the increasing controller frequency and corresponding larger
gains, the closed-loop poles move towards the open-loop zeros in the right-half plane.
Since the WindCrete’s pitch frequency is 0.024 Hz, the system turns out to be stable when
the controller frequency has been reduced to 0.01 Hz. Such low controller frequency
implies that the detuned basic controller is at the cost of generator over-speed. In addition,
as the positive zeros remain the same regardless of the controller frequency, the presence
of RHPZ limits the controller bandwidth.

4.3 Enhanced controller with torque compensator
This approach is to add an extra loop to the generator torque, parallel to the basic PI pitch
loop, as illustrated in Figure 4.4. The enhanced controller has the natural frequency of
0.05 Hz and damping ratio of 0.7, which is the common setting of an onshore wind turbine
controller. It is designed and implemented to both the WindCrete spar and UMaine semisub
ﬂoaters, while the gains and ﬁlters are different depending on the system properties.

Although the majority of recent research has addressed the practicability of the extra loop
to the blade pitch, it is proved that the feedback to the generator torque can effectively
compensate the RHPZ problem in contrast to the pitch damper. Through pole-zero analysis,
this approach moves both zeros and poles to the left-half plane ﬂexibly, as shown in
Figure 4.5. Another advantage is that it has an insigniﬁcant effect on the system imaginary

poles location can be referred to the desired damping of platform motion, by adjusting a
proportional gain. Apparently, since the usage of torque controller leads to a noticeable
variation in generator torque, the only drawback would be the maximum generator torque
and increased fatigue damage on the shaft. In this case, the maximum allowable generator
torque is kept as 120% in line with the original value of the DTU Wind Energy controller,
to avoid further extension on the generator capability and give a fair comparison on the
performance as well as the cost.

4.5 Summary
The basic DTU controller is detuned to reduce the bandwidth below the platform pitch
frequency to prevent instability above rated wind speeds. Since the platform natural fre-
quency has different designed value due to distinctive substructure, the baseline controller
is detuned differently. But the basic onshore tuning method is generally based on the
bottom-ﬁxed turbines considering 1-DOF drivetrain dynamics. However, for ﬂoating wind
turbines, an enhanced tuning method is proposed based on the 4-DOFs linear model,
which improves the controller quality. A control design of adding an extra loop to the
generator toque (torque compensator) is able to compensate the non-minimum phase in
the linear model. The linear model also gives an efﬁcient way to target the platform pitch
damping ratio and thus ﬁnd the required feedback gain at different operational points.
The implement of a second order band-pass ﬁlter is demonstrated in order to ﬁlter the
tower top fore-aft velocity. Besides, a control design of adding a loop to the blade pitch
angle (pitch damper) is discussed in the end. Due to the compromise between stability
and loads, and several limitations, this pitch damper remains debatable. Therefore, the
enhanced controller with torque compensator is only proposed in this thesis. The following
simulations and loads analysis are performed for the baseline controller and the proposed
controller, as listed in Table 4.5.

random seed and get the mean of the maximums at each wind speed, the extreme bending
moment is obtained. The ratio of the proposed controller to the baseline controller is
shown in Figure 5.17. In order to reduce the extreme loads of components, the smaller
ratio suggests smaller extreme loads. The greatest reduction happens to the tower base
fore-aft (FA) moment as expected, since the platform pitch is signiﬁcantly reduced and
the extreme thrust shrinks, causing less fore-aft bending moment in the tower base with a
large arm. The second biggest improvement is the tower base side-side (SS) moment, for
the reason that the rotor speed and platform pitch are better controlled. Although tower
side-side moment is always related to aerodynamic torque and the torque controller, for
the ﬂoating system with 6 DOFs, the tower side-side motion is also coupled with platform
roll and pitch. Last but not least, the extreme blade root out-of-plane (OoP) and in-plane
(IP) bending moments display relatively small difference. A conclusion can be drawn that
the proposed controller reduces the extreme bending moments of tower base, especially for
the UMaine semisub, while showing unsubstantial impacts on the blade root moment.

Conclusions and Future Work
6.1 Conclusions and key ﬁndings
In this thesis, the problem of negative damping for FOWTs in above rated region is
investigated. A linear model with 4-DOFs is developed in MATLAB for the following
control design. One of the highlights of this work is to present the symbolic equation
derivation process, illustrate how to obtain numerical values from HAWC2, and validate
the linear model with HAWC2 model in a detailed manner.
• The aerodynamic gradients are signiﬁcantly different for the same turbine with a
bottom-ﬁxed foundation and with a ﬂoating substructure.
• The linearization of hydrodynamic damping greatly depends on the ﬂoater geometry,
which differs from ﬂoater to ﬂoater. However, it is important for the linear FOWT
model to implement reliable damping matrices.
Despite that previous research raised several control methods, the control strategy of
torque compensator has not been exhaustively deployed and tested on many FOWTs,
which is addressed in this thesis.
• The basic onshore controller tuning that considers 1-DOF drivetrain dynamics is
inefﬁcient for the ﬂoating wind turbines, due to the coupling effects of platform
motion and rotor speed. Therefore, including one more aerodynamic gradient when
calculating the PI gains is proposed in section 4.2 to improve the control quality.
• It is proved that the torque compensator solves the right-half-plane zero problem.
The extra feedback gain kPx is determined by the linear model after selecting the
desired pitch damping ratio. A quadratic gain scheduling with respect to blade pitch
angle is implemented to DTU Wind Energy Controller in HAWC2.
• A second order band-pass ﬁlter is needed to capture the platform pitch and remove
the wave frequency from the tower top fore-aft velocity signal.
Two types of ﬂoaters are tested under the extreme turbulent wind in DLC 1.3 and severe
sea states in DLC 1.6, respectively. It gives an outlook of the FOWT performance in
adverse weather conditions. A threefold comparison is drawn: ﬁrstly between the baseline
controller and proposed controller, secondly between the two DLCs, and lastly between
the WindCrete spar and the UMaine semisub.
75
==PAGE:85==
• The proposed controller can better regulate the rotor speed and improve the platform
pitch stability on both ﬂoaters, especially near rated wind speed.
• The control method achieves to handle the disturbance of intense turbulence such as
in DLC 1.3, while it can also improve the wave-dominant cases to certain extent in
DLC 1.6.
• The surge and pitch of the WindCrete spar is highly coupled in contrast to the UMaine
semisub. Therefore, the controller also indicates signiﬁcant improvements in surge
for the WindCrete spar.
6.2 Future research opportunities
The linear model is set up based on the spar buoy ﬂoater with a slender submerged
body. It is more difﬁcult to linearzie the hydrodynamics of a large-volume substructure.
Furthermore, the case study uses an up-to-date 15 MW turbine mounted on two ﬂoating
support structures. Other adaptations and experiments can be arranged for the future.
The gain scheduling of the torque compensator aims to maintain the poles of the platform
pitch, so the platform pitch damps out to the same extent throughout the above rated
region. A sets of different targeted damping ratio of platform pitch could be tested,
and performance should be compared in order to optimise the trade-offs. In addition,
the proposed controller cannot reduce the wave excitation with high frequencies, which
causes large oscillating accelerations. More features could be integrated into the controller
framework to improve the wave-induced performance.
The preliminary apprehensions of the pitch damper is not yet solid, so further study is
required to understand the viability of this strategy.
