Abstract
This thesis investigates the performance, environmental impacts, and economic viabil­
ity of a photovoltaic­thermal (PVT) system integrated with a solar­assisted heat pump
(SAHP) for residential heating applications in Denmark. The base configuration features
a 40 m² PVT array (19 modules) coupled with a 3 kW water￿to￿water heat pump, two
insulated storage tanks (110 L for domestic hot water and 93 L for space heating), and
three circulation pumps. The study combines yearly simulations in POLYSUN with a com­
prehensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and Levelized Cost of Heat (LCoH) analysis to
evaluate the system holistically. The designed system, supplying space heating and do­
mestic hot water, achieved a solar fraction of around 44% and a seasonal coefficient of
performance (COP) of nearly 5, demonstrating its suitability for residential buildings in
cold climates.
The Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) followed the ISO 14040/44 guidelines, and the ReCiPe
method was applied to analyze the system throughout the entire life cycle: production,
installation, use, and end­of­life. The research constituted a detailed life cycle inventory
(LCI) of significant materials and energy used in manufacturing, including transportation,
and the adopted database was Ecoinvent 3.0. Results showed that manufacturing, includ­
ing the production of the PVT collectors and inverters, dominated all of the environmental
categories: human health, ecosystems, and resource depletion.
The Levelized Cost of Heat (LCoH) assessment was based on the simplified methodology
from IEA SHC Task 54, using current official Danish electricity prices (2.8 DKK/kWh for
imports and 0.5 DKK/kWh for grid exports) while acknowledging that these values may
fluctuate over time. Calculations revealed a steep decline in LCoH—from 16.94 DKK/kWh
in the first year to 0.86 DKK/kWh by year 20—underscoring the long￿term economic
viability of the system despite its high upfront investment.
The publication provides a methodology and design recommendations for engineers and
policymakers interested in designing effective low­footprint solar­assisted heating sys­
tems, emphasizing the importance of careful component sizing and lifecycle considera­
tions in achieving sustainable building energy solutions.

Introduction
The global transition towards decarbonizing the building sector has catalyzed innovation
in hybrid systems of renewable energy that have the ability to provide both heat and power
in an effective way. The photovoltaic­thermal (PVT) system is a new hybrid technology
that unites solar photovoltaic (PV) and solar thermal collectors into a single system so
that electricity and heat are generated simultaneously with the same surface area. These
systems are of greatest potential when combined with heat pumps, which work synergis­
tically to boost the coefficient of performance (COP), enhance operating time, and reduce
dependence on grid electricity [1, 2].
In cold and temperate climates such as Denmark, where electricity­based heating and dis­
trict heating systems dominate [3, 4], the integration of solar­assisted heat pump (SAHP)
systems using PVT collectors can reduce both operational emissions and primary energy
consumption. Recent developments in simulation and experimental studies suggest that
PVT­assisted heat pump systems provide improved seasonal performance factors (SPF)
and increased self­consumption of solar electricity compared to conventional combina­
tions of PV and solar thermal (PV+ST) systems [5, 6, 7].
To ensure the long­term viability of these systems, their design must be evaluated not only
in terms of energy efficiency but also from an economic and environmental standpoint.
The Levelized Cost of Heat (LCoH) is a crucial metric for comparing different heating tech­
nologies over their operational lifespans, accounting for capital, operational, and mainte­
nance costs [8, 9]. Moreover, Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) provides a structured method­
ology to quantify environmental impacts across the system’s life stages—production, use,
and end­of­life—according to ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 standards [10, 11, 12].
Previous studies have highlighted that while PVT systems offer improved thermal and
electrical efficiency, their embodied energy and material impact during the manufacturing
phase remain substantial [13, 14]. A growing body of literature emphasizes the necessity
of combining experimental performance data with LCA and LCoH modeling to provide a
complete sustainability profile of hybrid systems [15, 16].
This thesis contributes to that effort by simulating and evaluating the performance, cost,
and environmental impact of different PVT­assisted heat pump configurations suitable
for residential applications under Danish climatic conditions. Key performance indicators
include SPF, thermal output, electricity production, LCoH, and environmental impact cat­
egories such as global warming potential and resource depletion. The ReCiPe and CML
methodologies are employed in the LCA, and SimaPro is used as the primary modeling
tool [17, 18].
The goal of this work is to provide a holistic assessment of PVT­HP systems’ potential as
a sustainable heating technology and to offer insights into the trade­offs between system
complexity, performance, environmental impact, and economic feasibility. The findings
are intended to support engineers, researchers, and policymakers in designing more ef­
ficient and sustainable building energy systems.

2.1
Building
The studied system is designed to supply electrical and thermal energy for a single­story
residential building located in Copenhagen, Denmark, with climate data obtained from
Meteonorm 7.2. The building follows a multi­family dwelling consumption profile, which
represents typical energy use patterns of residential buildings. It has an annual electrical
demand of 3,500 kWh for appliances. The domestic hot water (DHW) consumption is
estimated at 150 liters per day, corresponding to an annual energy demand of 2,611 kWh.
Additionally, the space heating requirement is calculated at 11,404 kWh per year.
According to Section 474, a residential building qualifies as low­energy if its total energy
demand for heating, ventilation, cooling, and domestic hot water does not exceed 20.0
kWh/m² per year. Figure 2.1 shows the evolution of the energy requirement to classify

2.2.1
PVT modules
The system comprises 19 PVT modules, each with a nominal output of 480 W under
Standard Test Conditions (STC), defined as 1000 W/m² of irradiance and cell tempera­
ture of 25°C, which are used to determine the rated performance of PV modules. The total
installed PVT modules result in a total nominal DC capacity of 9.23 kW. The collector sur­
face area is 40 m², installed in the tilted roof (approximately 45°). The annual degradation
rate of the PVT system is estimated at 0.5% per year [20]. A low volume flow rate of 0.2
l/min/m2 (500 l/h) was selected for this application, expecting a high temperature outlet
from the PVT loop.
2.2.2
Inverters
To convert the electrical output of the PVT modules from direct current (DC) to alternating
current (AC), a 3.0 kWp inverter was selected. The modules are arranged into four strings:
three with 5 modules and one with 4 modules. To accommodate this configuration, two
inverters were used, each equipped with two independent Maximum Power Point Track­
ers (MPPTs) and DC inputs. MPPT is a feature that continuously adjusts the operating
point of each string to maximize the power output based on changing conditions such as
irradiance and temperature. The first inverter manages two 5­module strings, while the
second handles the remaining 5­ and 4­module strings, as illustrated in Figure 3.1.
This setup allows for independent optimization of each string, improving system perfor­
mance in the presence of mismatch or partial shading. It also ensures that the system
operates within the electrical limits: a maximum power point voltage (MPP) range of 115–
600 V, a maximum input voltage of 750 V, and a maximum input current of 13 A — com­
patible with the modules’ maximum output current of 10 A. The electrical output of the
inverters is then used to power household loads and the heat pump.
2.2.3
Pumps and Pipes
The pump for the solar collector loop was selected based on a design pressure drop of
0.6 bar, ensuring it could meet the required flow rate. A suitable model was identified that
delivers 570 L/h at this pressure level, with a power consumption of 47 W. Additionally,
the building return pump and the space heating pump were also selected to overcome a
pressure of 0.6 bar.

2.2.4
Heat Pump
The system utilizes a water­to­water heat pump that transfers heat from the PVT collectors
to the storage tanks and heating elements. It operates on an electrical power input of 1.7
kW at B0/W35 and a coefficient of performance (COP) of 5.4 under the same conditions,

in the evaporator and high­pressure failure at 70°C in the condenser. The heat pump
dynamically supplies thermal energy for domestic hot water (DHW) and space heating,
modulating its operation based on thermal demand, prioritizing DHW when it is needed.
2.2.5
Storage tanks and Space Heating Units
Thermal energy storage is managed using two storage tanks, with the aim of enhancing
operational flexibility and energy optimization. The DHW storage tank has a volume of
110 liters, a height of 0.85 m, and is constructed of steel with rigid polyurethane (PU) foam
insulation. It is 2.5 mm thick and has 100 mm of insulation thickness. The SH tank has
a volume of 93 liters, height of 1.0 m, and is made of stainless steel with rigid PU foam
insulation. The wall thickness is 2.5 mm, and the top and bottom insulation thickness is
80 mm and 50 mm, respectively. This tank isolates DHW and SH demands. This setup
contributes to the efficient utilization of the heat energy of the system.
Space heating distribution in the building is accomplished via the radiator system, deliv­
ering 1,000 W of thermal power. The system operates at a flow rate of 86 l/h, with the
overall system volume being 10 liters.
2.2.6
External Heat Exchanger
The system is equipped with an external plate heat exchanger. The unit has a 2,000
W/K transfer capacity and consists of 21 plates. The purpose of this heat exchanger
is to facilitate the transfer of thermal energy from the PVT circuit, thereby supporting the
operation of the heat pump or directly supplying heat to the system. In instances where the
thermal energy generated by the PVT collectors is sufficient, the heat exchanger could
enable direct heat delivery to the system, reducing the reliance on the heat pump and
enhancing overall energy efficiency.
2.3
System Safety Measures
The closed hydraulic circuit that links the photovoltaic­thermal (PVT) array to the balance
of plant must be protected against both abnormal pressures and temperatures. In a stan­
dard pressurised collector loop the expansion vessel absorbs the volumetric growth of
the glycol­water mixture, while a spring­loaded relief valve—generally set between 3 and
6 bar—provides a final safeguard should the vessel fail or become saturated. Together
with a pressure gauge, an automatic air­vent located at the highest point of the field, and
appropriately placed isolation and drain valves, these components maintain the integrity
and serviceability of the solar circuit throughout its operating envelope [21]
Beyond mere pressure control, user safety is ensured by a thermostatic mixing valve that
limits the delivery temperature of domestic hot water to about 50–55 °C, thereby avoiding
scalding, reducing lime deposition, and curtailing distribution losses. International good­
practice manuals recommend that storage temperatures remain above 60 °C for hygienic
reasons, which makes the anti­scald device mandatory even in small residential systems
[21]

Theoretical Background
3.1
Photovoltaic­Thermal (PVT) Systems
Photovoltaic­Thermal (PVT)is a solar energy technology that combines photovoltaic
(PV) cells with a solar thermal collector to generate both electricity and thermal energy
from solar radiation and ambient air using the same surface area. This hybrid system
improves the overall efficiency of solar energy utilization by generating electrical power
and capturing excess heat. The dual functionality of the panel reduces the space required
for energy generation and the Thermal part mitigates overheating of the PV module, en­
hancing the electrical performance [5].
The working principle of PVT systems consists of two main aspects:
In electricity generation, the PV component functions by capturing solar radiation through
PV cells, which convert it into direct current (DC) electricity via the photovoltaic effect, a
process in which sunlight hits the surface of cells and triggers the release of electrons,
creating an electric current. This electricity is then conducted through an inverter, which
changes the DC electricity into alternating current (AC). This step is essential, as most
household and commercial appliances operate on AC rather than DC. However, conven­
tional PV modules typically operate at an efficiency of approximately 16–22%, meaning
that a considerable fraction of the incident solar energy is lost as heat [9].
The thermal energy collection process addresses this inefficiency by integrating a heat
exchanger, behind the PV modules. This absorber extracts excess heat generated by
PV cells and transfers it to a circulating fluid, which may be air, water, or a glycol­based
solution[6]. The harvested thermal energy can then be utilized for various applications,
including domestic hot water (DHW) production, space heating, or industrial processes,
depending on the specific system design[9].
3.2
Heat Pumps
A heat pump is a thermal device that moves heat from a cooler area to a warmer one,
helping to heat buildings or water efficiently. Instead of producing heat directly, it uses a
small amount of electricity to transfer existing heat from the environment. Heat pumps are
classified according to where they collect heat from: air­source heat pumps (ASHPs)
take heat from the outside air, ground­source heat pumps (GSHPs) draw it from the
ground, water­source heat pumps (WSHPs) use heat from nearby water sources, and
solar­assisted heat pumps (SAHPs) combine solar energy with standard heat pump
systems to improve performance [5].
Heat pumps operate based on the vapor compression cycle, which consists of four main
processes. First, during the evaporation phase, the low­pressure refrigerant absorbs
heat from a source, such as air, ground, water, or a solar collector, within the evap­
orator, causing the refrigerant to vaporize. Subsequently, in the compression phase,
the vaporized refrigerant is compressed by a compressor, leading to an increase in both
pressure and temperature. Following this, the condensation phase occurs, where the
high­pressure, high­temperature refrigerant releases heat to the designated target area,
such as an indoor space or a water heater, through the condenser, thereby returning to
a liquid state. Finally, in the expansion phase, the refrigerant passes through a thermo­
static expansion valve, which reduces its pressure and temperature, allowing the cycle to
repeat[7].

3.3
Integration of Heat Pumps with Solar Energy
The integration of the two previously discussed technologies—Photovoltaic­Thermal (PVT)
panels and heat pumps—offers several advantages, which benefits not only the end user
but also manufacturers in both the solar and heat pump industries.
For solar companies, this integration provides an opportunity to deliver a complete heat­
ing solution while offering an alternative to conventional systems that rely on large­scale
thermal storage. Furthermore, this combined system has the potential to achieve a higher
solar fraction for both heating and domestic hot water, with an overall solar fraction ex­
ceeding 50%. From the perspective of heat pump manufacturers, this integration is ad­
vantageous due to the higher inlet temperatures provided by the PVT system, resulting
in an improved seasonal performance factor (SPF > 5) [23]. This aspect will be further
discussed in the Key Performance Metrics section.
In this study, a Solar­Assisted Heat Pump System (SAHPS) is investigated, wherein
the heat pump utilizes the thermal energy generated by the PVT system to operate more
efficiently, thereby reducing energy consumption. The SAHPS configurations can be clas­
sified based on the interaction between the two technologies. One such classification is
the parallel configuration (illustrated in Figure 3.1), in which the thermal energy har­
vested by the solar heating system is stored in a water tank. When the solar energy is
insufficient to meet demand, the heat pump serves as an auxiliary source, supplying the
additional required energy.[1]
Figure 3.1: Parallel connection of a SAHPS
In a series configuration, the solar heating system functions as either a primary or sup­
plementary heat source for the heat pump. This configuration can be further classified
into direct and indirect series setups. In a direct series configuration, the heat transfer
fluid from the solar heating system directly enters the evaporator of the heat pump, serv­
ing as both the heat carrier and the refrigerant in a process known as direct expansion.
In contrast, an indirect series configuration utilizes the heat exchanger on the evaporator
side of the heat pump to transfer thermal energy from the solar heating system [1]. The
two series configurations are illustrated in Figure 3.2.

Key Performance Metrics
To evaluate the performance of a SAHPS, which is the system configuration chosen for
this investigation, several key figures are used. These figures are related not only to the
system itself but can also be influenced by boundary conditions related to the context
and characteristics of the application, such as climatic zone and thermal efficiency of the
building [2].
This study focuses primarily on energy consumption and production (both thermal and
electrical), the Coefficient of Performance (COP) of the heat pump, and Seasonal Per­
formance Factors (SPF). These indicators facilitate the evaluation of the efficiency of the
system in utilizing solar energy while also providing insight into potential energy savings.

mance of the heat pump utilized in the system, which refers to the ratio of the heat flow
produced by the condenser (QoutHP ) to the electric power utilized by the compressor
(PelHP ) [2]. A high COP contributes to achieve greater system efficiency. The COP of
various heat pumps usually falls in the range of 3 to 5 [3]. Large­scale heat pumps gener­
ally exhibit higher efficiency compared to individual heat pumps designed for small­scale
applications, as these often rely on outdoor air as a heat source, leading to a reduced

3.5
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a systematic method used to evaluate the environmental
impacts of a product, process, or system across its entire life cycle—from raw material
extraction (cradle) to final disposal or recycling (grave or cradle­to­cradle), as defined in
ISO 14044 [24]. It offers a comprehensive view of how a system affects the environment
in terms of resource use, emissions, and waste generation, helping identify opportunities
for improvement and guiding more sustainable decisions.
LCA has become an essential tool in environmental research and policy­making, widely
applied in product development, eco­design, carbon footprint analysis, and sustainabil­
ity assessments [12, 25]. By quantifying impacts across multiple stages, it allows for
meaningful comparisons between alternatives and supports evidence­based strategies
to reduce environmental burdens.

LCA consists of four main phases:
Goal and Scope Definition: This phase sets the purpose of the study, defines the func­
tional unit, and determines the system boundaries. It outlines key assumptions, target
audience, and criteria for data quality, which are crucial for guiding the next steps.
Life Cycle Inventory (LCI): This involves collecting data on all relevant inputs (e.g., ma­
terials, energy) and outputs (e.g., emissions, waste) throughout the product’s life cycle.
Data is organized into foreground (direct) and background (indirect) processes and linked
to the functional unit.
Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA): In this phase, the inventory results are translated
into potential environmental impacts through classification (grouping related emissions)
and characterization (quantifying their effect), allowing for an organized evaluation of the
system’s environmental footprint.
Interpretation: The final phase synthesizes the findings, checks for completeness and
consistency, and evaluates uncertainties. This ensures the results align with the initial
goals and can support well­informed, robust conclusions.

technologies with no consideration of fluctuating energy prices or policy support. Although

For the purposes of this investigation, POLYSUN software will be used as the primary

The SAHPS was modeled using the POLYSUN software, incorporating all previously de­

The system begins with the roof­mounted PVT field, where solar energy heats the circu­
lating fluid. The solar­loop pump drives this fluid through a three­way valve, either to the
heat­pump evaporator or to the external heat exchanger. After the compressor raises the
temperature, the condenser side delivers useful heat to the Tanks. A building­return pump

heat either to the domestic­hot­water (DHW) storage or to the space­heating (SH) buffer
tank. The SH pump then circulates water from the buffer to the radiators before returning
it for reheating.

4.2.1
PVT Loop Control
Figure 4.2: Schematic of the PVT loop control system.
The PVT loop comprises the connection between the PVT modules, the heat pump, and
a heat exchanger, as shown in Figure 4.2. Flow distribution within this loop is managed
by a programmable controller that operates a three­way valve (Three­Way Valve 1). The
controller receives input from three temperature sensors: the outflow temperature of the
PVT system (Pipe TF FJV), the building return temperature (Pipr TR bygning), and the
outflow temperature of the heat exchanger (Pipe TF Heat Exchanger).
Based on these temperature measurements, the controller modulates the three­way in two
operating modes. In one mode, if the outflow temperature from the heat exchanger (Pipe
TF Heat Exchanger) is lower than the building return temperature, the valve is positioned
to direct flow from the PVT system towards the heat pump (Figure 4.2, “Operation Status
2” yellow arrow). In this configuration, the heat harvested by the PVT modules contributes
directly to the heat pump’s operation.
Conversely, in the other mode, if the PVT outflow temperature (Pipe TF FJV) is at least
2°C higher than the building return temperature, the valve redirects the PVT flow toward
the heat exchanger (Figure 4.2, “Operation Status 1” green arrow). Under these condi­
tions, the thermal energy collected by the PVT modules is transferred to the water loop
via the heat exchanger, thereby providing additional heating to the system.

System Configuration Optimization
A potential optimization of the system was explored by evaluating the necessity of the
external heat exchanger. According to the system control logic, this component oper­
ates during ”operation status 2” seen in 4.2, which is triggered when the thermal energy
output from the PVT panels exceeds the building’s return temperature by at least 2°C.
Additionally, based on the system layout, it was hypothesized that the heat exchanger
plays a role in enhancing PVT performance by recirculating cooler fluid, thereby lowering
the operating temperature of the PVT panels.
Based on the preliminary simulations, as seen in Table 4.1 from the previous section, re­
sults indicated that the heat exchanger supplied only 0.41% of the total annual thermal
demand (45.3 kWh out of 11.044 kWh), suggesting that direct energy transfer through this
path is minimal. This limited performance is likely due to the climatic conditions in Copen­
hagen, where the outflow temperature of the PVT system seldom reaches the threshold
required to activate operation status 2.
To investigate whether the low contribution was location­specific, a second simulation
was performed for a southern climate, using weather data for Greece. As expected, the
higher solar irradiance (as seen in Figure 4.9) led to increased thermal output from the PVT
system, as shown by the higher outflow temperatures (Figure 4.10). Nevertheless, the
heat exchanger’s contribution rose only slightly to 1.32% of the annual thermal demand,
which is still a marginal figure.

5.4.1
Damage Assessment
Endpoint H (2016) aggregates elementary flows into three damage categories: human
health, ecosystems and resources. These metrics express the potential harm of a product
system in socially meaningful units. These three concepts are widely adopted because it
captures the main areas of protection defined by ISO 14040 and facilitates communication
with non­specialist stakeholders [10].
Human­health damage expresses the projected loss of healthy life years and is quantified
in disability­adjusted life years (DALY) a metric developed by the World Health Organiza­
tion. One DALY corresponds to one year of life lost or lived with disability [37]. Ecosys­
tem damage measures the potential disappearance of species over time, expressed as
species∙yr, i.e. the fraction of species globally that is lost during one year [36]. Resource
damage represents the additional economic effort required to extract future mineral and
fossil resources; in ReCiPe 2016 this is monetised in 2013 US dollars (USD 2013) to
reflect marginal production costs rather than physical depletion [38].

To explore the effect of collector size on system performance, environmental impact and

the Levelized Cost of Heat (LCoH).

practical design decisions. By utilizing POLYSUN to simulate the different installed ar­

necessary outputs needed to carry out LCA and LCoH investigations were obtained.

much energy is actually being generated.

8
Discussion and Conclusion
8.1
Discussion
The present investigation has demonstrated that the integration of photovoltaic­thermal
(PVT) technology with a solar­assisted heat pump (SAHP) system offers both promis­
ing performance and notable trade­offs in terms of environmental and economic impact.
Through simulation­based analyses in Polysun, combined with methodical evaluation of
life cycle assessment (LCA) and levelized cost of heat (LCoH), this research examined
how key design parameters, most notably PVT collector area and battery capacity, involve
and influence the overall system performance, as discussed in Chapter 4 and Chapter 7.
The simulation results confirmed that the designed system is capable of achieving a sub­
stantial solar fraction of approximately 44%, while maintaining a high seasonal coefficient
of performance (COP) close to 5. This indicates that the hybrid system is well­suited to
meet the building’s space heating and domestic hot water demands with significant con­
tributions from renewable sources, as presented in Chapter 4. The implemented control
strategy ensured efficient prioritization between thermal needs and solar availability, lead­
ing to stable operation across varying seasonal conditions (Section 4.2). Despite strong
thermal and electrical performance, the external heat exchanger contributed minimally to
the overall energy balance. Its removal did not compromise system operation and, in fact,
simplified the configuration and reduced component use. This suggests that such com­
ponents may be unnecessary unless specific operational modes justify their inclusion, as
analyzed in Section 4.4.
From an energy performance perspective, increasing the PVT collector area improved
solar energy utilization, but the gains plateaued when paired with larger battery capacities.
Notably, diminishing returns were observed beyond 10 kWh of battery storage, indicating a
saturation point beyond which additional investment in storage yields limited performance
improvement. This emphasizes the importance of careful balancing between collector and
storage sizes to avoid oversizing and associated costs, as explored in Section 7.3.
The LCA results revealed that environmental impacts rise significantly with larger PVT
areas, especially during the production stage, due to increased material demand and
associated emissions, as detailed in Chapter 5. The PVT panels were the primary con­
tributors to environmental burden, followed by batteries, mainly due to their composition,
including lithium, copper, and aluminum. While batteries do have environmental impacts,
their contribution was smaller compared to that of the collectors. This confirms that the
collector area is the dominant variable influencing the system’s environmental footprint,
with battery integration being a secondary but relevant factor, as shown in Section 5.4
and Section 7.2.
Economically, the system exhibited a sharp decline in LCoH during the early years of
operation, stabilizing at 0.86 DKK/kWh after two decades, as presented in Chapter 6.
Although relatively high in the initial year, the results underscore the importance of long­
term operation to offset the upfront investment. The adopted LCoH methodology provided
a clear basis for comparing design alternatives (Section 6.1), though it did not account for
benefits from PV electricity use, an aspect that could be explored in future work. Across
all scenarios, systems with smaller PVT areas consistently delivered lower LCoH values
over the 20­year simulation horizon, as discussed in Section 7.1. Although larger systems
generated more thermal output, their higher capital costs led to less favorable economic
Investigation of a PVT System with respect to Performance and LCA/LCoH evaluation
53
==PAGE:62==
outcomes. Battery integration similarly increased LCoH due to higher initial costs, despite
improvements in self­consumption (Section 7.2). These findings suggest that systems
with smaller collector areas and minimal or no storage may offer the most attractive long­
term value for residential heating applications.
The sensitivity analyses provided additional insight. Increasing the PVT area enhanced
system performance but came with higher environmental and economic costs, confirm­
ing the presence of diminishing returns beyond a certain surface threshold (Section 7.1).
Similarly, battery integration improved grid independence and self­consumption but in­
troduced further trade­offs (Sections 7.2 and 7.3). Larger batteries and collector areas
reduced reliance on external energy sources but simultaneously increased both LCoH
and environmental impacts. The results indicate that optimal configurations are not nec­
essarily the largest, but those that achieve a well­balanced trade­off between generation,
storage, and cost.
The economic viability of battery storage is strongly linked to two key factors: the cost of
electricity purchased from the grid to operate the system and the earnings from exporting
surplus PV electricity back to the grid. In simple terms, when buying electricity becomes
more expensive, stored solar power becomes more valuable; when the revenue from
selling excess electricity declines, there is a greater incentive to keep that energy for self
use. In this study, battery integration was evaluated with the primary focus on increasing
self­consumption of PV electricity. However, as shown in Table7.6, the discharge energy
from the battery does not increase significantly as battery capacity grows. This is because
most of the system’s PV generation is already used directly by the heat pump and pumps,
leaving relatively little surplus to store, and because the battery often remains partially
unused during periods of low solar production. This unused capacity leaves room for
a concept called price arbitrage: rather than only charging from PV surplus, the battery
could also be charged from the grid when electricity prices are very low (or even negative)
and discharged either to serve the home or sell back to the grid when prices are high.
Negative spot prices occur during periods of oversupply, typically when high renewable
generation coincides with low demand, while high prices arise when demand peaks and
supply tightens, often during cold winter evenings or low wind periods. In the Danish
context, the frequency of negative price hours is expected to increase in coming years
due to the rising share of wind and solar power, while price spikes may also become
more common during scarcity events [52]. Developing and implementing the optimization
and control systems needed for such an arbitrage strategy would, however, add further
investment and operational costs, requiring additional study to assess their economic
viability alongside battery storage.
8.2
Conclusion
In conclusion, this study demonstrates that photovoltaic­thermal (PVT) systems coupled
with solar­assisted heat pumps (SAHP) offer a technically viable pathway for decarboniz­
ing residential heating, but achieving both environmental and economic optimization re­
quires careful system sizing and thoughtful integration of supporting technologies.
While PVT collectors deliver most of the system’s performance benefits, they also ac­
count for the largest share of its environmental footprint. This underlines the importance
of right sizing installations to avoid unnecessary material use and opens the door to ex­
ploring alternative, lower impact materials for future PVT manufacturing. Battery storage,
although not cost­effective in the current LCoH framework, represents an opportunity for
future functionality, particularly as electricity markets evolve toward greater price volatil­
ity and increased frequency of negative price hours. Leveraging such market conditions
54

through advanced control strategies like price arbitrage could unlock additional value,
but these approaches would also require further investment in intelligent management
systems. Overall, the findings highlight that the most promising configurations are those
that strike a balance between thermal and electrical generation, storage, environmental
impact, and cost while leaving open opportunities for future system enhancements as
market conditions, technology costs, and energy policies continue to develop.
