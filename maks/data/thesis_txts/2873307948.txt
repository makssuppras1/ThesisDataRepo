Abstract
This thesis investigates the potential of large-scale electrolyzer portfolios as dynamic
demand-side resources for frequency regulation within offshore energy hubs (OEHs).
By modeling a 3 GW offshore system interconnected via HVDC to the mainland,
and integrating 1 GW of electrolysis capacity divided between Alkaline (ALK) and
Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) technologies, the study explores advanced control
strategies for grid stability. The simulation framework developed in PowerFactory
includes several novel enhancements:
1. transitional operation modes such as standby and warm-start
2. a realistic power operational range bounded by minimum and maximum load
limits
3. an optimized Automatic Generation Control (AGC) response with a cascading
activation of electrolyzer units
4. a mixed control approach combining AGC and Primary Frequency Response
(PFR)
The dynamic behavior of electrolyzers under various fault scenarios is examined,
such as underfrequency due to WPP loss and overfrequency due to HVDC disconnec-
tion. Results demonstrate that while AGC and PFR separately mitigate frequency
deviations to an extent, the mixed strategy enables a full recovery to nominal fre-
quency (50 Hz).
This work highlights the importance of accounting for dynamic
load flexibility and coordinated multi-mode control when deploying electrolyzers as
ancillary service providers in future offshore grids.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background - Denmark Energy Transition
Soaring of the world’s average temperature between 1.5°C and 2°C has been a crucial
factor in recent decades for national and international energy policies, pushing for
an immediate and strong deployment of renewable energy sources (RES) in order to
reduce green house gasses (GHG) emissions [1], Figure 1, and following the obligations
under the Paris agreement [2].
Denmark, as one of the pioneers and actual leaders in the wind industry, is lead-
ing the path to reach the Net Zero CO2 goal before 2050 imposed by the IPCC
organization, to limit global warming to ∼1.5°C [1][3][4]. From the 1990s, Denmark
began to phase out the fossil fuel generation and substitute it with substantial wind
and solar, achieving a RES total share of ∼70% as shown in Figure 1.1. Indeed, in
2021 with more than 8 million tons of CO2 equivalent, electricity and heating was the
third most polluting sector after agriculture and transportation, respectively bringing
about 10.5 and 11.5 million tons.
Figure 1.1. GHG emission by sector & Electricity share by source [5]
Offshore wind tenders, like Hesselø, and Offshore Energy Hub (OEH) projects, like
Bornholm and the North Sea Energy Island, are just a part of Denmark’s broader
energy transition strategy plan [6][7]. In light of this, the Danish Energy Agency
(DEA), through the ”Denmark’s Climate Status and Outlook 2023”, is advancing
the country’s commitment to reaching carbon neutrality and enhancing the share of
low-carbon energy technologies [8].
==PAGE:16==

1 Introduction
To be precise, this technical energy assessment published by the DEA includes
these important milestones:
• Rise of offshore wind capacity to 18GW by the end of 2030, and by 2050 up to
35GW (2.7GW in 2024 [9]);
• Between 4-6GW of Power-to-X (PtX) goal by the end of 2030: conversion of
RES electricity surplus into synthetic fuels and green hydrogen;
• Net Zero target by 2045 and 70% of emission reduction by 2030 (emission re-
duction compared to 1990).
This expansion of such an enormous hybrid energy system, where not just elec-
tricity is involved but also hydrogen and biofuels, is supported and strengthened by
a hydrogen backbone project between Jutland and Germany. Here, the green hydro-
gen produced by the wind in Denmark can meet the notable needs and demand of
hydrogen for industries in the German territory [10][11].
Finally, two declaration acts have bonded Denmark to the neighbouring countries
regarding the future of offshore wind power in the North Sea and in the Baltic Sea:
• Esbjerg Declaration Act: May 2022, Denmark, Germany, Netherlands, and
Belgium jointly agreed to connect 65GW of offshore wind energy in the North
Sea by the end of 2030, and 150GW by 2050 (expanded to 300GW in 2023 at
the North Sea Summit [4]); an offshore renewable energy system connecting all
four countries in the North Sea [12];
• Marienborg Declaration Act: August 2022, it commits the Baltic states, Poland,
Sweden, Finland, and Denmark to accelerate the establishment of wind power
in the Baltic Sea basin. It has set an ambitious goal of at least 19.6 GW by
2030, with a maximum expansion up to 93GW [13].
The two summits jointly include 20GW of electrolyzer capacity by 2030 [14].
1.2
Motivation
Consequently, OEHs will have a central role in the new layout of the European power
grid, always more interconnected among countries and more dependent on decentral-
ized intermittent renewable energy sources, to reach a 100% RES-based electricity
supply by 2050 [15].
This leads to a power system with a high RES penetration, which faces challenges
in terms of insuﬀicient inertia in the network, inevitably due to a decrease in the
proportion of rotational inertia coming from the conventional synchronous generators
(SGs) and a lack of rotational inertia support from RES generators. In light of this,
to ensure that electricity is safely and reliably transmitted throughout the entire
system, a strict balance between consumption flows and production flows has to be
==PAGE:17==
1.2 Motivation
3
kept constant. This balance is reflected through the power system frequency, which is
directly connected and regulated to the speed at which large synchronous generators
are spinning [16] [17].
Moreover, low-inertia grids may experience unfavorable frequency patterns, such
as consistent frequency deviation and rate of change of frequency (ROCOF), follow-
ing a disturbance in the system, which could result in a cascade of negative effects,
including the shutdown of generators, outages, and load shedding [18].
Therefore, the lack of inertia in the system, together with the fluctuations in
RES power output due to their intermittent behavior, makes frequency support via
ancillary services substantially important when providing stable, secure, and resilient
electricity supply.
In this field, much more attention has been focused on electrolyzer technologies
due to their dynamic characteristics. Hence, lots of works are investigating the ability
of electrolyzers to mitigate the production fluctuation of RES to provide secure grid
stability. In this way, they can be implemented as demand-side flexible resources,
guaranteeing stable frequency during the operation of a wind-based power system
like the OHEs. By providing ramp up and ramp down responses, they operate as
demand response loads, either by drawing more active power from the system when
a loss of demand or an increase in production occurs, or by decreasing their active
power consumption when the system faces a lack of generation.

However, part of the recent studies regarding grid-connected water electrolysis
systems focus on subjects like optimal planning of electrolyzers [20], topics regarding

1.3
Problem Statement
After seeing how the actual power system is mutating into an inverter-based power
grid with a shortage of rotational inertia from SGs, this master’s thesis narrows the
focus to the dynamic modeling of electrolysers within a complex renewable energy
network like OEHs. This thesis shifts the scope from the general feasibility of in-
corporating electrolysers for active power control to a more granular investigation
of their dynamic behavior. In particular, the study examines a GW-scale portfolio
of electrolysers, with an emphasis on understanding how various operational modes,
such as normal and stand-by operation, cold and warm start-ups, different ramping
capabilities, and different loading ranges, affect their ability to balance power in an
OEH. The wind turbine parks would be connected through AC sea cables to the hub
where all the electrolysers are collected, and the network is modeled using converter-
based control systems, notably grid-forming (GFM) converters but also grid-following
converters (GFL), within a sophisticated HVDC framework [23].
The research involves a model of a 3GW AC interconnected OEH with 1GW of
water electrolysis production (WEP) through ALK and PEM electrolyzer: a 2GW
two-terminal bipole HVDC link connects the OEH with the onshore grid. Further-
more, it aims to develop a detailed electrical dynamic model using the simulation tool
PowerFactory and implement it in the consortium ”North Sea Wind Power Hub” sys-
tem [7], provided by DTU Wind and Energy System Department; thereby, capturing
the transient responses of the electrolysers under different faulty scenarios through
RMS simulations. These scenarios include variations in the type of fault, initial set
point of the GW-scale electrolyzer portfolio, and the type of frequency response. By
simulating these conditions, the study aims to quantify the impact of the electrolyser
system on the overall resilience and stability of the OEH. The hands-on experience
with the simulation tool also provides invaluable insights into the behavior of 100%
renewable systems, ultimately contributing to a broader comprehension of the techni-
cal and operational challenges faced by offshore energy islands, like the power system
stability and security.
In summary, the thesis not only seeks to evaluate the effectiveness of electrolysers
in enhancing grid stability but also aims to inform future strategies for integrating
flexible, dynamic assets into large-scale renewable energy infrastructures using differ-
ent WEP technologies.

Hub Topologies: DC and AC
Now, after having studied different structural technologies to build-up the hub-
and-spoke configuration, the offshore cable connections between the hubs (platforms)
and spokes (HVDC links) have to be investigated, considering different possible ar-

energy systems.
Hence, two main hub topology solutions can be taken into consideration: a mul-
titerminal DC-coupled hub where multiple HVDC links are interconnected on the
DC side of the offshore converters, and a multi-terminal AC-coupled hub where the
HVDC links are interconnected on the AC side of the offshore converters [14]:

one of the poles 2.5.

converters in parallel. Instead of individual controls for each DC connection
to the AC side of the hub, a master controller would be required for the co-

Converters:GFM & GFL
2.3.1
Grid Following Converters GFL - P/Q
Grid-Following (GFL) converters are VSCs that synchronize to an existing strong AC
grid via a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL). In this mode, the grid imposes the frequency,
and the converter controls its current injection to regulate power, like an AC current
source: id for P and iq for Q. Outer control loops command either the DC-link voltage,
thus setting active power flow P, or directly the active power, while the quadrature
axis q loop regulates either reactive power Q or AC voltage at the point of connection.
The GFL converter behaves as a current source tied to the grid, tracking the grid’s
voltage phase. During system disturbances (e.g. grid frequency dips or faults), a
GFL converter will ride through by adjusting its output within limits: it will saturate
current to protect itself and rely on the grid to stabilize frequency, offering only limited
inertial response unless a droop control is added [32] [33].
==PAGE:32==
18
2 Overview of the OEH Project
Figure 2.12. GFL Converter, current vector control strategy [32]
2.3.2
Grid Forming Converters GFM - V/f
In contrast, a Grid-Forming (GFM) converter controls the AC voltage magnitude and
frequency of its terminal, behaving like an AC voltage source (Figure 2.13). Instead of
a PLL, it uses an internal oscillator (voltage-controlled oscillator) to set the reference
angle, actively regulating the system frequency. In a GFM converter, the terminal
voltage and frequency are dictated by the converter’s controls, so the real and reactive
power it exchanges become the by-products of maintaining those setpoints.
This
means a GFM unit inherently shares load changes: for example, if generation or
load varies, the converter adjusts its power output, within its droop settings, to hold
frequency and voltage, much like a traditional generator governor. A GFM converter
can, therefore, stabilize an islanded or low-inertia network: it can even energize a
“passive” network with no other voltage source, a capability line-commutated HVDC
lacks [33] [32].
In offshore HVDC systems these roles are complementary: the offshore VSC (con-
necting an isolated wind farm) is typically grid-forming, establishing a stable AC
voltage (e.g. 66kV) and frequency (50Hz) for the turbines and adjusting its power
draw according to the wind generation to maintain frequency. The onshore VSC, tied
into a strong national grid, operates grid-following, because it locks to the mainland
grid frequency and injects the received power accordingly, often controlling the DC
link voltage as the “slack” converter for the HVDC link. This way, the GFM offshore

2.4.1
Alkaline Water Electrolyzer AWE
Temperature & Lifetime
Alkaline electrolyzers are the most established hydrogen electrolysis technology,
using a liquid potassium hydroxide (KOH) electrolyte in the cell. They operate at
roughly 70–90°C and have proven long-term durability: stack lifetimes on the order
of ≃60,000 hours) [34].
Dynamics
A key drawback is their slower dynamic response compared to newer electrolyz-
ers: traditional atmospheric AWE systems ramp up hydrogen production only about
simeq10% of full power per minute, whereas modern pressurized alkaline designs can
ramp ≃4% per second – roughly 20 times faster [38].
They can generally decrease load quicker than increase it (ramp-down rates up
to ≃25% per second), allowing a full shutdown from maximum output within a few
seconds. Even so, AWE units usually must operate above a minimum load (typically
≃20% of Pn) to avoid eﬀiciency losses and dangerous hydrogen/oxygen mixing in the
liquid electrolyte. Another limitation is cold start-up time: because the alkaline solu-
tion and cell stack need to be heated to operating temperature, starting from a cold
state can take on the order of tens of minutes, roughly 20–60 minutes, depending on
system size. Warm standby can shorten this, but it still means alkaline systems are
less agile for immediate on-off cycling [34].
Costs
Additionally, thanks to inexpensive materials and decades of manufacturing ex-
perience, AWE systems tend to have lower capital costs, approximately $500–$1,400
per kW installed, than other electrolyzer types. Their robust design and scalability
to large capacities (multi-MW stacks) make them well-suited for bulk hydrogen pro-
duction, but the trade-off is a larger footprint and slower responsiveness, which can
be a disadvantage for fast grid-balancing services [34].

2.4.2
Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer PEM
Temperature & Lifetime
PEM electrolyzers use a solid polymer proton-conducting membrane (typically
Nafion) as the electrolyte, enabling a compact cell design and high current densities
(often 1–2 A/cm2).
They operate at lower temperatures (≃50–80°C) [34].
Some
modern PEM stacks are achieving lifetimes on the order of 60,000–80,000 hours, com-
parable to alkaline longevity, despite operation in variable renewable power conditions
[39]. Studies also indicate that PEM electrolyzers handle intermittent power input
better than alkaline, showing less performance decay under frequent load fluctuations.
Dynamics
They are distinguished by an excellent dynamic response, the electrolysis reaction
can ramp up or down in essentially real time, limited mainly by power electronics
rather than inherent chemistry. In practice, PEM systems can adjust output at very
high rates (on the order of 10–80% of full power per second for ramp-up, and up to
≃40%/s for ramp-down). This means a PEM electrolyzer stack can go from idle
to 100% output in only a few seconds, far outpacing the tens of seconds or minutes
needed by alkaline units [34].
Moreover, PEM technology supports a wide operating range; they can run from
near 10% (idle mode) up to 100% of rated load, and even accommodate brief over-
loads above 100% in some designs. This low minimum-load capability (practically
0–5% in standby, or ≃10% in normal operation) contrasts with the higher turn-down
limits of alkaline systems. Startup is also fast: a PEM electrolyzer typically needs
only around 5 minutes to cold-start to full capacity, versus ≃20 minutes or more for
an alkaline of similar size. If the system is already warm (hot standby), it can re-
sume hydrogen production almost instantaneously (within seconds) due to the rapid
electrochemical kinetics. Thanks to these attributes, PEM electrolyzers are ideal for
applications requiring fast on-off cycling and precise control, for example, they have
been demonstrated providing frequency regulation with sub-second response times to
grid signals [34].
Costs
The main downsides of PEM electrolyzers relate to cost and materials: the cells
require precious metal catalysts (iridium and platinum) and corrosion-resistant hard-
ware, which currently make their capital costs higher, roughly $1,100–$1,800 per kW,
than alkaline systems. In addition, the polymer membrane necessitates very pure wa-
ter feed and can be susceptible to degradation under high current stress or dynamic
heavy cycling, although ongoing R&D is steadily improving durability [34] [39].

2.4.3
Solid Oxide Electrolysis SOE
Temperature & Lifetime
Solid oxide electrolyzer cells (SOECs) are a high-temperature water electrolysis
technology that operates in the 700–850°C range using a solid ceramic electrolyte. In
an SOEC, water (usually introduced as steam) is reduced at the cathode to produce
hydrogen, while oxygen ions travel through the ceramic membrane to the anode to
form O2.
This process can achieve very high electrical eﬀiciency, on the order of
80–90% LHV, especially when waste heat or high-temperature steam is available to
drive the reaction. SOEC technology also uniquely allows co-electrolysis of CO￿along
with water to produce syngas ( H2
CO mixtures), offering flexibility for e-fuel production.
Current SOEC systems have a shorter proven lifespan (on the order of ≃20,000 hours
for stacks) before significant degradation, much lower than the typical 60k+ hours of
PEM or alkaline stacks [34].
Dynamics
However, the dynamic characteristics of SOECs are less favorable for rapid grid-
balancing use.
Because the system must remain at high temperature for eﬀicient
operation, turning an SOEC on from cold is a slow process, heat-up times on the
order of hours are needed to go from ambient to 800°C. Thus, SOECs are not practi-
cal for daily on-off cycling or fast startup scenarios. When already hot (at operating
temperature), SOEC stacks can respond to load changes within a timescale of sec-
onds to minutes, so they do have some ability to modulate output in steady-state. In
fact, under controlled conditions, an SOEC can follow setpoint changes reasonably
quickly (a few seconds delay) while hot, which could handle secondary control or
load-following that doesn’t require instant response. But frequent swings in power or
thermal cycling can be very damaging, because the ceramic cells and interfaces can
crack or degrade under thermal stress and redox cycling [34].
Costs
They are also still in the early stages of commercialization and thus have the high-
est capital cost of any electrolyzer technology today: estimated roughly $2,800–$5,600
per kW. These factors make SOECs better suited for steady, continuous hydrogen
production coupled with high-temperature heat sources, i.e. integration with nuclear
plants or industrial processes), where their superior eﬀiciency can be fully utilized
and frequent cycling is not required. In contrast, using SOECs for fast grid frequency
regulation or other rapid ancillary services is not common. As the technology ma-
tures, material improvements may extend SOEC durability and allow more flexible
operation.

Anion Exchange Membrane AEM
Figure 2.16. AEM Cell [40]
AEM electrolyzers are an emerging low-temperature technology that attempts to
combine the advantages of alkaline and PEM systems. Like a PEM cell, an AEM
electrolyzer uses a solid polymer membrane, but in this case, the membrane conducts
hydroxide ions (2OH−) instead of protons H+, meaning the cell operates in an al-
kaline environment. This design allows the use of non-precious metal catalysts while
still benefiting from a membrane that separates product gases and permits differential
pressure operation [41].
The AEM concept shows promise for achieving high eﬀiciencies and dynamic flex-
ibility comparable to PEM electrolyzers, but without the costly noble metals. Early
demonstrations have indeed exhibited fast load response and wide turndown capabil-
ity, making AEM systems appear just as nimble in adjusting output as PEM units.
However, the technology readiness level is still low, mostly at lab or pilot scale, with
no mass-produced large commercial units yet. Durability and lifetime remain chal-
lenges due to membrane stability in strongly alkaline conditions, and current lifetimes
are significantly lower than those of PEM/AWE systems, far from tens of thousands
of hours at present [41].

2.5
Frequency Control Ancillary Services FCAS
Frequency Control Ancillary Services (FCAS) refer to the suite of grid support ser-
vices that maintain the system frequency at or near its nominal value of 50Hz by
continuously balancing active power supply and demand. In practice, FCAS encom-
passes several reserve products distinguished by response speed and control method.
In the ENTSO-E frameworks [42] [43], which Denmark follows, and in the Energinet
manual [44], these FCAS include:
1. Fast Frequency Response (FFR): it is an automatic upward regulation reserve
at frequency dips below 49.7/49.6/49.5 Hz and remains active until FCR-D
has been fully activated, with a maximum activation time of, respectively, 1.3,
1.0, 0.7 seconds. FFR fills the gap between the immediate inertia of spinning
machines, which acts in milliseconds, and the slower primary reserves, which
reach full output in ≃5–30 seconds
2. Frequency Containment Reserves (FCR): the fastest, autonomously-activated
reserves, formerly “primary control”, that arrest frequency deviations within
seconds. Droop control is the core mechanism used to deliver FCR, supplied
at a frequency deviation of ±200mHz (49.8-50.2 Hz), half capacity has to be
delivered within 15s and full capacity within 30s. In addition, there are other
two balancing markets:
• Frequency-Containment Disturbance Reserve (FCR-D), Upward: it acti-
vates between 49.5-49.9 Hz,
• Frequency-Containment Disturbance Reserve (FCR-D), Downward: it ac-
tivates between 50.1-50.5 Hz, activated within 7.5s
• Frequency-Containment Normal Reserve (FCR-N): it activates continu-
ously and linearly with frequency fluctuations between ±0.1Hz, within
3 minutes
==PAGE:40==
26
2 Overview of the OEH Project
3. Frequency Restoration Reserves (FRR): reserves that restore frequency to the
nominal value after FCR action and correct any imbalances on the interconnec-
tion. It includes:
• automatic FRR (aFRR), formerly “secondary control”: it is activated au-
tomatically via centralized control, control signal sent every 10 seconds
(tagc=10s, [42]), full activation within 5 minutes
• manual FRR (mFRR), formerly “tertiary control”: the only manual reserve
product used in the Nordics, it has to be fully activated within 15 minutes
4. Replacement Reserves (RR): slower reserves to replace FRR for prolonged bal-
ancing needs.
In this project, with the implementation of droop control and virtual inertia re-
sponse control, the FCAS products investigated regarding the active power frequency
response of dynamic electrolyzers are: the FCR (named ”Primary”) and the aFRR
(named Automatic Generation Control, ”AGC”).

3.4.4.1
Transitional State of the electrolyzer model
As already stated in chapter 1, this project wants to leverage a portfolio of different
electrolyzer technologies, which embeds different static designs and dynamic char-
acteristics. Moreover, not just the normal operation mode of electrolyzers is imple-
mented, but new features like stand-by mode and shutdown actions are implemented
as new sub-models and through modificatiosn inside the power control block diagram
of the DC/DC converter. This multi-state model aims to accurately describe the
transitional properties of offshore WEP [50].
Figure 3.12. Transitional modes of operation of the electrolyzer model [50]
During normal operation (or production state), the electrolyzer produces hydrogen
by drawing active power from the system supply and by keeping the temperature
range within the limits: furthermore, the WEP can vary its set point of operation
within the load range of the model, with the lowest production occurring at Pmin.
Here, the WEP plant is seen as a changeable load [50].
The model goes to stand-by state when needed by the system instability: if the
system is still facing a frequency deviation after all the electrolyzers have reached
the Pmin, with an optimized cascade effect they start to shut down trying to bring
the frequency back to 50 Hz. Once they shut down, they do not reach P = 0, but
they keep drawing a small percentage of Pn in order to feed the ancillary components
==PAGE:55==

inside the plants; in this way, the pressure and temperature are kept at high level
while still producing hydrogen. Here, the WEP plant is seen as a fixed load [50].
The off state sees the electrolyzer completely shut down and with consuming power
almost to zero: consequently, the temperature drops due to heat losses towards the
environment [50]. This happens when the electrolyzer stays for a long amount of
time in stand-by.

At this point, once the electrolyzer model is defined and the control strategies ex-

In this paragraph, the model is investigated under several cases and types of faulty

in one case the model starts at P=-1p.u. and in the other one P=-0.5p.u. For the

responding with FCR support (or named PRIM), all responding with AGC support,
and the last one a mix of PRIM/AGC among the ALK/PEM technologies.

response through the stand-by mode with the set up ”cascade effect”.
For all the RMS simulations, a time step of 0.01s is used, and a timeframe of 100s
is kept for all the cases under the two scenarios. These simulations discuss the effect of
dynamic frequency control of electrolyzers from an active power balancing perfective;
in light of this, the AC voltage and reactive power balancing are not touched in this
article.
As it is presented, it can be seen that the number of cases is big; hence, to keep

results of the most important cases are discussed in this section, and some comments

shares of ALK and PEM are not reported in this article because of not particularly

Conclusion
The thesis successfully demonstrates the role of GW-scale electrolyzer systems in
providing frequency regulation services for offshore energy hubs, focusing on their
dynamic behavior within an HVDC-connected wind-based system. A comprehensive
model was built in PowerFactory, representing both PEM and ALK technologies with
distinct operational parameters including standby state, ramp rates, and power limits.
A significant contribution was the implementation of transitional modes and the load
operating range, which added realism to the simulations and enabled accurate fre-
quency response assessments. Three control strategies were tested under major fault
scenarios: AGC, PFR, and a combined AGC/PFR strategy. The results indicate that
the individual AGC or PFR responses were insuﬀicient to restore the system to 50 Hz.
However, when both were used in a coordinated manner—assigning fast response to
PEM and sustained support to ALK—the system frequency was successfully recov-
ered. Additionally, the cascading activation mechanism optimized AGC deployment
and reduced overshoot. Overall, this work confirms the technical feasibility and value
of using dynamically controlled electrolyzers as fast-acting grid-balancing assets in
offshore renewable systems, offering valuable insight for the future integration of PtX
technologies in low-inertia power networks.
