Abstract 
 
Microstructure evolution during fatigue of polycrystalline copper was investigated for a plastic 
strain amplitude of 4 ∗10−4 under tension compression cyclic loading. It was found that the 
cyclic hardening curve, for this strain amplitude and used material, is divided into three 
regimes. Depending on the number of cycles: a hardening (1-8000 cycles), a saturation (8000-
10 000 cycles) and a softening (by above 10 000 cycles). Four samples were cyclically loaded 
for: 1000, 4000, 12 000 and 160 000 cycles, in order to analyse microstructure in the early 
stage and the established hardening regime, after the saturation was reached and in the 
softening regime. The microstructure of the fatigued samples were then analysed using a 
microhardness test, an optical microscope, x-ray diffraction and Electron Back Scatter 
Diffraction (EBSD). Hardness tests showed that most of a hardening was taking place between 
4000 and 12 000 cycles. EBSD showed strong <111>-<100> fibre texture along the tensile 
direction. Dislocation boundaries could not be observed. X-ray analysis did not give satisfying 
results because of lack of statistical data due to enormous grains size. It was found that the 
used heat treatment conditions (48h annealing in 800oC in hydrogen-argon atmosphere) 
resulted in an excessive grain growth.

1. Introduction 
Metals show g
ta
er
 mechanical properties and play the most important role when construction 
materials are concerned. Their strength comes from the microstructural evolution: the 
dislocation storage during plastic deformation processing. Although these defects make metals 
stronger and harder, at the same time contribute to the potential failure, lowering the resistance 
to the fatigue. Failure due to repeated and cyclically changing loading condition, known as a 
fatigue, is one of the most common sources of all materials failures. Under cyclic load or strain, 
dislocations rearrange into certain substructures. These patterns, especially ladder-like 
persistent slip bands, have essential impact on the materials’ lifetime when are cyclically 
plastically deformed. 
Last few decades brought significant improvements in understanding of cyclic plastic 
deformation mechanisms of metallic materials. Extensive studies of dislocations substructure 
evolution, under various fatigue conditions, resulted in numerous scientific papers reviewing 
and combining macro scale cyclic failure features with evolution of a microstructural 
dislocation patterns. A lot of researchers’ efforts were focused on pure face centred cubic 
materials, for instance nickel, austenitic steels and especially copper. 
The cyclic plastic deformation is an extremely complex phenomenon and dislocations 
substructures are dependent on numerous factors. Therefore, for the sake of simplicity, first 
investigations were conducted for single crystals. These and numerous further works on single 
crystals of various metals explained material behaviour during plastic deformation and directly 
combined its substructure evolution with a cyclic failure stage. Especially important discovery 
was an observation of persistent slip bands, in a short form PSBs, which create extrusions and 
intrusions on the material’s surface. These non-uniformities on the surface are points of stress 
concentrations and excellent places for a crack to form. Moreover, numerous not known 
dislocations arrangements were found depending on deformation conditions. It was found that 
differences in the microstructure are directly affected by a slip system activity: for example, in 
a single slip systems PSBs are dominating feature, although, in the case of multiple slip systems, 
cellular and labyrinth like structures are mainly formed. Double slip systems show features of 
both, single and multiple slip systems; the dominating microstructure character depends on the 
secondary and tertiary slip activity.  
 
==PAGE:6==
5 
 
The substructure evolution and fatigue mechanisms become more complex when the 
polycrystalline material is concerned. Findings for the single crystal can be applied for 
polycrystals, however, to the limited extend. Due to presence of more than one grain, relations 
between neighbours have to be taken into account. Firstly, type of boundaries have to be 
included as an essential factor, as they have direct impact on the material plastic behaviour. 
Secondly, grains orientation and material texture affect enormously activity of slip systems.  
Finally, grains size also cannot be omitted as a refinement is strictly connected with a sharpness 
of a material texture. As a result, new, not observed in single crystals, structures were found in 
polycrystals. 
Despite extensive investigations performed on a copper polycrystals under cyclic load, the 
fatigue mechanisms remain not completely understood. Most of researchers focused their work 
on the impact of a different strain rates on the microstructure evolution, when samples were 
cycled to the saturation stress. Although, not so much effort was put on analysing how the 
microstructure develops when samples were cycled by a certain number of cycles. Therefore, 
dislocation patterns before or long after reaching the saturation stress, for a given strain rate, 
are not well known.  
Furthermore, in numerous papers the annealing conditions were not treated as an essential 
factor. Usually, samples were shortly heat treated: not exceeding 3h. Short time limits grains 
growth, although, material is not able to fully anneal and some plastic deformation, coming 
from sample preparation processes, remains unrecovered. There is no denying that it definitely 
has an impact on the final materials fatigue behaviour and substructure evolution.   
The current work is devoted to broaden the understanding of a substructure evolution of copper 
polycrystals under cyclic push-pull conditions. The research is focused on the impact of the 
amount of cycles under the strain amplitude of 4 ∗10−4. Four samples were cyclically loaded 
for: 1000, 4000, 12 000 and 160 000 cycles, in order to analyse microstructure in the early stage 
and the established hardening regime, after the saturation was reached and in the softening 
regime. In order to remove any remains of a plastic deformation coming from samples 
preparation, material was firstly annealed for 48h in 800oC in hydrogen-argon atmosphere. 
After certain loading, all samples where then analysed for the microstructure evolution. Firstly, 
the hardness was measured to observe if and how fast is material strengthening due to increasing 
number of cycles. Then, optical microscopy was used in order to investigate the surface features 
of all analysed samples. Next, x-ray diffraction and EBSD analysis were done to analyse

microstructure, obtain orientation maps and misorientation profiles. On the basis of reached 
results, microstructure evolution during fatigue of polycrystalline copper was discussed. 
 
2. Crystalline materials 
Materials can be divided into two main categories: the one that shows a crystalline structure 
and other that do not have it and are known as amorphous. This work focuses on a copper which 
shows crystalline structure, therefore amorphous materials are not in the main interest and will 
be no longer deliberated. 
Crystals, depending on the type of bonding between atoms, can be further divided into four 
subgroups: ionic, atomic, molecular and metallic crystals. The first one and simplest in the 
explanation are ionic crystals. Positive and negative ions are alternately occupying 
neighbouring sites of a crystal lattice. The structure is bonded together by electrostatic forces 
and creating the ionic crystal. This type of structure is observed between elements strongly 
electrically positive and negative, for instance: NaCl, Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1. Ionic crystal [1] 
 
In the second case, covalent crystals, each atom is bonded with its neighbour with covalent 
bonds. A typical example representing this type of materials is diamond. Schematic bonding 
system is given in Figure 2.

Figure 2. Covalent crystal [1] 
 
The third category applies for materials that cannot create atomic crystals, usually elements 
showing very highly electronegativity. For instance, chlorine atom can only create one atomic 
bonding with a second chlorine atom. Elements within this group create molecule crystals 
which is built from molecules with internal covalent bonding but bonded with a neighbour by 
Van der Waals forces. Molecular crystals do not have to be based on elements strongly 
electronegative; all substances, which are gases in a room temperature, as well as most of 
liquids, usually create molecular crystals after solidification. This is due to the fact that these 
crystals are weakly bonded and can easily change into liquids and gases. Bonding schema is 
shown in Figure 3.

tend to create this type of structure. Atoms connects together, lose their valence electrons or at 
least part of them. Electrons create so-called electron gas and fill the entire volume. The whole 
structure is kept together due to electrostatic forces. It has to be pointed out that electrons in

3. Defects in crystalline materials 
The perfect crystal is defectless. However, in real materials there are always some imperfections 
present. The simplest division of defects divide them into point, line, surface, and volume 
defects. All of them introduce local disturbance of the atoms arrangement, what has significant 
impact on the properties of the crystalline solid.  
3.1. Point defects 
This type of defect is only possible when the pure metal is concerned. Therefore, two simple 
point defects can occur: vacancies and self-interstitial atoms. The vacancy is simply lack of an 
atom in the perfect array, while interstitials are extra atoms present in the structure, but as non-
lattice sites. The sources of these defects are usually plastic deformation or increase in 
temperature- then atoms have enough energy to change their position. Figure 14 shows the 
simplified scheme of a vacancy and a self-interstitial atom. 
 
Figure 14. a) Vacancy; b) Self-interstitial atom [2] 
The energy needed to form a vacancy in the structure is equal to the energy needed to remove 
one atom from its lattice site and put it on the crystal surface. From the experimental results for 
copper, the fraction of vacant positions in the lattice is around 10−5 when the temperature is 
1300 K, while only 10−22 for 300 K. On the other hand, energy needed to form self-intestinal 
atoms is equal to the energy needed to remove one atom from the crystal surface and introduce 
it inside of the crystal in the interstitial site. The energy needed to form it is around four times 
higher than needed to form a vacancy. This shows that amount of self-interstitial atoms is 
usually negligible in comparison to the number of vacancies. Although in non-metals, due to 
ionic charges and other effects, the situation might be different. [3] 
Other type of point defects, playing also an essential role and having an impact on the 
mechanical properties of materials, are impurities. These external atoms can occupy two types 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
of sites (Figure 15). The first one is substitution site: the original atom of a crystal is substituted 
by an impurity atom. The second one, known as interstitials, present a case when impurity atoms 
occupies interstitial sites. [2]

Any type of defect has an impact on the lattice, creating a local distortion. However, the amount 
of distortion depends on how much space is occupied by the introduced atoms and the space 
between them. Due to very small volume in the interstice site, a singular impurity atom may 
even produce a distortion in a scale of few atomic volumes. It has to be mentioned that removal 
as well as addition of atoms has an impact on the local electric charge in the crystal. It has a 
strong impact on structures where ionic binding is dominating. [3] 
3.2. Stacking Faults 
As mentioned before, in a perfect crystal, layers of atoms stack in a regular order. However, 
when one or more planes are removed, it results in a defect, which interrupts the stacking 
sequence. It is known as a stacking fault. Existence of this type of defect is not expected for 
ABABAB sequences in FCC and BCC metals as there is no alternative option for an A layer 
then to lie on a B layer. Nevertheless, ABCABC sequence in a closed packed plane for FCC 
metals enables more than one possible stacking order. For the atom layer resting on an A layer 
there is no difference, from the geometrical point of view, whether it will be in B or C position. 
As a result, FCC metals show two stacking fault possibilities: intrinsic and extrinsic one.

17 
 
 
Figure 16. Two types of stalking faults: (a) intrinsic; (b) extrinsic [2] 
Figure 16 shows both types of stalking faults: intrinsic, when the part of a C layer is not present 
in the structure; extrinsic, when an extra A layer is present between a B and a C layers. 
Obviously, as it was already mentioned, every distortion in the perfection of the crystal structure 
results in an increase in the energy, in this case, stacking-fault energy. Due to this fact, it 
becomes clear that stacking faults may have a significant impact on the properties of materials. 
3.3. Grain Boundaries 
It is common that material is not built from a single crystal. Therefore, usually whole structure 
bases on the large number of grains and grain boundaries between them. Depending on the two 
grains orientation boundaries may differ significantly. Large misorientation contributes to a 
complex arrangement of atoms at the boundary. On the other hand, for a low difference in 
orientation: low angle of the misorientation, atomic structure on the grain boundary is rather 
simple and may even occupy only single atomic layer. It shows that depending on the 
orientation relationship between grains, the boundary structure differs and can be well described 
with a use of an array of dislocations.

4. The phenomenon of fatigue 
The most common source of a mechanical elements failure is a result of cyclic or fluctuating 
stresses. The failure occurs when the maximum value of stress is below ultimate strength of 
material, and can even take place below its yield strength. This phenomenon is called fatigue. 
[4] There are many forms of a fatigue, depending on operating conditions of the element. The 
most frequent are mechanical fatigue, creep fatigue, thermo-mechanical fatigue, corrosion 
fatigue, sliding-contact fatigue and fretting fatigue [5]. 
Fatigue failure, based on a number of cycles, can be divided into two types: low cyclic fatigue 
and high cyclic fatigue. Generally, in low cyclic fatigue, the highest value of applied stress is 
above yielding point of the material and the number of predicted cycles is below 104. In high 
cyclic fatigue, the number of cycles is above 104, but usually it is above 107 and the maximum 
stress is below yielding stress (figure 18). [5] 
 
 
 
Figure 18. Stress versus time diagram, presenting low and high cyclic fatigue [6] 
Fatigue failure can be divided into three distinctive stages. The first one is responsible for a 
crack initiation and usually takes the most of the time and the largest number of cycles. There 
are many factors causing crack creation, such as micro-cracks. However, even for materials 
with no defects inside the structure, the fatigue can occur due to non-uniformities on the surface, 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
what contributes to local stress concentrations and micro plastic deformation. When the crack 
is present, the second phase is the crack growth. This phase usually can be controlled - it is 
possible to predict how fast crack will grow, as it depends on the number of cycles and material 
properties. In the last, third phase, fracture can occur at any time, because cross-section is too 
small to withstand stresses acting on the element. [4] Many investigations were performed in 
order to predict and understand mechanisms of fatigue. The phenomenon can be well presented 
graphically.  There are few common graphs that can be used and further experimental data will

5. Dislocations 
First predictions of the existence of dislocations go back to 1940s and 1950s when scientist 
compared theoretical model predictions with experimental results of the shear stress needed to 
plastically deform single crystal. Plastic deformation takes place when atomic planes slide over 
each other. The very early theoretical models predicted that in perfect crystals the slide of a 
plane would demand all atoms of a given plane to move together from one position to the 
second. In 1926 Frenkel has calculated the shear stress required for this process and turned out 
to be many magnitudes higher in comparison with experimental results. Due to these significant 
differences in results, in 1934 three scientists: Orowan, Taylor and Polanyi independently 
predicted existence of dislocation.  
Second prove of dislocation existence came with explanation of the crystal growth process. 
During crystal growth in a supersaturated vapour, atoms nucleate preferentially in the 
irregularities of the surface. However, when all these regions are filled, further nucleation 
should occur on the perfectly plane surface. This very energetically unfavourable situation 
would employ a very high supersaturation for further crystal growth, around 50 percent. 
Nevertheless, further nucleation still occurs even when a supersaturation is only on the level of 
1 percent. The answer came with a dislocation theory showing that presence of dislocations 
result in formation of steps which are not removed during deposition and act as preferential 
sites during nucleation and eliminates above mentioned obstacles. [2] 
5.1. The concept of slip 
In the very first beginning, before the dislocations geometry can be presented, it is important to 
firstly explain how the dislocation can move. Therefore, the concept of a slip needs to be 
explained beforehand.  
The movement of dislocations is possible by the two basic types of motion: conservative, 
known also as a glide, and non-conservative called climb. In the first one, movement of the 
dislocation takes place in the surface containing its line and displacement vector. Dislocations 
that are unable to move in that way are called sessile, while the active one, glissile. The climb 
calls the motion of the dislocation that is out of the glide surface, normal to the displacement 
vector. In this work, the climb motion will not be broadly discussed. [2] 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
When the deformation takes place, the resultant change in a shape of a material is a sum of 
numerous glides of dislocations, known as slips. These slides of the one atomic plane over 
another takes place on the so-called slip planes. However, displacement does not occur for every 
single atom layer: discrete blocks of crystals remain unchanged between two slip planes as long 
as further deformation will not contribute to the creation of a new slip plane inside the block 
(Figure 22). The direction, at which slip occurs, is known as the slip direction. [2] 
 
Figure 22. The graphical representation of slip [2] 
The crystallographic structure of a material strongly affects the slip plane as well as slip 
direction. The most widely spaced planes with the highest atom density are normally the one 
where displacement takes place. The direction at which atoms are the most closely packed 
defines the slip direction. For the FCC metal, for instance copper, there are four slip planes: 
{111} and three slip directions: <110>. Combination of all slip planes with all slip directions, 
results in twelve so- called slip systems. [2]

5.3. Burgers Vector and Burgers Circuit 
Burgers vector and Burgers circuit are two very useful concepts that can be used to define the 
dislocation in the structure. Burgers Circuit is a closed loop path that can be drawn moving 
from atom to atom, for instance, for simple cubic structures (Fig 27). [8] 
 
            Figure 27. Burgers circuit; a) a crystal free of dislocation; b) a crystal with the dislocation [8] 
Figure 27 presents two situations: the dislocation free crystal (a) and crystal with dislocation 
present in the structure (b). The Burgers circuit can be drawn on the closed path PQRT in the 
perfect crystal and PQRST in the deformed one. Comparing these two paths, the one for crystal 
containing dislocation is longer in comparison to the dislocation free crystal. The difference in 
paths, marked as RS distance, is so-called Burgers vector and, in a given simplified case, 
represents single dislocation. When the Burgers vector is normal to the line of dislocation, as 
shown in the case above, an edge dislocation is present. If a screw dislocation was under 
consideration, then the Burgers vector would be parallel to the dislocation line. It has to be 
underlined that other defects do not have an impact on the Burgers circuit closure failure.

translation vector which can connect two points in the lattice. For that case Burgers vector is 
named unit dislocation or perfect. [8]

5.5. Dislocation pile-ups 
During plastic deformation different dislocation array can be created and one of them is the 
dislocation pile-up. To explain this phenomenon, a source emitting series of dislocation which 
all lie in the same slip plane (Figure 30). 
  
 
Figure 30. Source emits dislocations due to applied shear stress 𝜏. Due to the barrier, the edge 
dislocations piled-up [2] 
When the first leading dislocation loop expands, at some point it may meet a barrier, for instance 
grain boundary or sessile dislocation, which will prevent further expansion. Then, the rest of 
dislocations piles up behind it: in this case none of them annihilates as all of them are the same 
sign. As a result of a pile-up, dislocations interact with each other and the distance between 
them decreases when approaching the leading dislocation, at which the highest shear stress is 
acting. If there is 𝑛 dislocations in the pile-up and the applied shear stress is equal to 𝜏, then the 
shear stress 𝜏1 acting on the leading dislocation is equal to: 
==PAGE:31==

 𝜏1 = 𝜏𝑛 
This directly states that the resultant stress in the first leading dislocation is magnified by the 
number of dislocations. As a result, dislocation pile-ups creates large, long –range stresses. This 
has enormous impact on the materials failure as at the grain boundary a pile-up may result in 
yielding in the neighbouring grains or create boundary cracks. Furthermore, it is known that 
pile-ups assist in the cross slip when the screw dislocation was stopped by the dislocation locks, 
immobilized by precipitants or other mechanisms. It is worth to mention that pile-ups are 
mainly built of edge dislocations as screw one can cross slip out of the plane of a pile-up. [2]

6. Crystal orientation in polycrystals 
It is quite simple to define the slip system and crystal orientation for single crystals with known 
material structure. Although, it becomes much more complex issue if polycrystals are 
concerned. In the multi grain material, each grain may have a different orientation to its 
neighbour. This fact indicates that in the macro scale the total material orientation and 
dominating slip system may be random or somehow oriented. Materials that display any 
dominating grains orientation are recognized as materials with texture. It is crucial element of 
the crystallographic analysis to be aware if the texture is present or not. [9] 
One of methods used for texture analysis, is x-ray diffraction analysis at the cross-section of 
the investigated material. X-ray diffraction method gives average orientation on the surface of 
the specimen what usually represents the whole bulk well enough. The outcome from the X-

projections of poles densities as a function of poles orientations. A simple illustration and 
explanation of the pole figure can be done by an example: [9]

different directions: P1, P2 and P3, respectively. Using stereographic projection, these poles 
can be plotted in polar coordinates: it results in the so called “pole figure” (Fig. 32a). In the

8. Types of dislocation structures, depending on the slip system, in copper 
single crystals 
Experiments ascertained that dislocations distribution in deformed metals almost never shows 
homogeneity.  Instead, dislocations are distributed heterogeneously what results in alternating 
areas with high and low dislocation density. [12] The driving force for the formation of these 
regions is the reduction of energy of dislocation. More favourable from the energetic point of 
view is for dislocations to form locally dense arrangements with well confined elastic strain 
fields. [13] The type of heterogeneous dislocation structure present in deformed materials 
depends on various factors such as orientation or deformation severity, which have an impact 
on the dominant slip systems. For instance, when the dominating slip system is multiple slip, 
the three dimensional cell structure dislocations can be found. [14] In these patterns, vast 
majority of dislocations are present in the cell walls, often called also as cell boundaries, while 
the rest of cell is almost dislocation free (Figure 45).  
 
Figure 45. An example of a cell structure in cyclically deformed copper single crystal [15] 
On the other hand, when single-slip condition dominates, the outcome is a structure of roughly 
uniformly separated elongated dislocation clusters. Each cluster contains primary edge- 
dislocation dipoles and multipoles which then, depending on the load conditions, are named 
differently. In the case of cyclic deformation they are called veins and walls, respectively 
(Figure 46). 
==PAGE:44==

Figure 46. Dislocation clusters in the form of veins (brighter) and walls (darker) [12]

9. Polycrystalline materials 
In previous chapter, all microstructures, depending on the different slip systems and strains in 
the single crystal, were discussed. These findings can be also applied for the polycrystalline, 
however, to the limited extend. Differences come directly from the fact that single crystal does 
not interact with its neighbours. Relations between grains and type of boundaries influence the 
material’s plastic behaviour. Furthermore, grains have different orientations what results in, to 
some extended, defined texture which has enormous impact on the activity of slip systems. Last 
but not least, the grains size also needs to be taken into account. The first element discussed in 
this chapter is a theoretical composite-grain model that can be well used in predicting properties 
of a deformed polycrystalline material, as it takes into account the effect of heterogeneity of the 
structure. Then, main factors having an impact on the polycrystalline copper cyclic response 
and the resultant microstructures will be discussed and presented. 
9.1. The composite-grain model 
The first proposition of this model came from Hirth [40]. The main assumption is that the 
deformed grain is composed of two distinct regions. The first one that corresponds to the region 
that is near the grain boundary is called as a “hard”, and the second that represents the interior 
of the grain and is known as a “soft”. Figure 54 presents it schematically.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 54. Scheme of a composite model. Grain is composed from the hard region (black) in the 
boundaries and a soft (white) in the interior [40] 
The division for two regions come from the severity of deformation. On the grain boundaries 
the multiple slip is a dominating system due to incompatibilities between neighbouring grains. 
This results in dislocations pile-up on grains edges. The size of the hard phase strongly depends 
on the deformation severity and may even occupy whole grain for a very large strains. On the 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
other hand, as the most of flow stress is already taken by the hard phase, the stresses inside of 
the grain are significantly lower. As a result, the single slip is a dominating system and the 
deformation is mainly taken by the PSBs formation [41]. The formula for the total flow stress 
acting on the grain can be written as: [59]

Obviously, some additional improvements to the model can be introduced. For instance, Llanes 
and co-workers [43-49] took into account the texture of a material: exactly, the volume fraction 
of crystals having an orientation which favours multiple slip. Researchers assumed that these 
type of grains should be treated as “hard” on the whole region of a grain. Their approach is 
strongly supported by experimental results. For instance, coarse grained copper has a hard 
texture with a complex dislocation arrangement, mainly created by a multiple slip [48].  
The impact of the composite-type models can be proved by numerous significant progresses in 
several areas of researches about the crystal plasticity. Here, few examples can be mentioned: 
work done on cyclic deformation effects [50-56], on monotonic work hardening and the 
Bauschinger effect [57] or on cyclic creep [58-59]. The popularity and effects of use come from 
the major strength of the composite-grain model: it is possible with its use to evaluate local 
microstructural parameters which can be directly measured. [59]. 
9.2. Grain size, texture and grain boundaries in the polycrystalline copper 
All researches done for a single crystals under cyclic load were essential for further work on 
polycrystalline materials, but needed broadening. Main factors having an impact on the active 
slip system and further microstructure development are grain boundaries, grains dominant 
orientation in the crystal and size. All these elements cannot be easily divided, especially texture 
and grain refinement acts as a combined factor.  
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
The influence of grain boundaries was firstly found by Chung et al. [60]. They stated that due 
to incapability between neighbouring grains, usually various multiple slip systems will be 
present around grain boundaries. These results were also confirmed by Li et al. [31] who 
pointed out the importance of single crystals, oriented for the multiple slip systems, analyses in 
order to understand polycrystalline respond. More recently, Sumigawa et al. [61] with use of 
FEM found that stress concentrates near the grain boundaries, what promotes PSBs formation 
close to them. 
In the case of a grain size, Pedersen [62] was one of the first researchers investigating its 
influence, finding saturation stress to be independent of a grain size in the low amplitude 
regime. However his work is in opposite to all further researches. Lukas and Kunz [63] were 
first to observe dependence of the cyclic behaviour of copper to be dependent on a grain size. 
The same results were obtained by Horibe et al. [64] in their research on age hardened Al-Cu 
alloys. Moreover, further confirmation was done by Llanes and Laird [43] who found direct 
dependence on the saturation stress with respect to the grain size in the low amplitude regime, 
proposing also a threshold grain size below which its impact becomes negligible. 
The first investigation of a texture influence was also done by Pedersen [62] who used X-ray 
diffraction. However due to special processing method texture was greatly randomized for wide 
range of grain size what resulted in the previously mentioned lack of correlation with future 
results. The more fundamental and extensive work done on the impact of grains size and their 
orientations in polycrystalline material was done by Mughrabi and Wang [42]. They found that 
copper polycrystals show different CSSC depending on the grain size and texture. Depending 
on the annealing conditions, they achieved samples with low grain size having a close to 
randomly oriented texture as well as coarse grained structure with strong <111>-<100> fibre 
texture. The same conclusions were also reached by Jensen et al. [65]. LLanes et al. [48] who 
proposed an assumption that the material’s texture and grain size cannot be treated separately, 
as both are strongly correlated. They investigated the cyclic respond of the three different grain 
size copper polycrystal and their results also go along with precursors, showing that for all grain 
sizes the mixture of  <111> and <100>  fibre components are present and become more sharp 
with an increase of a grain size.  
From all above presented results it becomes clear that due to presence of grain boundaries, size 
of grains and texture has an impact on the slip system and favours multiple slip behaviour in 
<111> and <100> directions.  
==PAGE:53==

9.3. Cyclic response and substructure evolution in copper polycrystals 
As presented above, the grain size and materials texture has significant impact on the active slip 
systems what results in various microstructures that can form in the polycrystalline copper. 
Moreover, additional factors coming from the type of cyclic load, such as strain amplitude, 
frequency or type of load: tensile or compressive, also play major role. Therefore, substructure 
development is influenced by many factors, which in various combinations favours different 
dislocation arrangements. 
From 1975 until 1995 there was a long debate whether the CSSC for polycrystalline copper 
shows a plateau region or not. However, after two decades scientists have approved that it exists 
in coarse grained copper polycrystals, with grains exceeding 100um [66, 67, 68, 69]. In the case 
of a fine grained material, the quasi-plateau is present and saturation stress increases slightly 
and constantly with an increase of plastic strain amplitude. However, in comparison to the 
single crystal, these plateau or quasi-plateau regions are much shorter [7]. 
9.4. Fine and coarse grained copper 
The CSSC for a fine grained copper polycrystal is presented in Figure 55. Curve shows three 
regions with different hardening behaviour, marked as A, B and C, which correspond to low, 
medium and high plastic strain amplitude values respectively. In the first one, the A region, the 
saturation stress increases significantly with an increase of plastic strain amplitude. The same 
behaviour can be seen in the region C. However, in the region B the stress increases slightly 
and constantly, resulting in the quasi-plateau. It is worth to mention, that this CSSC curve is 
very similar to findings in single crystals oriented for the multiple-slip. [7]. In the case of a 
coarse grained copper polycrystal, CSSC curve is comparable, however, instead of quasi-
plateau, the plateau can be observed.  
==PAGE:54==

Figure 55. CSSC for copper polycrystal [7] 
 
Obviously, each region shows different dislocation microstructure. In the region A, for both, 
fine and coarse grained copper, for very low strain amplitude values, below 1.5 ∗10−5, three 
types of dislocation structures are possible to occur. First possibility are cylindrical dislocation 
clusters, separated by an almost dislocation free areas (Figure 56a). These clusters are 
composed of parallel dislocation dipoles, what explains that they were formed by stacking of 
dislocation dipoles. The second type of structures and the most common one, also observed in 
single crystals [7, 71], are irregular loop patches (Figure 56b). These patches terminate into 
kind of a channel structure. The third observed structures are loop patches which formed 
incomplete cellular structure (Figure 56c). This structure can be formed due to interaction of 
multiple slip systems forming dislocation tangles and networks. Although, the cellular type of 
substructure is far more often to occur in the low end of the region B than in the region A, as it 
was observed for single crystals [71], as well as, in coarse polycrystals [72].

In the region B, when strain amplitude reaches values from 1.5 ∗10−5 to 7.5 ∗10−4, the vein 
structure (Figure 57) is dominant and is very common to the one observed in single crystals 
[26, 27, 73,74]. It is essential to point out that first PSBs formation was initiated from the 
breakdown of loop patches. The PSB structure for a fine grained copper does not differ from 
the one observed in single crystals [75-77] and coarse grained polycrystals [46, 72, 78]. 
However, in the coarse grained copper polycrystalline PSBs were the dominant form of 
substructure. For the strain amplitude in the middle of the plateau region, 10% of grains 
contained PSBs, reaching even up to 60% in the top of it. It is opposite to the findings in a fine 
grained one. Analyzing the quasi-plateau region, its lower end contains about 0.3% of PSBs 
while at the upper reaches only about 10% of the bulk area of the polycrystal. Therefore, there 
is a restriction present for the PSBs to create in the fine grained copper, what might be an answer 
in difference between plateau flatness of fine and coarse grained material [68, 69].

10.1. As received material analysis 
Samples were cut from larger copper rods. During rod processing, severe plastic deformation 
might have been already introduced into the material’s microstructure. As a result, it would has 
significant impact on further experimental results.  Therefore, in order to analyse whether 
material was plastically deformed or not, hardness tests were performed. In addition, the 
material’s microstructure was also investigated under the optical microscope.  
Microstructure analysis was performed on four specimens. In order to prepare them, two copper 
pieces were cut from a rod. Then, both pieces were cut into two parts: a first one was cut 
perpendicularly to the extrusion direction, while the second one along to the extrusion direction 
(Figure 59). As a results, the two first samples were cut in cross-sectional direction and the 
second two in longitudinal direction. Next, two pieces, one in each cutting direction, were

reasonable resolution and acceptable intensity, a lower step size of 0,02o was used but the time 
frame for each position was increased to 4minutes and 30 seconds resulting in circa 6 hours for 
a single measurement and around 24hours for all four. Used slits were: 0.6 divergence slit, 0.6 
scattering slit and 0.2 detector slit. The reached intensity was acceptable and could be further 
analysed with Topas® software to find best matching curves, remove background and K-alpha 
2 signals, and finally compare different analysed positions and samples. Further equipment 
specifications and test details are reported in the appendix 2.

11.3. Microstructural observations: optical microscope 
Optical microscopy was used in order to investigate the surface features of all analysed samples. 
In the previous chapter “materials and methods” the as received material was analysed in order 
to estimate grain size, compare the grain shape and observe possible plastic deformation. 
Furthermore, samples before and after heat treatment in air were compared.  
Optical microscope was used again to analyse surface features, on both cut sections, of all 
cyclically loaded samples annealed in hydrogen-argon atmosphere. Moreover, the sample that 
was not loaded, but heat treated in the hydrogen-argon atmosphere was also investigated. This 
enables to compare surface features and grains sizes of samples heat treated in two different 
atmospheres. 
Despite complex polishing of the extremely soft samples, scratch-free places were obtained and 
optical analysis was possible. In most cases two magnifications were used: 50x for a general 
microstructure overview of the samples and 200x for an interesting or specific microstructural 
features. Samples that were not loaded, but heat treated in different atmospheres, were only 
analysed on the cross-section, magnifications 100x and 50x were used for samples annealed in 
air and hydrogen-argon, respectively.  
Comparison of heat treatment conditions 
In the case of different heat treatment conditions the goal is to estimate and compare grain sizes 
and shape and check if some possible retained deformation structure exist after heat treatments. 
Figures 89 and 90 show microstructure of an as received material annealed in the air and in the 
hydrogen-argon atmosphere, respectively. It can be directly observed that there is a significant 
difference in the grain size. In the sample heat treated in the air (Figure 89) grains size can be 
estimated roughly most of grains falls in the range of 80-100 μm. Annealing in the hydrogen-
argon (Figure 90) atmosphere contributed to an excessive grain growth and, despite a very small 
magnification, only one grain can be observed within the field of view, which size exceeds 
1mm. Due to the Hall-Petch effect, there is no denying that large discrepancy in grains size has 
an impact on mechanical properties of both specimens and is one of possible explanations of 
the difference in hardness between two annealing conditions.

12. Discussion and summary 
Microstructure evolution during the fatigue of a copper polycrystal is a complex and demanding 
issue. To begin with, in order to understand the task, deep theoretical analysis was required. 
Therefore, the very first step was to learn basics of crystallography, the fatigue phenomenon and 
crystal plasticity. Then, it was required to master the microstructural response of a fatigued copper 
single crystal. Finally, the behaviour of copper polycrystals under the cyclic tension-compression 
loading, has been understood. Last but not least, experimental and observational techniques were 
needed to be learned. Then, experiments investigating and analyzing, how the microstructure of 
copper polycrystals evolves during fatigue, were performed.  
12.1. Samples preparation 
With the required theoretical background, the experimental work was started. The first point was 
to analyze as received material, if it could be used for the fatigue tests. Therefore, four specimens 
were prepared from the received copper rod. First two were obtained by cutting one piece of a rod 
in a longitudinal direction, with respect to the extrusion direction. The second two by cutting 
another piece into two elements in a cross-sectional direction, also with respect to the extrusion 
direction. Then two pieces, one in each direction, were heat treated in air for 48h in 800oC. 
Annealing was performed in order to compare microstructure and hardness of as received material 
before and after heat treatment, in both cutting directions. In both cases differences were clearly 
visible: the microstructure of an as received material was heavily deformed as twins were strongly 
curved; the annealed material was much softer: the difference in hardness between heat treated and 
not heat treated material was in the range of 18%. These findings directly stated that before any 
fatigue tests could have been performed, the as received material needed to be annealed. It needs 
to be stated that only microhardness tests were possible, macrohardness tests could not be used due 
to severe surface deformation around it and the irregular shape.   
The heat treatment process also needed to be optimized. Annealing in air resulted in a deep 
oxidation of the specimen’s surface. Further chemical attack on the oxidized layer, in order to 
remove it, resulted in non-uniform removal and, the most important, the loss of dimensions 
accuracy. This could not be accepted and other heat treatment conditions must have been applied.  
The solution for this problem was using hydrogen (20%)-argon (80%) atmosphere. It has to be 
mentioned that grains after heat treatment in air did not increase significantly, therefore, the same 
time and temperature was used in the second furnace. Heat treatment for 48h in 800oC in H2-Ar 
==PAGE:113==
112 
 
resulted in well annealed specimens, without any surface oxidation and dimensions accuracy was 
saved, however, unexpectedly revealed much larger grain size.   
12.2. Fatigue tests 
Having samples prepared, fatigue tests were performed. Although, before any measurements could 
be done, the machine needed to be calibrated. That required finding optimum working conditions 
empirically. Most of the problems came from the requirement of strain controlled measurements 
at which sample was buckling instead of being fatigue properly. It was solved by switching to the 
position controlled measurements. Then, planned displacement amplitude of ∆𝑙= 0.01 𝑚𝑚 was 
not achievable due to machine limitations. However, it was found that for the twice as larger 
displacement amplitude, equal to 0.02 mm, Instron 8872 was working properly and the desired 
triangle signal was obtained. It has to be pointed out that any distortion during the machine’s work, 
even opening of a spreadsheet with collected values, resulted in data loss as the software stopped 
collecting it for the time the sheet was opened. Moreover, too frequent data point collection also 
resulted data loss. That occurred during experiments, when data were saved every 0.02 s. Last but 
not least, before final cyclic tests could have been done, the stopping conditions needed to be set. 
It was decided to stop the machine in the maximum compression of a last cycle and then unload it 
linearly within 10 seconds. 
The first experiment was done for 160 000 cycles, to estimate the shape of a load amplitude versus 
number of cycles curve. Reached data set showed that material was firstly hardening, until it 
reached saturation regime between 8000-10 000 cycles and then the softening started. Therefore, 
next three specimens were loaded for 1000, 4000 and 12 000 cycles to get two specimens 
representing the microstructure in the hardening regime and one representing it just after the 
saturation was reached. The fourth sample, cycled for 160 000 cycles, represented the 
microstructure in the softening regime.  
Having data collected, curves for all four samples were compared in order to see if the saturation 
region is the same for all of them and to measure possible differences between samples. The first 
observation was that the sample loaded for 12 000 cycles reached saturation earlier, while the rest 
of samples were following the same curve as the sample loaded for 160 000 cycles. However, 
further analysis of discrepancies between curves showed that the absolute amplitude between the 
lowest point of a bottom curve and the highest point of a top curve was not more than 5.1% of an 
applied load. Furthermore, in most of the places these differences were far smaller. Therefore, it 
==PAGE:114==
113 
 
can be stated that mechanical behaviour of all four samples was following the same curve. Last but 
not least, some fluctuations of the load could have been observed for all samples. However, these 
fluctuations did not exceed 1% of the load amplitude and could be neglected. 
12.3.Specimens preparation and optical microscopy 
After fatigue tests were performed, samples needed to be cut for further microstructural analyses. 
Samples still very soft, therefore, special cutting machine was used to prepare specimens. For an 
each fatigued sample, two cross-sectional cuts and two longitudinal cuts, with respect to the loading 
direction, were done. The same was done for the sample heat treated in argon-hydrogen atmosphere 
for comparison purposes. From these cut pieces, for each loading time, two specimens were 
embedded in polymer matrix: one in each cutting direction, in total 8 specimens. The same way, 
two additional were prepared for as received material, annealed in hydrogen-argon atmosphere. 
Next step was finding proper polishing processes which was a complex problem itself as copper 
was still very soft. Again, it was found empirically. Then, specimens were analyzed with an optical 
microscope. For every sample two images were taken: one magnified 50x and second magnified 
200x for an interesting surface feature. The direct result of the optical microscopy was that the used 
annealing conditions unintendedly resulted in excessive grain growth: grains were visible with a 
naked eye and their size was estimated to be even exceeding 1 mm.  
12.4. Hardness tests 
After surface observations with the optical microscope, further microhardness tests were performed 
for all fatigued and as received and heat treated in hydrogen-argon samples. Results show that most 
of hardening occurred in the stage close to the saturation regime as there was significant: 10% 
increase in hardness for samples loaded for 12 000 and 160 000 cycles in comparison to samples 
loaded for 4000 and 1000 cycles. Furthermore, there is almost no difference in hardness between 
samples cycled for 4000 cycles, 1000 cycles and not loaded at all but heat treated in H2-Ar. It 
slightly differs from the load amplitude versus number of cycles curve: it was expected that 
hardness will follow the hardening curve. It is interesting observation and further experimental 
work need to be done.  
Last but not least, there was even a larger difference when two different heat treatment conditions 
were compared: hardness of samples heat treated in air was 18% larger than ones annealed in 
hydrogen-argon atmosphere. The most plausible explanation for this phenomena are differences in 
==PAGE:115==
114 
 
the heating conditions. The first evidence for this assumption comes from the fact that the sample 
heat treated in air did not anneal completely as some slightly curved twins were still observable. 
Moreover, there is enormous difference in a grain size between samples annealed in air and in H2-
Ar. Sample that was heat treated in air has grains 10 to 20 times smaller than the one that was heat 
treated in hydrogen-argon atmosphere. According to the Hall-Petch effect, excessive grain growth 
lowers materials hardness.  
12.5. X-ray diffraction analysis 
In total four specimens, two in both cutting directions, underwent x-ray diffraction analysis. Two 
were from the sample cycled for 1000 cycles and two from the sample cycled for 12 000 cycles. 
The first attempt was to analyze specimens from the sample loaded for 12 000 cycles with the 
maximum possible machine’s resolution, in order to reach very narrow peak and to be able to 
observe peak’s asymmetry. However, for both cutting directions, a reached signal intensity was too 
low and could not be analyzed statistically: further peak modelling did not give any results. 
Therefore, both specimens from the sample fatigued for 1000 cycles underwent x-ray analysis with 
larger slits, a larger step size and longer time frame. Furthermore, the sample cut in the longitudinal 
direction was analyzed in three different positions, in order to check the homogeneity of the sample. 
That gave in total four measurements. New testing conditions gave much more data points and a 
peak modelling could have been performed.  For all four data sets pseudo-Voigt split was used and 
matched very well. However, the outcome showed that the sample was heterogeneous. The 
heterogeneity may be intrinsic or comes from the lack of points for the statistics: due to few enormous 
grains, not enough data from different grain was collected. This states that x-ray diffraction cannot 
be used for analysis of the asymmetry when such large grains are present.  
12.6. EBSD analysis 
Before the EBSD analysis could be performed, specimens needed to be firstly electropolished. For 
all loading conditions as well as for a sample only heat treated in H2-Ar atmosphere, samples cut 
along the longitudinal section underwent electropolishing and further EBSD analysis. Calibrations 
were performed on the as received sample. It was found that primary polishing and electropolishing 
were insufficient to obtain high quality orientation map and additional treatments were need to 
improve samples surfaces. Different conditions were applied to each of samples, in order to find 
whether prepolishing or electropolishing were the most important factors having an influence on 
the surface quality. The result, which treatment process is more important, could not be 
==PAGE:116==
115 
 
unanimously stated. An additional precise mechanical polishing improved samples surface and 
reached maps with this preprocessing were good quality. On the other hand, orientation maps taken 
for a sample without this extra mechanical treatment were also acceptable. Nevertheless, probably 
the most affecting factor was precision of the electropolishing process itself. A liquid leakage 
resulted in areas not fully polished and contaminated, what was then seen on orientation maps. 
Despite problems with surface preparation, for all four cyclically loaded samples, orientation maps 
were obtained. The additional issue appeared as the orientation of the sample in the microscope 
chamber was not given, with respect to the X, Y and Z axis in the software. However, the most 
possible was that the X-direction was a tensile direction: the orientation map in X direction was 
showing mainly <111> and <100> grains and twins. That goes along with observations of other 
researchers: large grains promotes a <111>-<100> fibre texture. Furthermore, the misorientation 
analysis found that on grains oriented in <111> direction there are also twins oriented in the <111> 
direction seen etch-on in the map. Presence of such twins is the second evidence for the X- direction 
as loading direction.  
The very last element was the misorientation analysis across the grains itself, omitting grain 
boundaries and twins. If misorientation values were at least larger than few degrees, subgrains 
would be possible to observe with used EBSD detector. However, reached misorientations varies 
insignificantly: up to 2o-2.5 o. These values are in the range of a background noise for the used 
detector and subgrains could not be seen. However, it is plausible that for better samples surface 
preparation and smaller step size dislocation boundaries would be observable. If these 
improvements would not bring expected results, then another detector or methods, for instance 
electron channeling contrast or transmission electron microscopy, need to be applied.

13. Conclusions 
In this work, microstructure evolution during the fatigue of copper polycrystals was extensively 
studied and analyzed. Based on the obtained results, the following conclusions can be drawn: 
1. It is essential to control the heat treatment process in the austenitization oven. Excessive 
grain growth had a huge impact on all results and, for a most of used observational 
techniques, made it impossible to perform the desired analyses.  
2. The microhardness test showed that hardening mainly occurred between 4000 and 12 000 
cycles.  
3. Instron 8872 equipment was calibrated and samples were fatigued at the lowest achievable 
strain amplitude of 4 ∗10−4. However, if one has a chance, it would be suitable to use other 
machine, working on smaller loads. Then, it would be possible to perform analyses for a 
wider range of strain amplitudes.  
4. Due to enormous grains size, the X-ray analysis did not give satisfying results: there was 
not enough statistical data to observe, model and compare the asymmetries of the peaks. 
5. It is crucial to establish preparation techniques for the EBSD analysis. With a better surface 
quality, a smaller step size could be applied and dislocation boundaries may become 
observable.

14. Future Work 
As a very first step in further investigations, it is essential to establish and control grain growth 
during annealing. On one hand, too short heat treatment will not fully recover the microstructure 
of the material and the remaining plastically deformed regions will have large impact on its further 
evolution. On the other hand, too long annealing leads to the excessive grain growth.  
Moreover, it might be essential to apply another techniques in order to observe dislocations 
boundaries and subgrains. According to previous works [26-39], the Electron Channeling Contrast 
and Transmission Electron Microscopy were used with satisfying results. 
Last but not least, there was a lot of work focused on the impact of different strain amplitudes on 
the saturation stress values. These researches resulted in CSS curves for single and polycrystals of 
copper, as well as, other pure metals. However, it is far less known how these metals behave before 
the saturation regime, in a hardening stage, and after it, in a softening regime. Therefore, further 
research on microstructure evolution for different strain amplitudes, is needed in all three regimes.

 Appendix 3 
Twelve samples were analysed for the hardness and collected in twelve tables below. First two are 
for as received material before any treatment. Tables 7 and 8 show data collected for received 
material after 48h annealing in 800oC in the oven with air atmosphere. In this treatment material in 
the surface got oxidized; an oxide layer was removed and hardness was measured for not oxidized 
material. Tables 9 to 16 are for samples annealed 48h in 800oC in argon-hydrogen atmosphere and 
cyclically strained for a given number of cycles. Tables 9 and 10: 1000 cycles; tables 11 and 12: 
4000 cycles; tables 13-14: 12 000 cycles; tables 15-16: 160 000 cycles. All results from these tables 
were statistically analysed and collected in the table 18. For each case first table shows cross-
sectional hardness while second one in the longitudinal direction. It has to be stated that due to 
discrepancies in results, some additional tests were performed. The table 19 shows second test for 
the sample cycled for 160 000 cycles, cut in longitudinal direction. Tables 21 and 22 show 
measurements for material that was not cyclically loaded but heat treated in the hydrogen-argon 
atmosphere. Finally, table 24 combines all obtained results
