Abstract
This thesis conducts a literature review of landmarkВӯdependent and landmarkВӯagnostic
face image quality assessment, including face detection, face alignment, face segmentaВӯ
tion, landmark localization, and Facial Image Quality Assessment Measures. The primary
focus of the research is a comparative analysis between the landmarkВӯagnostic approach,
represented by the faceВӯparsing method, and the landmarkВӯdependent approach, repreВӯ
sented by the Dlib method. The evaluation involves measuring various quality compoВӯ
nents, such as head length, head size, interВӯeye distance, eye open, mouth closed, and
crop of the face image. In addition, a novel heuristic function for computing head length is
proposed and tested under various resolutions. The results of the comparative analysis,
heuristic function testing, and their addressing of the research questions are presented
through illustrative diagrams. The thesis concludes with suggestions for addressing limiВӯ
tations and outlines potential future work.

Introduction
Face image quality assessment (FIQA) is a research area in the field of facial biometrics.
It is primarily focused on evaluating the quality of a facial image in terms of its utility for
face recognition systems. Furthermore, facial detection by traditional landmark models
has a crucial role in assessing image quality, as the accurate localization of traits in the
face image can positively impact the reliability of the biometric process.
A novel methodology in the field of Face Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) is the вҖҷLandmarkВӯ
agnosticвҖҷ approach to measure the quality of facial images. This technique aims to reduce
the dependence on specific facial landmarks and landmark detection methods for tasks,
especially face image quality assessment. Instead of relying on landmarks localization, it
uses facial segmentation to extract facial features from a facial image.
In ISO/IEC 29794Вӯ5:2023(CD3) [1], face image quality assessment (FIQA) uses the qualВӯ
ity components (QC) to evaluate the quality of a facial image and validate the utility of
the face image for the face recognition systems. Moreover, QC is determined by features
extracted from the facial image and it could be influenced by the behavior of the image
subjects or the process of capturing the facial image.
SubjectВӯrelated QCs are a type of quality measure that can be used to evaluate and
provide feedback to the captured subject or biometric attendant. Such quality measures
determine compliance to specific quality requirements defined for the quality of facial imВӯ
ages and they are taken from the subjectвҖҷs facial expressions, poses, and actions. Some
of the SubjectВӯrelated QCs are:
вҖў Head size: It is a proportion of eyeВӯchin distance to image height. And eyeВӯchin
distance is the distance between the chin and the central point between the left and
right eye.
вҖў InterВӯeye distance (IED): It is a distance between the left and right eye centers afВӯ
fected by yaw angle (the rotation of the head to the left or the right side).
вҖў Eye openness: It is the eyes naturally open instead of being forced wideВӯopen or
close.
вҖў Mouth closed: It is the mouth naturally closed without smiling, opening, or being
forced to be closed.
вҖў Crop of face image: It is the facial image without leftward, rightward, downward,
and upward crop. If it has leftward and rightward crops, it has a poor horizontal face
position. While having downward and upward crops means a poor vertical face
position.
CaptureВӯrelated QCs are a type of quality components that can be used to evaluate the
proper settings of the capture device. They can be used to provide feedback to the system
operator or biometric attendant. Also, these measures determine compliance to specific
quality requirements defined for the quality of facial images and they are taken from the
acquisition process of the facial images. Some of the captureВӯrelated QCs are:
вҖў Resolution: It represents the number of pixels in an image. The higherВӯresolution
image has more pixels and detail. It can impact the clarity and precision of facial
features.

1.1
Background
Advancements in AI and computer vision help the development of facial recognition techВӯ
nologies across diverse domains, including security, entertainment, and healthcare. This
thesis focuses on the critical aspect of face image quality assessment, exploring landmarkВӯ
agnostic methods compared to traditional landmarkВӯbased approaches.
Automatic face recognition systems have a rich history spanning six decades, originating
in psychology in the 1950s and formalized by KellyвҖҷs work in 1970. Beyond computer sciВӯ
ence, researchers from diverse backgrounds, including psychologists, psychophysicists,
neuroscientists, and engineers, have contributed to the field. The 1980s saw a lull, but
interest surged again in the 1990s due to increased realВӯtime hardware availability and
the growing importance of surveillance applications [2].
Around the year 2006, the вҖҷLandmark Model Matching MethodвҖҷ was just a newly developed
method in face recognition based on, for example, Elastic Buncg Graph and Active Shape
Models. Most of the estimators published are very initial, and one of the examples from
the study [3] conducted the face localization process into 2 stages. Stage 1 is to estimate
the head and face region by resizing the image to half of its size. It reduces the space
for the Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) algorithm. In the Landmark Model, it modifies
its dimensions including coordinates (x and y), size factor, and weights for the first three
principal components. With an approximate head location, stage 2 gives higher weights
to interior nodes for a more accurate face region determination within the smaller search
space. Finally, the landmarks are adjusted to be as close as possible to the face features in
the image, like the process shown in figure 1.1. Since then, many researchers and experts
have been working on improving the landmark methods to get a better performance and
more efficient way of face recognition.

For instance, 10 years later, Panagiotis et al. [4] developed a method for 3D landmark
detection, proposed as a 3D Facial Landmarks Model (FLM). It uses a 3D technique
to automatically detect landmarks on 3D facial scans that exhibit variations in pose and
expression. The proposed approach ensures consistent registration and comparison of
facial datasets even in the presence of missing data due to selfВӯocclusion. In 2017, a 2D
technique to identify a person using facial landmarks was developed by Aniwat Juhong et

Apart from this, there are various landmark estimators that were developed, each of which
offers unique approaches to identify facial features, including Dlib [6] [7], MediaPipe [8]
[9], MTCNN (MultiВӯtask Cascaded Convolutional Networks) [10], InsightFace [11] [12] [13],
RetinaFace [14], and ADNet [15]. DlibвҖҷs facial landmark detector employs a combination
of shape prediction and image features, utilizing a facial shape model trained on diverse
datasets. It is known for its efficiency and is widely used in both research and practical
applications [7]. MediaPipe offers a facial landmark detection framework using machine
learning techniques. It is wellВӯknown for its realВӯtime performance and versatility, accomВӯ
modating various applications beyond facial landmark estimation, such as hand and body
tracking [9][8]. MTCNN, as a network architecture designed for face detection, includes
also the stage for landmark localization. InsightFace employs deep convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) to estimate facial landmarks, contributing to good performance in faceВӯ
recognitionВӯrelated tasks, While RetinaFace has high accuracy in detecting the landmarks
under different poses, scales, and occlusions [10]. ADNet introduces attention mechaВӯ
nisms to facial landmark estimation, allowing the model to focus on relevant facial regions
[15]. This attentionВӯbased approach enhances the precision of landmark localization, esВӯ
pecially in the presence of complex variations. More details about estimators can be found
in section 2.

proaches developed and used in many areas related to face recognition systems. While
facial landmarks technology develops more and more mature, there is another way in
which face segmentation has been published with experiments Вӯ Face parsing.

FaceВӯparsing, which segments the face into different regions, provides more detailed inВӯ
formation and can potentially handle variations in pose and expression better. Such esВӯ
timators generate a map to distinguish different facial features in various classes. For
example, in [1], Clause 6.8 categorizes the face into 19 different features, including backВӯ
ground, eyebrows in left and right, nose, mouth, and so on. This comes at the cost of
increased computational complexity and potentially higher data requirements. The use of
landmarks may be favored for its efficiency and simplicity, particularly when addressing
challenges related to pose and expression variations. For example, Minocha et al. [16] in
the year 2013, developed a method to parse the face images and detection, estimate their
poses, and locate landmarks on the face with higher reliability. The image or video frame
undergoes initial processing using a face detector, employing the VJ algorithm [16], with
a lowered threshold to minimize misses even at the expense of increased false positives.
The resulting bounding boxes are expanded by a scaling factor F and forwarded to two
subcomponents: FaceВӯTrack and FaceВӯDetect. FaceВӯTrack utilizes the ZR algorithm [16]
to track faces from the previous frame, optimizing detection efficiency. FaceВӯDetect, an
optimized ZR version, leverages the face structure and the face location indicated by the
detector. Computational costs of ZR are addressed through three optimizations: reducВӯ
ing scale space levels to 3, limiting spatial configurations based on key pointsвҖҷ localized
regions, and decreasing the number of components in FaceВӯTrack, considering the prior
information from the previous frame. These optimizations collectively enhance the algoВӯ
rithmвҖҷs speed while preserving accuracy in face detection and tracking, particularly under
unconstrained conditions and variations in pose and expression.
In 2023, Lu Yang et al. [17] developed the QualityВӯAware Network (QANet) for face parsing
by studying the performance of the stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart human parsing method. The method
follows the standard pipeline of QANet and replaces the body images with the face imВӯ
ages. It achieves a high score of 86.84% on the shortВӯvideo face parsing test. The same
year, the ComponentВӯLevel Refinement Network (CLRNet) presented an approach to adВӯ
dress challenges in face parsing, specifically focusing on accurately segmenting tiny facial
components [18]. Unlike conventional methods that rely on a twoВӯstage croppingВӯandВӯ
segmenting strategy, CLRNet introduces an attention mechanism to seamlessly connect
and jointly optimize these stages, creating an endВӯtoВӯend trainable pipeline for face parsВӯ
ing. This integrated approach enhances accuracy, overcoming limitations in predicting
pixelВӯwise labels for small facial components. Additionally, CLRNet incorporates global
context information during the refinement process for each cropped facial component
patch, providing valuable cues for precise parsing. Extensive experiments on benchmark
datasets, including LFWВӯPL and HELEN, demonstrate CLRNetвҖҷs superiority over stateВӯ
ofВӯtheВӯart methods, particularly in achieving accurate segmentation of tiny facial compoВӯ
nents. This research contributes to advancing face parsing techniques by addressing key
limitations and introducing an innovative, endВӯtoВӯend trainable framework.
Face Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) plays an important role in advancing facial recogВӯ
nition technologies by providing a systematic framework for evaluating the quality of faВӯ
cial images. The accurate assessment of facial image quality is crucial in various apВӯ
plications. Traditionally, FIQA methods have heavily relied on landmarkВӯdependent apВӯ
proaches, where the position localization of facial landmarks is taken as a basis for quality
assessment. The landmarkВӯbased methods have effects in controlled environments, but
challenges arise in realВӯworld scenarios characterized by pose variations, occlusions, and
other facial expressions.

1.2
Problem statement
Traditional approaches to FIQA rely heavily on landmarkВӯdependent methods, where faВӯ
cial landmarks are used to guide and evaluate image quality. The face landmarks are
anthropometric points in the image marking the contour and different parts of the face [1].
Figure 1.3 shows the example image of a face with 98 landmarks detected.
Figure 1.3: An example of face image with landmarks from [1]
However, the landmarkВӯagnostic FIQA approach came up to challenge the landmarkВӯ
dependent approaches for achieving highВӯperformance results.
This raises concerns
about the potential tradeВӯoffs between landmarkВӯagnostic approaches and landmarkВӯdependent
methods in terms of quality components. LandmarkВӯdependent methods have been demonВӯ
strated as successful, but they still could face limitations in handling variations in, for inВӯ
stance, pose, and expression. One of the common key limitations associated with using
facial landmarks for FIQA can be the sensitivity to occlusions, such as accessories, hair,
or other objects partially covering the face. Inaccuracies in landmark localization may
occur when prominent facial traits are obscured. Also, landmark detectors may struggle
with some potential extreme facial poses or expressions, leading to inaccurate landmark
localization. As faces deviate from a neutral position or display intense expressions, the
modelвҖҷs performance can degrade. The question is whether landmarkВӯagnostic FIQA
methods can offer comparable performance without relying on explicit landmark localizaВӯ
tion in measuring various quality components.
LandmarkВӯagnostic methods for FIQA garnered attention for their potential to optimize the
assessment process by eliminating the need for precise landmark localization. However,
concerns persist regarding the performance of the landmarkВӯagnostic approach against
the landmarkВӯdependent approaches while measuring quality components. On the one
hand, landmarkВӯdependent methods may be tailored to specific datasets with explicit facial
landmarks, ensuring stable and good performance. On the other hand, the performance of
landmarkВӯagnostic approaches across diverse datasets is not generalized enough, since

there is not too much research and experiments on that. As a result, the challenge lies
in determining whether landmarkВӯagnostic methods can maintain their effectiveness and
accuracy across various scenarios measuring different quality components.
Moreover, resolution can be a concern for face segmentation and landmark localization.
LowВӯresolution images, such as compression artifacts or noise, may have impacts on the
accurate face segmentation and landmark localization. Insufficient image quality based

To address these concerns systematically, the thesis must investigate, and evaluate the
performance of the landmarkВӯagnostic approach compared with the landmarkВӯdependent

1.4
Purpose & Motivations
The primary purpose of this thesis is to compare the performance of the landmarkВӯagnostic
approach against the landmarkВӯdependent approach in measuring the quality components
of FIQA. These components should be measured and included in the comparison between
the two approaches, such as head length, head size, interВӯeye distance, eye openness,
mouth closed, and crop of face images.
The computation of quality components assessment is defined in the ISO/IEC 29794Вӯ
5:2023(CD3) [1], except for head length. Thus, it is needed to formulate a function for
head length measurement and test under different resolutions.
Before the comparison, it is necessary to select one landmarkВӯdependent estimator to be
employed in this project.
From the concerns stated in section 1.2, this thesis aims to address the concern about
the performance of the landmarkВӯagnostic approach against the landmarkВӯdependent apВӯ
proach on FIQA by experimenting and comparing various quality component measureВӯ
ment results. This would be an advantage when dealing with variations in the availability
of landmarks or when certain landmarks are less discernible in certain facial expressions

3. Gray Information: In face detection, grayscale images are used to represent the
light conditions by pixel intensity. Compared to color processing, it is less efficient
and lower signalВӯtoВӯnoise ratio.
4. Edges: edgeВӯbased techniques involve detecting sharp changes in image brightВӯ
ness, employing operators such as Sobel, MarrВӯHildreth, and steerable filters.

2.4
Landmark localization
Landmark localization, as an important part of face recognition, has seen advancements
through stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart methodologies. It states several methods used in the field in [21]
that early facial landmark detection methods, predating 2015, relied on deformable face
mesh fitting, notably the Active Shape Model (ASM) implemented in the state library [46].
While these approaches were accurate under controlled conditions, they struggled with
challenges like rotated poses, poor illumination, or partially occluded faces. In 2014, a
breakthrough occurred with an approach that applied gradient boosting on an EnsemВӯ
ble of Regression Trees (ERT) using pixel intensities as features, resulting in improved
robustness and realВӯtime speed. After 2015, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) beВӯ
came the predominant methodology for facial landmark localization. The CNNВӯbased
methods discussed include the Stacked Hourglass architecture, the Wing Loss approach
with modified loss functions for training, the Style Aggregated Network (SAN) leveragВӯ
ing a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), the Look at Boundary (LAB) method usВӯ
ing boundary heatmaps, the Practical Facial Landmark Detector (PFLD) employing MoВӯ
bileNetV2, the AWing algorithm combining ideas from LAB and the heatmap approach,
and the Deep Adaptive Graph (DAG) utilizing Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) [21].
These methods vary in their techniques, such as predicting landmark locations directly or
using heatmaps. DAG is highlighted as the current stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart, offering the highest
accuracy in landmark localization. Notably, there are also approaches involving 3D modВӯ
els, such as 3D Dense Face Alignment (3DDFA) and SynergyNet, and GoogleвҖҷs method
predicting a 3D morphable model with 468 landmarks. However, it still lacks public evalВӯ
uation results for facial landmark localization algorithms in constrained environments.
In the year 2022, Ukrit et al and other researchers presented a novel TransformerВӯbased
facial landmark localization network named Localization Transformer (LOTR) [47]. It inВӯ
troduces a direct coordinate regression approach with a Transformer network, enhancВӯ
ing spatial information utilization. Comprising a visual backbone, Transformer module,
and landmark prediction head, LOTR allows endВӯtoВӯend training on croppedВӯandВӯaligned
face images, eliminating postВӯprocessing. The novel smoothВӯwing loss improves converВӯ
gence over standard losses. Experimental results on JD and WFLW datasets demonВӯ
strate LOTRвҖҷs superiority over existing methods, promising face alignment results, and
enhanced face recognition system performance.

2.5
Facial Image Quality Assessment (FIQA)
It plays a vital role in computer vision and image processing by evaluating the quality of
facial images. Therefore, the main goal of the FIQA is to assess facial image quality based
on several factors and ensure that this image is suitable for face recognition systems and
other applications that involve human interaction.
Facial Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) mainly employs facial features that are extracted
from a facial image in order to measure the factors influencing that image quality. This
assessment is crucial for improving the face recognition analysis, enhancing the biometВӯ
ric systems, and optimizing all the applications that require human interaction. So, by
addressing the weaknesses of facial image quality, FIQA contributes to the development
of facial analysis technologies worldwide.
According to the ISO/IEC 29794Вӯ5:2023(CD3) [1], the factors that affect the facial image
quality can be categorized into two types:
вҖў SubjectВӯrelated quality factors: They are quality components that focus on aspects
related to the subjectвҖҷs face and how much they impact the overall quality of the
facial image.
вҖў CaptureВӯrelated quality factors: They are quality components that focus on the techВӯ
nical aspects of image acquisition and how much they impact the overall quality of
the facial image.

4.8
Missing measurement results in FaceВӯparsing
Face parsing involves segmenting an image into different regions corresponding to difВӯ
ferent facial components, such as eyes, nose, mouth, etc. It is a more complex task
compared to face detection, as it requires understanding the detailed facial structure and
identifying various facial parts. In the beginning, the LFW dataset is run without resizing
the images. It keeps processing the images with their original image size. However, more
than 2/5 of the images in the dataset cannot be detected with either missing eye positions
or chin points. By learning the faceВӯparsing model again from the library [32], one of the
potential reasons as a guess is that the preВӯtrained model used was trained by a specific
group of datasets that are with one specific image size. The quality and diversity of the
training data used to train the faceВӯparsing model can significantly impact its performance.
If the model hasnвҖҷt been trained on a broad range of facial images, it may struggle to genВӯ
eralize to different faces. It could cause the neutral network to have limitations on lower
performance with another size of images.
To test if it affects the outputs, several trials have been done by resizing the image to 1350
and 512. And the results show that it does have an impact. By resizing to 512*512 and
1350*1350 pixels, it can detect more images. The numbers of returned results increase,
but while trying with image size 2025, the detected outputs reduce again.
LandmarkВӯAgnostic Face Image Quality Assessment

==PAGE:76==
Also, faceВӯparsing models may require higherВӯresolution images with better quality to acВӯ
curately identify facial components. If the input images are lowВӯresolution or noisy, the
performance of the faceВӯparsing model might be compromised. Most of the undetected
images from the LFW dataset return errors while extracting eyes because of the noise.
4.9
Duplicates measurement results in Dlib
While analyzing the outputs from the LFW dataset, it is observed that the Dlib measureВӯ
ment has 13723 lines of results instead of 13233 lines (which is the number of images
in the LFW dataset). This is simply because there are multiple faces detected in one imВӯ
age. Dlib measures also the background human faces. If there are indeed multiple faces
in the image, DlibвҖҷs face detector may correctly identify and provide bounding boxes for
each detected face. In this case, the multiple results are expected. Faces in the image
may vary in size and orientation. DlibвҖҷs face detector is capable of detecting faces at difВӯ
ferent scales and orientations, which can result in multiple bounding boxes for the same
face under different transformations. Also, if faces in the image are close to each other
or overlapping, the face detector may generate multiple bounding boxes for the same
face or parts of faces. DlibвҖҷs face detector may produce false positives in some cases,
where it detects regions that are not actual faces. FineВӯtuning parameters or using postВӯ
processing techniques can help reduce false positives. What is more, poor image quality,
low resolution, or noise in the image can be factors that affect the performance of the face
detector, leading to multiple or incorrect detection.

does not have a significantly noticeable difference from the outputs generated by the Dlib
estimator with 3 different resolutions, as the results are all displayed in relatively smooth
curves. However, the faceВӯparsing approach is easier to be affected by the resolutions
compared with the Dlib estimator. There are more outliers of head length values from the
faceВӯparsing measurement when the dataset resolution is set to 2025 pixels.

5
Limitations and Future Works
5.1
Limitations
5.1.1
Training Dataset Dependency
The methodology of the thesis relies on the assumption that the localization of landmarks
by the Shape predictor preВӯtrained model in the Dlib library and the parsing map generated
by the (79999_iter.pth) preВӯtrained model from faceВӯparsing are accurate and reliable. The
performance of both models is subjected to how closely the selected dataset, Labelled
Faces in the Wild (LFW) Dataset, for the comparative analysis corresponds to the training
data of both models in terms of age groups, ethnicities, or genders.
5.1.2
Sensitivity to face alignment
One of the main limitations is that the performance of both models depends on the frontal
alignment of the detected faces in the facial images. Consequently, a small rotation of the
face to the right or left side may result in inaccurate quality measurements such as interВӯ
eye distance and face crop quality components. Both of these measurements depend on
the cos(yaw angle) that was ignored in this thesis due to time limitations.
5.2
Future Works
Many future works can be applied to this thesis in different directions. Some of them could
be:
5.2.1
Training the Models
Training both models on a diverse dataset which includes a tremendous number of facial
and capturing condition variations. Not only that but also testing the implementation of
the quality assessment components on more datasets can lead to the generalization and
accuracy of the addressed research questions in this thesis.
5.2.2
Implementation Developments
The yaw angle can be estimated and added to the existing implementation. In addiВӯ
tion, many other facial Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) measurements could be impleВӯ
mented to enhance the accuracy of the quality assessment process in this project.
5.2.3
Algorithm and Mapping improvements
Some mapping formulas in ISO/IEC 29794Вӯ5:2023(CD3) [1] need to be optimized. For
example, in this project, the head size quality component is mapped to a value range
between (0,100) but the formula in 3.4 is too sensitive to small changes in the head size
quality component values. The optimization could be done by adjusting the values of the
SIGMOID function.
5.2.4
RealВӯtime Applications
In the future, the feasibility of realВӯtime quality assessment applications could be explored
and invested in applications such as surveillance. This includes optimizing the existing
implementation to be faster in computation and handle the parallel processing techniques.
5.2.5
Integration with Facial Recognition Systems
In the future, the feasibility of cooperating the facial quality assessment with facial recogВӯ
nition systems could be explored. This integration could enhance the robustness and
reliability of facial analysis systems.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis conducts a literature review on face detection, face alignment,
face segmentation, landmark localization, and Facial Image Quality Assessment MeaВӯ
sures. Furthermore, it produces a comparative analysis of the performance of the landmarkВӯ
agnostic approach represented by the faceВӯparsing method against the landmarkВӯdependent
approach represented by the Dlib method by measuring different quality components.
These quality components are head length, head size, interВӯeye distance, eye open,
mouth closed, and crop of the face image. The proposed heuristic function for head
length computation is tested under resolution variations. The results of the comparative
analysis and the function testing are presented in comprehensive diagrams evaluating
the research questions. Finally, some limitations and future works were suggested.
