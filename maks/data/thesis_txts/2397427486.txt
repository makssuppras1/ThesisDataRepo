1. Introduction 
 
Biogenic reefs can be constructed from an array of difference species (Teagle et al 2017, Liversage 
2018, Lathlean & McQuaid 2017, Geraldi et al 2017). Mussels are a prime example of an organism that 
creates biogenic reefs (Kent et al 2017, Commito & Rusignuolo 2000, Dubois et al 2006) and are 
recognised as ecosystem engineers (Chowdhury et al 2016, Arribas et al 2014, Bateman & Bishop 
2017). Ecosystem engineers are species that can transform environments, both in terms of structure 
and function, to provide habitat for both fauna and flora (Lathlean & McQuaid 2017, Hölker et al 2015, 
Koivisto & Westerbom 2010, Donadi et al 2015, Romero et al 2015). Mussel bed habitats are highly 
fractal in nature and therefore create complex habitats (Commito & Rusignuolo 2000). Increased 
complexity aids in elevating species richness (Gratwicke & Speight 2005, Kohn & Leviten 1976), 
abundance (Thomaz et al 2008) and reduces habitat monopolisation by a single dominant species 
(Höjesjö et al 2004). 
A variety of physical properties of mussel beds dictate the habitat that it provides; mussel age, patch 
size, individual physical structure and population structure (Palomo et al 2007). These properties are 
highly variable and can be affected by salinity (Westerbom et al 2002), temperature (Almada-Villela 
et al 1982), wave exposure (Westerbom & Jattu 2006), current speed (Wildish & Kristmanson 1985, 
Comely 1978), aerial exposure (Gillmor 1982), hypoxia (Jørgensen 1980), predation pressures 
(Freeman 2007, Christensen et al 2012), and substrate (Joschko et al 2008). Mussel bed habitats 
provide microhabitats for a large selection of species (Seed & Suchanek 1992) in addition to habitat 
for demersal fish species (Aarnio et al 1996, Gibson et al 1998, Hüssy et al 1997).  
In the Baltic region, three commercially important fish species use mussel bed habitat during one or 
more stages of their life history. European flounder (Platichthys flesus), European plaice (Pleuronectes 
platessa) and Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) all use mussel beds as a source of resource and habitat 
(Aarnio et al 1996, Gibson et al 1998, Hüssy et al 1997). Mytilus edulis and Modiolus modiolus form 
important habitats across the Baltic region (Kautsky 1981) and North Sea (Strong et al 2016), providing 
“islands” of biodiversity (Norling & Kautsky 2008).  
Impacts on mussel bed habitats have follow on effects on species composition directly on the mussel 
bed and the environment surrounding the beds (Dolmer et al 2001, Dolmer 2002). Threats to mussel 
bed habitats and its associated fauna and flora come in both anthropogenic and biological forms.  
Mussel dredging is perhaps the most studied and damaging factor attributed to the destruction of 
mussel bed habitat (Dolmer & Frandsen 2002, Dolmer et al 2001, Dolmer 2002, Fréchette et al. 1989, 
Fréchette & Bourget 1985). Mussel dredging in the Limfjorden alone is worth ~€6.2 million in annual 
profit with roughly 50,000 tones year-1 of mussel being removed (Dinesen et al 2011). Aside from the 
closure of mussel fisheries, restoration can take place to help counteract the impacts caused by 
dredging. Examining methodologies for M. edulis and M. modiolus restoration are important to 
determine the best form of action in order to restore damaged mussel bed habitat. In addition to this, 
abiotic and biotic factors must be taken into account as they may restrict or prevent establishment in 
at specific sites. 
Invasive species, the biological threat to biogenic reefs, pose a problem for mussel beds and the 
species that inhabit them (Karlson et al 2007). The round goby, Neogobius melanostomus, was first 
==PAGE:5==
established in the Gulf of Gadansk in the 1990s and by 2003 the species was already reported in 
several locations across Northern Germany (Corkum et al 2004). N. melanostomus has been found to 
have a negative impact on the commercially important European Flounder (Platichthys flesus) due to 
their similar diet of M. edulis (Karlson et al 2007). Corkum et al (2004) suggest that there is a possibility 
that this species may also compete with other native benthic fish species. Few native species predate 
on N. melanostomus, aside from the Great Cormorant (Phalacrocorax carbo) and a select group of 
other teleost fish species (Jakubas 2004, Bzoma 1998, Kornis et al 2012, Almqvist et al 2010, 
Oesterwind et al 2017). N. melanostomus has been predicted to be spreading in the direction of the 
oceanic North Sea at 30 km year-1 (Behrens et al 2017). As salinity increases towards the North Sea N. 
melanostomus will increasingly encounter the common shore crab (Carincus maenas) as they share 
similar habitats and food sources (Thiel & Dernedde 1994, Jubb et al 1983, Frandsen & Dolmer 2002, 
Legeay & Massabuau 2000). Due to the highly aggressive behavior that is typically displayed by N. 
melanostomus and C. maenas (Janssen & Jude 2001, MacInnis & Corkum 2000, Dubs & Corkum 1996, 
Phillips et al 2003, Kaiser et al 1990, Sneddon et al 1997), there is potential for direct competition 
between the two species.  
This thesis aims to answer questions on the competition of N. melanostomus with C. maenas and how 
maximum metabolic rate may affect this interaction. Specifically, the first part of this thesis examined 
the following four questions; 
1) Is MMR repeatable across a 4-week period? 2) Does increased MMR result in higher aggression by 
Neogobius melanostomus towards Carcinus maenas? 3) Does increased aggression result in higher 
rates of habitat dominance? 4) Can metabolic phenotype predict the outcome of habitat competition 
between Neogobious melanostomus and Carcinus maenas? In addition to this, a control experiment 
was carried out to examine the behavior of Carcinus maenas on a mussel bed habitat in the absence 
of Neogobius melanostomus. This final experiment tested the hypothesis that habitat use and 
residency time of Carcinus maenas is negatively affected by the presence of Neogobius melanostomus. 
The second part of this thesis is a literature review of the historical and current methodologies for M. 
edulis and M. modiolus restoration. It addressed the current knowledge on restoration methods for 
each species and revealed if there are any gaps in previous research. In addition to this, the review 
addressed the abiotic and biotic factors that are associated with mussel growth and population 
establishment. The final result is a guideline for considerations that should be taken before initiating 
a restoration project and suggested methods.

2.1 Abstract 
 
Invasive species are the second biggest threat to global biodiversity and are being transported globally 
at an increased rate. It is imperative that the outcomes of such invasions are understood and how 
invasive species will interact with native species. Successful invasive species display high levels of 
phenotypic plasticity, as seen with the invasive round goby (Neogobious melanostomus). The round 
goby is steadily spreading toward the oceanic North Sea and will begin to encounter higher levels of 
salinity that may favor low metabolic rate fish due to the osmoregulatory compromise. It will also 
increasingly encounter the common shore crab, Carcinus maenas, which is also a molluscivore and 
therefore compete over habitat space and resource usage.  
Typically, high maximum metabolic rate (MMR) individuals are found at the frontier of invasions due to 
their higher requirement for sustenance and therefore necessity for expansion. High MMR is often 
correlated with both higher levels of habitat dominance and aggression. Using intermittent flow 
respirometry, we measured MMR of individual N. melanostomus and revealed that MMR is a repeatable 
trait across a 4-week period consistent with previous studies on other fish species. Each individual N. 
melanostomus was subject to a behavioral assay, which involved competition for mussel bed habitat 
against C. maenas. Our results demonstrate that high MMR N. melanostomus display higher levels of 
aggression towards C. maenas when competing over mussel bed habitat, indicating that aggression is 
dependent on the individual’s metabolic phenotype. Our results, however, indicated that C. maenas 
residency time was not affected by the presence of N. melanostomus.  
Our study provides evidence of how metabolic phenotypes affect the behavior of N. melanostomus and 
ultimately the follow-on effects on the behavioral interactions with C. maenas.  
 
2.2 Introduction 
 
Invasive species are by definition, species that are present in environments outside of their native range 
(Prentis et al 2008, Facon et al 2006). Invasive species are currently a global issue, with the majority 
transported in ballast water world-wide (Bax et al 2003). According to Bax et al (2003), historically 
ballast water was an inhospitable habitat for any species and would result in the death of its inhabitants. 
In recent years, ballast water has become cleaner thereby aiding in the survival of its stowaways (Bax et 
al 2003). Invasive species are now considered the second biggest threat to global biodiversity, after 
habitat destruction, and are believed to be the driving force behind the decline in native species and 
ultimately population extirpation (Streftaris & Zenetos 2006). These effects are clear in the North 
Atlantic, where 13 of the 24 commercially fished coastal species are impacted by invasive species 
(Brown et al 2018).

Competition between native and invasive species is recognized as a major threat to the local 
populations’ stability (Byers 2002). The Indo-Pacific lionfish, Pterois volitans, was introduced to the 
Florida waters in the 1990s and has since been spreading through the Caribbean region. P. volitans 
competes with native predators via the consumption of reef dwelling fish and invertebrates. P. volitans 
has also been found to inhabit similar habitats and shelters to the Nassau grouper (Epinephelus straitus), 
this may lead to competition for shelter between the two species (Raymond et al 2015). Byers (2002) 
found a decrease in recruitment of native reef fish by 79% across a 5-week experiment. Although 
management is underway, this example highlights the necessity in understanding the potential impacts 
of invasive species in a competitive manner (Anderson et al 2017). The marked necessity for 
management is clear, as P. volitans continues to spread and is now present in the Mediterranean Sea 
(Azzurro et al 2017).  
To survive and establish in a new habitat, invasive species must rapidly adapt to the novel conditions 
which may depend on the phenotypic plasticity of the species (Prentis et al 2008, Novak 2007, Sakai et al 
2001). The initial invader’s survival will determine if the population will persist or not, this is mainly 
determined by the genetic diversity retained amongst the surviving invaders (Novak 2007). Identification 
of specific traits of non-native species can be used to predict the invasiveness of species when they 
threaten to enter or are already within a novel environment (Howeth et al 2016). Comparatively, species 
that have successfully invaded new environments often express a higher capacity for phenotypic 
plasticity than non-invasive species (Davidson et al 2011). For example; the round goby, Neogobious 
melanostomus, shows high levels of phenotypic plasticity in terms of habitat use and prey choice across 
Europe and North America (Pettitt-Wade et al 2015). This also includes the consumption of native 
species’ eggs, such as occurs in the Great Lakes of North America which has clear implications for the 
impact upon recruitment of the native species (Corkum et al 2004).  
Metabolic rate (MR) may affect individual behavioral phenotypes and could influence whether invasive 
species invade new areas. Different metabolic phenotypes often correlate with different patterns of 
aggressiveness (Lahti et al 2002), swimming speeds (Marras et al 2015), cover usage (Finstad et al 2007), 
maturation age (Sloat & Reeves 2014), dispersal (Robertsen et al 2014), risk-taking (Huntingford et al 
2010), dominance (McCarthy 2001) and emergence time (Vaz-Serrano et al 2011). These behavioral 
phenotypes may correlate with individual maximum metabolic rate (MMR) and/or standard metabolic 
rate (SMR) (Metcalfe et al 2016). Previous studies have found a positive correlation between SMR and 
MMR indicating that in many species these two variables are connected (Auer et al 2015, Killen et al 
2016). The use of MR phenotypes as predictors of behavior are useful when shown to be repeatable for 
extended periods of time. Repeatability of metabolic rate and associated phenotypes has been shown 
previously in Atlantic Char (Salvelinus alpinus) (Cutts et al 2001) and shown to be stable over time for 
Atlantic Salmon (Salmo salar) (McCarthy 2000). However, Norin & Malte (2011) found that MR was 
repeatable over a 5-week period, but repeatability decreased over extended periods of time and 
showed no repeatability at 15 weeks for juvenile brown trout (Salmo trutta). Metcalfe et al (2016) 
suggest that repeatability is preserved in laboratory settings due to the homogenous conditions that the 
individuals experience. Conflicting discoveries call for more attention in this area on a species level.

The round goby is a prime example of an invasive species that has displayed phenotypic plasticity to its 
advantage. It has successfully colonized both the North American Great Lakes and the Baltic Sea 
(Corkum et al 2004). According to Corkum et al (2004), the round goby was first established in the Gulf 
of Gadansk in the 1990s and by 2003 the species was reported in several locations across Northern 
Germany. It is already apparent that the round goby has a negative impact upon the native flounder 
species as they both compete for the same resource, Mytilus edulis (Karlson et al 2007). Neogobious 
melanostomus, so far, has been met with little local competition and resistance aside from the Great 
Cormorant (Phalacrocorax carbo) and few other teleost species (Jakubas 2004, Bzoma 1998, Kornis et al 
2012, Almqvist et al 2010, Oesterwind et al 2017).  
N. melanostomus is spreading from the Baltic Sea in the direction of the oceanic North Sea with the 
current rate estimated at 30 km year-1 (Behrens et al 2017). As salinity increases between the Baltic Sea 
and the North Sea, the round goby will increasingly encounter the common shore crab (Carcinus 
maenas) when foraging within biogenic reef habitat as both species share similar habitat preferences 
and food sources (Thiel & Dernedde 1994, Jubb et al 1983, Frandsen & Dolmer 2002, Legeay & 
Massabuau 2000). Both species display notably aggressive behavior when defending territory for both 
reproduction and food sources (Janssen & Jude 2001, MacInnis & Corkum 2000, Dubs & Corkum 1996, 
Phillips et al 2003, Kaiser et al 1990, Sneddon et al 1997), indicating that direct competition between the 
two species is a possibility.  
This paper examines the relationship between MMR and habitat dominance by N. melanostomus. High 
MMR and thereby predicted high levels of dominance can benefit individuals in environments with 
abundant food sources and habitat (Lahti et al 2002, Bochdansky et al 2005). Biogenic reefs are the main 
provider of food and territory for N. melanostomus (Skora & Rzeznik 2001, Sapota 2004, Corkum et al 
2004, Kornis et al 2012) as roughly 60% of the diet is composed of blue mussel, Mytilus edulis (Sapota 
2004). As individuals advance into more saline waters, it is likely they will experience an increase in 
metabolic expenditure to cope with the increased ion transfer across the gills (Behrens et al 2017). This 
may indicate that biogenic reefs will become an increasing necessity to compensate for the increased 
energy requirements from maintaining ideal osmoregularity (Behrens et al 2017). 
This study aimed to examine if MMR is repeatable and the relationship between MMR and aggression in 
Neogobius melanostomus during interactions with Carcinus maenas. Specifically, this study examined 4 
questions: 1) Is MMR repeatable across a 4-week period? 2) Does increased MMR result in higher 
aggression by Neogobius melanostomus towards Carcinus maenas? 3) Does increased aggression result 
in higher rates of habitat dominance? 4) Can metabolic phenotype predict the outcome of habitat 
competition between Neogobious melanostomus and Carcinus maenas? In addition to this, a control 
experiment was carried out to examine the behavior of Carcinus maenas on a mussel bed habitat in the 
absence of Neogobius melanostomus. This final experiment tested the hypothesis that habitat use and 
residency time of Carcinus maenas is negatively affected by the presence of Neogobius melanostomus.

2.3 Materials & Methods 
 
2.3.1 Animal Holding Conditions 
A total of 56 round goby (Neogobius melanostomus) (46.3g ± 5.7g; mean ± S.D.) were caught using fyke 
nets at Guldborgsund, Denmark and transported to nearby holding and laboratory facilities. Fish were 
held in 1 m3 tanks with the water temperature and salinity adjusted to 11oC (± 1oC) and 30ppt (± 1ppt), 
respectively. Three tanks were used with ~18 fish per tank (N=56). Tanks were covered with an opaque 
tarp to minimize visual stimuli, but an opening allowed daylight to enter. Using a flow-through system, 
each tank received U.V. sterilized, particle filtered and fully aerated water. Each tank contained pipes 
which provided shelter for the fish. Fish were fed blue mussels (Mytilus edulis) 3 times a week to 
satiation (Jørgensen et al 2017). Fish were acclimated at the laboratory for two weeks prior to 
experimentation. After acclimation, individual fish were weighed, sexed (Kornis et al 2012) and given a 
PIT tag via injection into the abdominal cavity (Ruetz et al 2006). Data collection started 10 days after 
tagging, following previous studies (Behrens et al 2017b). 
  
2.3.2 Respirometry Protocols 
Metabolic rates were measured by intermittent flow respirometry using established protocols (Genz et 
al. 2014, Peixoto et al 2016, Rosewarne et al 2016). Metabolic rate was calculated from the rate of 
oxygen consumption (MO2; mg O2 kg-1 h-1) (Svendsen et al 2016). The respirometry chambers contained 
1.1L of water including the connecting tubes and pumps. Water was constantly circulated from the fish 
holding system (Svendsen et al 2010). Air stones were used to ensure normoxia in the tank (≥ 95% air 
saturation (O2sat)). Each chamber was partially covered using opaque tape, the front of each tank was 
also covered with opaque black plastic (Behrens et al 2017, Rosewarne et al 2016, Peixoto et al 2017) to 
ensure a dim light inside the respirometry chambers. Each respirometry chamber operated as a closed 
system using short wait and measurement phases followed by flushing phases to prevent hypoxia inside 
the chamber (i.e. < 80% O2sat). Two submersed pumps were used per respirometer; one to flush the 
system with fresh aerated water and a second to circulate water within the closed system. The partial 
pressure of oxygen was measured every second inside the chambers using fiber-optic sensors, reading 
into a Witrox 4 oxymeter, controlled by the software AutoResp (Loligo Systems, Tjele, Denmark). MO2 
was calculated in R-Studio using standard equations (Svendsen et al 2015, Behrens et al 2017, 
Rosewarne et al 2016).  
Each cycle of the respirometry consisted of three stages - Flush, Wait and Measure. The total cycle 
lasted 360 seconds (Flush - 170s, Wait - 40s and Measure -150s). The flush stage was set at 170s as this 
ensured oxygen conditions close to 100% O2sat after the flushing. The measure stage was set at 150 
seconds because this period was found to be the maximum amount of time that the larger fish could 
endure without becoming hypoxic (<80% O2 saturation) (Svendsen et al 2016, Behrens et al 2017). The 
coefficient of determination (r2) associated with MO2 measure was always ≥ 0.90 (Rosewarne et al.

2016). The wait stage was set at 40 seconds to ensure that the chamber water was mixed and there was 
no water turbulence within the chambers during the measurement phase. 
 
2.3.3 Data collection 
Fish were selected 48 hours before each trial to facilitate fasting needed to ensure a post-absorptive 
state (Svendsen et al 2014). To achieve maximum metabolic rate (MMR), each fish was exercised for 2 
minutes by gently chasing and flipping in a circular tank (40 cm diameter) with well oxygenated water 
and then removed from the water for 1 min air exposure (Behrens et al 2017, Peixoto et al 2017). 
Subsequently, the fish was transferred to the respirometry chamber.  
Each chamber measured at least one background cycle before the fish were introduced. Background 
values were subtracted from the MMR values to account for bacterial activity (Behrens et al 2017, 
Peixoto et al 2017, Rosewarne et al 2016). Fish were added to the respirometry chamber towards the 
end of the flush stage of the previous respirometry cycle to ensure that the chamber was sealed right 
before the beginning of the wait stage (Rosewarne et al 2016). Each fish would remain in the chamber 
for at least 3 full cycles (15 minutes). The maximum metabolic rate (MMR) was estimated using the 
highest of the first 3 values of the recorded MO2 (Behrens et al 2017, Peixoto et al 2017, Svendsen et al 
2012). Post-trial fish were returned to new holding tanks to avoid interactions between tested and 
untested fish (Peixoto et al 2017).  
 
2.3.4 Metabolic repeatability 
Data normality was confirmed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. We developed a linear model between MMR 
(MO2; mg O2 kg-1 h-1) and body mass (kg) similar to previous studies (Behrens et al 2017, Roche et al 
2013) and used the residual difference between the predicted value and the actual value to determine 
the fish´s MMR grouping: High, Low or Neutral (higher than 0.5, lower than -0.5, or between 0.5 and -
0.5, respectively). Similar to Raffard et al 2017, fish that fell into the “Neutral” category were eliminated 
from further trials. Grouping was made to ensure that tested fish were equally spread across the MMR 
scope of N. melanostomus, with equal numbers above and below average MMR. 
Four weeks after the first measurement of MMR, a total of 12 fish (6 high MMR fish and 6 low MMR 
fish) were resampled for MMR following identical protocols. Residual values from the relationship 
between body mass and MMR were used to determine repeatability (Svendsen et al 2014). 
Repeatability of two sets of data (Initial and 4-weeks) were examined using a Pearsons Correlation 
Coefficient (Szafrańska & Konarzewski 2007, Boldsen et al 2013). 
  
2.3.5 Habitat Competition   
Mussel bed habitats (MBH) were created to investigate interactions between N. melanostomus and C. 
maenas. Each MBH was housed within a 1m diameter circular opaque tank and contained 10 kg (± 0.1

kg) of live blue mussels. The water depth was adjusted to 25 cm [8, 9]. The MBH was arranged at one 
side of the circular arena and had a diameter of 50cm. The remaining area in the arena was covered 
with sand from a nearby beach. A circular arena was used to avoid the use of corners as shelter by the

Fish were transferred to the arena without air contact  and acclimated for 15 minutes pre-trial (Poulsen

was 50 minutes, excluding acclimation time. The trials were recorded with two GoPro Hero 4 cameras 
situated directly above the circular tank. 
 
2.3.6 Controls 
Controls (N=12) contained only C. maenas and the mussel bed habitat. Identical protocols were 
followed, as outlined above, except that no N. melanostomus was present. Each C. maenas was video 
recorded for 50 minutes within the circular tank, with the two GoPro Hero 4 cameras filming constantly.  
 
2.3.7 Behavioral Analyses 
A total of six behavioral variables were examined using the 50-minute video footage; 1. Number of 
approaches by the goby to the crab (Ag) 2. Number of retreats by the goby (Rg) 3. Total time spent on 
the MBH by the goby (Tg) 4. Total time spent on the MBH by the crab (Tc). 5. Number of bites by the 
goby to the crab (Bg). 6. Number of forced retreats by the crab. 
An approach (1) was counted when a fish leaves its location to actively approach the crab. A retreat (2) 
was counted if aggressive behavior is displayed by the crab and the goby is forced to vacate the crab's 
area, this is counted when either the crab approaches the goby or vice versa. Total time spent on the 
MBH is counted as long as either individual is seen to be in direct contact with the MBH (i.e. 3 and 4). 
Biting was counted each time the goby is seen to bite the crab directly (5). A forced retreat was counted 
if the crab is vacated from its location by the goby due to biting or by a display of aggression (6). During 
the video analysis, the observer had no knowledge about the metabolic rate measurement (i.e. residual 
value) of the individual round goby (Milinski 1997).  
 
2.3.8 Behavioral Analysis 
Generalized additive models were made between each behavioral variable and metabolic residuals 
(Variable ~ Residual) with each behavioral variable being sequentially tested. Initially, a model was 
created with all included variables however a stepwise reduction revealed that models with each 
variable individually produced the most suitable AIC value. Each model was tested using ANOVA in R 
studio. The significance level was set at P<0.05 for all correlation tests. The same approach was followed 
for analysis of crab residency time. The residency time of the C. maenas control group was tested

This design was used to reveal the if residency time of C. maenas was affected when in the presence of

2.4 Results 
 
2.4.1 Metabolic Rate 
All fish were split into 3 groups; “High”, “Low” and “Neutral” using the linear model (MMR ~ Weight) 
and was confirmed as a significant relationship (P=1.31e-7). A Shapiro-Wilk test for both weight 
(W=0.95844 P=0.0514) and MMR (W=0.97288 P=0.2374) proved normal, indicating that both weight 
and MMR met the normality assumptions (Fig. 1). Repeatability was confirmed over a 4-week period 
with a significant result from Pearson’s Correlation Coefficient (P=0.009334 R2=0.5075) (Fig. 2). This 
indicates that, at least across several weeks, MMR for N. melanostomus is repeatable.  
 
2.4.2 Behavioral Analysis 
All six variables were tested against residuals from the MMR Grouping model (MMR ~ Weight). Number 
of bites was found to be a significant variable (χ²=17.04, P=3.7e-05 and R2=-0.0653) (Figure 3). This 
significant relationship indicated that heightened MMR resulted in increased aggression towards C. 
maenas. There was no significant difference found in crab residency time between the control 
individuals (N=12) and those that competed with high (χ²=0.624 and P=0.43) or low (χ²=0.048 and 
P=0.827) MMR N. melanostomus. This indicates that habitat usage (residency time) by C. maenas was 
unaffected by the presence of N. melanostomus, regardless of MMR (Fig. 4). 
 
 
 
2.5 Discussion 
 
  
Repeatability of MMR was revealed across a 4-week period for Neogobius melanostomus.  Number of 
bites correlated significantly with MMR for individual N. melanostomus indicating that elevated MMR 
resulted in increased aggression towards C. maenas. Interestingly, there was no difference in residency 
time between control crab experiments and trial experiments with N. melanostums present. This 
implicates that C. maenas has unhindered habitat usage, regardless of MMR, at least at the tested 
densities (0.79 N. melanostomus m-2 and 0.79 C. maenas m-2). Further studies are needed to describe 
interactions between Neogobius melanostomus and C. maenas at different densities and habitat types.  
N. melanostomus MMR phenotypes were repeatable across a 4-week period. This confirms an 
important assumption of the study, MMR repeatability, and that it may also be assumed that the

species  
 
behavioral phenotypes associated with high/low MMR are also repeatable over short time spans 
(Geffroy et al 2016). The results are in concurrence with previous studies that have shown repeatability 
over short time spans (Cutts et al 2001). Norin & Malte (2011) found that metabolic repeatability 
decreased over longer time spans for juvenile brown trout. However, repeatability of metabolic rate in 
laboratory conditions has recently come under question (Metcalfe et al 2016) due to the artificial and 
homogenous environment that the subjects are maintained in. Therefore, without information on 
repeatability of metabolic rate in individuals held in natural conditions, it is difficult to draw firm 
conclusions on how stable metabolic rate phenotypes are for wild N. melanostomus.  
Increased aggression and requirement for resources has previously been reported in other species, on 
an individual level, with high MMR and/or SMR (Metcalfe et al 2016). Increased aggression in the form 
of biting was elevated in individuals that displayed higher MMRs (Figure 3.). Heightened aggression has 
been previously shown to result in improved habitat, or resource, dominance (Höjesjö et al 2002, Bay et 
al 2001, Hamilton et al 2002, Ebersole 1977, Ariyomo et al 2013). An elevation in aggression may cause 
N. melanostomus to more successfully compete with its current benthic competitors (Corkum et al 
2004). 
Typically, at the frontier of species invasions, individuals with high SMR (or aggression) represent the 
majority of the frontier community due to elevated boldness and willingness to take “risks” (Myles-
Gonzalez et al 2015, Groen et al 2012). For example, Myles-Gonzalez et al (2015) found significant 
differences between the behavior and metabolic rate of individuals from the invasion front and those 
from a population that had been present for at least 10 years. Previous studies have given evidence to 
support the link between positive correlation between SMR and MMR (Auer et al 2015, Killen et al 
2016). Therefore, this suggests that at the frontier of the N. melanostomus invasion, individuals will 
display heightened MMR. Although the current study showed no effects of MMR on competition with C. 
maenas, there may be potential for N. melanostomus to compete with other benthic fish (Corkum et al 
2004). More research is required to examine the effects of MMR phenotypic variability and associated 
behavioral phenotypes in N. melanostomus when in competition with other Baltic fish species.  
Behrens et al (2017) found that N. melanostomus individuals, at high salinities (30ppt), displayed lower 
MMR and perturbed osmoregulation. The reduction in MMR was likely due to reduced oxygen diffusion 
into the individual caused by a thickening of the gill epithelium (Randall et al 1972). Thickening occurs to 
limit water loss via osmosis into the environment when entering above normal salinity environments.  
This trade-off between oxygen diffusion and water loss has been coined the “osmoregulatory 
compromise” (Gonzalez & McDonald 1992, 1994). Therefore, in environments with high salinity, low 
MMR phenotypes might be favored due to reduced gill ventilation rates (Svendsen et al 2012, Millidine 
et al 2008) which reduces the movement of ions across the gills (Randall et al 1972, King & Sardella 
2017). High salinities will eventually be encounted by the frontier of the N. melanostomus population as 
they approach the conjunction between the North Sea and Baltic.  
The required metabolic phenotype of N. melanostomus individuals at high salinities (low MMR) could 
come into conflict with the current phenotype (high MMR) that is expressed at the frontier of the 
invasion. This lowered requirement of MMR might impact the aggression that N. melanostomus will

Maximum metabolic rate predicts behavioural interactions between native and invasive 
species  
 
display towards native fauna, as suggested by the current paper. Such reduction in aggression and 
perhaps habitat dominance may impact individuals on the frontier of expansion leading to reduced 
habitat and resource monopolization. This may have the potential to limit or slow the expansion of N. 
melanostomus into higher salinity environments, as the low MMR individuals that survive the high 
salinities may not be able to compete with native fauna. In addition to this, as stated by Myles-Gonzales 
et al (2015), elevated MMR correlates positively with boldness and risk-taking behavior in terms of 
range expansion into novel areas. This may suggest that individuals could lack the aggression required to 
successfully compete and potentially not display the behavior associated with advancement through the 
habitat into new territories. 
In addition to the osmoregulatory compromise and its associated constraints in favorable metabolic 
phenotypes, habitat complexity may also reduce the impacts of the N. melanostomus invasion. Mussel 
bed habitats are highly complex habitats due to the fractal nature of their structure, providing a 
selection of niches for different species (Commito & Rusignuolo 2004, Commito & Dankers 2001). 
Habitat complexity has been previously shown to reduce competition and habitat monopolization by 
dominant individuals (Höjesjö et al 2004). This suggests a further reduction in competition and impact 
upon native fauna, when compared with habitat of reduced complexity (i.e. soft substrate or sandy 
bottom substrate). This could explain the results from the residency time of C. maenas during the 
control experiments, as there was no significant difference between control experiments and trial 
experiments with N. melanostums present, however this could also be due to other factors. The mussel 
bed habitat available may have been too large to compete for and, as previously indicated, the natural 
complexity of mussel beds reduced competition. The size of the habitat may have been suitable to 
house and supply resources to both species. This could explain why both species were seen to share the 
habitat, rather than have one or the other excluded onto the sand region of the tanks.  
The current study only observed individuals outside of their reproductive season however there are well 
documented cases of highly aggressive N. melanostomus individuals when nest guarding (Phillips et al 
2003, Dubs & Corkum 1996, MacInnis & Corkum 2000). The construction of nests within the habitat 
substrate and the following guarding of the nests by the males might elevate competition with native 
fauna. This species has been artificially stimulated to create and guard nests in laboratory conditions in 
previous studies (Meunier et al 2009). Artificial stimulation of reproductive behaviors could be of use for 
further research in this area. It remains unknown if individual aggression levels are repeatable across 
non-reproductive and nest guarding periods, and the relationship between MMR and aggression has not 
been investigated across the two periods.  
In conclusion, our study has shown that MMR influences the aggression displayed by Neogobius 
melanostomus. Increasing MMR resulted in heightened aggression, in the form of biting, when 
competing with Carincus maenas. In addition, repeatability for metabolic rate phenotypes was shown 
for Neogobius melanstomus across a 4-week period. More research though is required in this field, to 
examine the long-term repeatability of metabolic rate phenotypes of Neogobius melanstomus, 
especially across reproductive and non-reproductive periods. Furthermore, behavioral studies during 
the reproductive season or induced reproductive behavior should be carried out to assess the impacts of 
naturally heightened aggression due to nest guarding. Finally, the effects of Neogobius melanstomus

density relative to Carincus maenas density on competition patterns deserves further study to 
understand how these two benthic species will interact when their distributions are overlapping.

3.1 Abstract 
 
Mussels are recognised as ecosystem engineers and can transform environments to provide habitat 
for an array of species. Their decline due to mussel dredging could have impacts on the benthic 
fauna and flora of marine ecosystems. European flounder (Platichthys flesus), European plaice 
(Pleuronectes platessa) and Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) are all commercially exploited fisheries in 
the Baltic region that benefit from the presence of mussel beds during one or more stages of their 
life history and could be impacted by a decline in mussel bed abundance.  
Analysis of peer review literature explored the historical and current methods used in mussel bed 
reef restoration for Mytilus edulis and Modiolus modiolus. Abiotic and biotic factors were also 
considered and thresholds for each species were established. Our analysis reveals a severe lack in 
documented restoration projects for both species, with particular emphasis on Modiolus modiolus 
restoration. The majority of research that was accumulated is focused on restoration of Mytilus 
edulis, although methodology is not always clear.  
Our analysis also revealed a large gap in the abiotic and biotic factors that regulate the growth and 
establishment of Modiolus modiolus. Data for both abiotic and biotic factors were abundant for 
Mytilus edulis. Our review provides guidance and recommendations for methodology and 
environmental factors that should be taken into consideration for restoration projects on both 
Mytilus edulis and Modiolus modiolus. 
 
 
3.2 Introduction 
 
 
 
3.2.1 Background 
 
 
Various mussel species often dominate the animal biomass in estuarine habitats, including the 
largest estuary in the world, the Baltic Sea (Kautsky 1981). All mussel species are recognised as 
ecosystem engineers due to their ability to transform environments, both in terms of structure and 
function (Koivisto & Westerbom 2010, Donadi et al 2015, Romero et al 2015). Mussels can facilitate 
the availability of resources to a variety of organisms (Jones et al 1994). Mussel beds are also 
referred to as “biogenic reefs” which are structures that are built my ecosystem engineers that 
provide habitat for resident benthic species (Commito & Rusignuolo 2000, Dubois et al 2006). 
Mussel age, patch size, individual physical structure and population structures influence the habitat 
they provide (Palomo et al 2007). Individual physical structure is highly affected by its geographic 
location within any environment as salinity, temperature, wave exposure, current speed, aerial 
exposure, predation pressures and substrate impact growth rate (Westerbom et al 2002, Wildish & 
Kristmanson 1985), maximum size (Kautsky 1982), shell thickness (Christensen et al 2012), 
reproductive output (Kautsky 1982) and byssus production (Comely 1987). Mussel bed habitats are 
highly fractal and inherently complex habitats (Commito & Rusignuolo 2000) that provide for a host

(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
of species (Kent et al 2017). The complexity assists in reducing habitat monopolisation by a single 
dominant species (Höjesjö et al 2004).  
 
Mussel bed habitats provide a great variety of microhabitats for its inhabitants (Seed & Suchanek, 
1992). Waste products, dead mussel shells and the sediment that resides between individual 
mussels provide for infauna (Seed & Suchanek, 1992) whereas the base of attachment, the byssal 
threads, are inhabited by more mobile fauna, such as crustacea and amphipods (Seed & Suchanek, 
1992).  Aside from such motile species, sessile species reside upon the shells and surface of the 
mussel bed habitat. As indicated, the physical and biological status of the mussels affect the species 
that inhabit the mussel bed. Older reefs typically provide habitat for a larger abundance of species 
than younger reefs (Tsuchiya & Nishihira 1986). Alongside the direct benefits of residing organisms, 
mussel beds also directly and indirectly provide resources for demersal fish species. European 
flounder (Platichthys flesus), European plaice (Pleuronectes platessa) and Atlantic cod (Gadus 
morhua) are all commercially important fisheries in the Baltic region and all three benefit from the 
presence of mussel beds during one or more stages of their life history as a source of resources and 
habitat (Aarnio et al 1996, Gibson et al 1998, Hüssy et al 1997).  
 
 
3.2.2 Habitat Directive and Requirements of a Modiolus modiolus Reef 
 
 
In order for a horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus) reef to be an Annex I habitat, it must meet specific 
criteria. Three criteria have been identified by the Joint Nature Conservation Committee (JNCC); 1) 
Live adult M. modiolus are present. 2) A distinct community is associated with the live adults. 3) The 
distinct region containing the M. modiolus is greater than 25m2 (Morris 2015). For clarification, the 
second criterion dictates that the community associated with the reef must be completely distinct 
from that of the surrounding habitat. If M. modiolus communities are found to be in accordance 
with these guidelines, they must be designated as Special Areas of Conservation (SACs) to help with 
their conservation. The methods that will be set out in the current review could be used as tools to 
aid in the restoration of Annex I designated M. modiolus reefs. 
 
 
3.2.3 Mussel Fishery Impacts 
 
 
Mussel dredging occurs in several countries world-wide including; UK, Ireland and Denmark (Dolmer 
& Frandsen 2002, Smaal 2002, Frandsen et al 2014). Mussel dredging in Denmark takes place across 
3 regions; Danish Wadden Sea, east coast of Jutland and in the Limfjorden where 115,000 – 140,000 
tonnes are taken per year (Dolmer & Frandsen 2002). According to Dinesen et al (2011), roughly 
50,000 tones year-1 are landed in the Limfjorden which equates to ~€6.2 million annual profit. 
Dolmer & Frandsen (2002) studied two sites where mussel dredging had occurred and recorded the 
amount of shell debris and pebbles. These sites were compared with sites where mussel dredging 
was banned. A significant decrease in shell debris and pebbles was found on the dredging sites and 
in addition, a significant decrease in blue mussel (Mytilus edulis) spat due to the reduction in suitable 
hard substrate. The smoothened topography of these dredged sites decreases the amount of mixing

in the water column thereby limiting the vertical transport of nutrients to filter feeding benthic 
fauna (Fréchette et al. 1989). This may limit the availability of nutrients to mussel beds thereby 
limiting growth (Fréchette & Bourget 1985). Short-term impacts were investigated, again in brackish 
waters in Denmark, by Dolmer et al (2001) and species composition density was found to be 
significantly lower for at least 40 days post dredging. Dolmer (2002) found that areas could be 
affected in terms of species composition and density for up to 4 months post dredging and in one 
site differences were still seen after 4 years. Mussel dredging impacts both directly the mussel bed 
by physical removal and also the species that inhabit the mussel beds by removing vital habitat 
(Frandsen et al 2014, Dolmer et al 2001, Dolmer & Frandsen 2002, Fréchette et al. 1989). Aside from 
the closure of these fisheries, restoration should take place to counteract the impacts caused by 
mussel dredging.  
 
 
3.2.4 Historical and Current Methods of Bivalve Restoration 
 
 
There are a variety of techniques or combinations of techniques that have been used for bivalve 
restoration worldwide; artificial hard substrate, mussel shell deployment, long-line spat collection 
and whole mussel transplantation.  
 
The use of artificial substrate, coir nets (Fig. 1), has been found to be successful in creating suitable 
attachment sites for coral and mangrove restoration (Clark & Edwards 1995, Hashim et al 2010). De 
Paoli et al (2015) attempted to recreate this method for mussel bed restoration in the Wadden Sea 
using whole mussel transplantation implementing coir nets as artificial hard substrate. Wilcox & Jeffs 
(2017) also used artificial hardened substrate, mussel shells, and found no increase in mussel 
persistence for adult individuals of Perna canaliculus. However, for juvenile P. canaliculus, it was 
found that they would attach to both pre-existing adult conspecifics or any available hard substrate, 
although attachment to pre-existing adults resulted in reduced mortality (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). 
Christensen et al (2012) examined the use of long-lines for M. edulis establishment and growth, 
which could later then be transplanted to existing beds and found that long-lines were highly 
effective at collecting mussel spat. Comely (1978) also found reasonable levels of M. modiolus spat 
settlement on collector long-lines however M. edulis settlement was significantly higher.  
  
 
3.2.5 Abiotic and Biotic Requirements for Mytilus edulis and Modiolus modiolus Growth 
 
 
The flourishment of mussel beds is influenced by a variety of abiotic and biotic conditions 
(Buschbaum & Saier 2001). Salinity impacts the range at which mussels can successfully establish 
communities. In the Eartern part of the Gulf of Finland, salinity drops below 4.5‰ which seemingly 
inhibits growth of M. edulis (Westerbom et al 2002). M. modiolus is greatly affected by fluctuating 
salinities (Pierce 1970, Dinesen & Morton 2014) due to its inability to fully eliminate the effects of 
the external medium (Shumway 1977). Increasing wave exposure has been shown to positively 
impact mussel bed density, with this potentially being due to elevated particulate matter availability 
(Westerbom & Jattu 2006). Water current impacts the amount of available seston that is supplied to

Conservation of Biogenic Reefs protected by the Habitat Directive of the 
European Union: A review of restoration methodologies for blue mussel 
(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
the mussel beds (Wildish & Kristmanson 1985), however very high currents may impact overall 
growth in individuals due to energy expenditure on additional byssal threads (Comely 1978). Aeriel 
exposure typically only effects M. edulis due to its littoral distribution (Coleman & Trueman 1971, 
Ragnarsson & Burgos 2012). M. modiolus is only affected during spring tides due to its subtidal 
distribution and has a much lower threshold to aerial exposure than M. edulis (Coleman & Trueman 
1971, Coleman 1973). Temperature affects growth rate of M. edulis between 3 °C – 20 °C, however 
growth rate significantly declines above 20 °C (Almada-Villela et al 1982). Temperature changes are 
much more important for the survival of M. modiolus populations and they can only withstand a 
small temperature increase (>23 °C upper thermal limit) (Read & Cumming 1967, Davenport & 
Kjorsvik 1982). Hypoxia is less of an issue for both species of mussel as they are considered to have a 
high tolerance to short-term hypoxia (De-Zwaan & Mathieu 1992). Long-term hypoxia (< 0.1 – 0.2 
mgO2/l), however, may kill M. edulis after 1 – 2 weeks of exposure (Jørgensen 1980).  
Hard bottom substrate is an important settlement site for larval mussels. Larvae may delay 
metamorphosis without the presence of hard substrate causing prolonged movement within the 
water column (Joschko et al 2008). This may cause increased mortalities of up to 99% due to 
predation and starvation (Thorson 1966, Mileikovsky 1971, Jørgensen 1981). Hard substrate can 
come in the form of both pre-existing adult mussel bed populations, rocks/pebbles and artificial 
substrate and seem overwhelmingly preferable for larval, juvenile and adult individuals than soft 
substrate alternatives (McGrath et al 1988, Dolmer & Frandsen 2002, McGrorty et al 1993, Stillman 
et al 2000, Herlyn et al 2008, Commito et al 2014).  
Predation by the common starfish (Asterias rubens), shore crab (Carcinus maenas) and dog whelk 
(Nucella lapillus) on mussels is common (Holts et al 1998). Previous studies have shown that 
populations within the vicinity of such species have developed specific defences to enhance their 
survivability (Freeman 2007). Although the defences are asymmetrical, meaning that the defence 
that deters C. maenas does not deter A. rubens however, the defence that protects against A. rubens 
does deter C. maenas (Freeman 2007). M. edulis exhibits large abductor mussels in response to A. 
rubens and N. lapillus but not C. maenas (Freeman 2007). This is an important issue when trying to 
establish previously unexposed adult mussels to new predators because the mussels may not 
express adequate species-specific defences (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016). Additionally, predation 
pressures vary depending on the life history stage of the mussels and species (Holts et al 1998). 
 
3.2.6 Objectives of the Review 
 
There is a growing interest in coastal habitat restoration (Kristensen et al 2017, Baggett et al 2015, 
Basso et al 2018). To guide future restoration projects, this study documents historical and current 
advancements in the methods of Mytilus edulis and Modiolus modiolus biogenic reef restoration. 
Also included are relevant methods of biogenic restoration from alternate species, although not 
directly included within the search criteria. In addition, the required conditions for both species are 
presented. Abiotic and biotic conditions are included for the development of M. edulis and M. 
modiolus biogenic reefs. Peer reviewed literature was not restricted by location nor date of 
publication.

The search string was applied to three different databases; DTU-Findit, Scopus and Web of Science. 
The search string was identical for each database. Duplicates were removed by cross-matching the 
results from each of the databases.

3.5.1 Artificial Substrates – Coir Nets 
 
The importance of attachment site appears to vary depending on the life history stage of mussels 
(Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). Soft bottom substrates offer limited suitable attachment sites for the 
recruitment of larvae (Joschko et al 2008) or for seeding of mussels (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). The use of 
coir nets (Fig. 1) to provide suitable substrate has been tested previously for M. edulis as well as 
other bivalve species (De Paoli et al 2015, van der Heide et al 2014). Coir nets theoretically provide a 
hard substrate for bivalve species which sits on top of the sediment. However, the studies by De 
Paoli et al. (2015) and van der Heide et al (2014) used coir nets with limited success. Importantly, De 
Paoli et al (2015) revealed no increase in attachment force nor persistence of mussels when 
compared with bare soft sediment (i.e. mud or sand). Overall, the method failed due to the artificial 
substrate being almost immediately buried beneath sediment before mussel attachment occurred. 
Burial of the provided hard substrate voids its purpose and so should be avoided. As stated by De 
==PAGE:31==
Conservation of Biogenic Reefs protected by the Habitat Directive of the 
European Union: A review of restoration methodologies for blue mussel 
(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
Paoli et al (2015), the problem of burial could be avoided if mussel attachment occurred 
immediately after laying of the hard substrate. Van der Heide et al (2014) avoided the issue of coir 
net burial by the addition of small wooden blocks to create small “hummocks”. The hummocks 
elevated the coir nets and caused an increase in abundance of mussel spat when compared to open 
soft sediment, however, only when additionally protected from predation by C. maenas. Specifically, 
the study showed an increase in recruitment of mussel spat but sites that were exposed to predation 
showed little difference in final mussel abundance to those without the provision of hard substrate.  
This is in contradiction to the results reported by De Paoli et al (2015), who found limited effects of 
predation on the coir net established mussel beds. The difference could be explained by the age of 
the mussels used as older mussels have thicker shells (Palomo et al 2007).  
 
Although there is limited literature available on the use of coir nets to provide hard substrate for 
mussels, both for juveniles and adults, from the data available it suggests that coir nets have limited 
effects. The limited effects of coir nets may be mainly associated with substrate burial which is 
caused by both pseudo faeces and particulate matter that is carried onto the mats via water currents 
(De Paoli et al 2015) or predation of juvenile mussels by C. maenas (van der Heide et al 2014). The 
study by van der Heide et al (2014) has opened up the possibilities of using protected coir nets as 
recruitment sites when establishing novel mussel bed patches. The use of coir nets may seem to be 
an option for recruitment of juvenile mussels however the use of long-lines may prove to be more 
efficient (Walter & Liebezeit 2003). It seems that using substitute hard substrate has no improved 
effects on attachment of adult mussels compared to bare soft substrate as adult mussels 
preferentially attach to conspecifics over any other source of substrate (Commito et al 2014). 
 
 
3.5.2 Artificial Substrates – Mussel Shells 
 
Mussel shells are of great abundance due to the mussel fishing industry. From the Danish mussel 
fishery alone, ~50,000 tonnes of mussel shell are landed each year (Dolmer & Frandsen 2002). 
Dolmer & Frandsen (2002) hypothesised that the reduction of shell debris and hard substrate caused 
by mussel dredging, as seen in the Limfjorden, decreased the mussel abundance. Historically these 
mussel shells and associated debris have been classified as waste products and discarded on land. 
However, there is growing evidence that such debris can be used as attachment sites for both 
recruiting mussel spat and adult mussels (Dolmer & Stenalt 2009, Kangeri et al 2014, Frandsen & 
Dolmer 2002). In addition to this, the presence of empty mussel shells increases complexity of the 
substrate thereby reducing predation by C. maenas to less than 1% day-1 compared to 6% day-1 on 
sandy bottoms (Frandsen & Dolmer 2002). This was not the case for juvenile mortality when hunted 
by A. rubens, as shell debris attachment had similar mortalities to individuals that were present on 
soft substrate. Only when juveniles were attached to adult live conspecifics was predation by A. 
rbuens reduced (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). This is potentially due to the highly compact nature of 
established mussel beds (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017).  
The value of mussel shells as a source of substrate seems to vary depending on the life history stage 
of the mussel in question (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). Adult Perna canaliculus mussels displayed no 
preference in substrate use when confronted with a choice between soft substrate or a hard 
==PAGE:32==
Conservation of Biogenic Reefs protected by the Habitat Directive of the 
European Union: A review of restoration methodologies for blue mussel 
(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
substrate alternative, however they would consistently choose conspecifics as attachment sites 
(Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). M. edulis was also found to preferentially attach to adult live conspecifics 
rather than discarded mussel shells, regardless if other hard substrate was available (Commito et al 
2014). This, however, is not the case for juveniles of both P. canaliculus and M. edulis as both attach 
to shell debris or conspecifics in a non-discriminatory fashion (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017).  
As with coir nets, mussel shells should be in sufficient numbers to slightly elevate the attaching 
recruits from the bottom substrate.  This elevation reduces the impacts of heavy sedimentation and 
thereby voids the issues of burial (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017, De Paoli et al 2015). In conclusion, it is 
evident that juvenile individuals from mussel species will take advantage of the provision of mussel 
shells as a site of attachment and also in the form of increased recruitment (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017, De 
Paoli et al 2015, Commito et al 2014, van der Heide et al 2014). Seemingly adult individuals will 
attach to other conspecifics, instead of artificial or supplied hard substrate. The use of mussel shells 
appears to have the same limited impacts as coir nets, however additionally shells cause an increase 
in habitat complexity thereby reducing predation pressures.  
 
 
3.5.3 Spat Collection & Suspended Cultures – Long-Lines 
 
Long-lines have a multifaceted use for the restoration of mussel beds. They act as spat collectors 
when wanting to seed juvenile mussels and as a culture site for growing spat into larger individuals. 
The collection of spat for both restoration and aquaculture purposes has been extensively studied 
(Karayücel et al 2002, Drapeau et al 2006, Dare et al 1983, Kamermans et al 2002). Historically, 
mussel seed or spat was collected from wild mussel beds, for aquaculture, however the volume of 
collected spat does not always reach the required targets (Kamermans et al 2002, Walter & Liebezeit 
2003). Mortality of spat is high and could be due to lack of hard substrate (Joschko et al 2008). The 
use of long-lines could enhance the amount of spat collected to meet the demands of aquaculture or 
potentially be used in restoration (Kamermans et al 2002). Many biotic and abiotic factors influence 
the efficiency of spat collectors and long-line cultures; physical characteristics of the collectors (i.e. 
material and structure) (Filgueira et al 2007, Walter & Liebezeit 2003, Kamermans et al 2002), food 
availability (Rosland et al 2011), stocking density (Guiñez & Castilla 1999, Guiñez 2005, Hunt & 
Scheibling 1997), hydrodynamic processes (Rosland et al 2011, Aure et al 2007) and predation 
(Christensen et al 2012). 
High mussel settlement may be related to increased surface area available on spat collection ropes 
(Filgueira et al 2007). Additionally, filamentous structure of the collection ropes has been noted to 
increase settlement presumably due to more firm attachment (Lutz & Kennish 1992, Filgueira et al 
2007). Five distinguishable collector ropes were tested by Walter & Liebezeit (2003) in terms of 
volume of spat collected. “Tuft” and “Fringe” ropes were found to accumulate the highest biomass 
of mussel spat, 8.3 kg/m and 11.6 kg/m, respectively (Fig. 9) (Walter & Liebezeit 2003). The elevated 
collection was due to increased surface area provided by the “tuft” and “fringe” designs and their 
imitation of natural substrates (macroalgae or hydroids). Although increased spat biomass and 
densities are important qualities of collector ropes, density-dependent mortality is a major 
component of post-settlement mortality (Guiñez & Castilla 1999, Guiñez 2005, Hunt & Scheibling 
1997). Limited food supply due to such high densities is often the driving force behind mortality

(Filgueira et al 2007). In addition to density-dependent mortality on a single line, it is also important 
to consider the spacing between each individual collector. When spacing is too minimal it can reduce 
growth due to the insufficient delivery of seston to the mussels however nutrient influx is ultimately 
governed by the rate of flow across the long-lines (Rosland et al 2011 & Aure et al 2007). For

Predation is naturally limited due to the elevated status of the long-lines, meaning that crustacean 
or echinoderm predators may be unable to reach the mussel spats. However, predation by fish still 
impacts overall mussel spat density. The predation can be reduced by increasing the complexity of 
the collector ropes thereby creating refuge for mussel spat (Peteiro et al 2010). Complexity may be 
enhanced using a filamentous loop complement (Fig. 11). Suspended cultures and bottom cultures 
were grown from juvenile stages and exposed to predation by C. maenas. It was found that 
suspended cultures had lower mortality and higher growth rates than the bottom cultures due to 
tighter aggregations and increased byssus production (Christensen et al 2012). However, suspended 
individuals were found to have thinner shells due to the lack of predation which may have an impact 
of susceptibility to predation by C. maenas when transplanted to bottom cultures or beds 
(Christensen et al 2012). Documentation of the use of spat collectors for M. modiolus remains 
limited. Comely (1978) found comparatively low levels of M. modiolus spat settlement on collector 
long-lines with M. edulis settlement found to be significantly higher. 
 
In summary, the literature suggests that long-lines could be used to source mussel spat which could 
be used to seed wild beds or be used to lay mussels in the creation of new mussel beds (Christensen 
et al 2012, Kamermans et al 2002, Walter & Liebezeit 2003). This could potentially be an alternative 
source of mussel spat, as opposed to collecting spat from established wild beds (Kamermans et al 
2002). In addition to this, it appears that long-line spat collection has greater impact on 
accumulating biomass than simply the addition of an artificial or natural hard substrate.  
 
 3.5.4 Relaying or Translocation of Mussels 
 
The relaying or translocation of mussels into human made mussel patches for bottom culture has 
been extensively used in aquaculture for generations. When relaying mussels, it has been previously 
shown that homogeneously distributed mussels in relatively low density (e.g. 2.5 kg m-2 – 5 kg m-2) 
provide the highest rates of survival. The ideal approach is to deploy mussels in low densities, as 
redistribution by the mussels will likely be in the form of small patches which have higher survival 
rates than larger matrixes (Capelle et al 2014). Large, high density patches can often result in 
reduced growth rate and reproductive output of mussels in the centre of the bed due to insufficient 
supply of nutrients (Newell 1990, Capelle et al 2014, Okamura 1986). When transporting mussels 
before deployment, they should not be removed from water for longer than 24 hrs to ensure that 
the physiological condition is maintained (Calderwood et al 2014). M. edulis individuals were found

predation and dislodgement. Other species, P. canaliculus, were found to have a much shorter 
threshold when withstanding emersion from water and sustained reduced physiological

retention rates when seeding out mussels as nutritionally compromised mussel spat may have 
impacted performance (Carton et al 2007).  
Whole mussel translocation efforts were termed “techno fixes” by Pérez et al (2012), who claim that 
this is a quick solution even without addressing the actual fundamental issues that created the 
requirement for restoration. When sourcing adult mussels, it is important to recognise the 
differences between the source population and the target population for restoration. Differences in 
morphology to cope with the source environment (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016, Freeman 2007) may

differences in morphology (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016). Similarly, it is vital that the source population 
remains a self-sustaining population (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016), and if translocation is an option, 
ideally, younger mussels should be used as typically they are more adaptable than older populations 
(Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016). An assessment of potential alien species or diseases should also be 
carried out when translocating, as this could further decimate the planned populations (Fariñas‐

threat due to food limitation or oxygen depletion maybe a solution to forming new mussel patches.

starvation or food limitation, alternatively populations which have low growth rates due to 
insufficient seston from downflow depletion (i.e. mussels in the centre of the patch are starved)

creating bottom cultures for aquaculture, translocation could be used to restore or create novel

3.5.5 Salinity 
 
Salinity affects growth rates, maximum size, reproductive output, total life span and ultimately limits 
the range of mussel species (Kautsky 1982, Kautsky et al 1990, Westerbom et al 2002). M. edulis is 
fundamentally a marine species, however it also has the ability to persist in brackish waters 
(Westerbom et al 2002). Although mussels are present in less saline conditions of the Northern 
Baltic, they are dwarfed when compared with mussels from more saline conditions in the Western 
Baltic and North Sea (Öst & Kilpi 1997). The smaller size is due to osmotic stress experienced in the 
Baltic (Tedengren & Kautsky 1986, Tedengren et al 1990) and generally lower temperatures 
==PAGE:35==
Conservation of Biogenic Reefs protected by the Habitat Directive of the 
European Union: A review of restoration methodologies for blue mussel 
(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
(Westerbom et al 2002). At the extremes of their salinity range (4.5‰) in the Eastern Gulf of Finland, 
M. edulis forms small patches with a limited size distribution that is dominated by small individuals 
(Westerbom et al 2002). M. edulis can survive in salinities lower than 4.5‰, however only for brief 
periods of time by closing its shell to preserve saline water within its mantle during which growth 
ceases (Almada-Villela 1984). In the fully marine environments, M. edulis shows supressed growth 
rates when salinity drops below 18‰ (Almada-Villela 1984). Almada-Villela (1984) also found that 
shell growth seemingly ceased when salinity fell below 12.8‰ for marine adapted individuals 
however acclimation did eventually occur, and growth recommenced. Precipitation, alongside other 
sources of freshwater influx, can cause stress on mussel populations thereby reducing the ability of 
mussel beds to recover from extended periods of low salinity. This can be further compromised by 
predation (Westerbom et al 2002) by C. maenas and bird populations during aerial exposure. 
Typically, predation in lower salinity environments has limited impacts on mussel populations 
however the impact of predation is increased in times of salinity stress (Kautsky 1981, Kautsky 1982). 
M. modiolus suffers greatly under fluctuating salinity due to its inability to tolerate a large salinity 
range (27‰ - 41‰) (Pierce 1970). This inability is due to seepage of the external medium into the 
mussel via the byssal opening (Shumway 1977). Although Pierce (1970) found that permanent 
exposure at salinities lower than 27‰ were lethal to M. modiolus, Shumway (1977) found that 
temporary exposure, in a sinusoidal regime, was tolerated. A sinusoidal regime could be expected in 
certain tidal environments. Davenport & Kjorsvik (1982) found similar results with adult M. modiolus 
surviving in 16‰ salinity rock pools, with juvenile populations forming very dense communities in 
subtidal estuaries (Holt et al 1998). Although adult M. modiolus can withstand temporary 
fluctuations in salinity, it cannot tolerate the salinity ranges that M. edulis can tolerate in long term 
exposure situations.  
Salinity has great impact on habitat range, growth rates and maximum sizes which ultimately can 
affect reproductive output. In brackish and estuarine environments, it is important to recognise that 
fluctuations in salinity will impact the overall biomass accumulation in mussel beds for M. edulis. 
Alternatively, M. modiolus is a fully marine species and does not cope well with long term fluctuating 
salinities and may succumb even to high levels of precipitation (Pierce 1970, Holt et al 1998, 
Shumway 1997, Tyler-Walters 2007).  
 
3.5.6 Hydrodynamic Conditions – Wave Exposure & Water Currents 
 
Wave exposure is a vital factor when determining species distribution and abundance for sessile 
marine species like the mussel (Westerbom & Jattu 2006). Population densities for M. edulis 
increase with increasing wave exposure as the elevated water flow associated with high wave 
exposure maintains particulate matter in suspension for longer periods of time thereby allowing 
longer feeding times when compared with sheltered locales (Fréchette et al. 1989). Although 
densities were found to be higher, Westerbom & Jattu (2006) reported that growth rates were 
slower with increasing exposure due to the elevated energy expenditure required for thicker shells 
and increased byssus production, with extreme wave stress possibly causing the cessation of filter 
feeding. Predator related mortality is typically reduced as foraging efficiency by mobile predators is 
compromised when wave exposure is increased (Siddon & Witman 2003). Strong water movements

Conservation of Biogenic Reefs protected by the Habitat Directive of the 
European Union: A review of restoration methodologies for blue mussel 
(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
from wave exposure can cause the dislodgment of larger mussels, this generally causes smaller 
mussels to be dominant at exposed areas (Alvarado & Castilla 1996). When considering a population 
for restoration by translocation, it is important to determine the level of wave exposure that will be 
experienced. High levels of exposure will require individuals to have stronger attachment via byssal 
threads, therefore it is important to source from populations that experience similar conditions or 
have spat collection ropes in areas of high water movement. As M. modiolus is a subtidal species, it 
will only experience wave exposure during spring tides and therefore, typically, wave exposure does 
not need to be taken into account for this species (Coleman & Trueman 1971). When M. modiolus is 
exposed it could be expected to be less tolerant of wave exposure than M. edulis due to its large 
size, as typically large mussels are at greater risk of dislodgement (Alvarado & Castilla 1996). 
Current speeds affect the available seston supply that is delivered to mussel beds. High densities 
within mussel beds may cause downstream depletion and ultimately reduce overall growth if water 
flow is low (Wildish & Kristmanson 1985).  Wildish & Kristmanson (1985) found that low (0.1 cm sec-
1) and intermediate (~0.5 cm sec-1) current speeds negatively impact or have no effect, respectively, 
on mussel growth. High current speeds (~4 cm sec-1) provide adequate biological material for 
growth, although Wildish & Kristmanson (1985) hypothesised that at high current speeds filter 
feeding may cease by closing of the mussel shell. Cessation of filter feeding at high currents speeds 
would be in concurrence with Westerbom & Jattu (2006) who found inhibition of filter feeding at 
extreme wave exposure levels. Holt et al (1998) suggested that strong to moderate tidal currents are 
required for the creation of dense M. modiolus beds however, fouling by epifauna and algae greatly 
increases the chances of dislodgement (Witman 1985). As with M. edulis in wave exposed areas, M. 
modiolus has to increase byssus production to maintain sufficient attachment strength in areas with 
strong currents which results in overall lower growth rates (Comely 1987).  
Therefore, it is important to consider bottom current speeds, particularly at populations with high 
densities to avoid growth limitation due to seston depletion. The reverse is also true, as high 
currents will cause both M. edulis and M. modiolus to exert energy on byssus production which will 
ultimately cause a decrease in growth rate. Optimal current speeds approach 4 cm s-1 (Wildish & 
Kristmanson 1985), although highly dependent on the availability of seston.  
 
3.5.7 Aerial Exposure 
 
Aerial exposure will typically only affect M. edulis due to its littoral and sublittoral distribution, 
however M. modiolus will experience aerial exposure during lunar tides as it is a subtidal species 
(Coleman & Trueman 1971, Ragnarsson & Burgos 2012). M. edulis becomes largely anaerobic during 
aerial exposure and throughout anaerobic respiration there is a complete reduction in growth and 
digestion (De-Zwaan & Mathieu 1992, Buschbaum & Saier 2001). If individuals experience less than 
20% (4.8 hours a day) of aerial exposure, then there was little recorded difference in tissue growth 
when compared to mussels that were not exposed however growth rapidly decreases when above 
20% exposure time (Gillmor 1982). Baird (1996) found that at 55% (about 13 h day-1) emergence 
there was no growth by M. edulis, however the actual upper limit may be lower depending on air 
temperature (Holt et al 1998, Suchanek 1978, Seed & Suchanek 1992). The presence of 
contaminants greatly impacts the survival and tolerance to aerial exposure in mussel species

(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)  
(Eertman et al 1993). For example, as found by Eertman et al (1993), PCB and PAH concentrations 
impacted tolerance severely.   
Comparatively, littoral populations of M. edulis are at higher densities than subtidal populations due 
to reduced predation however this results in lower growth rates, in littoral populations, from 
increased intraspecific competition and aerial exposure (Buschbaum & Saier 2001). There may be 
phenotypic variation between littoral and sublittoral populations in the form of oxygen consumption 
and energy expenditure during emersion (Eertman et al 1993). Sublittoral individuals have been 
found to consume more energy and recover slower than littoral counterparts when emersed 
(Eertman et al 1993, Widdows & Shick 1985). Sublittoral populations will entirely seal their mantles 
to halt any water loss thereby becoming anoxic during aerial exposure (Eertman et al 1993). M. 
edulis has increased performance under aerial exposure comparatively with M. modiolus due to the 
ability of M. edulis to maintain water within the mantle cavity (Coleman & Trueman 1971). M. 
modiolus often spends periods of time “gaping”, allowing water to escape from the mantle cavity at 
a very fast rate, and even when the shell is closed, water can leak from the byssal opening (Coleman 
& Trueman 1971). Complete water loss will impair respiratory efficiency and ultimately osmotic 
balance within the tissues due to further water loss down the osmotic gradient (Coleman & Trueman 
1971). Tolerance to emersion is greatly reduced in M. modiolus exposed to high temperatures and 
wind speeds, which may cause death after 1hr of emersion (Coleman 1973). In low temperature 
environments with low wind speeds, Coleman (1973) suggested that M. modiolus may be able to 
survive for up to 3 days.  
The position of mussel communities within the tidal zone impacts the overall performance of 
populations. M. edulis is far more adapted to aerial exposure than M. modiolus. Phenotypic 
adaptations to aerial exposure should be taken into account for M. edulis, as sublittoral individuals 
have a comparatively limited tolerance to their littoral counterparts (Eertman et al 1993, Widdows & 
Shick 1985). In addition to this, the presence of anthropogenic pollution (PCBs and PAHs) should also 
be considered when attempting to restore or enhance intertidal mussel communities.  
 
3.5.8 Temperature 
 
As with salinity, M. edulis is considered a eurythermal species that can tolerate a wide range of 
temperatures (Bayne 1976), whereas M. modiolus has a lower thermal tolerance (Holt et al 1998). 
Upper thermal limit of M. edulis was found to be 27 °C - 29 °C however growth is significantly 
compromised above 20 °C (Almada-Villa et al 1982, Read & Cumming 1967). Growth rate seemingly 
is unchanged between 10 °C – 20 °C (Bayne 1976), however growth rate declines below 10 °C, with 
growth ceasing when <3 °C (Almada-Villela et al 1982). M. edulis can withstand temporary sub-zero 
temperatures with the minimum thermal tolerance at -30 °C and can tolerate temporary freezing 
(Loomis 1995). Individuals were able to survive repeated exposure to -8 °C in water, however 
continual exposure resulted in death (Bourget 1983). Although individuals managed to survive the 
decrease in temperatures, it has been suggested that this may make individuals more susceptible to 
predation due to their moribund or weakened state following unfavourable temperatures (Crisp 
1964).  M. modiolus, on the other hand, has an upper thermal limit of 23 °C (Read & Cumming 1967), 
perhaps partly explaining its limited distribution in only subtidal zones. Exposure to direct sunlight

Although, due to its sublittoral habitat, temperatures in many locations fluctuate less than in

distribution, low temperatures should not influence growth or site suitability for M. modiolus as 
individuals will be relatively insulated from decreasing temperatures (Holt et al 1998).  
When considering temperature as a controlling factor for restoration, the upper thermal tolerances 
for both species should be considered. M. edulis will encounter its thermal upper limits if the site is 
intertidal and therefore individuals may be exposed during low tides to elevated temperatures. M. 
modiolus is much more susceptible to thermal change and therefore sites should be carefully 
selected that will not exceed the thermal maxima of 23 °C.  
 
3.5.9 Bottom Substrate 
 
Hard bottom substrate is an important settlement site for larval mussels and delayed 
metamorphosis may occur if suitable substrates are not encountered (Joschko et al 2008). This 
causes larvae to be suspended in the water column for longer which may cause increased mortalities 
of up to 99% due to predation and starvation (Thorson 1966, Mileikovsky 1971, Jørgensen 1981). 
The importance of attachment site appears to vary depending on the life history stage of mussels 
(Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). Soft bottom substrates offer limited suitable attachment sites for the 
recruitment of larvae for M. edulis (Joschko et al 2008) and M. modiolus (Holts et al 1998) or for 
seeding of mussels (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). It seems that using substitute hard substrate (e.g. coir nets 
or mussel debris) has no improved effects on attachment of adult M. edulis than soft substrate as 
attachment to adult conspecifics seems overwhelmingly preferential (Commito et al 2014). M. 
modiolus has also been found to attach preferentially to adult conspecifics which additionally act as 
refuges for juveniles (Roberts 1975).  
 
Further details are provided in the sections on “Artificial Substrates – Coir Nets” and “Artificial 
Substrates – Mussel Shells”. 
 
3.5.10 Hypoxia 
 
Adult mytilid species are generally considered to be euryoxic (Theede et al 1969), meaning that they 
can tolerate a wide range of oxygen concentrations (De-Zwaan & Mathieu 1992). M. edulis is able to 
carry out anaerobic respiration, as seen when aerially exposed (De-Zwaan & Mathieu 1992), in 
response to low oxygen concentrations and maintain metabolism almost at aerobic levels (Newell & 
Moran 1989). Prolonged exposure to low oxygen levels will eventually result in death, as seen by 
Jørgensen (1980), where mussels survived in hypoxia (0.1 – 0.2 mg/l at 10 °C) for nearly 2 weeks. No 
data on M. modiolus were found on survival in varying oxygen levels, however similar responses to 
hypoxia (0.21 mg/l) with the addition of sulphide were seen in both M. modiolus and M. edulis

3.5.11 Predation 
 
Adult M. modiolus seems relatively unaffected by predation due to their large size, however C. 
maenas and A. rubens predate on juvenile individuals extensively. Predation on juveniles is 
minimized by the settlement and attachment of larvae and juveniles onto established adult M. 
modiolus beds (Holts et al 1998). The adult beds provide refuges due to the mass of byssal threads 
that restrict access by predators (Anwar et al 1990). Only very large C. maenas and A. rubens are 
able to predate and successfully open mussels of over 50mm in length (Holts et al 1998). Holts et al 
(1998) predicted that large (> 50 mm) M. modiolus are virtually predator free due to their size. The 
common Whelk (Buccinum undatum) has been found to predate upon large M. modiolus, but 
closure of the mussel shell rapidly can cause breakage of the tip of the B. undatum shell which may 
deter it from continuing its attacks (Nielsen 1975). It seems that in restoration projects, M. modiolus 
size is an impact factor determining the negative impacts associated with predator-related mortality.  
M. edulis, on the other hand, can be greatly impacted by a variety of predators at all life history 
stages; birds, plaice, flounder, crabs, starfish, and gastropods all play a role (Holt et al 1998). C. 
maenas is the main species of crab that predates on M. edulis in the low intertidal and sublittoral 
zone (Holt et al 1998). Seemingly, there is an upper limit in mussel size of 70mm for predation by C. 
maenas, although some reports suggest that a mussel size of 45mm may already be safe from 
predation (Davies et al 1980). Crab cages have been used to prevent predation and showed 
remarkable results with elevated survival of M. edulis compared with the unprotected sites (Davies 
et al 1980). Predation by C. maenas on recruits and juveniles seems to be reduced by up to 70% 
when attached to adult conspecifics (McGrorty et al 1990). A. rubens and C. maenas have a similar 
predation body size range of M. edulis however, A. rubens is very sensitive to desiccation and so 
does not travel inshore (Holts et al 1998). Additionally, A. rubens is also sensitive to salinity changes 
due to poor osmoregulation (Lawrence 1996), however displays phenotypic adaptations depending 
on location (8‰ minimum in Baltic individuals) (Sarantchova 2001, Binyon 1961) and hypoxia (50% 
mortality at 0.2 mg/l 10 °C) (Theede et al 1969), explaining the absence within the Eastern and 
Central Baltic regions. A. rubens invasions can cause catastrophic damage to mussel beds as 
reported by Dare (1973) in Morecambe Bay, England. A. rubens ocurred in densities of up to 450m-2 
covering ~ 2 hectares of the English coastline and consumed ~4000 tonnes of first year mussels 
(Dare 1973, 1982). The cause of such invasions and proliferations is unclear, and remain rare, 
however it may be associated with highly abundant food sources (Dare 1973, Sloan & Aldridge 1981, 
Saier 2001). Outside of explosive invasions, typically adult A. rubens only consumes a single mussel 
every 2 – 3 days (Saier 2001), which may have limited impact on mussel communities. Eider ducks 
(Somateria mollissima) can eat up to 150 mussels per day (Saier 2001) and can be responsible for the 
majority of mortalities, especially in wave protected bays and estuaries (Seeds & Suchanek 1992). 
Oystercatchers (Haematopus ostralegus) also consume similar numbers of mussels as S. mollissima,

Predator related mortality has large impacts in both M. modiolus and M. edulis at a juvenile stage as 
a large variety of predators are able to open smaller shells (Holts et al 1998). As individuals grow, the 
numbers of prospective predators decrease until only large C. maenas and A. rubens are able to 
predate upon both species with M. modiolus seemingly more resilient to predation pressures. 
Predation pressures on M. edulis may be lower on more exposed sites and sites of lower salinity 
(Eastern and Central Baltic). The only reported predator prevention method is the crab cages used 
by Davies et al (1980) which may assist in the prevention of predation on recruiting and juvenile 
mussels. Locations of bird colonies should be noted before sites are selected for restoration, 
specifically if M. edulis beds will be exposed at low tide due to the large negative impacts of 
predation.  
 
3.5.12 Fundamental Problems when Restoring Mussel Beds 
 
 
A fundamental issue when using adult mussels to create novel mussel beds is the phenotypic 
variations between the source and recipient locations (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016, Freeman 2007). 
Phenotypic variations, for example in the form of attachment strength, may have been an additional 
compounding factor for the failure of the De Paoli et al (2015) restoration project. Mussels present 
in the intertidal zone typically have stronger attachment strength than their subtidal counterparts 
(Hunt & Scheibling 2001). This is due to increased volume of water movement acting on the mussel 
community, along with the strength of the water movement. This increase in attachment strength 
due to water movement has also been observed in suspended mussel cultures (Lachance et al 2008). 
The same is true for predator defence, where phenotypic variation in shell thickness and abductor 
strength is important (Christensen et al 2012). On this basis, it is recommended that projects 
involving translocation of adult mussels to novel sites take phenotypic variation into account.  
 
Low density, small patches often result in higher growth rates and are therefore preferential when 
establishing novel beds (Newell 1990, Capelle et al 2014, Okamura 1986) due to the lower 
intraspecific competition (Svane & Ompi 1993) however, large patches result in elevated substrate 
stability which reduces predation risk (McGrorty et al 1990). A trade-off exists between growth rate 
and predation risk which are both directly related to patch size. Large patches have slower growth

(Mytilus edulis) and horse mussel (Modiolus modiolus)

currents.  
 
3.5.13 Important Considerations before a Restoration Project Commences 
 
1) Salinity 
Salinity should be taken into consideration mainly for the restoration of M. modiolus due to its 
limited ability to survive in low salinities (Pierce 1970). Areas which fluctuate greatly, in terms of 
freshwater influx, should not be considered as sites for restoration. M. edulis is much more resilient 
to changing salinities (Westerbom et al 2002) however low salinities will reduce growth rate 
(Almada-Villela 1984). 
 
2) Aerial Exposure 
Aerial exposure should not be considered for M. modious restoration as this species is subtidal and 
therefore will only very rarely be exposed (Coleman & Trueman 1971, Ragnarsson & Burgos 2012). 
When exposed, M. modiolus suffers greatly due to water loss and temperatures (Coleman & 
Trueman 1971, Coleman 1973) so when planning a restoration project, for this species, the chosen 
site should ideally be completely subtidal. M. edulis is more resistant to emersion and can withstand 
20% (4.8 hours a day) aerial exposure with little impact upon growth, however above 20% exposure 
time may lead to compromised growth rates (Gillmor 1982). Exposure times on sites for restoration 
projects should be examined to determine the optimal positioning of the project. 
 
3) Hydrodynamic Processes 
Tidal exposure and current speed are both important abiotic factors that will influence growth rates 
of M. edulis and M. modiolus (Westerbom & Jattu 2006, Comely 1987, Wildish & Kristmanson 1985). 
Tidal exposure effects on M. modiolus do not need to be considered due to its subtidal distribution 
(Coleman & Trueman 1971, Ragnarsson & Burgos 2012). Sites with high tidal forces should be 
excluded as extreme exposure can cause dislodgement and the cessation of feeding (Alvarado & 
Castilla 1996, Westerbom & Jattu 2006). Current speeds should be examined prior to initiating a 
restoration project, as low speeds may impact on seston delivery and ultimately growth rates 
(Wildish & Kristmanson 1985). 
 
4) Temperature 
Upper thermal limits need to be examined for both species on the chosen site for restoration. M. 
edulis may reach its upper thermal limits during aerial exposure (29 °C) (Almada-Villa et al 1982, 
Read & Cumming 1967), therefore this should be taken into consideration during the quantification 
of aerial exposure time. M. modiolus has an upper thermal limit (23 °C) which can be easily 
surpassed if subjected to aerial exposure (Read & Cumming 1967).

5) Bottom Substrate 
Bottom substrate is important for the natural recruitment and settlement of larvae from the water 
column (Joschko et al 2008). Juvenile and adult mussels will preferentially attack to live conspecifics 
(Commito et al 2014, Roberts 1975). When laying juvenile mussels collected on long-lines or when 
translocating mussels, previous research suggests that substrate should not be an issue as long as 
conspecifics already are present in the area or mussels are lain in sufficient quantities. 
 
6) Hypoxia 
Both species can support relatively high levels of hypoxia (>0.1 – 0.2 mg/l at 10 °C for M. edulis and 
>0.21 mg/l for M. modiolus) for short periods of time (1 -2 weeks) before death (Jørgensen 1980, 
Theede 1969). Oxygen levels should be examined prior to initiation of the project, along with 
possible sources of eutrophication that may lead to hypoxia via algal blooms (Howarth et al 2011). 
 
7) Predation 
The risk of predation can be difficult to quantify when mussels are immersed in water, however, bird 
predation on M. edulis during low tide could be minimised by not initiating restoration projects in 
the vicinity of bird colonies. A. rubens is one of the main predators for M. edulis and M. modiolus, 
with the main impact of predation occurring during swarming events. The causes for such events are 
unclear (Dare 1973), however, once known this should be a factor that is taken into consideration 
for minimising biomass loss during low tides. 
 
8) Phenotypic Variation 
 
Phenotypic variation between the source and target restoration population can have great 
influences even over relatively short distances (<1000m) (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016). Differences 
may be in terms of attachment strength, to combat elevated currents or tidal exposure, or in the 
form of predator specific defences (Freeman 2007). Maladapted individuals, when translocating, 
may not survive in new abiotic or biotic conditions and could result in failure to establish new mussel 
communities.

3.6 Conclusions 
 
There is a wealth of knowledge for the biotic and abiotic factors for M. edulis growth, however as 
reported here, similar literature is not available on M. modiolus. There is limited literature on each 
method of restoration, as described above, however some conclusions can be drawn from the 
reviewed studies   
The creation of artificial substrates from coir nets as a site of attachment for adult and juvenile 
stages often has limited impact on mussel bed populations (De Paoli et al 2015, van der Heide et al 
2014). Mussels preferentially attach to conspecifics above all other types of substrate. Van der Heide 
et al (2014) showed limited success in using elevated coir nets as a source of recruitment, however 
long-line spat collectors perform better as recruitment sites accumulating much higher biomass 
(Walter & Liebezeit 2003). The use of mussel shells as a hard bottom substrate for attachment has 
been shown in M. edulis and P. canaliculus, however as with coir nets, adult individuals 
preferentially would attach to live conspecifics as opposed to shell debris (Commito et al 2014, 
Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). Juveniles, however, would seemingly attach to both mussel shell debris or 
conspecifics without preference, although they would not attach to soft substrate if either live 
conspecifics or mussel debris were available (Wilcox & Jeffs 2017). The use of mussel shells appears 
to have the same limited impact as coir nets, however additionally shells cause an increase in habitat 
complexity thereby reducing predation pressures (Frandsen & Dolmer 2002). The use of mussel 
debris may be of use when translocating mussels for restoration to reduce initial predation 
pressures (Frandsen & Dolmer 2002). 
To date, long-line collectors are the most successful method for collecting mussel spat for both M. 
edulis and M. modiolus, although M. modiolus spat has been found to collect in far small numbers 
(Comely 1978). Depending on the design of the collector ropes used, up to 11.6 kg/m of mussel spat 
has been collected (Walter & Liebezeit 2003), which is far greater than from the use of elevated and 
protected coir nets (Van der Heide et al 2014). Due to the hanging nature of long-lines, they may act 
as a refuge from benthic predation during development, when seemingly both species are most 
vulnerable (Holts et al 1998, Hilgerloh 1997, Dare 1976, Seed & Suchanek 1992). Mussel spat is 
frequently collected from established wild beds, however long-lines could be a more ecological 
answer as it avoids the use of wild mussel beds as collection sites (Kamermans et al 2002). Of the 
three methods for recruitment or accumulation of biomass (1. Coir nets, 2. mussel shell debris and 3. 
long-lines as spat collectors) long-line use appears to be the most effective method. The biomass 
accumulated from long-line collection could be transferred to the benthic environment to help 
supplement current populations that are under threat from extirpation, assuming that the initial 
reason for the populations decline is addressed.  
Translocation of whole mussels has been used in the creation of novel beds for aquaculture, by 
either using the spat collected from long-lines or from the collection of wild populations. The use of 
spat from long-line collectors has been termed a more ecological approach to seeding mussel beds 
by Kamermans et al (2002). Dolmer et al (2012) suggested collecting mussels from wild beds that 
may suffer due to abiotic factors, thereby making them a resource that, if unused, would ultimately 
deteriorate. Both sources of mussels could be considered when establishing or supplementing 
mussel beds. As pointed out by Pérez et al (2012), it is important to identify the fundamental issues

behind the struggling populations, before simply translocation new individuals to that area. The 
relay of mussels has been found to be most successful when distributing them in a homogenous and 
low-density formation (e.g. 2.5 kg m-2 – 5 kg m-2) (Capelle et al 2014). A vital part of any restoration 
project, is to take into consideration the effects of local adaptation to the environment and if the 
mussels are suitable to the new target environment (Fariñas‐Franco et al 2016, Freeman 2007). For 
example, as seen by Fariñas‐Franco et al (2013), there may be differences in morphology between 
populations less than 1 km in distance apart.  
As shown, there are a host of biotic and abiotic factors that need to be taken into consideration 
when carrying out a restoration project of both M. edulis and M. modiolus. Seemingly, M. modiolus 
is much less tolerant to several abiotic factors and environmental conditions should be examined 
carefully prior to a restoration project.  
The size of M. modiolus, however, decreases the pressures of predation especially once mature 
(Holts et al 1998). Predation pressures seemingly have the most effect on M. edulis, however there 
are possibilities of using predator protection schemes (i.e crab cages) (Davies et al 1980) to help 
prevent juvenile mortality and assist in natural recruitment. In terms of abiotic factors, M. edulis 
displays a high tolerance to several abiotic factors. Although growth rates are seemingly affected by 
most of said abiotic factors (salinity, temperature, aerial exposure and hypoxia), overall survival 
seems to be affected to a limited extent if adverse conditions are temporary (hypoxia and aerial 
exposure). 
Globally, there is limited information available for restoration projects, specifically in terms of M. 
modiolus restoration. Additionally, growth and survival requirements for M. modiolus are limited in 
existing literature. Of the methods and approaches described in this review, it is clear that there 
needs to be an increase in communication between investigatory parties to attempt to accumulate 
current knowledge and carry out a more systematic approach to restoration.

4. Conclusions and Closing Remarks 
 
The initial section of the thesis has concluded that N. melanostomus shows aggressive tendencies 
towards C. maenas when competing for mussel bed habitat. This aggression is variable depending on 
the metabolic rate phenotype that is displayed, with increased maximum metabolic rate leading to 
heightened aggression. This has implications for the frontier of the invasion of N. melanostomus, as 
typically the frontier will consist of high metabolic rate individuals (Myles-Gonzales et al 2015, Groen 
et al 2012). Maximum metabolic rate was also found to be repeatable across a 4-week period, 
however as pointed out by Norin & Malte (2011) metabolic repeatability decreases over longer time 
frames. This indicates that metabolic rate repeatability should be re-examined in N. melanostomus 
but over longer periods of time. There was no difference in residency time of C. meanas with or 
without the presence of N. melanostomus and regardless of metabolic rate phenotype. This suggests 
that either the habitat used for competition was of the incorrect size, or that the presence of N. 
melanostomus will not affect the C. maenas population. Future research in this field should consider 
similar trials however during the reproductive season of N. melanostomus, as typically individuals are 
more aggressive when nest guarding (Phillips et al 2003, Dubs & Corkum 1996, MacInnis & Corkum 
2000). In addition to this, future research could examine the possible interactions of N. melanostomus 
with other native benthic species as there is potential for interactions (Corkum et al 2004). 
The second part of the thesis has concluded that there are large gaps in knowledge for the 
methodologies of biogenic reef restoration, specifically for M. modiolus. A similar lack of results was 
found for the abiotic and biotic conditions that regulate M. modiolus growth and population 
establishment. This clearly indicates that research is needed on both the required conditions for M. 
modiolus populations and the methods that could be used in restoration projects. From the acquired 
literature, some conclusions can be drawn.  
The literature reviewed suggests that the use of long-lines to acquire spat and hence juvenile mussels 
is the most effective method. Depending on the design of the long-lines used, up to 11.6 kg/m of 
mussel spat has been collected (Walter & Liebezeit 2003), which is far greater than from the use of 
elevated and protected coir nets (Van der Heide et al 2014).  The use of artificial substrate seemingly 
has no use for juvenile and adult individuals (De Paoli et al 2015, van der Heide et al 2014), however 
may result in settlement of larvae (van der Heide et al 2014). The acquired biomass from long-line spat 
collectors could then be used to create novel mussel beds to combat the negative effects of dredging. 
In addition to this, it has been suggested that this could be an alternative solution for aquaculture to 
obtain mussel spat to seed bottom cultures instead of harvesting from wild mussel beds (Kamermans 
et al 2002).  
In order for these methodologies to be successful, abiotic and biotic conditions must be met. M. 
modiolus seems to be more affected by abiotic factors and has a limited tolerance to the factors that 
were investigated (salinity, temperature, aerial exposure). The reverse is true for the effects of 
predation, as the literature reviewed suggests that M. modiolus has a limited number of predators 
once it reaches a threshold size of >50mm (Holt et al 1998). M. edulis, on the other hand, is predated upon 
by a large variety of predators when immersed in water and during low tide when large quantities of mussel are 
consumed by birds.  
==PAGE:61==
To conclude, there is limited information available for restoration projects, specifically in terms of M. 
modiolus restoration. Growth and survival requirements for M. modiolus are also limited in existing 
literature. Of the methods and approaches described in this review, it is clear that there needs to be 
an increase in communication between investigatory parties to attempt to accumulate current 
knowledge and carry out a more systematic approach to restoration.
