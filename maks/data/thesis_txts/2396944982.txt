Abstract 
Keratins are abundant as components of by-products produced in slaughterhouses, meat 
and poultry plants such as skin remains, bristle, animal hair/wool, horns, hooves and feathers. 
Accumulation of these wastes causes negative impacts on environment and human health; thus, 
they cannot be disposed without sufficient treatment. Nowadays, producers seek waste 
valorization by extracting their high protein content from keratin waste to be used in industry for 
example as animal feed supplement in fish farms. Methods of current state of the art such as 
incineration or acidic/alkaline hydrolysis cannot be used in waste valorization due to their high 
energy investment demands, their impact on the environment and destruction of essential amino 
acid content of keratinous substrate. Therefore, a novel enzymatic hydrolysis of keratin waste is 
investigated in this study. This report presents lab scale experimental results on enzyme stability 
and hydrolysis performed by a keratinolytic enzymatic cocktail produced by Amycolatopsis 
keratiniphila D2. Although the exact composition of the enzyme cocktail is not known, it is 
interpreted from experimental results that cocktail contains different types of enzymes and 
mesophilic character of enzyme cocktail is also revealed with enzyme stability test. Main 
behavior of hydrolysis is also presented as a fast-initial reaction within first 4 hours followed by 
a very slow reaction. Subsequently, the first reduced order mathematical model is constructed 
according to dynamic state data for the substrate and product concentrations. Mathematical 
model of enzyme system is expressed as the product of two independent functions where 
Michaelis-Menten function depends upon the substrate concentration and describes the reaction 
kinetics, whereas the other relates to time and expresses the kinetics of enzyme deactivation. 
Additionally, uncompetitive inhibition also included in Michaelis-Menten expression. A 
successful match between model fit and experimental data is obtained. It is proposed to use the 
model as a base to understand the general characteristics of enzymatic hydrolysis of keratinous 
material and to be used in its further improvement and optimization studies. 
Keywords:  Keratin, enzymatic hydrolysis, hydrolysis modelling, parameter estimation

1. INTRODUCTION 
In the last decade, the number of biofuels and bio-based chemicals which can be obtained 
through microbial fermentation technologies has continuously increased. In a broader sense, 
biotechnology encapsulates the use of cells and enzymes to produce chemicals, fuels, enzymes, 
metabolites, and compounds from renewable sources. Economics is the primary concern of 
developing new biological technologies since they enable highly efficient processes with low 
operational and capital costs. Biotechnology provides ecologically safe and sustainable 
production systems defined by political and social demands in combination with the increasing 
need for raw materials and energy [1]. Nevertheless, a significant step towards the sustainable 
and cost-effective production of such new bio commodities will undoubtedly be the possibility to 
employ, to a more considerable extent, cheaper and widely available renewable feedstocks such 
as residual biomass and organic waste materials. 
Keratins are fibrous animal proteins, and due to their high protein content, keratin-rich 
wastes are valuable. However, they are unavailable because of their high solidity and resistance 
to degradation. Keratins are abundant as components of by-products, wastes and residues, such 
as skin remains, bristle, animal hair/wool, horns, and hooves, and feathers which are products of 
slaughterhouses, meat, and poultry processing plants, and tanning and fur processing industry. 
Notably, development of the poultry industry led to an increased amount of keratin-rich waste [2, 
3]. The global poultry industry produces 2 million tons of chicken feathers yearly. Livestock 
populations also produce an additional 1 million tons of keratin and the rest of the keratin is a 
waste produced in fur industries, meat processing industries, and slaughterhouses. Estimations

consider that every year, 5 million tons of keratin remain as a waste by-product of various 
industries [4]. Moreover, barbershops release lots of keratin waste in the form of human hair and

wastes discarded without sufficient reuse every year [2]. 
Accumulation of non-treated keratin waste serves as habitat for many pathogenic 
microorganisms which cause many health problems in animals and humans. In fact, non-treated 
poultry wastes alone result in ammonia, nitrous oxide, and hydrogen sulfide emissions which 
cause unbearable odor. Respiration of this odor may lead to health problems like headache, eye, 
throat and nasal irritations, nausea, and diarrhea [5]. To sum up, these wastes create air, soil and 
water pollution. That is why it is a challenge to find ways to treat keratin-rich wastes. There is an 
increasing need for finding a way to treat keratin-rich residues that can lower the environmental, 
financial and public health risks [1]. 
Keratin waste is classified as category three material in regulation (EC) 1774/2002 of the 
European Parliament and Council of 3rd October 2002 which says that keratin waste is a low-risk 
animal by-product. This category of materials covers eggs and the other parts of animals which 
are suitable for human consumption as well as animal by-products such as animal skin, hooves, 
feathers, wool, horns and hairs that don’t show any sign of infectious agents. Thus, this category 
of animal by-products is utilizable for various purposes of human economic interests [2]. 
Recently, producers seek to valorize these keratin-rich wastes by extracting soluble proteins from 
them to produce valuable and sustainable materials. When the amount of keratin waste produced 
every year is considered, it is possible to say that these waste materials can be employed as low-
cost substrates in microbial conversion processes to treat the waste and add value. Microbial 
degradation methods can be considered as environmentally friendly and economical which seem

2. LITERATURE OVERVIEW 
2.1. Current State of the Art in Keratin-rich Waste Management 
Keratins can be abundantly found in animal production and processing sites as 
compounds of by-products such as; bristles, hair, down, horns and hooves. They contain 
approximately 15-18% nitrogen, 2-5% sulfur, 1.30% fat, 3.20% mineral elements and 90% 
proteins [2]. Due to their high protein content, it is desirable to use keratin waste in the 
production of animal feed. Keratins are insoluble and resistant to enzymatic proteases due to a 
high number of disulfide bonds in their structure. Thus it creates the major obstacle in native 
keratin processing. Nowadays, chemical, thermal and enzymatic methods have been extensively 
explored for keratin decomposition and reuse [8].

Possible applications of keratin waste after suitable treatment can mainly be listed as; leather 
processing, biofuel production, detergent additives, animal feed, fertilizers and peptide synthesis.

Treatment methods of keratins are investigated in groups as thermal methods, acidic and alkaline 
hydrolysis, microbial fermentation, enzymatic-chemical hydrolysis and aerobic and anaerobic 
digestion. 
2.1.1 Thermal methods 
The traditional method of dealing with this abundant keratinous waste is incineration. 
However, disposing of such waste by burning causes lots of fumes and awful smell [10]. 
Hydrothermal treatment was a popular method to convert feather and bristle wastes to 
animal feeds by pressure cooking followed by milling. In this process, steam pressure is applied 
at 10-15 psi with the temperatures between 80 and 140°C in the presence of acids like HCl, 
H2SO4, HCOOH or alkalis like NaOH, KOH, Na2CO3, K2CO3, NaSiO2. Treatment of keratinous 
material with acids or alkalis at their boiling point for 2-3 hours opens polypeptide chains inside 
keratins and releases water-soluble peptides, oligopeptides and amino acids. It is desired to use 
products of thermal treatment for feedstuff, e.g., chicken poultry, swine, rainbow trout and 
salmon. The products of hydrothermal treatment have a high content of protein, nitrogen, fat and 
mineral substances. However, due to the effect of temperature, pressure, and chemicals, 
hydrolysis may cause the partial or complete destruction of essential amino acids like lysine, 
methionine and tryptophan and formation of non-nutritive amino acids like lysine, alanine, and 
lanthionine. Thus, these methods decrease the nutritional quality and digestibility of the 
products. Consequently, they are classified as low-quality animal feed due to their low content of 
essential amino acids [2, 9, 31].  
In another study, it is stated that independently which method is used to reuse keratin-rich 
waste, newly produced materials will eventually reach the end-of-life and become waste again. 
In that perspective, pyrolysis can be a suitable treatment method for polymer waste as it allows 
==PAGE:16==

both energy and material recovery. In this method, keratin-rich wastes are thermally decomposed 
in two main steps. The degradation step occurs in the 170-300°C temperature range and is 
dominated by the evolution of inorganic gases, i.e., NH3, CO2, SCS, SCO, H2S and SO2 and of 
thiols. At the second step, nitriles and aromatics are formed above 300°C. Pyrolysis can be 
considered as an alternative method to treat keratin-rich wastes. However, it has economic and 
environmental problems which are caused by the need for high temperature and the production 
of undesirable by-products [4]. 
2.1.2 Acidic or alkaline hydrolysis 
Acidic or alkaline hydrolysis is a method which is used to solubilize keratin material by 
heating it up with some organic solvents to use in animal feed. This method can be applied 
gradually by solving keratin waste in an organic solvent, precipitation of proteins with acetone, 
distillation to remove solvents and drying. It was found that hydrolysate was rich in amino acids 
and polypeptides and its protein composition was similar to soybean. Thus, it was suitable for 
using it as a diet supplement in feeding ruminants. However, its arginine, histidine, lysine, 
methionine, and threonine content were low to use in animal feed [2]. 
In another application, keratinous waste is turned into keratin hydrolysate by controlled 
alkali hydrolysis. Subsequently, keratin hydrolysate is successfully used in leather processing 
during chrome tanning and re-chroming operations to enhance the uptake of chromium salt. It is 
also employed in filling cum retanning operations [31]. However, all these chemical and physical 
processes demand high energy investments and end up with high production costs. 
2.1.3 Microbial fermentation of keratin waste 
Microbial waste management becomes more popular every day due to high production 
costs and environmental impacts of thermal and chemical methods. When the amount of annual

keratin-rich waste production is considered, these waste materials can be employed as low-cost 
substrates in microbial conversion processes to treat the waste and add value. Microbial 
degradation methods can be considered as environmentally friendly and economical, which 
seems to be an attractive approach to manage keratin-rich wastes without energy waste and 
amino acid loss. Studies made on microbiological degradation of wool with fungus, Bacillus and 
thermophilic and mesophilic actinomycetes show that they can efficiently degrade keratin by 
synthesis of keratinases. Keratinases can show activity at a broad temperature and pH ranges. 
They attack keratin residues and convert them into valuable products to be used in feed, 
fertilizer, pharmaceutical, detergent and leather industries. In fact, products obtained from feather 
waste with keratinolytic strains show a high content of soluble proteins and amino acids. 
Differently, from thermal and chemical methods which are mentioned before, in microbial 
hydrolysis, products show a high content of lysine, threonine, leucine, isoleucine and valine and 
high digestibility that make them suitable to use in animal feed. Keratinolytic microorganisms 
have started to be used in some countries such as India, Brazil, and Venezuela to obtain protein 
hydrolysates. However, microbial hydrolysis is not commercially used for keratin reuse as 
animal feed [3, 6]. 
2.1.4 Enzymatic-chemical hydrolysis 
Enzymatic-chemical hydrolysis is a combination of microbial and chemical methods 
which shows improvement in digestibility and amino acid content to hydrothermal treatment. 
However, in this method, some enzymatic modifications such as enrichment with lysine should 
be done to obtain desirable products [2].

2.1.5 Aerobic and anaerobic digestion of keratin waste 
Another way to deal with keratinous waste can be a landfill of it. However, this method 
results in environmental pollution due to their degradation difficulties, the lousy smell in the 
surrounding area and in a danger of spreading pathogens that grow on keratinous waste. 
In the 1980s, waste feathers had been tried to be used in the production of fertilizing 
agents like keratin-bark-urea granulates. Experiments showed that these fertilizer agents affected 
soil properties and some plants positively. However, their microbial activity and nitrogen 
transformation were not equally positive everywhere. Moreover, in some cases, they reduced 
respiratory activities of light soils, caused a disturbance in nitrification and nitrogen losses [2]. 
Application of keratinous waste as fertilizer has been improved by the time. A study 
shows that waste feathers can be treated by aerobic or anaerobic digestion [2]. In figure 2.2, a 
pathway to utilize keratinous waste as biofuel and fertilizer is shown. Biofuel can be produced by 
anaerobic digestion, and keratinous waste can be used to manufacture slow release nitrogenous 
fertilizer for organic farming by aerobic digestion. 
Aerobic digestion involves composting chicken feather together with plant waste rich 
lignocellulosic material which is a biological conversion of keratin waste to a hygienic, stable, 
rich in humus and mature product that can be used to enrich soil properties and nutritional needs 
of plants. In fact, this method has emerged as one of the safest and most cost-effective 
technologies to employ keratinous waste as fertilizer. After application of this fertilizer, it 
enriches the organic C, N-NO3 and S-SO4 content of the soil, and it limits the growth of the 
phytopathogenic fungi Fusarium as well. As a result, it is shown that due to high nitrogen 
content, keratinous waste can be used as economical co-substrate in composing. With the help of

this fertilizer, supporting plant growth, promoting plant-microorganism interaction, improving 
soil texture, raising soil health status and preventing soil erosion can be accomplished [2].

Poultry waste can also be used in bioenergy production. It is possible to produce natural 
gas, methane gas fuel pellets and biohydrogen with the sufficient utilization of keratinolytic 
microorganisms. Keratinases hydrolyze keratin waste. Subsequently, anaerobic digestion is used 
to produce biogas from keratin waste via methane fermentation by methane-producing bacteria 
[2]. The remaining protein-rich feather after biogas production can be used as animal feed. 
Alternatively, it is an option to process the feather meal more to obtain keratinase from it, and the 
further spent keratinous material can act as fertilizer [30]. 
2.2. Keratins, Their Structure, and Properties 
Keratins are highly specialized fibrous proteins known as scleroproteins. They are robust, 
structural proteins with high stability against physical and chemical environmental factors. They 
==PAGE:20==
PROTEIN RECOVERY FROM KERATIN-RICH SLAUGHTERHOUSE WASTE 
 
10 
 
show low solubility in water, weak acids and alkalis and organic solvents and they provide 
protection against common proteolytic enzymes, e.g., papain, trypsin and pepsin [2,4]. The 
solidity of keratins comes from the adaptation of vertebrates to live on the land. Thus, they occur 
abundantly in epithelia such as keratinized epidermis and corneous skin products which are open 
to outside forces that create considerable mechanical stress. They are all produced in epithelial 
cells and are major components of the epidermis and epidermal appendages found in birds, 
reptiles, and mammals, e.g., horns, hooves, feathers, hair, nails, and wool or an outer layer of 
skin. Keratin-filled cells form these keratinous materials which are known as ‘dead tissues’ [17]. 
Keratins are primary components of particularly feathers and hair. In fact, they cover 
approximately 90%, w/w of feathers and hair. Specifically, feathers cover 7-10% of the body 
weight of domestic fowl [9]. Keratins are classified as intermediary filaments and forming 
cytoskeleton. Cytoskeleton can be defined as a microscopic network of protein filaments and 
tubules in the cytoplasm of many living cells, giving them shape and coherence. Therefore, 
intermediary filaments are more rigid than the others. 
The most important feature of keratins which makes them different from other structural 
proteins like collagen or elastin is their high cysteine and disulfide bond content. Disulfide 
cysteine bond content is the thing that makes keratin resistant to enzymatic lysis. There are 
different types of keratins and differences between their disulfide bond content determine how 
resistant keratin is. Characteristics of keratins can be listed as [15]: 
- 
High cysteine content, 
- 
High content of glycine, proline, serine and acidic amino acids, 
- 
Low content of lysine, histidine, and methionine 
- 
The absence of tryptophan. 
==PAGE:21==

Keratin waste is mainly composed of keratinous protein, and these proteins are defined as 
heterogenous proteins because of the amino acid structure and composition they have. Keratins 
are typically being discussed regarding α- and β- keratins. The keratin structure consists of the 
compact packaging of the protein as α-helices (α-keratins, ~ 40-68 kDa) or β-sheets (β-keratins, 
~ 10-22 kDa) into a supercoiled polypeptide chain, which is stabilized by a high degree of cross-
linking, mainly through disulfide bonds, and also hydrophobic interactions [8, 9]. X-ray 
diffraction leads to classify them as α-, β-, feather- and amorphous- pattern because of the 
various percentage of disulfide bonds. The amorphous pattern consists of non-structural keratins 
and is also called γ- pattern [16, 17]. Because the α- and β- patterns dominate the X-ray 
diffraction, and the α-helices and β-sheets are the two-internal supportive structures in proteins, 
they are being used to classify keratins [17]. 
It is also possible to group keratins as hard and soft keratins depending on their cysteine 
content. Epidermal keratins and hair core present a lower cysteine content (~2%), weak cross-
linking and smaller resistance to chemical factors and they are grouped as soft keratins. Hard 
keratins which are also called α-keratins have high cysteine content. Therefore they have high 
resistance to the influence of chemical factors and can be found abundantly in animal remains, 
i.e., animal skin and hair, human hair, horns, or claws [2, 15]. Approximately, the cysteine 
content of hair is 10%, of wool is 17% and of feathers is between 4-8% [1]. 
2.2.1 Alpha-keratins 
Alpha keratin is very important because of the high number of disulfide bonds in its 
structure. Disulfide bonds contribute to alpha keratin’s ability to resist outside effects and make 
alpha keratin insoluble and resistant to enzymatic proteases [2, 8, 9].  When compared to β- and 
γ- types, the α-keratins’ cysteine content is up to 14% to form S-S bonds between cross-linking

Two right-handed α-helices form a left-handed coiled-coil by disulfide cross-links. 
Dimers are placed end-to-end and side-by-side to form a protofilament via disulfide bonds. Two

intermediate filament.

microfibrils are placed on the sides while two microfibrils are placed in the center. Microfibrils 
form macrofibrils. Moreover, both protofibrils and macrofibrils are stabilized by disulfide

matrix proteins to form sulfur-rich amorphous keratin matrix. That matrix contains protein chains

2.2.2 Beta-keratins

glycine, proline, alanine and low amounts of lysine, histidine, and tryptophan [15, 16]. The 
amino acid structure of feathers confirms the general β-keratin structure. When it was 
investigated, it was found that feathers have the most significant amounts of serine, glutamic acid

as the best example of β-keratins [18].

2.3. Keratinases, Their Production, and Application Areas 
2.3.1 Keratinases 
Enzymes are biocatalysts which are being preferred over chemical catalysts in the 
industry because they are considered as part of the green technology [14]. Microbial enzymes 
cover approximately 80% of industrial enzymes, and around 65% of industrial enzymes are 
classified as hydrolases. At this point, proteases take place in hydrolyzing peptide bonds in 
proteins and peptides. Proteases are widely used in food, detergent and leather industries [1]. 
Keratinases are one of the most diverse enzymes regarding their sources and application 
areas. They are proteolytic enzymes that draw increasing biotechnological interest due to their 
applications in keratinous waste management. Because bacteria, actinomycetes, and fungi 
produce keratinases, they are classified as microbial proteases with the capability of releasing the 
peptides by hydrolyzing keratins [3]. In the microbial world, keratin degradation ability can be 
found in many microorganism families, in many places from Antarctic soils to hot springs and 
from aerobic conditions to anaerobic ones. That is why biochemical and biophysical properties 
of microbial keratinases show variety [28]. It was found that due to their broad substrate range, 
keratinases had immense potential in various environmental and biotechnological sectors where 
conventional proteases showed poorer ability [7]. They can hydrolyze both soluble and insoluble 
proteins; soluble proteins like albumin, globulin, hemoglobin, casein and insoluble proteins like 
==PAGE:27==

collagen, elastin, hair, feather and wool keratin. However, general knowledge on keratinases is 
relatively recent when compared to other enzymes and microbial keratinase resources are far 
from being fully explored [33].

2.3.3 Keratinolytic Microorganisms 
To be able to produce keratinases, keratin should be used as the only source of carbon, 
nitrogen, sulfur, and energy. In nature, some bacteria, actinomycetes, fungi, larvae of cloth moth 
and some of the macro-organisms can do that. However, only a few of them are used in industry 
[28]. 
The first class of organisms that can produce keratinases are the fungi. Keratinolytic 
fungi are a nutrient-specialized group, and they utilize keratin as a nutrient-rich source. 
Dermatophytes, non-dermatophytes, eurotiomycetes represented by Chrysosphorium, 
Aspergillus, Trichophyton and Paecilomyces genus, sordariomycetes represented by 
Doratomyces, Myceliopthora, Scopulariopsis and Myrothecium genus, dothideomycetes 
represented by Alternaria can produce keratinases [19, 33]. 
Regarding the bacteria with ability of degrading keratin, recently they have been isolated 
from soil and poultry wastes. Microbial keratinases are mainly produced by Bacillus genera.

Diverse strains of Bacillus are reported to produce keratinases with a wide range of biological

Bacillus licheniformis. Keratinase produced by these bacteria not only adds value to keratin

Actinomycetes are the domain that shows activity on hard keratin such as hair, wool and 
feather shaft. The Streptomyces and Thermoactinomyces groups mainly represent them, and they 
are mostly isolated from plumage, bird feathers and feather waste coming from fermentation and 
composting [2]. A novel actinomycete has been started to be used as a keratinase producer that is 
isolated from soil and baited with sterilized and defatted wool. This keratin-degrading strain is 
named as Amycolatopsis keratiniphila [32]. 
Classifying microorganisms in different ways is possible. The first way is defining their 
keratinolytic capabilities. According to this, microorganisms can be grouped as true and 
potentially keratinolytic. True keratinolytic microorganisms can degrade hard keratins and 
completely solubilize their structure, on the other hand, due to their proteolytic properties 
potentially keratinolytic ones can convert soft keratins or non-keratin proteins which occur

the ones that are produced by Streptomyces pactum, and Fervidobacterium pennavorens which

Another report shows that a mix of proteases which is produced by Streptomyces sp. shows high 
keratinolytic activity [22]. These examples show that a microorganism produces several types of 
enzymes and these enzymes work together to degrade native keratin. That is why the 
keratinolytic activity is affected by the other proteases in the medium [2]. 
The second way to classify the microorganisms is according to their capabilities to use 
keratin as a nutrient source. According to this, it is necessary to use keratin as a nutrient source to 
be able to produce enzymes that can degrade natural keratin. However, it is also possible that 
non-keratinolytic bacteria also can degrade keratin when keratin is denaturalized [2]. 
The third and the most reliable way of classifying keratinolytic strains is looking at the 
loss of substrate mass. For example, to classify fungi species as keratinolytic, in 8 weeks, they 
should be able to degrade more than 40% of the substrate, if they can degrade only 20-40% of 
the substrate they are classified as weakly keratinolytic, and if they degrade less than 20%, they 
are classified as non-keratinolytic fungi [20].

2.3.4 Production of keratinases 
Keratinases are generally extracellular enzymes and microorganisms which produce 
extracellular proteases can be cultured in large quantities through well-established strategies and 
yielding a regular supply of enzymes independently from the area and seasonal impacts [12]. 
Because of the macromolecular characteristics of keratin, microbial cells are not able to absorb 
keratin. Thus, to be able to use keratin as a nutrient source, microbial cells have to produce 
extracellular keratinases [3]. 
Microbial keratinase production is induced when keratin is used as the substrate. 
Therefore, keratin-rich residues are put into cultivation medium. Before putting it into the 
cultivation medium, the keratin-rich material should be pretreated to remove impurities like 
residual flesh, cartilage and blood in feathers or dust and cosmetics in hair coming from 
barbershops. Keratin should be washed to remove dust and impurities; solvent extraction should 
be applied to remove fatty acids, drying and milling should also be applied to bring them to an 
appropriate size that microorganisms can efficiently use. According to the mesophilic character 
of most keratinolytic strains, cultivation temperatures commonly vary from 20°C to 40°C and pH 
values of the culture medium vary from slightly acidic to slightly alkaline conditions which are 
pH 6 and 8 respectively [1]. The production of microbial keratinases can be inhibited by easily 
accessible carbon and energy sources like glucose are available in the medium. The effect can be 
explained as, when there are readily available nutrient sources, microorganisms choose to use 
them instead of keratin [2]. To efficiently produce keratinases, microorganisms should be forced 
to use keratin as a nutrient source by putting them into a medium which lacks of other easily 
==PAGE:33==

accessible carbon and energy sources. However, putting some extra nitrogen sources like urea, 
peptone, tryptone, yeast extract, ammonium chloride and sodium nitrate may increase enzyme 
titer [28]. 
On their active sites, keratinases have serine functional groups acting on peptide bonds. 
Thus, they are classified primarily in the serine- or less frequently in the metallo- endoproteases 
family with 30-50 kDa, showing optimal activity at 40°-80°C and under alkaline conditions with 
pH values that are between 8 and 10 [8, 13, 28]. Presence of cations such as Ca2+ and Mg2+ 
increases keratinase activity because they are required in the stabilization of the enzyme structure 
and during binding to substrate at the active site [28]. 
2.4. The Mechanism of Keratin Degradation 
The mechanism of microbial degradation of keratin is not fully known; only some 
hypotheses have been made and verified so far. The first accepted hypothesis was based on the 
geophilic dermatophyte Microsporum gypsum. A mechanism is proposed for fungal degradation 
of keratin which is divided into two stages; the first one is sulfitolysis and the second one is 
proteolysis. At the first stage, the fungus produces inorganic sulfite, and with the help of it, 
disulfide bonds between polypeptide chains of keratin are separated. A representation of this 
mechanism can be seen in the following scheme [24]: 
cys-SS-cys (cystine) + HSO3                  cySH (cysteine) + cyS.SO3 (S-sulfocysteine) 
The evidence of this hypothesis is the presence of sulfite bound and sulfocystine together 
with the presence of reduced cysteine as products. The sulfitolysis stage disrupts the keratin 
structure and makes it available for the attacks of keratinases. At the second stage, extracellular 
keratinases attack on the disrupted structure to break down keratin proteins to amino acids [24]. 
==PAGE:34==

Fungal keratinases reveal the proper mechanism to degrade complex keratinous substrates that is 
not utilizable for the other bacterial species [28]. 
This hypothesis is corroborated by micromorphological investigations of hair destruction. 
Investigations showed that fungal degradation of native keratin is a combination of enzymatic 
lysis and mechanical destruction [2]. It was proposed that an eroding mycelium complex takes 
part in the sulfitolysis stage with its mechanical impact. These structures which are called frond 
mycelium cause erosion on the substrate’s surface and penetrate inside perpendicularly which 
lead to decomposition of cytoplasmic membrane proteins and make intracellular disulfide bonds 
available for the sulfitolysis stage.

Bacterial degradation of native keratin involves reduction of disulfide bonds and 
enzymatic decomposition of keratin proteins. Bacteria produce two types of enzymes; one is 
disulfide reductase-like protein and serine protease. The following scheme which is obtained by

amino acids and peptides. To degrade keratin, those two enzymes have to work together, due to 
their specialties, and one without the other cannot consume the whole keratin.

2.5. Problem Definition 
The two most promising approaches for valorization of keratin in the current state of the 
art can be identified as microbial fermentation which provides amino acids and the composting 
method which leads to keratin conversion to be used as fertilizer. Apart from these two, keratin 
waste management methods in the current state of the art have some disadvantages that cannot 
be ignored. They can be listed as; their high production costs caused by the requirement of high 
energy investments, their impact on the environment caused by the by-products they produce and 
the losses in amino acid content due to harsh chemical and thermal applications. Moreover, new 
regulations regarding management of category three waste material emphasize the need for 
environmentally friendly technologies. That is why, there is an increasing need for finding an 
alternative way to treat keratin-rich residues which can lower the environmental, financial and 
public health risks [1]. 
Recent studies considered the keratin waste as a possible renewable source for production 
of sustainable materials. In this direction, the enzymatic hydrolysis of keratin waste biomass is a 
conversion process that employs microbial keratinases to decompose keratin-rich substrates. The 
soluble proteins, peptides and free amino acids released during the process are a viable 
alternative protein source to fishmeal for aquaculture. Here, in the light of microbial 
fermentation results, a novel two-stage process can be performed for the conversion of keratin-
rich waste material into a useful protein-rich product. Firstly, a keratin sample is used as a 
bacteria feed to promote the synthesis of microbial keratinases. Here keratin consumption is not 
of interest, and the process stage should be optimized solely for enzyme production and biomass 
growth. Subsequently, keratin hydrolysis may be performed using the enzyme produced in the 
previous stage. The two processes (enzyme synthesis and hydrolysis) are favored in drastically 
==PAGE:37==

different conditions. The two stages can be physically separated using a cross-flow ultrafiltration 
membrane step, with each stage cyclically repeated to achieve semi-continuous operation. 
It is desirable to perform the keratin degradation process at high solids loading to 
maximize product titer and reduce process water consumption, energy usage, and reactor size. To 
date, however, the mechanistic-based rate laws are unavailable, making rational reaction 
engineering for optimizing reactor operations and process design elusive. Therefore, in this 
study, it is desired to investigate the kinetics of enzymatic hydrolysis of keratin-rich waste 
material using a keratinolytic enzymatic cocktail with the filamentous bacterium Amycolatopsis 
keratiniphila D2 (DSM 44409), first reported by Al Musallam et al. [32]. Experimental work was

how long does it take to lose its activity at different temperatures? 
- 
What is the protein yield at different temperatures? 
- 
How is the hydrolysis rate affected by different factors such as different solids 
loading, reaction temperature, and different enzyme concentrations? 
- 
What is the mechanism of keratin degradation? 
- 
Is it possible to combine all this information in a mathematical model?

3. DESCRIPTION OF EXPERIMENTS 
3.1. Materials and Methods 
3.1.1 Reagents 
Pretreated pig bristles from DAKA Denmark A/S were used as a keratin-rich substrate to 
study enzyme kinetics. Tris (hydroxymethyl) aminomethane was used to prepare 50 mM Tris 
buffer to stabilize medium pH and 10% trichloroacetic acid (TCA) was used to stop enzymatic 
reactions completely. PierceTM BCA Protein Assay Kit was used to determine protein content 
after hydrolysis. 
3.1.2 Azokeratin 
A suitable quantity of Pig Bristles and Hoofs Meal was cooled with liquid nitrogen and 
subsequently hand ground with a porcelain mortar and pestle. Afterwards, larger particles were 
separated from smaller ones with sieves of different sizes to obtain a keratin powder with an 
average particle size ≤ 250 µm. The resulting bristle powder was stored at 5 °C until used. 15 
grams of the bristle powder (the keratin source) were placed in a 2-L round-bottomed reaction 
flask (Erlenmeyer flask) with 1 L of deionized water. The suspension was mixed with a magnetic 
stirrer. 100 ml of 10% (w/w) Sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO2) solution was (added with 
stirring) mixed into the bristles suspension. Simultaneously, 8.65 g of Sulfanilic acid were 
dissolved in 200 ml of 2.12 M Sodium hydroxide (NaOH); then 1.7 g of Sodium nitrite (NaNO2) 
were added. The solution was stirred, and 10 ml of 0.5 M HCl were added. The solution was 
stirred again for 2 min, and then 10 ml of NaOH were added while stirring. The reaction mixture 
was stirred for 5 minutes and dialyzed against water at 4 °C. The dialyzed solution was freeze-
dried. The resulting azokeratin is a deep red-orange compound with an absorption maximum at 
440-450 nm.

6. DISCUSSION 
It can be seen from figures 5.4, 5.5 and 5.6, that the proposed model for enzymatic 
hydrolysis of keratinous substrate is able to capture the key behavior that was observed in the 
experiments. When the calculated R-squared values are investigated, it can be said that the model 
fits well to both substrate consumption and protein production data. The data set for 5 w/w % 
solid loading that is not used in the parameter estimation, but used as validation of the model, 
also shows a good agreement between experimental data and the model prediction (figure 5.5). It 
is suggesting that the model can describe the general characteristics of enzymatic hydrolysis of 
keratin at 40 °C with the enzyme produced by Amycolatopsis keratiniphila D2. However, it is not 
good enough to capture the entire phenomena observed in the experiments. Thus, it is suitable to 
be used as a base to understand the keratin hydrolysis process and to be used in further 
improvement and optimization studies. With sufficient improvement, it may be used in batch 
reactor design to achieve the required rate of production by determination of the optimal 
substrate loading, necessary liquid volume and the reaction time at fixed temperature (40 °C) and 
pH (8). The mathematical model may be also used as a simulation platform for further model-
based optimization studies to reduce the cost of keratin hydrolysis. 
It should be noted that in the model development part, the protein/keratin yield (f) for 
each solid loading was assumed to be fixed. However, this assumption provides a fixed 
inhibition constant to be applied in all solid loadings, and the model cannot capture the 
phenomena which were observed in keratin hydrolysis experiments where protein/keratin yield 
improved with increased solid loading. As a result of the fixed parameters, the model under-
predicts the keratin consumption for 3 w/w % solid loading (figure 5.4), gives reasonable results 
for 5 w/w % (figure 5.5), and over predicts for 7 w/w % (figure 5.6). When the values of 
==PAGE:80==
PROTEIN RECOVERY FROM KERATIN-RICH SLAUGHTERHOUSE WASTE 
 
70 
 
protein/keratin yield are uniquely defined for each solid loading according to the experimental 
results, and the inhibition term (KI) is re-estimated separately for each solid loading, serious 
improvements can already be seen in figures 5.7, 5.8 and 5.9. The results of discrete modelling 
show that the both inhibition coefficient and protein/keratin yield increase together with 
increased solid loading. This might show that there is a correlation between the inhibition 
constant and protein/keratin yield, which results in a model parameter identification problem. 
This problem is quite common when performing parameter estimation for this type of processes. 
It is possible to see similar cases, especially in wastewater treatment processes [44]. Thus, it is 
very important to find the mechanism behind improving protein/keratin yield with increased 
initial substrate concentration. If the mechanism can be incorporated into an updated model it 
would be of even more value for accurately representing the system dynamics. 
Another important issue regarding the model can be observed, i.e. it can successfully 
capture the reaction rate within 24 hours, however it is not very good at capturing the low 
reaction rate which starts to be seen after 24 hours. After 24 hours, the reaction rate in the model 
also gets lower, however it is not as low as in the actual hydrolysis and it takes longer in the 
model to reach the point where keratin consumption and protein production reach a plateau. 
An interesting behavior of keratin hydrolysis can be identified as the stagnated keratin 
consumption and protein production despite of the presence of active enzymes after 24 hours. In 
the hydrolysis of cellulose, a reaction system known to have many parallels to keratin hydrolysis, 
it is a well-known fact that cellulose can be classified into easily and difficult hydrolysable 
cellulose and from the empirical data it is possible to calculate their fractions [35]. Also, it may 
be the case in this study. In cellulose studies, it is observed that easily and difficult hydrolysable 
parts show a variation related to enzyme activity [35]. Also, here it is possible to classify keratin 
==PAGE:81==
PROTEIN RECOVERY FROM KERATIN-RICH SLAUGHTERHOUSE WASTE 
 
71 
 
as easily and difficult hydrolysable according to the results of table 4.3. It was observed that the 
fraction of the hydrolyzed part of the keratin substrate decreases with increased solid loadings. It 
shows that the enzyme shows a poorer ability to degrade keratin at higher solid loadings. 
Regarding hydrolysis, negligible factors in low-solid loadings become more prominent in high-
solid loadings condition[42]. The first thing to discuss here might be the mass transfer limitations 
which increase with increased solid loadings. The free water content is very important to perform 
effective hydrolysis since it has a big role to play in mass transfer and lubricity. Presence of 
water increases the effectiveness of enzymatic reactions by providing a medium for solubilizing 
and aiding in the mass transfer of products. At the same time as the free water content of the 
medium increases, the lubricity of particles also increases. A low amount of free water causes 
high viscosity that makes mixing and handling of the material more difficult. Thus, also in this 
study, it is possible that mass transfer between the keratin and enzyme is becoming impeded at 
high substrate concentrations, due to reduced free water content as the keratin holds the water 
more tightly within its pores than the gravitational force acts on it. This situation is limiting the 
diffusion of products away from the enzyme. Moreover, the inhibition effect is not only caused 
by products, but also caused by released inhibitory compounds from the biomass. Therefore, 
limited diffusion of such products away from the enzyme may cause formation of an undesired 
product-enzyme complex instead of a substrate-enzyme complex [45], which decreases 
effectiveness of the enzyme and results in a higher inhibition factor as presented in table 5.3. A 
similar phenomenon is also observed in another cellulose hydrolysis study [43]. 
Another reason for the stagnated keratin consumption and protein production can be 
related to enzymatic decay since both enzyme stability and hydrolysis data show the same 
behavior (figures 4.1, 4.4 and 4.5). Both enzyme activity and hydrolysis data reach a plateau 
==PAGE:82==
PROTEIN RECOVERY FROM KERATIN-RICH SLAUGHTERHOUSE WASTE 
 
72 
 
after about the same amount of time has passed. Keratin degradation by keratinases is still a 
matter of debate as some research groups showed that keratinase alone could bring about 
complete degradation of raw feather [38], whereas other groups showed that keratin degradation 
was initiated and assisted by the disulfide reductase enzyme [39]. In the latter case, bacterial 
degradation of native keratin involves reduction of disulfide bonds and enzymatic decomposition 
of keratin proteins. Bacteria produce two types of enzymes; one is protein-disulfide reductase 
and the other type is serine protease. At the first stage of bacterial degradation of native keratin, 
protein-disulfide reductase takes place and breaks the disulfide bonds to denature the keratin 
structure and make it available for enzymatic lysis. At the second stage, protease decomposes 
keratin and releases amino acids and peptides. To degrade keratin, those two types of enzymes 
have to work together, due to their specific modes of action, and one without the other cannot 
consume whole keratin [2]. As it was stated before, the enzyme produced by Amycolatopsis 
keratiniphila D2 which was used in the hydrolysis experiments is a cocktail that contains 
different types of enzymes. Unfortunately, the exact composition of the cocktail is not known, 
but from the experimental results, it can be interpreted that A. keratiniphila D2 may synthesize at 
least three types of enzymes. The first type is disulfide reductase to make keratin available for 
keratinases, the second type is keratinase to degrade insoluble proteins to soluble proteins and 
the third type is protease for further degradation of soluble proteins to peptides and amino acids. 
All these types of enzymes demand different optimum conditions to operate and show different 
decays. Protein-disulfide reductase is reported to be showing the highest activity between 25 and 
30 °C. According to thermal stability tests it can resist temperatures until 37 °C, however after 
40 °C, it starts to lose its activity rapidly [36, 37]. On the other hand, optimum temperatures for 
keratinases vary between 40 and 80 °C [8] and they start to lose their activities above 50 °C [40, 
==PAGE:83==
PROTEIN RECOVERY FROM KERATIN-RICH SLAUGHTERHOUSE WASTE 
 
73 
 
41]. The protein-disulfate reductases lose their activity at lower temperatures compared to 
keratinases that retain their activities until higher temperatures. Thus, after protein-disulfate 
reductase loses its activity, keratinases cannot consume the non-denaturalized keratin which 
results in stagnation in substrate consumption and protein production. Nevertheless, further 
degradation of soluble proteins results in different protein/keratin yields which is observed in 
figure 4.7. 
Although, the proposed model is good enough to show the main characteristics of keratin 
hydrolysis, more experimental work is needed to update the model to a more accurate one. 
Further experimental work needs to be done to reveal the exact composition of the enzyme 
cocktail and to confirm whether the speculations on the different hydrolysable parts of the 
keratin for different solids loadings and mass transfer limitations are correct. Additionally, 
experiments in the future may involve the investigation of temperature dependence of the 
parameters to facilitate model application for coupled optimization of reactor temperature profile 
and keratin dosage schedule.

7. CONCLUSION 
In today’s world, it is very important to develop environmentally friendly and cost-
effective methods to treat biomass wastes. Regarding treatment of keratin rich waste, methods of 
the current state of the art demand high energy investments, produce undesired by-products and 
prevent keratin valorization by destroying part of its aminoacidic content. The enzymatic 
hydrolysis of keratin rich material is an attractive way to convert abundant keratinous waste 
produced in agroindustrial activities to valuable proteins to be used in industry. In this study, a 
lab scale experimental work on enzymatic hydrolysis with Amycolatopsis keratiniphila D2 has 
been performed to investigate the aspects of the hydrolysis mechanism and to propose the first 
dynamic model for it. The enzyme stability test has shown the mesophilic character of the 
enzyme cocktail while the hydrolysis test at different temperatures has shown better performance 
at 40 °C regarding the enzyme activity. For all initial substrate concentrations an initial fast 
reaction lasting for 4 hours that is followed by a slow reaction has been observed. It is shown 
that the initial fast reaction is dominated by a first order reaction of the enzyme-substrate 
complex and enzyme adsorption. Enzyme activity, Michaelis-Menten kinetics and product 
inhibition is used together to capture the observed behavior of the system in model development. 
The development of such a model is very important to increase the attractiveness of this novel 
method of waste revalorization via computational simulation and optimization of hydrolysis 
reactors. The proposed model is good enough to show the main behavior of enzymatic hydrolysis 
however, assumption of a fixed protein/keratin yield at each solid loading is not able to fully 
capture observed phenomena where protein/keratin yield increases and hydrolyzed fraction of 
the substrate decreases with higher solid loadings. It is concluded that, the proposed model is 
suitable to be used as a base to understand the keratin hydrolysis process and to be used in 
==PAGE:85==
PROTEIN RECOVERY FROM KERATIN-RICH SLAUGHTERHOUSE WASTE 
 
75 
 
further improvement and optimization studies. Further experimental work is suggested to 
increase the accuracy in an updated model by identification of exact composition of the enzyme 
cocktail and confirmation of the speculations on the different hydrolysable parts of the keratin 
for different solids loadings and mass transfer limitations. Also, investigation of the temperature 
dependency of the parameters would be of great value to increase model fidelity for application 
towards dynamically optimizing the keratin hydrolysis reactor.

function xtrans = xtransform(x,params) 
% converts unconstrained variables into their original domains 
  
xtrans = zeros(params.xsize); 
% k allows some variables to be fixed, thus dropped from the 
% optimization. 
k=1; 
for i = 1:params.n 
  switch params.BoundClass(i) 
    case 1 
      % lower bound only 
      xtrans(i) = params.LB(i) + x(k).^2; 
       
      k=k+1; 
    case 2 
      % upper bound only 
      xtrans(i) = params.UB(i) - x(k).^2; 
       
      k=k+1; 
    case 3 
      % lower and upper bounds 
      xtrans(i) = (sin(x(k))+1)/2; 
      xtrans(i) = xtrans(i)*(params.UB(i) - params.LB(i)) + params.LB(i); 
      % just in case of any floating point problems 
      xtrans(i) = max(params.LB(i),min(params.UB(i),xtrans(i))); 
       
      k=k+1; 
    case 4 
      % fixed variable, bounds are equal, set it at either bound 
      xtrans(i) = params.LB(i); 
    case 0 
      % unconstrained variable. 
      xtrans(i) = x(k); 
       
      k=k+1; 
  end 
end 
  
end % sub function xtransform end

function [ dC_dt ] = Model(t,C,Parameters) 
  
K  = C(1); % Keratin Concentration (g/L) 
P  = C(2); % Protein Concentration (g/L) 
Vmax= Parameters(1); %Maximum reaction velocity (g/L/h) 
kM  = Parameters(2); %Michaelis-Menten constant (g/L) 
eia = Parameters(3); %Initial relative activity of the enzymes affected by 
reaction temperature (%) 
kdsa = Parameters(4); %Enzyme deactivation constant of fast reaction (1/h) 
eib  = Parameters(5); %Initial relative activity of the enzymes not affected 
by reaction temperature (%) 
IC  = Parameters(6); %Non-competitive inhibition constant (g/L) 
fA  = Parameters(7); %Enzyme adsorption fraction 
kdsb  = Parameters(8); %Enzyme deactivation constant of slow reaction (1/h) 
R = Parameters(9); %Product yield (g/g) 
  
E =fA*(eia*exp(-kdsa*t)+eib*exp(-kdsb*t)); 
r= (Vmax/(1+P/IC))*(K/(kM*(1+P/IC)+K)); 
  
dC_dt(1) = -r*E; %Keratin consumption         
dC_dt(2) =R*(r*E); %Protein production 
dC_dt = dC_dt'; 
end

%

% with the variability of the original values. RSQUARE also outputs the 
% root mean squared error (RMSE) for the user's convenience. 
% 
% Note: RSQUARE ignores comparisons involving NaN values. 
%  
% INPUTS 
%   Y       : Actual data 
%   F       : Model fit
