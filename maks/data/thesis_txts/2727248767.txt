Abstract
Understanding behavior in a dynamic context is essential for predicting, intervening, and
making informed decisions. Travel behavior analysis helps to understand peopleÔÇÖs travel
habits and the factors that influence their decisions. While previous studies have exam┬¡
ined travel behavior based on specific transport choices, grouping individuals based on
multiple characteristics could provide a more comprehensive understanding. This work
explores the use of topic modeling, a machine learning tool to aggregate high dimen┬¡
sional data, and identify underlying patterns by finding topics ÔÇØtopicsÔÇØ in travel behavior
using multiyear household travel diary data from Denmark.
This study is exploratory, it aims to determine whether topic modeling can give new in┬¡
sights to travel behavior analysis. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) and dynamic topic
modeling (DTM), a static and a dynamic model, are tested. While LDA assumes that the
topics are constant across all times, DTM consider the ÔÇØevolution of topics over timeÔÇØ.
The studyÔÇÖs findings demonstrate that the tested topic modeling methods can effectively
analyze multiyear household travel diary data and provide meaningful topics that can
contribute to further research. Both of LDA and DTM resulted in meaningful topics that
consist of socioeconomic and travel attributes. Comparing the two models, LDA offers
more interpretable topics, while DTM exhibits greater coherence and robustness.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Understanding behavior in a dynamic context refers to the ability to comprehend how indi┬¡
viduals or systems behave and interact with their environment over time, considering the
evolving circumstances and changing conditions. It allows to make accurate predictions,
design effective interventions, and make policies based on informed decisions (Borysov
and Rich 2021). Travel behavior analysis is an important focus of studies that helps to un┬¡
derstand peopleÔÇÖs travel habitat and the factors that influence their decisions. There have
been several studies conducted focusing on the changing patterns of travel behavior.
A research have shown that people who cycle share certain characteristics, like pref┬¡
erence for shorter travel distances, a desire for convenient and safe cycling infrastruc┬¡
ture, and a willingness to endure some discomfort or inconvenience in exchange for the
health and environmental benefits of cycling (Buehler and Pucher 2012). This example
demonstrates that by identifying these characteristics, policies and infrastructure can be
developed that are better suited for the needs of cyclists.
However, grouping people based on the choice of transport may not give a full picture of
someoneÔÇÖs travel behaviour, therefore, it could be beneficial to find groups among people
based on more than one characteristics. A possible tool for finding underlying patterns
that can also aggregate high dimensional data could be topic modeling which is a widely
used machine learning tool for finding ÔÇØtopicsÔÇØ in discrete data (H. U. Asuncion, A. U.
Asuncion, and Taylor 2010).
By creating travel diaries of households and applying topic modeling on the data, a more
comprehensive understanding of the activities and preferences of travelers can be pro┬¡
vided by identifying common themes and patterns. Secondly, the factors that influence
travel behavior can be uncovered, like trip purpose or mode of transportation. Finally,
new groups of people can be identified with similar transport choices and preferences
that have not been recognized before.
Using dynamic topic models have the advantage of considering the time element, in other
words, the changes in topics over time (Blei and Lafferty 2006). By applying it to house┬¡

1.2
Objectives and research questions
The aim of this study is to test whether topic models could provide a more comprehensive
understanding of household travel behavior by using dynamic topic models and LDA.
In the existing literature, there are no studies that have used topic modeling on cross┬¡
sectional panel travel data before. Therefore, this study is exploratory, it aims to test a
new method to determine whether topic modeling can give new insights to travel behavior
analysis. Furthermore, most works focus on a single attribute e.g. car ownership and
analyse it across different groups. By analyzing multiyear travel diary data, trends and
patterns in household travel behavior can be uncovered, and the factors that influence
travel decisions can be identified, like trip purpose, travel mode, and demographic char┬¡
acteristics of the household members. The insights gained from this analysis could be
useful for the development of transportation policies and services so they better meet the
needs of households and promote sustainable travel behavior. Overall, this study seeks
to answer the question: how can dynamic topic models and LDA be used to analyze mul┬¡
tiyear household travel diary data, and what insights can be gained into household travel
behavior including patterns, trends and different topics?
The outline of the thesis consists of the followings. Section 2 covers the overview of
studies focusing on travel behavior changes. Section 3 is the methodology. Section 4
covers the details the data preprocessing and provides information about the data itself.
In Section 5 the results of the topic models are presented, and they are discussed in
Section 6 while also touching upon the limitations. Furthermore, the conclusion of the
thesis is presented.
1.3
Scope and Limitations
The data used for the analysis comes from the Danish National Travel Survey (TU, ver┬¡
sion: TU0621v1) covering the period from May 2006 to December 2021. It is an interview
survey whose purpose is to map the travel behavior of the Danish population. The survey
tries to clarify the following five central questions: how much, how, where, when, and why
people travel? It is based on an interview with one person covering questions of transport
and activities of the household during one day.

Literature Review
2.1
Analysing dynamic behaviour
Panel methods and pseudo┬¡panel methods are the most common ways to analyse dy┬¡
namic behavior (Kitamura 1990═¥ Deaton 1985). Panel data is also known as longitudinal
data, refers to the repeated observation that is collected over time from multiple units or
individuals. Panel data analysis is a statistical method that is used to investigate both
cross┬¡sectional and time┬¡series data (Frees et al. 2004). Panel data can be particularly
useful due its characteristics that it allows researchers to study changes over time within
a population while accounting for individual┬¡level factors that may influence the outcome.
However, it also comes with challenges: the respondents can get bored of the tasks re┬¡
lated to the survey and the quality of their responses drops (Borysov and Rich 2021). In┬¡
dividuals with certain characteristics are more likely to stop participating than others, and
this can cause bias in the survey results. Due all these reasons collecting longitudinal
data is expensive and there is also a limitation on the number of recurring respondents.
The will to overcome the challenges around the collection of longitudinal data led to in┬¡
creased attraction towards pseudo┬¡panel methods that come from repeated cross┬¡sectional
data (Borysov and Rich 2021). Pseudo panels involve aggregating data into cohorts, as┬¡
suming representative individuals within each cohort. However, creating cohorts requires
a trade┬¡off between the number of individuals within each cohort and the number of co┬¡
horts. Pseudo panels have been used in economic and transportation studies. Despite
overcoming the difficulties around data collection, these methods also have disadvan┬¡
tages. The quality of these types of data is less reliable since it involves aggregation
during the creation of cohorts or representative individuals, unlike longitudinal data that
consist of different, distinguishable parts or elements.
2.2
National travel surveys
National Travel Surveys are a unique data source that offer several advantages (Carrone,
Monteiro, and Rich 2021). First of all, they provide a way to access panel data with fraction
of the original cost that retains all the attributes of a regular cross┬¡sectional travel survey.
This is particularly advantageous as panel data allows for the examination of individual┬¡

Secondly, due the irregularly spaced observations, they are also representative (Carrone,
Monteiro, and Rich 2021). The panel composition is based on randomly selected individu┬¡

population.
Moreover, the irregular spacing of panel observations indirectly introduces heterogeneity

Understanding travel behavior is critical for developing effective transportation policies,
reducing traffic congestion, promoting sustainable transportation options, and improving
the overall quality of life.
Studying the dynamics of behavioral preferences in any field including transportation has
implications for both models and predictions, as well as policy design and its intended
targets (Borysov and Rich 2021). By research in this area different groups of people can
be identified who may be more likely to change their behavior, and this can highlight po┬¡

such changes may be more challenging to achieve.

3.1
Latent Dirichlet Allocation
LDA is a flexible generative probabilistic model that can be seen as a technique for dimen┬¡
sionality reduction of discrete data (Blei, Ng, and Jordan 2003). There are three stages of
the data structure in LDA: document, topic and word (unit of discrete data). A document
refers to a piece of text or a data sample that we aim to analyze using LDA e.g. article,
online review etc. (Vayansky and Kumar 2020). In the context of LDA, a document is
treated as a collection of words or tokens (Blei, Ng, and Jordan 2003). A topic represents
a theme or a concept that captures the underlying meaning or content of a set of words.
In LDA, each document is assumed to be generated from a mixture of topics, and each
word in the document is associated with one particular topic. Topics are represented as
probability distributions over words, indicating the likelihood of each word occurring in a
given topic. A word represents the unit of discrete data present in the documents. In LDA,
words are the basic building blocks that are used to construct documents. Each word in a
document is assumed to be generated from one of the topics present in the document and
their number is fixed for all documents. The distribution of words across topics provides
insights into the latent structure of the documents.

topic distribution and ╬▓ that controls the sparsity of the topic┬¡word distribution (Wang,
Feng, and Dai 2018). The generative process of LDA consists of the following steps

3.2
Dynamic topic modeling
Traditional approaches, like to above mentioned LDA, treat all documents within a cor┬¡
pus interchangeable, neglecting the temporal dynamics that can significantly impact the
topics (Vayansky and Kumar 2020═¥ Blei, Ng, and Jordan 2003). To address this lim┬¡
itation, dynamic topic models (DTM) were introduced which consider the ÔÇØevolution of
topics over timeÔÇØ (Blei and Lafferty 2006). Dynamic topic modeling is a technique for an┬¡
alyzing discrete data that evolves over time. By capturing the changing nature of topics
and uncovering temporal patterns in large corpora, it provides valuable insights beyond
traditional static topic modeling approaches. By incorporating temporal dynamics, DTM
provides a more nuanced understanding of topic evolution and enables the exploration of
shifting themes within a corpus (Vayansky and Kumar 2020). This is particularly crucial
when analyzing datasets spanning long periods, where topics can vary significantly due to
societal changes, scientific advancements, or cultural shifts. For example, by analysing
science articles its effectiveness was demonstrated in capturing temporal patterns that
e.g. the topic Atomic physics has different most popular words: the word matterÔÇÖs proba┬¡
bility gradually decreased over time while quantumÔÇÖs increased (Blei and Lafferty 2006).
By modeling the evolution of topics and their prevalence in different time periods, dynamic
topic modeling reveals shifts and patterns within discrete data over time.

not suitable for sequential modeling. Therefore, the natural parameters of each topic,
denoted as ╬▓t,k, are chained in a state space model that evolves with Gaussian noise.

Discussion
6.1
Interpretation of the results
The best LDA model had seven topics. Topic 1 includes households with child(ren), no
business┬¡related travel, and a lack of public transportation and bicycle usage. They pri┬¡
marily rely on private vehicles for their daily transportation needs. This topic is in line with
findings of previous studies that suggest that people are likely to get their first car when
there is an addition to the family (Khan and Habib 2021). Topic 2 appears to represent
households with couples and a lack of travel associated with work or school commitments.
These households typically have one car available and rely on passenger cars as their
primary mode of transportation throughout the day. Topic 3 focuses on travel patterns
in the Hovedstadskommuner (Capital Region) and highlights the characteristics of urban
areas with a high population density. The presence of long trip duration, relatively short
distance to the nearest station align with the commuting patterns and lifestyle in major
cities. Households here mainly consist of young adults who have no car. Topic 4 in┬¡
dicates a strong presence of bicycles as the primary mode of transport among student,
especially in Aarhus. It is interesting that these household typically have three or more
cars, but the distance taken by car is 0. Topic 5 reflects longer duration trips, covering
distances exceeding 60 km and taking more than 80 minutes. This suggests a focus on
travel activities associated with leisure and potentially includes multiple trips throughout
the day within Odense. It is interesting that travel time due to work or school is not a
significant factor in this topic. Topic 6 is about passenger car trips. The drivers and pas┬¡
sengers are usually family members or friends, and the purpose indicates that these trips
often revolve around school or educational institutions. This topic definitely could be a
focus of an analysis whether other types of transports are available here or what option
would make them change their habits since the distance with car is relatively short. Topic
7 emphasizes walking as the primary mode of transportation, particularly for distances
under 5 km. This topic aligns with the concept of walkability and suggests a preference
for shorter walking trips, potentially for leisure activities.
The DTM with nine topics was the most coherent. Majority of the wordsÔÇÖ probabilities do
not change significantly over time as it can be seen on Figure 5.4. Topic 1 seems to

6.2
Future research
Integration of post┬¡pandemic data: As mentioned, incorporating data from the post┬¡
pandemic period would provide insights into how travel behavior has been impacted by the
COVID┬¡19 pandemic. Analyzing the changes in topics and travel patterns during and after
the pandemic can reveal the long┬¡term effects on transportation choices and preferences.
Exploration of BERT topics: BERT is a natural language processing model that can
capture intricate semantic relationships in text data. Applying BERT┬¡based topic modeling
techniques to the household data can potentially yield more nuanced and contextually rich
topics, enhancing the understanding of travel behavior.
Investigation of a larger number of topics: The thesis discussed the analysis with
seven topics in LDA and nine topics in DTM. Expanding the number of topics could pro┬¡
vide a finer┬¡grained characterization of different household profiles and travel patterns.
By increasing the granularity, researchers can capture more specific behaviors and po┬¡
tentially identify previously unnoticed trends or subgroups within the data.
Comparison of LDA, DTM, and other topic modeling techniques: Alongside LDA and
DTM, future research can explore other topic modeling algorithms and compare their per┬¡

formance in analyzing household data. Evaluating the effectiveness and interpretability
of different models, such as Non┬¡negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) or Correlated Topic
Model (CTM) which uses the correlation between topics to help its predictions, can pro┬¡

6.3
Conclusion
In conclusion, the analysis of multi┬¡year household data using dynamic topic modeling
provided valuable insights into the travel behavior and characteristics of different house┬¡
hold types. The best LDA model identified seven topics, while the most coherent DTM
model had nine topics.
The LDA modelÔÇÖs topics revealed distinct patterns related to households with children,
couples, urban areas, students, leisure activities, school┬¡related trips, and walking as
the primary mode of transportation. These topics provided insights into the preferences,
habits, and travel patterns of various household types. Similarly, the DTM modelÔÇÖs top┬¡
ics highlighted different household profiles, including those with younger/older children,
couples with a single car, commuters, cyclists, carpooling families, urban dwellers, school┬¡
related trips, long┬¡distance trips, and walkers.
Comparing the two models, LDA proved to be more interpretable, allowing for easier
distinction and interpretation of topics. On the other hand, DTM models demonstrated
greater robustness in terms of coherence, even when the number of topics changed.
However, DTM models required more computational power.
This thesis proved that topic modeling methods such as LDA and DTM can be used to
aggregate high dimensional data and they also provide meaningful topics that can be the
foundation of other travel behavior related researches.
Overall, this thesis expands our knowledge of travel behavior analysis and provides a new
tool for future research.
