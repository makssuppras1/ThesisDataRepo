Abstract
The aim of this project is to evaluate two prototypes of ﬁlter technologies for cleaning
of ﬂue gas. Filter 1 is an electrostatic precipitation (ESP) ﬁlter operated together
with a chimney fan. Filter 2 is a bag ﬁlter system where the ﬂue gas is cooled down
before ﬁltration. The ﬂue gas is drawn through the bag ﬁlter by a pressure blower
placed on the top of the chimney.
Each ﬁlter was evaluated based on their removal of particulate matter (PM), el-
emental carbon (EC) and particle number, and a potential for future implementa-
tion. The ﬁlters were tested through successive test cycles. Particulate matter of
a size below 2.5 µm (PM2.5) was collected on quartz ﬁlters in a cyclone through 30
min. The quarts ﬁlters were subsequently analysed for organic carbon (OC) and
EC. The particle number was sampled continuously and evaluated for the ignition
period (0-10 min after reload), the intermediate period (10-40 min after reload) and
the combined period (0-40 min). For Filter 1 sampling of PM2.5 and particle number
was done in a dilution tunnel. Particle number was sampled by a P-Trak, a SMPS
and a MiniDiSC. A multi-function ﬂue gas analyser W¨ohler was sampling PM in the
hot ﬂue gas. Filter 1 was tested on a modern stove. For Filter 2 PM2.5 and particle
number was sampled before and after the bag ﬁlter. Particle number was sampled
with a P-Trak. Filter 2 was tested on an stove from before 2003.
The tests showed a PM2.5 removal of 87 % for Filter 1 and 94 % for Filter 2. For
Filter 1 PM sampling in the hot ﬂue gas showed a PM removal of 67 %. EC and OC
was removed by respectively 98.6 % and 74 % for Filter 1, and 99.6 % and 85 % for
Filter 2. The measured particle number for Filter 1 showed a large diﬀerence for P-
Trak, SMPS and MiniDiSC. The ESP ﬁlter changes the physical-chemical properties
of the particles, which may aﬀect the operation mode for some of the instruments.
Further more the MiniDiSC was operated out of its detection limits and the results
are therefore not valid. In the ignition period the SMPS data showed removal ef-
ﬁciency of particle number of 66 %. In contrary the P-Trak measured a particle
number increase of 91 %. In the intermediate period the particle removal was 97
% for SMPS and 86 % for P-Trak. For the combined period a removal of particle
number was not statistically proved for the P-Trak data, while the eﬃciency was 75
% for SMPS. The mean particle size decreased from 98 nm to 74 nm when the ESP
ﬁlter was turned on. For Filter 2 a particle number removal eﬃciency above 98.5
% was observed for both ignition and intermediate period. The ﬁnal product for
both Filter 1 and Filter 2 will be a user-friendly automatic cleaning system. Filter

1 will be able to remove particles from a good combustion while Filter 2 is thought
as an ”end of pipe” solution that will be able to remove particles from all types of
stoves. The ﬁnal price of the ﬁlters is expected to be 20,000 DKr for Filter 1 and
10,000-30,000 DKr for Filter 2. The cost of the ﬁlters is found to be an essential
barrier for an implementation of the ﬁlters on wood stoves in Denmark without any
governmental action.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A recent report from DCE - Danish Centre for Environment and Energy determine
yet again the negative impacts of air pollution in Denmark and the increasing part
that originate from wood stoves. The annual premature deaths in Denmark caused
by wood stoves (incl.
kettles and boilers) were revised upwards to 550 persons
(Brandt et al. 2016). The report conﬁrms what has been known for years; Emissions
from wood stoves aﬀect the human health and have been linked to both acute
health impacts and chronic diseases (Sigsgaard et al. 2015). One of the important
pollutants from wood stoves is black carbon (BC) which additionally to its negative
health impacts has been characterised as the second most important anthropogenic
emission contributing to global warming next to CO2 (Bond et al. 2013).
The
750,000 wood stoves in Denmark (Hansen 2015a) accounts for 64 % of the Danish
emission of particulate matter below 2.5 µm (PM2.5) (Olesen et al. 2010) and 62
% of the Danish BC emission (Winther & Nielsen 2011). The PM emission for 1
kg wood burned in a wood stove is 500 times higher than 1 kg wood burned in a
wood-ﬁred power plant (Olesen et al. 2010). The report from DCE hits an ongoing
heated debate concerning the future of wood stoves in Denmark. One of the latest
suggestions comes from the Danish Environmental Economic Council who propose
diﬀerentiated taxes on the use of wood stoves dependent on the geographic location
(urban/countryside) (Det Miljøøkonomiske R˚ad 2016). The Danish Parliament has
until now rejected motions for taxes on wood stoves by reference to a reduction
of emissions through better information to the users and new low-emission stoves
(Gjerskov 2006, Auken & Torp 2012, Clausen et al. 2015). Even thought the Danish
Environmental Protection Agency introduced a scrapping scheme in November 2015
to speed up the replacement of old high-emission stoves, it will take several years
before all the old stoves have been replaced (Hansen 2015a, Miljøstyrelsen 2016)
and modern low-emission stoves will still contribute to particle pollution (Olesen
et al. 2010, Seljeskog et al. 2013). A particle ﬁlter for ﬁltration of ﬂue gas from
wood stoves may be the solution. Until now ﬁlters for wood stoves have generally
been found to have a low particle removal and operation problems (Obernberger &
Mandl 2011, Schleicher et al. 2011). But this may change now. Two Danish ﬁlter
producers are working to overcome these problems and have come up with their
individual suggestion on how to solve challenges of air pollution from wood stoves.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.0.1
Planning of the project
The basis of this project is a pre-project with the aim to do initial investigation of
two Danish ﬁlter technologies under development. These ﬁlters were tested for their
ability to remove ultraﬁne particles. While one of the ﬁlters had little or no removal,
the other ﬁlter showed a good potential for further investigations (Drastrup 2016).
It was decided to continue with the ﬁlter that indicated a removal (Filter 2). My
attention was directed to another ﬁlter under development and it was included in the
project (Filter 1). A third ﬁlter under development in Germany was also suggested
but they were not interested in participating in the project. In the end the subject
was conﬁned to testing and evaluation of Filter 1 and Filter 2. The report is set up
so it can be used in various ways; As one project evaluating two ﬁlter technologies
or as two independent evaluations of Filter 1 and Filter 2. In this way the ﬁlter
producers have an individual evaluation for future use.
1.0.2
Aim of the project
The aim of the project is to test two ﬁlters for their ability to clean ﬂue gas from wood
stoves. The ﬁlters, Filter 1 and Filter 2, are both prototypes under development by
two Danish producers and are based on two diﬀerent technologies. The ﬁlters will be
tested for their ability to remove particulate matter, ultraﬁne particles and elemental
carbon (soot).
Based on their removal eﬃciency, price and user-friendliness an
assessment of the two ﬁlters is made. The evaluation of the ﬁlters removal eﬃciency
will be based on tests, while the ﬁlters price and usability will be assessed based on
the information from the producers and literature.
The following criteria have been set in order to evaluate each of the ﬁlters:
• A removal eﬃciency above 90 % for particulate matter
• A removal eﬃciency above 90 % for particle number
• A removal eﬃciency above 90 % for elemental carbon
• The ﬁlter must be easy to handle for a user, possible to install on most wood
stoves and at a cost of maximum 10,000 DKr if mass produced.

2.1
Air pollution
2.1.1
Particle emission
Particle emission in regard to air pollution are normally referred to as particulate
matter (PM). Particulate matter are divided into size and refereed to as coarse
particles; PM10 - particles with an aerodynamic diameter ≤10 µm, ﬁne particles;
PM2.5 - particles with an aerodynamic diameter ≤2.5 µm and ultraﬁne particles;
PM0.1 - particles with an aerodynamic diameter ≤0.1 µm. Particle emission from
wood stoves are dominated by particles in the size range of 46-300 nm (Kinsey et al.
2009, Pettersson et al. 2011).
Particulate matter is a very unspeciﬁc term that
include all kinds of particles; primary and secondary particles, solids and liquids,
particles with diﬀerent shapes and chemical composition. Historically particles have
been measured as a mass by ﬁlter collection and minor attention has been put on
the chemical composition (Colls 2002). When measuring particle emission by mass
another important factor is overlooked. Ultraﬁne particles have a very small mass
and they are not possible to measure as a weight on a ﬁlter. As observed on the
ﬁgure 2.1 the emission of ultraﬁne particles can be high in number but neglectable
in mass. As an example one coarse particle at 10 µm has the same mass as one
million ultraﬁne particle at 0.1 µm (Fenger & Tjell 2009). Particles are regarded as
an important pollutant in relation to both acute and chronic health eﬀects (Fenger
& Tjell 2009). The characteristics of the particles seem to have a large impact on
the health eﬀects. The toxicity is dependent on the deposition of the particles which
is highly dependent on the size, shape and density, but also the physico-chemical
properties (Bølling et al. 2009). There are limited knowledge of real life exposure
from PM which makes it complicated to evaluate the precise health eﬀects (Sigsgaard
et al. 2015). However, several studies links high levels of PM pollution from wood
burning with respiratory symptoms, decreased lung function and cardiovascular and
respiratory mortality (Sigsgaard et al. 2015). Large particle PM10 are deposited in
the upper airways like nose and throat, while PM0.1 can reach the smallest and
deepest parts of the lungs and be taken up by the blood. These properties make
the ultraﬁne particles suspected of the most important in relation to health, even

Primary and secondary particles
The particles emitted from combustion are characterized as primary and secondary
particles. Primary particles are formed directly from the combustion and emitted
into the atmosphere (Reddington et al. 2011). Secondary particles are formed from
organic gaseous compounds (OGC) that are emitted together with the primary
particles. The transformation processes are many and complex but they generally
happens because of oxidation and condensation due to dilution and cooling in the
atmosphere (Colls 2002).
In the atmosphere the particles do not only exist in two separated groups but often
as a mixtures where the primary particles are coated with secondary particles. The
particles undergo an altering and ageing process in the atmosphere which includes
coagulation with other particles, oxidation and restructuring (Zelenay et al. 2011).
2.1.2
Volatile organic compounds
Volatile organic compounds (VOC), non-methane volatile organic compounds (NMVOC),
semi-volatile organic compounds (SVOC) and organic gaseous carbon (OGC) are
overlapping concepts for gasses formed as part of the combustion.
VOC from
wood combustion includes carbonyls, alcohols, esters, ethers, aromatics, alkanes
and alkenes, while SVOC includes PAH (Lim et al. 2015). Pettersson et al. (2011)
found that the VOC emission have a large variation dependent on the stove and com-
bustion conditions. Methane had a variation of 9-1600 mg/MJfuel while NMVOC

2.1.3
Elemental carbon, organic carbon and black carbon
The carbon found in combustion particles are deﬁned as either elemental carbon
(EC) or organic carbon (OC). Elemental carbon is the chemical term for soot and
the right deﬁnition is ”thermally-refractory pure carbon with a graphite structure”
(Chow et al. 2009). Organic carbon is the fraction of carbon which is not elemental
carbon (Bond et al. 2004). The term black carbon (BC) is also used for soot and
covers the term ”highly light-absorbing carbon” (Chow et al. 2009).
The clear
deﬁnition between BC and EC is based on the method of measuring, but the two
terms are used interrelated, even though BC may contain some OC (Seljeskog et al.
2013). EC does not exist in pure form in the atmosphere but is likely to be coated
with organic material which may vary widely and be commonly refereed to as soot
(Poulsen 2015). The amount of OC can be expected to increase with increasing PM
emissions (Seljeskog et al. 2013).
As the name indicate black carbon is black and with that follows some highly prob-
lematic properties. Recently BC have been found to be the second largest anthro-
pogenic contributor to global climate change (Bond et al. 2013). Basically BC aﬀect
the climate in three ways: 1. Direct radiative forcing where BC absorb sunlight
in the atmosphere, which heat the air and decrease the amount of sunlight that is
reﬂected back to space. 2. Deposition on snow and ice which reduce the reﬂective
albedo eﬀect, improve the absorption of sunlight and as a consequence heat the
ice. 3. Inﬂuencing cloud formation and properties in several complex and not fully
understood ways. While the ﬁrst and second eﬀect from BC have a clear warming
eﬀect on the climate, it is still unclear if the 3. eﬀect has a net cooling or warming
eﬀect (Bond et al. 2013). Black carbon is emitted in combustion processes and the
four largest global sources are open burning, diesel engines, industrial coal and res-
idential solid fuels. Residential wood burning contribute in the last category (Bond
et al. 2013).
2.1.4
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) constitute a large group of chemical com-
pounds with 2 to 7 aromatic rings. (Kim et al. 2013). Like many other problematic
compounds from wood stoves, PAH’s are formed during incomplete combustion.
There is a big variation in the PAH emission dependent on the wood type, ﬁring
habits and stove. There seems to be a correlation between PM and PAH emission,
where high PM emissions also induce high PAH emissions (Glasius et al. 2005). The
combustion temperature inﬂuence the size of the PAH molecules: At high tempera-
ture larger PAH molecules are formed while lower temperatures induce smaller PAH
molecules (Orasche et al. 2013). Firing with trash wood give a high PAH emission
(Glasius et al. 2005). PAH’s are problematic because they pose both acute and long-
term risk to the human health. Some PAH’s are well known to pose a health risk
and have carcinogenic, mutagenic and embryotoxic eﬀects. Due to their properties

It is reported that residential wood burning is responsible for up to 90 % of the

2.1.5
Carbon monoxide
Carbon monoxide (CO) is produced during combustion of organic matter. Indoor
leaching of ﬂue gas from wood stoves can cause carbon monoxide poisoning which
can lead to acute toxicity and death. When CO is inhaled, it passes through the
lungs and binds to haemoglobin which inhibits the transport of oxygen in the blood.
CO is odour and colour-less and a toxic level due to leakage can be hard to detect.
The symptoms for carbon monoxide poisoning are headache, dizziness and reduced
mental performance (Dalhoﬀ2012).
2.1.6
Nitric oxide and nitrogen dioxide
The emission of NOx, which is the common name for nitrogen oxide (NO) and nitro-
gen dioxide (NO2) is often related to emission from motor vehicles, but residential
wood burning is also contributing to the emission. NOx is formed at high temper-
atures in combustion processes as an endothermic reaction between nitrogen and
oxygen in the air. NOx are mostly formed as NO during the combustion, while NO2
are formed in the atmosphere in reaction with ozone and NO. NOx emission can also
be formed from nitrogen in the fuel. Exposure to increased levels of NOx are related
to negative health impacts. Direct eﬀects have been seen as increased incidence of
respiratory illness and damages on the lung tissue (Colls 2002). NOx also impacts
the environment by acting as a fertilizer which can leads to eutrophication (Fenger
& Tjell 2009).
2.1.7
Dioxins
Dioxin is not a single molecule but a range of compounds in the chemical groups poly-
chlorinated dibenzodioxins (PCDD) and polychlorinated dibenzofurans (PCDF).
Dioxins are formed during combustion processes and can therefore be found in ﬂue
gas and ash. (Bossi & Glasius 2006, Lim et al. 2015). The emission has a high vari-
ation depending on the stove, ﬁring habits and fuel. Firing with impregnated wood
leads to a high emission of dioxins. It is illegal to ﬁre with impregnated wood in
Denmark (Glasius et al. 2005). In 2005 it was estimated that a yearly dioxin emis-
sion of 2-4 I-TEQ is caused by wood stoves in Denmark (I-TEQ is an International
Toxic Equivalent factor for dioxins). Old wood stoves have a tendency of higher
emission than newer stoves (Glasius et al. 2005). Dioxins are considered as persis-
tent in the environment. Their lipophilic properties means that they accumulate
in biota and biomagniﬁcate in the food chain. Dioxins are suspected of endocrine
disrupting properties, causing cancer and birth defects (Bossi & Glasius 2006).

2.2. WOOD COMBUSTION AND EMISSION FACTORS
2.2
Wood combustion and emission factors
A combustion process of biomass like wood can be divided into three processes:
Drying, pyrolysis and combustion.
In the ﬁrst phase moisture is evaporated at
>100 ◦C. The evaporation consumes energy from the combustion, which lower the
temperature, and slow down the combustion. During pyrolysis the volatile com-
pounds of the fuel is released. The combustion is an exothermic reaction where the
volatile compounds are oxidized by O2 from the air. The products are CO2, H2O
and heat. During a complete combustion all carbon are oxidized and converted to
carbon dioxide, here shown by the reaction of the simplest hydrocarbon methane
(Lackner 2011):
CH4 + 2O2 ⇒CO2 + 2H2O
(2.1)
If there is insuﬃcient air supply the combustion will become incomplete. However, in
reality it is not possible to obtain a complete combustion. Carbon monoxide (CO)
which is a product of incomplete combustion, is always released from a biomass
combustion. The same applies for pollutants like PAH and soot particles (Bølling
et al. 2009). The right amount of air is very important for the combustion in a wood
stove. The diﬀerent factors that aﬀect the air supply and quality of a combustion
are listed below:
Air supply The air supply are both dependent on the type of wood stove and
the user. Modern stoves have normally three diﬀerent air systems; Primary,
secondary and tertiary air supply, see ﬁgure 2.2. The burn rate is regulated
by the primary air supply, while secondary air is supplied after the primary
combustion zone to burn gases (van Loo & Koppejan 2008). A constant supply
of tertiary air helps the combustion. Turbulence of the ﬂue gases are important
to secure suﬃcient mixing of the gases and a better combustion (Burkhard &
Russell 2010).
Moisture The moisture content of the wood have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the
emission factor. Both PM, OC and PAH emissions increase with increasing
moisture of the wood (>14 %) (Shen et al. 2013). A high moisture content
also decreases the eﬃciency because energy is used for evaporation (van Loo &
Koppejan 2008).
Type of wood Two studies investigated the impact of wood type. Spruce and pine
was found to release higher PM emission than beech wood (Orasche et al. 2013,
Vicente et al. 2015).
Log size The log size and shape have an inﬂuence: Non-split wood have a higher
PM10 emission than split wood and a decreasing surface area will increase the
PM emission (Vicente et al. 2015).
Ignition The ignition phase have a big impact on the emission level: Danish EPA
recommend to light up the ﬁre at the top instead of traditionally at the bottom
because it can decrease the PM emission concentration between 50-80 % in the
start up phase (Nussbaumer et al. 2008, Vicente et al. 2015). Thus Vicente et al.

2.3.1
Norwegian particle test method
The Norwegian test method is described in NS 3058-1:1994 and 3058-2:1994. The
dust test requires a dilution tunnel which captures all ﬂue gas from the chimney.
The dilution tunnel cools down the ﬂue gas and mix it with clean air. The PM
sampling is done by leading a small fraction of the ﬂue gas through a sampling train
with two ﬁlters in series. The temperature between the two ﬁlters must not exceed
35 ◦C. This means that the sampling includes secondary particle. The result of the
test is stated in the unit g/kg dry wood (Norsk Standard 1994b). The standard
requires air-dried spruce with a moisture between 16-20 % and the test are repeated
at four diﬀerent burn rates (Norsk Standard 1994a).
2.3.2
Austrian and German particle test method
The PM emission can be tested according to the test method described in the
standard EN 13240:2001, and sampling method described in Annex A of standard
DS/CEN/TS 15883:2009. EN 13240 addresses the performance, safety, eﬃciency
and CO emission.
According to the testing method in EN 13240 the stove is preheated and pretested
before the real testing starts. The test consist of three reloads. The stove should
be operated according to the instruction from the producer and the draught should
be 12 Pa (Dansk Standard 2003, Seljeskog et al. 2013). The PM is sampled directly
in the hot ﬂue gas. Sampling starts 3 min after reload and last 30 minutes. The
temperature in the measuring area must be kept constant at 70 ◦C. The sampling
must be designed so the ﬁltrated air volume is 270 ± 13.5 litres during the 30
minutes. The result of the test is given in mg/Nm3 based on the weight of the ﬁlter

2.3.3
Future EU emission test method
It has been acknowledged in the EU that the diﬀerent methods for standard testing
have created conﬂicting results and unreliable information for the consumer. A Joint
Research group was established in 2012 by the European Committee for Standard-
ization (CEN) with the goal to come up with a: ”Common European method for the
determination of particulate matter emissions of solid fuel burning appliances and
boilers (EN-PME-TEST)”. The aim of the project was to develop a common method
to measure particulate matter emission for use in the EU. The projects Position Pa-
per from December 2015 shows that the group choose the measurement technique
where PM and OGC are measured in the hot ﬂue gas. The OGC measurements
will give an indication of the combustion quality and the secondary particles that
might be formed later. The reported PM emission will still be declared in mg/Nm3
and holds no measurements of particle number (Fraboulet 2016). This measure-
ment technique means that ultraﬁne particles remain an untested parameter. The
method is part of the standard EN 16510 which currently are in a hearing in the
EU Commission (Smit 2015).
2.3.4
Challenges in relation to test methods
As described in section 2.2 several factors inﬂuence the measured PM emission from
wood stoves. One of the most important factors is the method of testing (Fenger &
Tjell 2009). Comparing the two above methods where sampling is done in the hot
ﬂue gas (section 2.3.2) and in a dilution tunnel (section 2.3.1) may give some very
diﬀerent results. Because a fraction of the PM is formed during condensation and
dilution (section 2.1.1) sampling in the hot ﬂue gas may underestimate the total
PM pollution (Seljeskog et al. 2013). The ratio of PM in dilution tunnel and PM
in the hot ﬂue gas changes dependent on the combustion condition and there is no
clear relation between the result of the two methods (Andersen 2006, Lim et al.
2015). Additionally, it is widely acknowledged that standard testing in a laboratory
do not give a realistic picture of the true emission from wood stoves (Shen et al.
2013, Durand et al. 2014).
The laboratory test are done under very controlled
conditions, which does not reﬂect the use in real life. The standards do not include
the PM emission during the ﬁrst ignition which can be very high for a cold stove
(Nussbaumer et al. 2008). It is however very costly and time-consuming to do life
testing in the ﬁeld (Schleicher et al. 2011).

2.4. SAMPLING OF PARTICLES
2.4
Sampling of particles
Sampling of particles can be done in various ways dependent on the aim of the
measurements (particle number/ concentration) and conditions (in a laboratory/
ﬁeld test). Diﬀerent sampling and dilution instruments which are used for particle
sampling and analysing of the particulate matter are described in the following
section.
2.4.1
Sampling probes
There are several factors to take into account when sampling particles in a duct; the
sampling ﬂow, material of the probes and the probes position in the duct. As the
particle distribution may vary over the cross section in the duct, the probes must
be placed in the middle of the duct (Fenger & Tjell 2009).
Isokinetic sampling
The ﬂow in the duct compared to the sampled ﬂow will determine if the ﬂow is sam-
pling isokinetic, ”under sampling” or ”over sampling”. When sampling for particles a
non-isokinetic sampling may lead to overestimation or underestimation see ﬁgure 2.3
(Fenger & Tjell 2009). The error of sampling non-isokinetic decrease with the size
of the particles and when particle size is less than 1 µm the importance of isokinetic
sampling can be neglected, see ﬁgure 2.5 (HEI Dekati Ltd 2010, Fraboulet 2016).
Additionally the eﬀect of the probes orientation can be neglected for measurements
of particles in the size range from a few tenths to a few hundred nm (backwards
sampling (0◦)) (Tsuji et al. 2001, Wohlen 2008).
2.4.2

isopropyl. The vapour condenses on the particles so they grow into larger droplets
before they pass a laser beam, see ﬁgure 2.6. The particles makes the beam ﬂash,
which are sensed by a photo detector and the particle concentration is determined

assessments at workplaces, because it is portable and easy to use (TSI 2012b).

2.4.3
Particulate matter and particle analysis
OCEC Dual Optics Lab Instrument
An OCEC Dual Optics Lab Instrument from Sunset Laboratory analyse ﬁlter sam-
ples for elemental carbon (EC) and organic carbon (OC). The instrument uses a
thermal-optical technique and the analyse is done in two steps, ﬁrst for OC and
then for EC (Birch & Cary 1996). From a quartz ﬁlter a small 1.5 cm3 standard
ﬁlter sample is squared, and placed in a quartz oven. In the ﬁrst step the oven is
purged with helium and the temperature increases stepwise to 870 ◦C. The high
temperature makes the organic compounds thermally desorbe from the ﬁlter. The
carbon fragments ﬂow into a manganese dioxide (MnO2) oxidizing oven where they
are converted to CO2. Subsequent the gas ﬂows through a heated nickel catalyst
where it is converted to methane. The methane is measured by a ﬂame ionization
detector (FID). Afterwards the temperature decreases to 550 ◦C and the second step
begins. Instead of pure helium the carrier gas is switched to a mixture of helium and
oxygen. The mixture gas oxidise the elemental carbon oﬀthe ﬁlter and again the
carbon is oxidized to CO2 and methane and detected by a FID (Sunset Laboratory
Inc. n.d.). See ﬁgure 2.9. During the ﬁrst step some of the organic carbon can form
elemental carbon during the pyrolysis. Consequently the result will under-estimate
the organic carbon and over-estimate the elemental carbon. To correct for this mis-
take the samples absorption of light is continuously measured by a red light laser
and a beam recorder. Elemental carbon have a strong light absorbing property, so
any decrease of the light beam during the ﬁrst analyse will be recorded. Finally the
FID data and the laser beam data is compared and it is possible to calculated the
pyrolyticaly formed elemental carbon (Sunset Laboratory Inc. n.d.). Figure 2.10
show the temperature ladder from the analysing program ’Carbon Analyser’. The
principle of a FID is that burning of an organic molecule will form a number of
ions, which can be detected as a ion current if the ﬂame burns in an electric ﬁeld
(Brunner 1993). The instrument can detect OC in the range of 0.01 - 600 µg/cm2,
and EC in the range of 0.01 - 20 µg/cm2.

2.4.4
Dilution of raw ﬂue gas
The ﬂue gas sampled by a particle sensor is often diluted in a dilution tunnel or
other dilution instruments. The dilution is done to overcome two challenges:
1: The particle level is so high that is will exceed the detection level of the particle
sensor.
2: The water vapour will condensate inside the sample tubes or inside the instru-
ments.
Temperature and concentration determines a gasses dew point and it can be con-
trolled by dilution and heating. Dilution of ﬂue gas before cooling will avoid con-
densation of water vapour which is preferable for particle measurements (TSI 2009).
Ejector Diluter
The ejector diluter is a simple dilution instrument which only needs a stable supply
of compressed air. The diluter has a small nozzle which is placed in the exhaust gas,
a connection probe for the compressed air and two sampling probes to withdraw
samples.
The principle of dilution is based on negative pressure caused by the
compressed dilution air. The pressure will draw a gas ﬂow into the diluter chamber
through the nozzle, see ﬁgure 2.11. The ﬂow through the exhaust pipe is determined
by the ﬂow of the dilution air; if the dilution air ﬂow increases, the pressure will
increase and the sampling ﬂow will increase. The dilution ratio is dependent on the
pressure of dilution air and a stable air ﬂow ensures a stable dilution ratio (HEI
Dekati Ltd 2010). The dilution ratio is also inﬂuenced by the length of the nozzle
into the diluter chamber; the deeper into the dilution chamber, the higher dilution.
The dilution ratio can be determined by sampling of a gas from raw and diluted
gas. If the inlet pressure is varying it is recommended to check the dilution ratio
during the measurements (HEI Dekati Ltd 2010). A test for Dekati diluter found
that particle losses inside the diluter of particles less than 1 µm was negligible (HEI
Dekati Ltd 2010). To make sure that the dilution air does not contaminate the test,
a ﬁlter must be installed between the compressed air and the ejector diluter.

2.5. FILTER TECHNOLOGIES FOR WOOD STOVES
2.5
Filter technologies for wood stoves
Technologies for ﬂue gas cleaning are present and have been developed for several
decades (Biondo & Marten 1977, Schmidt 2008). Industry in Denmark are restricted
in their emission of air pollutants and are obligated to use ﬂue gas cleaning tech-
nologies (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 2012, Miljøministeriet 2016). Technologies
for industrial ﬂue gas cleaning includes cyclones which uses the centrifugal force to
separate dust, wet scrubbers where water are sprayed on the ﬂue gas, bag ﬁltration,
electrostatic precipitation, and catalytic incinerators (Nesaratnam & Taherzadeh
2014). But these technologies are very expensive and mostly developed for indus-
trial use (ANDRITZ AG 2014, G¨otaverken Milj¨o AB 2016, Jernforsen 2016). The
challenge is to downscale the technology for residential use and at an aﬀordable
price.
The technologies that have been used for cleaning of ﬂue gas from wood
stoves are presented below.
2.5.1
Electrostatic precipitators
Electrostatic precipitators (ESP) are one of the main particle reduction technologies
on the market (Obernberger & Mandl 2011). The principle behind electrostatic pre-
cipitation involves three steps: charging of particles, transportation and collection
on an electrode. The ﬂue gas passes an area with an electric ﬁeld that is made
between an emission electrode and a collection electrode. Ions will attach to the
particles and give them negative charge. The electric force will then transport them
to the positive collection electrode, see ﬁgure 2.13. ESP ﬁlters can also work in the
opposite electric ﬁeld where the collection electrode is negative (Lin et al. 2012).
The ESP operate at diﬀerent voltage depending on the electrode and type of gas.
Electrostatic precipitation has been used since the early 20th century, the system is
widely used for dust control and ﬂue gas cleaning in the industry (Schmidt 2008).
The technology is also used for indoor air cleaning in homes (Vu et al. 2011, In-
spiredLiving.com 2016). When applied for wood stoves the ESP is placed as part of
the chimney or at the top of the chimney. A typical construction of the ESP is one or
several thin metal sticks mounted vertical in the chimney as the emission electrode.
A metal pipe mounted at the inside of the chimney is the collection electrode, see
ﬁgure 2.14 (Oekosolve n.d., Hansen 2015b). The deposited particles will accumulate
in the chimney and will eventually need a cleaning. How often cleaning is needed
is unclear but the suggestions vary from every 100 h, to every second month or
annually (Schleicher et al. 2011, Obernberger & Mandl 2011, Oekosolve n.d.).

In a catalytic system the partially oxidised exhaust gas from combustion is con-
verted. This could be CO to CO2 or NO2 to N2. The catalyst change the transfor-
mation rate such that processes that would require higher temperature will happen
at lower temperatures (Lackner 2011). In US there is a market for catalytic wood
stoves where the catalyst is integrated in the stove. In this system the ﬂue gas is
passed through a coated ceramic honeycomb inside the combustion chamber (US

There are no Danish produced ﬁlter systems for wood stoves on the market. One
Danish retailer sell ESP ﬁlters but only one ﬁlter was sold in the last 5 years
(Kirkegaard 2016, R¨uegg Denmark 2016). A survey that investigated the European
market for wood heating technology in Austria, Germany, Denmark, Norway and
Sweden in 2010 found that the commercial technologies on the market were electro-
static precipitators (ESP), scrubber systems combined with condensation (Burkhard
& Russell 2010) and catalytic ﬁlters. At least four ESP ﬁlters for residential wood
stoves from German and Swiss producers are on the European market (Oekosolve
n.d., R¨uegg Denmark 2016, Spartherm 2016, Schr¨ader Abgastechnologie n.d.). Three
and possible more catalytic converters are sold in Europe (Ecolink Solutions 2015,
Caminos 2015, Obernberger & Mandl 2011). A new ceramic ﬁlter technology has
been launched by the German company Hark (Hark 2016) and a similar by a Nether-
landic company Interfocos (Interfocos 2016). A foam ceramic ﬁlter is integrated in
the stove and should be able to improve the combustion and reduce the particle

4.1
Description of the ﬁlter
Filter 1 is a ﬁlter prototype developed by the company PHX-innovation.
PHX-
innovation was founded in 2012 by PHX Holding A/S with the purpose of develop-
ing technologies to reduce particle emissions from biomass combustion. The sister
company is Exodraft which develop and sell chimney fans. Exodraft will be respon-
sible for marketing and sales when the ﬁlter is ready. PHX-innovation is ﬁnanced
by Peter Hermansen, Managing Director for the company. The company is situated
in Langeskov, Funen and besides Peter Hermansen are 3 people employed (PHX-
innovation 2016).
Filter design
The ﬁlter is an electrostatic precipitator (ESP). An ESP ﬁlter can de designed in
various ways but the exact ﬁlter design is not included in this paper. A chimney
fan is mounted on top of the ﬁlter to insure a suﬃcient draught. The concept for
the product is a ﬁnal unit that includes an ESP ﬁlter and a chimney fan.
Handling and maintenance of the ﬁlter
The description of the ﬁlter is based on conversation with Peter Hermansen and
product developers Mikkel Pedersen and Per Holm Hansen. The ﬁnal prototype
has not been made yet and the details are not settled. It is therefore important to
emphasize that the ﬁnal product can change before it reaches the market. The foun-
dation for the use of the ﬁlter is a good combustion. According to PHX-innovation’s
own surveys the ﬁlter is not capable of cleaning ﬂue gas from bad combustion caused
by over-load, wet wood or low air supply. The premise for the performance of the
ﬁlter is good practise and the user must demonstrate good handling before a good
result is guaranteed.
PHX-innovation’s assessment is that the ﬁlter needs to be cleaned daily. The idea
is that this will either happen automaticly ones a day at a time where the ﬁlter is
cold or be user-facilitated by a press on a button. The cleaning will then last 1/2
- 1 min and is not planned to require further involvement from the user. The ash
tray will need emptying occasionally. PHX-innovation expect that blocking of the
ﬁlter is not possible because the dust will fall oﬀbefore a blocking could build up.
In case of power failure the ESP ﬁlter and the chimney fan would stop working but
the ﬂue gas would still have free access through the chimney. The expected lifespan
of the ﬁlter is 15-20 years.
Price
Currently the expected price of the ﬁlter is 20,000 DKr, but PHX-innovation hope
that they will be able to provide a cheaper product. The power consumption of the
ESP ﬁlter and the chimney fan is around 60 Watt in total. That will give a monthly
cost below 20 DKr at a daily use of 5 hours (based on a price of 1.98 DKr/kWh)
(Strøm 2015). The price of installation is unknown.

Method
The ﬁlter test was done in PHX-innovation’s test laboratory in Langeskov at Funen.
The test period was 1. June - 10. June 2016. At the test site a dilution tunnel
was installed according to the Norwegian Standard. Concentration of particulate

gas analyser W¨ohler. Particle number was continuously counted by the use of a
P-TRAK (model 8525 TSI), MiniDiSC (Testo), and a SMPS (model 3910 TSI).
The SMPS and the MiniDiSC did also measure the particle size distribution. Flue
gas temperature and level of CO, CO2 and O2 were continuously measured by a
ﬂue gas analyse. The ﬂow in the dilution tunnel was measured by a MFS Flow
Sensor. The testing was done in collaboration with Mikkel Pedersen, employee at
PHX-innovation. During the test I was responsible for ﬁring, sampling with P-Trak
and the cyclone, while Mikkel took care of the ESP ﬁlter, SMPS, MiniDiSC, W¨ohler

Test set-up
The ESP ﬁlter was installed on top of the chimney of the test-stove. A chimney
fan was installed on top of the ﬁlter, which was turned on during all tests, both

into consideration. The intake of the dilution tunnel was placed above the chimney

from the laboratory that contained background pollution. The ﬂue gas analysing

the pump, see ﬁgure 4.3.

4.2.2
Test procedure
Firing
Before the test started the stove was heated up by ﬁring approximately 1
kg of small wood logs. At ignition, a 2-5 cm ash layer was present in the ﬁrebox.
During start up the CO2 level rose above 10 % and then decreased as the logs were
burning out. When the CO2 level reached 2.8 % the stove was reloaded with 2 logs
of a joint weight of 1.7 kg ± 100 g of beech wood. The ﬁrst test cycle started exactly
at the time of reload. One cycle lasted until the CO2-level again had reached 2.8
%. 4-5 reloads were reached in one day. The two logs were placed in the bottom of
the ﬁrebox next to each other with a few centimetres gap between. Before the stove
door was opened for reload the primary and secondary air supplies were opened.
During the ﬁrst 5 min of each test cycle the ﬁre was carefully observed and the air
supply was controlled in order to start a gently ignition. When the ﬁre was stable
the primary air supply was closed and the secondary air supply was set at 80 %.
The restocking process was based on guidance from Mikkel Pedersen and the stove
manual (Varde Ovne 2014).
Sampling procedure for particulate matter
PM2.5 was collected on quartz ﬁlters for
each test by the cyclone. Sampling started 3 min after a reload and the sampling
time was 30 min. Two ﬁlters were placed in the cyclone, a preﬁlter and a back-
ﬁlter.
The ﬂow of ﬂue gas through the ﬁlters was 4 l/min (volume of 0.12 m3)
per ﬁlter sample. The multi-functional ﬂue gas analyser W¨ohler was sampling PM
continuously throughout the day.
Sampling procedure of particle number
P-Trak, SMPS and MiniDiSC were measur-
ing throughout the entire day of testing. The P-Trak sampling interval was 1 sec.,
the MiniDiSC sampling interval was 10 sec., while SMPS sampling interval was 1
min.

Statistics
The removal of the parameters of interest was evaluated based on the following
statistical hypothesis:
H0 : Ux −Fx = 0
H1 : Ux −Fx > 0
Where Ux is the mean value of unﬁltered samples, Fx is the mean value of ﬁltered

groups (ﬁltered/unﬁltered) are independent and with diﬀerent variance (Johnson
et al. 2011).

can be rejected, if the p-value is above 0.05 H0 is maintained. A sample standard

Discussion
4.4.1
Result of the test
Particulate matter
The removal eﬃciencies obtained by ﬁlter collection of particulate matter with cy-
clone, show a PM2.5 removal of 87 % and a total carbon (TC) removal of 93 %.
The highest removal is observed for elemental carbon (EC) which show a removal of
98.6 %. The results for PM2.5 and the fraction of inorganic matter and water have
a large uncertainty because of the damage of some of the quartz ﬁlters. The PM
collection by the multi-function ﬂue gas analyser W¨ohler show a removal eﬃciency
of PM of 67-69 %. This diﬀerence in PM removal between cyclone and W¨ohler may
be due to the fact that W¨ohler sampled in the hot ﬂue gas and the cyclone sampled
in the dilution tunnel. It is assumed that only primary particles are present in the
hot ﬂue gas while sampling in the dilution tunnel will include secondary particles.
The PM concentration from the stove (without ESP) was 3.7 mg/Nm3 for W¨ohler
and 72.3 mg/m3 for cyclone. The PM for cyclone is 5 times higher which indicate a
high production of secondary particles in the dilution tunnel. The PM concentration
sampled by W¨ohler is normalized to 1 atm and 0 ◦C what the concentration from the
cyclone is not. This is however not estimated to have a big impact in the result. It is
expected that all particle emission were smaller than 2.5 µm. The PM concentration
in the hot ﬂue gas (13.7 mg/Nm3) was low compared with the reported emission
value for the stove (21 mg/Nm3 (Varde Ovne 2014)) and the emission limit value (40
mg/Nm3 (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 2015)). This indicate that the tests were
based on good combustions. The PM2.5 removal eﬃciency is oplifting compared with
the ﬁrst ﬁlter test from the Danish EPA 2011 (Schleicher et al. 2011). In that study
three ESP ﬁlters were tested using a dilution tunnel. No PM removal was found,
but some removal of soot was observed. The obtained removal eﬃciency for Filter 1
is more alligned with some of the results from the second ﬁlter test from the Danish
EPA (Hansen 2015b). Hansen (2015b) measured PM for three diﬀerent ESP ﬁlters
and observed a removal eﬃciency of respectively 52 %, 10 % and 92 % tested with a
chimney fan, and 89 %, 37 % and 79 % tested without a chimney fan. Obernberger
& Mandl (2011) summaries the removal eﬃciencies of ESP ﬁlters on the European
market and report removal of PM1 emission up to 97.5 % during testing. However,
large variations were observed for the diﬀerent products.
Particle number and size distribution
Particle number
Particle number was measured with three diﬀerent particle sensors.
The ﬁnal removal eﬃciency for the three sensors are not aligned and it is not possible
to get a common conclusion. In the ignition period after reload a high particle peak
is observed for the ﬁltered samples for all the instruments. This means that in the
ignition period the particle emission is so high that the ESP ﬁlter can not remove
the particles suﬃciently. The SMPS and MiniDiSC data show a reduced removal
of 66 % and 25 %. The result from the MiniDiSC is however not valid because the
52

particle load was exceeding the detection limit of the instrument. The SMPS result
is excluding two very high measurements test no F-1 and F-2. If they are included
the removal in the ignition period decrease to 27 %. On the contrary the P-Trak
data show a particle increase of 91 % in the ignition period. In the intermediate
period (Time 10-40) the data from the instruments are aligned and show removal

showed that the peak of ignition period had decreased after 10 min. A total time of
40 min was chosen because some of the tests did not have more data than 40 min.
A longer time period (f.x. Time 0-50 min) would have changed the overall removal
eﬃciency for the period of the entire cycle.
Particle size
SMPS measured particles of 13 diﬀerent sizes grops but only the mean
and median size were evaluated. MiniDiSC did also measure mean particle size but
the data are less truth worthy and will not be evaluated further. Initially the mean
size was measured based on the same time intervals as the particle number but it
was observed from the size plots that for the unﬁltered samples the size decreased
around 40 min after reload. One additional time period was therefore investigated
(Time 40-55). For the unﬁltered samples the mean size were stable during the ﬁrst
40 min (96-99 nm) and then decreased to 69 nm in the last part of a cycle which
is assumed to be the time where the ﬂames went out. For the ﬁltered samples the
mean size were stable for the entire cycle (74-82 nm) unless in the ignition period
where the size decreased to 57 nm. The median particle size showed the same trend.
The size diﬀerence for the ﬁltered/unﬁltered samples show that the ESP is slightly
better at removing larger particles.
The decrease in the ignition period for the
ﬁltered samples may be due to a change in operation of the ESP, a higher particle
load or a generation of nanoparticles (see section below). It can be excluded that the
combustion change the size distribution during ignition. Compared to other stydies
the particle size are in the low range of the particle size reported from Pettersson
et al. (2011) (100-300 nm measured with SMPS) but above the size reported from
Kinsey et al. (2009) for a good combustion (46-55 nm). Based on the result it is
assessed that measuring in the size range 10-420 nm (SMPS) will account the largest
fraction of the emitted particles. Even though a fraction of the particles were above
420 nm the ultraﬁne particles is expected to exceed in numbers, and would not
inﬂuence the result.

A test of ﬂue gas cleaning technologies
carried out for the Danish EPA tested three electrostatic precipitators. Two of the
ESP ﬁlters indicated an increase in particles numbers, while the third had a slight
decrease. One of the ﬁlters did also have an increase of NMVOC. An additional
test for one of the ESP ﬁlters showed a change in particle size distribution when
the ﬁlter was turned on. The smallest group of particles 7-100 nm had an increase
when the ﬁlter was turned on. The measurements were done with an electrical low
pressure impactor (ELPI), which has a similar operation as the SMPS (Schleicher

Increase of ultraﬁne particles measured by the P-Trak
The particle measurements
in Schleicher et al. (2011) was done in a dilution tunnel like the present study. One
hypothesis provided by Schleicher et al. (2011) for the observed increase in parti-
cles is that condensable gases normally condense on larger particles during cooling.
When the large particles have been removed by the ESP the gasses prefer to con-
dense into smaller particles (Schleicher et al. 2011). This also explains the decrease
in mean size for the ﬁltered samples. This hypothesis do however not explain why
the increased particle number for Filter 1 only are observed in the ignition period.
Another hypothesis for the observed particle increase during ignition is that nanopar-
ticles are generated because of ozone formation in the ESP ﬁlter. Some electrostatic
precipitators can generate ozone because of the corona discharge which is present
in an ESP. The amount of ozone depends on the voltage, ﬂow velocity and the
design of the electrode (Yasumoto et al. 2010, Liu et al. 2012, Lin et al. 2012).
It is reported that some ESP ﬁlters have the ability to generate nanoparticles un-
der certain conditions (Lin et al. 2012, Poppendieck et al. 2014). Lin et al. (2012)
measured aerosols penetration in an ESP. The penetration exceeded 100 % of the
inﬂowing aerosols (made in an aerosol generator) which proved that some particles
were produced by the ESP. Subsequently the ESP was tested with incoming air
which had been through a HEPA ﬁlter. A CPC detected nanoparticles from the
ESP, which immediately decreased to zero when the ESP ﬁlter was turned oﬀ. The
generated particles were in the size range of 5-40 nm and the concentration varied
with ﬂow, temperature and electric ﬁeld strength. There was an indication that the
nanoparticles could be captured by the ESP itself depended on the operation(Lin
et al. 2012). VOCs emitted from the wood logs can form secondary aerosol particles
through oxidation with ozone (Vu et al. 2011, Lamorena et al. 2007). Since the
big increase in particle number only were observed in the ignition period it could
relate to a similar high increase of VOC. Additional test of the variation of VOC
emission may reveal if that could be the explanation. Ozone formation of the ESP
ﬁlter should be investigated by measuring the ozone concentration before and after
the ESP. Lin et al. (2012) measured that the ozone concentration increased with
increasing current. If ozone are produced from Filter 1 and the current is higher in
the ignition period due to a high load of emission the explanation of the particle
number increase is maybe found.

Comparison of the particle sensors
A comparison of the three instruments shows that they all have the same tendency
but a large diﬀerent in particle count. Except for one period the MiniDiSC have
the highest particle number. This diﬀerence may be due to a particle loss in the
sampling tubes and Particle Diluter, which means that the SMPS and P-Trak have
a lower particle number. The MiniDiSC was measuring directly in the dilution tun-
nel which meant that part of the time the emission exceeded the detection range of
the MiniDiSC. It is more likely that the overestimation are due to an overload of
particles and an inaccurately measuring technique (up to 30 %). P-Trak and SMPS
samples at two diﬀerent intervals, respectively 1 sample per sec. and 1 sample per
minute. This means that a high peak of ultraﬁne particles during 30 sec. could be
overlooked by SMPS while measuring in the large particle range. But the sampling
interval can not explain for the diﬀerence in P-Trak and SMPS data. As observed in
the result section, ﬁgure 4.18 and 4.19 the P-Trak samples a consistently higher par-
ticle number for the ﬁltered samples while the SMPS samples a consistently higher
number for the unﬁltered samples.
The diﬀerence in particle number for the data from the two instruments is quite
crucial for the conclusion.
Which of the instruments that give the correct data
will determine if the ESP-ﬁlter is producing more ultraﬁne particle or not. The
diﬀerence can not be explained by the change in particle size. The SMPS have a
lowest detection limit which means that the SMPS should estimate more particle
for the ﬁltered samples which have a smaller size. It is however the opposite which
is observed. The diﬀerence must therefore be due to a change in particle properties.
The particles which penetrate the ESP are diﬀerent from the unﬁltered particles in
at least two ways: The particles chemical properties are changed because all soot
(98.6 % of EC) are removed from the ﬂue gas. The particles physical properties are
changed because the particles are charged by the ESP. This particle change leads to
a changed relation of the two instruments. Four hypotheses have been obtained in
order to explain the changed relation of the SMPS and the P-Trak data:
1. The two sampling tubes from the Particle Diluter to the two instruments were
of diﬀerent type. The charged particles were lost in the tube connected to the
SMPS. The particle loss of charged particles were not examined for any of the
tubes.
2. The P-Trak was not able to measure the true number for particles with changed
properties, which led to an overestimation of the particles number in the ﬁltered
ﬂue gas. This would make sense since P-Trak is original made for indoor air
quality and the producer specify that P-Trak is not the best instrument for
combustion/emission research (TSI 2015). The condensation operation mode
of the P-Trak may be inﬂuenced by the particles’ changed chemical properties.
3. The SMPS was not able to measure the true number for particles with changed
properties, which led to an underestimation of the particles number in the
ﬁltered ﬂue gas. The operation principle of the SMPS includes charging of
particles. It could be possible that the particle count is interfered by already

Removal of pollutants
The ﬁltration of the ﬂue gas by the ESP happens at temperatures around 200 ◦C
which means that only primary particles are removed. Little or no transformation
of gases to secondary particles are expected to happen before the ESP ﬁlter. The
ﬁlter will therefore only partly be able to remove the particle pollution that are
caused by residential wood burning. The ESP is not expected to remove VOC. If
the increased ”missing mass” observed for W¨ohler analysis is semi-volatile matter
it is an indication of higher emission of VOC from the ESP ﬁlter. PAH is normally
related to soot particles so a reductive of EC is also expected to reduce the PAH
emission.
However the ﬁlter test from the Danish EPA (Schleicher et al. 2011)
saw a diﬀerent result. PAH were measured for two samples from each ﬁlter which
means that the result was very uncertain. The ESP ﬁlter with highest PM and soot
reduction had nearly double as high PAH emission than the reference stove/boiler
(Schleicher et al. 2011). A possible increase of PAH emission for the ESP should
therefore be tested. Filter 1 is not assumed to have a positive impact in the NOx
emission. A possible ozone formation may promote a transformation of NO to NO2.

clogging of ESP-ﬁlters have not been found in literature. In case of power failure or
failure of the ESP or chimney fan, the ﬂue gas will still be able to enter though the
chimney. Obernberger & Mandl (2011) report spark-over during testing of two ESP
ﬁlters but that was not observed during the present test and is not assessed to be a

chimney. Possible it is only the chimney fan that will be visible.

4.5
Conclusion
Filter 1 is a prototype of an electrostatic precipitation (ESP) ﬁlter produced for
ﬂue gas cleaning from wood stoves. The particle removal eﬃciency of Filter 1 was
tested by measurements of the ﬂue gas in a dilution tunnel with and without ESP
ﬁltration. Filter 1 had a high removal eﬃciency of particulate matter. For ﬁlter
collection by a cyclone the results were; PM2.5: 87 %, TC: 93 %, EC: 98.6 % and
OC: 74 %. The PM samples collected by a multi-function ﬂue gas analyser W¨ohler
showed a reduction of 67 % for mg/Nm3. In relation to particle number a clear
particle removal is not obtained for Filter 1. In the ignition period from reload
and for the ﬁrst 10 min a 90.5 % increase of particles were detected by the P-Trak,
while the SMPS and MiniDiSC showed a particle removal of respectively 66 % and
25 %. In the intermediate period in the time 10-40 min after reload the particle
removal eﬃciencies are above 86 % for all three instruments. However the overall
result varies from no statistically signiﬁcant removal for P-Trak data respectively
66 % for MiniDiSC and 81 % for SMPS. The MiniDiSC was sampling out of it
detection range and the results are therefore presented to show a trend but the
removal eﬃciencies are not considered as valid. The conclusion is that Filter 1 have
a high particle removal eﬃciency in the intermediate period, while the removal is
reduced or negative in the ignition period. A decrease of the mean particle size
for the ﬁltered samples measured by the SMPS and MiniDiSC show that the ESP
is better at removing larger particles. A comparison of SMPS and P-Trak data
showed that the relation in particle number for the two instruments changed when
the particles had been through the ESP. This is assessed to be due to a chemical and
physical change of the particles which either cause of high particle loss in the tubes
or interferes with one or both of the instruments sampling mode. Filter 1 ﬁltrate the
ﬂue gas around 200 ◦C which means that only primary particles are removed. The
producer estimate that the price will become around 20,000 DKr. An automatic
cleaning system will make the product user-friendly. The ESP might only be able
to remove PM emission from a good combustion from newer stoves.
Further work
It should be tested if the ESP ﬁlter release ultraﬁne particles. Testing with other
CPCs might reveal this. A possible ozone formation of the ESP ﬁlter should also
be tested. Additionally it should be tested if the SMPS and P-Trak are aﬀected
by the physical-chemical change in particle properties which an ESP ﬁlter promote.
The removal of PAH should be tested to see if the ﬁlter have a positive or negative
eﬀect of PAH release. The durability of the ﬁnal product should be tested to see
the performance in the long run.

5.1. DESCRIPTION OF THE FILTER
5.1
Description of the ﬁlter
Filter 2 is a new invented ﬁlter prototype based on well known technologies. The
ﬁlter is invented and build by Tonny Sander Holm, a retired engineer who worked
with industrial ﬂue gas cleaning in his professional life. The ﬁlter is an ”end of
pipe” solution and the idea is that it can be installed on both existing and new
wood stoves. The system is not fully developed, but the ﬁlter part is working and
ready for testing. The prototype is set up with the wood stove inside a garage and
the chimney outside. All information about Filter 2 is based on conversation with
Tonny Sander Holm and the ﬁgures of the system are provided by him.
Technology design
The cleaning principle used in Filter 2 is condensation of the ﬂue gas and ﬁltration
through a bag ﬁlter. Filtration with a bag ﬁlter, also known as fabric ﬁlters or
baghouses, is a well known technology used in the industry. The operation principle
is like a domestic vacuum cleaner. A ﬂue gas or dusty air is drawn through the cloth
and the particles is deposit in the ﬁbres. In industry an eﬃciency of 99.9 % at 0.1
µm is often reported (Colls 2002). The bag ﬁlter is installed inside the chimney.
An important part of the design is cooling and condensation of the ﬂue gas before
ﬁltration. The idea is that because of the cooling, the ﬁlter will be able to remove
secondary particles. The design of the ﬁlter is found in ﬁgure 5.1. The cooling of
the ﬂue gas work through heat exchange with air from the inside. In contrary to
a normal stove, the air-intake at the front of the stove is sealed and instead the
air is drawn from the outside, just below the top of the chimney. The chimney is
separated into three compartments, and the cold air from the outside runs in the
outer compartment and into the stove nearly at the bottom of the chimney. The
hot ﬂue gas ﬂow upwards into the middle compartment of the chimney. When it
reaches the top it is forced downwards into the inner compartment. The bag ﬁlter
is situated in the inner compartment and the ﬂue gas is drawn through the ﬁlter by
a pressure blower on top of the bag ﬁlter housing. The bag ﬁlter causes a signiﬁcant
pressure drop which will increase as the particle layer on the fabric builds up (Colls
2002).
A future development for the system is that the pressure blower will be
controlled by the pressure drop so the ﬁre chamber will experience a stable pressure.
For the prototype the pressure blower was regulated manually. The bag ﬁlter in the
prototype is 3.5 meter long and with a diameter of 160 mm and coated with lime
(calcium hydroxide). It is installed at a metal grid and a cleaning compartment is
installed on the ﬁlter. The cleaning compartment is manually pulled down and up
to brush the ﬁlter but the idea is an automatic cleaning compartment that will start
when the pressure drop is too big. A safety valve will be installed in the ﬂue gas
channel which will open automatic and let the ﬂue gas out in case of power failure or
chimney ﬁre (if temperature exceeds 300 ◦C). The heat exchange between the outer
and middle compartments means that the ﬂue gas have a high temperature decrease
before outlet and temperature below 10 ◦was observed in winter, see appendix A.
The ﬁlter is produced by the Danish company Scanﬁlter which deliver ﬁlters for
industry, while the pressure blower is from the German company Elektror model

The removal of the parameters of interest was evaluated based on the following

5.4
Discussion
5.4.1
Results of the test
The results for testing of particulate matter (PM2.5) and particle number show a
very high removal eﬃciency. The result of the ﬁlter collection show a high removal
of PM2.5 (94 %) and total carbon (96%). The parameter with highest removal is
elemental carbon (99.6 %). The data for PM2.5 and inorganic matter are uncertain
because of damage of some of the quartz ﬁlters and the unknown water fraction
of PM2.5.
The removal eﬃciency for Filter 2 of particle number are above 98.5
%. During ignition a small peak is observed for most of the ﬁltered tests but in
relation to the high peaks of the unﬁltered samples the high removal is maintain
even during ignition. The ignition period is proven to be the diﬃcult part for other
particle ﬁlters (electrostatic precipitators and catalytic ﬁlters) (Schleicher et al. 2011,
Obernberger & Mandl 2011). The removal eﬃciencies obtained for Filter 2 come up
to the expectations of the bag house ﬁlter technology where a removal eﬃciency of
99.9 % for 0.1 µm often are reported (Colls 2002).
Parameters that inﬂuenced the result
Loss of particle due to over-saturation
The change in temperature and pressure
meant that the air became over-saturated with water and water vapour was easily
recognised. The outdoor summer temperatures and high air moisture, along with
evaporated water from the wood logs are assumed to be the reason for the over-
saturation. An over-saturation may not be observed on a winter day with other
temperature conditions.
The high content of water vapour might have led to a
high loss of particles because of sorption to the droplets. This might led to a high
underestimation of the number of particles in the ﬁltered samples. Since the water
condensed in the system it was not possible to measure before the condensation
occurred.
The particle sensor
P-Trak is original made for testing of indoor air pollution and
not combustion emissions. Two studies show that P-Trak generally measure lower
particle number than the SMPS and CPC tested in the studies (Zhu et al. 2006, Meier
et al. 2013). The underestimation of the P-Trak depend on the environment and the
worst performance was observed for measurements of freshly emitted nanoparticles
from combustion (P-Trak measured 20 % of CPC) (Zhu et al. 2006). This indicate
that the P-Trak may underestimate the particle number for the present test.
The test-stove
The test-stove connected to the ﬁlter was on older stove from before
2003. The air intake had been changed which meant that the air supply was diﬀerent
than the original air supply of the ﬁre-chamber. It is assumed that this reconstruc-
tion had a negative impact on the combustion. Overall the stove was assumed to
have a bad combustion, which also was observed in the high CO-levels (3812-9376
mg/Nm3) and PM2.5 emission. The PM2.5 emission for the unﬁltered ﬂue gas was
80
==PAGE:105==

5.4. DISCUSSION
84.4 mg/m3 which is twice as high as the PM limit measured in hot ﬂue gas for
new wood stoves (40 mg/Nm3) (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 2015). The PM2.5
concentrations are not normalized to Nm3 but the result is still considered as very
high. One study show that an eﬃcient combustion and higher temperatures lead to
a higher emission of particle number (size 0.1-10 µm) (Vicente et al. 2015). Another
study tested two types of typical stoves in Finland and found that a modern stove
had a higher particle number than a conventional stove (size 24 nm - 10 µm) (Tis-
sari et al. 2009). Additionally there are studies showing that the mean particle size
decreases during good combustion (Kinsey et al. 2009, Pettersson et al. 2011, B¨afver
et al. 2011, Vicente et al. 2015). These studies show that the bad combustion for
the test-stove of Filter 2 may cause a lower number of particles at a larger size than
a modern low-emission stove. If the test-stove was a modern stove with a better
combustion the diﬀerent particle size distribution may lead to a higher penetration
of particles through the bag ﬁlter. On the other hand the bad combustion of the
present test-stove led to a very high reference of PM2.5 emission when comparing
the ﬁltered test with the unﬁltered test. A modern wood stove with a lower PM
emission may lead to a lower removal eﬃciency. To conﬁrm that Filter 2 is an ”end
of pipe” solution, that can be applied to most stoves independent on the combustion
quality, the ﬁlter should be tested for diﬀerent types of stoves.
Condensation and formation of secondary particles
It is assumed that the inherent ﬂue gas cooling in the ﬁlter system means that not
only primary particles but also a fraction of the secondary particles are removed
by the bag ﬁlter. The temperature in the ﬁltered ﬂue gas was around 30 ◦C for
the present test and will be even lower in the winter. This means that a lot of
aerosols will condensate before the ﬁlter. Measurements of OGC before and after
the ﬁlter will give an indication of this. The removal of secondary particles might
also eﬀect the emission of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH). Larger PAH
that are formed in the combustion will condensate before the ﬁlter and adsorb to
the particulate matter removed by the bag ﬁlter (Kim et al. 2013). Tests of the
chemical composition of the particulate matter on the ﬁlter could show the removal
of PAH. It is also possible that the ﬁlter will have a positive eﬀect of dioxin emission
(Nesaratnam & Taherzadeh 2014). Filter 2 is not assumed to have an inﬂuence on
the emission of NOx.

5.5. CONCLUSION
5.5
Conclusion
Filter 2 is a bag ﬁlter prototype were the ﬂue gas is cooled down before ﬁltration.
The particle removal eﬃciency of Filter 2 was tested by measurements of the ﬂue
gas before and after ﬁltration. The PM2.5 removal was tested by ﬁlter collection
in 30 min and the removal was 94 %. The removal of TC was 96 % while the OC
and EC removal eﬃciencies were 85 % and 99.6 % respectively. The ﬁlter removal
eﬃciency of particle number was tested by a P-Trak and the data was evaluated
for three diﬀerent time periods after reload (0-10, 0-40, 10-40). The result showed
that Filter 2 remove particles with an eﬃciency above 98 % for all the periods. The
system cool down the ﬂue gas below 40 ◦C so it is assumed that the ﬁlter is able
to remove secondary particles. A qualiﬁed guess from the ﬁlter producer is a ﬁnal
price of 10,000-30,000 DKr for the ﬁlter. An automatic cleaning system and a safety
system will make the product user-friendly. The durability for a longer period of
ﬁring is unknown.
Further work
Filter 2 is a prototype in an early development and several of the features have
not been carried out yet. A ﬁnal evaluation should be made for the ﬁnal product.
Additional measurements should be done to determine the removal of OGC, sec-
ondary particles and PAH. The ﬁlter should be tested with diﬀerent stove types to
see the removal eﬃciency on other stoves. It is vitally important to test the ﬁlter’s
performance in the long run.

6.1
Comparison of Filter 1 and Filter 2
Two ﬁlters were tested in order to evaluate on their removal eﬃciency for ﬁne par-
ticulate matter (PM2.5), particle number and elemental carbon (EC). Additionally
the ﬁlters price, user-friendliness and possibility to install on most wood stoves have
been considered. Filter 1 is an electrostatic precipitation (ESP) ﬁlter. Filter 2 is a
bag ﬁlter where the ﬂue gas is cooled down before ﬁltration. Both ﬁlters are pro-
totypes under development. The tests of Filter 1 and Filter 2 were attempt to be
comparable by using the same method, instruments and wood type. Several param-
eters like the test stove, the method of dilution and the sampling tube were however
diﬀerent and is assumed to aﬀect the comparability of the results. For the test of
Filter 1, the ﬂue gas was cooled down in a dilution tunnel before sampling. For the
test of Filter 2, the measurements of the ﬁltered ﬂue gas were done in a measuring
pipe which was installed on the ﬂue gas outlet. The design of Filter 2 causes the ﬂue
gas to be cooled down before the outlet. For both ﬁlter tests the ﬁltered ﬂue gas
had a temperature around 25-40 ◦C . Accordingly, formation of secondary particles
is assumed for both ﬁlter test and therefore it makes sense to compare the results
for Filter 1 and Filter 2. Both ﬁlters had a high removal eﬃciency of respectively;
PM2.5: 87 % and 94 %, total carbon: 93 % and 96 % and EC: 98.6 % and 99.5 %.
In relation to particle number Filter 2 had a higher removal eﬃciency than Filter
1. The physical bag ﬁlter in Filter 2 means that it is able to remove particles even
during high emission loads which occurs in the period of ignition. The removal eﬃ-
ciency was above 98.5 % for both the ignition and intermediate period. Filter 1 had
a reduced removal eﬃciency during the high particle loads of ignition. Data from
the SMPS and MiniDiSC showed a removal of respectively 66 % and 25 %, while the
P-Trak measurements a 91 % particle number increase. In the intermediate period
the particle removal is 86-97 % for all three instruments. However, the MiniDiSC
data are not considered as valid and the diﬀerence between the P-Trak and SMPS
data indicate that one or both instruments were aﬀected by the changed properties
of the ﬁltered particles. The two ﬁlters were tested at two diﬀerent stoves. Filter 1
was tested at a modern low-emission stove and Filter 2 was tested at an older stove
which was assumed to have a bad combustion and a high emission of particulate

The Danish wood stove regulation is based on a standard which only measure dust
(PM) and organic gaseous carbon (OGC) (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 2015). If
the new European Standard EN 16510 is adopted by the EU Commission, future
wood stoves will be evaluated on primary particles measured as a mass (PM) and
OGC. There is no reason to believe that future standard test methods for particle
ﬁlters for wood stoves would be more ambitious. Filter 1 and Filter 2 have low PM2.5
emissions compared to the present regulation of 40 mg/Nm3 (Filter 1: 9.2 mg/m3
and Filter 2: 5.3 mg/m3). Without knowing a possible future EU ﬂue gas regula-
tion, it is assessed that the PM emissions are so low that both ﬁlters would pass a
future ﬁlter test. However, the standards are limited in relation to the real emission
from wood stoves: When Filter 1 and Filter 2 is evaluated on their real emission
to the atmosphere, Filter 1 have a limited eﬀect compared to Filter 2. Filter 2 is
assumed to remove secondary particles which is not assumed for Filter 1. This will
also mean a higher removal of PAH and dioxins for Filter 2. In relation to particle
number Filter 1 have a reduced particle removal eﬃciency in ”high load” periods.
The particle removal for Filter 1 is therefore reduced compared to Filter 2. Both of
the ﬁlters show that they are able to remove black carbon to a very high degree.

6.2. IMPLEMENTATION
6.2
Implementation
A technology like a particle ﬁlter for wood stoves is mainly interesting if there is a
possibility that the ﬁlter can be implemented. Filter 1 is assumed by the producer to
cost 20,000 DKr and to have an annually electricity consumption equivalent a price of
approx. 1000 DKr. The price of Filter 2 is assumed to be in the same range, 10,000-
30,000 DKr, and about the same annual cost in electricity consumption. Without
looking further into which of Filter 1 or Filter 2 that could be introduced on the
market, it is assessed that the price of a ﬁlter (10,000-30,000 DKr) is an essential
barrier for implementation at a larger scale. A new wood stove on the Danish market
vary from 7,000 - 35,000 DKr (Brændeovnsshoppen 2016a) and an additional price
of 10,000-30,000 DKr is a relative high price.
The producer of Filter 2 assesses
that the initial sale will be for an idealistic consumer to whom the environmental
impact is of big importance (Holm 2016). In a study of another ﬁlter system for
wood stoves (catalytic system), a survey was conducted among retailers in Denmark
and Northern Germany.
The experience of the retailers was that only a few of
the customers demand an environmentally friendly stove. However, if the retailers
choose to raise the subject it became part of the consideration when choosing a
stove. Nevertheless the users are mainly interested in price, user-friendliness, energy
eﬃciency and design (Brønnum et al. 2014). In relation to particle ﬁlters there do not
seem to be a current demand from Danish customers (Brændeovnsshoppen 2016b,
Brændeovnsbutikken 2016, Dansk Pejsecenter 2016). The only Danish retailer of a
particle ﬁlter for wood stoves had sold one ﬁlter in 5 years (an ESP ﬁlter) (Kirkegaard
2016). It is assessed that for an emission control technology of this price range some
governmental action needs to be taking to implement ﬁlters on wood stoves. This
assessment is shared by the two ﬁlter producers (Hermansen 2016, Holm 2016).
This could either be a restriction of PM emission, diﬀerentiated taxes for wood
stoves dependent on the PM emission-level, or a subsidy scheme. The ﬁnal product
for both ﬁlters are planned to be a highly automatic technology where the daily
cleaning requires little or no involvement from the user. This is assessed to be an
important factor for implementation. Both ﬁlters will probably be visible from the
outside.
There could be an aesthetic problem, especially in the case of Filter 2
(Brønnum et al. 2014)

E.1
Dilution ratio
E.1.1
Rotating Disk Diluter
The following calculations used to ﬁnd the dilution of Rotating Disk Diluter is based
on the operation manual (Wohlen 2008). The dilution ratio was determined by the
rotating speed of the disk. The speed was set on a button (potentiometer) which
could be set between 0-100 %. 100 % was full speed while the disk was stopped at
0 %. The dilution ratio is based on the following equation:
DF = f[◦C] ∗X
pot[%]
(E.1)
DF is the Dilution Factor
f[◦C ] is the temperature correction factor: f(OFF)=0.83, f(80◦C)=1.00
X is either 1500 (10 cavities disk) or 15000 (8 cavities disk)
pot[% ] is setting of potential
A chosen dilution ratio of 50 required a potential of 24.9 %, using a 10 cavities disk
and the temperature was set at OFF.
