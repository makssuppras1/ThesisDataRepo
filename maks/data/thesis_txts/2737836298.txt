Abstract
Therapeutic peptides have emerged as a crucial class of biopharmaceuticals due to their
high specificity, efficacy, and favorable safety profiles. However, their therapeutic po­
tential is often limited by poor stability, rapid degradation, and inefficient delivery to target
sites. Liposomes, as versatile drug delivery systems, offer significant advantages in over­
coming these challenges by enhancing the stability, bioavailability, and targeted delivery
of therapeutic peptides.Endomorphin 1, an endogenous opioid peptide, is a prime candi­
date for enhanced delivery systems. This project aims to better define the parameters for
remote loading of Endomorphin 1 into liposomes. Additionally, the study explores different
liposome formulations to determine the encapsulation efficiency and loading capacity of
Endomorphin 1 and its two analogues. The release kinetics of these peptide­loaded lipo­
somes will also be investigated to understand their potential for sustained and controlled
drug delivery.By optimizing remote loading conditions and analyzing the release profiles,
this research seeks to advance the delivery and therapeutic application of Endomorphin 1
and its analogues, contributing to the development of more effective peptide­based drug
delivery systems.

Introduction
1.1
Peptides as therapeutics
Peptides have been used in therapy for a century, starting with the discovery of insulin’s
therapeutic potential for treating type 1 diabetes .Over the decades, advancements in
solid­phase peptide synthesis, recombinant technologies, and the discovery of new pep­
tides from natural sources have driven scientific progress in developing peptides as ther­
apeutic agents [1].Currently, peptides are being developed to treat various conditions,
including microbial infections, obesity, and cancer, as well as for creating cell­targeting
platforms and enhancing cell­penetrating properties.Specifically, cell­penetrating peptides
are being investigated as drug delivery tools for anti­cancer, antibacterial, and antiviral
therapies [2].
Therapeutic peptides typically function as hormones, growth factors, neurotransmitters,
ion channel ligands, or anti­infective agents. They bind to cell surface receptors, triggering
intracellular effects with high affinity and specificity [3], much like biologics such as ther­
apeutic proteins and antibodies. However, therapeutic peptides have the advantages of
lower immunogenicity and reduced production costs compared to biologics. Conversely,
small molecule drugs have a long therapeutic history and offer inherent advantages, such
as low production and sale costs, the ability to be administered orally, and excellent mem­
brane penetration capabilities [4].
To assess the true value of using peptides as therapeutics, it is essential to compare their
benefits to those of small molecules. Peptide drugs offer several advantages over small
molecules, including increased target specificity and potency, often with EC50 values in
the nanomolar range or lower. This specificity usually results in fewer side effects due
to reduced interactions with unintended targets. The diversity of side chains in peptides
allows for a broad spectrum of potential targets. Additionally, peptides generally have
more predictable metabolism than small molecules, with their metabolites typically be­
ing non­toxic and metabolic interactions, such as cytochrome P450 inhibition, being less
common [5]. However, therapeutic peptides, being natural amino acid­based compounds,
face two inherent challenges: membrane impermeability and poor in vivo stability, which
significantly hinder their development as drugs. Peptides typically struggle to cross cell
membranes to access intracellular targets, with their ability varying depending on sev­
eral factors, including peptide length and amino acid composition. Moreover, their natural
structure lacks the stability provided by secondary or tertiary structures found in proteins.
As a result, the amide bonds in peptides are prone to hydrolysis and enzymatic degrada­
tion in vivo, leading to their rapid breakdown and elimination. These inherent properties
render peptides chemically and physically unstable, resulting in a short half­life. While
these challenges pose obstacles to peptide drug development, they also present oppor­
tunities for improving peptide design and optimization strategies [6].
1.1.1
Endomorphin 1
Endomorphin­1 is a naturally occurring opioid peptide, one of two endomorphins. It is
composed of four amino acids (Tyr­Pro­Trp­Phe­NH2), with an amidated phenylalanine at
the C­terminus. The recognition of the μ­opioid receptor requires the amino and phenolic
groups of tyrosine (Tyr) and the aromatic ring of tryptophan (Trp). Proline (Pro) at position
2 acts as a stereochemical spacer, guiding tryptophan (Trp) at position 3 toward the μ­
opioid receptor’s selectivity region in endomorphin­1 [7]. It’s molecular structure can be

The endogenous opioid system (EOS), which includes four opioid receptors (ORs) and
their endogenous opioid peptide (EOP) ligands, is an evolutionarily conserved innate re­
sponse system for nociception. The EOS is widely distributed across the central and
peripheral nervous systems, as well as the immune system [8]. Beyond pain control,
the EOS is involved in numerous critical biological functions such as reward processing,
perception, mood regulation, stress responses, food intake regulation, and modulation
of the immune response [9]. Opioid receptors, which include μ­opioid receptor (mOR),
δ­opioid receptor (dOR), κ­opioid receptor (kOR), and nociceptin receptor (NOPR), are G
protein­coupled receptors (GPCRs) that primarily signal through inhibitory Gi/o proteins.
These receptors can be activated by both endogenous opioid peptides and externally
administered opioid compounds, thereby blocking pain signal transmission in the central
and peripheral nervous systems and producing an analgesic effect [10]. Endomorphin­1
has shown good selectivity and high affinity targeting the mOR, by activating the G pro­
teins and inhibiting adenylyl cyclase [11].These characteristics of endomorphin­1 have
prompted researchers to explore its potential clinical applications.However, due to sev­
eral challenges, including metabolic instability, a short in vivo half­life, rapid clearance,
and low permeability across physiological barriers, the clinical use of analgesic peptides
like Endomorphin­1 ,has been limited [12]. There are various approaches and strategies
to address these peptide limitations and enhance their bio­clinical applications. Primar­
ily, structural modifications can improve physicochemical properties, leading to increased
overall stability. This stability can be further enhanced by using ”double­bridged peptides,”
where peptides are cyclized via two chemical bridges. This process reduces the flexibility
of the peptide backbone, thus limiting its availability to enzymes and increasing its sta­
bility [13]. Another mode of action is the use of liposomes as drug delivery systems for
the short­peptides. Therapeutic peptides and proteins typically have a short duration of
action due to rapid clearance from the bloodstream. Liposomes offer a potential solution
by serving as circulating reservoirs for these therapeutic agents. By encapsulating them,
liposomes can extend their release over time, providing sustained delivery. Additionally,
liposomes can offer protection to the therapeutic agents, reducing immunogenicity and
minimizing adverse side effects [14].

1.2
Peptide synthesis
Several synthetic peptides are important commercial and pharmaceutical products, rang­
ing from dipeptide sugar substitutes to clinically used hormones. Thus, developing rapid,
efficient, and reliable methods for their chemical synthesis is crucial. The advent of solid­
phase techniques has made peptide synthesis a more practical aspect of modern scientific
research [15]. Solid­phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) has been advanced through vari­
ous developments at both fundamental and technical levels. While new reagents and
modifications are continuously introduced to optimize methodologies and novel synthesis
strategies—particularly with a focus on greener approaches—the core principles of SPPS
remain unchanged [16].
The general process for synthesizing peptides on a resin begins by attaching the first
amino acid (AA) at the C­terminal residue (carboxyl group), and then constructing the
peptide sequence toward the N­terminal end. Amino acids are coupled to the supported
peptide sequence through their alpha amino group, with reactive side chains temporarily
protected by a protecting group. The carboxyl group of each amino acid must be activated
for coupling to the resin. After attachment, the resin is filtered and washed to remove
byproducts and excess reagents. The N­alpha­protecting group of the newly coupled
amino acid is then removed (deprotection), and the resin is washed again [17]. This cycle
is repeated, coupling the next amino acid to the growing chain until the peptide sequence
is complete. Finally, all protecting groups are removed, the resin is washed, and the
peptide is cleaved from the resin. This general scheme of solid­phase peptide synthesis
can be seen in figure 1.2 [17].

A defining characteristic of the chemistry in SPPS is the temporary protecting group
on the N­terminal of the amino acid. The two most commonly used protecting groups
are tert­butoxycarbonyl (Boc), which is sensitive to acids like trifluoroacetic acid (TFA),
and fluoren­9­ylmethyloxycarbonyl (Fmoc), which is sensitive to bases like piperidine.The
Fmoc strategy is often preferred over the Boc strategy for routine synthesis because it can
be removed under milder conditions, offering an orthogonal deprotection scheme. This is
beneficial as most linkers and protecting groups of amino acid residues are deprotected
under acidic conditions. However, the application of an in situ neutralization protocol has
made Boc chemistry the preferred strategy for increasing efficiency in difficult or longer
peptide sequences, even in automated synthesizers [18]. Achieving a nearly quantita­
tive yield in the coupling of the first amino acid is crucial for efficient SPPS. The yield
and scale of solid­phase reactions are determined by the amount of this first amino acid
coupled onto the resin [19].
Polystyrene (PS) is the most commonly used core resin in solid­phase chemistry. Most
polystyrene supports incorporate 1% or 2% divinylbenzene (DVB) as a cross­linking agent,
rendering them insoluble in all common solvents. Typically, these resins are small, spher­
ical beads available in two different sizes: 100–200 mesh (75–150 microns) and 200–400
mesh (35–75 microns). The swelling of the polystyrene material in organic solvents is
a significant concern. This phenomenon greatly affects the diffusion and accessibility of
reagents into the polymer’s core, thereby influencing the synthetic efficiency [20].
Linkers are chemical entities employed to attach an amino acid to a resin bead, facilitating
molecule synthesis in solid­phase chemistry. The type of linker chosen dictates the chem­
istry applicable and, crucially, the conditions required to detach products from the resin.
Over recent years, a diverse array of linkers compatible with polystyrene core resins has
been developed. Many of these employ acidic conditions to facilitate cleavage, resulting in
peptides that terminate with a carboxylic acid or an amide at the C­terminus. Alternatively,
hydrazides or sulfonamides attached to the resin enable the release of peptides adorned
with different functional groups using orthogonal and mild cleavage conditions.Once the
peptide sequence is completed, the protecting groups on the amino acid residues need to
be removed. This step can be carried out either before the final cleavage, as the last step,
or concurrently with it [21].Different protection groups have different deprotection condi­
tions than need to be followed. Ultimately, the peptide is cleaved from the solid support
based on the specific conditions required by the chosen linker [22].
1.3
Liposomes
Drug delivery systems,or DDSs ,are advanced technological systems that are designed
for aiding in the delivery of the potentially active drug to its site of action. These sys­
tems aim to enhance the therapeutic index of drugs, while mitigating undesirable char­
acteristics by improving drug pharmacokinetics and biodistribution [23].Despite the rapid
advancements in this field, many nanoparticle­based DDSs do not exhibit an adequate
loading capacity and lack target specificity. Therefore, future progress in DDSs should fo­
cus on designing nano­carriers with high, regulated loading capacities, functionalized with
recognition ligands that specifically target unique or overexpressed biomarkers. Among
the various nanocarriers, liposomes are the most extensively explored for targeted drug
delivery systems [24].
British hematologist Dr. Alec D. Bangham introduced the term ”liposomes” in 1961 at the
Institute of Cambridge, England.The discovery occurred while R.W. Horne and Bangham
were studying phospholipids and blood clotting using an electron microscope. These
microscopic images provided the first visual evidence of the bilayer lipid structure of cell

membranes.In the early 1970s, G. Gregoriadis was the first to propose that liposomes
could be used as carriers for drug delivery to cell membranes. However, it wasn’t until
the early 1990s, following numerous clinical trials, that liposome­based drug formulations
entered the market [25].
Liposomes are artificial vehicles composed of an aqueous core an a phospholipid bi­
layer. Their unique sizes, biocompatibility and combination of hydrophobic and hydrophilic
properties make them promising systems for drug delivery. Phospholipids naturally form
closed structures when hydrated in aqueous solutions due to their amphipathic nature.In
aqueous environments, the thermodynamic phase properties and self­assembling char­
acteristics of lipids drive the entropically favored organization of their hydrophobic sections
into spherical bilayers [26]. Consequently, the lipid bilayers surrounding the aqueous units
are formed, with the polar head groups of the lipids oriented at the internal and external
aqueous phases. An overview of a liposomal structure can be seen in figure 1.3.

The most common lipid components are phospholipids, which can be natural or synthetic.
Figure 1.5 shows the molecular structures of several lipids that are also utilized in this
thesis. Modifications to the polar and non­polar regions of natural phospholipids have
enabled researchers to develop a variety of the synthetic phospholipids, which are gener­
ally more stable [27]. The surface charge of liposomes, determined by the phospholipid
headgroup, can be negative, neutral, or positive, affecting liposome stability, pharma­
cokinetics, biodistribution, and cellular uptake. Negatively charged phospholipids, such
as DMPG , are recognized by macrophages and enter cells via endocytosis more quickly
than neutral phospholipids like HSPC and DOPE, leading to a shorter circulation time. A
slight negative charge can stabilize neutral liposomes by increasing electrostatic repul­
sive forces, thus influencing the aggregation­dependent phagocytic uptake mechanism.
Conversely, cationic liposomes interact with plasma proteins, enhancing uptake by the
phagocytic system and promoting clearance by the lungs, liver, or spleen. Additionally,
the uptake of positively charged liposomes is significantly higher than that of negatively
charged ones [28].

To improve the effectiveness of the liposomal delivery system, modifications like the ad­
dition of polyethylene glycol (PEG) to the surface are applied.PEG is a crucial element in
various nanoparticles due to its stability in the blood circulation, ability to extend release
rates, and high water solubility. PEGylated liposomes also exhibit reduced antigenicity
and immunogenicity. Consequently, incorporating PEG­lipid conjugates helps minimize
the leakage of entrapped chemicals [29]. Steroids are often added to liposomes as they
enhance their fluidity and adjust bilayer rigidity.Cholesterol is the primary steroid com­
monly used in liposome preparation, typically constituting less than 30% of the total lipids,
as it is incorporated into the liposomal lipid bilayer to enhance stability.Research indi­

amphiphiles destabilize the lipid bilayer of liposomal nanoparticles and enhance the de­

1.4
Liposome formulation Parameters
Understanding the factors that influence liposome characteristics such as size, drug load­
ing capacity, stability, and drug release is essential for designing liposomal formulations
with predictable behavior in biological environments. This knowledge allows researchers
to optimize liposome designs to ensure stability, efficient drug delivery, and desired ther­
apeutic effects. These properties include the chemical and physical properties of the
formulated liposomes, the phase transition temperature, the drug to lipid ratio , the critical
packing parameters and the drug characteristics.

1.4.1
Liposomal physicochemical properties
The physicochemical properties of liposomes play a crucial role in determining the bioavail­
ability of the encapsulated compounds and obtaining high and sufficient encapsulation ef­
ficiency. The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has outlined specific physicochemical
properties of liposomal formulations that must be defined and assessed prior to approval
[32]. During the pre­formulation stage, it is essential to characterize properties such as
size, zeta potential, polydispersity, phase transition temperature, morphology, encapsu­
lation efficiency, bilayer composition, thermal stability, and sterility.
Liposomes are classified based on their size and the number of bilayers, which influences
their suitability and capacity for encapsulating hydrophilic or lipophilic drug molecules.
Unilamellar vesicles (ULVs) have a single phospholipid bilayer and a larger aqueous
compartment, providing higher encapsulation efficiency for hydrophilic molecules. In con­
trast, multilamellar vesicles (MLVs) with multiple bilayers are better suited for delivering
lipophilic drugs. Additionally, multivesicular vesicles (MVVs) can encapsulate large quan­
tities of hydrophilic compounds [26]. Another critical characteristic is the polydispersity
index (PDI), which refers to the size distribution range of liposomes. PDI affects the qual­
ity of liposomes by influencing encapsulation rate, vesicular stability, drug release rate,
pharmacokinetics, and cellular uptake of drugs [33]. Generally, PDI values less than 0.3
and vesicular sizes between 50 and 200 nm are favorable for liposomal drug carriers in
clinical development [34].
The type and concentration of phospholipids used in liposome formulation also signifi­
cantly impact drug loading, liposome stability, permeability, drug release, and clearance.
Therefore, the lipid composition must be tailored to the properties of the lipid, the drug
of interest, and the desired application and release profile [35] . Specific phospholipid
features such as acyl chain length, side chains, charge, polar head group, degree of sat­
uration, and phase transition temperature have been shown to affect drug loading [36].
A synopsis of the phospholipid characteristics affecting the drug loading and release of
hydrophilic and hydrophobic compounds is shown in figure 1.5.
Zeta potential, an indicator of surface charge, is a crucial feature affecting the stabil­
ity and fate of liposomal formulations. Neutral or low­charged liposomes are prone to
agglomeration over time, while the high electrostatic repulsion on the surface of highly
negatively or positively charged liposomes contributes to their stability. Zeta potential
values between +16 and +55 mV are considered optimal for colloidal stability [37].Sur­
face modifications of liposomes are another important aspect of their physicochemical
characteristics. Liposomes can be classified into conventional, stealth, and targeted lipo­
somes based on these modifications. Conventional liposomes are quickly recognized and
cleared by macrophages following in vivo administration. To overcome the limitations of
conventional liposomes, such as drug escape in blood and short circulation half­life, PEG
moieties are added to create stealth or sterically stabilized liposomes. Targeted liposomes
(immunoliposomes) are decorated with surface antibodies to target specific tissues. Other
strategies, such as incorporating chemically modified lipids or creating liposomes that re­
spond to external and internal stimuli, have also been employed to improve drug delivery
to the target site [38].
Finally, a common challenge in liposome formulation is drug leakage. Membrane destabi­
lization, which leads to leakage, is often caused by the degradation or hydrolysis of ester
bonds linking glycerol to fatty acids and the peroxidation of double bonds in unsaturated
lipids. These processes break up or shorten lipid chains, creating solubilized byproducts
that compromise the integrity of the liposome bilayers. The extent of leakage depends on

1.4.2
Phase transition temperature (Tm)
Hydrated phospholipid membranes exhibit different phases characterized by distinct po­
sitions (lateral order) and orientations (rotational order) of the molecules. The two primary
phases are known as the gel phase and the fluid phase. In the gel phase, also referred
to as the solid­ordered phase, phospholipids form a tightly packed bilayer with minimal
mobility due to the hydrocarbon chains adopting an all­trans configuration, maximizing
their elongation. Upon undergoing a gel–liquid transition, lipid chains switch configura­
tion from all­trans to all­gauche, resulting in a less extended and compact structure. The
permeability of liposomes increases as the lipid bilayer transitions from the ordered gel
phase to a disordered fluid phase at its main phase transition temperature (Tm). This
transition temperature (Tm) plays a significant role in the liposomal formulation and its
ability to efficiently encapsulate compounds [40]. The transition temperature (Tm) varies
significantly among different lipids, influenced by factors such as the length and degree
of unsaturation of the acyl chains and the nature of the dispersion medium. Longer acyl
chains or lower degrees of unsaturation lead to higher Tm values. In some instances, the
transition from the gel to the liquid phase is preceded by a pre­transition (Tp), occurring
slightly before Tm. This pre­transition involves a gradual change in the distance among
polar heads and leads to the formation of periodic one­dimensional undulations on the
bilayer surface, known as the ripple phase. These ripples are attributed to the alternation
of gel and liquid lipid domains within a single monolayer, with different domains imposing
distinct geometrical characteristics on the lipid arrangement. The difference between Tp
and Tm is influenced by the length of the hydrocarbon chains, with longer chains resulting
in a smaller difference [41].

Cholesterol plays a crucial role in liposomal formulations alongside phospholipids, as it
regulates the fluidity of the lipid bilayer and stabilizes the membrane structure. Specifi­
cally, the hydrophobic steroidal moiety of cholesterol and similar compounds promotes an
all­trans configuration of the acyl chains, reducing the tilt angle in the gel phase. As a re­
sult , the inclusion of cholesterol in the bilayers leads to a broadening or elimination of the
transition temperature, as it induces an ordering effect on the liquid phase, which adopts
physical characteristics similar to the solid­ordered gel phase. Consequently, the configu­
ration of the bilayer in the presence of cholesterol is often referred to as the liquid­ordered
phase [42].
1.4.3
Drug to Lipid ratio (D/L)
Liposomal loading efficiency can sometimes be challenging, slowing down both formula­
tion development and the approval process of liposomal products. Successful liposomal
loading is achieved when high loading efficiency is attained, as it indicates that the drug
is almost entirely loaded, which is crucial from both pharmaceutical and cost perspec­
tives [43]. The drug­to­lipid (D/L) ratio is a critical parameter that affects the drug loading
process into liposomes.However, high loading efficiency at a low D/L ratio is impractical
because it prevents achieving sufficient intra­liposome drug concentrations, making it im­
possible to administer the therapeutic dose. If a large amount of phospholipid is needed
to encapsulate a small drug dose, the delivery system becomes neither administrable nor
economical [44]. A low D/L also indicates inefficient loading, which leads to a significant
loss of the active agent during the loading process and requires the removal of unloaded
drug, thereby increasing preparation costs. However,when the initial D/L ratio is too high,
loading efficiency is low, likely due to an excess of drug exceeding the liposomal loading
capacity, which can damage the liposomal membrane and cause drug release, resulting
in a lower final D/L ratio. Conversely, a lower D/L ratio allows for better loading efficiency.
Therefore, it is essential to know the required D/L ratio to achieve high loading efficiency
before starting the loading process [45].
The D/L ratio could also help determine the number of doses needed to reach the ther­
apeutic level. Given that there is a daily limit on the amount of lipids that can be ad­
ministered to a patient and that the D/L ratio influences the final drug concentration in
the product, this ratio sets the maximum dose that can be given and, consequently, the
amount of liposomes used per treatment. High lipid concentrations can lead to toxicity
and make large­scale production less feasible [46].
There are several parameters affecting the drug­to ­lipid ratio while formulating a lipo­
some. The D/L is influenced by all factors affecting the physicochemical stability of the
liposomes. According to QSPR models, the drug­to­lipid ratio is also influenced by the
drug’s structural characteristics and the experimental conditions, as well as the charac­
teristics of the lipid bilayer, especially when lipophilic drug are carried, and the loading
method chosen [47].
1.4.4
Critical Packing Parameter (CPP)
As stated, the liposomal bilayer is composed of amphiphilic bilayers known as phos­
pholipids. Phospholipids can spontaneously self­organize in aqueous solutions into mi­

1.4.5
Drug characteristics
The characteristics of the drug that is to be encapsulated in the liposome can be a factor
regarding the liposome formulation. Drugs with high aqueous solubility are best encap­
sulated within the liposome core. The phospholipid bilayer of liposomes serves as a site
for encapsulating hydrophobic drugs, thereby reducing their movement towards both the
outer aqueous environment and the inner compartments of the liposomes [52].For poorly
soluble drugs that are weak acids or bases, remote loading methods can achieve higher
encapsulation efficiencies compared to traditional direct loading methods [53].
The logD of a compound is a crucial factor for active loading into liposomes. LogD, or the
distribution coefficient, represents the distribution of all forms of the compound between
water and octanol at a given pH, including un­ionized, ionized, and differently ionized
species. This is in contrast to logP, or the partition coefficient, which measures the dis­
tribution of only the un­ionized form of the compound between water and octanol and
remains constant regardless of pH. LogD varies with pH due to the changing proportions
of each species.The logD of a compound is particularly important for remote loading into
liposomes because it affects the compound’s solubility and permeability, which are criti­
cal for efficient encapsulation. A suitable logD ensures that the compound can effectively
cross the liposomal membrane and be retained within the liposome at the desired pH.
Additionally, the right logD value helps in maintaining the stability and release profile of
the drug within the liposome, optimizing therapeutic efficacy [54].

1.5
Liposome formulation techniques
During liposome formulation, the primary goal is the formation of monodisperse particles
with a narrow size distribution. The desired degree of lamellarity much be achieved as
well, while maintaining long­term stability of the product. There are several different ap­
proaches to formulate the desired, some of them being more conventional and some of
them being novel [57].
1.5.1
Conventional methods for the preparation of liposomes
In conventional methods, liposomes are first dissolved in a volatile organic solvent and
then mixed with an aqueous phase. After the dissolution, there is a dry­down step of the
lipidic solution from the organic solvent, followed by hydrating the lipids with an aqueous
solvent.These methods involve loading the entrapped agents either before or during the
manufacturing process (passive loading). However, certain types of compounds with ion­
izable groups, as well as those that are soluble in both lipids and water, can be introduced
into the liposomes after the formation of intact vesicles (remote loading) [57].
The presence of an organic solvent can alter the chemical properties of the incorporated
active compounds and affect the stability or toxicity of the resulting formulations. As a
result, especially when the drug that needs to be encapsulated is a peptide, the method
must be carefully chosen in order to completely avoid the possible presence of an organic
solvent into the final formulation, as biologics like therapeutic peptides that are exposed
to these solvents can be denatured of chemically degraded [58].
The thin­film hydration technique, also known as the Bangham method, is the oldest and
simplest method for preparing liposomes. In this process, the main phospholipid ingredi­
ents are dissolved in an organic solvent, which is then evaporated to form a dry, thin lipid
film. This film is subsequently hydrated with an appropriate aqueous medium, usually at a
temperature above the phase­transition temperature of the lipids. The hydration process,
which takes 1–2 hours with stirring, results in a multilamellar vesicle suspension that is
highly heterogeneous in size and lamellarity. This method has been employed for the
encapsulation of various hydrophilic and lipophilic agents [59].
Currently, a commercially used method is ethanol or ether injection. In these methods,
lipids are dissolved in ethanol or ether and then injected into an aqueous medium at an
elevated temperature to form liposomes, followed by evaporation to remove the organic
solvents. Another simple method, although requiring a large amount of organic solvent,
is reverse phase evaporation. Here, following the same pattern,lipids are dissolved in an
organic solvent, sonicated to produce liposomes, and then the solvent is removed [60].
For biologics loading, the preferred method is the heating method, which does not involve
organic solvents. In this approach, lipids are hydrated under continuous stirring at an
elevated temperature for one hour, followed by a necessary downsizing step to achieve
uniform liposome dispersion [61].
For specific biomedical applications, precise control of particle size, PDI, lamellarity, and
homogeneity is crucial during manufacturing and is a key parameter in product specifi­
cations. To achieve this, post­formation processing is necessary to break down the ini­

sonication, extrusion, and high­pressure homogenization, which are the most commonly
employed post­formation treatments for size reduction in liposome production.
The sonication method applies high ultrasonic energy to the MLV liposome solution under
an inert atmosphere. There are two main sonication techniques: bath sonication and
probe sonication. In probe sonication, typically used for small volumes, a sonicator tip is
immersed in the liposome solution, which is kept in a water/ice bath to prevent overheating
and degradation. This method can cause contamination from metal particles released by
the probe tip and lipid degradation with prolonged use. In bath sonication, used for larger
volumes, the liposome dispersion is placed in a temperature­controlled vessel or under
an inert atmosphere. This approach may result in low encapsulation efficiency, potential
degradation of phospholipids or encapsulated compounds, and high size polydispersity.
Although sonication is commonly used to form small unilamellar vesicles with diameters
between 15–25 μm, it is not optimal for achieving precise physical properties in liposomes
[62].
The extrusion method utilizes membranes with small pores (filters) through which a lipid
mixture is forced, aided by heat to maintain the temperature above a certain thresh­
old. Multiple passes through filters help ensure the liposomes formed have a consis­
tent size distribution, providing reliable results. However, this method is not very efficient
for large­scale production due to significant product loss.Another technique, the French
press method, involves applying high pressure to lipid suspensions to force them through
a small orifice, resulting in the formation of larger liposomes. Originally developed for gen­
tler cell disruption compared to ultrasound methods, it offers advantages like preserving
sensitive biomolecules as peptides but has limitations such as challenges in temperature
control and small working volumes, which aren’t conducive to large­scale production [63].
In the homogenization method, a starting suspension of multilamellar liposomes under­
goes continuous injection at a consistent high pressure through an orifice, where it collides
with a stationary stainless­steel surface, causing liposome size reduction. This process
relies on cavitation, shear forces, and turbulence to shape the liposomes’ structure. How­
ever, this method often results in a broad and inconsistent size distribution among the
liposomes. The specific properties and size range of the liposomes are influenced by
factors such as pressure, temperature, and the number of passes through the homoge­
nizer. Initial characteristics of the sample, including lipid composition, ionic strength, and
initial size distribution of liposomes, also impact the outcome. Drawbacks of this method
include the requirement for very high operating pressures and the risk of metal and oil
contamination [64].
1.5.2
Novel methods for the preparation of liposomes
The primary limitations of conventional methods for forming liposomes include challenges
in creating a process that can be easily scaled up for large­scale production and struggles
in achieving high encapsulation efficiencies. Additionally, these conventional approaches
may not be suitable for processing many biomolecules due to the structural or functional
changes they may undergo when exposed to detergents, residues from organic solvents
and high shear homogenization or sonication processes, potentially impacting their clin­
ical applicability. To address these challenges, recent advancements have led to the
development of novel technologies aimed at producing liposome nano­formulations.
Water­soluble drugs incorporated into lipid­based nanoformulations often experience leak­
age during preparation and storage, potentially leading to degradation before their use in
drug­delivery applications due to oxidation or other chemical reactions. These challenges

hinder the commercial development of liposome nanoformulations. One approach to ad­
dress these issues involves removing water from the liposome systems while they are in
a frozen state and under low pressures. The freeze­drying method has been developed
for that purpose, which involves freezing the aqueous solution containing the liposome
formulation and then removing ice through sublimation. Initially, the product is frozen,
typically in a vial or flask, at atmospheric pressure. Subsequently, it is placed under deep
vacuum, well below the water triple point, to facilitate the sublimation of ice. Heating is
then applied to induce ice sublimation, resulting in a dry product. Following primary drying,
secondary drying under vacuum is necessary to remove any remaining unfrozen water,
after which the dried product is removed from the freeze dryer [65].
The unique characteristics of dense gases have been utilized to substitute numerous or­
ganic solvents, enabling the development of innovative processing methods, including
purification and size­reduction techniques. Dense gases above their critical point ex­
hibit mass transport properties similar to gases and dissolution characteristics resembling
those of liquids and traditional solvents. Specifically, supercritical fluids (SCFs) are ex­
tremely dense fluids at temperatures and pressures surpassing critical values, where the
boundary between gas and liquid phases disappears.Numerous novel liposome formation
methods employ supercritical fluids (SCFs), which are maintained at supercritical temper­
ature (Tc) and pressure (Pc). In this state, SCFs serve as excellent solvents for various
lipid components. Modulations in temperature or pressure induce significant changes
in SCF density, facilitating the solubility of a wide range of active components. Conse­
quently, SCFs are increasingly replacing organic solvents due to their ability to enhance
purification and separation processes effectively [66].
Finally, another exmple of novel approaches is the SuperLip method. In the SuperLip
method, known as Supercritical­Assisted Liposome Formation, an ethanol solution and
CO2 are continuously supplied to a homogenizer, where they form an expanded liquid.
This mixture is then transferred to a precipitation vessel, where water containing drug
droplets is generated through atomization within a high­pressure vessel. These drug­
containing droplets become enveloped by a lipid layer, promoting the formation of a wa­
ter/CO2 emulsion, and then descend into a water pool at the vessel’s bottom, where lipo­
somes are formed.The SuperLip technique enables precise control over liposome size,
typically ranging from nanometric to sub­micrometric dimensions, along with a significant
reduction in residual solvent. Additionally, it achieves high encapsulation efficiencies,
ranging from approximately 80% to 99% for hydrophilic compounds, facilitating the en­
trapment of diverse active drugs, including antibodies, proteins, antibiotics, antioxidants,
essential oils, and dyes [67].
1.6
Loading
The primary objective of liposomes is the effective transportation and delivery of lipophilic
compounds within the lipid bilayer, hydrophilic substances in the aqueous core, and am­
phiphilic molecules partitioned at the bilayer’s surface. The extent of drug loading is influ­
enced not only by the liposome preparation method but also by the chemical properties of
the drugs and the composition and physicochemical attributes of the liposomes, including
lipid concentrations, charge characteristics, and drug­to­lipid ratios.

In active loading, liposomes are first generated containing a transmembrane gradient, so
that the aqueous phases inside and outside the liposomes are different. Subsequently,
an amphipathic drug dissolved in the exterior aqueous phase can permeate across the
phospholipid bilayer, followed by interactions with a trapping agent in the core to lock­in
the drug (Figure 1). In 1976, Deamer and Nicols demonstrated that a pH gradient could
be utilized to load catecholamine into liposomes, leading to stable retention in vitro. They
formed liposomes in a low pH (pH 5) solution and then bathed the liposomes in an alka­
line solution (pH 8) to create a 1000­fold difference in H3O+ ions across the bilayer. As
the catecholamine molecules remained as the free form in the basic exterior, they freely
permeated through the bilayer into the low pH core, where they were subsequently proto­
nated. The charged catecholamine molecules were no longer membrane permeable and
thus locked in. An accumulation of catecholamine molecules in the core of the liposomes
was observed [70]. Subsequent to the work of Dreamer et al., Haran et al. demonstrated
that active loading can be achieved using an ammonium sulfate gradient. They produced
liposomes with an interior containing ammonium sulfate. A concentrated solution of an­
thracycline was added to the liposomes and loading was then achieved after incubation
at an elevated temperature. Anthracycline molecules formed aggregates in the core of
the liposomes through precipitation with sulfate ions, and a high drug loading efficiency
of >90% was achieved without burst drug leakage [71]. Several other pH gradients have
been established, including phosphate and calcium acetate. Similar to the calcium ac­
etate gradient, basified copper acetate gradients have also been employed for liposomal
loading [72].
When the ammonium sulfate gradient is the chosen mode of action, for each ammonia
molecule that exits the liposome, a proton is left behind, creating the pH gradient. The
resulting intraliposomal acidification prevents further dissociation of ammonium into am­
monia and a proton, thereby stabilizing the gradient. Consequently, the amphipathic basic
drug inside the liposome is protonated by the excess protons, which raises the pH and
resumes the dissociation of ammonium. This allows the loading cycle to continue until all
the ammonium ions are replaced by the loaded amphipathic basic drug. The strength of
the gradient can be determined by the ratio of ammonium concentration inside the lipo­

Remote Loading Parameters
Understanding a drug’s physicochemical properties and how these properties influence
its permeation through the phospholipid bilayer helps predict the drug’s remote loading
and transport throughout the body. Passive diffusion, the mechanism by which the drug
passes through the phospholipid bilayer of the liposome and into its aqueous core, is
an energy­independent process driven by the pH gradient rather than ATP­dependent
processes. For a drug molecule to cross the non­polar lipid membrane, specific physic­
ochemical properties are required. These properties include molecular weight and size,
solubility, partition coefficient, degree of ionization, surface activity, structural isomerism,
intermolecular forces, oxidation­reduction potentials, inter atomic distances between func­
tional groups, and stereochemistry [73]. Besides the drug properties, parameters such as
salt concentration of the internal aqueous phase of the liposome, temperature, gradient
magnitude, loading duration, and initial D/L ratio also affect the efficiency of remote load­
ing. Since predicting the loading efficiency is a multi­parameter problem, Zucker et al.
analyzed multiple data of remote loading drugs into liposomes using an algorithm, which
resulted into the creation of a decision tree that takes into consideration multiple loading
conditions and drug properties [45].

1.7
Drug Releaase
Drug release refers to the process by which a drug is released from its carrier system,
such as a liposome, into the surrounding environment, ultimately reaching the target site
within the body. Rational optimization of the rate of drug release is critical during liposome
design because it affects the local concentration of the free drug at the target site. For
liposomes to be effective as drug carriers, they must balance stability in storage and circu­
lation with the ability to release the drug at the desired dose within the treatment window.
This balance is particularly crucial for cell cycle­specific drugs, where the timing and rate
of drug release significantly impact efficacy [76].
Drug release from liposomes results from physical and chemical processes that compro­
mise membrane stability, leading to partial or complete leakage of the liposome contents.

2.2.4
Peptide Remote Loading
After overnight dialysis of the liposomes at room temperature, they were removed from the
dialysis cassette, and the volume of the solution was measured to assess any dilution of
the sample and, if so, by how much. The liposome solution and the peptide solution were
separately heated for one hour at 65°C. Then, based on the chosen drug­to­lipid ratio for
the remote loading, the peptides were added to the liposome solution. The mixture was
then stirred at 70°C for three hours, except when examining the remote loading parameter
of time, in which case the time of remote loading varied. After three hours, the mixture
was removed and placed in a freezer at ­4°C for 15 minutes to lock the bilayer into a liquid­
ordered state and prevent temperature­related endomorphin leakage. Once cooled, the
mixture was loaded into a dialysis cassette and dialyzed against 10 nM PBS solution with a
pH of 7.4 overnight in a refrigerator at 4°C to remove any free non­loaded compounds. All
measurements were performed in triplicate, and data were presented as mean ± standard
deviation (SD).

and reduced curvature. This stability promotes more efficient and effective loading of drug
molecules into the liposome’s aqueous core. Another possible reason could be the fact
that smaller liposomes, with their higher surface area­to­volume ratio, have the potential
for increased leakage of encapsulated drugs during loading or storage. In contrast, larger
liposomes tend to experience less leakage due to their lower surface area­to­volume ra­
tio. However, in the formulation of liposomes for medical applications, most systems

The key difference lies in the second amino acid. Kay et al. [87]studied the importance of

the failure of the other peptides to cross the phospholipid bilayer and undergo remote
loading could be the absence of the proline residue. This residue gives EM­1 a more
restricted structure, enabling it to fold in a specific way that facilitates hydrophobic Van der
Waals interactions of tryptophan and tyrosine, something that could play a key difference
in allowing the molecule to cross the phospholipid bilayer.
However, even if EM­R and EM­K could not remote loading into the first stealth liposomes,
there might be a way to enable this by altering the structure of the phospholipid bilayer.

inability to load. Therefore, modifying the lipid bilayer to provide more space for the pep­
tides to navigate might help. This necessitates studying the components of the lipid mix,
particularly cholesterol, which significantly affects the bilayer’s properties. De Meyer and
Smit [88] extensively studied the impact of cholesterol on the lipid bilayer, specifically how
the area per molecule of a phospholipid membrane changes with added cholesterol. At
relatively high cholesterol concentrations (above 20%), they observed the formation of
small, cholesterol­rich clusters, which could also disrupt the permeability for EM­K and
EM­R. As a result , new liposomes were formulated (LP2) , this time without cholesterol,

Future Perspectives
Therapeutic peptides represent a crucial area of research in pharmaceutical development
due to their specificity and potential efficacy in treating various diseases. Further inves­
tigation into these peptides is essential to fully understand their therapeutic benefits.The
findings from this study highlight the significant impact of active loading parameters such
as time, temperature, and drug­to­lipid ratio on the encapsulation efficiency. As a result ,
better understanding and optimizing these parameters are critical steps toward enhanc­
ing the formulation of peptide­loaded liposomes.Furthermore, the impact of cholesterol
on the encapsulation of EM­R opens up an intriguing area for future research. This find­
ing emphasizes the necessity for thorough investigations into the mechanisms by which
cholesterol influences the encapsulation process of these peptides, or possibly peptides in
general. Additionally, exploring alternative lipid compositions may provide insights into op­
timizing encapsulation efficiency.Regarding release studies, the experiments conducted
under specific conditions (incubation at 37°C with dialysis against PBS) provide a foun­
dational understanding of the peptides’ release kinetics. Future research could expand
upon this by exploring release profiles under varying environmental and physiological
conditions, thereby broadening the applicability of these peptide­loaded liposomal formu­
lations.

Conclusion
The peptide Endomorphin 1 and its two analogues were successfully synthesized and
characterized, achieving excellent purity and yield. Stealth HSPC:DSPE­mPEG­2k­chol
(3:1:1) liposomes were formulated in order to remote load these synthesized peptides.
The encapsulation of Endomorphin 1 in these liposomes was successful. Moreover, vari­
ous parameters, including ammonium sulfate concentration, liposome size, dialysis steps,
remote loading time, and drug­to­lipid ratio, were investigated to determine their effect on
the encapsulation efficiency of Endomorphin 1. Adjusting these parameters resulted in
the encapsulation efficiency of Endomorphin 1 ranging from 10.75% to 63.08% , proving
that the EE is directly dependent on these variables. However, the two analogues did not
successfully remote load into this liposome formulation. Further investigation suggested
that cholesterol might have hindered the remote loading of the analogues. Consequently,
new liposomal batches were formulated without cholesterol. This modification resulted
in some encapsulation of EM­R with an efficiency of 48.00% and results that suggested
a potential entrapment of EM­K in the lipid bilayer.Release studies were also conducted
and showed a fast initial release, followed by a sustained and prolonged profile, with both
peptides following a Higuchi Model of release kinetics. However, the actual release of
EM­R that was observed was minimal.
