Abstract
This project evaluates the transition from optical see­through to video pass­through XR
systems in training environments, with a strong focus on their usability, efficacy, and de­
sign considerations. Using comparative analysis, the performance of the Apple Vision Pro
was assessed against the Microsoft HoloLens 2 in contexts requiring precision­oriented
tasks. Key advancements observed in the transition to video pass­through technology
include an increased field of view and enhanced immersive fidelity, which contribute to im­
proved training experiences. However, persistent challenges, such as limited visual clar­
ity and cognitive overload from augmented elements, emerged as significant challenges.
Novel implementation of a camera enhancement mode showcases the potential for miti­
gating such limitations, though further refinement with newer hardware is necessary. The
findings emphasize the importance of designing XR training tasks with user capabilities
and task demands in mind, while also highlighting hardware innovations, adaptive frame­
works, and expert integration as critical areas for future development. Ultimately, this
thesis reflects on the promise and current constraints of video pass­through XR systems
in fostering more effective and practical training environments.

Introduction
Extended Reality (XR) is increasingly being adopted across various industries to improve
training, efficacy, and efficiency. In manufacturing environments, precision, consistency
and safety are critical and XR technologies offer unique training opportunities that could
reduce training errors, improve knowledge retention and improve costs.
Novo Nordisk, a global leader in the pharmaceutical sector, operates a high number of
complex and high­tech manufacturing plants as well employs thousands of employees
across the different production lines. The sheer number of employees, strict quality con­
trol and safety regulations require for efficient and effective training. Traditionally, this is
done with instructor­led courses and shadowing over employees, which can be resource
intensive. As an alternative, XR based training has emerged as a promising replacement,
offering a scalable solution.
At Novo Nordisk, they have already started to implement and explore XR training alterna­
tives utilizing the Microsoft HoloLens 2. Unfortunately, the Microsoft HoloLens 2 will be
discontinued in 2027 Community 2024, and with this being one of the last commercial and
enterprise viable optical see­through headsets, a need to explore other solutions arises.
Today, as the XR technology and space continues to mature, video pass­through head­
sets are increasingly becoming the technology of choice. With ongoing advancements
in camera resolutions, processing power, and displays, the headsets are set to improve
on several limitations of optical see­through headsets. Consequently, the trend is leaning
towards gradually phasing out optical see­though headsets in favor for more versatile, im­
mersive and advanced solutions.
Therefore, this research aims to investigate the transition from optical see­though head­
sets to the newer video pass­through headsets. Specifically, this master thesis will answer
the following research question: Can pass­through headsets supersede see­through sys­
tems in extended reality training environments? A comparative analysis of immersive
fidelity, user adaptability, and practical viability to identify strengths, limitations, and opti­
mal use cases for both technologies will be made to
This research is significant due to the present industry standard in extended reality head­
sets for Novo Nordisk, namely the Microsoft HoloLens 2, is going to be discontinued, and
an alternative is necessary if they want to continue with XR based training. Here’s a
revised version of the sentence to avoid the repetitive use of ”products such as”: Further­
more, Microsoft has no plans for any successors to the HoloLens 2, and industry trends
indicate a growing focus on video pass­through devices. This shift is evident in recent
releases from companies like Meta and Apple, including the Meta Quest 3 and the Apple
Vision Pro. As a result, this study seeks to explore potential strategies for organizations
like Novo Nordisk as optical see­through headsets become increasingly obsolete and
video pass­through devices emerge as the dominant paradigm for XR experiences.
Additionally, the transition to video pass­through technology offers the potential to deliver
more engaging and innovative user experiences, leveraging the strengths of video­based
platforms to unlock new opportunities for the XR based training.

Background
In the 1990s a paper by Milgram and Kishino (Milgram and Kishino 1994) presents the
Virtuality continuum trying to define and explain how terms like Augmented Reality (AR),
Virtual Reality (VR) and Mixer Reality (MR) interconnect. Since then the development of
AR, MR and VR has gone rapidly, especially since 2016 with the influx of development
of headsets, software and the interest in the products (Brigham 2017). Due to the recent
influx, the discussion of the terms for this field are still in debate. A paper by Speicher et al.
investigated the different terms and how they are used by experts and the literature, and
they found that while VR is more easily distinguished, AR and MR are quite fragmented,
as there was no agreed consensus across the experts and the theory (Speicher, Hall, and
Nebeling 2019).
2.1
XR Frameworks
The paper by Milgram and Kishino (Milgram and Kishino 1994) can be seen as the first
attempt at creating a taxonomy of the different virtual, mixed and reality headsets. The
core idea of the taxonomy is based on a ”virtuality continuum” (See Figure 2.1). To the
left of the virtuality continuum lies the complete real environment, which consists of no
digital elements, and is the real world as perceived by humans. At the opposite end is the
completely virtual environment, a fully digital space with no incorporation of real­world el­
ements. Between these two extremes, is the Mixed Reality area of the continuum, which
consists of Augmented Reality to the left of the center and to the right of the center is Aug­
mented Virtuality. Milgram and Kishino describes Augmented Reality (AR) as a primarily
real environment with virtual elements, and Augmented Virtuality (AV) as a primarily vir­
tual environment with real­world elements. In other words they differentiate between AR
and AV as the proportionate amount of either digital or real­world elements. Both of of
these terms AR and AV are under the bigger category Mixed Reality, which as the name
infers is some kind of mix between the real­world and the virtual world.

2.1.1
A new framework

paper where Rauschanbel et al. rightfully stated that the terms of VR, AR, MR and XR
are inconsistent between experts and academia, which leads to confusion and inconsis­
tencies and they propose a newer framework that can encapsulate all the terms used into
a single one, that also works for the new technology. Their new framework they call xRe­
ality and replaces the common term XR. xReality allows room for the newer technologies

2.1.2
xReality
The new framework from (Rauschnabel et al. 2022) which they call xReality, which en­
compasses all the terms, because they argue that X can be replaced with anything to
showcase any kind of reality, and not just extended reality, as Virtual Reality is a replace­
ment of reality and not an extension.
In xReality, Augmented and Virtual Reality are also deemed as two conceptually different
terms and therefore in contrary to the continuum by (Milgram and Kishino 1994) are not on
a single continuum. xReality splits the two terms apart so that there is a greater distinction
within each one.
Currently the concept of Augmented Reality gets used for different purposes and the figure
Figure 2.2 from Rauschanbel et al. showcases an overview on how the term gets used
for many different situations. Figure 2.2 shows how different devices can be classified
as AR, and how devices can use AR, and furthermore, how the displays within AR are
fundamentally different.
Figure 2.3 shows the proposed xReality framework by Rauschnabel et al. 2022. Here AR
and VR no longer share a continuum and are split between the inclusion of any physical
environments, as in any inclusion of the real world is AR and a totally virtual world is VR.
Therefore, this creates a harder split between AR and VR and with this new framework a
device or system can no longer be a combination of AR and VR.
Figure 2.3 introduces a new continuum for AR, to help distinguish between the different
types of AR as was shown in Figure 2.2. Here the continuum ”Local Presence” is set
between Assisted Reality and Mixed Reality.
Note that here, in opposition of the term mixed reality in the literature, mixed reality is
referencing the level of the local presence. The local presence is a spectrum of how a
user experiences AR objects to be a part of the physical environment. Some examples
of different types of AR can be found in the different levels of local presence.
A low local presence would be something that is head mounted and positioned at a fixed
location at all times, and could also be classified as a HUD (Heads Up Display). Another
example of increased local presence is fixed elements in the physical environment, that
are unable to be moved, and does not adapt to the physical environment by i.e. ignoring
occlusion of real world objects. An example of a very high local presence is that of a digi­
tal object, that merges well with the physical environment, by for instance, being partially
occluded by real world objects depending on the angle. Furthermore the digital objects
are fixed in the physical environment if for instance the user turns away or moves around
the environment or in other words the objects are persistent.
Similarly for the VR continuum there also exists a spectrum of telepresence, and this can
be viewed as the level of user presence, or ”sense of being there”. An example of an
atomistic virtual reality could be a low fidelity training demo the telepresence would be
low, as there would be a lack of environmental sounds or highly detailed world. A holistic
virtual reality would be a totally built for realism world, that includes everything that would
immerse a user.

Current xReality Assisted Training Environments
There are several papers and literature reviews on the use of xReality devices in profes­
sional training scenarios, and these papers identify some critical elements that xReality
help users excel in their training. These elements are: key­skill acquisition, safety man­
agement, human­computer collaboration and computer assisted cooperative work (S. Li
et al. 2024; Dodoo et al. 2025; Alnagrat, Ismail, and Idrus 2021). There have also been

several studies investigating the performance metrics of xReality based training compared
to traditional non XR training. Studies show that xReality based training shows similar or
better results compared to traditional training (A. D. Kaplan et al. 2021; Werrlich et al.
2017). The extra digital information and guidance from the xReality headset can help
trainees, however it has been shown that the xReality training is more efficient for inexpe­
rienced users of the training task (Greci 2022). This is due to experts, that already know
how to do the task, get slowed down by the extra digital information, that is not needed.
Its therefore recommended to use xReality training only until the user is sufficient with the
task.
One of the big advantages that comes with xReality training, is the accessibility, cost and
the ability to create specific training environments (A. D. Kaplan et al. 2021). For instance
with a VR training system, the users are able to do a training procedure in any location
at any time, compared to traditional training that requires scheduling and an instructor
(Greci 2022). Furthermore, an off­site VR training session could also be evaluated by
collecting data throughout the training session, and then later reviewed by experts (T.
Larsen, P. Larsen, and Bjørn 2024). However, xReality based training does also come
with its limitations from a human and technological aspects. For VR systems limitations
such as lack of immersion in VR systems, or restrictions in movement from wearing HMDs.
Other issues raised from users such as lack of image quality, field of view and general
ergonomics were also a hindrance for their training experience (Dodoo et al. 2025).
From the AR side issues such as interface and control methods limited users in their
experience. Issues with general image quality, screen brightness, positional accuracy of
the digital elements or lack of excitement due to low fidelity were also concerns for the
users (Dodoo et al. 2025).
2.3
Comparing Apple Vision Pro and Microsoft HoloLens 2
As this thesis only focuses on the Apple Vision Pro and Microsoft HoloLens 2 HMDs (Head
Mounted Display), this section will detail the main technological differences between the
two devices.
2.3.1
Microsoft HoloLens 2
The Microsoft HoloLens 2 is an Optical See Through Display and brings augmented reality
(or as Microsoft calls it Mixed Reality) immersive experience to the user (see Figure 2.4).
The HoloLens 2 was released in 2019 following the successful release of the first gener­
ation HoloLens which was released in 2016.
The Microsoft HoloLens 2 works by blending virtual objects with the real world. Since
the HMD is an optical see­through headset, and does not use any camera feeds to show
the real world, it needs to understand the real world in some other way. The headset
does this by creating primitives of the real world that represents flat surfaces in the envi­
ronment, and are called SceneQuads (Microsoft 2025). On top of SceneQuads you can
place digital objects, however SceneQuads themselves do not allow for the occlusion of
digital objects behind objects in the environment. To include occlusion the headset needs
to generate ”watertight” meshes that generates a planar understanding and 3D under­
standing of the environment, which allows the headset to calculate occlusion of digital
objects. However due to the extra processing power of watertight meshes means in a
greater latency Microsoft 2025.
The Microsoft HoloLens 2 display is a combination of waveguide and light projectors which
superimposes holographic images onto the users vision. The resolution of the display is

Apple Vision Pro
The Apple Vision Pro is a xReality headset that Apple describes as a ”spatial computer”
being able to both function as a Virtual Reality and Augmented Reality HMD and was
released throughout 2024.
The Apple Vision Pro uses a micro­OLED display, delivering an impressive 23 million
pixel resolution and a 100° field of view. The device is equipped with 12 cameras and 8
sensors (see Figure 2.5), supporting advanced spatial awareness, user interaction, and
environmental mapping. Its video pass­through design captures the physical environment
in real time, blends it with digital objects, and then displays the combined scene for the
user on the internal screens. A study also compared the video pass­through capabilties
of multiple HMDs and it was shown that the Apple Vision Pro had the best pass­through
capabilties (Vona et al. 2025). Sensors such as LiDAR and inertial measurement units
enable precise tracking and allow for realistic placement and occlusion of virtual objects
within the real world. Additionally, advanced eye­tracking capabilities provide features like
gaze­based rendering and gaze­based interaction (Zeng 2025; Inc. 2023).
2.4
Comparison of Video Pass­Through and Optical
See­Through Systems
The debate between the use of Video Pass­Through (VPS) and Optical See­Through
(OST) systems in AR applications has persisted in the literature, with foundational stud­
ies and recent advancements offering valuable insights into their respective trade­offs.
Rolland et al. conducted one of the earliest studies comparing these systems, highlight­
ing critical trade­offs such as system latency, occlusion handling, and fidelity in rendering
real and virtual elements (Rolland, Holloway, and Fuchs 1994).
While OST systems excel in providing an unprocessed and latency­free view of the real
world, their inability to render realistic occlusion remains a major limitation. This drawback
restricts their effectiveness in scenarios where highly realistic virtual content integration
is required (Rolland, Holloway, and Fuchs 1994). Modern VPS systems, have made sig­
nificant steps in this area, offering enhanced occlusion rendering via advanced video pro­
cessing and high­resolution displays. These improvements have made VPS preferable

Regardless of the display technology, the effectiveness of these systems is influenced

2.5
Cognitive Load Theory
Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), introduced by (Sweller, Merrienboer, and Paas 1998), pro­
vides a framework for understanding how the human cognitive system processes and
retains information, especially during learning and problem­solving tasks. CLT proposes
a understanding of long term memory (LTM) and working memory and their interaction.
When information is stored in long­term memory, it is organized into structures known as
”schemas”. According to CLT, working memory is very limited, and therefore needs to be
transferred to a schema and linked to other schemas.
When the short capacity working memory gets overloaded, it first makes learning inef­
fective, and it decreases the performance of the user (Merriënboer and Sweller 2005).
Therefore there must be efforts to reduce cognitive load, and CLT identifies three types
of cognitive load:
• Intrinsic Load: This refers to the inherent complexity of the task or material be­
ing learned. It depends on both the content and the learner’s prior knowledge. In
XR environments, intrinsic load is affected by the complexity of the tasks and the
interface’s design.
• Extraneous Load: This load results from irrelevant or unnecessary elements or
information to the task at hand. This can stem from poorly designed instructional
materials or interfaces that distract the learner from the primary task. Extraneous
load does not transfer to long term memory, and therefore needs to minimized to
learn effectively. In XR, extraneous load may arise due to overwhelming visual ele­
ments, poorly aligned overlays, or excessive interaction requirements (Parong and
Mayer 2018).
• Germane Load: This is the cognitive effort that comes when forming schemas and
linking schemas with existing schemas in LTM. In contrary to the other two cognitive
Evaluating the Transition from See­Through to Pass­Through XR in Training Environments
8
==PAGE:15==
loads, the germane cognitive load positively impacts learning, and schema gener­
ation, effectively improving performance and learning (Sweller, Merrienboer, and
Paas 1998).
In XR environments, the application of CLT involves balancing these three types of load,
ensuring that intrinsic load is appropriate for the learner’s expertise, extraneous load is
minimized to avoid unnecessary distractions, and germane load is optimized to boost
meaningful learning.

Method
In this project there were two different studies made to evaluate the transition from optical
pass­through to video pass­through. The first study consisted of a direct comparison
between two identical apps both on a OST headset and VPS headset. The goal of the
first study was to find the main differences based on a set of criteria between the two
technologies. The second study aimed to take full advantage of the capabilities of the
VPS headset by expanding the app from the first study with VPS specific functionalities
and having an expert evaluation on the benefits of a VPS system.
For the VPS system the Apple Vision Pro headset was chosen. This headset was chosen
due to it being a very new VPS headset that brings a very high quality camera, resolution
and comfortability. Based on the study by Vona et al. 2025 they concluded that out of
the three tested headsets (Meta Quest 3, Varjo XR3 and Apple Vision Pro) that the Apple
Vision Pro was the best in terms of pass­through quality.
The Apple Vision Pro also brings some form of familiarity with it being an Apple product
and many people are familiar with the UI design of Apple, which may aid the trainees in
navigating the headset.

Training Task

3.5
Implementation
This section will delve into some of the more technical details of the applications developed
for this thesis. This section will be split up into two different sections, one for the first
iteration of the AVP app and one for the second iteration of the AVP app. The technical
details of the HoloLens app will not be discussed as it was developed before the start
of the project. Both iterations of the app were developed in SwiftUI for VisionOS in the
XCode coding environment.
3.5.1
First Iteration of AVP App
The aim of the first app was to have it resemble as closely as possible to the HoloLens
version of the training task app. Fortunately, this process was streamlined by extracting
the JSON file from the HoloLens app, and using this to build the backbone of the AVP
app.
A specific JSON parser was made in Swift, that could read the specific parts that the
HoloLens version of the app does. These parts included:
• Current Task Step Text Information
• Current Task Step Picture
• Current Task Step 3D Model Name
• Current Task Step 3D Model Position
The step text information and picture were combined into a SwiftUI window to describe
the current step information. The step 3D model was used to load asynchronously into
physical environment. However, the 3D model position could not be directly translated
from the HoloLens version to the AVP version. This is due to the fact that the two sys­
tems use two different axis, and furthermore that the HoloLens positions are based from

==PAGE:21==
an anchoring of a QR code, that gets scanned as part of the calibration process for the
HoloLens app. To solve this issue, it was decided to utilize the pass­through aspect of the
Apple Vision Pro headset and to use object recognition for the anchoring of the 3D models.
An already pre­made 3D replica of the pipe system was used to train an object recogni­
tion machine learning model to identify and locate an anchor on the real­life pipe system.
Therefore, the 3D models for the steps, were manually set according to the anchor found
the pipe system. This also means that the system was constantly updating the positions
of the 3D models, according to the position found for he pipe system anchor for each
frame, which in contrary to the HoloLens app only sets the anchor point once before the
training task starts.

Enhance Mode
By far the most prominent issue from the participants was the lack of visual clarity for
seing smaller details. The training task required viewing smaller details in two steps. The
step when the user is instructed to insert the correct end of the pump output, and also
reading the pressure gauge current pressure. Most, if not all participants had issues with
both of these steps, as the small details became blurry, when participants tried moving
closer with the Apple Vison Pro. Therefore participants were either asking for help by the
investigator, or guessing.
The Enhance Mode aims to solve this problem by giving the user an enhanced image,
solely for viewing smaller details. It aims to do this by zooming in, and by using a sharpen
and luminance filter that helps pop out smaller details, especially in low light scenarios
(Horiuchi, Watanabe, and Tominaga 2007; Demir and N. Kaplan 2023; C. Li et al. 2022).
It was important to keep the image filter fairly basic, as to not use too much processing
power, and to keep the image processing as real­time as possible.
Voice Commands
Through questioning, some of the participants stated that they were discontent with the
need to use their hands to proceed to the next step in the training task. In the HoloLens
version, the users were able to point the headset at the next button to progress, as in
having a hands­free option.
Voice commands would therefore act like a hands­free option for the users to proceed
to the next step, leaving their hands free to be ready for the next step. The addition
of Voice Commands also opens up for more oppurtunities. One participant noted the
need to look back and forth between the floating instructions and the pipe system was
slightly annoying. The addition of voice commands can also lead to a hands­free option
to have the instructions spoken out loud, giving the user more freedom in how they want
to complete the task, and improving the user and training experience Kondratova and
Emond 2020.
Hand Tracking Technology
Some of the participants stated that the labels of the valves were hard to read with the
Apple Vision Pro, which goes back to the visual clarity issue. However, instead of relying
on the Enhance Mode feature to read the labels, a hand tracking approach was done
instead. This was due to the fact that the users needed to interact with the valves for the
task, and the labels popping up above the valves would act as a visual feedback system,
assuring the user that they are about to manipulate the correct valve.
Audio Recognition
An important suggestion that some of the participants noted was that they wanted to know
when they had successfully completed a step, as a form of concurrent feedback. For in­
stance, some of the users wanted the step information to automatically continue if the
headset recognizes that the step had been completed successfully. This form of auto­
matic detection of step completion was explored, by using object recognition to see the
current state of a valve, and this proved too unreliable. Therefore, an automatic check
was explored to determine if the user had completed the task successfully, indicated by
the whistle blowing. The audio recognition system was then created to listen for whistling
sounds, and only if it hears the whistle i.e. the user completes the training task correctly,
a confetti entity and animation will play from the location of the whistle. The confetti was
specifically chosen to hopefully give the user a pleasant experience, congratulate the user
on completing the training and to use the peak­end rule (Kahneman et al. 1993), leaving
the users satisfied with the experience.

The participants noted some quality of life additions, that would make their overall experi­

that the app has proceeded to the next step. This is also works in tandem with the voice
commands, if for instance, the user uses a voice command to go to the next step, the

therefore not be able to see that the highlighters change location, when they proceed to
the next step.
To combat this, the highlighters were updated, to be thicker, and changed the color and
material to a shiny pink/purple hue. This should make the highlighters easier to see, and
grab the participants attention easier.

Discussion
5.1
First Study Interpretation
The results of the first study provide several key insights regarding the implementation
and user experience of transitioning from see­through to pass­through HMDs. While the
task performance with the Apple Vision Pro system showed marginal improvements over
the HoloLens system, the results were not statistically significant, but there was a clear
trend. For the first study most of the users had never used any form of HMD before,
and the task performance results might therefore show that the Apple Vision Pro is more
intuitive to use. The larger FOV could also be of assistance as users would require less
head movements to find the next area of interest.
Figure 4.1 shows the results of the questionnaire for the first study. The only question
of note, that was significantly different, was Question 5, which regarded perceived delay
while wearing the headset. Apple has reported that the pass­through latency of the Apple
Vision Pro is around 12 ms, whereas in the HoloLens there is practically no latency, as
the user is always viewing the real world.
The perceived reduced delay experienced with the Apple Vision Pro can be attributed
to its advanced hardware, including its powerful processing capabilities and optimized
sensor integration. These features and sensors create a quicker and more stable ren­
dering of digital elements, particularly when users move their heads, ensuring that virtual
objects remain anchored in the environment with minimal jitter or lag. In contrast, while
the HoloLens integrates into the real world, its digital overlays may exhibit slight move­
ment or take longer to stabilize, especially during head motion, potentially contributing to
discrepancies in user perception of delay.
Participants generally expressed a preference for the AVP system, highlighting features
such as improved field of view (FOV) and visual clarity in terms of resolution. Some the­
matic analysis of user comments further underscored the importance of these factors for
overall satisfaction and usability. However, these subjective preferences were counterbal­
anced by usability challenges observed during the experiments. Notably, the outwards
cameras making the pass­through blurry, and therefore hindering them in the training
task. Multiple users also noted the ergonomics of the Apple Vision Pro, stating that it
was too heavy, especially compared to the more counterbalanced design of the Microsoft
HoloLens 2. This issue points towards a solution consisting of more robust hardware, with
better cameras and better ergonomics by making the headset lighter, or less front loaded.
Another significant observation was that the realistic visual design of the AR content oc­
casionally caused participants to misinterpret digital overlays as part of the physical envi­
ronment. Although the realism is advantageous for immersion, it also risks reducing the
clarity of system guidance. This finding suggests a need for distinct visual cues that dif­
ferentiate guidance overlays from the real­world environment, to ensure the participants
are aware of the digital elements.
Taken together, the findings from the first study reinforce the importance of iterative design
and testing to balance realism, usability, and user guidance in XR training systems. Future
iterations should focus on addressing the identified challenges, including visual clarity of
guidance elements, and mitigating cognitive overload, to optimize both user experience
and training outcomes.

5.2
Experts View on Video Pass Through
Expert Interviews (E1­E4) provided varied and nuanced perspectives on the feasibility
and efficacy of video pass­through devices within Novo Nordisk and XR training environ­
ments. Visual Clarity was something that all experts discussed and revolved around the
outward facing cameras of the Apple Vision Pro, and video pass­through headsets in gen­
eral. Despite the Apple Vision Pro’s impressive cameras and improvement from previous
generations of familiar headsets, the AVP was still not clear enough for all parts of the
training task. E2 explicitly stated that the limitation the base cameras, necessitates even
further advancements in display and camera technology for HMDs, particularly when train­
ing tasks require precision, or the need to see small details. This further goes into was E3
highlighted, that the lack of visual clarity on the AVP would limit, what training scenarios
are ideal for the AVP. They further explained that a training scenario with larger items, and
less small details would be more ideal training task.
The Enhance Mode feature was introduced to the experts, and the goal of the feature
was to help the visual clarity on the AVP. The Enhance Mode feature worked by taking
the outwards camera image, and streaming it directly in front of the user with an image
filter and the ability to zoom in.
Between the experts the feature worked differently, however the experts did agree that
the feature had potential. E1, stated that the Enhance Mode feature worked well for them,
and that it allowed them to see things they could not without the feature. In contrary, for
E2 the feature did not work, and in reality did the opposite and made the clarity worse.
Ilo, Zheng, and Bowman 2021 developed a virtual zooming function for a pass­through
system for surgeons, and received similar feedback from their participants. For instance
the resolution was too poor when zoomed in, but did recognize the potential benefits of a
zooming feature. Therefore, this suggests that an Enhance Mode or a zooming feature
for the purpose of seeing smaller details, is worth pursuing, but currently the hardware is
still not sufficient and results in poor resolution.
The findings on visual clarity in general align with other studies, that suggest visual clar­
ity of video pass­through systems to be a big hurdle (Kim et al. 2025; Domingues et al.
2024). Enhance Mode was an attempt at fixing the visual clarity, but is clearly either not
useful yet with the current hardware capabilities, or does not work as well for some users,
and is thus unreliable. Other suggestions have been made to help with visual clarity for
reading text, by for instance using a OCR or object detection, that would detect letters
or known objects and replace them with virtual ones (Kim et al. 2025). The problem with
this approach is that the virtual items need to be perfectly anchored, for this method to be
advantageous. In this project, OCR was implemented to help recognize the numbers on
the pressure gauge, but results were inconsistent and unreliable. Difficulties arose when
the numbers on the pressure gauge was partially obstructed, leading to incomplete recog­
nition. Similarly, object recognition was attempted on the valves but did not yield better
results, as the valves were too small for the detection algorithms to reliably identify. One
factor that was positively noted by all experts was the increased FOV, especially when
comparing the FOV of the Apple Vision Pro the HoloLens. E4 remarked that the wider
FOV creates a big benefit for the Apple Vision Pro, and that it positively impacted the
training experience. This also closely relates to the findings in literature, where the FOV
of the Microsoft HoloLens has been criticized, and can make tasks slower, compared to
a higher FOV headset (Brunzini, Papetti, Messi, et al. 2022).
However, a paper by (Klinkert 2023) discuss that increased FOV might not always be ben­
eficial for every training scenario. They argue that for nonstationary training tasks, where
the user needs to perform a task while walking, with a large search space, may lead to

==PAGE:37==
distraction or split attention. This is due to the user needing to observe their surroundings
meanwhile searching for digital objects in their view, and with a larger FOV means a larger
search space.
Thus, it is important to value the training task at hand, and if the search space or FOV will
not lead to distractions in the users environment.
The impressive display of the Apple Vision Pro was an important factor for the experts.
This was due to the possibility of adding high resolution videos to the training experience.
For this specific training task, it might not have been necessary, as the steps were very
simple. However, for more advanced and complex training scenarios, the addition of
videos could be beneficial (Tani, Manuguerra, and Khan 2022). With the precise gestures
of the Apple Vision Pro, the user could also be free to manipulate the video to the user
needs. Furthermore, the high fidelity digital elements could also be used for animations,
where animations has been shown to give users a better retention of demonstrated mo­
tions (Ploetzner, Berney, and Bétrancourt 2021). For instance there could be a floating
animation with a 3D replica of the model, showing the motion needed for the current step.
From the second iteration of the AVP app, experts largely agreed that the updated digital
highlighters, while designed to improve visibility and highlight key elements, were overly
distracting. This feedback underscores the importance of ensuring that visual aids in video
pass­through applications strike a balance between functionality and simplicity to avoid
contributing to cognitive overload (Bautista, Maradei, and Pedraza 2023). The concern
over cognitive overload was further emphasized with the introduction of hovering labels,
which appear when the user’s hand is near one of the valves. These labels aimed to pro­
vide additional guidance, E4 noted that any feature not absolutely essential to completing
the task, could unnecessarily increase cognitive demands and detract from the training
task. However, expert opinions were not entirely negative, as one expert expressed a
favorable view, noting that the hovering labels enhanced their overall experience, sug­
gesting that such features may require careful design and user­specific customization to
adjust to varying preferences and cognitive capacities. With Cognitive Load Theory (CLT),
this can also be seen as minimizing the extraneous load, by removing irrelevant informa­
tion of labels, and by optimizing the intrinsic load by adjusting the complexity of the task,
by either introducing or removing task information.
Figure 4.4 shows that out of all the features, the experts preferred the audio recognition
feature the most. This shows potential for other integrations of audio recognition, and can
create meaningful pass­through experiences.
Figure 4.5 demonstrates the individual responses of each expert, providing insight into
how different features performed across the different experts. From the results, it is ev­
ident that certain features were more effective for some experts than for others. For in­
stance, the Enhance Mode was perceived as highly beneficial by some, possibly due to
differences in eyesight. Similarly, one expert found the label pop­ups particularly useful as
they did not experience a significant cognitive overload, suggesting that these augmented
features can vary widely in effectiveness based on individual preferences. This variation
implies that, to ensure a successful and engaging experience, VPS training apps should
emphasize personalization, tailoring features to the specific needs of users. Such an ap­
proach would address diverse requirements, enabling each individual to benefit from the
features that suit their abilities, skill­level and preferences.
In summary, the experts characterized the second video pass­through iteration for the

Apple Vision Pro as a significant step forward in xReality training environments, while
underscoring the current limitations. The primary challenge remains the poor visual clar­
ity for tasks requiring precision, which limits the device’s suitability for certain training
scenarios with smaller details or that require precision. The improved field of view was
regarded as a strong advantage, but its effectiveness may be limited in dynamic condi­

as the search space increases. Meanwhile, the integration of high­resolution media and
interactive features holds promise but must be carefully designed to minimize extraneous
cognitive load. Finally, personalization of the experience is a must, to select or deselect
features as needed, so the user never feels overwhelmed by the inclusion of too many
features, or feel that the experience is underdeveloped and missing extra guidance.
Ultimately, the experts emphasize that video pass­through systems must evolve through
hardware improvements and user­centric design to become a viable replacement for op­
tical see­through systems for training within xReality.
5.3
Designing Training Tasks for Video Pass­Through
The feedback collected from the experts and participants underscores the importance of
thoughtful training task design in xReality applications. A key takeaway from the second
iteration of the AVP app was the necessity of simplifying augmented visual aids. Features
such as hovering labels and digital highlighters, while intended to improve guidance, often
contributed to cognitive overload. However some users did appreciate the extra guidance,
and therefore it would be beneficial to have the option to turn of extra guidance or not.
Similarly to the findings of (Greci 2022), that suggests xReality based training is best for
novices, the amount of guidance can be adjusted for the user’s expertise and cognitive
capability. For instance a complete beginner would need the extra assistance remember­
ing the names of the valves but as the user acquires more experience would no longer
need the extra cognitive burden with the labels of the valves, and could turn it off. This
can also be seen as optimizing the intrinsic cognitive load, to adjust the complexity of the
task according to the users previous knowledge and skills.
Training tasks must also consider the physical and spatial behaviors of users in XR envi­
ronments. Tasks requiring close proximity to artifacts, such as reading gauges or manip­
ulating valves, require careful designing, so that users are able to correctly manipulate or
use said artifacts. This was found to be an issue in both studies. One possible solution to
this is to physically scale the artifacts larger, so that the user can clearly see all the neces­
sary details. This approach does come with the disadvantage of not realistically reflecting
the real size of the artifacts outside of the training. Another attempt at solving this issue
with Enhance Mode feature shows to be a promising solution. However, either it does
not work as well for some users, or the current hardware of the cameras are not sufficient
yet and, therefore is unreliable. Lighting can also be considered for training scenarios,
as well­lit training environments make it easier for the participant to see the real world
with the pass­through cameras, and might assist the user with visual clarity. Adjusting
the lighting may also come with the disadvantage of creating a mismatch between the
training and production environments. In a factory, there is no guarantee for the lighting
conditions, and training in a perfectly lit environment might create unrealistic expectations
for the trainee.
The inclusion of multimedia is also an important consideration when building training tasks.
Allowing the user to have videos, showing exactly how a specific step of the training task
is done, could be more beneficial than a simple photo and accompanying text. Personal­
ization of the experience is still important, and the option to omit videos will also lead to

==PAGE:39==
less extraneous and intrinsic load for experienced users, who no longer needs the step
specific videos.
The experts preferred the audio recognition feature the most, and therefore is worth ex­
ploring when designing tasks for video pass­through. The inclusion of audio coming from
the training task, can trigger different functions from the pass­through HMD, and could be
used to closely replicate the real in­production task. For instance, a gas leakage sound
could be be set to a specific pitch, the headset recognizes this sound, and then plays
an in­headset gas leak visual, to illustrate to the user where the gas leak is coming from.
Furthermore, object recognition is also a strong attribute that video pass­through headsets
bring, and should be utilized. Object recognition could for instance, be used for anchoring
the visuals for the training task, or as previously mentioned anchor specific parts in the
training environment to work in collaboration with the aforementioned audio recognition.
5.4
Limitations
5.4.1
Experimental Design
Participants
A limitation that occured with this study was with the participant pool and number. Origi­
nally the study was designed to be tested on participants, that already use XR for training
purposes, or someone that has experience in production within Novo Nordisk. Due to
testing only being suitable in normal working hours, and the target group being very busy
with other work activities a compromise had to be made. Thus, participants were ran­
domly selected from the IT building, who have no experience within production, but they
were controlled to not be experts within XR, to simulate a new trainee without much prior
experience.
Training Environment
All experiments and trials took place in the Immersive Technologies Lab within Novo
Nordisk. The lab is located in the basement of the building and had poor lighting. This
may have caused an effect on the results of the studies, as some of the issues the par­
ticipants faced could be worsened by the poor lighting. For instance the pressure gauge
was poorly lit, which made it even harder to see from the inside of a HMD. Testing was
done by flashing a flashlight from a phone onto the pressure gauge, which confirmed that
it was easier to see the numbers from within the HMD, when the pressure gauge was lit.
The results might therefore be biased, however, compared to having a perfectly lit room,
the testing environment might better reflect an on­site training or production facility, where
perfect lighting is not always guaranteed.
5.5
Future Work
5.5.1
AI Agents
A promising avenue for future development is the integration of AI agents within the video
pass­through application. By leveraging the outward­facing cameras of devices like the
Apple Vision Pro, an AI­powered system could provide users with enhanced situational
awareness and contextual problem­solving capabilities. This AI agent could dynamically
analyze the environment, offering guidance, identifying objects, and diagnosing issues
in real time. Such functionality would be particularly valuable for complex tasks, where
users could rely on AI feedback to improve performance and efficiency (Bovo et al. 2025).
One especially engaging concept, highlighted by E1, is the addition of personalized AI
agents linked to the user’s personal account. These agents could adapt to each user’s
specific needs by tapping into historical data from previous training sessions or accumu­
lated knowledge. The ability to recall prior task histories, preferences, and skills would
Evaluating the Transition from See­Through to Pass­Through XR in Training Environments

==PAGE:40==
allow the AI to provide highly tailored support. For instance, an AI agent could act as
a personalized assistant, reminding the user of steps they previously struggled with or
proactively offering assistance when challenges arise. This personalized approach could
bring a more intuitive and immersive training experience, empowering users to develop
and long­term proficiency.
Image Filters
As an extension of the Enhance Mode feature, future AI agents could enable natural
language­driven image manipulation. For example, users could interact verbally with the
AI to adjust image filters dynamically in Enhance Mode. A user might command the AI to
highlight specific colors, such as asking it to ”make the green more prominent” to enhance
clarity around task­relevant elements. Similarly, users could request specific adjustments,
such as increasing brightness, sharpening edges, or focusing on high­contrast zones to
meet real­time needs. These dynamically adjustable filters would be a beneficial addition,
allowing trainees to customize their visual environment based on personal preferences or
the demands of the task.
5.5.2
Hardware
One of the reoccurring limitations identified in this project is the insufficient visual clar­
ity of current video pass­through technology. Both experts and participants in the both
both studies widely acknowledged the need for higher­resolution cameras and improved
display technology. Fine details, such as numerical readings or small mechanical compo­
nents, were noted as particularly challenging to resolve. Improved camera specifications
and display fidelity would not only enhance overall usability but could also open new av­
enues for training scenarios where precision is critical.
Future studies could focus on the specific impacts of improved visual clarity on task per­
formance and precision. For instance, research could investigate whether an upgraded
camera system significantly affects completion times, accuracy rates, or user satisfaction
metrics in training tasks. This approach would quantify the implications of hardware im­
provements, to investigate even further if the technological domain of video pass­through
will further improve, or if more outstanding changes are needed.

Conclusion
This project investigated the evolving role of video pass­through technology in xReality
training environments, highlighting its strengths, challenges, and future prospects. The
Apple Vision Pro demonstrates significant progress, with features like its improved field of
view and its impressive display that contributes to a more immersive training experience.
However, persistent limitations, particularly with visual clarity for tasks requiring high pre­
cision and the cognitive load from complex augmented overlays, remain as issues, if not
considered in the designing of the training task. The feedback from experts reinforced
the importance of tailoring features such as the Enhance Mode and hovering labels to
user preferences to reduce cognitive overload and adapt to varying needs and skill levels.
While features like high­resolution media and digital highlighters hold potential, they must
be carefully calibrated to align with individual user preferences and skill levels, ensuring
both usability and accessibility, while minimizing extrinsic and intrinsic load. Importantly,
this study emphasizes that xReality training environments for video pass­through must
consider the specifics of the task, such as the level of required precision, size of the arti­
facts and lighting of physical environment to maximize the efficiency of video pass­through
systems.
The hypothesis posed in this study—whether pass­through headsets can supersede see­
through systems in XR training environments—has been partially addressed. Pass­through
systems, such as the Apple Vision Pro, can potentially offer significant advantages in im­
mersive fidelity and flexible system adaptability due to technological advancements like
wider fields of view and digital overlay customization. However, their practical viability re­
mains limited by unresolved issues, including the need for better visual clarity, hardware
enhancements, and disconnection from the real world. Therefore, while pass­through
headsets show promise in enhancing certain kinds of training tasks, they are not yet
poised to fully replace see­through systems. Instead, both technologies have unique
strengths and limitations, with optimal use cases depending on the specific requirements
of the training task and the expertise of the user. For tasks requiring high precision or mini­
mal digital distraction, see­through systems may still be preferable, whereas pass­through
systems excel in immersive, media heavy, or larger­scale training scenarios.
To summarize, future efforts should prioritize advancements in hardware, personalization
of features and UX, and the integration of adaptive training experiences to address cur­
rent limitations. By doing so, pass­through xReality systems could solidify their role as
versatile tools that directly complement or supersede see­through systems in selected
training contexts, offering new possibilities for xReality based training.

Methods 
 
The study is conducted in a laboratory environment using one of two XR headsets. The 
participant puts on the XR headset and follows the instructions within the headset. The 
participant will need to go through a training demo with a real-life pipe prop.  
 
Tasks and Procedure 
 
The study proceeds as follows: First, we gather information about your job title, responsibilities, 
and prior XR experience. You will then be introduced to the XR headset and familiarize yourself 
with its controls. Afterwards you will start the XR application, which consists of a training demo 
using a real-life pipe prop. While using one of two XR headsets, you will follow the instructions 
provided within the headset and complete the required tasks. During the session, we will record 
your task performance, verbal feedback, and written feedback to assess the effectiveness of the 
experience. After this you will repeat the process with the other headset.  
 
 
Risk Assessment 
 
Please note that you may experience symptoms of motion sickness such as nausea, dizziness, 
headaches, disorientation or eye strain, or feel uncomfortable wearing the headset. If you have 
any previous experience with motion sickness within XR or augmented reality we recommend 
you to not participate. Otherwise, we do not expect any risks from participating in this study. 
 
 
Voluntary Participation 
 
Participation in this research is entirely voluntary. It is the participant’s choice whether to 
participate or not. They can change their mind later and stop participating at any point even if 
agreed earlier. 
 
 
Confidentiality 
 
The collected data through the performance measures and questionnaires will be made 
anonymous; your name will not be recorded and you will be provided with a participant number 
known only to the investigators. Participants’ data will not be shared with any third parties, only 
with the investigators.

The goal of this study is to find the differences between two different extended reality 
technologies in a training environment.

improve extended reality enhanced training within Novo Nordisk.

Methods 
 
The study is conducted in a laboratory environment using one of two XR headsets. The 
participant puts on the XR headset and follows the instructions within the headset. The 
participant will need to go through a training demo with a real-life pipe prop.  
 
Tasks and Procedure 
 
The study proceeds as follows: First, we gather information about your job title, responsibilities, 
and prior XR experience. You will then be introduced to the XR headset and familiarize yourself 
with its controls. Afterwards you will start the XR application, which consists of a training demo 
using a real-life pipe prop. While using one of two XR headsets, you will follow the instructions 
provided within the headset and complete the required tasks. After the first trial you will repeat 
this with the second version of the app. 
After both trials you will be interviewed by the investigator who will ask some questions 
regarding your experience with the app, and potential follow up questions. The whole interview 
will be recorded and transcribed, and you may be quoted for the report.   
 
 
Risk Assessment 
 
Please note that you may experience symptoms of motion sickness such as nausea, dizziness, 
headaches, disorientation or eye strain, or feel uncomfortable wearing the headset. If you have 
any previous experience with motion sickness within XR or augmented reality we recommend 
you to not participate. Otherwise, we do not expect any risks from participating in this study. 
 
 
Voluntary Participation 
 
Participation in this research is entirely voluntary. It is the participant’s choice whether to 
participate or not. They can change their mind later and stop participating at any point even if 
agreed earlier. 
 
 
Confidentiality 
 
The collected data through the performance measures and questionnaires will be made 
anonymous; your name will not be recorded and you will be provided with a participant number 
known only to the investigators. Participants’ data will not be shared with any third parties, only 
with the investigators.
