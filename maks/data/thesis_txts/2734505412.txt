Resumé
I løbet af de seneste år er konsekvenserne af den globale opvarmning og klimaforan-
dringerne blevet tydeligere og tydeligere.
Som konsekvens af disse vil vandstanden i
verdenshavene med tiden stige, hvilket vil føre til øget kysterosion. Samtidig truer biodi-
versitetskrisen mere end en million dyre- og plantearter med at uddø grundet ændringer i
arealudnyttelsen. Derudover har kysterosion også en negativ effekt på biodiversiteten i de
kystnære områder. For at håndtere disse udfordringer er der blevet undersøgt nye metoder
til kystbeskyttelse, som både tager højde for kystsikring og havmiljø. Disse studier har
affødt forslag om at bruge kunstige rev under havoverfladen som kystbeskyttelse.
I dette studie undersøges brugen af MIKE 3 Wave Model FM til at lave detaljerede anal-
yser af hydrodynamik over og omkring kunstige rev til kystbeskyttelse. Studiet fokuserer
på strømninger og hydrodynamik, når effekten af permeabilitet medtages. I analyserne
bruges både to- og tredimensionelle domæner.
Ved brug af et todimensionelt domæne er bølgetransmissionen for forskellige typer rev
blevet undersøgt ved brug af MIKE 3 Wave Model FM. Disse rev omfatter impermeable
og permeable rev, samt en kombination af disse to. Resultaterne fra simuleringerne er
blevet sammenlignet med resultater fra fysiske modelforsøg. Dimensionerne af det mod-
ellerede rev var identiske med de fysiske modelforsøg.
De indledende analyser viste, at simuleringstiden, antallet af sigmalag, medtagelsen af
turbulens, antallet og placeringen af bølgemålere samt metoden til at modellere perme-
able rev alle havde betydning for resultaterne. Den endelige modellering, med inddragelse
af konklusioner fra indledende analyser, viste, at resultaterne fra simuleringerne overord-
net set var identiske med resultaterne fra modelforsøgene. Dette var især tilfældet for
Rc/Hm0 ≈−1, men i mindre grad for Rc/Hm0 ≈−0.5. Anlyserne af bølgespektre for
de indkommende og transmitterede bølger viste, at middelperioden for de transmitterede
bølger var kortere end for de indkommende bølger.
Ved brug af et tredimensionelt domæne er strømningsmønstre og bølge set up omkring
et nedsænket impermeabelt rev ligeledes blevet analyseret ved brug af MIKE 3 Wave
Model FM. Til disse analyser blev der benyttet tre domæner med forskellige bredder.
Størrelsen af det maksimale bølge set up var mere end tre gange størrelsen af den teo-
retiske værdi, hvilket indikerer, at MIKE 3 Wave Model FM overestimerer denne værdi.
Strømningsmønstrene i de tre domæner gav forskellige forudsigelser af, hvorvidt erosion
eller aflejring ville være resultatet af sedimenttransporten bag det nedsænkede rev. De to
største domæner forudså erosion, mens det mindste domæne forudså aflejring.

Abstract
In recent years the presence and the consequences of the global warming and the climate
changes have become more and more evident. Over time this will lead to a rise in the
sea level enhancing coastal erosion. Simultaneously, the global crisis in biodiversity is
threatening the existence of more than a million animal and plant species, on land as well
as under water, due to changes of the land use. Coastal erosion also has a negative impact
on biodiversity in the coastal areas. In order to cope with both challenges, methods for
coastal protection, that both take the need for coastal protection and the need for a
strengthening of the marine environment into account, have been studied previously. In
recent studies the establishment of a submerged artificial reef for coastal protection has
been proposed.
This study examines the use of the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM to make detailed analyses
of the hydrodynamics at a submerged artificial reef for coastal protection. The study
focuses on the flow and hydrodynamics around a submerged reef when taking the effect
of permeability into consideration. The study has used two-dimensional domains as well
as three-dimensional domains for the analyses.
Considering a two-dimensional domain, the wave transmission has been analysed by use
of the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM for different types of submerged reefs. This includes an
impermeable reef, a fully permeable reef and a combination of the two types. Results
from the simulations have been compared to the reported results from physical model
tests. The modelled setup was similar to the setup in the physical model tests.
The initial analyses showed that the simulation time, the number of sigma layers, the
inclusion of turbulence, the number and location of wave gauges and the approach to
modelling a permeable reef all affected the results. The final modelling, taking all previous
experiences into account, showed that the results from the simulations generally were
comparable to the benchmark results. This was especially the case for Rc/Hm0 ≈−1
but to lesser degree for Rc/Hm0 ≈−0.5. Analyses of the wave spectra of the incident
and transmitted waves showed that the mean wave period of the transmitted waves was
shorter than for the incident waves.
Considering a three-dimensional domain, the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM has been used to
assess the hydrodynamics over and around a submerged, impermeable reef in terms of
the flow patterns and wave set up. Three cases were considered with domains of different
widths. The size of the maximum wave set ups were more than three time the theoretical
value indicating that the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM overestimates this value. The flow
patterns in the three domain sizes did not agree on whether erosion or accretion would be
the result of the net sediment transport at the shoreline behuind the submerged reef. The
two larger domains predicted erosion, while the smallest domain predicted accretion.

Introduction
Throughout the last decades the presence and the consequences of the global warming
and the climate changes have become more evident than ever before. According to Danish
Meteorological Institute (2023), the mean temperature in Denmark has increased by 1.5
◦C over the last 150 years. The main reason is the emission of greenhouse gases over the
entire world. The immediate consequences in Denmark are increasing amounts of rain,
more frequent heat waves and more frequent storms and storm surges. Generally, the
weather conditions are becoming more and more extreme. Over time this will lead to a
rise in the sea level, in Denmark this rise is expected to be around half a meter in this
century (Juhl and Røjle, 2023). As the Danish coasts are exposed to erosion from waves
and currents the rising sea level will cause increased coastal erosion. This will make some
coastal areas uninhabitable as houses and infrastructure risk to fall down on the beach or
become swallowed by the sea as a result of the coastal erosion.
Simultaneously, the global crisis in biodiversity is threatening the existence of more than
a million animal and plant species (Behrendt, 2019).
The main reason for this crisis
is the changes of the land use, both on land and under water, meaning that plants and
animals have less area to live in. In worst case this crisis in biodiversity could threaten the
existence of humans as the nature is essential in terms of food production and production
of medicine. Coastal erosion also has an impact on biodiversity in the coastal areas as
erosion of fine sediment will lead to a more steep cross shore profile.
This could be
devastating to places where plaice and eels typically live (Frausing et al., 2023).
1.1
Previous initiatives
In Denmark all the initiatives related to coastal protection are designed and constructed
in accordance with the Danish Act of Coastal Protection (Kystbeskyttelsesloven). This
act states that all initiatives related to coastal protection must aim to disrupt the natural
processes in the sea as little as possible even though all initiatives will have an impact to
some degree.
Coastal protection in Denmark, with the aim to protect a low-lying area from flooding,
will typically consist of dikes. An example is the Danish Wadden Sea where dikes protect
the landscape from tides and storm surges. Dikes take up a significant amount of space
and thereby affect the nature in the coastal regions.
At the west coast of Denmark the concept of beach nourishment is used. Beach nourish-
ment is a process where sediment is lost through longshore drift or erosion and is replaced
with sediment from off shore locations. By doing this, the net erosion at the coast is
compensated by adding new sediment to the coast. This new sediment is collected off
shore and transported to the coast. The use of beach nourishment has a negative impact
on the wildlife in the coastal regions and especially for those living on the bottom of the
sea. The reason for this is that the addition of a thick layer of sediment will suffocate the
ecosystem at the bottom of the sea (Juhl and Røjle, 2023).

As an addition to beach nourishment, large stones and other hard structures can be placed
along the coast. The purpose of the stones is to interfere with the waves to reduce the wave
height and the wave energy. This means that local erosion will be reduced. In Denmark
typical passive protection methods are groynes where the stones are placed perpendicular
to the shoreline, wave breakers where the stones are placed parallel to the shoreline and
slope protection. In most cases there will be a need of beach nourishment as a supplement
(Frausing et al., 2023).
All the methods mentioned above will to some extent affect the nature and the wildlife
in the coastal regions as they change the dynamic conditions when it comes to currents
and erosion. This is motivation for natural coastal protection. Back in time reefs made
of stones placed on the sea bottom have contributed to coastal protection as natural
coastal protection. Over the years many stones and stone reefs have been removed as a
result of construction of piers and other coastal structures. Even though stone fishing was
illegalised in 2009 the reefs have never been rebuild. This means that today the structures
are missing resulting in a naked sea bottom without any protection against waves and no
calm places for wildlife (Frausing et al., 2023).

The cross section of the submerged reef is sketched on figure 1.2. The reef is submerged

with a nominal diameter of d50 = 0.20 m. The stability of the reef is described in Erik
Damgaard Christensen (2023).

The performance of the reef in the BARREEF project has been analysed in B. E. Larsen
and Erik Damgaard Christensen (2023b) by use of the numerical model MIKE 21 devel-
oped by DHI. MIKE 21 has been used to perform morphodynamic simulations with the
shoreline model, which is a coupled model in MIKE 21. The waves are solved by using
a spectral wave model, where the inputs from the spectral wave model are passed to a
non-linear shallow water model (NLSW) to calculate the depth-averaged currents. The
report mainly focuses on the shore line response and to lesser degree on the hydrodynamics
locally around the submerged reef.
The technical report for the project (B. E. Larsen and Erik Damgaard Christensen, 2023b)
briefly assesses the wave transmission over the reef by assuming that the reef is submerged
and impermeable and results from the simulations are compared to an expression of the
wave transmission in van Gent et al. (2023). The reef is modelled as a change in the
bathymetry, so that the reef is considered impermeable. Hence the performance of the
numerical modelling of the submerged reef is not considered.
1.3
Detailed modelling of hydrodynamics
The existence of research on emerged detached breakwaters in the literature is substantial
(e.g. Erik D. Christensen, Zanuttigh, and Zyserman (2003) and Hsu and Silvester (1990)).

Contrary to this, the effect of submerged breakwaters has been studied to a lesser extent.
According to literature (e.g. Tsiaras, Karambas, and Koutsouvela (2020)) a submerged
breakwater will generate an onshore directed current over the reef, where the emerged
breakwater typically will block the flow. In existing literature the wave transmission over
submerged reefs for coastal protection is generally seen limited to physical model tests
and derivations of empirical expressions for the wave transmission based on the physical
model test (d’Angremond, Van Der Meer, and De Jong (1996) and van Gent et al. (2023)).
In this study the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM is used to make detailed analyses of the hydro-
dynamics at a submerged artificial reef for coastal protection. The study focuses on the
flow and hydrodynamics around a submerged reef when taking the effect of permeability
into consideration. The main focus is on wave transmission, wave reflection and wave
energy dissipation. This study aims to set up the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM in a way that
makes it possible to describe the wave transmission so that the results are comparable
to those obtained in physical model tests. In order to make the best possible setup, the
numerical model is calibrated against results from physical model test (van Gent et al.,
2023) considering both an impermeable reef and a permeable reef with a given porosity
and stone size. Following the calibration the numerical model will be used to analyse
different cases in order to assess the wave transmission, reflection and dissipation. The
calibration and the following analyses will performed in two-dimensional vertical domain
subject to one-dimensional irregular waves. Lastly the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM will be
used to assess the hydrodynamics over and around a submerged reef in a three-dimensional
model in terms of flow patterns and wave set up. This makes it possible to predict the
net sediment transportation behind a submerged reef.
With the ability to model artificial reefs on a detailed level, the positive effects of the
artificial reefs will increase and become much clearer. This is due to the ability during
the design phase to model and test various reefs in different sizes, with different shapes,
in different geographical locations, under different external forces and with different com-
positions of stones. Hence the reefs will have the best possible dimensions and properties
when used as part of the answer when dealing with the global challenges related to the
climate changes and the crisis in biodiversity.
1.4
Report structure
The structure of the report is as follows. In chapter 2 the relevant theoretical background
for this study is presented. The following chapter 3 presents the study used for calibration
of the numerical model and method for decomposing incident and reflected waves. The
model used in this study is described in chapter 4 and validated in chapter 5.
Chapters 6 and 7 present the use of the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM in a two- and three-
dimensional domain respectively. In each chapter the domains and the model set ups are
described. In chapter 6 the numerical model is calibrated against results from physical
model tests. In chapter 7 the behaviour of the three-dimensional model is assessed.
Lastly, a conclusion is made based on findings in chapter 8 followed by a short description
of recommendations for further studies in chapter 9.

2.5
Wave transmission
When a wave approaches a slope, a wave tongue will run up the slope. This phenomenon
is called a wave run-up. At some point the wave tongue will reach its maximum elevation
above still water level (SWL), where the vertical distance from to the momentary sea level
is called the run-up level. If the crest of the slope is lower than this run-up level, the wave
tongue will pass over the crest of the slope. This means that water will be transmitted
over or through the structure, which is called wave transmission. Wave transmission is
the average quantity of water passing over the crest expressed in m3 per running meter
crest per second. (d’Angremond, Van Der Meer, and De Jong, 1996)
If there is an area of plane water on the rear side of the structure, the transmission of
water will generate waves on the rear side. The size of these waves will generally be
smaller than the waves on the front side of the structure. The ratio between the wave
height of the transmitted waves on the rear side, Ht, and the incident waves on the front
side of the structure., Hi, is called the wave transmission coefficient, Kt. This is written

Time integration is performed by using a semi-implicit scheme, where vertical convec-

is chosen in order to remove the stability limitations related to the vertical resolution.
Additional terms are discretised using a second-order explicit Runge-Kutta scheme. The

simulation of flows involving discontinuities of any kind. This quality is absolutely essen-
tial when modelling waves in the breaking zone or porous structures, something highly
relevant in this study. Here, the numerical dissipation accounts for the dissipation in the
breaking waves so that numerical stability is continuous.
4.2
Areas of application
The MIKE 3 Wave Model FM has a wide range of areas, where the model can be applied.
One area is ports an terminals, where the model can be used to assess wave agitation
caused by short and long waves and to make input to a dynamic ship mooring analysis in
MIKE 21. Another area is coastal areas, where the model can be applied to the analysis
of fields and phenomena like nonlinear wave transformation, surf and swash zone hydro-
dynamics, wave breaking and run-up, coastal flooding and tsunamis modelling. In the
area of coastal structures the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM can be applied to the analysis of
wave overtopping and wave transmission and reflection through porous structures. The
last area is offshore environments where the model can be used to assess transformation
of steep nonlinear waves and three dimensional wave kinematics for structural load cal-
culations. Hence, the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM has a wide range of areas of application
within the field of waves and interaction between waves and structures.

Domain
The first thing to consider when setting op MIKE 3 Wave Model FM is the domain.
The domain is describing the area to be modelled in terms of bathymetry, resolution and
boundaries. In this study rectangular domains are used with xdomain describing the domain
size in the x-direction and ydomain describing the domain size in the y-direction. The mesh
consists of rectangular elements with size ∆x and ∆y, a total number of elements nelements
and a total number of nodes nnodes. For this study the map projection Local Coordinates
is used in all domains.
The vertical resolution of the domain is described by a structured mesh based on sigma-
coordinates. The elements in the sigma domain and can be prisms or hexahedrons, where

Flood and dry
This module takes into account situations where the model has areas where flooding and
drying may occur. This could for instance be a beach where areas close to the sea are
alternately wet and dry. In the event that the water depth is less than the selected wetting
depth, hwet, the flow problem is reformulated, where the momentum fluxes are set to zero
and only the mass fluxes are taken into consideration. In the event that of water depth
being less than the selected drying depth, hdry, the element is completely removed from
the simulation.

additional stress terms in the governing equations are present in order to account for
the non-resolved processes. These processes can be described through the eddy viscosity
using the gradient of the mean quantity, meaning that the effective shear stresses in the
momentum equations will both contain the laminar stresses and the Reynolds stresses.
This means that turbulence will occur.

sky formulation and two-equation turbulence model (k-ε). In the vertical direction the
options are no eddy, constant eddy formulation and two-equation turbulence model (k-

domain decomposition concept and Message Passing Interface (MPI). Depending on the
number of processor cores allocated for the simulation, the computational mesh is split (or
partitioned) into smaller subdomains, hence the workload for the domain is distributed
between the allocated cores. The data exchange between subdomains is performed by
message passing using the Intel MPI Library, which has multi fabric message passing
capabilities. This allows the use of mixed communication between the domains.
The partitioning of the domain is done by using the METIS graph partitioning library.
The generated computational mesh is converted into a graph, from which the METIS
uses a multi-level graph partitioning scheme to split the graph into subgraphs, that each
represents a partitioned subdomain. The subdomains are distributed among the allocated
cores. Next the METIS computes a balanced partitioning based on the horizontal 2D mesh
with the goal to minimize the connectivity of the subdomains. In order to get a balanced
partitioning for a 3D mesh, the weights corresponding to the actual number of vertical
elements are used. Hence, for both 2D and 3D meshes, this way of partitioning a domain
ensures that the number of elements in all subdomains are almost equal. This method
requires an overlapping domain decomposition, which is based on a ghost-cells approach.
This means that each subdomain contains elements from connected subdomains as a result
of calculations requiring values from the connecting elements.

varies from time step to time step. Hence linear interpolation is used to determine the
horizontal velocity in 80 position over the depth. If the horizontal velocity is not defined
in a location at a specific time step, the value is set to 0. Finally, the period averaged
velocity in each vertical position is calculated as a mean value over the entire time interval.

5.2
Wave reflection analysis
In order to separate the incident and reflected waves at different positions in a given
domain, a MATLAB tool irregularWRA is used. This is based on a paper by Zelt and
Skjelbreia (1992) where incident and reflected wave fields are estimated by using an ar-
bitrary number of wave gauges. In order to verify the usefulness of this tool for irregular
waves, a synthetic series of one dimensional surface elevations in time and space is gener-
ated and tested. By using this synthetic series the ratio between the size of the left and
right going waves, in terms of the significant wave heights Hm0 and Hm0−r, will be known
before using it as an input to the tool. Hence this ratio is expected to be equal to the
wave reflection coefficient Kr.

If a completely permeable reef (figure 6.6) is modelled, the reef is described by use of
porosity zones (the red area). This means that the depth of the domain will not vary
over the reef but will be constant. In the domain a volume (defined by a number of cells)

be able to flow through the porosity zone with a resistance that depends on the specified
friction coefficients and the local velocity.
If a combined reef is modelled (figure 6.7) a combination of the previous described tech-
niques will be used. In the BARREEF project the reef consists of an inner core layer
and an outer cover or armour layer, see figure 1.2, which is an example of a reef with two
layers. In the combined reef the inner core layer (the blue area) is described as a local
change in the bathymetry, meaning that the depth of the domain varies over the reef. The
outer cover layer (the red area) will be modelled by use of porosity zones with a specified
porosity and grain size diameter. This means that water will be able to flow through the
outer layer of the reef.

Until now the analyses of hydrodynamics over and around artificial reefs have been lim-
ited to two-dimensional cases. In order to avoid situations where water is held back on
the rear side of the submerged reef, a sponge layer was used in the model to absorb wave
energy and changes in the surface elevation. If the intention of the model is to model
an actual submerged reef for coastal protection, this would be an inconvenient way of
modelling the coastline. The sponge layer does not take the coastal profile and the re-
flection into consideration. Therefore a three-dimensional model is introduced to take the
three-dimensional behaviour of a submerged reef into consideration in the analyses. As
this analysis is much more heavy computational wise than the two-dimensional analyses,
the analysis is limited to an impermeable reef subject to irregular waves with a significant
wave height of Hm0 = 0.10 m, resulting in a size of the non-dimensional free board of
Rc/Hm0 = −1. The analyses in the three-dimensional cases cover both wave transmission
and flow patterns.

7.2
Setup
The setup of the three dimensional model is generally similar to the two-dimensional
model. However, the increased width of the domain requires some additional consider-
ations for reduction of the simulation time. For the two-dimensional model, the refine-
ment of the domain in the x-direction waves ∆x = 0.05 m and a single cell with size
∆y = 0.20 m in the y-direction. In the three-dimensional model the refinement is reduced
to ∆x = 0.10 m and ∆y = 0.10 m in order to reduce simulation time. Later results show
that the effect of the decreased refinement is limited.
In the simulations in the three-dimensional domain only the impermeable reef is consid-
ered. This means that the number of sigma layers can be reduced following the results
from the convergence analysis in figure 6.9. The figure shows that for an impermeable reef
with Nσ = 15, the wave transmission coefficient has almost converged to a stable value.
Therefore Nσ = 15 will be used in the three-dimensional model. This will also contribute
to the reduction of the simulation time.
For the three-dimensional simulations the turbulence module is enables using a k-ε for-
mulation. The reason for the inclusion of turbulence is that if there were no turbulence,
there would not be anything to damp the flow in domain, except for the bottom friction.
Hence the flow velocities will become too high.

Conclusion
In this study the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM has been used to make detailed analyses of
the hydrodynamics at a submerged artificial reef for coastal protection. The study has
focused on the flow and hydrodynamics around a submerged reef when taking the effect
of permeability into consideration.
Considering a two-dimensional domain, the wave transmission, wave reflection and wave
energy dissipation have been analysed by use of the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM for different
types of submerged reefs. This includes an impermeable reef described by a change in the
bathymetry, at fully permeable reef described by a porosity zone and a combination of
the two types where the inner core layer is described by a change in the bathymetry and
the outer cover layer is described by a porosity zone. Results from the simulations have
been compared to the reported results from physical model tests. The modelled setup
was similar to the setup in the physical model test.
The initial analyses showed that the simulation time, the number of sigma layers, the
inclusion of turbulence and the number and location of wave gauges all affected the results.
The modelling of the wave transmission over and through a permeable reef showed the
most similar results, when compared to physical model tests, when a constant peak wave
period was used for all values of the non-dimensional freeboard in combination with the
use of the combined reef with two layers.
The final modelling, taking all previous experiences into account, showed that the results
from the simulations generally were comparable to the benchmark results.
This was
especially the case for Rc/Hm0 ≈−1 but to lesser degree for Rc/Hm0 ≈−0.5. Results
showed that the wave transmission for permeable reefs with d50 = 10−3 mm and d50 =
10−6 mm compared to the wave transmission for an impermeable reef varied depending
on the non-dimensional freeboard. It was expected that the wave transmission would be
more identical. This could indicate that the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM behaves differently
when impermeable reefs are modelled by a change in the bathymetry and by a very small
grain size diameter in a porosity zone. Analyses of the wave spectra of the incident and
transmitted waves showed that the mean wave periods of the transmitted waves are shorter
than for the incident waves. Hence, the mean wave periods decrease to approximately 92
% to 98 % of the value for the incident waves when the waves are being transmitted over
or through the reef. These results were in accordance with the results from the physical
model tests.
Considering a three-dimensional domain, the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM has been used to
assess the hydrodynamics over and around a submerged reef in terms of the flow patterns
and wave set up. Three cases were considered with domains of different widths. The
analyses of the flow patterns showed that the size of the domain had a significant impact
on the shapes of the circulation cells as most of the water is leaving the coastal zone
as close to the domain boundary as possible. The size of the maximum wave set up at
the shoreline was compared to a theoretical value calculated by using a method from
the literature. The size of the maximum wave set ups were more than three time the
theoretical value indicating that the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM overestimates this value.
For future simulations the chosen value for the drying depth should be smaller than the

The flow patterns in the three domain sizes did not agree on whether erosion or accretion

Further studies
During this study many choices have been made, where other specifications and ap-
proaches could have been used as well.
Many choices have been made with respect
to computational time, computational capability, storage capability and progress in the
work. Therefore, suggestions for further studies within the study of hydrodynamics over
and around artificial reefs for coastal protection are presented in the following.
The analysis of the wave transmission over and through a submerged reef have been
limited to a range of the non-dimensional freeboard, Rc/Hm0, from Rc/Hm0 ≈−1 to
Rc/Hm0 ≈−0.5. Therefore, it could be interesting to consider other ranges, for instance
Rc/Hm0 ≈−2.5 to Rc/Hm0 ≈−1, to see how the MIKE 3 Wave Model FM behaves. In
addition, the influence of different parameters of the reef, for instance the slope of the
sides or the crest width, could be studied to a greater extent.
The analyses of the wave transmission have, in this report, been limited to a setup similar
to the physical model test described in van Gent et al. (2023). Hence the MIKE 3 Wave
Model FM has not been used to assess the wave transmission for a more realistic setup with
dimensions applicable in real life projects, for instance the BARREEF project. Therefore,
it could be interesting to use the model for more realistic setup and see how the model
behaves with a realistic coastal profile instead of a sponge layer.
The analyses of the wave reflection have shown that the accuracy of the wave reflection
analysis is highly sensitive to the number and position of wave gauges. For future work
using the WRA tool, it would be relevant to assess the wave gauge setup in more details.
In this study, the results for the wave reflection on the rear side of the submerged reef have
indicated a significant amount of reflection coming from the sponge layer. This amount
is assessed to be higher than what is realistic meaning that another setup of the wave
gauges would have been advisable.
For the analysis using the three-dimensional domains in varying sizes, a relatively steep
coastal profile with a slope of 1:10 was considered. For further analyses a less steep coastal
profile should be considered in order to make the dimensions more similar to situations
in real life. This would also move the submerged reef further away from the shoreline
leaving more space for development of the flow patterns between the submerged reef and
the shoreline.
For the analyses using the three-dimensional domains in varying sizes, only waves propa-
gating perpendicular to the shoreline were considered. This was due to the consideration
of reducing the computational costs and time, but still keep a relatively fine discretisa-
tion inside the domain. However, the flow patterns behind a submerged reef are highly
sensitive to the angle of the incoming waves, see e.g. B. E. Larsen and Erik Damgaard
Christensen (2023b), and thereby the sediment transport as well. Hence the modelling
of larger three-dimensional domains using MIKE 3 Wave Model FM would be of very
high interest. This accounts both to the assessment of the changes in the flow patterns
for various setups as well as the assessment of the flow patterns where the effect of the
boundary of the reef becomes less significant.

Throughout this study the transportation of sediment over and around a submerged reef
has been assessed to limited extend. One of the main purposes of creating these reefs
is to protect the coast from eroding away. Hence it could be interesting to consider the
derived transportation of sediment, both in a two-dimensional domain as well as in a
three-dimensional domain. This could for instance be scour around the submerged reef,
sediment transportation through a permeable reef or changes in the coastal morphology.
The inclusion of sediment transportation can be done using the MIKE 21/3 Coupled
Model FM.
