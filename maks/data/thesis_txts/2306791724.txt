Abstract 
Complexity in operation of electricity systems suggests the need to evaluate their threats in terms of 
environmental feasibility as well as potential hazards to external agents or their own efficiency. 
Environmental impacts are evaluated through Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), which allows for the 
evaluation of inputs, outputs and potential environmental impacts of a product system throughout its 
lifecycle. Threats may also be examined while identifying risk sources and their potential consequence of 
occurrence on a system. These two assessment tools are applied on a case study concerning electricity 
production in Vietnam, with focus on wind power. The aim is to assess whether the electricity sector is 
moving towards a beneficial and feasible future in environmental terms, and identify the contribution of 
wind power employment to this direction. The employment of wind power in Vietnam, under the guidance 
of UNEP Wind NAMA proposal, is investigated.  LCA is applied on five comparative sub-scenarios which 
illustrate electricity generation in Vietnam for years 2016 and 2030, incorporating different energy policy 
directions. Furthermore, a qualitative and semi-quantitative risk identification approach is followed in order 
to evaluate vulnerability to external factors for wind power employment in the country. The study proves 
that expected increase in total electricity and coal consumption in the future are crucial parameters that hide 
a potential rise of the environmental burden, compared to current electricity production practices. 
Nevertheless, projected incorporation of renewables, especially wind energy, is a clear benefit for the 
fulfillment of Vietnam’s sustainability development goals regarding greenhouse gas emissions. Focusing 
on wind power systems, it is concluded that the inclusion of risk-based considerations may affect LCA 
outcomes, since loss in expected electricity may account for additional environmental impacts, which would 
be otherwise ignored.

1. Introduction 
 
 
Vietnam has been recognized as one of the most vulnerable sites to climate change and extreme climatic-
related events globally (Khanh, 2011). Since 1992 Vietnam has been a Party to the United Nations 
Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and supports mitigation of Greenhouse Gasses 
(GHG) emissions, throught the development of  a low carbon economy (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(a), 
2016); (Decision 1775/QD/TTg, 2012); (Socialist Republic of Vietnam, 2003). Several strategies and 
national plans have been adopted towards promoting low carbon and sustainable development.  Relevant 
sustainability targets set by those include: the ‘National Energy Development Strategy (2007)’, which 
suggests that renewable energy (RE) technologies cover 8% of the total primary commercial energy supply 
by 2025, reaching 11% in 2030; the ‘National Power Development Plan for the period 2011 – 2020 (PDP 
7) (2011)’ outlines a target of RE share up to 4.5% by 2020 and 6% by 2030 in total power generation, as 
well as installed wind capacity reaching 1,000 MW in 2020 and 6,200 MW in 2030. Concerning GHG 
mitigation, the ‘Greenhouse Gas emission management (2012)’ suggests reducing GHG emissions 
generated from the energy sector by 8% until 2030, compared to 2005 (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(a), 
2016). Within this context, and under the guidance of United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), 
Nationally Appropriate Mitigation Actions (NAMAs) are prepared, offering the opportunity to evaluate 
alternatives to business as usual directions, with focus on low-carbon pathways (Olsen, et al., 2015). The 
sector of energy is selected, where expected increasing in future demands shall be aligned with GHGs 
mitigation, offering ground to the promotion of renewable energy technologies in the country (ISPONRE, 
2009); (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(b), 2016).

Latest reporting concerning Vietnam’s electricity generation mix shows a high dependence on fossil fuels 
(coal, oil products & natural gas, 53%) and hydropower (45%) in 2013 (IEA, 2013). Adopted policies and 
plans promoting green energy will facilitate the substitution of fossil fuels by renewable sources of energy. 
In alignment to this, current projections of future electricity demand and trend of fuels’ usage predict 
increased usage of renewable energy. Nevertheless, anticipated economic growth will affect electricity 
demand and may rise the country’s dependence on fossil fuels, especially coal (UNEP DTU 
PARTNERSHIP(a), 2016). Wind power possibilities have been identified to be high in Vietnam, which has 
the greatest wind potential among all countries in Southeast Asia (26,700 MW at speeds over 6 m/s) (AWS 
Truepower, 2012). In this context, deployment of wind as energy source and assignment of wind power 
plants seem as a relevant action to reduce dependency on fossil fuels. 
As part of this approach, and supported by national ministerial decisions (Decision No.: 37/2011/QD-TTg, 
2011), Supporting program for wind power development in Vietnam1  is an attempt which encourages the 
operation of 48 assigned wind power projects2 in Vietnam and potential additional projects that may 
contribute to the achievement of national sustainability targets (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(b), 2016). 
Within this NAMA proposal, special focus is put on targets set by the PDP 7, which suggests future 
substitution of fossil fuels for electricity production by wind power, with total capacity up to 1,000 MW in 
2020 and 6,200 MW in 2030.

Wind NAMA proposal provides an informed presentation and evaluation of the 48 wind power plants’ 
employment progress. Substantial factors and obstacles that affect their implementation are analyzed. Wind 
power plants require the safeguard of technical points, which derives from their complexity of infrastructure 
as well as dependency on meteorological and environmental conditions (Kuznetsova, et al., 2014). 
Technical support and infrastructure in Vietnam is not accordingly advanced, leading to greater exposure 
of community members and infrastructure to threats, and also additional efforts are needed for plants’ 
development and maintenance, compared to sites where wind power is fully practiced. Consequently, 
evaluating risks connected with wind resources exploiting in Vietnam, and the degree to which those affect 
wind power generation, is rendered an interesting assignment to support decision making around renewable 
energies (Tung, et al., 2012). In addition, the extent to which renewable energy facilitates GHGs mitigation 
is also of interest. Calculation of environmental impacts associated with the implementation of Wind 
NAMA projects is of concern, with the scope to broaden the evaluation of the plan’s environmental 
performance to additional impact routes beyond GHGs emissions. Identification of risks allows for the 
expression of uncertainties linked to wind power projects and Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) offers a collection

of the inputs and outputs as well as a visualization of potential environmental impacts of the system (Aven 
& Kørte, 2003); (Cámara, et al., 2010); (ISO 14040, 2008); (ISO 14044, 2008).  
The present study aims at assessing the environmental impacts that may derive from future electricity 
production in Vietnam, and also provides for an identification of risks related to the planned deployment of 
wind power in the country. Thereupon, five relevant sub-scenarios that describe electricity generation in 
Vietnam are developed and undergone life cycle assessment, allowing for a comparative evaluation of RE 
employment and alterations in electricity trends in future consumption of Vietnam, by year 2030. The five 
sub-scenarios are part of two developed comparative scenarios; Wind power deployment (Wind Power 
scenario) is compared against a business as usual situation (BaU scenario), where electricity is generated 
ignoring the wind NAMA projects.  
The ultimate goal is to provide policy-makers in Vietnam with insights on the potential of wind power to 
contribute to low-carbon development and important factors (hazards) which shall be taken into account 
while implementing the wind plants. Within its scope, the study provides an identification of environmental 
impacts generated by each of the examined cases, extending the forecast about CO2 – eq potential emissions 
to other environemtnal compartments. Moreover, important risks associated with wind power employment 
are outlined and their importance of their consideration while assessing the environmental burden of a 
system is tested. In this direction, a qualitative, site-oriented for Vietnam, evaluation of risks associated 
with wind power systems is conducted.

2. Methodology 
 
 
The section contains the description of the main elements of the followed methodology while conducting 
the present project work. It is segmented according to the different examined pieces, being the development 
of scenarios to be evaluated, Life Cycle Assessment and Risk Identification. 
2.1 Methodology review 
The scope of study is to evaluate the potential of wind power technology to contribute to low carbon 
development of Vietnam and identify important factors (hazards) which shall be taken into account while 
erecting wind power plants.   
The first element of the applied method is to define alternative decision cases for electricity generation in 
the country according to different temporal and policy criteria. Business as Usual circumstances are 
compared to the employment of RE and wind power in Vietnam, under the design of relevant comparative 
scenarios. The two scenarios (Business as Usual-BaU and Wind Power) allow for the evaluation of 
preferable solutions in a decision-making context.  
The scenarios in reference are undergone Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), with the aim to depict their 
environmental burden. This methodology has been proven to be significantly useful as support mechanism 
for sustainability policy making and twist towards more environmentally safe solutions (Varun, et al., 
2009). This justifies the relevance of the following analysis to support sustainability decision making,  
concerning the environmental burden of energy generation in Vietnam. 
In addition, the focus of study into wind power systems extends the assessment towards a diagnosis of 
hazards which may negatively affect their performance, making use of Risk Analysis guidelines. Potential 
causes and subsequent loss in electricity production from wind power systems are identified, and this loss 
is incorporated into LCA results in order to pinpoint the relevance of incorporating risks while evaluating 
==PAGE:21==

the environmental performance of a certain system. The two tools are worked on in parallel, deriving and 
evaluating their results.  
2.2 Description of scenarios 
Two comparative scenarios, which facilitate the evaluation of the environmental performance of the 48 
registered wind power projects, are generated. Electricity generation in Vietnam is assessed for both present 
and future time, with years 2016 and 2030 being the most representative ones. Year 2030 is chosen as being 
the latest time when electricity projections and targets are readily available, and also, as the earliest point 
when all Wind NAMA projects may be assumed to be operating. Business as Usual (BaU) scenario includes 
measures and policies that have only been set into action until 2016, thus Wind NAMA proposal is absent. 
Under Wind Power scenario, only 3 wind farms operate already in 2016 and the total number of 48 wind 
power projects are part of the electricity mix by the year 2030.

2.3 Life Cycle Assessment 
Life Cycle  Assessment (LCA) is a ‘compilation and evaluation of the inputs, outputs and the potential 
environmental impacts of a product system throughout its lifecycle’ (ISO 14044, 2008). International LCA 
standards suggest that an LCA application is fully described by Goal & Scope definition, Life Cycle 
Inventory Analysis (LCI), Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) and Interpretation of results (ISO 14040, 
2008); (ISO 14044, 2008); (European Commission, et al., 2010). It is applied on the described system with 
the goal to compare the environmental impacts of electricity production in Vietnam in five separate cases 
(sub-scenarios), differentiated according to temporal criteria and policy directions. The ultimate target is to 
identify which policy directions facilitate the alignment with reducing CO2 – eq emissions in the country, 
putting special attention on wind power systems.   
Results are obtained during Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) phase, which aims at ‘understanding 
and evaluating the magnitude and significance of the potential environmental impacts for a product system 
throughout its life cycle’ (ISO 14044, 2008). Inventory flows are translated into impacts to natural 
environment, human health and resource depletion through impacts to midpoint categories. In technical 
terms, this step offers an evaluation of the potential environmental impacts of the system under assessment.   
The application is realized with the aid of SimaPro 8.1 software, a program designed by Pré Consultants, 
which is facilitates LCA and other sustainability study types (PRé-Sustainability, website). The modeled 
system is segmented into five alternative sub-scenarios, all of which are designed according to LCA 
standards and methodology and their derived modeling results are discussed. The electricity mix in each 
case comprises of technologies as defined in Table 1, and included activities cover fuels acquisition, power 
plants’ construction, energy needs during start-up, operation and maintenance (O&M) of the plants, as well 
as decommissioning and waste management of infrastructure elements.  
The function of the system is ‘to provide for electricity from seven electricity technologies and cover the 
power needs of Vietnamese consumers in 2016 and 2030’. The modeled system follows a qualitative 
functional unit (FU) basis. Sub-scenarios are designed in order to be functionally equivalent in a qualitative 
ground at the overall electricity system level. This implies that they all fulfill the same function across year

2.4 Risk Identification 
Wind turbine enterprise is connected with risks which have a potential to affect their normal operation. Risk 
is defined as the ‘expected consequence associated with a given activity’. Within the scope of study, risk 
based decision making is a relevant approach to identify risks connected with wind power plants’ entreprise. 
Risk based decision making helps evaluate risks related with a system in an optimal way, in terms of safety, 
economic or environmental costs (Faber, 2009). It lies on conducting risk analysis of a system, which 
encapsulates all activities around identification of risk sources, consequences, and likelihood of 
consequences occurrence (ISO 31000, 2011). Final decision basis offers an optimal system solution, where 
costs are minimized, or benefits are maximized (Faber, 2009).  
==PAGE:27==
13 
 
The present study considers the basics of risk based decision theory in order to provide an insight into 
hazards that may affect wind turbines’ projected operation in Vietnam. Due to constraints related with time 
and data availability, the study is limited to a qualitative and semi-quantitative basis, rather than rigorous 
quantitative risk analysis and assessment. Constraints that limit the assessment to a qualitative level are 
related with time availability limitations regarding the complexity of the system in reference, as well as 
case-specific data need which have not been accessible (i.e. wind turbines type and technical characteristics 
in all projects, mechanical drawings, materials of infrastructure, specific location and distance from societal 
and agricultural areas, population in proximate locations).

Followed process within this context regards the final evaluation of wind NAMA projects’ performance, as

2.4.1 Hazard Identification 
The first step is Hazard Identification, in an attempt to identify hazards that pose a risk to the reliability of 
wind turbines (WTs), ot their ‘ability to operate under designated operating conditions for a designated 
period of time’ (Modarres, 1993). A failure factsheet (Table 3) presents all the identified hazards and 
resulted failures that any WT system may undergo, and offers a concrete understanding of the boundaries 
of the risk identification section of the study. In general, it may also serve as a useful datasheet in detailed 
==PAGE:28==
14 
 
reliability assessment applications. This factsheet is a result of literature review, which facilitated the 
collection of risks associated with WTs enterprise.  
Wind turbine enterprise is associated with various uncertainties which generate risks during their 
installation and operation. Some of those pose a threat to external agents, like, in example, the safety of 
wildlife and human beings in case of contact with turbine components due to an accident or malfunctioning 
(Carrete, et al., 2009); (Robinson, et al., 2013); (Sørensen, et al., 2012); (Macqueen, et al., 1983); (Cronin, 
2015). Other hazards lead to negative effects on turbines’ normal operation, because they may cause 
interruption of efficient power generation or, even, failure of components or systems that are part of a wind 
power plant. It is important to pinpoint these uncertainties in order to secure for a reliable and safe power 
system (Hill, et al., 2008). According to Spinato et al. (2009), failure describes the ‘inability of a component 
to perform its required function under defined conditions’. This definition implies that a failure mode leads 
to downtime and affects the availability of a wind turbine, or ‘the ability of a wind turbine to produce 
electricity when wind speeds are adequate’, as further explained in continuance (Hill, et al., 2008); (Kozine, 
et al., 2000).

2.4.2 Availability Assessment 
A following step in the risk evaluation segment is the availability assessment of wind turbine systems. 
Availability assessment is the most significant metric when evaluating the performance of wind power 
plants, given its paramount relevance for assessing energy production and operation of wind turbines, and 
lies within the guidelines of reliability assessment (Hill, et al., 2008). Availability assessment  provides 
insight of the probability that a system remains operational, accounting for all failures that affect a WT’s 
ability to produce electricity due to the time spent on repairs (Hill, et al., 2008). Therefore, availability 
factor offers information on the effect of failure and repair on energy production, whereas reliability does 
not account for time spent on repairing and maintenance (Kozine, et al., 2000). 
Various studies have examined wind power systems’ availability, accounting for outage due to failures and 
repairs in emergency cases. This type of assessment is specific for each wind turbine type, and collection 
of corresponding real data is required. Needed information concerns type and frequency of failures and the

resulting downtime during fault conditions5, repairing and maintenance. Evalutaion of the system in 
reference is connected with constraints regarding data extraction to describe fault conditions of wind 
turbines (failure rates of mechanical components), either due to inaccessibility of such information or 
because most of the projects are not already in operation (more elaborated in Appendix 2.I).

Due to aforementioned constraints, the study is performed on a literature-based approach, utilizing collected 
descriptions and data through relevant availability studies on wind power systems. Literature review proves 
that there is not one uniform way to represent wind turbines’ availability and that there are multiple 
available methods. Examples and detailed explanation of previously applied approaches, as obtained from 
literature, are presented in Table 4. Respective studies are either purely qualitative, or also quantitative, 
providing results to depict loss in performance of wind turbine systems.  
Collecting the main elements of previously realized studies on availability of wind turbine systems is 
rendered important for assessing differences or similarities among them. Despite the fact that each approach 
suggests different calculation method and formula, they are all based on a uniform concept. This 
encapsulates consideration of fault duration, either expressed by measured downtime or, alternatively, 
identified worked time. Frequency of failure events is also relevant, allowing for inclusion of annual outage 
period due to failures. Apart from failure modes, in some cases delays due to logistical issues or outage for 
scheduled preventive maintenance are also accounted for. There are studies which realize the assessment 
for one turbine, and others that encompass all wind turbines of an examined farm, simply by multiplying 
by the total number of turbines, all considered of identical type. 
Under the scope of the present analysis, results that derived from the study of (Hill, et al., 2008) are 
extracted and applied, since this study offers a well-informed presentation in terms of technique, considered 
factors and numerical results which can be further processed. According to their developed methodology, 
availability assessment is performed under the aid of Raptor Reliability Simulation software, which is a 
software tool that facilitates the simulation of systems’ operations and characterization of their reliability 
(Booz Allen Hamilton, 2016). Input data are obtained from the National Reliability Database (NRD). The 
database includes information obtained referring to multiple wind turbines, electronically or by other 
means, and it is developed by the same working group (Hill, et al., 2008). The methodology is based on 
modelling of an indicative wind farm, in which all wind turbines are assumed to operate independently 
from one another. The performance of a wind turbine depends on the functionality of all separate 
components, therefore no kind of failure event shall occur in order for a turbine to operate normally. Main 
components of a wind turbine are the tower & foundation, the rotor & rotor blades, the nacelle & drive 
train, the electronic equipment and other secondary elements. Failure of any component is assumed to lead 
to shutting down the turbine for repairing reasons, during which period the wind turbine is idle. All types 
of failure that prevent a turbine from producing electricity due to time spent on repairing are taken into 
account (Kozine, et al., 2000). Included events and factors are human resource requirements, operational 
dependencies, costs of preparations and repair, logistical issues and failure frequencies, as well as average 
time to repair or replace a failed component (Hill, et al., 2008).

2.4.3 Wind distribution and power generation calculation 
When designing wind power systems, the primary meteorological factor to be identified is local mean wind 
speed. Thereafter, extreme wind speed frequency is also of interest, meaning the highest anticipated wind 
speed over a long period of time, especially in the design phase of turbines, securing for their resistance to 
such undesired conditions (Manwell, et al., 2009). Since Hazard Identification has also identified wind 
loads as the most interesting hazard to be evaluated in the present study, an assessment of wind speed 
==PAGE:34==
20 
 
variability at specific locations of the assigned wind NAMA projects is performed and is further utilized to 
calculate and evaluate average electricity generation from wind turbines.  
Wind turbines’ performance is defined by design properties, which target in optimized power generation 
according to wind variability and are specific for each turbine type. Extreme wind speed is determined by 
setting cut-out speed, which serves as the upper threshold where a turbine is able to operate normally. Cut-
out speed is set to a point where risk for damage rises due to high load and mechanical forces. In order to 
avoid failure, the rotor is set not to operate when wind reaches or exceeds this speed. Cut-in speed is the 
low bound of operational wind speeds, and defines the minimum wind speed at hub height at which a 
turbine is able to generate electricity. Cut-in speed is not connected to turbines’ failure modes or extreme 
load, it nevertheless implies interrupted operation and loss in power generation. Another relevant property 
is the survival wind speed, being defined as the maximum wind speed that allows for avoiding structural 
damage (Burton, et al., 2001); (DVN, 2002). For reasons of data unavailability and simplicity, one wind 
turbine type is taken as archetype and assumed to be applied in all wind NAMA projects. This is the Avantis 
AV928 – 2.5 MW wind turbine which is reported to be used in one of the projects (Thanh Tung Group 
Project), with technical data as reported in Appendix 1.V (Tung, et al., 2012). 
Wind speed variability is calculated following methodology guidelines by Manwell, et al. (2009) and DTU 
Wind Energy Department. Site specific wind profiles for the 48 assigned wind NAMA projects locations 
are extracted making use of Global Wind Atlas and WAsP software, describing the probability of each wind 
speed occurrence during a time period at all sites (for details on applied method see supporting information 
in Appendix 1.V) (Global Wind Atlas, website). These 48 wind profiles are exploited for two purposes. 
First, they are useful for calculating average energy produced by each of the wind farms. Furthermore, an 
average probability of non-operational wind speed occurrence is derived , which is further utilized to 
express mean outage for a given electricity production due to wind variance.   
Power generation for each of the 48 wind power projects is depended on wind variability and nominal 
power output of the assigned wind turbine. Average Energy Production (AEP) by all wind NAMA projects 
are calculated according to the suggested pathway of Hansen (2015) and DTU Wind Energy Department 
(described in Appendix 1.V)

3. Results & Discussion 
 
 
The Results & Discussion section serves as an analysis of the obtained and processed results according to 
the aforementioned applied methodology. It contains results of LCA on tested sub-scenarios, the outcomes 
of risk evaluation of wind power systems, and a final case study on wind power production, where the 
combination of the two sets of results are combined and its uncertainties are discussed. 
3.1 Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
LCA study is realized at several levels on the developed sub-scenarios, which offer different 
communication ways of resulted impacts, after calculating and discussing characterized and normalized 
impact scores. Impacts are derived after following the guidelines set by the described methodology under 
Goal & Scope Definition (Appendix 1.VI) and using SimaPro 8.1 modeling software.  
Impact assessment in the present study is performed at three levels, in this way evaluating obtained results 
from different angles: 
1) Evaluation of energy technologies employed in Vietnam 
2) Evaluation of total electricity generation in all sub-scenarios 
3) Evaluation of electricity mix in all sub-scenarios 
An additional case of CO2 – eq emissions and reduction potential is presented, desiring to communicate 
environmental impacts in relevance to energy policy stakeholders’ interests.  
3.1.1 Energy technologies employed in Vietnam 
Characterized midpoint impact scores are obtained for 1kWh electricity produced by each of the seven fuel 
types employed in the electricity mix of Vietnam (coal, oil products, natural gas, nuclear, hydropower, 
biomass/biogas and wind). Considered FU is the ‘supply of 1 kWh per given electricity technology’. 
==PAGE:36==
22 
 
Impacts of every electricity technology are represented relative to the maximum contributing fuel in each 
impact category (Figure 8), which is indicated by the fuel marked with 1 in every category. Raw 
characterized results are accessible in Appendix 2.II. 
Among conventional fuels, coal and oil products are predominant technologies in environmental impacts. 
Oil is planned to be eliminated from the Vietnamese electricity mix by 2030, whereas coal consumption is 
projected to increase by 18 times. Their resulted impact score is considerably high, compared to the 
remaining means of electricity, in most midpoint impact categories, including ‘Climate change’ and 
‘Photochemical oxidant formation’, as expected (Laurent, et al., 2012). Nuclear energy is of little hazard to 
the environment, apart from ‘Ionizing radiation’ category, which results from the emission of radioactive 
material in controlled doses into all environmental compartments during nuclear fuel cycle, meaning fuel 
preparation and power plant operation (Beninson, 1984). Moderate to low contribution is noticed by natural 
gas power plants. Their low impacts justify, from an environmental aspect, their projected increase by 
double using the country’s own reserves, and simultaneous substitution of coal, by 2030 ( (UNEP DTU 
PARTNERSHIP(a), 2016) , as retrieved from APEC Energy Overview 2012).   
Regarding renewables, hydropower has negligible emissions in all impact categories, rendering the source 
the most environmentally sound out of the seven electricity sources. Biogas production results in relatively 
higher impacts to ‘Marine eutrophication’, ‘Agricultural land occupation’, ‘Terrestrial ecotoxicity’, and 
‘Water depletion’, due to the occupation of land, fertilizers’ application and irrigation water consumption 
to support cultivation of crops while providing for biomass. Considerably high impact to ‘Marine 
eutrophication’ from biogas production compared to the remaining technologies is noticed, which is caused 
by preparation activities of biomass resource, and the consequent agricultural nutrients (fertilizers) emitted 
through wastewater treatment. Wind power plants’ operation is connected with noticeable impacts in 
‘Freshwater & Marine ecotoxicity’ and ‘Metal depletion’, due to extraction and subsequent waste treatment 
of materials to sustain infrastructure.  
Given the focus of study on wind power systems, it is interesting to identify key sub-processes that 
contribute to diagnosed high environmental impacts. Main activities involved in electricity generation from 
wind in Vietnam are the manufacturing and disposal of wind turbines, transportation of wind turbines from 
manufacturer (China) to Vietnam by train, supply of lubricating oil and transportation by lorry for 
maintenance purposes and disposal of lubricating oil.

3.2.3 
Classification of Wind NAMA projects 
Quantitative assessment of the degree of risks to wind turbines deriving from hazards identified in hazard 
assessment would be a next step for a complete Risk Analysis. Such an assessment would cater a solid and 
specialized characterization of a specific power project according to its specificities. Nevertheless, available 
information on the existing system only allows for a qualitative evaluation and classification of the projects 
according to their degree of exposure to identified hazards. The evaluation is based on information accessed 
in literature, which describe details of the wind power plants or hazards related to those (as in Appendix 
1.IV), as well as on own estimations.  
Most relevant hazards to be assessed are considered those that may potentially deteriorate turbines’ 
performance, therefore force majeure environmental events, fatigue cracks and exceedance of designed 
allowed thrust load as described during hazard identification. Each factor may affect wind power systems 
in different ways, according to their varying properties. Key aspects for assessing hazards’ effect are related 
with turbines’ type and location of installation. Design properties of wind turbines specify their performance 
in different conditions, in ways analyzed subsequently. Moreover, site related factors are also of 
importance, given the resulting variance in climatological and other environmental events.

projects in reference.  
Force majeure environmental events (earthquakes, floods, lightning, thunderstorms, ice accretion) 
Environmental events which may have abrupt adverse effects on turbines are earthquakes, floods, lightning, 
storms and ice accretion. They all are site specific and thorough analysis of their frequency of occurrence 
in all wind farm locations is recommended in order to derive a sound and specific dependence level. 
Lightning, storms and ice accretion are assumed of equal significance within the borders of Vietnam, 
affecting all turbines in the same way. Ice accretion may be more critical in locations with colder climate, 
nevertheless it is not identified whether any project is designed to be located in such sites. Earthquakes in 
the central and southern zone of the country are of minor importance (see Table A 9), thus it is fair to be 
ignored in the design phase. The northern area needs to be more carefully looked at, rendering Project 1 in

Fatigue cracks are a result of creep and/or shrinkage within the material. Wind turbine towers are subject 
to this kind of deficiency, being usually manufactured by concrete or steel. Creep and shrinkage may affect 
concrete towers’ long-term structural performance, leading to cracks and deterioration of the infrastructure. 
Fatigue expressions in case of steel-based towers include corrosion, detailed causes and outcomes of such 
deficiencies are nevertheless not included. This fault type is aligned with specific wind turbine types, based 
on their design properties. Since all wind projects are assumed to comprise of the same wind turbine 
(Avantis AV 928 – 2.5 MW), equal degree of threat is accredited to all. It is worth mentioning, that presence 
of fatigue cracks also depends on environmental conditions, more specifically wind turbulence or humidity 
(Quilligan, et al., 2012).

3.3 Combination of Risk Identification and LCA on wind power production 
The present study serves as an attempt to incorporate the outcomes of the performed risk identification onto 
LCA of wind turbine systems. In this direction, life cycle impacts of electricity by wind per unit generation 
are assessed in case outage due to vulnerability to predefined risks is additionally accounted for. Given the

boundaries of the Risk Identification part, being limited to wind power generation, it is only relevant to 
combine its outcomes with wind power production LCA.  
Ecoinvent 3.2 process Electricity, high voltage {TW}| electricity production, wind, 1-3MW turbine, onshore 
| Conseq, U is used to describe wind power generation. This process is documented to consider outage due 
to failure and maintenance as well as non-operational wind speed occurrence, which reaches 1% of total 
operating time (Treyer & Bauer, 2013); (Treyer & Bauer, 2014). This factor is considerably low compared 
to the identified outage probability due to maintenance and non-operating wind speeds in the present study. 
Availability loss of 3.86% due to failures & maintenance, and also 6.07% loss in power production due to 
inappropriate wind speeds on site result in a total outage factor of 9.93%. Assuming that the considered 
outage is negligible compared to the calculated one after risk identification on wind turbine systems, it is 
chosen to evaluate for this types of losses into the LCA model. Therefore, three tests of incorporation of 
losses within wind power generation are performed. Availability and operational wind speed are examined 
in two separate evaluations, followed by a case where maintenance and wind speed variance are together 
taken into account.  
As resulted from Availability Assessment, availability of average wind turbines follows a factor of 0.96142.

4. Conclusions & Recommendations 
 
 
The key messages that the assessment has resulted in are analyzed in the Conclusions & Recommendations 
part. Derived conclusions are chosen to be segmented in relevance to their importance for the different 
groups of stakeholders of the study. In this direction, messages to energy policy makers and parties 
interested in the outcomes of the methodology are communicated. 
4.1 Recommendations to energy policy stakeholders 
Environmental Impact Assessment through LCA leads in characterizing current electricity generation mix 
under Business as Usual practices as the most preferable choice among all examined. A key aspect of such 
a result is considered to be electricity demand in each scenario. BaU 2016 is designed to follow present 
demand specifications, which are low, compared to future oriented approaches. It is, therefore, proven as 
important for policy makers to support limitation of energy consumption apart from other measures in the 
direction of low carbon development in Vietnam. Moreover, despite the incorporation of renewable energy 
sources, high proportion of coal power plants in the future (53%) is proven to contribute to increase of total 
environmental impacts in 2030, compared to nowadays. Future electricity provision in Vietnam is 
characterized as crucial in environmental terms, regardless the attempts for employment of green 
technologies. In this direction, moderate impacts of natural gas power plants characterize them as a possible 
alternative to coal to a certain share by 2030, exploiting the country’s own reserves. 
Among future oriented scenarios, Wind Power 2030 (Baseline) is the most preferable approach, meaning 
that substitution of coal power plants by wind farms under current policies, is a strategy which is able to 
create benefit for environmental considerations of the country. 
On the other hand, when focus is put on GHGs emissions,  AREP is presented as a benefitting move towards 
a more environmentally friendly electricity market, showing reduced emissions by 8.8% compared to 
==PAGE:56==
42 
 
current energy policy directions. Moreover, regarding electricity generation’s contribution to CO2 – eq 
emissions, it is implied that there is potential room for extension of wind power employment in Vietnam, 
beyond the already registered Wind NAMA projects.  
It shall be noted that when looking at CO2 - eq emissions, positive messages in relevance to reduction after 
RE promotion into the electricity sector are revealed, which is not the case when examining the impact in 
additional categories, as realized through LCA application. It is therefore, concluded, that despite the 
potential fulfillment of carbon dioxide reduction goals, energy policy makers should not ignore other 
environmental aspects of the employment of RE and wind power in the country. 
Given that wind energy helps a country be more self-reliant regarding energy resources, wind power 
projects seem as a potential economic asset for Vietnam. Furthermore, the sector of wind power offers the 
possibility for jobs’ creation, given that it is a  labor intensive activity (Hansen, 2015). In addition to the 
extensive environmental study on the designed wind power systems, an economic and social assessment of 
the aforementioned, and a lot more, aspects may be included in further assessments, providing for a 
complete sustainability evaluation of the system.  
4.2 Recommendations to methodology stakeholders 
Focusing on LCA results, attention is drawn to the increase of environmental impacts in future oriented 
electricity generation scenarios. While demand is projected to increase by 5.5 times, the majority of impact 
scores increases by more than 5 times in the future. Increased incorporation of coal power plants by almost 
20 times to meet expected consumption needs is the predominant factor of this trend.  
Worth mentioning outcomes concern as well the negative effect of Biogas technology in ‘Marine 
eutrophication’ and ‘Agricultural land occupation’ as well as of wind power to ecotoxicity and ‘Metal 
depletion’ in AREP scenarios. These are the aspects that need to be looked at while deciding for promotion 
of RE technologies, since for the remaining impact categories these seem to importantly reduce 
environmental burden.  
Regarding wind power enterprise, wind power plants infrastructure employment seems to be the main 
contributing activity in all 18 midpoint categories. Choosing to focus on ‘Climate change’ as an indication, 
provision of needed materials, such as iron, clinker, coke, epoxy resin and nylon as well as burning of diesel 
during this stage are identified are the most critical factors. Regardless the fact that wind power is 
considered as a clean and pollutant-free energy technology, equipment and other technologies that support 
its employment need to be looked at while evaluating deriving environmental contamination and emissions. 
==PAGE:57==
43 
 
It is important to outline the relevance of combined evaluation of a system’s risks and environmental 
impacts in order to enrich results in terms of completeness. The present study serves as a preliminary 
indication of such an approach, and concludes that inclusion of availability and wind speed variance into 
the wind power electricity system leads to increased emissions that reach 11%, which would be, otherwise, 
ignored. Two interesting sets of information arise from the case study on wind power. First, included outage 
in the ecoinvent process only accounts for a small share compared to that identified in risk analysis section. 
Therefore, in case outage is needed to be accounted for, an LCA practitioner may do it manually. Second, 
assumptions made while designing the system lead in absence of comparative outcomes along sub-scenarios 
when combining identified risks and LCA results. Within the limits of study, all cases are thought to follow 
identical average wind power system risk trends, which leads in their same level of expected outage. It is, 
nevertheless, expected that detailed specification of technical characteristics of a system shall derive case-
specific outcomes.

Appendix 1.III 
BaU and Wind Power Scenarios 
i. 
Basic Elements of Scenarios 
The comparative assessment is facilitated by the development of two scenarios which describe the 
electricity mix in Vietnam according to temporal criteria and policy directions. Besides assessing current 
power generation practices (year 2016), it is of great interest to look into future projections, accounting for 
potential incorporation of sustainability oriented policies to meet set targets. These projections refer to year 
2030, being the earliest time when all Wind NAMA projects can be assumed to be in operation, and also 
because it is the latest time frame when estimated data on electricity composition and demand are readily 
available (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(a), 2016). Such an approach shall provide for decision oriented 
outcomes for future applications of wind power in Vietnam. 
Additional to temporal specificities of the designed systems, two alternative policies that outline lack or 
employment of renewable energy technologies are included to further differentiate the two scenarios. Both 
policies have as a guideline the two respective scenarios, as described in the LCD Vietnam report, from 
which relevant reported datasets are extracted and used. Baseline Policy, under ‘Business as Usual 
Scenario’ in LCD Vietnam report, is based on the assumption that there is no change in governmental 
policies and actions both now and in the future. The second approach is an Alternative Renewable Energy 
Policy (AREP), based on ‘Alternative Policy Scenario’ from LCD Vietnam report. The key hypothesis of 
the policy is that actions towards achieving the goal of 5% renewable resources in primary commercial 
energy by 2020, set by the National Energy Development Strategy in 2007, will be applied. 
Within this mindset, two main scenarios are designed and segregated according to the aforementioned 
criteria. ‘Business as Usual Scenario (BaU)’ describes electricity generation practices under current 
common methods, therefore full implementation of Wind NAMA proposal is absent. ‘Wind Power Scenario 
(WP)’ attempts to additionally incorporate Wind NAMA projects, offering, thus, the chance to evaluate 
their environmental contribution in case of their future operation. This methodology results in the 
development of 5 sub-scenarios as part of the two main scenarios; One sub-scenario under BaU Scenario 
which represents the current situation, and two sub-scenarios in each of the BaU and Wind Power scenarios, 
describing electricity mix in 2030 under Baseline and ARE policies respectively (Table A 2: Power 
generation from different electricity technologies in 5 sub-scenarios). All data on electricity demand are 
extracted from LCD Vietnam Report (Table A 5), and further description of the development of the two 
scenarios is met in the following sections. The five respective scenarios are numbered and differentiated by 
ascending Latin numerals (I-V).

Looking at the data sets that describe power generation in Vietnam in a 20 years’ horizon, one can already 
comment on the fact that electricity increases significantly during that period. Moreover, all conventional 
fuels are projected to give ground to the empowerment of green energy technologies, nevertheless, coal is 
estimated to be prevailing as energy source in Vietnam in the future. Wind resources will account for 
additional 13,000 GWh by 2030, which leaves the possibility for Wind NAMA projects to be included in 
the calculation method. Power output in AREP is forecasted as slightly decreased compared to Baseline 
Policy due to the incorporation of Small Hydro Power Plants for residential sector needs. Nevertheless, as 
described in the following, due to lack of specific statement on consideration of those projects into the 
electricity mix, the case of their additional inclusion is also examined in Wind Power sub-scenarios. 
iii. 
Assumptions and criteria 
While developing the 5 sub-scenarios, several assumptions were made in order to secure for consistency of 
criteria and recoupment of missing data to better describe the system. 
 
Renewable energy (RE) sources, suitable for electricity generation in Vietnam, consist of small hydro, 
biomass & biogas, solar and wind power. Small hydropower plants have maximum capacity of 100 
MW. Ordinary hydropower plants are noticed to be separately included in the conventional fuels mix 
in the LCD Vietnam Report, therefore this approach is followed in the study. Regarding wind energy, 
==PAGE:79==
65 
 
small and household wind turbines, as well as projects reported to be out of order due to technical 
problems, are not accounted for in wind power potential capacity (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(b), 
2016). 
 
The two policies (Baseline and ARE) are established on the basis that the same energy services are 
provided by both, in terms of quality. In that sense, technical characteristics of power plants, raw 
materials, fuel types and production processes are identical for all sub-scenarios. Regarding quantity of 
electricity demand, it is chosen to be kept stable according to policy and year, offering ground for 
comparison between respective sub-scenarios. 
 
By 2016, 3 out of 48 wind NAMA projects are reported to be in operation, being those the Binh Thuan 
wind farm (120 MW), Bac Lieu wind farm (99 MW) and Phu Quy island wind farm (6MW) (numbered 
18, 46 and 25 in Appendix 1.II, respectively). Binh Thuan farm is installed in two phases, with reported 
operating capacity of 30 MW until 2016, whereas Bac Lieu is considered as entirely implemented in 
2016, (according to (DEVI-renewable, 2015), the last phase of the project will be set in operation within 
the second quarter of 2016).  Total annual electricity generated by these projects reaches 311 GWh in 
2016. By 2030, all wind projects are considered in operation and potentially generating 10,866 
GWh/year. 
 
For BaU scenario, reported data sets from the respective BaU and APS scenarios in LCD Vietnam 
Report are used unprocessed. For Wind power sub-scenarios, Wind NAMA projects are accounted for, 
providing for additional 10,866 GWh wind power in 2030. 
 
Total installed capacity of wind power plants is reported to replace electricity from coal power plants,

Appendix 1.V 
Wind distribution and Power generation 
In order to evaluate the threat posed to wind power production due to wind speed variability, it is of interest 
to gain understanding of local wind variance, given that wind energy is only produced when wind is blowing 
(Hansen, 2015). When designing wind power systems, the primary meteorological factor to be identified is 
local mean wind speed. Extreme wind speed frequency is also of interest, meaning the highest anticipated 
wind speed over a long period of time, especially in the design phase of turbines, securing for their 
resistance to such undesired conditions which may affect their structural reliability and performance 
(Manwell, et al., 2009). This section analyses wind speed variability at the specific locations of the assigned 
wind NAMA projects, which is further utilized to calculate and evaluate available wind power generation. 
Wind turbines’ performance, meaning obtained electricity output, is determined by design properties, which 
target in optimized power generation according to wind variability and are specific for each turbine type. 
Extreme wind speed is determined by setting cut-out speed, which serves as the upper threshold where a 
turbine is able to normally operate. Cut-out speed is set to a point where risk for damage rises due to high 
load and resulting mechanical forces. In order to avoid failure, the rotor is set not to operate when wind 
reaches or exceeds this speed. Cut-in speed is the low bound of operational wind speeds, and defines the 
minimum wind speed at hub height at which a turbine is able to generate electricity. Cut-in speed is not 
connected to turbines’ failure modes, it nevertheless implies interrupted operation and loss in power 
generation. Another relevant property is the survival wind speed, being defined as the maximum wind speed 
that allows for avoiding structural damage (Burton, et al., 2001); (DVN, 2002).  
For reasons of simplicity and data unavailability, one wind turbine type is taken as archetype and assumed 
to be applied in all wind NAMA projects. This is the Avantis AV928 – 2.5 MW wind turbine which is 
reported to be used in one project (Thanh Tung Group Project), and, due to lack of such information for the 
remaining projects, its specific technical properties are extracted and utilized where needed (Tung, et al., 
2012). A technical datasheet is accessed, which addresses its design properties, the most useful elements of 
which for the performed assessment are presented in Table A 10: Important extracted technical data of

Appendix 1.VI 
LCA – Goal & Scope Definition 
The present study is performed according to internationally set standards that characterize LCA, as they are 
specified by ISO 14040, ISO 14044 and ‘International Reference Life Cycle Data System’ (ILCD). These 
standards suggest that an LCA application is fully described by Goal & Scope definition, Life Cycle 
Inventory Analysis (LCI), Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) and Interpretation of results. 
Consequently, these four steps are followed the present assessment. Goal & Scope and Inventory are 
analyzed in this section, given that they are methodology-related segments, whereas LCIA and 
Interpretation are included in Results analysis.  
Goal & Scope is a methodology step in which the purpose and content of project work are defined. Goal 
Definition describes the intended application and the reasons for carrying out the study as well as the 
intended audience that the outcomes are to be communicated to. Scope of the study outlines specific 
elements of the assessed system, such as its function and boundary settings, as well as relevant background 
information supporting the development of the analysis. 
i. 
Goal Definition 
Intended application: The present study aims at comparing the environmental impacts of electricity 
production in Vietnam in five separate cases (sub-scenarios), differentiated according to temporal criteria 
and policy directions. The ultimate goal is to identify to which extent the employment of renewable 
technologies in electricity production facilitates the reduction of CO2 – eq emissions in the country. Special 
attention is put on wind power systems.  
Intended audience and commissioner: The case study is commissioned by UNEP DTU Partnership and 
intends to support decision making in Vietnam’s attempts towards a sustainable future in energy generation. 
In addition, the study is realized under the guidance of researchers in the GDSI group at DTU Managment, 
following their guidelines on system design and environmental performance evaluation. The outcomes will 
be internally communicated to respective supervisors of the QSA and GDSI divisions of DTU Management 
as well as to stakeholders from UNEP DTU Partnership. 
Decision context: The decision context of the study is situation B: Meso/Macro-level decision support. 
Technology-level systems (electricity generation mixes) are assessed with the aim to decide on best practice 
approaches according to specified policy directions in order to support strategic decisions (European 
Commission, et al., 2010). Alterations in the evaluated systems entail consequent changes in other elements 
of the technosphere, such as infrastructure, energy markets and applied technologies. In example, excessive 
==PAGE:93==
79 
 
employment of wind power generation results in reducing the need for other fuels and their relevant 
infrastructure, fuel import/export market and end-of life techniques. Moreover, the system is influenced by 
consumer behavior changes, given that demographic growth in combination with expected economic 
growth may increase the need and employment of specific sources of energy, in this case RE, in order to 
cover increasing needs and sustainability targets. 
Disclosure to the public: The results of the study are not intended to be publicly disclosed.  
ii. 
Scope Definition 
Function of system: The system is designed in such a way as to provide for electricity from seven different 
sources and cover the power needs of Vietnamese consumers in 2016 and 2030. The same main function is 
covered by all sub-scenarios regardless time and policy specifications.  
Functional Unit: The evaluated system does not follow a quantitative functional unit (FU) basis. Sub-
scenarios are designed in order to be functionally equivalent in a qualitative ground at the overall electricity 
system level. This implies that they all fulfill the same function across year and policy, but are not defined 
on the same quantitative basis (electricity generation varies along sub-scenarios according to policy and 
year). Impact assessment is decided to be performed at three levels, leading to the subsequent employment 
of three types of FU according to each approach. Evaluation of electricity technologies employed in Vietnam 
is assessed under ‘supply of 1 kWh per given electricity technology’.  Evaluation of total electricity demand 
in all sub-scenarios is designed in a manner as to ‘meet electricity needs in Vietnam in year 2016 and 2030, 
according to considered policies and actions’. Lastly, evaluation of electricity mix in all sub-scenarios 
secures for the ‘supply of an average kWh electricity in Vietnam, according to considered policies and 
actions’. Making comparison between sub-scenarios which do not refer to the same time frame and policy 
is facilitated by the division of impacts per unit of electricity produced (kWh), thus offering a more objective 
ground for comparison (Hertwich, et al., 2014). 
Reference flow: Reference flow is also described on a qualitative basis, and is separately defined for the 
first and the rest two impact assessment evaluation levels, as discussed in FU definition section. Reference 
flow for the  as ‘electricity generated by seven different types of conventional or renewable fuels (coal, oil, 
natural gas, nuclear, hydropower, biogass/biomass and wind) in order to meet electricity demand in 
Vietnam’. Each sub-scenario is assigned a different quantitative value of reference flow, according to power 
generation, total and of each fuel type, as shown in Table A 13.

Product system description: The assessed system is an energy system, more than a specific product system. 
The energy system provides for a specific function, nevertheless does not comprise of a unique product 
with a specified lifecycle. The production and delivery of electricity at the busbar is a process, which 
depends on several product systems and processes, which, from their side, are characterized by their own 
lifetimes and cycles. Therefore, the assessed system is referred to as an ‘energy system’, rather than product 
system, and is not assigned a specific lifetime with product stages, but is determined by a temporal scope 
when its operation is assigned. 
System boundaries: The boundaries of the system enclose all key processes and stages necessary to 
describe the provision of electricity to the national grid by different fuel types. Acquisition of fuels and 
materials needed for the construction, operation and maintenance of power plants, transportation of raw 
materials and components from other parts of the world to Vietnam, dismantling and waste management of 
end-of-life plants are included in the assessment. Grid network construction is out of the scope of the study. 
Therefore, the boundaries of the system encapsulate all processes that describe material and fuel needs for 
the construction, operation and decommission of power plants that provide electricity to the national grid.

LCIA methodology: Chosen Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method is (Goedkoop, et al., 2013) 
Midpoint Hierarchical. Midpoint level impact categories are assessed, given that they can offer an 
illustration of a system’s environmental performance in various aspects and with considerably low degree 
of uncertainty. Hierarchical perspective is followed, given that it describes the most common policy 
approaches applied in a balanced short and long-term scope (Goedkoop, et al., 2013). World normalization 
approach is used in this assessment, considered as more representative to describe the designed system and 
include uncertainties connected with considered locations where activities take place. Characterized and 
Normalized impact scores are calculated under Impact Assessment step. Characterized scores per impact 
category obtain different units, thus it is not recommended to compare impacts along categories. Therefore, 
characterized results only allow for comparison of systems within each impact category. Normalized results 
serve as more holistic indicators, allowing for evaluating a system considering its overall performance. All 
impacts are expressed in person equivalents (PE), or in ‘relative contribution compared to a person’s 
average emissions into a certain impact category’. Such scores are derived by applying normalization 
factors, specific for each impact category. Normalized results in the present assessment are obtained 
according to ReCiPe World Methodolgy, considering normalization factors that describe average global 
conditions. Water Depletion IC has been assigned a normalized score of 0, since the relevant normalization 
factor is not properly developed in the methodology yet, eliminating it from the assessment of normalized 
scores. This evaluation facilitates an understanding of the relative importance of the system’s contribution 
to all ICs and concluding for which environmental compartments it is mostly hazardous (European 
Commission, et al., 2010).  
ReCiPe methodology considers the following environmental mechanisms, which include 18 Midpoint 
Impact Categories that impacts are derived for (Goedkoop, et al., 2013). 
Climate Change considers agents’ radiative forcing residence time to assess their effect to temperature 
increase, human health and diversity of ecosystems and overall, their global warming potential (GWP). It 
is expressed in CO2 – equivalent emissions and substances considered are the ones introduced in the IPCC 
2007 (Solomon, et al., 2007).  
Ozone Depletion expresses the emission of substances that are ozone depleting through human activities. 
These are chemicals which normally contain chlorine or bromine, and it is measured in units of kg 
Chlorofluoromethane (CFC-11) – eq. 
Acidification describes the deposition of substances which may affect the optimal acidity levels of the soil 
and cause hazard for specific species. Such substances are normally NOx, NH3 and SO2. Impact category 
‘Terrestrial acidification’ is expressed in emitted kg SO2 – eq. 
==PAGE:97==
83 
 
Eutrophication characterized the additional enrichment of nutrient substances in aquatic environments, 
being them nitrogen and phosphorous, which may lead to algal bloom. In the used methodology, 
‘Freshwater eutrophication’ is expressed in kg P - eq and ‘Marine eutrophication’ in kg N – eq. 
 
Toxicity accounts for the hazard of presence and accumulation of a toxic agent into the human body and 
other organisms. Human, Terrestrial, Freshwater and Marine ecotoxicity are separately evaluated, all 
having as reference substance emitted kg 1,4 Dichlorobenzene (DB) – eq. 
Human health damage due to PM10 and ozone (Particulate matter formation) is caused by a mixture of 
organic and inorganic substances with diameter smaller to 10 µm (PM10). Moreover, the category expresses 
the emission of Non-methane volatile organic compounds (NMVOCs) that contribute to ‘Photochemical 
oxidant formation’. 
Ionizing Radiation category allows for the evaluation of release of radioactive material into the environment 
by a certain activity. It is expressed in released kg Uranium – 235 (235U) – eq. 
Impacts of land use express potential damage to ecosystems due to occupation and transformation of land 
during a certain time. It is characterized by ‘Agricultural land occupation’, ‘Urban land occupation’ and 
‘Natural land transformation’, expressed in agricultural or urban area occupied or transformed natural land 
area per year (m2a). 
Water depletion considers factors that evaluate the potential shortage of water in a region after the 
realization of certain activities (in m3).  
Mineral resource depletion evaluates the potential of depleting a mineral deposit after extraction. ‘Metal 
depletion’ is expressed in extracted kg Fe – eq. 
Fossil fuel depletion expresses the change in supply of conventional fuels, like oil and is measured in kg 
oil - eq

Representativeness of LCI data: In order to evaluate the validity and degree of uncertainty of the inventory 
results, the quality of input data must be evaluated and high representativeness of the real situation by the 
designed model shall be secured for. The most relevant aspects to describe representativeness concern 
technological, geographical and temporal specificities. In case of data unavailability, inventory values are 
covered by assumptions, which are in all cases justified. 
Technological and geographical representativeness: Regarding database processes used while 
modelling, the system is developed using Unit processes from Ecoinvent 3.2 database to express all sub-
scenarios’ fuel mix for electricity production in Vietnam. Site specific processes for Vietnam were not met 
in the database. Hence, respective processes for countries which are considered as site and technologically 
relevant to Vietnam were chosen and, in case needed, in some cases accordingly adjusted to more 
realistically represent the real case and minimize uncertainties. Thailand is selected as the primarily site-
relevant country, and when not possible, processes for Taiwan were selected both countries with relevant 
vicinity to Vietnam. Only for nuclear power production a Rest-of-the-World (RoW) process is extracted, 
which represents average situation. The assumption that technological level of infrastructure and 
transportation coverage of these countries resembles that of Vietnam may be solid. Specific alternations 
into database processes were performed so that the model best fits the real case. Those are specific for 
Vietnam and the technologies applied on site.  
Temporal representativeness: Input data and processes shall secure for time-related representativeness 
of the assessed system. Technological development over time, intended application of the examined system

during interpretation concern the constructed model, spatial and temporal variability of data and 
assumptions made while developing the LCA model

Appendix 1.VII 
LCA – Inventory Analysis 
Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) is an LCA step which includes ‘the compilation and quantification of inputs 
and outputs for a product throughout its life cycle’ (ISO 14044, 2008). Reference on input data, on a 
qualitative basis, has been made in the Goal & Scope section. Inventory analysis is an attempt to offer a 
more clarified idea about input and output materials, fuels and processes included in the product system, 
also on a quantitative basis. This analysis is combined along with a description of the generated software 
model in SimaPro 8.1, providing thus a transparent overview of the performed assessment. 
i. 
Inventory Analysis basics 
The system comprises of unit transformation processes from Ecoinvent 3.2 database, which describe the 
average production of electricity from seven different sources of electricity, according to the share of each 
in each sub-scenario. In general terms, database processes are used unprocessed, except for special cases 
where default background processes are modified according to case specific technological and site criteria. 
These changes are further explained in following corresponding sections.  
Selected database processes have several characteristics in common, a fact which derives from conditions 
of the modeled system that need to be fulfilled, as well as the need to provide for a uniformly developed 
model. Transformation processes are preferred, since a case of transformation of primary energy sources 
(inputs) to useable electricity is examined. Transport activities that backup this transformation are not 
included (PRé-Sustainability, website) and are additionally introduced if needed. High voltage electricity 
provision is modeled, which is the type of electricity arriving to the electricity grid, where system 
boundaries stop as explained in Appendix 1.VI.  
As explained in the Goal Definition section, included activities in unit processes go as back as the 
acquisition of fuels utilized as sources of electricity, construction of the respective power plants, operation 
and maintenance activities at the power plant and in some cases, specific processes, essential to back-up 
the plant’s operation such as provision of startup fuel and purification of air emissions. Waste management 
of end-of-life power plants, operating materials and residual fuels is also taken into account. Most included 
processes describe average global or site specific procedures, a fact which shall be considered when 
discussion the representativeness of the model results.

Consequential approach demands for the respective consequential unit processes to be employed while 
designing the model. This condition allows for a prognostic approach of changes that may occur to the 
system’s performance due to alterations in policy, decisions, human behavior and market (European 
Commission, et al., 2010). This condition is needed to be satisfied at all levels of the modeled system in 
order to avoid less realistic consequences resulting from background processes. The specificity of 
consequential approach lies in the inclusion of marginal data, instead of average, to describe background 
processes (European Commission, et al., 2010). Therefore, in cases where respective consequential 
processes were not available in the database, these were constructed by modifying their respective 
attributional ones into consequential, achieving the required solidity of LCA modeling.

Inventory specifications per fuel type 
In this section specific modifications and factors taken into account while modeling electricity generation 
by each of the 7 fuel types are analyzed. 
Electricity generation from coal: Hard coal is assumed to be entirely imported from China. The RoW 
default hard coal acquisition of was replaced with site specific market for coal produced in China, extracted 
from the database. This assumption derives from statements indicating that Vietnam imports 80% of its 
needs from China, Laos and Korea, which are expected to increase in the future to meet increasing demands 
in electricity (Conferences, Website); (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(a), 2016). China is chosen as the 
representative provider among the aforementioned. In order to account for this import activity, 
transportation included in hard coal acquisition to cover the distance between the mine and power plant is 
rejected and replaced by additional average transport activity from China to Vietnam by cargo train. The

calculated transport work is given by 2,900 km · 0.43 kg of coal consumed per 1kWh produced = 1.25 tkm. 
This alteration may ignore internal transit of fuel from mine to retailer before shipping, which is 
nevertheless considered negligible compared to importing.  
Electricity generation from oil products: Database process is left quite unchanged. Vietnam is reported to 
have its own reserves of oil, nevertheless average RoW production route of oil fuel is kept (APEC, 2013). 
Water for cooling and other purposes at the power plant is replaced from TH to VN.  
Electricity generation from natural gas: Modifications in database process follows the same direction as 
for oil type fuels. Combined cycle plant is applied, since this is the type of plants expected to develop in 
the future (UNEP DTU PARTNERSHIP(a), 2016). 
Electricity generation from nuclear power: An average global production of electricity from 92% fresh 
Uranium and 8% Mixed Oxide fuel (MOX) at Pressure Water Reactors (PWR) is taken into account.  
Electricity generation from hydropower: Generation of hydropower from run-of-river facilities is 
considered to best comply with Vietnam’s morphology and abundance of rivers and streams (i.e. Mekong 
river). 
Electricity generation from biogas/biomass: Electricity generation from biogas is modelled according to 
treatment of biogas deriving from biowaste and sewage sludge at a heat and electricity cogeneration plant. 
Such a process satisfactorily describes the market plan of Vietnam, which suggests exploiting biomass 
mainly from sugarcane waste, but also rice husks and straws, and secondarily, biogas (UNEP DTU 
PARTNERSHIP(a), 2016). Respective database process is modified to Consequential, replacing all 
included processes from attributional to consequential. In case the respective consequential process did not 
exist in the database, the same modification procedure was followed on a second level. Finally, it was 
possible to completely modify the process in compliance with the needs of the modeled system. 
The respective process includes input of low and medium voltage electricity needs to produce biomass 
around the world. The present system considers biomass being generated domestically. Hence, all separate 
low and medium voltage electricity provisions from different parts of the world are aggregated and 
expressed as totally afforded from respective low and medium voltage electricity mixes for Vietnam. The 
same is performed for water collected for irrigation purposes, which is chosen to follow an aggregated 
production path as in the Philippines, due to lack of such process for Vietnam. Input water to support the 
plant’s operation is also produced in Vietnam.

Appendix 2.I 
Availability assessment 
In the present, some complementary theoretical specification to the description of availability assessment 
are presented. They concern the description of reliability assessment, availability assessment and 
identification of the main constraints for not performing rigorous availability calculation in the study. 
The most widely accepted definition of reliability is the ‘ability of an item (product, system, etc.) to operate 
under designated operating conditions for a designated period of time or number of cycles’ (Modarres, 
1993). Reliability is the probability of a product to perform its intended functions satisfactorily (without 
failure and within specified performance limits) in a certain period. Reliability function R(t) describes the 
probability that the system is operational over the time interval 0 to t.  Reliability analysis takes into account 
the frequency of failure without considering the time spent for recovering the failed components. If repair 
is not permitted, then availability A(t) reduces to system reliability R(t) (Kozine, et al., 2000). 
Availability of power generated by wind turbines is depended on environmental and meteorological 
conditions as well as mechanical operating specifications of the equipment. Meteorological factors concern 
inherent uncertainty of wind speed variation and availability, whereas mechanical specifications describe 
failure modes of the installation and subsequent unavailability periods due to repair (Kuznetsova, et al., 
2014); (Ak, et al., 2014) 
According to IEEE (2007), the main factors that availability depends on are described by the following: 
Mean time Between Failures (MTBF)  is the ‘mean exposure time between consecutive failures of a 
component’ 
Mean Time to Repair (MTTR) is the ‘mean time needed to replace or repair a failed component. Logistics 
time associated with the repair is not included, such as acquisition of components or crew mobilization.  
Preventive Maintenance (PM): it includes performed activities that help keep the equipment clean, dry and 
correctly sealed in order to minimize friction and other undesired events’ occurrence that may lead to 
failure. 
Mean Downtime (MDT) describes the ‘average downtime caused by scheduled and unscheduled 
maintenance, including any logistics time’. 
The study of Hill, et al. (2008) proves the main contributing components to unavailability of wind turbines. 
Greatest relative impact is monitored to be caused by failure of generator, gearbox and blades. Additionally, 
failure of control system, yaw system and outage due to inspection are important contributors to 
==PAGE:108==
94 
 
unavailability. These failure modes have a contribution to annual operating cost of a wind turbine, deriving 
from their frequency of occurrence and the cost for replacing a flawed component. Again, higher 
contribution to cost has the replacement of gearbox, generator and rotor blades. (Hill, et al., 2008) 
Availability assessment is based on the description of the aforementioned factors. These are defined for 
each specific wind turbine type, making use of real failure data. Component failure data include a 
description of the component failure, the time period that the component was in use, and the necessary 
downtime to repair or replace it if failed. Other sets of information, such as performance, loads, and 
monitoring data are also encouraged to be provided to establish a more complete data set. These data are 
normally provided according to the willingness of the manufacturer/operator in the form of repair reports. 
Apart from input data and situation description, in order to be able to predict the reliability of a system one 
needs to develop a reliability model making use of component fault frequencies (Kozine, et al., 2000); (Hill, 
et al., 2008). Due to time limitation, a rigorous reliability model was not able to be developed during the 
conduct of the master thesis project. Moreover, repair reports for the assigned wind turbine types in 
Vietnam, including the archetype Avantis AV 928 -2.5 MW, were not accessible.

Appendix 2.III 
Interpretation of results 
Interpretation of results is a Life Cycle step which identifies corrections and improvements of the Inventory 
phase, as deriving from completing the LCA study. Interpretation in terms of evaluation of final results is 
performed in the present. This sets the ground for solid deriving of conclusions, after obtaining results in 
Impact Assessment phase and securing for a well representative Goal & Scope (European Commission, et 
al., 2010). It is normally performed in three levels; completeness, sensitivity and consistency check. The 
study evaluates the LCA outcomes only from a completeness and consistency point of view. Sensitivity 
analysis is not of relevance for the examined system, due to the sufficient variance of interesting parameters 
to be assessed within the tested scenarios. 
Completeness check 
During this assessment, the degree of fulfillment of Goal & Scope recommendations, in regards to how 
complete the inventory and included activities are.  
Regardless the focus of study towards wind power, special attention is given into adjusting all electricity 
technologies to represent the evaluated system in geographical and technological terms. Nevertheless, it is 
considered that there is room for improvement in the direction of modifying the modeled electricity mix 
with more detailed and site-specific characteristics.  
Consistency check 
This evaluation secures for a consistent employment of assumptions and criteria along the study. System 
boundaries, data acquisition and sources are assessed in order to improve the quality of outcome of the 
study.  
Regarding system boundaries, it is attempted to maintain a common setting along different included 
processes that the modeled system comprises of. Consistency of system boundaries, to encompass all, and 
only, activities as described in the Goal & Scope phase is considered to be achieved.  
Regarding data acquisition, most of them are accessed through Ecoinvent 3.2 database processes. Where 
possible, case-specific information and values are looked for, in an attempt to make the modeled system as 
case specific as possible. This generates uncertainty in geographical terms, given that used database 
processes are, mainly, referring to other countries or average global trends (RoW) than Vietnam, given the 
unavailability of respective processes for this country in Ecoinvent 3.2. Inconsistency deriving from the 
specification of selected sub-processes according to site specific characteristics (i.e. water and electricity 
input), while leaving others unprocessed.
