Abstract
Recent work has shown the potential of using geometric algebra in deep learning. How-
ever, many aspects of the approach remain underexplored. In this thesis, we investigate
the use of geometric algebra in the design of graph neural networks. We develop GA-GNN,
a novel graph neural network that is based on combining geometric algebra with equivari-
ant message-passing architectures. In particular we extend the PaiNN architecture from
scalar and vector representations to multivector representations and redesign the update
mechanism to use weighted geometric products as the central operation.
We evaluate this approach on the QM9 dataset for molecular property prediction. GA-
GNN achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art models. Additionally,
we demonstrate that geometric algebra has the potential to simplify model design. We
show that removing target-specific readout layers increases the performance of GA-GNN
while allowing for stable training at higher feature dimensions and message-passing depth.
Overall, this work underscores the feasibility of using geometric algebra in message-passing
architectures, and the insights gained throughout point to several exciting directions for
future work.

Introduction
Equivariant neural network models incorporate feature representations that transform
consistently when the input data is transformed. In tasks that involve geometric struc-
ture, like molecular property prediction, this has been shown to be highly beneficial. In
these models features are represented using scalars, vectors or more recently higher-order
tensors. In this thesis, we explore the possibility of instead using a mathematical object
called a multivector. Multivectors combine scalar, vector and higher-dimensional elements
including oriented planes and volumes into a single structure. Multivectors are derived
from Geometric Algebra (GA), a mathematical framework that builds a unified approach
for representing and manipulating geometric entities in arbitrary dimensions. In partic-
ular, GA introduces an operation called the geometric product, which gives a structured
way to construct higher-dimensional objects from basis vectors. Additionally, this prod-
uct is defined between any geometric entity within the algebra, including multivectors.
When applied to two multivectors the geometric product results in a new multivector that
combines contributions from interactions between components of not just the same type,
but also different types, such as scalars interacting with vectors, or vectors with oriented
planes. This enables an organized and simple way to mix information across representa-
tional levels of different dimensions, all within a single consistent operation. Meanwhile,
the geometric product is equivariant under rotations and reflections in 3D space.
For
these reasons, we find it compelling to design and test a Graph Neural Network (GNN)
architecture that is based on incorporating GA into the model design. We define three
main objectives:
1. To develop a novel GA based GNN architecture.
2. To evaluate its performance on molecular property prediction tasks.
3. To explore its scalability and robustness.
The foundation for our model is based on the PaiNN architecture [1], a well-established
equivariant message passing model. We redesign its overall structure to utilize multivector
feature representations and replace is scalar and vector update block with a weighted
geometric product between multivectors, which serve as the core operation of our model.
To evaluate this approach we perform experiments on the QM9 dataset for molecular
property prediction.
In addition to the main evaluation, we perform ablation studies,
explore variations of the architecture design choices, and analyze the model’s scalability
and robustness in this setting. GA is a relatively underexplored research topic. However,
it has recently begun to gain popularity in deep learning with recent work such as Clifford
Group Equivariant Neural Networks (CGENNs) [2]. This thesis contributes to that line
of research with our proposed GA-GNN architecture. Furthermore, we provide a detailed
investigation into the use of GA for molecular property prediction. To the best of our
knowledge this is the first application of a GA-based model to the QM9 dataset.
This report is structured as follows: Chapter 2 provides theoretical background on graph-
structured data, GNNs and the fundamentals of GA. Chapter 3 reviews related work with
a focus on message-passing networks for molecular property prediction and prior work
combining GA with deep learning. Chapter 4 details the proposed GA-GNN architecture,
and chapter 5 presents the experimental results and accompanying analysis on the QM9
dataset. Chapter 6 discusses the key findings, contributions and limitations of the work,
and proposes directions for future research. Chapter 7 concludes the thesis.

Background
2.1
Graphs and Graph-Structured Data
A graph is a data structure that can be used to represent collections of objects and the
relationships between them. An object is called a node, and a connection between two
nodes is called an edge. Formally, a graph is defined as G = (V, E), where V is the set
of nodes and E ⊆V × V is the set of edges. Additional data can be associated with the
nodes, edges, and the graph as a whole. For a given node v ∈V , the set of neighbors is
given by all the nodes that connect to it with an edge: N(v) = {u ∈V | (v, u) ∈E}. In
molecular graphs, nodes represent atoms and edges represent spatial proximity or chemical
bonds. Additionally, the atom type and spatial position xv ∈R3 is associated with each
node and the relative position of a node to its neighbor u ∈N(v) is used by computing
an edge vector rvu = xu −xv [3]. This structure makes molecules a great example of 3D
graphs (Figure 2.1). The figure shows examples of molecules from the QM9 dataset, which
is described in more detail in Section 5.1.
Figure 2.1: 3D molecular graphs from the QM9 dataset. Nodes correspond to atoms, and
edges to chemical bonds. Node colors indicate the atom type (white=H, red=O, blue=C,
black=N).
2.2
Graph Neural Networks
Graph neural networks (GNNs) are deep learning models that are designed with the pur-
pose of predicting or classifying properties of entire graphs, nodes or edges.
According to Bronstein et al.[4] most GNN models in the literature can be categorized
as one of three main ”flavors”: convolutional, attentional or message-passing.
These

categories form a hierarchy, where message-passing GNNs generalize the other two, such
that convolutional and attentional GNNs can be seen as special cases within the message-

Message passing neural networks (MPNN) were formulated by Gilmer et al. [5] who applied
them to molecular property prediction. The central idea behind MPNNs is to learn feature
representations of nodes, referred to as embeddings or hidden states, by computing messages
from neighboring nodes and then aggregating them in order to update the embeddings
of a node based on that information. This process occurs in successive message passing
rounds (also called GNN layers) that iteratively refine the embeddings of each node based
on the preceding rounds. Subsequently, a readout layer (also called the output layer) is
applied to the final node embeddings to compute the output of the network.

In this context, in-
variance and equivariance properties of the network extends from permutation to include
geometric symmetry groups. The Euclidean group E(n) includes rotations, translations
and reflections in Rn. The Orthogonal group O(n) include all rotations and reflections
that preserve the origin, and the Special Orthogonal Group SO(n), is a subgroup that
includes only rotations, excluding reflections.
MPNN architectures may be invariant or equivariant to different groups of symmetries
depending on the task and model design. Invariant MPNNs maintain invariance for both
the node embeddings and the final output, for example by using pairwise distances to
model relative positions, whereas equivariant MPNNs also use vector or tensor features
in addition to scalar features that require equivariant transformations. The readout layer
then maps equivariant features to an invariant graph-level output.

2.3.2
The Geometric Product and k-blades
The geometric product of two vectors a and b ∈V is defined as
ab = a · b + a ∧b
(2.8)
Where a · b is the inner product known from traditional vector algebra, and a ∧b is the
outer product, which is also called the wedge product. The outer product produces an
object that represents an oriented plane spanned by a and b, which is called a bivector [7].
More generally, the geometric product of k basis vectors produces a k-blade. Since the
basis vectors are orthogonal, the inner product between them equals zero. As a result,
the geometric product between the basis vector reduces to the outer product. Using the
shorthand convention eij := eiej, we can form the following blades from the basis vectors:
e12 := e1e2 = e1 ∧e2,
e23 := e2e3 = e2 ∧e3,
e31 := e3e1 = e3 ∧e1,
e123 := e1e2e3 = e1 ∧e2 ∧e3.
(2.9)

3.1.1
Invariant Message-Passing Networks
The earliest message-passing models for molecular property prediction are invariant MPNNs.
These models use scalar-valued node embeddings and use invariant features such as pair-
wise edge distances, bond angles, and torsions angles.
The earliest approach was Gilmer et al. [5], who in addition to formulating the MPNN
framework, applied several variations of it to the QM9 dataset, referred to as NMP in
table 3.1.
This model implemented the message function as an MLP that transforms
10
Graph Neural Networks based on Geometric Algebra
==PAGE:19==
the node features of the sender node and the pairwise edge distance between sender and
receiver nodes.
It was followed by SchNet [9], which introduced the idea of continuous-filter convolutional
layers, that expand the pairwise edge distances using radial basis functions (RBF), and
then multiplies the output elementwise with the node features. The idea of expanding the
edge distances using RBF was adopted by many models going forward, and is also used
in PaiNN and our GA-GNN model.
MGCN [10] proposed an architecture that models message passing as a hierarchy of
layers that operate on pair-wise, triple-wise embeddings, etc.
up to T layers creating
multilevel representations of each atom. The readout layer then sums over an MLP applied
to the concatenation of these multilevel representations. Besides node-level embeddings
initialized with the atom type, they include edge level embeddings that are initialized
similarly based on the atom type of the two nodes that the edge connects. Even though
the model is one of the earlier MPNN approaches to molecular property prediction, it still
has the best performance on predicting isotropic polarizability (α2
0) and is competitive in
predicting zero point vibrational energy (ZPVE) on the QM9 dataset.
DimeNet [25] expanded on the previous approaches by incorporating directional infor-
mation in addition to distances. This was done by computing the angles between pairs of
edges that share a central node, i.e. angles of the form ∠xkxjxi for triplets of atom nodes
(k.j, i). These angles are then transformed using spherical basis functions (SBFs), and
combined with the RBF expanded pairwise distances. DimeNet++ [11] is an improved
version of DimeNet through architectural optimizations that reduce training time and im-
prove accuracy. It includes an embedding hierarchy that temporarily reduces the feature
dimension of the embeddings via a down-projection layer and then later it is restored with
an up-projection layer.
SphereNet [12] builds on SchNet and DimeNet by formulating a spherical message pass-
ing framework, which represents atom triplets using spherical coordinates. It computes
the torsion angle, which describes how a plane of atoms is rotated with respect to another.
The messages are then computed in a local spherical coordinate system centered at the
receiver atom.
3.1.2
Equivariant Graph Neural Networks
As mentioned earlier, equivariant models incorporate equivariant internal feature repre-
sentations in addition to invariant scalar features.
EGNN [15] is an E(n)-equivariant model that maintains scalar node features alongside
continually updating embeddings of the 3D node coordinates of atoms. In the message
function the model uses the pairwise relative squared edge distance between these node
coordinate embeddings, as opposed to just the unchanging edge distance between nodes
used by other models. The node coordinate features are updated in each message passing
round using a weighted sum of pairwise distances to all other nodes and learnable networks.
PaiNN [1] represents atoms using both scalar and vector features in Cartesian coordi-
nates. As mentioned, the starting point for designing the GA-GNN model was the PaiNN
architecture. Overall, the model follows the message-passing framework with successive
message blocks and update blocks. The message block uses the continuous-filter convolu-
tions from SchNet with an additional cosine cutoff function applied. These are multiplied
elementwise with an MLP which transforms the scalar features of neighboring nodes. The
output of this multiplication is split into three parts. The first is used as scalar messages
directly, the second and third is used to compute vector messages, which also depends on
Graph Neural Networks based on Geometric Algebra
11
==PAGE:20==
the normalized edge vectors going from neighboring nodes to receiver nodes. In the update
block the scalar features are updated using the scalar product of two linear projections of
the vector features, and both the vector and scalar features are scaled by gates computed
from an MLP that uses the scalar features and the norm of one of the linear projections
of the vector features. For the readout layer PaiNN introduces gated equivariant blocks,
where the scalar features and the norm of a linear projection of the vector features are
used to scale the vector features and compute new scalar features, through an MLP. These
blocks are used to predict the dipole moment target in QM9 (µ), and for scalar-valued
targets they instead apply a two-layer MLP.
EQGAT [16] builds on PaiNN by using a very similar architecture, with the main dif-
ference being that the filter used in message aggregation uses self-attention coeﬀicients
computed from an attention mechanism that depends both on the scalar representations
of sender and receiver pairs as well as the RBF expanded pairwise distances. The model
achieves similar results to PaiNN on QM9 on most targets, but has the best reported re-
sult for predicting the dipole moment (µ) along with the Equiformer and TorchMD-NET
models.
LieConv [14] generalizes convolutional neural networks to continuous data. While it is
not defined as a graph neural network in the traditional sense, LieConv functions similarly
to a message-passing model when applied to molecular data: each atom is associated with
3D coordinates and a learned feature embedding that is updated in successive layers by
aggregating information from nearby atoms. This aggregation is performed using a learned
convolutional kernel that operates on the relative positions of atom pairs. Each LieConv
layer thus can be seen as playing a similar role to a message-passing step in graph networks.
Cormorant [13], SEGNN [18] and MACE [20] go beyond scalar or vector represen-
tations to feature representations that are tensors, which they update using operations
based on spherical harmonics and tensor products that preserve the equivariant structure.
MACE is based on computing higher body-order messages rather than 2-body order mes-
sages, i.e. messages that are computed using information from more than 2 neighboring
atoms at once. Most MPNNs are 2-body order, including PaiNN. However, DimeNet can
be seen as 3-body order as it uses angles computed from triplets of atoms, and SphereNet
can be seen as 4-body order as it uses 4 atoms to compute the torsion angle. MACE
computes these higher-body order messages via tensor products of the features based on
Atomic Cluster Expansion (ACE) [26]. These tensor products are multiplied with a learn-
able weight matrix that is specific to the atom-type of the receiver node. We apply the
idea of using atom-type specific weights by incorporating separate MLPs per atom type in
the GA-GNN architecture. Overall, MACE shows that with their 4-body messages they
can lower the number of message passing rounds to 2, and still achieve state of the art
performance.
TensorNet [21] also uses tensor features as node representations. However, instead of
computing higher-body order messages like MACE they focus on Cartesian tensor rep-
resentations, and they use matrix product operations that simplify feature mixing when
compared to the other tensor models. This reduces the computational cost, while the
model still achieves state-of-the-art results on the energy and enthalpy targets.
3.1.3
Regularization Techniques and Pretraining
A general problem that GNNs can suffer from is oversmoothing, where node represen-
tations become too similar after multiple rounds of message passing, which makes it
harder for the model to learn useful features because it can’t distinguish between dif-
ferent nodes [3].

==PAGE:21==
NoisyNodes [17] proposed adding random noise to the atom coordinates during training
to overcome this problem, and using an extra output head to predict the original coordi-
nates. The presence of a denoising task is then meant to encourage the model to retain
distinguishing information for nodes in order to reduce oversmoothing. They evaluated
another GNN architecture called Graph Network-based Simulators (GNS) [27] with their
Noisy Nodes method on QM9, reported in table 3.1 as NoisyNodes.
Later GNS-TAT+NN [19] extended this idea by using the denoising task as pretraining
instead of as a regularizer. The model is first trained to predict original atom coordinates
from noisy ones, and then the model is fine-tuned on the actual property prediction task
using clean coordinates. They tested this method on GNS as well, but with an added
normalization scheme called Tailored Activation Transforms (TAT) [28]. This approach
achieves the best performance on the Gap target (the gap between the highest occupied
molecular orbital energy (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital energy
(LUMO)), and on the ZPVE target in QM9.
3.1.4
Non Message-Passing Approaches
While this work focuses on GNN models, some non message-passing approaches have also
achieved strong performance on molecular property prediction tasks, including on the
QM9 benchmark. For completeness, they are briefly mentioned here without detailing
their architectures.
TorchMD-NET [22] and Equiformer [23] are transformer models. They both achieve
top-performance on the dipole moment target.

layers, and also performs well with a single layer.

3.2
Geometric Algebra in Deep Learning
There has been relatively little work that combines GA with deep learning so far. However
recently several models have been proposed, of which many are based on Clifford Group
Equivariant Neural Networks (CGENNs) proposed by Ruhe et al. [2]. CGENNs
represent neurons in neural networks as multivectors.
The networks consist of linear
layers and geometric product layers.
The linear layers operate independently on each grade of the multivectors, using a separate
learnable transformation for each grade. A bias term is additionally added for the scalar
component. In our model, we instead apply linear transformations to multivectors through
a shared linear transformation across all blades, using the same transformation indepen-
dently and identically on each. This reflects a difference in design choices, where ours is
more focused on learning how the multivector representation as a whole should evolve,
rather than specializing transformations per grade. Both approaches preserve O(3) equiv-
ariance. CGENN linear layers allow for greater per-grade expressiveness and the inclusion
of bias terms for the scalar component (and could also be added for the trivector compo-
nent in the case of G(3,0,0)), while ours reduces the number of learnable parameters and
emphasizes an idea of treating the multivector as a unified object rather than a collection
of separate grades. We tested both approaches during development, and found that in

our setting, shared linear layers improved stability during training. However, this could
be revisited given the results of the ablation study, as will be discussed later.
The geometric product layers operate similarly to the weighted geometric product we
derived in section 2.3. Rather than taking the product between each combination of pairs
of multivectors in the layer, they choose to take the product between each multivector and a
linear projection of itself to reduce computational cost. Analogously, we take the weighted
geometric product between each multivector node representation and a linear projection in
our update function. However, we also test using the weighted geometric product between
sender-receiver pairs in the message function. They define two types of geometric product
layers. (1) The elementwise variant computes one weighted geometric product per feature
channel and the output channel is computed from the corresponding input channel only.
(2) The fully-connected type also computes one product per input channel, but then forms
each output channel as a linear combination of all channel-wise products. Our approach is
analogous to the elementwise type, as we also compute one weighted geometric product per
feature dimension for the multivector node representations, and the output multivector is
derived solely from the corresponding input channel pair. If computationally feasible in a
message-passing architecture it could be interesting to explore the fully-connected variant
in future work.
In addition to linear layers and geometric product layers, CGENNs use normalization
techniques operating on multivectors.
First, before they compute geometric products,
they divide each grade of the multivectors by a factor based on a learned scalar passed
through the logistic sigmoid function and a measure that depends on the grades magnitude.
Moreover, they use layer-wide normalization from [6], which normalizes each grade of the
multivectors across channels by centering it, dividing by the average norm across channels,
and then multiplying by a learnable scalar. We do not apply normalization techniques
in our architecture, so that is another avenue of potential further development of our
approach.
As mentioned, the authors have proposed several models based on CGENNs. Clifford
Group Equivariant Simplicial Message Passing Networks [29] use CGENNs to
perform equivariant shared simplicial message passing, which is equivariant message pass-
ing on simplicial complexes rather than graphs (simplicial complexes are graphs that also
include higher-order objects such as triangles or tetrahedra). They show how to embed
simplicial complexes into GA by representing simplex features with the geometric product
of their edges. In Clifford-Steerable Convolutional Neural networks (CS-CNNs)
[30] they show how to use CGENNs to build a convolutional neural network architecture
that operates on multivectors. Specifically, the kernels, which they call Clifford-steerable
kernels, are parameterized using CGENNs.
Three different GA message passing architectures are proposed in [31]. However, they do
not apply weighted geometric products, like in the geometric product layers from CGENNs
or our model, but use non-weighted geometric products to focus on using GA in a less
computationally expensive way. We test non-weighted vs. weighted geometric products
in the context of our architecture as well. First, they propose Clifford-EGNN, which is
based on the EGNN model covered in the previous section. This model replaces the node

function. Second, they propose MVN-GNN, which extends Vector Neurons (VN)[32] to

4.2
GA-GNN Architecture
We now describe the GA-GNN architecture in detail. On a high-level, the model is based
on integrating multivector representations with the message passing architecture from
PaiNN, and using the weighted geometric product between multivectors as the main op-
eration.
GA-GNN is designed to operate on node features represented as multivectors in G(3,0,0),
consisting of scalar, vector, bivector, and trivector components. The scalar and trivector
components are initialized using learned embeddings of the atomic number associated with
the node. The vector and bivector components are initialized as zero.
The model performs a series of message passing rounds comprised of message blocks and
update blocks while preserving equivariance to O(3). The message block is an extension
of the message block from PaiNN to multivector representation. Additionally, one can
incorporate the geometric product between sender-receiver pairs into the message block, or
between senders and linear projections of themselves, which we also test. The update block
applies a series of linear projections and weighted geometric products to the multivector
representations of each node and then gates the residual update with separate MLPs per
atom type.
The readout layer differs depending on the target property.
Moreover, we define two
different models based on the ablation study we performed. The first called GA-GNN-
V1 utilizes the readout layers from PaiNN, but with separate atom-type specific MLPs
in the gated equivariant blocks. Following ablation on V1, we found that removing the
readout layers entirely and instead directly using the final multivector embeddings from
the last message passing round increased performance and training stability. We call this
model GA-GNN-V2.

5
Results
5.1
Experimental Setup
We evaluate our GA-GNN model on the QM9 dataset [36, 37]. The dataset consists of
data for 130,831 small molecules. Atoms can either be carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen
(O), nitrogen (N) and fluorine (F). We use the version of the dataset published by PyTorch
Geometric [38, 39].
Each molecule has 19 regression targets. In this work we limit ourselves to evaluating
the GA-GNN model on the first 3 targets, as well as the electronic spatial extent target.
These are:
• Target 0: Dipole moment (µ)
• Target 1: Isotropic polarizability (α)
• Target 2: Highest occupied molecular orbital energy (ϵHOMO)
• Target 5: Electronic spatial extent (⟨R2⟩)
5.1.1
Experimental Design
We perform three sets of experiments. As the V2 model was a consequence of the final
ablation study, experiment 2 and 3 are performed on the V1 model. However, we perform
the main evaluation on both models.
1. Main Evaluation on all 4 targets:
We train and evaluate the GA-GANN-V1 and GA-GNN-V2 on the four selected
targets. For each target we report the mean absolute error (MAE) on the test set
across 3 random seeds and compare to the performance of the models covered in
Section 3.1.
2. Feature dimension and depth study:
We investigate how the feature dimension of the multivector states and the number of
message passing rounds affect the performance and training dynamics by comparing
the V1 model performance across different configurations on the dipole moment
target.
3. Ablation study and testing different GA architectures:
We perform an ablation study on the GA-GNN architecture with the V1 model,
where specific components of the model are removed or modified in order to evaluate
their contribution to the performance. In addition, we test different variants of the
GA-GNN architecture. We report test MAE for each variant on the dipole moment
target.
The V1 model uses feature dimensions equal to 64 and 4 message passing rounds. This
was determined from the feature dimension and depth study. As mentioned, the ablation
study prompted us to repeat the main evaluation with the V2 model, but instead use a
higher feature dimension of 128. The V1 model is not consistently stable across seeds with
feature dimension 128 and 4 rounds, whereas the V2 model is.

6
Discussion
6.1
Overview of Key Findings
This thesis defined three main objectives: (1) to develop a novel graph neural network
architecture based on geometric algebra, (2) to evaluate its performance on molecular
property prediction tasks, and (3) to explore its scalability and robustness.
In pursuit of these goals, we have designed GA-GNN, a message passing architecture that
utilizes multivectors to represent nodes and incorporate the geometric product as the core
operation. The design of the model enables a structured way to incorporate interactions
across feature representations of different dimensions beyond scalars and vectors through
the geometric product between multivectors. We have shown that together with architec-
tural elements from PaiNN, this approach can be implemented eﬀiciently and trained on
a realistic dataset.
The evaluation on the QM9 dataset demonstrated that GA-GNN can achieve competitive
performance on four molecular property targets. The model matches or exceeds strong
baselines, such as PaiNN in several cases, and achieves the same performance as the best
models on predicting the dipole moment. Additionally, ablation studies and testing of
different architectures gave insights into the optimal design and placement of geometric
product operations in message-passing. We also identified a variant of the architecture
(GA-GNN-V2) that achieved improved stability and performance by simplifying the out-
put layer.
Finally, the scalability and robustness of the architecture was examined. The experiments
show that it is feasible to use GA, including weighted geometric products, in message-
passing architectures at scale. However, while V2 is able to train at higher feature di-
mensions and message passing depth than V1, stability remains a challenge. Analyzing
training dynamics and testing different model configurations reveal that both models are
operating on the upper limit of what is stable in terms of feature dimension and message
round depth. Nonetheless the results point to promising directions for further develop-
ment.
6.2
Strengths and Contributions
Several conceptual and practical insights can be taken from this work.
At a high level, the results show that using GA to redesign an existing message-passing
architecture (PaiNN) is possible and can also improve model performance. This is similar
to how CGENN’s were applied to redesign EGNNs into Clifford-EGNNs as mentioned in
Section 3.2.
From a conceptual standpoint, GA-GNN demonstrates that we can incorporate multiple
weighted geometric products into the update function in message-passing networks. And
this can be done even at relatively high feature dimensions, while maintaining stability
and achieving strong performance. This shows that the weighted geometric product is
not just mathematically well-motivated but also a feasible operation and an expressive
building block for GNN architectures.
A key insight that the ablation study revealed was that using the geometric product to
mix between grades within a node (in the update block) is more effective than using it
Graph Neural Networks based on Geometric Algebra
37
==PAGE:46==
to combine features across different nodes (in the message block). The latter approach is
conceptually appealing, but degraded performance and also adds significant computational
cost due to the per-edge complexity vs. per-node complexity. The initial part of the project
focused on using the geometric product between receiver and sender nodes, and only later
in the process did we discover that not using it in the message block improved stability and
performance. With that being said, the ablation study did show that an effective use of
the geometric product in the message block could be to take the product between senders
and linear projections of themselves (similarly to how we do it in the update block) before
aggregating to the receiver nodes. This was not tested further, but could be explored in
future work.
Another insight from the ablation study, as mentioned earlier, is that the expressiveness
of the geometric product provides the ability to remove the readout layer and use the mul-
tivector embeddings from the final message passing round directly instead. This enables
a simplification of the overall model design and improved both performance and stability
in the majority of cases. This suggests that the benefit of using GA is not necessarily in
adding more parameters, but in enabling more expressive representations through grade
interactions. The results concerning unweighted vs. weighted geometric products support
this interpretation, as they perform comparably, although a bit less accurate, showing
that he most benefit comes from the geometric product itself, rather than from additional
learned weights. However, unweighted variants did exhibit more training instability in
the development process. We also found that combining multiple geometric products in
a linear combinations resulted in a stable training process and higher performance. In
particular, two geometric products per update block appeared to provide the best perfor-
mance.
An additional strength of the multivector representation is the flexibility it can give in the
design of the output layer. The model can selectively use the grades that are best suited
for a particular prediction target. For example, in the prediction of the dipole moment
(vector-valued target), we can construct the output by summing over the vector and scalar
grades. This avoids the need for decoupled or task-specific representations and instead al-
low the same multivector structure to support targets of different geometric types within a
unified architecture. The flexibility of this grade-structure suggests that it may be possible
to predict higher-grade outputs, such as bivectors or trivectors by also simply aggregating
the corresponding components in the final layer in a suitable way. Although this remains
speculative as we have not tested on such targets directly in this work. Thus, a unique
benefit of using GA to design GNNs, highlighted in this work, is that it can represent and
then extract geometrically meaningful quantities without needing to redesign the model
significantly. Again, the ablation results supports this perspective by showing that remov-
ing the gated equivariant blocks which are directly designed to process vector features for
the dipole moment improved stability and performance while simplifying the architecture.
The fact that a task-specific module was unnecessary underscores the versatility of the
multivector representation. It shows that grade selection alone can be suﬀicient to tailor
the output to the target quantity, without requiring separate architectural mechanism for
each geometric type.
In regard to practical insight, we developed an implementation of batched weighted ge-
ometric products in PyTorch that can support both weighted and unweighted geometric
products and can generalize well to other message passing architectures. Hence, it pro-
vides a solid foundation for further experimentation, whether to improve on the current
design, adapting other models or designing completely novel architectures based on GA.

6.3
Limitations and Challenges
While the results of this thesis show the feasibility and potential of incorporating GA into
GNNs, several limitations and challenges revealed themselves during the development and
evaluation process.
First, one of the main challenges is the computational cost and parameter count of the pro-
posed models. GA-GNN has a significantly higher parameter count compared to PaiNN
or other state-of-the-art equivariant GNNs. Analyzing training dynamics show that the
model typically converges within a reasonable amount of epochs, but the time required per
epoch is substantial. The update block is the primary bottleneck because of the computa-
tion of successive weighted geometric products. So, while a full convergence is achievable,
it comes at the expense of long training times. Doing a more thorough complexity analysis,
beyond just parameter count and the number of epochs used in training, would be inter-
esting for future work. In such an analysis, we could include the training time per epoch,
memory usage, and inference time. Additionally, it should include detailed comparisons
to other models in terms of complexity. One thing that increases the parameter count
quite significantly, other than the weighted geometric products, is the use of atom-type
specific MLPs, rather than a single shared MLP for all atom types. While it improved
performance during development, we did not test it in the final ablation study, which
could be done to further evaluate the trade-off between performance and computational
complexity.
A second limitation is in regards to the scope of the experimental evaluation. In the main
evaluation we only test on 4 molecular properties from QM9. A better measure of the
models performance could be obtained by testing on all the targets. The feature dimension
and message round experiments, and the variation and ablation studies were limited to
a single target and a single seed. Regarding the seed, the low variability of performance
across seeds that we saw in the main evaluation indicate that this is probably not too
limiting. However, testing on other targets in the additional experiments, and testing the
V2 variation would have expanded the scope of the evaluation and possible led to greater
insights. Moreover, the performance of the model remains completely unexplored on other
domains and datasets, so it is not clear how well the architecture can generalize beyond
he small molecules in QM9. Hence, while the results are promising, broader claims about
the approach are still speculative and there are many things left to explore and learn.
Next, training stability is still a concern despite the improved stability of V2 compared to
V1. In the training process we observe large spikes in the training loss, especially at higher
feature dimensions and message passing rounds. Even though V2 was able to train stably
at 128 feature dimension and 4 message passing rounds, when we attempted to increase
the feature dimension to 192 (at 4 rounds) it led to unstable training. This indicates
that the V2 model operates near the upper limit of what is stable in terms of the feature
dimension. However, increasing the number of message passing rounds to 5 at 128 feature
dimension for the V2 model was not attempted due to time constraints.
Another limitation is that design choices made along the way in the development process
were primarily based on evaluating the model on a single target property, namely the
dipole moment. This focus was necessary because of long training times prohibiting con-
tinually testing on multiple targets, as well as the diﬀiculty of separating target-specific
implementations factors from architectural effects. In hindsight, incorporating more tests
on maybe just one of the scalar targets during development could have led to other choices
that may have been beneficial. Thus, the design strategy may have led to a risk of target-
specific bias in the final model design. For example, we use the vector norm in the MLPs
Graph Neural Networks based on Geometric Algebra

that compute the residual gates (similarly to PaiNN as well).
While this is probably
beneficial to predicting the dipole moment, it may be suboptimal for other targets. For
instance it could be the case that using the area of the bivector or including the trivec-
tor is beneficial to scalar targets, while it did not improve performance for the dipole
moment. This is of course speculative as we have not tested it. Regardless, while the
model performs competitively across the 4 targets, it is possible that design choices were
inadvertently better suited for vector prediction at the expense of the others, especially
since we see that the best performance of the model is on the dipole moment.
Overall, these limitations and challenges point to interesting directions for future work.
We can address the computational cost, improve stability, and evaluate the model across
more targets and domains in order to further explore the GA-based approach proposed in
this thesis.
6.4
Suggestions for Further Work
In addition to addressing the limitations of the thesis, several opportunities for further
exploration and improvement of using GA in the design of GNNs can be identified from
our work.
In terms of architectural development, we have shown that incorporating GA into the
PaiNN architecture to design a new model can improve or match performance on molecular
property prediction tasks. This is an indication that a promising direction for future work
would be to continue to integrate GA into other state-of-the-art models beyond PaiNN. A
related approach was taken by the authors of CLifford-EGNNs, MVP-GNNs and MVN-
GNNs as mentioned in the related work section. However, these models have not been
evaluated on QM9, and it would be interesting to revisit those architectures for that
dataset for a direct comparison to our approach. Additionally, following this comparison
one could experiment with incorporating/mixing architectural ideas between the models
and ours. For example, Clifford-EGNNs retained seperate scalar embeddings in addition
to the multivector embeddings, which we could try in our model. From CGENNs, another
promising idea would be to use their normalization layers before computing the weighted
geometric products in the update block. This could potentially improve stability in our
model and allow for deeper message passing or feature dimensions. Moreover, revisiting our
linear projections of multivectors by using the linear layers from CGENNs as opposed to
our shared linear layers could also be very interesting. We chose to use the latter because it
improved stability during development, but given that the ablation study showed increased
stability by removing the output layer, it could be worth trying it again on the V2 model.
Perhaps in a combination of grade-wide layers for some projections, and shared for others,
if not replacing all of them. Finally, the use of fully-connected geometric product layers
as opposed to elementwise layers also remain unexplored for our model.
Beyond other GA-models we can also draw inspiration from EQGAT, which modifies
PaiNNs message block by replacing the convolutional filters from SchNet with a self-
attention mechanism.
As mentioned in the related work section these coeﬀicients are
computed from both sender and receiver scalar features, as well as the RBF-expanded
distances. In contrast in our model, like in PaiNN, the filter weights are only computed
from the sender scalar features and RBF features. Looking closer at EQGAT and adapting
it to GA, similarly to what we have done with PaiNN, could potentially lead to an even
better model, and maybe the geometric product could also be more effective in the message
block.
Another unexplored direction is pretraining and denoising for a GA-GNN model. As men-

tioned in the related work section, this approach have proven effective for other equivariant
models, so given the representational capacity of multivector embeddings, it could maybe
be interesting to explore those methods in the context of a GA model.
The experimental setup in our experiments also have room for improvement in regard
to the training hyperparameters. In our experiments we reused hyperparameters from
PaiNN, but this may not be optimal for the GA-GNN architecture.
We did test the
most impactful architectural parameters (feature dimension and message rounds), but
other hyperparameters like learning rate, batch size or the weight decay factor were not

Hence, a more extensive hyperparameter experimentation could
potentially make improvements both in terms of performance and stability.
The choice of the GA space could also be tested further. For our model we only tested
G(3,0,0), but we could test extending the dimensionality of the multivectors from 8 to 16
by using G(3,0,1). This is what they do in the GA transformer model GATr as mentioned
in the related work section. While it would lead to even higher computational cost, it
could still be interesting to try as it would allow for richer multivector representations and
possibly lead to better performance.
Regarding the output layer, while the results show that removing it improves performance
and stability in our case, this does not necessarily mean that investigating other output
mechanisms could not be beneficial. A more carefully designed output layer that is better
aligned with the expressive capacity of the GA update block might provide further gains.
Developing such a readout architecture, or testing output layers from other models, re-
mains an open question. Moreover, while removing it improved performance on the dipole
moment target for V1, it is still unclear whether this holds for the other targets or if the
improved performance with V2 on those targets is mostly a consequence of enabling train-
ing at higher message rounds and feature dimension. Since the model became unstable at
128 feature dimension and 4 message rounds when using output layers, a direct compari-
son of output layer vs. none for V2 is not currently feasible. As a starting point, it would
be useful to conduct a similar ablation study on V1 for the scalar targets at 64 feature
dimensions, to evaluate whether removing the output layer directly improves performance
in those settings as well. Since the performance on the ⟨R2⟩target was worse for V2 than
V1 it would indicate that this is not necessarily the case. Furthermore, a direct continau-
tion of the work in this thesis could be to perform ablation and variation experiments on
V2 in addition to V1. Since V2 differed in its handling of the output and overall stability,
it is possible that architectural choices behave differently in that context and could bring
further insights or performance improvements. Moreover, these experiments should be
performed on multiple targets as well, beyond just the dipole moment.
Taken together, these directions highlight both the flexibility and the opportunities of
incorporating GA into GNN models.
While this thesis demonstrates the feasibility of
such an approach, many of its design choices remain underexplored, and further work is
needed to understand the implications and potential of multivector representations and
the geometric product in message-passing networks. Continued experimentation across
architectures, datasets, and tasks will be key to unlocking the broader potential of GA.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis we introduced a novel graph neural network architecture based on geometric
algebra and PaiNN, which we call GA-GNN. The model uses multivector node repre-
sentations and interactions based on weighted geometric products to perform structured
feature transformations across multiple geometric levels. We build on recent work such
as CGENNs, who explored multivector representations and the geometric product within
neural networks, and distinguish the work by investigating different design choices in the
context of equivariant message passing, and by evaluating the model on the QM9 molec-
ular property prediction dataset. Our results show that this approach is both feasible
and competitive with state-of-the-art models, giving new insights into the application of
geometric algebra in graph neural networks.
Along the way several insights into the design of GA-based message passing networks have
been revealed.
The experiments show that incorporating multiple weighted geometric
products into the architecture is possible at scale and can achieve strong performance. In
particular, we found that using the geometric product to mix between grades to update
node representations is more effective than using it to combine features across neighboring
nodes. Moreover, the grade-wise structure of multivectors can be used to simplify the
model design, especially in regard to the output layer, where different types of target
properties can be predicted without changing the architecture but simply by using the
relevant grade components.
Despite these promising results, the work has several limitations, which we discuss. First,
the weighted geometric product comes with heavy computational costs, and overall our
model has a relatively high parameter count compared to other equivariant MPNN archi-
tectures. Second, the scope of our experimental evaluation is limited. The model was only
tested on 4 targets from QM9, and subsequent experiments were limited to the dipole mo-
ment target. Third, the models operate on the limit of stable training in terms of what is
possible for feature dimensions and message passing rounds, and stability was a persistent
challenge throughout the development phase.
From these limitations as well as insights gained from the the overall work several directions
for future work have been identified. From an architectural standpoint, future work could
explore the possibility of analyzing and incorporating ideas from other GA approaches,
such as normalization techniques and grade-wise linear transformations from CGENNs.
Moreover, ideas from other equivariant MPNNs such as the attention mechanism from
EQGAT could be implemented and tested. Further evaluation and improvement of GA-
GNN could be found by comparisons with other recent GA approaches including models
like Clifford-EGNN, MVP-GNNs, and MVN-GNNs, either on QM9 or other datasets.
Improvements may also be found from developing output layers that are better aligned
with the geometric product update block. Beyond model design, there is also potential
in a more extensive hyperparameter search, and from a GA perspective experimenting
with alternative algebras, such as G(3,0,1) could be interesting. Finally, the scope of our
evaluation could be expanded by performing ablation and variation experiments on the
V2 model, as well as systematic testing across all QM9 targets and on new domains.
In particular we have not tested the prediction of target properties that are inherently
bivectors or trivector structured. Overall, the insights gained from the conduction of this
thesis can be used to further explore the possibilities of using geometric algebra for GNNs
going forward.

Listing A.1: Weighted geometric product in PyTorch
mv1 and mv2 each have shape RB×8×D, where B is the batch size, 8 is the number of basis
blades in G3,0,0, and D is the feature dimension. The function weighted_multivector_product
computes the weighted geometric product between the two, using two precomputed index
tensors:
• gp_idx (8 × 8) maps each blade-blade interaction (i, j) to an output index in the
range [0, 15]. The range [0, 7] is for positive contributions, and [8, 15] is for negative
contributions.
• w_idx (8 × 8) selects which of 20 learnable weight vectors W ∈R20×D to apply to
each blade pair (i, j).
All 8×8 = 64 pairwise interaction terms are computed in parallel using broadcasting, and
weighted using w_idx resulting in a tensor of shape (B, 8, 8, D). The weighted interac-
tions are then reshaped to (B, 64, D), and accumulated into a temporary tensor of shape
(B, 16, D) using gp_idx to determine the destination indices. The final result is computed
