Abstract
Multi-point evaluation (MPE) is the problem of evaluating a single polynomial in
several points simultaneously. In this thesis we will first present the problem of MPE
and discuss its various applications. Then we will provide a survey of historically
significant developments within MPE including an algorithm for fast univariate MPE
and the algorithms for bivariate MPE by Pan [Pan94], N├╝sken and Ziegler [NZ04], and
Kedlaya and Umans [KU08]. We will then especially discuss the recent development
of reshapers and reshaping by Neiger, Rosenkilde, and Solomatov [NRS20], as well
as the application of MPE allowing for precomputation. This discussion of reshapers
concludes with the presentation of an algorithm for computing reshapers, as well as
a generalisation of reshapers to the trivariate case.
Finally, we will present some simple experimental findings regarding the genericity
assumption of [NRS20].

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Though algebra has been studied for millennia, the advent of computers and hence
automatic computation is relatively recent. While algorithms had already been pre-
sented for various tasks prior to these recent advancements, many of them proved to
be highly ine’¼Ćicient. Thus around the 1970ŌĆÖs the study of e’¼Ćicient algorithms began
and computer algebra was born. Notably, multivariate polynomials have proven to
be extremely useful in many areas such as cryptography, algebraic coding theory, and
robotics by being rather simple objects that are nonetheless able to describe a wide va-
riety of phenomena and rules. As a result, they have become central objects of study
in mathematics, because as the problems we wish to solve continue to grow rapidly
in size, it is all the more important that we can manipulate polynomials e’¼Ćiciently.
This kind of manipulation continues to demand new and innovative solutions to keep
up with the increasingly di’¼Ćicult computational problems we wish to solve. Many
operations on multivariate polynomials such as multiplication, modular composition,
and factorisation have been shown to be reducible to multi-point evaluation (MPE),
which is the problem of evaluating a polynomial in several points simultaneously.
This thesis will conduct an investigation on some of the most important mile-
stones regarding MPE in the form of existing algorithms, as well as the insight that
they provide. We will start at the infancy of MPE and continue up until the most
recent cutting edge developments in the field. Following this, we will provide a novel
contribution in the form of a higher-dimensional generalisation of a recent algorithm
which was previously only capable of handling bivariate polynomials.
As mentioned one area where multivariate polynomials are used is in coding theory
for error correcting codes, specifically for algebraic geometry codes, which use such
polynomials to represent messages. Encoding is then done through evaluation of the
polynomials. Computers are communicating over the internet more than ever and in
order to have e’¼Ćicient communication across the internet this communication ought
to use error correction. It is therefore essential for the adaption of these codes that
the encoding and decoding of messages can be performed as quickly as possible.
When analysing algorithms we will measure their asymptotic algebraic complex-
ity meaning that we will count the number of basic field operations (i.e. addition,
subtraction, multiplication, and division) in the field that we are working in. This
is well-suited for finite fields as all elements can be represented within some fixed
size and thus the field operations are assumed to have complexity O(1). It is less
well-suited for settings such as infinite fields where the size of the representation of

The naive solution to the problem is to evaluate the given polynomial in each of
the points one after another. It is well known that evaluation at a single point of a
univariate polynomial of degree less than n canŌĆÖt be computed faster than by using
HornerŌĆÖs rule, which requires nŌłÆ1 additions and nŌłÆ1 multiplications, i.e. O(n) field
operations [Gat11, Section 5.2]. Consequently, performing multi-point evaluation as
n individual evaluations will cost O(n2) operations.
We can, however, do much better than this in terms of time complexity, as we will
discuss. For example, the method that we will discuss in Section 2.1 performs multi-
point evaluation in time O(M(n) log n) where M(n) is the complexity of multiplying
two univariate polynomials of degree n.
Some codes such as Reed-Solomon codes use univariate polynomials and thus
benefit from e’¼Ćicient multi-point evaluation algorithms. Other codes use multivariate
polynomials where still no similar algorithm is known. Over time several authors have
made contributions which try to solve the problem of MPE under different conditions.
Examples include [Pan94] which requires that the points lie in a ŌĆ£latticeŌĆØ, i.e. where
the set of points is the Cartesian product of subsets of the field and [NRS20] allowing
precomputation on the point set.
It is also interesting that so far no generalisation of the solution for univariate
MPE into multiple variables has been found. Many of the of the problems we will
encounter can be traced back to multivariate polynomial rings not being principal
ideal domains.
In other settings we can imagine that we wish to verify that a polynomial vanishes
at a number of points. This could be to verify a solution to a system of equations or
for zero-checking. In computer algebra it is very useful to quickly be able to verify
whether a given element ŌĆō in our case a polynomial from a given polynomial ring ŌĆō
is identical to the zero element. By evaluating the polynomial in su’¼Ćiciently many
points we can verify whether it vanishes everywhere and thus can be identified with
zero.
One of the most e’¼Ćicient ways of performing polynomial multiplication when the
polynomials are represented by their coe’¼Ćicients is by first representing the input
polynomials by their values on a sequence of points, multiplying these element-wise,
and finally interpolating the result to obtain a polynomial represented by its coe’¼Ći-
cients. However this is only more e’¼Ćicient than naively multiplying the polynomials
in coe’¼Ćicient form assuming that the multi-point evaluation and interpolation can be
performed quickly.
This paper starts out with a historical perspective on how the problem of multi-
point evaluation has been solved over time comparing the advantages and disadvan-

Having now looked at several historically significant algorithms for performing MPE
we will now turn our attention to a rather recently developed technique for MPE
called reshaping [NRS20]. Recall that PanŌĆÖs algorithm has quasi-linear complexity
but requires the points to form a lattice. On the other hand N├╝sken and ZieglerŌĆÖs
algorithm does not require such a lattice structure but does not achieve quasi-linear
complexity. Reshaping achieves both quasi-linear complexity and does not require
the points to form a lattice, thus achieving the best of both worlds but under two new
assumptions. The first assumption is that the points to be evaluated are available for
precomputation, i.e. given a set of points known ahead of time we can perform po-
tentially costly computations such that the algorithm can be invoked on these points
e’¼Ćiciently at some later point. One area where this scenario is highly relevant is error
correcting codes. Here the goal is to introduce redundancy into digital information
that is to be transmitted over an unreliable channel such as a wire, via radio transmis-
sion, or on physical storage media such as a CD. One way to methodically create such
redundancy is using algebraic geometry codes. In certain algebraic geometry codes
encoding of messages amounts to MPE of bivariate or even multivariate polynomials
on sequences of points that lie on algebraic curves. In this setting the communicat-
ing parties will have to agree on this sequence of points before communication can
begin. Therefore it makes sense to perform potentially expensive computations on
the points in order to ensure that the encoding required during communication can
be performed quickly. The other assumption is that the point set is balanced, which
requires the existence of certain polynomials that relate to the point set. It should
be noted that the assumption of balancedness is only required for the e’¼Ćiciency of
the algorithm and can be weakened in a way that makes the algorithm completely
general but potentially less e’¼Ćicient. As the authors point out in their paper it is
di’¼Ćicult to give a geometric interpretation of this property and so far the only known
way to check if a point set is balanced is to try to compute these polynomials. This
is still reasonable in practice as the check can be performed during precomputation.
While a probabilistic argument [NRS20, Theorem 8.5] shows that random point
sets over su’¼Ćiciently large fields are balanced with very high probability, it is still not

The general strategy that we will use in order to perform bivariate MPE e’¼Ćiciently
is to transform our input polynomial into a univariate one and apply fast univariate
MPE. As we have seen in Section 2.4, however, there is another piece to the puzzle
of performing MPE e’¼Ćiciently using this strategy, namely that this transformation
itself needs to be e’¼Ćicient. To transform our input polynomial into a univariate one

polynomial, which is exactly what we want for MPE. To ensure that the reshaping
transformation is fast we will find polynomials with a specific monomial support such
that the reduction in each step of the algorithm can be performed e’¼Ćiciently.

The ideas presented in this section should generalise to an arbitrary number of
variables as well. This includes the definition of reshaping pairs and the reshaping
algorithm. Reshaping sequences would in the general case consist of reshaping tuples,
where each tuple contains a number of reshapers, which are applied to the input
polynomial one after another. The degree bounds of the output of the algorithm in
each variable then straightforwardly generalise the pattern seen here. In particular,
we will be able to find reshapers such that the output degree in at least one variable
is guaranteed to be zero and the remaining degrees being bounded by known values.
This means that we can iteratively use reshaping in lower dimensions to arrive at a
univariate polynomial, which can then be evaluated using fast univariate MPE.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
We first discussed the case of univariate MPE, which can be performed for any point
set in quasi-linear time. We then turned our attention to the bivariate case, where
the previous algorithm does generalise. Instead we see saw three different historically
important algorithms, each of which have their own advantages and disadvantages.
PanŌĆÖs algorithm [Pan94] has quasi-linear complexity but assumes that the point set
forms a lattice, or regular grid. The algorithm by N├╝sken and Ziegler [NZ04] works
for general point sets at the cost of not having quasi-linear complexity.
Finally,
the algorithm by Kedlaya and Umans [KU08] performs MPE on general point sets
in ŌĆ£almost linearŌĆØ complexity, i.e.
╦£O(n1+╬Ą) for any ╬Ą > 0.
However, so far no
implementation exists which is e’¼Ćicient for realistic problem sizes.
We then discussed reshaping [NRS20] and its advantages and disadvantages. Re-
shaping performs bivariate MPE in quasi-linear time when allowing precomputation
and assuming that the point set allows for special polynomials called reshapers.
Finally, we provided a generalisation of reshaping for trivariate polynomials in-
cluding an algorithm listing and proved the algorithmŌĆÖs correctness and quasi-linear
complexity.
Further, we touch on how the algorithm could be generalised to any
number of variables.
Finally, in Appendix A, we begin an investigation of the genericity assumption of
[NRS20].
