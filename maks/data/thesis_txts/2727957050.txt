Abstract
The performance of three different solvents, aqueous MEA, aqueous KOH, and cholinechlo-
ride ethyleneglycol, ChCl:2EG, in absorbing CO2 was examined using a wetted wall
column setup. The aqueous solvents were tested at three different concentrations,
ranging from 15 to 50 %w/w and 5 to 25 %w/w for MEA and KOH respectively. The
third solvent, ChCl:2EG, ethaline, was tested with and without water added to the
solvent. The experiments were conducted at temperatures of 20 ‚ó¶C, 40 ‚ó¶C, and 60 ‚ó¶C,
for both MEA and KOH, and 60 ‚ó¶C, and 80 ‚ó¶C for ethaline.
To determine mass transfer parameters such as the liquid side mass transfer coeffi-
cient, certain physical material properties like density and viscosity were measured
in the laboratory. It was found that the measured quantities corresponded with the
data that could be found for both MEA and KOH with little deviation.
The study showed that the experiments run using MEA had a contact time between
the gas and the liquid that was too short. This resulted in poor CO2 absorption, and
inconsistent results. This resulted in the calculated kinetics being significantly lower
than those used for comparison. The experiments with ethaline showed that ethaline
absorbed more water from the saturated gas, than CO2, which affected the measure-
ments and made the results of the further calculations unusable.
The study revealed that the highest mass transfer occurred at 60 ‚ó¶C using 25 %w/w
KOH, and the lowest mass transfer occurred using ethaline as a solvent. It was deter-
mined that for both MEA and KOH the mass transfer increased with both the solvent
temperature and concentration. It also showed that for KOH, which had very con-
sistent result, a larger increase in mass transfer appeared when increasing the tem-
perature from 20 ‚ó¶C to 40 ‚ó¶C, compared to when the temperature was increased from
40 ‚ó¶C to 60 ‚ó¶C.
A sensitivity analysis made on the results from the experiment using KOH 25 w%/w
at 60 ‚ó¶C showed that the liquid side parameters were all highly sensitive to changes in
the density, the diffusion coefficient, and in Henry‚Äôs constant. The parameters showed
less sensitivity to changes in the viscosity, and changes in the pressure almost only af-
fected the gas side mass transfer coefficient slightly.

Resume
This thesis explores the determination of mass transfer, and kinetic coefficients for
MEA, KOH, and ethaline through experiments using a wetted wall column. The the-
sis is organised into several sections, which are each summarised below.
The introduction describes the overall global warming problem, and the effects of
an increased CO2 concentration in the atmosphere. Then it touches on different car-
bon capture techniques, which can be applied to industrial settings that emits larger
amounts of CO2.
The second section runs through the theory of the wetted wall column. It introduces
the two film theory which is used as a model for the mass transfer in the WWC. It
also goes through the theory of the different relevant coefficients for the gas, and liq-
uid side mass transfer. Additionally, the calculation methods for physical properties
like diffusivity, hydraulic diameter, surface area, and contact time are explained. The
last part of the section explores the kinetics of MEA, KOH, ethaline, along with the
reaction rate constant and the enhancement factor.
The experimental section discusses the experimental set-up, and the preparations of
experiments. It runs through a number of repairs and improvements that were per-
formed on the set-up before the experiments were started. It includes a detailed de-
scription of the operation and data collection carried out throughout the experiments.
It also presents the preparation of the solvents used for the experiments.
The experimental data are processed and discussed for all the experiments. This is
one of the main parts of the thesis, since it contains the analysis of the results. The
results obtained for MEA as a validation experiment are compared with kinetics ob-
tained from earlier experiments conducted on the same set-up. The deviation from
the expected results are discussed for all three solvents. Furthermore, a sensitivity
analysis is presented, focusing on how various solvents properties, system measure-
ments, and mass transfer parameters affect the final kinetic calculations.
The discussion section is followed by a conclusions, summarising the findings of the
research. Additionally, a perspective of what would be interesting and relevant to
work with in the future is presented. This includes ideas for improvement of the
experiments, and up-scaling the set-up to pilot scale.

Problem statement
In order to face the rising concentration of anthropogenic carbon dioxide in the at-
mosphere, new and feasible strategies for carbon capture and utilisation are required.
State-of-the art processes for CO2 capture from high concentration point sources use
amine or organic solvents for scrubbing and rely on thermal cycles for solvent regen-
eration. In recent years electrochemical CO2 capture has gained traction due to its
great applicability in small-scale industries and its coupling with renewable energy
sources (e.g., photovoltaic, wind power, and nuclear energy). New electrochemical
capture technologies often use either amines or hydroxides as solvents.
The solvents currently used in the carbon capture industry pose various challenges.
As an example, amines form toxic by-products, generate corrosion, and degrade over
time. Hydroxides are cheap, environmentally friendly, and form no toxic degradation
products. However, they exhibit slow rate of absorption resulting in a poor CO2 mass
transfer.
Optimised use of affordable absorbents, to obtain large loading capacities, fast ki-
netics, and minimal degradation rates, is crucial in the development of cost-effective
CO2 capture solutions.
The project aims to determine the kinetics of CO2 capture with novel solvents, hy-
droxides and deep eutectic solvents. These solvents are of increasing interest in ap-
plications involving solvent based carbon capture from large point sources (power
plants, and cement factories), biogas upgrading, and direct air capture.
Project learning objectives

reaction kinetics.

chemical.

system

‚Ä¢ Comment on the new absorbent‚Äôs effectiveness compared to other conventional
solvents, and consider what implications the new solvent has on the capture
process.

Introduction
According to the UN list of the top 10 world problems climate change is viewed
as the biggest problem, followed by topics such as wars, global health issues, and
poverty. Within the topic of climate change global warming is the largest contributor
and thereby the most urgent problem.
The most important factor within global warming is the accumulation of greenhouse
gasses in our atmosphere. A greenhouse gas is a gas that absorbs and radiates heat.
Currently there are no effective solutions to reverse the effect of the emitted green-
house gasses by somehow removing them from the atmosphere.
The greenhouse gas that is considered to be the most destructive is carbon dioxide,
CO2. Even though the amount of CO2 present in the atmosphere is very low, approx-
imately 0.04 %, it has a very large effect on the climate [1]. While CO2 is not even the
most abundant greenhouse gas in the atmosphere, which is water vapour, it is much
more effective at absorbing and re-releasing heat radiation. The absorbed heat is re-
released in all directions, thereby also back at the earths surface. This is causing rising
temperatures, with CO2 being responsible for about two-thirds of the total heating
influence of all human-produced greenhouse gases [2].
Since the start of the industrial revolution in 1750 the concentration of CO2 in the
atmosphere has increased about 47 %, with the steepest increase happening between
the 1960‚Äôs and present day, fig. 1. Furthermore the increase in concentration in the
past 60 years occurred 100 times faster than any previous natural increases, such a
those that can be seen at the end of the last ice age around 11-17.000 years ago. The
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change estimates that 350 ppm is the safe limit,
with the current concentration being above 400 ppm, at that concentrations above 500
ppm could lead to catastrophic consequences to the environment.
While many different sectors emit large amounts of CO2, the primary source is the
burning of fossil fuels [2]. Globally, during the period from 2009-2018, 42% of CO2
emissions from fossil fuels were from coal, 34% from oil, and 19% from natural gas.
At current levels the emissions from coal is decreasing slightly due to a slightly de-
creased use in the US and EU, but an increased use in countries such as China and
India. The emission from oil and gas is estimated to grow at the same rate as in the 10
year period [3]. In fig. 2 the emissions from fossils and cement is shown over a period
from 1960 to 2019.

One of the challenges with CO2 is that it stays in the atmosphere for a very long time
compared to reactive gasses such as ozone. Once emitted it will stay in the atmo-
sphere somewhere between 300 and 1000 years, and when the release rate of CO2
from the Earth is much quicker than the time it takes for the CO2 to be reabsorbed,
the concentration will increase [1]. When the CO2 is absorbed in the ocean, which is
the primary source of absorption, the CO2 reacts with the water molecules and pro-
duces carbonic acid. This results in ocean acidification, which has caused the pH of
the ocean surface waters to have dropped from 8.21 to 8.10 since the industrial revo-
lution.

In 2015 the UN presented the 17 Sustainable Development Goals, with number 13
being ‚ÄùClimate Action‚Äù. The overall goals for the climate is that the average increase
in temperature on the Earth has to be limited to 1.5 ‚ó¶C compared to pre-industrial lev-
els. This should be reached by reducing the global emission of greenhouse gasses 43
% by 2030, and by 2050 everything has to be carbon neutral. If this goal is not reached
the UN predicts that the sea levels will rise between 30-60 cm by 2100, drought will
displace 700 million people by 2030, and medium to large scale natural disasters will
increase 40 % between 2015 and 2030. Furthermore they note that the money being
spent on climate action currently falls $100 billion short of what is needed [4].
1.1
Carbon Capture and Storage
The purpose of carbon capture and storage, CCS, is to remove CO2 from gas, ranging
from bio-gas to exhaust gasses, and to either utilise the pure CO2 or store it, for exam-
ple in saline aquifer deep below in the ground. When analysing the possible effects
of CCS it can have a significant and direct effect on at least 8 of the 17 Sustainable
Development Goals, shown in fig. 3.
Figure 3: The 8 out of the 17 Sustainable Development Goals that CCS will have a direct
effect on if implemented in large scale on an international plan [5, 4]
Probably the most intuitive solution would be to remove the excess amount of CO2
in the atmosphere, a technology called direct air capture, DAC. However, this is not
a viable solution. While it initially seems like the best solution it is extremely energy
inefficient. Removing CO2 from the atmosphere using DAC requires around 1.200
kilowatt-hours per ton of CO2 removed, this is not only expensive, but most impor-
tantly it would require large amounts of carbon-free energy, which is a great challenge
since more than 60 % of the energy produced comes from fossil fuels including coal,
natural gas, and oil [6].

In 2021 the emission of CO2 reached a record of 37.12 billion metric tons globally,
if this was to be reduced by 40 % using DAC, it would require 1.8 ¬∑ 103 terawatt hours,
which is more than three times the annual energy consumption of Germany [7, 8].
Currently the most efficient method is post combustion carbon capture. For this rea-
son this will also be the focus of this project. However, the solvents researched in
this report could principally also be used for DAC, since the method is the same, the
concentration of CO2 is just much lower in the atmospheric air.
1.1.1
Storage and re-purposing of carbon dioxide
The main purpose of removing CO2 from flue gas is to avoid it being let out into the
atmosphere, but the possibility of re-purposing it also presents itself after the CO2
has been captured. One of the possibilities there is for storage of the captured CO2 is
injection into deep geological formations, called aquifers. These deep saline aquifers
have the largest identified storage potential. In factor, it it anticipated that they have
enough storage potential to hold all CO2 captured in this century [9].
Although this sounds like a viable solution, there are also challenges following this
method of CO2 storage. One of the most severe consequences that is of large concern
to the environment is that the CO2 could potentially leak out of these underground
reservoirs, saline aquifers and also others, into the surrounding air and the again con-
tribute to the global warming, or affect the composition of nearby water supplies.
Another large concern is that the risk of human-made tremors caused by the build-up
of pressure underground, known as induced seismicity [10].
Storing the CO2 in saline aquifers could also be combined with re-purposing the cap-
tured CO2 in the industry. This would be an even more environmental friendly so-
lution since the captured CO2 could be repurposed directly, or converted into value
added fuels.
Globally around 230 million tonnes, Mt, of CO2 are used every year, predominantly
within the fertiliser industry. Here 130 mT is used for the production of urea [11].
The annual emission of CO2 is 37.12 Gt on a global scale. If the goal is to reduce the
amount of CO2 emitted into the atmosphere of 43 % by 2030, this would mean that
approximately 1.45 % of the captured CO2 would cover the global industrial need.
1.1.2
Post Combustion Carbon Capture
Post-combustion carbon capture is a way to remove CO2 from flue gas streams af-
ter the combustion. This is the most developed carbon capture technology, and is a
lucrative solution since it can be fitted to existing power plants and other types of
manufacturing plants. This technology is of great importance for the environment,
but also of great interest for companies since it can ensure compliance with increas-
ingly strict CO2 emission thresholds.

In general post combustion carbon capture consists of two main unit operations, one
that removes the CO2 from the gas using a solvent, and one that recovers the solvent
with the purpose of reuse. In fig. 4 a schematic of a usual post combustion set-up is
shown.
Figure 4: Schematic of a basic chemical absorption process for amine based CO2 capture
[12].
As fig. 4 shows, the process consists of two distinct unit operations, absorption and
desorption. The adsorption unit, also called a scrubber works by counter-current gas-
liquid flow. The flue gas enters in the bottom of the tower, and the solvent from the
top. Usually the tower is packed, either with random packing or structured packing,
which ensures that there is a large contact surface for the gas and liquid. The solvent
used for the absorption is usually an alkaline solution containing an amine.
Post combustion absorption units can be parted into two categories depending on
how the CO2 is desorbed, and how the solvent in regenerated. The two categories are
thermal and electrochemical. For thermal units, heat is used for the release of CO2
from the loaded solvent, and thereby for the regeneration of the solvent. This exhibits
excellent removal of CO2, with outlet streams of a high purity. However, a signif-
icant amount of steam is needed for the heating, and therefore the method is very
energy demanding. Electrically driven carbon capture processes covers technologies
such as pressure, or vacuum swing absorption, and membrane separation, which are
gaining attention within the field due to a lower energy requirement, and greater pos-
sibilities within retrofitting absorption plants to the source of emission. However, the
disadvantage with these technologies compared to thermal amine scrubbing is that
the outlet streams has a low purity of CO2, and may therefore need further purifica-

meates the membrane, and the unwanted are retained. The selectivity can be a result
of difference in molecular size, affinity to membrane material, or molecular weight.

Furthermore, the membranes does not show fast decay, and can therefore run as an
almost continuous process, compared to a thermal absorption unit where the solvent
has to be replaced. Lastly the membrane separation can also be used to remove other
wanted components in the gas, such as Hg vapour or H2S [13].
1.2
Solvents
The primary purpose of solvent is to remove the CO2 from the gas, and therefore a sol-
vent that removes the maximum amount of CO2 could be seen as the optimal solvent.
However, a number of factors have to be taken into account when choosing a solvent
for the post combustion carbon capture process. First of all the solvent has to be able
to remove the CO2 from the gas, furthermore it is highly important that the solvent
is not corrosive since this, over time, will destroy the plant. Another factor that is
crucial to the viability of the process is that the solvent used can be regenerated, and
thereby reused, many times. If the solvent degenerates after only a few uses then it
will not only be expensive in operation, since the solvent has to be replaced very of-
ten, but is will also wont be very sustainable since large quantities of the solvent have

electricity and raw materials.
The most optimal solvent would therefore be a solvent that removed CO2 extremely
effectively, a solvent that is non-corrosive to the plant used, and furthermore a solvent
that can be produced in a sustainable way. A sustainable way to produce chemicals
could be power to x, which is a technology which is mostly within research but also a

1.2.1
Amine solvents
Carbon dioxide scrubbing by aqueous amine solution is the state-of-the-art carbon
capture solution, with many full scale implementation in different industries around
the world.
Many different amines, and combinations of different amines, have been researched
through simulations, laboratory work and pilot plant tests. One of the most thor-
oughly tested, promising, state-of-the-art amines used as a solvent for this purpose is
monoethanolamine, MEA. The mechanism given in this section is not written to be
specifically for MEA, but for a general amine solvent. Solvents such as methyldiethanolamine

Alkaline solvents
Alkaline solutions have been used commercially for many years for treating acid gas
streams in Benfield processes especially removing H2S. The main challenge associ-
ated with the use of alkaline solutions for CO2 capture is the slow reaction rate of
absorption, resulting in poor CO2 mass transfer.
Previous experimental work indicate that potassium hydroxide has improved CO2
adsorption efficiency compared with sodium hydroxide in both flue gas and air con-

suited for newer carbon capture technologies, such as electrochemical CC. And while
DAC is deemed to be an unsustainable technology, alkaline solutions are some of the
solvents that are generally most suited for DAC.
1.2.3
Deep eutectic solvents
Deep eutectic solvents, DES, are solutions consisting of a mixture between a hydrogen
bond accepter, HBA, and a hydrogen bond donor. The first DES that was found was
when choline chlorid and urea was mixed together. Choline chlorid has a melting
point of 302 ‚ó¶C and urea has a melting point of 133 ‚ó¶C. Mixing these two substances
resulted in a melting point of 12 ‚ó¶C. An eutectic mixture is therefore a mixture that
has a lower melting point than those substances it consists of [17].
DES have been recognised as having a good potential as a solvent for post combus-
tion carbon capture. There are many appealing factors about DES compared to ionic
liquids, this is both on the economical and environmental plan. Furthermore they also
have the possibility to replace water in amine solvents, and create hybrid solvents. By
reducing the water content the energy needed for the evaporation and condensation
of water when the solvent is regenerated would decrease significantly, which would
lead to a lower amount of heat loss. However, DES have a relatively high viscosity,
resulting in a slow mass transfer rate, or slow adsorption kinetics, which will thereby
increase the size of the equipment used [18].
In this report a number of DES will be examined using a wetted wall column. The
purpose with these experiments is to determine whether any of the DES are effective
as solvents for post combustion carbon capture. For comparison the same experiment
will be executed using MEA since this is one of the most commonly used solvents,
and is currently also viewed as the state-of-the-art solvent for post carbon capture.
From the experiments a number of kinetic parameters will be determined, both for
MEA and for DES to be able to compare their effectiveness both with each other, bot
also with results from preexisting literature.

2.1
Wetted wall column
On an industrial scale it is primarily packed columns and bubble columns that are
used for carbon capture since these can offer a large surface area for the reaction, and
is also generally large in size. The advantage of using the WWC for CC experiments
and kinetics modelling are:
‚Ä¢ Small in size making it easier implement for testing compared to pilot scale ab-
sorption facilities.
‚Ä¢ Only a small amount of solvent is needed due to the small size of the WWC.
‚Ä¢ The size of the column is well-defined, the active mass transfer area is there well
known.
This surface area is where there is created an interface between the gas and the liquid
where the reaction is occurring. A schematic of the WWC is shown in fig. 7. In fig. 7
the WWC functions by a liquid stream, the solvent, marked with the blue arrows,
flows through the central tube. When the central tube is filled the solvent then flow
down the outside of the central tube. This flow on the outside creates a thin layer, also
called a film, around the central tube. When the liquid reaches the bottom of the tube
it flows out of the WWC, also marked with blue arrows. The gas flow, marked by the
green arrows, comes in through a number of smaller pipes placed around the base of
the central tube. The gas then flows counter current with the liquid, thereby flowing
up along the central tube and thin liquid film, and then out of the top of the reaction
chamber, also marked with a green arrow. The counter current flow of liquid and gas
is also primarily used in the industry for CC in the larger columns. The purpose of
the counter current flow is that it ensures that the gas with the lowest concentration of
CO2 gets in contact with the lean solvent, and the gas with the highest concentration
of CO2 gets into contact with the solvent when it has a higher loading. This ultimately
results in a nearly constant reaction rate along the tube. In fig. 7 the red arrows solely
mark where the heating water flows in and out of the heating cap that is used for
heating up-, or cooling down the chamber where the central tube is and where the
reaction occurs. The following section will describe the theory of mass transfer and
chemical reaction within the WWC.

2.2
Mass transfer following two-film theory
In a two-phase system mass transfer is generally caused by diffusion, which is the
movement of particles from one region of high concentration to another region of low
concentration. This process will keep occurring until equilibrium is reached. In most
cases simple diffusion is insufficient to describe mass transfer between two phases,
and different theories can be used to predict the mass transfer behaviour. In this sec-
tion the two-film theory is described.
Two-film theory is a model used to model mass transfer between two phases. In the
model it is assumed that when mass is transferred from one phase to another it in-
volves transport from the bulk of one phase to a phase boundary layer, then through
the phase boundary layer of the other phase and lastly into the second bulk phase.
This phase boundary is also called a film, and exists on each side of the interphase be-
tween the two phases. The resistance to transfer from one phase to the other therefore
lies in the existence of these two thin films. The driving force of the mass transfer is
the pressure- and concentration gradient between the two phases. The concentration
profile in the two film depends on whether it is a physical absorption or a chemical
absorption, which is demonstrated on fig. 8.

3.1
Purpose
The purpose of the experiments were to measure and determine the reaction kinetics
of CO2 absorption in KOH and ethaline. The experiments were performed using a
lab-scale wetted wall column. In the experiment the change in the CO2 concentration
was measured, and from this the kinetics were to be determined. The purpose with
the lab scale WWC is to mimic the functions of a pilot, or industrial scale absorber.
As with an absorber, the liquid flows from the top down on the side of a pipe, and
the gas flows counter-current from the inlets in the bottom. This creates a surface for
the reaction to occur on. This is essentially the same mechanics as an absorber with
packing elements.
To be able to validate the results a number of experiments were also run using differ-
ent aqueous solutions of MEA. The results of the MEA experiments were compared
with data obtained for KOH and DES.

3.4
Improvements to setup
The setup has previously been used several times, and was principally functional as
was. However, the setup primarily consists of plastic tubes and metal fitting, neither
being good insulators. Since most of the experiments will run at higher temperature
ranges, from around 60 ‚ó¶C to 80 ‚ó¶C, there will be a larger heat loss to the surroundings
from the solvents through the pipes and metal fittings. To ensure that the temperature
measurements made are as accurate as possible the temperature sensor for the solvent
entering the WWC, was moved. This resulted in the temperature being measured ap-
proximately 1 cm before entering the WWC instead of being measured approximately
20 cm before entering the WWC. Since the inlet temperature of the solvents is used
for the kinetics calculations it is important to ensure that it is as accurate as possible.
For the sensor measuring the temperature of the gas exiting the WWC a layer of in-
sulating material was applied around the metal pipe the gas runs through before the
temperature sensor, with the same purpose as moving the temperature sensor for the
liquid temperature.
One of the problems with the large heat loss to the surroundings was that it was
difficult to heat up the solvent to the higher temperatures the experiments were run
at. A water bath is used to heat up the solvent, and the temperature of the water bath
is therefore limited to be below 100 ‚ó¶C. The solvent is pumped through a 6 mm metal
pipe that is in contact with the hot water in the water bath. This small pipe works
as a heat exchanger between the solvent and the hot water. While running the ex-
periments at 20 ‚ó¶C and 40 ‚ó¶C the heat exchange worked as expected, however when
the temperature of the solvent raised to 60 ‚ó¶C the water bath had to be adjusted to be
around 20 ‚ó¶C higher than the temperature of the solvent, and when the solvent was
supposed to be 80 ‚ó¶C this could not be reached. It was decided to try and increase the
heat transfer in the water bath by replacing the short metal pipe the solvent flowed
through in the water bath, with one that was significantly longer. The approximately
30 cm steel pipe was replaced with a pipe that was approximately 4 m, resulting in
the liquid running in the pipe trough the water bath more than 13 times longer than
originally. This resulted in a significant increase in the heat transfer in the water bath,
enabling the temperature of to solvent to reach above 80 ‚ó¶C.

During the experiments with the DES it was difficult to find a proper flow speed
where the solvent did not flow too fast through the pipe, since this led to the solvent
going into the gas system, but at the same time did not flow too slow which led to
the entire pipe not being covered by the solvent. Originally it was planned to run the
experiments with the DES at 40 ‚ó¶C, 60 ‚ó¶C, and 80 ‚ó¶C. However, it was determined that
the solvent had a viscosity that was too high for the set-up to be running smoothly
at 40 ‚ó¶C. The liquid did not cover the full pipe, but made small ‚Äùwaves‚Äù in the bot-
tom where there was no solvent covering the pipe. When the flow was turned up it
exceeded the range that the flowmeter was fit to accommodate, and it also ended up
being transferred into the gas stream. The experiment ran smoothly at 60 ‚ó¶C, with the
flow speed being at the very top of the flowmeter at around 9.5 mm.

Operation of setup and measurements
The experiments were performed using the following procedure. The pre-saturator
and the saturator are emptied and filled with clean demineralized water, to ensure
that the gas stream is saturated. The water bath is turned on and set to the desired
temperature. The water bath used for the condenser is also turned on, circulation is
turned on, and the temperature is set to 5 ‚ó¶C. The gas tanks are opened, and the pres-
sure is set to around 5 bar. The bronkhost device is turned on, and the computer is
turned on, the programs are opened, and the communication between the data collec-
tion system and the computer is also established. Initially, the flow of nitrogen is set
to around 0.3 nL/min, and then the liquid system is filled. The liquid system is filled
by first injecting the solution into the system using a syringe to ensure that the pump
does not run dry. Afterwards the solution is pumped into the liquid reservoir directly
from the blue cap bottle until it is filled. The gas flow of N2 is run until the CO2 con-
centration in the system is zero, and the solvent is pumped around the system until
it reaches the desired temperature. The experiment can then be run. After running
the experiment the calibration of the flow meter is made. This is done by pouring the
solution into a beaker for 15 seconds, weighing the beaker, and thereby determining
the liquid flow rate.
When running the set-up at higher temperatures, above 60 ‚ó¶C, a large amount of wa-
ter evaporates from the hot water bath. The water in the water bath needs to be at a
specific level for the temperature controller to work. Several times during the exper-
iments that was run at higher temperatures the water bath had to be turned off and
more water had to be added. After this was done both the water bath and the liquid
within the liquid system had to be heated up until they reached the temperature they
had before the water bath was refilled.

4.5
Cholinechloride ethylene glycol
Initially at least 6 experiments were planned using ethaline, at three different tem-
peratures, and with water added at the same three temperatures. Ultimately, it was
experienced that the DES had too high of a viscosity at lower temperatures, 20 ‚ó¶C and
40 ‚ó¶C, to run, and the experiments therefore had to be executed at higher tempera-
tures than planned, which were 60 ‚ó¶C and 80 ‚ó¶C. Since the use of ethaline for CO2
absorption is a relatively unknown process, the kinetics are therefore also unknown.
It was decided to try and add water to the solution, resulting in a solution consist-
ing of 20 mol % water, and 80 mol % ethaline. Initially this was done to investigate
whether this would decrease the viscosity of the solvent, since it was significantly
higher compared to the other solvents used in the experimental set-up. The second
purpose was not initially thought of, but it was decided that it would be relevant to
investigate what effect water would have on the reaction kinetics. Since the kinetics
are unknown this cannot be predicted based on theory solely. However, it is known
that the kinetics for both MEA and KOH are dependent on the hydroxide, [OH‚àí],
concentration. If no water is present, the concentration of hydroxide is zero, and this
should affect the reaction rate significantly, potentially leading it to be close to zero.
Furthermore, previous studies have resulted in conflicting conclusions about the ef-
fect of adding water to ethaline. Most studies show that water can greatly increase
the absorption of CO2, while other shows that ethaline with no added water results
in the best CO2 absorption capacity [33, 34].
After running an initial experiment at 60 ‚ó¶C with no water added to the solvent, it
was observed that the CO2 concentration increased in the WWC, and decreased again
when the gas bypassed the WWC. Several different reasons for this were considered.
It was considered that due to the high temperature of the solvent, a small amount
of the solvent evaporated inside the WWC, and ended up affecting the CO2 sensor.
However, this theory was dismissed by running the experiment with no CO2 in the
gas, which resulted in no increase in the measured concentration. The second theory

Sensitivity- and model analysis
A sensitivity analysis is necessary to determine how the mass transfer, on both the gas
and liquid side, is affected by changes to the system. All measured parameters must
be assumed to be subject to some deviation from the measured value, and while this
deviation might be small when looking at the specific parameter, the deviation might
have a significant effect on the overall system. This section is separated into a num-
ber of sub-sections for each of the parameters being evaluated. For each parameter, a
range of realistic deviations are investigated, the effects on the system are discussed,
and a model analysis is made to determine what causes the effect from that specific
parameter.
For this entire section data from only one of the 9 experiments are used. This choice
has been made to make the analysis more thorough, but also because most models
used for the calculations are the same, or very similar, and the deviations between
each experiment in regards to the sensitivity analysis would be small. The experi-
ment chosen for the sensitivity analysis is the experiment using KOH 25 w%/w as
a solvent, at 40 ‚ó¶C. This experiment is chosen since the data is consistent with what
was expected from the experiment, and the results from the sensitivity analysis are
therefore also expected to be consistent and representative of reality.

Industrial use
Based on the results from each of the solvents it clearly shows that KOH is most
effective as a solvent for capturing CO2 in the WWC set-up. While it worked well in
the WWC, it would also have to work well in a pilot scale absorber, and afterwards
also a industrial scale absorber if it is to be used for industrial carbon capture. KOH

absorb CO2, but also its low viscosity at high concentrations and low temperatures.
The viscosity does not only affect the kinetics, but also the fluid mechanics in the
system.

7
Conclusion and future work
The kinetics of CO2 absorption into MEA, KOH, and ethaline were studied using a
wetted wall column. The experiments were carried out using different concentra-
tions for each solvent, with MEA concentrations ranging from 15 to 50 w%/w, KOH
concentrations ranging from 5 to 25 w%/w, and ethaline was tested both with and
without added water. The experiments with MEA and KOH were run at 20 ‚ó¶C, 40 ‚ó¶C,
and 60 ‚ó¶C, and those with ethaline were run at 60 ‚ó¶C, and 80 ‚ó¶C due to the high vis-
cosity of the solvent. Density and viscosity were measured for each solvent, at each
temperature. It was concluded that KOH has a significantly lower viscosity compared
to both MEA and ethaline. Both density and viscosity were measured instead of cal-
culated to decrease the potential deviation.
The gas side mass transfer coefficient was calculated using three correlations, and
the Pacheco correlation resulted in the highest gas side mass transfer coefficient. The
coefficients only varied slightly between solvents, and were almost not affected by the
temperature. The liquid side mass transfer coefficient increased with the solvent con-
centration. For KOH, the coefficient increased with temperature, while for both MEA
and ethaline the results were more inconsistent. The enhancement factors calculated
for MEA were lower than expected. The factors calculated for KOH were significantly
higher than those for MEA, and those found for ethaline where less than 1. This was
explained by using the same diffusion coefficient as for KOH, which should be signif-
icantly lower due to ethalines high viscosity. The calculated enhancement factors also
showed that all experiments were within the pseudo first order regime.
The second order rate constants were calculated for all experiments. The constants
increased with temperature as expected. The rate constants for MEA were signifi-
cantly lower than expected. The cause was analysed, and it was concluded that the
gas flow rate had been to high, not allowing enough contact time between the gas
and the liquid. The rates constants found for KOH were significantly higher than
those for MEA. Furthermore, it was seen that they were also highly dependent on the
concentration of the solvent, which was not expected. The cause was analysed and it
showed that Henry‚Äôs constant increased significantly with the concentration, due to
the correlation that was used for the calculations, which ultimately caused the large
increase in the rate constant. The sensitivity analysis showed that changes in density
had a relatively large effect on the liquid parameters, but only little effect on the gas
parameters and overall mass transfer coefficient. Changes in the viscosity had signif-
icantly less effect. Changes in the pressure had little effect, but effected the gad side
parameters the most. The largest sensitivity was caused by changes in the diffusion
coefficient and Henry‚Äôs constant.
KOH showed the best overall CO2 absorption abilities.
KOH was effective at all
three concentrations and all three temperatures. The best absorption results was us-
ing KOH 25 w%/w at 60 ‚ó¶C, but the experiment run using KOH 25 w%/w at 40 ‚ó¶C
resulted in almost the same CO2 absorption. In an industrial setting it could therefore
be a better choice to use KOH 25 w%/w at 40 ‚ó¶C since it would save energy.

7.1
Future work
The areas that could be relevant for further investigation can be separated into two
part, wetted wall column experiments, and pilot scale experiments.
Wetted wall column
‚Ä¢ Absorption studies with ethaline and a larger amount of added water. Other
studies have tested different DES using up to 80 w%/w water. This could po-
tentially solve the problem with the DES absorbing more water than CO2.
‚Ä¢ Absorption studies with other DES. Different studies shows that amines are ef-
fective as HBD in DES. It would therefore be relevant to test different DES using
amines as the HBD, these could either utilise ChCl as the HBA, or test other
HBA.
‚Ä¢ Absorption studies with KOH at different concentrations, and gas velocities.
The experiments using MEA clearly showed that the MEA solvent was not in
contact with the gas for a sufficient amount of time, resulting in low absorption
rates. The experiments run with KOH used the same gas velocity, and it would
therefore be relevant to test whether the absorption rate increased significantly
is the gas velocity was decreased.
‚Ä¢ Since a hydroxide solution resulted in the best CO2 absorption it would be of
great interest to test other hydroxide solutions. Many hydroxides are easily
available and could therefore be used as affordable, and potentially effective
solvents for CCS.
Pilot scale experiments
‚Ä¢ An interesting area would be to upscale the experiments using KOH. It was
concluded that KOH showed great potential as a solvent for CCS and it would
therefore be relevant to test it in a significantly larger scale, that has a closer
resemblance to an industrial scale. Results from this could be compared with
those obtained in this thesis. This would also give some result in some knowl-
edge about how the WWC works compared to a pilot scale absorber. A pilot
scale absorber can be found at Center for Energy Resources Engineering (CERE)
at DTU.
