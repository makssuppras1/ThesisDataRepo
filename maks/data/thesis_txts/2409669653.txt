Abstract
Knowledge about the interaction between an organism and its niche, is important for
obtaining a deeper understanding of the organisms role in the niche. Investigating
the proteins involved in this interaction, can in addition lead to a deeper insight into
the forces driving the evolution of the organisms.
In this study protein families were generated using a dataset of 28 ascomycetes inhab-
iting the niches; dung, compost, grain or opportunistic (opp.) human pathogen. The
correlation of the protein families and each of the niches were calculated using the
hypergeometric distribution, adjusted by the Benjamini-Hochberg approach. Finally
the protein families were annotated using the Pfam database with the aim to identify
protein functions speciﬁc for each of the niches.
Annotated protein families correlated to dung, compost, grain and opp.
human
pathogen yielded respectively 14, 7, 14 and 104 families.
Four test cases investi-
gating the relation between the function and the niche, revealed that these functions
were important for the organisms lifestyle in the speciﬁc niches.

forståelse for organismens rolle i nichen. Undersøgelsen af proteiner involveret i dette
samspil, kan yderligere føre til dybere viden omrking organismernes evolution, og de

I dette studie blev protein familier genereret ud fra et dataset med 28 ascomyceter

Derefter blev protein familierne annoteret ved brug af Pfam databasen med det formål

Annoterede protein familier der korrelerede med; ekskrement, kompost, korn eller
opp. human patogen, resulterede i henholdsvis 14, 7, 14 og 104 familier. Fire tests
der undersøgte forholdet mellem funktion og niche, viste at disse funktioner var vigtige
for organismens livsstil i den speciﬁkke niche.

1
Introduction
Ascomycota is a phylum of fungi with a widespread distribution and can be found
in almost all types of habitats (Wijayawardene et al. 2017). Some of the species in
the phylum are cosmopolitan, while others are specialized to a few speciﬁc niches.
The question is what features makes some species capable of inhabiting a broad
range of environments, while others are only adapted to one or a few. The fungal
proteome is an essential factor in the interaction between organism and niche. A
study investigated the adaptation of Drechmeria coniospora, a model endoparasitic
nematophagous fungus, to its almost completely obligate endoparasitic lifestyle. This
revealed a loss of genes advantageous for the saprophytic lifestyle, and an expansion of
protein families necessary for the nematode endoparasitic lifestyle (Zhang et al. 2016).
Understanding the interaction between protein families and an organisms ability to
have a speciﬁc lifestyle, is important for understanding an organisms role in the niche.
Furthermore it can lead to a deeper insight into the evolutionary forces driving the
organism.
This report gives an introduction to ascomycetes.
It introduces four diﬀerent as-
comycete niches; dung, compost, grain and opportunistic (opp.) human pathogen, de-
scribing the niches and the characteristics necessary for inhabiting the speciﬁc niches.
Furthermore the report describes a method to investigate the niche speciﬁc proteins
by introducing enrichment analysis and the statistics behind this. It also introduces
protein families and ﬁnally the databases and programs necessary for generating and
annotating protein families.
1.1
Ascomycota
Ascomycota is a monophyletic phylum including the subphyla Saccharomycotina
(budding yeast) and the Pezizomycotina (generally ﬁlamentous fungi) among oth-
ers. It is the largest and most diverse group of fungi with over 80,000 species, and are
worldwide in distribution (Wijayawardene et al. 2017). Ascomycetes can thrive as
saprotrophs, opp. human pathogens, or parasites of plants, animals and other fungi,
and are involved in a variety of industrial processes such as production of antibiotics,
bread and cheese.
Ascomycetes are recognized by their ascus which is a sac-like structure hyphal cell,
where the spores (ascospores) are formed. However not all species are sexual and pro-
duces meiospores (ascospores), some produces mitospores which are asexual spores,
but some species does also have the ability to produce both such as A. nidulans, see
Figure 1 (Tsitsigiannis et al. 2004).
1
==PAGE:9==
The spore formation is important for the ability of the ascomycetes to inhabit and sur-
vive in certain habitats, as they can be very resistant to changes in the environment,
following the three examples; (i) when temperature rises above 60°C in compost, it
is only the spores of the fungi that survives (Mycology, Beﬀa, and Sa 1998); (ii) the
spores of the fungi inhabiting dung, can survive digestion and the passage through
the digestive tract of herbivores, till excreted with dung; and (iii) for the opp. human
pathogenic fungi Aspergillus fumigatus, the small size of the spores enables them to
penetrate deep into the alveoli, where they are capable of binding to proteins that
may play a role in the establishment of infection (Robson G.D., Huang J., Wortman
J., Archer D.B. 2005). Thus for occupying some niches, the formation of spores is
crucial.
Figure 1: Illustration of the ascomycete A. nidulans and its two types of spores,
mitotic and meiotic (Tsitsigiannis et al. 2004).
1.2
Niches
As mentioned, ascomycetes are widely distributed and can live under a variety of en-
vironmental conditions, spanning niches such as dung, compost, grain or opp. human
pathogen. The four niches are illustrated in Figure 2, 3, 5 and 6.
1.2.1
Dung
Dung is a very attractive habitat for many species of fungi, due to the availability
of free sugars and complex carbohydrates which are easily utilized by these species.
Coprophilous fungi grow on dung and are an important component of the ecosys-
tem, responsible for recycling the nutrients in animal faeces (Ing 1989). They are
abundant in herbivorous dung while carnivore and omnivore contain more bacteria
(Kruys and Ericson 2008). The hardy spores of the coprophilous fungi are consumed
2
==PAGE:10==
by herbivores through vegetation, and subsequently excreted along with droppings.
The fungi then ﬂourish in the dung, before spreading their spores to the surrounding
plants. The digestive process if of great importance in determining species compo-
sition and structure of the coprophilous mycota community, and it has even been
suggested that some dung fungi are required to pass through the gut of herbivores,
in order to complete their lifecycle (Ing 1989)
Composition of herbivore dung
Herbivore dung is a complex alkaline media comprising many components. Primarily
it consists of some free sugars, complex carbohydrates, cellulose, lignin and nitrogen.
However, many factors are in evidence when looking at the chemical composition. A
study by Richardson (Richardson 2001) presented the composition of dung from dif-
ferent herbivores including sheep, deer, cattle, rabbits, hares and grouses. It revealed
that no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the amount of nitrogen was found across the dung
samples. However, diﬀerent levels of ligning and total carbon resulted in diﬀerent
carbon-nitrogen (C:N) and ligning-nitrogen (lignin:N) ratios. These ratios are both
used in predicting decomposition rates of organic compounds, which is lead by the
mycota community (Richardson 2001).
Figure 2: The herbivore dung niche, illustrated by buﬀalo dung inhabited by fungi.
Species richness
Many factors in nature aﬀect the richness and composition of coprophilous ascomycetes
on dung from diﬀerent herbivores, i.e.
season, geographical area, altitude, dung
type and number of coprophagous insects. In another study highly signiﬁcant dif-
ferences in the community composition of the mycobiota on the herbivore dungs,
was observed, even though there were no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in species richness
(Richardson and Ig 1972; Richardson 2001). The overall taxonomic distribution of
zygomycetes, basidomycetes and ascomycetes, were evaluated, which revealed that

75% of all species were ascomycetes. The study also indicated that some dung types
have a far higher species richness potential and a 50% sample increase, i.e. 17-70
samples, might expect to yield 30-40 more species, demonstrating how rich in myco-
biota dung is.
Another study by Kruys Å et al. (Kruys and Ericson 2008) showed a correlation
between a low herbivore diet breadth, and low species richness suggesting that some
species are more associated with habitat and food choice of the herbivore rather than
a speciﬁc herbivore dung type.
1.2.2
Compost
Composting is decomposition of organic materials. It is a self-heating, aerobic pro-
cess that comprises diﬀerent mesophilic and thermophilic phases involving diﬀerent
microorganisms.
Composting can be divided into four diﬀerent phases; (i) an initial mesophilic phase
where the temperature is 10-42°C; (ii) a thermophilic phase with 45-70°C involving
a transition of mesophilic to thermophilic and thermotolerant species; (iii) a sec-
ond mesophilic phase where mesophilic species, often dissimilar to those of the ﬁrst
mesophilic phase, recolonize the substrates. It is also during this phase, the com-
posting process begins; and (iv) the maturation and stabilization phase where the
decomposition is at its highest.
Figure 3: The compost niche consisting of mostly plant material.
Parameters in the decomposition process
Compost is a constantly changing environment, and the process is determined from
various parameters such as pH, C:N ratio and temperature.
The temperature during a composting process is determined by the degradability and
energy content of the substrates, and the availability of moisture and oxygen (Rycke-
boer et al. 2003). In the initial mesophilic phase, the temperature will be 10-42°C
4
==PAGE:12==
and favor the mesophilic microorganism. However as the metabolic activity increases,
the temperature will as well, reaching 45-70°C which has a strong selective eﬀect, in
favour of a few aerobic sporigenous thermophilic organisms. Once the activity of
the thermophilic organisms ceases, due to exhaustion of substrates, the temperature
starts to decrease and the decomposition process will increase illustrated in Figure 4.
Figure 4: Temperature development and degree of decomposition (rottegrad) in com-
post (Ryckeboer et al. 2003).
Compost is mainly constituted of plant material where carbon compounds serve as
the energy source for the growth of microorganisms. Nitrogen is also a critical element
for microbial growth, and will slow the composting process if a limitation occurs. The
optimum C:N ratio has been reported to range between 25 and 35, which it will reach
in the second mesophilic phase and the maturation and stabilization phase. (Rycke-
boer et al. 2003). During the process the C:N ratio decreases as part of the carbon is
lost as CO2 upon microbial respiration, whereas the nitrogen is recycled (Ryckeboer
et al. 2003).
The pH in the compost will, during the initial mesophilic phase, be slightly acidic.
The pH will subsequently rise due to ammoniﬁcation, favouring growth of bacteria
(Bertoldi, Vallini, and Pera 1983). At the beginning of the composting process occur-
ring after the thermophilic phase, the pH will drop due to the activity of acid-forming
bacteria (Ryckeboer et al. 2003).
Species richness in compost
Microbial diversity is a prerequisite for a satisfactory composting process, and a large
variety of mesophilic, thermotolerant and thermophilic aerobic microorganisms are
5
==PAGE:13==
involved in this process (Boulter, Trevors, and Boland 2002). Organisms involved in
the degradation of organic materials are fungi, actinomycetes and other bacteria.
A. fumigatus is widely found on compost and has a special signiﬁcance due to its
capacity to degrade almost all components of organic waste including sugars, fatty
acids, protein, cellulose, pectin and xylane. Furthermore it is capable of growing at
30-52°C, and its conidia that can survive at 55-60°C for a fairly long period. Unfor-
tunately it is also known as a opp. pathogen and allergen as to why composting has
to be a controlled process to optimal hygienisation (Mycology, Beﬀa, and Sa 1998).
1.2.3
Grain
Cereal constitute major sources of dietary energy and protein for humans and livestock
(Orina, Manley, and Williams 2017). However, cereal have also been used in other
processes, most recently in energy production by fermentation yielding biogas or
bioethanol.
Cereals belong to the family Poaceae and includes wheat, corn, rice,
barley, oats etc. Cereals produce dry, one-seeded fruits called the "grain".
Figure 5: Malt grains, illustrating the grain niche
Chemical composition and species richness in cereal grains
Cereal grains contain 66-76% carbohydrates, where the major one is starch followed
by minor constituents such as arabinoxylans, beta-ﬂucans, sugars, cellulose and glu-
cofructans. A 13% moisture content is considered to be the maximum value for the
storage of wheat, corn, barley and rice during short periods, though temperature and
oxygen concentration also play an important role (Placinta, D’Mello, and MacDonald
1999; Brooker, Bakker-Arkema, and Hall 1992).
Cereal grains often contain contamination of fungi belonging to the genera Aspergillus,
6
==PAGE:14==
Penicillium, Fusarium and Alternaria. The contamination derives from several sources,
such as dust, water, ill plants, insects, soil and animal feces, and can aﬀect the
grain quality. However more important, the infecting fungi produce toxic secondary
metabolites and mycotoxins which pose health hazards to humans and animals (Orina,
Manley, and Williams 2017). Although these toxins are typically present in levels as
low as parts per million, acute or chronic exposure to mycotoxins has been associated
with immunosuppression, impaired growth in children, malnutrition, liver cancer and
death in some incidents. Thus early detection and if possible, removal of fungal con-
taminated grains is very important in order to ensure food safety and also storage
longevity and grain quality (Orina, Manley, and Williams 2017; Laca et al. 2006).
1.2.4
Opportunistic human pathogens
As mentioned in Section 1.2.2 and 1.2.3, controlled processes are important, not only
to improve composting or ensure grain quality, but to secure minimum or no absence
of pathogenic fungi producing mycotoxins.
Opportunistic pathogens have little invasive power and depend upon a pre-existing
break in the normal body defences or a weakened immune defence to cause disease.
They are a huge health problem for immunocompromised patients as this group is ex-
tremely exposed. Concurrently with the evolving medical ﬁeld and treatment oppor-
tunities, the group of immunocompromised patients increases, leading to an increase
in incidents of opp. invasive fungal infections, as well as novel opp. fungi (Kerl 2015;
Pappas 2010).
The opp.
human pathogen niche is very broad, thus diﬃcult to describe.
Many
fungi are also, under the right circumstances, capable of causing infection, thereby
acting as opp. pathogens. The human body comprise diﬀerent habitats with great
variance, e.g. the pH level in the stomach is acidic while the pH level in the blood
is neutral. However, despite these diﬀerences, opp. human pathogens share some
capacities making them capable of invading the human host.

==PAGE:15==

human pathogen niche.
Pathogenecity factors
Many factors inﬂuence the virulence and pathogenic capability of the microorgan-
isms, such as secreted enzymes including extracellular phospholipases or proteinases.
Other pathogenecity factors include melanin production, mannitol secretion, rapid
growth, heat tolerance and toxin production (Baidee and Hashemizadeh 2014). The
pathogens are dependent upon mechanisms that can protect it from the hosts im-
mune system, and as the host will ﬁrst recognize the outer cell wall layer, this plays
a major role in pathogenicity. Chitin is a primary component of fungi cell walls, and
can inﬂuence the immune recognition. This is done by blocking a receptor leading to
a signiﬁcant reduction in production of cytokines, which are key players in directing
immune responses to the fungal pathogens. Synthesis of melanin and other conidial
pigments in fungi can also reduce complement opsonization leading to phagocytosis,
by camuﬂaging the binding sites (Baidee and Hashemizadeh 2014).
Usual contaminants
Infections can be transmitted by the inhalation of spores from e.g. Aspergillus, Cryp-
tococcus or Histoplasma species, penetration into the mucosa by commensal organisms
such as Candida albicans or by ingestion of a toxin in contaminated food or drinks
leading to gastrointestinal disease (Baidee and Hashemizadeh 2014). Aspergillus and
Candida species are the main organisms isolated most frequently from immunocom-
promised patients. Invasive candidasis, i.e. infection with Candida species is mainly
caused by C. albicans and invasive aspergillosis i.e. infection with Aspergillus species
is mainly caused by A. fumigatus (Nucci et al. 2010). Both of these fungi can grow
at a wide range of pH values which is crucial for being capable of infecting diﬀerent
parts of the human body (Baidee and Hashemizadeh 2014).

1.3
Enrichment analysis
Enrichment analysis is a statistical approach used to discover overrepresentation of a
categorical class within a subset from a heterogeneous population (Charles A. Tilford
and Nathan O. Siemers 2009). Hence it can be used for investigating the correlation
between protein families and niches. Enrichment analysis are widely used in inter-
pretation of large data sets, however several factors can cause major concerns for the
analysis if these are not carefully considered. It is important that the reference set
is non-abundant and only contain possible outcomes from the analysis. Furthermore
correcting for multiple testing is important in order to minimize false discoveries, i.e.
false positives and negatives which are explained further in the section 1.3.2.
1.3.1
Hypergeometric distribution
P-values or probability values can be used to establish whether or not a certain result
is signiﬁcant, e.g. whether or not a protein family is correlated to a speciﬁc niche. To
calculate a p-value several methods can be used, one of them is the hypergeometric
distribution. The hypergeometric distribution describes the probability of k successes
in n draws from a population without replacement and is given by;

1.3.2
P-values and hypothesis
The p-value is the probability of ﬁnding the observed, or more extreme outcome when
the null hypothesis H0 is true. The null hypothesis is usually a hypothesis stating no
relationship between two measured phenomena or no association among groups (Ross
2011). When rejecting the null hypothesis an alternative hypothesis H1 is being ac-
cepted. There are two types of alternative hypothesis, one-sided and two-sided. The
one-sided has as the name indicates only one alternative, e.g. if a correlation among
two groups has to be positive in order for H1 to be accepted, whereas the two sided
has two, i.e. the correlation can both be positive or negative for H1 to be accepted
(Ross 2011; Whitley and Ball 2002).
Type I and type II errors
Distinguishing between the null hypothesis and the alternative hypothesis is aided by
9

identifying two types of errors; type I error and type II error, see Table 1. Type I
error is rejection of H0 when it is true, and can result in false positives, i.e. deeming
a result as signiﬁcant when it is not. Consequently type I error is often the most
critical. The probability of making a type I error is denoted by α, also known as the
level of signiﬁcance, and usually set to α = 0.05 or α = 0.01 (Whitley and Ball 2002).
Type II error is a non-rejection of H0 when H1 is true which can result in false
negatives i.e. deeming a result as non-signiﬁcant when it actually is.

As mentioned p-values are applied to identify signiﬁcant from non-signiﬁcant test
results. However they are only statistically valid when a single score is computed,
while for bigger datasets, the probability of having a type I error is bigger. Thus it is
important to adjust the statistical conﬁdence measures based on the number of tests
performed.
1.3.3
Multiple testing correction
There are many multiple testing correction methods. The simplest and most widely
used, Bonferroni adjustment, controls the familywise error rate i.e. the probability
that one or more of the signiﬁcance tests results is a type I error (Hochberg 1988).
The Bonferroni adjustment deems a score signiﬁcant only if the corresponding p-value
is ≤α/n, where n is the number of separate tests (Noble 2009). As the probability
for a type I error will increase for each test included in the study, it becomes more
diﬃcult to reject the null hypothesis, making the Bonferroni method too conservative
for studies including many tests.
The Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) method controls the false discovery rate (FDR), which
is the expected proportion of type I errors in the tests that are deemed as being signif-
icant (Benjamini and Hochberg 1995). It ranks the p-values in order to adjust them
and consequently this method relies on the p-values being uniformly distributed. P-
values adjusted by FDR approach are termed q-values (Noble 2009).
Hence contrary to the Bonferroni approach which control the probability of one or
more false positives, the BH approach controls the expected proportion of false posi-
tives.

1.4
Protein families
Protein famillies comprise homologous genes, that are genes descending from a com-
mon ancestral DNA sequence. The term hoomologous cover both orthologous genes
and paralogous genes. Orthologs are genes that are seperated by a speciation event,
while paralogs are genes seperated by a duplication event (Sivashankari and Shan-
mughavel 2007), illustrated in Figure ??.
When gene duplication occurs, it allows for introducing new functions, as one of the
duplicated genes might be altered slightly. In this way gene families thereby also
protein families, are created resulting in sometimes very big families that only varies
slightly in function. However, to classify two proteins as members of the same family,
the functions and structure has to be similar.
Several tools exist to identify protein families e.g. OMA that identiﬁes outer mem-
brane protein families, or eggNOG that identiﬁes protein families with functional
annotations (Altenhoﬀet al. 2015; Huerta-Cepas et al. 2016).

Databases and programs
In the following section the databases, tools and programs used in the analysis will
be introduced and a brief description of the function is given.
1.5.1
Basic Local Alignment Search Tool
The Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST, or BLASTP for proteins) is a local
alignment tool applied for aligning a nucleotide or protein sequence, the so-called
11
==PAGE:19==
query and a nucleotide or protein sequences within a database, also called subject
sequences. The local alignment match a query sequence with a substring of the sub-
ject whereas global alignment perform an end to end alignment with the subject,
sometimes resulting in a lot of gaps. A global alignment can also be approached us-
ing BLAST by setting a high coverage value) BLAST uses a heuristic approach that
approximates the Smith-Waterman algorithm. The Smith-Waterman algorithm com-
pares segments of all possible lengths and optimises the similarity measure, making it
very exhaustive, and time-consuming for searching in large databases. The heuristic
approach trades optimality, completeness, accuracy, or precision for speed and is con-
sequently used approximating the Smith-Waterman algorithm (Altschul et al. 1990).
Several parameters can be set to ﬁlter the hits, the most common being the expec-
tation value (E-value). The E-value is the number of hits that are expected to occur
in a database search by chance. Thereby, a lower E-value indicate a more signiﬁ-
cant score and is often used alone for ﬁltrating hits. Another parameter that can be
applied is the coverage including query coverage (qcov) and subject coverage (scov)
controlling the percentage of how much the respective sequences should be covered
by the alignment.
1.5.2
Pfam
Pfam is a large database of protein families containing more than 14,000 families in-
cluding their annotations and multiple sequence alignments. The alignments are gen-
erated using Hidden Markov Models (HMM) which are statistical models frequently
applied in the analysis of multiple sequence alignments by identifying insertions, dele-
tions, protein domains or homologies among others. The alignments form the basis
for HMM proﬁles which are searched against a large sequence collection based on
UniProt Knowledgebase (UniProtKB), a collection of functional information on pro-
teins. Sequence regions that score above the curated threshold, that is set for each
family to eliminate false positives, are aligned to the proﬁle HMM to produce the full
alignment (Finn et al. 2014).
A proﬁle database such as Pfam is applicable for performing a sequence search against
the database to derive the protein family, the given sequence might belong to.

2.1
Selecting organisms
The set of species included in the investigations were chosen by three requirements;
(i) they had to inhabit one of the four niches; (ii) they had to belong to the phylum
of Ascomycota; (iii) and ﬁnally they had to have a publicly available proteome. The
proteomes were downloaded at the JGI genome portal (Nordberg et al. 2014) or the
Uniprot protein database (Apweiler 2009), see Table 3 in September 2017. For each
specie it was noted whether or not they inhabit each niche.
2.2
Creating protein families from BLASTP alignment
To create the protein families, all proteomes from the dataset were aligned against
each other using BLASTP with an E-value set to 1e-5. A qcov and scov was calculated
from the BLASTP output by subtracting the start position of the alignment from the
end position of the alignment devided by the length of the sequence for both the
subject an query sequence. All the blast outputs were ﬁltered using a threshold qcov
and scov on 50%. The proteins left, were divided into protein families where proteins
that aligned with a scov and qcov above 60% were assumed to have the same function
14
==PAGE:22==
and were thus grouped into the same family. All singletons i.e. protein families with
only one member were removed from the set of protein families.

2.3
Measuring correlation between niche and protein families
A hypergeometric distribution was performed in order to measure the correlations
between the protein families and the four niches, an example of how this distribution

direction of the correlation was calculated by comparing the niche speciﬁc protein
distribution within each protein family to the distribution within the whole set. For
a positive correlation the distribution in the protein family would be greater than
the distribution within the whole set while the contrary would indicate a negative
correlation. For an example of calculating the direction of the correlation, see Section

3.4.1
Test case 1 - Peptidase S8
The protein family F5 contains 104 proteins and was shown to be correlated to the
dung niche as 55 of the proteins belonged to this niche. When mapped to the Pfam
databases, the 104 proteins yielded 170 family mappings from which 99 of these
mapped to the Peptidase S8 family and 71 of these mapped to the Peptidase inhibitor
I9 family, data not shown. All proteins that mapped to the peptidase inhibitor I9
family also mapped to the peptidase S8 family.
S8 Peptidase family
The peptidase S8 family are subtilases which is a superfamily of subtilisin-like serine
proteases. The subtilisin family is with over 200 subtilases, the second largest family
of serine proteases (Rawlings and Barrett 2010; Siezen and Leunissen 1997). The ma-
jority of the subtilases are synthesized as pre-pro-enzymes, subsequently translocated
25
==PAGE:33==
over a cell membrane via the pre-peptide (or signal-peptide), and ﬁnally activated
by cleavage of the pro-peptide, illustrated in Figure 14. However, not all subtilases
contain a pro-peptide, and a small number of subtilases are intracellular.
The subtilases are widespread occuring in Archaea, Bacteria, fungi, yeast and higher
eukaryotes. They also harbour many diﬀerent functions such as extracellular degra-
dation, intracellular pro-hormone activity and intracellular protein degradation. In
a study by Siezen et al. (Siezen and Leunissen 1997), the sequence of 100 catalytic
subtilase domains were compared in a multiple sequence alignment. Based on se-
quence homology, this study proposed a subdivision into six families whereas one of
them, subtilisin consists of the subgroups "true" subtilisin, high alkaline proteases
and intracellular proteases.
Cow dung is an alkaline substrate and has been used for generating high alkaline
proteases, indicating a strong correlation between this type of protein and the dung
niche (Vijayaraghavan et al. 2012; Muszewska et al. 2017).
Peptidase inhibitor I9 family
Peptidase inhibitor I9 is a family that contains a pro-peptide domain at the N-
terminus which is a part of the protein that is cleaved during maturation or acti-
vation. The propeptide in the peptidase inhibitor I9 family, belongs to the MEROPS
(a peptidase database) family S8A subtilisins. As mentioned above the majority of
subtilases contain a pro-peptide, whereas a small part does not. This explains why
the majority of proteins mapping to the subtilase family also mapped to the inhibitor
family which is a part of the peptidase before cleavage. In the output of the Pfam
annotation of family F5, data not shown, the inhibitors are also located in the end of
the protein. Once cleaved, the propeptides generally has no independent biological
function. For an illustration of the two domains; peptidase S8 and inhibitor I9, see
Figure 14.
Figure 14: An illustration of the two protein domains peptidase S8 and inhibitor I9
deriving from the Pfam database.

Glycoside Hydrolase Family 45
Glycoside hydrolases are as mentioned above carbohydrate active enzymes. The GH45
comprises enzymes with only one known activity; endoglucanase. Endoglucanases are
cellulases and have been found in bacteria, fungi and animals. They are involved in
hydrolysis of the glycoside bonds between two or more carbohydrates or between a
carbohydrate and a non-carbohydrate moiety. Endoglucanases have been extensively
studied because of their role in degrading cellulose, found extensively in herbivore
dung (Tang et al. 2014).
As cellulose also constitute a huge proportion of compost, it is expected that some

3.4.3
Test case 3 - LIP
Protein family F1481 consists of 82 proteins, and when mapping these against the
Pfam database, 65 proteins mapped to the LIP family consisting of secretory li-
pases. Lipases catalyze the hydrolysis of ester bonds of triacylglycerols, resulting in
the release of fatty acids. In almost all organisms, lipases play essential roles in lipid
metabolism, including digestion, transport, and the processing of dietary lipids. How-
ever, a number of studies have also shown that human microbial pathogens utilize
lipases in their pathogenesis (Park, Do, and Jung 2013).
One organism that has shown to contain a large number of diﬀerent lipases is Can-
dida albicans which is one of the 28 ascomycetes used in this study. C. albicans is
one of the most commonly isolated human fungal pathogens and possesses at least
27
==PAGE:35==
10 lipase-encoding genes, LIP1, LIP2 ect. C. albicans lipases have been shown to
contribute to its morphological transition, colonization, cytotoxity, and penetration
to the host (Park, Do, and Jung 2013; Hube et al. 2000).
The protein family and niche abundancy
The proteins in this family are virulence factors in pathogenic fungi, and the family
was thus expected to be signiﬁcantly correlated to the niche of opp. pathogen. In
concordance with this, F1481 was with an abundancy of 65 out of 82 proteins sig-
niﬁcantly correlated to the niche of opp. pathogen. The signiﬁcant correlation to
the other niches, compost, cereal and dung was either negative or non existing with

C. albicans posses many lipases, and 10 out of the 79 proteins in the protein family
belonged to this organism, respectively the LIP1, LIP2 etc, data not shown.
3.4.4
Test case 4 - alginate lyase
Protein family F4226 consist of 38 family members and correlated with 38 proteins in
the opp. human pathogen niche signiﬁcantly with this. When mapping the members
against the Pfam database, 25 of these belonged to the family of alginate lyases.
Alginate lyases are enzymes that catalyse the degradation of alginate. Alginate is
a gelling polysaccharide found in great abundance as part of the cell wall and intra-
cellular material in the brown seaweeds and is important for evasion of host defenses,
especially in patients with cystic ﬁbrosis (Wong, Preston, and Schiller 2000). Alginate
lyases have been found associated with a Chlorella virus, and have also been detected
in certain bacteriophages that are speciﬁc for Pseudomonas aeruginosa. However,
only P. aerugionsa strains found in the lungs of cystic ﬁbrosis patients produces al-
ginate, while strains commonly found in the environment or recovered from other
patient infections do not (Wong, Preston, and Schiller 2000; Jain and Ohman 2005).
The function of alginate lyase
In P. aeruginosa it has been shown that the proteins encoded by genes in the biosyn-
thetic cluster of alginate, all play an important role in the assembly of alginate. As
the alginate lyase encoding gene AlgL is located within this cluster, the lyase is im-
portant for optimal alginate biosynthesis (Penaloza-Vazquez et al. 1997).
It has been suggested that the lyase works as part of a polymerization complex in the
periplasm between the outer and inner cell wall, assembling an alginate polymer for
transport to the cell surface (Wong, Preston, and Schiller 2000). The lyase function
either by controling the length of the alginate polymer, or by providing short pieces
of alginate to prime the polymerization reaction. As alginate synthesis is increased
28
==PAGE:36==
upon attachment of the bacteria to the cell surface to make a stronger adhesion to the
surface, cleavage of the alginate polymer enhances the detachment of the bacteria,
allowing them to spread and colonize new sites (Jain and Ohman 2005).

grain, compost or opp. human pathogens yielded very diﬀerent results in the number
of protein families. Correlation to the pathogen niche yielded 103 families while it to
the compost niche yielded 7, represented in Table 5. It is diﬃcult to say how many
correlated families could be expected in each of the niches, as it depends on many
factors.
One major bias determining the outcome of the analysis is the initial dataset, and
especially three factors within this; (i) the selection of organisms, (ii) the selection of
niches and (iii) the assignment of organisms in the niches. The subset of organisms
are randomly chosen by the three requirements listed in Section 2.1. It contains very
diverse species, but also many members of the same genus, i.e. 17 species belongs
to the Aspergillus genus. The niches are all very diﬀerent however the opp. human
pathogen niche is much wider than the others, and under the right circumstances
almost all fungi are able to act as opp. human pathogens, thus this group is also
increasing concurrently with the number of immunocompromised patients. The as-
signment of all the organisms to the niches was done by literature search. Hence, it
does not provide a conclusive answer to whether or not the organisms inhabit each
of the niches, but rather the possibility if literature states that they do or do not. 17
out of the 28 organisms are stated to inhabit the opp. pathogen niche, while it for
the other niches is 9-10 organisms. Consequently the dataset is very biased towards
the opp. pathogen niche, and to cope with this a circumscription of this niche would
be necessary, e.g. opp. human pathogens in lung tissue.
The method used to create the protein families and measure correlation to the niches
could also be executed diﬀerently.
In this study a coverage and E-value was set to identify identical proteins, but a
question that is to be considered is; when are two sequences actually the same, and
encode the same function. Two proteins might function as cellulases and be 99%
similar in sequences. However, the two nucleotides diﬀerentiating the two sequences
might be the diﬀerence causing one of the cellulases to be specialized in dung and
the other in compost. In this study, proteins with identical Pfam annotated families
were considered to be the same.
When creating the protein families a coverage cutoﬀvalue on 60% was chosen. The
eﬀect of this is that small protein sequences will have diﬃculties aligning with large
sequences resulting in a ﬁltration of false positives. However, small protein domains
that actually belong to the protein family, can also risk being ﬁltered out. Hence, this
is always a trade-oﬀbetween the rate of false positives and the rate of false negatives.
Another approach such as bayesian statistics also called bayesian inference, could
30
==PAGE:38==
also be used to measure the correlation between protein families and niches. Con-
trary to the frequency interpretation, where probability is interpreted as the long-run
frequency of events in a repeatable experiment, the bayesian inference interpret prob-
ability as a way of quantifying uncertainty.
Some of the annotated protein families indicated a clear coherence to a speciﬁc niche,
represented in Section 3.4. However, to get a clear indication of the overall coherence
between protein functions and niches, a systematic qualitative analysis of the Pfam
annotations of the niche correlated protein families is required. This would illustrate
if certain functions are speciﬁc for a niche. An example of this could be that certain
cellulases correlated to the compost or the dung niches, while extracellular lipases or
proteinases would correlate with the opp. pathogen niche.

5
Conclusion
I sought to identify protein family functions correlated to the niches; dung, compost,
cereal or opp. human pathogen. This was done using BLASTP for creating the pro-
tein families and the Pfam database to annotate the families.
Using a 60% coverage cutoﬀvalue for creating the families, resulted in <antal familier
i alt> families, the biggest on 7730 and 7409 proteins.
For investigating the protein families correlation to the niches, a p-value was cre-
ated using hypergeometric distribution, and subsequently adjusted using Benjamini-
Hochberg procedure. A level of signiﬁcance was set to α = 0.05. This resulted in 69,
10, 22 and 135 protein families correlated to respectively dung, compost, cereal and
opp. human pathogen.
Annotation of the protein families was performed using the Pfam database, and
yielded respectively 14, 7, 14 and 104 annotated protein families correlated to the
niches; dung, compost, cereal and opp. human pathogen.
The four test cases investigated peptidase S8 and glycoside hydrolase family 45’s func-
tion in dung, and also the LIP and alginate lyase families function in opp. human
pathogens. These four test cases indicated a clear coherence between protein function
and the dung and opp. human pathogen niche. However in order to demonstrate a
general tendency, systematic qualitative approaches are necessary.
