Abstract 
Photochemistry is an essential tool in organic chemical synthesis, where light is used as the energy 
source. An important photochemical transformation is the [2+2] cycloaddition reaction. In the 
photochemical [2+2] cycloadditions, two alkenes react with each other by light irradiation to form a 
cyclobutane. The cycloaddition can form either a crossed or a straight product depending on the rule 
of five. This project aims to develop [2+2] cycloaddition products from kojic acid and maltol. Kojic 
acid and maltol were previously used in compound libraries in [5+2] cycloaddition reactions. However, 
there are no compound library publications with [2+2] cycloaddition for kojic acid and maltol.  
 
In this project, different building blocks from kojic acid and maltol were synthesized. Additionally, 
the starting materials were used in cycloaddition reactions. The project concluded that the [2+2] 
cycloaddition from the starting material (3) and (4) from maltol formed a crossed (5) and a straight (6) 
product (Scheme 1). Furthermore, an allylation product from the starting material (7) from kojic acid 
was synthesized. The reaction produced product (8) where the OPMB-group was removed by light 
irradiation (Scheme 1).

1 Introduction 
In the last decades, there has been a significant increase in the development of organic photochemical 
reactions, and photochemistry has become an essential tool in organic chemical synthesis. 
Photochemical reactions are widely used in biology and medicine and can be used for treatments such 
as phototherapy or photochemotherapy.1  
 
This introduction includes a description of photochemistry in organic chemistry and a description of 
the photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition reaction, its mechanistic pathway, and its advantages. This 
section also touches upon different photocatalysts, as well as examples from the literature describing 
the use of different photocatalysts and the synthesis of crossed and straight cycloaddition products. 
Lastly, the introduction includes a description of how the natural small molecules kojic acid and maltol 
have been used in [5+2] cycloaddition derived compound libraries in literature. Kojic acid and maltol 
were used as starting materials for the different building blocks synthesized in this project.  
 
1.1 Photochemistry 
An organic photochemical reaction uses light irradiation as its energy source. Figure 1 shows an 
overview of how the photochemical reaction works and is described further below. 
 
Figure 1: Overview of a photochemical reaction. A molecule in S0 is irradiated by light and then excited to S1 or T1 
where it can further react to give the product. 
In photochemistry, light affects a molecule. A photon from the molecule will be absorbed and create 
an electronically excited state. The photon will be excited from its ground state (S0) to its excited states 
(S1 and T1). This conversion makes it possible to create a high electronic spectrum. The different light 
irradiations which are used in a photochemical reaction are the ultraviolet light with a wavelength 
==PAGE:14==
2 
 
between 200-380 nm, the visible light with a wavelength between 380-760 nm, and the near infrared 
light with a wavelength between 760-1000 nm.2 
 
Figure 2: Jablonski energy diagram. The absorption is the transition from S0 to S1. Internal conversion and vibrational 
relaxation get rid of the excess of energy. Fluorescence is the transition from S1 to S0. Intersystem crossing is the 
transition from S1 to T1. Phosphorescence is the transition from T1 to S0. Reproduced from Edinburgh Instrumentals.3 
The Jablonski diagram illustrates molecule’s different electronic energy states, where energy is shown 
on the y – axis (Figure 2). The first radiative transition is absorption shown in blue in Figure 2, where 
an electron is excited from its ground state (S0) to its singlet state (S1 or S2). After absorption, the 
energy can scatter in different ways which are indicated in the figure by wavy lines. One way is a non-
radiative process called vibrational relaxation presented in yellow in Figure 2. All the energy not 
deposited by the photon is distributed to other vibrational modes as kinetic energy. Additionally, the 
energy can scatter as internal conversion, as shown in purple, where the electron will be converted to 
a lower-lying electronic state. The fluorescence transition shown in green is the following process after 
absorption where a photon is emitted as a result of the transition from the excited state (S1 or S2) to the 
ground state (S0). 
Lastly, a way that the energy can be dissipated is intersystem crossing presented in turquoise in Figure 
2. The intersystem crossing is a non-radiative transition from the singlet state (S1) to the triplet state 
(T1). After the intersystem crossing, the electron undergoes vibrational relaxation (yellow) to the 
vibrational level of T1. The final step is the radiative transition which is the emission of a photon from 
the triplet state (T1) to the ground state (S0). This process is known as phosphorescence shown in red.3,4 
 
A photochemical reaction differs from a thermal reaction due to its different energy source. A 
photochemical reaction uses light irradiation, whereas a thermal reaction uses heat. Often 
photochemical reactions can be performed at low temperatures compared to thermal reactions, which 
==PAGE:15==
3 
 
usually need higher temperatures. Another advantage of a photochemical reaction is that it only uses 
light, solvent, and sometimes a photocatalyst, which makes the reaction relatively inexpensive, where 
the cost defining factor is often the price of the photocatalyst. Inorganic photocatalysts derived from 
iridium or ruthenium are typically more costly than organic photocatalysts.5 
Additionally, the workup is often easy because the reaction may not give any side products needing 
removal. Lastly, utilizing light makes the photochemical reaction more sustainable because the light 
is clean and traceless.6  
 
1.2 Photochemical [2+2] Cycloaddition 
One important and interesting photochemical transformation is the [2+2] cycloaddition. This section 
describes the photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition reaction in organic chemistry, including examples 
from the literature. Additionally, this section describes different photosensitizers, such as organic and 
inorganic, which can be used in the [2+2] cycloaddition.  
 
1.2.1 General [2+2] Cycloaddition 
The [2+2] cycloaddition is a class of pericyclic reaction which is broadly used in organic chemistry. 
Photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition products are biologically active sometimes due to their strained 
four membered ring. Reported bioactivities include anticancerogenic, antibacterial, and anti-
inflammatory.7 
 
Scheme 2: Photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition. Inspired from Poplata et al.8 
The photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition reaction shown in Scheme 2, depicts the reaction between 
alkenes in order to form the strained cyclobutane ring by using light irradiation. The [2+2] 
cycloaddition is a 4π reaction which can only be carried out using photochemistry. The cycloaddition 
reaction cannot occur using heat because it will result in irreconcilable symmetry of the cyclobutane. 
This challenge can be solved using light irradiation, where an orbital from one of the alkenes can be 
excited from π to π* which is described further in the next section.9 
 
1.2.2 The Mechanistic Pathway Triplet Energy Transfer 
There are a number of mechanistic pathways for the cycloaddition reaction, including direct excitation, 
triplet energy transfer or single electron transfer. In this section, the triplet energy transfer pathway is 
described. The pathway is also called sensitization and is shown in Figure 3. 
==PAGE:16==
4 
 
 
Figure 3: A: The mechanistic pathway triplet energy transfer. The sensitizer will be excited to its T1 by light and 
intersystem crossing (ISC). An alkene will then be excited to its T1 by triplet energy transfer from the sensitizer. At last, 
the alkene will react with another alkene which has not been excited to form a cyclobutane. B: Energy diagram of triplet 
energy transfer from the sensitizer and the alkene. Reproduced from Poplata et al.8 
The sensitizer (photocatalyst) is a photoexcited molecule which is used to excite one of the alkenes in 
the cycloaddition to its triplet energy state. First, the sensitizer is excited by light irradiation from its 
ground state (S0) to its singlet state (S1). Subsequently, the sensitizer is excited to its triplet state (T1) 
by intersystem crossing (ISC). The photocatalyst then transfers its energy to one of the alkenes in the 
cycloaddition. The energy is transferred using a Dexter mechanism depicted on Figure 4 and described 
further below.8 
 
Figure 4: Dexter mechanism of the sensitizer and the alkene. 
Firstly, Figure 4 shows how the orbitals of the sensitizer change from the ground state to its excited 
state by light irradiation. Then, in the Dexter mechanism one electron from the LUMO orbital of the 
sensitizer is transferred to the alkene and one electron from the HOMO orbital of the alkene is 
transferred back to the sensitizer. Only after the triplet energy transfer has occurred, the alkene can be 
==PAGE:17==
5 
 
excited to its triplet state and become a diradical. The sensitizer can then return to its ground state after 
having received an electron from the alkene.8,9 
 
Figure 5: Orbital diagram of the reaction between the excited alkene and the ground state alkene. 
Figure 5 shows an orbital diagram of the next step in the mechanistic pathway. After the Dexter 
mechanism and the triplet energy transfer from the sensitizer, the excited alkene has formed a new 
HOMO π* orbital. The excited alkene further reacts with another alkene which has not been excited. 
The new HOMO π* will interact with the LUMO π* from the other alkene. The interaction will create 
two new C-C bonds to form the cyclobutane.8,9 
 
There are a requirement that need to be met in order to carry out the triplet energy transfer. The 
sensitizer needs to have a higher energy triplet state than the alkene. The cycloaddition reactions can 
be performed using a sensitizer, which needs a longer wavelength compared to direct excitation.8 
 
The photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition reaction forms a strained four-membered ring. Strained four-
membered rings often easily revert back to their starting material, however, photochemical [2+2] 
cycloaddition is not reversible. This is due to the fact that the formed cyclobutane will have lost its π 
orbitals, and therefore, it will only have low-lying empty orbital. The cyclobutane can therefore not 
absorb light in the low-lying orbitals. As the reverse reaction needs to absorb light again in order to 
occur, the cycloaddition is not reversible.9 
 
1.2.3 Regioselectivity 
The regioselectivity of the intramolecular [2+2] cycloaddition depends on the rule of five meaning that, 
if possible, the [2+2] cycloaddition will always happen by a 1,5-ring closure to form a five-membered 
==PAGE:18==
6 
 
ring. Therefore, the rule of five for cycloaddition will give rise to two different regioisomers, either a 
straight or a crossed cycloaddition product which is presented in Scheme 3A.10 
 
 
 
Scheme 3: A: Crossed or straight cycloaddition products. Reproduced from Rigotti et al.10 B: An example from the 
literature of the crossed cycloaddition product (10) (TX: Thioxanthone). Reproduced from Graßl et al.11 
The two alkenes in a cycloaddition are linked together by a tether (Scheme 3A). The regioselectivity 
in a cycloaddition depends on the length of the tether. If the tether consists of three or four atoms, it 
may form a straight cycloaddition. If the tether has three atoms, it will create a five-membered ring; if 
the tether has four atoms, it will form a six-membered ring. The alkenes will then connect in a 1,2-
fashion between α/α’ to give the straight cycloaddition product. 
Furthermore, if the tether consists of two atoms, it will prefer to undergo the crossed cycloaddition 
because of the rule of five. The alkene will then connect in a 1,3-fashion between α/β’ or α’/β to give 
the crossed product. The crossed product is unique and cannot be obtained in a thermal reaction.8,10,11 
An example from the literature of the crossed product is shown in Scheme 3B. Alkene (9) forms a 
crossed cycloaddition product (10) due to the length of the tether which consists of two atoms.11 
 
  
1.2.4 Photosensitizers 
One of the essential tools in photochemistry are the photocatalysts. A photocatalyst is a molecule which 
can be excited to different energy states by light, and thereby, enable the production of a cyclobutane 
by sensitization of an alkene starting material in [2+2] cycloadditions. Some of the most widely used 
photocatalysts are photoredox catalysts such as ruthenium (11)- or iridium (12)-based catalysts (Figure 
6A). These photocatalysts are metal complexes, the downside of which is that they can be very 
expensive and quite toxic, meaning that they have a negative economic and environmental impact.12 
Therefore, modern photochemistry uses organic photocatalysts instead. These are called photoorgano 
catalysts and in contrast to the metal complexes are relatively inexpensive. Photoorgano catalysts are 
small molecules such as benzophenone (13) and thioxanthone (14) (Figure 6B) which are also more 
environmentally friendly reagents.13

In this section both photoorgano catalysts and photoredox catalysts are described further.  
 
Photoorgano catalysts 
A photoorgano catalyst is a small molecule which will encourage a chemical transformation when it 
is irradiated with ultraviolet, visible, or near-infrared light. Photoorgano catalysts can promote the 
formation of radicals under mild and controlled conditions.5 
 
Scheme 4: Mechanistic pathway of the photocatalyst benzophenone (13) (BP). Reproduced from Sideri et al.13 
Benzophenone (13) (BP) is a photoorgano catalyst which is mainly used in organic photochemistry. It 
has a long-lived triplet lifetime, and it allows an UV absorption at 365 nm for excitation. Additionally, 
BP is used in the mechanistic pathway called triplet energy transfer (Scheme 4). First, BP is excited to 
its singlet state by light irradiation and then excited to its triplet state by intersystem crossing (ISC). 
BP can then transfer its triplet energy to another molecule.13 
 
Scheme 5: Mechanistic pathway of the photocatalyst iso-propyl thioxanthone (15) (ITX). Inspired from Sideri et al.13 
Other organic photocatalysts are thioxanthone (14) (TX) and its more soluble derivative iso-propyl 
thioxanthone (15) (ITX). TX has a high triplet energy and long-lived triplet lifetime. It has an UV

absorption region up to 400 nm excitation. Similar to BP, TX and ITX can be excited to their singlet 
state by light irradiation and then to their triplet state by intersystem crossing, then undergo triplet 
energy transfer to excite another molecule to its triplet state (Scheme 5).5,13

Photoredox catalysts 
Photoredox catalysts undergo single electron transfer (SET). The photoredox catalyst is a metal 
complex which will react with an organic molecule and visible light. Some of the most widely used 
photoredox catalysts are ruthenium- and iridium-based catalysts.  
 
Figure 7: Energy level diagram of the photoredox catalyst Ru(bpy)32+.14 
Figure 7 shows an orbital diagram of the photocatalyst Ru(bpy)32+. First, an electron is excited from 
the ground state to its singlet state by metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT). Next, the electron is 
excited to its triplet state by ISC. Once in the triplet state, the electron undergoes SET. The 
photocatalyst can then act as either an oxidant or a reductant in the triplet state. Conversely, when the 
==PAGE:21==
9 
 
photocatalyst acts as an oxidant, an electron will be accepted in the t2g orbital. When the photocatalyst 
acts as a reductant, an electron will be removed from the π* orbital.14 The oxidative quenching cycle 
for the reductant and the oxidant is described below.  
 
Figure 8: Reductive and oxidative quenching cycles of a photoredox catalyst. 
The transformation of the photocatalyst will then go either by a reductive quenching cycle or an 
oxidative quenching cycle (Figure 8). The photocatalyst is excited to its excited state with irradiation. 
In the reductive cycle, the excited photocatalyst (PC*) will function as an oxidant and receive an 
electron from a donor (D) to get the reduced species which is a strong reductant. The photocatalyst 
will return to the ground state and complete the cycle by donating an electron to the acceptor (A). In 
the oxidative quenching cycle, the excited photocatalyst will act as a reductant. First, the photocatalyst 
will donate an electron to the acceptor (A) to become the oxidized species which is a strong oxidant. 
Finally, to complete the cycle the oxidized photocatalyst will accept an electron from the donor (D) to 
get back to its ground state.14,20 
 
1.2.5 Examples from the Literature

cycloaddition reaction has increased. Padwa et al. carried out an intramolecular [2+2] cycloaddition

1.3.1 Oxidopyrylium [5+2] Cycloaddition 
Oxidopyrylium cycloaddition chemistry has been studied for many years and its important role in 
synthetic organic chemistry is well established. A well-known oxidopyrylium cycloaddition is the 
[5+2] cycloaddition reaction which enables access to a large library of bicyclic compounds also called 
bridged polycyclic ethers. The bicyclic compounds can be formed rapidly and easily using [5+2]

Scheme 8 shows a [5+2] cycloaddition of an oxidopyrylium ylide (28). First, the oxidopyrylium ylide

in this case, an alkene to generate a bicyclic product (29). The reaction can create both intramolecular 
and intermolecular bicyclic compounds. 
 
The natural small molecules kojic acid and maltol are a part of the group called 3-hydroxy-4-pyrones

This master thesis aims to develop different methods for [2+2] cycloaddition employing the natural

3.1.1 Synthesis of Starting Materials from Kojic Acid 
This section discusses and analyzes the synthesis of the starting materials derived from kojic acid. The 
commercially available kojic acid contains two alcohol groups, a pyranone alcohol, and a primary 
alcohol. As illustrated in the synthesis of kojic acid with allyl bromide below, the allyl reagent will 
first react with the pyranone alcohol. The reaction is presented in Scheme 13. 
 
Scheme 13: Synthesis of the monoallylated product (45) from kojic acid and allyl bromide. 
Kojic acid reacted with allyl bromide in the presence of potassium carbonate (K2CO3) in acetonitrile 
(MeCN) at 80 °C overnight. Allyl bromide reacted with the pyranone alcohol to afford the 
monoallylated product (45). The main product (45) was isolated by a column alongside a side product, 
whose structure which could not conclusively be determined. The monoallylated product (45) was 
obtained as a yellow oil with a yield of 22%. The reaction was also investigated under different 
conditions, in the presence of sodium hydride (NaH) in dry tetrahydrofuran (THF), but the LC-MS 
spectra only showed a low conversion of monoallylated (45). Therefore, this approach has been 
abandoned. To see if the same reactivity holds true for homoallyl bromide, the same reaction could be 
performed of homoallyl bromide. Unfortunately, due to time constraints a reaction between kojic acid 
and homoallyl bromide has not been investigated.  
 
As illustrated above, the allylation reaction will first be carried out on the pyranone alcohol. The project 
aims to synthesize analogs via an allylation on only the primary alcohol. Therefore, to ensure that the

protecting group (PG). Different synthesis of various protecting groups on the pyranone alcohol were 
investigated (Scheme 14, step 1). The protecting groups used were benzoyl (Bz), tert-
Butyldimethylsilyl (TBS), and 4-Methoxybenzyl (PMB). 
 
Next, the compounds with the protected pyranone alcohol undergo an allylation (SN2) reaction with 
allyl bromide or homoallyl bromide (Scheme 14, step 2). The number of reactions were synthesized to 
optimize the yield and afford the best reactions for the next steps in the study. The different protection 
reactions and allylation reactions are described below. After the allylation reaction, the starting 
materials were then ready to undergo the photochemical cycloaddition reaction.

3.2.2 Tests using the Nail Gel-Dryer

the cycloaddition reactions with or without a photocatalyst, different solvents and the product 
conversion per reaction time.  
 
Test 1

photocatalyst. The stock solutions contained the starting materials: (3), (4), or (7) in either of the

4 Conclusion and Future Work 
Scheme 30 presents a summary of the synthetic pathways of all the different starting materials and 
cycloaddition products which have been synthesized in this project.  
 
To conclude, the building blocks were synthesized. The building blocks are derived analogs from kojic 
acid and maltol. The starting material (45) was synthesized from kojic acid and allyl bromide and 
yielded 22%. The starting material (7) was synthesized from kojic acid in two steps: a protecting 
reaction and an allylation reaction. After investigating three different protecting groups, Bz, TBS and 
PMB, it was found that the most successful protecting group was PMB due to a moderate yield in both 
reactions. The allylation reaction with the PMB-protected kojic acid and allyl iodide afforded the 
allylated PMB-protected product (7) in the best yield of 18 - 44%. Furthermore, three starting materials 
(3), (4) and (55) were synthesized in a successful one step reaction from maltol. The starting materials 
was carried out without a purification in a yield of 59 - 95%.  
 
The starting materials were then used in photochemical intramolecular [2+2] cycloaddition reactions. 
Different conditions such as irradiation, photocatalysts, solvents, glassware were investigated. It was 
concluded that the most optimal conditions for the cycloaddition were using a LED chamber with a 
wavelength of λ = 375 nm, 20 mol% ITX, DCE degassed with argon as the solvent and a microwave 
tube. Table 10 show the best working [2+2] cycloaddition addition reactions.

also isolated mixed fractions with both starting material and product. By characterize the NMR data,

Future work should focus on synthesizing a larger compound library of the [2+2] cycloaddition 
products derived from kojic acid or maltol. Only two cycloaddition products derived from maltol were 
synthesized in this project. The cycloaddition of the starting materials derived from kojic acid did not 
work so far and could therefore be investigated more. The cyclobutane did not form in the reaction 
with (7) instead the OPMB-group was removed. However, if more mol% photocatalyst is used, the 
reaction might form the cyclobutane after removing the OPMB group which could be explored in 
future work. In future work, other allylation reactions with more substituted allyl halides could also be 
investigated, which will also result in a larger compound library of kojic acid and maltol. Finally, for 
future work other protecting groups e.g., THP could be explored to see whether they work more 
successful in both the protection reaction and the allylation reaction. The allylation reaction with the 
Bz-protected kojic acid could be tried again to find a better synthetic pathway.

5 Experimental 
This section describes the general directions and procedures for the experiment and its analytical and 
characteristic data for afforded compounds.  
 
5.1 General directions 
Chemical reactions were carried out under a nitrogen, an argon atmosphere, or an air atmosphere. 
The solvents were of HPLC quality and the dry solvents (MeCN, DCM, DMF and THF) were from a 
pureSolv system (Innovative Technology, Tronyx). The chemical starting materials and reagents was 
commercially available from Thermo Scientific, Sigma Aldrich, Fluka, Combi Blocks, Merck and 
Acros Organics, VWR chemicals without further purification. All the reactions were monitored by 
thin-layer chromatography (TLC) and reversed-phase ultra-performance liquid chromatography-mass 
spectrometry (RP-UPLC-MS).  
Analytical TLC was performed on Merck aluminium sheets covered with silica (C60). The plates 
were visualised under ultraviolet light (λ = 245 nm) or stained by dipping in a developing agent 
followed by heating. KMnO4 stain was used as the developing agent [3 g in water (300 mL) along with 
K2CO3 (20 g) and 5% aqueous NaOH (5 mL)].  
Flash column chromatography (FCC) was performed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 μm) 
silica gel. 
Characterization of all new compounds were done by TLC, NMR, LC-MS, HRMS (side products 
were not fully characterised).  
Structural assignments were made of new compounds. A combination of 2D NMR techniques 
(COSY, HSQC, H2BC, HMBC, NOESY) were used. For the recording 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR, a 
Bruker Avance III 400 spectrometer with a Bruker Ascend 400 magnet and a Prodigy CryoProbe 
(operating at 400 MHz for proton and 101 MHz for carbon. The chemical shifts (δ) are reported in 
parts per million (ppm) and the coupling constants (J) are reported in Hz. Spectra were referenced 
using the residual solvent peaks of the respective solvent: DMSO-d6 (δ = 2.50 ppm for 1H-NMR and 
δ = 39.52 ppm for 13C-NMR), CDCl3 (δ = 7.26 ppm for 1H-NMR and δ = 77.0 ppm for 13C-NMR). 
The following abbreviations were used to report peak multiplicities; s = singlet, d = doublet, t = triplet, 
m = multiplet, dd = doublet of doublets, dt = doublet of triplet, ddd = doublet of doublet of doublet, 
ddt = doublet of doublet of triplet, dddd = doublet of doublet of doublet of doublet.  
Analytical RP-UPLC-MS (ESI) analysis was performed on a S2 Waters AQUITY RP-UPLC system 
equipped with a diode array detector using an Thermo Accucore C18 column (d = 2.6 μm, 2.1 x 50 
mm; column temp. = 50 °C; flow = 1.0 mL/min). Eluents A (10 – 15 mM NH4OAc in H2O) and B (10 
==PAGE:60==

– 15 mM NH4OAc in 9:1 MeCN/H2O) were used in a linear gradient (5% B to 100% B) in 2.4 min 
and then held for 0.1 min at 100% B (total run time = 2.6 min). The LC system was coupled to a SQD 
mass spectrometer.  
Melting point were obtained using a Stuart SMP30 melting point apparatus.  
Photoirradiation was performed using a Nailstar professional 4 x 9 W lamp (NS-01-UK&EU from 
Amazon.de) with peak 365 nm wavelength. The 96-well MTP was placed in the middle of the UV-
