Summary (English)
The project deals with cryptographic implementations on mobile devices. Of
particular interest is the AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) hardware sup-
port in modern ARM CPUs such as ARMv8. In speciﬁc, this thesis demon-
strates AES under the NIST recommended modes ECB, CTR, CBC, CFB,
OFB, and CCM with the use of the NEON engine intrinsics. In addition, this
paper implements an advanced instruction scheduler, allowing the subset of se-
quential modes to perform similar to those of parallelisable nature. Essentially
allowing CBC, CFB, and OFB to perform at approximately 1.3 cpb under a
realistic online message distribution, optimising the modes by a factor 2 com-
pared to their genuine implementations.
Finally, the scheduler is applied to
the authenticated mode CCM, contributing a factor 1.44 allowing the CCM to
perform at 2.74 cpb.

Chapter 1
Introduction
85% of all mobile devices featured ARM based processors in 2015 [riE15b]. In
addition, the mobile platform exceeded desktops, by accounting for 60% of all
digital media time spend already in mid 2014 [Lip14], clearly illustrating the
size and demand of the mobile infrastructure. In 2011 ARM Limited (ARM)
introduced the ARMv8 architecture as a successor to the ARMv7 instruction set
architecture. The ARMv8 implements a 64 bit address space, contrary 32 bit
on its predecessors. Commercially, ARMv8 grounds the basis of most modern
System on Chip (SoC), including the Apple A7, A8, A8X, and the Exynos 7 and
8, found in mobile devices such as iPhons 5s, 6, 6Plus, and SE, and Samsung
Galaxy 6, Note 4 and Nexus 9 on the Android domain.
This new architecture provides, for the ﬁrst time on ARM, dedicated hardware
support for AES. This new instruction set features both encryption and decryp-
tion, allowing tremendous execution speed-up compared to code based solutions,
factor 10 according to ARM.
This paper investigates the AES hardware support and performance on the
ARMv8 architecture. In speciﬁc, this thesis addresses the ARMv8 environment
present on the Samsung Galaxy S6, providing a quad-core 2.1 GHz Cortex-
A57 and quad-core 1.5 GHz Cortex A-53 environments. The presented analysis
will ground its investigation based on the Cortex A-57 environment. What is
more, this thesis will provide genuine implementations of AES utilising these

In fact, this paper will demonstrate ARM based im-
plementations of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)
recommended modes of operation. Covering for the ﬁrst time, in a public pa-
per, ARM supported implementations of Electronic Code Book (ECB), Counter
mode (CTR), Cipher Block Chaining (CBC), Cipher Feedback (CFB), and
Outpu Feedback (OFB). Along with the Authenticated Encryption (AE) mode
Counter mode with CBC-MAC (CCM).
The special NIST publications [Dwo01],[Dwo07] recommends the following ba-
sic modes of operation: ECB, CBC, CFB, OFB and CTR.
Moreover, they
recommend CCM and Galois/Counter Mode (GCM) as authenticated encryp-
tion modes, and Cipher-based Message Authentication Code (CMAC) as au-
thentication mode. Of these modes of operation CBC, CFB, OFB, CMAC and
CCM are inherently sequential. While GCM performs signiﬁcantly better than
CCM, it is prune to authentication attacks as shown in [Fer05],[Jou06]. Hence,
it makes sense to investigate the performance optimisation possibilities of CCM.
Likewise within the basic modes of operation, CTR, ECB are inherently paral-
lel. Thus these modes beneﬁts well from the pipeline interface. However, while
the sequential modes of operations does beneﬁts signiﬁcantly from the pipeline
interface, their subsequent design cannot draw complete pipeline utilisation.
As the thesis will demonstrate, executing pipeline instructions provides two
measurements of particular interest, namely throughput, T −1, and latency, L.
The latency describes the time from instruction execution, until the output is
ready. That is, the time of the instruction through the pipeline. The throughput
however, measures the frequency of the pipeline. Hence it is readily observed,
that while the pipeline can execute at frequency T −1, sequential messages are
forced to stall the execution by the latency L. Thus, optimal performance cannot
be achieved by a single message encryption under these modes.
This paper will, for the ﬁrst time on the ARM architecture, describe, imple-
ment, and analyse the multi-message scheduling algorithm, The Comb Sched-
uler [BLT16], proposed by Andrey Bogdanov and Martin M. Lauridsen and
Elmar Tischhauser. The algorithm allows, the otherwise sequential modes, to
act in a non-sequential behaviour, drawing optimal beneﬁt of the pipeline. Its
principles grounds in the observation, that multiple independent messages can
be processed in parallel according to the parallelisation degree, P, which can be
predetermined given the latency and throughput of the system.
Hence the paper at hand, seeks to investigate AES based cryptographic algo-
rithms on the ARMv8 architecture. In speciﬁc, this investigation will utilised the
proposed Comb-Scheduler [BLT16], as the pipeline performance optimisation
algorithm. The purpose is to demonstrate the performance gain of sequential
modes. Essentially allowing the non-parallel modes to perform close to those

1.1
Motivation
While ARM documents the existence of their supported instructions set, they
provide little, if any, information upon the usage of these. In [riE14], where
the cryptographic intrinsics are documented, in fact no further information are
given, other than the instruction interface. Hence, the domain is in need of
proper investigations. Not only analysis of the instructions performance, but
also in the sense of use. Since addressing these subjects, can advance the imple-
mentation discussion, evidently leading towards better implementations. More-
over, clarifying the instructions will simplify, a so far abstract domain, grounding
better public knowledge and providing programmers with stronger motivation
going towards faster and more secure encryption.
Furthermore, investigating fast AES is important.
Especially, providing fast
CCM is a huge gain for the AE community, as the faster alternative Oﬀset Code-
book Mode (OCB) is patented, and thus expensive to use. However, fast encryp-
tion in general is of great importance, as it would allow eﬃcient hard-drive en-
cryption, similar to that of FireVault, and better Internet performance. Further-
more, as the demand of mobile technologies are exploding [riE15b], lightweight
cryptology is demanded. Modern mobile devises access vulnerable information
each day. Mobile banking, private or corporate emails, user tracking, and vul-
nerable private user information are just some of the information, which are
contained within these devices, yielding the need of security and privacy. More-
over, the mobile devices has limited access to power, contrary high availability
is required, since these devices are becoming standardised in all everyday tasks.
Lastly, mobile devices are expected to perform with high responsiveness, since
they are used on the ﬂy, requiring actions performed by the user to be close to
instant. In fact, according to Miller and Card et. al. [Mil68, SKCM91], the sys-
tem is required to provide response within an 100ms time-frame to be conceived
as instant, while 1 second will interrupt the ﬂow of the user, and ﬁnally stating
that 10 seconds is the limit to which you will keep the attention of the user.
These demands, in a cryptographic sense, yields specialised security measure-
ments. AES is an obvious candidate for this purpose, as it enables high privacy.
However, AES requires multiple arithmetic operations, along with multiple it-
erations of the same message. Thus it make sense to investigate its performance
and how to improve it.

1.2
Related work
With the emerge of AES New Instructions (AES-NI), AES received signiﬁcant
speed-up along with the possibility of using pipeline optimised algorithms. This
break-through allowed for new implementations design allowing, eg. CBC, to
perform at 0.36 cycles per byte (cpb), provided a multi-message encryption on
6 cores [KAZ10]. Hence, the results was provided by utilising multi-threading.
Andrey Bogdanov, Martin M. Lauridsen, and Elmar Tischhauser [BLT16] pi-
oneered single threaded pipeline parallelisation, in the domain of AES, with
the proposal of the Comb-Scheduler in 2015. The paper implements its mea-
surements on the Intel Haswell microarchitecture, i5-4300U CPU running at
1.90GHz, as well as the more recent Skylake microarchitecture, i7-6700 CPU
running at 3.40GHz. By applying Comb they demonstrated an entire factor
5 optimisation level for the AES modes: CBC, OFB, CFB, and CMAC. In
fact, the sequential algorithms performs within a mere 10% slower than that
of CTR, provided a realistic message distribution following the TCP and UDP
distribution, also shown in ﬁgure 3.4. These results clearly illustrates the power
of ﬁlling the pipeline.
This paper extends the Comb Scheduler to the ARMv8 architecture. Illustrat-
ing the power of combing cross platform. In addition, Andrey Bogdanov et.
al. implemented Insertion Sort, as the sorting engine, optimising for constant
space, while this paper extends the research by applying the Radix Sort algo-
rithm [PMM93], sorting in linear time.
In a recent study, 2015, Gouvëa et. al. [GL15] demonstrated usage of the ARMv8
AES instruction set. In the article, Gouvëa et. al. implements GCM along with
CTR, using the binary polynomial multiplication intrinsic achieving factor 9
seed-up, compared to the predecessor ARMv7, in speciﬁc they obtained 1.21cpb
for the CTR mode responsible of message encryption under GCM, and a mere
0.51cpb for the GCM authentication 11 times faster than its predecessor.
While Gouvëa likewise tested their implementation on the Cortex-A57, they
only tested it using the AArch32 encoding. This study, builds its research to

Problem deﬁnition
The project deals with cryptographic implementations on mobile devices. Of
particular interest is the AES hardware support in modern ARM CPUs such
as ARMv8. This paper will provide a complete performance investigation of
multiple AES modes, utilising the pipeline instruction set. In speciﬁc, this pa-
per will investigate the modes ECB, CBC, OFB, CFB, and the authenticated
encryption mode CCM. In addition, this paper will seek to optimise these im-
plementations by enhancing the pipeline usage with a multi-message optimised
algorithm, the Comb Scheduler.

2.1
Block Ciphers
Cryptology is the science of securing communication between trusted parties,
preventing a potential third party, the adversary, of obtaining knowledge about-
or altering the contents of the communication undetected. This paper deals with
cryptology in the sector of Symmetric Encryption. Symmetric encryption relies
on the parties sharing the same secret key k. Hence, a communication ﬂow
between A and B relies on an invertible two-way algorithm allowing encryption
and decryption using the shared secret k. For the purpose of this thesis, it will
be assumed that the key-exchange is done in a secure manner, and will not be
investigated further. This chapter and the rest of this thesis follows the below
listed cryptographic notations:

Block cipher algorithms are the most used cryptographic environment. As men-
tioned these algorithms executes by encrypting blocks of n bits. However, most
messages are not likely to be sized of exactly on block, neither of to have a length
multiple of n. Leading to the question of, how to encrypt messages bigger or
smaller than n bits? In order to overcome this issue block cipher algorithms
utilises modes of operation. Modes allows encryption of plaintexts of any bit
length, by reusing the k-bit key over multiple blocks of n-bits, padding the
last block if necessary. A disadvantage to keep in mind, when reusing the key,
is redundancy. That is, given a set of ciphertext blocks all encrypted by the
same key, could enforce ciphertext only attacks based on ciphertext redundancy
evidently allowing eﬃcient key recovery. Thus it is crucial to understand the
strengths and weaknesses of a mode. Throughout the following pages this paper
will introduce the NIST recommended modes, ECB, CTR, CBC, OFB, CFB,
and CCM.

2.2
Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)
From the previous section, this thesis explored a speciﬁc variation of crypto-
graphic systems. Namely the Symmetric Key environments. The outline pro-
vided insights into block ciphers, as encryption schema. This section introduces
the NIST standardised block cipher algorithm called AES.
AES is a symmetric cryptographic algorithm, which is based upon the Rijn-
dael cipher [oST01]. The Rijndael cipher was developed and submitted by Joan
Daemen and Vincent Rijmen to the NIST AES competition (1997-2001). The
modern AES is a 128-bit block cipher, with 3 diﬀerent key-sizes available, ta-
ble 2.1. The algorithm is based on the Substitution-permutation network (SPN)
principles [Sti06].
A SPN network implements 3 functions to obtain security.
That is, a non-
linear function, known as the Substitution Box (s-box). The Permutation Box
(p-box) responsible of diﬀusing the non-linearity. The secrecy obtained from an
Exclusive-or (XOR) operation with a secret key.

AddRoundKey corresponds to the SPN secrecy step. The SubBytes operation
is the non-linear s-box, whilst ShiftRows in combination with MixColumns
contributes the permutation layer, p-box.
The key schedule is in charge of
expanding the key into r+1 round keys. As an example AES-128 would expands
the key space into (10 + 1) × 128 = 1408-bit key space.

Radix sort is a non-comparative sorting algorithm [PMM93, THC09], meaning
that the sorting is done without comparing the entire set of items.
Instead
Radix sorts by looking at the least signiﬁcant digit and combines the items into
separate bins, such that bin0 contains the items whose least signiﬁcant digit is
0, bin1 holds the items having 1 as the least signiﬁcant bit, and so on. It then
==PAGE:52==

in this manner until all digits has been processed. Thus, the entire sorting is
completed by iterating all n items k times, where k is the number of digits in

int preComputeWindows(struct Message *M, int r, unsigned char *rho,
unsigned char *beta){
int w = 0;
int qlast = 0;
int i = r;
while (i > 1){
int q = M[i-1].l;

while (j >= 0 && M[j].l == q){
j -=1;
}
rho[w] = i;
beta[w] = q - qlast;

i = j+1;

The very issue with the time-based counter most be addressed. Its vulnerability
lies in the fact that this measurement is relying on the processor performing at
max frequency at all time. Meaning that eg. if the CPU design implements
frequency scaling based on work-load, introducing increase or decrease of clock

constant. Leading to the question, why even introduce a time-based counter?
The experiment design was chosen out of simplicity. That is, the ARMv8 does
not provide out-of-the-box access to the cycle counter, even when running under
root. This means, that in order to utilise the cycle counter, a custom kernel
driver module most be implemented. In order to at least minimise scenarios
where the processor could run with a lower frequency, this thesis provided a
warm-up phase. The warm up phase provides a 0.25 × iterationmeasure loop,
ensuring that the processor is not in some idle stage upon measurement start.
A important aspect of the results provided in this research is the multi-message

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Recall the objectives of this thesis listed below. Besides the enumeration, this
thesis successfully assembled the existing research in the area of AES on modern
mobile devices. As the thesis implies, this domain is not very covered, leading to
the very ﬁrst objective in the below list. To investigation and analyse the cryp-
tographic instruction support on the ARMv8 architecture.
This investigation
was conducted in chapter 4. The goal of the investigation was to introduce the
intrinsics available and their usage, primarily with the focus of AES. The very
mapping of the instruction on the AES algorithm was provided. In addition,
these instructions was tested in the degrees of latency and throughput. The
results yielded 6 cc and 1 instruction per cycle respectively. This analysis was,
likewise, conducted for the SHA1 and SHA2 instructions. In addition, essential
claeriﬁcation upon the ARMv8 NEON engine was provideded. Especially the
SIMD design is of particular interest, as it allows for a specialised vector repre-
sentation. A representation that is optimised for processing of multiple vector
entries concurrently. This is essential, as it allows the instructions to execute
with a very low cpb utilisation, explaining high performance of 0.375 cpb for a
single AES round.

uint8x16_t aes_cbc_mac(const unsigned char* auth_block,
const unsigned char* in, int length, const char *expkey){
uint8x16_t lastblock = {0};
uint8x16_t block = veorq_u8(vld1q_u8(&((int8_t *)auth_block)[0]),
lastblock);
lastblock = aes_enc(block, (uint8x16_t *)expkey);
for (int i = 0; i < length; i += 16){
block = vld1q_u8(&((int8_t *)in)[i]);
block = veorq_u8(block, lastblock);
lastblock = aes_enc(block, (uint8x16_t *)expkey);
}
return lastblock;
}

ated Authentication header block as IV, otherwise it executes similar
to CBC however only outputting a single block at the very end.

void aes_ctr(const unsigned char* in, unsigned char *out,
int length, const char* counter, const char *expkey){
uint8x16_t
block;
for (int i = 0; i < length; i += 16){
block = vld1q_u8(&((int8_t *)counter)[i+1]);
block = aes_enc(block, (uint8x16_t *)expkey);
block = veorq_u8(vld1q_u8(&((int8_t *)in)[i]), block);
vst1q_u8(&((int8_t*)out)[i], block);
}
}
Figure 8: AES CTR implementation. The method accepts a fully expanded
CTR set, and executed by encrypting the CTR and XORing the
plaintext with the result.
void aes_ccm(uint8_t M, uint8_t L, uint32_t lm,
const unsigned char *message,
unsigned char *out,
const unsigned char *nonce, unsigned char *counter,
const unsigned char *expkey){
unsigned char auth_block[16];
uint8_t aflag = aes_cbc_mac_auth_flag(M, L);
uint8_t cflag = aes_ctr_counter_flag(L);
ctr_counter_generation_function(counter, nonce, cflag, lm);
create_aes_nonce_block(auth_block, nonce, aflag, lm);
uint8x16_t cbc_mac_tag = aes_cbc_mac(auth_block, message, lm, expkey)
;
aes_ctr(message, out, lm, counter, expkey);
cbc_mac_tag = veorq_u8(cbc_mac_tag, vld1q_u8(&((int8_t *)counter)[0])
);
vst1q_u8(&((int8_t*)out)[lm], cbc_mac_tag);
}
Figure 9: The very CCM mode operator. This method is responsible of ini-
tialising the entire CCM mode. Remark that the authentication tag
is appended to the result.
