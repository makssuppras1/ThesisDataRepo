Abstract
This project aims to enhance fungal identification by employing machine learning tech­
niques to address and overcome challenges such as bias, noise, data inconsistencies,
and the poor definition of fungal growth. It explores the complexities of image process­
ing and analysis in the context of training various image recognition models, including
DenseNet, ResNet, ViT, and LeViT. The primary objective is to identify the optimal dataset
through the examination of several factors, including bias mitigation via background re­
moval through masking, image pruning based on growth time, and additional feature min­
ing through average color analysis. The original dataset comprises 114 images, which
underwent preprocessing to produce four distinct datasets, each trained on five differ­
ent models. Ultimately, the training resulted in multiple models achieving over 95% test
accuracy, with the ViT models delivering the highest accuracy. Regarding the datasets,
segmenting the images into individual plate images and training them separately produced
the most favorable outcomes.

Introduction
The collection and annotation of data on the appearance of fungal species are crucial to
biological research. Accurate data is essential for understanding biodiversity, monitoring
climate change, and addressing species extinction. Comprehensive datasets improve
data quality and add significant value to mycological studies. These datasets are critical
for enhancing our understanding of fungal diversity and distribution, which in turn supports
effective conservation strategies and ecological research.
Correct identification of fungi remains a significant challenge due to the high degree vari­
ation in the amount of biodiversity and sheer number of sub categorizations of fungi. Poor
data quality is often cited as a major concern when species data is provided by untrained
eye. To address this issue, some projects reduce the complexity of the identification
process by merging species into non­comparable indicator groups or focusing on easily
identifiable species. Others involve human expert validation or science communities in
the data validation process. Automated image­based species identification can act as
both a supplement and an alternative to these traditional approaches for classification.
Despite these efforts, automated image­based species identification offers a promising
alternative to traditional methods, in addition to more efficient and potentially more accu­
rate identification. Advances in computer vision and machine learning have enabled the
development of systems that can accurately and efficiently identify species from images.
These automated systems can process large datasets quickly, reducing the burden on
human experts and minimizing the risk of human error. By leveraging deep learning al­
gorithms, automated identification systems can achieve high levels of accuracy, making
them valuable tools for enhancing species identification efforts.
The rapid progress in computer vision over the past decade has been facilitated by the
existence of large­scale image collections. The introduction of the ImageNet database
and its use in the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) played
an important role in initiating the adoption of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). Simi­
larly, datasets and challenges like PlantCLEF, iNaturalist, CUB, and Oxford Flowers have
driven advancements in fine­grained visual categorization. These developments have led
to novel approaches in domain adaptation, transfer learning, image retrieval, unsuper­
vised visual representation, few­shot learning, and classifier calibration. These advance­
ments are particularly relevant for fungal identification, where fine­grained classification
is essential.
[1] The isolation and identification of fungi play a crucial role in clinical microbiology, par­
ticularly in light of the rising drug resistance observed among pathogenic fungi. As such,
antifungal susceptibility testing has become increasingly important to guide effective treat­
ment strategies. Proper specimen collection and transport methods are essential to avoid

gal culture and identification is critical, with direct smear examinations and special stains

With these given challenges, this research/project aims to address the problem of ac­
curate fungal species identification by developing an automated system that utilizes ad­
vanced machine learning techniques. The primary objective of this study is to create a
robust model capable of achieving high accuracy in identifying various fungal species
while minimizing misclassifications. Furthermore, this research will explore the perfor­
mance of different machine learning architectures under varying conditions and datasets,
thereby providing valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
To achieve these objectives, the research will focus on several key questions that are

critical to the development of an effective automated identification system. Firstly, what
preprocessing techniques can be employed to enhance image quality for fungal identi­

Secondly, how do different machine learning architectures, particularly Convolutional Neu­

in feature extraction and classification accuracy. This research will involve a systematic

Furthermore, the complexity of fungal morphology presents an additional hurdle. Fungi
exhibit a wide range of shapes, colors, and textures, which can vary significantly even
within the same species. This biological diversity necessitates a fine­grained approach
to image processing, where the model must be trained to recognize subtle differences.
Utilizing advanced techniques such as deep learning­based feature extraction can help
address this challenge, allowing the model to learn intricate patterns that distinguish be­
tween closely related species.
The significance of this study lies in its potential to substantially improve the accuracy and
efficiency of fungal identification, which is nessesary for biodiversity monitoring and con­
servation efforts. By harnessing the advancements in computer vision and deep learning,
this research aims to contribute to the development of reliable automated systems that
can process large datasets quickly and accurately. Such systems have the potential to
alleviate the burden on human experts, reduce the risk of human error, and ultimately
enhance our understanding of fungal diversity.
The methodology of this research will involve a multi­step approach, beginning with data
preprocessing to prepare the collected images for analysis. This will include resizing
images to a consistent dimension, normalizing pixel values, and augmenting the dataset
through techniques like rotation, flipping, and cropping. This step enhances the dataset’s
variability and robustness, making it more suitable for training a machine learning model.
The preprocessed images will be used to train various machine learning models, with
a carefully divided dataset to facilitate training, validation, and testing to ensure reliable
performance evaluation.

Literature Review

2.0.1
Masking Strategies for Background Bias Removal in Computer
Vision Models
[3] The study examines how background bias affects the generalization abilities of Convo­
lutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Vision Transformers (ViT) in detailed image classifi­
cation, especially with out­of­distribution (OOD) data. It reveals that background bias has
a considerable impact on these models’ effectiveness. To tackle this problem, the authors
suggest two masking techniques—early and late masking—to reduce these biases and
improve model performance. This research investigates the influence of dataset bias from
image backgrounds on results and proposes three training setups: Baseline: Training a
standard image classification model on a comprehensive dataset without using any mask­
ing. Early Masking: Applying background masking at the image level with foreground­
background (FG­BG) masks before fine­tuning the model. Late Masking: Masking el­
evated features associated with background regions at a midpoint in the model’s pro­
cess using FG­BG masks. The models are evaluated on both in­distribution and out­of­
distribution datasets to assess their generalization skills. Findings from the study show
that early masking consistently outperforms late masking, especially during model back­
bone fine­tuning, which enhances OOD robustness. A significant observation is that a ViT
variant using GAP­Pooled Patch token classification along with early masking achieves
the best OOD performance among all configurations tested. This indicates that back­
ground masking successfully reduces dataset bias caused by image backgrounds. The
literature review highlights the importance of context in object recognition, noting that it
can clarify indistinguishable categories, particularly in pre­deep learning systems. It out­
lines the progression of deep learning, which, despite automatically extracting features,
may create false correlations due to contextual bias. Earlier computer vision techniques
employed foreground segmentation to tackle background bias, whereas current methods
utilize top­down attention strategies to highlight relevant features. The review stresses the
need to investigate the impact of background masking on OOD generalization in modern
models. In summary, the study confirms that background­induced bias significantly influ­
ences the generalization abilities of CNN and ViT models in fine­grained image classifi­
cation, particularly regarding OOD data. The suggested masking techniques, particularly
early masking, improve OOD performance and generalization skills for both types of mod­
els. The research underscores the necessity of accounting for background information in
object­focused tasks and recommends future investigations into larger datasets and real­

An App for Fungal Disease Detection on Plants
[4] The research developed a deep learning (DL) model utilizing DenseNet architecture
to identify fungal keratitis (FK) from corneal images, addressing the challenges of clinical
diagnosis in primary care settings. The model achieved an average sensitivity of approxi­
mately 71% and specificity of 68%, comparable to non­corneal specialty ophthalmologists
but lower than that of corneal specialists. Its effectiveness suggests potential for improv­
ing early FK diagnosis, particularly in rural areas with limited ophthalmologist availability.
Future enhancements include expanding training datasets and fine­tuning the model for
better diagnostic precision. The DL model’s average diagnostic accuracy was around
70%, with an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) nearly at 0.65.
Although it demonstrated higher sensitivity than non­corneal specialty ophthalmologists,
it had lower specificity and positive predictive value compared to expert ophthalmolo­
gists. The study emphasizes the difficulties in diagnosing FK, which is known to be the
most challenging type of microbial keratitis, with traditional methods showing low sensi­
tivity and specificity rates. The literature review highlights the promise of DL techniques
in improving diagnostic accuracy in ocular imaging and points out the limitations of con­
ventional diagnostic approaches. The study employed a retrospective design, examining
patient records with clinically suspected microbial keratitis and laboratory verification of
microbial invasion. Corneal images were captured using standardized methods by certi­
fied technicians with specific camera configurations. Five­fold cross­validation was used
to evaluate the DL model’s performance, ensuring robust validation of results, and im­
age preprocessing techniques, including normalization and Gaussian blur, were applied
to enhance data quality.

Classification of Microscopic Fungi Images Using Vision
Transformers for enhanced Detection of Fungal Infections
[7] This study explores how well vision transformer frameworks classify images of micro­
scopic fungi, which is crucial for managing diseases and ensuring food safety. It contrasts
the pre­trained Vision Transformer (ViT) model with the Swin Transformer model, showing
that the Swin Transformer achieves a better accuracy of 98.36% compared to 96.55% for
the ViT model. The research underscores the value of transfer learning and data aug­
mentation in boosting model effectiveness, suggesting that vision transformers can no­
tably enhance the detection of fungal infections, thereby improving medical imaging and
healthcare solutions. The paper utilizes two main techniques for classifying images of mi­
croscopic fungi: fine­tuning and feature extraction. In the fine­tuning method, pre­trained
vision transformers are modified by substituting their classification head with a new layer
designed for the specific output classes, enabling the whole network to learn from the
dataset. The feature extraction method uses pre­trained models to create feature repre­
sentations of images, keeping the pretrained layers fixed and training new fully connected
layers for classification. Data augmentation strategies are also implemented to improve
model performance by diversifying the training dataset. The Swin Transformer model,
when fine­tuned with data augmentation, reached an accuracy of 98.36%, exceeding the
ViT model’s 96.55%. The Swin Transformer enhanced its performance from 82.32% to
90.87% accuracy, while the ViT increased from 86.70% to 88.39%. Data augmentation
expanded the image dataset from 9,114 to 22,004, boosting the models’ ability to rec­
ognize patterns. Both models exhibited high precision, recall, and F1­scores, confirming
their reliability in classifying images of microscopic fungi. The literature review investi­
gates the use of vision transformers in image classification, emphasizing their benefits
over traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs). It discusses the novel approach
of segmenting images into smaller patches and employing self­attention mechanisms to
capture global dependencies, enhancing feature extraction. The review references mul­
tiple studies comparing vision transformers and CNNs, indicating a rising interest in their
application for medical imaging, particularly in diagnosing fungal infections.

Data description
The Data utilized in this study originated from DTU Bioengineering which has provided

3.5.2
Methodology for Detecting Significant Changes in Images Over Time
To determine exactly which images should be included in the dataset we can look at the
incremental changes over time from the initial inoculation time and then set a specified
minimum requirement where this would be acceptable to include.
For detecting significant changes in images over time, This process involves several key
steps: loading and processing images, computing differences, applying thresholds, de­
tecting significant changes. The approach ensures that only meaningful changes, such
as significant growth or other notable alterations, are identified and recorded for further
analysis.
The process begins by loading and preprocessing the images. This involves reading the
image from the specified path and performing any necessary pre­processing steps, such
as resizing, normalization, or filtering, to prepare the image for analysis.
Next, the difference between the baseline image and the current image is computed.
The baseline image represents a reference state, such as an initial state with no growth,
and the difference image highlights changes that have occurred since the baseline was
captured. This is done by calculating the pixel­wise absolute difference between the two
input images, resulting in a difference image that emphasizes the areas of change. This
is then repeated in sequence for all images in under a specified IBT.
The baseline image is found by analyzing the first image in the sequence for that specific
IBT number. The analysis determines if the baseline is suitable based on the noise or
artifacts prescient in the image. For example it is not uncommon for images to contain
petri dishes with condensation of the lid. Therefore this analysis step performs a blob
analysis the determine if there are too many artifacts prescient compared to a expected
value. If it is found that the baseline image is not suitable the next image in the sequence
will be selected and the same analysis will be performed. This is repeated until a suitable
baseline is found.
To highlight significant changes, an optimal threshold value is calculated using a combi­
nation of Gaussian filtering and [15]Otsu’s method. Gaussian filtering smooths the image
to reduce noise, and Otsu’s method determines the threshold value that minimizes the
18
Advanced Fungal Identification, Leveraging Machine Learning Techniques and Image Analysis
==PAGE:27==
intra­class variance of the threshold pixels. The calculated threshold is then applied to
the difference image. Pixels with values above the threshold are considered significant
changes, while pixels with values below the threshold are considered insignificant. The
result is a binary image where significant changes are highlighted.
The threshold difference image is then analyzed to detect significant changes. This in­
volves finding contours in the threshold difference image, which represent the boundaries
of the changed regions. The area of each contour is computed, and the total area of all
contours is summed. This total area is then compared with a predefined area threshold. If
the total area of the changed regions exceeds the threshold, it is considered a significant
change. If significant changes are detected, the image is marked as having significant
growth.
If a significant change is detected, the image number is extracted by parsing the filename
to extract relevant numerical information. The image is then added to the list of significant
images. This helps in indexing and recording images that exhibit significant changes,
facilitating further analysis and monitoring.

Image Segmentation for feature extraction
Image segmentation is achieved by converting the image to grayscale, reducing noise,
and applying the watershed algorithm. The input image is first converted to grayscale
using the rgb2gray function, simplifying the image by reducing it to a single channel. Noise
is then reduced using a median filter (rank.median) with a disk­shaped structuring element
of radius 5. This filter smooths the image while preserving edges by replacing each pixel’s
value with the median value of its neighbors within the disk.
Disk Structure In this context, a disk structure (or disk­shaped structuring element) is
a binary mask with a circular shape used in morphological operations like median and
gradient filtering. The radius of the disk determines its size, and it helps in defining the
neighborhood of each pixel for filtering operations. The disk structure is crucial for pre­
serving the shape and edges of objects during noise reduction and edge detection. In
the median filter, the disk structure helps in smoothing the image while preserving edges
by replacing each pixel’s value with the median value of its neighbors within the disk. In
the gradient filter, the disk structure helps in highlighting edges by calculating intensity
differences between neighboring pixels within the disk.
Markers for the watershed process are created by applying a gradient filter (rank.gradient)
with a disk­shaped structuring element of radius 3 to the denoised image. The gradi­
ent filter highlights edges by calculating intensity differences between neighboring pixels.
Thresholding is applied to identify low­gradient areas, which are labeled using ndi.label to
serve as markers. A finer gradient image is produced using a smaller disk­shaped struc­
turing element of radius 2. The watershed algorithm is then run on this gradient image
using the markers, treating the grayscale image as a topographic surface. The algorithm
simulates flooding from the markers, filling basins and merging them at watershed lines,
resulting in labeled regions.
Filtering Segments The filtersegments function processes the labeled regions to filter out
segments based on their area and the presence of black pixels. Unique labels are ex­
tracted from the segmented image, and for each label (excluding the background), a mask
is created to isolate the segment. The segment’s area is calculated by summing the mask
values. Segments smaller than a set area threshold (areathreshold) are removed. For
the remaining segments, the number of black pixels (pixels with RGB values of [0, 0, 0])

3.7
Machine Learning Models
3.7.1
Selected models
In order to determine the best models to train the images with, multiple models are trained
on. The models are as following:
• ResNet50
• ViT­B/16
• LeViT­128S
• DenseNet
• VGG16
All of these models have their own quirks and features that distinguish them from each
other and make it interesting to compare them.
ResNet50, or Residual Network with 50 layers, is a deep convolutional neural network
(CNN) architecture that addresses the vanishing gradient problem commonly encountered
in very deep networks. Introduced by He et al. in 2015, ResNet50 uses residual learning
through shortcut connections, allowing the network to learn identity mappings that facil­
itate the training of deeper models. This architecture comprises 48 convolutional layers
along with 1 max­pooling and 1 average­pooling layer, organized into a series of resid­
ual blocks. Each block contains convolutional layers and identity shortcuts that enable
the network to learn residual functions, effectively mitigating the degradation problem as
network depth increases. ResNet50 has been widely adopted for various image classifi­
cation tasks due to its ability to train deeper networks without compromising performance.
[25]
Vision Transformer (ViT) models, such as ViT­B/16, represent a shift from traditional CNN
architectures by employing transformer­based architectures for image classification tasks.
Introduced by [26] Dosovitskiy et al. in 2020, ViT models divide an input image into fixed­
size patches, linearly embed each patch, and then process the sequence of embeddings
using standard transformer encoders. The ”b” in ViT­B/16 denotes the base model size,
and ”16” indicates the patch size of 16x16 pixels. ViT models have demonstrated com­
petitive performance compared to CNNs, especially when trained on large datasets.
LeViT­128S is a hybrid neural network architecture that combines the strengths of con­
volutional neural networks and transformers to achieve efficient image classification. De­
signed for speed and efficiency, LeViT­128S employs convolutional stem layers to extract
low­level features, followed by transformer blocks to model global relationships. This
architecture is particularly notable for its fast inference times and lower computational
requirements, making it suitable for deployment in resource­constrained environments.
DenseNet, or Densely Connected Convolutional Network, is a CNN architecture that in­
troduces direct connections between all layers with the same feature­map size. Proposed
26
Advanced Fungal Identification, Leveraging Machine Learning Techniques and Image Analysis
==PAGE:35==
by Huang et al. in 2016, DenseNet ensures that each layer receives the feature maps of
all preceding layers as input, promoting feature reuse and mitigating the vanishing gra­
dient problem. This dense connectivity leads to more compact and efficient models, as
it reduces the need for learning redundant feature maps. DenseNet architectures, such
as DenseNet­121 and DenseNet­169, have been shown to achieve high accuracy with
fewer parameters compared to traditional CNNs.
VGG16 is a deep CNN architecture introduced by Simonyan and Zisserman in 2014. The
model consists of 16 weight layers, including 13 convolutional layers and 3 fully con­
nected layers. VGG16 is characterized by its simplicity, using small 3x3 convolutional
filters throughout the network, and stacking multiple convolutional layers before applying
max­pooling. This design allows the network to capture complex features while main­
taining a uniform architecture. Despite its depth, VGG16 has a relatively large number
of parameters, approximately 138 million, which can lead to increased computational re­
quirements. Nevertheless, it has been widely used as a benchmark in image classification
tasks and has inspired subsequent architectures that aim to improve efficiency and per­
formance.
In summary, these architectures represent significant milestones in the evolution of neural
network design for image classification. ResNet50 addresses training challenges in deep
networks through residual learning, while ViT models like ViT­B/16 leverage transformer
architectures to capture global context. LeViT­128S offers a hybrid approach combin­
ing CNNs and transformers for efficient processing. DenseNet enhances feature reuse
through dense connectivity, and VGG16 provides a straightforward yet deep architecture
that has influenced many subsequent models.
3.7.2
Practical applications to fungi recognition
Fungal colony classification in petri dishes presents unique challenges due to variations
in colony shape, texture, pigmentation, and growth patterns. Selecting the right machine
learning model is essential for achieving accurate and efficient classification. The selected
architectures—ResNet50, ViT­B/16, LeViT­128S, DenseNet, and VGG16 offer distinct ad­
vantages in analyzing fungal images.
ResNet50 is particularly useful as it captures fine details in colony morphology while miti­
gating training difficultiesm. The hierarchical feature extraction of ResNet50 is well­suited
for distinguishing between similar fungi petri dishes, making it a strong candidate for high­
accuracy classification tasks.
ViT­B/16 is beneficial for fungi in petri dishes, as it captures spatial relationships across
the entire colony rather than focusing solely on local texture. This model excels when an­
alyzing high­resolution fungal growth patterns, colony spread, or contamination detection,
making it a powerful tool for comprehensive image analysis.
LeViT­128S is a an efficient yet powerful solution for fungal image classification. Since
fungal colony datasets may require large­scale processing or even real­time analysis,
Advanced Fungal Identification, Leveraging Machine Learning Techniques and Image Analysis
27
==PAGE:36==
LeViT­128S provides a strong balance between computational efficiency and accuracy. It
effectively captures both fine­grained fungal details, such as spore texture, and broader
structural patterns, making it an excellent choice for applications requiring fast inference
with moderate computational resources.
DenseNet (Densely Connected Convolutional Network) enhances feature reuse by di­
rectly connecting each layer to all preceding layers, ensuring efficient gradient flow and
robust feature extraction. For fungi in petri dishes, this means the model can accurately
track subtle morphological changes over time, making it particularly useful for time­lapse
studies of fungal growth. Additionally, DenseNet requires fewer parameters than tradi­
tional CNNs, making it a computationally efficient option for high­accuracy classification.
VGG16, one of the earlier machine learning architectures, consists of stacked convolu­
tional layers that extract deep feature representations. Although it has a high number
of parameters, making it computationally demanding, it remains a viable choice for fungi
classification, particularly when using transfer learning on pre­trained weights. While it
may not be as efficient as newer models like ResNet or ViT, its straightforward design
makes it a reliable option for smaller datasets or baseline comparisons.
In order to determine the most effective model for fungal image classification, all the above
mentioned models are trained on the same number of datasets with the same parameters.

3.7.4
Dataset Split
The dataset is split using K­Fold for cross validation. The split ratio is of the initial dataset
80 percent is training and 20 percent is test. From the train split, 20 additional percent is
removed and used for validation.
It was chosen to use StratifiedShuffleSplit [27] as the type of cross validation split. Strat­
ifiedShuffleSplit ensures that the class distribution is even. This is crucial with datasets
that are imbalanced, such as the dataset this project is using. The dataset consists of
many different types of fungi, but also many duplicates. That results in some fungi having
a larger presence than others. Also some of the image sets contains more images than
28
Advanced Fungal Identification, Leveraging Machine Learning Techniques and Image Analysis
==PAGE:37==
others. Using this split ensures that each fold contains the same distribution of classes,
reducing the risk of bias in the individual folds.
3.7.5
Additional Feature addition
When it comes to additional features, the pretrained ViT­B/16 model needs to be updated
with additional layers in order to take the additional features into account. This is done
by modifying the classification head and leaving the backbone untouched. The model is
training on the image data and extracts embeddings. A separate branch processes the
additional features added to the dataset, in this case average color of the fungi converted
from hex to int64. This is then concatenated to the ViT­B/16 embeddings in an attempt to
create a richer representation with the combined visual and non­visual information.

Analysis and Discussions
Single plate images and multi plate images
The significance of using a dataset that incorporates two different approaches for machine
learning lies in the potential to enhance model performance and accuracy through a more
diverse data representation.
The first approach utilizes the original 3x2 petri dish image, which encompasses multiple
fungal specimens within a single frame.
This method allows for the analysis of inter­specimen relationships and growth patterns.
However, this approach introduce challenges related to resolution and detail, as the pres­
ence of multiple fungi/petridishes in one image can lead to reduced clarity for individual
species. To address these limitations, preprocessing techniques such as resizing and
augmentation are employed. Resizing standardizes the dimensions of the images, facili­
tating uniform input for machine learning models. Augmentation techniques, including ro­
tation, flipping, and cropping, introduce variability into the dataset, enhancing the model’s
ability to generalize and recognize different fungal species under various conditions. This
approach is particularly beneficial for training models on diverse growth scenarios, specif­
ically for the interconnection between the different growth mediums, and can potentially
help mitigate overfitting by exposing the model to a wider range of visual representations.
In contrast, the second approach involves cutting the original 3x2 image into individual
images, each representing a single petri dish. This method significantly increases the
resolution of the images, allowing for a more detailed examination of the morphologi­
cal features of each fungal specimen. By isolating each dish, the model can focus on
the unique characteristics of individual fungi, which is better for accurate identification,
especially in cases where species have subtle differences in morphology. The higher
resolution images provide more data for the model to learn from, potentially leading to im­
proved classification accuracy. This approach also reduces the risk of confusion arising
from overlapping features present in a single image.
Single plate images typically focus on a single specimen or fungal growth, allowing for
detailed analysis of specific characteristics such as morphology and color variations. In
contrast, multi­plate images encompass multiple specimens within a single frame, which
can introduce complexities in distinguishing between different species and assessing their
interactions.
The original multi­specimen images allow for the analysis of growth dynamics and interac­
tions between species, while the individual high­resolution images enable precise iden­
tification based on distinct morphological traits. By leveraging both strategies, we can

Analysis of the Strengths and Weaknesses of the Models Used
The models employed in this research, including CNNs and ViTs, exhibit distinct strengths
and weaknesses that impact their effectiveness in fungal identification.
Strengths:
1. High Accuracy: The models demonstrated impressive accuracy levels, particularly with
the ResNet and DenseNet architectures, which effectively minimized loss and maximized
classification accuracy across various datasets.
2. Feature Extraction: CNNs excel at automatically extracting relevant features from im­
ages, which is crucial for identifying subtle morphological differences in fungi. The use of
ViTs also contributed to enhanced feature extraction capabilities.
3. Adaptability: The models were able to adapt to different datasets, showcasing their
potential for generalization and robustness in diverse scenarios.
Weaknesses:
1. Underfitting and Overfitting: Some models, particularly VGG16, struggled with under­
fitting, indicating a lack of complexity to capture the nuances in the data. Conversely,
certain configurations showed signs of overfitting, where the model performed well on
training data but poorly on unseen data.
However through further training the the models, it appears to reduce the underfitting
presented in the afflicting models, most prominently the vision transformers. The growth
curve both trend towards convergence if allowed to train for longer.
2. Dependency on Preprocessing: The performance of the models was significantly influ­
enced by the preprocessing techniques applied. While effective preprocessing enhanced
accuracy, it also highlighted the model’s sensitivity to data quality and representation.
This also includes the images used to infer which class it belongs. Due to the dataset
being preprocessed to remove the background using a mask, images uploaded to the
API must also be preprocessed. This is however done on the API backend if the user
requests it.
Consideration of Challenges Encountered During the Research
Throughout the research, several challenges were encountered that necessitated adjust­
ments in coding and model development:
1. Data Quality and Variability: The initial datasets contained inconsistencies due to vari­
ations in lighting, background noise, and image quality. To address this, preprocessing
techniques were implemented, such as normalization, resizing, and background removal,
which improved the overall dataset quality and model performance.

2. Model Selection and Tuning: Selecting the appropriate model architecture was a chal­
lenge due to the diverse characteristics of the fungal images. A systematic evaluation of
multiple architectures was conducted, which involved tuning hyperparameters to optimize
performance. This iterative process allowed for the identification of the most effective con­
figurations for fungal identification.
3. Balancing Data Augmentation: While data augmentation was essential for enhancing
model robustness, it also posed the risk of generating unrealistic images that could mis­
lead the model during training. Careful consideration was given to the types and extent of
augmentations applied, ensuring that the integrity of the original images was maintained.
4. Computational Resources: The training of deep learning models on large datasets
required significant computational resources. Efficient coding practices and leveraging
DTU HPC solutions helped mitigate resource limitations, allowing for smoother training
processes. Restrictions to how long training is allowed on the DTU HPC clusters impacted
the results of the training. As mentioned, further training could improve the results even
further.
5. Consistent Masking strategies and hough circles: The first approach for masking im­
ages consisted of automatically computing the outline of every single petri dish, by utilizing
hough circle transform. This process produced a clear and accurate representation of the
petri dishes, allowing for precise segmentation of the areas of interest within each dish.
By applying a consistent masking strategies, it becomes easier to isolate individual dish
and analyze their results effectively. While this approach worked well for the vast majority
of the dataset, the method produced consistent errors when the growth stage was near
the end of the time frame. Here the issue lied in the ability for the hough circles algorithm
to accurately identify the circles. As the fungi grows and takes up the available space in
the petridish, the identification of circles becomes increasingly difficult, which eventually
leads to failure in circle detection and centroid placement which distorts the placed circle
to a suboptimal place, which is not suited for analysis.
Another approach is to compute the the segmentation mask for the very first image in the
sequence for a IBT strain, in order to ensure that the images is not cluttered with artifacts of
the fungi which can, hinder the accuracy of subsequent analyses. Then copying the seg­
mentation mask to all of the other iamges in the IBT sequence. However this introduced
an unexpected issue in the form of learned bias, that stemed for the specefic placment
of the mask. Effectivley this meant that the analysis and traning could become skewed,
as the model learns the placement of the mask rather that what the image depicts. This
unintended consequence highlights the importance of developing robust preprocessing
techniques that can adapt to variations in image content, ensuring that the segmentation
masks accurately represent the relevant features without introducing bias.

Conclussion
In this project, we developed an automated system for the identification of fungal species
utilizing advanced machine learning techniques. The primary findings highlight the effec­
tiveness of various preprocessing methods and the comparative performance of different
model architectures in achieving high accuracy in fungal identification.
The project revealed that preprocessing techniques, played a crucial role in enhancing
the quality of the input data. By systematically addressing issues such as variability in
lighting conditions and background noise, we significantly improved the robustness of the
dataset. The introduction of distinct datasets, such as DataSetUniform and DataSetSig,
allowed for a nuanced understanding of how specific preprocessing strategies impact
model performance. This led to the identification of optimal conditions for training, thereby
contributing to the overall success of the automated identification system. Additionally
splitting the petri dishes into separate images, resulted in even more robust and accurate
results, due to higher resolution of definitive features of the fungi.
Furthermore, the research demonstrated the strengths and weaknesses of various ma­
chine learning architectures, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vi­
sion Transformers (ViTs). Models like ResNet and DenseNet achieved impressive ac­
curacy metrics, showcasing their capability to extract meaningful features from complex
fungal images. However, challenges such as underfitting and overfitting were noted, em­
phasizing the importance of careful model selection and tuning.
Addressing the challenges posed by bias in different settings and datasets, the accuracy
of models can be increased by focusing on capturing the characteristics of featues press­
net in images to enchance the overall model performance and realibility. This approach
mitigates the risk of skewed training outcomes but also advances the quality of segmen­
tation tasks as well as paving the way for more effective identification and classification.
Looking ahead, several recommendations for future research directions emerge from this
study. First, further exploration of advanced preprocessing techniques could yield ad­
ditional improvements in data quality. Investigating alternative augmentation strategies
and incorporating unsupervised learning methods may enhance model robustness and
generalization.
In conclusion, through many different image analysis procedures and bias removal through
masking of background features not applicable to training of image recognition models,
robust models to classify images of fungi in petri dishes was established. Several datasets
was trained and compared with each other, determining the best approaches to bias re­
moval and preprocessing. Based on the training, Vision Transformers perform the best
across the board, with the single plate image dataset performing the best of all of the
