Abstract 
As of January the 1st 2015, short sea shipping companies operating in the so-called 
Sulphur Emission Control Areas (SECAs) are forced to meet a 0,1 % sulphur limit 
in their marine fuels. Different options to comply with the regulation are presented 
in this report.  
DFDS Seaways is one of the companies affected by the new regulation. They serve 
more than 15 routes across SECAs. The thesis presents all these routes and selects, 
on the basis of pre-determined criteria, six routes as the most representative ones 
for further analysis. 
The analysis of the DFDS representative routes and comparison with their possible 
alternative ones seek to identify the impacts that the new legislation is causing 
mainly in relation to fuel costs. The analysis is based on data kindly provided by 
DFDS Seaways.  
The analysis provides conclusions about the fuels cost for five different scenarios. 
In general, the drop in fuel prices since 2014 makes difficult to isolate the impact 
of the new sulphur legislation, as MGO price levels in 2015 are comparable to IFO 
price levels a year ago. Furthermore, the comparison of the fuel cost of the selected 
DFDS routes with the corresponding cost of their road alternatives shows that 
routes served by ships running on MGO find it harder to compete with trucks than 
those using scrubber equipped ships.  
In addition, exogenous events such as the crisis in the Russian economy which has 
resulted in decrease of traffic in some routes, have added to the difficulty of picking 
out the effect of the new SECA rules.   
So even though we believe that this thesis has shed some light on the issues that are 
relevant to evaluating the impact of the new sulphur legislation on RoRo shipping 
in Northern Europe, we also believe that additional data and a deeper analysis are 
necessary in order to be able to provide more concrete insights on the problem and 
on what can be done to alleviate it.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Motivation  
Transportation is responsible for the emissions of many air pollutants that harm the 
environment and health of many citizens around the world. 
Road transport has always been the main focus of concern and thus, it has been 
subjected to very strict limits in order to reduce the negative effects it causes to 
humans and nature. 
Historically, maritime transport has not suffered so many restrictions, since it has 
always been considered by many as the most environmentally friendly mean of 
transport, which is true in terms of greenhouse gases. However, in recent decades 
political bodies are increasingly aware of the negative effects sea transport has on 
the environment. In addition to CO2, sea transport mainly emits sulphur oxides 
(SOx), nitrogen oxides (NOx) and particles, so regulations on ship emissions are 
basically focused on these compounds. 
This project focuses on the new SOx legislation imposed by the International 
Maritime Organization and the European Union on some areas in Northern Europe. 
As of January 1st 2015, shipping companies operating in the North Sea, Baltic Sea 
and English Channel are not allowed to use marine fuel with a sulphur content 
exceeding 0, 1%. Those affected by the legislation are many. This thesis is focused 
by one particular sector, Ro-Ro shipping (1).  
The reasons for analysing the consequences that the new sulphur regulation has 
caused in the Ro-Ro short sea shipping industry are given below. 
Firstly, the 0,1 % sulphur limit on bunker fuels has brought much concern among 
shipping companies. The main concern is the need to purchase low-sulphur fuels, 
significantly more expensive than those permitted in the past, increasing 
considerably the total fuel costs.   
 
(1)Ro-Ro is the short name given to the term Roll-on/ Roll-off, which refers to the ships that 
are designed to carry wheeled cargo. Ro-Pax refers to Ro-Ro ships which can also transport 
passengers.     
11 
 
==PAGE:12==
Secondly, the legislation has caused serious concern on the possibility of modal 
back shift to road and rail. If shipping prices become too high, the industry will 
become uncompetitive with road and rail modes. Shippers might find these two 
modes more attractive.  
Thirdly, a limit of 0.5% will apply to all territorial waters of the European Union 
by January 2020 and of the rest of the world by 2025 at the latest. Analysing the 
current situation in Northern Europe can provide important conclusions about the 
impacts of the new legislation, which can be useful to shipping companies not 
affected yet.

1.2 Objectives 
The aim of this thesis it to analyse the current situation of Ro-Ro routes operating 
in the European areas affected by the IMO´s introduction of stricter sulphur limits. 
To do so, DFDS Seaways kindly agreed to be the partner company. They 
collaborate with the project providing data on the routes they serve and the vessels 
deployed 
DFDS Seaways1 is an international shipping and logistics company which currently 
operates 25 routes around Europe and owns 50 freight and passenger ships. Its Ro-
Ro route network provides services across the North Sea, the Baltic Sea, the English 
Channel and the Mediterranean Sea. Due to the fact that for the time being the 
Mediterranean Sea lies outside the designated areas where stricter sulphur 
emissions apply, it has been excluded from the scope of this thesis.The objective is 
to understand the impacts that the new environmental legislation has caused on the 
Ro-Ro shipping industry in Northern Europe. In order to ensure the complete 
understanding of the matter, the project is divided in the following targets: 
• Collection and description of information about the new legislation.   
• Description of the most important factors affecting modal choice. 
• Examination and evaluation of the data provided by DFDS, on the most 
important characteristics of the cargoes and services offered of each Ro-Ro 
route under examination. 
• Selection of the most representative Ro-Ro routes according to previously 
established criteria 
• Analysis of the representative Ro-Ro routes and their land-based 
alternatives. 
• Report the obtained results in a structured, comprehensive, brief, clear, 
critically concluding way, and in accordance with good practice for a 
written presentation.

1.3 Methodology 
In relation to potential impacts of the new regulation, the thesis draws heavily on 
previous studies. The same applies to the discussion of possible compliance 
measures that are available to the ship operators.  
A literature search is also used for identifying factors affecting the modal choices 
of shippers.  
The information concerning the DFDS routes and the compliance measures applied 
comes from the website of the company (http://freight.dfdsseaways.com/) and a 
number of interviews with high-level professionals of DFDS Seaways that the 
author has had during the 5-month period of project execution. 
The selection of the representative routes that exhibit the most interest for the 
analysis is made on the basis of criteria that include the geographical balance, the 
length of the sea route, the cargo volume, the commodity mix, the type of vessels 
deployed and the degree of competition that the routes are facing from alternative 
transport solutions. 
The fuel costs analysis done for the representative routes is based on fuel 
consumption figures that DFDS kindly provided, supplemented by information on 
the voyages realised and the relevant schedules. External sources are used for fuel 
prices and exchange rates. 
In order to capture the effect of the new regulation on the fuel costs, the current 
conditions (fuel qualities and quantities consumed during March 2015) are 
compared to those of 2014. In fact, two months are examined in 2014; one before 
the dramatic drop in fuel prices (June 2014) and one just before the enforcement of 
the new regulation (December 2014). Two additional hypothetical scenarios are 
examined; one concerning the current fuel mix in the case that prices had remained 
at the levels of last year and, one reflecting the case that DFDS had never opted for 
installing scrubbers.

In relation to the selection of alternative land-based routes, the web-based

1.4 Structure of the thesis 
The thesis is organised in eight chapters as follows:  
The next chapter sets the scene by presenting the results of the literature review 
performed. A brief description of DFDS Seaways and a short summary of the 
regulatory framework concerning the sulphur content of marine fuels provide the 
background information. Chapter 2 also discusses the available compliance 
measures with emphasis on switching to marine gas oil and the installation of 
scrubbers. The benefits and concerns associated with each solution, as reported in 
previous studies, are also discussed here. 
Chapter 3 is devoted to the main factors that affect the behaviour of the shippers in 
relation to selecting transport mode. The basic results of three European and one 
American study are presented. 
Chapter 4 presents the measures that DFDS has selected for meeting the regulatory 
limits and the effect that this has had on the freight rates through a modified fuel 
price surcharge scheme introduced by the company. 
All DFDS routes in the North Sea, the Baltic Sea and the English Channel are 
presented in Chapter 5. The information provided includes the services offered, the 
distance and time required for the crossing, the terminals used and the hinterland 
areas served, as well as the basic cargoes transported. 
Chapter 6 concerns the selection of six representative routes after presenting the 
selection criteria, summarizing the relevant results of previous studies and, 
discussing the prevailing conditions in relation to the competition that these services 
face. 
The selected routes are analysed in Chapter 7. A section on the methodology applied 
is followed by a separate section for each one of these routes. The results of the 
scenarios examined are presented and discussed together with estimates of the fuel 
cost of the main alternative land-based route. 
The conclusions of the thesis are presented in Chapter 8.

2.1 DFDS Seaways 
DFDS is one of northern Europe’s leading transportation networks. It was founded 
in 1866, when C. F. Tietgen merged the three biggest Danish steamship companies 
of the day. Since 1866, DFDS transports both freight and passengers in domestic 
and international services. The international activities of the company started in the 
North Sea and the Baltic Sea, followed later by the Mediterranean Sea. Although 
by the end of the 19th century routes were also introduced to the USA and South 
America, since 1983 the shipping operations of DFDS have been focused on 
northern Europe. 
Meanwhile, land-based transport and logistics services were also introduced, and in 
the 1960s the company started offering a door-to-door concept for freight. 
Through a number of acquisitions, the latest one being completed as recently as in 
July 2010, DFDS became the leading integrated shipping and logistics company in 
northern Europe. It consists of two divisions. The shipping division, DFDS 
Seaways, operates the DFDS network of 25 routes with 50 ships in the Baltic Sea, 
the North Sea, the English Channel, and the passenger business area. On the other 
hand DFDS Logistics, the group’s logistics division, provides transport and 
logistics solutions in most of Europe. 
2.2 Sulphur Regulation Policies 
This thesis focuses on the SOx emission limits imposed on some European sea 
areas: As of January 1st 2015, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) 
has imposed on ships sailing in the so-called Sulphur Emission Control Areas 
(SECAs) the use of marine fuel with maximum sulphur limit of 0.1%.  
2.2.1 IMO´s regulation  
IMO is a specialized agency of the United Nations, responsible for developing and 
implementing regulations that ensure the shipping industry operates effectively, 
efficiently and safely all over the world.  
International shipping is the main mode of transport in global trade, offering all 
kind of advantages compared to other transport options, and therefore it plays a very 
important role in the transport sector.  
18 
 
==PAGE:19==
IMO is aware of all these facts and hence, it works for setting the right standards 
and measures that lead to a sustainable maritime industry. 
IMO signed in 1973 the first International Convention for the Prevention of 
Pollution from Ships, known as the MARPOL Convention. Since then, MARPOL 
has been modified and enhanced in several occasions. 
So far, six different MARPOL Annexes have entered into force. Annex VI, 
introduced in 1997 by the so-called 1997 Protocol, refers to the prevention of air 
pollution. It covers all regulations related to sulphur oxides (SOx) emissions and 
nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions, among other air pollutants. Regulations on 
shipboard incineration and on emissions of ozone depleting substances (ODS) and 
volatile organic compounds (VOC) are also included in this Annex.  
In 2005, the 1997 Protocol entered into force. In 2008 an amendment to Annex VI 
was approved. Stricter limits for SOx, NOx and particulate matter were set and the 
Emission Control Areas (ECAs) were established.

2.2.2 EU´s regulation   
The European Commission is also concerned about the negative consequences air 
pollutants emitted from ships have on the European area. The first legislation about 
sulphur emissions from ships gas exhaust, Directive 1999/32/EC, was amended and 
became Directive 2005/33/EC. This latter Directive established the following limits 
and considerations: 
‘- 1.5% sulphur limit for fuels used by all ships in the SECAs of the Baltic 
Sea, from 11 August 2006, and the North Sea and English Channel, from 
either 11 August 2007 or 12 months after the entry into force of the IMO 
designation, whichever is the earlier; 
20 
 
==PAGE:21==
- 1.5% sulphur limit for fuels used by passenger vessels on regular services 
between EU ports, from 11 August 2006; and 
- 0.1% sulphur limit on fuel used by inland waterway vessels and by 
seagoing ships at berth in EU ports, from 1 January 2010 (the limit does 
not apply to ships due to be at berth for less than two hours according to 
published timetables). 
- Allows ships to use an approved emission abatement technology, provided 
that these ships continuously achieve emission reductions which are at least 
equivalent to those which would be achieved through the limits on sulphur 
in fuel specified in [the] Directive.’ 
Directive 2012/33/EC is the current legislation. All the limits included in IMO´s 
regulation are included in this Directive, but the European Commission forces all 
the Member States to meet the 0,5 % limit by January 2020, even if the outcome of 
the 2018 MARPOL´s review is to postpone the limit to 2025.  
2.2.3. The regulation consequences on short sea shipping 
Some analysts have shown their concern about the future of short sea shipping in 
SECAs from 2015 onwards. Notteboom T. (2011) lists some of these worries: “(a) 
a serious disruption of the commercial dynamics of shipping in the ECAs, (b) a 
considerable increase in vessel operating costs, (c) a lower competitiveness 
compared to other transport modes and (d) a modal ‘back shift’ from sea to road, 
which would contradict the objective of the European Commission of promoting the 
use of sea/short sea transport” 
Switching from sea to road transport can have serious consequences for both 
transport modes. More road traffic could lead to congestion and may increase the 
CO2 emissions in the overall supply chain. Short sea shipping companies would 
lose cargo volumes and profits. Customers might find more beneficial to purchase 
products from suppliers outside SECAs and even outside EU. 
Ship operators inside SECAs will face a competitive disadvantage against those 
operating outside the controlled areas due to significant fuel price differentials.

Transcontinental routes do not face such big challenges, as they are only required 
to meet the 0,1 % sulphur limit when they sail within SECAs. 
2.3 Available Solution: Marine Gas Oil (MGO) 
2.3.1 General considerations  
MGO is a pure distillate oil with sulphur content of less than 0.1%. MGO is 
discussed in most of the available literature as it seems to be a very realistic solution. 
The MIDNORDIC (2013) study qualifies MGO as the easiest solution.  In addition 
to the environmental benefits, Notteboom T. (2011) indicates that using MGO will 
reduce the need of engine maintenance.  
However, MGO is much more expensive than any other conventional marine fuel 
(HFO, IFO 380, IFO 1802), since MGO needs to be distilled and go through a 
desulphurization process. Moreover, some studies, such as the ECSA (2010), 
SKEMA (2010) and the Finland (2009) studies, expect MGO demand to increase 
from January 2015, when IMO´s limit comes into force in SECAs. This increase in 
MGO demand will make its price even higher. MGO´s high price means ship 
operators have to deal with increased fuel costs and fuel costs are an important 
component of total sea transport costs. 
The studies considered for this thesis evaluate the negative economic impacts that 
MGO has on total sea transport costs, analysing its past and possible future price. 
To fully understand how MGO price affects transport costs, it is necessary to clarify 
that bunker prices are determined by two factors: supply and demand level in the 
market for marine fuels and the crude oil price.

2.3.2 Impact of total transport cost increase 
Ship operators need to manage this extra transport costs. According to Notteboom 
T. (2011) and EMSA (2010), there are two possible ways to deal with the new 
situation: 
- 
Charge the customer, and therefore increase freight rates. EMSA 
(2010) points out some risks that this option entails. Firstly, the 
competitiveness of the short sea shipping industry deteriorates in 
comparison to land-based transport modes, mainly road. Secondly, the 
industries within SECAs will be penalized against those outside of it. These 
latter industries enjoy lower transport costs for their raw materials and 
products than their counterparts within SECAs.  
 
 
26 
 
==PAGE:27==
- 
Absorb the increased cost, and therefore reduce their own profit 
margins. Both papers notice the negative impacts of this decision. Short sea 
shipping attractiveness would be affected, as ship operators would have to 
offer older vessels for the service, and the innovation drive within the sector 
would be weakened. In fact, Theo Notteboom states “A more logical 
strategy for short sea operators is to charge their customers to recuperate 
the additional fuel costs linked to the use of low sulphur fuel” 
Before 2015, governments and organizations, through several studies, tried to 
foresee which routes and commodities would be more at risk assuming that the 
increased fuel costs would be charged to the customers. Those studies include the 
COMPASS (2010), ECSA (2010), Finland (2009), Sweden (2009), SKEMA (2010) 
and the German (2010) ones.  
The EMSA study summarizes the conclusions made by all these studies: 
● The most affected routes are the medium distance ones. 
● Low utilization routes face higher risks  
● Low value goods are the most affected commodities.  
2.4 Alternative Solutions  
MGO is not the only way to reduce sulphur emissions. Other options are 
investigated in the available literature. They include the installation of scrubbers 
and the use of LNG as marine fuel, as well as a number of other less commercialised 
alternative fuels. These options are briefly presented below. 
2.4.1 Abatement Technology: Scrubbers 
Using scrubbers to meet the stricter sulphur requirements is the option that ship 
operators can use to avoid switching to other marine fuels such as MGO or LNG.

every day, as scrubbing allows operators to keep burning HFO and meeting the new 
sulphur limit. Its performance to eliminate harmful emissions is more than effective 
as it is able to remove more than 95% of the sulphur emissions and 30-80% of the 
particle emissions (MIDNORDIC, 2013)

2.4.2 Liquefied natural gas (LNG) 
Natural gas is considered the most environmentally friendly fossil fuel. Methane is 
its basic component. Natural gas is normally found near oil sub terrain reservoirs, 
but it is also possible to find natural gas pools by themselves. 
29 
 
==PAGE:30==
Nowadays, natural gas is one of the most important energy sources. Its uses cover 
many sectors and industries, including electricity generation, heating generation 
and transportation. 
To be able to use natural gas as marine fuel it needs to be liquefied, so the fuel´s 
energy content is increased and the storage capacity requirements are limited. The 
process is basically cooling the gas to a temperature around -160º, so LNG is 
obtained. 
LNG fulfils IMO´s current requirements perfectly as its sulphur content is 0%. Even 
if sulphur limits get stricter, vessels with LNG propulsion systems onboard would 
still meet the regulation requirements. 
Sulphur oxides are not the only air pollutants that ship operators can get rid of by 
using LNG, particulate matter and NOx are also reduced significantly. Although 
less impressive, CO2 emissions reduction is estimated at 25% compared to bunker 
fuels (MIDNORDIC, 2013) 
For these reasons, from an environmental point of view, LNG seems to be a very 
good option. However, it is important to ensure that there is not any leakage of 
methane during the whole distribution process, as it is a very powerful GHG 
pollutant. Methane has 20 times more impact on global warming than CO2. 
(MIDNORDIC, 2013). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
30 
 
==PAGE:31==
From the economic point of view, LNG seems to be a good option. It is one of the 
cheapest available marine fuels. EMSA provides recent LNG prices and their 
comparison to HFO and MGO in the form of Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: Bunker fuel prices fluctuations between October 2006 and March 2009 (EMSA 2010) 
Installing LNG engines is possible on new and existing ships. The price for a new 
LNG vessel is higher than for a conventional one. MIDNORCID considers that the 
price for new LNG-powered ships increases by 10-15 %, while EMSA estimates 
that the extra cost is between €1- 8 Million, depending on the type of installation. 
EMSA mentions that this extra cost is expected to decrease as the market grows. 
According to the MIDNORDIC study, there are two possible ways to convert the 
engines of existing ships. Converting to a dual fuel engine enables switching 
between LNG and conventional fuel when running. This option is the most suitable 
one for sea routes where the LNG bunkering system is still not completely 
developed. In the cases where there are plenty of bunkering stations around, 
converting to a Lean burn gas engine, running only on LNG, is the recommendation. 
The EMSA study considers retrofitting as the last available option for existing 
ships.

studies conclude that new ships are more appropriate for running on LNG than 
existing ships.

2.4.3 Other alternatives: Biofuels, methanol, hydrogen and nuclear fuel 
The MIDNORDIC study presents some other alternatives that are found to be 
interesting, even though they involve fuels that either are not commercially 
available yet or have still not been tested on ships.  
Firstly, the study discusses the use of biofuels. From all types of existing biofuels, 
bio oil is the one presented in more detail. Bio oil has a sulphur content close to 
zero (< 0.05 %)   and it is the result of a fast pyrolysis process. This process uses 
dry biomass as feedstock, which is fast heated in the first place, to temperatures 
around 500 °C, and then fast cooled.  
Secondly, the study presents the use of methanol as a marine fuel. Methanol is 
sulphur-free and is expected to produce less particulate matter and NOx emissions 
than conventional fuel.  
Finally, the MIDNORDIC study briefly discusses the use of hydrogen and refers to 
some interesting projects in this area.  
In addition, the EMSA study mentions the interest of some industries and ship 
operators on researching and improving the use of nuclear technology for powering 
commercial ships.

2.5 Human and Environmental Impacts 
2.5.1 Benefits 
All these new regulations on sulphur content in marine fuels have led to many 
concerns about the continued competitiveness of the short sea shipping industry in 
the SECAs. However, it is important to remember that the new requirements just 
aim at improving the environmental and health conditions of nature and citizens, 
especially those placed in coastal areas. In fact, quantifications of other types of 
benefits can also be found in the relevant literature.  
The EMSA (2010) study reviews a number of papers that have analyzed 
quantitatively the benefits of the new regulation. Examples are the ENTEC (2009), 
AEA (2009), ECSA (2010), and the Sweden (2009) studies.  
In terms of the health results, special attention is given to the conclusions of the 
AEA study. It is mentioned that: 
” Based on its calculations the AEA study estimates that the net health benefits to 
society of the new rules (ranging from EUR 8 to 16 Billion) will be far greater in 
2015 than the costs of the new measures (estimated at EUR 3.7 Billion in the highest 
estimate). By 2020 the net health benefits will have increased to 10-23 Billion” 
(EMSA, 2010) 
Environmental benefits are equally impressive: “In the AEA study the reduction in 
acidification is expected at -25% in SECA areas while eutrophication is estimated 
to be reduced by -3% in 2015” (EMSA, 2010). 
Furthermore, the ENTEC (2009) study estimates that the new regulation will lead 
to savings of about £6.32 Million in relation to lower building and material damages 
(EMSA, 2010) 
 
 
34 
 
==PAGE:35==
2.5.2 Concerns 
It seems that if all ship operators meet MARPOL´s standards, the improvement of 
the air quality in the vicinity of ports will become a reality.  
However, there are also concerns about the negative consequences on the 
environment that the regulation might cause. All compliance options presented in 
Sections 2.3 and 2.4 lead to increased shipping costs. The MIDNORDIC study 
concludes that: “Most estimates show that this (the new regulation) will increase 
the total cost of sea transport by between 25-50%”. 
Road transport is the major competitor of short sea shipping. If maritime freight 
rates become too high, shippers will be inclined to switch to the road mode: “Some 
(sources) estimate that sea transports will decrease by between 10 and 21 % in 
2015 and rail transports will increase by 5-11 %. Some estimate that road 
transports will also increase by 5-6 %, causing more CO2 emissions, which would 
be a paradox” (MIDNORDIC, 2013).  
Moreover, if some short sea shipping routes are shut down, industries within 
SECAs might have to rely on suppliers outside SECAs (mainly Asia and America). 
This would mean that short routes will be substituted by transcontinental routes, 
which can increase both CO2 and SOx emissions in the supply chain. 
Finally, the MIDNORDIC study states that the lack of high sulphur fuel demand 
from the shipping sector will decrease its price, making this fuel very attractive to 
the electricity industry. Using high sulphur fuel for electricity generation entails 
bringing harmful emissions closer to cities and villages.

3. Factors Affecting Modal Choice 
Modal choice is a very important decision influenced by many different aspects:  
transport price, cargo requirements, infrastructure access, customer location, 
distance door-to-door, environmental conditions...   
Shippers need to evaluate carefully the advantages and disadvantages that every 
transport mode offers and make sure the chosen one meets their needs properly.   
This chapter tries to shed some light on the matter by analyzing different opinions 
from several studies, so that some conclusions about the most relevant factors can 
be made. 
3.1 The MIDNORDIC Study  
According to this study, shipping transport is the most suitable mode for bulky and 
heavy cargo: “For bulky and heavy cargo it is easier, more economical and also 
more environmentally friendly to use sea transport”  
Time plays a very important role for some types of cargo, especially perishable 
goods and very expensive cargo. The reason for the latter case relates to the time 
capital needs to be locked in the form of inventory cost. The MIDNORDIC study 
refers to the case of Boliden AB, which carries cooper by rail: “Rail transport is 
preferred because copper is too expensive to ship by sea since the value of a fully 
loaded ship would bind too much capital for a longer time”

that the company needed to address. Firstly, they had to ensure that the size and the 
age of the ships would not be a problem when installing the scrubbers. Secondly, 
they had to take into account the extra fuel consumption and the sludge treatment.

4.2. Fuel Price Surcharges  
As any other shipping company operating in SECAs, DFDS Seaways faces since 1 
January 2015 the important challenge of having to deal with the increased transport 
cost imposed by the stricter regulations. The two theoretically possible paths of 
action that companies may resort to have been mentioned in Section 2.2: either 
absorb the additional cost themselves or increase the price that customers have to 
pay for a service by increasing the freight rates. Given that the profit margins in the 
European shortsea shipping are thin, it is no surprise that important operators (Stena 
Line, Color Line P&O Ferries) have decided to pass on this additional cost to their 
customers. DFDS Seaways is no exception. Their way of increasing the freight rates 
is through introducing the so-called MGO BAF (Marine Gas Oil Bunker 
Adjustment Factor).  
Up until December 2014 and in order to cope with fuel oil price fluctuations, DFDS 
was using the Bunker Adjustment Factor (BAF) to calculate a fuel price related 
surcharge applicable on sea freight. The BAF was based on the difference of the 
average price of LFO (1% sulphur content) during the last month in relation to the 
previous month3 expressed in Euro through the average EUR/USD exchange rate 
during the period. Since January 2015, DFDS has adapted the system to reflect not 
only the price fluctuations but the change in fuel quality as well.  
The system works as follows:  
The January 2015 MGO BAF has been calculated “on the basis of the average price 
difference between 1% LFO measured from 20 October 2014 to 20 November 2014 
and 0,1% MGO measured from 20 November 2014 and 20 December 2014 adjusted 
to EURO based on average price of EUR/USD measured from 20 November 2014 
to 20 December 2014” (DFDS, 2014). 
 
 
3 In order to allow for sufficient time to calculate and publish the BAF, month-denominated 
figures do not refer to calendar months but to periods starting and ending on the 20th day of 
consecutive calendar months.  
48 
 
                                                          
==PAGE:49==
Due to a diminishing trend throughout the second half of 2014, the price differential 
between 1% LFO and 0.1% MGO was at the end of the year 185 €/metric tonne. 
Taking into consideration the total quantity of fuel oil consumed within a month 
and the corresponding capacity offered in the market, one can transform this figure 
into an average MGO BAF per lane meter. According to DFDS (2014), this 
preliminary DFDS Seaways MGO BAF was estimated at 2.7 €/lane meter. 
As a final step, this overall DFDS Seaways MGO BAF is differentiated per route 
in order to correct for the length of the route, the speed and the type of ships serving 
the route, which are all characteristics that affect the fuel consumption and, as such, 
the cost increase. This correction is effected through the so-called MGO Factor, 
which “is fixed for one calendar year and is based on the budgeted freight volumes 
and the budgeted fuel consumption”. The MGO Factors for each year are published 
on 22 December of the previous year and remain fixed for 12 months unless 
significant changes occur in the DFDS route network, e.g. acquisitions, 
opening/closing of routes or significant changes in capacities employed.

6. Selection Process 
The previous chapter presented important features of the services DFDS Seaways 
offers to their clients within the European SECAs. However, further analysis is 
required to fully understand how the new legislation is actually affecting the 
maritime freight. For practical reasons related to the scope and availability of 
resources for the present thesis, this deeper analysis cannot cover all DFDS routes. 
The aim of this chapter is to select among the DFDS routes of Chapter 5, those that 
adequately represent the company’s maritime network. This is not an easy task, as 
routes can differ from one another in many aspects.  
Different factors will be taken into consideration for the selection process:  
• a set of pre-determined criteria,  
• results of previous studies in relation to route characteristics that affect the 
impact of the new stricter limits on the sulphur content of marine fuels, and 
• feedback received from DFDS.  
At present, DFDS Seaways operates 9 routes in the North Sea, 5 routes in the Baltic 
Sea and 3 routes in the Cross Channel. The objective is to identify, among these 18 
routes, between 5 and 8 representative ones for further analysis in the next chapter.  
6.1 Criteria 
The first step in identifying a representative sample is to lay down criteria ensuring 
that the selected routes exhibit a range of features enabling the assessment of how 
sensitive the expected impacts are with regard to such factors. These pre-
determined criteria are listed below:  
• Geographical balance: There should be at least one route for each SECA 
(North Sea, Baltic Sea and Cross Channel), so that all geographical areas 
affected by the new regulation are examined.  
• Chain configuration: To the extent possible, there should be routes with 
short (less than 275 nm), medium (between 275 and 500 nm) and longer 
(above 500 nm) sea legs.  
67 
 
==PAGE:68==
• Volume: The selected routes should collectively cover a non-trivial fraction 
of the DFDS Ro-Ro traffic volume.

6.2 Results of Previous Studies 
The relevant conclusions drawn by earlier studies need also be considered. These 
studies were published between 2009 and 2011 and their results and conclusions 
about 2015 were just predictions, but then can still be very useful. For instance, as 
mentioned previously, EMSA (2010) summarizes all important findings of other 
studies [ECSA (2010), SKEMA (2010), COMPASS (2010), etc.] about the 
consequences of the new legislation. The most interesting conclusions are repeated 
below:  
• The larger the share of fuel cost is in the cost structure of a type of vessel, 
the harder the new legislation will affect it. COMPASS (2010) estimates the 
share of the fuel cost at 32% for Ro-Ro ships, whereas for small Ro-Pax and 
large Ro-Pax vessels the estimated shares are 10% and 22% respectively.  
• Middle to long journeys are more at risk of modal shift to road and rail. For 
those shipping companies that decide to switch to MGO, the smarter 
solution to complete the transport service would be to shift to journeys with 
shorter sea legs and longer land legs.  
72 
 
==PAGE:73==
• Routes crossing the English Channel will have more difficulties to compete 
with the combination of road and rail if the MGO price is around USD 1,000 
per ton.  
• Low value goods, such as metal and agricultural products, will face 
difficulties after the adoption of the new legislation.  The forest industry will 
be a matter of concern, too.  
6.3 Alternative Routes 
Modal shift to road and rail is one of the major concerns that different specialists 
have highlighted since 2009. The competition that shipping companies face with 
these modes is a factor that needs to be taken into consideration. Routes that provide 
such land-based alternatives should, therefore, be examined.  
In addition, other ship operators (Stena Line, Color Line, Cobelfret, Brittany 
Ferries…) offer Ro-Ro services across the North and the Baltic Seas. Competitors 
might offer the same connections or shorter sea routes than DFDS between the same 
countries. These competing alternatives should be examined.   
6.3.1 North Sea routes  
With the exception of the Eurotunnel, which has limited freight capacity, yet not 
insignificant, the UK can only be connected to the rest of Europe by sea. 
Nevertheless these routes, too, face competition from alternatives with shorter sea 
legs.  
Routes exhibiting increased risk potentially include:  
• Gothenburg – Immingham: From Gothenburg it is very easy to reach by 
road Germany and Netherlands and take a shorter Ro-Ro route to 
Immingham, like Cuxhaven – Immingham and Rotterdam – Immingham. 
Therefore, Gothenburg – Immingham might compete with other DFDS 
routes with shorter sea legs. 
• Gothenburg - Ghent:  The route competes mainly with the road mode. 
Trucks drive from Sweden to Belgium across Denmark, Germany and 
Netherlands. It is not necessary to take any marine route.  
73 
 
==PAGE:74==
• Cuxhaven – Immingham: Truckers can drive from Cuxhaven to Netherlands 
or France and sail across the sea through shorter sea leg routes. These kind 
of routes are offered by DFDS competitors but also by DFDS itself, so 
competition between DFDS routes might happen in this case as well.  
• Esbjerg – Immingham: Stena Line also offers this link, with the same 
frequency of sailings (6 departures / week in both directions)  
• Rosyth – Zeebrugge: A number of alternative routes with shorter sea leg 
distances exist within the DFDS network (Amsterdam-Newcastle, 
Rotterdam-Immingham, Rotterdam-Felixstowe, Dunkirk-Dover, Calais-
Dover) alone. Other operators like Brittany Ferries connect Northern France 
with Southern UK through several routes, while the Eurotunnel offers a pure 
road/rail alternative. 
• Rotterdam – Felixstowe: Stena Line offers two connections between 
Netherlands and UK, Hoek van Holland – Harwich and Rotterdam - 
Harwich. Hoek van Holland is only 30,6 km away from Rotterdam and 
Harwich is 52 km away from Felixstowe  
• Rotterdam – Immingham: Stena Line connects Killingholme (near 
Immingham) to Rotterdam and Hoek van Holland.   
• Copenhagen – Oslo: Color Line and Stena Line provide Ro-Ro services 
between Northern Denmark and Norway. Color Line offers two connections 
between Denmark and Norway, Hirtshals – Larvik and Hirtshals - 
Kristiansand. Larvik and Kristiansand are situated in Southern Norway and 
Hirtshals is one the northernmost cities in Denmark. The two routes have 
much shorter sea legs than Copenhagen – Oslo route, making Color Line a 
big competitor in this area, especially as far as Jutland is concerned. Stena 
Line provides services between Frederikshavn and Oslo. Its sea leg is not as 
short as those of Color Line, but still shorter than the DFDS connection 
examined here. Besides, motorways E20 and E6 offer a pure road alternative 
through the Øresund Bridge and Sweden.

6.3.2 Baltic Sea routes  
For geographical reasons, the routes Paldiski - Kapellskär and Klaipeda - 
Karlshamn appear unrivalled. On the contrary, the remaining Baltic Sea routes face 
competition from land based modes: 
• Klaipeda – Kiel: This competes strongly with the road mode. The easiest 
alternative is driving through A2 across Poland.  
• Klaipeda – Copenhagen – Fredericia: It is possible to reach Denmark from 
Lithuania, crossing Germany and Poland by road (either A2 or S8) 
• Kiel – St. Petersburg: There are two main road alternatives for this route. 
First, take A2 and cross Latvia, Lithuania and Poland. Second, cross Belarus 
and Poland, trough A2 and M20.  
6.3.3 Cross Channel routes 
All cross channel routes have one major competitor, the Eurotunnel. This 
infrastructure is a rail tunnel that connects Folkestone (United Kingdom) to Calais 
(northern France).  Shipping companies compete with a rail service that is available 
every day of the year around the clock. At peak times, the frequency of service can 
reach 6 departures per hour. The transit time between the truck checking-in and its 
arrival on the motorway on the other side of the Channel is 90 minutes.  
The Dover – Calais route competes directly with the Eurotunnel, as Calais on the 
French side is a common gateway, while Folkestone and Dover on the British side 
are just 14.5 km away.   
6.4 Representative Routes 
The first area to be examined is the North Sea. EMSA (2010) concludes that Ro-
Ro ships are more affected than Ro-Pax vessels by the new regulation, therefore the 
selections will be first focused on routes served by Ro-Ro ships. These include all 
routes in the DFDS North Sea network with the exception of Copenhagen-Oslo and 
Amsterdam-Newcastle, which are served by Ro-Pax ships. Given that routes 
connecting the continent to the UK will be analysed separately, we are left with the 
Gothenburg/ Brevik – Ghent.

7.1 Methodology  
7.1.1 Fuel cost of the selected routes 
The total monthly fuel bill of each route is the sum of the fuel costs of all vessels 
serving exclusively this route during the month examined. The fuel cost of a ship is 
calculated by multiplying the volume of fuel consumed during a month by the 
average market price of this fuel during the month. Given that the ships examined 
in this thesis operate exclusively within SECAs, there is no need to accommodate 
different fuel qualities.  
 
 
81 
 
==PAGE:82==

necessary information for calculating the fuel consumption of a ship is:  
(a) its speed-consumption curve for an average loading condition, and 
(b) its average speed during the month of the analysis.

7.1.2 Fuel cost of the alternative routes. 
The aim of calculating the fuel cost of the alternative routes is to draw conclusions 
about the fuel cost differential between DFDS routes and their alternatives which, 
together with other cost differentials, affect the behaviour of the shippers. The 
period considered for the calculations is March 2015. The alternative routes 
examined are obtained from the EcoTransIT World web-based tool 
(http://www.ecotransit.org/calculation.en.html). The tool suggests routes between 
any two points in the world. The default values of the model permit the calculation 
of energy consumption and air emissions associated with a route proposed by the 
system on the basis of vital information provided by the user which, in the case of 
the ‘standard’ input mode of the tool, is limited to the origin, destination and the 
preferred transport mode. The mode selected for this analysis is road. However, the 
‘extended’ input mode of the tool has been used for our application, as the user can 
now affect the route selected either by stating their preferences in relation to the use 
of ferries (in road transport) or by specifying intermediate locations along the 
preferred route. The EcoTransIT model also provides the distance of the alternative 
route to be examined. 
 
85 
 
==PAGE:86==
The objective is to calculate the total fuel cost of both route/mode combinations 
examined (the DFDS marine route and the road/sea alternative) and compare them.  
The total fuel cost of each sea route is calculated following the method explained 
in Section 7.1.1.  
In order to ensure meaningful comparisons, for each selected route the number of 
trucks on the road alternative will be determined by the capacity (in lane metres) of 
each ship employed on the route and its utilization. Calculations are based on a 
uniform load factor of 70%, which has been suggested by DFDS as a rough 
approximation of the industry-wide figure. 
As for trucks, EcoTransIT considers seven size categories ranging from <=3.5t to 
44-60t. Due to the trend towards longer heavy duty vehicles in the region covered 
by the study (in particular Sweden, Germany and The Netherlands), the second 
largest category of 26-40t has been selected as our average truck using Ro-Ro 
services.  
The ship space occupied by such a vehicle is estimated at 19 lane metres, while a 
figure of 26 metric tons has been assumed as its maximum payload. Under these 
assumptions, the number of trucks that a ship carries is obtained by dividing the 
70% of the ship’s lane metres by the 19 m required by the average truck. 
As with ships, the fuel cost of a truck is calculated by multiplying its fuel 
consumption by the diesel price in March 2015. The fuel consumption per truck is 
obtained from Eco Transit. It needs to be mentioned here that EcoTransIT calculates 
fuel consumption and emissions for two alternative system boundaries: ‘tank-to-
wheel’ and ‘well-to-wheel’ considerations5. Given that the figures provided by 
DFDS for their vessels concern solely the fuel purchases, the ‘tank-to-wheel’ 
perspective has been selected for the trucks in order to ensure comparability. On 
the other hand, diesel prices are obtained from the website indexmundi.com.  
5 The ’tank-to-wheel’ view restricts the analysis into the fuel consumed by the vehicle under 
examination, while the ‘well-to-wheel’ view adds on top the fuel consumed upstream for the 
extraction, production and distribution of the fuel itself. 
86 
 
                                                          
==PAGE:87==
The fuel cost of the road alternative to one ship trip (one dimensional voyage) is 
calculated by multiplying the fuel cost per truck by the number of trucks. The 
monthly fuel cost of the trucks carried by one particular ship is obtained by 
multiplying the fuel cost of the trucks carried in any given trip by the number of 
trips realized during the month. At the route level, the total fuel cost of the road 
alternative is the sum of the fuel cost of all trucks carried by all ships serving the 
route.  
Before moving on, it is necessary to clarify that for the purposes of this analysis the 
comparison described above concerns only two alternatives: the DFDS marine 
route and one road/sea alternative. In real life, no such restriction applies, as the 
shipper might have numerous transport logistics options depending on the 
geographical area of interest.

7.8 Conclusions 
From January 1st, Gothenburg – Ghent and Klaipeda – Kiel routes are served 
exclusively by retrofitted vessels. Their analysis helps to assess some of the 
advantages and disadvantages of installing scrubbers. Gothenburg – Immingham 
route differs from the Gothenburg – Ghent and Klaipeda – Kiel routes in very little. 
Gothenburg – Immingham is served mainly by retrofitted vessels, Ship 5 is the only 
MGO fuelled ship It is worth mentioning that declaring that installing scrubbers 
was the right decision needs the analysis of factors beyond fuel cost. The lost 
capacity in favour of the scrubbing equipment, the maintenance cost and sludge 
treatment cost should also be considered.  The methodology used for the 
calculations did consider the extra fuel consumption.   
The Gothenburg – Immingham and Gothenburg – Ghent routes offer the same 
transport services offered by the same ships in Scenarios 1a and 1b. Therefore, the 
cost difference between these two scenarios shows how the drop in prices affects 
considerably the fuel costs.  
In Scenario 1a all engines run on IFO 380, while in Scenario 2 main engines run on 
IFO 380 and auxiliary engines run MGO , with exception of Ship 5 in Gothenburg 
– Immingham route. Although the MGO price in Scenario 2 is $ 82,8 more 
expensive than IFO 380 in scenario 1a, the total fuel costs decrease in the three 
routes.  The reason is that main engines run on cheaper IFO 380 in Scenario 2.  
Scenario 3 shows the fuel cost if prices returned to more usual levels. As expected, 
fuel cost in scenario 3 is higher than scenario 2. If prices returned to last year levels, 
the total fuel cost would increase between 63% and 69%.  
The reason to include Scenario 4 is to provide input in assessing the financial 
performance of scrubbers. The results comparing scenario 2 and scenario 4 are as 
expected, scenario 4 shows higher costs. However, it difficult to make a strong 
statement about the issue,as scenario 2 does not consider many scrubbing costs.  
104 
 
==PAGE:105==
The maintenance and sludge treatment cost and the lost in cargo capacity could 
make costs in scenario 2 increase significantly.  
The conclusions for routes switching from IFO 380 to MGO are quite different, 
considering that there are no scrubbers involved in any of the scenarios 
Rosyth – Zeebrugge, Klaipeda – Karlshamn and Dover – Calais routes show equal 
fluctuations between scenarios. The fluctuations follow the prices variations.  The 
cost difference between scenarios 1a and 1b depend on IFO 380 price variation. 
The cost difference between scenarios 2, 3 and 4 depends exclusively on the MGO 
price variation. 
Regarding the analysis of the road alternatives, Gothenburg – Immingham, 
Gothenburg – Ghent and Klaipeda – Kiel show road fuel costs as twice as much as 
sea route costs, which is what was expected. 
Rosyth – Zeebrugge road alternative cost is cheaper than the sea cost. The reason 
lies on fuel type running the ship (increasing the sea route costs) and the small cargo 
capacity of the ship (reducing the trucks on the road alternative and consequently 
the road fuel costs) 
Routes served by retrofitted ships seem to compete with their road alternatives in 
better conditions than Rosyth – Zeebrugge.

8. Conclusion  
 MARPOL Annex VI and EU´s Directive 2012/33/EC caused a lot of concern 
among short sea shipping companies. The new environmental legislation requires 
shipping companies operating across SECAs to meet a 0,1% limit of the sulphur 
content of marine fuels.  The most evident consequence of the legislation is the 
increased fuel cost due to the need to switch to more expensive marine fuels meeting 
the stricter standards, and consequently the increased transport cost. 
Shippers consider transport cost a very important factor in many cases when 
deciding the transport mode. Short sea shipping competes mainly with land-based 
mode. If the legislation causes marine transport prices to increase significantly, a 
modal back shift from sea to road could occur.  
In order to comply with the legislation, DFDS has decided to follow a mixed 
strategy involving the installation of abatement technology in a number of ships and 
switching fuel from the IFO 380 used before 1st January 2015 to the much costlier 
MGO. The resulting cost overruns are passed on to their customers through a new 
BAF MGO policy.  
Fuel cost estimations of DFDS six routes serving across SECAs are made in order 
to analyse DFDS decision and evaluate some of the impacts that the legislation has 
caused to those routes. 
The analysis shows that the fuel expenses on the routes employing ships that use 
scrubbers are different than those on routes using MGO-fuelled ships. Routes using 
abatement technology have very similar fuel cost to last year figures. The reason 
lies on the fuel price drop and the necessity to run the auxiliary engines with MGO. 
Routes switching from IFO 380 to MGO increase their fuel cost quite more 
(approximately from 20 % to 45%). Analysing the drop in prices, it is clear that if 
prices were at March 2014 levels, the fuel cost would be much higher for all the 
routes examined.  
Analysing the alternative routes and comparing them to DFDS routes, it can be 
concluded that short sea shipping companies have more difficulties to compete with 
the road mode if the route is served by ships running on MGO.  
107 
 
==PAGE:108==
In general, the drop in fuel prices since 2014 makes difficult to isolate the impact 
of the new sulphur legislation, as MGO price levels in 2015 are comparable to IFO 
price levels a year ago. This was confirmed by the fact that many Scenario 2 results 
are quite similar to Scenario 1a results. In addition, exogenous events such as the 
crisis in the Russian economy which has resulted in decrease of traffic in some 
routes, have added to the difficulty of picking out the effect of the new SECA rules.   
So even though we believe that this thesis has shed some light on the issues that are 
relevant to evaluating the impact of the new sulphur legislation on RoRo shipping 
in Northern Europe, we also believe that additional data and a deeper analysis are 
necessary in order to be able to provide more concrete insights on the problem and 
on what can be done to alleviate it.
