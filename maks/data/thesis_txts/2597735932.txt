Abstract
This report aims to provide a set of approaches to addressing the growing macroplastic emissions
in global riverine environments. The state of microplastic pollution in riverine environments is
presented in general, but mostly in the context of the Saigon River environment and the Ho Chi
Minh City waste management systems. The project focuses speciﬁcally on interactions with
macroplastic in the 25-500mm range. The Cleanmatrix, the main concept presented, suggests
strategies on how macroplast could be extracted, in a compatible way with the existing activities
and requirements of the Saigon Riverine environment. Bubble curtain technology is one of the
central approaches of the Cleanmatrix concept. The use of bubble curtains enables e.g. the
existing Saigon River traﬃc to be unrestricted. The Cleanmatrix approach appears promising,
but has shown to be, as of the current iteration, insuﬃcient and further work to establish this
approach is needed.
keywords: Bubble curtain, Saigon River, macroplastic, riverine environment.

Introduction
1.1
Plastics
The revolutionary discovery of a completely synthetic substance in the beginning of the 20th
century, which we today recognize as modern plastic, gave mankind a unique material with a
very broad spectrum of properties (Andrady & Neal, 2009; Cressey, 2016; Emmerik & Schwarz,
2019; Lebreton et al., 2019; Lebreton & Andrady, 2019). Not only is it relatively lightweight and
durable, but it’s also quite inexpensive to produce, even in any desirable shape imaginable. It was
not until only recently plastic became a name for a category of materials called synthetic polymers
(Andrady & Neal, 2009; Emmerik & Schwarz, 2019).
Synthetic polymers are artiﬁcially produced substances mainly derived from hydrocarbons and
consist of long chains of macromolecules composed by multiple repeating subunits, often much
longer than natural polymers which originate from biological processes. It’s to little surprise that
synthetic polymers have been given a lot of popularity in the industrial sector, considering that it’s
one of very few known materials having such a versatile characteristic proﬁle and manufacturing
itself are no longer constrained by the limitations of nature (Andrady & Neal, 2009; Emmerik &
Schwarz, 2019).
1.2
Production and waste
Looking over the past century, the global annual plastic production has increased at a tremendous
speed from 2 million tonnes in 1950 to 381 million tonnes in 2015, that is an absolute annual
change by 379 million tonnes in just 65 years (Geyer et al., 2017). These numbers are today still at
alarming levels and are forecast to double by 2050, to near 800 million tonnes annually (Lebreton
et al., 2019).
Nowadays the largest fraction of plastic items produced enters the packaging sector and it out-
competes all the other branches by more than a double (Geyer et al., 2017). As a comparison,
only in 2015, 146 million tonnes of primary plastics were produced solely for packaging purposes
while the second (building and construction) and third (textiles) largest sectors together utilized
less plastic than packaging alone (ﬁgure 1) (Geyer et al., 2017).
Comparing the plastic waste generated in the same year by the two largest producing sectors,
packaging was responsible for 141 million tonnes, while building and construction had a much
smaller amount (ﬁgure 1) (Geyer et al., 2017). It’s quite clear that an item’s lifetime plays a vital
role when understanding the magnitude of plastic waste generated within each sector. Majority of
single-use items fall under the category of packaging, in fact, most of these items have a lifetime
no longer than 6 months. Whereas in building and construction the estimated mean lifetime is
around 35 years (Geyer et al., 2017).

Deﬁning plastics
All synthetic polymers do not share the same fate, some get discarded more frequently than others
and in order to get a better understanding of which kind of plastics ends up where, it’s important
to know their categorization by size and type of polymer (Eriksen et al., 2014; Jambeck et al.,
2015; Veiga et al., 2016; Geyer et al., 2017; Lebreton et al., 2018; Emmerik & Schwarz, 2019;
Chatterjee & Sharma, 2019).
Considering their size, plastic items can either be deﬁned as nanoplastics (<0.1m), microplastics
(0.1m - 5mm), mesoplastics (5mm - 2.5cm), macroplastics (2.5cm - 1m) or megaplastics (>1m).
The primary production of diﬀerent size classes are intentionally created for various end purposes
by the industrial sector, while the secondary production of items are not. Secondary production

1.4
Issues with plastics
Even though polymers play essential roles in everyday life, one of the main diﬃculties with plastic
is that it’s not biodegradable like natural polymers (Andrady & Neal, 2009). It takes several
decades, if not centuries, for a plastic item to degrade by natural processes which makes it highly
durable in nature, even after it has fulﬁlled its production purpose. The harmful eﬀects that they
pose (depending on size, shape and chemical composition) inevitably lead to large environmental
problems (Cressey, 2016; Lebreton et al., 2017; Boucher & Billard, 2019; Meijer et al., 2019).
Emmerik & Schwartz (2019) divides the main environmental eﬀects of plastic into ﬁve categories:
• Ingestion - Many animals lack the ability to diﬀerentiate plastic from prey, which after
ingestion then gives a false impression of being satiated or even reduces ingestion rates
as a result of plastic being trapped internally.
This might eventually lead to starvation
and a reduction of ﬁtness (Cressey, 2016; Veiga et al., 2016; Emmerik and Schwartz, 2019;
Chatterjee & Sharma, 2019).

1.5
Ocean environment
Between 70-80% of plastic pollution in our oceans arise from land-based sources (e.g.
waste
discarded directly into the environment), while the remaining 20-30% comes from marine sources
(e.g. abandoned ﬁshing boat equipment) (Macfadyen et al., 2009; NOAA, 2014; Cressey, 2016; Li
et al., 2016; Veiga et al., 2016; Lebreton et al., 2018).
The true contribution from each of both sources will vary by quite a lot depending on geographical
location, for e.g. many regions in Asia discards far more plastic than any other regions of the
world together (Jambeck et al., 2015; Lebreton et al., 2017; Emmerik et al., 2018; Lahens et al.,
2018; Boucher & Billard, 2019; Calcar & Emmerik, 2019; Emmerik et al., 2019; Meijer et al.,
2019). Some of the many reasons for this are poor waste management systems, increased plastic
production, intensiﬁed ﬁshing activity, large population densities in close connection to waterways
and within 50 km of coastline (Jambeck et al., 2015; Geyer et al., 2017).
Once the plastic enters the sea it either remains near the coastlines or gets distributed towards the
open ocean mainly by oceanic surface currents and prevailing winds. As plastic is highly buoyant
and remains at water surface level it can travel to remote locations and even accumulate in oceanic
gyres, with increasing concentrations at the centre of ocean basins (Eriksen et al., 2014; Veiga et
al., 2016). Although most of plastic debris originates from the northern hemisphere, some of it is
still found in the Southern oceans suggesting that plastic can travel very far from where they once
got discarded (Eriksen et al., 2014).
In 2013, it was estimated that approximately 269 thousand tonnes of plastic waste was ﬂoating in
surface waters across the world, including both macro- and microplastics (macroplastics dominate
the surface by mass, while microplastics dominate by particle count) (Eriksen et al., 2014). It’s a
relatively low estimation considering the global amount of more than 8 million tonnes of plastic
being discarded into the oceans annually (Eriksen et al., 2014; Jambeck et al., 2015). However,
this “missing amount” can be explained by:
• Imprecise measurement - Overestimate the amount of plastic waste we release into the
ocean or underestimate the amount of ﬂoating debris at the waterline (Lebreton et al., 2019).
• Fragmentation - The secondary production of plastic waste might get incorporated into
sediments or ingested by diﬀerent organisms (Woodall et al., 2014).
• Washed up debris - Plastic might also degrade slower in the ocean than on land and could
therefore re-enter the shorelines through time (Lebreton et al., 2019).

Not all plastic ﬂoats at the water surface and a considerable amount of plastic waste migrates

1.6
Riverine environment
One of the main inputs from which plastic can enter the ocean environment is through river
systems, these are highly polluting pathways as they bring large amounts of debris from further
inland towards coastal regions (Lebreton et al., 2017; Schmidt et al., 2017; Meijer et al., 2019).
This is highly troublesome since rivers are not only ecologically important, but they also act as
home for billions of people around the world (Lebreton et al., 2017; Schmidt et al., 2017; Meijer
et al., 2019).
The global estimated riverine amount entering the oceans from which exact rivers are still under
debate depending on the study. Lebreton et al. (2017) concluded that approximately 67% of the
global riverine output originates from 20 rivers, while Schmidt et al. (2017) found that 88-95% of
the global output comes from only 10 rivers. A more recent study by Meijer et al. (2019) estimated
that more than 1000 rivers are accountable for 80% of the global riverine output.
Nonetheless, still all rivers around the world do not account for the same fraction of waste entering

1.7
Presentation of issues to be solved
Plastic production has outpaced almost all other manufactured materials in the world and most
companies today are in fact highly dependent on plastic. If the production growth rate increases
any further for the next 30 years to come (also known as the “worst case scenario”), then by
2050-2060 we will have reached environmental life-threatening-levels of no return. It’s estimated
that approximately 12 billion tonnes of plastic waste will end up both on land and at sea (Geyer
et al., 2017; Lebreton & Andrady, 2019).
Even in the brightest of scenarios (if in 2020 plastic emissions around the world would completely
come to an end) the remaining eﬀect would still linger on for many years to come since plastic
waste discarded in the past would reappear as monstrous amounts of microplastics. To date, the
best outcome would still be to globally decrease the amount of plastic used and limit waste from
reaching the environment by disposing it properly (Lebreton et al., 2019; Lebreton & Andrady,
2019). Plastic items are as mentioned relatively cheap to produce, especially when not taking into
account the recycling process. The main challenge when recycling plastic lays in their chemical
composition. The diﬀerent kinds of additives and blends used for each type of item limits the
product for how many times it can be recycled back into the system (Brooks et al., 2018)
Although the majority of today’s research regarding plastic pollution is more aimed at the various
impacts of microplastics, there are still a lot of unanswered questions which involve macroplastics
(Boucher & Billard, 2019; Calcar & Emmerik 2019). Not only does macroplastics cause severe
direct problems for various wildlife, but it also poses a great risk towards human livelihood (Boucher
& Billard, 2019). As a large fraction of microplastic debris originates from the fragmentation of
macroplastics, more attention should be given in the retrieval of larger plastic items from our
waterways (Meijer et al., 2019). Due to the eﬀect of dilution distribution, extracting plastic debris
is also much harder to achieve once it reaches the open ocean. It would be possible to follow the
ocean currents to pinpoint the plastic pathways, but this would still not be enough because most of
plastics will already have entered the more deeper levels of the water column once it leaves a river
system (Reisser et al., 2015; Meijer et al., 2019; Royer & Deheyn, 2019). It would therefore be
more beneﬁcial to focus on the retrieval of plastic debris closer to the source, in this case diﬀerent
river systems.
One might argue that the amount of microplastics in the rivers are almost the same as the macro,
but there is a great technological challenge of dealing with particles within that size range in the
environment. E.g. detecting exactly where in the water column the particles are present and the
fouling eﬀect in a ﬁlter increases, especially if the water is already containing large concentrations
of organic materials (Royer & Deheyn, 2019).

Literary review
River focus evaluation This document outlines the process of ﬁnding, evaluating and evaluating
data, leading to choosing the Saigon river as the project’s environment focus. This document
includes process description, outlines of research on several river candidates and an evaluation
process description. Process description First the reference listing in the already found research
material was utilized and some central leads were found (Eriksen et al., 2014; Jambeck et al., 2015;
Geyer et al., 2017; Lebreton et al., 2017; ourworldindata - Plastic Pollution, 2018; Meijer et al.,
2019).
As there are 31 913 rivers around the world contributing to 100% of the amount of riverine plastic
waste entering the ocean (Meijer at el., 2019), it became of key importance to narrow this number
down by looking at a speciﬁc region. Asia was chosen, since it in 2010 was the region with the
highest fraction of global mismanaged plastic waste (MPW) and would still be estimated as the
most polluting region in 2025 (ﬁgure 4 & 5) (Jambeck et al., 2015).
To narrow the scope down even further, the most polluting countries (>5% of MPW) within this
region were chosen. In 2010: China (27.7%), Indonesia (10.1%), Philippines (5.92%) and Vietnam
(5.76%) (ﬁgure 6). These countries were further conﬁrmed as the right choices by looking at the
estimated predicted values in 2025: China (25.79%), Indonesia (10.73%), Philippines (7.37%) and
Vietnam (6.04%) (ﬁgure 7).
The combined number of rivers in these four countries was still too high to make a more detailed
individual river research (table 3). One more iteration was made of narrowing the number down
even further by choosing the three most polluting rivers for each country (table 4) (Meijer et al.,
2019; The Ocean Cleanup - Sources, 2020). Choosing only three rivers was a simpliﬁed and fast
decision since there were still too many rivers to consider which had a higher than average amount
of MPW entering the ocean (table 3).
These rivers were then researched (using ‘google scholar’, ‘DTU Findit’ and ‘elsevier Scopus’) and
scored 0-5 according to the amount of data found on each speciﬁc parameter for each river. The
speciﬁc parameters was based on previous research material (Eriksen et al., 2014; Jambeck et al.,
2015; Geyer et al., 2017; Lebreton et al., 2017; ourworldindata - Plastic Pollution, 2018; Meijer
et al., 2019; The Ocean Cleanup - Sources, 2020). These parameters highlight some important

2.2
The Saigon River
The project will investigate the Saigon river which is a highly complex riverine system and this
section will give an overview of the project environment, as well as its main parameters that will
later be considered as limitations during the conceptualization phase.
2.3
Vietnam
Vietnam is today facing many environmental issues, whereas one of these is water pollution by
plastic waste (Danh & Hoi, 2019; Huynh, 2019). Vietnam is also one of the ﬁfth largest countries
in the world generating plastic waste, reaching up to almost 6% of the annual global amount
(Jambeck et al., 2015; European Commission, 2018; Danh & Hoi, 2019; Thang, 2019; McCarthy,
2020).
This is considered a consequence from anthropogenic activities like urbanization and intensiﬁed
industrial development, but furthermost due to a poor waste management system (Thanh & Mat-
sui, 2011; Danh & Hoi, 2019; Huynh, 2019). In 2010, the country generated approximately 1.83
million tonnes of mismanaged plastic waste and 0.73 million tonnes was classiﬁed as marine debris
(Jambeck et al., 2015; European Commission, 2018; Thang, 2019; McCarthy, 2020). The most
common plastics found in the waterways of Vietnam are food containers, shopping bags and bottles
made out of plastic, such as polyethylene (PE) or polypropylene (PP) (Danh & Hoi, 2019).
Danh and Hoi (2019) explains that waste in general is still not properly disposed in most of the
cities, which suggests that mismanaged plastic waste will keep on rising within the near future.
They also noticed that there is a large gap of awareness amongst many Vietnamese people that
have yet to realize the grave danger behind plastic pollution in the marine environment and those
who believe that the situation is considered as very serious. According to Lebreton et al. (2019),
Vietnam will most likely be one of the main contributors of mismanaged plastic waste in 2060.

2.5
Main environmental parameters
Seasonal variability: The annual seasonal variability in Vietnam can be divided into one dry
season (December-April) and one wet season (May-November) (Strady et al., 2017; Duy et al.,
2018; Wan & Koontanakul- vong, 2018; Aygun et al., 2019). 90% of the annual rainfall occurs
during the rainy season, which means that the Saigon River gets an increased ﬂow rate between
54-85 m3/s during these months. Whereas during the dry season the water ﬂow is decreased to
around 20-22.5 m3/s (Strady et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2019; Strady et al., 2020). The large
amount of rainfall, decreased drainage eﬃciency and the city residing in a low elevation area (more
than 50% of HCMC is 1.5 m below sea level) creates annual ﬂooding disasters (Nguyen et al., 2016;
Givental, 2017; Duy et al., 2018; Thu et al., 2018; Wan & Koontanakulvong, 2018; Nguyen et al.,
2019).
Diurnal tidal ﬂuctuations: The river is mostly characterised by freshwater chemistry and
aﬀected by tidal physics such as, diurnal tidal ﬂuctuations and bidirectional ﬂow. The diurnal
tidal ﬂuctuations means that it experiences one high- and one low tide, and the water level can
change by 2 m on a single day. This phenomenon also causes bidirectional ﬂow which means that
during speciﬁc hours of the day, the water ﬂows downstream towards the ocean and once the tide
turns the ﬂow moves upstream again (Nguyen, 2013; Oya et al., 2015; Strady et al., 2017; Duy et
al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2020).
Riverine debris: Majority of solid waste that enters the Saigon River originates from industrial-,
agricultural- and human activities. Depending on the geographical location, approximately 60-
90% of the solid waste is traced back as organic (mostly vegetation e.g. water hyacinths), 0-26%
as plastic and the remaining fraction is categorized as other materials. As the SR is classiﬁed as
meandering, it’s still relatively unclear how seasonal variation aﬀects the riverine waste input. One
theory explains that trapped debris in shallower areas located further up the river gets released
into the main waterway once again during these events. This would suggest that more debris is

Waste management system in HCMC
This study will not include the waste management treatment (WMT) in HCMC after the plastic
extraction, as that area in itself would create a separate project of its own. However, this study
will take into consideration the current waste management system (WMS) acting in the SR today,
by becoming a part of it in such a sense that it will follow the limitations of handling waste in
this environment. This underlines a general understanding of how the WMS works in HCMC and
what kind of already existing solutions have already been implemented.
Based on the estimated numbers from Kieu-Le & Strady (2016), HCMC are today collecting
approximately 9800-10200 tonnes of waste per day and there is no modernized large-scale waste
collecting establishment that can properly handle these enormous amounts of daily waste inputs.
This estimated annual number will keep rising as the population density in HCMC increases.
The current public- and informal sector that collects the garbage have both infrastructural- and
technological weaknesses (e.g.
lacks proper facilities, vehicles and equipment).

remarkable amount of waste is daily introduced to the environment and thereafter transferred to

Bubble curtain (pneumatic barrier)
A bubble curtain, or pneumatic barrier, is a technique that produces bubbles in a deliberate
arrangement in the water column.
The system works in such a way that bubbles of air gets
released (normally close to the bottom) under the water surface and as the bubbles rise they
create a barrier, or curtain, which breaks the propagation of substances in the water column (“The
great bubble barrier”, 2019). The bubble curtain can be used to resurface plastic items which
have entered the water column and thereafter redirect them towards one designated area. Zaat
(2020) showed that entrapped air in plastics can positively increase its buoyancy and is therefore
an important factor when determining how plastic migrates in the water column. (ﬁgure: 14)

Approach and method
3.1
Project structure
As the project’s assumed tasks and deliverables were of high uncertainty in regards to time and
other resources needed, a traditional waterfall development method was avoided. Instead an agile
sprint cyclical approach was adapted. This so to make sure the project development could adapt

Sprint cycle phases
The development period was divided into 4 sprints, with an initial partial 5th sprint.
Each of the sprint cycles, in the development period, Consisted of a planning session and 5 steps:
0. Planning - Before a sprint begins a plan is made. The plan consists of an evaluation- and list

dedicated to planning must be kept to a minimum, normally about 1 workday. This is also a good
time to take a look at the backlog. The plan is lastly ﬁnalized in the project calendar.
1. Discover - A diverging phase focused on broadening the understanding of the research subject,
central to the given sprint. The goal being understanding the breadth of data available and the
quality, so to gauge a responsible scope of the given sprint and the associated tasks.
2. Problem deﬁnition - Within a deﬁned scope, a set of problems will be present. This phase
deals with deﬁning these problems and their origin. The goal here being to make sure the project
is focused on solving root problems and not just dealing with symptoms thereof.
3. Prioritize criteria - Determining what parameters and features, unveiled in the discover phase,
best address the problem deﬁnition. A selection of the most relevant parameters are compiled into
a development tool. The tool is in this project is structured in the form of a design speciﬁcation.
4. Conceptualization - The production of concepts to a ﬁdelity level that will facilitate eval-
uation and/or physical testing. The concepts are developed in accordance with the previously
prioritized criteria.
5.
Test and evaluation - This phase is utilized, in a metered fashion, to convey how well
developed concepts compare. This With the goal to produce an understanding of how well a given
solution and concept adhere to the criteria, and how well they address the overarching problems
deﬁnition.

Biocards and ideation
Biocards give a short but summarised description of underlying biological mechanism and func-

General concept layout and function
The solution presented utilizes redirection, concentration, separation and elevation to extract

sides of the river are similarly redirected by the secondary bubble curtains (BC2), and either be
redirected into the middle of the river for further redirection by the BC or directly into one of the
two APs. As the containers in the APs ﬁll they are replaced. The ﬁlled containers are transported
to the local waste management facility by boat.

4.1.1
Bubble curtain (BC)
The BC stretches diagonally between the two main bridge supports of the Thu Thiem bridge.
Creating a curtain of bubbles rising from low in the water column to the top. This with the goal
of redirection macroplast items to the surface and more importantly to the AP for collection. This
is achieved by sending compressed air through a high pressure tube, from the surface, down the
sides of the two bridge supports. The high pressure tubes are tethered to a steel rig, which in turn
is bolted into the concrete bridge support at a depth of 14 meter. The compressed air is then sent
from the steel rigs into perforated tubes stretched diagonally across the river and connected in the
middle. The most compelling reason for utilizing a BC in this section of the Saigon river, is due
to the BC’s ability to redirect macroplast items without interfering with river traﬃc.

4.1.3
Accumulation pool (AP)
The accumulation pool’s (AP) main function is to accumulate and capture plastic items (ﬁgure
28). The AP accumulates items from 2 directions. Plastic items redirected from the BC2 will ﬁrst
pass through the guard rail, given the item is smaller than 500 mm Ø, and through the ﬂow gates
entering the central accumulation pool. Items larger than 500 mm diameters will be redirected to
the middle of the river. This is aided by the 20 degree angle of the long side perpendicular to the
river. Alternatively when the tide is driving the river in the opposite direction, plastic items will
be redirected into the pool along the BC. When the river direction changes the one way ﬂow gates
opens and a motor lowers the ﬂip gate. So that only one sets of gates are open at any one time.
See ﬁgure 29 for clariﬁcation on the path of the plastic items into the accumulation pool.
As more items accumulate, they are pushed and concentrated around the conveyor belt. Here the
items are lifted out of the water and into a 40ft container pontoon. The container ﬁlling tarp helps
distribute the items along the whole length of the container. As more items enter the container the
ﬁlling tarp recedes until the container is full. (ﬁgure 30) On a periodically check and collect cycle,
the container pontoon is collected and replaced with assistance of a designated team working with
the local waste management company.
4.2
Concept detailing and argumentation
4.2.1
General project choice argumentation
Why focus the concept on utilizing bridges as anchor points?
Firstly it is assumed in the project that there is a general correlation between high urbanization
and high macro plastic pollution in bodies of waters adjacent. It is also assumed that collecting
this macro plastic is not a solution to the overall plastic pollution problem, but rather a way to
limit the symptoms thereof. This environment can then be argued to need a temporary system,
while a more permanent solution is developed. Furthermore, such as macroplast likely correlate
with urbanization so do bridges. From that trail of thought, one could argue that it could therefore
be beneﬁcial to utilize existing structures like bridges as anchor points for such a system.
Why focusing on the Thu Thiem bridge?
The focus on the Thu Theim bridge is in short mainly a choice derived from the fact that this
bridge was the environment with the most comprehensive data found during the scope of the
project environment research period.
How will it aﬀect the river traﬃc?
The choice of using a BC instead of a conventional buoy and curtain to redirect plastic debris is
mostly due to the limitation of minimising the impact on the river traﬃc. The middle section of
the river will not be aﬀected by the BC since it will operate at a depth of 14m. The BC2’s will
operate at a lower depth than the BC, but also at the shallower parts of the river so it will most
likely only aﬀect a portion of the smaller boats.

Safety measures
As a safety measure a controlled breakage system is implemented in case a heavy item e.g. a
sinking boat or general cargo is dropped onto the outstretched middle section. This safety device
is internally in the project called the safety switch. The function is achieved with an acrylic rod
in the middle of the BC, linking the two perforated tubes. The acrylic rod is made in such a way
that it will break when force slightly less than equal to the force needed to break the perforated
tubes is applied. Mounted alongside the acrylic rod is a magnetic sensor, and as the acrylic rod
is broken and the perforated tube separated, the magnetic sensor is activated. This will send a
signal to turn oﬀthe compressors and alert the maintenance crew. Installation
As the steel rig is designed to be installed at a 14 meter depth, a team of divers are needed to bolt
the steel rig to the concrete surface of the bridge support, and connecting the high pressure tube
with the perforated tube and tether them to the rig itself. This action on both sides of the bridge.
Lastly, the diver team will also be needed to connect the two ends of the perforated tubes, for the
installation of the safety switch.
Air compression
Two industrial grade ~0.5 KW air compressors are needed to deliver the air for the system. One
for each side of the bridge. Each compressor will need to deliver compressed air for half the BC
and two secondary BCs (one at a time). The compressors will be mounted to the bridge support in
a water sealed case. As the air quality in the greater SR area is highly traﬃcked a ﬁlter is needed
on the intake of the air compressor so as not to pollute the river.
Maintenance and fouling
It is expected that the tube system will in turn have a reduction in eﬀectiveness, as fouling will
in time impair the system. It is assumed fouling will consist of but will be limited to sediment
and water entering the tubes,. It can also be argued that there is a possibility of algae growth
externally and internally at the tube system, due to the high oxygen attractive. As the tubes are
of relative low cost, the tubes will periodically be replaced and washed for reuse, or recycled if not

Wildlife disturbance
On the subject of wildlife interference or disturbance it seems like there is some conﬂicting data.
This makes the eﬀects unclear of introducing the sound of bubbles released from the BC and the
additional oxygen injected into the Saigon river environment. To still include safe passages for any
potential wildlife in the SR, the BC’s are not anchored directly to the bottom of the river, hence
allowing the organisms to pass either through the bubbles or beneath the BC’s.
Why is the BC implemented at a depth of 14 meters?
As the general river ﬂoor is around at its deepest ~16 meters and ships traversing under the Thu
Thiem bridge have a maximum draught of ~12 meters, 14 meter can be argued to be the safest
depth the BC placement.
Why is the BC not just laying on the river ﬂoor?
As the river ﬂoor is uneven and volatile, consistent operation can not be expected in the BC way
to function is such an environment. Similarly, one could argue for the possibility of having the BC
ﬂoat but then have anchors to maintain a consistent position. But again due to the volatile nature
of the Saigon river ﬂoor consistent operation can not be expected.
Why does the BC not have valves, for e.g. reducing internal fouling?
The cost of utilizing even simple one way plastic valves would be more costly than changing the
Perforated tube in itself. As the purpose of the addition of valves would be to increase the lifespan
of the perforated tube, the relative high cost would make the addition of valves counterproductive.
Expected forces on the BC
As the SR is a relatively slow river (0.0125 m/s) and the surface of the BC is below 20 square
meters the forces applied to the BC are nearly negligible (about 1.6 N).
Fd = 1/2 ∗998kg/m3 ∗0.01252m/s ∗20m2 ∗1.05 = 1.6N
How will it diverge larger items?
The bubble curtain will redirect most plastic debris in the range, but other large items (e.g. logs or
large industrial debris) will simply just pass through it. The AP’s are also designed in such a way
that the guard rails are only allowing items below 500 mm Ø to enter the pool. A large fraction of
the debris in the river is counted as organic material (water hyacinths), which this solution has not
been able to take into consideration more than the assumption that heavier debris will go through
the BC’s. But in case the organic material (e.g. water hyacinths) reaches 100 mm above the water
surface then it will not be able to enter the AP’s and will simply just get redirected, while smaller
organic elements will still be extracted together with the plastic debris.

Saigon river.

Discussion
It is already understood that the Cleanmatrix approach is not solving the main problem of plastic
items entering the ocean, but instead act as a potential mechanical solution towards the removal
of a large fraction of riverine plastic debris from HCMC before it enters the open ocean. The
assumed weaknesses of working in this kind of area mostly lay with the already existing WMS
and WMT. In regards to further work in this area, it is suggested that more work is put into the
tracking of plastic around the greater HCMC area. Having an understanding of where, why and
when plastic is or can be located would open up for more direct and eﬀective solutions to the core
problem of insuﬃcient plastic management.
The SR is unsurprisingly a complex environment, but at the end of this project a set of general
assumptions have been found to be consistently important, when working towards interacting
with the SR environment. Open pathways for traﬃc is unnegotiable, organic material is generally
the most abundant fraction of the river composition apart from the water, solutions need to be
relatively cheap to build and install and lastly having an understanding of the PPC’s opinions and
doctrines are essential.
Although the Cleanmatrix might not be able to be implemented as it is on the SR, the Cleanmatrix
might still be able to be implemented on and around bridges in other environments.
Looking at all of the parameters that have been taken into consideration in this project, it is very
likely that a future iteration of the Cleanmatix will be able to work in this speciﬁc environment.
It is still not completely clear how this would operate in less dynamic environments, but according
to other companies similar solutions are looking promising.
Considering the large amount of plastic entering the river, a single collection point for the whole
of the SR is unsustainable. The project research suggests there should be several collecting points,
where the plastic can get extracted from, especially around the densely populated- and industrial
areas where the plastic input is predicted to be considerably higher. Furthermore, having only one
extraction area could speciﬁcally cause diﬃculties with the rate needed for emptying the container
pontoons, and plastic further up the river might get trapped before it even reaches the extraction
point.
The project’s work with the utilization options with bubble curtains have suggested that this
technology has high potential in regards to manipulation of macroplastic in dynamic river systems
and simultaneously a low impact on the human activities in the same riverine environment. Bubble
curtains seem a speciﬁcally good choice for waterways with high traﬃc. In comparison to more
traditional approaches, like the buoy and curtain, bubble curtains seem to have a more cost eﬀective
approach. Most importantly the bubble curtain as an approach needs more testing.
The project’s ﬁndings suggest that the bubble curtain speciﬁcally needs further testing in regards
to its redirection eﬃciency, plastic item elevation and long term use complications.

Project outline 
Introduction 
For the past decades, plastic pollution has become one of the most researched areas in 
regards to global environmental issues. The urgency of the problem has also become more 
increasingly apparent to the public, therefore the idea of both extracting and recycling 
discarded plastics is a viable solution when limiting the amount of plastic reaching the 
environment, such as the open oceans. A large fraction of the plastic that enters the oceans 
are originating from multiple sources in Asia and it is believed that land based debris 
escapes through rivers and thereafter enters the ocean. 
 
The aim of this project is to create a solution for the extraction of riverine macroplastics 
(25-450 mm), which will be powered by the river itself. Our concept will be anchored to 
bridges (figure 1) and is divided into three different parts.  
 
 
Figure 1:​ Illustration of the entire solution connected to a bridge. Redirecting bubble 
curtains, accumulation swirls and extraction screws (water flow shown as blue arrows and 
debris shown as red arrows). 
 
What is the solution 
1.
Redirection. 
Plastic debris in the water column gets resurfaced and redirected by the use of 
something called a bubble-curtain. The bubble-curtains are created by compressed 
air which escapes through submerged tubes. The air bubbles entrap plastic particles 
1 
Appendix A
==PAGE:84==
 
and make them more buoyant while moving them towards one designated point 
(figure 2). The benefits of using air bubbles are the low levels of disturbance towards 
the riverine traffic (e.g. boats), as well as causing very small negative environmental 
impacts.  
 
 
Figure 2:​ Illustration of a bubble-curtain. (A) Main principle using bubbles for resurfacing and 
redirecting debris. (B) Bubble-curtain applied in an aquatic system. (C) Surface redirection of 
debris together with the main water flow (blue arrows) passing through the curtain. 
 
2.
Accumulation. 
Once the particles have been guided towards one specific point of the river, in our 
case a submerged pool that is connected to the bridge supports, a swirl will 
accumulate the debris in its center. The accumulation swirl will also have floating 
extendable parts that are attached to the bridge supports on the riverside. These will 
not only act as redirection units for the debris that are outside of the main river traffic 
lane but also diverge some of the water flux, hence creating the swirl inside the 
submerged pool (figure 3). 
 
2 
==PAGE:85==
 
 
Figure 3:​ Illustration of an accumulation swirl. (A) Main principle of perpendicularly 
redirecting a portion of the water flux to the main flow, thus creating a swirl. (B) Accumulation 
swirl applied in an aquatic system (seen from above) and redirection of debris. (C) 
Accumulation of debris inside the pool (seen from the side). 
 
3.
Extraction. 
The extraction screw (works similar to an Archimedean screw) will be placed in the 
center of the pool and remove plastic particles by its large openings. The entire 
structure will work almost like a turbine and the extended blades will harness the 
momentum created by the water flux itself in order to spin. For each rotation, a 
portion of the accumulated plastic migrates further up the helix until it eventually exits 
at the very end where recycling containers reside (figure 4). Our initial estimate is that 
the screw will need to be around 4 meters long and with a diabetes of 1.5 meter to be 
effective. 
 
 
Figure 4:​ Illustration of an extraction screw. (A) Main principle of an extraction screw 
elevating debris. (B) Extraction screw applied in an aquatic system where the water flow 
3 
==PAGE:86==
 
causes rotations and thereby elevates debris into a recycling bin. River water will exit the 
screw through small holes in the bottom part of the screw (C) Extraction screw applied inside 
an accumulation pool. 
 
The concept is designed to be implemented in a wide selection of high pollution riverine 
environments. This is not a long term solution, but instead a semi-permanent system. 
Therefore the system relies on being attached to a bridge foundation for full functionality 
(figure 1 and 4). It is expected that this system will be active for a maximum of 10 years in a 
given riverine environment, as the system at its core only acts on symptoms of a greater 
issue. 
 
Since a large amount of plastic migrates through river pathways in Asia, we investigated the 
Saigon river (Vietnam) to get a better understanding of what kind of environmental variables 
our solution needs to take into consideration. This under the assumption that real 
environment data is vital for a real solution (e.g. tidal dynamics, seasonal variability and the 
intensity of river traffic). It is internally understood that a greater cross section of river 
environment data would be the most effective tool for development, but this is not plausible 
for this project’s scope. 
 
The recycling process is not a part of this project. We are instead aiming to connect our 
system to already existing recycling systems.  
 
Riverine environment parameters 
As a part of our research we have compiled a set of parameters, that on a metered level 
describing the riverine environment (e.g. river temperature, river flux, etc). A subset of these 
parameters together with a set of design conventions (e.g. safety regulation, river area 
occupied. etc.) have been compiled into a design specification. In this project the design 
specification is used to evaluate how effective a concept is on a macro level. see design 
specification in appendix A

Appendix E 
Interview with Tim van Emmerik 
A summary of the interview conducted with Tim van Emmerik on the 2nd of April. The goal of 
the interview was primarily to attain a better understanding of the environment in HCMC, the 
Saigon River and also the main parameters to take into consideration when creating a 
riverine plastic extraction solution. Tim van Emmerik has done multiple projects regarding 
plastic pollution in different parts of the world, including the Saigon River and more 
specifically in HCMC. Addressing our questions with Tim van Ememrik, regarding the 
parameters and the project in general, would give useful information on which design 
solution this project should aim at and which part of the river ought to be targeted. 
Interview process 
The interview was conducted via skype, on the 2nd of April  at 11.00 and lasted half an hour. 
The interview commenced with a brief introduction of the project participants and their 
academic background and an outline of the project’s progress so far. Next, a questions and 
answers session that leads to open conversation towards the end. 
 
Tim van Emmerik confirmed that further contact is encouraged if needed. 
Interview highlights 
Tim van Emmerik explained that the Saigon River is a highly complex system, with various 
parameters affecting the plastic concentration, water column mixing and also the amount of 
limitations the solution would receive in this specific river. When asked about his overall view 
of plastic pollution in HCMC and in the Saigon River, the main considerations should be at: 
 
-
How does the diurnal tidal dynamics work in this environment, hence how can our 
solution adapt to changes in the river direction. The tidal dynamics are most likely the 
main parameter affecting the amount of plastic in the river. Seasonal variation might 
not necessarily be the largest contributor affecting the plastic concentration in the 
river, but it should still be included. 
 
-
How would a large abundance of water hyacinths, which grows ​en masse​, limit the 
extraction of plastics since the amount of vegetation in the river is larger than that of 
plastic. 
 
-
The air entrapment in the plastic debris, since it plays a vital role when determining 
how much plastic is actually in the river and a large amount might be reintroduced to 
the surface. It is also important to have a good understanding of how the plastic 
behaves in the water column (such as mixing), thus understanding its characteristics. 
Not all plastics are structurally the same, hence their properties are most likely 
different from one and another. 
 
1 
==PAGE:107==
Appendix E 
-
The river contour. It is also important, since this will change not only the waterflow, 
but also the plastic concentration because some debris might actually get trapped in 
different parts of the river.  
 
-
The river traffic. The Saigon River is heavily navigated and some areas are always 
occupied by cargo and raw material shipping. Since some areas of the river receive a 
higher degree of traffic than others, the solution should not, or at least have a low as 
possible interference with the river traffic.

Concept 1 
Key words: ​Smart-net, passive filtration, sorting belt, reusable energy 
 
The idea behind the first concept was to create an automatic sorting solution which can 
move towards different parts of the river and change the nets placement. 
 
This concept is a boat carrying a ‘smart-net’ (net connected to floating appendages that can 
move in all three directions) in the rear, which will move according to the river-traffic and 
seasonal variation. This will entrap macroplastics at both the surface and 0.5m below, using 
the natural flow of the river and therefore create a passive filtration unit. A sorting belt will 
then remove the accumulated plastics around the centre of the net and sort each plastic item 
according to size into large bins. The boat will use the natural flow of the river to generate 
reusable energy from a turbine inside the sorting belt, as well as solar panels mounted on

Concept 2 
Key words: ​Sections, saloon gates, ram-filtration, cross-filtration 
 
This concept is based on the idea of adding different sorting sections of the solution to 
create a self-managing river-width filtration unit. 
 
The unit is anchored (both in the seabed and riverbanks) at one part of the river and extracts 
plastics from almost the entire river-width. Depending on the size of the river, the structure is 
made out of three sections (1a-3a) and the triangular-shape will direct the plastic debris 
towards one end of the river (1a, closest to the waste management site) instead of 
accumulating at different spots. The 3a-section will have an open passage that enables 
river-vehicles to travel through “saloon-doors” from one side to the other, this section will 
also have the flow-through groove. The natural water flow taken up by the flow-through 
groove will create a cross-filtration system on the two macrofiltration grooves (1 and 2) and 
push the debris over towards the waste management site connected to the 1a-section. All 
sections connected with the filtration grooves will be submerged approximately 0.5m to 
extract plastic in the water column (this depth can change depending on the force of the 
river, e.g. during seasonal variation) just like normal ram-filtration. The first groove will target 
plastic smaller than 40mm and the second groove will target items larger than 40mm.

Concept 3 
Key words: ​local, primitive, low cost, non industrial

Spiral Lift
Key words:
passive operation, Archimedes lift, river driven, simple
The central goal with this concept is to achieve mostly passive operation and minimal external
power input. This by limiting the function complexity, for minimal human intervention in the
form of operational assistance and/or maintenance.
This concept consists of 4 steps. 1) redirection, 2) concentrate, 3) separation and lift , 4) depositing.
First the plastic items in the central lane (the traﬃc lane) under the bridge are redirected with
bubble curtains.
The items are redirected to both sides of the bridge, behind the two main
pillars.The remaining items not in the traﬃc lane are redirected with a buoy and curtain layout.
The buoy and curtain layout also redirect the items to the concentration zone. As the concentration
zone increased plastic item density, according to river ﬂow, the “spiral lifts” lifts the items out of
the water by skimming the river surface there. The surface water with the plastic items travels
up the spiral lift as it turns. while the surface water is ﬁltered out along holes in the bottom half
other the spiral tubes. As the items reach the top of the lift, the plastic items are deposited into
waste bins on boats ready to ship the items to a waste management facility.The spiral lift is mainly
made of hollow plastic shapes for buoyancy. The spiral lift is driven by 4 large ﬁns. This design
of the Spiral lift only functions in one river ﬂow direction at a time, as the spiral direction of the
tube spiral determines the eﬀective rotations, and each design can only spiral in one direction at a
time. Therefore mirrored spiral lifts are deployed to accommodate both tidal river ﬂux directions.

Appendix J 1 
Utilization of bubble curtains in the Saigon river 
- a limited technical test of concepts 
1.
Introduction 
The objective of this experiment is to develop augmentations for bubble curtains (BC) effect 
on macroplastic items in a dynamic riverine environment. More specifically the experiment 
will try to illuminate the ability of a BC to not just repel macro plast items, but to redirect 
macro plastic items in a given direction. Lastly, the experiment will be used to argue for the 
validity use of the BC in the section of the Saigon river at the Thu thiem bridge. The velocity 
of the river and other factors are e.g. tuned to simulate this environment.  
It is hypothesized:  
1) As a floating macro plastic item increases proximity to a bubble curtain, the bubble 
curtain will apply an increasing force to the item away from the bubble curtain. 
2) As a floating macro plastic item is moving towards a bubble curtain, an increasing 
angle of the bubble curtain will increase the bubble curtain's ability to redirect the 
item from the most protruding point to the least.  
The argumentation for the hypothesis (1) and (2) hinges on the existing uses of BCs. The 
company CanadianPond.ca had working concepts for movement and redirection of seaweed 
in coastal regions in 2016 (​CanadianPond 2016​), and the company The Great Bubble 
Barrier’s 2019 pilot project demonstrated a BC’s capabilities regarding redirection and 
catchment of plastic debris (​Great Bubble Barrier 2019​). The function of the BC is in general 
shown to have an effect that aligns with the intended purpose in some environments. This 
study will look at the function in an environment with river velocities comparable to the 
Saigon river environment.  
2.
Methods and Materials 
The experiment environment was conducted in a 500 x 500 x 1000 mm transparent glass 
tank. The tank was filled to a 450 mm height effectively making the dimensions of the body 
of water 500 wide, 450 tall and 1000 long. On the side of the tank a measuring tape was 
attached (figure 1). To simulate the BC a section of tubing was attached to an aluminum rod 
as a handle (figure 2).  
 
 
==PAGE:133==
 
 
Figure 1: ​test water tank. 500mm x 500mm x 1000mm 
Hypothesis (1) was tested by submerging the BC simulator into the tank and pushing it along 
the bottom of the tank. The BC simulator was attached to an air compressor that delivered 
the compressed air so a bubble curtain could be formed. As the BC simulator was pushed 
along the tank floor from one side of the tank to the other, a 330 ml PET bottle with 150 ml 
water was placed in front of the BC simulator. The effect of the BC on the PET bottle was 
then recorded (see figure 3). 30 passes were performed. 
Alternatively to moving the water in the tank towards the BC, in this experiment the BC is 
moved in the water instead as a practical measure. As the relational velocity between the 
water and the BC simulator is the relative important factor, this inversion is assumed 
acceptable.  
The BC simulator is to be moved at such a rate so to simulate the saigon rivers velocity. The 
Saigon river has been recorded to be at its highest flow 85 m​3​/s (see summary). Given an 
estimate of a river cross section of 6800 square meters at the Thu Thiem bridge, the 
volumetric flow rate converted to velocity is 0.0125 meters per second. (see calculation in 
figure 5).  
The BC simulator is connected to an air compressor with a silicone tube. The air compressor 
is set to deliver just enough pressure so to let out a uniform curtain of bubbles. The exact 
pressure could not be recorded for practical reasons. 
Hypothesis (2) was tested in a similar fashion to hypothesis (1). Instead of the BC curtain 
being straight while being pushed along the tank floor an increasing angle was introduced. 
(figure 4). Staring at a 0 degrees angle 15 passes was made. Subsequently, the angle was 
increased with 10 degrees after each 15th pass. This continued until a given angle would 
consistently and predictably redirect the PET bottle. When the PET bottle is consistently 
redirected the test conclutes. The interaction of the PET bottle and the BC simulator is 
recorded at the beginning of each reset and pass.

Conversion of volumetric flow rate to velocity 
Q = 85 [m​3​/s], Maximum volumetric flow rate of the Saigon river 
p = 998 [Kg/m], water density 
Change the volumetric flow rate into a velocity (v) [m/s] instead. Assume that the 
Saigon river has a cross section area (A) of ≈ 6800 m2. 
Assume that I need to change the volumetric flow rate into a velocity (v) [m/s] 
instead. 
Step 1: ​To get the velocity, we first need to find the mass flow rate (m) [Kg/s] which 
can be found through this equation: 
m = Q * p 
m = 85 m3/s * 998 Kg/m3 = 84 830 Kg/s 
Step 2: ​We can thereafter find the velocity by using the mass flow rate through this 
equation: 
v = m/(A*p) 
v = 84 830 Kg/s / (6800 m2 * 998 Kg/m3) = ​0.0125 m/s

4.
Discussion 
The data collected seem to both support hypothesis (1) and hypothesis (2). The data 
collected in relation with hypothesis (1) looks to correlate towards the BC's ability to repel 
macroplastic items. During the setup of the experiment and test of the equipment it was 
observed that in some situations the PET bottle would pass through the BC. That interaction 
was not replicated during the test at all. One possible explanation might be that the PET 
bottle might be able to pass if the BC simulator were to be moved much faster than the 
speed during the test. The results of Hypothesis (2) test suggest that as the angle of the BC 
simulator is increased the more effective the BC will be at redirecting floating macro plastic 
along the bubble curtain. 
In conclusion it can be argued that the as the data collected in this experiment concur with 
both CanadanPond.ca’s projects (​CanadianPond 2016​) and the Great Bubble barrier’s 
project (​Great Bubble Barrier 2019​), utilization of BC technology in the Saigon river area 
around the Thu Thiem bridge might yield positive results. This both as a Macro plastic 
repellent and a redirection method. For more specific assumptions more elaborate testing is 
needed on our part.

Appendix J 2 
Utilization of cyclonal hydro movement for macro 
plastic entrapment 
- a technical pre-experimental test 
1.
Introduction 
In regards to extraction of macro plastic in the Saigon river a set of techniques for catchment 
can be considered. One method of plastic item extraction and concentration in a select area, 
might be achieved with a cyclonal hydraulics approach. In this record 2 approaches are 
discussed. 1) water movement along the side of a cylindrical container and 2) water exiting a 
funnel. Conclusively none of the approaches showed any consistent beneficial effects in the 
pre-experimental phase. Testing of the approaches were thereafter terminated.  
2.
Approaches 
Approach 1​ ​- in a 1000 mm diameter tub, a 3000 liters per minute pump was set up along 
the side of the tub in such a way that a flow was created along the wall of the tub (figure 1). 
A 330 ml PET bottle and an extra bottle top was placed in the tub and the water pump was 
turned on. The effect with the pump in the tub and without while the water in the tub was still 
in motion were observed. 
 
Figure 1: ​Setup for testing approach 1.  
==PAGE:139==
Appendix J 2 
Approach 2 - a 450 mm diameter Funnel was placed over a bucket  (figure 2). The funnel’s 
hole was plugged while it was filled to the brim. A 330 ml PET bottle top was placed at the 
edge of the funnel as the funnel’s outlet was unplugged. This was tested multiple times. A 
set of tests with a 3000 liters/hour pump refilling the funnel in a closed circuit was also 
conducted. 
 
Figure 2: ​Setup for testing approach 2. 
3.
Outcome 
Approach 1 showed no consistent pattern of movement of the plastic items, either with or 
without the pump in the water. The bottles would move unpredictably between the middle of 
the tub and the edge.  
Approach 2 showed no useful patterns of movement of the plastic items, in either of the test 
setups. The setup without the pump would not show any sign of moving the item to the 
middle of the funnel, and in the situations the items reached the middle the item would try to 
exit the funnel. The setup with the pump showed no significant difference in behaviour to the 
setup without. 
The pre-experiment did not show any immediate cyclonal water effect that was deemed 
likely to be useful. More testing or alternative approaches might change this assumption.
