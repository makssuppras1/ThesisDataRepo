Abstract
The aim of this Master Thesis is to investigate the collaboration between a provider of a
digital ecosystem, platform partners, and customers/end-users. This is done by investi-
gating the three stakeholders of a digital ecosystem, and they are: the platform provider,
which in this study is Siemens Healthineers A/S, the platform partners, which in this
study is represented by Cercare Medical ApS, and the customers/end-users, which cov-
ers both the hospitals and the healthcare professionals. Cercare Medical ApS is 1 out
of 19 partners of the digital ecosystem.
A digital ecosystem is a common platform, which collects applications from third-
party developers. This platform makes it possible for healthcare professionals to ac-
cess small, innovative, and specialised solutions. The digital ecosystem is a potential
solution to an array of issues. One example is that it can be difficult for small start-up
companies to enter the market and reach customers/end-users. Another example is that
healthcare professionals have less and less time for the patients and are straining to
sustain the level of treatment. The digital ecosystem can be an important tool to solve
these examples of issues, as it brings provider and requester closer together, with an
effective and innovative platform, which can help solve routine tasks.
There is a lack of knowledge about the importance of the digital ecosystem and how the
collaboration between the stakeholders can be improved. The methods used to inves-
tigate this is: research interviews with Siemens Healthineers A/S and Cercare Medical
ApS, an anonymous survey to the other partners of the ecosystem, and a questionnaire
for the customers/end-users. One participant from Siemens Healthineers A/S and two
from Cercare Medical ApS participated in the research interviews. Three partners par-
ticipated in the survey, and five customers/end-users participated in the questionnaire.
The findings obtained using these methods were held against each other and used to
conclude on the investigation.
The conclusion of this study is, that the collaboration, in general, is functioning well
between Siemens Healthineers A/S and the partners. However, there is space for im-
provements at the customers/end-users. They do not seem well informed about the
advantages of the digital ecosystem and do not see it as a tool to solve their issues.
Another big obstacle is the organisational structure at the hospital, which impede the
customers/end-users to benefit fully from the digital ecosystem.

Introduction
Imagine, that you are a doctor at an oncology department. You have just read about a
new software solution on the market, which can automatically identify cancer tumors on
magnetic resonance images (MRI) using an algorithm. Normally, it is your job to iden-
tify tumors on images manually. This is a daily routine, but it is still a time-consuming
and an important task for you to solve. The task requires your full concentration and
attention. You are in advance frustrated about the level of treatment, that you deliver, as
you feel insufficient. You do not have enough time to render the necessary care, which
the patients need. Long daily workflows claim all your time, which you could have
used on e.g. patient care. This new software you have read about could ease your job.
It can save you precious time, which you can then use on work-tasks, where you feel
insufficient. You ask the Medico-Technical Department at the hospital for permission
to get that new software solution, but you meet a ”no” because it is too expensive and
time-consuming to implement that software at the hospital, because it is from a devel-
oper that is not already used at your hospital. The digital ecosystem is a solution to this
problem, as it makes it easier and less costly for the hospitals to implement software
solutions from a developer, that is not already used. It can help to free up time in your
daily work as a doctor and raise the level of treatment at Danish hospitals.
The world is moving towards a more standardised[1] and regulated world. Digital
ecosystems are a step in the standardised direction. Digital ecosystems in healthcare
technology are a relatively new phenomenon. A digital ecosystem is a common plat-
form, where applications and software targeting different healthcare specialisations are
collected. The platform makes it easier for healthcare professionals to access the latest
software and associated features on the market. This could help healthcare profession-
als to make the right diagnosis in time and provide the best and most efficient treatment
for the patients. Seen from the perspective, that the hospitals constantly have less time
for the patients this is an important tool to maintain a high level of treatment by facili-
tating e.g. trivial (but still time-consuming) tasks.
The digital ecosystem is also an advantage for small MedTech companies and start-ups,
as it makes it easier for them to reach the market with their solutions and engender value

at the healthcare professionals. The healthcare professionals requires a guarantee for
the solutions’ quality and safety, before they implement it at the hospital. Therefore,
certification is crucial to enter the digital ecosystem. Certification of a medical device
is important, as it is a warranty to the responsible purchaser at the hospital, that the
product is safe in use for both the healthcare professionals and their patients and meets
a certain standard. However, the certification process is getting more complicated, as
the legislation concerning medical devices is getting more strict.[2] This means that the
certification of medical devices is becoming more time-consuming, costly, and unclear
for the companies. This affects especially the smaller companies and the start-ups, as
they do not have a big regulatory department and the necessary resources to accom-
plish certification as fast as possible compared to larger companies. They do neither
have much experience with the certification of products, as their product-portfolio is
still narrow and few products have been through the certification process. The result is,
that the product reaches the market later than expected, which is costly due to the loss
of potential revenue. The digital ecosystem can remedy this problem. The ecosystem
creates a collaboration between the larger companies and the smaller companies. The
larger companies can be helpful with the exchange of experience, be a sparring partner,
and offer guidance from their regulatory experts to the smaller companies. This can op-
timise the certification process relative to time and money for the smaller companies.
The digital ecosystem in healthcare technology is considered a success when all parts
of the ecosystem capitalise on it. These parts include both, the platform provider, the
partners, and the customers/end-users, who covers both the hospitals and the healthcare
professionals.

how is the certification process getting more overseeable and clear for the companies?
Another motivation for this investigation is that there is a lack of knowledge about the
importance of the digital ecosystem and how the collaboration between the stakehold-

2.1
Digital ecosystem
First, what is a digital ecosystem? And how does it work? A digital ecosystem is ele-
mentary similar to the biological ecosystem. They both contains some of the same el-
ements. The biological ecosystem consists of different factors, which help each other
and together constitute the ecosystem. The big trees in the wood creates shade for
the smaller trees, plants etc. The small trees, plants, flowers etc. are essential in or-
der to keep small animals and insects alive. New plants need breeding ground, which
earthworms and bugs provides by turning fallen leaves into topsoil. The plants needs
nutrition in terms of glucose and oxygen to survive. This is created thanks to photosyn-
thesis, by every leave, which is able to convert water from the rain and carbon dioxide
from the air into the necessary nutrition using sun light.[3] This is shortly said, how the
biological ecosystem functions. The digital ecosystem contains the same elements as
the biological. A digital ecosystem is based on a common standardised infrastructure.
A digital ecosystem is an interface and an integration between different developers’
software solutions. It allows e.g. the region to have systems, that work together and
exchange data, even though the software solutions are from different developers.[3]
Like in the biological ecosystem, every part or element of the digital ecosystem are not
able to function/work properly without the others. The optimal conditions for a well-
functioning ecosystem is when all system parts work together.[4]
When you download an application to your smartphone to solve a specific problem,
there is a lot of different applications from both big companies and small start-up com-
panies to choose between. Every single application contributes with their offer for a

2.1.1
Cloud vs. on-premise
An application in a digital ecosystem can be settled as a service at the customers/end-
users in two different ways: on-premise or cloud based, Figure 2.1.[6] The fundamental
difference between on-premise software and cloud computing software is where it is
installed.[7]
Figure 2.1: The two different ways an application
or a software can be settled as a service.[6]
On-premise software is installed locally on the customers’/end-users’ server [8] and a
cloud computing software is installed on the vendors’ server (in this case on Siemens
Healthineers A/S’s server). Besides the fundamental difference between on-premise
and cloud computing, there are some other differences between these two, Tabel 2.1.
On-premise software is as earlier mentioned installed and stored locally on its own
server. This means, that it is the customers’/end-users’ own responsibility to take care
of maintenance. On the other hand, the customers/end-users have fully control with
the generated data and how it is handled and stored. A on-premise solution is ideal if a
customer/end-user likes to fell ownership. The deployment of on-premise software is
done in-house using the customers’/end-users’ infrastructure. The deployment time is
long as the software needs to be installed on individual computers. On-premise soft-
ware is more secure as it is in-house, but multiple measures need to be maintained in
order to maintain the security. The upfront investment is large, but the annual cost is

2.2
Certification in EU
The procedure from starting the development of a medical device until it can be mar-
keted in EU will in this section be explained and described. The procedure of getting a
medical device legalised and certified is a long and twisted road, and it is an advantage
to have it in mind already in the start of the development of the new medical device.
Siemens Healthineers A/S and their partners provide medical devices and software,
so in this section there will be focus on how to certify a medical device. Therefore
the Medical Device Directive (MDD) and Medical Device Regulation (MDR) will be
used. In order to reach the certification, it is important to know what a medical device
actually is. The following definition is cited from MDD:[18]
”’medical device’ means any instrument, apparatus, appliance, material
or other article, whether used alone or in combination, including the soft-
ware necessary for its proper application intended by the manufacturer to
be used for human beings for the purpose of:
• diagnosis, prevention, monitoring, treatment or alleviation of dis-
ease,
• diagnosis, monitoring, treatment, alleviation of or compensation for
an injury or handicap,
• investigation, replacement or modification of the anatomy or of a
physiological process,

2.3.1
Classification
The FDA separates medical devices into three regulatory classes. The classes are di-
vided upon the degree of risk. Class I has the lowest risk, class II has medium risk, and
class III has the highest risk. These classes shall not be confused with the classification
in EU, even though the names of the classes are spelled the same way. This division
is made on the basis to ensure the safety and effectiveness of the device.[24] Each class
also have regulatory controls. Class I requires general controls, class II requires both
general and special controls and class III requires both general controls and premarket
approval. Premarket approval will be elaborated later. General controls, which apply
to all three classes, concerns e.g. adulteration of devices, registration and listing of
devices, and records of devices due to the provisions. For class II the special controls
are the regulatory requirements. Class II are used when class I and the general con-
trols are insufficient. The special controls include e.g. performance standards, patient
registries and guidelines. The special controls are also often device-specific.[35] In the
medical area more and more software are used. The FDA had issues by regulating
this. Questions have swarmed about what kind of software it is, is it a medical device
and which regulation could be required for it. FDA have therefore divided software
into three categories: medical device software, software used in manufacturing, and
software information systems used for clinical decision-making. As medical devices
software also gets divided into three classes depending on their risk.[24]
2.3.2
Regulatory pathways
When a manufacturer wants to have a medical device approved in the USA the two most
common regulatory pathways is the pre-market notification process 510(k) and the pre-
market approval application (PMAA).[24][36] When a manufacturer decides to use the
510(k) process he/she has to demonstrate that the device is substantial equivalent to an
already legally marketed device in the USA. Figure 2.6 shows the flow of the 510(k)
process. The manufacturer does not have the permission to market the device until it is
clarified by the FDA, that it is substantial equivalent to an already legally device in the
USA. Cited from the FDA a device is substantial equivalence to a predicate device, if
it:[37]

• has the same technological characteristics as the predicate; or
• has the same intended use as the predicate; and

The 510(k) process can be used in all of the classes, when a premarket approval ap-

3.1
Identify the theme
In this phase, the theme and the aim of the research interview should be clarified, be-
fore the interview begins. It is clarified by answering the interview’s ”what”, ”why”,
and ”how”. The ”what” determines the theme of the interview, the ”why” determines
the aim of the interview, and the ”how” determines the method for the interview.[40]
The “what” or the theme in this research interview is to investigate the digital ecosys-

of their product-portfolio to be certified as medical devices and which considerations
they make concerning that. The interview’s “how”, is how to answer the interview’s
“what” and “why”. The “how” determines the best and most efficient way to collect the
essential empirical evidence, in this case, regarding Siemens Healthineers A/S’s digital
ecosystem. It is not necessarily the best and most efficient way if the entire market
should be analysed, as ecosystems’ construction and function can vary from company

Discussion
In this Chapter, the results from both the research interviews, the additional research
interview, the anonymous survey, and the questionnaire will be discussed. In continua-
tion of this, the hypotheses stated in Section 3.3.2, page 29 will be accepted or rejected
based on the findings in the results. Afterward, the methods used in this thesis will be
discussed and sources of errors and uncertainties will be reviewed.
When looking at Hypothesis 1, which treats that the advantage of the ecosystem is to of-
fer more specialised solutions, it is quickly clear, that it can be accepted. Christian Gra-
versen emphasises it by explaining, that their customers/end-users request collabora-
tion with smaller and more specialised third-party developers. He says that it is unclear
for the customers/end-users what they can expect from a collaboration with Siemens
Healthineers A/S, because they have a wide scope. Although the customers/end-users
request these collaborations, they are still nervous about being a part of these, because
they fear IT-instability, because the software is from a small and to them unknown de-
veloper. Siemens Healthineers A/S creates trust between the customers/end-users and
the small developers when they let the small developers enter the digital ecosystem.
Christian Graversen mentions another motivation for Siemens Healthineers A/S to cre-
ate a digital ecosystem is, that they are able to create revenue that they would never be
able to create themselves. So, more satisfied customers/end-users are clearly an advan-
tage of the digital ecosystem, but a larger revenue is also an advantage.
Due to Hypothesis 2, Kristian Tusch says, that the biggest advantage of being a part of
the digital ecosystem is the short set-up time of the solution at the customers/end-users.
This results in time-saving and cost-saving, even though Siemens Healthineers A/S re-
quires 30 % of the sales price. This is confirmed by the participants of the anonymous
survey, as two out of three agree in Statement 1 (the results of the anonymous survey
are available in Section 4, page 37). Kristian Tusch mentions another advantage, which
is the support-process, where Siemens Healthineers A/S is the first line of support. He
also mentions that an advantage is, that Siemens Healthineers A/S is in the top 5 of
companies, which are farthest in the process. This is confirmed by the other partners
where two out of three agree in Statement 3. Christian Graversen says that it can be
attractive for the partners to see the ecosystem as a sales channel. This is not clearly
Product Development Strategy in Ecosystems
for Digital Healthcare Technology

==PAGE:67==
backed up by the participants in the survey, Statement 4, as they answer strongly dis-
agree, neutral, and strongly agree on that. One of the participants elaborates, that he/she
is not convinced of the effectiveness of the digital ecosystem as a sales channel. Chris-
tian Graversen also mentions, that being a partner of the digital ecosystem will make
them appear more reliable. This is confirmed in the anonymous survey, Statement 2,
where two out of three strongly agree on that. Hypothesis 2 can therefore be accepted,
because the majority agrees, that reaching out to a wider customer segment is a clear
advantage together with the cost-saving, time-saving, and the acknowledgement of be-
ing a part of the digital ecosystem. The participants in the survey are not completely
agreeing on whether it is the biggest advantage or not, Statement 7. One of the partic-
ipants comments, that the level of integration is also important.
Regarding the difficulties with selecting the right partners to the ecosystem, Hypothe-
sis 3, Christian Graversen tells, that Siemens Healthineers A/S have set some general
requirements, that the potential partners must meet. The general requirement is, that a
potential partner should add a new solution, that is not already in the digital ecosystem.
The solution should likewise add clinical value to the customers/end-users and there
should be a technical fit between Siemens Healthineers A/S and the potential partner,
because Siemens Healthineers A/S will not let anything in, that can make the operat-
ing environment unstable. A very important general requirement is, that the solution
is CE-approved. The process of selecting the right partners for the digital ecosystem
is therefore time-consuming, but it is not difficult for Siemens Healthineers A/S, as
they have some general requirements they use as selection criteria. Hypothesis 3 can
therefore be rejected.
Hypothesis 4, the partners are subjected to Siemens Healthineers A/S’s agenda, can be
rejected, as Kristian Tusch says that the collaboration is good, and they have a good
influence. This is confirmed by Christian Graversen, as he says that there is certain
responsiveness from Siemens Healthineers A/S to the partners. The participants of the
survey, Statement 14, do not largely agree with Kristian Tush in that they have much
influence. Two participants answer neutral and one answer agree that the collaboration
is good in Statement 13. The answers in Statement 15, where the participants are both
agreeing and strongly agreeing that Siemens Healthineers A/S is professional in the
collaboration with their partners are backed up by Kristian Tusch. He mentions that
Siemens Healthineers A/S is very professional, open-minded, and listening to their
thoughts and ideas. He mentions that a disadvantage of being a part of the digital

with that statement. Even though there are contradictions in whether the partners have
much influence or not, the majority express that the partners have influence. Hypoth-
esis 4 can therefore be rejected.
Regarding the de-classification of solutions to save time and money, Christian Gra-
versen tells that Siemens Healthineers A/S has worked with de-classifying for the last
few years. He suggests that the small companies should dare to manage the de-classifying

Regarding Hypothesis 6, which states differences in perception of medical devices,

to operate. Christian Graversen confirms that. He says that it is make or break for the
small companies, as their product-portfolio is narrow, and shutting down one solution
has big consequences compared to a larger company with a wide product-portfolio. He
also mentions, that money is an important factor when certifying solutions, and larger
companies have a larger money stock than smaller companies. Peter T. Løkke agrees
with Christian Graversen, as he mentions, that larger companies have the money, the
experience, and the necessary resources to go through the certification process. How-
ever, he emphasises that it is not a possibility, that the big companies can help the
smaller ones with the certification process because it requires that you are well-known
in their process and familiar with their products. The only place, he could imagine

T. Løkke says. He says, that small companies do not always have certification in mind

5.1
Choice of methods
The research interview method was chosen to collect data from Siemens Healthineers
A/S and Cercare Medical ApS. This method was chosen at the beginning of the project,
as it e.g. allows asking elaborating questions and avoids misunderstandings. It was ex-
pected at the beginning of the project, that the research interviews would be conducted
with a physical presence, but the COVID-19 pandemic inhibited that. The interviews
were conducted with a virtual presence, where body language analysis was lost. Body
language can support or weaken an argument, and can therefore be important. Body
language can also show the emotions connected to the argument e.g is the person ner-
vous, insecure, or extroverted. An advantage of the virtual conduction was that both the
interview subjects and the interviewers stayed in their individual secure environments.
After the execution of the interviews, it was decided to gather empirical evidence from
other partners of the ecosystem and the customers/end-users. It was decided to do an
anonymous survey for the partners and a questionnaire for the customers/end-users,
as it turned out to be a time-consuming process to prepare, conduct, and analyse a re-
search interview. In proportion to the anonymous survey, it was expected that more
partners would have liked to participate, as they appear anonymous, and the survey
treats potential sensitive information. In proportion to the questionnaire targeting the
customers/end-users, the response time needed to be short, as they already have a busy
workday. The time from when the questionnaire was received by customers/end-users
until they had answered it was extended due to the increasing pressure on the hospitals
and the employees due to the COVID-19 pandemic. In relation to time, it was not pos-
sible to ask elaborating questions to neither the partners nor the customers/end-users.
The results from the anonymous survey and the questionnaire would maybe be influ-
enced if more and other professions participated. It became clear, that the one end-user
who participated in the questionnaire was often disagreeing with the other participants,
who were customers. It would have been interesting to have more end-users participat-
ing in the inquiry, as it could have enhanced his opinions in both directions. It could
also be interesting to divide the participant group into customers and end-users and tar-
get the questions in the questionnaire to each group. This would give a more accurate
picture of the situation at the hospitals, as there would be more answers and they would
be more specific. It would have been an advantage if more participants participated in
the survey and the questionnaire because it could support or weaken the findings.

Conclusion
The following Chapter contains the conclusions obtained in the Master Thesis ”Prod-
uct Development Strategy in Ecosystems for Digital Healthcare Technology”. Finally,
possible future work will be suggested.
Based on the findings in this Master Thesis, it can be concluded that Christian Gra-
versen from Siemens Healthineers A/S has a clear perception, that the digital ecosystem
creates value for the customers/end-users with its specialised solutions. Kristian Tusch
from Cercare Medical ApS, says that there is no doubt that the digital ecosystem is very
meaningful for the customers/end-users. Both Christian Graversen and Kristian Tusch
agree, that the ecosystem creates value. The customers express, that they think that the
ecosystem is an interesting solution and a potential advantage for the clinicians. The
end-user disagrees with that, due to the organisational structure at the hospitals. He
says, that the procedure is not easier than before the implementation of the ecosystem.
It still requires a lot of permissions and that is time-consuming. The above shows, that
the opinions differ on whether the ecosystem adds value or not, but the majority thinks,
that the digital ecosystem creates value for the customers/end-users.
Siemens Healthineers A/S’s purpose of creating a digital ecosystem is not only to get
more satisfied customers/end-users but also to create revenue. Christian Graversen em-
phasises, that the ecosystem is still a new solution, and it is not yet where they want it
to be. The partners do only get orders occasionally, so the revenue is not optimal. The
digital ecosystem is first considered as a success when all parts of the digital ecosystem
benefit from it, and it is not there yet.
Christian Graversen emphasises, that the digital ecosystem and Siemens Healthineers
A/S should not be considered as a sales channel. Even though, he mentions, that he is
aware, that it can be attractive for the partners to perceive it as a sales channel. Christian
Graversen underlines, that the digital ecosystem is created to get the smaller compa-
nies to appear more innovative. Kristian Tusch says, that the ecosystem is a very easy
way to offer their solutions and services at the hospitals because the installation time
is shortened. He highlights, that the digital ecosystem is a really good way to reach
the customers/end-users and get started. The other partners are overall agreeing with
that. So, the conclusion is, that the ecosystem helps the partners to reach out to new

customers/end-users by appearing innovative.
One of the findings in the Master Thesis is, that there are differences in the certifica-
tion process relative to a large and a small MedTech company in proportion to time,
money, and resources. It was considered if Cercare Medical ApS could capitalise on
the collaboration with Siemens Healthineers A/S concerning the certification process.
It became clear, that it was not a possibility, as Peter T. Løkke from Cercare Medical
ApS states, that their certification processes are different from each other and they do
not know each other’s product-portfolio well enough to contribute with help. He does
mention, that it could be helpful if Siemens Healthineers A/S could help establishing
contact with authorities (e.g. notified bodies). The overall conclusion of this is, that
the digital ecosystem could not be helpful in relation to the certification process.
When looking at, the customers’/end-users’ feedback it is not crystal clear if the ecosys-
tem facilitates the work at the hospitals. The customers express, that it is an interesting
solution, but it is difficult to implement a new application. The end-user does not
think, that the digital ecosystem has importance in his job. The conclusion is, the dig-
ital ecosystem does not facilitate the work at the hospitals yet, because the ecosystem
is not fully utilised, as they do not fully benefit from it so far, so there are possibilities
for optimising the digital ecosystem and create further benefits for the end-users.
In general, the collaboration between Siemens Healthineers A/S and their partners of
the digital ecosystem is functioning well. A place for improvements could be at the
customers/end-users. Right now, they do not benefit fully from the digital ecosystem.
They do not seem informed well enough about the advantages of the ecosystem. Be-
sides that, the organisational structure at the hospitals is an obstacle in order to benefit
from the ecosystem.
6.1
Future work
For future work, it could be interesting to talk with more partners of the digital ecosys-
tem. The same applies to the customers/end-users. Furthermore, it could be of interest
to investigate other digital ecosystems both within the healthcare field, but also ecosys-
tems in other fields, to get inspiration and ideas to how the digital ecosystem could be
improved. It could also be exciting to explore MDR and how its implementation will
affect both the certification and the digital ecosystem. Lastly, it could be intriguing to
investigate how General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) affects when a third-party
developer enters the ecosystem.[50]
