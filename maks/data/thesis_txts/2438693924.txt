Abstract

Abstract
Inﬂammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), including Crohn’s disease and Ulcerative colitis,
are multifaceted chronic gastrointestinal diseases with an increasing prevalence world-
wide and no established cure.
IBD is suggested to result from aggravated immune
responses against the gut microbiota however, the adaptive immune response and im-
mune dynamics remain largely undetermined. The microbiome constitutes an enormous
antigenic search space and the features of immunodominant epitopes are poorly under-
stood, which together impedes the discovery of commensal antigens capable of driving
an robust adaptive immune response.
By integrating the algorithm Bacteria-origin T cell Antigen (BOTA) with netMHCIIpan
in a computational pipeline, novel experimental insight into immune cell autophagy has
been combined with a state-of-the-art epitope predictor to enable commensal epitope
discovery in the human gut microbiome.
Using this pipeline, highly prevalent and
expressed epitopes in both healthy controls and IBD patients have been identiﬁed in
conserved genomic loci of commensal proteins displaying features of extensive immune-
recognition. Veriﬁed immunogenic epitopes can be employed in an experimental plat-
form allowing the generation of quantitative and qualitative adaptive immunoproﬁling
using patient’s T-cells. The unlocked ability to proﬁle and quantify the immune re-
sponse towards the microbiome may be in the future translated into diagnostic appli-
cations for tracking the onset and progression of IBD.

Introduction
Inﬂammatory Bowel Disease is a chronic gastrointestinal disease with two clinical man-
ifestations, Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC). CD and UC represents
distinct pathophysiological entities that aﬀect diﬀerent parts of the gastrointestinal
tract, yet they share the same main components which are believed to inﬂuence disease
onset and progression such as: Genetic susceptibility in people that encode the IBD
risk allele DRB1-01:03 [1] or a mutation in the NOD2 gene [2], numerous environmental
factors such as poor diet, antibiotics or smoking [3, 4], a dysbiotic gut microbiota that
interacts with an over reactive immune system causing persistent inﬂammation.
Despite the substantial progress that has been made by studying the immunopathogen-
esis of IBD, our understanding of the intricate disease mechanisms remains inadequate
and slows the progress of novel medical applications for disease diagnosis and treatment.
In the meantime, IBD prevalence is on the rise and foreshadows a substantial burden
and cost to the global health care system as well as a signiﬁcant impact on patients
physical and psychological health [5, 6].
The microbiota and host immune system interaction is limited by immune compartmen-
talisation
A major component of IBD immunopathogenesis is the enhanced immune reactivity
against microbial antigens [7] and as the GI tract is constantly exposed to an enormous
load of antigenic material including food, microbiome and environmental compounds,
this hyperreactivity is continuously renewed.
During homoeostasis, the bidirectional communication between the mucosal immune
system and the microbiome is tightly regulated through several structural and cellu-
lar components to prevent immune hyperactivity. A layer of intestinal epithelial cells
(IEC) constitutes the ﬁrst line of defence acting as a self-maintaining physical barrier
that sense bacterial molecular patterns through innate pattern recognition receptors
(PPRs), such as the Toll-like receptor (TLR) or NOD-like receptor [8]. The IEC also
sustains a second line of defence by pumping out a glycoprotein-rich mucous layer
nested with antimicrobial peptides and B-cell derived immunoglobulin A (IgA). The
mucosal barrier physically limits the number of pathogens that reach the IEC and sus-
tains colonising symbiotic bacteria which are able to out compete pathogenic species
for nutrients [9, 10].
As a third line of defence, the immune system commands innate and adaptive immune
cells that respond quickly to immediate invasion while preserving putative bacterial
species during homoeostasis [11]. The constitutive secretion of B-cell derived IgA anti-
bodies across the epithelial barrier prevents pathogens from reaching IEC but also binds
symbiotic bacteria to the mucous layer allowing beneﬁcial host-microbial interactions
[12, 13].
The immune and bacterial partnership is maintained through mutualistic bacterial de-
rived compounds
The symbiotic host-bacterial relationship is sustained through beneﬁcial bacterial molecules
derived from carbohydrate fermentation such as short-chain fatty-acids (SCFAs), which
are recognised by host-receptors on the epithelium and immune cells, as seen in Fig-
ure 1.
SCFAs comprise acetate, propionate, and butyrate that acts as energy sub-
strate for host-cells but also exhibits tolorogenic properties that help promoting an

Outer membrane vesicles allows high exposure of immuno-regulatory proteins and outer
membrane proteins
One direct pathway used for delivery of immunoregulatory bacterial compounds like
PSA to host immune cells is via outer membrane vesicles (OMVs), as seen in Figure 2.
OMVs are loaded with secreted, periplasmic or outer membrane proteins that can be
delivered to host immune cells through a budding secretion mechanism [22]. However,
OMVs are also used for inter-cellular bacterial communication, extracellular nutrient
digestion and defence mechanisms [23]. This has been established for species such as
B. fragilis and B. thetaiotamicron that exhibit a preferential packing of glycosidases,
peptidases and proteins belonging to the starch utilisation system (Sus) family such
as the TonB-dependent receptors (TBDTs) [24–26]. Proteins like TBDTs are by no
means suggested virulent factors, but may achieve higher exposure to the immune
system through OMV pathways [23, 27].
Figure 2: OMVs are known to be produced by both pathogenic and bacterial gastrointestinal members.
These nanoparticles are loaded with bacterial peptides and can be transported to the epithelial barrier
and enter IEC through endocytosis. Within the IEC, OMVs travels to the early endosomes where they
get recognised by intracellular immune receptors resulting in protein cargo processing in autophagic
vacuoles.
Subsequently, the processed peptide cargo is released in exosomes that are digested by
APCs leading to antigen presentation through the TCR to CD4+ T-cells. The immunogenic epitope
presentation leads to clonal expansion of Th cells which mediate maturation of immature B-cells to
antibody secreting plasma cells, speciﬁc to their cognate antigen.

When mucosal tolerance breaks
Traditionally, opportunistic pathogens such as S. enterica or P. aeruginosa are able to
raise a protective an pro-inﬂammatory CD4+ Th1 driven immune response by causing
inﬂammation through infection [28, 29]. This response is stimulated by abundant ﬂag-
ella antigens such as FliC (Figure 3) recognised by both the adaptive and innate arm
of the immune system. However, before maturation, the immune system is incapable
of distinguishing between symbionts and pathogens based on their shared molecular
structures such as peptidoglycans, lipoprotein and ﬂagella. This may have prompted
symbiotic bacteria to develop mechanisms to suppress unwanted inﬂammation and es-
tablish mucosal tolerance as a requirement for colonisation and to coexist with the
healthy host [16]. However, when the host is immunocompromised during inﬂamma-
tory pathologies like IBD, tolerance against antigens of the microbiota is partly lost,
making intimate symbionts like SFB inadvertently detrimental and capable of propa-
gating inﬂammation [30].

the taxonomic signature of IBD patients as a volatile dysbiosis, which sporadically ap-

To understand how microbial dysbiosis aﬀects microbial expression patterns which leads
to an aggravated immune response, multi’omic approaches that leverage both metage-
nomics and metatranscriptomics in concert with longitudinal sampling have proven a
powerful tool [39].
While microbial expression patterns and higher taxonomic resolution helps in pinpoint-
ing species and pathways relevant for IBD, our ability to quantify immune function and
health in IBD patients is virtually non-existing. Without any established commensal
antigens capable of stimulating a robust immune response, we are restricted from gener-
ating immune proﬁling data that would allow insight into the adaptive immune system
during IBD. Not knowing how the adaptive immune system recognises components of
the microbiome prevents us from identifying what to measure. This unfortunate cycle
is further ampliﬁed by our incomplete understanding of the features that shapes im-
munodominant epitopes.
CD4+ T-cell response is constrained to a limited set of immunodominant epitopes
Canonical immune CD4+ T-cell responses are conﬁned to antigen derived epitopes
presented on the Major Histocompatibility Complex II (MHCII) on APCs. The de-
generacy of the epitope-MHC interaction allows a broad spectrum of epitopes to be
bound by the MHCII receptor and presented to host immune cells leading to a focused
immune response towards pathogens or commensals displaying the cognate epitope. In
humans, the MHCII loci (Figure 4 encodes several hyper-variable MHCII receptors and
this variability provides an evolutionary high recognition capacity and allows the dis-
crimination of self from non-self. Each individual inherits 12 diﬀerent alleles allowing
APCs to express up to 12 diﬀerent MHCII receptors.

yet been integrated into the current state-of-the-art epitope prediction algorithms [45].

Finally, the potency and direction of the immune response also depends on additional
factors such as the cytokine milieu that is largely determined by the presence of inﬂam-
mation. Naive CD4+ T-cells express cytokine receptors to sense the state of the current
cytokine milieu and diﬀerentiate accordingly to e.g. Th1 and Treg during inﬂammation
or homoeostasis, respectively.
Speciﬁc bacteria such as the strains of Clostridium isolated by Atarashi et.al, prime an
anti-inﬂammatory milieu through the release of IL-10 resulting in the induction of Tregs
during homoeostasis [47]. Such strain speciﬁc properties may be shared by multiple gut
microbial members and inﬂuence the immune system’s ability to maintain tolerance
during the constant antigenic pressure of the microbiome [48].
So far, in silico epitope-prediction tools have been essential in novel vaccine discovery
within immune oncology [49] and infectious diseases [50]. However, the translation of
these results into human microbiome antigen-discovery remains an enormous under-
taking. First, the microbiome makes up an enormous and dynamic genomic search
space. Second, little is known about the hierarchy that shapes immunodominant epi-

3.1
Project aim and ﬁndings
Validated immunogenic epitopes in both the healthy and inﬂamed microbiome could
be promising targets for generating experimental immunoproﬁling data ex vivo that
would enable novel insight into the aggravated adaptive immune response of IBD. To
establish a representative immunopeptidome for down-stream experimental veriﬁcation,
the project featured two major aims:
• Humanise the BOTA framework for antigen discovery and epitope prediction in
the human microbiome.
• Deﬁne the landscape of epitopes in the commensal proteome of HMP2 and identify
epitopes with features allowing high immune exposure in the healthy and inﬂamed
microbiome.
For these purposes, a computational pipeline has been established that leverage the ﬁrst
steps of the algorithm BOTA (accepted in Nature Medicine, D. Graham et. al 2018)
in concert with netMHCIIpan [40] to deﬁne the microbial immunopeptidome of human
subjects in the HMP2. In other words, antigen discovery in the human microbiome
can be pursued by integrating novel experimental proteomic insight into immune cell
autophagy with state-of-the-art immunoinformatic epitope prediction methods. Fur-
thermore, with the aim of designing novel medical applications to diagnose onset and
progression of IBD, this pipeline has enabled the discovery of conserved and highly ex-
pressed epitopes in the microbiome of both IBD patients and healthy controls. Several
of these epitopes originated from members of Bacteroidetes, Firmicutes, Proteobacteria
and Verrucomicrobia. Surprisingly, some epitopes found in the human microbiome were
predicted with ’Pan’-MHC2 binding properties overlapping with the mouse MHCII al-
lele H2-iAb, including a SusC epitope that has been found in species of Bacteroidales
isolated in the mouse metagenome.
Based on the SusC epitope’s predicted human
MHCII binding properties, the immunogenicity of the epitope was validated ex vivo by
its’ capacity to induce a potent IL-10, IL-17 and IFN-γ response using PMBCs from
healthy subjects. Furthermore, by mining additional genome databases from soil and
human gut bacteria, we have established the SusC epitope’s high conservation in mem-
bers of Bacteroidetes colonising humans and other mammals, which may suggest an
co-evolutionary bacterial colonisation beneﬁt.

Materials and Methods

Materials and Methods
4.1
Experimental model and subject details
Human cohort
A total of 132 subjects recruited from ﬁve medical centers and hospitals participated in
the Integrative Human Microbiome Project (HMP2). The subjects were followed for a
year and stool samples were collected at clinical visits as well as biweekly by mail see:
http://ibdmdb.org/protocols. The stool samples were received at the Broad institute
and immediately processed prior to meta’omic sequencing as described bySchirmer et
al. [39]. After signing a consent form, subjects were screened by colonoscopy and di-
agnosed with either CD, UC. Healthy controls were screened in a similar fashion and
assigned a nonIBD annotation. Participants were excluded from the study if they were
pregnant, had a known bleeding disorder, were undergoing chemotherapy, had an acute
gastrointestinal infection or a stoma or pouch.
Sequencing methods
Sequencing of metagenomics and metatranscriptomics were performed as described by
Schirmer et al. [39]. Metagenomes and metascriptomes were generated for 1638 and 855
stool samples with 10 million PE reads and 13 million PE reads, respectively. From
these, 1327 metagenome (MGX), 777 metatranscriptome (MTX) and 741 MGX-MTX
paired samples were chosen for downstream analysis (Figure 24 in Appendix 7.1). In
addition, 91 host exomes were sequenced following previously described methods.
4.2
Quantiﬁcation and statistical analysis.
Preprocessing and quality control
Sequence reads from the metagenomic samples (MGX) and the metatranscriptomic
samples (MTX) were processed with the quality control pipeline KneadData (0.7.0)
which uses Trimmomatic [51] and BMTagger in order to generate high quality sequence
reads without human contaminants, see http://huttenhower.sph.harvard.edu/kneaddata.
Fastq ﬁles were trimmed for reads shorter than 50 basepairs and reads with a Phred
score below 20. The resulting set of high quality sequences were ﬁltered for any hu-
man contaminants by removing reads that aligned to the human genome (hg38) or the
human transcriptome (hg38 mRNA), and subsequently ﬁltered for Nextera and Truseq
adapters. Following the quality control procedures, all samples contained minimum 2
million reads.
Deﬁning the landscape of epitopes in HMP2
Quality controlled fastq ﬁles were assembled independently using Megahit (v.1.1.2 - de-
fault settings) [52]. Assembled contigs with a minimum length of 500 nucleotides were
used for gene calling using Prodigal (-meta and default settings) then concatenated
into one redundant gene catalog and homology clustered with CD-hit [53] (b-aS 0.9,
-c 0.95, -r 0, -d 0 and -B 0, version 4.6.5) to produce a ﬁnal non-redundant genecata-
log consisting of 3.914.507 genes. The gene abundance and expression, normalised to
transcript-per-million (TPM), was subsequently calculated by mapping metagenomic
and metatranscriptomic reads using BWA (mem and default settings) [54] and retaining
reads mapped uniquely at min. 95% mapping identity. Co-abundance binning as de-

scribed by Nielsen et al. [55] was done to deﬁne clusters of co-abundant genes (CAGs).
Metagenomic species (MGS) containing at least 400 genes were annotated on phylum,

The collective HMP2 gene catalog was parsed through a series of ﬁltering steps to locate
epitopic genes with predicted strong-binding epitopes. To accomplish this, each gene
was parsed through BOTA which prioritised candidate protein sequences in the follow-
ing manner: Each protein was annotated according to either a gram negative or gram
positive database (1) then selected if contained either a signal peptide or was annotated
to the outer membrane or periplasmic region of the cell (2). Hereafter each sequence
was scanned for sequence fragments that were not located within the transmembrane
region of the outer membrane (3), and subsequently a biochemical score of accessibility
was calculated for all 9-mers within each candidate sequence to prioritise stretches of
amino acids displaying structural accessibility (4). Each candidate sequence required a
minimum sequence length of 20 or 30 amino acids if found interdomain or intradomain,
respectively (5).
In order to process each sequence according to the above mentioned criteria, BOTA
applied the following algorithms: HMMTOP for transmembrane annotation [58], HM-
MER to calculate sequence homology to known protein domains in PFAM [59] and
Psortb which annotates sequences according to their sub-cellular structure using Blast,
HMM models and support vector machines [60].
In order to establish the array of relevant HLA’s for epitope prediction, patient spe-
ciﬁc exome fastq ﬁles (n=92) were prepared by mapping paired-end exome data to the
human chromosome 6 (hg38) using BWA mem [54] and subsequently ﬁltered for all un-
mapped and secondary alignments using Samtools (-F 260 -q 30) [61]. The output bam
ﬁles were annotated with xHLA [62] using two digit sensitivity and default settings.
The resulting set of sequence fragments from 65491 unique genes in the HMP2 genecat-
alog was then analysed for strong-binding epitopes (scale rank < 2 %) with NetMHCI-
Ipan [40] against 67 diﬀerent HLA alleles and one mouse allele (default settings) result-
ing in 948241 core-epitope motifs found in 61338 genes. See Table 10 Appendix 7.6 for
the complete list of alleles.

Diﬀerential disease associated epitopes in HMP2

vs UC or CD were statistically signiﬁcant. False-discovery-rate (FDR) multiple-test

2.5% MGS based on mean abundance were prioritised for further analysis.

5.1
A small fraction of the microbiome immunopeptidome is
highly prevalent in health and disease
To establish a representative commensal proteome for commensal antigens with high
abundance and high immune exposure in the human gut microbiome of healthy and dis-
eased patients, an non-redundant genecatalog was established using a de novo assembly
approach. The genecatalog was based on 1327 metagenomic samples from 92 subjects
diagnosed as either nonIBD, UC or CD. The assembly process resulted in a gene set of
3.914.507 non-redundant genes with a sequence similarity up to 95%, deﬁning the total
search space for epitope prediction in the HMP2 cohort.
Commensal antigens are typically exposed either as extracellular or secreted proteins
exposing them to recognition by host immune cells. In addition, immune autophagy
in APCs is suggested to prioritise peptides with structural epitope accessibility in their
native form as better candidates for MHCII presentation. To identify this subset of
protein candidates, BOTA was applied to identify extracellular and secreted proteins
with the ideal biochemical properties promoting structural epitope accessibility. BOTA
returned 85.159 sequence candidate fragments derived from 65.491 distinct genes, a
98.32% reduction in genecatalog size, see Figure 6. Applying BOTA, the search space
was dramatically reduced for a more focused investigation.
To deﬁne the immunopeptidome of widely recognised epitopes by the immune hap-
lotypes of the HMP2 cohort, exomes of the 93 individuals of the HMP2 cohort were
subsequently HLA-typed with 2-digit precision, see Figure 33 in Appendix 7.6. In ad-
dition to the identiﬁed MHCII alleles in the HMP2 cohort additional available HLA
models were included resulting in 68 alleles encoding DRB1, DPB1, DQB1 and the
mouse H2-Ab.
The 68 MHCII receptor models were used for epitope prediction parsing the 65.491
candidate genes through netMHCIIpan. The prediction returned 61.679 epitopic genes
containing at least one strong-binding core-epitope motif deﬁned by a scale rank score
< 2% and a total of 948.241 distinct core-epitope motifs. The large number of possible
strong-binding epitopes predicted in the prioritised candidate sequences, suggests that
several possible binding areas exist in the microbiome proteome.

of the TBDT-SusC protein recognised by the mouse MHCII receptor was predicted to
exhibit cross-binding properties with the majority of human MHCII receptors. In addi-

between phyla aﬀected the predicted MHCII binding properties, resulting in phylum
speciﬁc species displaying a higher degree of immune recognition.

have been traced to conserved genomic loci with extensive MHCII-binding. Indeed, the
cross-reactivity observed for the TBDT-associated epitopes with the mouse and human
immune system lead to an ex vivo cytokine assay using human PBMCs.

5.5
Diﬀerential disease-associated epitopes in the HMP2 co-
hort
The search for highly abundant and recognised epitopes from commensal antigens in
both health and disease relies on the premise that the immune health of subjects can be
captured by ex vivo presentation of epitopes to host immune cells. Generated immune
proﬁling data may enable novel insight and characterisation of the adaptive immune
system during inﬂammatory pathologies, however, this unbiased identiﬁcation method
prioritises ’universal’ epitopes and does not address diﬀerentially expressed epitopes as
a response to inﬂammation with disease propagating attributes. By tracing disease-
associated epitopes to their protein origin, novel species-speciﬁc virulence factors with
immune exposure can be identiﬁed and used as a reference for potential therapeutic
applications.
To identify epitopes upregulated in response to disease, 709 metagenomic and metatran-
scriptomic paired samples were leveraged to calculate the individual epitope expression
signal, corrected by epitope abundance. Signiﬁcant diﬀerentially expressed epitopes
were identiﬁed in UC and CD, especially within the two phyla Bacteroidetes and Fir-
micutes (P-value < 0.05 - FDR adjusted - Mann Whitney U test), as seen in Figure
16. The largest signiﬁcance and magnitude of diﬀerential change was also observed
in Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes (Table 4 and 6, Appendix 7.5). In response to dis-
ease, several signiﬁcant diﬀerentially expressed epitopes were identiﬁed in UC and CD,
suggesting that disease related eﬀects on the microbiome aﬀects bacterial expression
patterns resulting in primarily down-regulated epitopic-proteins.

Figure 16: Overview of diﬀerentially expressed epitopes in microbiome phyla from CD and UC. Similar
to the two disease states, within the phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes several epitopes are found
to be down-regulated and up-regulated. Within this subset of diﬀerentially expressed epitopes few
display recognition of up to 50 diﬀerent MHCII receptors.
UC: Patients with Ulcerative colitis, CD: Patients with Chrohn’s disease.
The microbial architecture of IBD patients is highly dysbiotic and dynamic due to
an overreactive immune response against the gut microbiota. Highly abundant species
during disease may sustain this unwanted inﬂammation through an increased expression
of epitopic proteins with or without associated virulence properties. To identify epitopes
with a higher expression signal in either UC or CD, the analyses was focused on the
diﬀerentially expressed epitopes in proteins of highly abundant species in the disease
cohort (Top 2.5% mean species abundance). In CD, multiple epitopes speciﬁc to B.
fragilis were highly upregulated including some epitopes with high predicted immune
recognition (Figure 17 a). Most of the upregulated epitopes of B. fragilis originated
from metabolic or maintenance associated proteins like the TBDTs, furanosidases and
glycosylases, except for the epitopes (Log2fc > 3) in LytB and phosphatidylinositol-
speciﬁc phospholipase C (PI-PLC) (Figure 17 b).

5.6
Investigating the conservation of the TBDTs immunogenic
hotspot
A subset of the suggested coevolutionary mechanisms for bacterial colonisation in the
mammalian gut are based on symbiotic interactions such as bacterial production of SC-
FAs and PSA with anti-inﬂammatory properties allowing bacteria to peacefully co-exist
with the healthy host. While SCFAs are directly absorbed across the gut epithelium,
PSA is transported via bacterially derived OMVs and presented directly to host im-
mune cells along with a suite of molecular cargo, including TBDTs [25, 67]. Following
the assumption that TBDTs do not exhibit non-immunoregulatory properties, yet have
been transported and presented to APCs along with PSA for millenia, this continuous
immune exposure could have lead to highly recognised epitope hotspots as a side ef-
fect of coevolutionary colonisation. In addition, widely recognised genomic sites shaped
over generations may provide bacteria with a colonisation advantage compared to xeno-
colonising species of the human gut, a theory which could be addressed via competitive
colonisation experiments in mice models.
Bacteria have been colonising mammals for millenia, and to establish that the con-
served epitope site in TBDT was present in other mammals than human and mice,
TBDT genes were extracted from 24 Bacteroides genomes derived from a variety of
mammals (Table 3 Appendix 7.3). Compared to other mammalian metagenomes, the
MHCII binding and amino acid sequence pattern was preserved (Figure 29 Appendix
7.3). To address if the conserved pattern could be found in bacteria without immune
system inﬂuence, TBDT plug domains from both gut and environmental bacteria were
necessary to allow such an comparison. An unbiased approach leveraging two stages of
HMM searching was applied to make a plug domain database by searching through the
HMP2 genecatalog, Bacteroides and Prevotella genomes from NCBI’s RefSeq and an
environmental/soil dataset Refsoil primarily containing genomes from Proteobacteria
(Figure 28 in Appendix 7.3).
The HMM search process resulted in 6.040 Plug positive TBDTs sequences in species of
Proteobacteria and 1.116 in species of Bacteroidetes from Refsoil, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 19. From the HMP2 and Refseq, 4.894 and 42.211 Plug positive TBDTs sequences
were mined from species of Bacteroidetes, respectively.
Plug domain extraction from various datasets with gut and environmental bacteria pro-
vided an extensive sequence collection that could be used for massive multiple sequence
alignment.

Assessing the role of variable TBDT plug domains in re-
lation to bacterial antigenicity
Bacteroides such as B. fragilis and B. thetamiotamicron are suggested to deferen-
tially regulate the expression of TBDTs with varying surface antigenicity as a potential
immune-evasion mechanism. To address whether this could be related to the observed
clusters in the phylogenetic tree, an expression comparison analysis was conducted by
grouping the plug domains based on the presence of the LKD and non-LKD sequences
in all members of Bacteroidetes in HMP2.
Of the species with a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in gene expression between the diagnosis
groups, a B. fragilis strain with high prevalence in patients with CD, UC and nonIBD
was identiﬁed, as illustrated in Figure 22. This strain displayed a signiﬁcantly increased
mean expression (abundance corrected) of TBDTs genes with the LKD motif in both
CD and UC (P-value < 0.01, Mann Whitney U test). The increase in gene expression
was also true for TBDTs without the LKD motif and non-TBDTs random genes.

Discussion
IBD is the result of multiple factors such as genetic susceptibility, environmental in-
ﬂuences and life-style. The dysregulated nature of the immune system during IBD is
poorly understood although it is suspected to be sustained by microbial antigens and
inﬂammatory compounds.
Yet, virtually nothing is known about how the adaptive
immune system responds to microbiome antigens and without any known microbiome
antigens, the quantiﬁcation and characterisation of adaptive immune cell dynamics
during gastrointestinal pathologies remains unexplored. Bacterial antigen discovery is
challenging for several reasons: The microbiome represents an enormous genomic search
space, the features of immunodominant epitopes are poorly deﬁned and variable human
immune haplotypes complicate the identiﬁcation of widely recognised antigens.
To address these issues, a computational pipeline has been established by combin-
ing BOTA, which leverages novel experimental proteomic insight into immune cell au-
tophagy, with the state-of-the-art immunoinformatic epitope prediction tool netMHCI-
Ipan.
By applying the pipeline on the data from the HMP2 cohort, several highly
abundant and recognised epitopes within commensal antigens have been predicted in
both healthy controls and patients yielding a rationally deﬁned immunopeptidome with
several epitope candidates for experimental validation of immunogenicity.
Validated immunogenic epitopes in both the healthy and dysbiotic microbiome could
be promising targets for generating experimental immunoproﬁling data in ex vivo stud-
ies which may enable insight into the dynamics of the adaptive immune system dur-
ing mucosal homoeostasis and in response to the microbiome during gastrointestinal
immune-pathologies such as IBD.
6.1
Prevalent epitopes in conserved TBDT SusC proteins stim-
ulates potent cytokine responses ex vivo
With the aim of identifying immunogenic epitopes with high immune exposure in both
the healthy and dysbiotic microbiome, epitopes in secreted and outer membrane pro-
teins also exhibiting high prevalence and expression were predicted by leveraging BOTA,
netMHCIIpan and multi’omic data from the HMP2 cohort.
Several prevalent epitopes could be traced to highly conserved genomic loci with ex-
tensive MHCII-recognition, in several cases originating from proteins exhibiting high
taxonomic distribution. Interestingly, genomic locis in epitopic proteins shared by mul-
tiple phyla, displayed micro-amino acid variations notably inﬂuencing the degree of
predicted MHCII-recognition.
The eﬀect of micro-amino acid variation on immune
recognition and immunogenicity was highlighted by an ex vivo cytokine assay exper-
iment using PBMCs from healthy controls, which were incubated with peptides from
the TBDT-SusC epitope hotspot. Only a fraction of the relatively similar peptides were
capable of stimulating a robust cytokine response in PBMCs from healthy donor with
unknown HLA proﬁles. Importantly, the potent cytokine response which were obtained
comprised: A) IL-10, a key cytokine for the induction of immune-suppressive Tregs [47]
and B) the protective IL-17A cytokine, suggested to support epithelial barrier function
and increase production of epithelial AMP during homoeostasis [19, 68]. The precise
inﬂuence on adaptive immune cells needs to be established with additional experiments
verifying the clonal expansion of Tregs and CD4+ Th17 cells displaying TCRs matching

the TBDT-SusC epitopes.
6.2
Prevalent epitopes are located in bacterial proteins rele-
vant for bacterial adaptation and self-maintenance in the
human gut
The functional proﬁle of the prioritised epitope subset comprising TBDTs, Sus-proteins
and secreted enzymes, suggests that high immune exposure may be achieved through
pathways such as luminal sampling of exogenous proteins by APCs or OMV trans-
portation to local immune cells [69]. The OMV pathway is known to transport pro-
teins involved in inter-bacterial communication, luminal nutrient digestion and bacterial
survival which do not necessarily exhibit immunoregulatory properties. The TBDTs,
enzymes involved in polysaccharide utilisation and peptidoglycan hydrolases represent
typical OMV cargo [24, 25, 70, 71], and constitute the majority of epitopic proteins dis-
covered in the analysis. Prevalent epitopes were also predicted in the following known
virulence factors.
a. The ﬂagella associated proteins hookbasal body protein and ﬂagellar hook protein ﬂge
which supports epithelial cell adhesion in e.g. P. aeruginosa [72]. Yet, ﬂagella proteins
are common and highly abundant in the gut microbiome and may only induce a pro-
inﬂammatory response accompanied with disease inducing toxins [73].
b. The spoiid lytb domain protein/LytB, originating from the versatile protein LytB, an
peptidoglycan hydrolase which can be employed by S. pneumonia for immune evasion
and lung epithelial invasion, but is also a common enzyme for physical separation of
daughter cells [74].
c.
The enzyme 5’nucleotidase, which catalyses the substrate ADP to adenosine an
immune-suppressing compound when bound by A2a receptors on innate immune cells,
is employed by S. pyogenes for immune evasion [75]. However, this enzyme has also
been found in OMV cargo and could be produced by symbiotic bacteria to suppress
unwanted inﬂammation and maintain tolerance [25, 76].
These results highlight the antigenic potential of proteins employed by pathogens dur-
ing invasion. However, these ﬁndings were not surprising since the immune system
does not exhibit any capacity to discriminate between pathogens and symbiotic species
based on their molecular structures. Thus, epitopic proteins presented in this report
provides a suite of predicted antigens, normally employed by bacteria for adaptation
and self-maintenance, with an possible exacerbating inﬂammatory inﬂuence in IBD
patients.
6.3
The TBDT plug domain is highly conserved within Bac-
teroidetes colonising the human gut
Commensal bacteria are suggested to have co-evolved with the mucosal immune system
over millenia, securing their niche in the human gut through symbiotic interactions with
the host leading to tolerisation [16]. The interactions include the production of PSA
which maintains and primes diﬀerentiation of anti-inﬂammatory Tregs through TGF-β
and IL-10 induction [18], which are transported via OMVs and presented directly to
host immune cells along with a suite of molecular cargo, including TBDTs [25, 67].
Based on the observed degree of conservation and immune recognition of the TBDT
Page 40 of 66
==PAGE:42==
Discussion
plug domain, it was questioned whether the immune recognition properties of the do-
main have been shaped as a side-eﬀect of co-evolutionary colonisation. Additionally,
could symbiotic bacteria gain a gut colonisation advantage through immune recogni-
tion, compared to xeno-colonising species? Following the assumption that TBDTs do
not exhibit non-immunoregulatory properties, yet have been transported and presented
to APCs along with PSA for millenia, this continuous immune exposure could have lead
to highly recognised epitope hotspots as a side eﬀect of co-evolutionary colonisation.
To prime future experimental investigation on the inﬂuence of conserved epitope do-
mains for mammalian colonisation, a phylogenetic tree was created through MSA of
TBDT plug domains extracted from both human gut and environmental bacteria. The
phylogenetic tree revealed that a minimal number of TBDTs with the conserved and
highly recognised LKD-motif were present in soil and that the plug-domain was primar-
ily conﬁned to human gut colonisers. To verify a potential advantage, phylogenetically
adjacent gut and soil bacteria need to be identiﬁed and cultivated for a competitive
colonisation experiment in a germ-free mice model. However, no ideal pairs were iden-
tiﬁed in this study. An alternative experiment to assess the inﬂuence of the conserved
epitope site in bacterial colonisation ﬁtness, could be the introduction of mutations to
the conserved epitope site without disturbing protein function followed by transplanta-
tion of human gut species to a germ-free mice model. However, TBDTs are not ideal
proteins for this experiment due to the high number of TBDT encoded homologs in
single species (See Figure 36 Appendix 7.8), instead other functional proteins with sim-
ilar conservation patterns needs to be identiﬁed. Ultimately, the questions regarding
the inﬂuence of conserved and highly recognised epitope domains in human colonising
species are best answered through in vivo experimental setups.
Earlier experiments with xeno-microbiotas have illustrated that ecological succession
in the gut cannot be solely predicted based on the habitat associations of organisms
colonising it [77]. In the same study, the major predictor of colonisation success corre-
lated with the species capacity to metabolise and utilise the available nutrient pool. In
this context, members of Bacteroidetes are highly adaptable species with a plastic and
extensive polysaccharide utilisation loci, which have enabled these species to colonise
virtually all sequenced habitats [27, 78]. These earlier results suggest that even phy-
logenetically distinct Bacteroidetes selected from the gut and soil metagenome could
be leveraged in a competitive colonisation experiment in germ-free mice to asses the
role of conserved epitopes in proteins without a suggested immunomodulatory function.
6.4
Variable MHCII recognition of TBDT plug domains did
not yield new information in bacterial immune evasion
mechanisms
Bacterial pathogens and viruses are known to escape immune surveillance by harbour-
ing hypermutations in surface exposed proteins and by altering surface antigenicity
through phasing and antigenic variation mechanisms [79, 80]. Similarly, opportunis-
tic pathogens such as B. fragilis are capable of assuming a symbiotic, commensal and
pathogenic role in the microbiome [27], where the latter may be successfully realised
through a phasing-like mechanism, enabling B. fragilis to change surface antigenicity
and avoid immune recognition [71, 81]. Novel evidence for this mechanism was sought
Page 41 of 66
==PAGE:43==
Discussion
by comparing expression proﬁles of species-speciﬁc TBDT genes, with and without a
widely recognised and conserved plug domain, in the healthy and inﬂamed microbiome.
A signiﬁcant up-regulation of TBDT with and without the conserved plug domain ex-
pression was observed by comparing nonIBD to UC and CD in the species B. fragilis and
P. distasonis. However, non-TBDT genes were similarly up-regulated when comparing
UC and CD to nonIBD, suggesting that B. fragilis is overall more transcriptionally
active in the inﬂamed gut.
Even if diﬀerential expression was observed between the two compared gene groups,
a highly explanatory variable for diﬀerential expression is the available nutrient pool.
Bacteroides species express hundreds of TBDT-related genes with varying substrate
speciﬁcity, which enables these species to adapt during conditions aﬀecting the avail-
able nutrient pool.
Based on this complex gene-model, the grouping of the TBDT
genes may have been too simple as it did not capture the complexity of the underlying
inversion system employed by B. fragilis to change surface antigenicity. Further char-
acterisation of the underlying gene loci is likely necessary before their role in bacterial
immune evasion mechanisms can be properly determined with follow up studies.
6.5
Diﬀerentially expressed epitopes in IBD revealed two pos-
sible virulent properties of B. fragilis
Tracing disease-associated epitopes to their protein origin may reveal species-speciﬁc
virulence factors which are employed by opportunistic pathogens and drive an aberrant
immune response during IBD. Certain members of the phylum Proteobacteria are sug-
gested to assume an opportunistic pathogenic role during IBD through multiple strate-
gies that sustains their own survival and contributes to disease [82, 83]. For instance,
the adherent-invasive Escherichia coli (AIEC) is known to cause epithelial barrier dys-
function by selectively targeting tight junctions leading to intestinal permeability and
inﬂammation [84]. In a similar fashion, certain B. Fragilis strains produce enterotoxins
that damages the epithelium leading to translocating bacteria and excessive immune
activation [27].
To identify relevant proteins leveraged by opportunistic pathogens for disease aggra-
vation or survival during IBD, epitope predictions were utilised for the identiﬁcation
of up-regulated specie-speciﬁc proteins in abundant species of IBD. Two proteins with
established virulence properties including LytB and PI-PLC were both found to be
highly up-regulated by B. Fragilis during UC and CD and possibly enables the strain
to cope with inﬂammation. LytB is employed by certain pathogens for immune evasion
and epithelial invasion [74] while PI-PLC promotes survival and immune-evasion of S.
aureus from innate immune cells [85]. The presented expression patterns suggest that
the B. fragilis strain is actually thriving during inﬂammation and based on its abun-
dance, appears to be widely distributed amongst the diseased patients of the HMP2
cohort. These observations corresponds well with the above mentioned results from
the variable TBDT plug domain expression analysis and suggests that the PI-PLC and
LytB up-regulation could be the result of overall higher transcriptional activity in B.
fragilis and demands further analysis. Arguably, the exact way that B. fragilis employs
LytB and PI-PLC during disease needs to be experimentally veriﬁed.

6.6
Conclusive summary
The multifaceted nature of the IBD presents a major challenge with respect to the iden-
tiﬁcation of possible pathological triggers. Genetic susceptibility, environmental factors
and the gut microbiota are all suggested to contribute to disease onset and the con-
tinuation of pathology. The immunopathology in IBD patients is largely characterised
by an dysregulated and hyper-reactive immune response toward the gut microbiota,
resulting in microbial perturbations. Reduced mucosal barrier integrity and bacterial
translocation prompts a continuous renewal of the pro-inﬂammatory immune response
which sustains the persistent state of inﬂammation. Microbiome sequencing projects
have displayed progress in identifying taxonomic structures of the dysbiotic gut micro-
biome in IBD patients. However, the adaptive arm of the immune system in response
to microbial members remains largely uncharacterised.
The host CD4+ T-cell response is generally restricted to a subset of epitopes exhibiting
immunodominant properties shaped by autophagy that governs antigen processing and
presentation, this subset was sought by integrating BOTA and netMHCIIpan into a
computational pipeline used to establish the immunopeptidome of both healthy control
and IBD patients. Experimentally validated immunogenic epitopes derived from com-
mensal antigens can be leveraged in an experimental immunoproﬁling assay pipeline
to proﬁle the adaptive immune cell response from host T-cells derived from healthy
controls and patients with IBD.
To identify the immunogenic candidates with a consistent and high immune exposure,
metatranscriptomics and metagenomics were integrated to capture highly prevalent and
expressed epitopes.
Several of these epitopes were traced to highly conserved genomic loci with extensive
MHCII-recognition in proteins exhibiting high taxonomic distribution.
In addition,
epitopic proteins were found to be relevant for polysaccharide uptake and digestion in
microbial self-maintenance and inter-bacterial interactions, where the latter is likely
governed by OMV transportation. In addition, prevalent epitopes were also predicted
in the protein LytB, which expression seems to be up-regulated by an abundant B. frag-
ilis in IBD, together with PI-PLC which are known virulence proteins in pathogens S.
pneumonia and S. aureus. These epitope predictions highlighted a potential pathogenic
B. fragilis in the HMP2 cohort but also their utility in identifying potential species-
speciﬁc pathogenic features in disease.
A large correspondence was observed between the functional annotations of the pre-
dicted epitopic protein set and typical OMV protein cargo, which enables direct trans-
portation and presentation to immune eﬀector cells [25]. A typical class of proteins
often found in OMV cargo is TBDTs. In addition, based on the predictions of the es-
tablished pipeline, TBDTs encode several highly prevalent epitopes localized in a highly
conserved loci of the TBDT plug-domain with extensive immune recognition. It was
questioned whether these properties of the domain reﬂect co-evolutionary mammalian
colonisation optimisation. To prime further experimental investigation, a phylogenetic
tree was constructed based on the database of TBDT plug-domains mined from envi-
ronmental and gut bacteria, which helped in identifying two highly distinct clusters of
plug-domains. Importantly, the largest of these clusters was primarily conﬁned to Bac-
teroidetes containing the highly conserved and recognised plug-domain, which draws
a route for gut microbes to achieve higher immune exposure relative to soil bacteria.

Future studies will have to reveal if immune exposure proves an advantage to human
gut colonising species.
In summary, the established epitope prediction pipeline has enabled the identiﬁcation of
several conserved epitopic regions of commensal antigens, encoding highly prevalent and
expressed epitopes. Indeed some epitopic regions have been predicted to be recognised
by the immune system in patients of various immune haplotypes, providing a high
sociodemographic coverage in medical applications.
Promising experimental results
have been generated on TBDT-SusC epitopes capable of inducing IL-10 and IL-17A in
PBMCs from healthy human controls and needs to further analysed with clonal T-cell
experiments. Additional epitopes with experimentally veriﬁed immunogenicity repre-
sent ideal targets for quantifying the adaptive immune cell response. More importantly,
veriﬁed immunogenic epitopes may enable insight into the dynamics of the adaptive im-
mune system during mucosal homoeostasis and in response to the microbiome during
gastrointestinal immune-pathologies such as IBD. This insight may unlock the genera-
tion of future antigen-speciﬁc applications for diagnosing the onset and progression of
IBD.
6.7
Future directions
The impact of amino-acid variation in TBDT-SusC epitopes on the immune response
in the ex vivo cytokine expression experiment using PBMCs, illustrated that even tiny
variations in protein sequences can alter MHCII-binding and T cell activation. Yet,
these results also indicated the value of the current pipeline that identiﬁed peptide
sequences with epitopes capable of inducing a potent IL-10 and IL-17A response. Ad-
ditional experimental validation will have to assert these preliminary results and overall
guide the project in the next steps toward identifying strong epitope candidates in vivo.
On the computational side, additional bioinformatic analysis can be conducted by inte-
grating additional databases to A) support the relevance of predicted epitope candidates
or B) aid in identifying relevant proteins for colonisation experiments in germ-free mice
models.
Integrating additional metagenomic datasets
The massive search space reduction of the HMP2 commensal proteome enabled with
a multi’omic dataset, has been imperative to identify a manageable number of strong
candidates for experimental validation. Based on this subset of relevant commensal
antigens, additional metagenomic datasets could be mined for similar functional pro-
teins and leveraged to expand the genomic conservation analysis of epitopic hotspots
identiﬁed in the commensal proteome of HMP2. For instance, the Glucose galactose
binding lipoprotein identiﬁed in Firmicutes species displayed an high degree of MHCII-
binding based on a conserved region with few distinct epitopes. However, this protein
was represented by few taxonomically annotated sequences and could beneﬁt from the
integration of additional data.
Additional sequence representatives may also aid in identifying proteins in phyloge-
netically adjacent gut and soil bacteria, displaying conserved sequence and immune
recognition patterns similar to TBDTs. Given that these proteins are found in lower
copy-numbers they would also represent more ideal candidates for mutation experi-
ments.
