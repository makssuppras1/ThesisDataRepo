Abstract
The advent of large-scale astronomical surveys, such as COSMOS-Web, demands ef-
ficient methods to analyze high-dimensional photometric datasets. Traditional spectral
energy distribution (SED) fitting techniques, while robust, face computational bottlenecks
when applied to millions of galaxies.
This thesis explores two machine learning ap-
proaches to address these challenges: a dimensionality reduction method using Uniform
Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) and a multi-task deep neural network de-
signed for simultaneous SED reconstruction and photometric redshift estimation.
UMAP projects high-dimensional photometry into a 3D latent space, allowing fast param-
eter inference via local neighborhood analysis. Applied to the COSMOS-Web catalog,
it achieves a median absolute deviation (MAD) of 0.22 for galaxies with spectroscopic
redshifts, and 0.465 when compared against the full catalog’s photo-z measurements.
The deep learning model uses an encoder-decoder architecture and provides redshift
predictions along with an estimate of their uncertainty. Trained on synthetic spectra and
validated against COSMOS-Web, it outperforms UMAP, achieving a MAD of 0.105 for
spectroscopic redshifts and 0.242 when compared to photo-z values, while also recon-
structing broadband spectral features. By incorporating spectral reconstruction as an aux-
iliary task, the model learns a latent space structured around the underlying SED, resulting
in improved redshift predictions.
Both methods demonstrate scalability, processing hundreds of thousands of galaxies in
minutes. The results highlight the potential of machine learning to accelerate the analysis
of next-generation surveys.

Fundamental Properties of Galaxies
1.1
Overview of Galaxies and Their Formation
Galaxies are gravitationally bound collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter, residing
within the potential wells of dark matter halos. In the standard ΛCDM cosmological model
[1], galaxy formation begins when small density fluctuations in the early universe collapse
to form dark matter halos [2, 3]. Baryonic matter then cools and accretes into these
halos from the intergalactic medium, starting star formation and forming the initial stellar
populations [4, 5]. Over time, mergers and gas accretion shape galaxies into the diverse
and complex systems observed in the modern universe [6].
Galaxies are often classified by their morphology; elliptical, spiral, lenticular, or irregu-
lar, with spirals and ellipticals being the most commonly observed types [7, 8]. Elliptical
galaxies have a smooth, near ellipsoidal appearance and are typically dominated by older,
lower-mass stars, which gives them a reddish hue [9]. They generally contain little gas
and dust, resulting in low or negligible star formation. By contrast, spiral galaxies have a
rotating disk structure with prominent spiral arms made up by stars, gas, and dust. These
arms often contain active star-forming regions with bright, blue, short-lived stars, while a
dense, more spheroidal bulge, often hosting a supermassive black hole, is at the galaxy’s
center. Lenticular galaxies appear as an intermediate class between ellipticals and spi-
rals. They are disk shaped but lack spiral arms, and often have intermediate levels of star
formation. Irregular galaxies lack a distinct shape and tend to have chaotic appearances,
often as a result of gravitational interactions or mergers [10].
Figure 1.1: Hickson Compact Group 92, Stephan’s Quintet. HST WFC3 [11].
Beyond basic morphology, galaxies are also part of larger structures such as groups,
clusters, and filaments of the cosmic web [12]. The cosmic environment in which galax-
ies inhabit has a large effect on galaxy evolution. For instance, galaxies in dense cluster
environments can go through processes like ram-pressure stripping, which removes their
gas [13], or tidal interactions. Both processes suppress star formation and may cause
morphological transformations [14, 15]. Galaxies in more isolated environments may ac-
crete gas over longer timescales, allowing them to sustain star formation activity [16].

Observational surveys (e.g., the Sloan Digital Sky Survey [17], the Cosmological Evolution
Survey (COSMOS) [18], and more recently, JWST surveys [19]) show how galaxy proper-
ties such as mass, star formation rate, and dust content evolve with time. Observational
results, combined with theoretical models and simulations, reveal that galaxy evolution
comes from a number interconnected processes. Together, these processes decide how
galaxies grow and what their structure looks like. Modern galaxy formation models include
a step-by-step assembly process in which galaxies grow through a complex interaction
of internal and external factors [20, 21]. Star formation bursts are regulated by feedback
from stars and supermassive black holes, which control the cold gas supply and influ-
ence the timing and efficiency of future star formation. Tidal interactions and accretion
events reshape galaxy structure over time. Together, these factors shape the timing of
star formation, chemical enrichment, mass growth, and overall galaxy evolution.

Interpreting galaxy observations across redshifts is complicated by the presence of dust
[25, 26, 27]. Galactic dust grains, mostly made up of carbonaceous and silicate parti-
cles, interact with starlight by absorbing and scattering photons, especially in the UV and
optical bands, and then re-emit the absorbed energy in the infrared [25, 28]. One of the
primary effects of dust is the attenuation of starlight, which can make young, UV-bright
stars appear much dimmer. Without accounting for and correcting for dust, estimates of
a galaxy’s star formation rate and stellar mass can be systematically underestimated [27,
29]. The irregular absorption and re-emission of light by dust has a large impact on ob-
servations of high-redshift galaxies, where intervening systems along the line of sight can
worsen the challenges of distinguishing intrinsic dust effects from those external to the
galaxy [30].
The thermal re-emission of absorbed radiation results in strong far-infrared and submil-
limeter emission, making dust-rich galaxies, such as starbursts or those hosting active
galactic nuclei, especially luminous at these wavelengths [31]. To correct for dust effects,

1.4.1
Multiwavelength Observations and Catalogs
Because dust and redshift together can drastically change the observed spectral energy
distribution of a galaxy, one of the most effective ways to derive physical parameters is to
combine observations taken across the electromagnetic spectrum. Data spanning from
UV to radio can help extract the relative contributions of unobscured stellar light and dust-
reprocessed emission.
Multiwavelength surveys like SDSS (optical), 2MASS (near-infrared), and Herschel (in-
frared) have shown that using data across multiple wavelengths gives a clearer picture
of galaxy evolution. At higher redshifts (z ≳2), features like the Lyman and Balmer
breaks, along with key emission lines, shift into the near-infrared and beyond. This means
ground-based optical observations miss important details about the stellar populations.
Space-based infrared data fill in these gaps, capturing rest-frame UV-to-optical light at
early times. Together, these multiwavelength datasets help astronomers better separate
stellar emission from dust effects and infrared re-emission, improving galaxy SED mod-
eling [35].
The growing complexity and volume of such multiwavelength catalogs have spurred the
development of new approaches to data analysis. Traditional SED-fitting methods (LePhare
[36], CIGALE [37]) generally rely on searching large grids of galaxy models, which can
be time-consuming for massive catalogs. As observational datasets expand in both sky
coverage and wavelength range, adopting strategies that more efficiently extract phys-
ical parameters—while remaining robust against dust, redshift, and other confounding
factors—has become a key priority. Machine learning and deep learning techniques of-
fer promising pathways to handle the complexity of such catalogs, potentially reducing
computational bottlenecks without compromising accuracy.
1.5
Spectral Energy Distribution Fitting
Spectral energy distribution (SED) fitting is a fundamental technique for inferring the phys-
ical properties of galaxies from their observed fluxes across multiple wavelength bands.
In its most classical form, SED fitting proceeds by comparing measured photometry or
spectra to sets of model templates, each of which encodes assumptions about stellar
populations, dust, star formation histories, and metallicities. The aim is to identify the
model that best reproduces the observed data and, in doing so, to constrain parameters
such as stellar mass, star formation rate, age, dust attenuation, and sometimes redshift.
Because galaxies often span a wide range of intrinsic and extrinsic properties, no single
model family can capture every diversity. As a result, a successful SED-fitting framework
often requires well-considered approximations and a carefully chosen parameter space.
At the heart of most SED-fitting methods lies stellar population synthesis (SPS) modeling,
which predicts the luminosity contribution from stellar populations at different ages and
metallicities. These theoretical or semi-empirical models are then convolved with filter
transmission curves to match specific survey passbands. Additional layers of physics,
such as nebular emission lines and continuum, are often included. Dust attenuation is
incorporated through models such as in [27] or [26], which specify how dust absorbs and
scatters light.

Traditional SED-fitting codes, such as LePhare [36] and CIGALE [37], iterate through a
multidimensional grid of models. For each galaxy, these programs compute synthetic pho-
tometry from each template and compare it to the observed fluxes. The level of agreement
is quantified to see how well the model predicts the data within the reported uncertainties.
The output is the set of parameter values associated with the best fit template. Since
these programs search through large parameter grids, they most often provide a robust
solution, but they can be computationally intensive. As surveys expand, both in terms of
the number of galaxies and the wavelength coverage, the size of these template grids can
grow dramatically, motivating efforts to find more efficient approaches.
Photometric redshift estimation (photo-z) is often performed in tandem with SED fitting.
If spectroscopy is not available, the redshift becomes an additional free parameter to be
constrained. In this setting, SED-fitting pipelines attempt to match not only the broadband
shape of the galaxy spectrum but also specific spectral breaks, such as the Balmer or
4000 Å break, and in some cases strong emission lines, as they shift through the filter set
with increasing redshift.
Bayesian approaches to SED fitting have gained popularity because they return posterior
probability distributions rather than single best-fit values. By applying priors on parame-
ters like star formation histories or dust attenuation, Bayesian methods can more naturally
capture degeneracies. While Bayesian sampling can be computationally more expensive
than simple χ2 minimization, it can give a fuller picture of how uncertainties on different
parameters correlate with each other.
In recent years, a promising approach for improving both speed and flexibility in SED fitting
has emerged; machine learning. Neural networks, trained on synthetic model libraries
or on subsets of galaxies with reliable spectroscopic measurements, have been applied
to predict properties such as stellar mass or photometric redshift. Once trained, these
networks can process new datasets of hundreds of thousands of galaxies with much less
runtime than traditional methods. However, purely data-driven methods can be prone to
biases if the training sets are not representative or if the models fail to extrapolate beyond
the parameter range on which they were trained.
1.6
Cosmic Star Formation History and the Role of JWST
The cosmic star formation history describes the global star formation rate density as a
function of redshift, indicating when most stars were formed in the universe [38]. Mea-
surements of the SFRD rely on multi-wavelength observations of galaxies, each providing
a snapshot of the universe at a particular epoch. These measurements extend from the
local universe out to high redshifts, where detections become increasingly difficult due to
the faintness of early galaxies. Observations at UV, optical, and infrared wavelengths help
constrain the star formation rate. UV continuum and nebular emission lines trace short-
lived massive stars, while infrared measurements capture reprocessed light from dusty
regions. Early surveys using telescopes such as the Hubble Space Telescope (HST) and
the Spitzer Space Telescope established that the star formation rate density increases
from the present day to around z ∼2–3, then decreases at higher redshifts [38, 6].
To improve constraints on the cosmic star formation history, JWST’s infrared coverage
is provided by two main imaging instruments, NIRCam and MIRI, which together span
roughly 0.6–28 μm. NIRCam operates at near-infrared wavelengths from about 0.6 to 5
μm, while MIRI extends into the mid-infrared from about 5 to 28 μm [39, 40, 41]. JWST’s
Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec) provides spectroscopy between approximately
0.6 and 5 µm, allowing detailed characterization of emission lines and spectral features

in distant galaxies.
This broad range of wavelengths allows JWST to observe distant galaxies that formed
within the first billion years of cosmic history. NIRCam’s near-infrared filters capture rest-
frame optical and UV features at redshifts z ≳6. At such redshifts, emission lines like Hα,
Hβ, and [O III] are shifted into the NIRCam passbands. In addition, NIRCam is sensitive to
the stellar continuum emitted by intermediate- and low-mass stars, which helps estimate
total stellar masses. At z > 10, this rest-frame optical regime moves into NIRCam’s
coverage.
MIRI observes longer wavelengths, where dust re-emits absorbed starlight as thermal
infrared radiation. Dusty star-forming regions that may be faint or undetected at shorter
wavelengths can become prominent at 5–28,µm. MIRI therefore adds information about
dust-obscured star formation, an important component of the cosmic star formation history
at high redshift. At very high redshifts (z > 10), MIRI can also detect stellar emission
directly, as stellar light shifts into the mid-infrared range [27]. By detecting emission from
polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and thermal continuum emission from warm
dust, MIRI enables estimates of star formation rates in heavily obscured environments
that might otherwise be missed in ultraviolet or optical surveys. Together, NIRCam and
MIRI build a continuous view of galaxy properties from the near- to mid-infrared. Initial
results using these wavelengths suggest that galaxies at z ≳10 can form stars more
rapidly and earlier than previously thought, implying that the galaxies were more active in
the first billion years of cosmic history than previously thought [19].

The study of galaxy properties lies at the heart of modern astrophysics. As new tele-
scopes and instruments continually expand the volume and quality of observational data,
astronomers face the challenge of developing efficient and accurate methods for analyz-
ing these datasets. This challenge is evident in the context of spectral energy distribu-
tion (SED) fitting, where one attempts to infer the underlying physical parameters of a
galaxy by comparing its observed photometry (broadband fluxes) to theoretical or empir-
ical model spectra.

redshifts to identify the best-fitting model. The computational load grows disproportion-
ately with the size of the catalog and the complexity of the model space.
The increasing dimensionality of astronomical surveys, which may measure fluxes through
dozens of filters, presents an opportunity. Such high-dimensional data raise the question
of whether there exists a meaningful embedding of galaxies, a low-dimensional repre-
sentation that preserves the essential features of each object’s photometric or spectral
energy distribution. If such an embedding can be learned, it could form the basis for rapid
parameter estimation, efficient clustering of galaxy populations, or even automated outlier
detection. Machine learning algorithms, and deep learning techniques, in particular, have
shown promise in tackling problems of comparable complexity in other fields. The possi-
bility of achieving an accuracy comparable to or better than grid-based methods, coupled
with significantly reduced computation times, has motivated this research. In essence, if
we can show that machine learning models capture the complex relationships between
observed fluxes and underlying astrophysical parameters, we open the door to analyzing
large and complex galaxy catalogs with relative ease. This becomes especially impor-
tant as missions like Euclid [43] will soon provide catalogs containing billions of sources,
making efficient and accurate analysis methods essential.

Dimensionality Reduction and
Clustering
The high dimensionality of modern astronomical datasets poses considerable challenges
for analysis, as relationships among data points can become obscured when the number
of variables or features grows large. This chapter explores a method that has gained
prominence in recent years—Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP).
3.1
Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP)
UMAP is a nonlinear dimensionality reduction algorithm grounded in manifold learning.
The method presumes that high-dimensional data lie on a manifold that can be mod-
eled accurately in fewer dimensions. Unlike linear techniques (e.g., PCA), UMAP cap-
tures local and global data structure. UMAP first identifies the nearest neighbors of each
data point and constructs a weighted graph where edge strengths reflect similarity—
closer points have stronger connections. It then estimates the underlying data structure
by modeling this graph as a fuzzy topological representation, capturing both local and
global relationships. To embed the data in a lower-dimensional space, UMAP optimizes
a layout that preserves these relationships. It minimizes the difference between the orig-
inal high-dimensional structure and the low-dimensional representation, keeping nearby
points clustered while maintaining separation between distant points. This balance allows
UMAP to show patterns and structures that might be hidden in high-dimensional space.

3.1.2
Fuzzy Topological Representation and Optimization
After constructing the weighted graph, UMAP interprets the resulting structure as a fuzzy
simplicial set, where each edge weight wij represents the strength of the connection
between points.
The goal of the embedding step is to find a low-dimensional layout
{y1, y2, . . . , yN} ⊂Rd (with d ≪D) that preserves these fuzzy relationships.
UMAP uses stochastic gradient descent to minimize the cross-entropy between the high-
dimensional and low-dimensional fuzzy sets. Points that are strongly connected (high
wij) in the original space are encouraged to remain close in the reduced space, while
points with weaker connections are not forced to cluster. By balancing local neighborhood
preservation and global structure, UMAP often reveals manifold geometry more effectively
than linear projections.
3.1.3
Key Parameters: n_neighbors and min_dist
Two of the most important parameters in UMAP are n_neighbors (number of neighbors)
and min_dist, as they decide how the algorithm balances local versus global structure
in the final embedding. The parameter n_neighbors controls the size of the local neigh-
borhood used in the high-dimensional graph construction. Larger values of n_neighbors
allow each data point to connect with more neighbors, making the embedding more glob-
ally consistent and smoothing out local fluctuations, while smaller values encourages of
local relationships to preserve at the expense of global coherence.
The parameter min_dist dictates the minimum spacing between points in the low-dimensional
space. A smaller min_dist encourages UMAP to pack clusters more tightly, revealing
fine-scale structure and sharper separations. A larger min_dist increases the distance
between points in the embedding, which can help preserve more global features but may
blur or merge nearby clusters. Although the parameter has a somewhat meaningful effect
on the embedding, [51] rightfully views it as an essentially aesthetic parameter, governing
the appearance of the embedding.

4.1
Rationale
4.1.1
UMAP
Modern astrophysical surveys produce large photometric datasets in high-dimensional
spaces, making direct analysis challenging due to the “curse of dimensionality” [52]. Tra-
ditional linear methods like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) often fail to capture com-
plex nonlinear relationships, and manifold learning techniques such as t-SNE can be
prohibitively slow for large datasets. UMAP strikes a balance between computational
efficiency and the preservation of local and global structures in the data [51]. Its hyper-
parameters allow users to fine-tune how much local manifold structure or broader global
relationships are represented.
While UMAP preserves local neighborhoods, it does not strictly preserve pairwise dis-
tances between clusters. Groups of points may appear visually well-separated even if
Machine Learning Approaches to JWST data
19
==PAGE:28==
their separation in the high-dimensional space is less pronounced. This makes it impor-
tant to assess whether the reduced embedding genuinely reflects physical or astrophysi-
cal distinctions, rather than relying solely on apparent spatial separations [51].
4.1.2
k-Nearest Neighbors
Once the photometric data have been projected into a lower-dimensional space by UMAP,
we use k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) to predict various galaxy parameters. kNN is a simple
but effective algorithm that classifies or regresses based on the properties of local neigh-
bors in the embedding. This approach is non-parametric, meaning it does not require
training a complex model, and it directly uses the structure UMAP provides.
In a typical application, each new galaxy is mapped into the UMAP space, and the algo-
rithm finds the k points closest to it (according to some distance metric, usually Euclidean)
among the labeled examples. The new galaxy’s parameter—e.g., photometric redshift—
can then be inferred by averaging or taking a weighted combination of the corresponding
labels of the k neighbors. This method is computationally straightforward and benefits
from the locality-preserving properties of UMAP. However, its accuracy depends on the
density of labeled samples in the embedding: sparse regions lead to less reliable predic-
tions, underscoring the importance of robust dimensionality reduction that places similar
galaxies in proximity.
4.2
UMAP Implementation Details
Several hyperparameters in UMAP influence how well the embedding captures meaning-
ful structure in high-dimensional photometric data. The most important are n_neighbors,
which sets the size of each point’s local neighborhood; min_dist, which governs how tightly
points can be packed in the reduced space; and n_components, the dimensionality of the
final embedding.
In our experiments, we fix n_components to 3. One motivation is that we often visualize
data in three-dimensional space, so having a 3D embedding aligns with our intuitive sense
of spatial organization. In practice, two dimensions can sometimes blur or overlap clusters
that are separable, and higher dimensions can be harder to interpret or may dilute local
structure. A three-dimensional embedding is a reasonable balance between simplicity
and detail.
We explore n_neighbors and min_dist through a grid search:
n_neighbors ∈{5, 10, 20, 30, 50},
min_dist ∈{0.0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0}.
By testing these ranges, we identify the combination of neighborhood size and minimum
distance that gives the most coherent separation of galaxy populations in the reduced
space. For each combination, the resulting embedding was evaluated against by project-
ing the COSMOS-Web data into the embedded space. Once the best parameter set was
determined, we adopted that embedding for the rest of the analyses. We used a GPU-
accelerated implementation of UMAP through NVIDIA’s RAPIDS library, reducing the run-
time and memory overhead compared to CPU-based methods. The pipeline first loads
the photometric data, then iterates over a range of values for n_neighbors and min_dist.
For each pair of hyperparameters, UMAP is fit to the data, yielding a low-dimensional
embedding (Step 1 in Figure 4.1).
4.3
k-Nearest Neighbors for Parameter Prediction
After creating a suitable low-dimensional embedding, the next step (Figure 4.1) is to pre-
dict galaxy parameters using the kNN algorithm. This method relies on the idea that local
20

==PAGE:29==
neighborhoods in the UMAP space correspond to galaxies with similar physical properties.
The workflow begins by splitting the dataset into a labeled subset, which has known spec-
troscopic or high-quality photometric redshifts, and an unlabeled subset with uncertain
parameters. UMAP is fit on the labeled set, and the unlabeled data are projected into the
same low-dimensional space. For each new galaxy in the unlabeled set, kNN identifies
the k labeled galaxies that lie closest in the embedding, usually under a Euclidean dis-
tance metric. The redshift of the unlabeled galaxy is then estimated by aggregating the
neighbors’ redshifts (e.g., by taking their average or median).
This method uses the locality-preserving property of UMAP, since galaxies with similar
spectra tend to appear nearby in the embedding, thus increasing confidence that their
physical parameters will also be similar. Figure 4.1 shows an example of how redshift es-
timation works in the combined UMAP–kNN framework. This two-step method is not com-
putationally intensive, even for large photometric surveys, and provides an interpretable
way to understand how galaxies cluster in lower dimensions based on their observed
properties.

6
Multi-Task Neural Networks
After initially applying UMAP, which gave promising results, we were motivated to develop
a neural network of our own. The rationale was to have more control over the latent
representation of the galaxies, and to be able to apply more specific transformations on
the data. This chapter describes the theoretical ideas underpinning our deep learning
approach for SED fitting. We begin with basic principles of neural networks and build up to
modern components such as normalization methods, residual connections, and attention
mechanisms. We then discuss 1D convolution in the context of spectral data, highlight
channel-wise squeeze-and-excitation for reweighting wavelengths, and explain why group
normalization can be preferable to batch normalization for astrophysical datasets. Finally,
we address multi-task learning with uncertainty estimation, focusing on specialized loss
functions for simultaneous spectrum reconstruction and probabilistic redshift prediction.
6.1
Foundations of Neural Networks
A neural network can be viewed as a function that maps an input vector to an output
vector through multiple layers of parameterized transformations. Let x be an input (e.g.
broadband flux measurements) and y be a target (e.g. a reconstructed spectrum or a
redshift). The network’s layers consist of weight matrices and bias terms, which, when
trained, let the model approximate complex relationships between x and y.
Each layer generally computes:
z = W x + b,
h = σ(z),
where W and b are trainable parameters, and σ(·) is a nonlinear activation function. By
chaining multiple layers, we obtain a flexible, hierarchical representation of data.
6.1.1

While dense layers treat each input dimension as independent, convolutional layers use
spatial or sequential structure by applying local “filters” (kernels). In 1D data such as
spectra, each convolutional kernel slides over wavelength bins:

6.1.2
Activation Functions
Activation functions essentially decide whether a neuron should be activated or not. They
help train neural networks by introducing gradients that allow the network to adjust weights
during training. A neural network without activation functions is just a linear regression
model. Common choices include ReLU = max(0, x) and Leaky ReLU = max(0.01 x, x).
The latter avoids zero gradients when activations become negative.

Group normalization is a layer that normalizes features by dividing channels into groups
and normalizing within each group. It does not rely on the batch dimension.
6.2.2
Channel-Wise Squeeze-and-Excitation for 1D Signals
Squeeze-and-Excitation blocks [53] reweight feature channels to highlight important as-
pects of the signal. In 1D spectra, each channel typically corresponds to a distinct set
of learned filters (or intermediate feature maps) at each wavelength position. SE uses
global pooling along the wavelength dimension to produce a summary vector of shape
(batch, channels). Fully connected layers then learn how to amplify or suppress each
channel, returning a set of excitation factors in [0, 1].

6.2.3
Attention Mechanisms for Long-Range Dependencies
Attention mechanisms [54] allow each position in a sequence to attend to every other
position, guided by similarity scores. In 1D spectra, attention can capture correlations
between widely separated wavelength bins. For example, far-ultraviolet lines can relate
to infrared features if they trace the same physical processes. A multi-head self-attention
layer aggregates information across the entire spectrum.

latent representation. Let x be the photometry and z be the latent vector produced by the
encoder:

In this chapter, we apply the theoretical principles from chapter 6 to design and train a
deep neural network that reconstructs galaxy spectra and predicts redshifts. Unlike the
UMAP model, here we only predict redshift and not galaxy mass. Our primary goal is
to infer a high-resolution SED from sparse broadband photometry, while simultaneously
estimating the redshift of each galaxy. We share an encoder among the reconstruction
and redshift-prediction tasks, which pushes the model to learn features that are useful for
both. By joining these objectives we encourage the model to learn a latent representation
that closely reflects the true distribution of galaxies.
7.1

Our approach uses an encoder–decoder framework as described in chapter 6. The en-
coder compresses broadband fluxes and a detection mask into a latent 2D representation,
which the decoder then expands into a reconstructed spectrum. The redshift part of the
model then predicts a mean and log-variance for the galaxy’s redshift. The encoder in-
gests a concatenated vector of photometry and the detection mask. We apply several
dense layers with batch normalization and leaky ReLU to transform the inputs into a com-
pact representation.

7.2
Decoder Architecture for Spectrum Reconstruction
Each decoder stage includes a residual block that helps stabilize gradient flow in deep
convolutional stacks. Unlike the encoder, we use group normalization instead of batch
normalization. GN provides consistent statistics per group of channels, avoiding the vari-
ability that BN experiences when batches are small or non-uniform.
To selectively emphasize important channels, each block includes a squeeze-and-excitation
module. Global average pooling along the spectral axis “squeezes” features, and a small
fully connected stack “excites” channels by reweighting them in [0, 1]. This is particularly
useful for spectra, where some ranges may be more informative than others.
We apply 1D convolutions to model local features, and then multi-head self-attention to
capture long-range interactions. Convolutional filters are good at smoothing noise and
detecting peaks, but attention can link widely separated wavelength bins (e.g. correlated
UV and IR flux).
7.3
Redshift Head
After the decoder reconstructs the high-resolution spectrum, we pool the latent represen-
tation to produce two scalars: the redshift mean µ and log-variance log σ2. Predicting
both mean and variance encodes heteroscedastic uncertainty. We use an adaptive aver-
age pooling along the latent’s spectral dimension and feed the result into a small dense
network. Layer normalization or batch normalization can be used here; the key point is
that each galaxy’s latent vector is mapped to a pair (µ, log(σ2)).

9
Conclusion and Discussion
We introduced two main approaches for analyzing large surveys. The first was a UMAP-
based method combined with kNN that embeds and transforms photometric data from
a high dimensional space down to three dimensions, then infers galaxy properties from
local neighborhoods. The second was a multi-task neural network (deep learning, DL) that
reconstructs spectra and predicts redshift at the same time. We used synthetic galaxies
with known parameters [49] to train or validate our models. Finally, we tested them on the
COSMOS-Web dataset [44] and compared against LePhare’s fitted parameters.
9.1
Key Findings
UMAP:
UMAP effectively compressed high-dimensional photometry into a low-dimensional space
while preserving local relationships.
Galaxies with similar redshifts or stellar masses
tended to cluster together, showing that these parameters shape the underlying mani-
fold. kNN regression in that embedding gave redshift estimates with a median absolute
deviation (MAD) around 0.22–0.23 compared with zspec. Although less accurate than tra-
ditional SED-fitting codes, UMAP is very fast. It can thus serve as a quick way to explore
datasets or to flag outliers.
Deep Learning Model:
Our network predicted redshifts with a 0.105 MAD vs. zspec, outperforming the UMAP
approach. Being able to predict both a value and an uncertainty is a big advantage to
the UMAP method. When the model’s internal confidence was high (low σ), the accuracy
improved further (MAD ≈0.09). However, the uncertainty does not match with the real
residuals, but simply seems to increase (albeit reliably) with higher error. The network
reconstructed spectra that tracked broad spectral features well. On mock data, recon-
structions matched ground-truth flux distributions. Compared to grid-based SED-fitting
approaches like LePhare [36] or CIGALE [37], the DL model ran much faster, processing
hundreds of thousands of galaxies in minutes. As a benchmark, LePhare attains a MAE
of 0.029 on the spectroscopic subsample of COSMOS-Web but uses more filters than our
method (e.g., four additional Spitzer/IRAC bands, as well as several Subaru and HST
narrow/medium bands). We therefore interpret performance gaps in light of these extra
data channels.
Our experiments showed that having the model reconstruct spectra alongside redshift
estimation significantly boosted performance. When we trained the network only on pho-
tometry (without the spectral reconstruction task), the best MAD we reached stayed in
the 0.20–0.25 range, comparable to UMAP. Increasing the model size, tweaking hyper-
parameters, or generating more mock galaxies did not overcome this plateau. Only when
we forced the encoder to predict coarse spectra did the MAD drop to about 0.10. This
indicates that tying the representation to the actual SED helps the network disentangle
dust, star formation, and redshift effects more effectively.
However, if the spectra are too detailed, the network may overfit minor features not al-
ways relevant to broadband photometry. Coarsening the spectral resolution nudged the
model to focus on the primary SED traits that shape photometric fluxes. An alternative

option might be to lower the loss weight on the spectral reconstruction, but simply reduc-
ing resolution proved a straightforward fix. We also tested various layer configurations
and network depths and found that the final architecture balanced accuracy with stable
training more effectively than either a simpler or a more complex design.
9.2
Interpretation
These results confirm that machine learning can capture the non-linear mapping from
photometry to galaxy properties. Adding a spectral reconstruction head helped the en-
coder learn a more physically grounded representation, which in turn improved redshift
estimates. This multi-task strategy appears to work better than other methods when data
are noisy or when degeneracies exist between redshift and other factors (for example,
between dust reddening and high redshift). At the same time, the approach remains fast
enough to handle next-generation surveys that measure fluxes across many filters for
millions of galaxies in minutes.
9.3
Advantages and Limitations
A key advantage of this framework is its speed and scalability. While grid-based meth-
ods can handle complex physics, they often become computationally expensive when
applied to large catalogs. In contrast, once trained, a deep network can process data
rapidly, making it feasible for modern surveys with millions of galaxies. Another benefit is
its ability to capture non-linear relationships between observables and physical parame-
ters. Moreover, by predicting a probability distribution for the estimated parameters, the
network provides error bars that indicate how confident the model is about its predictions.
However, the method depends on how well the training data reflect real galaxies. Even
though mock spectra can fill in parameter space, rare or extreme dust laws and star forma-
tion histories may not appear in synthetic catalogs. The training also inherently contains
non-physical galaxies, since it is generated from a uniform distribution within the param-
eter space. Filter coverage also matters. We used fewer filters than LePhare, and the
lack of additional infrared or medium-band information might explain some performance
gap. Lastly, like many machine learning models, our approach may not extrapolate well
for objects lying far outside the training distribution.
9.4
Related Neural Network Approaches to Photometric
Redshifts
In recent years, several studies have used deep learning to estimate photometric redshifts
with approaches differing from ours. For instance, NetZ [55] is a convolutional neural
network that operates directly on galaxy images from the Hyper Suprime-Cam Subaru
Strategic Program. This differs significantly from our synthetic photometry pipeline: by
using real images in five filters instead of extracted fluxes, NetZ can capture morphological
cues. Reported precision is σ ≈0.12 (68% interval), for sources with redshifts out to z ≈
4.
Zhou et al. [56] used a long short-term memory neural network on sequential flux data
for the future China Space Station Telescope. Their approach interprets photometry as
a time series, achieving a normalized median absolute deviation of 0.023 and an outlier
fraction of 1.43%.
Finally, a hybrid approach by Zhou et al. [57] combined an MLP for flux data with a CNN
for image data. This merges morphological and photometric information, reporting an
NMAD of 0.020 and an outlier fraction of 0.90%.

Compared to all of these, our key difference is the multi-task design that joins photomet-
ric redshift estimation with a coarse spectral reconstruction. Previous studies typically
work with single-task regression. We instead force the model’s encoder to learn spec-
tral features. That extra spectral head, even at reduced resolution, significantly improves
accuracy over single-task flux-to-redshift predictions.
9.5
Future Improvements
One promising direction for future work is to make the model flexible enough to handle
varying sets of filter observations without having to predefine or hard-code a fixed number
of filters. This could involve tokenizing each filter—treating the filter name or wavelength
range as a token, paired with the observed flux—so that a neural network can ingest a
sequence of (filter, flux) tokens of arbitrary length. Such a design would allow new filters
to be added or old ones omitted without retraining the entire model for a specific set of 17
or 20 bands. It would also let the model learn filter-specific embeddings that account for
calibration differences, bandwidths, or other observational factors. This approach may
naturally integrate with transformer-based architectures, which are good at processing
variable-length sequences and can learn attention weights that highlight the most infor-
mative filters for a given galaxy.
Generating more synthetic galaxies can further improve coverage of parameter space,
particularly in extremes of metallicity or star formation rate. Even a small fraction of highly
unusual objects in training may help the network generalize better to real sources at the
edges of known distributions.
Additionally, fine-tuning on spectroscopic calibrators at
high redshifts—where degeneracies become more pronounced and noise is greater—
can raise accuracy. Transfer learning or a two-stage training pipeline, in which the model
is first trained on a broad synthetic set and then refined on smaller spectroscopic samples,
might improve accuracies.
9.6
An Extra Note: Euclid NISP + Spitzer IRAC
We trained our models on photometric data similar to what Euclid NISP will provide. Eu-
clid’s NISP bands (0.9–2 µm), combined with the Spitzer IRAC bands, extend coverage
into the mid-infrared. On their own, these datasets have broader coverage but shallower
depth compared to JWST. Still, applying our machine learning methods to this combi-
nation can efficiently estimate galaxy parameters—such as photometric redshifts, stel-
lar masses, and star formation rates—on very large catalogs without high computational
costs. This data can significantly improve our models by increasing their accuracy and
generalizability. The arrival of Euclid data highlights the importance of developing effi-
cient, scalable analysis methods like those explored in this thesis.

9.7
Concluding Remarks
We studied two machine-learning approaches for galaxy SED analysis. UMAP + kNN
is quick and intuitive, showing how high-dimensional photometry clusters by redshift and
other properties. A deep network can predict redshift and reconstruct spectra with good
accuracy and speed. Combined with training sets from synthetic spectra, these methods
avoid some biases of older template-fitting catalogs.
The results suggest that modern surveys like COSMOS-Web, which gather tens of photo-
metric bands including deep JWST coverage, can be analyzed at scale with these meth-
ods. Future expansions in training data and filter coverage will keep improving the fidelity
of redshift estimates, stellar masses, and dust constraints. Ultimately, bridging advanced
ML approaches with astrophysical insight may accelerate our understanding of galaxy
evolution, from local spirals to the earliest galaxies seen by JWST.
