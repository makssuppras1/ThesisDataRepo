Abstract
A system for measuring indoor environment in oﬃces was developed. The system consists of
a Particle Argon with on board microcontroller, a CO2 sensor, TVOC sensor, temperature
and relative humidity sensor to measure the surrounding air and gases present in the envi-
ronment, light sensor measures illuminance and sound sensor for measuring the intensity of
sound.
The system has been developed based on the IoT technology to eﬃciently monitor the in-
door environment quality parameters and transmit the data to a cloud, ThingSpeak and
Google Sheets via Wi-Fi in real time. The microcontroller was programmed to communi-
cate with the sensors, read the measurements, process the data and send the data to the
cloud, ThingSpeak as well as save the data on a Google Sheet. The developed system was
tested in the lab with controlled environment and its performance was assessed based on
measurements using the measuring instruments at the same location.
During the operation, the developed system was stable throughout the experiments and no
data loss was noticed. Regarding the reference system, noise, light, temperature and humid-
ity measurements were accurate except there were slight diﬀerence in the measurements of
CO2. While performing the tests, some factors such as glare, natural light for light sensor
and temperature and humidity for CO2 concentration that can aﬀect the accuracy were not
considered for this project.
The estimated cost to develop a system is 1.392,61 Danish Krone(DKK). Based on the
results and total cost, the developed system could be implemented in the oﬃce buildings
since it is less expensive, easily customizable, and provides accurate results.

Introduction
Indoor environment quality refers to the quality of a building environment in a relation to
the health and well beings of those who occupy space within it [1].The indoor environment
quality (IEQ) depends on many factors like the temperature, humidity, lighting, noise and air
quality. As nowadays people spend most of the time inside the building, so the environmental
quality of these spaces is recognized as an important factor that may aﬀect human health.
Due to the popularity of the Internet of Things (IoT) around the world in many sectors
such as smart cities, smart homes etc. IoT makes it possible for physical object such as
televisions, refrigerators, vehicles and etc to be connected to the internet and generate a
huge amount of data from diﬀerent sensors that can help us to improve the way we live.
IoT sensors and microcontrollers are cost-eﬀective, small and can be installed on any object
to monitor or control its surroundings.
In the last decades many studies have been carried out in order to determine how IEQ
inﬂuences an occupants comfort, health, productivity and living quality [2][3][4].
Poor
thermal comfort was identiﬁed as the main source of occupant dissatisfaction in commercial

Objective
The aim of this thesis was to develop a fully automated environmental monitoring device
using IoT in real time, from hardware to software and a dashboard to visualize the collected
data from the indoor environment, which enables the end user to measure the important
indoor environment parameters that eﬀects the human behavior as well as give awareness
and knowledge of health and condition to the user about their indoor environment.

• Create a system consisting of all the sensors in real world to gather real time data.
• To perform tests with the sensors in the indoor environment.

2.1
Internet of Things(IoT)
Internet of Things or Internet of objects [9] was proposed by Kevin Ashton in 1999, and it
became popular through related market analysis and publications. IoT makes it possible
to transform everyday objects into internet connected objects that can be aware of their
surroundings. It consists of several building blocks and these blocks or objects that are
connected to the internet can observe, understand, react, and provide information about
their environment [10].
For instance, it works as an ecosystem in home where a fridge
can communicate with our mobile phone. Apart from that, it has become important in
environmental monitoring speciﬁcally in areas that require continuous monitoring, analysis
and decision making. Such as, in agriculture where crops are equipped with sensors to give
feedback on the health of the crops. In the recent years the focus of the monitoring air
quality in big cities has become increasingly popular due to the health problems cause by
dust particles [11].
The architecture of IoT consists of four layers with diﬀerent goals as illustrated in the
Figure 1. In Perception layer, the physical object captures the data such as temperature,
humidity, air particles and so on. Then, processes and transmits the data through a wireless
transmission to a network gateway. The Network layer transmits the data in analog format
to a network gateway using Bluetooth, LoRaWAN, WiFi, Zigbee and so on.
The data
processing layer consists of processing units( microcontroller or microprocessor like Arduino,
Raspberry Pi) and software applications(Arduino IDE, TinyOS) which makes it possible for
diﬀerent IoT devices to communicate and translates the data. The Application layer is the
interface/visualization of the data by the user [12].

The development of IoT would not have become a reality this fast without the advance
of cloud computing and wide application of wireless sensor networks (WSN) [9]. Today,
there are more IoT devices connected to the internet than smartphones per person. Cloud
computing has become very important because of the requirement of the space needed to
store the huge amount of data generated by the devices. Data storing, sharing, analysis,
and communication are some functions of cloud computing. Cloud computing is often used
in air monitoring systems because of quick deployment and low-cost handling of data [11].
IBM Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services, and Google cloud platform are few
examples of public clouds where the user can deploy applications, data analytics, services,
store data, and visualize the data. A cloud platform gives ﬂexibility to the user by not
having to build their own infrastructure and platform with high costs of maintenance instead
simply pay for the service they have used. Moreover, the user can also access their data
from anywhere with internet access [13].
IoT not only provides the capabilities of a low cost, unobtrusive solution by monitoring the
indoor environment in real time but also provides a signiﬁcant amount of quantitative data,
which will prove beneﬁcial in understanding patterns and identifying factors that impact on

2.2
Indoor Environment Monitoring
There has been much research on monitoring indoor climate in the past years. Most of the
research found for this project on air monitoring systems using the Internet of Things (IoT)
is from 2009 and later. The focus of monitoring the climate has grown drastically due to
climate change and the rising temperatures of our planet. The focus of indoor environment
has also become an important research area as most of our activities occurs indoor and
therefore indoor environment is so crucial to monitor.
A climate monitoring system using IoT was created by Pradhayini et al. [14] in 2009. The
main goal was to control the temperature in operation theatres in hospitals. They created
the system using a Peripheral Interface Controllers (PIC) microcontroller (PCI16F877) with
ﬁve I/O ports and an analog to digital converter (ADC). The authors chose to use this
microcontroller due to its low cost and easy use. They implemented a temperature sensor
and the system could give warnings to the user when temperature levels were too high
or low. The system was designed to control a chilling value in accordance with measured
temperature.
Another system was made by Kim et al. [15] that not only monitored the air pollutants
such as CO, CO2, V OC, O3 and airborne particles but also controlled the pollutants by
controlling the ventilation. The authors used a Zigbee module that has a communication
range of 30 m that works as a transceiver and ADC. Their aim was to keep the indoor
climate balanced, avoiding over-cooling or heating and automatically adjust windows, fans,
the ventilation system, and so on to remove pollutants in the indoor air.
Later, Lee and Lee [16] also created an indoor system with the use of Zigbee module. Their
system also focuses on the controlling the ventilation, but only fan was used. Because, the
main focus was more on the user experience and how they would control and perceive the
pollution levels handed to them on their smartphones. The users only needed access to the
internet and a link to a speciﬁc website. Arduino UNO board was used and interfaced with
CO, CO2, PM2.5, and a temperature and humidity sensors.
A system to monitor the air and climate inside a tech lab in Malaysia was developed by
Saad et al [17]. They monitored temperature and humidity, particulate matter (PM10),
carbon dioxide (CO2), carbon monoxide (CO), oxygen (O2), volatile organic compound
10
==PAGE:22==
(VOC), methane (CH4) and chloroﬂuorocarbons (CFC). They used sensing modules which
consisted of one microcontroller, temperature and humidity sensor, a dust sensor, various
gas sensors, and a Wireless Sensor Network (WSN) node which was connected through wire-
based connection. The data collected from the sensor module was sent through the WSN
node and to the web through a wireless connected computer where the data was stored.
They placed eight sensor nodes in diﬀerent locations in the building and one base station
which collected all the data. The placement of the sensor nodes was not random, they
placed them between 75 cm and 120 cm above the ﬂoor. This was to get a more precise
measurement of the air that we breathe, also called the breathing zone [17].
Firdhous et al. [18] created an IAQ system to monitor the levels of air pollution next to a
photocopy machine in an oﬃce. Basically, in this they raised the awareness of air pollutants
released by the electronics in the indoor environment. They built a sensing node using
the Arduino BT prototype board with an ozone (O3) sensor (input), speaker (output),
microcontroller (ATmega328P), and a Bluetooth module. They used Raspberry Pi-3 with
built-in Bluetooth as a gateway and a computer to process the data with a web application
to plot a graph. The system they created could scale up by adding more sensing nodes at
diﬀerent locations and more sensors to measure more types of pollutants.
Chiang et al. [19] conducted continuous measurement of indoor environment quality for
senior centers across the United States over 7years. The monitoring parameters consist
of carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, airborne dust, air velocity, air temperature, relative
humidity, noise, and illuminance. IEQ were determined with spot and continuous mea-
surements along with the occupant’s satisfaction in the buildings. In these analyses of 400
workstations linked with characteristics and environment qualities to occupant’s satisfac-
tion was carried out. Speciﬁc recommendations for improving the current standards and
guidelines were outlined after validating the current IEQ standards.
Kim and Hing [20] developed an integrated psychological response score of the occupants
based on 22 experimental participants’ activities and the IEQ condition changes by using
the weighted Euclidean distance. The analysis was done like that because psychological
response is based on the indoor environmental quality (IEQ) condition changes and activities
of the occupants and it is diﬃcult to analyze both at the same time as they measured on
diﬀerent scale.
The experiment was carried out with three scenarios by simulating the
IEQ condition changes which were carbon dioxide concentration and operative temperature
during an 8 Hours working period. At the end of each phase, questionnaire survey on IAQ

presents psychological responses of the occupants based on the IEQ condition, including
indoor air pollutant and indoor climate factors. It was concluded that the occupants were
more aﬀected by the operative temperature than the CO2 concentration.
Li and He [21] designed a indoor environment monitoring system based on WiFi. Their
system mainly focus on getting the temperature, humidity and light intensity data on the
user’s phone. The users needed access to the internet to receive the data in the app. Arduino
UNO board was used and interfaced with ESP8266, light and a temperature and humidity
sensors. The system they created was having low power consumption, low cost and simple.

The communication protocol options, in general, include analog signals, UART communi-
cation, SPI communication, and I2C communication. Analog signals are simply ones where
the sensor gives an analog voltage that is related to the parameter being sensed based on a
known equation/relationship. UART communication denotes Universal Asynchronous Re-
ceiver Transmitter and is based on a physical circuit within the sensor. This communication
system uses only two wires to send data; however, it is limited to only being able to con-
nect two devices (one slave device and one master device) and having a maximum of 8
bits per data packet (more bits allowed in SPI and I2C). SPI communication, in compar-
ison, uses four wires to connect devices. These include master-out-slave-in/serial-data-in
(MOSI/SDI), master-in-slave-out/serial-data-out (MISO/SDO), serial-clock (SCLK), and
slave-select/chip-select (SS/CS). Because of the SS/CS connection, multiple slave devices
can be connected to one master devices by controlling the slave select pins on the devices
separately so that only one device is reading the sent data once at a time. The disadvan-
tages of SPI communication include the lack of an acknowledgement that the data has been
successfully received (present in I2C) and the lack of an error checking mechanism (present
in UART). Hence, I2C combines the best features of UART and SPI communication. Like
SPI, multiple slave devices can be connected to one master while it is also possible to have
multiple master devices controlling each slave device. Like UART, I2C only uses two wires
to transmit data (SDA and SCLK). I2C communication works based on the fact that each
slave device being used has a diﬀerent inbuilt address. These addresses are 7 to 10 bits
long and are used to identify which device for which a particular data packet is meant. In
I2C, each data packet is led by the address of the device that it is meant for to allow for
selectivity while only using two wires.

3.3.1.2
I2C Multiplexer
An 8-channel multiplexer is a device that has 8 channels and I2C interface to allow the
interaction of many slave controllers to the master controller as long as device ID’s of the
slave controllers is diﬀerent. If two slave controllers share the same ID then the master
controller attempts a connection to that speciﬁc device ID, both of them respond as a result
there is a communication error.
In this project, it was not necessary to use I2C mux as each sensor had diﬀerent device ID,
but it was used for testing two same sensors at a time and for not having any communication
error between the master and slave devices. The I2C multiplexer used was TCA954A8A
I2C multiplexer from Adafruit.
The TCA954A8A was an eight channel bidirectional translating I2C switch.
The mas-
ter SCL/SDA signal channel was directed to eight channels of slave devices (SC0/SD0-
SC7/SD7). Any individual downstream channel along with any combination of the eight

31

Figure 9: SCD30 Sensor
The CO2 concentration was measured at regular interval of time by non-dispersive in-
frared(NDIR) technology. The features of SCD30 is given in the Table 2. By default, the
interval between two measurements was 2 seconds. The accuracy of sensor was ±30 ppm.
Additionally, it incorporated temperature and humidity sensor with an accuracy of ±0.3 °C
and ±2 % respectively. According to the datasheet, there were two methods to calibrate
the sensor namely, Forced Re-Calibration(FRC) and Automatic Self-Calibration (ASC). In
FRC, the user has to feed a reference value to SCD30 sensor. The value is then used for up-
dating the calibration to restore the full accuracy. Whereas, ASC autonomously generates
a reference value by analysing the measured CO2 concentration over a period of time.
NDIR analyzer make use of infrared radiation to perform their function. In operation an
infrared lamp directs waves of light through a tube ﬁlled with a sample of air toward an
optical ﬁlter in the front of an IR light detector as shown in the Figure 10. Then, the IR
light detector measures the amount of IR light that passes through the optical ﬁlter.
Figure 10: NDIR working
The band of IR radiation generated by the lamp is very close to the absorption band of
CO2 that is 4.26 micron. Because of that matching the light source wavelength serves as a
32
==PAGE:44==
signature to recognize the CO2 molecule. So, when the IR light travels through the tube,
the CO2 gas molecules absorb the particular band of light while letting other wavelength of
light passes through. At the end of the tube, the remaining wavelength of light is absorbed
by the optical ﬁlter except the wavelength absorbed by the CO2 molecules. Then, an IR
detector reads the remaining intensity of light at that particular wavelength. The CO2 level
present in the air is measured by taking the diﬀerence between the amount of light radiated
by the IR lamp and the amount of IR light received by the detector.

Discussion
This chapter discusses the implementation of the developed system for measuring the indoor
climate in a chamber along with the possible improvements that could be implemented to
make the developed system more compact and accurate.
The BME680 temperature and relative humidity sensor module was used by the sensor
system to perform the corresponding measurements. Compared to a Testo 400 instrument,
there were not many diﬀerences in the results of the two devices. After applying the correc-
tion to the BME680 sensor there was still minor diﬀerence in the temperature readings of the
BME680 sensor and reference meter. The sensor used in the project is from Adafruit and on
PCB there was a voltage regulator which dissipate heat. Due to which the temperature of
PCB is diﬀerent from the ambient temperature and that is why it shows a diﬀerence of 1.5
°C. The accuracy of the Testo 400 for temperature is ±0.5. There was a maximum diﬀerence
of 0.5 °C between BME680 sensor and reference instrument for the selected temperature
which was acceptable as the value comes in the tolerance range of reference instrument.
For relative humidity the accuracy of the Testo 400 is ±2% and the maximum diﬀerence
recorded in the readings of relative humidity was of 0.6 % diﬀerence. So, the measurements
noted from both the experiments for relative humidity was acceptable.
The SEN0232 sound sensor was used to measure the noise level in the chamber. There was
not much diﬀerence in the readings of SEN0232 sensor and SL-4023SD instrument. But,
there were ﬂuctuations in the output of SEN0232 sensor and there could be many factors
that can introduce noise to the system. Hence, ﬁlter was added to smoothen the output of
the SEN0232 sensor. After adding the ﬁlter, measurements were taken from experiment 2
and 3 and found there was a diﬀerence in the readings of experiment 2 and 3 for the same
setup. The reasons for not accurate readings were the system was tested in two diﬀerent
chambers for each experiment. Also, there was much more noise present in the chamber
compared to another chamber. For this project, volume levels with diﬀerent frequencies were
chosen to measure the accuracy of the SEN0232 sensor. No other calibration or correction
was done in the ﬁrmware because noise level changes with the change in the frequency level
as well as how far the source of noise placed. So, if any correction was implemented in the
ﬁrmware then the reading may be accurate for this project but will not be universal.
The TEMT6000 sensor was used to measure the intensity of light in the room. Similarly
to the SEN0232 sensor, there were some ﬂuctuations in the illuminance obtained from the
91
==PAGE:103==
experiment 1. Also, the measurement values were comparatively lower than the reference
meter. The reason for this could be optical lens installed on the reference instrument which
helps to focus the light coming from diﬀerent angle to fall on the photo-sensor. The tests
that were performed after correction indicated that accuracy was improved. But, there was
a variation in the readings of experiment 2 and 3. The placement of the sensor or how the
light fall on the sensor could aﬀect the readings. The correction method used was two point
calibration and average mean ﬁlter. Two point calibration was used because it is capable of
correcting oﬀset error and it was only used to check performance of the TEMT6000 sensor.
For these experiments the range selected was from 200 lux to 500 lux. For third experiment,
it was expected to have a bit of diﬀerence in the measured reading from TEMT6000 and
actual reading from reference meter as there was distance of 5.5 cm in between them.
The SCD30 sensor which was used to measure the CO2 concentration had an acceptable
performance in terms of accuracy compared to the Testo 400 instrument.
The average
absolute diﬀerence of the measured CO2 concentrations noted for the ﬁrst experiment was
15 ppm. Whereas, for the third experiment it was observed to be 39 ppm. Based on the
accuracy of SCD30 the absolute diﬀerence of the experiment 1 was acceptable. The reasons
for a large diﬀerence in the absolute diﬀerence between two senors of the experiment 3
could be SCD30 sensor placement in the enclosure or not enough opening for air ﬂow which
may aﬀected the readings of CO2 or dynamics as decay of the tracer gas concentration was
used . Moreover, the SCD30 sensor readings appeared to be lower than Testo 400(reference
meter). The reason could be SCD30 seems to be less responsive to rapid changes in the
CO2 concentration compared to the reference meter meaning diﬀerence of time that each
sensor performed the measurements.
For monitoring and uploading the data, Wi-Fi capability of the system was tested. The
results from both tests seemed to be successful and useful. The system successfully up-
loaded the CO2 concentration, noise level, illuminance, temperature and humidity to the
Thingspeak website and Google Sheets without any data loss. It was possible to perceive
the uploaded data in real-time from anywhere. The ability to monitor the data on the dash-
board and storing of data helps the end user to analyze the data and performs the required
task.
In the future, there are numerous possible improvements that could be made to the system
including options that are currently being explored. Although, in the existing system, there
are two sources of giving the power to the system. Apart from that, the sensors module
92
==PAGE:104==
used for testing are big. The use of one power supply and System Mount Device(SMD)
could allow the further shrinking of the system. In addition, improvement in 3D design for
the system for instance, more opening on the top and bottom so that there will be proper
airﬂow and sensors can read the measurements more correctly. Moreover, use of lens for light
sensor(TEMT6000) to have full use of light emitted on the solid angle and soundproof room
for sound sensor experiment as some external noise was present in the room. Furthermore,
visualization of data can be further improved by creating our own dashboard using ReactJS
which give more freedom to add widgets, colors etc. Apart from that, in this project some
factors like glare, natural light, and humidity and temperature that can aﬀect the accuracy
of the system were not considered. So, it is recommended to consider parameters to improve
the accuracy.
The programmable controller not only can be used to develop a system for measuring and
monitoring the indoor climate, but also to enhance the intelligence of building. For example,
microcontroller has digital outputs pins which can programmed to control the indoor climate
of a building such as controlling the heating in the building or open the ventilation when
the amount of CO2 gas reaches the suitable limit according to the environment. The system
could also be used to monitor the indoor environment much cheaper and easier than before.
This could be a sort of building intelligence that both could be used to control building
services and to document the indoor environment ,which is an important factor in building
commissioning (a kind of quality control of buildings and their systems).

Conclusion
This chapter presents the conclusion related to the developed device for measuring the IEQ
parameters.
The intended goal of the project was fulﬁlled by creating a system with the help of IoT and
coordination between the particle device, sensors, and the third party integration such as
ThingSpeak and Zapier. The process of developing a sensor system was successful in terms
of accuracy for almost all the sensors that were used.
The cost of the developed system is 1392,61 DKK. The limitation of such a system is some
factors that were not considered for the observations. However, the developed system is
very ﬂexible in terms of it can programmable from anywhere via internet connection and
customizable. Based on the performance and considerably lower price, it can be a reliable
solution for oﬃces.
