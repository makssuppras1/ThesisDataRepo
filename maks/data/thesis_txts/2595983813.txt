ABSTRACT 
 
A methodology for the estimation of copper loads in urban runoff was developed in a 
catchment located in the centre of Copenhagen. In this study, aerial imagery, geographic 
information systems (GIS), literature reviews and calculation processes were investigated in 
order to compute the total copper flux in the catchment. 
 
Firstly, the pollutant sources and their quantification were analysed. In fact, in urban 
areas, the major metal sources originate from traffic automobile, buildings’ metallic surfaces 
and atmospheric contributions. The understanding of the pollutant release from these sources 
and their spread into waters was achieved to estimate the weight of each source on the overall 
copper flux. It permitted to define all the input parameters with ranges in order to develop 
optimistic or pessimistic scenarios. To estimate some of these parameters, land cover 
characterisation was conducted in the studied catchment. The objective was to provide a GIS 
framework suited for visualisation and computation. In this way, different land cover 
classification methods, namely aerial photography interpretation (API), pixel-based detection 
(PBD) and semi-automatic classification plugin (SCP) were developed. The scope was to 
investigate whether a more automatic image analysis could replace the visual interpretation 
for the detection of land covers, and especially roofing materials at the catchment scale. In 
addition, uncertainty and sensitivity analyses were carried out in order to evaluate the relative 
impact of key assumptions and input parameters. This last step allowed to better understand 
and potentially optimise the developed methodology. 
 
 
Overall, the entire methodology allowed to target the main sources of copper in the 
studied area, namely the copper roofs with a contribution varying between 30 and 84%, and 
the brake wear releasing 16 to 70% of the annual mass of copper. Thus, the most influential 
parameters were linked with these two sources. In comparison, the atmospheric deposition 
and the tire wear were both insignificant regarding their contribution to the overall copper flux. 
For the land cover characterisation, the estimated copper surfaces by using semi-
automatic methods were 23 to 36% lower with the SCP, but 24% higher with the PBD, 
compared to the API. They revealed sensitive parametrisations and the main difficulty was 
linked to the spectral similarity between land covers. Regarding only the copper roofs’ 
contribution, a linear correlation with a coefficient of 1 between the estimated copper surface 
and the copper flux was observed. The difference between the minimum and maximum values 
of the resulting load revealed an order of magnitude of ten. However, the influence of the 
detected copper surface on the total copper flux was lower by taking into account the 
contribution from all pollution sources. The variability of the copper surfaces fluctuated by 
±32%, but it only impacted the global copper flux by ±13.2%. In fact, a linear correlation with a 
coefficient of 0.41 was observed between the two variables. As a consequence, the influence 
of the copper roofs areas was obscured at the global scale.  
The uncertainty analysis significantly reduced the value ranges of the copper fluxes for 
each source. In fact, it caused a decrease in the pessimistic scenarios and an increase in the 
optimistic ones, allowing to neglect the outlier values. However, some inputs required to be 
refined in order to reduce the variability of the parameters and stabilise the total copper flux.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background overview  
Nowadays, climate change and urbanisation are current and major concerns. Urban 
environments are constantly expanding, increasing the impervious areas and generating 
pollution in runoff waters. Particularly, metal sources are numerous and commonly examined 
in stormwater due to their toxicity, omnipresence and the fact that they cannot be chemically 
transformed or destroyed (Davis & al, 2001; Gasperi & al, 2014). Heavy metal pollutants are 
released by complex mechanisms and accumulate on impervious surfaces. They originate from 
human activities, especially the construction materials or road transportation, but also from 
atmospheric deposition (Petrucci & al, 2014). Then, heavy metals are wash off under rainfall-
runoff processes (Davis & al, 2001) and degrade the receiving waters (Brudler & al, 2019). At 
the urban scale, these contaminants are often considered as nonpoint source pollutants 
because of resulting from many diffuse sources. In order to mitigate water quality degradation 
with efficiency, the mapping of pollutant sources is imperative. 
 
 
Different studies have been conducted on the Substance Flow Analysis (SFA) to examine 
the pollutant sources in stormwater runoff based on the average pollutant concentration for 
each land use (Chèvre & al, 2011). However, the estimation of contaminant sources with these 
approaches is often judged as too simplistic (Petrucci & al, 2014). In fact, they are unable to 
estimate correctly which source is responsible for a specific pollutant load, thus it is unsuitable 
to source control of nonpoint emissions (Cui & al, 2010). Considering that each material surface 
has different physical and chemical properties, it is possible to focus on the weight of each 
source on the global pollutant flux (Fraga & al, 2016). The main difficulty of this approach is the 
diversity of parameters influencing the emitting sources (Petrucci & al, 2014). In fact, it requires 
a significant need for data, but their availability and homogeneity are not always guaranteed. 
Some factors are often difficult to quantify or even completely unknown. Thus, assumptions 
and estimations can be necessary, overcoming the lack of information but also increasing the 
uncertainties of the results.  
 
The characterisation of the catchment by defining land uses can help to determine some 
of these parameters. One land use is often composed of multiple land covers, especially in 
urban catchments which have heterogeneous characteristics and properties (Mitraka & al, 
2016). As a consequence, the understanding of land covers is essential to more accurately 
quantify pollutant contributions. 
 
Since several decades, the application of Geographical Information Systems (GIS) has 
significantly been developed for the estimation of pollutant loads and the identification of 
critical areas (Atanasova & al, 2008). GIS environments are flexible because they allow to enter, 
analyse and display digital data, which are useful for the identification of land covers by using 
digital imagery. Moreover, when no detailed data are available, the use of digital imagery and 
automatic tools to obtain land cover information is widespread (Judex & al, 2006).  
Traditional aerial photography interpretation (API), which is a manual classification of 
land covers, is considered as the most accurate method, but it is not efficient in terms of time 
(Yuan & Bauer, 2006). In fact, photointerpretation is labour-intensive and often expensive, 
especially for large areas. In addition, land covers are continuously changing due to the rapid 
population growth and ongoing urbanisation (Liu & Yang, 2015). As a consequence, digital

The pixel-based approach assumes the independence of each pixel within the image whereas

and sensitive step (Song & al, 2005), called image segmentation which requires users’

demonstrated the usefulness of this approaches. The adaptation of these methods at the urban 
scale could allow to develop an in-depth characterisation of the watersheds in order to catch 
the pollutant sources. However, it is a challenge because of the presence of heterogeneous 
features and the spectral similarity between different cover types. 
 
 
1.2 Project aim 
The purpose of this project was to develop a general and preliminary framework 
permitting the estimation of copper loads in urban stormwater runoff, with a focus on the land 
cover characterisation by using GIS, aerial photography, manual and pixel-based methods. The 
goal was to design a simple, fast and effective methodology which could be transferable from 
one project to another, in order to improve procedures for assessing stormwater pollution. 
To initiate this methodology and explore its possibilities, copper was chosen as a target 
pollutant because the studied catchment was located in an area with numerous copper roofs, 
being a potential pollution source. This choice can also be explained by the fact that copper is 
one of the most important and toxic heavy metals in water pollution (Brudler & al, 2019). In 
addition, copper loads were analysed rather than concentrations, because this parameter is 
more relevant for an annual period of time at the catchment scale. 
Finally, the objective of such methodology was to assess the quality of the receiving 
water by assuming the release of the runoff into the environment. As a consequence, the 
related copper concentration was expressed in order to be compared with the legislation limit 
established by official Danish documentation (Retsinformation, 2017). 
 
 
To summarise, the project investigated the following issues: 
 What are the relevant copper sources in the studied catchment?  
 What is the weight of each source on the annual copper flux?  
 What is the influence of the variability of source release parameters on the annual 
copper flux? 
 How to achieve a more automatic land cover characterisation? 
 What is the impact of the copper surfaces estimated with different land cover 
classification methods on the copper load? 
 How to assess the environmental impact regarding the release of the urban runoff?

2. DESCRIPTION OF THE STUDY CASE 
To conduct this project and perform the calculations, it was necessary to use a study 
case with specific data. The city of Copenhagen in Denmark was chosen as a field of 
investigation, and especially the inner centre. This area is mainly a residential, but with also 
various shops, restaurants, and other equipment. In this district, the urban fabric is dense and 
the constructions are relatively old. The road network is mainly composed of narrow paved 
streets, where the pedestrian is given priority over the vehicles. Regarding the covering roofs, 
a lot of tiles were observed, but also metal and especially copper.  
 
The aerial photography of Copenhagen was downloaded online on the website called 
kortforsyningen, giving access to most recently updated geographic data via a web service.

the traditional sewer network map was not available. SCALGO is a digital tool allowing to 
visualise, compute and export hydrological data through an interactive map. It is also used for 
assessment and monitoring of flood risk with up-to-date and high-resolution terrain data. In 
particular, the investigation of flow accumulation and watersheds with different rain amount 
scenarios.

3. INVESTIGATION APPROACHES AND METHODS 
The objective of the methodology was to provide a comprehensive approach regarding 
the estimation of the annual copper flux in the catchment. As a consequence, the definition of 
the relevant copper sources in the catchment of interest, the explanation of the pollution 
processes and their computation methods were analysed. 
 
3.1 Copper flux estimation from different pollution sources 
Nonpoint sources of pollutants may come from human activities, land-cover, 
behaviours or even atmospheric deposition (Petrucci & al, 2014). More specifically, in urban 
areas, the major sources of metals are traffic automobile, especially brake and tire wear, 
metallic surfaces on buildings and atmospheric contributions (Davis & al, 2001). These sources 
were investigated in this study. Their quantification required the definition and estimation of 
different parameters which are summarised below.  
Moreover, general assumptions were established such as the homogeneity of the 
pollutant emissions and uniformity of the precipitation distributions on the watershed. 
 
3.1.1 Quantification of the copper sources 
After identifying the different responsible copper sources, their quantification was 
necessary. As commonly used in SFA analyses (Hansen & Lassen, 2002), a mass-balance 
approach permitted to calculate the annual emission of copper T 𝑬𝒑 (kg.year-1) by summing 
the emissions from each source (s) by the following equation coming from Petrucci & al (2014):

For each copper source, the meaning and the units of these parameters varied. Thus, 
the definition of these parameters constituted a delicate step in the project because 
adjustments were required according to the pollutant source. The parameters were estimated 
according to the existing literature or retrieved data. A range was defined for each parameter, 
representing pessimistic or optimistic values. 
 
For roof covers, the extent represents the exposed copper surface (m2) in the 
catchment. The copper runoff rate (kg.m-2.mm-1) was used to express the pollutant content 
and it was multiplied by the rainfall data (mm.year-1).  
However, for brake and tire wears, the source extent represented the total traffic 
volume in the catchment (km.vehicles.year-1) multiplied by the mean wear (kg.vehicles-1.km-1). 
Cp,s was the copper content emitted by the source (g.kg-1) and ηp,s the fraction of wear product 
passing to stormwater. 
For the atmospheric deposition, the contribution of copper transported into the 
catchment was calculated with a shorter and easier equation: the deposition rate (kg.m-2.year-
1) multiplied by the impervious areas (m2).

Page | 7  
 
These calculations allowed obtaining the annual copper flux [kg.year-1] from the 
different sources. This factor represents the mass of copper which is discharged into a water 
body during a period of time. On the contrary, the concentration of copper expressed the mass 
in a defined volume of water [kg.L-1]. Both of these factors furnish significant information, but 
copper flux is more appropriate to measure water quality for an annual period. Indeed, heavy 
metal concentrations depend upon the hydraulic conditions (Tedoldi & al, 2017), they show a 
high variability from a rainfall event to another at the catchment scale. In addition, the annual 
copper flux represents the sum of the copper mass released during each rain event on one year 
whereas the annual concentration is an average. As a consequence, the annual scale can create 
a masking effect on the pollutant concentration because it is governed by the frequent rain 
events (Flanagan & al, 2017).  Moreover, the use of an average concentration during one year 
required information about the considered recipient. For all these reasons, the copper flux was 
chosen as the reference factor. However, it is possible to calculate the overall concentration 
from the copper flux if necessary. 
 
 
3.1.2 Copper roofs surfaces 
 Material of the exposed surface to runoff 
The land covers’ materials are a significant characteristic in the estimation of the 
pollutant loads. In fact, construction materials influence the apparition and the number of 
specific pollutants in stormwater runoff. For example, copper roofs and gutters may release 
copper ions (Sörme & Lagerkvist, 2002) but the load depends on the patina (Karlén, 2002).  
In fact, copper roofs are subject to atmospheric corrosion due to the exposure to the 
climatic conditions (Karlén, 2002). Over a long time period, copper roofs are oxidised, creating 
a patina on the surface of the material. This patina reacts like a protective surface film on the 
copper roofs. So, the metal content in runoff decreases and tends to stabilise over time 
(Odnevall Wallinder & al, 2001a) as the thickness of the natural patina layer increases and 
becomes more resistant (Hedberg & al, 2014). Thus, the patina and the exposure period of the 
roofing material are both important parameters in the estimation of the copper pollutant load. 
In order to take into account these characteristics, the calculation was conducted with two 
different time periods, but also with two types of patina, one with a “brown” recent patina and 
one with a “green” old patina more porous and rough.

3.1.3 Road covers and traffic intensities 
Copper is also disseminated into the environment from road transport, especially in 
urban waters. It mainly originates from the wear of brakes and tires. A part of the pollutant is 
emitted into the atmosphere, and the other part is deposited on the road and wash off during 
a rainfall event (Van Bohemen & Janssen Van de Laak, 2003).  
 
 Annual traffic volume 
Firstly, the annual traffic volume in the catchment was estimated because influences the 
amount of copper release. The calculation was performed by a simple multiplication between 
the Annual Daily Traffic (ADT) value, the length of the road and the number of days during one 
year. Knowing the percentage of heavy vehicles regarding the motor vehicles’ data, coefficients 
were applied for the computation of the annual traffic volume for passenger cars (PC) and 
heavy vehicles (HV) of each road category. Moreover, bicycles were considered as they were 
considerably numerous in the studied area. 
 
 Brake wear 
Brake wear is an abrasion process during which brakes are forced against a metal piece 
in order to decelerate the vehicle. The composition of brakes is really variable from one model 
to another but generally, brake linings are composed of four main components: binders, fibres, 
fillers, and friction. Metal compounds can be found in fibres, fillers, and friction (European 
Environment Agency, 2016).  
In this project, only frictional brakes were studied because they are most common and 
known as heavy metal sources. Two different types exist, the drum and the disc brakes.

3.1.4 Atmospheric deposition  
Pollution is present in the atmosphere and can pass into runoff water. Two processes 
ensure this transfer. The first is the dry deposition, pollutants are deposited on land covers 
areas and then wash off. The second is the wet deposition, it is the capture of pollutants by 
drops during a rainfall event (Petrucci & al, 2014). The chemical composition of the atmosphere 
is hence a significant parameter in the estimation of the pollutant loads in runoff waters. 
However, it is complicated to distinguish the pollutant sources, especially the external 
contributions which can interfere. But it is possible to quantify the cumulate atmospheric 
pollutant rate by measurements or modelling. 
Atmospheric deposition of heavy metal, especially copper, has been measured by 
several Danish stations. The more recent estimation, dating from 2014, was considered in this 
study. The yearly total deposition of copper (wet and dry) on Danish land was measured as 680 
µg/m2 (Ellermann et al., 2015; mentioned by Eres Pekec, 2017). In this study, it is mentioned 
that the uncertainty of this estimation was evaluated to be approximately between 30 and 50%. 
As a consequence, a value range of [340-1020] µg/m2 was applied for the calculation. Finally, 
the area of deposition was estimated at 239 300 m2, representing only the impervious areas, 
namely the roads and built surfaces.

3.2 Towards a more automatic method for land cover characterisation  
In this study, land uses were composed of various land cover characteristics regrouping 
infrastructures, green spaces, and built-up areas. In order to estimate the copper fluxes from 
roofing surfaces, land cover detection was conducted at a smaller scale, by using aerial 
photography and pixel-based approaches on QGIS 3.4. This software was chosen because it is 
one of the most popular open-source GIS around the world. Copper roofs can have different 
colours but usually, they are known for their distinctive green patinated colour, which comes 
with time due to atmospheric corrosion. In this way, copper surfaces are highly visible and on 
aerial photographs. Thus, the use of colour with image analysis, in order to detect this pollutant 
source, was possible and it permitted fast processing. 
 
The materials covering the building facades also impact the pollutant loads (Davis & al, 
2001). However, the report focused on roofs and not facades because siding buildings are 
significantly less exposed to the rainfall and hence wash off process (Odnevall Wallinger & al, 
2000). Moreover, it was outside the scope of this study but it could be a part of future 
improvements.   
 
3.2.1 Image analysis 
Image analysis is a complex process that may be influenced by many factors, such as for 
example the characteristics of the studied area, the available data, the image quality and 
obviously the classification method (Manandhar & al, 2009). Hence, some precautions were 
required. In order to realise a pertinent classification of aerial photography, backgrounds and 
knowledge were necessary regarding the characteristics of an image.  
 
Firstly, aerial photography is composed of numerous pixels, which are assigned one 
information. A quality of a picture clearly depends on its resolutions which are determined by 
the characteristics of the sensor system. Different types of resolution exist. In this study, the 
following resolution environments were studied:  
 
3.2.1.1.1 The spatial resolution 
It is the most known resolution which represents the density and the size of pixels in an 
image (Liang & al, 2012, ch.1 P.7).  In other words, it represents the smallest object that can be 
detected by the sensor.  Higher is the spatial resolution, smaller and more numerous are the 
pixels. Generally, it is expressed by a set of two positive integer numbers: the first one 
represents the number of the column and gives information on the width of a pixel, while the 
second one gives the number of rows so the height of a pixel.

[https://www.silverfast.com/show/resolution-target/fr.html]

3.2.1.1.1 The spectral resolution  
Any pixel of aerial photography contains a specific spectral profile. Materials reflect a 
percentage of light energy emitted from the sun in the different wavelengths of the 
electromagnetic spectrum (Baghdadi & al, 2017). When the reflectance of a material is plotted 
with respect to the wavelength spectrum, the plot represents the spectral signature. Such plots 
can be compared between different pixels’ in order to classify the image into different 
categories (Congedo, 2018). The classification is conducted more readily when the image 
spectral resolution is higher because it is possible to detect subtler the differences between 
features. 
 
Spectral resolution may describe the sensor’s ability to obtain fine wavelength intervals 
regarding the spectral characteristics of the image. The parameters influencing the spectral 
resolution are the spectral bands’ number and their width. The finer the spectral resolution, 
the more bands are detected, and the narrower the wavelength range is for a particular band. 
 
Spectral resolution can be classified into four categories:  
 Panchromatic imaging which has one single colour band with a wide wavelength 
interval, thus, its spectral resolution is fairly coarse, as the various wavelengths of the 
visible spectrum are not individually distinguished.  
 Colourspectral imaging which possesses three colour bands: the red, green, and blue. 
Thus, they are sensitive to the reflected energy at the wavelengths of the visible 
spectrum, and they can represent features of various colours.  
 Multispectral imaging which is composed of minimum four spectral bands throughout 
the visible, near-infrared, mid-infrared, etc, portions of the electromagnetic spectrum. 
 Hyperspectral imaging which is a special case of multispectral imaging where hundreds 
of spectral narrow bands are available. Thus, it possesses finer wavelength ranges and 
a better spectral resolution. Their very high spectral resolution facilitates fine detection 
between different targets. 
 
           
  
 
Figure 9: Illustration of the spectral resolution of a multispectral (left) and hyperspectral (right) images [Giannoni & al, 2018] 
 
 
3.2.1.1.2 The radiometric resolution  
When an image is acquired by a sensor, its sensitivity to the intensity of the 
electromagnetic energy determines the radiometric resolution. The radiometric resolution of 
aerial photography represents the level of distinction between very slight differences in energy,

so between the colour intensities in a given band (Liang & al, 2012, ch.1 P.8). Higher is the 
radiometric resolution and better is the representation of the differences of intensity. This 
characteristic is expressed as a number of bits. For example, 8 bits are the usual value for a 
computer image file. Each bit records an exponent of power 2. In this way, 256 digital values 
are available for each band for a computer file, ranging from 0 to 255. Each pixel is encoding 
for with a specific value per band.

3.2.1.1.3 Resolutions and characteristics of the studied aerial photography 
For the study case, an orthophoto was downloaded, which means that aerial 
photography was geometrically corrected in order to remove the distortion effect, which was 
created by the camera. The aerial photography was generated by Airborne Light Detection and 
Ranging (LiDAR). This technique is equipped with an active optical sensor that uses a laser in 
the visible and near-infrared spectrum to transmit a light pulse and a receiver with sensitive 
detectors to measure the back-scattered or reflected light.  
The orthophoto is dating from spring 2018 and the pixel size is 0.125 x 0.125 m2.

green, blue, and near-infrared). Finally, regarding the radiometric resolution, the orthophoto is 
an 8-bit format, thus included 256 intensity values for each band. 
 
 
3.2.2 Roofs detection on aerial photography 
Roofing materials are a significant nonpoint source of pollution in stormwater runoff 
(Davis & al, 2001; Chang & al, 2004; Petrucci & al, 2014). Two phenomena may provoke an 
increase in pollutant loads: the flushing of the pollutants accumulated by dry deposition on the 
roofs but also the release from roofing materials (Tobiszewski & al, 2010). To evaluate the 
impact of roof covers on the copper flux, it was indispensable to estimate the exposed copper 
surfaces to rainfall. Different procedures were developed for assessing the abundance of 
copper material in the catchment, such as aerial photography interpretation and pixel-based 
methods. These latter methods treat individual pixels with a classification algorithm. To 
perform the calculation by employing these approaches, multiple mathematical tools can be 
used such as probabilistic or statistical models with the maximum of likelihood (Yuan & Bauer, 
2006; Liu & Yang, 2015), the Bayesian networks (Park & Stenstrom, 2008), but also the 
Euclidean distance (Cleve & al, 2008), spectral angle classifier (Sohn & Rebello, 2002). A set of 
rules can also be developed as a new algorithm (Vezhnevets & al, 2003). In this study, the 
creation of an easy classifier code and the use of a plugin including various algorithms, as 
described above, were implemented and are defined below.

Page | 17  
 
 Aerial Photography Interpretation  
The first applied method was a visual interpretation, roof by roof, by examining aerial 
photography to identify and classify objects. Platforms such as google earth and google street 
view were helpful to recognise the materials. On QGIS, the orthophoto and the buildings layer 
were opened with the aim to characterise the materiality of each roof with the creation of two 
additional columns on the attribute table: one called “surface” and the other “type of material”. 
With the exportation of this new information in an excel format, a calculation was completed 
in order to obtain the total area for each roofing material and its related percentage at the 
catchment scale.

 Pixel-Based Detection  
In this study, the pixel-based method aimed at the classification of each pixel as copper 
or non-copper material, individually and independently from its neighbours. Digital per-pixel 
classification was applied on QGIS software to classify the land cover into these two classes by 
establishing a simple and basic code.  
Different data were opened on QGIS such as the orthophoto, the buildings layer and 
the considered catchment area. The first step represented the compilation of a spectral library 
in order to characterise the spectral-radiometric information of the copper class and ensure 
the accuracy of its classification. So, the RGB ranges of an extended sample, which was 
manually delineated on QGIS with the “click information tool”, was then reported in an Excel 
file. It represented 80 reference pixels scattered in orthophoto. Moreover, the pixels selection 
was conducted in order to characterise the diversity of the class, as a consequence, it included 
pixels in the shade, overexposed to the light, or even at different levels of the corrosion process. 
Then, computations and comparisons between colour bands were completed on an excel file. 
The used mathematical tool was to define explicitly, by defining a set of rules, the 
characteristics of the material in the RGB colour space. Different trials were carried out on 
QGIS, to define these rules by entering an algorithm in the “raster calculator” tool. In fact, this 
tool enables to run computations for each existing pixel value of the photography. After running 
the calculation, a new layer appeared with a pseudogrey composition representing the copper 
roofs fraction in white pixels’ and the non-copper roofs in black pixels’.

 Semi-automatic Classification Plugin  
Finally, this third approach implemented a plugin on QGIS, which required the 
installation of the “Semi-automatic Classification Plugin”.  
 
The orthophoto was designated as the image of reference on the plugin. After that, the 
creation of a training input was required to define the future land cover classes, but also to 
store the different Regions Of Interest (ROIs). ROIs are polygons defining the spectral 
characteristics of an area. They are created by the user according to the objectives and the 
scale of the classification. The creation of ROIs polygons can be conducted by different 
manners: by selecting similar pixels by click (region growing algorithm with editable 
parameters) or drawing polygons in the photography. The creation of ROIs defines the different 
macroclasses and classes for the future classification. A macroclass is the clustering of classes. 
In other words, classes are subcategories of a given macroclass. For example, a macroclass 
called roofs could be composed of different classes such as “copper roofs”, “tiles roofs” and 
“concrete roofs”, having each specific ROI(s). Identification number, name and colour must be 
attributed for each class and macroclass. 
 
In the study, the creation of the ROIs was conducted in order to be as accurate as 
possible, with the objective to correctly assign pixels into the adequate macroclasses. Six 
macroclasses called “copper roofs”, “tiles roofs”, “concrete roofs”, “metal roofs”, “vegetation”, 
and “roads” were created.  
Two technics were applied for the creation of ROIs. 
- 
1. In each macroclass, three different training areas were selected by polygons drawing. 
The ROIs were created manually in order to take into account the spectral variability of 
classes.  
- 
2. In each macroclass, three different classes were defined by clicking with the growing 
algorithm. The ROIs were created automatically to obtain more homogeneous spectral 
signatures. Three training areas were selected to define each ROI.

In general, when the comparison is conducted within a given macroclass, so between the three 
ROIs composing it, the Euclidean distance has to be relatively small but with a necessary array 
of spectral values to detect the different shade of the target colour. It is the same principle with 
the spectral angle distance. On the contrary, the percentage of similarity has to be high because 
two spectral signatures are qualified as ‘similar’ if the value reaches between 90 and 100%. 
However, by comparing two different macroclasses together, the Euclidian and spectral angle 
distances have to be as high as possible, and the value range must be more extended than 
those of each specific macroclass. In contrary, the percentage of similarity has to be low.  
 
In SCP, spectral signatures of the different land cover classes are computed taking into 
consideration the pixel values under each ROI having the same class ID (Congedo, 2018). The 
spectral signatures represent the radiometric values with respect to the spectral bands. They 
can be plotted for each ROI. In general, it is important to obtain distinct spectral signatures 
between the macroclass to correctly detect the different land covers. 
 
After defining and assessing the ROIs, the system of classification must be configured. 
Firstly, the choice between different algorithms is necessary. All the proposed algorithms 
permit to classify the whole image thanks to a comparison between the spectral characteristics 
of any pixel present in the orthophoto and the spectral signature of the training areas (ROIs). 
SCP implements the following classification algorithms.

3.2.2.3.4 Land Cover Signature Classification (LCSC):  
On the plugin, the choice between these three algorithms but also with the Land Cover 
Signature is offered to make the classification. It is based on the definition of a spectral range 
with minimum and maximum values for each band of each class. Thus, these ranges represent 
the spectral region linked to a land cover class. Then, a comparison between the spectral 
signatures of the orthophoto pixels and the training spectral signatures is conducted. If the 
spectral signature of a pixel from the image is totally contained in the spectral region defined 
by a given training area, then this pixel belongs to this class. However, if the spectral signature 
of the pixel is falling inside overlapping regions or outside any spectral region, the use of 
additional algorithms (see above) is necessary to classify it. The land cover signature 
classification can thus be combined with an algorithm to solve the problem of unclassified 
pixels. The algorithm can also be used only for class overlapping pixel by checking “only overlap” 
on the plugin.  
 
Figure 14: Illustration of the principle of the land cover signature classification - Edited from [Congedo, 2018] 
A threshold can also be added with this classification, such as the manual modification 
of minimum and maximum values of the bands or with an automatic threshold calculated as 
“band value + (𝜎 * v)”, where 𝜎 is the standard deviation of each band and v is the defined 
value. When spectral signatures are very similar, the addition of threshold can improve the 
classification results.  
Operation of the algorithm is quite similar to the PBD but various differences can be 
enounced. Firstly, with the LCSC the detection rules are not defining manually but semi-
automatically with the creation of the ROIs. In fact, the code of the PBD method takes into 
account the RGB bands but also the colour rates whereas the LCSC considers also the near 
infrared band. Moreover, the PBD method classifies the orthophoto only into two classes 
(copper or non-copper surfaces) while the LCSC is working with eight classes (copper, metal, 
concrete, tiles, vegetation, roads surfaces, overlap and unclassified).

 Comparison and assumptions 
3.2.2.4.1 Assessment and comparison of the SCP approaches 
After trying the different algorithms, an assessment of each approach and its level of 
accuracy was conducted in order to identify and measure classification errors. The resulting 
classification file was compared to the ROIs references by calculating an error matrix. An error 
matrix expresses the number of ROIs pixels that are correctly classified with respect to the total 
number of ROIs pixels. This measure is widely used for accuracy assessment (Congalton, 1991).  
 
Different measures of accuracy can be calculated thanks to the error matrix, such as the 
overall accuracy, the user’s and producer’s accuracies, the omission and commission errors, 
and the kappa coefficient. The calculations were conducted automatically on the plugin but 
they were verified manually. 
 
Figure 15: Accuracy calculation processes from the error matrix - Edited from [Foody, 2002]  
 
The computation of the overall accuracy, which is the simplest and most popular 
accuracy measure, is defined as the ratio between the number of references pixels, so from 
ROIs, that are correctly classified and the total number of reference pixels (Congalton, 1991). 
This computation assesses the overall classification, by taking into account all the macroclasses. 
The value of 85% is widely used as target overall accuracy to judge a classification (Anderson & 
al, 1976; Foody, 2002, Wulder & al, 2006). 
Accuracy of individual classes can also be computed. The probability of a reference pixel 
being correctly classified is called “producer’s accuracy”, and the probability that a pixel 
classified on the map represents that category on the ground is known as the “user's accuracy” 
(Congalton, 1991). These values are respectively linked to the “error of omission” and the 
“error of commission”. The error of omission represents the percentage of pixels that are not 
classified in the target category whereas they should belong to it. On the contrary, the error of 
commission represents the percentage of pixels that are classified in the target category 
whereas they should not belong to it. 
Finally, the kappa coefficient (Cohen, 1960) is a measure of the agreement of the error 
matrix. It is calculated with more information from the error matrix than the other measures. 
This factor gives information concerning the overall accuracy of the process but it can also be 
calculated for every single class.  
 
Moreover, the unclassified pixels were analysed. It is a significant parameter because it 
gives information about the potential neglected surface.

By testing three different methods to estimate the surface of copper roofs, the

characterise the catchment. As a consequence, the results were qualitatively compared, based 
on a visual approach by using orthophoto as the reference.  
The identification of both benefits and disadvantages of each approach was conducted, 
to investigate their accuracy and to assess if a more automatic tool could replace manual 
interpretation. 
 
 
3.3 Uncertainty and sensitivity analyses  
The uncertainties linked to the calculations were also analysed because they can be 
important in the assessment of pollutant loads (Petrucci & al, 2014). Moreover, as the 
methodology was constituted by different steps, through which different estimations were 
conducted, a cascading uncertainty effect could appear and hence raise uncertainty.  
As no data were available concerning the distribution of the different parameters, the 
method of Monte Carlo was employed to analyse the contribution of each copper source. This 
method uses randomness to approximate numerical value by employing probabilistic 
techniques. Each parameter was linked to a series of random values between its own minimum 
and maximum potential values, to simulate a large number of calculations. The 5th, 25th, 50th, 
75th, and 95th percentiles were computed according to the size of the sample to learn about 
the repartition of the parameter and refine the ranges.  To define the size of the sample, the 
computation was repeated with a larger size as long as the percentiles became stable.  
 
Sensitivity analysis allows evaluating the impact of key assumptions and input 
parameters in order to better understand and optimise the developed methodology. Regarding 
all sources (copper roofs areas, traffic, and atmospheric deposition), a sensitivity analysis was 
interesting to implement in order to assess the contribution of each source concerning the 
copper flux. To support this, two different approaches were conducted. 
Firstly, the influence of the variability of each parameter was analysed in comparison 
with the average of the total copper flux. As a consequence, the parameters were modified one 
by one in order to assess their impact. Most of the parameters were defined with minimum 
and maximum values, so a variation coefficient was expressed. For the parameters with only a 
single value, the variation coefficient was determined at ± 10%. The impact of each parameter 
was calculated as a percentage. However, this approach was mathematically wrong to talk 
about parameter influence.  
As a consequence, the second approach analysed the influence of all parameters on the 
average of the total copper flux with the same variation coefficient (± 10%) in order to identify 
the most influential parameters.

4.1 Environmental impact

estimated between 950.4 and 9768.8 Cu.kg.year-1, which is equivalent to a range of [2.6-26.8]

5. DISCUSSION  
This section discusses the results calculated previously regarding the values of copper 
flux estimated from each source and their uncertainties, the source release parameters 
influence, the environmental impact of the runoff discharge, and the automation of the land 
cover classification. Some outlooks and recommendations are enounced in order to pursue and 
improve this project. 
5.1 Copper flux estimation from different pollution sources 
5.1.1 Copper roofs surfaces 
As the calculation of the copper flux released from roofs in the catchment was a simple 
multiplication between the tree inputs (exposure surface, rainfall data, and the pollutant runoff 
rate); by varying one parameter, it influenced equally and linearly the output. The relation 
between the inputs and the output was a linear correlation with a coefficient of 1. As a 
consequence, the estimation of a larger surface resulted in a higher value of copper flux. Hence, 
the calculated copper fluxes were higher with the PBD method, followed by the API, the SCP 
(polygons) and SCP (pointer). The difference between the maximum and the minimum copper 
fluxes revealed an order of magnitude of ten, which was not negligible. 
In addition, the copper runoff rate showed a high influence on the global copper flux. 
The patina composition and the time of exposure were important factors.  The variability of 
this parameter impacted the average of the global load by more or less 20.3%, more than the 
roofing surfaces with a value of 13.2%. However, this difference can be explained by the 
variation coefficient which was higher for the copper runoff rate; ± 49%, against ± 32% for the 
roofs. This difference is also called into question by the second approach of the sensitivity 
analysis, where the three input parameters influenced equally the copper flux. By fluctuating 
them by 10%, the copper load was affected by only 4.1%. Nevertheless, these three parameters 
were part of the ten most influential.  
 
 
5.1.2 Road covers and traffic intensities 
Concerning the traffic, the calculation was not straightforward as it was conducted for 
several categories of vehicles.  
 Annual traffic volume 
Passenger cars represented the biggest copper source followed by bicycles and finally 
heavy vehicles. This latter category possessed a low annual traffic volume. In fact, as the studied 
area is located in the centre of Copenhagen with many pedestrian streets, few heavy vehicles 
cross the catchment. The annual traffic volume from passenger cars was between five and one 
hundred forty-six times higher than that of heavy vehicles, and from bicycles, it was between 
seventeen and five hundred and eight times higher.  
 
As a reminder, the road categories were divided into only two sections: the small and 
intermediate roads; and the ones located on the borders of the catchments were neglected. 
However, bigger roads are present in this area, notably with an ADT value rising up to 24 800 
vehicles per day. As a consequence, this assumption could have modified the estimation. 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
Page | 47  
 
The roads parameters were analysed as influential factors according to their variability. 
Regarding the ADT values for the passenger cars, they influenced the copper load by ±15.1% 
for the small roads, and ±12.7% for the intermediate ones. They represented the fifth and 
eighth more influential parameters. However, the variation coefficient was significantly higher 
for the small roads; ±80% against ±49% for the intermediate ones. Concerning the bicycles, the 
ADT values impacted the load by only ±4.9% and ±4.5% respectively. Finally, the lengths of the 
roads were very less influential. 
Nevertheless, by varying these parameters by an equal coefficient, the most influential 
road parameter was the length of the intermediate road, with an influence of ±3.4% on the 
copper load, being part of the ten more influential parameters, as well as the cars ADT value of 
the intermediate road with a percentage of ±2.6%. The influence of the length is higher because 
it influences the contribution from all vehicles. 
 
 Brake wear  
Concerning the brake wear of passenger cars, no differentiation was made for the 
copper emission from the front and the rear wheels, while several studies indicate that it is 
higher at the front (Westerlund, 2001; mentioned by Sörme & Lagerkvist, 2002; Budai & 
Clement, 2011). As a consequence, a coefficient could have been applied in order to take this 
aspect into account. Similarly, the Danish car pool was not known so values from the drum and 
disc brakes, but also from branded and independent suppliers were mixed together to obtain 
the range with the minimum and maximum extremes. These simplifications impacted the 
calculation of the copper flux, of which brakes presented the largest value range in comparison 
with the contributions from the other sources. Moreover, by performing the   sensitivity 
analysis, the variability of the brake content from passenger cars impacted the global copper 
flux with the highest value of ±41.8%. However, its variation coefficient rose up to 100%, so the 
estimation of this parameter could be revised by taking these criteria into consideration to 
precise its range.  The brake wear from cars revealed also a consequent influence on the copper 
load by ±30.8%, with a variation coefficient of 74%. 
Moreover, by fluctuating by ±10% the copper content in car brakes or the related wear rate, 
the copper flux value was impacted by 4.2%. It represented the second highest influential value. 
 
Concerning the heavy vehicles, similar comments could be enounced but the 
parameters were not assessed as influential. In addition, knowing that the low proportion of 
heavy vehicles in the catchment, it is not relevant to refine the parameters’ estimation of this 
category.  
 
Regarding the bikes, the computed copper flux range was very important, with a 
potential contribution of 17.2% on the annual copper flux, representing one third of the cars’ 
contribution, which questioned the estimation. In fact, as no data were available, many 
assumptions were made in order to estimate the input parameters. As a consequence, the 
results may be biased. For example, concerning the braking wear rate, the contact surface was 
estimated in an arbitrary way, by assuming 10 cm2 per segment. Moreover, only one reference 
was used for cars. By assuming an order of magnitude of 100 rather than 10, the absolute 
copper flux was between [0.003-501.7] Cu.kg.year-1. After running the Monte Carlo method, 
the refined range was found as follows: [3.7-170] Cu.kg.year-1. In this case, the major 
contributions were coming from the brake wear, representing 65% with 59.4% from the cars, 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
Page | 48  
 
3.4% from the heavy vehicles and only 2% from the bicycles, and the remaining fraction of 35% 
concerned the roofs. The bicycle contributions declined, showing the importance of the 
parameters’ estimation. As a consequence, measurements could be conducted to improve this 
uncertainty. Moreover, the copper was estimated from that of passenger cars, but the 
composition may not be the same. Likewise, this study analysed only disc brakes but other 
types of brakes exist, notably the coaster or drum brakes.   
The influence of the variability of the copper content in brake bicycles was the sixth 
most influential parameters on the copper load, with a possible impact of ±14.1% and a 
variation coefficient of ±100%, whereas the brake wear rate was the tenth, with an influence 
of ±10.4% and a variation coefficient of ±74%. In fact the by applying the same coefficient of 
variation of ±10% to all the factors, these two parameters affected the global copper flux by 
only ±1.4%. 
 
Finally, the brake efficiency was assessed as an important parameter. In fact, this value 
is applied for all the vehicles’ categories with a defined range revealing an order of magnitude 
of 3 between the minimum and maximum values. It affected by ±29.4% the copper load 
according to its variability, which was expressed by a value of ±50%. Brake efficiency was the 
third most influential parameter within this sensitivity analysis, and the first one by considering 
the same variation for all the parameters. 
 
 Tire tread wear 
The results demonstrated a significant difference regarding the contribution from brake 
and tire wear on the global copper load, with an order of magnitude of one thousand. It may 
come from the low values of the copper emission, smaller than those of brakes with an order 
of magnitude between ten and ten thousand. By reviewing the contribution from all sources in 
the catchment, the tire wear was negligible. Moreover, the tires’ parameters were not assessed 
as influential on the copper load. The different assumptions and estimations which was 
established could be discussed, but they are not priorities.   
 
 
5.1.3 Atmospheric deposition 
As the calculation of the copper flux from atmospheric deposition was a simple 
multiplication between the exposed surface and the deposition rate; the relation between the 
deposition rate and the related copper flux was a linear correlation with a coefficient of 1. As a 
consequence larger deposition rate resulted in a higher value of copper flux. However, the 
copper flux from atmospheric deposition was assessed as negligible in comparison with the 
roofing and braking sources. It may come from the very low value of the deposition rate in 
Denmark.  
 
 
5.1.4 Comparison with other studies 
In this study case, the major identified copper sources were roofs (30 to 84%) and 
brakes (16 to 70%). These results are linked to the studied catchment, with its own 
characteristics. So, for another watershed, these contributions could be different.  
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
Page | 49  
 
For example, in the study conducted by Petrucci & al (2014), in Sucy in France, the major 
sources were brakes (79%), atmospheric deposition (12%) and roofs (9%).  Davis & al (2001), 
on a hypothetical American area, concluded on the dominance of brake wear (47 %), followed 
by the buildings’ contribution (31 %), then the atmospheric deposition (21 %), and finally the 
tire wear (1%). Chèvre & al (2011), in Lausanne, found that the main copper sources were 
trolleybus catenaries and roofs, followed by brake and train catenaries. 
Differences can be observed between these examples but brakes and roofs still 
constitute important copper sources. Their contribution varied according to their importance 
in the studied catchment. For example, Petrucci & al (2014) highlighted that copper is not a 
common material in the studied area, that is why the related contribution was lower. Similarly, 
if the traffic intensities are different, it will impact the contribution from brakes. In addition, 
the atmospheric deposition appeared more significant in some studies. Other sources were 
identified in the study from Chèvre & (2011) where the catenaries were important, a copper 
source which was absent in this project. 
In this study, the studied catchment was characterised by many copper roofs, with low 
car traffic but an important fraction of bicycles, and a very low deposition rate.  
 
 
5.2 Uncertainty and sensitivity analyses 
The uncertainty analysis with the Monte Carlo method permitted to refine the copper 
flux value ranges and obtain more representative results based on occurrence probabilities. 
Generally, it reduced the pessimistic scenarios whereas it increased the optimistic ones for 
each source, allowing to remove the extreme and outlier values found with the minimum and 
maximum and obtain more representative ranges. 
 
Concerning the sensitivity analysis, the braking parameters were analysed as the most 
influential on the total copper flux, especially from passenger cars, followed by the copper roofs 
surface whereas the tire wear and the atmospheric deposition were negligible. 
Regarding the influence coming from the variability of the parameters, the variation 
coefficient gave important information. In fact, certain parameters had statistically the same 
influence on the copper load but as their variability was different, the parameter possessing 
the larger range was considered as more influential. This approach allowed to target the 
parameters to refine.    
Moreover, the second approach permitted to identify the influential parameters in a 
particular situation, by taking as reference the average of copper flux. But if the same 
calculation was performed with other reference values, the order of the influential parameters 
and their computed impact could have been completely different. However, the use of the 
average values was more relevant in order to neglect the influence of the variability of the 
parameters, which was already discussed above  
 
 
5.3 Environmental impact 
The calculation of the daily harbour’s volume was based on arbitrary assumptions as the 
daily flow was not known, which are questionable. A deeper analysis or measurements could 
permit to reduce these doubts. 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
Page | 50  
 
Otherwise, if the release of the urban runoff into the environment is unreasonable, 
technical solutions can be considered to drain the area and treat water pollution.  
Firstly, end-of-pipe solutions can be envisaged.  
By carrying the runoff to a detention pond, the pollutant will settle before to release the water 
into the environment by a separate sewer or upstream in a combined system. These techniques 
have shown a good ability for removing metal (Belizario & al, 2015). However, these devices 
are constituted with storage capacity, so they need space to be installed.   
Moreover, many run-of-water devices exist. Concerning metals, bioretention systems, porous 
pavement, infiltration basin have good metal removal potential. For example, bioretention 
systems allowing to retain temporarily the water and then trap the pollutant particles through 
vegetation and soil layers. Specific and enhanced vegetation can be used to improve treatment 
performance. These techniques can take different shapes and be integrated into the 
streetscape (Woods Ballard & al, 2007). Moreover, it permits to reduce the imperviousness. To 
adapt these devices to the study, the design of the devices according to the copper load is 
necessary. However, the centre of Copenhagen may not provide enough space to implement 
these technologies.   
Other space-saving water quality technologies exist as the hydrodynamic separation using the 
density difference between the particles and the water to separate them, or the lamellar 
clarifier removing particles from liquids by settling. 
However, all these techniques need deeper study in order to evaluate the copper removal 
potential but also the possibilities according to the catchment characteristics. 
An intervention at the source could also reduce the copper with preventive actions such 
as the reduction of the pollutant emission, relatively complicated, or also the reduction of the 
urban runoff. This latter procedure can be conducted by limiting the soil sealing and increasing 
the direct infiltration. 
 
 
 
5.4 Towards a more automated method for land cover characterisation  
In view of the importance of the roofing copper source, the achieved results from the 
experiments conducted on the land cover classification methods are discussed. 
5.4.1 Benefits and inconvenient for each land cover classification method 
In general, the parametrisation of pixel-based methods influenced the accuracy of the 
resulting projected surface, obtained from copper roofs. However, each developed method 
possesses its own benefits and disadvantages. 
 Aerial Photography Interpretation 
With this method, the omitted features concerning the copper roofs detection were 
normally very low because they are really visible on the orthophoto. However, the largest 
limitation of this method was the dependence of the outlines of the building layer on QGIS, 
with a significant fraction of composite roofs (37% of the entire roofing surface). This category 
was constituted with different materials, in particular, copper which were not quantified. 
Knowing that the estimation of a part of the metal accessories increased the surface of this 
category by approximately 67%, this omission may have impacted the considered surface in 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
Page | 51  
 
the catchment. To improve these gaps, a visual recognition could be conducted but it will be 
time-consuming, except if it is performed on a sample and extrapolate to the catchment scale. 
Although the copper roofs were easily detected, other roofs having similar colours were 
difficult to distinguish. However, given the good quality of the orthophoto, the materials were 
easily distinguished and the unknown category was relatively low, only 1% of the entire roofing 
surface.  
This approach allowed a contextualisation by considering or the excluding features with 
human intelligence. For example, after having classified several recognisable roofs, its materials 
and texture were known. By comparing features, it was possible to classify the unknown 
category by making assumptions. However, the API method is a very time consuming and 
subjective method with possible human errors. 
 
 Pixel-Based Detection 
By comparing the results with the orthophoto, the omitted features did not seem to be 
numerous with this procedure. To ensure good recognition, the parametrisation of the spectral 
library and the created code was essential in order to detect the copper surfaces in the 
photography. In the study case, the colour sampling was carried out with the aim of containing 
a wide array of colours. It permitted to detect the copper under different lighting conditions 
(shadows) and roofing accessories. However, many “parasite areas” were observed. In fact, 
non-copper pixels were not always correctly classified; hence it may have affected the 
estimated surface. In addition, the arbitrary rule concerning the “parasite areas” could have 
been more developed by creating a script taking into account the surrounding pixels.  
Furthermore, different parameters affected the recognition of the pixels such as 
brightness, contrast, and saturation of the photography. The sampling was performed in 
specific lighting conditions. If the methodology must be transferable to another type of aerial 
photography, some settings and adjustments will be probably required. The PBD method was 
only conducted to detect copper surfaces. To extend it to other colours, new spectral libraries 
must be created, which is time-consuming. The green copper has a characteristic and flashy 
colour, making for easy identification of it. However, for other materials with more common 
colours, this method can be more difficult to implement or even unsuitable. 
The main benefit of this method was the simplicity of the rules regarding copper roofs 
detection, permitting a very fast classification process. However, the difficulty was to 
adequately define these empirical rules in order to achieve high recognition rates. 
Furthermore, the pixel-based approach does not take into account textural and contextual 
information.  
 
 Semi-automatic Classification Plugin  
The greater interest of this method is the classification of all pixels present in the 
orthophoto in the catchment studied. Moreover, according to the objectives of the study, it is 
possible to choose the number of (macro)classes and their parametrisation. As a consequence, 
the SCP method is flexible for different types of study. However, in the same way, this 
adaptability makes the process difficult and the input parameters sensitive. Special care is 
needed with respect to the step of the ROIs creation. In fact, this phase is determinant to obtain 
an accurate classification. That is why trials are recommended to understand and correctly 
master the plugin.  
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
Page | 52  
 
In the study, it was difficult with the SCP method to clearly distinguish certain 
macroclasses. Concrete roofs, metal roofs and roads surfaces had similar spectral signature 
plots. Such information was used by the algorithm for calculation, resulting in an insufficient 
classification. However, it could be possible to add more ROIs to improve this result, but it was 
not performed in the interest of time and efficiency. However, the method permitted to detect 
and quantify the copper roofing accessories such as dormers or roofing decorations. 
The unclassified fraction was significant, approximately between 20 and 30% of the 
catchment area, which may explain the small estimated copper surfaces. However, by 
improving the parametrisation, the unclassified class can be decreased or even removed. The 
use of the threshold leads to the creation of the unclassified class. A classification without 
threshold can result in overestimation or underestimation of a given class. In the study, the 
threshold allowed to not involve the shadowed surface to the same category.  
 
Depending on the scale and the objectives of the studied area, this method can be more 
or less fast in terms of parametrisation and calculation process. It also depends on the 
resolution of photography. By working at a larger scale, spectral characteristics can be more 
heterogeneous between the expected classes. For example, to distinguish agricultural land, 
urban areas, and water bodies, the spectral characteristics are normally contrasted, facilitating 
the classification.

 Comparison between the polygonal and the pointer approaches from the SCP 
Concerning the overall accuracy, the SCP pointer approach was higher than the 
threshold of 85% with a value of 98.6%, indicating that the classification is acceptable. On the

contrary, the overall accuracy of the polygonal approach was only 72.4%.  However, this 
threshold was applied by Anderson & al (1976) in a particular application scenario: it was used 
for mapping broad land-cover classes. As a consequence, this threshold is not a standard and 
it can be inappropriate and unrealistically high. It depends upon the scale of the study, nature 
and number of the classes, but also the classification process, hence it is important to question 
its suitability (Foody, 2008) although generalisation is difficult because of a high degree of 
context dependency.  
The overall accuracy and the general kappa coefficient were really higher with the 
pointer approach. However, by focusing on the accuracy of the classified copper roofs pixels, 
the differences were more or less visible. The pointer approach was still assessed as more 
accurate. In fact, the producer accuracy was two-time higher and the error of omission was 
fifteen-time lower. This difference should come from the heterogeneity of the created ROIs by 
drawing polygons, notably the “shadow pixels” which have spectral similarity with other 
macroclasses, causing many overlaps. That could explain why many pixels were classified in 
concrete, metals or road covers.  
In addition, the user accuracy and error commission were closer between the two 
approaches. It should mean that the specific green coloured pixels were well detected and very 
few of these were wrongly classified. The same was true for the kappa coefficient, which means 
that the copper roofs macroclass was in general quite well detected according to the 
parametrisation of the ROIs. Indeed, the difference of the detected surface was not very 
pronounced between both approaches. The polygonal approach resulted in a 20% higher 
surface than the pointer approach. 
As a consequence, these two ways of parametrisation questioned the representability 
of the results. With the pointer method, the assessment was better, but it should be noticed 
that the ROIs were consequently smaller, 4.8-times for the copper roofs macroclass, and 6-
times concerning all the pixels. In fact, the pointer algorithm selected more homogeneous 
pixels for the creation of ROIs, with five-times less overlaps between macroclasses, affecting 
the unclassified class which was 5% higher. A balance must be struck between homogeneity 
and heterogeneity, because otherwise many pixels will be incorrectly classified.

5.5 Future outlooks and recommendations 
5.5.1 Possible improvements of the entire methodology and estimations processes 
 Regarding the parameters’ estimation, the ranges were relatively large with sometimes 
extreme minimum and maximum values. As a consequence, these inputs could be refined to 
increase the precision of the resulting copper flux, especially for the influential parameters such 
as the brake data. In addition, some parameters need measurements since no data were 
available. It was the case for the bicycle brake inputs. By improving this step, the methodology 
may become more specific according to the studied catchment.  
 
It could be interesting to apply the methodology to another time period, as a single rain 
event because the pollutant flux can be very different from one event to another. In fact, the 
manner of spreading of pollutants depends on the amount and the distribution of rainwater 
over time (Hedberg & al, 2014). Hence, it will be interesting to develop the approach with 
various sizes of a single rainfall event, such as a cloudburst or small precipitation, in order to 
assess the influence of rainfall intensity, duration or the first flush effect with the length of dry 
period before the rainfall event. In this case, the copper concentration would be meaningful 
and the environmental impact could be assessed more adequately. However, it will be 
necessary to adjust all the parameters according to this new time scale. 
 
The land cover recognition was conducted only for the roof and ground areas, but not 
the building façade, which can also be a significant pollutant source (Davis & al, 2001). Thus, 
this source could be added to the methodology. Similarly, the inclinations of the roofs were 
defined as equal, which is a simplified assumption but this parameter could be developed to 
obtain a more representative result given this impact on the copper flux.  
 
The methodology was only developed for copper load estimation but with the objective 
to be expanded to other pollutants. To make it possible, researches and adjustments will be 
necessary such as the definition of the main sources for each pollutant and their related 
parameters. However, each catchment possesses its own characteristics so generalisation it is 
not possible. The methodology provides nevertheless the general framework with the different 
steps to follow. However, each step can be independently improved, allowing an enhancement 
of the methodology at different levels. In addition, it could be interesting to develop the same 
approach with another urban catchment in order to conduct comparisons between the results. 
 
 
5.5.2 Towards a more automated detection for land cover characterisation 
The main problem of the semi-automatic methods which was observed during this 
project was the spectral similarities between classes and the dependency on the lighting 
conditions of the image. To improve this aspect, the anticipation of the location of shadows 
and the development of a script to classify these pixels according to the ones classified on the 
surrounding could be helpful. It could be also developed to overcome the consistent fraction 
of the unclassified class with the SCP Plugin, which was a significant limitation of the method. 
However, the parametrisation can also be more analysed to resolve this issue.  
 
 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
Page | 58  
 
One of the major limitations with the semi-automatic methods developed in this 
project, either PBD or SCP, is that they recognise only pixel and spectral characteristics but not 
contextual, textural and spatial information. Pixel-based detections are criticised because they 
are closely related to the particularities of the image (El Merabet & al, 2015) and real-world 
objects are not characterised by single and square pixels (Kampouraki & al, 2007). 
To resolve these issues, another approach could be explored: an object-oriented image 
analysis. It is based on pixel groupings which contain spectral, shape and texture information. 
In fact, a pre-processing step called segmentation allows extracting objects (similar 
neighbouring pixels) which are then classified (Cleve & al, 2008) according to the average, or 
other, spectral properties. In this way, this method reduces spectral variation and creates 
relationships between the different objects, which can be used in the classification process 
(“close to”, “surrounded by”…). With high-resolution imagery, pixel-based techniques can be 
challenged whereas object-oriented methods are more efficient. However, at the catchment 
scale, it is not really necessary. In addition, Pixel-based methods preserve the heterogeneity of 
urban areas which can also be considered as a benefit in terms of representativeness. 
Moreover, Object-oriented methods are more complete but they require more attention with 
an additional step. With the objective to develop a fast and easy methodology, to estimate 
approximately the order of magnitude of the pollutant flux, pixel-based methods may be 
sufficient and adequate. 
 
However, even if the detected copper surface varied between -36% and +24%, the 
impact of the overall copper flux was assessed to ±13.2 %. The influence of the variability of 
the roofing source on the overall copper flux was not the most significant parameter of the 
methodology. 
 
Finally, the decision to replace the API approach by a semi-automatic method depends 
on the objective of the study, but to estimate the order of magnitude of the copper pollution 
in an urban catchment, these techniques seem to be adequate. However, special care must be 
taken with parametrisations.

6. CONCLUSION  
This work was designed as an introductory study to estimate copper loads in the urban 
stormwater runoff. The project investigated the influence of the different copper sources, input 
parameters, and land cover characterisation methods on the copper loads in the studied 
catchment. The main conclusions are: 
 
 The assessment of the influence of the different sources showed that roofing and 
braking sources were significant with, respectively, a contribution between 30 and 
84%, and between 16 and 70%.  As a consequence, the most influential parameters 
were linked with these two sources, and especially the brake wear as its inputs were 
expressed by wide ranges. The most influential parameter according its variability was 
the copper content in brake wear for passenger cars. It affected the global copper flux 
by ±41.8%. On the contrary, atmospheric deposition and tire wear were negligible.  
 
 Aerial photography was an effective tool for mapping land covers due to its high-
resolution over large-scale areas. However, the used semi-automatic methods were 
limited by the insufficient detection accuracy because of the lighting conditions and 
spectral similarities between the land covers. As a consequence, they required 
different manual interventions and involved a part of subjectivity. Time-saving of the 
proposed methods was relative because the parametrisation demanded attention. 
Regarding the classification, they were nevertheless fast in terms of computation and 
affordable to implement and apply. They determined hotspots and offered the 
possibility to be transferable in other studies even if adjustments will be necessary.  
The automation level was not as high as expected because of the consequent manual 
and sensitive parametrisation. As a consequence, the objective to create a completely 
automatic tool should still be investigated. 
 
 The impact of the land cover classification methods on the copper flux was important 
regarding only the copper roofs contribution. In fact, the output was linearly 
correlated to the copper surface parameter, with a coefficient of 1. However, this 
influence decreased by taking into account the contribution from all sources. The 
variability of the copper surfaces was ±32% but it only impacted the global copper flux 
by ±13.2 %, a linear correlation with a coefficient of 0.41 was analysed. As a 
consequence, the copper roofs surfaces were masked at the global scale. 
 
 Concerning the quality assessment, the resulting copper concentration was higher 
than the authorised threshold with an order of magnitude fluctuating between 
approximately one hundred and one thousand. It seems unreasonable to release the 
runoff, but the related uncertainty prevents to certify it. 
 
 
These results are linked to the studied catchment, with its own characteristics. So, for 
another watershed, these conclusions could be different. As a consequence, this methodology 
gives the general framework to apply but adjustments are required in order to be transferable.   
The methodology involved a number of uncertainties due to simplifying assumptions, as well 
as the variability of the numerous parameters. Nevertheless, it provided an order of magnitude 
regarding the copper estimation in urban stormwater runoff. Hence, it can help to planning and 
management activities at the city scale, by detecting pollutant hotspots.

8. APPENDICES  
8.1 APPENDIX A: Detailed explanation concerning the land cover classification on QGIS  
Opening of QGIS in the 3.4 version 
Download the aerial photography as a raster layer and the catchment area as a vector layer on 
the software. 
 
 
The used aerial photography was a multiband picture composed of five color bands: 
[1-Red, 2-Green, 3-Blue, 4- Red edge, 5-Near infrared] 
As aerial photographs are generally huge files, which it is difficult to work with, it is 
recommended to clip it with the catchment boundary to reduce the size of the calculation.  
In the main menu, click on “Raster” – “Extraction” – “Clip raster layer by mask layer” or “Clip 
raster layer by extent”. The second choice can be necessary if you need some context around 
your catchment. In this study, the clip was done by extent. Aerial photography represents the 
input layer. For the clipping extent, click on “…” box on the right, and choose “Use layer extent”. 
A window will open, select the catchment layer.  
 
 
 
Click on “Ok”. Verify that the box “open output file after running algorithm” is checked and 
choose a file path to save the new layer. Then “Run”.

8.1.1 API 
For the Aerial Photography Interpretation, the used method was very easy.  
The clipped aerial photography, the buildings, and the catchment layers have to be opened on 
QGIS. If the buildings layer is bigger than the catchment area, it is possible to change it. In the 
main menu, click on “Vector” – “Geoprocessing tools” – “Intersection”. 
 
 
 
 
The input layer is the buildings layer and the overlay layer represents the catchment. Save the 
layer and choose a file path, then “Run”. 
 
 
To define the roofing materials, two additional columns should be created on the attribute 
table of the building layer. Click on this layer and then on the following icon: 
. The attribute 
table of the layer will open. Click on

step is to repeat twice because the objective is to create two columns.  
 
 
 
 
After that, it is possible to write the materiality for each roof by visual interpretation. To 
compute the surface, it can be automatized. Click on the field calculator 
. A window will

box where the surface field has to be selected. In the text area, type “$area”.

Finally, click on “ok”. All the surface will appear in the attribute table. 
 
You can export the attribute table as an excel file. Right click on the buildings layer and click 
“Export” – “Save the features as”. A window will open. In the box called format, choose “MS 
Office Open XML spreadsheet [XLSX]”. For the file name, click on “…” box, give a name and select 
the file path. Click on “Ok”. 
 
In Excel, you can manually classify the file and calculate the total area of each material. 
 
 
8.1.2 PBD 
The clipped aerial photography has to be opened on QGIS. You can manage different settings 
of aerial photography by a right click – “Properties”. A new window will open, select the 
symbology tab. For instance, in the color rendering section, it is possible to enhance the 
contrast, brightness, and saturation of the picture. 
 
After that, the code must be entered to realise the classification. In the menu click on “Raster” 
– “Raster Calculator”. A new window will open and the code can be copied in the text area 
called Raster Calculator Expression. 
 
Code:  
( ( 0.248 < ( "mask@1" / ("mask@1" + "mask@2" + "mask@3") )  AND  ( "mask@1" / ("mask@1" + "mask@2" + 
"mask@3") ) < 0.335 ) AND ( 0.339 < ( "mask@2" / ("mask@1" + "mask@2" + "mask@3") ) AND ( "mask@2" / 
("mask@1" + "mask@2" + "mask@3") ) < 0.366 ) AND ( 0.313 < ( "mask@3" / ( "mask@1" + "mask@2" + 
"mask@3") ) AND ( "mask@3" / ("mask@1" + "mask@2" + "mask@3") )  < 0.393 ) 
AND "mask@1" * ( "mask@1"  >  63 AND "mask@1" < 218 ) AND "mask@2" * ( "mask@2"  >  78 AND "mask@2" 
< 236 ) AND "mask@3" * ( "mask@3"  >  89 AND "mask@3" < 233 )) 
 
Give a name and a path file for the output layer. Click on “Ok” and wait until the computation 
has completely finished. A new raster layer will appear with black and white pixels. The white 
pixels (class 1) represent the target materiality, copper in this case. And the black pixels (class 
0) are the non-copper fraction.

 to alleviate the layer. Similarly, add a surface

8.1.3 SCP 
With this method, the first step is the download of the SCP Plugin on QGIS. Click on “Plugins” 
– “Manage and install plugins…”. A window will open.  
 
 
 
In the search bar, type Semi-Automatic Classification Plugin 
 and click on “Install 
plugin”. After the installation, you must close QGIS and reopen the software. A toolbar should 
be added. 
 
 
 
 
After opening the aerial photography as a raster layer, it is possible to make the 
classification. First, in the “Home” tab of the “SCP Dock” (on the left of you screen), click on the 
Band set icon 
. A window will open and the aerial photography has to be selected in the 
‘Multiband image list’. Click and the drop-down menu and choose the good file. If nothing is 
visible, you must refresh by clicking on 
. After realising this step, the colour band will appear 
in the ‘Band set definition’ tab. You can now close the window. 
In the “SCP Docks”, click now on the “Training input” tab. Create a new training input with the 
following icon: 
. Give it a name and a file path. The training output permits to store the 
different ROIs, which are polygons defining the spectral characteristics on the area.

The creation of ROIs defines the different macroclasses and classes for the future classification.

Choose the colours of the classes and the macroclasses, it will be used for the final mapping 
classification.

signatures. This step is important because different materials can have similar spectral

permitting to compare the highlighted signatures for two color bands.

All these tools give information concerning the spectral signatures. It permits to validate 
the created signatures by comparing the different classes of each macroclass. 
 
 
In the “Plot” tab, a visual graphic allows comparing really quickly the different 
signatures. The signatures that are highlighted orange indicate that they overlap with other 
signatures. A column called “Color [Overlap MC_ID-C_ID]” indicates the relevant signatures.  
Concerning the plots of the different macroclasses, if they are too similar, it could be 
problematic for the classification.  
 
Click on the “Signature details” tab. A table will appear and you can see the average 
pixel value and its standard deviation for each signature in order to the color band. It is also 
possible to know the total number of pixels contained in the ROIs. 
Smaller is the standard deviation, better is the homogeneity of the created ROIs. This 
parameter is not really useful if the ROI pointer was used for the creation of the ROIs. In fact, 
with the ROI pointer, it is possible to specify the maximum spectral distance of the ROI, so the 
standard deviation should be quite small, unless a large spectral distance is defined. On the 
contrary, it can be practical if the polygon drawing was used.

 to run the

Different tables will appear. The two first lines represent the two compared signatures and 
their characteristics. Below, four different measures are presented:  
- 
Jeffries-Matusita Distance: If the value is close to 0, the two signatures are very similar. The 
scale raises to a maximum of 2. This parameter is useful with the maximum likelihood algorithm 
but not measured in this case  
- 
Spectral Angle: If the value is equal to 0, both signatures are identical. The scale raises to 90, 
which means that the signatures are completely different. It is practical for spectral angle 
mapping classifications.  
- 
Euclidean Distance: Smaller is this parameter, more similar are the signatures. It is useful for 
minimum distance classifications.  
- 
Bray-Curtis Similarity: This parameter is a percentage. If it is closed to the 0 value, the two 
signatures are completely different whereas if it raises to 100, they are identical. This 
information can be used in a general context. 
 
 
 
If a value is displayed in red, it means that the signatures are particularly similar.

, in the “SCP dock”. It is also possible to merge highlighted signatures with

First, you have to choose if you want to make the classification by using the

After the parametrization of the thresholds, close the window.

be used only for class overlapping pixel by checking “only overlap” box. 
 
Finally, there are some parameters concerning the output file such as the application 
of “Apply mask” to exclude the area outside the added shapefile and thus accelerate the 
calculation process. It is also possible to “Create vector” to obtain automatically the shapefile 
layers for all the materials. In fact, if you do not check this box, the output will be in a raster 
format. Concerning the studied classification output, a vector layer was generated to quickly 
compute the surfaces of the different classes and have their localisation.

The Kappa coefficient gives information about the accuracy of the classification process. 
If the value is close to 1, it means that the classification is highly accurate. If it is close to 0, it is 
not better than random. 
Concerning the overall accuracy, the more the percentage is high (near to 100%), the more the 
classification is accurate. Furthermore, a raster layer will appear showing the errors in aerial 
photography. The pixel values represent the categories of comparison between the 
classification and the reference files.   
 
To obtain the final percentages for each macroclass, click in the post-processing icon
, in the “SCP dock”. A window will open. In the “classification report” tab, choose the 
classified file and run the calculation. After a few time, the result will be displayed. Caution, if 
the area is given in square degree, just take into account the percentages and calculate the 
area in square meters. 
 
 
 
 
In the study, aerial photography was in a rectangular shape with a surface of 756 392 
m2. A mask was applied to compute the classification only on the catchment area, and the 
classification report was executed. After the calculation, the report mentioned a very high 
percentage of unclassified pixels because the inverse of the catchment area was classified in 
this macroclass. Thus, manual computation was necessary to readjust the results. 
In the table below, illustrating the example with the pointer method, the percentage was given 
by the classification report. The corresponding surfaces were hence calculated. 410 953 m2 was 
removed to the unclassified macroclass, representing the difference between the total aerial 
photography and the catchment areas, and so the final surfaces and percentages were found.
