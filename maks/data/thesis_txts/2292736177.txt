ABSTRACT 
Due to their specific properties, related to their small size and increased specific surface area, nanoparticles 
are increasingly being embedded in consumer products. The increase in use will enviably lead to a potential 
increase in nanoparticles released to the environment. One of the tools used to assess the environmental 
impacts of consumer products is Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). This tool accounts for all the phases included 
in the life cycle by aggregating the material flows in the product system and characterizing them in relation 
to predefined impact categories. So far these impact characterization models have not been modified to 
account for the specific properties and behavior related to nanoparticles. Therefore, the aim of this study 
(1) to develop characterization factors for TiO2 nanoparticles for the three toxic impact categories included 
in USEtox. (2) to adapt the USEtox framework so that it also accounts for dermal exposure of both 
nanoparticles and organic chemicals. (3) to implement the methodologies developed in a case study on 
sunscreen containing nano-TiO2 as a UV absorber and based on this assess the environmental burden of the 
nanoparticles.   
The characterization factors for nano-TIO2 was determined for freshwater ecotoxicity to be 6.48E+02 
PAF.m3.d/kgemitted and 1.66E+03 PAF.m3.d/kgemitted for emission compartments air and freshwater, 
respectively. When compared to the characterization factors also developed for nano-TiO2 by Salieri et al. 
(2015) and Miseljic (2014) these are several order of magnitude larger. The large difference is mainly due to 
the inclusion of the toxic impact of aggregated particles, which was included in this study. The 
characterization factors for Human toxicity cancer was determined to be 4.56E-06 cases/kgemitted for 
emission into air. For human toxicity non-cancer the CFs are, 5.88E-05 cases/kgemitted and 6.05E-06 
cases/kgemitted for emissions to air and water, respectively. To our knowledge these are the first 
characterization factors for human toxicity developed for nanoparticles.  
These factors were determined by implementing the fate model Simplebox4nano into the USEtox 
framework and by accounting for nanospecific properties when deriving the exposure and effect factors. 
Overall it is recommended to ensure that nanospecific transformation processes is accounted for when 
modeling the fate and that the empirical data applied for the exposure and effects modeling is evaluated 
according to reliability relevance for nanoparticles.   
Furthermore the USEtox framework was adapted to account for dermal exposure by developing 
characterization factors for this exposure route as well. CFs for dermal exposure of nano-TiO2 and a range 
of organic chemicals normally included in consumer products such as sunscreen was determined based on 
empirical data collection. However, these values came out several orders of magnitude larger relative to 
the human toxicity – non-cancer CFs determined for the same compounds. Hence, they are assessed to be 
very high and it is thus recommended to apply these factors with caution.   
Characterization factors for both nano-TiO2 and other sunscreen ingredients were applied in a LCA case 
study on sunscreen containing nano-TiO2. This assessment showed that the nano-TiO2 contributed 
significantly to the environmental burden of the sunscreen at that an increase in nano-TiO2 concentration 
lead to an overall increase for most impacts categories. This highlighted the importance of including 
nanospecific considerations when conducting LCA and also led to some recommendations regarding 
nanoparticle concentrations in consumer products.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The use of nanoparticles in consumer products can create stronger, lighter, cleaner, smarter and more 
efficient products depending on the physical and chemical properties of the particle. The small size of 
nanoparticles, which leads to an increased specific surface area, changes the properties of conventional 
materials. Because of this the production, development and use of nanoparticles has increased significantly 
and the list of products embedded with nanoparticles is still growing (Mangematin and Walsh 2012; “The 
Nanodatabase” 2015). One of the most commercially used nanoparticles is nano-TiO2 (See graph in 
Appendix A ) (“The Nanodatabase” 2015). The increased surface area leads to greater catalytic activity and 
UV absorption capabilities which is exploited in products such as self-cleaning surface coatings, solar cells, 
sporting goods and sunscreens (Davis et al. 2010).   
The increased use of nanoparticles also leads to an increased release of these particles into the 
environment. So far mapping the fate of nanoparticles has proven to be difficult as they often act 
unpredictably compared to conventional materials (Therezien, Thill, and Wiesner 2014; Gottschalk et al. 
2015).  
There have been discussions whether a precautionary principle should be adopted in order to avoid 
environmental impacts relative to the potential benefits gained by applying nanotechnology to certain 
products and systems (Klöpffer et al. 2007; Hansen et al. 2013). Because of this and in order to ensure a 
better understanding of the impacts of applying nanoparticles, new methodologies for modelling the 
behavior and testing the toxicity are currently being developed in order to ensure that the impacts of the 
increasing use is predicted and understood (Meesters et al. 2014; Kühnel and Nickel 2014a; OECD 2014).  
One of the tools used to evaluate the environmental impacts of a product or a system is the comprehensive 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). In LCA the impacts of a given product or system are assessed from cradle-to-
grave. A typical LCA consist of four parts: 
 
Goal and scope which sets the frame of the assessment and defines the objective of the study. 
 
Life cycle inventory analysis (LCI) which quantifies the elementary flows exchanged between the 
product system and the environment.  
 
Life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) which classifies and characterizes the relationship between 
emissions and impacts. 
 
Finally the interpretation which identifies the most environmentally important issues and evaluates 
the consistency and sensitivity of the data and the assumptions made throughout. Overall LCA 
provides a tool which aims at assessing the environmental impacts of a system in holistic 
perspective (European Comission 2010). 
For the Life cycle impact assessment the aim is to aggregate the inventory into some predefined impact 
categories, such as climate change, Ozone depletion etc., which enables interpretation of the results. 
Depending on the impact assessment methodology this characterization of impacts is determined by 
applying a set of characterization factors (CFs), which is derived based on the fate, exposure and effects of 
a given substance in a given environmental compartment (European Comission 2010). An example of a 
methodology used to derive CFs is USEtox. This methodology applies a mass balance equation to the cause-
effect chain of the product system and thereby characterizes the related impacts. Three impact categories 
is included in the USEtox methodology: freshwater ecotoxicity, human toxicity cancer and human toxicity 
non-cancer (Hauschild et al. 2008; Rosenbaum et al. 2008). Several other impact categories also exists (see 
Appendix C) depending on the framework applied. The choice of impact assessment methodology should 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
12 
 
comply with the intended application of the study and is thus closely linked to the goal and scope of the 
study (European Comission 2010). 
Several LCA studies on nanoparticles have been conducted and despite some difficulties it is deemed 
feasible to study and compare impacts of nanoparticles using LCA (Jolliet, Rosenbaum, and Laurent 2013; 
Klöpffer et al. 2007).  
So far approximately 29 LCA studies have been published on nano-embedded products (Miseljic and Olsen 
2014). However, most of these studies have been limited in their scope or in the characterization of the 
nanoparticles. 20 out of the 29 studies conducted, only included the product system from “cradle-to-gate”, 
which means that only the production stages are included. Nanoparticles are often applied in products due 
to their novel properties because it is considered to be beneficial to the functioning of the product. When 
not including the whole product system in the assessment the functional benefits obtained by applying 
nanoparticles is neglected and conducting the life cycle assessment thus seems redundant. Only four out of 
the 29 studies include the characterization of the release of nanoparticles. So far, none of the impact 
assessment methodologies applied in LCA have so far incorporated characterization factors for 
nanoparticles. This means that the few studies that have attempted to include this in their assessment have 
either developed these individually or made assumptions regarding the release. See, for instance studies 
conducted by (Walser et al. 2011a; Pourzahedi and Eckelman 2015). In these studies the release of silver 
nanoparticles is characterized as the dissolved fraction and the potential release of the pristine particle is 
thus neglected. Another example is the study on CNTs by Eckelman et al. (2012) where characterization 
factors for freshwater ecotoxicity for the pristine particles were developed by adapting the USEtox 
methodology. An additional issue identified when conducting LCAs that include nanoparticles is the lack of 
inventory data. Almost all commercial data on the production on nanoparticles is confidential and is thus 
not publicly available. So far most studies have resolved this by simply applying or upscaling lab data  
(Walser et al. 2011a; Meyer et al. 2009). This could possible result in a larger impact of the production 
phase as it is assumed that the production on a larger commercial scale is more energy efficient (Eckelman 
et al. 2012).    
If LCA is to be a useful tool to evaluate the impacts of nano-embedded products, it is evident that some 
adjustments and developments are needed in order to fully cover the complexity of a product system that 
includes nanoparticles. The aim of this study is (1) to develop characterization factors for TiO2 nanoparticles 
for the three toxic impact categories included in USEtox. (2) to adapt the USEtox framework to also account 
for dermal exposure of both nanoparticles and organic chemicals. (3) to implement the methodologies 
developed in a case study on sunscreen containing nano-TiO2 as a UV absorber and based on this assess the 
environmental burden of the nanoparticles.   
In order to develop the LCA methodology to account for nanoparticles some the newest fate and behavior 
models for nanoparticles in implemented into the USEtox framework. As nano-TiO2 was adopted as a case 
study, a substantial literature review on the ecotoxicity of nano-TiO2 was therefore conducted in order 
evaluate the possible effects with the purpose of developing a set recommendations for the development 
of generic effect factors for nanoparticles. More specifically, the environmental impacts of a sunscreen 
lotion embedded with nano-TiO2, as a UV absorbers was evaluated with a focus on the environmental 
impacts of the nanoparticles’ contribution to the total environmental burden of the sunscreen compared to 
the burden of the other chemicals included in the sunscreen.

2 METHODS 
The relationship between nanotechnology and LCA is assessed at two levels in this study. Firstly the 
methodology of LCA is expanded to comprehend nano-TiO2 by developing characterization factors for both 
freshwater ecotoxicity and human toxicity which covers three exposure routes: inhalation, ingestion and 
dermal. Secondly the developed methodology is applied to a case study on topical sunscreen which 
contains nano-TiO2 as a UV-blocker.  
2.1 DEVELOPMENT OF CHARACTERIZATION FACTORS FOR NANO-TIO2 
2.1.1 
Characterization framework 
In order to assess the cause-effect-chains that link emissions and impacts through the environmental fate, 
exposure, and effects of a given substance characterization factors are developed (Rosenbaum et al. 2008). 
The development of these factors depend on the methodology applied and impact category to which it is 
related. In this study the USEtox methodology, which covers the three impact categories freshwater 
ecotoxicity, human toxicity non-cancer and human toxicity cancer, was adopted for developing 
characterization factors for nanoparticles.  
In general the USEtox model is set up in a matrix framework which includes matrices that combines the 
fate, exposure and effects for a given substance (Henderson et al. 2011). The relation between these stages 
can be seen in formula (1) where FF, XF and EF represents the fate factor, exposure factor and effect factor 
respectively.

2.1.2 
Fate factors 
The fate factor (FF) represents the residence time in a given compartment, often in the unit of days, and it 
determines the link between the concentrations in a given compartment to the quantity released by 
applying multimedia mass balance modelling (Mackay 2001; Rosenbaum et al. 2008). The USEtox fate 
modelling for conventional substances accounts for the following processes:  
 
Advection out of the system, which could for example be advection to the sea for water 
 
Volatilization to air 
 
Degradation, which covers e.g. hydrolysis, photolysis and biodegradation 
 
Sedimentation after sorption to suspended particles 
These type of processes is modelled by using partitioning coefficients for the given substance, however due 
to the colloidal behavior most partitioning coefficients are not applicable for nanoparticles (Hankin et al. 
2011). Furthermore some fundamental differences in the fate was identified by Quik et al. (2011). Firstly 
volatilization is found to be negligible for nanoparticles. Secondly processes such as sedimentation can 
occur without the presence of organic carbon as the nanoparticles aggregate or agglomerate until big 
enough to cause sedimentation. Finally the nanoparticles is found to be entirely suspended immediately

Based on these major differences in the fate modelling applied in USEtox and the predicted behavior of 
nanoparticles it is evident that the fate modelling needs to be adapted for nanoparticles. In order to do so 
the Simplebox4nano (SB4N) model developed by Meesters et al. (2014) was adopted. This model also uses 
mass balance equations but is more comprehensive as it accounts for nanospecific processes such as 
aggregation, attachment and dissolution (Meesters et al. 2014) 
The most significant transformation process for nano-TiO2 is the aggregation/agglomeration process 
(Nanna B. Hartmann et al. 2014). This process is modelled in SB4N by applying the DLVO (Derjaguin Landau 
Verwey Overbeek) theory which calculates the interactions between particle surfaces in dispersions. In 
short the energy between the particle surfaces is the sum of the attractive van der Waals forces and the 
repulsive electrostatic Coulomb forces (Meesters et al. 2014; Nanna B. Hartmann et al. 2014). The amount 
of aggregated particles depends on the particle properties such as size, surface charge etc. and the 
environmental conditions such as pH and natural organic matter (NOM) etc.. In the SB4N the amount 
aggregated is calculated based on the attachment efficiency which accounts for most of these aspects. A 
detailed overview of the equations applied to calculate the attachment efficiency in SB4N can be seen in 
Appendix A  (p. Error! Bookmark not defined.). It should be noted that all data needed was determined 
empirically either from the literature review conducted as part of the effect factor development or by 
(Kounina et al. 2015).

human toxicity exposure pathways: inhalation and ingestion of water, fish, exposed and unexposed 
produce, were linked to the form and compartment modelled in Simplebox4nano. In Table 2-1 the

2.1.3 
Exposure factors 
To relate the presence of nanoparticles in an environmental compartment to the actual intake by aquatic 
organisms and humans, exposure factors (XF) need to be derived. For human exposure, several pathways of 
uptake are covered in the USEtox model. Firstly, the exposure can be either direct or indirect. Direct 
exposure can be through inhalation of air or ingestion of drinking water. These pathways do not require 
additional substance specific parameters, but rely on USEtox landscape parameters such as urban or rural 
population density. Indirect exposure covers the ingestion of agricultural produce, meat, dairy products and 
fish. The agricultural produce are further divided into “exposed” and “unexposed produce”. Unexposed 
produce covers everything grown under the ground and exposed everything above ground (Rosenbaum et 
al. 2011). In order to determine nano-specific XF, relevant bioaccumulation factors (BAF) have to be 
identified that are specific for the nanoform of a material (Huijbregts et al. 2010). It should be note that 
dermal exposure is not initially part of the USEtox framework and it will thus be described and developed 
individually in section 3.2.

ingestion of drinking water and inhalation of particles. Indirect exposure includes ingestion of food such as meat, 
milk, fish and edible plants and roots. 
 
Exposure of fish: bioaccumulation factors, BAFfish 
For the indirect intake by humans XF, BAFfish is a necessary component. In general, the BAF for fish is 
determined as the ratio of the concentration in the organism, in this case fish, over the concentration in the 
surrounding water. What is important in this definition is the fact that the concentration in the organism 
has to include all exposure routes including food intake (Sijm et al. 2007).  
This value can be determined in two different ways in USEtox. The more accurate and preferred approach is 
by using an experimentally determined BAFfish value. If no experimental data is available, the

In order to derive the EF a substantial set of data was collected for ecotoxicity testing on TiO2 by conducting 
a literature review. The studies were mainly identified through other reviews and papers including Holden 
et al. (2014), Salieri et al. (2015),  Miseljic (2014), Kahru and Dubourguier (2010), and Lützhøft, et al. (2015) 
and by a general search on Web of Science using words such as ”ecotox”+ ”nano TiO2”. Overall 85 
publications were collected covering 22 different species and 7 trophic levels. An overview of the data 
collected can be seen in Appendix A. To ensure the reliability of the EF, a set of specific demands for the 
data has been set up by Rosenbaum et al. (2008) and Henderson et al. (2011). 
Firstly, when deriving the HC50, chronic data are preferable, mainly due to the fact that LCA is 
representative of constant exposure to low concentrations rather than peak exposure such as in the case of 
accidental releases. In order to account for the lack of chronic data, an extrapolation factor is used to 
extrapolate acute data. In general, the extrapolation factor depends on the toxic mode of action and the 
substance. As per LCA standards, a non-conservative, best estimate for the extrapolation factor has been 
calculated by Payet (2004). This factor is based on a ratio of 2 between acute and chronic HC50EC50 values 
(meaning that chronic HC50EC50 values are half as large as acute ones).  
Secondly, data from three trophic levels are to be included:  
 
Primary producers (i.e. algae) 
 
Primary consumers (i.e. crustaceans, invertebrates) 
 
Secondary consumers (i.e. fish) 
Due to a low sensibility compared to other trophic levels and to some extent due to lack of data, it is 
recommended by Larsen & Hauschild (2007b) and Payet (2004) that the reducers, often represented as 
bacteria or fungi, are excluded from the effect factor calculation. Therefore the influence of this trophic 
level is examined when deriving the EF.   
Thirdly, only tests that followed a standardized method, such as ISO, OECD or ATSM, were included. If no 
test methodology was mentioned specifically, it was assumed that a standardized test method was not 
followed. If severe alterations, such as conducting the experiment in darkness, were applied to the 
standardized methods, these values were also excluded. Only studies, where effect concentrations were 
given as endpoints were included. Studies not clearly stating an effect concentration for 50% of the species 
were not included in the data set.

Nano-TiO2 has the property that it exists in different crystalline forms; mainly the rutile and anatase phases.

anatase phase, and these were used to calculate the EF for the anatase phase, while the remainder were 
used to calculate the EF for the rutile phase. 
Human toxicity 
The human effect factor relates the amount of a substance taken in by the population via inhalation or 
ingestion to the probability of adverse effects of the substance in humans (Rosenbaum et al. 2008). These 
adverse effects accounted for in the USEtox model are distinguished between carcinogenic or non-
carcinogenic effects. In general, four different effect factors can be calculated for human toxicity, including 
either carcinogenic or non-carcinogenic effects for either inhalation or ingestion routes. What differentiates 
them is the effect doses used to calculate the final effect factor (Rosenbaum, et al. 2008). The unit for the 
effect factor is cases/kgintake and is calculated based on the assumption of linearity in a concentration-
response graph up to the point where the lifetime disease probability is 0.5.

2.3 LCA OF SUNSCREEN 
In this section the methodology applied to conduct and LCA on a nano-TiO2 embedded sunscreen is 
presented. The methodology applied aimed at following the good practice guidelines for LCA presented in 
the ILCD handbook (European Comission 2010).  
2.3.1 
Goal and Scope 
The primary function of a sunscreen is to provide protection from the Sun’s ultraviolet radiation. This is 
done by adding an active ingredient like nano-TiO2 which absorbs or reflects the suns radiation and thus 
avoids burning of the skin. In this LCA, the environmental impacts of the nanoparticles contribution to the 
total environmental burden of the sunscreen is evaluated and compared to the burden of the other 
chemicals included in the sunscreen.   
In order to ensure a common basis for comparison between product systems the function of the product 
assessed in the LCA is quantified in a functional unit. The functional unit applied in this study is based on 
the recommendations for sunscreen use and the behavior pattern of the users (See Appendix C for more 
details) and is thus determined as follows: Provide a sun protection factor (SPF) of 20 for an adult with 
average body surface area (equal to 20000 cm2) by applying 1/4th of the recommended amount of 
2mg/cm2 /40 ml per use once a day for 20 days.  
Based on the functional unit just defined the reference flow is determined as the amount of product 
needed in order to fulfill the functional unit (European Comission 2010). In this study the reference flow 
needed is 1 bottle of sunscreen containing 200 ml of sunscreen.   
The product system including the main flows can be seen in Figure 2-3. The system boundaries cover from 
cradle-to-grave, which means the whole life cycle is included. Transportation and infrastructure is not 
included in Figure 2-3 but these processes where also included in the assessment. For some of the processes 
emissions were neglected because it was not possible to evaluate the impacts of the emissions (e.g. nano-
TiO2 emission to soil) in other cases this was done for simplicity reasons. A detailed description of the 
product system including all assumptions can be seen in Appendix C. The geographical scope is also shown

2.4 UNCERTAINTY ANALYSES AND SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS  
In order to strengthen the validity of the results a sensitivity analysis has also been conducted on some of 
the major assumptions and uncertainties in the life cycle. This is done in order to test how sensitive these 
parameters are to change. In order to perform this analysis a set of scenarios was set up (see Table 2-4).   
As the chemical composition and especially the amount of nano-TiO2 embedded in the sunscreen may vary, 
depending on the brand the sensitivity of the concentration of nano-TiO2 is tested (Scenario 1-2). The 
standard concentration applied in this study is 15%, however the maximum allowed concentration of nano-
TiO2 in cosmetic products is 25% (European Commission 2007; Davis et al. 2010). Thus a maximum 
concentration of 25% is tested in scenario 2. In a study conducted by Botta et al. (2011) five different 
commercial sunscreens where tested for the concentrations of nano-TiO2. The average in these studies 
showed a concentration of 4.55% nano-TiO2 which is thus tested in scenario 1.   
Secondly the fate of the sunscreen after application is set up in different scenarios. In scenario 4-6 the 
absorption rate of the sunscreen to the skin is tested. The ability for a chemical to penetrate is highly 
uncertain and thus is hard to model. Therefore different orders of magnitude of absorption is tested 
including 0.1%, 1% and 50%. As a second part of the fate of the sunscreen a worst case scenario (scenario 
3) is set up where no WWT occurs (this could be the case if the sunscreen was washed off during swimming 
in either the sea or lakes).

3 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
3.1 CHARACTERIZATION FACTORS FOR NANO-TIO2  
 
3.1.1 
Fate 
Hartmann et al. (2014) have identified five transformation processes which are deemed to have high or 
medium importance in fate modelling of nano-TiO2. These processes include photochemical 
transformation, aggregation/agglomeration, sedimentation, NOM adsorption and sorption to other 
surfaces. The photochemical transformation is the only process that is not in some way covered by the 
SB4N model. This process is assumed to have medium importance because the photoactivation has been 
found to alter the binding to dissolved organic matter. The rest of the processes is mostly covered in the 
simplebox4nano by either the aggregation/agglomeration or deposition processes. Thus the SB4N seems 
like a good tool to model the fate of the nano-TiO2.

based on an Anatase/Rutile nano-TiO2 with an average size of 21nm and a density of 4.23E+3 kg/m3 
(Appendix A). The aggregation between nanoparticles is determined by the DLVO theory preciously 
described in section 2.1.2.

When modelling the aggregation of nanoparticles and natural colloids experimentally derived values for the 
attachment efficiencies is preferable compared to theoretically derived values based on the DLVO theory 
(Meesters et al. 2014). This is due to the complexity of the conditions under which the aggregation occurs. 
Especially the repulsive forces between the two types of particles is very situation specific in relation to the 
morphology, surface structure etc.(Petosa et al. 2010; Arvidsson et al. 2011). It was however not possible to 
identify experimentally derived attachment efficiencies, and DLVO theory thus had to be applied in order to 
determine the attachment efficiencies between nano-TiO2 and natural colloids and suspended solids. This is 
deemed acceptable as long as the uncertainty is accounted for (Meesters, et al. 2014). Despite low 
attachment efficiencies the aggregation of nano-TiO2 and natural colloids have been shown to occur rapidly 
(Dale, Lowry, and Casman 2015; Therezien, Thill, and Wiesner 2014). Based on this, the attachment 
efficiencies based on DLVO theory is used as a guideline indicating the order of magnitude but necessarily a 
specific value.  
3.1.2 
Exposure 
Bioaccumulation in fish, BAFfish 
Several studies have measured and demonstrated the uptake of nano-TiO2 in fish, including the uptake in 
gills, brain, gut, liver, skin and other organs (Johnston et al. 2010; Pavagadhi, Sathishkumar, and 
Balasubramanian 2014; Federici, Shaw, and Handy 2007; Ramsden et al. 2009; Al-Jubory and Handy 2012). 
However, only a few of them have derived bioaccumulation factors based on the measured concentrations. 
The experimentally derived factors that were reported can be seen in Appendix A. The lack of reported 
values can be due to a number of issues that arise when dealing with bioaccumulation of nanoparticles. 
Firstly, the assumptions behind the calculation of BAF assume that the test substance reaches steady state. 
However, when dealing with nanoparticles and colloidal chemistry, the system is dynamic and thus the 
methods for determining BAF may not be applicable at all (Handy et al. 2012).  When the system is 
dynamic, the concentration in the water changes. This is witnessed e.g. in a study by Zhu et al. (2010) 
where a concentration of 1 mg/L was prepared but the concentration was measured to be 0.55 mg/L 
shortly after at the beginning of the experiment.  
Another issue is the fact that many studies do not account for the whole range of uptake routes needed to 
calculate the BAF. Often the uptake from dietary exposure in the aquatic environment is neglected because 
it is relatively small compared to uptake of material present from the aqueous environment, thus resulting 
in the calculation of bioconcentration factors (BCF) instead. This factor could be used as a representative 
for the BAF, but may result in a slightly lower uptake in the fish. A study by Zhu, Chang, and Chen (2010) 
showed that the body burden is actually higher for D. rerio when exposed to nano-TiO2 contaminated D. 
magna compared to other chemicals. This indicates that the dietary exposure could play a significant role in 
the uptake of the nanoparticles. This was also showed to be true for nano-ZnO (Skjolding, Winther-nielsen, 
and Baun 2014) and for nano-TiO2 in O. mykiss (Fouqueray et al. 2013). 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
29 
 
With these issues in mind, the identified BCF values were evaluated as a proxy for BAF. As the species 
tested in López-Serrano et al. (2014) consisted of embryos and the study by Fouqueray et al. (2013) only 
accounted for dietary exposure, these values were disregarded. The geometric mean was determined from 
the remaining values, which led to a value of 21.4 (Table 3-2). However this value does not account for the 
dietary exposure and is thus a BCF value. It could be argued that the factor derived in the study by Yeo & 
Nam (2013) is the closest estimation to a BAF value. That study was set up as a microcosm including several 
trophic levels resulting in the fish being exposed both through water and diet. Table 3-2 therefore reports 
two values of BCF/BAF for fish, where the value given by Yeo and Nam (2013) is considered more consistent 
than the derived mean, and is thus advocated as default BAF value. The derived mean value is used as a 
sensitivity analysis when interpreting derived characterization factors in section 3.1.4. To obtain a more 
accurate BAF value, studies similar to that of Yeo & Nam (Yeo and Nam 2013a) need to be conducted with a 
full evaluation of the uptake of nanoparticles through all pathways.

Based on the established BAF values, nano-TiO2 would not be considered bioaccumulative according to the 
REACH regulations (European Comission 2006). In a worst case scenario the value is only 181.38 and 
according to REACH regulations a BCF is considered bioaccumulative when it exceeds 2000. This is also in

are not based on nanoparticles.  
Unexposed produce pathway 
To determine the BAFunexposed, the Root concentration factor (RCF) was determined. A review of current 
studies involving accumulation and uptake in plants was conducted to determine the concentration of 
nano-TiO2 in plant roots. The review was mostly based on Ma & Gao (2015) and Zhang et al. (2015). Several 
studies reported uptake of nano-TiO2 (Rico et al. 2011;Servin et al. 2013; Servin et al. 2012). However only a 
few reported a concentration (Larue et al. 2012a,b; Jacob et al. 2013; Vittori Antisari et al. 2014). The 
retrieved studies that did report concentrations can be seen in Appendix A. Based on the concentrations 
reported, an RCF was determined, thus leading to the determination of the BAFunexposed value.  
However, a number of issues are associated with this calculation.  
First, the concentrations in the plants were reported in dry weight. If the dry weight is used for calculating 
the BAFunexposed, the nano-TiO2 concentrations may seem bigger resulting in a too high BCF. In order to 
derive the wet weight concentration, the moisture content of the plant is needed. However this parameter 
was not reported in any of the studies. In order to determine the wet weight, a standardized value of 0.89 L 
water/kg plant dry weight was adopted (Trapp & Matthies 1995).  
The second issue relates to the soil water concentration. As previously mentioned, the concentration of 
nanoparticles in aqueous media is highly unpredictable (Handy et al. 2012). The concentrations applied in 
the RCF calculation were the initial concentrations prepared in the utilized studies. These are however 
unlikely to be the actual concentrations but in the absence of alternative values, these were retained as 
such.  
==PAGE:30==
 
 
30 
 
With these uncertainties in mind, several BAFunexposed values were determined based on different plants and 
concentrations. A geometric mean of the derived values was calculated resulting in a final BAFunexposed.of 2.9. 
As this value is based on falsely high soil water concentration and a standardized moisture content, this 
result is highly uncertain. Nonetheless this value is very low in regards to bioaccumulation factors, 
indicating that the bioaccumulation in roots and hence in the unexposed produce is very small.  
As for most of the factors used to describe chemicals incorporated in the USEtox model, the methodology 
to derive RCF and BAF is based on organic chemicals, which could result in biased results. For example the 
size and the specific surface area of the nanoparticles may play an important role in plant uptake that is not 
detected by these tests. In a study done by  Larue, et al. (2012), the size was showed to play an important 
role for the uptake. They showed that nano-TiO2 < 140 nm accumulated in the plant, but also that nano-
TiO2 > 140 nm did not.  
Exposed produce pathway 
As indicated in Section 2.1.3, no exposure factor could directly be determined for the exposed produce 
pathway. So far, nano-TiO2 particles have not been shown to be bioaccumulative in either fish or in 
unexposed produce. This suggests that nano-TiO2 is likely not very accumulative in exposed produce either. 
This is further supported by the study conducted by Larue et al. (Larue, et al. 2012), who investigated the 
amount of nano-TiO2 that reached the stele (the inner part of the root) and thus had a chance to 
disseminate through the rest of the plant. A reported value of 5.9 mg Ti/kg dry wt was reported, thus 
demonstrating a concentration 12 times smaller than what was taken into the root and which resulted in a 
low BAFunexposed of 2.5. The same study also showed that nano-TiO2 < 36 nm would accumulate in the stele. 
This size barrier could be the reason for the limited uptake and translocation as nano-TiO2 is known to 
agglomerate, thus creating bigger particle clusters. To further underline the limited chances of 
bioaccumulation of nano-TiO2 in exposed produce, a study conducted on tomatoes showed no 
translocation from the root to the stem or the leaves. It should however be noted that translocation for Ag 
and Co has been observed (Antisari et al. 2014). This indicates that the predicted behavior of the 
nanoparticles is very particle specific. Thus, the predicted small accumulation in exposed produce may only 
be applicable to some nanoparticles. 
If no translocation of particles is occurring it can be argued that the BAFexposed in relation to the soil 
compartment is equal to the concentration in the roots of the plants and thus is equal to the BAFunexposed. 
Until exact values for the BAFexposed can be empirically determined for nanoparticles, approximating the 
BAFexposed with the value of BAFunexposed is considered to provide the best available estimate given the 
current state of knowledge. This results in BAFunexposed of 2.9.  
3.1.3 
Effect factors  
Freshwater ecotoxicity 
Different sets of EF were determined to reflect the influence of different modelling assumptions and the 
variability of TiO2 nanoparticles on the effect factors. These sets are presented in Table 3-3, and their 
settings and rationale are addressed below.  
For each set of EF, factors at the species level and at the trophic levels were first calculated. The difference 
between these two levels is the aggregation of the HC50. For the specie level, this value is based on the 
geometric means of the EC50 values for the different species e.g. D. pulex, C. dubia, D. magna etc. For the 
trophic level, the HC50 is based on the geometric means of the different trophic levels e.g. algae, fish, 
crustaceans etc. Because some species, for regulatory purposes, are highly prioritized for toxicity testing 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
31 
 
compared to others, there may be an unequal representation of the different species. This can for example 
be seen in the data set for the USEtox-compliant EFs. There are 23 relevant data points for crustaceans 
based on 3 different species, but only 2 data points for fish based on 2 different species. It has been shown 
by Aldenberg et al. (2002) that biased species selection, as can be seen here, will lead to biased, estimated 
parameters. Therefore, we selected the EF calculated at the trophic level. This ensures that species that has 
been given a high priority due to regulatory purposes do not influence the EF more than other ecologically 
relevant species (Larsen and Hauschild 2007). 
A first EF was calculated based on all the data identified. The only restrictions were that the studies had to 
report an endpoint that was applicable, meaning an effect concentration for 50% had to be reported. Based 
on these criteria the “All data” EF was derived resulting in factors of 22.8 PAF m3/kg and 12.8 PAF m3/kg for 
the species and trophic level respectively. This is approximately a factor 2 lower than the USEtox compliant 
factor at the trophic level (see next paragraph). One of the criteria set up by Larsen & Hauschild (2007) and 
Payet (2004) was the exclusion of the reducers as these are often less sensitive than other species. As both 
bacteria and fungi were included in the “All data” EF calculation, it could be argued that the decrease of 
this EF is mainly due to the inclusion of these species. This could indicate that the criteria set up by Larsen 
and Hauschild (2007) and Payet (2004) also applies for nano-TiO2.    
A second EF was evaluated based on the USEtox model criteria described by Rosenbaum et al. (2008) which 
are also in accordance with Larsen & Hauschild (2007b) and Payet (2004) described in section 2.1.4. The 
calculation of the HC50 is designed so that only one data point for each trophic level is needed. In this 
study, all data points that complied with the criteria were included in the EF calculations. This resulted in 
the effect factor referred to as USEtox-compliant, as reported in Table 3-3. This EF was based on the refined 
data set, which included 33 data points covering three trophic levels. The EF derived according to USEtox 
standards was found to be 24.1 PAF m3/kg and 30.6 PAF m3/kg for the trophic and species level 
respectively. This is in line with the EF of 28 PAF m3/kg and 39.1 PAF m3/kg for nano-TiO2 reported by Salieri 
et al. (2015) for the trophic and species level, respectively. Miseljic (2014) also reported an EF of 26.1 PAF 
m3/kg for the species level which is also within an acceptable range of the findings of the current study.  
One of the criteria set up for the USEtox is that the effect concentrations used need to be based on a 
standardized test (Rosenbaum et al. 2008). The studies behind these tests are however mostly based on 
narcotic and polar narcotic chemicals and the methodologies are developed to ensure that the test setup 
has minimum influence on the speciation of the tested chemical (Lützhøft et al. 2015). It can however be 
argued that these standardized tests may not be applicable in the same sense for nanoparticles as they are 
known to behave differently than soluble chemicals. Instead of dissolving, nanoparticles can undergo 
various mechanisms, such as forming suspensions or agglomerating (Quik et al. 2011). These 
transformations may change the exposure in both qualitative and quantitative terms, thus making the 
methodologies unsuitable for testing nanoparticle ecotoxicity (Lützhøft et al. 2015). Therefore the 
standardized test criteria were discarded when trying to derive a nanospecific EF.  
A third EF was evaluated based on the report by Lützhøft et al. (2015). In this work, the adequacy of 
ecotoxicological studies to test nanoparticle ecotoxicity was determined by setting up a two-dimensional 
scoring system. This system was based on the reliability and the relevance of the studies. The reliability 
score is based on the fulfilment of the extended data demands for nanoparticles including the physical and 
chemical properties, such as the particle size, surface area, crystal structure etc. and the characterization 
before and during testing. Overall the studies are evaluated with regard to the test setup, the compound, 
the tested organism, the exposure conditions, the statistical design and the observed biological response. 
The score for the relevance is based on the relevance of the study compared to the objectives for the use of 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
32 
 
the data from the study i.e. Risk assessment or LCIA (Lützhøft et al. 2015). Based on the developed scoring 
system, Lützhøft et al. (2015) identified six studies as “adequate for regulatory use” and another six as 
“may be adequate for regulatory use”. The evaluation of the studies was directed to risk assessment (RA) 
applications. Thus, they were evaluated on the reliability and relevance of the lethal, effect and no effect 
concentrations (LC50, EC50, NOEC). As these are also LCIA relevant endpoints, these studies were deemed 
relevant for inclusion into a specific set of calculated EFs. Therefore, a new set of EF was calculated solely 
based on the 9 endpoints identified in the 12 identified studies. The EF based on these studies can be seen 
in Table 3-3 (under the name “adequate quality studies from Lützhøft, et al. 2015”). The determined values 
are 18.6 m3/kg at species level and 9.4 m3/kg at trophic level. At the trophic level, this is almost a factor of 3 
smaller than the USEtox- compliant EF.  
One outlier was observed in the data sets: the EC50 value of 7215.2 mg/L for the protozoa P. 
multimicronucleatum (K. Li et al. 2012). If this value is excluded from the EF calculation, the finals EF comes 
to 39.6 m3/kg and 28.8 m3/kg, which is quite similar to that of the USEtox-compliant EF at the trophic level. 
This confirms that some species may be more or less sensitive to the particles and suggests that the 
recommendations to exclude less sensitive species such as bacteria and protozoa may be justified (Larsen 
and Hauschild 2007b). It could however also be another indication that the test methodologies currently 
applied are not suitable for testing nanoparticles.  
TiO2 can be present in three different forms: anatase, rutile and brookite, as well as mixtures thereof (Davis 
et al. 2010). Since most ecotoxicity tests were conducted with 80% ≤ anataseTiO2 as the dominating 
structure the effect factors were derived based on anatase TiO2. Nonetheless, in some products, like 
sunscreen and other cosmetics, the most common form of TiO2 is rutile (Botta et al. 2011). Thus an effect 
factor solemnly based on rutile data was derived. It should be noted that the number of data points for 
rutile TiO2 are very limited and many studies do not report an endpoint usable for EF calculations. Thus only 
2 data points for two different trophic levels were identified. As three trophic levels are needed in order to 
comply with the USEtox guidelines, the EF for rutile nano-TiO2 are marked as interim. The final factor 
derived, seen in Table 3-3 as rutile nano-TiO2, are 21.7 PAF m3/kg. This value is within the same range as the 
USEtox-compliant EF, which is based on anatase nano-TiO2. However as the “rutile nano-TiO2” is marked as 
interim, this value has a high uncertainty and should be applied with caution.

Freshwater ecotoxicity 
The recommended CF for freshwater ecotoxicity for emissions to freshwater compartment is 1.66E+03 
PAF.m3.d/ kgemitted. Salieri et al. (2015) and Miseljic and Olsen (2015) derived corresponding characterization 
factors for nano-TiO2 of 2.8E-01 PAF.m3.d/ kgemitted and 1.48E-01 PAF.m3.d/ kgemitted, respectively. The latter 
factors are several orders of magnitude smaller than the CF developed in this work (see Figure 3-4). The 
large difference is mainly due to the fact that we include the toxic impact of aggregated particles. Salieri et 
al. (2015) and Miseljic and Olsen (2015) accounted the aggregation as a removal process and therefore did 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
37 
 
not include this process in the final calculation of the CF. When calculating the CF and not including the 
aggregates, a factor of 1.82 PAF.m3.d/ kgemitted was obtained, confirming that including the aggregates 
makes an important difference for the final CF. 
Most toxicity studies on nanoparticles report that aggregation/agglomeration occurs immediately in the 
prepared suspensions, which changes the particle size but also the concentration in the suspension. Thus, it 
can be hard to determine the exact exposure concentration for the species (Kühnel and Nickel 2014b; 
Nanna B. Hartmann et al. 2012). Several studies report that the species both take up the pristine but also 
the aggregates when doing toxicity testing (Kühnel et al. 2009; Patra et al. 2011). Therefore, determining 
the exact nanoparticle state contributing to nanoparticles toxicity for a defined specie can be difficult. As 
most of the current toxicity studies are based on suspensions covering both pristine particles and 
aggregates (Nanna B. Hartmann et al. 2012), including both states of the particles when deriving the CFs for 
nanoparticles is recommended. It could be argued that a separate EF should be assigned to the aggregated 
fraction as it is likely that these are less toxic than the pristine particles. Several studies have indeed shown 
that the bulk form is less toxic than pristine particles indicating size dependency in relation to toxicity. This 
distinction is thus not addressed in this current calculation due to lack of toxicity tests monitoring 
nanoparticle state across time. As the same EF is assigned to both the pristine particles and the aggregates, 
it is assumed that the EF is representative of the toxic effect of both nanoparticle forms.  
Eckelman et al. (2012) also derived CFs for freshwater ecotoxicity for CNTs (Carbon Nanotubes). The only 
removal process considered in the latter study was the advection into the ocean, which resulted in a 
conservative CF of 2.9E+04 PAF.m3.d/ kgemitted, thus in similar range to the CF derived in our work. The 
higher CF for CNTs could be due to the inclusion of aggregates but could also be attributed to the higher EFs 
derived for CNTs as a result of greater toxicity of CNTs than nano-TiO2.

According to a review by Davis, et al (2010) the amount of nano-TiO2 absorbed through the skin is minimal 
if any at all and the health effects related to this, is, for healthy unflexed skin, expected to be none (Butz et 
al. 2007; Nohynek et al. 2010). In general nano-TiO2is believed to have a relatively low toxicity, however 
some health effects were related to both the inhalation and ingestion of nano-TiO2 (Davis, et al 2010). It 
therefore seems peculiar that the CF for dermal absorption is several orders of magnitude larger than the 
CFs for inhalation and ingestion. Poland, et al (2013) have raised some issues regarding dermal absorption 
testing for nanoparticles which to some extent also covers the dermal testing of other chemicals as well.  
One major issued raised is the absence of clear guidelines for this type of testing which is believed to result 
in a diversity of test models which can make it hard to compare data (Poland, et al. 2013). This is believed 
to be true for all dermal testing not just for nanoparticles. Some of the more nanospecific issues which are 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
41 
 
raised are the controlling of the dispersion concentration, detection of the compounds during and after the 
testing and the state of the skin (Poland, et al 2013). These are all issues that increases the uncertainty of 
the test conducted and may be some of the reasons for the high CFs developed in this study.   
Another CF for nano-TiO2 was developed based on the assumption that only 0.01% of the nano-TiO2 makes 
it through all the three layers of the skin and that this is the fraction of the exposure dose which causes an 
effect. This factor is only a factor of three larger than the CF for ingestion which seems more sensible. 
However it is still higher which does not correspond to the reported effects of nano-TiO2 testing. This CF is 
also based on intravenous testing which may not necessarily be linked to dermal absorption. It should also 
be noted that the 0.01% reported by (Gulson et al. 2012) was based on the nano-ZnO ion concentration in 
the blood. Since nano-TiO2 does release ions the concentration in the blood may be zero and thus the effect 
will be none.  
Overall the dermal CFs derived are assessed to be too high and should therefore be applied with caution. 
The exact reason for this is hard to determine but there are several aspects that could contribute: 
 
In general the influence of dermal exposure is very complex. Basically it is hard to determine 
whether the chemicals or nanoparticles penetrate the skin (unless they are engineered to) and if 
they do how to measure and quantify the amount. Therefore it is also difficult to determine the 
effect concentrations related to the dermal exposure.   
 
Some of the data used to derive the dermal CFs were based on subacute data. Despite using the 
time correction factor this could still cause some uncertainty regarding the exposure doses applied.  
 
Some of the studies did not report an actual endpoint but just concluded that the effect dose was 
higher than the highest testing concentration.  
A final point that should be considered is that the USEtox framework which was applied for developing the 
CFs has an uncertainty of 2-3 orders of magnitude for human health (Rosenbaum, et al. 2008). This could 
explain some of the inconsistencies between the developed CF and the reported effects of dermal 
exposure.   Nonetheless the LCA on sunscreen.

4 CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The aim of this study was (1) to develop characterization factors for TiO2 nanoparticles for the three toxic 
impact categories included in USEtox. (2) to adapt the USEtox framework so that it also accounts for dermal 
exposure of both nanoparticles and organic chemicals. (3) to implement the methodologies developed in a 
case study on sunscreen containing nano-TiO2 as a UV absorber and based on this assess the environmental 
burden of the nanoparticles.   
In order to meet these aims, a new methodology to conduct LCA on nanoparticles and nano-embeded 
products was developed. This was done by adapting the USEtox framework so that it takes account for the 
specific fate, exposure and effects of nanoparticles. A substantial literature review was conducted to 
develop guidelines for how to select the data on exposure and effect modelling and the Simplebox4nano 
model was incorporated to account for the different behavior of nanoparticles in fate modelling. Based on 
this, Characterization factors for nano-TIO2 were developed for eco- and human toxicity for the freshwater 
and air compartment. The characterization factors for freshwater ecotoxicity was determined to be 
6.48E+02 PAF.m3.d/kgemitted and 1.66E+03 PAF.m3.d/kgemitted for air and freshwater respectively. The 
characterization factors for Human toxicity cancer was determined to be 4.56E-06 cases /kgemitted for 
emission into air. For human toxicity non-cancer the CF is, 5.88E-05 cases /kgemitted and 6.05E-06 cases 
/kgemitted for air and water respectively.  
Furthermore, the USEtox framework was adapted to account for dermal exposure as well by developing 
characterization factors for this exposure route. However, as these values came out several orders of 
magnitude larger relative to the human toxicity – non-cancer CFs and hence they were evaluated to be very 
high. Thus it is recommended to apply these factors with caution.   
Finally, the methodology developed was tested i on sunscreen containing nano-TiO2 as a case study. Here 
the impact of nano-TiO2 was compared to the other ingredients in the sunscreen. Based on this 
assessment, it is clear that the nano-TiO2 contributes significantly to the environmental impacts caused by 
sunscreen and thus highlights the importance of including nanospecific considerations when conducting 
LCA.

APPENDIX C  
Elaborated Goal and scope 
Objectives and intended application 
The overall objective of this study is to evaluate the environmental impacts of a sunscreen lotion 
embedded with nano-TiO2, as a UV absorbers.  More specifically the environmental relevance of the 
nanoparticles contribution to the total environmental burden of the sunscreen is evaluated and compared 
to the burden of the other chemicals included in the sunscreen.  
This study includes the evaluation of all chemicals in the sunscreen and not just the nanoparticles. Thus 
enables the evaluation of whether the burden of the nanoparticles is significant.     
System function, functional unit and reference flow 
The primary function of a sunscreen is to provide protection from the Suns ultraviolet radiation. The 
sunscreen either absorbs or reflects the suns radiation in order to avoid burning of the skin. Most 
sunscreens is labelled with a sun protection factor (SPF) which is an indicator of the sunscreens ability to 
prevent damaging/bruning of the skin caused by UVB. A SPF of 20 delays the redness, often related to 
burning, 20 times. So instead of reddening after 10 min it will occur after 200 min. However this is only a 
measure of UVB, which is the reason why reapplication of sunscreen every 2 hours is recommended when 
exposed for longer time periods (Kræftens bekæmpelse, n.d.) 
According to Danish EPA (MIljøstyrelsen), The Danish Cancer society (Kræftens Bekæmpelse) and The 
Danish health and medicines authority the recommended amount of sunscreen to be applied is  2mg/cm2 
or 40 ml for an average grown person (the average body surface of a person is approximately 20000 cm2). 
However many studies have shown that most people use less than the recommended amount. A study 
conducted on Danish people sunscreen knowledge and habits show that only 50% knew how much 
sunscreen they actually needed to apply in order to obtain the desired sun protection and further studies 
shows that people approximately apply ¼ of the amount needed (Behrens and Darsø 2013; Davis et al. 
Functional unit: 
Provide a sun protection factor (SPF) of 20 for an adult with average body surface area (equal 
to 20000 cm2) by applying 1/4th of the recommended amount of 2mg/cm2 /40 ml per use  once 
a day for 20 days. 
Reference flow: 
As an average sunscreen bottle contains 200 ml of sunscreen the reference flow for this 
functional unit will be 1 bottle of sunscreen containing 200 ml of sunscreen.

Based on the recommendations for sunscreen use and the behavior patterns of people regarding sunscreen 
use the functional unit and the reference flow is determined.

Distribution and storage 
As no specific location of production and storage is known a European average distribution scenario is 
applied (Quantis 2015). This includes 1500 km transportation by truck from manufacturing to storage 
location and 700 km transportation by truck from the storage to the retailer (Quantis 2015). For the 
transportation of the purchased product it is assumed that 80% is transported by a passenger car, where 
5% is allocated to the actual product. The remaining 20% is assumed be transported by foot or public 
transport. Here the allocation of the product would be so small it is neglected (Quantis 2015).  
The electricity consumption at the distribution center and the retailer is also unknown and is thus based on 
best estimated averages. For the distribution center an electricity consumption of 6 kWh/m3 year is 
assumed (Humbert, 2009). The size of the sunscreen is determined to have a volume of 17cm x 7cm x 3cm 
based on measurements of random sunscreen products and it is assumed to be stored for 1 month. For the 
retailer the electricity consumption is assumed to be 700 kWh/m2 year (Schönberger, Martos, and Styles 
2013) and the bottle occupies 7cm x 3cm for 1 month. It should be noted that the retailer is assumed to be 
in Denmark thus Danish electricity mix is applied. Finally the infrastructure for the distribution center is 
modelled assuming a lifetime of 80 years of the building and that a sunscreen bottle occupies 10 times the

Use stage 
The use stage is not modelled in SimaPro as the only process that occurs here is the application of the 
sunscreen. This process leads to absorption of the chemicals into the skin. However as this is difficult to 
model in SimaPro the dermal absorption is added separately afterwards. It is assumed that 14% of the 
sunscreen is absorbed to the skin (R. Jiang et al. 2001). It is likely that most of the chemicals will be washed 
or worn off. However for simplicity purposes it is assumed that these 14% accumulates in the skin and 
contributed to the dermal toxicity. See Figure c-2 for fate of the sunscreen.     
End-of-life sunscreen 
Once the sunscreen is applied it is assumed to either absorb to the skin, or it is washed off during 
showering. Realistically for Denmark some may be washed off during swimming in the sea and thus emitted 
to the marine environment however this possibility is neglected. Thus it is assumed that 86% of the 
sunscreen ingredients is washed off during a shower. The amount of water needed for the shower is based 
on the FU. It is assumed that there is 20 uses per bottle of sunscreen and thus 20 showers is needed. It is 
assumed that 50 L of water is needed pr. Shower and based on this 15 L is allocated the removal of 
sunscreen, thus resulting in 0.3 m3 of water being used pr. FU.  In Figure c-2 the fate of the sunscreen 
ingredients is presented from the application (use) stage to the end-of-life.

Data quality analysis 
Time related representativeness 
As nanotechnology is rapidly evolving technology new methods to develop the particles on especially 
commercial scale is likely to occur. The rest of the processes are quite generic and well established and is 
not likely to change significantly within recent time.  
Could be a few issues as the recipe is from 2007 however when compared to labelling on sunscreen bought 
in stores seems quite similar.  
In general the data is assumed to be of good quality if it is from 2010 or newer. See Table c-8 for evaluation 
of data quality.  
Geographical representativeness 
It is assumed that the sunscreen is purchased and used in Denmark thus the disposal of the plastic 
container and the emission of the chemicals takes place in Denmark. It is however assumed that the 
ingredient production and the manufacturing takes place somewhere in Europe. As no specific locality is 
identified generic data for Europe is used for the sunscreen and nanoparticle production. 
In general the data is assumed to be of good quality if the geographical location is Europe for the 
production, manufacturing and distribution stage and Denmark for Use and EoL stages. See Table c-8 for 
evaluation of data quality.   
 Technological representativeness 
The efficiency of the incineration process is very high in Denmark thus specific data for the newest 
technology is used for this process. For most other processes generic technology used in Europe is applied. 
In regards to the production of the nanoparticles and aggregated process is used based on commercial 
scale data where 2 different methodologies is applied.
