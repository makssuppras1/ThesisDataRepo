Master Thesis
Abstract
Energy flexibility is the ability of an energy system to control its energy demand and
generation based on local climatic conditions, user needs and network requirements. For
this reason, the present Master Thesis consists on the evaluation of the energy flexibility of
the energy systems of buildings through the implementation of a mathematical model using
the R software. With this model, the aim is to control and optimize their energy demand
by applying flexibility functions whose performance depends on temperature setpoints and
CO2 emissions as penalty signals. In this way, buildings are able to store energy during
off-peak periods in order to use it during peak periods, helping to decrease fossil fuel-based
power generation by reducing the peak demands on the grid. This model was applied to two
cases. First, the flexibility of a heating floor system of an apartment located in Copenhagen,
Denmark is studied. Later, the flexibility of a combination of energy systems containing: a
heat pump for space heating and DHW, a rooftop PV installation, an EV and a stationary
battery of a building located in Dübendorf, Switzerland are studied to control their energy
consumption and evaluate the potential energy savings obtained. Lastly, the results ob-
tained from the simulated cases were used to develop observations and proposals about the
mathematical model in order to improve the precision of the simulations.
Keywords: energy flexibility, flexibility functions, penalty signals, energy demand,
optimization, buildings, energy systems

Introduction
1.1
Background
The EU has set the target to achieve at least a 40% reduction in greenhouse gas (GHG)
emissions compared to 1990 levels by 2030. To do so, an increase in the share of renewable
energy sources (RESs) and an improvement in energy efficiency of 32% are needed to be im-
plemented. However, in September 2020, the European Commission proposed reviewing the
targets and increased the levels of energy efficiency and renewable energy share to achieve
a reduction in GHG of 55% by 2030. This ambitious goal tries to move the EU even closer
towards achieving a climate neutral economy by 2050 [8].
One of the challenges when increasing the share of RESs is the matching process of the
energy generation and energy demand. Nevertheless the control of the RESs generation is
difficult mainly because it depends on the weather conditions and so, although it can be
turned down, it cannot be turned up. Therefore, the idea of controlling the demand side
instead seems to be a solution [9].
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), the building sector represents
one third of the global energy consumption for both heat and electricity [2]. Buildings are
important factors to the contribution of the stability of the grid and thus, their ability to
be flexible towards it is important. In this way, when the demand increases and the energy
prices are high, the energy flexibility (EF) from a building can contribute to reduce them.
What is more, if instead of analyzing them individually, they are aggregated forming building
clusters (BC), an effective strategy for providing low-carbon and decentralized energy supply
can be achieved.
1.2
Annex 82
The Annex 82: Energy Flexible Buildings towards Resilient Low Carbon Energy Systems
is a project developed under the program Energy in Buildings and Communities (EBC) of
the International Energy Agency (IEA). This project started in 2020 and it will be devel-
oped till 2025 as a continuation of the work presented in the previous project of the IEA
EBC: Annex 67: Energy Flexibility Buildings. One of the main focuses is to expand the
building EF methodologies developed during Annex 67 to consider clusters of buildings and
multi-carrier energy systems.
The project is divided in four subtasks that are carried out in parallel [10]. However,
only the first two of them are more related to this report:
• Subtask A: is focused on scaling from single buildings to clusters of buildings with the
aim of developing and testing EF methodologies and control strategies for clusters of
buildings.

• Subtask B: studies about the flexibility achieved from multi-carrier systems which are
the combination of different energies (electricity, district heating/cooling, gas).
• Subtask C: is focused on understanding the motivations and barriers of the different

• Subtask D: investigates and develops business models for that all the stakeholders to

1.3
Problem statement and goals
The aim of this report consists in applying a methodology to characterize the EF of
buildings in order to be able to simulate data from different energy systems.
The main outcomes of this report are the following:
• A review on flexibility functions (FF) concepts.
• The simulation of the EF model to two case studies and an analysis of the results
obtained from it.
• Observations and improvement proposals of the EF model.

Literature Review
2.1
Energy Flexibility: Definition
During the years of 2014 till 2019, the IEA EBC introduced the concept of EF in the
project of Annex 67. They defined it as: the capacity of a building to manage its demand
and generation according to the local climate conditions, user needs and grid requirements.
Energy Flexibility of buildings will thus allow for demand side management/load control and
thereby demand response based on the requirements of the surrounding grids [11].
This definition allows to differentiate two types of buildings:
• Passive buildings: their design helps them to maintain a comfortable ambient by pro-
viding heating and cooling coming from natural sources (e.g. wind or sun) and without
the need of purchasing energy [12]. These are also called Zero Energy Buildings because
the energy balance between consumption and production is zero.
• Active buildings: can adapt their energy demand according to the electricity grid
prices, consuming and storing more energy during periods when prices are lower and
there are more RESs in the network. The stored energy is then used when there is a
high energy demand and therefore an increase in energy prices. In conclusion, they
participate as active consumers and producers, also known as prosumers.
2.2
Flexible energy systems
There are different ways of obtaining EF in a building when the demand increases. They
can be classified into the following 5 sources [1]:
First, the thermal energy storage (TES) of a building is an important source to
consider as they have demonstrated that they can improve the building’s performance [13].
Well designed TES systems can reduce initial and maintenance costs as well as significantly
reduce energy use and demand. In addition, they increase the flexibility of operation, improve
indoor environmental quality and help reducing pollutant emissions. The TES flexibility can
be divided in two types:
• The building mass: refers to the passive elements such as walls, floor, ceilings and
furniture that are able to store thermal energy in their mass. Their stored energy can
be used when the HVAC systems are switched off during the peak demand periods
to maintain the comfort levels of the building. However, the storage capacity is very
variable as it depends on the buildings characteristics and climate conditions. For
example, in [14] the thermal energy dynamics 39 buildings in Denmark were analyzed
in order to study their EF potential. The results showed that higher values of the time
heat transfers between the indoor air and the thermal mass and the outdoor air can
increase the flexibility potential of a building.
Page 3 of 41
==PAGE:13==

• The thermal energy systems: refers to active elements such as domestic hot water
(DHW) storage, buffer tanks or space heating systems like radiators or underfloor heat-
ing. In [15] the energy flexibility provided by two sources of energy storage connected
to a district heating network were studied: the water thermal mass in the network
pipes and the thermal inertia of the buildings connected to the network. The results
showed that the energy flexibility delivered by the network pipes is much lower as the
pipes have a thermal inertia factor one thousand smaller. However, they can stand
higher temperatures than the building mass, so they can contribute and cooperate in
increasing the total EF of a building.
Secondly, flexibility can also be obtained from switching the fuel during the high peak
demand. For example, the boiler can be used instead of the heat pump during high electric-
ity prices.
Batteries are the third source. They can be the connected to a PV system or to an
electrical vehicle. This source of EF is charged during the off-peak hours in order to use them
during the rest of the time. However, storing energy in the form of heat instead of power
seems a better solution for reducing peak loads, as it it less costly and has a better efficiency.
This is why solar thermal plants are gaining more importance as it can be combined with
thermal storage tanks [13].
Figure 1: Flexible energy systems. Source: [1]
The on-site generation can also increase EF. Nowadays a lot of buildings have their
own PV installation, solar thermal system or micro wind turbines. The cogeneration and
trigeneration is another option for decreasing GHG emissions because they archive a decrease
in losses as they use the heat excess to transform it into electricity instead of releasing it
to the air. Lastly, EF can also be achieved from the networks at which the buildings are
connected like the electricity grid and the district heating or gas grid.

2.3
Characterizing EF with FF
The amount of EF that a building can offer is difficult to determine as it is not a con-
stant value. It depends on different factors such as the type of building, systems and control
strategies, climate conditions, time of the day and the acceptance of the stakeholders in-
volved. However, having the ability to offer EF makes systems and buildings smart which
means that under a Penalty Signal (PS) they can change their behavior. Therefore, a smart
building is an energy-flexible building that has penalty-aware controllers [9].
Figure 2: Control structure of a smart building responsive to PSs. Source: [2]
The IEA EBC Annex 67 introduced a methodology to quantify the amount of EF that
a building can offer without compromising the occupant comfort conditions and taking into
account the technical constraints of the building and its HVAC system. This quantification
is obtained when a PS is imposed to the energy systems of the building in order to shift
their demand. As a result, they can minimize their energy consumption, their cost or the
CO2 footprint [2]. This PS can be:
1. A step response e.g. a sudden change in the energy price or in the temperature setpoint
of a heating system. It can be represented as shown in the following figure:

2.4
Energy flexibility in building clusters
The potential savings of using EF in a single building has led to the idea of aggregating
them into clusters in order to increase the benefits. This supposes a change of perspective as
there is not only a focus in having single efficient units, but they are also interconnected and
can manage their energy flows according to their needs. Annex 67 defined that a cluster of
buildings can be formed by aggregating buildings using one of this two types of connection
[1]:
• A physical connection: the buildings are connected to the same grid and can share their
energy. This can be possible with systems like district heating which connects different
buildings to a central sources or PV panels from one building that can generate enough
energy for the rest of them.
• A market connection: the buildings are managed by the same company even though
they are not located in the same area.
Figure 5: Types of connection in a BC. Source: [1]
The cluster development can contribute to the creation of Net Zero Energy Commu-
nities (NZESCs) in which there’s null or positive difference between the annual consumed

energy and on-site generated energy. This is mainly because the community level ensures
a larger accommodation of RESs and an easier flattering of load profiles due to the highly

The cluster scale is large enough to allow the systematic aggregation of energy infor-
mation from different types of sources: building infrastructure, heat, electricity, DHW and
networks and its transportation [19]. In addition, if we focus on the amount of data and
computation needed, the cluster scale is an intermediate solution between the city scale and
one single building scale, where is possible to analyze the energy infrastructure without com-
promising the computational capacity.
Several European studies have revealed that the smart operation in clusters enables an
improvement of RESs usage too. For example, in [20] two indicators were studied to observe
the behavior of the EF in a cluster. These were used to quantify the flexibility as a reduction
in the demand not covered by RESs and as a decrease in the carbon emissions when a smart
control of the cluster is made. The results showed that it is possible to achieve savings up
to 13% for the annual heating demand and a reduction of 18% in energy-related carbon
emissions.

At the cluster scale there are different influencing factors that affect the EF [19]. Some
of them are the following ones:
• The spatio-temporal scale of the cluster: the optimal spatial dimensions for a cluster
should be between 100 m and 1 km for energy system simulation purposes. However,
for the temporal analysis, two different time scales must be considered. On the one
hand, the time of reaction to the building components/envelopes can be hours or days
while the time of reaction of the energy systems can be seconds or minutes.
• The density of the building block: a high value it’s usually related to an increase in
energy consumption. However, in general terms the density should be between the
range of 0.11 - 0.69 (plan area density), 0.12 - 0.33 (frontal area density) and 1.5 - 2.5
(floor area ratio).
• The RES solution implemented: depending on the RES that the building has, the
available EF can differ. The solar power and solar thermal solutions are the most
usually used solutions for generating energy in buildings. Consequently, the BIPVs
(Building Integrated PV) and BISTs (Building Integrated Solar Thermal) systems are
regarded as one of the most important solutions nowadays [19]. In addition, solar/air
solutions like solar thermal facades, heat recovery envelopes and double skin facade
seem to be more adaptable to buildings connected to heat pumps.

2.5
Motivation and barriers for the application of EF
Nowadays the spread of awareness of the use of RESs to replace fossil fuels has increased
significantly. This can be seen in the building sector, as consumers are more and more inter-
ested in achieving their homes to be as energy efficient as possible, not only to fight global
warming but to improve their economy.
Page 11 of 41
==PAGE:21==
Master Thesis
The EF can be applied to all the stages of a building construction. Therefore, start-
ing from the planning and design phase, the construction, to the maintenance phase, they
must be developed under the energy efficiency requirement. Consequently, the stakehold-
ers affected go from the occupants of the buildings and building owners to the architects,
contractors, project developers and product suppliers. The consumers of it are aggregators,
DSOs (District System Operators) and TSOs (Transmission system operators) [1].
For the success of the EF, there is a need for the collaboration and negotiation of all the
participants to benefit all parts. An overview of the motivation for the different stakeholders
[24] is developed below:
• For the prosumers: the motivation can be impulsed by the governmentâs subsidies
and incentives which help them to adapt their building to the energy legislation and
building regulations. Other like Power Purchase Agreements, Net Metering and Green
certificates are becoming more popular [25] .
• For the DSOs: the EF helps with the reduction of peak loads at specific nodes in the
distribution grid to avoid thermal overload of the system components. At the same
time, they can control the voltage to not overcome the established limits.
• For the TSOs: they can benefit of more frequency control of the grid by increasing or
decreasing load or generation in the area to maintain its stability.
However, all of them are affected by barriers [26]:
• For the prosumers: their privacy can be affected because of the continuous collection
of information of its activity from the DSOs part. This is why their privacy must be
protected when participating in the EF market to not lose their trust and willing of
participation. On the other hand, the use of EF is related to the use of dynamic tariffs,
which can add uncertainty to their bills.
• For the DSOs: they might be affected by an increase in their operations costs. This
is because of the need of processing and collecting all the data from the grid in order
to inform about the grid status to the suppliers. Furthermore, other conflicts between
them may appear when the PSs and demand response from the supplier does not match
the current grid status.
• For the TSOs: they need continuous communication and data transmission with the
DSOs to maintain the grid’s stability.

3.1
Mathematical modelling
Mathematical models are often used in engineering to explain energy systems and study
the effects of their components in order to make predictions about their behaviour. These
models are classified according to how much information is available of the system into 3
types [27]:
• White-box models: are constructed using the knowledge about the physics of the
system. They are based on ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and they can predict
the future parameters exactly. One example of a white box model can be Fourier’s
equation of heat conduction where the results of this type of models often lead to an
analytical solution. But these model have also disadvantages such as ending with a
complex solution and not being able to include the uncertainties into the model. The
common structure of an ODE is:
dA = −kAdt
(13)

(14)
• Black-box models: are not based on any theoretical considerations, they are made
directly from the data and represent the relationship between the inputs and the out-
puts observed. They are used when the system’s behaviour is unknown or when the
mechanisms are too difficult to model efficiently. However, some disadvantages of this
model are that the applicability of the model can be restricted to that experimental
data because they are not taking advantage of previous knowledge in the area.
• Grey-box models: are a more advanced method that replaces the ODEs with stochastic
differential equations (SDEs). They are called grey because they can be seen as a
combination of the theoretical knowledge (white model) and the statistical information
based on observation (black model) of a system.

4.3
Observations and modifications of the EF model
During the simulation of the two case studies presented in Section 3, four main obser-
vations can be made related to the behavior of the EF model, the data used and the results
obtained.
4.3.1
Function g
On the one hand, the function g represented with Equation 25 was found to be effective
using only one type of PSs. This one needs to be a continuous signal in order to build
the regression splines explained before. This is because, the data of the PSs are previously
classified in the quantiles of 20%, 40%, 60% and 80% which are used to decide where the
knots of the splines are located. However, when applying this quantiles classification to
the temperature setpoints of one room, only two quantiles are formed as only two values
of temperature (e.g. 21.5◦C - 23◦C) are being changed during the simulation period. This
generates that the regression spline to not be so accurate as the it would be when other
types of PSs more variable like the CO2 emissions.
Nevertheless, when increasing the number of rooms for evaluating the entire apartment,
the knots of the splines are able to be created and therefore the regression spline is formed.
This is shown with the increase of the R2 for the whole apartment compared to only a room.
In addition, as the objective is to use the EF model for clusters of buildings, there seems to
be no inconvenience with building these splines.

4.3.2
Function f
Secondly, the function f represented with the Equation 23 and 24 is a polynomial
regression equation elaborated from specific set of data of a previous study. The latter was
based on the control of the electricity consumption of water towers and so, the polynomial
equation was relating the SOC and the demand for that specific situation. Therefore, a
possible improvement can be made to this equation if it is adapted to every data set. To do
so, two strategies can be studied as showed in Figure 21:
1. Establish the equation f following the original sequence of the code.
This would
mean adapting the equation for each set of data every time the SOC is updated after
applying the effect of the g function and the PSs. The regression equations proposed
are a smooth regression spline or a simple polynomial regression. The values obtained
from applying these equation should give values between [-1,1] while generating a
monotonous decreasing function. It is important that they follow this characteristics
in order to match the range of values used in the EF model as well as show the inversely
relationship between SOC and demand. These ones can be build using the functions
iSpline or glm of the software R.
2. Establish a previous equation f. This would mean, establishing the regression equation
for that specific set of data using their known baseline and demand to establish their
relationship with the SOC. The same characteristics as in the first strategy are needed
to be followed.
However, the first method was found to not be efficient because updating the f function
every time-step increases exponentially the computational time without improving propor-
tionally the precision of the results. Therefore, the best strategy would be to not follow the
sequence of the code and establish the function f (Xt,α) as the second strategy. The final
structure of f is formed by the product of α parameters and I-Splines as showed in Equation
39. It was seen that is the best way to represent the relation between the SOC and the
demand as a monotonous and decreasing function. Thus, for very time step, if f(1)=-1, the
SOC is full and the demand is reduced. On the other hand, if f(0)=1, the SOC is low and
the demand is increased.

4.3.3
Parameter C
In addition, another observation can be made when using temperature setpoints as PSs.
It was seen that the parameter C which represents the time that it takes to fulfill the SOC
has a irregular behavior. When observing the results for only one room, this parameter
has a value of approximately 1 hour. This value seems a reasonable time for the heating
floor to reach the maximum temperature setpoint of that particular room. Contrarily, the
C parameter needed for the entire apartment, has a value of 20 hours. This is because the
model does not differentiate the needs behind the energy flexible system simulated and thus,
it increases the time on order to achieve a 100% flexibility represented with the parameter
∆. As the boundaries of the parameter C are flexible, they can be changed to be closer to
2 hours for all rooms, however the flexibility potential decreases to 50%.

4.3.4
The prediction method
The EF model is based on a multi-step time series prediction method. The original ob-
jective of using this type of predictions was to obtain the predicted values of the next time
steps of the horizon by using the EF model and the data provided. However, this method
uses constantly the previous value predicted to calculate the following one and therefore the
errors can be easily propagated over the next predictions [32].
From the 4 main equations of the EF model (Equations 19 - 22), the first is the one
where the prediction takes place and the errors can start to accumulate.
To solve this,
the change from multi-step predictions to one step predictions is proposed. With one step
predictions, the very next value of the horizon is predicted using the real observed data
instead of the calculated. In this way, the main change in the EF model is the one showed
in Equation 40 where instead of the predicted demand resulting from equations 21 and 22,
the measured demand is used for every time-step. The objective of applying this change is
to reduce possible errors that are being accumulated.

Conclusions
This Master Thesis was based on analyzing the energy flexibility of buildings using flex-
ibility functions. A previous literature review of the most important concepts was done and
later the EF model which constitutes the base of this report [9] was studied and analyzed.
On the following sections, two Case Studies were presented with the objective of verifying
the validity of the EF model when being applied to different energy systems and different PSs.
One the one hand, a heating floor system with temperature setpoints as PSs was stud-
ied. The results obtained showed that the model was able to reduce the demand according
to the PSs applied and the SOC available. The errors RMSEY and RMSEB were of 0.1 kW
and the R2 of the model was 56%. However, this case showed that the model had difficul-
ties when understanding step signals and thus, it has a prevalence for continuous functions
with a higher variability of its values. In addition, when simulating the heating floor of the
entire apartment, the time needed to obtain the flexibility was 20 times higher than the one
obtained when simulating only one of the rooms.
On the other hand, the Case Study 2 was focused on applying the EF model to a combi-
nations of different energy systems including: two heat pumps for space heating and DHW,
an electrical vehicle, a rooftop PV system, a stationary battery and other uncontrolled loads
related to the lighting and other appliances. The optimized demand for this case study
showed worse results compared to the first one. The RMSEY and RMSEB errors were 30
times higher and the R2 of the model is reduced drastically to 4%. This change in the per-
formance of the EF model led to the study of its parameters in order to identify where the
model can be improved.
On Section 4, observations were made in relationship with: the function g, the function
f, the parameter C and the prediction method. First, from the function g, the difficulties
found on the Case Study 1 where explained by the method used for building the regression
splines. Nevertheless, as the EF model is wanted to be used for a bigger scale, the regression
splines seems to be a smoother solution for representing the relationship between the demand
and the PSs. The parameter C also showed a relationship with the quality of the predictions
of the model (R2). It could be concluded that, when for achieving a faster response from the
energy systems, their flexibility potential is reduced. The prediction method was also studied
in order to see if the quality of the results could be improved. However, the structure of the
equations did not allow the model to converge, showing the multi-step prediction method to
be the most effective one.
Finally, the function f was studied in order to make the model adapt to each data set.
The function was changed from being a polynomial regression equation to a monotonous
decreasing I-Spline which adapts the parameters to each case. The results showed improve-
ments compared to the original model. For the Case Study 1, the R2 of the model was
increased while for the Case Study 2, the parameter ∆and thus the flexibility potential of

5.1
Scope for future work
The EF model that has been used to evaluate the flexibility of energy systems in build-
ings in this thesis was affected by some factors. First, the quantity of the data used for
the simulations. The forecast results could be improved in order to be more realistic if the
amount of data is increased. Thus, that would allow taking conclusions at a larger time scale
for comparing the optimized results between different seasons of the year for example.
In addition, the energy systems that were studied were from only one apartment of a
building and it would be interesting if the simulation included a cluster of buildings. This
could lead to the analysis of the possible share of energy through a private network by
combining their batteries, for instance. As a result, the homeowners can benefit of the con-
sequent economic profit for sharing and receiving energy from their neighbors.
Lastly, to increase the usability of this model, changes and simplifications according to
its interface could be made. In this way, it would be easier for users to understand where to
introduce their data and how to apply the model to obtain their specific conclusions.
