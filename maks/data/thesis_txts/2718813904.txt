Abstract
The construction industry considerably impacts global CO2 emissions, with the production and
use of structural materials being a significant emitter. Steel and iron production accounts for
approximately 7% of global CO2 emissions, with growing demand. As global efforts to mitigate
the impacts of climate change continue to gain traction, it has become increasingly clear that
the construction industry must change its practices to become more sustainable.
A potential approach to reducing the CO2 emissions of the construction industry is the reuse of
structural steel. It reduces the consumption of raw materials and the energy required to produce
new and recycled steel. Despite the numerous benefits of reusing structural steel, it still is a
relatively new concept within the construction industry.
This study aimed to find, execute and evaluate non­destructive test methods for determining
the structural properties of reclaimed steel elements. Laser scanning methods were used for
point cloud generation to determine cross­sectional dimensions, element length, and deflec­
tion of steel elements. A steel thickness gauge was employed to measure the flange and web
thickness, as well as corrosion­related cross­sectional thickness loss. Coating thickness tests
were carried out using the same steel thickness gauge. Older and traditional techniques, such
as using a meter stick to measure the cross­sectional dimensions and a string to measure the
deflection, were also employed. An asset management system stored all data retrieved from
the tests.
The key findings of this study show that the determination of the deflection with laser scanning
methods and corresponding point cloud generation and processing leads to accurate results.
On the other hand, the traditional string method resulted in unreliable measurements. Using
a steel thickness gauge, in conjunction with laser scanning techniques and a traditional me­
ter stick, resulted in accurate cross­sectional dimension measurements, with minor exceptions.
The steel thickness gauge yielded reliable measurements of cross­section reduction due to cor­
rosion. However, the device employed for measuring coating thickness led to incorrect results.
It was determined that Speckle is a viable option for an asset management system, as it im­
proves the exchange and accessibility of non­destructive test data.
The research has given new insights into the feasibility of such methods and added to the body
of knowledge in this field by developing new and applying existing methods on reclaimed steel
elements.

Introduction
Iron and steel production is responsible for 7% of global CO2 emissions and with the increase in
infrastructure and housing construction activities, mainly in emerging economies, the demand
for steel is expected to grow [1]. Approximately 30% of the global steel produced in 2019 was
recycled, utilizing steel scrap [2]. However, because the scrap is re­melted, it still emits roughly
26% CO2 of new steel [3]. To further reduce the carbon impact of steel structures, elements from
existing buildings could be reused. In order to reuse steel elements, many structural properties
of the steel need to be obtained non­destructively to verify the element’s structural capacity.
These non­destructive test methods have not been established in the context of reuse of struc­
tural steel elements.
This thesis aims to identify and evaluate non­destructive test methods to help improve pro­
cesses and the implementation of the usage of reclaimed structural steel in new construction
projects. This chapter introduces the study, beginning with a discussion of the background.
Next, the research problem, objectives, and questions will be presented, followed by an exam­
ination of the significance and limitations of the study.
To reduce the CO2 emissions of steel structures, the potential of reusing steel has been inves­
tigated in recent years. Reusing structural steel is described as the act of reclaiming steel from
a demolished structure and repurposing the individual members in a new building, which leads
to environmental and potentially cost advantages [4]. Brown et al. [4] was the first guidance
proposing investigation, and testing techniques to determine properties from reclaimed steel­
work, recommending safety factors for structural design and documenting responsibilities for
the holder such as identification, assessment, control procedures and declarations of confor­
mity. Gustafsson et al. [5] outline a classification system for the reuse of steel elements in the
danish construction industry, while [6] shows how the implementation of digital workflows can
help extract and store necessary steel characteristics.
Research problem
Numerous guidance [4, 5, 6] have listed the required steel element properties needed for the
structural design. These studies also document for many of these steel characteristics which
destructive and non­destructive test methods can be used to determine the properties [4, 5, 6].
However, these test methods have yet to be documented or tested on reclaimed steel elements,
raising the question if they work or if newer methods exist due to technological enhancements.
Furthermore, existing literature lacks test methods determining properties such as the cross­
sectional dimensions or tolerances. As a result, the existing literature is insufficient for a cost­
effective and safe implementation of reused structural steel elements in traditional structural
systems.
Research Aims
Due to the lack of research, testing data, and test methods on reclaimed structural steel ele­
ments, this study aims to identify and evaluate non­destructive testing methods for determining
steel properties necessary for structural design.
Research Objectives
The objectives of this study are 1. the identification of requirements for reclaimed structural
steel elements for the purpose of reuse, 2. the identification of possible non­destructive test
methods that determine structural steel properties, and 3. the execution of non­destructive
tests on reclaimed steel and documentation and evaluation of the tests and their results.

Research Questions
The research questions this thesis addresses are, first, can the non­destructive test methods
described in the literature be applied to reclaimed steel? Secondly, what non­destructive test
methods still need to be developed? Thirdly, can digital tools and processes help implement
the reuse of structural steel elements?
In 2015, 196 parties signed the Paris Agreement to limit global warming to 1.5 ­ 2 degrees
Celsius [7] to avoid and reduce climate catastrophes. With the iron and steel industry being one
of the leading CO2 emitters and, therefore, contributors to global warming, the reuse of structural
steel can considerably impact the climate. This thesis aims to enhance the existing literature on
the reuse of structural steel elements by examining and evaluating non­destructive test methods
to determine structural properties of reclaimed steels. This research aims to address the gap
in the literature, and helps develop new and validate existing methods.
One limitation of this study is the small sample size of beams used for testing, which is limited
to 5 due to time constraints. This may have affected the generalizability of the results and con­
clusions, as the sample may not be representative of other reclaimed steelwork. However, it
should be noted that the sample includes 4 out of 5 beams with diverse characteristics, such
as different cross­sections, coating systems, and levels of corrosion.
Another limitation is that this thesis focuses on a selected number of non­destructive test meth­
ods due to the time constraint of five months. However, the missing tests are summarized in
the literature review.
In chapter one, the research topic is briefly introduced. Chapter two covers the literature review,
assessing the requirements for reusing structural steel elements and examining how the most
important steel properties for structural design can be obtained. A digital workflow for storing
and accessing the data retrieved from the test will also be explained. Chapter three describes
the sample of beams and methodology of the non­destructive test methods used in this study.
In chapter four, the observations and test results will be presented, followed by an evaluation.
Chapter five discusses the test results. Finally, chapter six presents the conclusions and points
out ideas for future studies.

Theory
The need for more sustainable building materials has led to increased research on the topic
of reuse of structural steel elements. Previous studies [4, 5, 6] have focused on the topic and
list requirements. These guidance point out where non­destructive testing can be implemented
to help make the process of reusing steel elements possible, cheaper, and faster. However,
such methods have to be developed. This thesis addresses what and how non­destructive test
methods could be implemented.
2.1
Requirements for Reuse of Steel Elements
To design a structure with reused steel elements, geometrical and material properties need to
be known as for new elements. While some properties are required in all design situations,
others are not [4]. Table 2.1 gives an overview of steel properties, whether they are required,
and the type of test for the determination of the property. As of January 2023, reclaimed steel
elements need to fulfill the following requirements [4]:
• Assembled after 1970
• Steel does not come from bridges due to potential fatigue subjection
• Components without plastic hinges
• Cross­sectional loss due to Corrosion less than 5%
• No built­up and spliced members
• No signs of fire exposure
• Geometric tolerances from EN 1090­2 must be fulfilled
Et al. Gustafsson [5] describe the importance of finding a non­destructive test method to detect
defects and the thickness of coatings because the current state­of­the­art process, described
by Brown et al. [4], requires all existing coating to be removed and new coating to be applied.
This results in high labor and costs.
Lastly, as the stockholder needs to provide information about cross­sectional dimensions and
length of the steel elements [4], it is of interest, firstly, to develop a method to extract that infor­
mation and secondly, to find a process which stores the data.
The research of this study aims to identify non­destructive test methods for four properties men­
tioned in this section. These properties are:
• Coating thickness
• Cross­sectional dimensions, length and dimensional tolerances
• Strength (yield and tensile)
• Cross­sectional loss due to Corrosion

2.2
Coating Systems
Coating systems are applied to all structural steel elements used in buildings and bridges. In
general, coatings are used for two reasons ­ protection and aesthetics [8]. They protect the
steel, for example, from moisture penetrating from the outside to the inside or from corrosion
[8]. As important as the type of the coating system is, the surface treatment of the steel before
the coating application plays an equally important role, as many coating failures result from in­
adequate surface preparation [9].
As discussed in section 2.1, the current guidance [4] requires, first, to remove the entire coating
and, second, to apply a new coating, even if there is no indication of failures or defects. Excep­
tions can be made if all steel elements of a building are disassembled and rebuilt mostly in its
initial design. However, it is not recommended. [4].
Therefore, et al. Gustafsson [5] points out the importance of finding a process including a non­

destructive test method which allows non­damaged coatings to stay on steel elements.
This section addresses what coating types are used on structural steel, typical coating fail­
ures/defects, possible methods for the detection of coating thickness and failure, and repair
specifications and recommendations.
2.2.1
Coating Types
Steel coatings are mainly applied on carbon steel of rust grade A, B, and C (ISO 8501­1), hot
dip galvanized steel, as well as thermal­sprayed metallic coating [10].
Coating systems consist of prime coats, which are the first layer applied on the steel surface.
This coat provides adhesion to the metal and the subsequent coats [10]. Types of primer are:
• Zinc­rich primers, Zn (R) [10]
• Miscellaneous: All other categories [10]
For coating systems with three or more layers, intermediate coats are applied on top of the
primer, which acts as a barrier for corrosion [10].
Lastly, topcoats are the paint system’s last layer [10]. If only one coat is required, the primer
must also function as a topcoat [10].
The types of paint used depend on the corrosivity category, which are further explained in [11].
According to [10] five categories are relevant:
• C1 ­ very low
• C2 ­ low
• C3 ­ medium
• C4 ­ high
• C5 ­ very high
Examples of common types of paints are as Alkyd paint, Acrylic paint, ethyl silicate paint, paint
for epoxy coatings, paints for polyurethane coatings, paint for polyaspartic coatings, and paint
for polysiloxane coatings [10].
Special attention must be paid to the surface preparation of the steel in the shop [8]. Poor
surface preparation decreases durability significantly [8]. The surface is commonly prepared by
solvent cleaning, which removes grease, oil, and surface dirt or dust [8].
The different coating systems with the number of coats and thicknesses, dependent on the type
of steel and corrosivity category, are specified in [10] Table B.1­5, Table C.1­6, Table D.1 and
Table E.1.
2.2.2
Coating Failures and Defects
Reasons for failures and defects of coating systems are generally not evident and difficult to
identify due to the large variety of influences such as formulation, surface preparation, applica­
tion, drying and curing times and conditions, and environmental exposure, with more than one
contributing factor often being involved [12].
In the following, the most applicable coating failures and defects for structural steel elements
are summarized:
• Abrasion: This happens when the steel element is in contact with another element and
some of the coating is removed. Construction­related examples are when a steel compo­
nent is lifted with chains or transported [12].
• Adhesion failure: This failure type is characterized by paint not sticking to the steel sur­
face or another coat. Reasons for adhesion failure are incompatible coating systems or
Reuse of Structural Steel Elements
7
==PAGE:14==
excess of overcoating time [12].
• Cracking: Visible Cracks on the coating surface occur when dry paint splits due to surface
movement, aging, and a general lack of flexibility [12].
• Delamination: Occurs when two layers of paint loose contact due to adhesion loss [12].
• Rust spotting: Rust appears locally as small dots and can turn into rust rashing. Probable
causes are a low coating thickness or lamination and inclusion defects in the steel [12].
• Rust rashing: Local rust spots develop rapidly into areas of rust. A preventive measure
is to ensure the coating system has an adequate primer coat thickness [12].
• Rust staining: This failure possibly appears when wet rust dries and water runs over it.
Additionally, the color of the coating changes [12].
2.2.3
Detection of Coating Thickness and Failures
As described in section 2.1, the current requirements of removing the entire coating system and
applying new coats are time costly and financially expensive. Instead, an alternative method is
wished for [5], which allows keeping good coating on, but only coating areas with failures would
be repaired. The detection of coating failures is the first step in achieving the goal of a faster
and less expensive process. In the following, potential coating failure tests are described:
• Adhesion test: Determines the relative adhesion or cohesion. There are two commonly
used tests ­ a tensile adhesion test or also called pull­off adhesion test and the shear
or knife adhesion test [9]. However, these tests detach the coating and are, therefore,
destructive test methods.
• Coating thickness gauges: As failures of coating systems are often due to insufficient
thickness, determining the thickness is essential [9]. To measure the thickness, pull­off
thickness gauges and electronic thickness gauges can be used [9].
• Infrared cameras: Et al. Wiggenhauser [13] shows that infrared cameras, together with
visual inspection, can identify severe corrosion. Superficial corrosion, however, did not
result in a temperature change.
• Field microscope: To identify pinholes, cracking, and other defects that could lead to
premature failure [9].
• Clean rags: A clean surface is required to facilitate tests [9].
• Digital camera: For photo documentation [9].
2.2.4
Repair of Coatings
This section presents repair specifications and recommendations for maintenance of coating
systems in accordance to Denham et al. [14]. The following list summarizes the most important
measures:
• Before repairing and repainting, surfaces should be cleaned with a detergent and water
solution to remove dirt. Surfaces, then, should be rinsed with fresh water [14].
• Spot corrosion should be removed using an orbital sander or bristle blaster to St3 in ac­
cordance with BS EN ISO 8501­1. The coating can be removed with cutting discs [14].
• Larger than 75 mm x 75 mm, exposed steel corrosion areas must be manually prepared
to St3 following BS EN ISO 8501­1 [14].
• A sound edge shall be attained by feathering and abrading loose paint at least 50 mm
beyond the edge [14].

• Typically, an orbital sander and an 80 grit self­adhesive aluminum oxide abrasive pad are

• An approved compatible surface tolerant anti­corrosive epoxy primer must be applied by

2.3
Cross­Sectional Dimensions, Length and Dimensional
Tolerances
In order to verify structural steel elements, new and reclaimed, structural engineers need to
know the geometrical cross­sectional properties such as width, height, flange thickness, web
thickness, root radius, and the element length [4]. Furthermore, because the reclaimed ele­
ments have been exposed to loading, which might lead to a change in geometry, their dimen­
sions and shape need to be within tolerance values outlined in guidances [4], [5] and presented
in subsection 2.3.4.
This section discusses potential non­destructive test methods to detect the properties men­
tioned above of reclaimed steel elements and the limits they need to fulfill.
2.3.1
Determination of Cross­Sectional Dimensions and Element Length
Lineham et al. [6] indicate that laser scanners can be used to extract the cross­sectional dimen­
sions and element length from steel elements. However, tests have not been done on reclaimed
components. The idea is to scan existing steel structures to generate a point cloud and isolate
each element [6]. Tools like FARO AsBuilt or Leica Cyclone can automatically detect the steel
cross­section and generate Revit objects from the discretized point clouds [6]. Parameters that
are required for the design of reused steel elements can then be attached to the digital object
and sent to a database/cloud [6]. This process is further described in section 2.6.
2.3.2
Thickness Measurement of Flange and Web
Ultrasonic thickness measurements, a method described in [13], can determine the steel flange
and web thicknesses. The tests should follow DS/EN ISO 16809 [10]. This method calculates
the thickness by measuring the time that an ultrasonic pulse needs to travel through the steel,
a single or multiple times [10]. The material thickness is calculated by multiplying the known
sound velocity of the material with the transit time and dividing it by the number of times the
pulse transits the material wall [10]. Before executing the measurement, the surface must be
prepared accordingly. A clean and even contact area is essential, as the ultrasonic pulse needs
to penetrate the contact surface between the test object and probe twice [10].
2.3.3
Determination of Horizontal and Vertical Deflection
A method measuring the horizontal and vertical deflection of I (parallel flanges) and H sections
is described in EN 10034 [15] Annex A as follows.
• Horizontal straightness: The steel profile is placed in the I position on a flat surface, and
a taut string is pulled between the ends along the tip of the flange. The distance between
the string and the flange can then be measured [15].
• Vertical straightness: The steel profile is placed in the H position on a flat surface, and a
taut string is pulled between the ends along the center of the flange. The distance between
the string and the flange can then be measured [15].
The standard EN 10034 [15] is from 1993, and due to technological development, there might
be better methods nowadays to measure the deflection. However, there has yet to be any
literature on this topic.

2.4
Steel Strength
Previous research has demonstrated that the determination of yield and tensile strength char­
acteristics of steel elements is necessary for their reuse, and that non­destructive test methods
are employed for this purpose [4, 5, 6]. It is furthermore specified, that non­destructive test
methods are utilized to determine the yield and tensile strength [4]. However, these parameters
12
Reuse of Structural Steel Elements
==PAGE:19==
should not be used for further calculation. Instead, the steel grade is determined [4]. The yield
and tensile strength values are then retrieved from product standards of the determined steel
grade [4].
Additionally, non­destructive tests give helpful information about inconsistencies between group
members [4]. A group includes steel elements that have the same form, had the same function
in the old building (beam, column, etc.), come from the same structure and in total weigh less
than 20 tonnes [4]. To confirm yield and tensile strength determined by non­destructive testing,
destructive tensile tests are done on selected elements of a group [4].
2.4.1
Hardness Testing
Following [4], a hardness test should be done on all steel elements that will be reused to find the
yield and tensile strengths because the hardness and steel grade have a sufficiently accurate
relationship [4]. The hardness test should consider the following points [4]:
• Tests should be done on flanges at locations of little stress. For simply supported beams,
this is the case for support areas.
• The hardness of an individual steel element is calculated as the mean of three measure­
ments taken at the same place.
• A member can not be reused if the average hardness value of that member is more than
10% off the average value of the group.
• The test area has to be free of surface treatment.

2.5
Cross­Sectional Loss due to Corrosion
Corrosion is a phenomenon that degrades steel irreversibly due to influences on its surface,
such as acids, moisture, and oxygen [26]. In other words, corrosion reduces the flange and web
thickness of steel beams, leading to a smaller structural resistance. For steel structures which
are exposed to weathering, such as bridges, corrosion is a significant factor on its reliability and
durability [27].
As discussed in section 2.1, it is allowed to reuse steel elements if the thickness reduction due
to corrosion is less than 5% [4]. The reduced steel thickness of corroded elements can be
determined with an ultrasonic thickness measurement [13]. These measurements shall follow
[10]. This method is presented in subsection 2.3.2. Importantly, surfaces of corroded areas
must be prepared before measuring [10]. The preparation should lead to ground and clean
surfaces to increase the accuracy of the measurements [10].
2.6
Asset Management System
Wiggenhauser et al. [13] describe that procedures need to be developed to integrate the data
and results of non­destructive tests into a database. Such a repository, also described as an
asset management system, helps to document and visualize data. Furthermore, procedures
are required to efficiently integrate inspection data into Building Information Models [13].
An example of an asset management system for steel elements is Speckle [6]. Digital BIM
objects representing the physical steel elements are generated in design software such as Au­
todesk Revit in combination with tools like FARO As­Built or Leica Cyclone [6]. These tools
detect the cross­section from the point cloud and model the corresponding steel beam or col­
umn [6].
The non­destructive test data and results can then be attached to the digital objects as param­
eters and sent to a Speckle server, which stores the data. Objects stored on a Speckle server
can be imported in a variety of different software programs and used for design and drawings
[6].

Summary
This section summarizes the main findings from the literature review.
2.7.1
Requirements to Reuse Structural Steel
The literature review presents various requirements for reusing reclaimed structural steel el­
ements, including material and geometric parameters that are essential for structural design.

Reuse of Structural Steel Elements
==PAGE:21==
This thesis research focuses on examining specific requirements and properties, such as coat­
ing thickness, cross­sectional dimensions, element length, yield and tensile strength, and the
effect of corrosion on cross­sectional loss.
2.7.2
Coating Systems
The importance of identifying a non­destructive test method that allows for the preservation of
undamaged coatings on reclaimed steel elements is highlighted. Current guidelines necessitate
the complete removal and replacement of coatings, even without failures or defects. To gain an
understanding of this issue, the research examines typical coating types and failure modes used
for structural steel, including abrasion, adhesion failure, cracking, delamination, rust spotting,
rust rashing, and rust staining. Additionally, the research presents various tests and tools for
identifying potential coating failures, such as adhesion tests and measurements with a coating
thickness gauge, infrared cameras, field microscopes, and digital cameras. Lastly, recommen­
dations for the repair of coating systems are provided.
2.7.3
Cross­sectional Dimensions, Element Length and Dimensional
Tolerances
The literature suggests that laser scanning can be utilized to obtain cross­sectional dimensions
and element length of steel elements. Additionally, it has been demonstrated that ultrasonic
thickness measurements can be employed to determine the thickness of steel flanges and webs.
Furthermore, research suggests that the vertical and horizontal deflection can be determined
using a string. In accordance with European Standards, tolerance values for dimensions and
shape of steel elements are presented, including those for section height and width, web and
flange thickness, out­of­squareness, web off­centre and straightness.
2.7.4
Steel Strength
The literature demonstrates that Vickers hardness tests are employed to establish the yield and
tensile strength of reclaimed steel, from which the steel grade is determined. The yield and
tensile strength of that steel grade can then be utilized for the structural design of reclaimed
steel elements.
2.7.5
Cross­sectional Loss due to Corrosion
The reuse of structural steel is permissible, provided that the reduction in thickness resulting
from corrosion does not exceed 5%. The thickness of corroded areas can be determined via
ultrasonic thickness measurements.
2.7.6
Asset Management System
Studies have demonstrated the necessity for developing procedures to integrate data and out­
comes from non­destructive tests into a database. This technical foundation also referred to
as an asset management system serves to document and visualize data. Additionally, there
is a requirement for procedures that seamlessly integrate data from inspections into Building
Information Models.

The use of laser scanning technology was employed in this study to generate point clouds of the

1. Price: Relatively cheap device compared to advanced laser scanners.
2. Easy to use: Most people are familiar with smartphones. Using the LiDAR scanner is
similar to taking a video on a smartphone.
3. Quick: A scan of one beam took between 1–3 minutes.
For laser scanning beams 1­5, the app Scaniverse was utilized. To generate a point cloud of a
steel element with Scaniverse, the following methodology was used:
1. Installation of the app Scaniverse.
2. Selection of small object size as scan size.
3. The steel beam was scanned until almost no red surface area was visible on the screen
anymore. The red areas visualized areas that had not been scanned.
4. Selection of area or detail mode as processing mode.
5. The point cloud was cropped to remove as much of the surrounding objects as possible.
6. Export of the point cloud to the file format .las.

4.2
Laser Scanning
This section presents the computational workflow of laser scanning beam 1­5 and importing
the generated point clouds in various design software programs to extract the cross­sectional
dimensions and the deflection from the digital objects.
4.2.1
Computational Workflow
The following list presents the process of extracting the measurements of the cross­sectional
dimensions, deflection and element length from point clouds generated by a laser scanner.
1. Laser scan with Scaniverse: The laser scan is carried out as explained in section 3.2.
Two separate laser scans are conducted to detect the cross­sectional dimensions and
deflection. For determining the dimensions, only roughly 1 m of one end of the beam
was scanned, while for the detection of the horizontal and vertical deflection, only the top
flange was scanned from above and from one side.
2. Export of point cloud: The generated point cloud was exported to the file format .las and
sent by e­mail. Scaniverse can also export to other file formats, such as .fbx, .obj, .glb,
.usdz, .stl and .ply.
3. Import of point cloud in Autodesk Recap: The point cloud with file format .las was
imported to Autodesk Recap. In Recap the point cloud was saved with the format .rcp.
This step was required to import a point cloud into Autodesk Revit.
4. Import of point cloud in Autodesk Revit: The .rcp file was imported into Autodesk Revit.
Then section views, perpendicular and parallel to the beam’s length were made to align the
object to the vertical and horizontal planes. Other software programs such as Rhinoceros
were also tested. However, Revit was evaluated to be the most suitable because it could
create multiple floor plans and section views.
Detection of cross­sectional dimensions: A section view perpendicular to the beams’
length was created to measure the cross­section height, width, flange and web thickness.

In the section view, many points from the point cloud were visible, and the circumference
was drawn with detail lines, which could be measured with dimension lines. Figure 4.10
shows two section views of the point cloud of beam 2, while in Figure 4.11 detail and
dimension lines are added. Table 4.1 presents the measured dimensions of beam 1­5
and subsection 4.2.6 evaluates the results of this method.
Detection of deflection: The vertical deflection of the beam was measured in a section

the upper flanges at both ends of the point cloud of the beam. The distance between
this line and the highest point of the flange at the midpoint was measured as the vertical
deflection of the beam. Figure 4.13 shows a section view of beam 2 demonstrating the
measurement of the vertical deflection.
The horizontal deflection, compared to the vertical, was measured in a floor plan. A line
is drawn between the outermost points of the two ends of the flange. Then, the distance
between the flange at midspan and this line is measured as the horizontal deflection. An
example of a horizontal deflection measurement is presented in Figure 4.12.
Measurement of length: The length of the beams was determined my measuring the
length of the lines created for measuring the horizontal and vertical deflection.
5. Creation of a BIM object in Revit: Once the cross­section is classified, a Revit family
type representing the steel beam was created and sent to a Speckle server. However,
this process is explained in more detail in section 4.9.

5
Discussion
This chapter will summarize the key research findings concerning the research aims and ques­
tions and assess the findings’ value and contribution. Additionally, the chapter will examine the
study’s limitations and suggest potential applications of the findings.
This study aimed to identify the requirements for reusing structural steel, investigate non­destructive
test methods, and apply these tests on reclaimed structural steel elements to evaluate the re­
quirements and determine the material and geometric properties needed for structural design.
Non­destructive tests for the determination of the cross­sectional dimensions, deflection, cor­
rosion thickness and coating thickness were investigated. Furthermore, a digital workflow for
the storage and accessibility of data retrieved from the tests was proposed. The following list
presents the findings from the non­destructive tests and an evaluation of whether the deter­
mined properties met the requirements:
Cross­sectional dimensions
• The three tests used to determine the cross­sectional dimensions and their profile were
laser scanning, steel thickness gauge testing, and measuring with a meter stick. Utilizing
a meter stick resulted in the most accurate and quickest method to measure the height
and width.
• The laser scan method detected the cross­section width for the five test beams with
0.4 mm accuracy and the height with 2 mm. Bigger differences for beams 2 and 4 oc­
curred. However, because the meter stick method led to similar results, it was evaluated
that the profile dimensions did not precisely match the dimensions specified in [11].
• All three methods led to accurate results for measuring the flange thickness. However,
bigger inaccuracies occurred for the web thickness.
• Laser scanning and steel thickness gauge were the preferred method for thickness mea­
surements smaller than 10 mm. These thicknesses measured with the meter stick could
only be determined in ranges of 1 mm.
• The cross­section dimension measurements of beams 1, 3 and 5 with all three test meth­
ods fulfilled the tolerance requirements given in DS/EN 10034 Table 1 [15]. The measure­
ments of the section height of beam 2 and 4 did not meet these criteria.
• This method has further potential for automation in a factory compared to manual mea­
surements with a meter stick. The laser scans could be done by robots and 3D point cloud
processing could detect the cross­section.
Deflection
• The method outlined in DS/EN 10034 [15], which utilizes a string, was found to produce
unreliable deflection measurements. Instead, an alternative approach of utilizing a point
cloud of the steel element, obtained through laser scanning, was demonstrated to yield
more accurate and dependable results.
Corrosion thickness
• The non­destructive tests with the thickness gauge resulted in reliable and accurate val­
ues. The steel thickness of corroded areas was measured and compared to the minimum
56
Reuse of Structural Steel Elements
==PAGE:63==
allowable thickness, which is 95% of the thickness of new steel sections according to the
requirements of steel reuse [4].
Coating thickness
• The coating thickness was determined utilizing the same device employed for measuring
the thickness of the steel, as the device was capable of measuring the steel thickness
with and without coating. The results, however, turned out to be unsatisfactory, as mea­
surements with the setting ”without coating” were often bigger than with the setting ”with
coating”.
Speckle as Asset Management System
• A cloud platform for 3D geometry called Speckle was tested as an asset management
system. The requirement of integrating data from non­destructive tests into Building In­
formation Models (BIM) [13] could be done.
• Speckle could be used as a database, where the results from non­destructive tests were
stored and accessed.
• 3D objects with data attached were imported and exported to different design software
programs used in the AEC industry.
The research presented in this thesis has several limitations that should be acknowledged.
Firstly, the study’s sample size was limited to only 5 beams. While this allowed for a detailed
analysis of the properties of the beams, it may not reflect the population as a whole. Therefore,
the limited sample size is a consideration that should be taken into account when generalizing
the results to a larger population. Secondly, the five­month time limitation allowed testing only
some properties required for structural design (section 2.1). The tests that were performed fo­
cused on key properties such as cross­sectional dimensions, deflection, corrosion, and coating
thickness. Other properties, such as yield strength, tensile strength (section 2.4), web off­centre
tolerance, and out­of­squareness tolerance (Table 2.4), were not evaluated.
This study has made a significant contribution to the field of reuse of structural steel by identi­
fying and executing non­destructive test methods to determine some of the required geometric
and material parameters of structural design. Through the development of new methods and
the application of existing methods on reclaimed steel elements, this research has provided new
insights into the feasibility of such methods and has expanded upon the existing knowledge in
this area. These findings have important implications for future research and implementation of
reclaimed structural steel elements in construction projects. Some of the presented methods
could already now help extract valuable information from structures for renovation or mainte­
nance purposes.
In my own experience working as a student assistant for ABC Rådgivende Ingeniører, I have
had the opportunity to use the laser scan method developed in this thesis in a real­world set­
ting. A trapezoidal roof sheeting had to be verified with the additional dead load of solar panels.
However, the geometry was not documented in the existing report from 1971, and the cross­
sectional dimensions had to be measured on a site visit. The results from the laser scan were
beneficial for areas that could not easily be reached with a meter stick, such as the bottom
surface width of the top flange. On projects like this, the thickness gauge could also be used
to determine the steel thickness, as the state­of­the­art method, drilling a hole in the sheeting
done by an external craftsman and measuring the thickness with a meter stick, leads to higher
costs and irreversible damages. However, this parameter was not required on the project, so
the thickness gauge was not used.

Conclusion
Several non­destructive tests were done on 5 steel beams to determine necessary properties
for the structural design. The tests involved measurements on point clouds generated by laser
scans, steel thickness gauge tests, corrosion thickness tests, coating thickness tests, measure­
ments with a meter stick, and a method utilizing a string.
The following conclusions can be drawn from the tests done for this study:
• Laser scanning, a meter stick, and the steel thickness gauge could determine the cross­
sectional dimensions. Compared to the cross­sections with the most similar dimensions,
the meter stick measurements were most accurate for width and height dimensions, while
the flange and web thickness were measured with higher accuracy by the laser scanning
and steel thickness gauge method.
• Measuring the deflection from point clouds of the steel elements created by laser scans
suggested accurate results, while measuring the distance between a pulled string and the
flange as the deflection seemed to lead to incorrect results.
• The steel thickness of corroded areas could be measured with a steel thickness gauge.
The requirement that the thickness is not reduced by more than 5% could be investigated.
• Determining the coating thickness with the steel thickness gauge led to incorrect results.
The coating thickness was calculated by subtracting the steel thickness measurement
without coating from the steel thickness measurement with coating.
• Speckle was identified to be a possible asset management system of structural steel ele­
ments. The data retrieved from the non­destructive tests could be attached to 3D objects
representing the beams and accessed from various design software programs.

6.1
Recommendations for Future Work
In conclusion, this study has provided insights into the field of reuse of structural steel. However,
there are still areas that require further investigation. Therefore, future studies should expand
upon the current findings and delve deeper into specific aspects of the research topic. Future
research may include:
• Measurement of cross­sectional dimensions: Execution of the three non­destructive
test methods, laser scanning, meter stick measurement and thickness gauge testing, on
a larger sample and on elements where the cross­sectional dimensions are known.
• Measurement of cross­sectional dimensions and deflection: Tests with professional
laser scanning setup could increase the accuracy of the point cloud from which the cross­
sectional dimensions and deflection are measured. With a higher accuracy, the web off­
centre tolerance and out­of­squareness tolerances could be evaluated.
• Measurement of cross­sectional dimensions: Automated cross­section geometry de­
tection of the point clouds of steel beams using 3D point cloud processing [29].
• Coating thickness: Determination of the coating thickness with a coating thickness gauge.
• Tensile and yield strength: Vickers hardness testing to determine the tensile and yield
strength
• Tensile and yield strength:
Destructive tensile testing and comparison of the results
with the tensile and yield strength obtained from the hardness test.

Transducers
Elcometer NDT offer a state-of-the-art range of Ultrasonic Gauge transducers. 
When selecting a transducer it is important to choose one which will meet the specific application's 
needs. The type of material to be tested, the measurement range, the shape of the substrate (curved 
or flat) and the size of the material should be considered when selecting the appropriate transducer. 
Choosing the right frequency and diameter 
Different materials have different acoustic properties. 
In some a sound wave can travel easily, in others it 
is absorbed so achieving an accurate measurement 
can be difficult.  To overcome this it is essential to 
choose the right frequency and diameter for your 
material.
High frequency transducers are ideal for precision 
measurement because the pulse they emit is highly 
focused, reducing the risk of return echoes outside 
of the measurement area. The high frequency and 
shorter wavelength also lends itself to measuring thin 
materials.
Low frequency transducers are designed for materials 
that absorb sound like plastics or composites.  The 
pulse penetrates deeply into the material ensuring a 
strong return echo and therefore a measurement.  
This high penetration also means that they are 
suitable for high material thicknesses.
Larger diameter probes feature larger crystals which 
transmit and receive the sound wave. A large crystal 
transmitter will produce a larger sound wave and a 
larger receiving crystal will be more sensitive. 
As a result, larger transducers tend to have better 
penetration characteristics than the smaller ones. 
If this extended range is not required, the smaller 
transducers can be placed more precisely and in hard 
to reach areas such as narrow grooves in a material.
What connection does it have?
Potted: The transducer is strongly secured to the cable 
at the factory.
Microdot: The transducer is attached using two small 
screw type connectors, enabling replacement of the 
cable in case of accidental damage or wear.
All transducers are intelligent; when connected to the 
MTG or PTG range, the gauge instantly recognises what 
transducer has been attached.
Selecting the right transducer
Selecting the right transducer for your application is 
essential to maximise performance.
