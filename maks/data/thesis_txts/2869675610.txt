ABSTRACT 
This master thesis seeks to investigate the concept of sufficiency as a counterpart to 
overconsumption and develop a practical tool for companies to take an active role in 
mitigating overconsumption of clothing in the apparel industry. The final problem 
statement explored is: How to develop business strategies for the apparel industry that 
support consumers in achieving sufficiency-driven clothing consumption through 
prolong practices without change of user? 
 
Using the Design Research Methodology, the study includes an exploratory literature 
review on the concept of sufficiency and sufficiency-orientated business models. The 
findings revealed a lack of a universal definition of sufficiency, discrepancies 
between theoretical models and practical implementation, and an overlap between 
sufficiency-driven strategies and circular economy strategies. This necessitated a 
clarification of the definition of sufficiency: "Sufficiency aims to limit resource use by 
reducing overconsumption to levels compatible with social well-being within the 
planetary capacity.” 
A systematic literature review identified key barriers to implementing 
sufficiency strategies in business contexts. The review revealed a gap related to 
research on specific sufficiency strategies. The identified barriers are mainly from a 
consumer perspective and related to sustainable consumption as a broader concept 
than of sufficiency. The key barriers identified for consumers are current social 
norms, economic factors, negative contextual factors, and lack of knowledge and trust. 
For companies, the main challenge lies in aligning sufficiency objectives with the 
current profit-orientated economic paradigm.  
An empirical data collection through an online survey examined consumer 
clothing consumption patterns, purchasing motivations, and perceptions of garment 
longevity strategies. 
 
The study’s outcome is a practical tool consisting of a sufficiency-driven business 
model canvas (SUFFit BMC) and a deck of strategy cards (SUFFit Strategy Cards), 
designed to help companies outline and develop sufficiency-driven business models 
and implement actionable strategies.  
 
The SUFFit BMC contributes to theory by clarifying the elements of a 
sufficiency-driven business model, and the SUFFit Strategy Cards define concrete 
strategies for prolonging garment lifetime without changing users.

Future imminent work includes refining the financial aspects and a second cycle of

prolonging the lifetime of clothing through several users, and 3) supporting 
established companies in the transition into sufficiency-driven companies.

1.​ INTRODUCTION 
The global climate crisis and environmental challenges are more urgent than ever. 
With rising temperatures, loss of biodiversity, and severe disruptions to ecosystems, 
human activity has pushed natural ecosystems beyond their limits (Calvin et al., 2023; 
Richardson et al., 2023). Six of the nine planetary boundaries are transgressed, 
pushing natural systems to dangerous tipping points (Beulque et al., 2023; Richardson 
et al., 2023).  
These challenges are largely a result of humans’ unsustainable linear economic 
models, based on a produce-use-dispose mindset, which is heavily dependent on 
fossil fuels and the use of scarce resources (EEA, 2019). To avoid the worst 
consequences of climate change, there is an urgent need for comprehensive changes 
to the current linear consumption and production systems (Calvin et al., 2023). One 
industry that exemplifies the destructive consequences of these systems is the 
apparel industry, more specifically fast fashion (EEA, 2019; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 
2015a). Consumption of household textiles, clothing, and footwear is among the 
highest consumption domains in the EU (EEA, 2019). It is the fourth highest pressure 
category for primary raw material consumption and fifth highest for greenhouse gas 
emissions (EEA, 2019).  
1.1.​
The apparel industry 
This section begins by outlining the historical development of the modern apparel 
industry. This is followed by an examination of the industry's social and 
environmental impacts, along with an exploration of its key driving factors.  
1.1.1.​
Purpose of clothing 
The central role of clothing in human lives today is evidenced by the global scale of 
the apparel market, which in 2024 accounted for 1.63 percent of the world's GDP and 
is estimated to value 1.79 trillion dollars (Global Apparel Industry Statistics (2024) | 
UniformMarket, 2024). The consumption of clothing is deeply embedded in human 
culture, fulfilling both functional needs and socio-psychological needs. Practically, 
clothing offers protection against weather and environment as insulation and a shield 
against harsh weather such as wind, rain, frost, and UV radiation. Clothes also protect 
the body from abrasions and harmful substances and act as a barrier against dirt, 
bacteria, and other contaminants. Beyond the practical needs, clothing serves as a 
medium for personal and cultural expression and social belonging (Fowles, 1974). 
7 of 119 
==PAGE:9==
 
Clothing enables individuals to convey their personal style and creativity and reflect 
their cultural identity, such as heritage, religious beliefs, or other individual values 
(Kühnhardt, 2019). While the practical needs for protection remain universal, the 
psychological and social aspects of clothing differ around the world and have evolved 
significantly over time (Kühnhardt, 2019).   
1.1.2.​
Historical background 
Historically, apparel production and trends followed a much slower pace compared to 
today's fast fashion, which is defined as mass production of clothing replicating 
recent high-fashion trends (EEA, 2019; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). Before the 
Industrial Revolution, clothing was a scarce and expensive commodity, produced 
locally by hand and worn for many years (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). The rise of 
factories in the early 19th century and the advent of mass production brought 
significant changes to the way clothing was made and consumed. Garments became 
more affordable and widely available, leading to an increase in consumption 
(Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015).  
With globalisation and technological advancements in production and 
logistics, the fast fashion business model that relies on the rapid production of 
low-cost clothing truly emerged in the last half of the 20th century (Ozdamar Ertekin 
& Atik, 2015). In 40 years from 1975 to 2018, global textile production increased by 
120% (Olivar Aponte et al., 2024).  
Brands like Zara and H&M revolutionised the industry by reducing the time 
from design to store to just a few weeks, creating a relentless cycle of new collections 
(Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015).  
Fast fashion retailers today introduce between 12 and 24 collections a year, 
corresponding to a change in collection every two or third week (Freudenreich & 
Schaltegger, 2020). H&M and Zara put 23.000 and 40.000 new products each on the 
market per year (Masters, 2023), with an even faster pace seen among so-called 
ultra-fast-fashion brands, like the Chinese-owned company Shein, which puts 1.5 
million products on the market per year (Masters, 2023). Despite the growing 
environmental concerns, global demand for fashion continues to rise, and the apparel 
industry is expected to grow (Olivar Aponte et al., 2024), driven by a culture of 
overconsumption that fast fashion has helped normalise (Jensen & Minter, 2024; 
Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015).  
1.1.3.​
Social and environmental impact 
Fast fashion has intensified the demand for raw materials, leading to the widespread 
exploitation of natural resources and labour (EEA, 2019; Olivar Aponte et al., 2024; 
Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015; Sarokin & Bocken, 2024). The high production speed 
8 of 119 
==PAGE:10==
 
combined with low costs fosters a clothing production system closely tied to poor 
working conditions, low wages, and health risks such as respiratory and 
musculoskeletal problems, especially in developing countries (EEA, 2019; Olivar 
Aponte et al., 2024).  
Led by fast fashion’s intensive resource use, the apparel industry is 
responsible for about 4–10% of global greenhouse gas emissions and about 20% of 
global clean water pollution (European Parliament, 2020; Olivar Aponte et al., 2024). 
The most significant environmental impacts occur during fibre production and wet 
processing of textiles (Olivar Aponte et al., 2024).  
Additionally, textile waste is a growing environmental concern, generated 
both during production and consumption (Olivar Aponte et al., 2024). The fast 
fashion’s dependence on mass production and short-lived trends foster 
overproduction, with many items being discarded shortly after purchase (EEA, 2019; 
Olivar Aponte et al., 2024; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). It is estimated that only 
one-third of clothing produced is sold at full price, with another third sold at a 
discount and the remaining third left unsold, often incinerated or sent to landfills 
(EEA, 2019). In 2017, approximately 87% of all fast fashion garments produced globally 
ended up in landfills or were incinerated (European Parliament, 2020b), and a growing 
proportion of textile waste is exported to countries ill-equipped to manage the influx 
of textile waste (Jensen & Minter, 2024). Only one percent of textile waste is recycled 
into new clothing (European Parliament, 2020). 
This is compounded by the fact that much of the clothing is barely worn. 
Studies show that around 40% of clothing in consumers’ wardrobes is inactive. 
meaning it is rarely or never worn (Jensen & Minter, 2024).  
The low price of garments has made fashion clothes more accessible than ever, 
but at the same time, the industry has become far more challenged in terms of 
sustainability (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). The fast fashion industry is currently 
able to continue operating in such an unsustainable way because there is a 
misalignment between the true environmental and social costs of production and the 
prices consumers pay (EEA, 2019). The negative impacts of clothing production are 
not reflected in the retail prices of garments (EEA, 2019).  
As a result, consumers can purchase clothing for just a few euros, with little 
consideration for its environmental impact or the working conditions of those who 
produce it. The ease and affordability of purchasing new garments, combined with 
fast fashion’s relentless drive to introduce new trends, has accelerated unsustainable 
consumption patterns (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). While consumers in wealthier 
countries, particularly in the Global North, stay largely insulated from the damaging 
realities of clothing production, the environmental and social costs of fast fashion are 
disproportionately borne by poorer nations (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015).  
9 of 119 
==PAGE:11==
 
This disconnection between production and consumption allows fast fashion 
to thrive (EEA, 2019). While there are emerging textile start-ups focused on 
sustainable innovation in the industry, the fast fashion model remains the 
predominant force driving the industry’s unsustainable practices (EURATEX, 2024). 
To avoid the environmental and social costs associated with the industry, the fast 
fashion business model and current linear consumption practices cannot maintain 
their dominance (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020).    
1.2.​
Circular economy as a mitigation strategy 
This section describes the concept of a circular economy with the use of relevant 
frameworks. Additionally, it is described how the concept has been deployed in the 
apparel industry. 
1.2.1.​
Circular economy  
The concept of a circular economy, introduced as an alternative to the current linear 
economy, has within the past decade gathered significant momentum (Kirchherr et 
al., 2023), particularly as a key component of EU’s sustainability strategy, A new 
Circular Economy Action Plan from 2020 (European Commission, 2020).  
The circular economy model aims to limit the use of resources and the 
generation of waste by maintaining the value of materials and products in the 
economy as long as possible (Bocken et al., 2016; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019; 
European Parliament, 2023). There exist several circular economy frameworks in 
literature (Blomsma & Brennan, 2017; ready2LOOP, n.d.-b), one of the most broadly 
known models is the butterfly diagram (Figure 1) developed by Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation.  
10 of 119 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
Figure 1: Butterfly Diagram (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2021). 
 
The butterfly diagram illustrates the flow of materials in a circular economy by 
dividing resources into two main cycles, the biological cycle (left side on Figure 1) 
and the technical cycle (right side on Figure 1).  
The biological cycle focuses on renewable and biodegradable materials, such 
as food, organic waste, and plant-based materials (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 
2022a). In the biological cycle, materials are processed and used in ways that allow 
them to return to the environment through natural processes, like composting or 
anaerobic digestion. For instance, organic waste from agriculture can be composted 
to produce nutrient-rich soil, which can then support new plant growth. This cycle 
emphasises processes that regenerate natural systems, maintaining an ongoing loop 
of renewal (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022a).  
The technical cycle deals with non-biodegradable materials, such as metals, 
plastics, and other manufactured goods. In the technical cycle, products are designed 
to be reused, repaired, refurbished, remanufactured, or recycled (Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, 2022b). The goal is to retain these materials within the economy at their 
highest value for as long as possible to minimise waste. In a circular economy, the 
smaller the loop, the more resource-efficient the strategy. Circular processes, in order 
of preference, include: share (maximising the use of products/materials by having 
multiple users), maintain/prolong (repairing or updating a product to extend its 
lifetime), reuse (using a product multiple times in its current form), 
refurbishment/remanufacture (restoring a product to good condition) and recycling 
11 of 119 
==PAGE:13==
 
(breaking down a product to create raw materials for new products) (Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, 2022b).  
The two cycles require careful management to avoid waste and pollution while 
maximising the utility of materials. The butterfly diagram demonstrates how 
materials can be kept in circulation in ways that are either regenerative (for biological 
materials) or restorative (for technical materials), aiming to minimise waste and the 
need for new resource extraction (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2021).  
Bocken et al. (2016) presented another circular economy framework, with a 
specific focus on product design. The framework (Figure 2) illustrates how resources 
can circulate within a system based on three main mechanisms: slowing, closing, and 
narrowing resource loops (Bocken et al., 2016). This classification offers more specific 
guidance on how individual products and resources can be managed to reduce waste 
and extend utility.  
 
Figure 2: Framework that illustrates linear and circular approaches for reducing resource use 
developed by Nancy Bocken (2016).  
 
Slowing strategies focus on extending the lifetime of products through extended 
utilisation. Examples are encouraging repairability, reuse, maintenance, and 
durability (Bocken et al., 2016). Closing strategies aim to circulate resources by 
creating closed-loop systems where products are recycled or remanufactured at the 
end of their life cycle rather than being discarded as waste (Bocken et al., 2016). 
Narrowing strategies focus on reducing the amount of resources required per product 
by enhancing efficiency in production and consumption (Bocken et al., 2016).   
These three mechanisms—slowing, closing, and narrowing— Bocken et al. 
(2016) addresses how resources should be managed within a circular economy from 
product design to disposal.  
12 of 119 
==PAGE:14==
 
By applying these frameworks, businesses, designers, policymakers, and other 
relevant actors can work towards more sustainable systems that align with circular 
economy principles. 
1.2.2.​
Circular economy in the apparel industry 
As a response to the negative social and environmental impacts of the fashion 
industry, implementation of circular economy principles is frequently highlighted as 
a promising solution (Abdelmeguid et al., 2024; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, n.d.; 
Niinimäki, 2017; Nike, n.d.; Patagonia, 2021; Radhakrishnan, 2021). An example is the 
EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles, which, through a list of actions, 
such as requirements for repairability and recycled content and extended producer 
responsibility, aims to change the way textiles are produced and consumed 
(European Commission, n.d.-b).  
Looking at the overall apparel industry, the specific circular economy 
strategies applied refer to narrowing flows in terms of efficiency through technical 
improvements in production e.g. arranging pattern pieces in a way that optimises 
fabric use. Secondly, closing flows in terms of consistency through production with 
natural material flows, such as biodegradable materials or creating closed-loop 
systems (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). The prevalence of slowing strategies, in 
terms of reuse of clothing through secondhand stores and platforms, have also 
increased rapidly in the latest years (Secondhand Apparel Market Worldwide 
2021-2028, 2024), and an example is the peer-to-peer secondhand platform Vinted 
(Vinted | Sælg og køb tøj, sko og tilbehør, n.d.).  
However, despite the effectiveness of these approaches in reducing 
environmental and social impacts, their overall benefits are undermined by the 
growing production and consumption of new clothing (Bocken et al., 2022; 
Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). Therefore, the aim of this project is to investigate 
how companies can play an active role in mitigating overconsumption of clothing 
among consumers. The project consists of developing a support for the apparel 
industry that focuses on the concept of sufficiency (as the counterpart to 
overconsumption).

2.​ METHODOLOGY 
This project follows the Design Research Methodology (DRM), which is structured 
into four phases: Research Clarification, Descriptive Study I, Prescriptive Study, 
Descriptive Study II, as illustrated in Figure 3.  
 
 
Figure 3: Illustration of the phases that constitute the Design Research Methodology. Adapted 
from Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009).  
 
The overall objectives and method used for each phase are briefly described in this 
section, followed by a more detailed description throughout the report. 
2.1.​
Research Clarification 
The objective of the Research Clarification phase is to establish a preliminary 
understanding of the current and desired situation within the research topic (Blessing 
& Chakrabarti, 2009). This phase focuses on identifying the goals of the study, 
defining the research focus, and formulating the main research problems, questions, 
and hypotheses (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). The primary output of this phase is to 
outline the overall research plan and determine the specific focus areas for the 
subsequent Descriptive Study I phase (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).  
 
The following methods were applied: 
●​
Desk research: This involved investigating the social and environmental 
impacts of the apparel industry to provide a contextual foundation for the 
study. 
●​
Exploratory literature review: This review focused on understanding how the 
concept of sufficiency is defined in existing literature and mapping

sufficiency-orientated business models and strategies currently discussed in 
academic literature.  
 
2.2.​
Descriptive Study I 
The objective of the Descriptive Study I phase is to gain a more detailed 
understanding of the current situation by identifying key influencing factors 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). The primary output of this phase is to define the most 
suitable factors to address during the Prescriptive Study phase to improve the 
existing situation (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Additionally, it provides a reference 
for evaluating the developed support during the Descriptive Study II phase (Blessing 
& Chakrabarti, 2009). 
In this phase, a more detailed understanding of existing sufficiency strategies 
was obtained, and barriers to their implementation were investigated. 
 
The following methods were applied: 
●​
Systematic literature review: This method was used to explore the barriers 
preventing the implementation of sufficiency strategies in a business context. 
●​
Empirical data collection through an online survey: The survey examines 
consumers' clothing consumption patterns, their reasons for purchasing 
apparel, their priorities when buying, and their perceptions of existing 
strategies aimed at prolonging garment lifetime. 
 
2.3.​
Prescriptive study 
The main objective of the Prescriptive Study phase is to define the desired situation 
and develop preliminary solutions to address the factors identified in the Descriptive 
Study I phase (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). The primary output from this phase is 
the formulation of success criteria, conceptualising potential solutions, and deciding 
on the focus for implementation (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).  
During this phase, the specific support for sufficiency-driven business 
practices in the apparel industry was developed, along with the requirement for 
evaluating its success.  
 
The following methods were applied: 
●​
Support requirements and criteria: Specific support requirements and 
criteria were developed to define the necessary parameters for a successful 
support.

●​
Design criteria: Design criteria were used to guide the development of the 
support. 
●​
Brainstorming: Brainstorming was used to generate and refine ideas for the

●​
Conceptualisation: Conceptualisation was used to develop a full concept for a

2.4.​
Descriptive Study II 
The objective of the Descriptive Study II phase is to evaluate the applicability and 
success of the developed support (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). This involves 
assessing its usability, usefulness, potential implications, and any unintended side 
effects (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).  
 
The following methods were applied: 
●​
Expert evaluation: Field specialists were engaged to assess the content, 
feasibility, and usefullness of the support. 
●​
User testing: User tests were performed to gather feedback from potential 
end-users and evaluate the usefulness and usability of the support.  
 
2.5.​
(Initial) Prescriptive Study 
The initial steps for a second Prescriptive Study phase were included in this project. 
In this phase, the support is further developed through implementing feedback from 
the evaluation sessions. The primary output from this phase is refinement of the 
proposed support and detailing its design based on feedback from stakeholders 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). 
 
The following methods were applied: 
●​
Conceptualisation: Further conceptualisation was used to refine the support. 
●​
Personas: Personas was developed to present various consumer types. 
 
By systematically progressing and iterating through these phases, the DRM provides 
a structured approach to understand challenges, designing innovative solutions, and 
refining them through empirical evaluation and stakeholder involvement (Blessing & 
Chakrabarti, 2009).

RESEARCH CLARIFICATION 
3.​ THEORY 
To initiate the research on the concept of sufficiency, an exploratory literature review 
has been conducted. This section details the concept of sufficiency as the 
counterpart to overconsumption and investigates how current academic literature 
suggests it to be operationalised in a business context. 
3.1.​
Defining sufficiency 
The concept of sufficiency is interpreted across multiple disciplines 
(Jungell-Michelsson & Heikkurinen, 2022) and lacks a universal definition (Gossen & 
Kropfeld, 2022; Beulque, 2023). However, there are common themes in literature.  
Sufficiency is centred around upstream waste reduction strategies and is 
positioned at the higher levels of the waste hierarchy, with a focus on the principles 
of Rethink, Reduce, and Refuse (see Figure 4) (Bocken et al., 2022; Sakorin & Bocken, 
2024). 
 
Figure 4: Circular hierarchy depicting sufficiency strategies. Source: (Bocken et al., 2022). 
 
Sufficiency is a response to current unsustainable production and consumption 
practices and is viewed as the third strategy alongside efficiency and consistency 
within the circular economy  (Beulque et al., 2023; Bocken et al., 2016; Gossen & 
Kropfeld, 2022; Stahel, 2001). To distinguish sufficiency from efficiency, Freudenreich 
& Schaltegger (2020) describe efficiency as “making and doing more with less," 
whereas sufficiency offers a different approach of seeking to “make do with less." This 
contrast in phrasing emphasises how sufficiency raises the level of sustainability by 
questioning the fundamental need for resources. Adding sufficiency as a third 
strategy alongside efficiency and consistency suggests that aligning resource use 
with natural cycles (consistency) and reducing resource use per unit (efficiency) are 
17 of 119 
==PAGE:19==
 
not enough. Instead, sufficiency challenges us to consider when we have enough. 
What constitutes an appropriate level of consumption, and when does consumption tip 
into excess?  
 
As one can argue that overall resource reduction is embedded in a circular economy, 
sufficiency emphasises the original aim for a circular economy as it aims for an 
absolute reduction in resource use through reducing consumption levels (Gossen & 
Kropfeld, 2022; Soulis & Mont, 2024; Sakorin & Bocken, 2024).  
Sufficiency is adding the element of to what level the resource use needs to be 
restricted to. It is referred to as a concept that focuses on the sense of “enoughness” 
or a sufficient consumption level (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022), that is, ensuring 
social well-being for all while operating with restricted resource use to stay within 
planetary boundaries (Beulque et al., 2023; Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Bocken et 
al., 2022; Garcia-Ortega et al., 2023; Niessen & Bocken, 2021b).  
Sufficiency is thereby tied to political ecology, addressing social justice and 
equitable distribution, in contrast to the rapid pace of globalisation and 
industrialisation (Jungell-Michelsson & Heikkurinen, 2022). Sufficiency further seeks 
to decelerate social metabolism, which refers to the high intensity of human activity 
driving ecological degradation (Jungell-Michelsson & Heikkurinen, 2022), through 
avoiding overconsumption (Gossen & Kropfeld, 2022; Bocken et al., 2022; Beyeler, 
2022; Bocken & Short, 2016; Kelleci, 2021), and is thereby linked to ecological 
economics, challenging the prevailing economic models and aligning closely with 
degrowth principles (Jungell-Michelsson & Heikkurinen, 2022). Finally, sufficiency 
can be linked to ecological philosophy, specifically sufficientarianism, which centres 
around meeting the minimum needs necessary for a good life for all 
(Jungell-Michelsson & Heikkurinen, 2022).  
To summarise and capture the social and ecological dimensions of sufficiency, 
the following definition is proposed: 
 
“Sufficiency aims to limit resource use by reducing overconsumption to levels 
compatible with social well-being within the planetary capacity.” 
 
Two critical questions arise from this definition.  
(i) What level of consumption is ‘enough’ to ensure social well-being?  
(ii) Is this level of consumption achievable within the planet’s ecological 
boundaries?  
 
The answer to these questions is complex, and a global threshold may not exist, as it 
likely depends on numerous factors, such as cultural, economic, and geographical 
contexts.  
18 of 119 
==PAGE:20==
 
Establishing such thresholds of consumption levels is an active area of 
research in itself, and, although extremely relevant, it is not the focus of this project. 
Given the high levels of overconsumption in the apparel industry and the lack of 
strategies and initiatives aimed at reducing and preventing such excess (Freudenreich 
& Schaltegger, 2020), it is nonetheless highly relevant to explore why sufficiency is so 
challenging to implement in this sector and to identify practical strategies that could 
facilitate a sufficiency approach, which is addressed by this research.  
Thus, this thesis will not attempt to define a threshold for the consumption 
level of clothing, but we aim to underscore it as a priority for future research and 
emphasise the importance of debate on what constitutes a sufficient level of clothing 
consumption.  
3.2.​
Sufficiency-driven business models  
Historically, the responsibility for sufficiency has largely been placed on consumers, 
with an emphasis on changing their behaviour (Beulque et al., 2023). As sufficiency is 
viewed as the counterbalance to overconsumption, strategies have traditionally been 
framed as primarily driven by consumer choices and actions (Freudenreich & 
Schaltegger, 2020)​.  
Rather than solely focusing on this aspect, businesses have a great potential 
for becoming enablers of sustainable consumption among consumer by adopting 
sufficiency-driven strategies (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020)​. The investigation of 
companies’ role within this paradigm is accelerating in the literature through the 
development of sufficiency-driven business model frameworks (Beulque et al., 2023; 
Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Bocken et al., 2022; Bocken & Short, 2016; Freudenreich 
& Schaltegger, 2020; Garcia-Ortega et al., 2023; Gossen & Kropfeld, 2022; Niessen & 
Bocken, 2021b; Tunn et al., 2019).  
A sufficiency-driven business is seen as two-fold (Bocken & Short, 2016; 
Gossen & Kropfeld, 2022). On one side, the business should focus on satisfying 
genuine needs instead of promoting unnecessary wants, creating conditions that 
enable consumers to adopt a sufficient level of consumption (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 
2022; Bocken & Short, 2016). On the other hand, the consumer plays a pivotal role, as 
the ultimate decision to purchase or abstain is within their control. 
 The business must shape the environment as well as their offerings to 
facilitate alternative consumption patterns based on slowing resource loops 
(Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). This involves increased focus on durability, 
repairability, and longevity in the product design phase as well as offering services 
that prolong the use phase of the product and in that way encourage 
sufficiency-driven behaviour (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). Meaning that the 
main aim of a sufficiency-driven business is to make products that last and allow 
19 of 119 
==PAGE:21==
 
users to hold on to them as long as possible through high levels of service (Bocken & 
Short, 2016).  
 
Figure 5: The role of sufficiency in the circular economy illustrated through a modified version of 
the butterfly diagram by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation (2021). 
 
Figure 5 illustrates the role of sufficiency within the circular economy. Reflecting its 
twofold nature, businesses influence consumption indirectly by implementing 
circular practices such as sharing, maintaining, reusing, and redistributing. In 
contrast, consumers directly impact consumption by refusing or reducing the 
acquisition of newly produced products and thereby decreasing the input of 
resources into the circular economy.  
Some may argue and ask why recycling is not included as a sufficiency 
strategy. Recycling is tricky because it positions itself right on the border of 
sufficiency. Recycling can ensure that when a product reaches the end of its life, the 
material resources are not sent to landfills or incinerated but are instead reused as 
input in the production of new products. Thus, recycling contributes to reducing the 
demand for new resources and helps lower the environmental impacts associated 
with purchasing new products. However, it does not enable extended use of the 
product and prevent consumer's need for new purchases. Therefore, recycling cannot 
be considered a sufficiency-driven strategy. This also applies to the strategies, 
refurbishment, and remanufacture, as they also do not necessarily entail reduced 
consumption but rather focus on reduced resource use as products are made into 
20 of 119 
==PAGE:22==
 
other products. Nonetheless, they should all still be regarded as an important option 
when a product ultimately reaches the end of its lifecycle. 
 
Several frameworks for sufficiency-driven business models and strategies have been 
developed in the literature (Beulque et al., 2023; Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Bocken 
et al., 2022; Bocken & Short, 2016; Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020; Garcia-Ortega et 
al., 2023; Gossen & Kropfeld, 2022; Niessen & Bocken, 2021b; Tunn et al., 2019). These 
frameworks aim to provide a comprehensive understanding of the essential 
components required for businesses to operate within the sufficiency principles. The 
exploratory literature review revealed roughly two types of frameworks: frameworks 
that address characteristics of a sufficiency-driven business and frameworks 
containing specific sufficiency business strategies. The content of these frameworks 
is elaborated on in the next sections.  
3.2.1.​
Characterising a sufficiency-driven business 
Bocken et al. (2022) have developed a framework based on an analysis of 150 
companies that are engaging in sufficiency strategies. Her framework draws upon 
Doughnut Economics Action Lab’s (DEAL’s) work, which advocates for a radical shift 
in economic thinking (Bocken et al., 2022). To derive the core elements of a 
sufficiency-driven business, the framework of Bocken et al. has been modified and 
the content merged with insights from Beyeler and Jaeger-Erben (2022) and 
Freudenreich and Schaltegger (2020) (Figure 6). The framework consists of seven core 
elements for a sufficiency-driven business: Purpose, Ownership, Governance, 
Finance, Network, Impact and Scale-up.

A key aspect of a sufficiency-driven business is the alignment of the organisation’s 
overall purpose, which includes both social and environmental objectives (Bocken 
2022). Additionally, the business has to differentiate between products/services that 
satisfy basic human needs for a good life and products/services that create new 
consumer wants (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022).   
To obtain this alignment of purpose throughout the organisation, it is 
advantageous to have a high degree of autonomy in the ownership structure, enabling 
flexibility for inducing the social and environmental values in the organisation’s 
practices (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Bocken et al., 2022).  
Additionally, active development and participation in networks relevant for 
operating a sufficiency-driven business is essential for success (Bocken et al., 2022). 
The financial approach of a sufficiency-driven organisation must reflect their 
commitment to slow growth and have a long-term impact (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 
2022; Bocken et al., 2022).  
This financial philosophy is closely tied to the process of scaling up the 
organisation, as it is crucial that growth does not compromise with the overall 
purpose (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022) leading to growth being tied to expanding 
market segments rather than units sold (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). To 
safeguard these principles, it is essential that the business measures and documents 
its impact on society and the environment (Bocken et al., 2022).  
In regard to governance structure, Bocken et al. (2022) found conventional 
governance structures effective for sufficiency-driven businesses, while Beyeler & 
Jaeger-Erben (2022) find it beneficial to integrate purpose and values into the control 
mechanism of the organisation.

Essential for a sufficiency-driven business is adopting a holistic and inclusive 
approach that engages all actors involved. Vital competencies to obtain are the ability 
to create transparency and foster a knowledge-sharing culture across the entire value 
chain. Additionally, companies must learn to use continuous feedback and learning 
cycles to adapt to the new way of operating a business. The involvement of various 
stakeholders can strengthen and refine the company’s practices ´(Beyeler & 
Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Kropfeld & Reichel, 2021). 
It is equally essential to establish a social norm that underscores the value of 
nature and social well-being. These core values must be represented in all practices 
and commitments. Leading to companies needing to embrace patience as a core 
principle. Results may not be immediate, but fostering patience ensures sustained 
efforts, continuous improvement, and long-term perspectives, which are needed in 
order to shift to a sufficiency-driven circular economy (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; 
Kropfeld & Reichel, 2021).

Observed is also a general over-representation in the literature of strategies in

3.2.3.​
Reflections and identified gaps 
A particularly noteworthy observation during our review of case studies on 
sufficiency-driven business models was the difficulty in categorising these businesses 
as truly sufficiency-driven. Many of the companies used as case studies have 
incorporated sufficiency strategies but are simultaneously promoting increased 
consumption through activities such as discount offers, Black Friday campaigns, and 
other sales incentives (e.g., Eileen Fisher, Decathlon, VAUDE, and Patagonia). These 
practices undermine the overarching goal of sufficiency, raising questions about 
whether these companies are truly sufficiency-driven. 
While the existing literature seems to align on the theoretical definition of 
sufficiency-driven business models, the case studies cited often fail to meet this 
28 of 119 
==PAGE:30==
 
definition. To clarify what constitutes a sufficiency-driven business, we argue that 
certain business activities are inherently incompatible with sufficiency strategies. 
Moreover, sufficiency must be implemented holistically across the organisation. It is 
essential that a sufficiency strategy is not treated as an isolated initiative but is 
integrated as a mindset in the whole organisation and reflected in all business activities.  
The discrepancy between the aligned theoretical definition of sufficiency-driven 
business models in the literature and the used case studies that fail to exemplify these 
models fully in practice underscores the novel and primarily conceptual status of 
sufficiency-driven businesses, with very few practical examples. This finding is also 
supported by the literature (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). Companies focusing on 
sufficiency appear to cater to a niche market with a small and specific customer base 
(Jungell-Michelsson & Heikkurinen, 2022). This suggests a gap between the existing 
conceptual frameworks and their practical implementation in businesses.

4.1.​
Systematic Literature Review methodology

consistency and comprehensiveness in the search of relevant studies and hereby 
ensure the inclusion of relevant studies within the topic under investigation

research strategies used to retrieve the results are disclosed so the SLR can be 
replicated by other peers (Biolchini et al., 2005) see Appendix 1: SLR protocol.

Studies suggest that knowledge and concern about environmental challenges 
are not sufficient to drive action (Jacobs et al., 2018; Park & Lin, 2020; Skinner et al., 
2021). This barrier is also referred to as the attitude-behaviour gap, which highlights 
that concerns and knowledge about environmental impacts do not always translate 
into action, revealing a disconnect between intentions and behaviours (Blas Riesgo et 
al., 2023; Joyner Armstrong et al., 2016; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015).  
This paradox is supported by the recurring barrier of low prioritisation of 
environmental considerations compared to other personal values and needs (SN1) 
(Hewitt et al., 2023; Jacobs et al., 2018; Kovacs, 2021; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015; 
Seock et al., 2024; Skinner et al., 2021).  
Environmental concerns are often overshadowed by factors such as perceived 
quality of life, economic prosperity, material needs, and self-expression (SN2) 
(Kleinhückelkotten & Neitzke, 2019; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). Particularly in 
affluent countries, clothing consumption is driven by socio-psychological needs like 
self-expression and social status rather than functional needs such as warmth and 
coverage. For many consumers, consumption of clothing fulfils hedonistic desires 
and is closely related to feelings of pleasure (Kleinhückelkotten & Neitzke, 2019; 
Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015) and social connectedness (McNeill & Venter, 2019). This 
phenomenon is sometimes referred to as “shopping well-being,” underscoring the 
complexity of clothing consumption, which extends beyond utility to include 
33 of 119 
==PAGE:35==
 
relational and emotional dimensions (Kleinhückelkotten & Neitzke, 2019; Ozdamar 
Ertekin & Atik, 2015). Several studies show that consumers who adopt sustainable 
clothing practices enjoy the treasure hunt for unique items and see it as a way to 
express themselves as environmentally conscious while seeking to stand out with 
unique clothing items (Han & Sweet, 2021; Liu et al., 2024; McNeill & Venter, 2019; Park 
& Lin, 2020).  
Furthermore, a barrier frequently mentioned is that current social norms 
favour fast fashion and hinder the adoption of sustainable clothing consumption (Das 
et al., 2021; Hewitt et al., 2023; Hur, 2020; Joyner Armstrong et al., 2016; Kovacs, 2021; 
Maguire & Fahy, 2023; McNeill & Venter, 2019; Seock et al., 2024; Skinner et al., 2021; 
Tunn et al., 2019; Zhang & Hale, 2022). The pressure to conform to fast fashion’s 
rapidly changing trends reinforces the social norm that “new is better" (SN3) (Das et 
al., 2021; Joyner Armstrong et al., 2016; Kovacs, 2021; McNeill & Venter, 2019; Seock et 
al., 2024; Skinner et al., 2021; Zhang & Hale, 2022). Several studies found that 
consumers perceive a risk of social status loss when engaging in sustainable 
consumption practices. For example, consumers frequently associate second-hand 
clothing or sharing systems with feelings of embarrassment, insecurity, and lower 
social status (Hewitt et al., 2023; Hur, 2020). Additionally, Maguire and Fahy (2023) 
found that consumers feel reluctant to repeat garment wear, as the current social 
norm is not to wear the same clothes too often (SN3). (Maguire & Fahy, 2023) 
Studies also address the challenge of a lack of emotional attachment to 
clothing among consumers (SN4). This is a key barrier, as emotional attachment plays 
a critical role in promoting sustainable practices, as it works as a driver for care and 
prolonged use of clothing items (Han & Sweet, 2021; Liu et al., 2024; McNeill & Venter, 
2019; Zhang & Hale, 2022). A lack of emotional attachment has the potential to fuel a 
‘use and dispose’ mindset where clothing is viewed as short-lived items, intensifying 
social, environmental, and economic challenges in the fashion industry.  
This barrier is compounded by a lack of incentives to act responsibly, as many 
consumers question the value of their engagement when they feel others are not 
making similar efforts (SN5) (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020; West et al., 2021). This 
barrier is important to solve, as studies show that self-efficacy, namely, the belief that 
individual sustainable actions and practices contribute positively to environmental 
protection, is vital for consumers to engage in sustainable practices (Liu et al., 2024; 
Park & Lin, 2020)​. Unfortunately, studies report that consumers experience a lack of 
this type of self-efficacy, which reduces their motivation to engage in sustainable 
consumption (Hewitt et al., 2023; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015; Zhang & Hale, 2022).  
Another important driver frequently mentioned in literature is the importance of 
self-transcendence values. Strong biospheric and altruistic values are both drivers for 
sustainable clothing practices (Han & Sweet, 2021; Jacobs et al., 2018; Jung et al., 
2020).

building a sense of community among individuals who share an interest in 
sustainable clothing practices can be particularly effective (Han & Sweet, 2021; 
McNeill & Venter, 2019; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015; West et al., 2021). 
This driver aligns with Liu et al. (2024), who also put emphasis on the power of 
social influencers, such as celebrities and social media bloggers, to drive a change of 
social norms. They can inspire and normalise sustainable clothing practices, making 
them more widely accepted and adopted.

Lack of knowledge and awareness among consumers is also a significant barrier in

practices across acquisition, use, and disposal (KT1) (Chhabra et al., 2022; Joyner 
Armstrong et al., 2016; Kreuzer et al., 2019; Maguire & Fahy, 2023; Ozdamar Ertekin &

One of the fundamental barriers lies in the dichotomy of promoting sufficiency while 
operating within a profit-orientated economic system (CP1) (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 
2015). The traditional focus on profit expansion conflicts with the sufficiency 
approach, which prioritises long-term sustainability over short-term profits. Many 
companies struggle to reconcile these opposing goals (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). ​
​
Sufficiency-driven businesses often encounter higher costs associated with 
implementing sufficiency principles (CP2). For example, bulk orders conflict with 
customised and on-demand production, leading to higher transportation and 
shipping costs per unit compared to mass production models (Rathinamoorthy & 
Prathiba Devi, 2021). Additionally, more environmentally friendly materials, 
packaging, and eco-labelling further contribute to sufficiency being cost-intensive 
(Rathinamoorthy & Prathiba Devi, 2021). ​
​
On top of this, sufficiency-driven companies report a limited customer 
demand for sufficiency-driven services, such as rental, resell, or repair offerings 
(Gossen & Heinrich, 2021). In line with this, a notable paradox is that 
sufficiency-driven companies often operate within niche markets, which can 
paradoxically limit their long-term impact. While sufficiency-orientated marketing 
today can be used as a unique selling point and a competitive advantage for 
pioneering companies, this advantage may diminish if more companies adopt similar 
approaches and make it harder to differentiate themselves (CP3) (Gossen & Heinrich, 
2021).​
​
Another barrier is related to sufficiency-driven companies' networks and 
external relations. According to Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik (2015), sufficiency-driven 
businesses often lack institutional legitimacy and support from key stakeholders, 
who have the potential to act as change agents (CP4). This lack of support poses a 
significant obstacle to establishing the credibility and influence needed to drive 
widespread adoption of sufficiency strategies in companies, which further restricts 
the scalability of these business models (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015).  
Therefore, Freudenreich & Schaltegger (2020) highlight the current economic 
system as a key barrier, which fails to provide systemic incentives for companies to 
adopt sufficiency-driven business models (CP5). Without stronger incentives or 
regulatory frameworks, companies may continue prioritising short-term profits over 
long-term sustainability.

4.2.3.​
Research gaps and recommended future research 
The investigation of the concept of sufficiency and its barriers to implementation 
shows that consumer engagement is vital for operationalising sufficiency-driven 
business practice in reality. Greater knowledge and awareness are crucial but not 
enough. The attitude-behaviour gap presents a major obstacle, resulting in 
consumers often prioritising individualistic and hedonistic values over altruistic 
values and environmental concerns.  
In order to overcome the identified barriers that hinder adoption of 
sufficiency-driven business models, the consumer must perceive sustainable 
consumption as more accessible, trustworthy, and financially equitable. Efforts to 
reduce economic constraints, improve transparency, and change prevailing social 
norms are critical to forster a widespread adoption of sustainable consumption 
practices among consumers.  
From the company perspective, the literature review shows that larger 
systemic changes are required if more companies should adopt sufficiency-driven 
business models. It is crucial to foster institutional support and regulatory 
frameworks that incentivise companies to focus on long-term sustainability instead 
of mass production or short-term profit to change the dominant fast fashion 
paradigm. Additionally, raising customer awareness and demand through innovative 
strategies is important. 
 The complexity of these challenges highlights the need for further research. 
This review has revealed a research gap related to research on barriers to adoption of 
specific sufficiency strategies from a consumer perspective and especially from a 
company perspective, where the current research field is very limited. Furthermore, 
we recommend investigating whether sufficiency-driven strategies effectively lead to 
reduced overconsumption and changes in consumption behaviour.

DESCRIPTIVE STUDY I 
5.​ PROBLEM DEFINITION 
The following section defines the scope of the problem space and presents the 
problem statement to be addressed. Its purpose is to establish a clear understanding 
of the problem definition and outline the objectives to be achieved. 
5.1.​
Research scope definition 
As earlier illustrated in Figure 5 sufficiency is centred around the concept of slowing 
resource loops through prolonging strategies such as maintenance, sharing, and 
reuse. These strategies can be further categorised into those that involve a change of 
user, such as sharing and reuse, and strategies that do not require a change of user, 
such as maintenance, e.g., repairing one’s existing clothing. 
In designing a framework to support the development of sufficiency-driven 
business models in the apparel industry, it was chosen to focus on sufficiency 
strategies that enable consumers to reduce consumption specifically through 
practices that prolong garment use without a change of user, abbreviated to PWCU 
(prolong without change of user).  
PWCU refers to practices that extend the use phase of clothing within an 
individual’s existing wardrobe. This approach aims to reduce the need for new 
purchases by sustaining or increasing the perceived value of owned garments. 
Strategies involving a change of ownership, such as sharing or reuse, are therefore 
excluded from this scope. PWCU directly challenges the linear consumption model as 
it shifts the focus from purchasing new items to maintaining what one already owns. 
This makes it a compelling alternative to fast fashion and the societal norms that 
drive frequent clothing acquisition, and an interesting aspect to focus on. 
However, achieving sufficiency in clothing consumption through PWCU 
requires decoupling the socio-psychological needs from the acquisition of new 
garments, enabling these needs to be met with the existing clothing in one’s 
wardrobe. Figure 8 illustrates the user journey of consuming clothes today compared 
to consuming clothes based on a PWCU approach.

Figure 8 illustrates how consuming clothes through the PWCU approach sets new 
requirements for the production of garments and challenges the current linear 
consumption and production practices in the fashion industry. In a webinar 
published by the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (Nielsen, 2024) PhD

due to wear and tear, 28% to poor fit, and 35% to a loss in perceived value. In order to 
succeed with PWCU, the fashion industry needs to rethink how it fulfils consumers 
socio-phylogical needs of social belonging and individual self-expression. Garments 
need to be designed with high durability for extended wear and need to be adaptable 
to changes in the consumer's body shape and taste throughout their life.

5.2.​
The problem statement 
The remainder of this report will focus on developing tangible business strategies for 
the apparel industry that support consumers in achieving sufficiency-driven clothing 
consumption through PWCU practices, with the following problem statement to 
guide the process: 
 
How to develop business strategies for the apparel industry that support consumers in 
achieving sufficiency-driven clothing consumption through prolong practices without 
change of user? 
5.3.​
Investigation of consumption patterns 
To make PWCU strategies successful in engaging consumers, it is essential to address 
the core reasons for why we use clothes and what makes fast fashion so appealing. 
To gain a deeper understanding of consumers current consumption patterns 
and the reasons why consumers are buying clothing, the knowledge and data 
obtained from the research clarification were supplemented with data gathered 
directly from consumers through an online survey. 
5.3.1.​
Survey methodology 
Surveys are an effective method for collecting larger quantities of quantitative data 
and were thus selected for this purpose. The survey consisted of 21 questions divided 
into six themes: 1) introduction with questions of age, gender, residence, and income; 
2) shopping habits and patterns; 3) the clothing in one’s wardrobe, including 
characteristics of both inactive and active wear; 4) the respondent’s experience with 
tailor services; 5) if the respondent has experience with tailor services, they are asked 
what they gain from using it. If the respondent has no experience, they are asked 
what they would need in order to use such services; 6) communication about prolong 
services. Denmark was chosen as the region of interest, and the survey was therefore 
drawn up in Danish. It was shared through various online platforms (multiple 
Facebook groups and LinkedIn) to increase diversity among the participants. A

5.3.2.​
Consumption behaviour 
The survey revealed that approximately half of the respondents purchase clothing 
1–5 times every six months, while one third buys clothes 1-3 times every three 
months. These purchases include both new clothing and secondhand clothing. The 
top five motivations for buying clothes, according to the survey, are:  
1. “I would like to renew my wardrobe." (61.9%) 
2. “I buy new clothes because my clothes are worn out.” (56%) 
3. “It makes me happy to buy clothes.” (38.1%) 
4. “I buy clothes for special occasions.” (36.9%) 
5. “I buy clothes very spontaneously.” (32.1%) 
 
The survey further examined the parameters consumers value when purchasing 
clothes. This question was asked in two rounds: 1) allowing the respondents to select 
all relevant parameters, and 2) asking them to prioritise the three most important 
ones. 
 
In the first round, the following parameters emerged as the most valued ones: 
1. “The clothes match my personal taste/style.” (90.5%) 
2. “The clothes are of good quality.” (77.4%) 
3. “I will use the clothes many times.” (76.2%) 
4. “The clothes have a good fit.” (70.2%) 
 
Whereas the second round, focusing on prioritisation, revealed another sequence:  
1. “The clothes match my personal taste/style.” (72.6%) 
2. “I will use the clothes many times.” (57.1%) 
3. “The clothes are of good quality.” (54.8%) and “The clothes have a good fit.” 
(54.8%) 
 
Interestingly, while good quality is highly valued when all parameters are considered, it 
is deprioritised when consumers select their top three factors. In contrast, the number 
of expected use times as well as a good fit gain promenance. The parameter of expression 
of personal taste/style remains the most critical factor throughout. Two additional 
parameters—whether the clothing is secondhand and whether the clothing is 
inexpensive—are also noted as significant by the respondents. 
 
46 of 119 
==PAGE:48==
 
Roughly 92% of the respondents answer that they have inactive clothing (clothing 
they never or rarely use) in their wardrobe. Among these, 25% estimated that 50% of 
their wardrobe is inactive, another 25% estimated that 10%, and the largest group 
(40%) estimated that one-third of their wardrobe is inactive. 
Notably, a fairly high percentage of the respondents emphasise the importance 
of expecting many use times as an important parameter for purchasing new clothing. 
This appears somewhat contradictory, given the substantial proportion of inactive 
clothing in the respondents’ wardrobes, suggesting a discrepancy between purchasing 
intentions and actual usage behaviour.  
 
The respondents were further asked, “What characterises the clothes in your wardrobe 
that you rarely or never use?” These findings substantiate the reasons for purchasing 
clothes. The characteristics include:  
1. “I no longer find the clothes attractive or in line with my style.” (60.8%) 
2. “The clothes are for special occasions (e.g., ball gowns or suits).” (57.1%) 
3. “The clothes don’t fit well (poor fit).” (48.8%) 
4. “The clothes no longer fit me.” (36.9%) 
5. “I forget what clothes I have.” (34.5%) 
 
The reasons for purchasing clothes are additionally closely linked to the characteristics 
respondents identified for the clothing in their wardrobe that they are actively and 
often wearing. These include a good fit (89.3%), alignment with one’s personal taste as 
well as comfort to wear (81%), and high quality (41.7%).  
 
In summary, the findings highlight that clothing that matches with one’s personal style, 
offers a good fit, and, to a lesser extent, demonstrates high quality are critical 
parameters. They are strongly influencing the purchasing moment and the use phase of 
the garment, underscoring their importance in designing strategies for increased 
clothing utilisation. 
5.3.3.​
Perception and use of prolong strategies 
An additional part of the survey was the investigation into consumers current 
perception of and engagement with various prolong strategies.  
The respondents were asked which actions (if any) they were taking to 
prolong the lifetime of their clothes. 15.5% responded that they didn’t do anything 
actively to extend the lifetime. The majority answered that they were aware of how 
they best care for the clothes when washing (65.5%), that they do small repairs on the 
clothing (65.5%) or alter the clothes (28.6%) themselves or with help from family or 
47 of 119 
==PAGE:49==
 
friends (e.g., mend a hole), and that they take care of the clothes when wearing them 
(44%). A very limited number of respondents were utilising paid services (ca. 7%). 
 
A part of the survey focused solely on tailor services as a specific prolong strategy 
that is currently offered. Well above half of the respondents had never used tailor 
services (57.1%), and just below 20% had only used the services 1-2 times. The two 
main reasons stated were.  
-​
“It is too expensive.” (50%) 
-​
“I repair it myself or get help from family and friends.” (48.6%) 
 
And a quarter of the respondents answered:  
-​
“I am unsure how it works (e.g., regarding price, interaction, process).” (27%) 
-​
“I have never considered the possibility of going to a tailor.” (27%) 
-​
“My clothes don’t break often.” (27%) 
-​
“My clothes aren’t worth repairing.” (24.3%) 
 
This is substantiated by the fact that lower prices (53.1%) and knowledge about how 
to use a tailor (45.1%) will weigh positively in increasing the use of tailor services. 
Easier accessibility to tailor services, e.g., short distance (29.6%), pickup and delivery 
(25.4%), and services connected to one’s working place (24%) would increase the use 
according to the respondents. Additionally, the option for redesign (25.4%) could 
increase the use of tailor services.  
 
In summary, the findings highlight that lower prices for repair services and greater 
awareness and knowledge about repair services among consumers are crucial. 
5.3.4.​
Reflection of the method 
The sample size was not entirely representative, as it exhibited a gender bias (with 
77.4% of the respondents being women), and no individuals below age 18 and above 
age 75 were among the respondents.  
The objective of gathering a larger sample size was relatively easily achieved 
through the survey method. To facilitate the collection of quantitative data, 
pre-defined answer options were developed for each question with the option of 
adding ‘other’. While this format provides structure and ease of analysis, it also 
introduces potential biases. A downside to this format is that respondents might 
select answers that feel closest to their perspectives rather than perfectly matching 
them, potentially skewing the data. Significant effort was invested in designing a 
broad and inclusive range of options to mitigate this risk. On the other hand, a 
predefined question can prompt respondents to consider aspects they might not have 
48 of 119 
==PAGE:50==
 
otherwise reflected on, thereby enriching the data. As such, this format carries 
advantages and disadvantages.  
Additionally, it was chosen to focus on one type of prolong strategy in the last 
part of the survey, namely tailor services. This choice was made because tailoring is 
considered one of the most prevalent for business purposes. The goal was to explore 
consumer engagement with prolongation activities and identify the barriers 
preventing participation in existing offerings. However, broadening the scope to 
include less widespread activities, such as asking about consumers use of brands 
that, e.g., offer lifetime warranties, might have provided the analysis with a more 
nuanced understanding of the barriers that consumers face. Further an expanded 
focus might have captured activity-specific barriers.

6.​ CONCEPTUALISATION 
 
This section describes how the conceptualisation and development of the support 
were carried out and presents the first version of the support. The conceptualisation 
and development of the support were carried out in two separate developing 
processes: 1) development of a sufficiency-driven business model canvas 
(abbreviated as the SUFFit BMC), and 2) development of PWCU-specific strategies for 
the apparel industry (abbreviated as the SUFFit strategy cards). The following section 
is divided into two parts, reflecting the two development processes.  
6.1.​
Development of the SUFFit BMC  
Revealed in the explorative literature review, there seems to be a discrepancy between 
the aligned theoretical definition of sufficiency-driven business models in the literature 
and the used case studies, which fail to fully exemplify these models in practice (cf. 
Section 3.2.3). To clarify what elements and characteristics constitute a 
sufficiency-driven business and provide these as practical guidance for companies, a 
business model canvas for sufficiency-driven businesses was developed. The SUFFit 
BMC is an adaptation of the traditional Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2010), 
which is widely known and frequently used to guide business model development 
(Osterwalder et al., 2010). By building upon this familiar structure, the aim is to ease the 
adoption of the SUFFit BMC within companies. 
The adaptation involved incorporating the identified key characteristics that 
define a sufficiency-driven business, found in the literature review on sufficiency and 
frameworks for sufficiency-driven businesses (cf. Section 3.2.1, Figures 6 and 7). As 
these characteristics are not industry-specific, the SUFFit BMC is aimed to be 
applicable to companies across various sectors. 
The developed SUFFit BMC is presented in Figure 9 (Appendix 06: 
Sufficiency-driven business model canvas). The new elements added to the original 
Business Model Canvas are highlighted in red, whereas the aspects covered in the 
element Ecological and social impacts overlap with elements in the Sustainable 
Business Model Canvas (CASE, n.d.).  
 
52 of 119 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
Figure 9: The developed sufficiency-driven business model canvas (SUFFit BMC). 
 
The purpose of a sufficiency-driven business is to limit resource use through 
moderating or reducing consumption while still delivering value to customers 
(Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Bocken et al., 2022). This differentiates significantly 
from a conventional business model, which is often solely centred around maximising 
financial profits. To emphasise this shift, a new Purpose element has been added. The 
Purpose element should help to define why the company exists and consider what 
social norms, visions, and values are vital to establish in the company to support the 
overall purpose. This is the foundation of the business model, and therefore we 
suggest it be the element to use as the starting point.  
Additionally, the element Ownership has been added. A strong alignment 
between the owners’ values and the company’s overall purpose can be a large driver 
and support and can play a fundamental role in ensuring that the company remains 
committed to its sufficiency-driven purpose over time (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; 
Bocken et al., 2022). This element, Ownership, shall encourage companies to reflect on 
their ownership structure, how owners are selected, and whether their values align 
with the company’s purpose.  
53 of 119 
==PAGE:55==
 
Further, a subsection, Key Partners Values, is included in Key Partners, 
ensuring alignment in values and purpose for all stakeholders involved. All 
companies depend on partners for suppliers, distribution, and complementary 
services, but for a sufficiency-driven business, they must be carefully chosen to 
support the company’s purpose (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Bocken et al., 2022). 
For instance, working with suppliers that offer full transparency, take-back 
programmes, high quality, or value ethical labour practices will reinforce the 
company’s sufficiency objectives.   
Furthermore, it is essential for a company to understand what social meanings 
and competencies are necessary to establish for the consumer in order to achieve a 
behavioural change towards sufficiency-driven consumption (Beyeler & 
Jaeger-Erben, 2022; Kropfeld & Reichel, 2021). Therefore, two new subsections, Social 
Meanings and Competencies, are integrated into Customer Segments. These elements 
should help companies gain a comprehensive understanding of the social norms, 
values, behaviours, and competencies that define their specific customer segments. 
Recognising these characteristics allows companies to design products and services 
that resonate with their targeted customers.   
Another fundamental change from the conventional business mindset is the 
shift in value proposition. A sufficiency-driven business model does not deliver value 
by selling more but by enabling consumers to consume better and less (Bocken & 
Short, 2016; Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). To emphasise this shift in mindset, an 
extra question is integrated in Value Proposition to support considerations of what 
value the company delivers to their customer and how they ensure it aligns with the 
overall purpose of enabling reduced consumption. 
To operate effectively, a sufficiency-driven business must secure the right 
types of resources to support its Value Proposition and Key Activities. However, these 
resources should be managed in a way that aligns with the objectives of sufficiency 
(Bocken et al., 2022). To ensure this, two extra questions are integrated in Key 
Ressources to support companies in identifying the resources critical to their 
operations and encourage reflection on how these can be acquired without producing 
new goods.  
Common for the sufficiency-driven business mentioned in literature is a 
customer relationship built on shared values and a high degree of customer 
engagement (Bocken & Short, 2016; Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). To emphasise 
this, two questions are integrated in Customer Relationships to support companies in 
considering how to foster a high degree of brand loyalty. The company should try to 
encourage a sense of community between themselves and the consumer, driven by 
shared environmental and social values.  
Another key characteristic of sufficiency-driven business models is that 
scaling is primarily achieved by broadening the customer segment rather than 
54 of 119 
==PAGE:56==
 
increasing the number of units sold per customer (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020). 
To ensure that the company considers this shift, a new element, Scale-up is 
integrated. This element encourages companies to define their growth strategy and 
discuss how to avoid that growth leads to compromising their overall sufficiency 
objectives.  
This focus is further emphasised in the extra questions integrated in the 
elements Cost Structure and Revenue Streams which should help companies assess 
the most significant costs inherent and generate revenue while maintaining their 
commitment to reduced consumption. 
Adapted from the Sustainable Business Model Canvas (CASE, n.d.), the element 
Ecological and Social Impacts is included to assess the company's total impact. This 
element ensures that companies actively assess both the ecological and social effects 
of their business model. It encourages businesses to measure and reduce absolute 
resource use, identify potential rebound effects, and implement impact assessment 
methods such as life cycle analysis and social responsibility standards. 
Lastly, no extra questions are added to the element Key Activities and 
Channels, as the existing ones are adequate, but the answer to the questions will 
differ from those of a conventional business. Key Activities of a sufficiency-driven 
business tend to be more service-orientated, as they rather focus on product 
longevity and product service systems than product volume (Beyeler & Jaeger-Erben, 
2022; Bocken et al., 2022). And instead of promoting impulse consumption, channels 
should be designed to educate, inspire, and facilitate sufficient consumer behaviours.  
 
The additional elements incorporated into the traditional BMC reflect the significant 
number of new considerations that businesses face in the transition to a 
sufficiency-driven business. These additions underscore the complexity and 
commitment required to shift from a traditional profit-centred business model to a 
sufficiency-driven business approach. The SUFFit BMC illustrates how the integration 
of sufficiency objectives must be embraced by all aspects of the company and 
requires a larger degree of purpose-orientation both internal in the company and 
across the value chain.

Ideation process 
To structure the ideation process of PWCU strategies and foster a creative mindset 
where ideas could flow freely, an initial brainstorm session was performed, following 
a silent brainstorming approach (TWOWP, 2019). Each researcher spent a set amount 
of time to individually and silently generate as many ideas for PWCU strategies as 
possible concerning one ideation creation at a time. By allowing each researcher to 
brainstorm silently, we aimed to ensure that ideas were captured without the 
influence of the other researcher’s opinions and minimise the risk of groupthinking. 
After each silent brainstorming session, the PWCU strategies were presented, 
followed by a group discussion and collaborative brainstorming to further expand the 
collection of PWCU strategies. See Appendix 08: Ideation brainstorm output for a 
collection of all ideas. 
After this initial brainstorming session, unfeasible ideas were excluded, and 
similar ideas were merged and refined through a structured process. Each idea was 
discussed through an iterative process that included desk research to enhance and 
validate the ideas, resulting in further development of the ideas into more tangible 
and final PWCU strategies. See Appendix 09: Refined strategy ideas.  
Finalising strategy card deck 
Inspired by the circular economy business cards (ready2LOOP, n.d.-a), the PWCU 
strategies were turned into a card format, and the deck of cards was named SUFFit 
Strategy Cards. The cards were categorised based on which phase of the garment's 
lifecycle they aimed to influence: production, acquisition, or use. Additionally, some 
PWCU strategies were found to focus more on how to promote and communicate the 
objectives of sufficiency. The strategies were collected in a new category, named 
marketing strategies. The final card deck containing all ideated PWCU strategies is 
further explained in Section 6.3.2.

6.2.2.​
Discussion of the assessments 
The level of innovation varies across the different strategies. Within all 
categories—production, acquisition, use, and marketing—there are strategies with 
high, moderate, and low innovation levels. Strategies with a low innovation level may 
already be familiar to businesses and consumers, which can be advantageous for their 
implementation, as these strategies might require less knowledge sharing and 
63 of 119 
==PAGE:65==
 
investments within the company, among customers, and across the value chain. 
Conversely, strategies with a high innovation potential may be more demanding for 
companies as they operate on the frontier of innovation, pushing new strategies into 
the market without established examples to follow. However, it also offers the 
opportunity to strengthen competitive advantage by establishing a unique selling 
point. 
When examining the other two parameters, sufficiency potential and adoption 
potential, the strategies generally score low to moderate. Only one strategy, Longevity 
in design, is evaluated as having high adoption potential. The same trends are 
observed for sufficiency potential, where only five strategies (Prolong services 
connected to consumers’ everyday whereabouts, Clothing repair café, Online DIY 
platform, Repair, alteration and redesign services and Prolong events and workshops) 
out of the total 27 strategies achieve a high sufficiency potential score. A low score in 
these parameters does not imply that the strategies should be dismissed, but rather 
highlights the importance of not relying solely on one strategy. Instead, companies 
should combine the strategies so they address each other's weaknesses in specific 
parameters. Combining the strategies is particularly critical for the four acquisition 
strategies, as they achieve low scores in both adoption potential and sufficiency 
potential.  
Another general observation is the production strategies, which tend to score 
high in adoption potential but low in sufficiency potential. E.g., the three production 
strategies Longevity in design, Durable clothing, and Repairable clothing all score high 
in adoption potential due to their ease of implementation, as they require minimal 
behavioural change from consumers. However, they score low in sufficiency 
potential, as they only meet two of the four sufficiency criteria. The opposite pattern 
is observed among the use strategies, such as Repair café and Prolong events and 
workshops, which achieve the lowest score in adoption potential but achieve the 
highest possible scores in sufficiency potential. This suggests that companies could 
benefit from combining use strategies with production strategies to maximise the 
chances of a successful adoption while also achieving a high level of sufficiency. 
Another observation is the distinction between strategies that require active 
engagement from consumers and companies during the use phase and those that 
affect the use phase more indirectly. For instance, the production strategy Clothing 
deconstructed into new designs involves active consumer participation after 
purchase, as the strategy requires them to return the garment to the company to get 
the garment redesigned. In contrast, strategies like Durable clothing or Longevity in 
design where the prolonging initiative solely takes place in the production phase and 
is indirectly influencing the use phase through design. Active strategies are 
predominantly found in the use category and among certain production strategies, 
64 of 119 
==PAGE:66==
 
while acquisition strategies and some production strategies are influencing 
consumption without continuous active engagement from the consumer.  
The strategies where the company takes responsibility for the active consumer 
participation through a service, they score higher in adoption potential than those 
that place the action for the prolong activity entirely on the consumer. For example, 
the use strategy Repair alteration and re-design service involves the company offering 
repairs as a service, whereas Repair café relies entirely on the consumer to allocate 
time and effort to repair their clothing. This contrast underscores the importance of 
company-facilitated services in driving higher adoption potential. 
6.2.3.​
Reflections on the methodology 
The assessment of the developed PWCU strategies based on the three parameters was 
an iterative process, and the methodological foundation has limitations. 
A limitation of the assessment of innovation level is that it relies on the 
researchers’ own knowledge of existing business strategies, supplemented by desk 
research. Consequently, the process is not fully robust or unbiased. To improve the 
reliability and objectivity of the innovation level assessment, future work could 
include a more comprehensive and systematic research process based on a structured 
search protocol.  
Additionally, the adoption potential is assessed using binary yes (1) or no (0) 
responses, which do not allow for nuanced scoring, as this approach limits the ability 
to distinguish between strategies that partially or strongly meet the criteria. 
Furthermore, the criteria vary in complexity; for instance, Time and Effort were 
assessed based on five parameters, while Price was assessed on three. This 
discrepancy arises because the barrier Time and Effort was more nuanced in the 
literature review on barriers than Price.  
Moreover, due to a lack of data to justify differential weighting, all criteria for 
both the assessment of the sufficiency potential and the adoption potential are 
weighted equally. Future research could investigate whether weighting some criteria 
more heavily would provide a more accurate reflection of their relative importance in 
enabling a high sufficiency potential and adoption potential.  
A general limitation of the assessment is that it is conducted without input 
from external experts or other relevant stakeholders. Future iterations of the method 
could benefit greatly from expert involvement. Engaging stakeholders from the 
apparel industry, sustainability experts, and behavioural scientists could enhance the 
robustness and credibility of the three assessments. Especially the translation of the 
general sufficiency requirements into the apparel-specific criteria used to determine 
the sufficiency potential could be relevant to review by experts.

Support (first version) 
The conceptualisation resulted in a developed support, which aims to facilitate the 
propagation of sufficiency-driven business and enhance the understanding of how 
this business approach can be applied in the apparel industry to actively reduce 
current overconsumption of clothing among consumers, thereby taking 
responsibility for the industry’s social and environmental impact.  
The support consists of two tools: a business model canvas for developing 
sufficiency-driven business models (abbriviated the SUFFit BMC) and a deck of cards 
featuring tangible PWCU strategies specifically tailored for companies operating in 
the apparel industry (abbriviated the SUFFit strategy cards). The two frameworks can 
be used together or individually.  
6.3.1.​
The SUFFit BMC 
The SUFFit BMC (Figure 11) is a strategic tool that provides a structured way to 
outline, develop, and analyse a sufficiency-driven business model. The SUFFit BMC is 
not industry-specific and can also be useful for companies that do not operate in the 
apparel industry. The SUFFit BMC framework consists of three elements: a blank 
business model canvas that participants can fill out with key notes, a canvas with 
questions and examples for elaboration on the elements and as input for the 
discussion, and a step-by-step guide to support the facilitator in how to facilitate the 
SUFFit BMC effectively.

The SUFFit Strategy cards 
The SUFFit Strategy Cards (Figure 15) are specifically designed for companies 
operating in the apparel industry. The cards can be used as a source of inspiration for 
businesses seeking to actively address the issue of overconsumption of clothing. The 
card deck consists of 36 cards, divided into four categories of PWCU strategy cards, 
each addressing a distinct area of influence: 
 
●​
Production-focused PWCU strategies (8 cards): these are represented by light 
blue cards and aim to optimise the design and manufacturing processes to 
enhance garment longevity. 
●​
Acquisition-focused PWCU strategies (4 cards): represented by dark-blue 
cards, these focus on the moment when a garment is purchased, aiming to 
encourage mindful consumption and sufficiency practices. 
●​
Use-focused PWCU strategies (14 cards): represented by green cards, these 
address how garments are maintained, repaired, redesigned, etc. during their 
use phase to extend the lifetime. 
●​
Marketing strategies (10 cards): represented by red cards, to help companies 
promote and communicate their chosen PWCU strategies effectively.  
 
The cards can be used individually or in combinations that align with the specific 
needs and goals of the company. 
​
The front side of the card features 1) the name of the PWCU strategy, 2) a 
guiding question to prompt discussion and inspire ideas, and 3) a score indicating the 
PWCU strategy's innovation level, sufficiency potential, and adoption potential. To 
further explain the meaning and the methodology behind the assessment of the 
PWCU strategies sufficiency potential and adoption potential, an enclosed 
explanation paper is attached to the card deck (Figure 16). While the back of each 
card offers practical examples of how the PWCU strategy has been implemented in 
real-world contexts. If the PWCU strategy has not yet been adopted, the card 
highlights the source of inspiration and describes the key challenges the strategy 
seeks to address.

6.3.3.​
How SUFFit BMC and SUFFit Cards interconnect 
The SUFFit BMC and the SUFFit Strategy Cards are complementary frameworks 
designed to support companies’ operations in the apparel industry to develop 
sufficiency-driven business models. The SUFFit BMC and SUFFit cards are intended 
to be used for three user scenarios: 
●​
Established companies that aim to transform their current business model into 
a sufficiency-driven model. 
●​
Start-up companies that seek to develop a new business idea based on 
sufficiency principles. 
●​
Educational settings provide students who aspire to work in the apparel 
industry with the tools and knowledge to foster sufficiency-driven practices 
 
To ensure a successful use of the support, it is recommended that the tools are used 
with the assistance of a facilitator. Since sufficiency is a novel concept, a facilitator 
plays a crucial role in guiding the process and ensuring a correct understanding of 
the frameworks. In the future, if knowledge of sufficiency becomes more widespread, 
it may be possible to use the tool without facilitation. 
 
While the two tools can be used independently, they are also designed to work 
together seamlessly as part of two distinct workshop sequences. The SUFFit BMC 
serves as a structured guide for outlining and analysing the key elements of a 
sufficiency-driven business model. While the SUFFit Strategy Cards provide targeted 
inspiration for specific elements of the SUFFit BMC by offering actionable strategies 
for sufficiency-driven practices.  
Figure 19 illustrates how the strategy cards are linked to the elements in the 
SUFFit BMC. The strategy cards focus on production, acquisition, and use and can 
directly inform elements such as Value Proposition and Key Activities. These cards 
help identify strategies for designing, producing, and maintaining garments in ways 
that promote prolonged use and reduce overconsumption.  
Meanwhile, the marketing strategy cards are particularly relevant to the 
Customer Relationships, Customer Segments and Channels elements of the SUFFit 
BMC. These cards provide ideas on how to effectively communicate and promote the 
sufficiency strategies to customers, ensuring alignment with the broader sufficiency 
objectives of the business model.

broader sufficiency objectives.

Tech along with the researchers of this project. Prior to the test, there had been 
preliminary discussions about launching a sufficiency-driven business within the 
apparel industry, and we had reached the step of outlining a potential business

solely focusing on the usefulness of the SUFFit BMC. 
The session was scheduled to last approximately three hours. Each participant

7.4.1.​
An educational setting 
In the first exercise, the Value Propositions of the case company H&M were defined to 
be the offer of being trendy and making it possible to express oneself through 
clothing as well as making the sustainable choice. They cater to both women and men 
and children and do thereby include the entire family in their Customer Segment. This 
is done through clothing sales online and in stores all over the world (‘About Us’, n.d.) 
(Key Activities). 
The exercise, including the strategy cards, was thereafter addressed. Both 
groups were addressing the task through the premis that the selected strategies 
should work within the case company’s current practices. E.g., a reasoning stated for 
not selecting the strategy Clothing with a good fit was that the group did not see it 
realistic that a good fit can be a priority when you “send out 700 collections a year,” as 
one of the students stated. The task was thereby solved by approaching the strategies 
with what resources and systems the company currently operates with and within. 
Thereby, not with an explicit focus on customer needs and how they could be 
addressed using other strategies than the ones currently utilised by H&M.

Evaluation with a start-up 
At the start of the session, each participant received a copy of the SUFFit BMC.

go through and answer all questions. As a result, it felt natural to deviate from the 
guide and first gain an overview of all the different elements and move back and forth  
to develop the actual business case. This highlights the importance of clarifying that 
the SUFFit BMC can be used in multiple sequences and iterations rather than 
following a pre-defined structure.  
​
Once it was understood that not all questions needed immediate answers, they 
became a valuable tool for sparking discussion and introducing new aspects to the 
discussion. Especially the examples served their purpose of making the elements 
more tangible very well. However, the examples and statements within certain 
elements could benefit from facilitation, emphasising that they are based on what 
characterises existing sufficiency-driven businesses and have proven to work in 
practice. While there is flexibility in how the businesses choose to approach these 
elements, the exception is for the purpose, where it remains essential for 
sufficiency-driven businesses to aim at moderating or reducing consumption.  
Beyond guiding discussion, the questions also helped identify areas requiring 
further investigation. Overall, the BMC served as a strong foundation for discussion 
rather than have to be reviewed to the letter.  
​
After exploring different potential business cases through an iterative use of 
the canvas, the BMC was used to consolidate all thoughts and ideas as well as 
summarise preliminary decisions. This final element further ensured that all relevant 
elements and aspects had been considered. The exercise resulted in a fully outlined 
business model with identified questions and aspects requiring further investigation 
in order to proceed with the development of a business.

10.​ DISCUSSION 
10.1.​
Discussion of final support  
In this section, the final support is discussed through various perspectives. Firstly, 
through the anticipated user scenarios of the final support. Followed by a discussion 
of potential rebound effects associated with the PWCU strategies and what clothing 
categories the PWCU strategies cater to. Lastly, other important actors essential for 
promoting and achieving sufficiency within the apparel industry are discussed. 
10.1.1.​
User settings 
The potential for deploying the support in various user settings has been evaluated. 
The tests showed the promising usefulness of the support. It became clear that the 
support included a two-fold purpose. Both to work as a concrete tool to use in 
business development as well as a way of communicating the objectives and mindset 
behind the novel concept sufficiency. 
However, a challenge might lie in reaching the right stakeholders and 
motivating them to use and interact with the support. As the system currently stands, 
transitioning into a sufficiency-driven business is largely motivated by strong moral 
and environmental objectives rather than financial incentives. Unlike traditional 
models that emphasise growth and profit, a sufficiency-driven business requires a 
fundamental shift in the purpose of operating a business. This entails a vital barrier in 
the support’s adoption by especially established companies that are deeply 
integrated in the linear economy, which often demands a strong financial argument in 
order to make changes. This barrier was revealed in the user testing with an 
established company, leading to the criteria 2.2 being considered partly fulfilled.  
Using only the strategy cards might be more feasible for an established company as it 
is less comprehensive. Companies can simply choose a strategy to incorporate into 
their existing business model and practices. This would not fully transform them into 
a sufficiency-driven business, but it would potentially spark a mindset change and 
engage some consumers in sufficiency-driven consumption and behaviour. 
 The two other user test settings, startups and educational settings, might be 
more open to exploring new ways of thinking and operating, and as the concept of 
sufficiency is in large contrast to current practices, these user scenarios might be the 
most suitable for an effective use of the support. Incorporating the concept of 
sufficiency into relevant education programmes could play a key role in fostering a 
new mindset on operating within the apparel industry. This could potentially 
99 of 119 
==PAGE:101==
 
encourage future professionals to rethink the traditional business approach and 
induce a transformation within the industry.  
                  Other potential use scenarios can be interesting to explore, especially 
considering how it can be possible to reach the relevant and vital stakeholders. 
Applications such as consultant services, workshops, or courses can provide a 
structured setting for introducing and discussing sufficiency-driven transitions and 
implementations. In such settings, it would be vital to emphasise the financial 
aspects and the business benefits and advantages to keep the use of the support as 
relevant as possible for businesses.  
This further opens the debate of the level of facilitation needed to ensure the 
most effective use of the support. The consideration of whether an expert in 
sufficiency is necessary to communicate and facilitate the use of the support is 
important. This would ensure correct and effective communication of the concept 
and highlight possible pitfalls, such as contradictory activities within 
sufficiency-driven business practices. However, requiring expert facilitation could 
limit the tool’s accessibility and widespread adoption, so if this is to be implemented, 
it should be ensured that the tool remains scalable and easy to use. 
10.1.2.​
Potential rebound effects 
A crucial aspect to consider when operating a sufficiency-driven business is the 
potential rebound effects of the different sufficiency strategies. Sufficiency strategies 
inherently entail self-imposed restrictions on consumption, which can lead to 
unforeseen negative effects (rebound effects) or possibly unforeseen increased 
positive effects (double sufficiency effects) (Figge et al., 2014). For instance, 
self-imposed restrictions by consumers will decrease demand, which may lead to 
lowering prices, thereby making products more attractive to other consumers and 
increasing their demand.  
Similarly, supplier-imposed restrictions can drive up prices, which can 
encourage other producers to increase production. Thereby mitigating the intended 
effect of the restrictions (Figge et al., 2014). On the contrary, the decrease in demand 
can lead to higher prices, thereby reinforcing lower consumption beyond the 
self-imposed restrictions. Lower supply can potentially lead to higher prices 
occupying more of the consumer’s purchasing power, leading to decreased 
consumption. Thus creating a double-sufficiency effect (Figge et al., 2014).  
 
The discussion of potential rebound effects of the specific sufficiency strategies 
developed for the support takes basis on the framework for rebound effects 
presented by Metic & Pigosso (2022). This includes both direct rebound effects 
100 of 119 
==PAGE:102==
 
(related to the same product/service), indirect rebound effects (related to other 
products/services), and economic-wide rebound effects.  
The strategies are, as previously mentioned, risking to inflict a direct 
sufficiency rebound effect as a result of aiming to decrease the demand for new 
clothing through self-imposed restrictions. Lower market prices can lead to increased 
consumption by other consumers, offsetting the effect. If self-imposed restrictions of 
the supply are not accompanied by a general mind shift within the consumers, the 
higher market prices will make space for other companies to overtake the supply and 
keep the consumption up.  
The strategies involving an aspect of personalisation introduce the risk of a 
symbiotic rebound effect, where one decision in regards to the selection of a strategy 
compromises the possibility of utilising another strategy (Metic & Pigosso, 2022). The 
SUFFit strategies in question are aiming at expanding the lifetime of the garment by 
increasing the user’s attachment to the garment, either through design, e.g., Engraved 
design or through personalised fitting, e.g., 3D avatar with body measurements. This 
approach might reduce, e.g., the resale value, making a potential reselling scenario 
less viable. That said, personalised alterations, especially in the case of poorly 
constructed garments, could also possibly increase its usability and be more likely to 
be worn through out its technical lifetime by one or several users.   
A symbiotic rebound effect can also arise from user-involvement strategies, 
such as, e.g., Reparation and alteration DIY kits. These require a certain level of skills, 
and failure can result in unusable garments. Secondarily, negative experiences may 
discourage users from engaging in sufficiency-driven practices again. This would lead 
to a reduction of the strategy’s effectiveness. 
The consumers are likely to save money when entering sufficiency-driven 
practices, as it stands in contrast to buying new garments. This can potentially lead 
to consumers spending the money saved on other products with an equal or higher 
environmental and social cost; this is referred to as the re-spending rebound effect 
(Metic & Pigosso, 2022). 
​
A part of the marketing strategy can be to emphasise all the environmental 
and social benefits that come with utilising these sufficiency strategies. This could 
potentially lead to a change in the consumers’ perception of the practice and result in 
an increased use of the strategy, leading to a direct motivational rebound effect, which 
is the possibility of increased consumption due to changed perception and behaviour 
from the utilisation of the strategies (Metic & Pigosso, 2022). An example is the 
strategy 3D avatar of body measurements, which allows the consumer to easily see if 
the garment actually fits one’s body when online shopping. This certainty might lead 
to a motivational rebound effect in the shape of an overuse of this practice. This is less 
likely to be a rebound effect for the strategies that use repair as an approach, as it is 
difficult to overuse repair services. An indirect motivational rebound effect can occur 
101 of 119 
==PAGE:103==
 
from the probable scenario of these strategies not being fully widespread, leading to 
increased transportation in relation to utilising the strategies.  
​
All strategies risk being subject to the rebound effect of indirect consumption 
accumulation, which is describing the scenario where the supply of these sufficiency 
strategies will solely complement existing consumption (Metic et al.). None of the 
strategies can directly restrict the consumption of new clothing, as the strategies are 
set by the companies. Therefore, there is a risk of the strategy being a supplementary 
purchase of a product or service. E.g., the strategy On-demand personalised clothing 
might not be used for the purpose of participating in frugal consumption practices 
but solely because the consumer enjoys the feature of having fitted garments.  
​
The strategies are aiming at substituting the purchase of new clothing, which 
can fully succeed or partly succeed, leading to an indirect imperfect substitution 
(Metic & Pigosso, 2022). An imperfect substitution might occur for the strategies that 
are addressing the consumer's need for self-expression and a change in style. The 
level of renewal and alteration within the strategies, e.g., Modular clothing, might not 
be enough to fully substitute purchasing a new garment. Additionally, a physical, 
imperfect substitution can occur. The strategies that incorporate an element of DIY 
(Do-It-Yourself) might lead to a lower quality of alterations and redesign, ultimately 
decreasing the lifetime of the garment, thereby imperfectly substituting the 
acquisition of new clothing or the use of professional services.  
For the strategy 3D avatar of body measurements, further potential rebound 
effects are discussed. The utilisation of the strategy will likely require a high amount 
of computer power, and as this is substituting current practices of trying on clothing 
in the stores or doing measurements oneself, this substitution might lead to an 
increased resource consumption—a substitution rebound effect (Metic & Pigosso, 
2022). Further, it is possible that this strategy increases the efficiency surrounding 
purchasing clothing, as it is likely to ease the process of selecting the right size as 
well as minimise the potential returns of purchases. The strategy then becomes 
subject to a time rebound effect, as the savings in time efficiency can lead to increased 
consumption (Metic & Pigosso, 2022).  
Considering the economy-wide effects from the strategies, the effects from 
widely implementing 3D scanning or printing might result in a significant increase in 
electricity consumption. If this becomes the new standard within production and 
repair of clothing, a rebound effect from the economy-wide structural change can 
potentially occur. This happens when a shift in production leads to a higher resource 
consumption (Metic & Pigosso, 2022). It is possible that widespread implementation of 
3D printing spare parts can lead to an increased market for 3D printers and a potential 
new market for spare parts production. Consumers might purchase spare parts not 
out of necessity from wear and tear but rather as a way to renew their wardrobe 
102 of 119 
==PAGE:104==
 
potentially creating a new fast fashion trend—a so-called new market rebound effect 
(Metic & Pigosso, 2022) (Metic et al.).  
 
The discussion above highlights a significant number of potential rebound effects 
and emphasises the importance of the element Ecological and social impact in the 
SUFFit BMC, which aims to make companies aware of these risks when implementing 
sufficiency strategies and encourage them to consider initiatives that can mitigate 
them. An overview of the PWCU strategies and their related potential rebound effects 
is to be found in Appendix 22: Rebound effects. 
10.1.3.​
Clothing categories 
Another perspective important to consider when companies have to choose among 
the different PWCU strategies is whether some strategies are more suitable for some 
clothing types (such as babywear, sportswear, occasion wear, everyday wear, etc.) 
than others.  
Most strategies are broadly applicable across the different clothing types. 
However, some strategies are more relevant to specific clothing types due to the 
specific requirements and challenges associated with those garments.  
One observation is that the strategies such as Durable clothing, Clothing with 
spare parts and Repairable clothing are particularly important for clothing types like 
workwear, sportswear, and underwear. These types of clothing are exposed to 
significant wear and tear, making durability and repairability critical for extending 
their lifetime. For instance, workwear often needs to withstand harsh conditions and 
frequent washing, while sportswear must endure intense activity, frequent sweating, 
and repeated laundering at high temperatures. Similarly, underwear, due to hygiene 
concerns, requires robust construction to ensure a longer usable life despite frequent 
washing. 
Also, clothing types such as babywear, childrenswear, and maternitywear are 
characterised by rapid changes in body measurements. Strategies targeting alteration 
or redesign, such as Clothing deconstructed into new designs, and strategies that can 
accommodate size changes or adapt to the changing needs of the wearer, such as 
Modular clothing and Clothing with spare parts are particularly suitable for these 
clothing types. Additionally, On-demand personalised clothing and 3D body 
measurement avatars may help to gain a good fit, although the benefits may be 
short-lived due to the rapid growth of children or the temporary nature of maternity 
wear. 
For occasion wear, social stigma, or the perceived risk of repeated wear is a 
critical factor. Strategies designed to mitigate this risk, such as Clothing 
deconstructed into new designs, Modular clothing, and Clothing with spare parts are 
103 of 119 
==PAGE:105==
 
particularly relevant. These strategies enable the wearer to customise, adapt, or 
transform the garment for different occasions, thereby reducing the likelihood of 
social judgement for repeated use. Additionally, marketing strategies such as 
Movement creation can further enhance the desirability of repeated wear by 
promoting positive connotations, such as sustainability or creativity, around 
re-wearing garments. 
Furthermore, some clothing categories, such as outdoor wear, occasion wear, 
and certain types of sportswear, may require specialised cleaning, care, or 
maintenance. Here strategies like Cleaning, care, and storage services, Maintenance 
products, and Prolonging services connected to consumers’ everyday whereabouts are 
especially relevant. These services can help preserve the quality and functionality of 
garments that are either delicate, exposed to extreme conditions, or challenging to 
maintain at home.  
Additionally, strategies that rely on active user engagement, such as DIY repair 
and alteration kits or Repair cafés, are broadly applicable but may be less relevant for 
clothing types with delicate, complex construction, such as underwear or highly 
technical workwear. These strategies require a certain level of repair skill from the 
user, which may limit their adoption of garments that are difficult to repair or alter 
without professional assistance. 
 
To summarise the general observation, the strategies are generally applicable across 
all clothing types. However, some stand out as more relevant for specific categories 
therefore, businesses should prioritise tailoring their choice of PWCU strategies to 
the specific needs of the clothing types they offer.  
10.1.4.​
The importance of other actors  
Transforming the current fast fashion paradigm is a complex challenge that requires 
the involvement of multiple actors (Bocken et al., 2022; Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 
2020; Iten et al., 2024; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). The influence of individual 
companies is limited, as they can enable and encourage consumers to adopt more 
frugal consumption practices but cannot force them to do so. Likewise, 
sufficiency-driven businesses cannot compel other companies to adopt a similar 
approach but only inspire and lead by example.  
 
This project has focused on exploring the barriers that prevent both consumers and 
businesses from engaging in sufficiency practices and developing tangible sufficiency 
strategies for the apparel industry. One question that arises from this challenge is 
whether the concept of sufficiency is a useful term for engaging both consumers and 
businesses in adopting reduced consumption behaviours.  
104 of 119 
==PAGE:106==
 
Our investigation shows that it is difficult to convince consumers to prioritise 
altruistic and biospheric values over their individual and hedonistic values 
(Kleinhückelkotten & Neitzke, 2019; Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015). This is further 
supported by the well-documented attitude-behaviour gap that demonstrates that 
knowledge and concerns about the environmental challenges do not necessarily lead 
to action (Ozdamar Ertekin & Atik, 2015; Ronda, 2024). We also know that clothing 
consumption often serves socio-psychological functions (e.g., identity, status) 
beyond functional needs and that consumers generally resist feeling restricted 
(Fowles, 1974) and might connect negative connotations with the term sufficiency 
(Kovacs, 2021). These challenges made us question the benefits of using the term 
sufficiency.  
As a concept, sufficiency makes sense academically, but it is difficult to 
translate effectively for consumers and businesses. Our findings indicate that the way 
sufficiency is currently framed in the literature, with its emphasis on limitation, 
enoughness, and basic needs, conflicts with current consumption patterns, which are 
largely driven by identity, status, and hedonistic values.  
This framing risks alienating both consumers and businesses and might limit 
the chance for a broader engagement with the concept. If the concept of sufficiency 
should gain more traction from consumers, companies, and other relevant 
stakeholders, we argue that it is essential that they can see themselves reflected in 
the concept. Without this alignment, sufficiency risks remaining an academic 
exercise rather than a viable strategy fit for practical implementation. 
The academic field of sufficiency has an important role to play in influencing 
how the concept of sufficiency is communicated. Reconsidering how the concept of 
sufficiency is framed may play a role in its adaptation. Instead of framing sufficiency 
with terms like enoughness or basic needs, the reframing could shift to a narrative 
about well-being, quality, flourishing, and long-term satisfaction, highlighting the 
positive experiences and benefits sufficiency-driven products and services can 
provide the consumer. This reframing may be necessary to engage and inspire more 
consumers and companies toward sufficiency-driven business models.  
With that said, a general mindset shifts from valuing hedonistic values higher 
than environmental and social objectives is crucial. As Mickey Gjerris, bioethicist and 
theologian, is discussing in the book “Mennesket og tøjet” (Danish title), it might be 
time to incorporate the important questions from the virtueetics of what kind of 
person do I want to be? He is pointing out that most people would appreciate values 
such as being respectful, considerate, and mindful of the community, and as he says, 
“It is just not that kind of people our society is creating right now. It is small rational 
egoists who are just out to take as much as possible.” Stakeholders of all kinds can 
play a role in driving this necessary mindset shift among both businesses and 
consumers (Freudenreich & Schaltegger, 2020; Iten et al., 2024; Ozdamar Ertekin & 
105 of 119 
==PAGE:107==
 
Atik, 2015), including both governmental institutions and non-governmental 
institutions.  
 
We acknowledge that reframing and hoping for a mindset shift within the population 
is insufficient. Our identified barriers show that achieving widespread adoption of 
sufficiency, particularly among businesses, requires systemic changes at a broader 
level. Today traditional metrics of success, such as GDP growth and profit margins, do 
not incentivise sufficiency-driven business models, and existing regulatory 
frameworks often favour mass production and overconsumption (Rathinamoorthy & 
Prathiba Devi, 2021). The sufficiency-driven business model and the PWCU strategies 
proposed in this project require a system that actively supports these types of 
businesses. Crucial to this is the introduction of new legislative frameworks and the 
emergence of social movements that can help slow down the accelerating pace of fast 
fashion. Policymakers and legislators have the power to establish structural market 
conditions that support sufficiency-driven business models.  
An encouraging example of how the aim of sufficiency-driven business models 
can be supported by legislative forces is the vision outlined in the EU Strategy for 
Sustainable and Circular Textiles, adopted in 2022. The strategy outlines a vision for 
the textile sector in 2030, where “fast fashion is out of fashion” and textiles are more 
durable, repairable, and recyclable (European Commission, n.d.-b). This strategy 
includes upcoming regulations that can directly benefit sufficiency-driven business 
models and support the uptake of several of the PWCU strategies developed in this 
project. 
For example, has the European Commission revised the Waste Framework 
Directive and introduced mandatory Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) 
schemes for textiles in all EU member states. EPR will require producers to take 
responsibility for the end-of-life management of their textile products (Europa 
Kommissionen, 2023). If successfully implemented, the EPR has the potential to 
encourage fashion brands to integrate sufficiency practices into their operations by 
offering consumers the options of returning garments for, e.g., repair, reuse, or 
redesign. Implementing our PWCU strategies such as Clothing deconstructed into new 
designs, Repairable clothing, Life time warranties and Repair, alteration, and redesign 
services can help fashion brands comply with this forthcoming legislation by having a 
closer relationship with their customers.  
Additionally, the European Commission is developing specific Ecodesign 
Requirements (ESPR) for textiles sold on the European market (European 
Commission, n.d.-b). While the specific requirements of the ESPR have not yet been 
published, it is certain that textile products sold on the European market will need to 
meet a range of sustainability performance and information criteria, e.g., design 
requirements for durability, reusability, and repairability (European Commission, 
106 of 119 
==PAGE:108==
 
n.d.-a). It is expected that compliance with these requirements will partly involve 
reporting through the forthcoming Digital Product Passport (DPP). The DPP mandates 
that all products sold on the European market must be accompanied by a passport 
containing specific information such as product identification, material composition, 
and details about the garment’s life cycle (European Union, 2024).  
Companies adopting a sufficiency-driven business approach are expected to 
benefit from and gain an advantage in complying with these new requirements, as 
their business models are inherently aligned with the goals of the legislation. 
Sufficiency-driven companies have already implemented strategies that prioritise 
durability, repairability, and ecodesign, and measures to mitigate social and 
environmental risks are deeply integrated into the purpose of their business model. 
This proactive approach might be an advantage for sufficiency-driven businesses, as 
they are more likely to be better prepared and more competitive in adapting to the 
forthcoming EU regulations.  
 
However, despite the encouraging opportunities presented by upcoming regulations, 
the European textile sector is primarily composed of small and medium-sized 
enterprises, with 99.5% of companies employing fewer than 50 people (European 
Commission, 2022). While some companies may be proactive, many fear they lack the 
resources to prepare the necessary documentation to prove compliance with these 
regulations (Kirsi et al., 2024). Therefore, it is crucial that the forthcoming regulations 
are introduced alongside investment and funding initiatives that can support 
companies with the right expertise and knowledge.  
While these regulatory initiatives from the EU Commission are providing 
better grounds for implementation of sufficiency-driven business models in the 
apparel industry, it remains a long-term goal dependent on radical systemic 
transformations. Therefore, we approach this development with a sense of humility, 
recognising that sufficiency-driven business models as a fully realised concept are 
likely only achievable in the distant future and will depend on profound systemic 
changes. With this in mind, the contribution of this project hopes to be a small 
pragmatic step in the right direction that can create a foundation for wider 
acceptance and engagement with sufficiency.  
This project has contributed to theory by defining the concept of sufficiency 
and its connection to the circular economy. What constitutes a sufficiency-driven 
business model has been further concretised through the development of a business 
model canvas and corresponding strategies specific for sufficiency-driven businesses. 
The contributions to practice include providing companies with clarity on the 
essential elements required to establish a sufficiency-driven business model and 
concretising how specific sufficiency strategies can address different consumer needs 
and offer various business benefits.

10.2.​
Limitations and future work 
The aim of this project was to develop a framework that can help companies engage 
consumers in sufficiency-driven consumption. To ensure the usability and usefulness 
of the support, expert evaluations and user testing sessions have been conducted, 
leading to multiple iterations and refinements. The result is a mature and detailed 
framework. However, further research and development are suggested in this section 
to further enhance its usefulness and applicability. 
 
One key area for future investigation is a third evaluation and test of the SUFFit 
Strategy Cards, particularly the integration of a financial perspective in the form of 
potential business benefits and the emphasis on specific consumer needs through 
personas. It is essential to evaluate whether the integration of personas and financial 
benefits is understood and if their purpose is clearly communicated. Additionally, 
further assessment is needed to determine whether the five consumer personas 
sufficiently cover all relevant consumer types or if modifications are required. 
Furthermore, it must be evaluated if these additions bring any value to the companies 
and ease the selection of relevant strategies for the companies. Testing these aspects 
is crucial before broader deployment of the framework. 
It is further important to strengthen the financial aspect of sufficiency 
strategies by gathering insights from companies that have successfully implemented 
sufficiency-driven business models. Conducting interviews with these businesses 
could help identify best practices and provide practical guidance on how to make a 
strong business case for sufficiency. 
 
Furthermore, expanding the SUFFit Strategy Cards with an additional deck focusing 
on tangible sufficiency strategies related to user transfer, e.g., strategies with a focus 
on different reuse models and sharing services, could provide companies in the 
apparel industry with more concrete solutions for implementing sufficiency-driven 
strategies. 
Another potential avenue for future work could explore the development of a 
tool designed to optimise the combination of sufficiency strategies. Such a tool could 
assist companies in strategically pairing different sufficiency strategies to create the 
most effective sufficiency-driven business cases tailored to their specific customer 
segments.  
Lastly, future research could investigate how to support established 
companies in successfully transformation process from a conventional business 
model into a sufficiency-driven business model. This could entail selecting 
company-relevant PWCU strategies and using the SUFFit BMC to develop a concrete 
implementation plan in cooperation with a specific company.

powerful resource for companies aiming to take action towards overconsumption of 
clothing through sufficiency-driven business models.

11.​ CONCLUSION 
This master thesis investigated the concept of sufficiency as a counterpart to 
overconsumption, defined as: Sufficiency aims to limit resource use by reducing 
overconsumption to levels compatible with social well-being within the planetary 
capacity. 
By mapping the barriers for implementation of sufficiency strategies and 
collecting data on clothing consumption patterns and behaviours, this research has 
led to the development of a support that can aid companies in the apparel industry to 
take an active role in enabling sufficiency-driven consumption of clothing by 
consumers. The support consists of a sufficiency-driven business model canvas 
(SUFFit BMC) and a deck of sufficiency-driven strategy cards (SUFFit Strategy Cards). 
The SUFFit BMC contributes to theory by clarifying the elements that 
constitute a sufficiency-driven business model while also providing companies with a 
structured way to outline and develop sufficiency-driven business models in practice. 
The SUFFit Strategy Cards contribute to theory by defining specific 
sufficiency-driven strategies for prolonging the life time of garments with no change 
of user. In practice, the strategies can support companies with concrete tools to help 
their consumers adopt more sufficiency-driven clothing consumption practices.  
Together, the support provides actionable initiatives for businesses, serving 
both as a source of inspiration and a catalyst for shifting mindset change away from 
conventional business approaches towards sufficiency.

13.​ LEARNING OBJECTIVES 
 
1.​ Identify the state-of-the-art on Circular Economy and Sufficiency Strategies 
within the textile industry with specific focus on longevity 
a.​ The state-of-the-art on Circular Economy is elaborated in the 
introduction; see Section 1.2. Circular economy as a mitigation strategy  
b.​ The state-of-the-art on sufficiency and its relation to Circular Economy 
has been identified as part of an exploratory literature review. Further 
reviewing sufficiency definitions, frameworks, and strategies in current 
academic literature has been conducted; see Section 3. Theory.  
c.​ Longevity has been the main focus of this project, as the aim of a 
sufficiency-driven business is to make products that last and allow 
users to hold on to them as long as possible; see Section 3. Theory. 
Further, it was found relevant to narrow down the project scope and to 
focus solely on prolong strategies with no change of user; see Section 5. 
Problem definition.   
 
2.​ Analyse how Sufficiency Strategies can affect the apparel industry value 
chain and the consumer 
a.​ Is fulfilled as a part of the exploratory literature review on sufficiency 
definitions, frameworks, and strategies; see Section 3. Theory. 
b.​ Is fulfilled in the problem definition Section 5.1. Research scope 
definition, clearly emphasised with illustration in figure 8.  
c.​ Is fulfilled as part of the development of sufficiency strategies for 
different customer types; see Section 6. Conceptualisation. 
 
3.​ Identify and map the barriers to sufficiency business models’ scalability 
a.​ Is fulfilled by conducting a systematic literature review on the barriers 
to operationalising sufficiency within business models in the apparel 
industry; see Section 4. Literature review. 
 
4.​ Map the consumption patterns of clothing for specific target groups 
a.​ A specific target group has not been defined, as the primary goal of the 
project has been to provide companies in the apparel industry—each 
with potentially different target audiences—with concrete sufficiency 
strategies tailored to their respective customer segments. Instead, the 
focus has been on identifying and categorising different consumer  
needs through the development of five personas; see Section 8. 
Iteration and Section 9. Final support.

==PAGE:119==
 
5.​ Identify the needs that consumption of clothing fulfills amongst the specific 
target group  
a.​ A survey has been conducted to identify consumers’ needs when 
buying new clothing; see Section 5.3. Investigation of consumption 
patterns.  
b.​ We have investigated literature on why people consume clothing; see 
Section 1.1.1 Purpose of clothing. 
c.​ Personas have been developed and used to link different consumer 
needs to the development of sufficiency strategies; see Section 8. 
Iteration and Section 9. Final support. 
 
6.​ Analyse the identified sufficiency business models against the identified 
needs and consumer behaviour, and identify potential barriers, gaps, rebound 
effects and controversies 
a.​ It was not found relevant to analyse the identified sufficiency business 
models from literature against all the aspects mentioned in this 
learning goal. Instead, the aspects have been used to analyse and 
evaluate the new support developed in this project. This is fulfilled 
through an analysis of the potential rebound effects of the support (see 
Section 10.1.2 Potential rebound effects), an evaluation of what clothing 
types and consumer types the strategies of the support cater to (see 
Section 10.1.3 Clothing categories), and an evaluation of the innovation 
level, sufficiency potential, and adoption potential of the different 
strategies contained in the support (see Section 6.2.1 Asssesment of the 
PWCU strategies).  
 
7.​ Develop a support that addresses the identified barriers, gaps, and 
controversies towards the development of economically, environmentally 
and social sustainable business models 
a.​ The barriers, research gaps, and controversies identified in the research 
clarification and descriptive study I have been used as a foundation for 
the conceptualisation of the support. 
b.​ Economic-, Environmental- and social sustainability is especially 
emphasised in the element Ecological and Social impacts in the SUFFit 
BMC (see Section 6. Conceptualisation) 
 
8.​ Facilitate a forum where the support can be tested 
a.​ Expert evaluations have been performed with 5 experts within business 
development and sustainable development and with 5 experts within 
the fields of sustainable development and the apparel industry; see 
Section 7.1 Expert evaluation methodology.  
b.​  A test has been performed in an educational setting with an 
established company and with a group that aims to create a start-up; 
see Section 7.3 User test methodology.
