ABSTRACT
Biomarkers are a fundamental step in the evolution of monitoring methods for Parkinson’s
Disease patients. The potential for an earlier diagnosis and risk assessment proves to be a
significant benefit to research and improve the procedures for biomarker data collection
and processing.
In this thesis, the current standard for handling parkinsonian tremors is investigated, and
an analysis of innovative Deep Learning models is performed to establish the methodologies
used subsequently.
An experimental protocol composed of data collection,
data processing,
model
implementation, and biomarker investigation was conducted on PD patients and a healthy
control group. Through three modalities of sensors, EMG data was gathered, and an
Attention-based Multiple Instance Learning model assessed the quality of the data labels.
With an evaluation of the accuracy at 73%, further investigation on larger datasets could
achieve higher accuracy and trust. Nonetheless, it is demonstrated how, utilizing an
Attention-based MIL model, EMG data can be a reliable modality for tremor detection.

INTRODUCTION
Despite decades of research, the diagnosis and management of Parkinson’s Disease (PD) are
hampered by suboptimal methods for detection and prognosis [4]. The current standard
for PD evaluation consists of a clinical examination of patients by a neurology specialist
to assess motor function and self-reported symptoms [5]. The results of the disorder
assessment are dependent on subjective clinical judgment, and on the patient’s report,
which is possibly compromised by wrong self-perception due to cognitive impairment [4].
Thus, an accurate evaluation is difficult and potentially hinders the patient’s condition
and disease progression. There is a need for continuous and objective monitoring of motor
symptoms in PD to improve the therapeutic regimen, and enhance the outcomes of clinical
trials [6].
Furthermore, most of the cardinal symptoms of PD (e.g. tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia)
occur late in regard to the disease onset [5]. When a patient is diagnosed most dopamine-
producing neurons have already degenerated completely [4]. It is imperative to develop
techniques able of early diagnosis.
Digital biomarkers for Parkinson’s Disease are critically needed but are currently lacking
[6]. High-sensitivity and high-specificity biomarkers that provide continuous and objective
monitoring of motor symptoms are required to detect the disease in early stages and aid in
monitoring the disease progression [6].
This thesis aims to investigate Parkinson’s Disease Biomarkers by classifying Electromyo-
graphic (EMG) data for rest tremor detection, with an Attention-based Multiple Instance
Learning Model. The biomarkers to be investigated can become objective measures of
the disease’s progression and symptom severity, after sufficient validation. Validated
biomarkers assist with an unbiased assessment of each patient’s clinical case, therefore
improving the quality of the care provided and the disease’s management.
1.1
Research Questions
The project will attempt to answer two research questions:
1. Can EMG data be used for tremor detection?
2. Will an Attention-based Multiple Instance Learning Model perform comparably with
EMG data, as opposed to Accelerometer data?
To address the first question, some methods developed for other data modalities, such as
accelerometer, were implemented in EMG data and employed as observations for a Deep
Learning Model. The selected model is an Attention-based model, which addresses the
quality of the labels created during the data collection process.

namely, Parkinson’s Disease and Parkinsonian Tremor, and Biomarkers. It provides the
needed, prior knowledge to better understand the work of the following chapters.
2.1
Parkinson’s Disease
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder in the
world and affects about 2% of people older than 65 years [2]. In 2016, approximately 6.1
million people were diagnosed with PD worldwide, 2.4 times higher than in 1990 [2]. The
disease’s prevalence is increasing more rapidly than other neurological disorders.
Age, family history (genetic link), and environmental exposures (e.g, pesticides, solvents,
metals) are etiologic factors. PD’s prevalence increases with age, it is more common in
individuals above 50 years old, and peaks between the ages of 85 and 89 years old [2].
Pesticide, herbicide, and heavy metal exposures associated with industrialization are linked
to an increased risk of Parkinson’s disease in some epidemiologic studies, whereas smoking
and caffeine use is associated with decreased risks [2]. Although its ultimate cause is
still unknown, it is widely believed that most cases are caused by an interaction between
environmental and genetic factors [2].
It is characterized by the development of Lewy Bodies (neuronal inclusion consisting largely
of α-synuclein protein aggregations) and progressive loss or degeneration of dopamine-
producing neurons in the substantia nigra of the basal ganglia (Figure 2.1) [1]. The clinical
syndrome arising from the degenerative changes in basal ganglia function often is referred
to as parkinsonism [1]. Parkinson’s Disease is the most common form of parkinsonism, and
it is also known as idiopathic parkinsonism [1].
Both motor and non-motor symptoms are characteristic of the disease.
Non-motor
symptoms include olfactory loss, sleep dysfunction, psychiatric disturbances, and
cognitive impairment [2].
These affect many organ systems, such as gastrointestinal
and genitourinary systems, and are heterogeneous in their manifestation [2]. The cardinal
motor manifestations of PD are rest tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (slowness of movement),
and postural instability [2].

2.1.1
Rest Tremor
Parkinsonian tremor is the most visible manifestation of the disorder. Around 70 % of the
individuals diagnosed with Parkinson’s Disease notice tremors as the early symptom[2].
Parkinsonian tremor manifests in different types: rest, postural, and action tremors [7].
Rest tremor (RT) happens when a body part is relaxed [7]. The postural tremor (PT)
occurs while a body part is held straight out from the body in a stable position against
gravity [7]. The action tremor (kinetic tremor, KT) happens when a voluntary contraction
of a muscle follows a certain action [7]. A PD patient generally experiences rest tremor
associated with postural tremor or action tremor, however, they present different movement
frequencies [1]. Rest tremor is the characteristic of parkinsonism, and it is the one that
will be studied in this project.
RT affects the distal segments of the limbs, mainly the hands and feet [1]. It is characterized
by involuntary, rhythmic, alternating flexion and contraction movements that resemble the
motion of rolling a pill between the thumb and the index finger [1]. RT occurs when no
movement is being performed and the limb is fully rested [1]. It temporarily disappears
when the limb is outstretched, and then returns [1]. Usually, it starts in a finger and
expands to the whole arm, eventually progressing to involve both sides of the body [7].

2.1.2
Diagnosis
PD diagnosis is based on history and physical examination upon the presence or
manifestation of the aforementioned symptoms [2]. Individuals diagnosed with Parkinson’s
disease typically have gradual development of non-motor symptoms for years before motor
symptoms begin, but often they will not mention these symptoms unless specifically queried
[2].
Differential diagnosis can be challenging since the classic PD symptoms (e.g. rest tremor,
rigidity, etc) can be present in other neurodegenerative disorders. Patient medical history
taking and physical assessment, coupled with initial medical therapy (e.g., response to
Levodopa), are necessary to distinguish idiopathic PD from other disorders (Figure 2.2)
[2].
Dopamine transporter single-photon emission computed tomography (DaT SPECT) scans
can improve the accuracy of diagnosis when the presence of parkinsonism is uncertain [2].
This technique identifies the presynaptic dopamine neuronal dysfunction by demonstrating
reduced uptake of a radioactive tracer that binds to dopamine transporters in the basal
ganglia [2]. It has proven to be highly accurate (98%-100% sensitivity and specificity) in
detecting dopaminergic neuron loss in individuals with parkinsonism [2]. However, DaT
SPECT scans are usually only performed when there is uncertainty in the presence of
parkinsonism on examination [2].
The clinical diagnosis of parkinsonian tremor is carried out using rating scales. Among the
different rating scales, the most widely used is the Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale
(UPDRS) [4]. The UPDRS provides a subjective assessment performed by the qualitative
judgment of neurologists and is the clinical standard for parkinsonian tremor assessment [4].
Its results are based on the physician’s observations, which can sometimes be inaccurate,
therefore providing unvalidated results[4] . Part III of the UPDRS scale concerns the
assessment of motor symptoms. Tremor ratings are evaluated on a five-point scale (0 to 4),
which denotes normal, slight, mild, moderate, and severe, respectively.

2.2
Electromyography
Electromyography (EMG) is a diagnostic method, which enables the recording of bioelectric
signals resulting from the contraction of the skeletal muscles representing neuromuscular
activities [3]. It is often performed while stimulating the relevant motor and peripheral
nerves [3]. The recordings may be performed either in an invasive or surface-based way, at
the level of a single muscle fibre, single motor unit, or the entire muscle [3].
Surface Electromyography (sEMG), provides a non-invasive way of studying muscular
function by measuring the compound muscle action potential (CMAP)[3]. CMAP records
the entire muscle activity which consists of the sum of the individual Motor Unit Action
Potential (MUAP)[3]. Motor units are the smallest portions of muscles, and MUAP can be
measured invasively with needle electrodes directly into the muscle in the area of interest[3].
The EMG signal frequency ranges vary from 0.01Hz to 10kHz, depending on the type of
recording (invasive or non-invasive)[3]. The most useful and important frequency ranges
are within the range from 50 to 150 Hz. Figure 2.3 illustrates an example of a surface
EMG measurement, and a time and frequency representation of the signal.

Digital Biomarkers
A biomarker is a feature that can be measured and serve as an indicator of a biological
process [9]. It indicates the particular disease state of an organism and evaluates the
progress of the disease and the treatment effects [6].
Digital biomarkers are characteristics that can be reliably collected, measured and quantified
using digital devices, like wearables, phones, and smartwatches, as markers of a biological
process[9]. According to Shah et al., some of the advantages of using digital devices are: 1)
frequent and unobtrusive measurements in passive monitoring conditions (like activities of
daily living) to objectively quantify and/or predict health-related outcomes with greater
sensitivity; 2) replace endpoints for testing the efficacy of new treatments; 3) improved
clinical decision making and help to detect presymptomatic disease; 4) help in monitoring
existing treatment, and 5) modernized clinical trial endpoints [10].

3.2
Tremor Detection
The current golden standard for tremor detection and assessment is the rating scale UPDRS.
Its use provides a subjective assessment which can be inaccurate. It requires the presence
of a neurologist to conduct the assessment, meaning that it is performed during sporadic
doctor appointments and might not represent the patient’s daily living. By applying Deep
Learning to different data modalities (EMG, Accelerometer and Gyroscope) collected with
continuous monitoring systems insightful features can be extracted. Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNNs) and Multiple-Instance Learning (MIL) Models were the most recurrent
models applied.
Of the five selected papers, all of them employed wearable sensors to collect data. Four of
them analyzed Accelerometer data, and the remainder combined Accelerometer data with
touchscreen typing data from a smartphone.
Papadopoulos et al. conducted three of the selected studies [? ][? ][? ]. Sensor data was
collected unobtrusively, under unscripted and in-the-wild conditions, in other words in the

natural living environments of the subjects. In [? ][? ], they implemented an Attention-
based Multiple Instance Learning Model to detect parkinsonian tremors from accelerometer
signals. The implementation of an Attention-based Multiple Instance Learning Model
was justified due to the weak labels generated by the in-the-wild setting, and consisted

Parkinson’s Disease Digital Biomarkers
Several studies have identified and proved the usability of digital biomarkers for Parkinson’s

investigated digital biomarkers for resting tremor by classifying
accelerometer measurements using a decision tree estimator [8]. The Rest Tremor was
assessed based on the measured constancy, and the measured amplitude. Constancy
estimates the percentage of time that tremor is present in a recording, and amplitude
measures how fast and wide the tremor-related movements are. The proposed continuous

the mPower dataset and the L-Dopa dataset. The mPower dataset was used to assess the
ability to detect if an individual presents with Parkinson’s Disease, and the L-Dopa dataset
was used to assess symptom severity. One of the winning teams who studied the L-Dopa
dataset created features based on tremor occurrence from accelerometer measurements.
The "Balint Armin Pataki" team generated eighty-one features, from clinically derived,
Fourier-derived, and signal-processing derived ones[9].

MATERIALS AND METHODS
This chapter will describe the methods employed in the various phases of this study and
the used recording equipment. The study can be divided into four main phases: data
acquisition, data processing, model implementation, and biomarker investigation. For
each method, a small introduction will preface it, followed by a detailed explanation of its
application.
4.1
Recording equipment
To record movement data the wearable Paragit Sleeve was used. The sleeve was designed
by Paragit Solutions and is equipped with movement (accelerometer and gyroscope) and
muscle activity sensors (surface electromyography, sEMG). Its intended use is in a wild
setting (f. ex. at-home) for long-term recordings (24-hour monitoring) of Parkinson’s
Disease patients during their daily activities. The patient is instructed on how to wear
the sleeve by a clinician to ensure correct positioning and compliance. The sleeve should
be placed on the forearm as close as possible to the elbow. A sleeve for each forearm was
created to ensure the correct placement of the EMG electrodes (Figure 4.1).
It records real-time data from tri-axial accelerometer, gyroscope and sEMG sensors and
saves it into a micro SD-card (Figure 4.2). The EMG is recorded at a 2000 Hz sampling
frequency, and the tri-axial accelerometer and gyroscope are recorded at a 500 Hz sampling
rate and are automatically upsampled to match the sampling frequency of the EMG. There
are two surface EMG sensors located above the flexion muscle group of the forearm, which
enables muscle activity measurements of hand and finger movements. Additionally, a
reference sEMG electrode is placed at the edge closer to the wrist, since most people do
not have as much hair around the wrist as the rest of the arm, and wrist sizes do not
differ as much as forearm sizes. The EMG measurements that are saved to the micro
SD-card represent a bipolar measurement. The measurements of each sEMG electrode
are compared to the reference electrode, and then the difference between the two sEMG
electrodes is the measurement saved.
The EMG electrodes are covered with a hydrogel that is in direct contact with the skin.
The goal of the hydrogel is to secure the sleeve around the forearm and ensure that
throughout a long-term recording the sleeve does not shift position, introducing motion
artifacts into the signals. Additionally, the subject’s body heat and sweat will impact the
hydrogel activation. The contribution of the increase in body temperature and production
of sweat to the hydrogel activation becomes optimal after the first 90 minutes of recording.
The hydrogel will become more ionic and more adhesive to the skin and will enable more
sensitive and stable EMG data.
The sleeve is also composed of Paragit’s patented Impedance Compensation Circuit (ICC).
12
==PAGE:27==
4. Materials and Methods
There are two signal generators which produce two sine waves with frequencies of 825
Hz and 925 Hz. The circuit evaluates the resistive part of impedance in real-time and
balances the resistance of each electrode to be the same with digitally controlled impedance
loads (resistive part only). In a wild setting, the real-time impedance mismatch correction
enables the filtering out motion artifacts, like bumping the arm on a surface which can
slightly shift the electrode placement and either increase or decrease the effective area of
the electrode/skin interface. The ICC will ensure that the electrodes’ impedance will be
balanced throughout a long-term recording.
The signal generators introduce some resonance in the signals, due to their power quality.
The resonance frequencies can be identified when analysing the power spectrum of the raw
EMG signals (Figures 4.10 and 4.11). As a part of the product’s development, Paragit
intends to replace the signal generators with ones of better quality, which won’t introduce
resonance in the signals.
Since the sleeve’s intended use is for long-term recordings, and the project’s individual
recording sessions totalled a maximum of 60 minutes, some features were altered to ensure
the optimal use of the recording sleeve.

4.2.1
Experimental protocol design
Data from Parkinson’s Disease (PD) and Healthy Controls (HC) subjects was collected
following similar experimental protocols. The experimental protocol was not consistent
across all PD subjects, in regards to the recording device. The differences will be introduced
and explained in further detail in Section 4.4.
The recording sessions consisted of one visit per subject, and each recording session lasted
between 45-60 min. Detailed information on the recruitment of subjects is provided in
Section 4.2.2.
The recording sessions took place either at Paragit Solutions’ office or the subject’s home,
according to the agreement before the visit. The PD subjects’ visits were scheduled
according to their L-Dopa doses to be one hour before their next medication intake, to
ensure the subjects were in an OFF state (i.e., when motor symptoms are poorly controlled).
In the cases of the PD subjects who were not prescribed L-Dopa, the visits were scheduled
according to the subject’s convenience. For the HC visits, the scheduling was dependent
on their calendar availability.
Before the start of the recording session, the PD subjects were instructed to fill out a
questionnaire with a few questions about their medication and which arm was the one
which tremor was more predominant (see Appendix A). According to their answer, the
sleeve was chosen (right/left) and the forearm was cleaned with an alcohol solution. The
sleeve was positioned as close as possible to the elbow, identical to Figure 4.3. In regards
to the HC subjects, the sleeve of their dominant arm was selected.
The subjects were comfortably sitting in a chair, and their forearm was fully resting on a
14
==PAGE:29==
4. Materials and Methods
table with their palm facing up.
The flow of the experiment is periodic consisting of specific tasks, followed by a resting
period of, approximately, two minutes. These tasks can be broadly divided into activities
of daily living (ADL), and tasks from the motor assessment section of the MDS-UPDRS
Part III (see Appendix B). The tasks were chosen based on Mahadevan et al. ?? prior
work.
A diagram of the experiment is depicted in Figure 4.4. At the beginning and end of each
task the sleeve’s button was pressed to create a timestamp. The "examiner" instructed the
subject on what each task consisted of during the resting periods.
After correctly positioning the sleeve on the forearm, it was turned ON and, a calibration
period ensued. When the sleeve started recording, the subject was instructed to perform
no movement for 30 seconds. After this initial period, the "examiner" pressed the sleeve’s
button, and the first task was performed. As soon as the subject finished the first task,
the sleeve’s button was pressed again and a timer was started for 2 minutes. The subject
was instructed to not perform any movement and try to relax as much as possible, and the
"examiner" explained the next task. Once the resting period was over, the second task was
started. This protocol was repeated until the last task, consisting of a resting period per
se, so the resting period after the penultimate task totalled approximately three minutes.
The sleeve’s button was pressed after that, and another 30 seconds were recorded before
shutting off the sleeve.

4.4
Data Processing
In the following section, the methods applied in data processing will be described. Data
handling was performed in a Python environment (Python v3.7.9). The code developed

Data from the different modalities (EMG, Accelerometer, Gyroscope) will be processed to

4.4.1

To study the EMG signals, visualization was an intuitive first step to identify the main
differences between measurements of HC and PD subjects. Not only some of the signal
features in the time domain but as well artifacts can be more easily identified. Therefore,

suitable filter.

wipe the arm, and once the sleeve is correctly positioned, gently press the sleeve onto

electrodes’ impedance and remove the high-frequency artifact. There was an improvement

4.4.3
Data filtering
Another method to ensure that artifacts are removed, at the same time that the signal
of interest is conserved, is filtering the data. Bandpass filtering was the selected filter
technique and it was applied to allow that signals between an interval of frequencies are
preserved, whilst discriminating against signals not represented in this frequency interval.
Several frequency bands were experimented with in an attempt to find the optimal one.
Butterworth filters of order 4 were designed with frequency bands [3, 500] Hz, [3, 60] Hz,
and, ultimately, [1.5, 8] Hz. According to the research previously performed, our signal of
interest (tremor) has a frequency between 3 and 8 Hz (Table 2.1). Additionally, human
EMG signals have a maximum frequency of 500 Hz, and lower frequencies around 0 Hz
can cause baseline wandering. These factors were taken into account when designing the
bandpass filters to ensure the preservation of the tremor measurements. Filtering was
applied to the EMG Signals of all subjects, though the analysis of its effects will, firstly, be
evaluated in the subjects in which the intended removal of artifacts had a greater impact.

their power spectrum. In Figure 4.10 and Figure 4.11 the power spectrum of the unfiltered
EMG data of ID1 is illustrated. As mentioned in Section 4.4.1, the two frequencies with

4.6.1
Attention-based Multiple Instance Learning
The Deep Learning Model chosen for the classification problem was an Attention-based
Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) Model.
A Multiple Instance Learning Model addresses the challenge of weakly annotated data to
be used in a medical context. The concept was introduced by Dietterich et al. and they
explained the problem with a simple example. Imagine a situation when someone attempts
to open a door and has multiple key chains with different keys on them, but some key
chains can open the door and others can’t. The information of which key chains open the
door is given, but not specifically which key. To find out which key opens the door, the
person must examine the shapes of all the keys on the key rings and infer the answer. This
analogy intends to simplify the learning problem in a multiple instance learning situation.
In a healthcare environment, the problem arises when there is partial or incomplete
knowledge about each training example. It has been used in medical imaging where an
image is generally described by a single label or a Region Of Interest (ROI) is roughly
given, and the goal is to give a medical diagnosis.
MIL is a supervised learning technique and it assigns a single class label to a bag of
instances. A bag of instances constitutes an object which can be represented by one of a
set of potential feature vectors (fixed-length vector of attribute values). In the key chain
example, the person does not have information on the keys, only on the key chains. For a
binary classification problem, a bag is considered to have a positive label if it contains at
least one positive instance, and a negative label if it contains only negative instances.
Nonetheless, MIL presents a major challenge in trying to discover what instances are key
instances, i.e. the instances that trigger the bag label.
Ilse et al. proposed a new method that aimed at incorporating interpretability to the
MIL approach and increasing its flexibility, the Attention-based Deep Multiple Instance
Learning. The MIL model was formulated using the Bernoulli distribution for the bag
label and trained by optimizing the logarithmic likelihood function. The process to model
the bag label probability is executed in three steps: 1) transform the instances to a
low-dimensional embedding, 2) a permutation- invariant (symmetric) aggregation function,
and 3) final transformation to the bag probability. All transformations are parameterized
by using a combination of convolutional and fully-connected layers, and this corresponds
to the attention mechanism. The attention weights are calculated according to

The hyperparameters that were investigated were the learning rate and the dropout rate.
The learning rate controls how much the model should change in response to the estimated
loss. The loss between the input and the target was calculated with the Cross-Entropy
Loss Function, according to Equation 4.7. The optimizer is responsible for updating the
network weights and the learning rate. The Adam optimizer was the algorithm applied by
Papadopoulos et al., as well as in this project.

L represents the loss measure, and it is calculated with the output of the model’s softmax ,
and the ground truth y.
The dropout rate is characteristic of the Dropout layers which are responsible for randomly
setting the input units to 0. The number of input units to be dropped depends on the
dropout rate. The inputs that are not set to 0 are scaled up such that the sum over all
inputs remains unchanged. The goal of these layers is to prevent overfitting, which happens
when a model performs very well on training data but not on unseen observations.
The hyperparameter optimization of the learning rate was executed on a Python Automated
Hyperparameter Optimization Framework, called Optuna. Optuna reduces the optimization
time and does not require manual hyperparameter combination, such as in the Grid Search
Method. It consists of iterative steps in which an objective function is evaluated, and the
hyperparameter is adapted according to the direction of the optimization. In this project,
the objective function was the loss function and it was intended to be minimized, since the
closer the loss measurement is to zero, the closer the predicted value is to the target label.
The dataset was partitioned, and training and validation sets were used and partitioned
again in a 5-fold Cross-Validation technique (Fig. 4.24). A range of learning rates between
{1 exp −4, 2 exp −4} was tested across more than ten trials, and the learning rate that
produced the best model, in terms of minimizing the loss, was 0.0001542. Figure 4.25 shows
the training and validation loss of the best model produced with Optuna. Optuna trials
were carried out on DTU’s High-Performance Computer due to the extensive computational
power of such operations.
Optimization of the dropout rate was conducted after optimizing the learning rate, and
the best learning rate was used. Concerns about overfitting resulted in an increase in the
dropout from 0.2 to 0.5 and from 0.5 to 0.6 in Layers 1 and 2 of the Classification module
(Fig. 4.23).
The final hyperparameters were 0.0001542 as the learning rate, 0.5 and 0.6 as dropout
rates of the Classification module, and the model trained with 400 epochs.

DISCUSSION
This chapter discusses the results of the project in a critical manner, and intends to provide
some guidelines in what could be improved in future work.
6.1
Attention Based Multiple Instance Learning
The results obtained after training the Attention-based MIL model were considered
satisfactory, due to the limited duration of the project. However, some remarks can
be raised in relation to its performance.
Throughout the model training process even though there was an improvement of the
validation accuracy, the training accuracy decreased. The increase of the dropout rate
in the dropout layers of the Classification module was the cause of this behavior, since,
at least, half of the observations were dropped and not considered in the following step.
When validating the model there are no observations to be dropped, therefore the model is
more robust and achieves a higher accuracy.
The model’s performance is also very dependent on the dataset it was trained upon. The
dataset created in this study can be considered small for the complexity of the employed
model. As can be seen in Figure 6.1, the model has 70 287 parameters to be trained in a
dataset of size 32 observations. Due to difficulties encountered when recruiting subjects,
only nine PD subjects, and five HC subjects participated in the experiment.
6.2
Digital Biomarkers
One digital measurement was inspected as a biomarker in the test observations of the MIL
model. The attention parameters were taken into account to isolate the instance which
contributed the most and the least to a bag label.
The power spectrum of accelerometer data showed some correspondence between the
expected and the resulting frequency analysis.
Further validation of this digital
measurement in different observations of this dataset, and in sensor data collected with a
different experimental protocol, is necessary to draw supported findings.
6.3
Future Work
Other digital biomarkers are valuable to be investigated. As Mahadevan et al. described
Constancy and Tremor Amplitude showed good agreement with the subjects’ clinical
assessment. At the same time as the winning team of the DREAM challenge explored,
the tsfresh Python package to extract features of time-domain signals is something to
considered in a future investigation.

CONCLUSION
The current study aimed at studying Digital Biomarkers of Parkinson’s Disease. An
experimental protocol was designed, and both subjects diagnosed with Parkinson’s Disease
and healthy subjects were recruited to participate. Sensor data of three distinct modalities
was collected and examined in different methods of the Data Processing Phase. EMG data
was processed and divided into windows to create bags of observations.
An Attention-based Multiple Instance Learning model was implemented to address the
quality of the labels, by assigning a label to a set of observations. Despite the fact that the
validation loss reached, approximately, 73 % of accuracy, the model’s performance should
be assessed further due to the small dataset size and the limited model training time.
The investigation of biomarkers was carried out on the correctly classified positive
observations and the highest instances were extracted to compute the power spectrum of the
correspondent accelerometer data. The validation of the power spectrum biomarker in other
datasets must be conducted to demonstrate its specificity and sensitivity . Additionally,
the validation of the labelling by a medical expert is crucial when estimating disease
progression, and symptom severity.
The research questions raised in the beginning can now be addressed. By obtaining similar
performance to previously described work, EMG data has proven to be a valid modality to
use when implementing an Attention-based Multiple Instance Learning Model for tremor
detection. More extensive research should be carried out in an attempt of validating the
proposed digital biomarker.
Nonethless, this study delivered a novel data-driven approach in the context of Parkinson’s
Disease tremor detection and digital biomarkers.
