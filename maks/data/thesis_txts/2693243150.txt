Abstract
The usual route to diagnosis for colorectal cancer is an inherently invasive and time-consuming pro-
cess of histopathological analysis based on resection of tissue (biopsies) during an endoscopic exam.
Developing an optical biopsy approach used during the endoscopic exam by utilizing light and tissue
interaction can prevent the need for invasive biopsies altogether. Multiple imaging modalities can
oﬀer optical biopsies based on the morphological and biochemical characteristics of colorectal tissue.
When measuring the optical properties of colorectal cancer tissue in vivo, the imaging modalities
needed will have to transition from biological imaging studies at the cellular level of samples in
Petri dishes to that of studying cells in their natural environment, i.e., in biological tissues. This
poses an interesting engineering problem. One that optical ﬁbers will be able to solve.
This thesis focuses on making minimally invasive optical biopsy methods accessible in endoscopy
using multicore ﬁbers (MCFs). The goal is to show that Multi-photon light-sheet ﬂuorescence mi-
croscopy (MP-LSFM) through a hexagonal MCF is feasible. Endoscopic adaptation of MP-LSFM
through optical ﬁbers requires clinical translation of colorectal cancer diagnosis based on optical
biopsies. It will produce deeper and faster ﬂuorescence imaging for real-time cancer diagnostics in
vivo.
Through wavefront shaping using spatial light modulators, I show how to achieve MP-LSFM en-
doscope adaption without using optical components at the distal end of a ﬁber. This "lensless"
endoscopy approach can achieve distal wavefront control by modulating the phase of diﬀerent cores
in the proximal end of MCFs. Lensless endoscopy enables fast, non-photo toxic, 3D volumetric
image acquisition at depth by a small imaging probe. Steps are taking to implement MP-LSFM in
endoscopy by showing a successful optical setup able to couple into MCFs with an ultrafast pulsed
laser and simultaneously execute phase control over individual cores. Using this setup, phase cali-
bration of the cores in an MCF and imaging a focus spot using 19 cores is achieved. These results
are compared with the open-source light propagation tool BPM-MATLAB to showcase the ability
to model real cases. The models will aid in future research into the challenges of lensless endoscopy
still to be solved.

Resumé
Den normale vej til diagnose for colorectal kræft, er en invasiv og tidskrævende process, udført
ved en histopatologisk analyse, baseret på en vævsprøve (biopsi), taget under en endoskopisk un-
dersøgelse. Udvikling af en metode til optisk biopsi under den endoskopiske undersøgelse, ved at
udnytte lys-væv interaktioner, kan forhindre behovet for invasive biopsier. Forskellige billedtagn-
ingsmodaliteter kan tilbyde optiske biopsier baseret på morfologiske og biokemiske karakteristika
af colorectalt væv. Ved måling af de optiske egenskaber af colorectal kræftvæv in vivo, kræver det
at biledtagningsmodaliteterne overgår fra biologisk billedtagningsstudier på det cellulære niveau af
prøver i petriskåle, til at studere cellerne i deres naturlige miljø, dvs. i kroppen. Dette udgør et
interessant ingenørmæssigt problem. Et, som optiske ﬁber vil være i stand til at løse.
Dette speciale fokuserer på at gøre minimalt invasive optiske biopsimetoder tilgængelige i endoskopi
ved at bruge multikerneﬁber. Målet er at vise, at mulitphoton lysark ﬂuorescens mikroskopi gen-
nem en hexagonal multikerneﬁber er muligt. Endoskopisk tilpasning af dette gennem optiske ﬁber
er et krav for klinisk translation af colorektal kræft diagnostik baseret på optiske biopsier. Det vil
muliggøre dybere og hurtigere ﬂuorescensbilleddannelse af realtids kræftdiagnostik in vivo.
Gennem bølgefrontsformning ved brug af Spatial light modulators, vises det, hvordan tilpasning af
multiphoton lysark ﬂuorescens mikroskopi til endoskopi kan opnås uden at bruge optiske kompo-
nenter i den distale ende af ﬁberen. Denne "linsefrie" endoskopiske tilgang kan opnå bølgefronts-
formning i den distale ende, ved at modulere fasen af lys i forskellige kerner i den proximale ende af
multikerneﬁberen. Dette muliggør hurtig, ikke-fototoksisk, 3D volumetrisk billedtagning i dybden,
ved brug af en lille billedprobe. Det bliver vist, hvordan det byggede optiske setup kan bane vejen
for at implementere multifoton lysark mikroskopi i endoskopi. Dette gøres, ved at demonstrere
koblingen af femtosekund pulseret laserlys ind i multikerneﬁberen og samtidig eksekvere individuel
fasekontrol over hver enkelt kerne. Fasekalibrering af disse kerner og billedtagning af et fokuspunkt
generet ved brug af 19 kerner demonstreres ligeledes. disse resultater vil blive sammenlignet med
simuleringsværktøjet BPM-MATLAB, for at vise evnen til at modelere virkelige scenarier. Dette
vil assistere fremtidig forskning i de udfordringer ved linsefri endoskopi der stadig skal løses.

Introduction
Each year, about 450.000 people in Europe are diagnosed with colorectal cancer. Of those 450.000
people, half of them will not recover. Only Lung cancer, prostate cancer, and breast cancer outrank
colorectal cancer in terms of mortality [1]. At the same time, colorectal cancer is the most commonly
diagnosed form of cancer in Europe [2]. How can this be? Why can we diagnose colorectal cancer
at a better rate than cure it, compared to other cancer forms? There are several reasons for this,
but the most important of those are:
• Vague and unspeciﬁc symptoms: Colorectal cancers usually have mild to no symptoms,
such as blood in the stool, diﬀuse pain, and defecation irregularities.
• Late stage diagnosis common: As an indirect result of the unspeciﬁc and generally mild
symptoms, colorectal cancer diagnosis happens at a late stage of the disease.
When we want to diagnose colorectal cancer, earlier detection will lead to a lower mortality rate
[3]. The regular route to diagnosis requires the patient to take the initiative and go to a GP based
on those vague and unspeciﬁc symptoms. Here the GP will perform a rectal exploration, and based
on this, an endoscopic exam follows. During the endoscopic exam, a doctor will visually inspect
the colon, and if something looks wrong, the doctor carries out a tissue resection (biopsy).
A
histopathologist will then do a histopathological evaluation of the biopsy. There exist noninvasive
tests used as screening, such as stool tests including guaiac faecal occult blood test (gFOBT or
FOBT) and faecal immunochemical test for haemoglobin (FIT) [4, 5]. However, these tests only
oﬀer moderate sensitivity, and the detection of advanced stages is limited since it requires multiple
screening rounds. Usually, the road to diagnosis is invasive, time-consuming, unpleasant and only
starts with a clue in the patient’s mind. What if we could detect colorectal cancer during the
endoscopic exam? Then we could eliminate the need for invasive biopsies, reduce human errors and
decrease the time it takes to get a diagnosis. It is reasonable to think that such a tool would lead
to more endoscopic exams, increasing the survival rate for colorectal cancer.
My work begins here by enabling the measurement of tissue’s optical characteristics in endoscopy
using light. When measuring the optical properties of colorectal cancer tissue in vivo, we must
transition from biological imaging studies at the cellular level of samples in Petri dishes to that
of studying cells in their natural environment, i.e., in biological tissues. Such a transition is an
exciting engineering problem where optical ﬁbers pose a compelling solution.
For the work presented here, I have chosen to investigate the use of multi-photon ﬂuorescence
excitation (MPEF) microscopy combined with Light-Sheet ﬂuorescence microscopy (LSFM) in en-
doscopy using the variant of optical ﬁbers called multicore ﬁbers (MCFs). Combining MPEF and
LSFM (MP-LSFM) in endoscopy will make it possible to probe the optical properties of colorectal
cancer tissue for clinical translation of a diagnosis based on the light-matter interaction. MPEF
enables us to increase the imaging depth since we can use longer wavelengths. LSFM allows de-

These methods are equipment heavy, creating the challenge of getting all that equipment close to

2.1
Lensless Endoscopy
Endoscopy is nothing new. The ﬁrst endoscope was developed in 1806, and the ﬁber endoscope
saw its birth in 1957 [15]. The ﬁber-optic technique uses a long ﬂexible probe, usually made of
fused silica, with a white light-source at the proximal end (input end) to relay an image of a sample
located at the distal end (output end) of the probe, as seen in ﬁgure 2.1a.
(a) Illustration of the colonoscopy procedure.
(b) Principle of a lensless light-sheet endoscope.
Figure 2.1: (a) Endoscopic procedure illustration adapted from [16]. (b) lensless endoscopy illus-
tration, adapted from [9].
Endoscopy enables us to examine the interior of a hollow organ such as the colon.
Although
this technology gives a health practitioner the ability to inspect cavities in the body visually, the
information attainable is qualitative, and the next step would be to take a biopsy and analyze the
tissue in a microscope [16]. With the advent of microscopy techniques such as single-photon and
multi-photon excited ﬂuorescent microscopy (MPEF) [7], we gain a new way to produce optically
sectioned images in the highly scattering medium tissue is. This we can achieve by utilizing the
auto-ﬂuorescent reactions tissue can give at speciﬁc wavelengths. We can do it label-free without
invasive biopsies, so we name it a minimally invasive optical biopsy. The ultimate goal of the work

presented in this thesis is to go from an invasive biopsy procedure seen in ﬁgure 2.1a to a minimally
invasive optical biopsy procedure as seen in ﬁgure 2.1b. In ﬁgure 2.1b shows light guided through
optical ﬁbers to the tissue, controlled by a Spatial Light Modulator to generate the light sheet
and collected with a objective perpendicular to the light sheet. These are the basic components
of a lensless endoscope. When going from ﬁgure 2.1a to 2.1b we cannot just use a standard light

also need many new optical components to couple laser light into a ﬁber and a method for steering
the laser beam at the distal end of the ﬁber. If we want to shape the light to make a thin sheet,
we need many degrees of freedom in the optical ﬁber and some way to modulate the phase proﬁle.
Many of these challenges can be solved and readily understood, as will become apparent in this
chapter. However, the concept of steering the laser beam at the distal end of the ﬁber requires a
thorough explanation. Understanding how this is done starts with the realization that the Maxwell
equations are time-reversible [17].

a copy of the original input wavefront emitted by the coherent point source. What this means is
that each distal point can be correlated with a certain proximal wavefront that, after propagation

2.2
Light Propagation in optical ﬁbers
To do lensless endoscopy, proper knowledge of the physics, which determines how light propagation
through optical ﬁbers work, is needed. Here, I will explain the basic principles for understanding
light-propagation in optical ﬁbers. Along the way, it should become apparent why I have chosen a
MCF to transfer the laser light to the distal end, and the dispersion mechanisms described should
help explain why the phase calibration process is needed.

2.3
Wavefront Shaping in optical ﬁbers
So far, I have talked about wavefront shaping without really explaining the principle. The following
is a brief introduction to wavefront shaping devices such as an SLM.
2.3.1
SLMs
A SLM is a device that can manipulate or modulate light properties such as phase, amplitude, and
polarization. The technology works by the properties of birefringent liquid crystals. The liquid
crystals have a favourable trait that they will change their orientation with the voltage applied
to them, which in turn will change their refractive index [28]. Because of this RI change, a beam
incident on a liquid crystal display diﬀracts to diﬀerent locations based on the RI value.
By
changing the voltage over the liquid crystals, we can thus modulate the light incident upon a SLM.
In its simplest form, a SLM is a pixelated display formed by hundreds of thousands of liquid crystal
ﬁlled cells that the user controls individually. The beauty of this technology lies in the fact that it
does not require any speciﬁc software to make this work. One simply needs to connect a PC to the

SLM has a pixel depth of 8 bits, giving 28 = 256 discrete greyscale levels ranging from 0 to 255.

can separate a Gaussian beam incident on the SLM into segments of beams that one then couples
into each diﬀerent core in a MCF. By encoding the transfer function of a lens [29] onto the SLM,

2.3.2
Diﬀraction gratings
It is important to note that the SLM will act as a diﬀraction grating because of the pixelation on
the SLM screen. Any optical component with a periodic structure will be a diﬀraction grating,
meaning it will diﬀract light into several beams travelling in diﬀerent directions. In ﬁgure 2.9 you
see a qualitative illustration of this principle (the ﬁgure shows the principle using slits for better
visualization, but the same holds when you replace slits with grooves and light is reﬂected of the
grooves). When a plane wave is incident on multiple slits, the resulting interference pattern after
passing through the slits will produce multiple orders of peaks. The beam from each slit will travel a
diﬀerent distance to reach the same spot, thereby having diﬀerent phases. By this, the interference
pattern will consists of periodic peaks as the phase-shift between beams shift from π = λ/2 to
π = λ. For many slits, the interference pattern will consist of bright spots that we name orders.
Orders are denoted with an m, and the brightest spot in the middle is called the 0th order. The
position of these orders is given for waves with a normal angle of incidence as:
mλ = d sin Θ
(2.18)
Figure 2.9: An illustration from the interference pattern resulting from a grating.
Here Θ is an angle drawn for the centre slit to the 0th and 1st order, and λ is the wavelength. When
we do wavefront shaping using a SLM, we usually only want one order to pass through the system
while blocking the other orders. The 0th order will not undergo any phase change, so it must be
blocked out of the system. As the 1st order will have a higher intensity than the other higher
orders, this will be the modulated order we couple into the MCF. The number of grating lines per
unit length, otherwise called the grating constant, determines the displacement of the beam, so
designing a phase mask coupling the 1st order into the system involves choosing the correct grating
line. When using the SLM, there will also be an unmodulated order stemming from the small
spacing between each liquid crystal pixel, which by itself will be a diﬀraction grating.

3.2.1
Phase mask generation
There are many diﬀerent ways to generate phase masks for beam-shaping. However, the approach
used here is based upon an investigation into the optimal phase mask for coupling into MCFs by
Vettikazhy [25]. In that work, it was found that microlenses with gratings oﬀer separate core-phase
control and eﬃcient separation of desired beamlets from the background.
Microlenses with focusing gratings
Figure 3.5: 7 microlenses with a focusing grating and a background grating. Screenshot of SLM
when used a second monitor on a pc.
Figure 3.5 shows the phase mask used to couple light into the MCF. The SLM creates a series of
quadratic phase microlenses according to equation 2.17 with a focal length of 98 mm. By this, the
IFP is 98 mm in front of the SLM. Each microlens will create a beamlet that couples into one core,
so the number of microlenses and cores that needs to be excited should always match. A grating
is applied to the microlenses to decouple them from the unmodulated 0th order. A background
grating in the x and y dimension is applied to reduce the interaction of the light modulated in
this region. Otherwise, the background might excite the cladding modes of the ﬁber. This phase
mask gives separate core-phase control and eﬃcient separation of the desired beamlets from the
undesired diﬀraction orders. This can be seen in ﬁgure 3.6. Here, a tilted line of orders is visible,
coming from the diﬀerent diﬀraction orders of the background. Just below the brightest spot (0th
order of the background) is the unmodulated order. Just to the right of the unmodulated order is
the beamlet pattern, which we can separate from the rest by the two pinholes placed before Lobj.

Discussion
In this chapter, the work and the results are analysed and evaluated. First, I look at the optical
setup and methods used. Here, I discuss the imaging conﬁguration and the beam size concerning
SLM illumination for optimal diﬀraction eﬃciency. Next, I compare the phase mask generation
using microlenses and a background grating, emphasising the grating constants used. Following
that is a discussion on the ﬁber alignment process and the power scan method for ﬁnding the MCF
cores. Then I discuss the results of coupling into a larger number of cores with the presence of
higher-order modes. The dispersion mechanisms, the wavelength used, and how these aﬀect the
ability to do light shaping will then follow. Afterwards, I will go over the steps taken in the phase
calibration and why the new system can calibrate the phase diﬀerence between the cores. In the
last section, I turn my attention to the 2D and 3D focus spot images generated for 19 cores and
compare them with the simulation results.
5.1
Analysis of the setup and methods
The results of large-scale ﬁber coupling and image generation in sections 4.2, 4.5 show that building
an experimental setup for coupling light into a hexagonal multicore ﬁber and creating lensless distal
beam shaping is possible. Still, there are improvements to the system and alternative approaches
to explore.
5.1.1
Optical setup
Optimal use of optical components in the setup will ensure that the laser beam is focused correctly
into the ﬁber as explained by the MFD in ﬁgure 2.5. Therefore, it will be wise to investigate the
used optics and discuss the problems they might impose.
Based on the same equations presented in 3.1 and 3.2 and with the Leica objective Lobj having a
focal length of fobj = 5mm, it can be calculated that lens L3 needs a focal length of Fobj/M =
5mm/0.0215 = 232.56mm. The closest focal length available is the Thorlabs lens AC254-200-B-ML
with f = 200mm. Having a shorter focal length will bend the rays more sharply, bringing them
to a focus in a shorter distance or diverging them more quickly. This means that the laser beam
entering Lobj will not converge to the point we expect, which will create a sub-optimal MFD for the
MCF. Changing the L3 lens to match the calculated focal length better will mitigate this problem.
5.1.2
SLM considerations
The power lost because of the SLM and its diﬀraction orders can severally limit the ability to do
TPEF, which needs a photon ﬂux in the range 1020 - 1030 photons/cm2s [34]. Therefore, it is vital
to investigate how to improve the diﬀraction eﬃciency for making the system incorporating MPEF
41
==PAGE:56==
Chapter 5. Discussion
through the optical ﬁber.
When designing the optical system, we use an approach that ﬁlls the shortest dimension of the
SLM (y; vertical) when a laser beam is incident on the SLM. Another approach is to Overﬁll the
SLM such that the active area has a uniform illumination. Overﬁlling the SLM screen will result
in the modulated wavefront having more of a top-hat beam proﬁle. This will ensure constant am-
plitude and phase-only modulation to the wavefront exiting the SLM, which is desirable. However,
overﬁlling the SLM reduces the power available for ﬁber coupling. This might not be a problem
currently, with the Sprite Laser having the ability to produce 1.5W of power[35], but it will reduce
the eﬃciency of the system. Moreover, overﬁlling the SLM will produce even more power in the
unmodulated 0th order and higher-order modes. Since these cannot contribute to coupling, they
will act as noise in the system. Therefore, the decision to ﬁll the SLM in one direction only is best.
A drawback of this method is that the sectioned active area chosen (in the inner circle in ﬁgure
3.4.a) will by itself create another grating that must be decoupled from the setup.

The diﬀraction eﬃciency of an SLM is deﬁned as the Power incident on the SLM compared to the
power in the desired order. Here the 1st order is the desired order. Due to a ﬁnite ﬁlling factor
of liquid crystals, the pixelated structure of the SLM display, and the unmatched anti-reﬂection
(AR) coating of the front electrode of the SLM, the diﬀraction eﬃciency is reduced in the setup
[36, 37]. The eﬀects will yield an unmodulated beam component when light is incident on the
SLM. Figure 3.6 shows this beam component where it is almost co-propagating with the desired
beamlet pattern. This is a problem, especially if using many cores for coupling, as they might
overlap then. This makes it desirable to increase the diﬀraction eﬃciency and reduce the power in
the unmodulated order. One way to increase the diﬀraction eﬃciency is to change the linear phase
shift of the SLM. In an ideal case, the phase shift each pixel can produce in the SLM goes from 0
to 2π, corresponding to a greyscale value from 0 to 255. In reality, a greyscale level of 255 will not

5.1.3
Phase mask generation optimization
When creating a phase mask to couple diﬀerent segments, or beamlets, into each core in the MCF,
it is vital to do it right. Without proper phase mask generation, it will not be easy to couple the
desired 1st order into the system since other modes might overlay the 1st order and also couple
into the system. This will act as noise in the system. If such noise is too high, it can hinder the
combination of amplitudes from the diﬀerent cores, detrimental to generating a focus spot or light
sheet. This makes it essential to analyse the generated phase mask, especially the grating constants
and how they might inﬂuence diﬀraction orders.
When generating the phase mask, the grating constant of the background should be carefully
designed. Since two pinholes are used just before the objective Lobj to decouple the unmodulated,
0th and higher-order diﬀraction orders from the 1st order, a background grating in both x and y
with a grating constant of 5 works to separate them. Figure 3.5 shows such a phase mask with a
resulting XY-position of the diﬀerent diﬀraction orders seen in ﬁgure 3.6. Although a higher grating
constant would make it easier to separate the diﬀerent diﬀraction orders as the background will
be moved further away, there will tend to be produced aliasing on the SLM screen with a higher
grating constant. With a grating constant of 100 in both the x and y direction, the resulting phase
mask with microlenses will look like ﬁgure 5.2a. Here, the visible presence of aliasing is indicated
by the background having more than one frequency component. This will result in uncontrolled
diﬀraction orders with phase proﬁles producing diﬀracted lines in undesired places. This can be
seen in ﬁgure 5.2b.
These uncontrolled diﬀracted orders produced by aliasing are in the same
position as the desired beamlets, making them impossible to separate.

5.2
Fiber coupling results
As ﬁgures 4.2 and 4.3 show, successful coupling into 7 and 19 cores is achieved. Even so, the
coupling into each core is not optimal, evidenced by higher-order modes present in some of the
excited cores and surrounding cores. It is postulated that this is due to a MFD and beam diameter
mismatch. Without the ability of large-scale ﬁber core coupling, there will not be enough degrees
of freedom to create a light-sheet focused down in the z-direction, thereby giving enough power
to generate a MPEF signal. Since the current "light-sheet" and focus spot will not be conﬁned in
the z-direction, it is not possible to achieve MPEF by only using 19 cores. Therefore, the reasons
behind the higher-order modes should be investigated.
5.2.1
Higher order modes
Using equation 2.2 and 2.3 the V parameter can be calculated as 2.01 when using a 800 nm wave-
length. This indicates that only the fundamental mode will propagate in the single-mode cores
having a proﬁle similar to the TM00 mode seen in ﬁgure 2.4. In reality, there can be observed
higher-order modes in ﬁgures 4.2 and 4.3 when exciting 7 and 19 cores. These higher-order modes
appear as non-circular excitations in core 10, 11 and especially 19 in ﬁgure 4.3. The higher-order
modes present and the troubles with large-scale ﬁber coupling can be because of several diﬀerent
factors or a combination. It might be due to the lens L3 having a wrong focal length for the 4f
imaging system, as discussed in section 5.1.1. This can produce a mismatch of the mode ﬁeld di-
ameter of the single cores and the beam diameter incident on the cores. This will produce a beam
diameter larger than the mode ﬁeld diameter, which will make a large portion of the optical power
incident on the ﬁber’s cladding. This will produce cladding modes of guided light. Cladding modes
have the ability of coupling with higher-order modes in the cores and thereby make higher-order
modes propagate inside the cores [22]. This can explain the presence of higher-order modes seen in
the ﬁgures. The mismatch might not only be due to the diameter diﬀerence. If the beamlets are
not centred around the cores, some light will couple directly into the cladding, producing the same
eﬀect. This can be due to the misalignment of the proximal ﬁber facet and the IFP produced by
the power scan procedure. It can be the non-optimised pitch and angle values, both explained in
the previous section.
There might be a more simple explanation creating the beam diameter and MFD mismatch, which
should be improved upon ﬁrst. If the ﬁber proximal end ﬁxed to the XYZ-stage is not completely
parallel with the objective Lobj, some of the cores will experience a more diverged beam and thus
larger beam diameter incident upon them. There is a groove in the XYZ stage that should run
parallel with the objective to ensure this is not possible, but to what degree of precision is unknown.
Having a stage that can adjust the angle of the ﬁber will mitigate this. It is also possible that de-
spite the eﬀorts taken to generate a phase mask with clear separation of the 1st order, some higher
diﬀraction orders are still coupled into the system. This could be due to the linear phase ramp on
the SLM producing too much power in the undesired orders (see section 5.1.2). This will mean

the higher order modes present are due to noise in the system and not the beam diameter mismatch.
5.3
Phase and multicore ﬁbers
From ﬁgure 4.11c it is evident that the phase calibration method outlined in 3.2.5 has worked
since a focus spot is generated from a defocused image by proper phase diﬀerence compensation
between core one and all other cores. This means that the memory eﬀect described for MCFs in
2.2.3 holds due to the low coupling between cores. From ﬁgure 4.6 it is shown that even poorly
coupled cores such as core 19 will be able to produce an interference image, thereby enabling us to
calibrate the phase diﬀerence. Without the phase calibration, the addition of amplitudes from the
diﬀerent cores in the distal end of the ﬁber will not add up to a focus spot or light sheet, which
will jeopardise the goal of lensless endoscopy. For this reason, the process is discussed here, with
eﬀorts taken to understand it better and improve upon it. The calibration process assumes that
the phase is a constant value, but the phase will be frequency-dependent in general. As the phase
calibration process is crucial to lensless endoscopy, investigating into whether this assumption holds
is essential.

5.3.2
Dispersion
When executing the phase calibration procedure explained in 3.2.5 we are treating the phase diﬀer-
ence between each core as a constant value that is frequency-independent. In reality, the light pulse
coupled into each core consists of a bandwidth of wavelengths. These diﬀerent wavelengths will
experience a diﬀerent phase change since the phase change is frequency-dependent. An important
question then becomes if this simpliﬁed approach will be detrimental to doing lensless endoscopy.
If we take a simpliﬁed equation for the laser pulse coupled into a core as:
Ein(t) = E0(t)ei(kz−ω0t)
(5.1)
The phase Φ at frequency ω can be well approximated by a Taylor series expansion about the
central frequency ω0 as:
Φ(ω) = Φ(ω0) + dΦ
dω(ω −ω0) + 1
2
d2Φ
dω2 (ω −ω0)2 + ...
(5.2)
The ﬁrst term is the constant phase, which we compensate for in the phase calibration procedure.
The coeﬃcient of the second term dΦ
dω = Φ′ has units of time and is called the group delay time.
The coeﬃcient of the third term d2Φ
dω2 = Φ′′ has units of time squared and is called the group delay
dispersion, or GDD [22]. The eﬀects of the higher-order terms will be a broadening in time of
the pulse duration, which can be detrimental to the MPEF generation through the MCF, where
ultrashort pulse durations are needed. It can also result in a phase diﬀerence between the cores
so large that the coherence length is lost. Here the coherence length is regarded as the maximum
optical path diﬀerence between two beams, which results in a discernable interference pattern [41].
However, with the current system, the contribution of these diﬀerent terms when a laser pulse
travels through each core is unknown. Based on the successful phase calibration results producing
a focus spot with 19 cores as seen in ﬁgure 4.11c it is reasonable to argue that the higher-order
dispersion terms are negligible for the given ﬁber length of 22cm. By this, it is indirectly shown
that the constant term will be the dominating phase term for shorter lengths of the MCF.
In general, the longer the ﬁber length, the more signiﬁcant the higher-order dispersion properties
will be [42]. This means that for a real-case of lensless endoscopy, where ﬁber lengths of 1-2 meters
are needed to deliver light to the colon, the higher-order dispersion terms will become essential,
and steps to analyse and compensate for these terms should be explored [42, 40, 43].

Conclusions
This thesis aims to develop a minimally invasive method for deeper and faster ﬂuorescent imaging of
colon cancer diagnostics in vivo. It has been demonstrated how the successful phase calibration and
resulting images of a focus spot with 19 cores is an essential step in the right direction. Although
there is still quite a way to realise MP-LSFM lensless endoscopy with MCFs, the results shown of
using a MCF for coherent addition of ﬁeld amplitudes of the beams exiting the diﬀerent cores and
using this to create focus spot in the distal end, indicates that MP-LSFM lensless endoscopy by
MCFs is possible using the developed setup.
By the use of a SLM, complex wavefront control at the MCF proximal end is shown. A phase
mask of microlenses with a background grating has enabled distal beam shaping and individual
phase control over each core in the MCF. A possible improvement to the large amount of power lost
because of the SLM and its diﬀraction orders is brought forward, based on changing the linear phase
ramp of the SLM. It is also shown through analysis of grating constants how aliasing in the phase
mask can be detrimental to lensless endoscopy as diﬀerent diﬀraction orders will co-propagate with
the desired 1st. Steps to avoid this by using two pinholes in the setup and keeping the constant
grating low is put forward.
The experimental design and MCF coupling conﬁguration have enabled a new power scan pro-
cedure to be carried out. By this, the possibility to couple into 7 and 19 cores as indicated in ﬁgure
4.2 and 4.3 is shown, although a successful coupling into 169 cores is not achieved as seen in ﬁgures
4.4. Analysis of the presence of higher-order modes in these ﬁgures has been discussed in sections
5.1.2, 5.3.2 which have revealed possible solutions for succeeding in coupling into a large number
of cores.
The setup has facilitated the generation of the interference fringes from exciting diﬀerent pairs
of cores with the mode-locked femtosecond laser, which has been used to do a successful phase
calibration. The results of exciting 19 cores and creating a focus spot using the phase calibrated
phase mask is seen in ﬁgure 4.11c.
A look into dispersion mechanisms in the 22cm ﬁber has revealed that the ﬁrst-order dispersion,
group delay dispersion and higher-order dispersion eﬀects seem to play a minor role for the given
ﬁber length compared to the constant phase term.
Furthermore, it has been shown how the simulation tool BPM-MATLAB and the experimental
data align, indicating its usefulness for designing future MCFs able to overcome the challenges of
lensless endoscopy still to be solved.

Outlook
The goal has from the start been to incorporate MP-LSFM with a MCF ﬁber. Even though this is
not fully achieved, I would like to elucidate how the current system is closer than one might think
and explain some of the steps needed to get there.
Automating phase calibration procedure
The ﬁrst immediate step for the project is to get the large-scale ﬁber coupling working. Since this
has already been proven in [23], it is expected to be achievable with the current system. Next
would be to develop the phase calibration procedure further. In its current state, the process is
slow and has a high degree of manual labour involved. Eﬀorts to automate the process will greatly
beneﬁt the future of the project. One way to do this is to develop a script that ﬁnds the zero phase
value in the centre of the 2D Fourier transform argument plot in 4.7b. This will probably involve
a blob detection approach [46]. It also involves real-time integrating of the images taken with a
camera in MATLAB to know whether the phase change in the blobs improves the phase calibration.
By this, the project is maturing into the ﬁelds of image analysis and real-time hardware integration.
Developing a sample imaging setup for the system
With the successful implementation of the large-scale ﬁber core coupling and an automated phase
calibration process, it should be possible to use a large number of cores in the hexagonal MCF to
generate a focus spot or light-sheet for the 2-step absorption process in MPEF. MPEF requires a
photon ﬂux in the range 1020 - 1030 photons/cm2s [34], and since more cores will produce a tighter
focus and the phase calibration process will do the same, these steps are crucial. A simple imaging
setup can easily be incorporated into the system to verify that MPEF through the hexagonal
MCF can work. One way to do this will be to place a 90-degree imaging objective just after the
collimation objective Lout (see ﬁgure 3.1). After the imaging objective, a sample mount can be
installed. By imaging some ﬂuorescent plates and collecting the ﬂuorescent light with a camera,
the ability to verify MPEF is created. With such a system incorporated, the project will develop
to involve speciality ﬁbers needed for better MPEF delivery and LSFM generation.
Realizing a MP-LSFM lensless endoscope using MCFs in a clinical setting
By incorporating the steps described above, the project will need more investigations to realize
MP-LSFM lensless endoscopy in a clinical setting. The ﬁrst investigation will be using diﬀerent
speciality ﬁbers needed for better MPEF delivery and LSFM generation. While the hexagonal ﬁber
is useful for building and testing the system, the sidelobe energy will be detrimental for a real-case
scenario. Suppressing side lobes by aperiodic MCFs is possible [47, 33, 48] . This will also have the
52
==PAGE:67==
Chapter 7. Outlook
added beneﬁt of an extended ﬁeld of view.
Secondly, dispersion pre-compensation will have to be addressed. The longer ﬁber length of 1-2
meters needed to reach the colon will increase higher-order dispersion terms in the optical ﬁber.
Steps to do pre-compensation is described in [42]. With a longer ﬁber, a concept called polarization
scrambling will also have to be investigated. Polarization scrambling comes from the fabrication
process where it is not possible to make a ﬁber core completely circular. The small ellipticity of
the cores will create a fast and slow axis of light propagation, producing the scrambling eﬀect.
polarization-maintaining ﬁbers using stress rods have been shown to mitigate this problem [45].
Collecting and analyzing the ﬂuorescent light will be the second investigation needed. While double-
clad ﬁbers have been shown able to collect ﬂuorescent light from MPEF generation [49] this ap-
proach will not be able to do light-sheet imaging that needs the light collection being perpendicular
to the illumination. There are currently no proven solutions to this challenge. Analyzing the ﬂuo-
rescent light from colon samples is needed to establish a protocol for the diagnosis of healthy versus
cancerous tissue based on the optical properties of the tissue. Molecular markers for this seems to
be working, based on detecting the COX-2 level or measuring glucose uptake of colon cancer tissue
[50, 51].
Lastly, real-time imaging should be pursued for making lensless endoscopy applicable in a hospital
setting. In 3.1 the wavefront shaping approach based on a single SLM is described. The setup can
be enhanced by adding a beam-scanning device, such as a pair of galvanometric scan mirrors, by
inserting the scan mirrors in a plane conjugated with the MCF proximal end face. The update rate
of the SLM will be limited by the reorientation time of the liquid crystals to typically around 10
Hz, the added scan mirrors will be able to add a tip and tilt to the input wavefront, thus liberating
SLM from performing this task. This will realize video-rate imaging [47].

Simulation results
The simulation results presented here are omitted due to the systems current inability to generate
experimental data to compare with the simulations. Still, they will provide valuable information
for validating the system once the large-scale core coupling and automated phase calibration are
completed. In the ﬁrst simulations B.1 and B.2 it is showed how an excitation of 169 cores resembles
a light sheet.
Here it is also shown how changing the focal length of the focusing phase from
zfocus = 400µm to zfocus = 200µm will aﬀect the light sheet generation. Characterizing this light
sheet is done for both cases to see the change in the main lobe. In the last simulation, a light
sheet’s ability to do MPEF is shown and how this requirement aﬀects the properties of the light
sheets.
