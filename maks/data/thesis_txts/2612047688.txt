Abstract
This study aims to identify how well different sensor placement techniques can cor-
rectly identify the modal parameters; natural frequencies, damping ratios, and mode
shapes. It is also investigated whether the evaluation measures; condition number,
root mean square of the modal assurance criterion, and the determinant of the Fisher
information matrix are good indicators for good sensor conﬁgurations. Sensor place-
ment techniques covered are: uniformly distribution, effective independence, maxi-
mizing the determinant of the Fisher information matrix, observability Gramian, and
a suggested measure of gross modal observability. The algorithms used in this work
is exhaustive search and sequential sensor placement. Through this work, it has been
found that the observability methods were poor choices of objective functions. The
remaining results were inconclusive, and no method was found to perform better than
all other methods. In no case was the determinant of the Fisher information matrix the
best performing method. The results were also inconclusive about using evaluation
measures as indicators for good sensor conﬁgurations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In modal analysis, one is interested in maximising the information gathered from sen-
sors, which raises the problem of optimal sensor placement (OSP). This study will
revolve around the central question of: Where should the sensors be placed on the
structure? It is of interest to use as few sensors as possible to reduce the cost (time,
money, weight) of a modal analysis. Having only a few sensors to work with (in re-
lation to the available degrees of freedom (DOF)), one needs to place the sensors in
optimal locations. Sensors in optimal locations will identify modal parameters with
low error, and limit unnecessary information, i.e. redundant information.
Optimal sensor placement is a problem in several areas. These include modal analy-
sis, control theory, structural health monitoring, and several others. In modal analysis,
one wishes to place the sensors so that modal parameter estimation and/or control are
optimised. For simple or well-known structures sensor placement may be trivial, but
for modern complex structures, with the use of novel materials or assembled struc-
tures, sensor placement is not trivial. To help place the sensors in optimal locations,
researchers have put their trust in ﬁnite element analyses (FEA). The mode shapes ex-
tracted from a modern FEA, serve as reasonable approximations to the real structure’s
mode shapes. However, due to discretisation, numerical errors, and uncertainties, one
cannot expect FEA to predict complicated modes correctly, why it is important to do
experiments. The quality of data collected from experiments is related to the positions
sensors, which explains the need for OSP.
Prior to and during this study, several sources of material have been investigated to
establish knowledge of the former and current studies within the subject. The method
of effective independence (EFI)[1] is found to be the most common method[2, 3]. The
EFI method is a speciﬁc optimisation technique for maximising the Fisher information
matrix (FIM), which is assumed to equal the inverse of the covariance matrix of the
estimation/sensor error. Generally, the FIM is widely used for OSP purposes, but for
optimisation algorithms different from EFI, a speciﬁc norm of FIM must be chosen. A
variety of information theory-based methods exists, of which information entropy is
highlighted[4]. IE is a measure of uncertainties of the estimated parameter, which one,
of course, wish to minimise. Minimising the information entropy results in maximising
the determinant of FIM, which resolve the issue of choosing which norm of FIM to op-
timise for. Other optimisation entities include, but is not exclusive to, modal assurance
criterion (MAC), condition number (CN), and observability. FIM, MAC, CN, and ob-
servability are therefore also used as measures of goodness for a given solution, where
FIM, MAC, and observability can be evaluated in different ways since they are matrix
entities. Comparisons between these evaluated entities serve as assessing which so-
lution is the best one [5, 6]. MAC and FIM are both measures of linear independence

between mode shapes but do have differences. CN describes the importance of each
mode by non-zero singular values (achieved by singular value decomposition (SVD)

from control theory and describes how many states (DOFs) can be described when only
a selection of states can be observed (sensors). If one has poor observability, one cannot
expect to extract correct modal parameters [7]. The aforementioned papers all have in
common that they seek to ﬁnd optimal sensor positions for n sensors. The number
of sensors n is set by the user, and are generally signiﬁcantly larger than the number
of modes investigated. In [8] the rank/order of the singular values determine n, but
only when the order is larger than the number of modes investigated. A grouping
tendency has been observed when algorithms are placing sensors beyond the modes
of interest [2, 9]. A wide variety of optimisation techniques have been encountered
throughout the literature study, from simple algorithms such as forward/backward
sequential sensor placement (FSSP/BSSP) to more complicated methods such as ge-
netic algorithm [10], and several other biology-inspired techniques.
In this report, the focus is on the objective functions of optimisation, of which the ob-
jects of FIM and observability are investigated. FIM will be objectiﬁed through EFI
and by taking the determinant to FIM. Observability will be objectiﬁed through the
observability Gramian (OG) and gross modal observability (GMO). GMO is made as a
suggestion in this work. Since the optimisation algorithms are not a focus point in this
report, computationally speed is sacriﬁced for simpler algorithms, i.e. FSSP and BSSP.
The optimisation techniques will be tested on a spring-mass chain, a free-free beam,
and a free-free plate. For each test structure, an ’ideal’ model is made, from which the
sensor locations will be determined, based on mode shapes extracted from the corre-
sponding FEA, and optimisation technique. Each set of sensor locations will be tested
by simulating experimental modal analyses. The simulated experimental modal anal-
yses will be performed on the ’ideal’ structures and structures with perturbed stiffness
properties. The goal is, to determine each objective function’s ability to predict modal
parameters from an uncertain structure. In order to simulate a realistic scenario, mea-
surement noise is added to the simulated acceleration measurements as well. Discrep-
ancies between parameters found by simulated experimental modal analysis and FEA
will serve as a measure of goodness. It will also be investigated, whether the sensor
conﬁgurations can estimate modal parameters which it has not been designed for, i.e.
modal parameters of modes higher than the initial modes of interest. Each method
will be evaluated using multiple assessment tools: the determinant of FIM, root mean
square of the off-diagonal elements of the MAC matrix, and the CN. Furthermore, an
attempt will be made to determine the number of sensors needed in order to estimate
the modal parameters correctly.
By applying the above methods, it will be shown, whether sensors placed from an
’ideal’ structure will sufﬁce, in order to conduct an acceptable modal analysis, on an
uncertain model. Furthermore, it will be quantiﬁed, which of the applied methods
proves best in this regard. The techniques’ ability to predict modal parameters outside
the design range will be tested as well. If a method proves to be successful in this, one
would not necessarily recompute new sensor conﬁgurations, if more modes becomes
of interest at a later state.

3.6
Validation Summary
When validating EFI, section 3.1, it was found that the implemented method was able
to recreate SCs from two different articles [20, 8]. When using the evaluation measures,
table 3.1, it was found that all the recreated values were within ±2% of the original val-
ues. The deviations can occur from a difference in the numerics generated by the used
software. The deviations are so small that the method is validated as being imple-
mented correctly. Furthermore it was found that EFI obtained the optimum solution of
det(A0) ≡det(Q1), for each tested case. This can explain its wide use. It is not given
that EFI will ﬁnd the optimum for all problems.
When validating the determinant of FIM as an objective function, section 3.2, only one
article provided readily re-creatable examples, [5]. The examples found lacked much
information, and the beam was determined by guessing on material properties and
cross-sectional dimensions. Despite these challenges, it was possible to get reasonably
close to the result in Case (a), which used a simple formulation of the covariance ma-
trix, see (2.16a). In the second example, there were larger deviations between the SCs
and the values of assessment tools. All values of assessment tools, table 3.5, deviated
in the absolute range of [8.86 ; 13.0]%, which is rather large deviations. It is unknown
whether these deviations originate from a difference in the model, or differences by

Comparing the solutions found by FSSP compared to global optima, it was found that

Investigating GMO as an objective function, it was found to have multiple ﬂaws. If a
free end is present in the model, sensors will likely be placed here due to large de-

ing no free ends and only having an odd number of modes of interest is very limiting.
Sensors placed by this algorithm may be good for estimating natural frequencies and
damping ratios since sensors are placed where large amplitudes occur. However, es-
timating mode shapes will be difﬁcult since the sensors are clustered in fewer groups
than modes of interest.

5.4
Discussion
When performing the no noise simulations, it was expected that all modal parameters
of the desired modes were possible to estimate. However, this proved only to be true
for the spring-mass chain, which were limited to 3 modes of interest. The modal pa-
rameters of natural frequencies and damping ratios were reasonably estimated for all
but the free-free beam’s two highest modes. The estimation of mode shapes for the
free-free beam and plate failed with the current models used. It is unknown why it
has been possible to estimate the ﬁrst three mode shapes of the beam, but not the three
higher modes, and why none of the plate modes was estimated to a satisfactory de-
gree. There seem to be three possible explanations to this issue. First, the frequency
response functions created by modalfrf() can be incorrect. In this regard, it is most
likely that the phase information is wrong, and that the following use of modalfit()
then predicts wrong signs (+/-) for the mode shapes at the given sensor positions.
However, wrong signs do not seem to be the issue, since the 4th, 5th, and 6th modes
of the beam, seem to resemble the ﬁrst mode shape. This leads to the second possible
explanation. Maybe the 4th, 5th, and 6th modes are highly dominated by the ﬁrst mode
and is therefore not able to be properly identiﬁed. The structural damping is high,
and in the time domain, the displacements related to higher modes will go towards
zero fast. The structural damping is not that high for the plate, and for that model, no
mode shapes are reasonably estimated. Thirdly, it is possible that due to the increasing
complexity of the models, the corresponding discretisations has been too coarse for the
problem at hand. Unfortunately, it was not possible to reﬁne the discretisation of the
plate, since the computer used, did not have enough available memory (RAM).
When performing the simulations with noise added, it is expected that the deviations
increase with the level of noise and that the mode shapes become distorted, compared
to what was seen in the no noise simulations. The increase of deviations seen, in a very
small scale for the spring-mass chain, where the deviations with respect to natural

increase. It is believed that the wider range of frequencies, that are excited and for

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This study aims to identify which optimal sensor placement methods best estimate the
modal parameters: natural frequencies, damping ratios, and mode shapes. Through-
out this report, different optimal sensor placement methods have been explained and
explored. This report has been focused on the Fisher information matrix and observ-
ability but also covers the condition number and the modal assurance criterion, which
are commonly used in this ﬁeld. The methods’ ability to estimate the modal parameters
has been investigated through 3 different test cases. The ﬁrst case considered simulat-
ing the structure and extracting data corresponding to the sensor conﬁgurations. The
extracted data is then used for an experimental modal analysis to estimate the modal
parameters. In the second case, three different noise levels were added to the extracted
data, to investigate its effect on the ability to estimate the modal parameters with noisy
measurements. The third case considered the ability to estimate modal parameters
beyond those initially of interest. If the ﬁrst three modes are of interest originally,
how well does a corresponding sensor conﬁguration then estimate the fourth and ﬁfth
mode’s modal parameters?
In chapter 3 it was found that using the observability Gramian and gross modal ob-
servability were poor choices for optimal sensor placement. The observability Gramian
failed when the number of DOFs became too large for a computer to handle the numer-
ics. A new measure has been formulated: gross modal observability. Unfortunately, it
was found that gross modal observability failed since it could not distribute sensors in
a meaningful way across the tested models. For a ﬁnely discretised system, all sensors
would most likely be placed closely together, effectively only measuring a single point.
Placing all sensors closely together would disable the ability to predict mode shapes.
In the ﬁrst test case, it was expected that all modal parameters could be estimated with
small deviations. There were large differences in the ability to estimate the modal pa-
rameters across the different models. The simplest model, a spring-mass chain had
all modal parameters estimated well within the set limits, and the mode shapes could
easily be interpreted from the estimates. The model of medium complexity, a free-
free beam, had modal parameters exceeding the set limits. Only the ﬁrst three mode
shapes, of a desired 6, were identiﬁed from the estimates. The most complex model, a
free-free plate, had the estimated natural frequencies and damping ratios well within
the set limits, but non of the mode shapes could be identiﬁed from the estimates. It is
unknown why the differences between the models are so large. It is possible that the
model discretisations were too coarse to give reasonable representations of the struc-
tures.
In the second case, it was expected that the deviations of the estimates increased with
47
==PAGE:56==
DTU
the level of noise. In this case, there were found to be large differences between the
models as well. The effect of noise on the spring-mass chain was insigniﬁcant, only af-
fecting the estimates in a minuscule amount. The effect of noise on the free-free beam
was large, and any noise level had a signiﬁcant effect. Regarding the mode shape esti-
mates, these changed when noise was added, but they did not change with the noise
level. The sensor conﬁguration derived by effective independence was the only one
that could portray the mode shapes in a somewhat recognisable way. However, one
would not be able to recreate the mode shapes from these estimates correctly. The
free-free plate responded to noise by a minor increase of the deviations with respect to
natural frequencies and a large increase of deviations with respect to damping ratios.
Since the mode shapes could not be estimated from the previous case, these were not
considered here. All natural frequency estimates were within limits, but the damping
ratio estimates for some modes deviated far beyond the limit.
Only the spring-mass chain was considered in the third case since the higher mode
shapes of the beam could not be estimated. The sensor conﬁgurations derived by
maximising the determinant to Fisher information matrix and uniform distribution
were able to estimate the natural frequencies within ±5% of the true values, where the
method of effective independence failed to stay within this limit for the highest (5th)
mode. All methods were able to predict the damping ratios within an order of mag-
nitude. It was further shown that the sensor conﬁgurations derived by the effective
independence method and maximising the determinant of the Fisher information ma-
trix by forward sequential sensor placement, did not predict the mode shapes. Only
the uniformly distributed sensors were able to predict the higher mode shapes cor-
rectly.
From the above, the method of effective independence performs best when only the
modes of interest are excited or solved for. When simulating the beam with added
noise, it was the only method which vaguely estimated the ﬁrst three mode shapes,
where the other methods failed. However, the effective independence method failed,
compared to the other methods when considering modes beyond the initial interest
on which the sensor conﬁgurations are based. The deviations of the effective indepen-
dence method were larger, and it could not predict the mode shapes. It makes sense
that the uniformly distributed sensors perform best in this case since effective inde-
pendence and maximising the determinant of the Fisher information matrix are based
on the mode shapes initially of interest. In contrast, the uniform distribution is "blind"
to mode shapes. This is what is shown from the presented results, but to make a gen-
eral claim of this tendency, a more thorough study of this exact behaviour must be
made. Furthermore, there is no clear answer to which of the evaluations measures can
indicate a good sensor conﬁguration. In the case of added noise to the beam, the best
sensor conﬁguration produce a large determinant to the simpliﬁed Fisher information
matrix and the condition number. In the out-of-scope case, the best sensor conﬁgu-
ration produces a small determinant of the simpliﬁed Fisher information matrix and
condition number.
For future work within this subject, it would be interesting to investigate the limita-
tions of sensor conﬁgurations further when estimating modal parameters out of scope.
This could be done as in this report, or by generating sensor conﬁgurations from the
1st, 2nd, and 4th mode, and then try to estimate the modal parameters of the ﬁrst four
modes (mode 1, 2, 3, and 4). It would be interesting to explore other uncertainties of

ble boundary conditions could be varied by changing the stiffness of the boundaries.
No boundaries are rigid, and usually, boundaries have more agency over the mode
shapes than changing the stiffness of elements in a ﬁnite element analysis. It could
not be determined in this work whether the condition number, root mean square, or

Appendix A
Information Entropy
Information entropy (IE) is a method derived from information theory and statistics. IE
is a measure of uncertainty in the model parameters. IE is a scalar by a single equation
that has a clear target for optimisation compared to MAC and FIM. This section is
developed as a summary of section 3 and 4, from [4], and should provide sufﬁcient
understanding for the reader. It is assumed that the reader has common knowledge of
statistics, but some concepts covered in this section may be unknown for a vibration
analyst. Therefore, references are given to material which explains these concepts in
greater detail.
Statistical Representation of Model Parameters
To arrive at the applied expression for IE, one starts by considering the dataset D =
⟨us(s), z(s)⟩, where us(s) ∈Rno and z(s) ∈Rni is the output and input respectively,
for s = 1, 2, . . . , ns being the index of samples, ns is the number of samples, no is the
number of outputs, and ni is the number of inputs. Since IE is a measure of parametric
uncertainty, i.e. uncertainties of parameters such as stiffness and density, we introduce
the parameters as θ ∈Rm, where m is the number of parameters of interest, or in the
case of sensor positioning, the number of modes of interest. With respect to sensor
positioning the model parameters are equivalent to the modal coordiantes, i.e. θ ≡q.
The entire sampled system response are deﬁned as x(s, θ) ∈Rn (x ≡Φq, section 2.1.2),
and the model prediction error (uncertainties + noise) are introduced as n(s, θ) ∈Rn,
where n is the number of DOF. From the above we can then write the measured data

Perturbed Element Stiffness Matrices
In this case, the stiffness matrix of each model is perturbed by a small amount. The
stiffness matrix is perturbed since this can account for uncertainties of physical dimen-
sions, Young’s modulus, and Poisson’s ratio. The perturbation is made by multiplying
each element stiffness matrix by a random scalar, generated from a Gaussian normal
distribution with mean µ = 1 and standard deviation σ = 0.04. The system stiffness
matrix is assembled, and the eigenvalue problem is solved to ﬁnd the natural frequen-
cies, damping ratios and mode shapes. These are needed since the perturbed system
is the one which we are trying to estimate. The simulations are then performed, and
the response corresponding to the original SCs are extracted (table 4.2). This is done
30 times, and for each iteration, the deviations between true and estimated modal pa-
rameters are computed. The average of the deviations is computed and presented in
table B.1. Comparing these deviations to the ones found for case 1 (no noise simula-
tion) in table 5.1, it is seen that the results are very similar, and therefore produce trivial
results, i.e. it is possible to estimate the natural frequencies and damping ratios using
simulated EMA. Because small perturbations of the stiffness do not alter the mode
shapes signiﬁcantly (from which the SCs are generated), we do not see any signiﬁcant
change of the estimated modal parameters.

%% Effective Independence Method
function [idx,ass_crit_val] = EFI_2(Phi,N_o)
idx = 1:size(Phi,1);
% Initialising sensor indexes/locations (all DOFs)
n_o = size(Phi,1);
% Current number of sensors
while n_o>N_o
FIM = transpose(Phi(idx,:))*Phi(idx,:);
% Simplified Fisher Information Matrix (FIM)
[Psi, lambda] = eig(FIM);
% Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are computed
G
= (Phi(idx,:)*Psi).*(Phi(idx,:)*Psi);
% Creating G by the current sensors
F_E = G*inv(lambda);
% Creating F_E by the current sensors
% Effectiveness vector
E_D = zeros(size(idx,2),1);
% Initialising the effectiveness vector E_D
for i = 1:size(idx,2)
E_D(i) = sum(F_E(i,:));
% Computing each element of the E_D
end
% Removing the index of lowest contribution/effectiveness
[~,delete_idx] = min(abs(E_D));
% Finding the index of least effectiveness
idx(delete_idx) = [];
% Removing the index with lowest effectiveness
n_o = n_o - 1;
end
