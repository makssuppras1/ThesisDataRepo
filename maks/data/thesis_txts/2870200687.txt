Abstract
This master’s project dealt with a reinforcement technology for asphalt pavements having
the potential to delay the generation and lessen the severity of rutting and fatigue
cracking. Specifically, the work focused on asphalt grid reinforcement technology, which
refers to planar products having a mesh-like geometry and composed of high-modulus
materials; these grids are designed for installation in asphalt concrete layers.
The stated objective of the work was twofold: (i) create a new laboratory procedure for
the quantifying the grid effect. The sought effect is defined by a single unitless factor
named κ that captures the grid’s influence on the viscoelastic properties of the asphalt
concrete that encapsulates it; and (ii) apply and demonstrate the new procedure over
one asphalt concrete type reinforced by one specific grid product.
In general terms, the new procedure calls for: (i) producing a cylindrical asphalt con-
crete specimen for uniaxial testing with a height-to-diameter ratio of 2:1 including a
grid at mid-height; (ii) instrumenting the specimen with two sets of LVDTs, each set
designed to capture the reinforcement effect differently; (iii) loading the specimen under
harmonic (compressive) excitation spanning different temperatures and frequencies; and
(iv) creating two mastercurves based on the two LVDT sets, and extracting the κ factor.
Overall, the new laboratory procedure was successfully carried out and is deemed viable.
The derived κ factor in the demonstration test was validated against a full-scale field
experiment conducted in a separate study. Ultimately, the new procedure is flexible to
cover any asphalt concrete type and any grid reinforcement products.

1.1.1
Asphalt pavement distresses
Pavement infrastructure is designed to facilitate the safe and eﬀicient transportation of
people and goods on land, with asphalt pavements as the most common type [1]. In
general terms, an asphalt pavement structure is composed of compacted granular layers
overlaid by layers of Asphalt Concrete (AC).
Repeated traﬀic loads and environmental factors cause asphalt pavements to deteri-
orate over time, creating a variety of distresses that lessen pavements’ functionality.
The Pavement condition index (PCI) system [2] is a common approach to quantifying
pavement distress and serves as the decision basis for maintenance and repair activi-
ties. This system defines rutting and fatigue cracking, which are the two most prevalent
load-associated distresses [3].
Rutting refers to permanent deformation in the wheel paths of a pavement, caused by
repeated traﬀic loadings. Rutting is increased under conditions of high traﬀic volumes
and high-temperature levels. It leads to deficient/incomplete surface drainage and so
increased risk of skidding [4].
Fatigue cracking (or alligator cracking) refers to the breakage and fragmentation of the
AC created by repetitive traﬀic loadings [5]. These cracks reduce the capacity of the
pavements, allowing for water infiltration that accelerates degradation [6].
This work deals with a reinforcement technology designed for installation in AC layers,
having the potential to postpone the appearance and severity of the two above-mentioned
distress types. Specifically, the work is focused on asphalt grid reinforcement technology,
which refers to planar products having a mesh-like geometry and composed of high-
modulus materials.
1.1.2
Effect of pavement reinforcement
If reinforcement grids are embedded in asphalt layers, strain levels are reduced, and load-
related damages are diminished [7]. More specifically, reinforcement can delay rutting by
reducing vertical AC strains; also, reinforcement can delay fatigue cracking by reducing
horizontal (tensile) AC strains.
Research indicates that reinforced asphalt pavements exhibit lower permanent deforma-
tion rates under repeated loading, improving their long-term rutting resistance [8].
The presence of a reinforcement layer creates a thin coat in which aggregates can be
accumulated so when facing bending moments it’s the grid that takes most of it.
In terms of fatigue resistance, reinforcement increases pavement durability by delaying
crack initiation and propagation. The grid layer reduce tensile forces that are concen-
trated in the crack area so they extend the fatigue life of the pavement [9]. Studies have
shown that reinforced pavements experience a slower rate of fatigue damages, resulting
in higher PCI values [10].
Reinforcement of pavements also confer economical benefits because they decrease the
need of maintenance. By reducing the frequency of major rehabilitation works, reinforced

1.2
Motivation
The study was motivated by the need for a controlled laboratory method to evaluate
the carbon fiber grid reinforcement in asphalt AC layers. To analyze the advantages of
carbon fiber grids, earlier research, such that done by Julius [12] and Partl [13], has
mostly relied on field assessments and modeling approaches. Other laboratory studies
observed the effect of the grid by effectuating laboratory tests aiming the failure of
the specimen.Even while these tests provide more information on the improval of grids,
it usually struggles to isolate specific elements (such as temperature or load frequency)
and limit other influences (such as additional loads) on pavement behavior. However,this
laboratory experiment offer a controlled environment and apply small strains so LVE
theory can be applied.
The contributions of carbon fiber grids, such the relaxation modulus, may be examined
from laboratory testing.
The improvement of mechanistic models depends on these
empirical discoveries.By simulating a palette of environmental circumstances (different
temperatures), laboratory-controlled experiments make it possible to evaluate how well
carbon fiber grids work across a range of climates, traﬀic volumes, and grid locations.
In conclusion, a technique for measuring the mechanical consequences of carbon fiber
grid reinforcement in asphalt pavements was provided by including laboratory testing
for small strains into the evaluation process. By supporting field research, this technique
promotes pavement reinforcement studies and leads to improved pavement design.
1.3
Thesis structure
This thesis contains six chapters and two appendices.
Chapter 1 provides an intro-
duction of the topic, emphasizing the need for strengthening AC layers in a grid and
motivation for this study. Chapter 2 is a literature review of time and frequency domain,
viscoelasticity, and lab methods of quantifying reinforcing effects.Chapter 3 defines this
study’s objectives and describes the process for determining the reinforcing parameter κ
using dynamic modulus testing.Chapter 4 describes test procedure, test methods, and
equipment calibration for lab testing of unreinforced and reinforced AC. Chapter 5 con-
tains an analysis of test data, for example, master curve generation and calculation of
reinforcing parameter κ. Chapter 6 gives a summary of key results, an evaluation of the
success of grid strengthening, and directions for additional studies. Lastly, Appendices
A and B consist of additional data, e.g., raw measurements and κ calculation forms.

Boltzmann superposition principle
The Boltzmann Superposition Principle (BSP) is the concept in linear viscoelasticity
(LVE), describing how the current strain of a material is the cumulative effect of past
applied stress. The principle is mathematically formulated as an integral that accounts
for historical stresses in materials.
A material is viscoelastic (VE), if the response to a load it presents both elastic (instan-
taneous recovery) and viscous (time-dependent flow) characteristics. Figure 2 illustrates
this concept.

dependent. To understand and predict this behavior, two fundamental tests are com-
monly employed: the creep test and the stress relaxation test.

Unlike time-domain analysis, which examines stress and strain evolution over

modulus.

2.5
Knowledge gaps
Existing research on grid-reinforced asphalt pavements relies on destructive laboratory
testing methods to evaluate improvements in rutting resistance and fatigue performance.
While these tests provide additional information into the mechanical behavior of rein-
forced mixtures, they permanently damage the specimens, preventing further evaluation
under different conditions. Information about the effects of reinforcement effects over
time and the applicability of LVE theory remains uncertain.
The use of non-destructive laboratory tests could address these limitations by allowing
repeated measurements on the same specimen under controlled conditions. If reinforce-
ment effectiveness is accomplished using small strain levels, the asphalt mixture remains
within its LVE range, ensuring that material properties do not change due to excessive
loading. Small-strain testing would allow researchers to quantify strain recovery and
reinforcement factor over multiple test cycles without introducing permanent deforma-
tion.
There are limited studies have applied small-strain cyclic loading, to compute
reinforcement effects in asphalt mixtures.
Furthermore, while dynamic modulus testing provides a framework for evaluating mate-
rial behavior across different loading frequencies, current protocols often suggest higher
strain levels, which may induce damage before the full VE response can be analyzed.
Adjusting laboratory methodologies to incorporate non-destructive frequency tests at
small strain amplitudes would allow a more accurate representation of reinforcement
contributions to VE behavior while keeping specimen intact.
The absence of direct validation between destructive and non-destructive test results
is another research gap.
Although the association between non-destructive modulus
characterisation and long-term reinforcement effects cannot be measured, destructive
testing can validate the reinforcement effects.
Addressing these knowledge gaps requires more attention towards nondestructive labo-
ratory procedures that allow repeated material evaluations under controlled conditions.
Implementing small-strain dynamic testing methods within the LVE framework could
offer another approach to quantify the reinforcement effects over time, minimizing spec-
imen destruction, and improving the accuracy of predictive pavement performance mod-
els.

3
Objectives and methodology
3.1
Objective
The main objective of this work is to develop and outline a new laboratory procedure
for the measurement of the κ parameter as defined by Nielsen [29], see also Equation
37. This parameter embodies the grid reinforcement effect – as can be seen in Figure
11. A secondary objective of this work is to demonstrate and validate the newly pro-
posed laboratory procedure over one AC type and one grid reinforcement type. A third
objective is to openly share all laboratory-collected data to enable interested others to
revisit the measurements and employ different analysis techniques.
3.2
Methodology
The methodology (Fig. 12) for determining the κ parameter includes several phases:
specimen fabrication, non-standard instrumentation with LVDTs, cyclic loading in uni-
axial compression, complex modulus mastercurve construction, and calculation of the
reinforcement factor κ. Figure 12 offers a graphical representation of the methodology.
Figure 12: Methodology of calculating k factor
The first step is the preparation of uniform-density cylindrical AC specimens along both
the width and height. Specimen homogeneity is needed to obtain reliable results. To
achieve this, AC mix was first densified, in several layers, using a gyratory compactor.
Then, specimens were obtained by coring and trimming the gyratory pills.
The second step is to measure and calculate the linear VE properties. This is accom-
plished by applying sinusoidal loading to the AC specimens at different frequencies and
temperatures. The resulting stress-strain response is then used to determine the complex
modulus E∗and the time-temperature shifting properties.
The procedure is carried out for specimens with and without a reinforcement grid. The
presence of the grid is expected to influence the complex modulus within the Zone-of-
Influence. The overall idea herein is to measure near and away from this zone. Ulti-
mately, the κ parameter is obtained by analyzing the difference in the complex modulus
from different sections of the specimen.

Importance of a 2:1 Specimen Aspect Ratio
The 2:1 height-to-diameter aspect
ratio is important to improve the accuracy of stress and strain measurements in a uniaxial
test. This ratio minimizes the effects of end constraints, which cause stress and strain
gradients near the contact surfaces of the specimen. At the ends, where the loading plates
make contact, frictional forces and rigid constraints prevent the material from deforming
freely. These effects generate localized stress concentrations and strain discrepancies in
these regions, which can distort the results if the specimen is too short. [36]
For specimens with an aspect ratio smaller than 2:1, the non-uniform stress state at the
ends dominates a larger proportion of the total volume, making it diﬀicult to isolate
the uniform stress region in the central part. However, excessively tall specimens (with
an aspect ratio greater than 2:1) are going to buckle or to deform lateral under axial
loading, which destroys the uniaxial nature of the test. [37]
The 2:1 ratio is in balance by ensuring that the central portion of the specimen is suf-
ficiently far from the edge effects, allowing stresses to redistribute before reaching the
measurement zone. This ratio also reduces the likelihood of end-induced strain discrep-
ancies coming into the region of interest. Therefore, the 2:1 aspect ratio provides the
optimal geometry for achieving uniform axial stresses while maintaining the structural
stability necessary for dynamic modulus testing.
4.1.2
Gyratory compaction
This machine features a chamber where the mold is placed. The compactor operates by
raising a piston from the bottom and so it applies pressure to the asphalt mix within
the mold. Specifically, a resistance mechanism at the top of the mold is coupled with a
plate at the bottom, which is then rotated by a piston with 1.16deg. The piston pushes
the plate (on which is situated the specimen) and applies load until the resistance read
is 600MPa.
To achieve a homogeneous material during compaction, initial efforts focused on apply-
ing the same number of gyrations uniformly across the specimen. With this idea, the
middle layers of the specimen had higher density than to the layers near the edges. This
uneven distribution of density indicated that the middle layers were compacted more
effectively, while the edge layers were under-compacted, resulting in a specimen that
lacked homogeneity.
Another strategy is to expose the layers to different number of gyrations. This method
was good only to reach maximum theoretical density involving 100 gyrations for each
layer. This method aims to reduce density variations within the specimen and achieve
a more uniform and homogeneous structure. The approach was explained in detail in
subsection 5.1.
One problem it can appear during compaction is the edge effects caused by the rigid
mold. Aggregates located at the edges are often not optimally placed, resulting in local
variations in density. To reduce the effect, the initial sample was fabricated with a height
of 230 mm and a diameter of 150 mm. Afterwards, the specimen was trimmed to a final
diameter of 100 mm, removing the outer edges and minimizing the influence of edge
effects. This method is consistent with observations in the existing literature on the

4.3.3
Determine the complex modulus
The complex modulus measurement constitutes a crucial component of the mechanical
behavior analysis of the test pieces under dynamic loading. The test machine provides
the basis for this analysis through displacement and load measurement. Direct analysis
of this information has the added benefit of precision and flexibility, as discussed below.
The raw data from the machine will include built-in defects such as non-linearity and
noise that are created during the test. Even though the machine produces processed
information, this will be based on algorithms that are predefined and perhaps will not
capture the precise characteristics of the test pieces or the precise test conditions. If
the raw data are inspected independent of the machine, an adapted strategy can be
employed to define the behavior of the material correctly.
Instead of machine-computed values, the raw data was subjected to a specially formu-
lated mathematical fit. The method has the following advantages:
• Flexibility: The math function can be tailored to the experimental conditions to
match them closely to guarantee the model that fits the observed behavior.
• Noise Reduction: Curve fitting to the raw data smoothes the randomness and
inconsistencies of the noise to enable simpler analysis of the material’s response.
• Parameter Control: The parameter control throughout the process of fitting
offers direct control over the desired parameters such as stress, strain, and phase
lag to ensure that the values are calculated based upon the characteristics that are
specific to the specimen.
After curve-fitting the raw data to the provided mathematical model presented in subsec-
tion 5.2, further analysis was conducted in subsection 5.3 to find the crucial parameters
such as the elastic modulus at each frequency and temperature. The independent anal-
ysis has the advantage that the results are taken directly from the raw experimental
data rather than machine assumptions or approximations. The technique improves the

4.4.1
Uniaxial test
A uniaxial test is employed to determine how the material responds to an imposed force
along one axis along the longitudinal direction. The test consists of applying a constant
force along the test specimen that causes the desired stress and strain that are perfectly
parallel to the imposed force. The basic intention is to ensure that the stresses within
the middle region of the test specimen are uniform and free from the disturbance created
by boundary effects along the two ends. [44]
To maintain uniform stress during a uniaxial test, the test specimen is designed to
prevent large concentrations of stress near the contacts of the loading plates. At the end
regions where the force is applied, the force distribution can be non-uniform to some
extent through contacts between the deformable material and the rigid loading plates.
[45] As the force travels through the bulk of the test specimen, however, the force slowly
redistributes to create more uniform stress within the center region. This is the region
where the measurement is made. If the test specimen geometry and loading conditions
are under control, the center region experiences essentially uniform axial stress and strain
to generate precise and consistent results.
4.4.2
Chosing the LVDT gauge lenght
Representative Volume Element (RVE) concept determines the macroscopic behavior of
an individual material element. The minimum volume within which the material can
be treated statistically as homogeneous to the extent that the behavior characterizes
the entire material correctly is an RVE. For the situation where the asphalt mixtures
are geometrically heterogeneous with respect to the aggregates, the binder, and the
voids, the appropriate selection of the size of an RVE is crucial to arrive at meaningful
information.
The selection to use the span of 70 mm was made in this research based on the theory of
the RVE. If the span was small, there was a greater likelihood that the measurement zone
was to be affected by heterogeneities (Figure 33), such as clusters of large aggregates or
zones of dense bitumen. This will result in unrepresentative measurements and greater
variations in the results.
Making the span 70 mm, makes the measurement volume
represent the whole material to a larger extent, as it has a close ratio of the various
components, therefor decreasing the effect of irregularities that occur within certain
zones. This span makes the information obtained using the LVDT represent the average
behavior of the asphalt mixture, making the results more precise and reliable.

Mastercurve construction is an important process for describing material VE behavior
over a very wide range of loading frequency and temperature. This chapter provides a
description of the mathematical basis for building the mastercurve and highlights the use
of time-temperature superposition (TTSP) and representation in terms of the complex
modulus.
The TTSP allows data obtained over a range of different temperatures to be shifted
horizontally along the frequency axis to create a single, uninterrupted curve. This is
calculated from a temperature-dependent shift factor, aT , using the following equation:
log aT = −C1(T −T0)

(49)

of the shift factor to temperature changes.
Equation (49) ensures that the VE response of the material at various temperatures
can be represented as a single curve, enabling better interpretation of the material’s
performance over a wide range of conditions. The Huet-Sayegh model is a rheological
model used to describe the VE behavior of asphalt materials. Originally proposed by
Huet [49] and later expanded upon by Sayegh [50], this model provides a mathematical
framework for representing the complex modulus of bituminous mixtures over a wide
range of frequencies and temperatures. The model was further refined and validated by
Pronk [51], who demonstrated its applicability to master curves of asphalt mixes.

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
6
Conclusion and future work
A new laboratory test procedure was successfully developed to determine the reinforce-
ment effect of the grids in AC layers, in terms of the κ factor (see Section 2.3). The test
method was created to determine the gain in the complex modulus under reinforcement
based on the comparison of the dynamic modulus along different positions of the test
specimen.
The new test procedure was demonstrated over one type of AC and one type of reinforce-
ment grid. A factor κ = 2.48 was found, which agrees with the results of Nielsen et al.
[29] that were obtained in a full-scale field test with the same AC type and grid type.
The agreement between the laboratory results and the field results provides a strong
validation that the new procedure is successful at obtaining the reinforcement influence.
Overall, the research demonstrates that the reinforcement effect of the grid can be quan-
tified in the laboratory and that the effect is positive, with a potential for prolonging
the remaining life of AC layers. The reinforcement changes the relaxation function of
the AC within the ZoI. This is shown in the frequency domain by Figure 54.
Three suggestions for future work are: (i) assess the procedure’s repeatability by per-
forming the testing several times with the same AC type and grid type. This will allow
the quantification of the variability in κ; (ii) apply the procedure to other types of AC
and reinforcement grids to establish the method’s universality; and (iii) in order to re-
duce the time spent performing the tests, temperatures bellow 0 can be removed. The
reason is that κ is defined at low frequencies and high temperatures.

The results and analyses of this study have been structured to ensure replicability and
transparency for future research. To this end, a digital folder is attached to the submis-

experimental work. This section outlines the contents and instructions for utilizing the
shared resources, focusing on the process for analyzing the maximum theoretical density
test.
B.1
Maximum theoretical density analysis
The attached digital folder includes the necessary files and templates for analyzing the
maximum theoretical density. These resources are intended to streamline the calculation
process and ensure consistency in future analyses. The folder contains:
• A template file named Template calculation.xlsx.
• Raw data files in .DAT format, generated by the testing machine.
Procedure for Using the Files The analysis of maximum theoretical density can be
performed using the following steps:
1. Open the .DAT files in a compatible program (e.g., Microsoft Excel) and convert
them to .csv format.
2. Copy the results from the height measurements into the template file, specifically
into the cells B11:B111.
3. Update the mass of the specimen (in kg) in cell B6.
4. Use the Solver tool to adjust the following parameters:
• Cell B1,
• Cells B3:B5.
The objective is to set the value of cell B7 to 1.
Generated Plots Once the calculations are completed using the template, two plots
will be automatically generated:
• Density vs.
Infinite Number of Gyrations: This plot illustrates how the
density approaches the maximum theoretical density as the number of gyrations
increases.
• Height vs. Infinite Number of Gyrations: This plot shows the compaction
behavior of the specimen as a function of the number of gyrations.
Output Results The maximum theoretical density is displayed in cell D1 of the tem-
plate file, highlighted in green font for easy identification.
Future work recommendations The digital folder provides a robust starting point
for further research. Future work can use the included files to:
• Expand on the analysis with additional experimental data or alternative specimen
types.

Complex modulus calculation
The calculation of the complex modulus for AC specimens is facilitated using the re-
sources provided in the digital folder named ”Calculation of Complex Modulus”.
This folder contains a comprehensive template and raw data files to streamline the cal-
culation process. The following instructions detail the steps for completing the analysis.
The folder includes:
• A template file named Asphalt 30 55-105 MID_Runtime.xlsx.
• Raw data files in .csv format, containing the experimental data for various fre-
quencies.
The steps for calculating the elasticity modulus are as follows:
1. Open the provided .csv files and copy all the data into the template file.
2. Paste the data into the range A2:F10001 in the template.
• Replace the formula in column C with =-B to ensure correct sign inversion of
the load data.
3. Filter the data by frequency. Each frequency should be processed separately.
4. Copy the data from columns A to F and paste it into the respective frequency-
specific sheet.
• Each sheet in the template is named after the respective frequency.
• For each frequency, only the last 10 cycles (a maximum of 300 rows) should
be pasted.
5. Use the Solver tool to minimize the error in the calculation:
• Set the objective cell C6 to be minimum.
• Adjust the variables in the ranges C1:F4 and C7:F7.
Outputs Generated Upon completing the calculations, the following results will be
displayed in the template:
• Strain Amplitude: Located in cell L5.
• Dynamic Modulus: Located in cell L6.
• Phase Lag: Located in cell L7.
Error Analysis The error values are calculated and displayed in the following locations:
• Errors for load and each LVDT are shown in cells C6:F6. These values should
be minimized during the Solver optimization process.

B.3
Creating the mastercurve
The mastercurve for asphalt concrete specimens is a critical tool for analyzing viscoelas-
tic behavior across varying temperatures and frequencies. The process of creating the
mastercurve is facilitated using a Python script and a designated template file.
All
necessary files are provided in the folder named ”Creating the Mastercurve”.
The folder includes:
• A Python script named Overview.py.
• A template file named MastercurveWorksheetBOT.xlsx.
The process of creating the mastercurve involves the following steps:
Step 1: Running the Python Script
• Open the Overview.py script in a Python environment.
• Specify the directory containing the .xlsx files in line 5 of the script.
• Execute the script. The program will:
1. Navigate through all Excel files in the specified directory.
2. Extract data from each sheet in each file, including:
– Temperature,
– Peak-to-peak strain values,
– Position of the LVDT,
– Frequency,
– Dynamic modulus,
– Strain amplitude,
– Phase lag (in radians),
– Alpha errors for each LVDT and load.
3. Create a summary of the extracted data in a new file.
Step 2:
Filtering and Averaging Data
After the Python script generates the
summary, the following steps should be performed:
• Filter the data by:
1. Position of the LVDT,

2. One specific frequency,
3. One specific temperature.
• Calculate the average of the three peak-to-peak strain values for the selected
frequency and temperature.
• Record the corresponding Dynamic modulus and Phase lag values.
• Repeat this procedure for each temperature and frequency in the dataset.
Step 3: Populating the Template File

B.4
Calculation of κ Value
The final step in the analysis involves calculating the Kappa value, which provides
critical insights into the variation of elasticity modulus across different pavement layers.
The required resources and instructions for this calculation are included in the folder
named ”Calculation of Kappa Value”.
The folder contains:
• A Maple template file named Kappa calculation from test.mw.
To calculate the Kappa value, follow these steps:
1. Open the Kappa calculation from test.mw file in Maple.
2. Input the low-frequency elasticity modulus values for the following pavement layers:
• Top Layer: Etop,
• Middle Layer: Emiddle,
• Bottom Layer: Ebottom.
These values should be taken from the results of the previous step (mastercurve
calculation).
