Abstract
The advancements in unmanned aerial vehicle technology has made it possible to survey
smaller areas of interest (0 −300.000m2) with a higher accuracy and resolution than the
traditional aerial surveying methods at a more favorable price. Digital elevation models
are produced from aerial surveys and are used in various settings, such as infrastruc-
ture planning or flood mapping. Two different methods of creating elevation models are
assessed in this thesis, LiDAR and ground control point based photogrammetry meth-
ods. The two methods make use of different georeferencing techniques, namely indirect-
and direct georeferencing. Data is collected from three survey sites in Denmark with two
different unmanned aerial vehicles, one carrying a LiDAR payload, another with an imag-
ing sensor and an on-board real-time-kinematic GNSS system which adds high accuracy
location tags to the images obtained. In order to validate the models, elevated ground
control points are set up on the survey sites, these are not used when adjusting the mod-
els and are easily visible on the elevation models. The software used for processing is
Agisoft Metashape and Python scripts written by the author. Accuracy and processing
costs are assessed for each of the methods and the effect of eliminating physical ground
control points are investigated. Photogrammetric surveys without ground control points
are immensely time saving, as the time spent measuring and setting out ground control
points is saved. The findings from this thesis suggests that it is possible to obtain higher
accuracy using unmanned aerial vehicles than traditional aeroplane surveying. Further-
more photogrammetry models created without ground control points can be viable if the
horizontal accuracy is of higher importance than the vertical accuracy. In general the
ground control based photogrammetry method is the most reliable method, delivering ac-
curate results from all three sites, while improving both the vertical and horizontal accuracy
of the photogrammetry models without ground control points. The LiDAR models varies
in accuracy and needs more testing, where one of the models is more accurate than the
photogrammetry models and other models performs worse.

Introduction
Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) surveys have become increasingly popular over the last
two decades. As commercial UAV-platforms has become cheaper and the quality of the
sensors mounted on the UAVs has improved, this provides new possibilities for both recre-
ational pilots and professionals. A clear advantage of UAV-surveying is the competitive
pricing. [1]
Figure 1.1: The size of a survey area vs. the price of mapping. Figure from DroneApps
[1].
For smaller survey areas, UAVs are also superior in resolution as the vehicle is closer to
the ground than manned vehicles (planes, balloons, kites etc.)
Digital elevation models are useful products derived from UAV data collection. Different
kinds of elevation models can be used for various applications. Surface models can be
useful when modelling cities and infrastructure or even used for mapping vegetation or
other populations, where terrain models can be used in flood or drainage modelling.
Throughout this thesis two methods of UAV-surveying for digital elevation model produc-
tion, will be analyzed and compared in order to assess the accuracy, strengths and weak-
nesses of each processing method. The two main methods are based on LiDAR mea-
surements and photogrammetry.
Different processing solutions do exist (commercial) for photogrammetry processing, but
due to the dissimilarities in LiDAR systems and LiDAR setups, one solution does not fit all
when processing LiDAR data. Along with the surveying and analysis done for the thesis,
a Python script is designed in order to process and georeference LiDAR data from DTU
Spaces LiDAR setup (DJI M600 UAV and Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR).

Photogrammetry data is collected using a DJI Phantom 4 RTK UAV, where each image
is tagged with a high-accuracy GNSS stamp, which might cut the time and effort spent

the accuracy needed for the task at hand. The direct georeferencing method used in Li-
DAR processing eliminates the requirement of ground control points, whereas the indirect

Three individual sites are surveyed for the project, the sites are located in Holte, Værløse
and Hals. The sites are all located in Denmark. The varying features of the sites make for

Here the features in focus are the banks of the small stream and other natural features

Multiple methods of processing are used in order to compare different processing tools.
For the photogrammetry models, the commercial software Agisoft Metashape is used,

Open-source photogrammetry software package OpenDroneMap is used for additional
comparisons (time consumption), but not analyzed in depth. For LiDAR processing, mul-
tiple Python scripts written by the author are used.

Theory and Methods
In order to understand the processing, it is vital to have an understanding of the principles
behind the results and the data collection. This section will provide a brief overview of the

2.1
Digital Elevation models
Digital elevation models (DEMs) is a collective term for different types of elevation models.
2.1.1
Digital Surface Models
Digital Surface Models (DSMs) are as the name indicates, models of the actual surface.
In DSMs all of the elements in the survey area is mapped, including trees, man-made
buildings and other artificial features of the area.

treelines are seen as yellow spots on the DSM and other smaller bushes can also be seen
on the DSM.

2.1.2
Digital Terrain Models
Digital terrain models (DTMs) are created from the same data as DSMs. The main dif-
ference here is that the DTM does not include surface features. The DTM is created
by sampling and discriminating between surface points and ground points. The surface
points are removed and instead ground point values are interpolated in order to fill the
gaps from the removal of surface points.
Figure 2.2: Digital terrain model created using Agisoft Metashape. Background imagery
from Google.
The same color scales are used in figure 2.1 and 2.2 in order to illustrate the differences.
Here it is obvious that the treelines and smaller bushes are not included in the elevation
model. The elevation difference between the terrain and the small lake in the bottom left
corner of the model is also more prominent in the DTM.

2.3
LiDAR
LiDAR (light detection and ranging) works by calculating a distance travelled, using a
known speed and the time of travel. A LiDAR consists of a photon emitter (laser) and
receiver, which applies the speed of light (c, 299792458 m/s) and accurate clocks to
determine ranges (distances to objects). It can be put as:
R = cT
2
(2.1)
From equation 2.1 it can be seen that the ranges R, can be determined using the speed
of light c and the travel time of the laser pulse T, meaning the time it takes the energy
to leave the emitter and return to the receiver. In the LiDAR setup used in this project
16 lasers are combined with a prism in order to obtain ranges in multiple directions and
angles.
The footprint of the LiDAR increases with distance, this principle is known as beam diver-
gence. The increased size of the footprint can result in multiple returns when surveying.
Figure 2.5: Illustration of return modes. Figure by Juha Hyyppä [2].
The LiDAR used in the project (Velodyne VLP-16) has different return modes, strongest
return, last return and dual return. For data acquisition dual return mode is used on all
survey sites. On Figure 2.5 the consequence of beam divergence is illustrated. An object
close to the LiDAR is illuminated, this corresponds to the strongest return (first echo), in
this case the footprint of the LiDAR extends over the edge of the roof, thus illuminating

2.4
Ground Control Points
Ground control points (GCPs) are used both when creating and validating the models.
For the photogrammetry models, a series of flat GCPs are used. These are identical in
size and shape and can be easily recognized on aerial images.
Figure 2.9: Two of the flat GCPs seen from the UAV. Image from Holte survey.
Figure 2.9 shows two of the GCPs used when processing photogrammetry models. Each
of the GCPs on the survey site are mapped with a Real-Time-Kinematic (RTK) rover, in
order to obtain very high accuracy coordinates of these points. The high accuracy data
is used to shift the resulting models in the vertical and horizontal directions, in order to
improve the accuracy of the models.
Another form of GCPs are used when surveying, cross-validation GCPs. These are not
used when shifting/adjusting the photogrammetry models and are therefore used for vali-
dation and accuracy assessments of the both the photogrammetry and the LiDAR models.
These GCPs are elevated (one meter above the ground) and have a slightly larger surface
area than the flat GCPs. The elevation and the larger size of the cross-validation GCPs
are registered in the models as small elevation spikes, and makes it possible to assess
the accuracy of the LiDAR models, as it is not possibly to visually locate the flat GCPs in
the LiDAR models.

2.6
Photogrammetry
The three survey sites investigated in this project, have been surveyed with an UAV carry-
ing a camera. The camera takes overlapping images along the flight path (approximately
70% overlap) which are used for aerial photogrammetry. The outputs from photogram-
metry desired in this project are maps and 3D models (orthophotos and DEMs).
In digital photogrammetry, the first step is image matching. Here it is determined which
points (pixels) correspond to the same points in other images. As the images are captured
with a 70% overlap, there will be identical features in the images. The identical features
can be used as tie points to stitch the images together, in order create a larger image.
The exterior orientation is the position and orientation of the camera when the image was
taken. The exterior orientation is basically a transformation from the real-world coordinate
system to the image coordinate system. Each image taken, essentially corresponds to
have a stereo-camera pair taking images due to the overlap. If the camera parameters
(intrinsic and extrinsic) are known, the exterior orientation of the stereo pair is also known
[6], this facilitates triangulation, and makes it possible to estimate real world points from
a set of stereo images.
Statistically it is beneficial to have multiple views of the same 3D point, which is the case
of overlapping aerial images. Bundle adjustment is used to improve the estimates of the
3D points which have already been estimated from the camera images, while also de-
termining the exterior orientation of the cameras. With a bundle adjustment the aim is
to minimize the reprojection error. It is assumed that the location of the camera and the
real world point is known, and a projection of the point on to a camera image is done, this
provides a pixel value of the point, which is compared to the actual pixel value of the point.
The difference between the reprojection and the actual pixel value is the reprojection er-
ror. This is an iterative process and has to be done for all feature points in the images,
which leads to a large least squares problem that needs to be solved.
This process is automatically done in photogrammetry software (Agisoft [7], OpenDroneMap
), and is used to provide a 3D reconstruction of the features in the images. The 3D recon-
structions are used to create elevation models, as the reconstruction contains information
of all points reconstructed (coordinates). Image matching and bundle adjustment with tie
points and GCPs thus delivers models using indirect georeferencing. As all of the model
points are assigned coordinates iteratively through the processing.

3
Surveying
A structured workflow is an important part of a survey.
For the Værløse data acquisition workflow looked as following:
• Mission planning
• Preparing equipment (UAV, LiDAR, GNSS/IMU)
• Surveying
• Data processing
• Assessment of results
Mission Planning
Mapping out the survey is one of the crucial parts of the planning. Questions and thoughts
about the size of the area, the features of the area and the actual purpose of the survey
should be clear from the start. For this survey, buildings and other man-made structures
were in focus, therefore Værløse Flyvestation was chosen. This is an area where no
special permissions are needed and where different structures can be included in the
models. The main purpose of the missions for the thesis are to assess the results, which
is why areas with different features are chosen for the data collection, some with large
elevation changes and bodies of water, others with artificial structures and less elevation
differences.

the batteries. For surveys in Denmark, the M600 fits well with an operating temperature
range from −10◦C to 40◦C [9].
NovAtel OEM7700
The NovAtel OEM7700 GNSS receiver used in the setup is a fairly small device with
a weight of 31 grams. The maximum data rate of the unit is 100 Hz (lower than the
LiDAR output). It is equipped with advanced interference mitigation features which ensure
high performance and reliable output in challenging environments. The multi-frequency
receiver supports tracking of signals on all major GNSS constellations (GPS, GLONASS,
BeiDou, Galileo, GZSS, NAVIC, SBAS) [10].

Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR Puck
The LiDAR used to collect the range data is the Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR puck. As the
name suggests it is formed as a puck. The VLP-16 collects 360◦range data by scanning
the surrounding environments with its 16 laser/detector pairs. As mentioned in the theory
section, the lasers achieve a vertical FOV of 30◦(−15◦to 15◦). The LiDAR returns dis-
tances up to 100 meters with a rotational speed of 5-20 rotations per second. The lasers
fires thousands of times per second and therefore supply a very high point density [3].
The LiDAR puck has a weight of 830 grams, which brings the payload to a total weight of
around 1 kg.
No calibration or setup is required in order to use the VLP-16. It works out of the box and
delivers data as soon as power is supplied. The data delivered by the VLP-16 is saved as
user data protocol (UDP) broadcasts. Using a network analyzing tool (wireshark) these
broadcasts can be picked up and saved as packet capture (PCAP) files. The data format
and the parsing and processing steps will be further explained in section 4.

4
Processing
Different workflows are used when computing the models (LiDAR and photogrammetry).
In this section both methods will be briefly introduced.
4.1
LiDAR Processing
The idea behind the LiDAR script was to implement and all-in-one solution, which takes
the PCAP-file from the LiDAR and the GNSS/IMU-file from Inertial Explorer (software
used to process GNSS/IMU data after acquisition) and process the data into a georefer-
enced point cloud in a specified coordinate system. The script is written in Python, which
was the programming language chosen due to the open-source nature of the language.
Throughout this section, the workflow of the LiDAR data processing will be described.
The structure of the output data from the LiDAR and brief introduction to the functions can
be found in the appendix along with the full processing Python script.
Figure 4.1: Flowchart of the LiDAR processing flow.
Analysis and Comparison of LiDAR and Photogrammetric Methods for Digital Elevation Models
23
==PAGE:32==
Figure 4.1 displays the different steps when processing the LiDAR data. The first step
is the data collection, which provides two sets of data. GNSS/IMU data is obtained from
the system. This needs to be processed in Inertial Explorer (software form NovAtel) in
order to obtain a data file with the desired outputs (X, Y, Z, roll, pitch and heading) in
the reference system specified. LiDAR data is saved as a PCAP-file, which needs to be
parsed. After the initial processing of he raw data, the output from the data collection is
processed in the Python script, which outputs a georeferenced point cloud (XYZ-file) in
the specified reference system.
4.1.1
Processing costs
Depending on the size of the survey site, the LiDAR processing time varies. For sur-
veys in the 60 −100.000m2 range, the full LiDAR processing from parsing to a final point
cloud product takes around 90-120 minutes. The script is written and run on an aver-
age consumer PC (16 GB RAM) which does not have sufficient memory to run all of the
data through the script at once, therefore the PCAP file is split using Wireshark and the
data is processed in chunks. After obtaining the XYZ-point cloud file, this is inserted into
CloudCompare (software for handling point clouds) and rasterized in order to obtain a
GeoTIFF-file which can be opened in QGIS (open source GIS software). A system with
higher amounts of memory (64+ GB RAM) can run the data without chunking, this might
speed up the processing time.
4.2
Photogrammetry Processing
The photogrammetry processing is done using Agisoft Metashape and ODM. For this part
of the processing no programming is needed.
Figure 4.2: Flowchart of the photogrammetry processing flow.

==PAGE:33==
As seen on figure 4.2 the first step is also data collection. From the data collection images
and GNSS information of the GCPs are obtained. The data is put into the photogrammetry
software and most of the processing work is automatized except for few checks of GCPs.
Orthophotos are generated as a bi-product of the processing, DSMs can be created from
the 3D-models in the software. The DSMs can be re-sampled using a ground-point clas-

4.2.1
Processing costs
All of the photogrammetry models from the Agisoft software are processed on DTU Envi-

(GPU) which is not found in an average consumer PC and does speed the processing up
significantly. The processing including manually adjusting the GCPs takes around 60-90
minutes for surveys which are the same size as Holte and Værløse surveys. Compared to
the models created in OpenDroneMap which is run on the same PC that runs the LiDAR
script, the photogrammetry model processing takes 12-16 hours for the same sites.

Discussion and Conclusions
The analysis has shown some interesting results. The models created in Agisoft and The
LiDAR models are all detailed enough to pick up the cross-validation GCPs, which are
seen as one meter spikes in the different models.
The GCP-photogrammetry georeferencing method is definitely the most user-friendly and
fastest processing method of the two. Simple workflows are defined in the software and
the only work required is the visual assessment of GCPs and adjustment of these. The
physical labour included when doing GCP based surveys are slightly larger than direct
georeferencing surveys, as GCPs have to be set out and mapped with a high accuracy
GNSS system. The direct georeferencing surveys require more work on the processing
side, as a processing script needs to be written in order to obtain elevation models from
the raw data.
The LiDAR setup is complicated as the payload needs to be designed and fixed to the
UAV, in contrast to photogrammetry where many commercial UAVs which are survey-
ready from the time of purchase. The physical labour when surveying using the direct
georeferencing method is slightly lower, as the requirement of GCPs is eliminated.
When examining the photogrammetry models created in Agisoft Metashape, sub 10 cen-
timeter accuracy is obtained in the horizontal direction both when using GCPs and when
relying fully on the RTK-image tags from the Phantom 4 RTK UAV, however, adding
the GCPs to the model does increase the accuracy in the horizontal direction with 1-3
cm. Comparing these results with a study of the assessment of Agisoft software done
in 2018 [14] where aerial images are processed in the same fashion as in this project.
The study reports RMSE in the horizontal directions, which is 8-10 times larger than the
results achieved in throughout this thesis. The images analyzed in the study are taken in
a height of 457m (from an aeroplane), around 10 times the height of the UAVs used in the
surveys for this project. The large difference in the RMSE between UAVs in the project
and manned aerial vehicles in the study indicates that a higher accuracy is achievable for
smaller survey sites using UAVs.
In the vertical direction the GCPs does make a significant difference. In both cases where
photogrammetry models are analyzed, GCPs shift the models upwards with 10-20 cen-
timeters. In general it appears that the models created based on the RTK-tagged imagery
only, underestimate the elevation of the area. This might be caused by inaccuracy in the
RTK-tags, which is improved by adding GCPs to the model.
The LiDAR models perform differently depending on the survey site. The data set from
Holte delivers RMSE values around 15-16 centimeters in the horizontal directions and a
vertical RMSE of 13.3 centimeters. This might be caused due to improper initialization of
the hardware before data collection (a series of accelerations and sharp turns before the
actual data collection) or GNSS outages during the mission, which decreases the posi-
tional accuracy significantly (see figure 3.1).

The LiDAR survey in Værløse delivers high quality results. In both the horizontal and
vertical directions the LiDAR DSM outperforms the photogrammetry models in accuracy.
The LiDAR survey in Hals is compared to control points obtained by Orbicon. All of the
control points are placed along the bank of a small stream and are in some cases cov-
ered by trees or or other vegetation growths. Some of the points which are clear outliers
(directly under trees) are visually assessed and removed and a RMSE value of 0.71 m is
obtained.
In the cases with Holte and Værløse, the base station for the GNSS/IMU system of the
LiDAR setup was located around 10 km from the survey sites, which might also introduce
additional errors of around 1 cm per km (1 ppm). As the models are visually assessed in
QGIS after processing, this might also introduce some human error in the order of 1-2 cm
when pinpointing the GCPs.
The accuracy assessment are based on four cross-validation GCPs for each survey site,
increasing the number of GCPs for future surveys would improve the estimation of the
accuracy.
UAV surveys are a cheap and efficient way to model elevation in smaller areas. Out of
6 models compared (Holte and Værløse) only one model performed worse than similar
models created using images from aeroplane cameras. For survey areas in the range
0 −300.000m2 UAV surveying proves beneficial in both price and time consumption com-
pared to aeroplane surveying while delivering results with a higher accuracy.
LiDAR and photogrammetry processing are similar when discussing time consumption,
if the software and the scripts are already at hand. Photogrammetry software is more
user-friendly with interfaces and guides, whereas the LiDAR software might require pro-
gramming.
Various methods are examined throughout the thesis, where GCP-based photogramme-
try has repeatedly performed with a high accuracy. Taking out the GCPs for adjusting of
the model and relying solely on RTK-tags from the Phantom 4 UAV, increases the hori-
zontal RMSE of the models with 1-2 centimeters but can still be used in projects where
sub 5 centimeter horizontal accuracy is not needed. The removal of the GCPs does how-
ever have a significant impact on the vertical accuracy. In both of the cases, the result of
not adjusting the model with GCPs is an increase in vertical RMSE of 12-13 centimeters.
The elevation is in general underestimated.
The performance of the LiDAR setup varies between the two surveys in Holte and Vær-
løse. The model from Holte has large errors in the horizontal directions and the relative
distances are off, where the model from Værløse has the lowest RMSE values in both
directions out of all models created. The setup and the script should be tested at an ad-
ditional survey site in order to verify whether the LiDAR models are actually in fact as
accurate as the model from Værløse or if the accuracy is closer to the accuracy of the
model from Holte.

Processing in detail
Data Structure
The LiDAR data is transmitted over ethernet as UDP packets. Around 754 data packets
are transmitted per second. The 754 packets are split into 2 different categories, position
packets and data packets.
As position information is already obtained from the GNSS/IMU system, the position pack-
ets which include timestamps and position information are discarded. It is possible to
distinguish between the types of packets due to to their sizes. Position packets contain
554 bytes, whereas data packets contain 1248 bytes. The overall structure of the data
packets can be seen on figure E.1.
Figure E.1: Data structure illustration. Figure by Velodyne.
The first 42 data bytes in a data packet is a header. This information is not useful when
processing the LiDAR data and is therefore discarded from each data packet. The block
of data of interest is the next 1200 bytes. The 1200 bytes are split into 12 data blocks
of 100 bytes each. Each data block holds 32 range (distance) and reflectivity measure-
ments. The azimuth angle for each block is also included. All of the data is represented
in hexadecimal values, and therefore needs conversion and sorting before the actual pro-
cessing takes place. Example conversions from the Velodyne manual can be seen on
figure E.2.
56
Analysis and Comparison of LiDAR and Photogrammetric Methods for Digital Elevation Models
==PAGE:65==
Figure E.2: Example calculations, screenshot from Wireshark network analyzing tool.
Figure by Velodyne.
After handling the 1200 bytes, six bytes remain. These comprise of a timestamp value
and an indicator of LiDAR model and the return mode (dual, last, strongest).
Each data block (12x100 bytes) contains data from 2 firings of each laser (2 times 16
lasers), but only includes the azimuth angle of the first firing. The azimuth angle for the
second firing is calculated as the average of the azimuth angle from first firing in the block
and the first firing in the next block. The timestamp is only transmitted once per data
packet, this is the time of the first firing. Each packet contains up to 384 firings (12 times
32 firings) and therefore 383 timestamps need to be calculated for the rest of the data
in each packet. The lasers have a cycle time of 2.503µs followed by a recharge time of
18.43µs which results in a total firing time of:
16 · 2.304µs + 1 · 18.43µs = 55.296µs
(E.1)
This leads to a time difference of 110.592µs (2 · 55.296µs) between each data block. The
firings have their timestamp calculated as following:
offset = (55.296µs · sequence index) + (2.304µs · firing index)
(E.2)
Where each block consist of 2 firing sequences of 16 firings. The indexation starts at 0. An
example of calculation of timestamp offset for the seventh firing in the second sequence
of the fourth data block is:
offset = (55.296µ · 7) + (2.304µs · 6) = 285.696µs
(E.3)
The exact time for that specific firing is then calculated by:
time = timestamp + offset
(E.4)
All of these calculations are collected in a table, as the cycle times are equal in all firings.
For each data point, the offset is found from the table using the data index (number of
firing) and the offset for that data point is added the the timestamp from the first firing.

def bytestrip(p):
This function relies on Scapys PcapReader in order to read in a packet p. The first 42 bytes

This function calculates the 384 time offset values discussed in previous section and or-

def ranges(p, vlp_timing_tbl):
This function calculates the X, Y and Z coordinates from the ranges as well as extraction
of the scan angles. Two initial data structures last and strong are initialized (in the di-
mensions 384x6 to hold data for time, X, Y, Z, scan angle and range ). The timestamp (to
be interpolated) is extracted from each packet.
An outer loop is initiated. The outer loop runs through 12 iterations (the 12 blocks per data
packet) and unpacks the azimuth angle A for each block. A nested loop is introduced, this
loops through the two firing sequences in each block, here the distance data is obtained.
A third and last loop is introduced, this is nested under the previous loops and runs through
16 iterations (each firing per sequence). First a time offset is calculated using the function
gen_vlp16_timing_offsets(dual_mode) and for each firing the corresponding offset is
added to the timestamp from the data packet. The equations 2.3, 2.4 and 2.5 are applied
to the range data extracted from the packets and the scan angle is acquired through the
Laser ID (order of firing corresponds to Laser ID). Depending on the iteration of the first
loop (odd/even) the data is sorted in two different structures (last and strongest returns).
Two data structures including timestamp, X, Y, Z, scan angle and ranges are returned.
