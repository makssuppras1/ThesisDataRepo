Usage of Behavioural Design Methods - An International Study of Experts’ Best Practices 
Danish title

Project description: 
Behavioural design plays a tremendous role in shaping society, however, the methods 
used to develop interventions remain unexplored. This thesis sets out to identify 
behavioural design experts’ best practices, identify behavioural design methods, and 
provide insight on how these are used. This goal is approached by considering academic 
literature for a theoretical foundation, and combining it with empirical data, obtained by 
recurring contact with worldwide behavioural design experts, in order to disclose 
current practices.

Abstract 
Behavioural design affects people’s behaviour through the development of 
interventions, which are based on the understanding of how humans think and make 
decisions. For its capacity to influence society, it is gaining a prominent interest among 
traditional designers, organisations, and institutions, as well as among the general 
population. Multiple frameworks can be found in literature, however, the methodology 
that behavioural designers follow to design interventions is tacit and experience based. 
This thesis explores behavioural design best practices, with special interest in 
behavioural design methods for their capacity to influence thinking patterns, design 
performance and facilitate learning. 15 behavioural design experts from multiple 
international locations constitute the empirical foundation of the research. Such experts 
were approached in three touchpoint rounds, allowing an iterative process, and 
ensuring cross-validation of the data. The results of the research are, firstly, a collection 
of currently used behavioural design methods, which have been characterised and listed 
according to validated behavioural design processes. Secondly, a further study of the 
identified methods has allowed to reveal how the multiple disciplines that compose 
behavioural design relate in practice, and how such relation fluctuates in the 
behavioural design process. This has provided the insight needed to determine gaps in 
the methodology where additional development would be beneficial. Thirdly, the 
interpretation of the experts’ observations provides insight about the reasons behind 
the method usage, as well as its consequences. In essence, the present thesis has 
identified currently used behavioural design methods, and conveys their practice, 
establishing the fundamental groundwork for the development of an operationalised 
and teachable catalogue of behavioural design methods. Such findings enable further 
research where behavioural design methods should be further described and included 
in design guides to be implementable. Additionally, making methodology available 
diminishes the existing entry barrier that traditional designers face when engaging with 
behavioural design.  
Keywords: behavioural design, design methods, modified Delphi Technique, method 
catalogue

Nomenclature 
Behavioural design. Approach to design with the aim to develop interventions to 
impact behaviour (see Section 2.1. What is Behavioural Design).  
Behavioural design method. Structure of a specific activity playing a part in developing 
interventions to impact behaviour (see Section 2.3. Behavioural design methods). 
Common/common approach. Category to classify the method origin referring to 
methods linked to usual project procedures. 
Design method. Description of the structure of a specific activity taking place during a 
design process (see Section 2.2. Design methods). 
Design thinking. Creative and iterative human-centred discovery process to 
understand the user, challenge assumptions, and redefine problems with the aim to 
identify solutions to meet user needs (IDF, 2020; Norman Group, 2016). 
Design thinking methodology. Way of working, including values, principles, process, 
methods and tools, in order to achieve the design thinking aims (Daalhuizen et al., 
2019). 
Expert. Individual who is socially recognised as such and/or can be distinguished by the 
experience and knowledge of the domain (see Section 3.2, Phase 2. Definition of unit of 
analysis) 
Relevant method. Behavioural design method pertinent for the present research. Main 
criteria are that the method can be formally described, is non-redundant and that has 
behavioural design distinctive traits (see Section 5.1. Managing raw data). 
Social science. Study of the relationships, behaviours, and processes of a social system 
(Adhikari, 2016). 
User-centred design. Approach to design characterised by a detailed definition of the 
context of use (Bevan & Curson, 1999; Still & Crane, 2017) and focus on understanding 
the individual user through research (Gulliksen et al., 2010; Wendel, 2020), with the 
aim to synthesise the obtained data into solutions directed at the user (Eason, 1995).

 Introduction 
Products and services are not independent, but their design influences the overall 
experience of the user (Redstrom, 2006). Behavioural design affects how people act in 
the environment and influences how we behave towards others and ourselves (Lockton 
et al., 2010a). For the individual, these choices are usually unconscious (Thaler & 
Sunstein, 2008) but the reasons behind people’s decisions can be studied. Such research 
provides insights on the motives behind people’s behaviours. Behavioural designers 
have given profound thought to how to use these insights with the aim to create 
interventions that endorse specific behaviours. Designing with the objective of 
influencing choices has great potential for shaping society (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019; 
Wendel, 2020), and the concrete means that behavioural designers use to develop these 
interventions have a large impact on the actual behaviours of the population. Given the 
importance of behavioural design in society, it is a matter of course that design students 
and design practitioners aim to acquire knowledge on how to design for behaviour 
change. Currently multiple frameworks on how to approach a behavioural challenge can 
be found, but best practices and the actual methodology followed by behavioural 
designers is tacit and experience based. There is no public collection of behavioural 
design methods for practitioners or apprentices to consult and methods are neither 
reviewed nor supported by a theoretical foundation.  
1.1. Aim of the project 
The aim of this master’s thesis is to disclose behavioural design best practices and 
particularly to uncover the methods that behavioural design practitioners use to 
develop effective interventions. The tacit knowledge of behavioural design experts is 
explored to obtain insights onto their practices and to develop a behavioural design 
method collection. The results aim to bridge current practices with theoretical 
knowledge and provide the necessary groundwork for the development of an 
operationalised and teachable collection of behavioural design methods.

1.2. Research question 
To achieve the project aim, a main research question has been formulated: 
The following sub-questions are formulated, providing direction to answer the research 
question, and ensuring a holistic approach to the research: 
RQ1. Which behavioural design methods are used? 
RQ2. Do multiple experts use the same methods? 
RQ3. Where in the process is each method used? 
RQ4. Are the methods extracted from other design domains, adapted from them, or 
created by practitioners? 
RQ5. Are there gaps in the process where new methods are needed? 
1.3. Methodological framework 
The results of this thesis are based on empirical data extracted from current practices of 
behavioural design practitioners. To ensure rigour and provide value to the qualitative 
research, a modified Delphi Technique approach has been followed, accompanied by an 
accurate plan for the research. 15 behavioural design experts from all over the world 
constitute the empirical foundation of this thesis, selected according to pre-defined 
criteria. The insights into their practices and use of methods where obtained by 
performing semi-structured interviews, followed by analysis and a feedback round. A 
detailed explanation on the applied methodology can be found in Chapter 3.  
Methodology.

1.4. Ethics in behavioural design 
Behavioural design aims to influence behaviour, and as such, the ethical aspect of the 
discipline cannot be neglected. The debate on the ethics of behavioural design is open 
(Lilley, 2019; OECD, 2019; Wendel, 2020), and as the discipline develops additional 
considerations need to be taken into account. Ethics are not the focus of this master’s 
thesis and it is presumed that all interviewed experts act with good intentions and 
ensuring freedom of choice for the users. Therefore, the ethical aspects of behavioural 
design will not be further considered.

 Literature review 
This chapter presents a review of the literature used in order to define the scope of the 
project, understand the current situation and identify gaps, and to establish the 
research questions and project approach. Behavioural design and what it encompasses 
has been defined (see Section 2.1.). Design methods have been described and 
characterised (see Section 2.2.), as well as its use in practice. Next, the situation in the 
context of behavioural design regarding the existing methods is analysed (see Section 
2.3.). Finally, the main findings of the literature review are summarised (see Section 2.4.) 
and used as arguments to expose the research questions for this master thesis.   
2.1. What is Behavioural Design 
Designing and design theories, methodologies and methods have been researched since 
around 1960 (Eder, 1999), during what is called the ‘design method movement’. These 
investigations were based on the belief that making design more scientific would help to 
produce a better world (Langrish, 2016). According to Eder (1999), during the design 
method movement several trends can be found, one of those being the pragmatic trend. 
This suggests that designing is assisted by models, which “make the designing to some 
extent learnable” (p. 2). From this branch a new trend formed, leading to the definition of 
‘design science’ which tries to include the results of the other design trends, the artistic 
and research trends. According to Eder (1999), ‘design science’ is a scientific view of 
designing where “the development during the design process […] is followed step by step 
in a logical sequence, and in parallel the processes of designing are coordinated” (p. 2).  
In line with ‘design science’, the behavioural-science paradigm has its roots in natural 
science research methods (Hevner et al., 2004) and “it seeks to develop and justify 
theories (i.e., principles and laws) that explain or predict organizational and human 
phenomena surrounding the analysis, design, implementation, management, and use of 
information systems” (p. 76). The terms behavioural science and social science are often 
used interchangeably. The concepts are interrelated but while behavioural science 
investigates human behaviour through controlled systematic structures, social science 
studies the relationships, behaviours and processes of a social system (Adhikari, 2016). 
==PAGE:22==
8 
In this thesis, social science is used as an all-encompassing term. Behavioural design 
applies these scientific insights into practice with the objective of tackling social issues 
(Design Council, 2020).   
It can be argued that all design is intended to result in certain user behaviour (Redstrom, 
2006). Design thinking solves problems with a hands-on, user-centric mindset (Norman 
Group, 2016) and practices such as interaction design, usability and human factors have 
an error-proofing approach (Lockton et al., 2010a). Behavioural design can be considered 
an emergent sub-category of design practice (Lockton et al., 2010b) which differentiates 
itself from design thinking by considering users own impacts (Salovaara, 2008), their 
prior experience (Chamorro-Koc et al., 2008), and by using social science insights to 
design (Wendel, 2020).  
Behavioural design consists of many different approaches and theories that all have in 
common that they enable the designer to create a change in behaviour within a 
specified scope. Fogg (2003) describes the term ‘persuasive technology’ as “any 
interactive computing system designed to change people’s attitudes” (p. 20). 
“Persuasion implies attitude change leading to the target behaviour” (Lockton et al., 
2010a, p. 4). Lilley (2009), argues for a strategy to ensure sustainable behaviours and 
Tromp et al. (2011), defines a socially responsible design approach of behavioural 
design where “a desired social implication, based on collective concerns, defines what 
behaviour is desired from a social perspective” (p. 7). Kursat Ozenc (2014) challenges 
the understanding of ‘change’ with a human-centred design method-based framework 
that is used to analyse “[…] issues of change and to synthesise products accordingly” (p. 
30). Kahneman (2011) integrates insights from psychological research into economic 
science, contributing what is known as ‘behavioural economics’, studying the effects of 
psychological, cognitive, emotional and cultural factors on economic decisions. Cash et 
al., (2017), argues that behavioural design is concerned with designing the behaviour of 
the user and consists of “[…] a spectrum of behavioural strategies; from fully conscious 
to fully unconscious or combinatory” (p. 2), and defines behavioural design as 
“designing for antecedent behaviour change strategies using implicit interventions to 
impact behaviour” (p.8).  
==PAGE:23==
9 
Behavioural design originates from classic design thinking of the 1960’s, and its 
methodology is combined with social science research methods. Even though it is still 
an emergent genre of design approach, it has branched out in several different sub-
practices. In this thesis an all-encompassing definition is used, and ‘behavioural design’ 
is understood as designing to develop interventions to impact behaviour.   
Behavioural design theory in practice 
There are multiple ‘behavioural change theories’ that attempt to explain the reasons 
behind changes in behaviour. Michie et al., (2014) have identified 84 different theories 
on behavioural change across 14 domains. The authors of these theories focus on 
understanding behaviour and synthetize their findings so those can be used in practice 
(Cash et al., 2017). The actual shaping, change or influence occurs when these insights 
are applied. However, challenges are “not easily addressed by the straightforward 
application of theories” (Hook et al., 2013, p. 624). I.e. various theories can be 
recognised but their application is not straightforward, as their connection to practical 
use relates to personal experiences of designing in a defined context.  
2.2. Design methods 
In design a direct master-apprentice learning process has been predominant (Alexander, 
1964), but the utilised procedures can be documented for a systematic approach and 
established as methods in order to mediate social learning.  Methods act as intermediates 
that allow people to learn from others more efficiently and with greater effect 
(Daalhuizen, 2014). In particular, design methods are assumed to improve design 
performance by transferring know-how between people over time and space (Tromp & 
Hekkert, 2016). Hence, although design has been traditionally based on expertise, 
resources exist to make design practices teachable. 
Definition of a design method 
During the design method movement in the 1960’s, a design method was simply defined 
as “a way of solving certain classes of problem” (Gregory, 1966a).  Cross et al. (1981) 
described it as “a pattern of behaviour employed in inventing things of value which do 
not yet exist” (p. 195). These definitions do not clarify what a method is and lead to 
==PAGE:24==
10 
loosely descriptions of methods. The lack of clarity on their definitions affects the efforts 
made to introduce new methods into industrial practice (Gericke et al., 2017), but it also 
makes it difficult to use, learn or teach the methods (Koper & Tattersall, 2005).  
Therefore, methods must be clearly defined for the emergent field of behavioural design 
to avoid the “growing pains” that traditional design thinking went through when 
developing new practices. 
As a consequence of this inherent need for clarification, researchers have focused on 
being precise and consistent in the use of terminology. Myrup et al. (2015), define 
method as “a goal-oriented rationalization or simplification of engineering work in the 
form of a standardised work description” (p. 52). Daalhuizen et al. (2019) define ‘design 
method’ as “a description of the structure of a specific activity, outlining the basic 
sequence or rules that make up single comprehensive activities within a practice,” and 
clarify that they “function as mental tools as they provide structure for a designer’s 
thinking and behaviour” (p. 44). Aligned with academia, for this thesis, ‘design method’ 
will be defined as the description of the structure of a specific activity taking place 
during a design process. 
Characterisation of a design method 
Design methods in particular are normally synthesis and clarification oriented, seeing 
as the results depend on the designer’s know-how, mindset and the interpretation of 
how the method should be used (Myrup et al., 2015). A set of instructions for how a task 
should be completed is not enough (Andreasen, 2011). The description of the 
procedures for how a method is used (i.e. step by step guide) is complementary to the 
description of the method itself (Gericke et al., 2017). The goal of the method, the 
expected result, the required designer’s knowledge, and the activity itself reflect the 
domain where the method belongs (Myrup et al., 2015). But in addition to the method 
description, “[designers] need to be ‘mentally equipped’ to master a repertoire of 
methods”(Daalhuizen et al., 2014, p. 138). When characterising methods, it is necessary 
to also describe the required mindset. Myrup et al., (2015) defines mindset as “the 
proper understanding of a method’s use in accordance with the designer’s reality and 
the method’s background and proper use” (p. 57).  The definition is aligned with 
Daalhuizen et al., (2014) who defines it as the designer’s own approach and preferences 
towards a method, related to prior exposure to it. Literature created for application in 
==PAGE:25==

practice, such as design manuals (Kumar, 2013; Hanington & Martin, 2012; Müller-
Roterberg, 2019; Chasanidou et al., 2015), have a similar approach to the elements that 
are characterised and include not only the method description and step by step guide 
but also the method required mindset, operationalising it as established attitudes 
needed to use a method. An overview of the required elements to describe a method 
(and its use), adapted from the characterisation of methods by Kumar (2013), can be 
found in Table 1.

Methods in practice 
Many companies claim that design methods are central to their activities (Gericke et al., 
2017). Companies use methods in a modified form and may use different names for the 
methods they use. Methods researched by academia are modified by individuals, or 
methods developed by organisations are adapted by incorporating academic insights 
(López-Mesa & Bylund, 2011). Design methods need to be made to fit organisational 
values and principles, innovation processes, other methods and tools, as well as the 
organisational context (Daalhuizen et al., 2019). Methods should also be used in a 
==PAGE:26==
12 
flexible manner, befitting the mindset of the designers, the design context, and the 
desired goal (Daalhuizen, 2014).  Therefore, even if the description of a method is 
shared, the details regarding its use may vary across users and situations.  
When following a design education, it can take a considerable amount of time for a 
designer to develop confidence and professional competence (Gericke et al., 2017). 
Design methods “extend the ability to design,” “enhance reflection on designing,” and 
“enhance learning to design” (Daalhuizen, 2014, p. 23) and therefore remain a central 
element of design education. Thus, students transfer knowledge about design 
methodologies into practice (Gericke et al., 2017). However, “novices perceive a gap 
between the theoretical knowledge they have acquired and their actual performance in 
concrete settings” (Bromme & Tillema, 1995). Design is based on experience and 
intuition (Green & Bonollo, 2004) and requires the development of practical knowledge: 
it is necessary for future designers to be familiar enough with methods to apply them 
(Eder, 1999). Design researchers have attempted to address the issue by systematically 
mapping frameworks, processes and methods (Mora et al., 2017; Salmon et al., 2003). In 
general, there is a need to better understand how designers design (Green & Bonollo, 
2004) and specifically, design researches do not sufficiently understand method use by 
practitioners (Daalhuizen, 2014). Methods require experience and rich understanding 
for being used efficiently (Myrup et al., 2015). Hence, academic knowledge on current 
procedures is necessary to allow apprentices to develop in the professional world and 
to relate and apply academic insights to real practices. 
2.3. Behavioural design methods 
By combining the definitions of ‘behavioural design’ and ‘design method,’ the research 
team define ‘behavioural design method’ as the structure of a specific activity playing a 
part in developing interventions to impact behaviour.  
The literature related to behavioural design in practice is, for the most part, focused on 
describing frameworks, methodologies and considerations for the design process (e.g. 
Fogg, 2009; Michie et al., 2011; OECD, 2019; Wendel, 2013). Only a few of the 
publications focus on the practical application of behavioural design (Cash et al., 2017; 
Tromp et al., 2011), and even fewer explore methods. Few examples can be found, such 
==PAGE:27==
13 
as OECD (2019) who along with describing the BASIC framework, depicts tools for 
behaviour change which are specific for the policy-making domain.  
During the desk research on design methods, multiple books, design manuals and 
papers which present design methods were found (Chasanidou et al., 2015; Hanington 
& Martin, 2012; Kumar, 2013; Müller-Roterberg, 2019). However, when these are 
examined, only a few can be categorised as what has been defined as ‘behavioural 
design method. Some examples are the “Design with Intent Method” (Lockton et al., 
2010b), “Behavioural Prototype” (Kumar, 2013, p. 231) and “Behavioural Mapping” 
(Hanington & Martin, 2012, p. 18).  
The lack of clarity with regard to the actual use of methods adds to the insufficiency of 
the scientific literature on the topic. According to our findings, the knowledge on used 
methods is immature and greatly rooted into the practitioners’ experience and 
expertise. Still, there is no systematic study on which behavioural methods are used by 
these experts and how these methods are linked to academia. 
2.4. Key conclusions and research questions 
Summarising the findings of the literature review, behavioural design is currently an 
emerging discipline based on design and social science and strongly related to user 
centred design. Due to its foundation, behavioural design is theoretically heavy, 
resulting in a large number of methodologies and frameworks. In this thesis, 
‘behavioural design’ is defined as designing to develop interventions to impact 
behaviour.  
As argued in Section 2.2 Design methods, the existing theories cannot be directly 
operationalised but rather need to be adapted to contextual factors and designer skills. 
This requires a learning process which is based on expertise. However, the procedures 
can be documented for a systematic approach and make the design process teachable. 
This is achieved through ‘design methods,’ defined as the description of the structure of 
a specific activity taking place during a design process. The description of how a method 
is used is complementary to the description of the method itself but also to the designer 
mindset. When studying design manuals illustrating operationalised methods, these are 
==PAGE:28==
14 
commonly characterised by: method tag, goal, description, mindset, when to use, input 
& outcome and step-by-step procedure. 
Methods are central to design activities and provide structure to the thinking process, 
extend the ability to design and enhance the designer’s learning, but it is necessary to be 
familiar with a method to make use of it. Even if a description of a method is shared 
among individuals, its usability needs to be adapted to the specific context. Hence, it is 
necessary to understand how methods are used by designers. Academic knowledge on 
current practical procedures is needed to allow apprentices to develop in the 
professional world. Traditional design researchers have approached this aspect in 
publications, such as design journals, books and manuals. However, although 
behavioural design is heavily based on theoretical insights, the application of these in 
designing for behaviour change is currently tacit. The small amount of behavioural 
design methods that can be found in literature are not operationalised. Such situations, 
combined with the fact that behavioural design is still an emerging discipline, result in a 
chasm between theory and practice and an expertise-based usage of methods.  
As such, literature on behavioural design methods is immature and strongly rooted in 
practitioners’ experience and expertise. Still, we have not found a systematic study on 
which behavioural methods are used by experts and how these methods are linked to 
academia. This, has led us to the following research question:  
The following sub-questions are formulated, providing direction to answer the research 
question, and ensuring a holistic approach to the research: 
RQ1. Which behavioural design methods are used? 
RQ2. Do multiple experts use the same methods? 
RQ3. Where in the process is each method used? 
RQ4. Are the methods extracted from other design domains, adapted from them, or 
created by practitioners? 
RQ5. Are there gaps in the process where new methods are needed?

 Methodology 
This chapter presents the methodology that has been applied with the objective of 
answering the research question and sub-questions (see Section 2.4. Key conclusions and 
research questions). 
Qualitative data was selected as the primary data type to answer the defined research 
questions, as it allows the research team to investigate the current usage of behavioural 
design methods. The selected approach was to contact multiple proficient experts in 
behavioural design, who work in the field in practical terms, and not only academically. 
The experts were expected to have tacit, as well as explicit, knowledge in the areas of 
the thesis’ exploration. A reliable overview of the current state in the field has been 
provided by combining the insights from multiple experts from different locations, 
educational backgrounds, and approaches to behavioural design. The corresponding 
analysis contributes an understanding of their practices and their use of methods. This 
information is the fundamental source of data to answer the research questions.  
3.1. Modified Delphi Technique 
The Delphi Technique approach has been selected as the research methodology as it 
offers the best fit with the needs of the research. It allows a structured approach to 
collecting data where the alternative would be an entirely subjective approach 
(Broomfield & Humphris, 2001). According to Linstone & Turoff (2002), the Delphi 
Technique is preferred when the number of experts needed exceeds the resources of 
the research team and cannot, in a sensible manner, be held in a group session. 
Additionally, the anonymity of experts can remedy differences among experts caused by 
differences in opinions and personalities. According to Davidson (2020), the defining 
characteristics of the Delphi Technique are: 
1. Participants are experts in their field.
2. A series of rounds or iterations are used where information is given back to the
researchers for review.
3. Participants work anonymously. They do not know who the other participants
might be.
==PAGE:32==
18 
4. Focused on forecast solutions to future problems.
5. The goal is to approach a consensus among the expert panel.
The above-mentioned aspects are aligned with the research team approach to 
answering the research questions. An exception can be found in the focus on the future 
since the project takes a phenomenological approach to characterise the current use of 
behavioural design methods. However, the Delphi Technique can be modified to be 
applied in scholarly research (Davidson, 2013), in fact, “there are so many variations of 
the original, that Delphi is often preceded by the word ‘modified’” (Stewart, 2001, p. 
922). 
A match between the problem and the research method and design makes the research 
credible and easier to defend (Davidson, 2013). Therefore, the Delphi Technique has 
been somewhat adapted to ensure the best fit with the research, as the aim of the 
research the is an analysis of the current state of method use instead of the formulation 
of future predictions.

3.3. Limitations of the methodology 
The research team is aware of some of the limitations of the methodology and 
mitigation strategies have been considered to minimise their effects.  
The first element to consider is the purposeful selection of experts rather than random 
sampling. However, for the purpose of the study it is necessary that the subjects of the 
research have specific knowledge on the topic of interest (Millar, 2001). To ensure such 
competence, a definition of expert has been previously established, see Section 3.2. 
Phase 2. Definition of unit of analysis. Moreover, among the individuals who fulfil the 
requirements, the research team made sure to avoid selecting respondents whose 
==PAGE:40==
26 
reputations are based on only self-ratings or on the research team’s network. However, 
because of their ease of identification and willingness to participate, most of the 
interviewed experts, 13 out of 15, work as consultants for other companies and as such, 
the sample is biased in that aspect.  
Secondly, the objective of obtaining anonymous consensus can be a disadvantage 
because this results in considering all opinions as equally valuable, leading to the 
obtention of a diluted version of the best opinion (Powell, 2003). However, this is also 
applicable to other methods where insights of a collective are considered, such as 
working groups. The effects are also minimised by the fact that only validated 
behavioural design experts are included in the research. 
Thirdly, Williams & Webb (1994) argue that extensive time and commitment is needed 
to perform the Delphi Technique due to the complexity of the data processing and the 
number of contact rounds. The research team acknowledged this responsibility and had 
the willingness to devote that amount of effort to the research.  
Next, semi-structured interviews were the selected approach to extract information. 
According to Owen & Noonan (2013), on occasions researchers are unable to identify 
where to ask prompt questions or probe responses, and as such, some relevant data 
may not be gathered. Another central concern is to identify the depth of the information 
to be collected (Kallio et al., 2016). To obtain the desired answers, the questions need to 
be carefully planned, and the right people and in the right quantity need to be 
interviewed (SSWM, 2020). To stay attentive to the detected challenges and keep the 
research goals in mind, the research team developed an interview protocol with prompt 
examples, which also ensured a systematic approach to the interview. 
Regarding the data analysis, a descriptive presentation of results has been followed by a 
thematic analysis. A difficulty for developing a thematic analysis is to maintain a sense 
of continuity among elements. To do so, it is recommended to use a theoretical frame to 
anchor the claims made (Braun & Clarke, 2006). To provide such structure, the design 
process presented by each expert was fitted into a generalised behavioural design 
process framework. Moreover, the findings of the thematic analysis were further 
discussed, by comparing and contrasting them to existing theory.  
==PAGE:41==
27 
Finally, it is necessary to note that the research team was not previously familiar with 
the Delphi Technique nor thematic analysis, nor highly experienced in conducting semi-
structured interviews. Hence, the insights obtained during the first dialogues were 
immature in comparison to the understanding achieved in later interviews. There has 
been a learning curve on the use of the methodology, making the procedures more 
accurate as the project has progressed.

5.2. Descriptive results 
While the experts did not have difficulties presenting the main phases of their 
behavioural design processes, multiple experts pointed out that the procedures 
followed in each step are strongly dependent on the context. 
Expert F concurs with Expert N on having a standard process and emphasises the 
uniqueness of each problem (Expert F, 06:25). Although each project is different, the 
process can be generalised. The process is commonly explicit and used as a promotion 
and communication tool regarding the experts’ practices. 
Some of the experts described the phases according to academical frameworks while 
others described their own personal process. The BASIC framework (OECD, 2019) has 
been selected to facilitate the descriptive analysis. It has been selected for its fit with the 
primary activities described by the experts. 
1. Identify Behaviour. Identify and better understand the problem.
2. Analyse Biases. Review the available evidence to identify the behavioural drivers
of the problem.
3. Design Strategies. Translate the analysis to behaviourally informed strategies.
4. Test Interventions. Design and implement an intervention to test which strategy
best addresses the problem.
5. Scale for Change. Develop plans to scale and sustain behaviour.
It is necessary to note that not all projects necessarily are conformed by all the 
characterised phases. As clearly described by G, “Every phase can be seen as an 
“While the process itself is standard, different projects will require very different 
approaches [...]. For us, it has to do with really getting to know our client and what their 
goals are.” (Expert N, 17:35) 
==PAGE:54==
40 
individual project.” (Expert G, 26:30). Moreover, the process has been described linearly 
to facilitate its understanding and interpretation. Nevertheless, the process is flexible. 
The largest amount of iterations is commonly present between designing and testing. 
“At that phase [4. Test Interventions], we might loopback to kind of a deliberate or 
design, depending on whether we are seeing successful results.” (Expert N, 16:01). 
Some experts even considered these steps as blended. For example, Expert F 
mentioned: “Creating the prototype and discussing and ending it, is its own creative 
process, separate from the test.” (28:38). There is a strong connection between ideating 
and testing processes in behavioural design.’ 
Methods can be used in multiple stages of the process. For readability and analysis

1. Identify Behaviour
The process begins with the first contact with the client and establishing an initial 
project definition. Experts focus on collecting information to compile the existing 
knowledge on the subject. Ten methods have been detected in this phase, and the 
majority are extracted from common practices (4) or social science (3).  
The method ’26. Desk Research’ is used to gain an understanding of the problem and 
solution space during the early stages of the design process. The method is based on a 
traditional online search to gain an understanding regarding the current status of the 
topic. Moreover, nine of the experts emphasise that their research is based on academic 
literature and scientific papers. This use of ‘44. Literature Review’ is aligned with the link 
of behavioural design to theoretical insights, and even autodidact experts (e.g. Expert A 
and H) use this method.  
Since most of the interviewed experts, 13 out of 15, are consultants, they need to extract 
the clients’ recorded information in connection to the problem. ‘14. Clients’ 
Documentation Review’ is used when the client has documented the current behaviour, 
the context or the users. ’13. Clients’ Data Review’ is used to collect relevant quantitative 
==PAGE:55==
41 
insights. When further measurable data is necessary, ’80. Survey’ is the most used 
method. 
Although it may seem that the documentation is complete, the clients’ knowledge is 
often tacit or ambiguously defined. As described by Expert B: “People have a tendency 
to think they have defined something when it is, actually, very vaguely defined.” (14:50). 
Expert H makes a similar point and adds that “they sometimes find it quite hard to 
describe the behaviour that they do want because they often describe the outcome 
instead.” (32:55). To extract this implicit knowledge, experts have traditional contact 
with the client (e.g. email, calls, meetings), both formal and informal. Some experts (7) 
facilitate an ‘39. Initial Workshop with the Client.’ Expert J comments: “Clients are not 
used to having conversations about behaviour for a problem.” (15:54). When the client 
is not used on talking on behavioural terms, the workshop can serve an educational 
purpose. 
To understand the involved parties ‘78. Stakeholder Analysis’ is commonly used. ‘41. 
Interview Stakeholders,’ ‘33. Focus Groups,’ and ’27. Diary Studies’ are used to collect the 
stakeholders’ perception qualitatively. However, as Expert C notes “you can’t conclude 
too much based on peoples’ own conclusions.” (17:30).  These methods for qualitative 
data collection have been criticised by the experts. “Those people are going to be biased 
by Hawthorne effect (Expert P, 22:25).” To work around this bias, experts defend the 
use of methods detached from the stakeholder perceptions, the most mentioned being 
‘53. Observations.’. 
In the ideal situation, the individuals should not notice that they are being observed: 
“We try to do experiments and observations where people don’t know.” (Expert H,

us to do.” (Expert O, 08:29). 
This phase results in a collection of information, a ’64. Project Definition,’ including the 
’23. Targeted Audience,’ and a ’22. Definition of Objectives.’ 
“We are strong believers in understanding people’s real behaviour and, therefore,

2. Analyse Biases
The experts now have the necessary resources to characterise, analyse and synthesise 
the problematic behaviour. Twenty methods have been identified. Some (Expert C and 
P), start the process with ‘34. Hypothesis of Bias,’ where they presume and later assess 
the cognitive biases that are expected to have an impact. 
Actions and attitudes are linked to the environment. As Expert G affirmed, “to 
understand the behaviour you need to understand the context.” (17:15). To obtain this 
holistic comprehension, half of the experts use a systematic approach. The research 
team has identified this practice as the method ’59. Problem Analysis with a Framework.’ 
The framework consists of a set of elements that need to be considered to characterise 
the problem. Two relevant approaches to the method have been identified: 
- 
Self-created. The expert or the company has developed the framework, which is 
usually protected under intellectual property regulations. The framework may 
be the core of the expert/company’s practices and an element of differentiation 
from competitors. These are commonly developed based on existing knowledge. 
“Itself [the created framework] is not a major innovation, it is just a 
consolidation of some of the most prominent science approaches.” (Expert G, 
05:56).  
- 
Extracted. The expert uses existing frameworks defined in the scientific 
literature. The most mentioned are Fogg’s B-model (B. Fogg, 2009), the ABC 
Model (Ellis, 1991), the Behaviour Change Wheel and APPEASE (Michie et al., 
2011), MINDSPACE (Dolan et al., 2009), BASIC (OECD, 2019) and EAST (Service 
et al., 2014). Most of these experts do not believe in an ideal approach and select 
the framework to use based contextual factors. 
The framework provides a setting for organised discussion around the topics of interest. 
The result is an understanding of the targeted behaviour and main factors surrounding 
it. Three outputs have been repeatedly mentioned as of interest. First, the detection of 
“We just find the model that allows us to understand the behaviours and what stands in 
the way of those behaviours [...]. We are quite neutral in terms of which model we use 
and what theory we apply.” (Expert H, 26:35)

root causes, which are the true reasons inducing the problematic situation. Secondly, 
the identification of which cognitive biases affect the situation. Finally, the recognition 
of friction points, which act as barriers for the desired behaviour. The use of the 
frameworks is particularly tacit and experience based.  
The use of the framework is combined with other methods. The remaining experts 
mentioned other methods to achieve the same purposes. In some cases, these methods 
are used in the setting of a ‘7. Behavioural Workshop,’ in which the focus of attention is 
on analysing a behaviour.  
‘54. Personas’ is used to empathise and gain insights from the stakeholders’ point of

’54. Personas’ can be used as a resource for ’79. Stakeholder Requirements,’ resulting in 
knowledge of the stakeholders’ needs and understanding of the design paradoxes. 
One recurrent method, used by eight out of 15 experts, is ‘User Journey,’ a traditional 
design method. Behavioural designers use the adaptation ‘88. User Journey, Behavioural’ 
“with a focus on mapping every aspect of the behaviour” (Expert A, 45:25), linking them 
to emotional states, cognitive biases and drivers, as well as to other behaviours. Another 
adaptation is ‘89. User Journey, Reenacted’ where behaviours are reproduced in context. 
In ‘90. User Journey, Retrospective,’ a user describes his actions after these have taken 
place. 
’18. Cognitive Mapping’ is used to represent the spatial information that users receive. 
Interaction points can be identified and evaluated with ‘86. Touchpoint Analysis.’ The 
insights can be used as a resource to perform an ‘2. Affordance Analysis,’ where existing

find recurrent behaviours. ‘17. Cognitive Biases Analysis’ is used when biases have been 
identified, and its triggers and consequences are considered. The insights can be used 
for ‘75. Root Cause Analysis’.  
Once an undesired behaviour is defined, the methods are targeted towards analysis. ‘50. 
Model the Decision Process’ consists of exploring the decisions that have led to the action 
classified as undesirable, identifying barriers that prevent the behaviour. Another

method is ‘46. Mapping the Choice Architecture,’ Expert C describes choice architecture

The diagnosis is focused on a behaviour of interest, but its global influence is

Experts mention multiple methods to reach diagnosis conclusions. Experts B and P

allows cross-verification and ensures that the analysis results are consistent and

Traditional design methods are used during 2. Analyse Biases but they have been

necessary to properly diagnose the problematic situation. However, to make proper use 
of the design methods, knowledge of cognitive science is essential. 
As a result of this phase, the problematic behaviour is analysed, as well as the relevant 
elements and its relations in the system. This is summarised in a ‘61. Problem Definition’

human behaviour is unpredictable. Testing has a prevalent role to evaluate the 
interventions and twenty methods have been identified for this purpose.  
Tests are also used to detect unexpected situations before implementing. In case the 
results do not fulfil the expectations, it is possible to loop back and iterate without 
major consequences (Expert N, 16:17). Experts A and H affirm that they use ‘29. Early

improving them before moving to substantial testing. In some cases, experts 
acknowledge that testing it is not part of their process for economic reasons or because

This last phase is characterised as the finalisation step, which consists of planning how

methods have been identified.

necessarily have the same ability to always run interventions in the purest form. It is 
called market reality.” (Expert J, 45:50)

5.3. Thematic analysis 
The descriptive results have been analysed and patterns and elements of interest have 
been detected for further study. The topics have been clustered in themes that are 
important and relevant to answering the research question and sub-questions. Three 
themes have been identified: ‘Scientific approach’, ‘Advancing from design thinking 
methods’ and ‘Linking problem to solution’. The sub-themes of the thematic analysis and 
their interpretations, accompanied by relevant quotes can be found in  
Table 5, Table 6 and Table 7, respectively under each theme. 
Scientific approach 
The theme refers to the detected keenness of behavioural design experts to relate the 
scientific approach to their practices, and to present behavioural design as a discipline 
linked to this scientific approach. There is a recognisable parallel between the ‘scientific 
method’ and the ‘behavioural design process’ (see Section 5.2. Descriptive results). In 
both cases, assumptions are made, predictions are derived from them and experiments 
are carried out to see if these were correct. In this theme these parallels are discussed 
considering how the process is approached, what elements are distinctive and how 
methods are used. The perception of behavioural designers in relation to the association 
of behavioural design and scientific disciplines is also taken into account. 
Some experts initiate their design process similarly to a scientific research by 
formulating a research question: “How can we help X user group to do Y behaviour at Z 
context at W time?” (Expert A, 19:20). Moreover, in multiple steps of the process, 
hypotheses are established which are later validated or rejected. Concrete examples are 
’34. Hypothesis of Bias’ and ’35. Hypothesis of the Intervention Outcome.’ To collect 
knowledge on the topic, rigorous approaches dominate, e.g. ’44. Literature Review’ is 
preferred over ’26. Desk Research’ since it encloses academically endorsed knowledge 
on the topic. “There is a lot of good literature on the descriptive side, evidence-based 
interventions from around the world.” (Expert P, 25:30). All experts mention that they 
“Taking a research-based and evidence-led approach counters decision making based 
on assumptions and potentially biased judgements.” (Expert G, FR) 
==PAGE:69==
55 
use multiple methods to obtain reliable conclusions. Expert B explicitly notes: “We like 
to do method triangulation, at least to do two different kinds of data collection, because 
we know that methods are faulty.” (Expert B, 25:47). 
Methods adapted or extracted from social sciences are prevalent when collecting 
information. Out of ten detected methods in 1. Identify Behaviour, four are from social 
science. Examples are ’33. Focus Groups’ from sociology, ’27. Diary Studies’ from 
psychology and ’53. Observations’ based on ethnography research. These methods focus 
on qualitative aspects of the users’ behaviour since this information is essential to 
understanding the behaviour (Expert G, 22:21).  However, in some cases the behaviour 
is noted in a quantitative manner by, for example, noting the frequency of a certain 
behaviour (Expert F, 43:50). This allows it to be translated into quantifiable objectives 
that can later be evaluated. “We try to work with a key metric, resulting in statements or 
goals like; we want to reduce sick leave with 20%.” (Expert A, 16:20).  
For designing the interventions, methods extracted from traditional design thinking 
methodology are predominant, which is further explored in Theme 2. Advancing from 
design thinking methods. For testing purposes, nine out of 20 methods are from the 
domain of design thinking. However, when examining them, most have user-centred 
characteristics, e.g. ’87. Usability Testing’, where evaluation adopts a prominent role. 
These methods coexist with highly systematic methods adapted from science, e.g. ’68. 
RCT’ has its origin in psychology and has been quoted as preferred by six experts 
because of its reliability: “for us, RCT is the most robust academic method.” (Expert N, 
34:50).   
11 out of 15 experts have explicitly mentioned the use of ‘3. & 4. Baselines’ to measure 
the effects of the interventions and their success. Behavioural experts focus on 
quantitative criteria: “it is important to know that there is some way to measure.” 
(Expert P, 40:05), and Expert J elaborates o the topic: “statistically validated measurable 
data is seen as reliable.” (FR).  Experts also argue about the promoting capacity of 
quantitative data (Expert O, 53:45). The dominance of quantitative information can also 
be observed in the use of methods, e.g. in ‘8. Biometric Measuring’ insights of qualitative 
nature are quantified. Of the 11 experts who mentioned baselines, 10 explicitly prefer 
when the baseline is quantitative and, among them, two perspectives can be 
distinguished. While for some experts “data is all real numbers” (Expert J, 23:32), and

they only work quantitatively, the vast majority prefer a combination of qualitative and 
quantitative data.  
Qualitative data has an important role in behavioural design because it provides insight 
of contextual factors and real-world effects. As Expert M notes “hypotheses are 
validated by experimenting and testing but also by evaluating real-world applications.” 
(FR). However, qualitative results are not the main outcome: “Qualitative evaluation is 
immersed mostly in intentions, attitudes and beliefs, which behavioural design has zero 
interest in understanding.” (Expert J, FR). In behavioural design, “the aim is not to see if 
the intention or motivation have changed but if it actually has materialised in change in 
behaviour.” (Expert F, 30:50).  
There is an eagerness for behavioural design to be considered scientific based and 
combined with design, which is made explicit by Expert A when he describes 
behavioural design methods “like if you combine behavioural insights with the scientific 
methods and design thinking.” (Expert A, 51:37). Expert D follows the same lines and 
does not describe himself as a designer but as a behavioural strategist and jokes, “I don’t 
even think I could draw a stick dog, in that sense, it is weird to be called a designer.” 
(02:05). However, on occasion, when trying to describe a systematic approach to the 
use of methods, experts hesitate: “it is difficult for me to explain exactly how we do it.” 
(Expert C, 17:45). This might be, in words of Expert M, because behavioural design is 
“half creative, half science; there has to be some room for messiness.” (Expert M, 49:06). 
Moreover, they argue that the study of behavioural design differs from its practice. 
Expert P indicates that “your decision isn’t about your significance level, it is not about 
how confident we can be that a particular mechanism is at play here, it is really about 
what is the impact of this intervention.” (30:49). The interventions can be theoretically 
relevant, scientifically proven and applicable to the context but still not be adequate. 
The intervention is not universal as science would be but needs to fit the values and 
goals of the company; “We try to look for the sweet spot where the insights also fit the 
brand strategy and identity.” (Expert M, 08:08). And this is not easy, as Expert A affirms:  
“We definitely do want the quantitative baseline, but for me in an ideal world we would 
have both.” (Expert N, 19:28)

The theme ‘Scientific approach’ discusses the similarities and relations between 
behavioural design and scientific development. There are parallels in the way to 
approach the initial stages and when testing potential interventions. For these 
purposes, methods are commonly extracted from social sciences or adapted with inputs 
from this domain. Quantitative methods are more prominent than qualitative, as they 
are perceived as reliable and commonly repeatable in multiple contexts. Moreover, they 
make comparisons easier for assessment purposes, but are sellable and convincing as 
well. The measurable data, when statistically validated, is seen as robust. Interventions 
are compared to a baseline which is, again, primarily measured in quantitative terms. 
Despite the eagerness to ensure an empirically reliable approach, experts affirm that 
creativity also plays a major role and some methods are ambiguously described. There 
is no rule of thumb for how to use behavioural insights since behavioural design does 
not look for true answers. The fit of interventions for changing a behaviour in a specific 
context depends on the singularity of it.

Advancing from design thinking methods 
The theme addresses how classic design thinking methods usage is extended in the 
context of behavioural design. Behavioural design has previously been described as an 
emergent sub-genre of design thinking (see Section 2.1. What is Behavioural Design). The 
behavioural design process can be compared, with some alterations, to the design 
thinking process.  As a result, it is expected that some of the methods used by 
behavioural design experts are extracted and adapted from design thinking 
methodology. The theme focuses on the use of design thinking methods in the 
behavioural design context, when and why experts use them most, and how they are 
adapted.  
To develop interventions, experts follow the traditional design thinking process in a 
more or less adapted version. “We experimented with the design process and sort of 
tinkered it, tailored it to our situation.” (Expert P, 09:25). Hence methods fit the 
behavioural design approach. This leads to the analysis of the behavioural design 
methods that have been adapted from design thinking methodology. 31 design thinking 
methods have been identified as relevant for the research, of which 12 have been 
somewhat modified.  
A clear example is the use of ‘User Journeys,’ where three particular variations have been 
detected: ’88. Behavioural User Journey,’ ’89. Reenacted User Journey’ and ’90. 
Retrospective User Journey.’ In all three cases, experts express how the original method 
has been modified to focus the interest on the users’ behaviour and its antecedents and 
effects (Expert A, 45:25). The same happens with the use of ’54. Personas’ where 
attitudes and emotions are the focus elements: “We give our personas objectives, needs 
and define their cognitive capability and emotional state.” (Expert E, 36:30). As other 
adapted methods are described in a traditional manner, they have this distinct focus on 
considering the aspects of the conduct, e.g. ’79. Stakeholder Requirements’. Created

Linking problem to solution 
This theme discusses the importance of determining the causes and reasons behind a 
problematic situation. It analyses how behavioural design connects the root causes, to 
potential solutions, in order to generate effective interventions that tackle the problem 
holistically and not only symptomatically. The use of methods for ideation is examined 
in relation to linking the essence of the problem with the interventions. 
In behavioural design, the problematic situations are not tangible but rather related to 
cognitive biases and friction points. The root causes of a problem are not easily 
discernible nor straightforward. The scope of the research evolves along with the 
problem description (Expert F, 08:05). In turn, the problem description develops with 
the research itself: “Key here is the flexibility in changing the original problem, the data 
may reveal a completely different problem or added problems that had not been 
considered.” (Expert J, FR). The collection of information and analysis is used by the 
experts to avoid jumping to conclusions and to limit their own bias when iterating the 
problem definition. As Expert M ratifies, experts endorse their research with data “to 
not just go with our own intuition, we don’t want to just go by the way we see it.” 
(Expert M, 36:45).  
Moreover, on some occasions, the client firmly believes they know what the true 
problem is (Expert H, 32:55) or they strongly believe in a solution. “Sometimes they 
have this fantastic product and it is so good - you need to take that out of their head 
before.” (Expert M, 21:40). These biases need to be overcome since the client is a major 
influence on the intervention. There needs to be an agreement on perspectives, “ok, 
what is it we are trying to do for you?” (Expert O, 40:10), and the interventions need to 
be properly applied to be effective (Expert L, 24:00). Trusting relationships, 
understanding of behavioural concepts and commitment of the involved parties make 
the process easier. 
“We don’t know what we are going to make, because we don’t know what we are going 
to find.” (Expert F, 17:05)

Behavioural experts express their interest in having reliable data to perform robust

problematic behaviours. The data analysis, both quantitative and qualitative, provides 
material that can be used to define the root causes and understand the problematic 
behaviour.

The bridge is achieved through insights from cognitive science which, in behavioural

shortcuts or biases, and some common examples are social proof, awareness, 
commitment, etc. These are applied in the solution by the designer to favour the desired 
behaviour in an intervention (Expert M, 07:45). Most experts have researched and 
extracted them from cognitive science and seven of the experts have explicitly 
mentioned having their own collection in an ’40. Internal Archive’ format.

The theme ‘Linking problem to solution’ shows evidence on why, in behavioural design, 
it is essential for practitioners to identify the causes of the targeted behaviour. For this 
purpose, the research and analysis of information tends to be extensive. The problem

the research is targeted. Minimising bias is essential, and experts prefer quantifiable

behavioural change principles to apply become obvious. That bridging between 
problem and solution is where behavioural design can be clearly discerned from other 
disciplines. To do so, experts commonly rely on an ’40. Internal Archive,’ where they 
have collected previous interventions and behavioural change principles that have 
proved effective. Traditional design methods are used to design interventions befitting 
the context but maintaining focus on the application of the detected behavioural change 
principles. A proper and usable archive is one of the most useful methods in behavioural 
design to enable the development of efficient interventions.

 Discussion 
This chapter aims to discuss the current state of behavioural design methods in relation 
to literature (see Chapter 2.  Literature review) by utilising the results of the research. 
These include the clustered methods (see Section 5.1. Managing raw data), the 
descriptive results (see Section 5.2. Descriptive results) and thematic analysis (see 
Section 5.3. Thematic analysis). References to further literature are made in order to 
compare the detected practices to theoretical insights. The first section of this chapter 
provides arguments to support the validity of the obtained data. This is followed by a 
discussion of the data showing the relationship of the multiple disciplines that compose 
behavioural design and how these interrelate across the behavioural design process. 
Finally, the relevant findings and contributions of the research are discussed.  
6.1. Behavioural design as a clash of disciplines  
A behavioural design method is defined as ‘the structure of a specific activity playing a 
part in developing interventions to impact behaviour’ (see Chapter 2.  Literature 
review). Hence, for the research purposes, it is fundamental to ensure that the detected 
methods are actually characteristic of behavioural design. Experts within the domain 
were chosen to ensure a reliable source of information, but the obtained results are 
discussed for further validation.  
According to literature (see Chapter 2.  Literature review), behavioural design is 
described as an emerging sub-category of design, connected to design thinking and 
characterised by using insights from social science. The nature of the detected methods 
is aligned with the presented academic narrative. In Appendix C.4. Quantifiable data of 
relevant behavioural design methods, it can be observed that the identified methods are 
primarily from design thinking (31 out of 76) and, while 12 methods are adapted with 
social science insights, 16 methods are derived from social science itself. The methods 
heavily rely on the use of data, both qualitative and quantitative, and this is aligned with 
academia as can be seen in Wendel's (2013) quote: 
==PAGE:88==
74 
 “When you bring these raw ingredients together—behavioural research, product 
design expertise, and data analysis—you have what’s needed to design for behaviour 
change” (Wendel, 2013, p. xxii) 
The detected methods are characterised by this three-dimensionality: social science, 
design thinking and data focus. A visualisation of the relationship between domains is 
illustrated in Figure 3. The alignment with theoretical insights validates and confirms 
that the identified methods are, indeed, particular for behavioural design. 
Figure 3. Relationship between social science, design thinking and data analysis in behavioural design. 
Adapted from (Wendel, 2013) 
To characterise behavioural design practices, it is necessary to better understand how 
design thinking, social science and the use of data are related to each other. However, in 
the researched literature, it is not described how the relationship among the domains 
translates to actual practices. The Collection of behavioural design methods (see 
Appendix C) is, in itself, a contribution to bridge behavioural design practice and 
academia. By studying the current use of behavioural design methods, this research 
provides the groundwork for understanding how these overlaps are translated into 
practice.  
Firstly, the link between design thinking and data is observed in the selection of user-
centred design methods. User-centred design is characterised by detailed definition of 
the context of use of a product (Bevan & Curson, 1999; Daalhuizen et al., 2019; Still & 
Crane, 2017) and for having a strong focus on understanding the individual user and 
their behaviour through research (Gulliksen et al., 2010; Wendel, 2020) with the goal of 
synthesising the obtained data into solutions directed at the user (Eason, 1995).  In 
behavioural design, the users, and in particular their behaviours, are at the forefront. 
Most methods adapted or extracted from design thinking methodology, 25 out of 31, 
==PAGE:89==
75 
have strong user-centred design characteristics. Experts follow user-centred design 
strategies, such as defining target users, observational field visits, usability tests or 
scenarios (see Dopp et al., 2019). The relation to data is most notorious during 
researching stages, where qualitative information plays a dominant role due to the 
importance of the context, and while testing, where quantitative evaluation is 
predominant. Multiple iteration rounds are common at this point, intertwining the 
development of the solution with its evaluation (Andersen et al., 2015; Gregory, 1966b; 
Wendel, 2020, p. 9). This approach is again data-oriented. Test insights provide the 
fundamental knowledge and information which is used to iterate and further develop 
the intervention, that is at the foundation of user-centred design (Göransson & 
Sandbäck, 1999).  
Secondly, regarding the link between social science and data, it is well-known that social 
sciences utilise both qualitative and quantitative data in their respective domains to 
analyse the subject of interest (Kuper, 2004; Lawrence Neuman, 2004; Vause, 2002). 
The limits of using only quantifiable insights when dealing with behaviour are 
acknowledged, since context plays paramount role in the actions that the individual 
actually performs (Johns, 2006). Political, socioeconomic, cultural and emotional factors 
are relevant (Parker, 2004). Behavioural design experts are aware of the influence of 
the environment and for that reason social science methods and research conclusions 
are complemented with situational information. Experts are especially interested into 
social science methods based on quantifiable data, because these allow the 
establishment of clearly defined goals and comparison mechanisms. On occasion, 
qualitative inputs are translated into quantitative information in the form of biometric 
measuring to facilitate the development of statistical analysis. 
Finally, design thinking, and social science are particularly distinct in their objectives. As 
discussed in Section 6.2. Rationale of the usage of behavioural design methods (below) 
design thinking is constructive while social science has an analytical nature. These two 
areas do, however, simultaneously complement each other in behavioural design. Such 
link is, of the three overlaps, the most distinctive to perceive in the use of methods: 
Behavioural design requires a deep understanding of the behaviour of interest to 
construct effective interventions. While social science provides the necessary 
knowledge of human behaviour, design thinking builds upon these insights to develop 
==PAGE:90==
76 
solutions. Methods are used as mental tools to reflect on the problem, for 
communication and alignment inside and outside the team, and for supporting an 
interpretation of the situation and provide direction for taking action (Daalhuizen, 
2014). Methods also need to fit the situational needs and, as exposed in Chapter 2.  
Literature review, should be used in a flexible manner to achieve the desired goal. The 
adaptation of methods to the behavioural design needs can be observed in their 
modifications: 12 out of the 31 design thinking methods have been adapted with 
elements of social science. The relationship between these domains is therefore explicit 
in the use and adaptation of methods.  
Fluctuations in the relationship across the process 
The relationship between domains is not constant throughout the behavioural design 
process. The research is not characterised by analysing the behavioural design process. 
Hence, an academically validated framework, BASIC (OECD, 2019) has been selected to 
present the descriptive results. A synopsis of these relationships across domains 
organised accordingly to the BASIC framework (OECD, 2019) is visualised in Figure 4.  
Figure 4. Relationship of social science, design thinking and data analysis across the behavioural design 
process. 
BASIC (OECD, 2019) describes five main steps, each of them to fulfilling a need in the 
process (p.18). It has been selected for its fit with the steps described by the experts. It 
is logical to reason, then, that the methods used in a specific step are directed at 
==PAGE:91==
77 
fulfilling the particular aim. This fact has been validated by the detected methods, e.g. in 
1. Identifying Behaviour common methods to extract information are used, while in 2.
Analyse Biases the scope of the methods is analytical, etc. This alignment is not 
surprising, but the particularity of the methods used in each phase is not limited to the 
goal-oriented approach. As mentioned, the research results show evidence that 
fundamental aspects of the methods vary among process steps. This research is the first 
to identify and present these fundamental differences, which are supported by data in 
Section 5.2. Descriptive results, visualised in Figure 5 and presented below according to 
the BASIC framework process (OECD, 2019). 
1. Identify Behaviour. The methods used in this phase are mostly extracted from
common project practice alongside some from social sciences, and this choice of
methods secures the ability to gather qualitative information and enables the
designers to establish quantifiable goals.
2. Analyse Biases. To ensure a correct understanding of the current situation and
the stakeholders, experts chose to utilise methods adapted from design thinking
methodology by applying social science insights. Examples are different versions
of user journeys, behavioural system mappings and the creation of personas.
3. Design Strategies. The ideation of new interventions is defined by the methods
from design thinking methodology, where the majority have been extracted
without adaptation.
4. Test Interventions. This phase is dominated by methods with strong user-
centred design characteristics. This selection of methods results in a focus on
testing with end-users in the true context when possible in order to ensure the
most efficient interventions (Jeffrey & Chisnell, 2008; Vredenburg et al., 2002).
These coexist with methods from the social science domain, with a strong focus
on quantitative data.
5. Scale for Change. The last phase is defined by extraction of methods from
common project management, such as presentations, recommendations, and
plans for implementation. This is the result of the experts often not being an
active part of the implementation but having the role of advisor, and therefore
this phase is not emphasised in this research.

It is necessary to note that the methods have been clustered according to a linear

6.2. Rationale of the usage of behavioural design methods 
The scientific approach in behavioural design is not only desired by experts but also 
seen in practice. Firstly, parallels in how the process is approached are found (e.g. using 
research questions and hypothesis, emphasis on method triangulation). Secondly, 
design thinking methods are frequently adapted with social science insights and also 
social science methods are used. Among experts, there is a shared willingness to link the 
discipline to the scientific approach. This desire is not novel nor exclusive to 
behavioural design. The ambition to make design more scientific can be traced to the 
==PAGE:93==
79 
1920s, when “we see a desire to produce design based on objectivity and rationality, 
that is, on the values of science” (Cross, 2001, p. 1), but in behavioural design this 
approach is accentuated. The results of the research provide insight into why this 
phenomenon occurs, as well as arguments to understand the fluctuation across the 
behavioural design process. An overview of the most relevant elements can be found at 
the end of the section in Figure 6. 
Handling uncertainties 
Uncertainty is a fundamental element in the nature of design (Beheshti, 1993; 
Daalhuizen et al., 2009) since the outcome of innovative elements cannot be predicted.  
Daalhuizen et al., (2009) shows evidence that uncertainty attributed to the task is a 
common source of uncertainty in design and is of special concern among designers 
before individual (own skills) and social context (collaborative) uncertainty. 
Behavioural designers, in their work, are faced with traditional design uncertainty 
added to behavioural uncertainty. Combined, these factors make behavioural design 
extremely complex. Behavioural uncertainty is associated with the difficulties of being 
able to predict or explain a person’s behaviour (Redmond, 2015) and due to its 
stochastic nature (i.e., it presents a random pattern), behavioural uncertainty may be 
analysed statistically but not predicted precisely (Ronchi et al., 2014). Behavioural 
designers use methods as systematic procedures, particularly, scientific procedures and 
social science insights, to handle the complexity of behavioural uncertainty.  
Ideation and the methods used for this intent are not of special interest to the experts 
compared to the traditional design thinking approach. The underlying cause can be 
argued to be that experts’ focus on solving issues related to behavioural uncertainty, to 
the detriment of handling design uncertainty. Reducing uncertainty related to the 
behavioural aspects is necessary to identify and understand the true cause of the 
behavioural problem. Experts focus on understanding how people think and how they 
make decisions in the situation of interest. This knowledge is essential in developing 
effective interventions, hence, experts focus their efforts on this task. This is a 
challenging undertaking which requires time and is resource consuming. Through 
experimentation, assumptions are validated or rejected, and this ‘developing by testing’ 
==PAGE:94==
80 
approach is where design uncertainty is considered. Consequently, ideation stages 
become a secondary element and methods for this purpose are undervalued. 
Needs vary across the process 
Experts' insights lead to two additional potential motives for the fluctuation on the use 
of social science insights across the process. An overview of the differences across the 
process can be found in Figure 4 and Figure 5 (above). The motives have been presented 
in Section 5.3. Thematic analysis, under the section Advancing from design thinking 
methods. These are, first, that behavioural design is still an emerging field and as such, 
ideation methods have not yet been completely adapted. Second, that those adaptations 
are only relevant in specific phases. When consulting literature, the end tasks of the 
disciplines are seen as a major distinction between science and design. While science 
analyses existing elements, design develops them. “Science is analytic; design is 
constructive” (Gregory, 1966a, p. 6).  Based on the results, the research team argues 
that the imbalance in the method adaptation through the phases is partially due to the 
predominant objectives of each phase. On one hand, creatively oriented tasks are 
characteristic of the ideation stages and do not require major scientific insights. On the 
other hand, contributions from science are required in analytical stages. 
The clash of scientific needs and design needs has also been noted by experts while 
describing their practices. There is a willingness to follow scientific procedures, but the 
process requires some messiness and room for creativity, and this juxtaposition is also 
discussed in academia (Cross, 2001; Gregory, 1966a; Kursat Ozenc, 2014). Cross et al. 
(1981), argues that it may be that “this attraction [to science] lies not so much in the 
method of science, but in the values of science” (p. 195), referring to the values of 
rationality, objectivity and universalism. This can be considered partially true for 
behavioural design. In reiterated occasions (see Section 5.2. Descriptive results) experts 
mentioned that even if an intervention is scientifically proven, it will only be applicable 
if it fits the context, the goal and the brand’s mission and vision. Moreover, it is 
mentioned that quantifiable goals are used, partly, because they “sell.” These elements 
are considered by the research team as consequences of the industry-driven approach 
to behavioural design. The results of the research show that behavioural design has a 
==PAGE:95==
81 
true connection with the scientific approach. Again, this can be directly seen in the 
procedures and in the scientific soundness of method use: initial hypothesis are 
evaluated, the exploration is systematic and based on reviewed research, insights are 
and accompanied by data, triangulation is used to ensure reliability, biases are 
minimised, etc. Moreover, frameworks are used to ensure a holistic approach and 
experimenting, and testing have a predominant role in developing and assessing 
potential solutions.  
The role of designers in behavioural design 
The analysis of the problematic situation is performed thoroughly, to a degree where it 
becomes obvious which behavioural change principles to apply to develop 
interventions. The ideation stage consists of adapting the detected behavioural 
principles to the specific context and needs. The underpinned link between behavioural 
design and behaviour is evident and a way to explain the detected emphasis placed on 
analytical aspects. However, there are other reasons why the more “design” elements of 
behavioural design are to some extent subordinate to the behavioural analysis and 
testing. The academic backgrounds of the experts have been taken into account, since it 
was noted that only four of the 15 behavioural design experts have design education 
(see Appendix D. Behavioural design experts). The majority of behavioural design 
experts, 11 out of 15, do not have an educational background in design but most have an 
educational background in either social science (5), marketing (2) and economics (3). 
Social science education focuses on providing skills to study people and their 
interactions (Kuper, 1996), while marketing directs the flow of products from producer 
to consumer (ACTE, 2020) and economics studies the production, distribution and 
consumption of such products (Krugman & Wells, 2012). Hence, it is understandable 
that ideation is not considered a focal element. The insights on the root causes are 
transformed into interventions with use of the knowledge of previous results and 
testing. To do so, experts use private archives where they have a compilation of the 
most important behavioural change principles and proven interventions to apply them.   
The professional identity of practitioners is based on attributes, beliefs, values, motives 
and experiences (Slay & Smith, 2011). Because of the described elements, the current 
professional identity of behavioural designers is perceived as analytical and research 
==PAGE:96==
82 
based. Current behavioural design experts perceive themselves more as behavioural 
scientists/strategists rather than designers and have the tendency to underestimate the 
skill set of designers. This is an issue since behavioural design is of a special interest for 
developing products and services for its power to deliberately change behaviour 
(Tromp et al., 2011). The relevance of the design aspects in behavioural design should 
not be underestimated and it needs to be reminded that behavioural design is the 
combination of social science, design thinking and data analysis. To avoid the 
undervaluation of the design elements in behavioural design, it is logical that design 
focused individuals should take part in behavioural design activities. However, the 
strong link that behavioural design has with social science knowledge is a barrier to 
traditional designers to thrive in the field. Designers lack fundamental tools in their 
toolbox and access to education approaches to achieve them.

6.3. Conclusion and key findings of the discussion 
Behavioural design is commonly described as a clash of disciplines namely social 
science, design thinking and data analysis (see Figure 3). The methods identified in this 
research present characteristics from these domains and are therefore considered 
behavioural design methods. This research has made it possible to identify how this 
relationship translates into practice by studying the methods that behavioural experts 
select. User-centred approaches display the relationship between design thinking and 
data, and a preference for quantitative social science methods exhibits the relationship 
==PAGE:98==
84 
between social science and data. Finally, the link between design thinking and social 
science is the most obvious, as design thinking methods are often adapted with social 
science insights. The relationship between domains is not constant, and this fluctuation 
has been described and characterised (see Figure 4). A fluctuation in the origin and 
adaptation of the methods across the process has also been identified and described 
(see Figure 5). In general terms, social science and use of data are prioritised in 
analytical stages and for testing purposes while ideation phases display design thinking 
methods without modifications.  
The collaboration with experts has allowed us to gain insight into their practices and to 
determine the reasons behind the particularities on the method use. An overview of the 
findings and their relations can be found in Figure 6.  Firstly, the focus on the scientific 
approach and the use of social science insights can be linked to the need to handle 
design uncertainty, and in behavioural design, the particular requirement of dealing 
with behavioural uncertainty. This last is prioritised and tackled via analysis and 
testing, in detriment of the ideation stages. Secondly, the challenges that behavioural 
designers face, vary across the process. There is a juxtaposition between on one side 
scientific procedures, that study existing phenomena and, on the other side, innovation, 
which is constructive. Moreover, behavioural design is industry driven and the scientific 
insights need to fit the brand and situational context. Lastly, the current analytical 
approach to behavioural design determines the professional identity of the 
practitioners. These perceive themselves as scientists/strategists. Design methods are 
undeservedly undervalued but the strong link to social science is a serious entry barrier 
for traditional designers.  
6.4. Research limitations 
Several limitations are acknowledged for the present research. Those are principally 
related to the nature of the sample, the available time and skills, and to external factors. 
The most critical constraints are discussed in the current section. 
==PAGE:99==
85 
Nature of the sample 
For the research purposes, the selected individuals need to be and are, indeed, 
behavioural design experts. However, the vast majority of the chosen candidates work 
in consultancies. The ease of recognition of such candidates and their willingness to 
participate have been argued to be the causes of such tendencies when compared with 
experts working on internal teams. This bias in the sample might influence the 
identified methods and display distorted focus in method usage, e.g. the research team 
has observed that experts rely on obtaining data from external sources such their 
clients. Further research on the practices of non-consultant experts is required to refute 
this potential bias and avoid deviations between the identified methods and 
behavioural design practices.  
Moreover, behavioural challenges are approached collaboratively and each individual 
focuses on specific tasks according to his/her skills. To obtain a holistic approach 
towards behavioural design methods, further research directed at teams is suggested. 
At the same time, intellectual property rights are of extreme importance. On occasion, 
experts do not want to depict their specific procedures when using methods since those 
are considered private approaches to tackling behavioural design challenges. 
Time and experience 
The research presented in this project is the empirical basis on which to develop a 
thesis as a final delivery of a master’s degree. This inherently implies limited time and 
clear deadlines for developing the project. The number of experts that have been 
interviewed is satisfactory for the objectives of the project but as mentioned some 
geographical areas have not been considered and further interviews with non-
consultant experts would increase the sensibility of the obtained results. On the other 
hand, as mentioned in methodology limitations, the research team had some experience 
with semi-structured interviews but had never applied the Delphi technique. This 
project has been a learning process in itself. Finally, although the student role has made 
the process of approaching experts easier, the research team has experienced a “need” 
to prove credibility and some difficulties in obtaining a continuous commitment from 
the experts. Finally, although having followed a design engineering education, before 
the project, the team was not familiar with behavioural design. 
==PAGE:100==
86 
External factors 
It is necessary to mention that the project was first planned to integrate both physical 
and online meetings, depending on the location of the experts. A face-to-face approach 
was preferred because it made easier to interact with the co-creation template. This 
format was expected to be beneficial to the research itself but also for our individual 
learning, providing a stronger skill foundation to proceed with online meetings in 
international settings. Due to the global COVID-19 pandemic, the format of the 
interviews was modified to be a videoconference with an online co-creation tool. The 
research team has not identified major consequences of this modification and, in turn, 
argues that the process has been more systematic and repeatable. However, the 
situation has further influenced the project regarding the availability of the experts. 
Behavioural design has gained importance in this scenario for the need to modify 
people’s behaviour towards social interaction, social distancing and hand hygiene, 
among others. On several occasions, experts have cancelled or rescheduled touchpoints 
in order to be able to handle their increased workload.

 Conclusion 
This master thesis argues, in Chapter 2.  Literature review, that methods play an 
important role in facilitating the design task and enhancing learning. And even though it 
is necessary to be acquainted with methods to use them, research on the use of 
behavioural design methods has not been found and neither has a publicly available 
collection of such methods. The aim of this thesis was to disclose behavioural design 
best practices and particularly to uncover the methods that behavioural design 
practitioners actually use to develop effective interventions. To do so, the following 
research question was formulated: 
To provide an answer, the current behavioural design best practices have been 
systematically investigated by interviewing 15 international experts in the field. The 
main research question was subdivided into five sub-questions to provide a direction 
for the research and ensure a holistic approach to the method usage.  
RQ1. Which behavioural design methods are used? 
RQ2. Do multiple experts use the same methods? 
RQ3. Where in the process is each method used? 
RQ4. Are the methods extracted from other design domains, adapted from them, or 
created by practitioners? 
RQ5. Are there gaps in the process where new methods are needed? 
To disclose which behavioural design methods are used, the methods that were 
mentioned by experts during the first touchpoint were listed. A total of 92 relevant 
methods were identified and characterised, describing their name tag, expert source, 
goal, description, when to use, mindset, divergent/convergent nature, outcome, and 
origin. The collection of behavioural design methods can be found in Appendix C.2. 
Identified behavioural design methods, providing an answer to RQ1 and RQ2.  
Of these, 76 were considered relevant to the research purpose and categorised 
according to their distinctive use in the process, providing an answer to RQ3. Such 
==PAGE:104==
90 
methods are presented in Appendix C.3. Relevant behavioural design methods 
categorized according to the behavioural design BASIC framework (OECD, 2019). The 
methods are also presented in Appendix C.5. Alternative cluster of behavioural design 
methods, categorized according to “The Behavioural Design process” (Cash et al., 2017) 
to showcase that the identified patterns correlate when methods are clustered in an 
alternative process framework. 
To answer RQ4, the origin of each behavioural design method has been considered and 
included to characterise the methods. A and a quantitative overview of the method 
origin can be found in Appendix C.4. Quantifiable data of relevant behavioural design 
methods. The results display 31 design thinking methods, 19 extracted and 12 adapted 
with inputs from social science,16 social science methods, 7 extracted and 9 adapted, 23 
common methods 22 extracted and 1 adapted, and 6 created methods.  
The relationship between the domains conforming behavioural design -design thinking, 
social science and data analysis- can be observed in the behavioural design methods 
themselves. Design thinking methods are modified with social science inputs, 
quantitative methods from social science are preferred, and user-centred approaches 
that rely on data are used. The relationship between the domains is not constant 
throughout the process, and while social science and data are prioritised in analytical 
phases and for testing, design thinking methods without modifications are used for 
ideation purposes. These differences across the process are a clue to answer RQ5 and 
suggest a need for further development of methods in the ideation stages of the 
behavioural design process.  
The thematic analysis and the interpretation of experts’ insights provide support for 
discussion on how methods are used beyond the purely descriptive results (see Figure 6 
for an overview). Such insights have enabled an integrated understanding of 
behavioural design methods usage. The different stages of the behavioural design 
process present differences on objectives. Behavioural designers approach such 
fluctuations by combining analytical and constructive methods. The differences also 
translate in the fluctuation of the relationship between design thinking, social science, 
and data analysis across the process, as well as in the origin of the methods used. It has 
been argued that methods are used to handle uncertainty and that behavioural design 
practitioners, due to the complexity that dealing with behaviour presents, focus on 
==PAGE:105==
91 
behavioural uncertainty. It is necessary to note that, due to the industry driven nature 
of behavioural design, interventions which have proved effective will only be applied if 
they fit the context and the industry needs. However, analytical phases are still at the 
centre of interest. To deal with this unpredictability surrounding behaviour, 
practitioners adopt scientific procedures, use social science methods and adapt design 
thinking methods with insights from social science. These enable a thorough 
comprehension of the behaviour of interest, fundamental for developing effective 
interventions. Because of such prioritization, behavioural designers identify themselves 
as scientists/strategists rather than designers. The underdevelopment of ideation 
methods can be traced back to the analytical-based professional identity of behaviour 
design practitioners, and on their approach to handle behaviour uncertainty by using 
scientific procedures and social science insights. Behavioural design strongly relies on 
social science knowledge and such a relationship supposes a barrier for traditional 
designers to prosper in the field. 
7.1. Implications 
As discussed in Chapter 2.  Literature review methods are a powerful mean to mediate 
learning and hence can be used in design education to teach behavioural design. An 
operationalised collection of methods is a necessary resource for designers to be able to 
understand them and achieve the knowledge to use them in practice. This approach has 
been researched in Section 2.3. Behavioural design methods: Design academics have 
collected design thinking methods and defined them so they can be usable when fitted 
to context. However, when researching guidebooks on design thinking methods, only a 
few methods particularly from behavioural design could be found, and their 
presentation was neither systematic nor operational. Only a few research efforts focus 
on the practical application of behavioural design. Currently, a systematic collection of 
behavioural design methods cannot be found. 
When expanding the research to non-scientific literature, only a few behavioural design 
methods can be identified. Most of these publications belong to consultancy companies 
within the field of behavioural change. On those webpages, the methods are, in most 
cases, only enumerated or vaguely described and used to differentiate themselves and 
==PAGE:106==
92 
to promote their practices. However, there is no compilation to turn to in order to 
explore and understand these methods. The methods that practitioners use are tacit and 
experience based. There is no public collection of behavioural design methods, which is 
essential to make its fundamental aspects teachable but also to provide reliability to 
behavioural design methodology. This thesis has researched these tacit procedures, 
with special focus on the methods, and provides the groundwork to understanding the 
current use of behavioural methods. The collection of methods presented in this thesis 
(see Appendix C.2. Identified behavioural design methods) is the first of its kind. It 
consists of a compilation of methods from an international and diverse group of 
behavioural design experts. The process of identifying methods has been critical and 
systematic, and the elements selected to describe the methods are based on operational 
definitions. The result is a unique and broad behavioural design method compilation, 
which is a starting point for an operationalised and teachable characterisation of 
behavioural design methods. 
7.2. Further work 
The research team suggests that in order to complete the list of identified methods, 
further experts need to be contacted. First, the missing geographical areas should be 
considered, principally Asia and South America. Secondly, behavioural designers 
working internally in companies should be approached, since their context and work 
environment differ from consultancies and this might influence their method usage. 
Building on such a foundation, the methods that have been identified and characterised 
need to be further described to be usable. The next logical step is to dive into each 
method and identify the step-by-step procedure, including examples of use, tools and 
templates. Moreover, concerted effort is needed to further describe the required 
mindset to properly implement each method. Techniques to enable flexibility and to 
tailor each method to context and design needs should be considered. These additional 
considerations allow for a replicable approach to methods, making the collection 
operationalised and teachable.  
==PAGE:107==
93 
Finally, it is necessary to remember the constantly evolving nature of design. 
Behavioural design methods develop simultaneously to the field of behavioural design. 
As such the method collection is not static in time and should be updated as behavioural 
design as a discipline unfolds. 
7.3. Final words 
This thesis has systematically researched, identified, and characterised behavioural 
design methods currently used by behavioural design experts. The underlying reasons 
for how methods are used have also been considered and discussed. The result of the 
research presented in this report is the basis for an operationalised and teachable 
collection of behavioural design methods.

Project plan 
Preliminary assignment 
Project title:  Use of Behavioural Design Methods – An International Multiple-case 
Study of Experts’ Best Practices 
Scope & aim 
Being able to understand and shape the behaviour of users is crucial for companies. 
Consumer behaviour is shaped by products as much as products are shaped by 
consumer behaviour (Lockton, Harrison, & Stanton, 2010). Corporations are aware of 
the power and consequences of people’s actions and attitudes, hence there is an 
increased focus on comprehending behaviour and modelling experiences. Therefore, 
several consultancies have specialized on behavioural design, i.e., are dedicated on 
explicitly intending to influence users towards particular behaviours. Behaviour design 
consultancies promote themselves claiming to use behaviour design methods. The 
methods provide structure for the designer’s thinking and describe the structure of the 
design activity (Daalhuizen, Timmer, Van Der Welie, & Gardien, 2019). However, in the 
behavioural design field there is not a standardized approach. The knowledge and the 
uses of these methods have been developed by working on the domain and by hands-on 
experience. Hence, a common description and comprehensive cluster of behaviour 
design methods is not available for practitioners or apprentices as a premise for 
practice, neither methods are reviewed or supported by a theoretical foundation. 
The aim of this master thesis is to scrutinize the methods that are used by behaviour 
design experts. There is a need to explore the tacit knowledge of specialists, and to 
research best practices that have been proved to be successful in the behaviour design 
field. By analysing the use of methods in real cases it is expected to collect expert 
knowledge to describe, validate their maturity and cluster the methods. Moreover, it is 
likely that gaps will be identified, where new approaches should be developed or 
adapted from other design domains. With this information, it is expected to be able to 
bridge practice with theory and to build the necessary groundwork for the development 
of a playbook of behavioural design methods.

Research question 
Based on the aim of the thesis, a main research question is formulated: 
How are Behaviour Design Methods used in practice? 
To be able to answer the research question, following sub-questions are stated: 
RQ1. Which Behaviour Design Methods are used? 
RQ2. Are the Methods extracted from other design domains, adapted from them 
or created by practitioners? 
RQ3. Where in the process is each Method used? 
RQ4. Are there gaps on the process where new Methods are needed?

Project management 
The empirical data used in master thesis will be collected from a holistic multiple-case 
study of companies within the behavioural design consulting industry. Multiple-case 
study research has been chosen as the research method, with the scope defined as an 
empirical inquiry into “a contemporary phenomenon […] in depth and within its real-
world context” (Yin, 2014), while relying on a triangulation of data from multiple cases 
and both behavioural and general design theory. 
The gathering empirical data from best-practice companies will be done with one in-
depth case complemented by several smaller case studies from other companies. Here 
both successful and less successful cases will be selected for a “two-tailed” designed 
research (Yin, 2014). Four interviews will be conducted per case, while a workshop will 
be facilitated with the in-depth case company. 
As part of an analytical generalisation of the findings from the case study, interviews 
will be conducted with several experts, mainly from Denmark, to ensure triangulation of 
the obtained evidence from the case study. 
Agile project management methodology will be used as a reference for best practice for 
the development of this master thesis, due to its alignment with the intent of following 
an iterative and incremental development approach. The SCRUM framework has been 
considered but rejected due to its rigidness; the structure was found to be inadequate 
for this mater thesis, due to the nature of the data collection. The 12-guiding principle 
from the Agile manifesto will be used as guideline. These consist, as described by 
Croitoru (2017) on: Value product early, Welcome change, Iterative delivery, Daily 
business collaboration, Trust motivated team, Face to face, Working software, 
Sustainable pace, Technical excellence, Simplicity, Self-organize, Reflect and adjust. 
According to these principles a set of conventions have been defined, to create the 
team’s own methodology for agile work practice as described by Agile Alliance (2019). 
*  The timeline of the project has been determined in advance to guarantee the
fulfilment of deadlines and realization of the tasks.
*  Meetings will be held physically, with working hours set to be weekdays from
09:00h until 16:00h in a predefined office styled location, e.g. public libraries.

Postliminary assignment 
Main objective 
The goal is to evaluate activities of the project in comparison to the preliminary project 
plan, and reflect about the modifications, to improve project management skills, and to 
reflect on the learning objective 12. Adjust the staging of the project based on an 
assessment of the magnitude of the task, complexity and desired result. 
Scope & aim 
The initially described scope and aim of the thesis has not suffered changes. The main 
research question has been maintained, as well as the sub-questions, although these are 
presented in a different order. Additionally, a sub-question has been added:  
RQ2. Do multiple experts use the same methods? 
Main changes and motives 
Methodology 
The empirical data for the master thesis was expected to be collected from multiple 
companies within the behavioural design consulting industry. The desired approach 
was to select a main case and complement it with several smaller cases from other 
companies. To do so, a multiple-case study approach was selected as a research method. 
Nevertheless, the company providing the main case could not share case related 
information due to intellectual protection problems. Consequently, a new main case 
should be found or the methodology to approach the project and fulfil its aim had to be 
reconsidered.  
The particularities of the cases beyond the use of methods were irrelevant for the 
research aim, hence, it was decided to contact multiple behavioural design experts and 
gain information about their practices, without linking them to specific businesses or 
projects. Such approach enabled the internationalisation of the research and the 
involvement of a greater group of behavioural design experts. In that event, the initial 
obstacle resulted to become an opportunity that can be now evaluated as positive.  
==PAGE:128==
114 
The methodology was adapted subsequently to the new approach, applying the Delphi 
Technique as framework. Accordingly, behavioural design experts were approached in 
three touchpoints and the anonymous responses analysed, aggregated, and shared 
among the experts for feedback. The Delphi Technique traditionally focuses on 
forecasting future events which was not in line with the aim of the research. To ensure 
the fulfilment of the project aim, it was modified to assure focus on current practices.  
Due to such adjustments, the title of the thesis was revised and the word ‘multiple-case’ 
removed. The final title is: Usage of Behavioural Design Methods - An International 
Study of Experts’ Best Practices. 
Project management 
As initially planned, agile project management methodology has been used as a 
reference. The main modifications on the agreed terms have been: 
*
Meetings have been carried as planned, physically and during weekdays, but from
09:00h - 15:00h instead to 16:00h. Such reduction in the working time was
accorded because, at the end of the day, productivity decreased. This approach
facilitated maintaining motivation, as well as work-life balance, and ensured the
consistency of the meeting schedule.
*
The working location was set to be public office-like space. Due to the global
pandemic of COVID-19 the working space was shifted to our personal residences in
a home office format. Face-to-face meetings were therefore internal among the
research team but the contact with experts was internet-based.
*
The meetings with the supervisor were settled to be once a week. Such planning
has not been rigid but the frequency of encounters has fluctuated according to the
needs. This has allowed for flexibility and the option of obtaining additional advice
when needed, which has benefited the development of the thesis.

Learnings 
Although it has been argued that the scope has not suffered changes its understanding 
has evolved, becoming more detailed and tangible. The initial scope facilitated that the 
research aim was always in sight, providing direction for the project development.  
Difficulties arose when the original main case company was not able to collaborate. 
Although the team adapted to find a solution, it was a challenging drawback in order to 
kick-start the project. To depend on a single source of data is risky, and commitment 
should have been formalised beforehand. In future occasions, such topics will be 
formally agreed during earlier stages of the project scoping.  
Having multiple sources of empirical data translated into an increase of the required 
time to obtain data. Potential candidates were screened, contacted, and once they 
agreed, the interviews and a feedback round could take place. Moreover, since 
international experts were part of the study, local times needed to be considered to 
schedule the touchpoints. The research team developed tables to record the activities, 
however, in further projects a specialized tracking tool might be beneficial for such 
purpose. 
Because of these modifications the data collection was delayed, and such delay 
transferred to the data analysis. In future occasion, the preliminary project plan will 
explicitly consider buffer time. Additionally, considering the domino effect that a single 
delay creates, the extra time will be considered for each project phase.   
The preliminary project plan has enabled us to grow simultaneously with the project 
while being aligned with the research purposes. The development of this thesis has 
allowed us to improve our ability to approach research challenges as well as our 
knowledge regarding behavioural design, while providing valuable output for the 
research community.
