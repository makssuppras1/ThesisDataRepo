ABSTRACT
In his opening speech to the Danish Parliament, in October 2006, the Prime Minister set
an ambitious long-term target: 100 % independency of fossil fuels by 2050. One of the keys
of reaching the target set, is to improve synergies among heat and power sectors where
the district heating is expected to play a key role in the future Danish energy system.
Nevertheless, the lack of transparency and growing competitiveness of alternative heating
sources, are posing at risk the future role of the district heating.
This study analyzes the eﬀects on both consumers and system costs, of alternatives pricing
mechanisms for district heating. The economic impact of two district heating tariﬀs,
namely time of use tariﬀand real-time tariﬀ, has been evaluated on two diﬀerent district
heating systems: one based on fossil fuel sources, and one based mainly on renewable
sources. The economic analysis has been made in comparison with the current tariﬀ, the
ﬂat tariﬀ. In addition, a household heating system which has the possibility of using
both district heating and heat pump to supply consumers heating requirements has been
designed in order to give the ﬂexibility to properly react to price variations, given by the
tariﬀs adopted.
The outcome of the analysis showed that the adoption of the time of use and real-time
tariﬀs can reduce consumers heating expenditure by 27.3 % and by 27.9 %, respectively,
in a system based on fossil fuel production units. Moreover, a large deployment of heat
pumps for heating purposes would have a positive eﬀect on the system costs too. By
supplying 4 % of the heat demand with heat pumps, system costs are reduced by 5.25 %
and 5.48 % with the time of use and real-time tariﬀs, respectively.
The study showed that even in a system mainly based on renewable sources, the possibility
of using two alternative fuel sources, district heating and heat pumps, would provide
beneﬁts to the consumers, if heat pump energy taxes are reduced by 50 %. Moreover, the
adoption of dynamic tariﬀs provide signiﬁcantly higher beneﬁts, and the system costs are
reduced by 6.65 % and 8.88 % with time of use and real-time tariﬀs, respectively.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Research motivation
During the 2015 Paris Climate Conference (COP21), the ﬁrst ever legally binding climate
deal was agreed, with the aim of maintaining the global temperature rise below 2◦C
compared to the pre-industrial level. In this perspective, the massive deployment of
Renewable Energy Sources (RES) for generating clean energy is considered the cornerstone
in order to comply with the target set. European countries have always been at the
forefront in investing in green technologies, especially in the electricity sector [1]. This can
be observed by the continuous growing of the share of renewables in the gross ﬁnal energy
consumption, which reached 17 % in 2016, more than doubled compared to 12 years before
[2].
In Denmark, the government began to invest signiﬁcantly in RES in order to be fossil-fuel
independent and increase the security of supply. In 2016, energy from renewable sources
accounted for 30% of total energy production, with a peak of 42% in the electricity sector
[2]. The current Danish government set a goal to phase out all the fossil, to achieve a free
carbon society for the year 2050 with a mid-term target of having heat and power sectors
fossil fuel free by 2035 by relying on wind and District Heating (DH) [3].
The Danish energy system is today based upon three major national grids; the DH grid,
the power grid and the natural gas grid. One of the keys for reaching the target set,
is to improve the synergies among these grids, facilitating the integration of renewable
sources and contributing to the displacement of fossil fuels [4],[5]. Already today there
are interactions between systems due to the high share of Combined Heat and Power
(CHP) units which supply energy to both heat and electricity sectors . However, the
increased generation of green energies, mainly wind energy, in the future would require
more investments in those technologies such as heat storages or Heat Pumps (HPs), which
would be able to increase the ﬂexibility of the energy sectors and the interaction among
them [6],[7]. In this respect, the DH will play a key role. Denmark has always been
at the forefront in the DH sector, and today approximately 60 % of the heating needs
are covered by the DH networks [8]. However, due to the increasing competitiveness of
individual heating systems such as heat pumps or electric boilers, considerations about the
lack of eﬃciency on the actual DH structure have been raised. Indeed, as opposed to the
power sector whose size of the transmission grid allows diﬀerent suppliers to participate
in the electricity market, DH networks are geographically bounded to very small areas,
limiting the number of participants in the market. The competitiveness on both production
and retail side is limited and DH markets tend to have an oligopolistic structure where
few competitors exercise a consistent market power. This market structure has lead to
ineﬃciencies of the systems since the lack of competition does not push producers on
reducing the production costs, which in turn has resulted in a progressive rise of the DH
price [9] . In this perspective, several studies evaluated the possibility of interconnecting

DH markets in order to increase the size of the market and facilitate the participation
of small producers (small CHPs, solar thermal units and HPs) which would increase the
market competitiveness and the system eﬃciency [10],[11].
Another aspect that limits the market eﬃciency is the fact that heat price, at both
wholesale and retail level, is ﬂat across the year, makes the DH pricing system rigid and not
transparent towards the consumers. The gap of production costs between the winter season,
where the very expensive oil or gas boilers might be used for covering peak demands, and
summertime, where demand is mostly covered by the low costs renewable energies (mainly
biomass units), is not reﬂected on the heat price. As a result, the current pricing scheme
does not appropriately incentives end-users’ to provide ﬂexibility to the DH system.
Considering the growing competitive environment faced by DH companies, a new pricing
mechanism for DH markets could be the right approach for motivating DH companies
to improve the eﬃciency of the system, reducing operation costs and increasing the
proﬁtability [12]. Indeed, an appropriate heat pricing scheme, reﬂecting production costs,
can help harness end-user’ ﬂexibility and increase heat demand elasticity. Furthermore, in
systems with a high penetration of CHPs and HPs, an appropriate heat and electricity
retail pricing scheme is essential in order to align potential synergies between heat and
electricity systems, by allowing consumers to shift fuel. Consumers would opt to use
electricity for heating purposes during period of low electricity prices facilitating wind
integration in the energy sector providing beneﬁts to both heat and electricity markets.
Considering all of these aspects, it seems clear that the restructure of the pricing mechanisms
will be one the keys for targeting the ambitious environmental and energy-eﬃciency goals
set by the Danish society.
1.2
Research questions
The project aims at answering the following research questions:
1. Would the introduction of a price mechanism that reﬂects production costs be able
to increase the competition at the wholesale level, and pass these costs to the end-
consumers?
2. Which price scheme harnesses the most the ﬂexibility of consumers and increases
heat demand elasticity?
3. Which price scheme harnesses the most the ”fuel-shif” ﬂexibility of the consumers
improving system eﬃciency and synergies between heat and electricity sectors?

highlighted and literature is profuse of studies with regard to this topic. In particularly,
much eﬀort has been placed in Nordic countries, and to some extent Germany, where the
energy system is mainly based of RES and CHPs and where DH is the primary source for
supplying heat.

PRINCIPLES OF ENERGY MARKETS
This section gives an overview about the structure of energy markets: how they operate,
which are the limitations and potential improvement.
The section is divided into two halves. The ﬁrst half explains the basic principles of energy
market with a focus on heat and electricity markets, followed by a description of the issues
related to the current district heating market structure. The second part illustrates which
are the most promising initiatives, supported by concrete examples, in order to design a
fully renewable and eﬃcient energy market. In particularly, interactions among energy
sectors and use of dynamic tariﬀs are presented and described in details.
2.1
Energy markets: Structure and evolution
Energy markets are commodity markets that deal speciﬁcally with the trade and supply
of energy. After the liberalization of energy market has been introduced, energy markets
moved from a vertical integrated structure, typical of monopolies, towards a more horizontal
oriented structure where more stakeholders are directly involved in the trading activities.
Figure 2.1 illustrates the structure of energy market before and after the liberalization
took place.

Before markets had been deregulated, see Figure 2.1(a), production, transmission and
distribution of energy were managed by one utility only and, in order to avoid speculation
from the latter, energy price was regulated by law. Figure 2.1(b) depicts the ultimate
form of competitive energy market in which no central organization is responsible for the

Transactions between producers and retailers take place at the wholesale market which

stipulate bilateral contracts with producers. Once suﬃciently competitive markets have
been established, both wholesale and retail prices do not have to be regulated anymore,

better price is oﬀered. Therefore, market price is established by the market itself rather
than a government authority. In a fully competitive market, as the one presented, only
network operators (as the transmission network in the electricity market or the district
heating network in the heat market) remain monopolies.
2.2
Electricity market
Among energy markets, the electricity market is probably the closest one to a fully
competitive market, where perfect competition is established between all the stakeholders.
One virtuous example is Nord Pool AS, a commodity exchange that runs the largest market
for electrical energy in Europe. Nord Pool operates in Denmark, Sweden, Finland, Norway
and the Baltic countries and it trades more than 80% of the electrical energy in the Nordic
market [21].
Trading between the parties take place at the The day-ahead market or spot market.
Exchange of electricity for the day d is organized in an auction cleared at d −1. Market
participants usually submit 24 oﬀers (producers) and bids (retailers and large consumers),
one for each hour of the following day. Each oﬀer is characterized by a set of price-quantity
that indicates the amount of electricity the producer is willing to sell and at which price.
The same procedure is carried out by the retailers. Successively, the market operator
sorts the aggregate oﬀer curve according to increasing price and aggregate bid curve in
the inverse order, and clears the market at 12:00 noon of the day prior the delivery. The
outcome of the market deﬁnes the amount of volume traded and the market price, as
shown in ﬁgure 2.2.
Since the day-ahead market is cleared the day before the energy is dispatched, deviations
between the the demand scheduled at the day-ahead market and the real-time demand
might occurs. Consequently, once the day-ahead market is closed, producers and retailers
reschedule their bids in order to adjust the deviation and to guarantee the balance between
production and demand.
Unlike the wholesale market, the energy at the retail market is traded through bilateral
contracts. Electricity retailers and end-consumers stipulate long term contracts in which
the electricity price is set at the time of the agreement, thus, the consumer tariﬀis not
aﬀected by the price deviations that might occur at the wholesale market. The way the
retail market is structured reduces the consumer risk of incurring in ﬁnancial losses, since

While the electricity sector has a centralized structure in which the ﬂow of electricity

sparsely populated areas, high infrastructure costs and low volume traded do not encourage
the development of centralized systems, and consumers rely on individual heating systems,
usually natural gas boilers, to supply their energy requirements. In areas where the heating
sector is centralized, and energy markets had been liberalized, the heat market structure is
similar to the one depicted in Figure 2.1.

2.3.1
Oligopolistic nature of district heating markets
The liberalization of the energy markets has led heat and electricity markets in diﬀerent
directions. The liberalization of electricity sector has increased the competition among the
producers and consistently reduced the market power of the companies. Price is established
by the market itself and even the biggest companies cannot manipulate the dynamics of the
market. The liberalization of the district heating sector did not change the rigid structure
of the market, on the opposite, it led to an even worse situation from the consumer point
of view. Heat markets have kept their vertical structure without any regulation from the
government, as a consequence, companies are able to exercise market power and set
prices that are better for their own interest. An example is the Swedish district heating
market which, after the liberalization, has been characterized by a progressive growth of
the DH price [9].
Therefore, the question arisen was why the market deregulation has had diﬀerent eﬀects
on the two markets. The main factor that can explain the diﬀerences between the heat
and electricity sectors is the size of the two markets. Electricity markets are designed
for national or even international scales. The amount of energy traded and the number
of participants are huge, and thanks to a well developed infrastructure (electrical grid),
electricity can be easily dispatched thousand of kilometers away from the production site.
In contrast, long distance infrastructure to supply heat are much more expensive than the
ones required by the electricity, and most of the time is not economically convenient to
build the pipelines for connecting diﬀerent regions. Furthermore, there is also a technical
reason: unlike electricity which travels at the speed of light, hot water has a low velocity
which limits the operational area of a district heating network. As a result of these factors,
district heating markets are designed on regional scale with no interconnection between
them and where the low amount of energy traded limits the number of participants. This
fact is even more evident in rural areas compared to urban areas [25], [26], since the low
size of the market implies that there is room for only one company within a geographically
area. Nevertheless even in the major urban areas heat markets are far away from being
fully competitive markets, rather, they have a oligopolistic structure where few companies
exercise a consistent market power.
In this perspective, a ﬁrst approach in order to establish a more competitive market, is
to increase the number of small producers in the system. In general, there is a trend
to have more and more small players, such as solar thermal units, HPs or small CHPs,
on the production side. Small production units have a higher ﬂexibility compared to
large production units and they are able to manage in a better way the demand volatility.
Moving toward a heat market in which the majority of the heat is provided by small
producers would result in several beneﬁts: market competitiveness would increase (there
is a need of a higher number of producers to cover the demand), as well as the overall
eﬃciency of the system (due to the higher ﬂexibility ensured by the producers) [4].
Another approach could be the design of a regional heat market through the
interconnection of geographically separated district heating networks. Enlarging the
volume of energy traded, would result in increasing the number of participants and giving
the choice to retailers and consumers to buy the energy from the cheapest producers
[10]. In particular, connecting industrial areas to the district heating networks to exploit
the excess heat from industrial processes, has been proven to be highly beneﬁcial [11].
Introducing excess heat, indeed, would have the double advantage of injecting into the

and increasing the eﬃciency, and to decrease the CO2 emission levels.
2.3.2
The DH pricing mechanism: limitations and solutions
According to section 2.1.2, the heat day-ahead market is cleared following a merit order
principle. Producers with the lower production costs enter the market while the most
expensive producers are usually cut out. Heat load dispatch is then based on marginal
production costs. However, the price at which the heat is then sold at the wholesale
market, does not reﬂect marginal production costs, but it is established through bilateral
contracts between producers and retailers (or large consumers). This is a huge diﬀerence
compared to the electricity market where the price at which electricity is traded at the
wholesale market reﬂects marginal production costs. Furthermore, the price agreed between
producers and distribution companies (retailers) is strictly conﬁdential, meaning that end
consumers are not able to establish whether what they are paying is fair or subject to
speculation.
Therefore, the actual DH pricing system is characterized by a lack of transparency and,
since pricing system is quite rigid, does not reﬂect the actual production costs in diﬀerent
situations. Figure 2.4 shows the average marginal heat production costs for diﬀerent
seasons in Sweden (since there are 364 district heating systems in Sweden and each one
has a diﬀerent generation mix technology, Figure 2.4 represents the average costs of the
364 systems).

2.3.3
Increasing heat and electricity markets synergies for increasing
heat demand elasticity
Another factor that limits the competitiveness of heat markets is the high inelasticity
of the demand. The price elasticity of demand is deﬁned as the variation in demand in
response to a variation of the price. If the demand is elastic, consumption of a service
tends to drop when price increases and vice versa. However, when a service is a basic need,
as electricity or heat are, and there are no substitute services to supply the need, demand
tends to be inelastic and consumers cannot react to the price growth of the good. In this
context, a stronger interaction between heat and electricity sectors would allow consumers
to use electricity to heat the house when DH price is too high, increasing the elasticity of
heat demand and the competitiveness of the heat market.
A better integration between heat and electricity markets would provide beneﬁts to the
producers too. As mentioned earlier, an interaction among the sectors already exists,
especially in markets dominated by CHPs. However, since the heat dispatch is determined
before the opening of the electricity market, and due to the constraints on the their feasible
operation region (see section 3.1.1) CHPs have a low ﬂexibility when participating in
the electricity market. As a consequence, CHPs might incur in economic losses due to
low or even negative power prices. The risk is even higher in systems, as the Danish one,
with a high share of wind production because of the higher number of hours with low or
negative prices. In this perspective, the adoption of HPs, electric boilers or heat storages
would carry ﬁnancial beneﬁts to the CHPs [29], [30]. When power price is high, CHPs
coupled with heat storages would be able to produce at high capacities, maximizing proﬁts
from the electricity market, and at the same time store part of the thermal energy that
is not sold at the heat market. The energy stored then, can be released during hours of
negative power prices reducing the CHP heat and power production, and consequently the
economic losses when they participate in the electricity market. Similarly, electric boilers
and heat pumps would allow CHPs to convert the power output into heat when power
prices are low or negative. Each of these solution increases the ﬂexibility of the whole
system, and would provide beneﬁts not only to the heat market, but also to the electricity
one. For instance, Chen et al [31] demonstrates that the coupling of heat storages or
electric boilers with CHPs, in a system with a high share of wind production, would limit
the power production from the CHPs during period of high wind generation, reducing the

2.4
Retail pricing mechanisms
2.4.1
The need of redesigning consumer tariﬀs
While the pricing system at the wholesale market is dynamic (at least in the electricity
market), meaning that price changes every hour and it reﬂects production costs, the pricing
mechanism at the retail market is quite rigid, since retailers oﬀer electricity or heat at
constant price all over the year. This mechanism has been properly working until few years
ago, however, due to the high penetration of renewable energies, it needs to be reviewed.
Since power generated from renewable energies has almost zero marginal production costs,
it enters the supply curve as ﬁrst and tends to push the market equilibrium to lower prices.
Conventional producers, hence, are often cut out from the market, and they are subject
to ﬁnancial losses. However, power production from renewables such as wind or solar
cannot be scheduled as the one generated from fossil fuel, rather is subject to the weather
conditions. Consequently, when production from renewable is low due to adverse weather
conditions, conventional producers oﬀer the energy at a very high price, since they have to
recover their costs. The eﬀect is to have an electricity spot price more volatile, with
very low prices during hours of both high renewable production and low demand, and vice
versa. Figure 2.5 compares electricity spot price in Denmark (DK2) for the ﬁrst week of
May of the years 2013 and 2018.

2.4.2
Dynamic tariﬀs: Description and diﬀerences
The term dynamic tariﬀis referred to a type of tariﬀwhere the price varies according to
the production costs, in contrast with the ﬂat tariﬀ, where the price of the commodity
traded is constant throughout the year. Today, several dynamic tariﬀs exist and each
one of them could be the more suitable for a certain system according to the speciﬁc
characteristic of the system itself. The following paragraph presents and describes some of
the most promising type of dynamic tariﬀs.
Time of Use Tariﬀ(TOU): Prices are set for a speciﬁc period on an advance basis, and
typically they do not change more than two or three times during the year. TOU tariﬀs
do not reﬂect properly the production costs of one commodity, which vary on hourly basis,
however, compared to the ﬂat tariﬀ, they tell to the costumers which are the prices in
pre-established periods, allowing them to change their consumption in response to price
variations and manage their energy costs. Moreover, due to the simple structure of TOU
tariﬀs, consumers risk of incurring in ﬁnancial losses is very limited when they are subjected
to TOU tariﬀs.
Critical peak tariﬀ: The structure is very similar to the TOU tariﬀ, however, compared
to the latter, it tends to gives higher rewards towards costumers willing to consume during
hours of low demand and to penalize more costumers who consume during period of high
demand. In some cases, prices during peak periods may reﬂect the costs of generating
and/or purchasing the energy at the wholesale level.
Real-time tariﬀ: Energy price changes as often as it varies at the wholesale level, usually
on hourly basis, and the retail price always reﬂects the marginal production costs . Theo-
retically, is the most beneﬁcial tariﬀs for both system and costumers, since it encourages
the consumer to reduce its consumption during periods of high production costs towards

period of low production costs. Moreover, in case of being adopted by a consistent number
of costumers, it would help to reduce the price volatility at which energy markets are
subjected. Nevertheless, in practice, it is diﬃcult to adopt it because prices are known
only one day in advance (when the wholesale market is cleared) meaning that consumers
have to schedule their consumption every day. Even though the schedule process could
be facilitated by the use of smart meters, a real-time tariﬀrequires the willingness of
the consumers to radically changes their habits, exposing them to the risk of ﬁnancial losses.
In the last decades, several companies have begun to oﬀer dynamic tariﬀs, especially in
the electricity sector, to the costumers. In Italy and France, for instances, some companies
oﬀer a TOU tariﬀwith high prices during daily hours and low prices during the night
[33], [34]. The tariﬀseems to be particularly proﬁtable especially in France, where most
of the heating systems are fuelled by electricity, and consumers are able to charge water
tanks during night hours. In UK, the TOU was ﬁrst introduced in the 80s with a similar
structure as the one proposed in Italy and France. However, the fact that the daily price
oﬀered is higher compared to the ﬂat tariﬀ, together with the decreasing price of fossil
fuels, led to a reduction of subscriptions. In 2017, however, Green Energy UK was the ﬁrst
company to oﬀer a critical peak tariﬀ, where daily prices are equal to the ones oﬀered by
the ﬂat tariﬀ, night price are consistently lower, and only between 6 to 9 p.m. prices are
high. [35] In this way, consumers are encouraged to reduce their consumption only in a
limited time slot compared to the TOU introduced before.
Unlike the electricity sector, in heating markets there are very few examples of dynamic
tariﬀs for the consumers. The main reason, as previously mentioned, is that even at the
wholesale level the pricing system is quite rigid and retailers are barely predisposed to
encourage end-consumers to be ﬂexible. In addition, most of the countries still rely more
on individual heating systems rather than centralized DH systems meaning that volumes
traded are relatively low. Only in those countries, mainly the Nordic ones, where DH
infrastructures are well developed, initiatives for introducing dynamic tariﬀs have been
made. In Stockholm, for instance, the DH provider oﬀers a kind of TOU tariﬀwhere
the heat price varies with regard to the outside temperature. Since the heat demand is
usually higher when outside temperature is low, heat price is high during winter and low
in summertime [9]. Similarly, some Danish companies are evaluating to introduce dynamic
tariﬀs in the form of both TOU and real time tariﬀs, where TOU tariﬀwould have seasonal
prices (unlike in the electricity market where the high demand variation is between day
and night), in the heating market the higher variation is between winter and summer, and
real-time tariﬀwould have daily prices (the large use of heat storages in the Danish system
allows to smooth the production costs, at least on a daily basis) [36].

3.1
Optimal dispatch problem formulation
The aim of the problem is to model the day-ahead market clearing for two diﬀerent heating
markets where the heating dispatch is optimized. Figure 3.1 illustrates the processes
involved in the problem formulation.
Figure 3.1. Procedure for the optimal heat dispatch.
The model calculates marginal heat production costs of each unit and it clears the day-
ahead market matching demand and supply of the market, optimizing the heat dispatch.
The model also deﬁnes heat and power production of each unit.
Compared to the procedure used by Varmelast (see Figure 2.3) the one adopted in this
study has several assumptions: the heat demand and electricity price are assumed
to be known at the time of the scheduling, meaning that there are no deviations between
the heating plan and the time of the delivery.
Since the electricity price is known,
CHPs marginal heat production costs and incomes from the electricity market are known.
Furthermore it is assumed that the DH network is not subject to hydraulic bottlenecks.
In the way the problem is formulated, there is not distinction between the role of the
market operator and the producers, and the whole process is managed by the market
operator.
3.1.1
CHP feasible operation region
CHP plants are divided into two big categories: Extraction and back-pressure units.
Extraction plants are designed mainly to generate electricity, and they have a variable

power-to-heat ratio, meaning that the ratio between the amount of heat and electricity
produced can vary. They also have the option to produce electricity only. On the opposite,
back-pressure units have a ﬁxed power-to-heat ratio with heat as the main output (power-
to-heat ratio P
Q is always lower than 1). The result is a lower ﬂexibility in terms of energy
output but also a higher eﬃciency.
Due to their variable power-to-heat, extraction plants have a great number of possible
combinations of heat and power outputs. The sum of all the combinations is called feasible
operation region, where each point inside the region represent a possible operation mode,
whilst points outside the region are technically not feasible to reach. Figure 3.2 depicts
the feasible operation of a CHP extraction unit.
Figure 3.2. Feasible operation region of CHP extraction units.
The term γ represents the amount of electricity lost, in term of production, when the
heat output is increased by one unit whereas the term σ is the power-to-heat ratio. More
speciﬁcally, lines AD, AB, BC and CD represent the minimum limit of fuel injection,
maximum limit of power output (condensation mode), the maximum limit of fuel injection
and the maximum limit of heat rate, respectively. For technical reasons, most of the units
cannot operate until point B, and the maximum heat output is delimited by the line EF,
therefore the feasible operation region is reduced inside the area AEFCD.
3.1.2
Heat production costs
Heat production costs depend on the technology of the production units. The two most
used technologies to generate thermal energy are CHP units and heat-only boilers. The
method used to calculate CHP heat production costs (pc) is expressed in Eq. (3.1).
pc = e(pt + γqt) −ptct
(3.1)

Where pt and qt represent power and heat output at time t, respectively. The parameter e
includes fuel costs, OM costs, emission costs and energy taxes of the producer unit whilst γ
represents the amount of electricity lost when the heat production is increased by one unit,
at constant fuel input. The electricity price at time t is expressed by ct. Since the goal is
to deﬁne production costs for heat only, the proﬁts derived by the sale of the electricity
are subtracted from the overall production costs in order to identify the heat production
cost only.
Boilers production costs are easier to calculate since heat is the only unit output, and they
can be calculated by:
pc = eqt
(3.2)
Where qt is the heat output at time t and e is the same term used in Eq. (3.1)
3.1.3
Marginal heat production costs
While heat production costs are the total costs of producing a certain volume of heat
(expressed in e), marginal heat production costs are deﬁned as the cost of producing an
additional unit of heat, and they are expressed in e/MWh.
Heat-only boilers marginal production costs are easy to allocate. Marginal production
costs can be calculated simply dividing Eq. (3.2) by the boiler heat output. Since e is
constant over the time (it is assumed a constant fuel price for the period studied), any
additional heat unit produced does not inﬂuence boiler heat production costs. Therefore,
boiler marginal production costs are constant over the time and they are equal to e.
In contrast, CHP heat production costs vary in function of the electricity price, and the
diﬀerence between e and ct (electricity price as deﬁned in Eq. (3.1)) determines the method
to allocate CHP marginal heat production costs. Whenever e is higher than ct, the cost of
producing electricity is higher than the revenues derived by the sale of the electricity. The
CHP, then, operates in back-pressure mode in order to reduce the electricity output with
the same level of heat output. Back-pressure operation mode corresponds to the line CF
of the CHP feasible operation region depicted in Figure 3.2. Since in back-pressure mode
any additional unit of heat produced leads to a higher electricity production, marginal
heat production costs (mpc) are positive, and they can be calculated by:
mpc = (e(pt + γqt) −ptct)/qt
(3.3)
Where Eq. (3.3) is simply Eq. (3.1) divided by the CHP heat output. Since pt/qt is ﬁxed
in back-pressure mode, the equation can be simpliﬁed to:
mpc = e(δ + γ) −δct
(3.4)
On the opposite, when ct is higher than e, the CHP operates in full-condensation mode
(along the line AE in Figure 3.2) in order to maximize the electricity production, with
the same level of heat output. However, in contrast to the back-pressure mode, any
additional unit of heat produced leads to a lower electricity production and consequently

to a opportunity loss for the producer. Marginal heat production costs are positive in
full-condensation mode too and they can be calculated by:
mpc = γct
(3.5)
Marginal heat production costs in full-condensation mode are calculated multiplying the
amount of electricity lost when the heat production is increased by one unit for the
electricity price.

3.2.4
Consumers inﬂuence on the wholesale market
This study evaluates also the impact on the marginal heat production costs, when a large
share of consumers opt to use the HP as an alternative fuel source. The adoption of the
HP reduces the use of the DH and increases the electricity consumption. Therefore, the
aim of the analysis is to investigate the potential retailer savings when the number of
consumers with ’fuel-shifting’ heating systems allow to reduce the DH demand of the
market by 1,2,3 and 4 % of the overall market demand. The inﬂuence on the retailer costs
when the DH demand is reduced is evaluated for the three diﬀerent tariﬀs.
In order to make a comparison with the case where consumers rely on DH only, it is
assumed that the aggregator provides both heat and electricity to the consumers. In this
way, the lower volume of DH consumed is compensated with a higher volume of electricity
supplied, and the overall energy provided by the retailer remains the same. However, whilst
the lower DH consumption has an inﬂuence on the marginal heat production costs, it is
assumed that the increasing amount of electricity used does not inﬂuence the electricity
price at the wholesale market.

3.3
Model assumptions
In this section, all the assumptions taken are summarized.
3.3.1
Wholesale level
• Electricity price and heat demand are known before the day-ahead market is
cleared.
• Network constraints, which include potential congestion issues and network losses
are not considered in the model. The amount of heat produced at the generating
unit is equivalent to the one dispatched at the end-consumers.
•
Ramp rate constrains and part load operation are neglected for generation units.
• Heat storage eﬃciency does not take into account charging and discharging losses.
Temperature eﬀects on solar collector eﬃciency are not considered.
• Starting up an CHP unit takes time. Typically a period up to 8 hours. Also, when
the unit is online it must typically also stay online in a period of up to 8 hours before
it can be shut down [38]. In this study is assumed that CHP units can be switch
on/oﬀwithin a hour
3.3.2
Retail level
• Electricity price and DH price are known at the time of the scheduling.
• Thermal dynamics and tank water stratiﬁcation are neglected. Only the energy
balance is modelled
• Tank charging and discharging losses are not considered.
• The grow of electricity consumption does not aﬀect the electricity price. Retailer is
assumed to be a price taker in the wholesale market.

CASE STUDY
In this chapter the case study of the project is presented. Since the aim of the project
is to evaluate the impact of dynamic tariﬀs on energy markets at both wholesale
level and retail level, the present chapter is divided in two main parts: the ﬁrst
part is focused on the design of the energy system with a detailed description of
the generation and distribution processes, whereas the second part describes the
design of the system adopted by an end-consumer able to cope to the load scheduling
under dynamic tariﬀs. Finally, at the end of the chapter, a brief description of the
technology used in the system is given.
4.1
System description
The present analysis focuses on the design of an energy system able to fulﬁll the heat
demand of a Danish city. Figure 4.1 represents the structure of the energy system.
Figure 4.1. Energy system structure: Heat and power production units, DH networks and
electricity grid.
As DH plays a prominent role in most of large Danish cities, it is assumed the all
the heating demand of the city is supplied by district heating networks. There are
currently two separate DH networks in operation in the city, the District Heating
West Company (DHWC) and the District Heating East Company (DHEC) which
27
==PAGE:42==
4. Case Study
provide the heating demand to the west part and east part of the city respectively.
Production units located in the west part of the city are fuelled by fossil fuels,
where natural gas is used for base and intermediate load units while light oil is used
for peak units. In contrast, the DHEC relies mainly on renewable energies since
the majority of the heat generation is given by a biomass unit and a solar thermal
plant. Natural gas is used only for peak units. Moreover, the biomass unit (CHP) is
coupled with a heat storage to optimize the unit operation. The two system have
been expressly designed in this way to evaluate the impact of dynamic tariﬀs on two
opposite systems.
Even though the main purpose is to study the heating systems, due to the presence
of CHPs among production units, also the electricity market is taken in consideration.
Combined heat and power units participate at the Nord Pool market in the area
DK1 (the one that corresponds to the Jutland peninsula). Hourly spot prices for
DK1 with reference to the year 2017 (is the reference year for this study) have been
taken from the Nord Pool website [39]. However, unlike the heat market, there are
no constraints on the electricity demand and CHP units are able to sell all the energy
they produce avoiding the risk to be excluded from the market.
Heat demand
In order to represent a realistic system, load proﬁles of the city of Aarhus (Denmark)
are used as the overall demand of the city considered. Data are taken from the Centre
for IT-Intelligent Energy System in Cities (CITIES) project which has published the
hourly load consumption of the last 7 years for the city of Aarhus [40]. Since the
system considered has two diﬀerent operation areas and consequently two diﬀerent
demands to satisfy, data has been split in two parts, where one network covers
around 33% of the demand (DHEC), whilst the other provides the rest of the energy
(DHWC). However, the hourly ratio between the two load proﬁles has been varied
avoiding to have two symmetric consumptions, and only the annual average load
reﬂects the ratio one-third, two-thirds.
The simulation was performed using the hourly load proﬁle of Aarhus for the year 2017.
Location
Even though the model does not represent any real place, most of the input data used
reﬂects the Danish panorama. Heat demand and solar production, and indirectly
weather conditions, are modelled using the city of Aarhus as a reference. Moreover,
generation mix technology and all the technology data of production plants are based
on the Danish Energy Agency catalogues.
4.1.1
DHWC: Unit dimension
The district heating network that supplies heat in the western part of the city,
provides the major part of the demand, in average equal to 2/3 of the overall load. In
order to ensure a secure and adequate supply, a certain number of production plants
are present in the region: one CHP plants fuelled by natural gas, several natural
28
==PAGE:43==

gas boilers and light oil boilers. It is worth mentioning that natural gas boilers and
oil boiler are modelled as unique plant located in one speciﬁc position. Since the
technology and fuel used in a natural gas boiler (or oil boiler) are the same, the
assumption to consider one unique plant does not compromise the reliability of the
model. However, in a more realistic model, the boilers should be located in diﬀerent
areas of the city in order to reduce distances between producers and consumers and
avoiding network bottlenecks or congestion.
In an energy system, power and heat production plants are usually split in three
diﬀerent categories; base, intermediate and peak plants. Base load plants have a
huge capacity and the provide most of energy required by the system. A base load
plant is characterized by high investment and ﬁxed costs, meaning that they need to
operate for the majority of the time in order to recover their expenditure. Moreover,
the lack of ﬂexibility of these units may require hours or even days to start-up and
shut down the plants, and consequently huge losses in economic terms. For these
reasons, base load plants supply most of the energy required by the system for all
the year (except in case of repairs or maintenance). In contrast, peak plants are
generally small in dimension, and they are characterized by a great ﬂexibility, which
allows them to consistently alter the production on hourly basis. Therefore, unlike
base load units, boilers do not incur in start-up costs. Since the energy produced by
these units is quite expensive, due to high variable costs, peak plants sell the energy
to market only when the demand is high enough to not being covered by the other
producers, consequently, they operate for few hundred hours per years. Nevertheless,
they are able to recover their costs and make proﬁts even operating for few hundred
hours because of their low investments and ﬁxed costs. In this context, intermediate
load plants represent transition units between base and peak plants.
In order to establish the proper dimension of each unit, the criteria described below
is used as a reference The ﬁrst step consists in creating a load duration curve,
where the hourly demand is plotted for the whole year. Figure 4.2 shows the load
duration curve for the west part of the city in 2017, in which the time period is
represented in percentage (meaning that 1 is equal to 8,760 hours).
Successively, the overall system capacity is dimensioned according to the highest
peak demand happened during the year, equal to 554 MWh (occurred January 5th
at 18:00). Therefore, the sum of the three capacity plants must be enough to cover
the demand on that speciﬁc hour. Finally, the dimension of each production unit
is allocated reﬂecting their own characteristics. The CHP unit, considered a base
load plant, has a maximum heat production capacity of 330 MW, and it operates
continuously for the whole period (under the assumption of neither repairs and
maintenance events). The natural gas boiler, which could be considered both an
intermediate and peak plant, has a maximum production capacity of 130 MW ,and
theoretically would operate for around 23.5% of the year, corresponding to 2,059
hours. The light oil boiler, has a maximum heat production capacity equal to 120
MWh and operates only for 1.8% of the year or 157 hours.

A heat-only boiler station produces thermal energy in the form of hot water used for

4.3.2
Combined heat and power plants
Cogeneration or combined heat and power (CHP) means the use of a heat engine
or power station to generate electricity and heat at the same time. Cogeneration
is a more thermally eﬃcient use of fuel than producing heat alone because in the
electricity production, some energy is rejected as waste heat, whilst CHP units recover
the waste thermal energy for heating purposes. Historically, they were designed with
the main purpose of producing electricity and the heat generation was seen merely
as energy surplus that would have otherwise been wasted. However, today, especially
in the Nordic countries, CHPs are more focused in the heat generation, and they
are the major energy providers on the heat market (they supply 60% of the Danish
heat demand [29]), since the electricity market is dominated by the cheaper, and
politically prioritized renewable energies like wind, solar or biomass.
Unlike boilers, CHPs have a lower ﬂexibility and they cannot operate under a certain
capacity. The minimum production output is typically between 10-20 % of the
maximum capacity [47].

CHPs face additional costs every time they change their status from oﬄine to online

[51] of capacity installed, meaning that start-up costs for gas and biomass units
are 37,125 e and 19,300 e, respectively.
4.3.3
Solar thermal unit
Solar thermal units exploits the energy coming from the sun for heating up the water
through the use of solar collector and a liquid handling unit. Solar thermal plants,
once built, are characterized by very low production costs, since they do not face
fuel costs and are not subject to energy taxes. In addition, since they provide clean
energy, they do not have to pay CO2 emission costs. Therefore, solar units provide
cheap energy to the network helping to reduce the overall system costs, pushing
out of the market peak boilers that otherwise would supply a much more expensive
energy. Nevertheless, solar peak generation occurs during summer time, when the
heat cost is consistently lower compared to winter, and the peak boilers do not
operate, meaning that the inﬂuence on system costs is negligible.

4.3.5
Electrical air to water heat pump
Heat pumps employ the same technology as refrigerators, but in the opposite direction,
moving heat from a low temperature level heat source (input heat) to a higher
temperature level (output heat). Electrical air to water HPs draws heat from ambient
air and supply heat for space heating and domestic hot water through a water based
distribution system. The big advantage of using HPs for supplying heat compared
to other technologies, is the high eﬃciency they can reach. The eﬃciency of an
air source heat pump is measured by the Coeﬃcient Of Performance (COP), which
can be seen as the ratio between the heat output and the drive energy of the process
(electricity of electrical HP). Typically, air-to water HP can reach COP equal to 3-3.5
[53].
The temperature diﬀerence between the heat source and heat delivered inﬂuences
the COP. When the temperature gap between the heat source and heat delivered
increases, the COP will increase and vice versa. This implies, that the COP varies
signiﬁcantly among the year, with high values in summer time and low during the
winter. Even though other parameters aﬀect the COP, it has been demonstrated
that at ﬁxed relative humidity the COP is correlated in a linear manner with the
temperature diﬀerence between the heat source and heat delivered [54].
Figure 4.10 shows the COP of an air to water heat pump in function of the outdoor
temperature, considering that heat delivered temperature is set at 40◦C. Since the
lowest and highest temperature recorded in Aarhus, during the year 2017, were -9
◦C and 28◦C respectively, COP has ﬂuctuated between 1.85 and 3.65.

5.1.2
Unit Commitment
Due to the high start-up costs, the gas CHP is never shut down, and it operates
even when the electricity price is negative. Typically, during hours of low or negative
electricity prices, the CHP unit operates in back-pressure mode, in order to reduce
the power output. As an example, the December 23th at 2:00 a.m. the electricity
price is -50.04 e/MWh (the lowest of the whole year), and the CHP marginal heat
production costs reach the value of 88.70 e/MWh, even higher than the oil boiler
marginal production costs. Since the heat demand (241.5 MWh) during these hours
is low enough to be covered by combined capacity of the two boiler, the CHP unit is
forced to bid at the same marginal heat production costs of the oil boiler, equal to
73.9 e/MWh, in order to participate in the market and avoiding to shut down the
unit. In order to reduce the ﬁnancial losses, the unit produces at its minimum power
output allowed by the feasible operation region, equivalent to 30.93 MWh (point
C in Figure 3.2), with a corresponding heat output of 41.25 MWh. CHP ﬁnancial
losses, hence, are equal to 610.5 e (41.25*(88.7-73.9)), still way less than the the
start-up costs.
The biomass unit located in the DHEC operates for the whole year too. However,
in order to optimize the revenues when the electricity price is high, it generates
solely electricity for 210 hours, where the heat demand is supplied by the heat storage.

5.2.1
Tariﬀs price deﬁnition
Based on the results of the previous section it has been possible to identify marginal
heat production costs for the whole year, for both markets. Marginal heat production
costs are used for trading the heat at the wholesale market, meaning that retailers
buy the heat based on marginal heat production costs.
Exchange of heat at the retail level can be in the form of diﬀerent tariﬀs. The retailer
oﬀers three type of heat tariﬀs to the end-consumers: Flat tariﬀ, TOU tariﬀand
real-time tariﬀ. The ﬂat tariﬀis based on the average marginal heat production
costs of the whole year. The TOU tariﬀoﬀers three diﬀerent heat prices: a high
price for the winter period, a low price for the summer period and a medium price for
the mid-season period. Where winter period lasts from December to March, Summer
period from June to September, whilst the rest of year is considered mid-season. The
prices of the TOU tariﬀare deﬁned by the average marginal heat production costs of
the three periods. The real-time tariﬀis based on marginal heat production costs,
meaning that consumers buy the heat at the same price of the retailer. Based on the
results shown in section 5.1.6, Table 5.5 deﬁnes the price for the ﬂat and TOU tariﬀs
for both DHWC and DHEC markets.

CONCLUSION
One of the keys for reaching the target set by the Danish government, is to improve
the synergies between the heat and power sectors. In this perspective, the develop-
ment of a competitive DH system will facilitate the integration of renewable energies
and it will contribute to the mitigation of climate change. However, the limited
number of participants in the DH market and the increasing appeal of individual
heating systems, are challenging the competitiveness of the DH. Moreover, the actual
rigid DH pricing mechanism is not able to properly reﬂect the heat production costs
towards the consumers.
Motivated by these challenges, the present study was aimed at analyzing the eﬀects
of alternative pricing schemes on the system eﬃciency. Special focus has been placed
on both retailer and consumers cost impacts. A new approach to model consumers
heat load scheduling base, on the ’fuel-shit’ concept, has been proposed and tested
with three diﬀerent DH tariﬀs. The economic impact of the model has been tested
on two diﬀerent DH systems: one where the heat production is exclusively based
on fossil fuel units, and one that mainly relies on renewable generation units. In
addition, the second network can rely on a heat storage which guarantees a higher
ﬂexibility.
Results have shown that the adoption of two heat sources, DH and HP, would be
highly beneﬁcial for the consumers, in a system based on fossil fuel production units.
Consumers can reduce their heat annual costs by 25.9 %, even when they are subject
to a ﬂat tariﬀ, if they have the possibility to exploit a HP to supply part of their
heating requirements. Consumer savings increase when they are subject to dynamic
tariﬀs, because they can harness more the ﬂexibility given by the fuel-shifting. Annual
costs are reduced by 27.3 %, with a TOU tariﬀ, and by 27.9 % with a real-time
tariﬀ. Nevertheless, the study has demonstrated that a wholesale heat price based
on marginal heat production costs is a necessary condition for promoting dynamic
tariﬀs, because, otherwise the retailer would not be encouraged to oﬀer tariﬀs that
reﬂect heat production costs to its consumers.
In addition, a large deployment of HPs would be highly beneﬁcial for the wholesale
heat price, if dynamic tariﬀs are adopted. For instance, with only 4 % of system heat
demand supplied by HPs, retailers that oﬀer either a TOU or real-time tariﬀ, reduce
their costs by 5.25 % 5.48 %, respectively, on annual basis. These savings can be
eventually redistributed among the consumers, increasing even more the proﬁtability
of dynamic tariﬀs.
In contrast, the beneﬁts of the fuel-shifting are less relevant in a system based mainly
on renewable production units. The low DH price, together with the high HP energy
taxes, does not encourage consumers on investing in HPs. However, if HP energy
taxes are reduced by 50 %, consumers are incentivized to supply part of their heat
load with an HP. Moreover, the beneﬁt on the system are even higher compared to

the one based on fossil fuel units, when part of the system heat demand is provided
by HPs. When the system heat demand supplied by the DH is reduced by 4 %,
retailers that oﬀer a TOU tariﬀ, save 6.65 % of the overall annual costs, savings that

storage allows to react better to the demand variation.
The outcomes of this study could be used for expressing the following considerations:
– A wholesale heat market where the heat price is based on marginal heat pro-
duction costs is necessary for promoting dynamic tariﬀs at the retail market.
– Dynamic tariﬀs are advantageous only when consumers demand is supplied by
more than one fuel source. The use of both DH and HP has been proven to be
an eﬀective solution.
– A large adoption of HPs for supplying part of the system heat demand, provides
economic beneﬁts for both retailers and consumers, if supported by dynamic
tariﬀs. Beneﬁts increase in systems with a high degree of ﬂexibility. Flexibility
can be improved by the use of heat storages, large HPs or electric boilers.
As a ﬁnal recommendation, reducing HP energy taxes in the Danish market, would
be one of the solution to reach the target set by the government.

6. Conclusion
6.1
Future works
Environmental analysis
Quantitative analysis of the CO2 emission level when part of the heat demand is
supplied by HPs. Even though, qualitatively, it can be stated that CO2 emissions
are reduced since the lower heat demand corresponds to the lower usage of peak
boilers, it would be interesting to evaluate also the increased CO2 emissions due to
the higher electricity consumption.
Inﬂuence on the electricity market
In this study, it is assumed the increased electricity consumption does not aﬀect the
power price at the at the day-ahead market. However, it would be interesting to test
the validity of the assumption, and eventually, to assess how the retailer economic
beneﬁts are aﬀected by a change in the electricity spot price.
Interconnection between the two networks
Interconnecting local DH network, as mentioned in the study, is one of the solution
for improving both the competitiveness and the eﬃciency of the DH market. It
would be interesting to analyze the beneﬁts provided by the interconnection of the
two systems, and assess if the economic beneﬁts of the interconnection are enough to
recover the investment costs for the infrastructures.

B DAY-AHEAD MARKET CLEARING
To better understand the output of the mathematical model, ﬁgure B.1 depicts
the wholesale market clearing for two diﬀerent hours of January. The model ﬁrst
calculates the production costs of each generation unit and successively identiﬁes
the equilibrium point between supply and demand. Comparing the two ﬁgures, it is
worth noting that whilst the bids from the boilers (gas and oil) are equal, CHP bids
vary signiﬁcantly.
In ﬁgure B.1(a) the CHP unit enters ﬁrst the market due the low production costs,
equal to 10.8 e/MWh, whereas the marginal producer is represented by the gas
unit, which has a production costs equal to 32.21 e/MWh . Furthermore, since the
combined capacity of the CHP and gas boilers is enough to cover the demand, the oil
boiler does not sell heat at the wholesale market. On the opposite, in ﬁgure B.1(b)
the marginal unit is represented by the CHP unit, since it has a higher production
costs, 33.04 e/MWh, compared to the gas units, which enter ﬁrst the market. As
explained before, the great diﬀerence between the two CHP production costs (more
than three times higher in the second case) is related to the power price, which for
the two hours analyzed was equal to 32.450 and 2.76 e/MWh respectively.
Once the market is cleared, and CHP units know exactly the amount of heat to
supply, they calculate the amount of power they are able to sell at the Nordpool
market.
(a) January 5th at 6:00 a.m
(b) January 11th at 2:00 a.m
