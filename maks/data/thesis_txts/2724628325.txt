Abstract
The interactions of multiple domains defining the indoor environment, and the effect on per-
ception and performance of 25 subjects were investigated in three field laboratories, with the
purpose of ranking the most important domains in terms of acceptability.
The experiment
consisted of two parts: Part I consisted of a four day single-exposure testing period to bench-
mark test subjects’ dissatisfaction at three levels of deterioration of noise (48 dB(A), 54 dB(A),
60 dB(A)), glare (10 UGR, 22 UGR, 30 UGR), temperature (18 ◦C, 20 ◦C, 22 ◦C), and air quality
(2.5 m2, 7.1 m2, 11.7 m2 old carpets). The only variable parameter on each day was tempera-
ture, noise, glare, or air quality, while the remaining three was in good condition. Prediction
of percentage dissatisfied was created based on acceptability with the conditions in Part I, and
a low-quality condition was made aimed at 40 % PPD with each domain. For Part II, each
subject chose one improvement in the low-quality condition with the following condition having
the improvement implemented. In addition to their choices, subjective answers were obtained
regarding acceptability, sensation, and comfortability.
Main results indicate the hierarchy was visual, thermal, acoustic, and air quality, regarding
amount of improvement votes and acceptability. Glare was perceived as most annoying, but
temperature and noise level had the largest impact on the overall evaluation of the environment.
Finally, cooler temperatures increased air quality acceptability in an otherwise good condition
(p > 0.04), and it was suggested that subjects closer to thermoneutrality experienced a decrease
in acoustic acceptability. A trend of increasing speed (correct pr. min) in one performance test
for the first improvement was established. It is plausible that the increase was partially caused
by a learning curve. A larger sample size is strongly suggested as some conditions consisted of
too few subjects to draw conclusions.

Resumé
Interaktioner mellem forskellige indeklimafaktorer og deres påvirkning af opfattelse og præsta-
tion blev undersøgt i et feltlaboratorie forsøg med 25 testpersoner, med henblik på at rangere de
vigtigste domæner i henhold til acceptabilitet. Eksperimentet bestod af to dele: Del 1 var en fire-
dages periode, hvor testpersonerne blev udsat for enkelt eksponering af tre niveauer af lydniveau
(48 dB(A), 54 dB(A), 60 dB(A)), blænde (10 UGR, 22 UGR, 30 UGR), temperatur (18 ◦C, 20 ◦C,
22 ◦C) og luftkvalitet (2.5 m2, 7.1 m2, 11.7 m2 gamle tæpper). Bortset fra det testede domæne,
havde de tre andre domæner gode betingelser. For hvert forhold blev der udarbejdet en model,
med henblik på at forudsige antal utilfredse baseret på acceptabilitet. Derefter blev der lavet
et rum med forværrede domæner, hvori målet var 40 % utilfredse testpersoner. I Del II valgte
testpersonerne at forbedre et domæne ad gangen på basis af lav-kvalitets forholdene.
Resultaterne indikerer, at rangeringen af de fire domæner var visuelt, termisk, akustisk og luftk-
valitet med henblik på acceptabilitet og antal forbedringer. Blænde blev opfattet som værende
mest irriterende og skal forbedres først, mens temperatur og lydniveau påvirkede opfattelsen
af det overordnede indeklima mest. Derudover antyder resultaterne at koldere temperaturer
påvirkede opfattelsen af luftkvaliteten positivt og at acceptabiliteten af lydniveauet blev nega-
tivt påvirket af personer tættere på termoneutralitet. Dette var kun gældende når lydniveauet i
forvejen var forringet. En præstationstest viste tendens af hurtigere svar efter første forbedring,
men en del af forbedringen kunne have været udgjort af en indlæringskurve. En større test-
gruppe er kraftigt anbefalet, da mange forhold blev udgjort af for få testpersoner til at kunne
konkludere ud fra.

1 | Introduction
The understanding of the indoor environment is constantly evolving and is a crucial factor of our
daily lives. It has the potential to influence well-being, productivity, and health of residents and
oﬀice workers [1–5]. As defined by WHO, it includes thermal, atmospheric, acoustic, actinic,
and mechanical environments. However, occupants’ perception of the indoor environment mainly
relates to the thermal, atmospheric, acoustic and visual domains.
Thus far, environmental parameters constituting the indoor environment and their impact on
comfortability and performance has been thoroughly investigated.
As an example, thermal
comfort is presently defined by certain criteria and extensive research on how air temperature,
draught, asymmetrical temperatures, and more affect comfort and satisfaction. However, the
experiments have mainly examined the effects of each domain in isolation, and parameters from
other domains were seldomly documented, as they were, at the time, not a focal point in the
isolated domains’ acceptabilities and comfortabilities.
Research conducted over the last decades, has observed possible interactions between the do-
mains with a change in one domain being able to affect the perception of another domain, and
subsequently introduced compound effects in simultaneous exposure of multiple domains. This
knowledge has led to a shift in research methods, where researchers have begun examining the
combined effects of multiple factors on the indoor environmental perception.
Exposure to natural daylight has been shown to affect not only the perception of the visual
domain, but the acceptability of the thermal conditions as well [6]. Balazova et al. [3] found
a decrease in the acceptability of IAQ (Indoor Air Quality) with elevated temperature. Other
studies [7–10] investigated the actions, perception, and comfortability of humans when exposed
to subpar conditions of two or more domains all with various results. These studies indicate that
the optimal perception of the indoor environment is not achieved by optimizing each domain
individually, but should be created as a whole - just as a human would perceive it.
Still, research investigating priorities, perception, and performance of occupants in typical oﬀice
environments are still uncommon. In a typical oﬀice setting, all conditions are rarely ideal and
knowledge regarding combined effects of the domains, and how to balance them, could potentially
increase occupants’ performance and satisfaction. With an increased focus on reducing energy
demands for buildings, new resilient solutions are needed. Building operations are responsible
for approximately 10% of the global energy-related CO2-emissions [11, p. 43]. Active solutions
maintaining a comfortable indoor environment, contribute to these emissions and can also further
increase noise and pollution levels locally. Understanding human responses towards compound
effects in the indoor environment could be utilized in new development of passive or hybrid
solutions using less energy than today’s solutions.
1.1
Aim and Objective
The aim of this study is to obtain knowledge regarding the perception of the domains, as well
as providing information about how it can be implemented in resilient solutions for building
systems. The objective is to:
• Identify and rank the most important indoor environmental domain and the influence
and perception of IEQ (Indoor Environmental Quality). On that basis, predict occupant
preferences when exposed to equal amount of discomfort in each domain.
• Investigate how occupant performance and acceptability is influenced when exposed to
different combinations of indoor environment domains.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Scope
The study used three field laboratories, to investigate different indoor environmental conditions
in a typical oﬀice setting. Data was gathered from a total of 25 young adult test subjects over
the course of two weeks in January 2023 at DTU (Technical University of Denmark). One week
was used to determine dissatisfaction of single-domains for the test group. The second week
was used to assess performance and rank the domains based on equally polluted conditions for
the domains. This was achieved by making people choose which domain to improve first, when
exposed to the same amount of discomfort of each domain. Evaluations of the conditions were
carried out as subjective questionnaires and performance was assessed both subjectively and
objectively.

2 | Theory
The IEQ is affected by multiple factors, usually divided into the four main domains: Thermal,
Acoustic, Visual, and IAQ. Standards related to the design of the indoor environment, are used
to define values and conditions of each domain to ensure acceptable comfort levels in different
buildings and for different usages. The standards usually divide the indoor environment into
categories, expressed by roman numerals. The categories are divided based on PPD (Predicted
Percentage Dissatisfied) of the occupants [12]:
• Category I corresponds to a high level of expectation
• Category II corresponds to a medium/normal level of expectation
• Category III is used for existing buildings with a moderate level of expectation
In some standards, category IV is also introduced. Category IVs requirements are more forgiving
than category III and is only allowed during a small time period of the year.
2.1
Domains
In this thesis, a domain constitutes all parameters that can affect the perception of a specific
environment. For the experiment, one parameter in each domain was chosen to modify the
response in the domain. Therefore, the following chapter defines the four domains focusing on
the relevant parameters, how they are measured, and their current design criteria when treated
in isolation. It is however unclear, how the chosen parameter affects the overall perception of
the environment in comparison to other parameters in each domain.
2.2
Acoustic Environment
Sound is always present no matter our location or activity. The acoustic environment of any
space is the sound from all sources that could be heard by someone in that space [13], whereas

2.3
Visual Environment
The visual environment consists of entering daylight and artificial lighting and is designed to
achieve a satisfactory lit space, while also including a pleasant view. Ideally, artificial lighting
should only be used supplementary, when natural daylight does not supply suﬀicient illuminance
to the work plane and surrounding area.
The most common method of determining expected light satisfaction, following building regu-
lations, are illuminance measurements comprised of both artificial lighting and natural lighting.
Illuminance is defined as luminous flux per area of a surface [20, p. 17] and measured in lux.
Light is an important factor when it comes to comfort and performance. Guidelines to ensure
proper lighting conditions plays a significant role in building design today. Light has direct
effects on humans neural and hormonal signals, especially on the production of melatonin [20,
p. 6]. Lack of both artificial and natural lighting can cause Sick Building Syndrome (SBS),
Seasonally Affective Disorder (SAD), and depressions [20]. Availability of daylight in buildings
have shown to positively affect occupants’ mood and performance through reduced stress and
fatigue [21].
When designing the visual indoor environment colour of light and illuminance is of great im-
portance. Colour of light is measured as the correlated colour temperature (CCT) in Kelvin.
When the CCT is below 3300 K the light is perceived as warm, 3300−5300 K is perceived as
intermediate, and above 5300 K is perceived cold.
2.3.1
Glare
Entrance of sunlight into buildings, along with poorly arranged luminaires, introduces the risk of
glare. Direct glare is caused when a bright light source, compared to the surrounding luminance
level, enters the field of view. Reflected glare is caused by bright lights reflecting off of surfaces.
The high contrast between luminance levels can cause either disability or discomfort glare, and
harm occupants’ eﬀiciency and comfortability [20, pp. 52–53]. Disability glare impairs the vision
resulting in reduced visual performance, whereas discomfort causes annoyance without visual
impairments.

2.4
Thermal Environment
Thermal balance exists when the internal heat production in the body is equal to the loss of
heat to the environment [24]. The internal heat production depends on occupants’ metabolic
rate and body size. External heat loss is caused by the body’s interaction with external factors
and how protected the body is by clothing. Insulation from clothing is usually measured in clo,
with 1 clo being the clothing required to keep an average resting human comfortable at 21 ◦C.
One can lose heat through the difference in radiant temperature between the body and the
surroundings, by transferring heat through a conducting medium, or through convection caused
by difference in air temperature and skin temperature. All these factors affect perception and
acceptability of the thermal environment. Whole body thermal sensation can also be impaired by
local discomforts, such as vertical air temperature differences, radiation asymmetries, draughts,
or warm or cold surfaces [25]. The likelihood of thermal satisfaction increases when the internal
heat production and external heat loss converges.
Temperature can have a significant impact on performance. Temperatures above thermoneu-
trality can lead to reduced work performance, with decreased speed and accuracy of tasks [2].
Elevated temperatures are also connected with SBS symptoms, fatigue, mental workload, and
negative mood disturbance, all affecting performance [1]. A decrease in core body temperature
can lead to reduced performance, affecting speed, memory [26], and finger dexterity [27].

to an expected whole-body dissatisfaction during the heating season, at different indoor envi-

An acceptable indoor air quality is defined as air in which there are no known contaminants
at harmful concentrations and with which a substantial majority of the people exposed do not
express dissatisfaction [29].
The largest impact on air quality perception is the sensory pollutants from bio effluents from
humans, and from volatile organic compounds (VOCs) emitted from building materials and
furniture. Microbes such as fungi and bacteria contribute to contamination, as well as other
contaminants originating from outdoor sources.

them all is diﬀicult and time consuming. The most common indicator of degraded air quality
is CO2, as it indicates the presence and amount of bio effluents from occupants in the confined

3 | Background
In the past decades, articles on the interactions and compound effects between domains have
increased, but the approaches varies and some results even contradicts one another. Knowledge
on how the weight of domains affect the overall environment is inconsistent, therefore the same
weight is usually given to each domain.
In current standards or methods, no other domain than thermal is taken into account when
estimating thermal sensation and acceptability. However, newer studies might have found new
interactions that affects the thermal sensation other than parameters within the thermal domain.
Albers et al. [35] and Nakamura et al. [36] concluded that differently coloured lights could
affect thermal sensation, where subjects tend to have slightly warmer thermal sensation in
yellow/orange light, and slightly cooler sensations in blue light; therefore preferring a higher
colour temperature (blue/cool appearance) in rooms with high air temperature, and vice-versa
in a colder room. In the 1970s, Fanger et al. [37] achieved the same results, but concluded
that the negative effect of visual impairment from the extreme red and blue colours, would
hinder oﬀice worker’s ability to perform tasks more than the positive effect of energy savings.
Additionally, Azmoon et al. [38] concluded, that one’s thermal comfort response is increased
alongside artificial light intensity, especially for night shift workers.
Other than artificial lighting, natural daylight might have the potential to affect our thermal
sensation. Chinazzo et al. [6] compared thermal sensation to the influence of the total illu-
minance on the work surface, where participants reported being more thermally comfortable
in high illuminance levels compared to low illuminance levels in cold conditions of 19 °C, and
vice versa in warm conditions of 27 °C. Furthermore, the same study states, people are more
forgiving in warm environments when their space is naturally lit, compared to the same con-
ditions in an artificially lit space. Kulve et al. [39] concluded light being capable of evoking
thermophysiological responses and alter perception of the thermal environment, but the level of
capability is inconclusive.
Thermal perception could be affected by the acoustic environment. Studies enforced by Pellerin
& Candas [8], Yang & Moon [10], and Nagano & Horikoshi [40] all agreed on thermal sensation
being negatively affected by the acoustic domain. A higher noise level yielded worsened thermal
comfort responses from test subjects. Furthermore, an additional study by Yang & Moon [41]
presented that specific noise types are able to alter thermal comfort. In this case the sound
of running water improved the thermal and overall indoor environmental comfort in thermally
unsatisfactory conditions, by increasing pleasantness, calmness and naturalness. However, nor
the noisiness, loudness, or annoyance were decreased in the study.
Both the thermal and acoustic domain might influence the perception of indoor air quality. At
a constant pollution level, IAQ was perceived less acceptable with increasing temperature and
humidity, as shown by Fang et al. [7]. Balazova et al. [3] found similar results, where air quality
acceptability was significantly decreased in polluted rooms, but also in rooms with elevated
temperatures and no pollution. Bourikas et al. [42] found comparable results, as test subjects
perceived a worsened air quality when reporting a warmer thermal sensation and preferring to
be cooler. A field study conducted by Gauthier [43] reported a significant correlation between
noisy environments and the perception of IAQ. Furthermore, quiet environments were associated
with poor air quality and warm thermal perception, while noisy environments were associated
with good air quality and cold thermal perception. This tendency was only evident when a
HVAC system was in use.
Several studies investigated other domains’ influence on the acoustic domain, with varying con-
clusions. Nagano & Horikoshi’s [40] results present a slight effect of operative temperatures on
acoustic comfort and proposes a new thermal comfort index to accommodate effects of traﬀic
noise. In contradiction, Witterseh et al. [44] found no change in acoustic perception in different

thermal conditions. A study aiming to confirm the hypothesis that a higher noise level could
increase thermal discomfort was conducted by Pellerin & Candas [8]. Their results showed the
operative temperature changed the perception of the acoustic environment. In the previously
referenced study by Yang & Moon [10], they further indicated that acoustic comfort increases
closer to thermoneutrality.
Laurentin et al. [45] studied the visual appraisal affected by thermal conditions, light level, and
light type. The test subjects verified the hypothesis that visual satisfaction was not affected by
temperature changes. No other article reviewed in this section studying how the visual domain
affects the sensation and acceptability of other domains mentions any reverse effects of other
domains affecting the visual domain.
When assessing the overall indoor environmental quality, the perception of domains does not
necessarily contribute equally to the assessment.
Yang & Moon’s [10] ranking of the most
influential domains of the indoor climate in order of importance are: acoustic, thermal, and
visual. The study did not include the air quality domain. In the study done by Balazova et
al. [3], the following ranking was achieved: acoustic, thermal and IAQ, with the visual domain
not being included. The subjective ranking was endorsed by performance tests, as subjects in a
room with only deteriorated air pollution performed best.
Huang et al. [46] tested a combination of several setpoints for temperature, illuminance, and
sound pressure level in order to find acceptable levels.
Acoustics and temperature had the
greatest influence on the indoor environmental comfort. This could be interpreted as, when the
indoor environment is acceptable, it is more likely that the thermal and/or acoustic domain is
acceptable. In a study by Candas & Pellerin [47], it was concluded that the initial disturbing
factor will always be the most disturbing in trade-off situations between noise and temperature.

4 | Methodology
The foundation of the experiment is based on a literature study. Among the tools used to find
and sort relevant literature was DTU Findit, Google Scholar and Mendeley. The main approach
was to explore new literature and discover research gaps in the field, was using references and
keywords from known literature. The initial search for relevant literature, included keywords
such as ”multi domain exposure”, ”interactions of domains”, ”effects of multiple domains”,
”combined effects”, ”influence of perception” etc.

Participants
All of the 25 participants (63 % male and 37 % female) were young adult university students
attending or with connections to DTU. Exchange students accounted for 84 % of the group,
whereas the remaining spend most of their life in Denmark. The participants’ BMI was in the
range of 19.6-29.8 (mean: 22.85, median: 22.70) and three were regular smokers.
Subjects were hired through an invitation letter that was shared in three different ways. The
first method was DTU Inside, an online forum webpage used solely by DTU students and

such as the library, canteen and bars. Lastly, an invitation letter was given to test subjects of an

Subjects were asked to attend day one with clothing they would normally wear at 21 ◦C, cor-

4.4
Data Processing
Data processing was primarily executed in the software environment R with RStudio IDE.
Microsoft’s Excel Spreadsheet Software was briefly used to sort generated data by the cognitive
performance tests, thereafter transferred to RStudio for statistical analysis.
4.4.1
Preparation of Data
Throughout the experiment, generated data from the performance test and questionnaires had
to be sorted into relevant groups of days, rooms, time of completion, and test subject ID.
Part I of the experiment was treated first, in order to determine values used in Part II of
the experiment.
In Part I, dissatisfaction was calculated based on the acceptability vote of
each subject. Additionally, the mean acceptability vote for the entire test group was used to
determine perceptual changes.
In Part II of the experiment, subjects’ acceptability votes were only compared to themselves and
other subjects who chose the same domain to improve. As an example, even though the total
amount of test subjects were 23, the displayed results of the thermal domain will only show the
acceptabilities of the eight test subjects who chose temperature - not all 23.
Test results of the performance tests were split into two parts. In Part I of the experiment,
generated data was declared non-representative, as there were too many uncertainties regarding
20
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 4.
METHODOLOGY
a potential learning curve, the level of pollution of each deteriorated state, and the inability to
compare single-domain exposure to compound multi-domain exposure. Nevertheless, subjects
completed the tests with the intent of getting rid of or reducing unforeseen technical errors and
to quantify a potential learning curve of the subjects.
The performance test website was intended to run on a strict timer and fluctuated with a few
seconds in the multiplication- and D2-test for most subjects. This was however not the case
for the Baddeley test, as the timer often fluctuated with numerous minutes, with no trend in
increasing number of completed answers with the increased time. Therefore, results from the
Baddeley test were labeled unreliable and were discarded, to ensure the validity of the cognitive
performance results.
The generated and extracted data from the multiplication test included:
• Number of completed calculations
• Number of correct calculations
• Time spent
The multiplication test allowed the ability to ”Continue” onto the next page before all multipli-
cations on one page were attempted. The test did not treat missing inputs as an error and was
simply not accounted for.
Extracted data from the D2 test, including a short description:
• D2 Count: Amount of clicks on correct D2 letters
• Omissions: Failing to click on a correct D2 before a new row appears
• Confusions: Falsely identifying symbols for a correct D2
• Total Mistakes: Omissions + confusions
• Corrected score: Total amount of mistakes subtracted from total clicks
• Concentration score: Confusions subtracted from total D2 clicks
• Time spent
In this test, total completions for any subject in any room was defined as the Total Clicks, calcu-
lated as Confusions + D2 Count. The amount of correct answers were defined as Total Mistakes
subtracted from the Total Clicks, and consequently, success rate defined as the Corrected score
divided by the Total Clicks.
For both performance tests, data processing included a summation of each subjects’ completed
and correct answers in each room, divided with the individuals’ time spent to regulate for time
fluctuation.
The number of observations were not constant throughout the experiment, due to subjects being
unable to attend every experiment day, some subjects joined the experiment after a few days,
and some failed to answer the questionnaires on the first day due to technical issues.
Any
missing data for subjects in HQ-5 were substituted with the data for HQ-9 if there were no
significant changes between the votes in the two rooms. Subjects with missing data in other
rooms than HQ-5 were not used in any comparisons. The Excel sheet documenting subjects’
cognitive performance in both tests can be found in the attached folder.

6 | Discussion
The purpose of the experiment was to determine a hierarchy of the domains, to investigate the
effects of improving domains, and the affect on overall perception of the indoor environment.
Additionally, performance of the participants was assessed for the different conditions through
cognitive performance tests. Throughout this chapter, results and methods are discussed, in-
cluding limitations and recommendations for future work.
6.1
Interactions
Throughout the second part of the experiment, test subjects were exposed to different subpar
conditions based on their own choices of improvements. In general, there was consensus between
the test subjects of improving domains they voted as most unacceptable. However, subjects had
different conceptions of what the most unacceptable domain was, as they perceived the domains
differently. Through sections 5.2.2-5.2.4, subjects’ mean acceptabilities of a specific improved
domain was generally voted lower than those who improved another domain. It is apparent that
one subject could find a specific domain acceptable, whereas another subject would perceive the
identical condition as unacceptable due to preferential differences.
In the low-quality room, the hierarchy of measured dissatisfaction with each domain was 65 %
for visual, 52 % for thermal, 44 % for acoustic, and 22 % for air quality, corresponding to how
frequent each domain was improved by the subjects. As expected, this hierarchy is also found in
the mean acceptability votes in the low-quality condition, where the order of domains from least
acceptable was: Glare (ACC = 11.7), temperature (ACC = 12.0), noise level (ACC = 13.7),
and IAQ (ACC = 17.0). Only the mean acceptability of glare and temperature, were located
below the division between acceptable and unacceptable.
This suggests that acceptabilities
could be used to predict which domain to improve in trade-off situations, and is easily obtained
by sending out a survey asking oﬀice workers to quantify their acceptabilities of the domains.
Additionally, when investigating the bold values in Table 5.8 for the first improvements, the
visual domain improved the most (9.3 points), followed by thermal (5.9 points), noise (1.7
points), and air quality (1.0 point). Air quality had the highest mean acceptability in the low-
quality condition, and air quality was the only domain with no significant change between the
low-quality condition and any of the two high-quality conditions. This indicates, the state of the
air quality has minimal affect on the test subjects’ acceptabilities. However, VOCs and other
pollutants have long term effects on the well-being of humans. Therefore, building designers
should have considerable focus on ensuring good air quality, as occupants have a harder time
recognizing deteriorated air quality.
Looking at the impact of the first improvement on the overall environmental quality perception,
the thermal and acoustic domain impacts the overall acceptabilities the most, with an average
increase of 2.0 and 1.7 points respectively, and lastly the visual domain by 1.4 points. The one
air quality vote is discarded, as it is not representative for the sample size. Furthermore, the
largest increase in overall acceptabilities in the second improvement, is found when improving the
acoustic domain after the thermal domain, as the acceptability increases by 6 points compared
to the first improvement. The combination of improved temperature and noise level gives the
biggest change in the acceptability of IEQ, which is also consistent with research by Balazova
et. al., Yang & Moon, and Huang et al. [3, 41, 46]. Although the visual domain affects its own
domain the most by increase of acceptabilities, it does not have the same effect on the overall
environment, suggesting that thermal and acoustic weighs more when assessing the environment
holistically. As no significant relations were discovered, the above is based on observed trends.
In the low-quality room, there was a large discrepancy between the expected and actual per-
centage of dissatisfied for the visual and air quality domain, where both domains had too many
dissatisfied subjects. However, having more subjects feeling dissatisfied with air quality, was a
benefit as it brought the amount of dissatisfied subjects closer to the experiment goal of 40 %.

CHAPTER 6.
DISCUSSION
The acceptabilities of different glare conditions in Part I showed no clear trends linking glare and
acceptability, as the slightly deteriorated condition, V-2, had higher acceptabilities than V-1 and
V-3. This could have lead to a displaced PPD model, resulting in too high glare rating in the
LQ condition, causing more annoyance than intended. Furthermore, the subjects were generally
accepting of all air quality conditions in Part I, ranging from 2.5 m2–11.7 m2 polluted carpets.
Therefore, the model had to extrapolate far from measured values to achieve high dissatisfac-
tion, resulting in a higher probability of inaccurate predicted values. Decreasing acceptabilities
with increasing glare rating and area of carpets in the low-quality room was anticipated, but
the tendency was not as evident as expected.
Predicted percentages in Part I were made under the assumption of domains not influencing
each other. Any interactions or compound effects between the domains were disregarded, as
the magnitude of interactions were unknown. Meaning, the discrepancy between the amount of
expected and actual dissatisfied subjects in each domain could also be induced by compound
effects. In regards to the air quality acceptability, it is likely that test subjects were not capable of
perceiving differences in the air quality, perhaps due to the ventilation rate being too substantial.
Moreover, the possibility of other domains influencing the visual perception seem unlikely, due to
the glare rating and glare acceptability in V-1 and LQ being comparable, even though the other
domains were improved between the two conditions. No previous research found any relations
of other parameters affecting the perception of the visual domain.
A decrease in acceptability of noise level was observed when subjects were closer to thermal
neutrality. This trend was noticeable in C1-T and C2-VT, where the acoustic acceptabilities
were reduced by respectively 2 and 3.5 points when temperature was improved. These relations
might be occurring due to the annoyance of the sound level being masked by the annoyance of
the cooler temperature in the low-quality condition. In both cases, the annoyance with noise
became more prominent when improving temperature, resulting in lower acceptabilities than in
the LQ condition. This trend was only noticeable under subpar conditions, as the noise level
acceptability was not lessened with elevated temperatures in Part I (day 3), where the remaining
domains were of high quality.
Furthermore, at low air quality pollution levels, air quality was perceived more acceptable with
decreasing temperatures, confirming previous findings by Balazova et al. [3] and Bourikas et al.
[42]. This is likely caused by fresher air from the ventilation system or outdoors air often being
colder than indoor temperatures, causing occupants to connect cold air with fresh air. Another
reason for this observation could be caused by the cooling of the respiratory tract. A previous
study explained that air quality could be perceived fresher and more acceptable due to cooling
of the mucosa [7].
Between the two high-quality rooms, being identical conditions four days apart, the performance
tests showed a significant cognitive performance increase in the multiplication test. This sug-
gests, if an increase in multiplication test speed occurs in the different conditions in Part II, it
is possible that it was partially influenced by this on-going learning curve, also observed in Part
I. However, the D2 test performance stagnated between the two rooms, suggesting improved
performance is due to better working conditions. When comparing cognitive performances for
the first improvements, the thermal domain sees the largest increase in multiplication speed
(correct pr. min) of 21 %, thereafter air quality by 19 %, acoustic 16 %, and glare 5 %. Disre-
garding the one vote in air quality, the order of thermal, acoustic, and visual was found earlier
in the effects of IEQ, suggesting a pattern of thermal and acoustic domains affecting overall
environment perception and cognitive performance the most.

6.2
Limitations
The largest limitation in regard to the validity of results is an insuﬀicient sample size. In this
experiment, there were a total of 25 subjects whereas 2 subjects had already left the experiment
by the beginning of the second week. In conditions with more than 20 subjects present, results
showed statistically significant changes fairly often - this number shrunk in the second part, as
test subjects were separated into smaller groups. There were no significant differences when
the sample size was below five subjects, being the case in half of the conditions for the first
improvement and more than half of the second improvement conditions. In these conditions,
the acceptability votes and change in performance show clear trends and tendencies suggesting
significance could have been reached, had there been a larger sample size.
The installations in the field laboratory put limitations to the level of deterioration very early in
the process, and could possibly explain the low deterioration of the air quality. Given the pollu-
tion cabinet’s vertical ventilation, it was concluded that laying carpets on top of the vertically
hanging carpets would reduce the amount of VOCs being distributed into the room. Therefore,
the maximum amount of carpets in the pollution cabinet was limited to 19 m2. Alternatively,
pollution levels could have been controlled with varying ventilation rates and a constant amount
of carpets in the rooms. This would however decrease the amount of removed bio effluents. The
pollution in the room would therefore be a varying mixture of bio effluents and building mate-
rials, depending on the ventilation rate in each room. Having a constant ventilation rate was
decided as the better option, though it created another problem of building installation noises
from the ventilation system. It was not possible to achieve minimal noise and minimal bio efflu-
ents at the same time, due to the ventilation system being too noisy at the required ventilation
rate.
Controlling IAQ parameters and pollution levels are diﬀicult, which is why CO2 is often used
as an indicator of bio effluents. The amount of TVOCs can be quantified, but the compositions
of VOCs from building materials, furniture, clothing, and other sources are diﬀicult to quantify
and control. As the olfactory sense is not necessarily triggered by harmful VOCs, the presence
of odours does not equal harmful air, nor does absence of odours equal harmless air.
The
carpets used to control the air quality did not emit any notable odours, which could explain
the high acceptability with the air quality and potentially affected the modeling conditions. No
measurements of TVOCs was done to verify the differences in deterioration.
The visual acceptability in the first week was not as expected. To know the glare rating for
each condition, pictures from the LMK photometer had to be analyzed and data processed
with equipment not always available. Therefore, pictures were taken and analyzed before the
experiment began, to determine the three levels of glare used on the second experiment day.
Due to time constraints between Part I and II, verification of the glare condition for the low-
quality condition was not possible and the glare rating had to be estimated.
Based on the
documented glare rating in Appendix B.1, it was decided to use 230 lx, 255 lx, and 290 lx in the
first week, estimated as 10 UGR and documented as 22 UGR and 31 UGR. Afterwards, based
on the dissatisfaction model on Figure 5.4, an UGR of 13 was determined as the low-quality
value in the second week, and the corresponding illuminance was estimated at 240 lx. After the
experiment, pictures were analyzed to document the actual unified glare rating of the low-quality
condition in all three rooms.
Upon returning to adjust the light bulbs between the first and second part, it was noticed that
the illuminance in condition V-1 was, for some unknown reason, between the initial setpoint
and the illuminance in V-2. It could perhaps be the light bulbs resetting to ”standard” when
turning on or off, the background illuminance could be different in that room, or a test subject
found the remote behind the division wall. The acceptability graphs for day two (see Figure 5.3)

confirms, that it is very likely the UGR in condition V-1 was closer to 20 UGR. Consequently, the
dissatisfaction model predicts a higher glare rating than actual values at the same PD, and the
value used in the second week should have been lower. This is also noted from Table 5.1, where
the visual dissatisfaction was 65 %. The documented glare rating in Appendix B.2 is shown to
have a much higher variation and glare rating than expected, ranging from 16 UGR–25 UGR in
all three rooms. The increased UGR is likely the cause of having more than 40 % dissatisfied in
the LQ condition.
The phrasing, and subsequently interpretation of certain questions, could have influenced the
answers given by the participants. When evaluating the air quality, subjects were asked to judge
the odour intensity first, and then comfortability and acceptability of air quality. At the end of
the experiment some subjects reported, instead of judging the comfortability and acceptability of
the air quality, they judged the comfortability and acceptability of the odour in the room. This
could have led to more people answering ”acceptable” for the air quality evaluation, when instead
they were reporting their odour perception. Absence of odour is not necessarily equivalent to
good air quality, especially when the ventilation rate was designed to only leave the pollution
of carpets. For the visual domain, subjects were asked to rate the lighting conditions based on
glare, whereas the following questions asked them to evaluate the visual environment. In this
case, it was the subjects’ interpretation of the ”visual environment” that was judged, and could
have included background illuminance levels or the availability or inaccessibility of daylight. In
the dissatisfaction modelling, it was assumed that any kind of glare will result in dissatisfaction,
though some glare could be perceived as satisfying if it contributes to a higher illuminance level
and better visibility in a dimmed space. If subjects found the latter parameters unacceptable,
this could be reflected in the answers. Different interpretations of the environments could also
have affected the answers regarding the thermal and acoustic domains.
Throughout the entirety of the experiment, the sound pressure level decreases from 52 dB(A)
to 44 dB(A)–46 dB(A). Keep in mind, the intended sound pressure levels were measured in the
middle of the room, thereafter the sound measurement equipment was placed behind the division
wall as seen on Figure 4.10. The ventilation rate and sound absorption area in the room was
unchanged in all conditions, with the exception of the amount of carpets inside the cabinet. The
decrease in sound pressure level is most likely caused by the test subjects being more calm and
moving around less.
During single-domain exposure of air quality in Part I, significant changes in the acoustic per-
ception was discovered. The condition with the least amount of carpets had the lowest accept-
abilities, suggesting carpets in some way might absorb some sound or affect acoustics despite
being located inside the cabinet. It cannot be disproven that the ventilation system caused
discomforting noises on that day, as sound measurements were only available for condition I-3.
As mentioned earlier, the four domains are comprised of all parameters that affects them. This
study only investigated one parameter’s affect in each domain, and it is unknown if other pa-
rameters within the domain follows the same dose-response found in section 5.1. Furthermore,
it is unknown if the hierarchy and relations found within this study are only applicable within
a small range of dissatisfaction of the tested conditions, as other relations might be present if
dissatisfaction is 10 %, 60 %, or even 90 %, for all domains. Without this knowledge, our results
cannot safely be applied universally.
6.3
Recommendations
It is recommended to create environments that resembles a more realistic oﬀice space as it
could change the perception of the domains. Inclusion of daylight could influence the results, as
previous studies have shown relations between thermal perception and the presence of daylight
45
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
[6]. Introducing daylight should however be done with meticulous care, due to the large variation
in intensity and position over time. One idea of introducing daylight could be with a north-
facing façade on days with few or no skies in sight, giving no sudden changes of the influx of
light.
In order to further validate and understand performance and behaviour differences, an inclu-
sion of physiological response measurements such as ECG (electrocardiogram), EEG (electroen-
cephalography) or skin temperature is recommended. Moreover, performance tests being closer
to standard oﬀice assignments, such as text typing or reading comprehension should be consid-
ered, and could potentially flatten out a learning curve.
The amount of TVOCs or other indicators of air pollution should be measured to validate the
level of deterioration of the air quality. Validation of the dissatisfaction prediction models with
a small sample test is recommended before creating the conditions used in Part II.
It is strongly recommended to have a broader and larger sample size. In this experiment, there
were very few statistically significant differences in the performance tests and acceptabilities in
different conditions after the high-quality and low-quality conditions. Therefore, it is recom-
mended to have a minimum of 10 test subjects in each condition. If this experiment was to
be duplicated with the intention of investigating all second choices, the total amount of test
subjects would be around 200 subjects. Alternatively, exposing all subjects to all combinations
could help understand how humans react to conditions they did not choose to improve. The
relationships between each domain and the perception of the IEQ would then be investigated in
further detail and represent a larger group.
Furthermore, all test subjects in this experiment were young adults. If possible, one should
recruit a wider age demographic to represent a larger segment of oﬀice workers, as hearing,
vision, and thermal balance can change with age. Lastly, it is recommended to investigate if
sex, size, and cultural background can be used to predict if the interactions or hierarchy of
domains change. If the probability of a groups’ hierarchical order of domains is known, in terms
of comfort and performance, this could contribute to the development of automatic operations
of e.g. windows and shading systems relying on knowledge of the occupants and environmental
measurements.

7 | Conclusions
The acceptability, performance, and sensation of 25 subjects were investigated in a two-week field
laboratory experiment. The domains were ranked in terms of acceptability and number of votes,
and the influence of each domains’ effect on the perception of overall indoor environment and
themselves were investigated, as well as establishments of interactions between the individual
domains.
The hierarchical order of the first improvement was visual, thermal, acoustic, and IAQ. There-
fore, the visual domain should be one of the main focuses when predicting annoyances within the
indoor environment. The same hierarchy is present when investigating the four domains’ effect
on themselves. In general, subjects improved the domain they voted least acceptable in the first
and second improvement, however, the lowest rated domain for each subject was determined by
the subject’s personal preference.
The biggest impact on the overall environmental quality perception follows the order of thermal,
acoustic, and visual. An improvement of the thermal and thereafter acoustic domain generates
the largest acceptability increase of all second improvements. Even though it is suggested that
the visual domain is perceived as most annoying, it seems to have the least impact on the overall
environment.
It is suggested that elevated temperatures causes occupants to perceive the air quality worse
in an otherwise good condition and in subpar noise conditions, noise may be perceived as less
satisfactory by thermoneutral occupants.
A trend of increasing speed (correct pr. min) in the multiplication test was established, where
the hierarchy of increasing speed was thermal, acoustic, and visual. It is plausible that the
increase was partially caused by a learning curve.
Further research are needed to expand knowledge in regards of how parameters affect the do-
mains, and how the weight of domains vary at other levels of dissatisfaction, in order to provide
useful information for the development of hybrid solutions.
