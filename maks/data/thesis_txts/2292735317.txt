Abstract
A liner shipping network consists of a number of routes sailed by a ﬁxed num-
ber of vessels. The liner shipping network design problem (LSNDP) optimizes
the ports sequences and the deployed capacities by maximizing proﬁt. The
proﬁt consists of the revenues obtained from satisfying a demand and the costs
of sailing the shipping routes. The optimal schedule should return routes that
minimize the sailing costs and only serve demands that are proﬁtable for the
company by deploying only the vessels needed. A poorly utilized vessel is ex-
pensive.
The LSNDP was modelled with column generation where a route formulation
was used. Every column in the model does therefore represent a route, which
has a certain vessel class, a lifting pattern, revenue and cost. The LSNDP model
was applied to Containerships’ sailing routes.
The model was tested on real life instances obtained from Containerships Ltd.
The results showed that by optimizing the schedule the company can review
the ports serviced as well as the number of vessels deployed. The model also
optimizes the cargo allocation on the vessels which leads to a choice of more
proﬁtable schedules. By optimizing the sailing schedules for Containerships it
can be seen that there is deﬁnitely room for some improvements.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Around 90 percent of world trade is carried by the international shipping in-
dustry. Seaborne trade continues to expand bringing beneﬁts for consumers
across the world through competitive freight costs. The shipping industry can
be categorized into two, equally important, major sectors. One is the bulk
shipping which provides services mainly in the transportation of raw materi-
als. They are shipped to manufacturing companies in great quantities that use
it in their production. The other is liner shipping which provides services in
the transportation of ﬁnal or semi-ﬁnal products. This is the kind of shipping
that most people are familiar with and can easily relate to. Liner shipping is
the service of transporting goods by means of high-capacity vessels that transit
regular routes on ﬁxed schedules. Liner vessels carry about 60 percent of the
goods by value moved internationally by sea each year (Council, 2015). The
service is open to all shippers and resembles in a sense a public transportation
service. The majority of liner cargo is containerized, carried in sealed metal
containers from origin to destination. Liner services is deﬁned in deep see and
short see, deep see is deﬁnition of long distance routes and short see is deﬁni-
tion of short distance, e.g. Intra Europe and Intra Asia. This analysis is focused
on short see service in intra Europe.

A liner shipping network consists of a number of routes sailed by a ﬁxed num-
ber of vessels. A route is a sequence of ports that perform a round trip. That
means a vessel will end in the same point as it started. Vessels are deployed
on the route and a weekly port call frequency is obtained. The liner shipping
network design problem optimizes the ports sequences and the deployed ca-
pacities. The transit time in this thesis is deﬁned as the duration it takes to
transport the amount demanded between a pair of ports, i.e. the combina-
tion of the port call duration and each vessel’s speed during the sailing. The
demand is a ﬁxed weekly number of containers that are required to transfer
between two ports. Each port has multiple demands, meaning that different
sets of containers have to be shipped to several different ports. This increases
the complexity of the scheduling.
The European Union would not be what it is today without its seaports and
their infrastructure. The member states are 28 in total, but 23 of those states
are so called maritime member states. They give access to more than 1200 sea-
ports through which 90 per cent of Europe’s cargo trade in goods passes.ESPO
(2015) One of those maritime members is Finland. About 90 per cent of Fin-
lands exports and 80 per cent of its imports are carried out by sea so well orga-
nized cargo ship companies are of great importance to the nations economy as
well as prosperity and well being (Ministry of Transport and Communications
Finland, 2014).

1.1. KEY CONCEPTS
1.1
Key concepts
In this section the key concept used in this thesis will be explained.
• Ports: A port is a node in the route network. A certain amount of cargo
is picked up from one port and delivered to another.
• Legs: A leg is a combination of any port pair. A single leg has one arc
connecting any two nodes or ports.
• Route: A route is a sequence of ports that perform a round trip, i.e. a
combination of multiple legs. A round trip means a vessel will end in the
same point as it started.
• TEU: A TEU is one of the most dominant container sizes. It is based
on the volume of a 20-foot-long (6.1m) standardized shipping container
that can be loaded onto ships, railroad cars, trucks and planes. In the
following, demand and vessel capacities are measured in TEUs.
• NSF: The NSF stands for net sea freight, which is the revenues per TEU.
In this project all leg combinations have a ﬁxed NSF obtained from the
company.
• Demands: The demand is the volume requested by the customer and
the shipping company has to adjust the supply accordingly in order to
meet the demand.. In this project a ﬁxed number of TEUs obtained from
the company will be used as the quantity demanded for each leg.
• Customers: The requested demands comes from the customers. The
only revenue ﬂow in this project is from the amount demanded by the
customer.
• Transshipments: The shipment of demands to an intermediate destina-
tion and furthermore another vessel picks the shipment up and delivers
to the ﬁnal destination. This is for example done for legs that do not

keep transshipments to minimum so they will not be used in this project.
• Transit time: The time it takes a vessel to deliver cargo for a certain leg,

1.3
Problem deﬁnition
The liner shipping network design problem consists of creating routes for a het-
erogeneous ﬂeet of ships, i.e ships that share properties such as size, loading
capacity, fuel consumption etc. This ﬂeet is responsible for delivery of goods
between ports and should do that with the lowest possible cost for the highest
available revenue. The demand in the network is deﬁned as the merchandise
that needs to be shipped between two ports. Each demand is identiﬁed by a
leg that speciﬁes the origin and the destination port as well as the number of
containers requested. In liner shipping, demand is considered to be depen-
dent on the service that the liner shipping company provides. One of the most
important service parameters is the frequency of service offered for each port
pair. The shipping companies generally have a ﬁxed schedule similar to airlines
where the customers can reserve a transportation according to the schedule.
For customers the transit time is one of the most important factors when choos-
ing a company to ship your goods. The vessels sail at a ﬁxed speed so there is
little time to be gained on the sea. But the route scheduling and optimization
is where the companies can stand out from the crowd. Therefore the network
must be optimized to keep the transit time to a minimum. This is extremely im-
portant for the companies since the market is very competitive and customers
can easily move their business to a competitor.
All demand is shipped in containers which come in several sizes. The two

most dominant sizes are TEUs (Twenty-foot Equivalent unit) and FEUs (Forty-
foot Equivalent Unit). Transport companies have to optimize the supply and
demand at all by increasing and decreasing supply accordingly in line with
demand. In order to obtain competitive advantages a transport company has
to operate on lower unit cost compared to competitors by having economic of
size and utilization which is the name of the game in competitive environment
as short see services in Europe.
The vessels in liner shipping are never expected to be empty Ronen (1983).
Sailing a cargo ship is very costly and when no merchandise is onboard, i.e
no revenue, the trip is a big loss. Routes are cyclic and a vessel is assigned
to one route which it sails continuously. All cargo loaded in a port will be
discharged in some other port on the route and thus there is no need for a port
priority requirement. An agreement between the vessels and the ports visited is
required to ensure that the ports can serve the vessels calling. A ﬂeet consists
of a number of vessels that can be divided into vessel types. Vessels of the
same type have similar properties like speed, fuel consumption and loading
capacity. Since all routes are sailed every week and the demand is identical
week after week, all cargo will be served. This guarantees that the number
of vessels required will be ﬁxed. The weekly frequency on a route is obtained
by having ships sail in a staggered pattern so that any two consecutive ships
are exactly one week apart. This can be achieved by ensuring that the route
takes an integer number of weeks and have the same number of vessels sail
the route exactly one week apart. This means that the number of vessels of
the same type sailing the route equal the route’s duration in weeks. Agarwal
and Ergun 2008 came up with the idea og having vessels on a route equal to
the routes duration in weeks. The model they present has a ﬁxed speed for the
vessels like in the model presented later.

1.4
Containerships Ltd.
Containerships Ltd. is a transport company based in Finland with headquarters
located in the capital, Helsinki. Since it was established in 1966, and delivered
its ﬁrst container between Finland and the United Kingdom, it has developed its
service and increased its ﬂeet of vehicles. Acquisition of shipping companies
based in both Lithuania and Turkey strengthened its network, tapping into
growing Mediterranean economy and improving its service to countries in the
Baltic Sea. Today Containerships Ltd. has 19 ofﬁces around Europe and is one
of Finland leading transport companies. They offer frequent transport services
through combining sea vessels, road trucks, barges and railway transportation
modes. The shipping routes are divided into two regional services. They are
ﬁrst and foremost a leading short sea door-to-door operator, operating frequent
sailings that link ports in North-Western Europe and the United Kingdom, with
ports in Finland, Scandinavia, The Baltic states and Russia. Secondly, they
have an Intra-Mediterranean container service operating between Turkey and
North-Africa. Figure 1.1 displays the current service network of Containerships
Ltd.

The aim of the thesis is to improve the shipping schedule for Containerships
Ltd. by formulating a mathematical model. Today the company plans its routes

objective of the model is to maximize the difference between the revenue from
transporting containers and the cost of operating the vessel. The problem that
is to be solved is called Liner Shipping Network Design Problem (LSNDP). The
demands and time restrictions from customers are the main factors in forming

of the route depends on the amount of vessels within each class. The method
used will be further explained in section 1.6.
1.6
Related Literature
In this section the literature, which is relevant to this thesis will be summarized.
Although maritime transportation is one of the main global trading methods
the quantity of research on ship routing and scheduling is quite low. In the
past 20 years the research has been increasing rapidly.
The ﬁrst article on ship routing and scheduling was reviewed by Ronen 1983.
He compared several algorithms for single voyage scheduling. Two years later
Rana and Vickson 1991 presented a nonlinear integer program for container
shipping in the North Atlantic. They allowed multiple pick-ups and used a
method where a vessel ﬁrst travels in one direction to its assigned end port
and then turns back in the opposite direction . This suites the straight coastline
well but when the Baltic, Mediterranean and Black Sea are considered a more
optimal method can be found by relaxing the above mentioned method.
The frequency of a departure at a port should correspond to the demand at
the port. Fagerholt 1999, Christiansen and Nygreen 1998, and Agarwal and
Ergun 2008 have weekly frequency on the routes. In international shipping the
routes can quickly become longer than a week. Agarwal and Ergun are the ﬁrst

ones to handle this problem by covering longer schedules with more vessels of
the same type. The number of vessels should be greater than or equal the
amount of weeks in the route. Agarwal and Ergun 2008 solve larger problems
in their article using a heuristic based on Benders’ Decomposition. This method
is then compared to another instance that uses column generation. They do
not include transshipment costs in their initital model. Alvarez 2009 used tabu

optimization. In the model he includes transshipment costs as a part of the
overall cost evaluation.
For a liner-shipping network to be competitive it must offer low transit times
and the number of transshipments should be kept to a minimum. Furthermore
it should offer frequent service to the main ports of a region, with good connec-
tions to feeder ports with high schedule reliability. Notteboom 2006 discovered
in his analysis of the number of port calls on a service and competitive transit
times that the time spent in ports is signiﬁcant and thus the number of port
calls in a service is critical to keep the transit time competitive. The combina-
tion of port coverage, price, transit time, transshipments, schedule reliability
and corporate social responsibility thus makes a competitive position for the
carrier. A constraint that ensures that surplus ports are linked to the deﬁcit
ports is necessary to ensure availability and make the network more efﬁcient.
Visiting many ports in a round-trip leads to a good utilization of vessel capacity
at the cost of long transit time.
The LSNDP model is an extended version of the capacitated vehicle-routing
problem (CVRP), which is the initial diverse model of the original vehicle-
routing problem (VRP). It can be viewed as VRP with split pick-ups, split deliv-
eries, multiple cross docking, no depots, and a heterogeneous vessel ﬂeet. The
most important factors that are different between the VRP and the LSNPDP
are the following three. First the number of calls at a port is unknown since
split delivery is allowed amongst more than one vessel. Secondly, all ports
can serve as the port of origin as well as the port of discharge and third a

1.6.1
Column generation
Column generation has been used for solving several transportation problems.
This method is useful when solving larger problem with a huge amount of
variables. Dantzig and Wolfe (1960) developed a strategy to extend linear pro-
grams column wise. This method was ﬁrst used by Gilmore and Gomory (1961,
1963) as a part of an efﬁcient heuristic algorithm for solving the cutting stock
problem. Desrosiers, Soumis and Desrochers (1984) solved a vehicle routing
problem under time window constraints and thereby introduced an embed-
ded column generation technique within a linear programming based branch-
and-bound framework Lübbecke & Desrosiers (2005). Christiansen and Ny-
green1998 use column generation to solve marine inventory routing problems.
Lane et al.1987 applied set partitioning and column generation where a set of
columns are generated before and the set partitioning problem chooses the
least cost set og column that satisfy the criteria. The branch-and-cut method
of Blander Reinhardt and Pisinger 2011 introduced an exact solution method
using branch-and-cut allowing transshipments and calculating the transship-
ment cost. This method as well as Alvarez 2009 solve smaller instances to
optimality. Remaining solution methods are heuristic methods. Brouer et al.
2014 extended the model of Alvarez to handle the structure of a complex net-
work and a heuristic column generation algorithm was presented using route
generation.
Applegren (1969) observed that the branch-and-price method involves more
than combining the previously known ideas for solving linear programs by col-
umn generation with branch-and-bound. The conventional integer program-

2.2. COLUMN GENERATION
2.2
Column Generation
Some linear programs are too large to be solved by a computer. Most of the
variables are non-basic and have a value of zero in the optimal solution, i.e.
only a subset of variables will be needed to solve the problem. The variables
that have the potential to improve the objective value are added to the ba-
sis during the iterations. The reduced cost is used to determine whether the
column has the beneﬁts to improve the objective value in each iteration.
Column generation provides a decomposition of the original problem into a
master and a sub problem. The master problem (MP) is the original problem
including all the columns, the restricted master problem (RMP )is the reduced
MP with only a subset of columns considered. The subproblem is used to iden-
tify new columns to enter the basis. The number of the non-zero variables is
equal to the constraints. Therefore the number of possible columns may be
large but only a small subset is needed to optimize the problem in each sim-
plex iteration. Figure 2.1 shows the basis variables in the shaded area and the
non-basis variables in the white area.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of the column generation.
Column generation starts with a Master Problem (MP), which in most cases is
an IP Problem. Referring to the master problem introduced in chapter 2.1 we
go through the process of the column generation. Since solving the MP for all
columns in R might be computationally impossible when |R| becomes huge, a
small subset ¯R ∈R of columns is used. The new problem is called the restricted
master problem (RMP). This problem needs to be LP relaxed, which is done by
removing the intagrality constraint. In each iteration we look for a non-basic
variable to price out and enter the basis. The pricing step or the subproblem

the reduced cost for all the column and then searches for the route with the

The column generation method was formulated for the problem in this project

2.2.1
Initial solution
In order to begin the column generation some initial columns have to be pro-
vided. The initial RMP must have a feasible LP relaxation to make sure that
the dual values used in the pricing problem are useful. Poorly chosen initial
columns can lead the algorithm off the course, resulting in many iterations.
An initial restricted master can be found using a two-phase method. Start
by adding a set of artiﬁcial variables with large negative costs and associated
columns that form an identity matrix. The artiﬁcial variables ensure that a
feasible solution exists.
In this problem the initial RMP was chosen to be an ’empty’ restricted master,
i.e. having big negative proﬁts and empty demand matrices.

2.2.4
Branch-and-Price
Volleneuve er al. (2003) demonstrate the defect that if feasibility can be en-
sured, without branching the optimal integer solution might be missed. When
solving the RMP linear relaxation by column generation each node of a branch-
and-bound tree ﬁnds an upper bounds on the best possible integer solution.
The idea of branch-and-price is similar to the branch-and-cut approach except
that this method focuses on column generation instead of row generation. That
is, in branch-and-price columns are cut out of the LP relaxation because of the
large number of columns. This is done to handle the columns more efﬁciently
and most of them have an associated variable equal to zero in the optimal
solution. The sub problem solved to ﬁnd new columns is the pricing problem
from the column generation algorithm. The LP relaxation is optimized until

no more columns are found. The branching then occurs when no columns
price out to enter the basis and the LP solution does not satisfy the integrality
conditions. Applying a standard branch-and-bound to the RMP with its existing
columns will not guarantee an optimal solution. After branching there might
be an existing column that will price out favorably, but is not in the basis for the
master problem. Therefore to ﬁnd the optimal IP solution the columns must
be generated after branching (Barnhart et al., 1996).
In this project the branch-and-price method will only be described, not actually
developed, because of time limitation. To achieve an IP-solution the columns
generated in the LP relaxation problem where added to an IP model which
managed to solve but did in few cases reach the same objective value as the LP
solution returned in all cases.

3.3. OPTIMAL CARGO ALLOCATION
3.3
Optimal cargo allocation
One of the problems concerning cargo transportation is to ﬁnd the optimal al-
location of cargo between the sources and the destination by minimizing costs.
In this thesis the problem has an extension to transportation since it has mul-
tiple loading ports and multiple ports of discharge. The number of containers
has to be in ﬁrm correlation because of the capacity of the vessels. Now that
all possible routes for the network have been generated, they need an optimal
loading allocation to minimize expenses. In this case the general network op-
timization approach is applied having lined-up all available demands on each
route. The program now has to choose the most proﬁtable cargo without vio-
lating the capacity constraint.
The general minimum-cost ﬂow problem (MCFP) ﬁnds the best delivery route
from place A to place B where the network has a given capacity and cost asso-
ciated. For further explanation the deﬁnition of the problem is as follows.
Let G = (V, E) be a directed network deﬁned by a set V of nodes and set of E
edges. For each edge (i, j) ∈E there is a capacity that denotes the maximum
amount that can ﬂow on the edge. Each edge (i, j) ∈E also has a cost that
stands for each unit ﬂow on that edge. Each node i ∈V has a number di which
stands for the supply/demand of the vertex. If di > 0 node i is a supply node
and if di < 0 node i is a demand node. The min-cost ﬂow problem ﬁnds the
cheapest possible way of sending a certain amount of ﬂow through a network.
In this project it will be used to ﬁnd the most proﬁtable cargo allocation for a
route with respect to the vessels capacity. In this approach the revenues are
viewed as costs of transferring each TEU between a pair of ports.
The revenues depend solely on cargo loaded on to the vessels. Each leg of ports
has a demand and a corresponding NFS rate per each TEU shipped. Until now,
the cargo has been loaded in two different ways. First the greedy algorithm was

Chapter 7
Conclusions
The objective in this project was to develop a Liner Shipping Network Design
Problem model for Containerships Ltd. The model was developed for the ship-
ping company as a tool to generate a route schedule. Currently Containerships
Ltd. constructs its schedules manually and the aim here is to improve and fa-
cilitate the process as well as increasing proﬁts. The company provided a data
set that it has assembled over a period of time, consisting of demand, vessel’s
speed, port times, revenues and bunker cost. The accuracy of this data set was
crucial to guarantee reliable results from the model.
This problem was solved with a route formulation, i.e. the variables repre-
sent a route instead of combinations of ports. The generation of the route was
achieved by lining up the ports in all possible combinations for three time hori-
zons; 1-week, 2-week and 3-week long routes. Each route has corresponding
revenue, cost and a lifting pattern. The cost is calculated during the route gen-
eration process and the revenues and lifting patterns are obtained from the
cargo allocation methods. The number of possible routes in a shipping net-
work, with several vessels and ports, is substantial. The number soon reached
multi millions and proved too large for a MIP solver. In order to solve the
75
==PAGE:90==
76
problem a column generation algorithm was introduced.
The column generation approach gave in almost all cases a LP solution with
multiple fractional variables. An IP solution was found by adding the columns
generated into the MIP solver. This gave a satisfactory IP solution which was
used to determine. Furthermore the MIP solver was also tested with all columns,
giving an optimal IP solution and good running times.
Two cargo allocation methods were presented, the greedy cargo allocation and
the optimal cargo allocation. Both methods were tested for all time horizons
which concluded in the optimal cargo allocation giving much better results.
Furthermore the optimal cargo allocation method was applied to four more
instances where the time horizons were mixed.
Instances 3 and 4 showed interesting results that were compared to the sched-
ule Containerships Ltd. is using now. This comparison showed that the com-
pany can improve its proﬁts by using this tool. Sudden changes in the demand
or supply causes the company to want to act fast and change its schedule in
order to prevent sailing with a half-empty vessel on each route. The utilization
of the vessels is important to make the schedule viable and therefore the op-
timal cargo allocation tool will be valuable when lining up the demands. The
best demands satisfaction obtained from the program with an IP solution was
around 82%. This leads to the conclusions that the sailing route between some
ports is too expensive compared to the revenues and amount of demand.
The company can amongst other use the model presented as a management
tool. It can help make decisions regarding the amount of vessels to be used or
which ports to service as well as optimizing the cargo allocation for any route.

7.1. FUTURE WORK
7.1
Future work
The approach used in this project to optimize the route schedule for Container-
ships Ltd. is good but it can be improved in several ways. The following points
are improvements that I was not able to implement during the work on this
thesis because of time restrictions but will be explored further in the future.
• The implementation of the optimal cargo allocation algorithm where the
vessels are not empty in the beginning of the routes. In this approach it
would be assumed that the routes are cyclic and the vessel is never empty.
This is a more realistic method and thus something the company would
rather use. The implementation was tried for a part of the program and
it is not complicated to program and it was returning interesting results.
Unfortunately it did take more time than expected and thus no remaining
time to implement the rest.
• An interesting test would be to see how the model reacts to adding more
columns in each iterations. For example if 10 columns were added at
a time. The ideal reaction to this would be fewer iterations and thus
shorter running time. This can also improve the IP solution retrieved
since more columns are added which gives the MIP solver more options
and a possibility of being closer to the optimal solution. When testing
the model by adding more columns it would also be interesting to test
how it reacts to adding all columns with positive reduced cost in each
iteration.
• An interesting test would be to increase the revenues made for each TEU
transported. This might make the demand satisfaction percentage a little
bit better. By increasing the revenues the cargo in question becomes more
appealing to transport and thus higher demand percentage. It would be
assumed that this increase would be equivalent of what the company
receives for the land transportation of each TEU.
