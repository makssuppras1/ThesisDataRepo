Abstract
This thesis deals with investigating the performance of three methods for
solving bi-objective stochastic programs. The problem these are tested on hier-
archical facility location problem in which costs and delivery time are being
optimized for. All methods are based upon a dichotomic search procedure and
are all tested using different combinations of number of cheese manufacturers
and number of scenarios. The three tested methods are a Mixed-integer pro-
gramming (MIP) model, a Benders decomposed model (BD), and a Benders
decomposed model using k-means clustering (BDK).
Besides testing these methods, the method for picking scenarios is also tested
using different combinations of number of cheese manufacturers and number
of scenarios. The tested methods for generating scenarios are by picking scenar-
ios directly using Monte Carlo sampling, picking a large number of scenarios
using the same method, and reducing these using two different methods. The
two tested scenario reduction techniques are k-medoids clustering and Fast
Forward Selection.
The two scenario reduction techniques were found to generate more consistent
objective values compared to using Monte Carlo sampling directly. However,
the difference in precision for the three methods was found to be insignificant.
Results showed that the MIP model performed the best when the number of
scenarios were low, whereas the BDK method was found to perform the best
for instances containing a large set of scenarios. The BD model performed the
worst in all test instances and thereby having k-means clusters in the master
problem was found to be beneficial for all tested instances. Both the BD and
BDK models was found to be exposed to highly skewed distributions in the
computational costs of finding each point in the efficient frontiers.

Introduction
Many real-world problems contain different objectives which a decision-maker
wants to optimize for. Some of these objectives may be possible to optimize
simultaneously. However, some objectives may conflict with each other, making
it impossible to find a single solution optimal for all objectives.
In some cases, there can be a lexicographic order in the importance of the
objectives. However, in other cases, the importance of the objectives may not
possess a lexicographic order and instead needs to be weighted.
In such cases, a set of rational trade-off solutions needs to be found, and for
this purpose, a multi-objective optimization problem must be solved.
Another feature of many real-world problems is that they often contain un-
certainty to some extent. Examples of problems in which uncertainty is a
factor, include transportation problems where the time between two points
can be uncertain due to rough seas in sea transportation, or congestion in road
transport, deciding which stocks to buy where the future value of stocks is
unknown, or in energy planning where neither the future demand and the
future supply from renewable energy sources are known, as these normally
depend on factors such as the wind or the amount of sunlight.
When dealing with uncertainty, a finite set of scenarios is needed for doing
mathematical optimization [14]. In cases where only the distribution of the
continuous uncertain parameter is known an infinite number of unique scenar-
ios can be generated. As more scenarios are picked the more the distribution
of scenarios reflects the full continuous distribution. However, the more sce-
narios are included in the optimization, the more computationally expensive it
becomes to solve, making it necessary to balance the quality of the solution
and the computational cost.
Previously the Benders decomposition algorithm has shown promising results
as a strategy for solving problems in which uncertainty is a factor [7]. This is
due to the decomposable structure of stochastic programs where the number
of variables quickly become large, but only a fraction of these variables are
complicating variables. This means the complicating variables can be solved
1
1
==PAGE:6==
in a master problem and the rest of the variables can be found by solving a set
of smaller linear problems each representing a scenario.
Besides the number of scenarios included in the optimization, the quality of
the solution also depends on how these are chosen. One way to pick scenarios
is by using the Monte Carlo method for doing random sampling. This can be
done either by using randomly picked scenarios directly or by picking more
scenarios initially and reducing the scenarios using scenario reduction tech-
niques to obtain the desired number of scenarios to include in the optimization
process. Stochastic programming becomes more computationally expensive
as the number of scenarios increases. At the same time, the number of points
in the efficient frontier can be large making it necessary to run the program
multiple times, making it highly relevant to find a method for reducing the
computational cost.
This thesis deals with comparing methods for finding a set of rational trade-
off solutions for a bi-objective problem with two conflicting objectives in
which uncertainty is a factor. The thesis consists of two comparisons. The
first comparison consists of comparing three methods for picking scenarios
to include in the optimization, as being able to pick scenarios such that the
desired quality of the solution is obtained at a lower number of scenarios
naturally leads to less computational cost.
The first method for picking scenarios is by doing Monte Carlo sampling in
which scenarios are picked randomly from a known distribution. The two
other methods also pick scenarios from a known distribution, however, in both
cases, a large set of scenarios are initially generated and afterward reduced,
using two different scenario reduction techniques. The two tested methods for
doing the scenario reduction are using Fast Forward Selection and k-medoid
clustering.
The second comparison consists of comparing three methods for solving the
stochastic problem. The first method is by solving the problem as a MIP. In the
second method, the problem is solved by applying the Benders decomposition
algorithm. The last method also utilizes the Benders decomposition algorithm,
however, in this method, k-means clusters of the scenarios are kept within the
master problem. The methods are tested on a hierarchical facility location
problem, namely a reverse whey logistic problem.

1.1 The Hierarchical Facility Location
Problem
The hierarchical facility location problem deals with finding the optimal place-
ments of collection centers and production plants. The network consists of a
set of cheese manufacturers who all produce whey that formerly was consid-
ered a waste product in the production of cheese [6]. However, developments
in technology have turned whey into a valuable dairy ingredient making it
meaningful to collect the whey and process it [6].
For processing, the whey must be transported to a collection center and af-
terward to a production plant. In the collection centers, each liter of raw
whey is concentrated into 0.339 liter of concentrated whey [6]. The possible
placements of collection centers and production plants are at all the cheese
manufacturers. However, it is only possible to place a production plant at sites
where a collection center is established. This property is called nested hierarchy
[6]. An example of how such a network could look can be seen in figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: An example of how a network for collecting whey could look. [6] The
placement of the collection centers and plants is to be decided before
demand is known. The flow within the established network can be
decided once the demand is known.
The decision of where to construct collection centers and production plants,
has to be taken prior to demand - or amount of produced whey - at each
location is known. However, the distribution of demand is known, and by
generating a set of scenarios of the demand, this thesis tries to exploit this
information.

The construction of both collection centers and production plants come with
a monetary cost. Transportation via arcs in the network are both associated
with monetary costs and a cost in time spent transporting the whey. These two

desired to collect the whey at low monetary costs while on the other hand it

The problem is a linear relaxation of the full problem where each combination

problem. This is done to reduce computational cost.
In regard to both objectives, the problem is solved risk neutral, meaning that
it is solely the expected value of the two objectives which are optimized for.

2.1 Dichotomic Search
When dealing with a problem in which two objectives conflicts with each other,
and assuming the objectives possess no lexicographic order, a procedure for
finding a set of rational solutions is needed. The dichotomic search is such a
procedure and is the procedure which all methods in this thesis builds upon to
find the set of rational solutions.
A bi-objective optimization problem can generally be formulated as:
Min f(x)
Min g(x)
s.t. x ∈X
, where f(x) and g(x) are the two objectives to minimize. X defines the feasible
region of the decision variables.
A feasible solution, x, is said to be Pareto optimal or efficient if no feasible
solutions for x’ exist such that f(x′) ≤f(x) and g(x′) ≤g(x), where one or
both of these inequalities are strict [8].
A feasible solution x is called weakly Pareto optimal or weakly efficient if no x”
solution exist such that f(x′′) < f(x) and g(x′′) < g(x) [8].
The point (δ, ω) ∈R2 is called an efficient point if a Pareto optimal solution
x exists such that f(x) = δ and g(x) = ω [8]. The efficient point (δ, ω) ∈R2
is furthermore said to be supported if a scalar λ ∈[0, 1] and a feasible point x
exists, such that x minimizes λf(x) + (1 −λ)g(x) and f(x) = δ, g(x) = ω. The
points not belonging to the supported set are defined as unsupported efficient
points [8]. The set of efficient points which also belongs to the convex hull of
all efficient points, are said to be extreme supported efficient points [8].
In this thesis, the aim is to find the full set of the extreme supported effi-
cient points to generate the full convex hull of efficient solutions. For this

2.2 Stochastic Programming
Uncertainty is a factor in most real-world problems. The uncertainty can take
form in many ways such as travel time, returns of investment, demand, or
supply of energy from a renewable source.
In this case, the demand from each cheese manufacturer is uncertain. However,
even though the exact demand is unknown, information about the distribution
of the demand is known. Stochastic programming deals with exploiting this
information to make a solution optimizing the expected outcome.
In this project, the locations of collection centers and plants must be decided
before demand is known. These decisions are called first stage decisions or
here-and-now decisions. The flow of whey through the established system, can
be decided once the uncertainty is revealed, and the demand is known. These
decisions are second stage decisions and are called the recourse decisions. The
method is called two-stage stochastic programming, since the problem deals
with two stages in which decisions are to be made.
The uncertain parameter in this project is the demand. It is known that the
distribution of the demand comes from a continuous uniform distribution, with
a minimum of one and a maximum of five. The solver can not directly exploit
this information, as it needs a finite set of scenarios. Since the distribution of
the demand is continuous, an infinite number of scenarios can be generated,
making it impossible to make a discrete representation of the demand, which
fully represents the continuous distribution. How many, as well as how these
scenarios are chosen, can be essential for the performance of the solution.
2.2.1 Handling the Uncertain Demands
To handle the uncertain demand and generate a finite set of scenarios, the
Monte Carlo sampling method can be used to pick scenarios from a known
distribution. In this method, demands are randomly picked from the uniform
distribution repeatedly until the desired number of scenarios is achieved. One
of the drawbacks of using this method is that a large number of scenarios

2.2.2 Value of Stochastic Programming
An alternative to stochastic programming is simply to optimize for the expected
value of the uncertain parameter. For this application, this means the weighted
mean of demand in all scenarios. This solution method is called the expected
value problem (EV) [14]. By evaluating the first stage decision found by EV
in all scenarios, the objective for all scenarios can be obtained. By weighting
the objectives of all scenarios with their probabilities the expectation of the
expected value solution (EEV) are obtained [14]. The following relation for a
minimization problem holds between the EEV and RP, where RP is the optimal
value of the stochastic program [14]:
RP ≤EVV
(2.7)
This also makes sense as the EV first stage decision is also a feasible solution for
the stochastic program, while the stochastic program contains the information
for the scenarios. The difference between these two, EEV −RP, is called the
value of the stochastic solution (VSS)[14]. VSS tells if dealing with uncertainty
in a multi-stage stochastic program adds value to the solution. This value
will be high, if much value is added to the solution by utilizing stochastic
programming. Contradictory, this value will be low, if the use of stochastic
programming adds little value to the solution.[14]

2.2.3 Expected Value of Perfect Information
Assuming the future demand could be unveiled at some cost, how much would
one be willing to pay to obtain this information?
If the exact future demands were known one should optimize for this specific
demand. This corresponds to being able to choose a set of first stage decisions
for all scenarios and choose the first stage decisions once the demand is
unveiled. This method is called the wait-and-see approach [14]. The expected
objective for this approach can be calculated by weighting the objectives for
all scenarios with their probabilities. The objective for the expected value of
the wait-and-see (WS) approach has the following inequality [14]:
WS ≤RP
(2.8)
The difference between the two approaches gives the expected value of perfect
information (EVPI), EV PI = RP −WS [14]. This value tells a decision-maker
how much they should be willing to pay for obtaining perfect information
about the future demand prior to deciding the first stage decision.
2.2.4 Scenario Reduction
As the two conflicting objectives of including few scenarios in the optimization
to reduce the speed of the optimization procedure, and including many sce-
narios in the optimization to improve the quality of the found solution exists,
a natural question arises. Is it possible to include fewer scenarios, while still
obtaining similar solution quality?
When generating scenarios, there is no guarantee that these scenarios are
different from each other or that they represent the full range of possible
scenarios. The method for covering the most range of possible scenarios is to
generate a high number of scenarios. When generating many scenarios, some
of these will be similar. This means that in the solution process there is opti-
mized for two very similar scenarios. One way of saving time is then to simply
cluster these two scenarios, optimize for the cluster and assign the probability
of the cluster as the sum of the probabilities of the scenarios included in the
cluster.
This property can be expanded to include more scenarios and use several
clusters. Two different methods for assigning scenarios to clusters are tested,

K-medoid Clustering
The k-medoid clustering algorithm works similarly to the k-means algorithm.
However, instead of the cluster center being the mean of the data points within
each cluster, the cluster centers are chosen as the data points minimizing the
distance from all points to the nearest cluster center.
The algorithm is also initialized using the k-means++ algorithm. The data
points are also assigned to the nearest cluster using equation (2.9), which in
this case is the medoids, being a data point in itself. Once data points are
assigned to the nearest medoid each cluster is tested for if any other data
point within the cluster generates a lower overall distance. If a data point
within a cluster reduces the overall distance the data point becomes the new
medoid. This procedure continues until no data point is changing the cluster
it is assigned to and no data point within each cluster reduces the overall
distance [9]. The probability of each cluster is the sum of the probabilities of
the points assigned to the cluster.
The k-medoid procedure utilized in this thesis is referred to as a "k-means like
algorithm for k-medoids" in [16], and is different from the more widely used
Partitioning Around Medoids (PAM) method. The main difference between the

2.2.5 Evaluating the Performance of the Solutions
When dealing with optimization of problems in which uncertainty is a factor,
two things are essential. The first thing is that consistent results are generated.
This means that the scenarios should be representative and not biased. The
method for testing this property is by doing in-sample testing.
In-sample testing is conducted by repeatedly using the same method for picking
scenarios and optimizing for these scenarios to check if the objective value is
stable or if it fluctuates heavily [5, 10].
The second essential thing is to check the robustness of the solution. The
method for this is called out-of-sample testing and the aim is to test scenarios
that are possible but not considered in the optimization. [5, 10]
For real-world optimization problems, scenarios would normally have been
generated based on historical data, e.g. previous demand in an electricity
system. Then an out-of-sample test would be performed by doing another
method for sampling, such as bootstrapping or ARIMA, and picking a large
number of scenarios [17].
Once other scenarios have been generated using another method the first
stage decisions found by the first method are fixed and evaluated in the newly
generated scenarios. As the scenario generation method in this report builds
upon an assumption of how the distribution of demand is instead of being
based on historical data, it is impossible to use bootstrapping. As the demand
for each producer is independent of each other and does not depend on time
steps the ARIMA model would also not be suited for this purpose either.
Besides doing the out-of-sample test, backtesting can also be applied in which
the first stage decisions are tested directly on historical data. As no other
obvious method for sampling has been found, the out-of-sample testing for
this application is performed by picking 100.000 scenarios using the Monte
Carlo method and evaluating the first stage decision in these scenarios.

does not return any cuts. When the master problem is optimal and this solution
does not return a cut from the subproblem, the optimal solution for the whole
problem is found.

2.3.2 Implementation
When the Benders decomposition algorithm has been used in the models these
are implemented using what is often referred to as ’the modern way’ or the
Branch-and-Benders-Cut [7].
The classical way is to solve the master problem in each iteration, which in itself
is a mixed-integer linear program (MILP). For this reason, the convergence is
often slow [7].
In the modern way, a single tree is built where a Branch-and-Benders-Cut
is generated at the nodes. This procedure is implemented using the Gurobi
callback function which then returns the generated cut as a ’user cut’ for the
linear relaxed master solution. For master problem solutions that are not linear
relaxed a lazy constraint is added to the master problem as an optimality cut,
if the value of η is found to be lower than the optimal objective value of the
subproblems.
Preliminary tests showed that the models built upon the Benders decomposi-
tion algorithm performed the best using a single core in the master problem.
For this reason this is the setting for the two models, BD and BDK. The two
models, BD and BDK, still used four cores when solving the subproblems in
which the scenarios were solved in parallel with each scenario solved using
a single core. The BDK model is implemented using 5 k-means clusters. All
other settings were set to the default settings in Gurobi.
All methods were implemented using warm start for all points in the efficient
frontier besides the point in which all weight were on monetary cost as no
solution were known prior to this. When optimizing exclusively for delivery
time the opening of all collection centers and production plants is injected as
warm start. For all other points found in the efficient frontiers both points

used to generate the new λ-value is tested as warm start. The best of these
is injected as warm start to find the new point. By warm starting the solver

The two endpoints in the efficient frontiers, which are those found using λ = 1
and λ = 0, are not solved lexicographical in the implementation. This means
that the initial point is not necessarily a part of the efficient frontier, but could
be. In cases where these are not part of the efficient frontier are these points
substituted later in the procedure. For λ = 1 the endpoint is substituted if
a new point with the same costs are found with a lower delivery time. This
is correspondingly the case for λ = 0 where the endpoint is substituted if a
new point is found having the same delivery time but spending less monetary
cost.

Discussion
The stochastic results showed a low relative EVPI and a high relative VSS for
all tested number of demand points and scenarios. This is seen as a sign of
that modeling the problem using stochastic programming is well suited.
The out-of-sample tests did not show any significant performance gains from
using the two tested clustering techniques. Both reduction techniques were
found to obtain a slightly too low objective value compared to the out-of-
sample objective value.
An explanation for this could be that the probability distribution of the reduced
scenarios is uneven, and therefore the solver finds a solution that is very good
for some scenarios but not all. However, both scenario reduction methods were
found to produce more consistent objective values than by picking scenarios
using Monte Carlo sampling directly.
A comparison of the computational costs of the three tested methods for solving
the stochastic programs was also conducted. The test showed that the method
using Benders decomposition without k-means clusters in the master problem
was unable to compete with the MIP formulation. A possible explanation for
this could be that the BD model contains no information in the master problem
of how the scenarios affect the objective value. Further, when the weighting
of the two objectives is on minimizing delivery time, most of the objective
value originates from the subproblem. In such cases, when the weight on
monetary costs is low, opening collection centers and production plants only
affects the objective value to a small extent directly. This property could make
it difficult for the solver to rule out solutions in the master problem before
they are checked in the subproblem, making it necessary to generate a high
amount of optimality cuts.
The same tendencies were found for the Benders decomposition using k-means
clusters in the master problem, however only for 20 and 30 demand points.
As the k-means clusters’ function is to give some information to the master
problem about the objective value of the subproblem the problem has not
been found as severe for the BDK method as for the BD method. However,
49

distributions for finding each point in the efficient frontiers using the BDK
method were still found to be highly skewed for more than 10 demand points,
making it highly relevant to explore ways of reducing the impact of this
property.
4.1 Perspectives
For further investigation of the obtained results in the out-of-sample test, an
investigation of the output distribution for the different scenarios optimized
could be conducted. A similar investigation could also be conducted for the
distribution of the output for the out-of-sample scenarios. Such investigations
would contribute to a deeper understanding of why the difference in perfor-
mance between the methods was found to be relatively low.
Furthermore, it would be highly interesting to see if the k-medoids using the
PAM technique would generate a set of scenarios performing significantly
better than the tested clustering techniques.
Besides these studies a study of how the stochasticity in the demand distribu-
tions would be highly relevant. Such studies could include testing different
uniform distributions as well as distributions having different interval lengths
to test the impact this would have on the performance of the methods for
selecting scenarios. Furthermore, other types of distributions could also be
tested to see if these are more well suited for scenario reduction techniques.
In addition to further studying the elements dealing with stochasticity, the
methods for modeling the optimization problem could also be further investi-
gated and developed.
The focus could be on dealing with the heavily skewed distribution in compu-
tational costs for the BDK method.
This could be done by having a MIP and the BDK method running in parallel.
However, this would naturally affect the performance of both methods as
computational power is divided, given computational power is limited.
A way to reduce the influence of dividing the computational power could be to
study how well the two methods scale using multiple cores. This would help
to decide how the computational power should be divided.
In contrary to dividing the computational power, one could also develop an
algorithm guessing which of the two methods would perform the best finding

5
Conclusion
Three different methods for picking scenarios were tested. The first tested
method was picking scenarios at random from the known demand distribution.
The other two methods utilized scenario reduction techniques in which 10.000
scenarios were generated initially using Monte Carlo sampling, and afterward
reduced using clustering techniques. The two techniques were k-medoids and
fast forward selection.
Both scenario generation techniques were found to produce more consistent
results compared to using scenarios picked using Monte Carlo sampling directly,
especially when the number of scenarios used in the optimization was low.
However, both of the methods using scenario reduction techniques were
found to produce a slightly too low objective when compared to the out-of-
sample objective. All three methods were found to produce more precise and
more consistent results as the number of scenarios increased. Based on the
tested instances with the underlying continuous demand distributions, the
scenario reduction techniques were not found to significantly improve the
performance.
The stochastic programming method was compared to the performance of the
expected value approach and the wait-and-see approach. This was done to
find the value of stochastic programming and the expected value of perfect
information. The tests found a mean gap between the stochastic program and
the expectation of the expected value of 14% for 10 demand points, 19% for
20 demand points, and 29% for 30 demand points across all tested scenarios.
The mean gap between the stochastic program objectives and the wait-and-see
solution was found to be 4.6% for 10 demand points, and 3.9% for both 20 and
30 demand points across all tested scenarios. The number of scenarios used in
the optimization was found not to impact the size of these gaps significantly.
The generated efficient frontiers using stochastic programming for each test
instance were found to be similar for all number of scenarios. However, the out-
52
52
==PAGE:57==
of-sample test showed that the number of scenarios used in the optimization
affected the reliability of each point in the efficient frontier.
Three methods for generating the efficient frontiers were also tested. All of
these methods were used in a dichotomic search procedure in which only the
extreme efficient points were searched for. The three methods consisted of a
MIP model, a Benders decomposition model, and a Benders decomposition
model with k-means clusters in the master problem.
For test instances with a low number of scenarios, the MIP model was found
to perform the best across all tested number of demand points. Test instances
containing a high amount of scenarios were found to be solved most efficiently
using the Benders decomposition model with the k-means clusters method. The
Benders decomposition model without the clusters was found to be the least
efficient method for generating the efficient frontier across all test instances
and consequently failed to generate any efficient frontier with 20 or 30 demand
points.
The limit, at which the number of scenarios included in the test before the
Benders decomposition model with k-means clusters was found more efficient
than the MIP model in finding the efficient frontier, was found to depend on
the number of demand points. For 10 demand points, the Benders decompo-
sition model with k-means clusters was found most efficient from instances
containing 30 scenarios or more. For 20 demand points, this number was 300,
and for 30 demand point, this number was 1000.
By inspection of the computational costs for finding each point in the efficient
frontiers, it was found that the Benders decomposition model with the k-means
clusters method suffers from a skewed distribution of computational costs
for 20 and 30 demand points. All tests for 20 and 30 demand points had a
mean above the median in the distribution of computational costs, and a large
number of outliers. The MIP method was also found to have a mean above
the median in the distribution of computational costs for 20 and 30 demand
points, however with a lower amount of outliers. The Benders decomposition
model without the clusters showed similar tendencies for 10 demand points.
The heavily skewed distributions for the two methods utilizing Benders de-
composition were found to be caused by solutions generated in the λ range
]0;0.1] in which these methods were found ineffective compared to the MIP
53
==PAGE:58==
method. This is also where most of the points are generated as the median
in the λ distribution for generating points is below 0.1 for both 20 and 30
demand points making these peaks in computational costs critical for the
overall performance.
