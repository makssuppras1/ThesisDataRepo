Abstract
This project’s main target is the development of a work-ﬂow of algorithms to properly identify the
modal parameters of any given structure based on sensor data while in this case, a steel frame will be
investigated. It is simulated to be objected to random vector forces, reformulating to the estimation of
modal coordinates from the pseudoinverse of the modeshape matrix that can be directly derived exploiting
operational modal analysis techniques and then leading to the systems frequencies through short time
segments called windows that are based on the fundamental frequency of the structure examined. The
identiﬁcation technique used in this project is the time domain poly reference TDPR and is applied
directly into the data while at some cases the use of correlation function is considered mandatory and its
product to be used ﬁrst for eliminating small outlier information.
An identical ﬁnite element model is constructed for the steel frame, with its corresponding natural
frequencies, mode shape matrices, mass matrix M, and stiﬀness matrix K. This will give data inputs
on the methodology to be tested in the time variant scenario within the state space formulations, while
for the time invariant case two systems will be tested linearly, ﬁrst the experimental data of the steel
structure that is located at the laboratory facilities of DTU and a literature data set of the Heritage
Court Tower located in Vancouver, Canada. Diﬀerent settings of the algorithms will be tested aiming for
a proper sensitivity analysis.
The evaluation method used will be through the relative mean errors, mean relative mean errors, and
relative errors of the mean frequencies of both the scenario’s and the cases of each time variant and time
invariant system meaning diﬀerentiation in ﬁltering, simulation variables and duration. To conclude, a
ﬁnal result juxtaposition will be made with the initial response to be undamped and the quantitative
extend of the statistical errors calculated.

Computational dynamics in civil engineering are expanding with the overgrowth and alignment of pro-
gramming to all the scientiﬁc and engineering ﬁelds. Simulation information, is considered essential for
the future of structures integrity since realistically every, and especially large structures are subjected to
constant random vibrations meaning that their dynamic proﬁle needs to be determined in detail. Human
safety and its interrelated link to the active societal mechanism, make’s the tracking of the modal pa-
rameters of each structure paramount. Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) ﬁeld indicates the changing
structural properties of each system and has expanded not only due to the diﬃculty of some types of
structures to be proﬁled like bridge elements and oil rigs but also, it continuously indicates the minor
damages caused that can be linked to a more major incident or a fatigue point so it can be characterized
as a safety assessment methodology.
Random vibration subjection through traditional dynamic methodologies, are performed in laboratory
environment and in identical scaled constructions that can be translated to a part of a speciﬁc structure
ex. a coupled beam between two shear walls of a high-rise building. Of course the real structure cannot
be held in maintenance easily and thus the Operational Modal Analysis enables the researchers and
engineers to perform analysis based on white noise and ambient vibrations that exist while the structure
operates normally during the testing process.[2].
Therefore, data about the physical properties can be estimated directly from the measured sensor re-
sponses. Into the future, a structure equipped with monitoring devices can theoretically continuously
estimate potential threats-damages to its structural safety using the OMA methods while the building
is still in use. Damages on structures can be attributes to many sources like strong ground motions in
areas with seismic and wind importance or unexpected loading like explosion or nearby demolition. Such
changes can be simulated and will be tested (ex. sudden frequency drop) in this project but only in the
linear regime and not in the non-linear behavioural spectrum.

Part One: Theoretical Background
The application of OMA tools is performed on the investigated systems in order to demonstrate their
dynamic proﬁle. These properties are identiﬁed using the operational data provided by the sensor of each
system that is examined.
1.1
Operational Modal Analysis
In operational modal analysis the excitation forces that are applied on a system are not required since
they are conceptualized as Gaussian White Noise [2]. An imaginary ﬁlter is added then making the inputs
not only Gaussian. Then, the input energy is to be dispersed over a wide spectrum of frequencies aiming
that the modes of interest are suﬃciently excited for the measurement methods to succeed into mapping
their participation. The referred structures are subjected to natural "random" loads like wind and waves
that tend to be Gaussian ideally.
In order to approach an estimate about the modal proﬁle of a structure the idea of OMA utilises as
aforementioned an imaginary ﬁlter and the response of the structure. The natural frequencies fn, mode
shapes bn and the modal damping ζn are the essential parameters to be considered. When interpreting
the outcome of the research it is considered paramount for the physical modes to be separated from
the ones that create noise and thus the bandwidth of the loading information not to be small is really
important. Because if this is the case, then the OMA toolbox can conceal certain modes of interest.
At this point certain assumptions for the studied system must be made. For a structural engineer a
multiple degree of freedom system MDOF has the concept to be considered as viscous in terms of damping,
although realistically, the free decay of a system and its ability to absorb energy is a challenging and
a complex subject.
Furthermore, the formula of motion is given traditionally by Eq.1.1 and can be
considered as linear representation for multiple SDOF’s to be placed in a matrix where it describes a
NDOF system.[3]
M¨y(t) + C ˙y(t) + Ky(t) = x(t)
(1.1)
Where,
• y(t), the response vector
• x(t), the input force vector
• M, the mass matrix
• K, the stiﬀness matrix
• C, the damping matrix
To conclude with this project’s initial thoughts, the damping estimation is neglected in the spatial ap-
proach because it is considered of small magnitude compared to the inertia’s restoration forces. The
physical information must be derived from the response y(t) and hence due to the fact that OMA can be
applied on both FD frequency domain and TD time domain, both will be utilized for the proper result
optimization. The CF correlation functions will be used as free decays to demonstrate the physicality on
the properties of the system and on the other hand the spectral densities serve the same in terms of the
FD frequency domain.

1.4
Identiﬁcation
The identiﬁcation is the core strength of the Operational Modal Analysis due to the fact that this is
where the modal parameters of the examined system are located. Additionally it can be performed to
time and frequency domain, each with its advantageous points and its disadvantageous accordingly. [2]
This dissertation uses, for the TD, the CF as free decays and within the time domain all modes are present
and their in-between contributions are present at all times. The physical information that underlies in the
structure is extracted from the CF by linear regression on a parametric ﬁtting scheme. The identiﬁcation
method that is utilized in this project uses for the formulation of the linear regression problem the
auto-regressive models and their according coeﬃcients which will be analysed more further down in the
report.
As aforementioned in the Frequency Domain, the Spectral Density makes a good visualisation plot for
the frequency content of each studied response. A distinguished characterization can be performed on
selecting the harmonic vs the structural modes. The domination of the higher peak modes can be easily
depicted from the indicative according magnitudes. If the signal is periodic though and the number of
cycles in a segment is not integer spectral, leakage will occur, which means that the respected frequencies
will not cross where they are supposed to.

Filtering Methods
1.7.1
Digital
All the acquired data are fed into a ﬁltering procedure. This achieves to remove any noise tails existent
that can lead to certain bias problems and also to ﬁlter out the data and keep only the meaningful for
the investigated problem.
The main ﬁlters utilized by the OMA signal processing toolbox are the highpass, bandpass and lowpass
ﬁlters. For this dissertation the lowpass ﬁlter is used in most cases due to the fact that the main focal
point of this project is around the lower modes of the structures and their behaviour when subjected to
external excitation. Bandpass ﬁlter is used partially too, for the HCT data-set provided by the thesis
supervisor Sandro Diord Rescinho Amador in order to check the integrity of the algorithm.
Structural systems that are enormous have a well distributed mass and stiﬀness and hence the fundamental
frequencies are characteristically low. With this in mind, the ﬁlters will be applied to limit the frequencies
found in the Nyquist band. The sampling frequency determines the upperlimit as:

In addition, another set of data processing tool-sets from the OMA toolbox are applied on the data of

1.7.2
Modal
To achieve methodology robustness and to lower the computational resources meaning gaining time, the
modal ﬁltering is applied. Introducing this method is similar to the modal decomposition with some
milestone diﬀerences. Modal decomposition reduces the number of modes. Instead of solving the entire
aﬀected system, a certain mode is isolated and focused. When an arbitrary response y(t) that consists
of n channels is taken into account for a system that is investigated the known mode shapes are placed
into the B matrix. The formulation among the aﬀected parameters is:
yn(t) = Bqn(t)
(1.35)
The response yn(t) and mode shapes B are known while the modal coordinates qn(t) need to be estimated
using the pseudo-inverse of the modeshape matrix as:
ˆqn(t) = B+yn(t)
(1.36)

Thus, a matrix with all the modal coordinates will be emerged with each line to be dedicated for each
mode of examination. Since the number of sensors needs to be larger than the number of modes estimated
from the response it is important that the modeshape matrix should contain more rows than columns.
With this in mind, the pseudo-inverse needs to not have been fed with a decomposed mode shape matrix.
To verify this modal coordinates plot should include the single peak which corresponds to the desired
mode. Important information of the inﬂuence among the modes can be lost in the process if those steps
are not followed and create a fuzz when isolating and extracting the modes of interest. For the modeshape
matrix B only the real part is used.

Part Two: Applied Methods
The implemented steps followed will be presented and explained. These steps are performed in MATLAB
environment and each script for each case is segmented into a work-ﬂow of minor scripts that follow a
certain sequence due to the trial and error nature of the research based project.
2.1
Initialization
The methodology development followed aims to focus on a single mode at a time but the ﬁrst step is
the determination of the number of modes of interest. This number is of paramount importance since it
determines and needs to be identical to the modeshape matrix B real part size. Then, the identiﬁcation
to export the modal parameters fn, ζn and bn can be initialized. Having the modal coordinates of the
system for the modes of focus a normalization needs to be performed in respect to their largest magnitude
to have unity as the max value.
Modal and digital ﬁltering are then applied and the mode of interest can be distinguished and examined.
With this in mind the next step to segment in short time frames using the windows procedure can
commence.

rqp that increases with each time the the while loop procedure for the Modal Coordinate and CF criteria

5.1
State Space Simulation
This chapter will enable the system to be simulated in a time-varying domain. This is achieved by utilizing
the state space formulations modeling approach as aforementioned in the Theoretical Background Chapter
of this dissertation. The logic is to chain multiple time histories into one meaning that a time-varying
system that is composed of many time invariant systems is constructed. Thus, for the reader clarity
when the author refers to the response in this chapter it will mean the structured time-series product.
Furthermore the individual time history with its unique natural frequency that consists of the total
response if considered simulated.
In addition the FE model was constructed mainly for this chapter. And this is why it required to be
extremely similar to the experimental data in order to have a corresponding model to the reality meaning
its mode shape matrix bn to be almost identical.
Due to the nature of this approach the results will be presented diﬀerently due to its numerical approach
but in the end the procedure will be test to the undamped case, to sensitivity with the top and lower
boundaries of the σmin to be cross validated in the summary tables at the end of the chapter.
Most of the times certain settings need to be stable but at this case due to the fact that the modes of the
steel structure are closely spaced but need each other’s inﬂuence in order for them to be distinguished
properly and their respect modal parameters to be eﬃciently extracted without outlier information the
ﬁltering process diﬀers and the use of low pass ﬁlter is added.

Discussion
The main purpose of this project was to develop a methodology to identify the modal parameters of a
given structural system via only its response data from sensors. Robustness and versatility were in the
focal point and that is why the algorithm was tested on a wide variety of system types parameters and
threshold limitations.
The eﬃcient application of the method sequence, for a diversity of structural systems enables to the
characterization that the procedure is versatile.
Having as origin the experimental data of the steel
frame when investigating other time invariant structures initial considerations must be made regarding
the proper calibration of the orientation for the initial modal assurance criterion that might be cross
validated along a ﬁnite model technically-based, and software-based to input the new responses from the
existing sensor and ﬁlter the proper mode shapes.
On the other hand for the time variant the interrelation becomes complex in a paramount manner, since
many inputs need to deviate from the initial condition and be revised to include factors that are related
to the simulation process. However a good example of a method that worked perfectly for both the time
variant and the time invariant are the stability criteria extending to the identiﬁcation procedure.
Robustness was achieved from the moment that TDPR identiﬁcation was eﬀective both on the product
of correlation function and directly into the data having an extend to almost every response data-set and
especially when it is tested on a triple while loop and is forced to identify more than the ﬁrst stable
condition.
The sensitivity analysis was performed to a wide variety of extends through out the scenarios and the
cases presented quantifying the impact of the most paramount input information to the ﬁnal result. With
the proper balance of the settings the enabled optimization of the enumerations was clear. It is evident
that the method kept integrity for most of the variation of the parameters.
Since the scope and main objective of this dissertation is to implement the auto-regressive models meaning
the AR coeﬃcients of the TDPR identiﬁcation method, from trial and error and to an extend where the
algorithm is pushed to its limit, larger number of coeﬃcients was utilized with not a real optimization in
the results. It seems that the number 3 of coeﬃcients is suitable for most cases except the HCT data set
where na = 2 was used for the initial investigation and was later changed to 3.
A main outcome was that for higher modes the window sizing was kept decreasing. Hence, speaking of
results RE and RME values can be determined as of a certain stable quality. There are no values that are
presented with enormous outlier variance from the average threshold that the quantitative value yields.
Thus, the consistency is considered coherent.

7
Conclusion
The main question about this project was "if" the auto-regressive models can eﬀectively predict the modal
parameters through the Operational Modal Analysis Toolbox, for a time varying structural system.
Meaning that "if", the TDPR identiﬁcation methodology can be applied directly into the data and
enumerate trustworthy results the method is considered successful.
The key parameter into not having minor errors that lead to a snowball eﬀect when plotting and validating
the results is the proper signal processing method. That was a main challenge of the applied methodology
since the Correlation Function product makes those errors more smooth and the bias is not as signiﬁcant
in the end even though it still exists.
As aforementioned after the modal ﬁltering and the identiﬁcation procedure the stability criteria play a
major role into the results variances. These variances can be mapped using the statistical magnitudes
RE, RME and MRME. With this in mind the level accuracy for this research is satisﬁed and proved even
though some abnormal small scale values are present.
It has been shown not only at the cases that the undamped scenario had lower statistical error but
even when it had larger RME’s and MRME’s than the damped, that the results when applying the
auto-regressive models directly, were optimized with better window lengths and computational eﬃciency.
Furthermore for the state space simulations the undamped cases veriﬁed that the lack of damping make
the scatter much closer to the theoretical ideal frequencies verifying the integrity of the TDPR as an
identiﬁcation tool.
8
Future Potential
Technically based, the futuristic potential of this method is to have an instantaneous structural health
monitoring mechanism that predicts potential material fatigue, real time frequency alternations due to
the operational use of the structure and even when something accidental happens to estimate its impact
on the modal and static’s proﬁle of the structure.
Approaching the problem in an algorithmic manner it would be of paramount interest to have a parametric
window that could self-improve from the relative errors, like a machine learning agent and yield optimized
results based on the input thresholds of the identiﬁcation.
A more traditional approach to test the versatility of the methodology is to apply it on special type of
structures and on simulated loads that are marginal in terms of ordinary like accidental loads that might
occur.
To conclude, from a structural engineering perspective, an improvement could be to have a constantly
changing mode shapes for the most realistic approach that could extend to parametric mass and stiﬀness
matrices in respect to the minor vibrations that the structural skeleton is subjected constantly, aiming
again to approach the real time health monitoring framework.
