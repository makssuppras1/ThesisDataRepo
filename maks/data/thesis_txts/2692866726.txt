Abstract
Throughout this project two methods are developed utilising Operational Modal Analysis (OMA)
based techniques and short time series to track the modal properties of vibrating structures. The
differentiator between the two developed methods and classical OMA applications is the fact
that modal ﬁltering is applied on the response of the structure to obtain the modal coordinates.
In order to apply the modal ﬁltering, the mode shape matrix is required, which is obtained
through an initial identiﬁcation on the entire response. Once the modal coordinates are acquired,
these are subsequently divided into small segments (windows) and the random decrement
(RD) signature is estimated from each segment. The RD is used as the input free decay for
time-domain modal identiﬁcation through the time-domain poly-reference (TDPR) and the
Ibrahim time-domain (ITD) techniques. The distinguishing feature between the two developed
methods (RD and RDHT2 methods) is the fact that RDHT2 method utilises the Hilbert Transform
to remove the inﬂuence of the damping from the modal coordinates.
In the ﬁrst phase of the project the methods are applied on two time-invariant systems, a small
scale steel frame and the Heritage Court Tower (HCT), located in Vancouver, Canada. For the
steel frame the response of the structure to ambient vibrations is simulated, through the use
of an FE model, while for the HCT experimental data are used. To obtain the optimal input
parameters for the methods a sensitivity analysis is performed. In the second phase, the steel
frame is used to simulate a response with varying frequency. This is done by chaining together
several stationary responses, each one with a different frequency, forming ﬁnally the response of
a time-variant system. Finally, the methods are applied on a reinforced concrete frame structure
exhibiting a non-linear response.
In order draw more robust conclusions the accuracy of the modal identiﬁcation from each
separate window needs to be determined. For the time-invariant and the time-variant systems
this is done by simple comparing the identiﬁed value with the value from the initial identiﬁcation.
In the non-linear scenario, an initial identiﬁcation was not implemented and thus, the identiﬁed
frequencies are compared with the frequency evolution of the studied structure due to the
earthquake, which was provided by the supervisors. Throughout this whole process, it can be
concluded that both the RD and the RDHT2 exhibit high adaptability, but in terms of reliability
only the RD method prevails across all studied systems.

Introduction
Dynamic loading often governs the design of civil, mechanical and aerospace engineering
structures but also constitutes a signiﬁcant parameter on the assessment of the reliability of
existing structures. Therefore, identifying the factors linked to the dynamic response of a
structure, i.e., the modal parameters, gives an indication for its condition. The term modal
parameters includes the natural frequencies, damping ratio and mode shapes which all depend on
and affect the stiffness of the structural system. The identiﬁcation of these parameters falls under
the scope of several Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) techniques, utilising nondestructive
assessment methods based on vibration measurements and computer analyses. These techniques
ﬁnd application in structures whose structural integrity is of paramount importance, e.g., high-
rise buildings, offshore platforms, bridges, etc., and the continuous identiﬁcation of the modal
parameters could prove beneﬁcial in terms of maintenance and safety.
The two engineering ﬁelds that focus on the identiﬁcation of the modal properties of systems by
utilizing vibration measurements are called Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) and Operational
Modal Analysis (OMA). The fundamental difference between these two identiﬁcation techniques
is the input force that excites the structure. In the case of EMA, the traditional modal analysis,
the physical measurements are obtained through forced vibration testing methods (e.g. shaker,
impact and pull back tests), mainly carried out under controlled laboratory conditions. The
response of the structure to the applied known excitation forces is used for the modal identiﬁca-
tion. Regarding to OMA, which is the analysis used herein, the loading input which excites the
structure has white noise characteristics, meaning that it has a constant amplitude over a wide
frequency band which includes also the examined natural frequencies of the structure. The main
beneﬁt of this method lies in the fact that the vibration tests can be conducted either in the lab,
or while the structure is in operation, utilizing measurement of ambient vibrations, generated by
wind, occupants, ventilation equipment, etc. and thus, a possible shutdown for testing is avoided
[1].
Under normal loading conditions, civil structures are assumed as linear, time-invariant systems,
meaning that their dynamic properties are time independent, i.e., they do not change over time.
However, there are several reasons that cancel this assumption, leading to a perturbation of
the modal parameters and indicate a possible damage. Examples of such systems can be any
typical time-variant structures, such as systems with rotatory motion, e.g wind turbines, or in
the case of civil structures, a railway bridge during train passage. Considering the main dynamic
property of such systems, i.e., the natural frequency, its behaviour correlated with time has been
1
==PAGE:10==
extensively studied within the academia and it is shown that a variation can be triggered due
to either large or minor earthquakes, weather conditions or even due to modiﬁcations of the
internal spaces in the case of a building system [2]. The magnitude of the variation is off course
higher for a large excitation force such as a strong ground motion, where possible nonlinearities
are introduced to the system. An interesting case study regarding this frequency variation is the
the Millikan Library, a nine-storey reinforced concrete building located in the Caltech campus,
where vibration measurements were collected over a period of around 40 years. A part of the
study conducted by Clinton, 2004 [3], showed that during each seismic event that the structure
experienced in this period, a frequency drop of around 10-20% was observed. However, the
structure managed to recover its stiffness and natural frequency to a level close to that prior to
the earthquake.
A ﬁeld correlated with the modal parameters variation and interests lately the research com-
munity is the real-time modal identiﬁcation. A widely used method for state estimation and
identiﬁcation of civil structures is the Kalman ﬁlter [4]. This method utilises a simpliﬁed non-
parametric model of the structure, whose properties are constantly updated at each time step.
Based on acceleration measurements for the current time step, the method utilises the state of
the structure at the previous time step in order to estimate the modal parameters. An alternative
approach for modal identiﬁcation studied extensively by researchers is the use of wavelet-based
methods which utilise the Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT) on acceleration measurements
[5]. In a not so recent publication, Trifunac et al, 2001 [6] tracked the frequency change of a
seven storey reinforced concrete building in California by utilising the recorded acceleration
measurements. The methods used were the Short Time Fourier Transfer (STFT), also called
Moving Window Fourier analysis [7] and the zero crossing analysis and both methods successfully
captured the variation of the frequency. Vu et al, 2011 [8] investigates the Short-time Autore-
gressive (STAR) Method on a steel plate emerging from water, using ambient vibrations. It was
shown that with this kind of excitation, the STAR method outperforms the previously mentioned
STFT.
The ﬁndings of the current literature review indicate that the implementation of OMA-based
techniques for real-time identiﬁcation is under-explored, since only the research by Vu et al, 2011
[8] is the most relevant one. The former DTU student Kjetil Solheim [9], initiated the research
on this ﬁeld by developing a method using short-time series and reformulated OMA-based
techniques to track the modal properties. A similar method is developed in the present thesis,
with the main difference that Solheim uses the correlation functions (CF) as the input data
used for modal identiﬁcation, while here the CF are replaced by the random decrement (RD)
signature. The RD technique has been widely used in literature for modal identiﬁcation of linear
systems, using ambient vibrations. For instance, Rodriguez and Brincker, 2005 [10] implemented
modal identiﬁcation both in the time and frequency domain utilising the RD signature of a
response obtained through ambient vibration tests on a railway bridge. Lin et al, 2013 [11]
proposed a method to accurately estimate the modal parameters using the RD signature from
non-stationary random vibration data. However, no literature have been found utilising the RD
2
==PAGE:11==
technique to track the modal parameters of time-variant systems and this signiﬁes the novelty of
this thesis.
Apart from the proposed modiﬁcation with regard to the RD signature, an additional avenue is
explored. Based on the ﬁndings documented in [9] and [8], the instantaneous estimation of the
damping proved to be very unreliable when the identiﬁcation is based on short time-series. This
lead to the assumption that if the inﬂuence of the damping is removed from the response, then
the overall accuracy and performance of the method may be improved. Therefore, the Hilbert
transform is utilised to generate the analytical signal and by that remove the damping from the
studied response. Based on articles reviewed by the author, the removal of the damping has not
been investigated and applied for modal identiﬁcation in this context. Therefore, the novelty of
this thesis is extended through the addition of this feature.
Considering the challenges that real-time identiﬁcation imposes in terms of accuracy and the fact
that, according to Brincker et al. 2015 [1, p. 159], problems in OMA applications arise when
short duration time series are used, the main question that this project sets out to answer is the
following.
Can OMA-based time domain identiﬁcation techniques accurately estimate the modal parameters,
using short time-series?
1.1 Objectives
The main objective of this MSc project is the development of a method that utilises OMA-based
identiﬁcation techniques applied on short-time vibration response histories in order to identify
the modal parameters of various structural systems with high accuracy and high reliability. An
additional aim would be to achieve a high level of adaptability on the method, meaning that
it will be applicable across different systems and data sets. The method will be also evaluated
in terms of its sensitivity in order to examine the uncertainty level of the identiﬁcation part of
the method for different input parameters, across different systems. The aim here is to achieve
low sensitivity for various examined parameters. To improve the method’s both reliability and
adaptability, certain stability criteria have been developed and applied. The method will be
applied on a simulated response of a time-invariant system and time-variant system, on real
acceleration measurements from a time-invariant system and ﬁnally, on a non-linear response of
a frame structure subjected to an earthquake.

1.2 Method outline
The initial step of the followed procedure is to identify the modal parameters of the examined
system by utilising a signal with sufﬁcient duration. Through this, the mode shape matrix of the
system is obtained, an essential element for the implementation of the whole method, since it
is used for the modal ﬁltering to decompose the response into modal coordinates. Each modal
coordinate represents a single mode of the structure and by this process, the identiﬁcation is
applied separately on each mode, reducing in that way the computational complexity of the
developed algorithm. A crucial intermediate step is the application of proper digital ﬁltering
to the signal in order to remove the utmost possible noise and ﬁnally, extract all the physical
information about the system.
As mentioned above, the identiﬁcation is applied on the time domain, however, unlike the
classical OMA applications where the correlation function is used for the identiﬁcation, here,
the random decrement signature is used. The term short time-series for the sake of simplicity
henceforth will be referred to as windows. Each window represents a small division of the entire
length of the time series and its length is equal or multiple of one quarter of the structure’s
fundamental frequency.
Regarding the algorithm’s stability criteria, the ﬁrst is applied on the modal coordinates, prior to
the identiﬁcation and it aims to exclude the parts of the modal coordinates with weak excitation,
since these parts are considered as noise contaminated. The second criterion assesses the
identiﬁcation in terms of accuracy by implementing a ﬁtting technique and synthesizing a free
decay with the use of the estimated modal coordinates and comparing it with the estimated
random decrement for each window, which is a free decay as well.
As mentioned earlier, the development of two methods is examined in this thesis. An abbreviation
is used to describe each method and hence, the main method will be referred to as RD method,
while the one where the damping is removed, will be the RDHT2 method. The differentiating
feature between the two methods is the fact that in RDHT2, once the modal coordinates are
obtained and divided into windows, the damping is removed from each separate window. Apart
from that, the overall procedure that the two methods follow is identical.
The methodology will be described in detail in Chapter 3, however, the following ﬂow chart
summarises the method’s main steps.

Theoretical background
The principal theoretical framework that forms the basis of this MSc thesis is OMA, along with
basic signal processing techniques. This chapter presents the basic theory concerning OMA,
basic principles of the utilised ﬁltering methods as well as the theory behind the Hilbert-Huang
Transform used to generate the undamped modal coordinates.
2.1 Basic OMA theory
The fundamental characteristic of OMA is the fact that the forces which excite the examined
structure have white noise characteristics. A loading input such as this can excite a large number
of the structure’s modes, since it’s energy is distributed over a wide frequency range which
includes the examined natural frequencies of the structure. In reality, it is not necessary for
the loading input to have exact white noise characteristics as long as the modes of interest are
adequately excited and their contributions are captured by the measurements. This means that,
as shown in Fig. 2.1, the non-white excitation forces are thought to be created by an imaginary
loading ﬁlter loaded by white noise. This loading ﬁlter does not affect the physical modes of
the structure and hence, the modal identiﬁcation is applied on a system which includes the
structural system and the loading ﬁlter as well.

By applying the OMA techniques on the measured responses and utilising the loading ﬁlter
as explained above, the modal parameters of the system can be determined, i.e., the natural
frequency fn, the damping ratio ζn and the mode shapes bn. A crucial point when assessing the

result of the identiﬁcation is the fact that the modes of the load itself may be included in the
result. Thus, the user needs to be able to distinguish the modes related to the structure, those
resulting from the load and the noise modes which may not have any physical signiﬁcance. Apart
from that, the bandwidth of the load needs to be carefully determined as a small bandwidth may
not excite all the modes of interest of the structure.
A signiﬁcant assumption that is made in most OMA techniques is the fact that the energy of
single degree-of-freedom (SDOF) or multiple degree-of-freedom (MDOF) system is dissipated
through viscous damping. In reality, damping often involves non-linear characteristics which
complicate a lot the computational modelling of the structure. However, keeping this assumption
in mind, the equation of motion of an N-DOF viscously damped system can be expressed as

where, the input load x(t) and the acceleration response y(t) are vectors with N elements; the
mass matrix M, the damping matric C and the stiffness matrix K are NxN matrices. In OMA
applications, the solution to the equation of motion assumes that the damping forces is relatively
small as compared to the inertia and restoring forces. Thus, the effect of damping may be
neglected on the spatial solution.
In OMA, the measured responses can be processed either time domain (TD) or in the frequency
domain (FD). Since the loading input has characteristics close to white noise, the function
of the load is unknown and thus, the frequency response functions (FRF) between the load
and the response is not possible to be calculated. Therefore, regarding the TD, the correlation
functions estimated from the measured responses are used, whereas in the FD, the spectral
density functions are utilised.
2.1.1 Correlation function
The motion of a system can be either deterministic or non-deterministic. In the latter case, the
future behaviour of the system cannot be precisely predicted, which means that its vibration is
random. This is the case for OMA applications, where the loading input, from which the opera-
tional responses result, is random. However, it has a normal probability distribution, meaning
that the Probability Density Function (PDF) p(y) obtains a "bell shaped" form, characteristic for
the Gaussian distributions. In the majority of the random events occurring in nature, the PDF is
not known in advance. However, the only known is the signal y(t) in a speciﬁc time interval,
long enough so that the PDF can be estimated. Knowing the PDF, the mean µy and the variance

The noise tail creates the so-called side lobe noise on the spectral density function and in order

One tapering method usually applied is the exponential window. However, according to [1], the
application of the exponential window will bias the spectral estimate by blunting the spectral
peaks. An alternative method, where the bias is minimised is the ﬂat-triangular window. Finally,
a Hanning window with 50% overlap can be applied to smooth and abrupt the end of the
function.
2.1.2 Random decrement
This MSc thesis will not follow the classical approach where the identiﬁcation is applied on the
correlation functions, but instead, the random decrement (RD) function will be used. The RD
technique was introduced by Henry Cole in the late 1960s [13] as a dynamic response analysis
of space structures under ambient vibrations and later associated with the Ibrahim time domain
identiﬁcation technique, which will be discussed in detail in a subsequent section.
The estimation of the RD function is simply done by averaging time segments of the response
measurements, with a common initial or triggering condition. The RD technique is mainly
associated with time domain modal identiﬁcation methods, such as those applied on the present
thesis. However, from the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) of the RD function the spectral densities
of the system can be estimated, meaning that the method can be also associated with the
frequency domain modal identiﬁcation methods [10].

associated with a speciﬁc triggering point, meaning that the total number of triggering points is
equal to S as well. Therefore, the cross RD signature for the two signals is deﬁned as follows.

2.2 Time-domain identiﬁcation techniques
The part to which the whole process followed in OMA concludes is the estimation of the modal
parameters, which happens through the identiﬁcation. There is a wide variety of identiﬁcation
techniques, but the basis for the identiﬁcation is either the TD or the FD. This thesis will focus
only on TD identiﬁcation techniques and more speciﬁcally, two of the many developed will be
implemented.
Regarding the TD identiﬁcation techniques, a widely researched approach in the context of OMA
is the use of Autoregressive (AR) models applied on the free decay. A similar approach is the use
of Autoregressive-moving-average (ARMA) models, a method which bypasses the use of a free
decay and instead ﬁts a model directly to the measured signal. Furthermore, other techniques,
such as the Stochastic Subspace Identiﬁcation (SSI), has been quite popular over the last years
[1]. The attribute of SSI is the fact that the estimation of the correlation function (free decay)
is integrated into the identiﬁcation algorithm. The techniques selected for this thesis are both
based on AR-models and examine mainly the use of the random decrement function as the free
decay.
2.2

Due to the fact that in TD mainly free decays are used for the identiﬁcation, all the modes are
present in the measured signal, meaning that the identiﬁcation problem has full rank and this
complicates the process. This is not the case in the FD where rank depends on the number of
modes included in a selected frequency band. The main advantage of TD methods is that the bias
on the data can be more easily avoided than in the FD. Nevertheless, as stipulated by Ibrahim, if
there are no closely-spaced modes and no noise, all TD techniques work well.

2.4 Signal processing
2.4.1 Digital ﬁltering
Digital ﬁltering is an essential process applied usually in the early stages of the any signal
processing procedure. In OMA, it is used mainly to remove unwanted features from a signal, i.e.,
noise, or exclude frequencies that are beyond the examined frequency band at each case. The
ﬁlter types most commonly used in OMA are high-pass, low-pass and band-pass ﬁlters. Generally,
the ﬁlters are used to focus identiﬁcation on the frequencies within the Nyquist band, that is
deﬁned as half of the sampling frequency.
The high-pass ﬁlters cut off lower frequencies in the Nyquist band and are applied on applications
that is reasonable to focus on higher frequencies, e.g., audio or image processing. Contrary to
this, the low-pass ﬁlters are used to exclude the higher frequencies and the band-pass ﬁlters,
all the frequencies outside a speciﬁed frequency band are ﬁltered out. This thesis utilises
low-pass and band-pass ﬁlters, since the main interest lies in the lower modes, typical for civil
engineering structures. The following ﬁgure illustrates the frequency response functions of the
above mentioned ﬁltering methods.
Two additional ﬁltering techniques often used in signal processing and are not associated mainly
with excluding frequencies are the detrending and decimation. Detrending removes the trends
from the signal, meaning that it forces the signal to obtain zero mean. This trend may be
caused due to high noise levels in low frequency band which increases the uncertainty of the
Digital Converter (DC) component. Furthermore, temperature variations in the electronics of
the measurement instrument may also create some trend on the signal. A detrending technique
often applied in signal processing is the segmenting of the signal with the use of an overlapping
window and simply removing the mean from each segment. More speciﬁcally, a simple way to
apply this technique is with the use of a Hanning window with 50% overlap.

It is often the case that the selected sampling frequency is higher that what is actually needed
for the analysis. Since the sampling frequency depends on the Nyquist frequency, this results
to a wider frequency band than what is necessary. Therefore, a common practice in OMA
applications is to re-sample the signal using a lower sampling frequency. This procedure needs to
be handled with care since the energy from the previous Nyquist frequency needs to be removed
before reducing the frequency band, otherwise it will pollute the signal. This is implemented by
applying an anti-aliasing ﬁlter before the detrending.
2.4.2 Modal ﬁltering
In order to focus the identiﬁcation on one mode at a time instead of all those included in the
frequency band, modal ﬁltering is applied. In that way the modal coordinates of a random
response, therefore all the individual modes, are obtained and the identiﬁcation is applied on
each modal coordinate separately. The following description, adapted from [1] assumes that
the response can be described by normal modes and thus, only the real part of the mode shape
matrix is considered.

in the frequency domain. Ideally, the only one peak should be plotted, which should correspond
to the modal coordinate in question. One ﬁnal note on this is that the higher the ratio of number
of DOFs to number of modes the more noise is excluded from the modal coordinates. Therefore,
it is suggested that the response should be digitally ﬁltered before applying the modal ﬁltering.

3
Methodology
In this chapter a detailed description of the developed method will be presented. More speciﬁcally,
the basic principles and considerations of the analysis, as well as the the concept of short time-
series, henceforth referred to as windows, will be explained. Then, the applied stability criteria
and a description regarding the identiﬁcation method and the results presentation method, will
be presented. Throughout the development of the method, extensive use of MATLAB [12] have
been made. In favor of simplicity, each examined system has a separate MATLAB script and
within the scripts, several functions are used. The majority of the functions are developed by the
author except of those used for OMA applications, supplied by the supervisor Mr Rune Brincker.
For illustrative purposes, a simulated random response of the Frame structure presented in
section 4.1 is used in this chapter.
3.1 Initial considerations
The initial step of the method once the measurements data are obtained, is the determination
of the modes of interest. Following this step, an initial identiﬁcation is conducted considering
the entire response, which is ﬁrst digitally ﬁltered. Regarding the digital ﬁltering process, the
frequency resolution, determined by the factor Nfft is chosen, as well as the manually selected
frequency cut-off limits. Once the identiﬁcation yields satisfying results in terms of frequencies,
then the mode shape matrix, B only for the modes of interest, is obtained. Following the
procedure described in subsection 2.4.2, the matrix B is used in order to perform the modal
ﬁltering on the response and obtain the modal coordinates. From that point onward, the method
examines one mode at a time. To introduce the concept of short time lags, the response is
divided into smaller segments and the identiﬁcation is applied on each segment separately.
3.2 Windows
As mentioned above, the windows are considered as small segments of the modal coordinates
for each studied mode. Each of these segments is used to identify the modal parameters by using
the identiﬁcation techniques presented in section 2.2. Therefore, a sequence of identiﬁcations
is applied until the entire time series is used. For each set of identiﬁcations, the windows have
equal length and the starting point of each window coincides with the ending point of the
previous one. Thus, the windows are considered as non-overlapping. The length of each window

==PAGE:33==
is determined based on the quarter period of the fundamental frequency of the structure and
a scaling parameter rqp = 1, 2, ... is used to increase the window length. In that way the same
windows lengths are examined for each iteration across all the modes.

3.3 Stability criteria
In order to fulﬁll the objectives of the method in terms of accuracy and adaptability, stability
criteria are necessary to be applied. The ﬁrst criterion is applied directly to the modal coordinates,
whereas the second is applied on the RD function.
3.3.1 Modal coordinates criterion
Before the identiﬁcation process launches, the ﬁrst stability criterion needs to be applied on
the modal coordinates. This criterion determines which windows are eligible to proceed for
identiﬁcation by excluding those corresponding to a weak excitation. This is done because the
parts of the response with weak excitation are considered unreliable for identiﬁcation due to the
presence of noise.

3.3.2 Random decrement function ﬁt criterion
After the weakly excited parts of the modal coordinates are excluded, the eligible windows
continue for the identiﬁcation. However, since the result of the identiﬁcation is an estimation,
the quality of this estimation needs to assessed. This is done by ﬁtting the modal model to the
estimated RD function. Due to limited amount of information included in each window, a small
bias on the identiﬁcation is expected, meaning that a small deviation of the synthesised RD
function Dqs from the estimated one Dq is accepted.
The estimation of the RD function Dq was implemented through the MATLAB function rddc.m
included in the OMA toolbox. This function utilises the deﬁnition of the RD function as expressed
in Eq. 2.9. The applied triggering condition was a level band determined by a = e · Std(q(t)),
where different band of values where applied on e. The main difference of the used method
compared to the general deﬁnition of RD function is the fact that the triggering condition and
the time averaging was applied in the same signal, therefore the auto RD function was estimated,
which is a vector. The rddc.m function estimates two RD signatures, one resulted from the
triggering points captured from the positive slopes of the modal coordinates and one from those
captured from the negative slopes. Following this, the symmetrical part of the RD signature is
calculated through the expression deﬁned in Eq. 2.13 and ﬁnally the second half of this part is

Time invariant systems
This chapter investigates the performance of the developed method on linear time-invariant
(TIV) systems. Initially, the examined structures and the experimental or simulated data will
be introduced followed by a sensitivity analysis in order to assess the inﬂuence of the principle
input parameters of the method. Finally, the results of the developed method on the TIV systems
will be presented.
To assess the developed method, both simulated and real measurements data were used. The
structure that this method was based on while being developed is a small scale steel frame
located at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). In a later stage, the ﬁnalised version
of the method was validated using real measurements from the Heritage Court Tower (HCT),
located in Vancouver, Canada. The response of the frame structure was simulated since no real
measurements were obtained. Regarding the measurements from the HCT, real data were used,
provided by the supervisor Mr Rune Brincker.
Regarding the frame structure, no real measurements were in possession and thus, the response
was simulated as described in the following subsection. The simulated response provided the
ﬂexibility to select the sampling frequency and hence, the sampling time step. This was selected
for the frame structure as fs = 3200Hz, resulting to a sampling time step ∆tFS = 3.125∗10−3sec.
This is not the case for the HCT, as the sampling time step of the measurements was by deﬁnition
∆tHCT = 2.5 ∗10−2sec.

4.1.1 Response simulation procedure
Since no experimental data are in possession during the implementation of this thesis, the
measurement data need to be simulated. For that, the modelling of the structure in ANSYS
software is necessary in order to obtain the mass, M and stiffness, K matrices of the structure.
Utilising the script AnsysExample.m, supplied by the supervisor Mr Sandro Amador, the M and
K matrices are retrieved from ANSYS and subsequently, their eigenvalue problem is solved to
obtain the mode shape matrix B and the frequencies of the structure. Through the Fast Fourier
Transform (FFT), by utilising the function fftsim.m, included in the OMA-toolbox, the response
of the structure subjected to a random Gaussian load is simulated. For the response simulation,
a damping ratio equal to 1% for all the modes is assumed and a selection on the sampling
frequency is made.

compared to the real structure. The validation is implemented in two stages. First, a modal

4.3.1 Modal coordinates criterion - σq,min
This parameter is associated with the ﬁrst applied stability criterion of the method and as
described in subsection 3.3.1 it determines the sufﬁciently excited segments of the response,
based on a speciﬁed minimum threshold σq,min. The need for this criterion was created based on
the fact that the weakly excited parts are considered noise-contaminated. Therefore, this gives
the impression that the higher σq,min threshold, the more accurate the results. The sensitivity
analysis conducted here showed that this is actually true, however, above a certain limit the
eligible windows are reduced signiﬁcantly and in real-time identiﬁcation, this is not a desirable
outcome. Therefore, a minimum and maximum value of this parameter should be deﬁned in
order to obtain optimal results in terms of accuracy and number of eligible windows.
It is suggested that before applying this criterion, the modal coordinates of all the modes of
interest should be examined. This is due to the fact that a mode may not be sufﬁciently excited
through the entire response and in that case, the selected threshold may not be enough to
capture a sufﬁcient number of eligible windows.
Considering one simulated response, as can be seen in Fig. 4.13, two different limits are examined,
σq,min = 0.30 and σq,min = 0.50. It is evident from the plots that in the case of σq,min = 0.50,
large parts of the response are excluded, reducing in that way the number of eligible windows.
More speciﬁcally, in the upper plot 128 windows continue for identiﬁcation, while in the bottom
plot, this number drops to only 46.

Frame structure

Nyquist frequency fv = 1600Hz. This response was subsequently down-sampled (decimated)

factors,Ndec were 0, 2, 4 and 8. Decimation factor equal to 16 excludes modes 5 and 6 from the

but the duration is kept constant. Furthermore, the time difference between two successive
window lengths is not affected by the change in the decimation factor, since it depends only on

in two windows with the same length but different decimation factor. This raises the question,
whether the reduced data points affect the accuracy of the identiﬁcation or the required shortest
window length.

decimation factor used. As it was explained in the previous subsection, the length of the RD

4.3.7 Removal of damping
As mentioned earlier, this thesis examines the removal of the damping from the response as an
approach that may improve the accuracy of the identiﬁcation and also decrease the required
window length for identiﬁcation. This is achieved through the implementation of the Hilbert
transform to the modal coordinates which generates the analytical signal, as explained in
subsection 2.4.3. Since the modal coordinates do not have damping, the RD signature does not
have either, meaning that it has a constant amplitude. This results in a more accurate estimate
of the RD signature since more triggering points are captured. Section 3.4 states the need for
developing two alternative methods (RDHT1 and RDHT2). In RDHT1 the Hilbert transform is
implemented on the entire time series while in RDHT2 it is applied on each window separately.
It assumed that RDHT1 is more accurate since the transform is applied to more data points.
Therefore, this sensitivity analysis is ﬁrst comparing the results between the main method and
RDHT1 and then between RDHT1 and RDHT2.

5
Simulated time-variant system
The developed method is applied also to data from a time-variant (TV) system. However, such
data are not in possession and thus, the frame structure is utilised in order to simulate a response
with time-variant characteristics. More speciﬁcally, the state-space model, as described in 2.3,
is used to simulate multiple responses from the time-invariant system (TIV) and change the
modal properties of the system, i.e. frequencies, between each simulation. By chaining the
separate simulated time series, a response with varying frequency is formed. This is considered
as the response from a time-variant system. This simulation will be described further in a later
subsection.
The simulated response is used now as the input data for the developed method. It is worth
mentioning that white noise is used as the excitation for the response simulation and thus, all
the modes of interest are sufﬁciently excited since the spectrum of the excitation is ﬂat over
a wide range of frequencies. Therefore, this response can be used in the context of OMA. A
sensitivity analysis is implemented, but unlike the TIV system, fewer parameters are examined.
The parameters that were not examined remain the same as those established in the previous
chapter. Therefore, the decimation factor is chosen as Ndec = 4, the length of the RD function
is again varying between the modes, level band a = std(q(w)) · [1.2, 1.8] triggering condition
is considered and the limit for the ﬁrst stability criterion is kept as σq,min = 0.30. Finally, the
accuracy of the results obtained through the use of the RD method and the RDHT2 method is
investigated, in a form of a sensitivity analysis.

script and used later in ss_sim.m for the state space simulation:

5.2.1 Low-pass ﬁltering
As mentioned in subsection 2.4.2 regarding the modal ﬁltering, in order to have an over-
determined system the number of modes included in the mode shape matrix need to be smaller
than the number of sensors. Otherwise, the modal ﬁltering is not robust to noise and estimation
errors [1, p. 272]. Therefore, before applying modal ﬁltering, it is necessary to digitally ﬁlter the
signal in order to exclude the modes that are not examined. The developed method examines
one mode at a time and in the case of the TV system, the lack of robustness to noise was observed
mainly in the ﬁrst mode. Thus, a low-pass ﬁlter was applied in order to exclude the higher
modes. The question though, was how many modes needed to be excluded in order to have the
least noise possible on the modal coordinates. It is interesting to mention that by including all
the 6 modes of interest, the noise on the resulted modal coordinates was very obvious.
Through this sensitivity analysis, the accuracy of the identiﬁcation through the developed method
is examined by changing the number of modes included in the ﬁltering process. The analysis
presented here regards only mode 1 since this was the most sensitive to noise. Three cases are
examined, the ﬁrst includes only mode 1 in the ﬁltering process, the second includes modes 1
and 2 and the third case, modes 1 to 4. Therefore, the bandwidth of the ﬁlter is 0-10Hz, 0-30Hz
and 0-100Hz, respectively. In that way, the effect of the number of modes used in the modal
ﬁltering is investigated. For all three cases, a low-pass ﬁlter is used. The inclusion of modes 1
to 4 is quickly rejected since it resulted in an SSW of length equal to 7.23sec. Therefore, only
the ﬁrst two cases are presented here. The MAPE of the stable window lengths between 0-5sec
is considered and shown below. Furthermore, the effect of the low-pass bandwidth on the ﬁrst

5.2.2 Frequency variation
It was shown in the previous chapter that even for a response of a TIV system the identiﬁed
frequencies present a small variation from the true frequency of the system. In this chapter, the
frequency variation per se is the main focus. Therefore, this raises the question of whether the
identiﬁcation is meaningful in a situation where the variation is very small. The following analysis
examines a simulated TV system by simulating several responses, as described in section 5.1,
with a different frequency variation. This variation is governed by the sinus function and a
variation of 1%, 2%, 10%, 20% is considered. This analysis is performed only for mode 1.
The difﬁculty of this analysis stems from the fact that when simulating responses with a different
variation, the excitation force is different for each simulated response. This means that in the
end several different responses with different frequency variation are compared and not the
same response with different frequency variation. Therefore, the comparison of two different
responses is not meaningful. However, it is meaningful and more statistically sound when a large
sample of responses with different frequency variation is considered. According to the central
limit theorem, if the sample size is large enough the distribution can be considered normal and
based on [19, p. 202], a sample size of 25-30 is enough for the sample to be considered large.
Therefore, for each frequency variation, 30 different responses were simulated. The analysis
focused only at a window length of around 3.30sec. The MAPE was calculated for each of the
30 responses and then, the mean MAPE was derived. In this analysis, the modal coordinates
criterion limit σq,min was included. The results are summarised in the following table.

System exhibiting non-linear response
In this chapter, the ﬁnal examined case is presented, which is a system exhibiting a non-linear
response. Due to time restraints, the conducted analysis is not as detailed as the two previous
cases and thus, only the ﬁrst 2 modes are examined. The method through the acceleration
measurements will attempt to capture the frequency drop caused due to nonlinearities introduced
through an earthquake load. The non-linear responses, the structure’s mode shapes, the period
evolution and the acceleration data were kindly provided by the supervisor Mr Evangelos
Katsanos. The data are part of an MSc thesis investigating the evolution of the modal properties

7
Discussion
The overall outcome of the analysis indicates an accurate modal identiﬁcation for the various
systems, at least for the main developed method (RD method). A stated clearly in the objectives
subsection, the basis for the evaluation of the method can be summarised with three different
terms. These are, the adaptability, the sensitivity and the reliability that the method exhibits and
all are discussed further.
Adaptability signiﬁes the level of applicability of the method across different systems. Initially,
the method was developed for a time-invariant (TIV) system, i.e. the frame structure, utilising a
simulated time series. Later on, experimental data sets from the Heritage Court Tower (HCT)
were used as input. For the transition, generally, between simulated and experimental data, no
modiﬁcations on the method were necessary as the input data in both cases is a time series.
The transition between different structures though required minor adjustments in the input
parameters, such as the length of the RD function, ND, which depends on the values of the
examined frequencies. Subsequently, a simulated time-variant (TV) system was examined.
Again, the method takes as input the ﬁltered signal and until the step where the frequencies are
identiﬁed, no changes are necessary. However, the percentage error (PE) calculation followed a
different procedure in this case, since the theoretical frequency was not a constant value. Similar
type modiﬁcations were applied for the non-linear response. With regards speciﬁcally to the
stability criteria, the exact same functions and thresholds were used for all the different systems.
The above-mentioned remarks apply to both the RD and the RDHT2 methods. All in all, in terms
of adaptability the method performed very well, since the core part of the MATLAB code required
minor adjustments between the different systems and only the presentation of the results and
calculation of the error required the development of separate functions.
Sensitivity deﬁnes the uncertainty level of the identiﬁcation part of the method for different
input parameters across various systems. Since the method depends on several parameters
which affect the accuracy of the identiﬁcation, a sensitivity analysis was performed mainly for
the TIV system and subsequently for the TV system, for which, less parameters were examined.
The result of the identiﬁcation was evaluated in terms of accuracy, required window length
and number of stable windows. Regarding the core of identiﬁcation, i.e. the identiﬁcation
techniques, almost non-existent differences were observed between the use of TDPR and ITD.
A large increase in the value of the ﬁrst stability criterion limit (σq,min) proved to increase the
accuracy, but decrease signiﬁcantly the number of stable windows. As for the second stability
criterion, the higher modes of the frame structure exhibited high sensitivity to it and ﬁnally
80
==PAGE:89==
deﬁned the threshold value. The required window length depended also on the length of the
RD function and this is why it was kept as short as possible. Given the high sampling rate of
the frame structure, the increase in the decimation factor did not affect much the identiﬁcation.
Level band triggering for condition for the estimation of the RD signature lead to more accurate
identiﬁed frequencies, however, when a certain level was exceeded, it resulted to an increase of
the SSW as well. High sensitivity was noted for the TV system when the damping was removed
(RDHT2 method). Regarding the range of the frequency variation of the TV system, the method
exhibited low sensitivity on this parameter, as the error was maintained in a similar level in all
the examined cases. The selection of the ﬁltering method for mode 1 of the TV system proved to
be a challenging task, since the isolation of the mode was required in order to obtain accurate
results. All things considered, although some parameters seem to slightly affect the identiﬁcation,
as a general conclusion, both the RD and the RDHT2 methods exhibited overall medium to high
sensitivity, highlighting in that way the importance of selecting carefully each parameter.
Reliability is perceived as the level of accuracy of the modal identiﬁcation across various systems,
as a function of the applied window length. The accuracy is mainly measured through the PE
and MAPE, and a wide range of window lengths in all the conducted analyses is considered,
except for the non-linear scenario where only the shortest stable window (SSW) is investigated.
Regarding the TIV and TV systems, the results on the SSW showed a decreasing required window
length as the modes increased. This trend was more obvious in the frame structure, whose
modes are well separated, rather than the HCT, which has closely spaced modes. The analysis
on the non-linear response, even though applied on the ﬁrst two modes, showed the opposite
trend. This is because the earthquake excitation force excited mainly the ﬁrst mode, as the
frequency content of the force has a narrow frequency band. The MAPE, studied for various
window lengths showed a downward trend with increasing window length, until it reached a
point at which the error was maintained more or less constant. In both TIV and TV systems, the
MAPE stabilised sooner on the higher modes rather than the lower ones. Regarding the SSW for
these systems, this was kept rather small with an acceptable PE level. It is worth mentioning
that for the TIV system, the RDHT2 method generally lead to even smaller window lengths with
accurate identiﬁcations. The only discordance with that was observed for mode 2 of the HCT,
where the maximum PE was considerately high. The same applies generally for the TV system
with the RDHT2 method, where the accuracy is decreased. As for the non-linear application,
a small window is achieved but with low accuracy on the frequency drop range. In general,
considering the above-mentioned, the RD method exhibits high reliability in all the different
applied systems, while RDHT2 only for the TIV system.
After the method has been evaluated in terms adaptability, sensitivity and reliability, now, it is
deemed appropriate to compare the results with other studies on this topic. The most relevant
study is the MSc thesis by Kjetil Solheim [9]. Solheim developed a very similar method as
the one presented here, however, instead of the random decrement (RD) as the free decay of
the system used for identiﬁcation, he uses the correlation function (CF). Furthermore, similar
stability criteria are used to increase the accuracy and reliability of the method. The studied
81
==PAGE:90==
structures are a steel T-structure, 1.5m high, the Heritage Court Tower and the same frame
structure examined in the non-linear application. Similarly, a time-variant system is simulated.
In general, the method developed in [9], manages to accurately track the frequencies using
short-time series. First the results for the small scale structure studied here, i.e. frame structure,
and the one in [9], i.e. T-structure are compared. Regarding the SSW for the TIV system,
Solheim manages to maintain its duration under 1sec, whereas in the frame structure studied
here the ﬁrst two modes obtain an SSW between 1-2sec. However, the RD technique seems to be
more efﬁcient when applied to the HCT, with improved results, especially for the second mode.
Regarding the TV system, the frequencies of mode 1 are identiﬁed with better accuracy and
slightly shorter SSW. Finally, for the non-linear scenario, both the CF and RD seems to perform
very well for the modal identiﬁcation of the ﬁrst mode.

8
Conclusion
The main focus of this thesis is to examine whether OMA-based identiﬁcation techniques can
accurately estimate the modal parameters of a system, using short-time vibration response
histories.
In order to answer this, two methods have been developed. The ﬁrst method (RD method)
utilises the random decrement (RD) signature estimated from the modal coordinates of the
response, in order to identify the frequencies of the system. The second (RDHT2 method)
again uses the RD signature, but ﬁrst the damping is removed from the modal coordinates
through the use of the Hilbert Transform. Initially, a sensitivity analysis is performed in order
to evaluate the inﬂuence of each input parameter and select the optimal values. The method
is applied on simulated and experimental data from two time-invariant systems to perform
modal identiﬁcation. It is proved that for these systems both methods can accurately identify the
frequencies, with the shortest stable window exhibiting the highest error, as shown in Tab. 4.14
and 4.17. The trend of this error is downward as the length of the applied window increases.
Comparing the RD method and the RDHT2 method, applied for the time-invariant systems, the
latter yields accurate results with an even shorter required window. This is not the case though
for mode 2 of the HCT, where an increase in the PE is observed. In the second phase of the
project, the two methods are implemented on data from a simulated time-variant system aiming
to capture the frequency change. This is succeeded with an acceptable error level. Similar to
the time-invariant systems, the largest error is exhibited for the shortest stable window of each
mode, shown in Tab.5.3. In this system, the implementation of the RDHT2 method resulted to
less accurate results and hence, considered unreliable for such applications. Regarding the third
and ﬁnal phase of the project, the RD method was applied on a system exhibiting a non-linear
response. Comparing to the frequency time-history from OpenSEES, high accuracy is achieved,
except only for the moment around the frequency drop.
Each method is applied independently on each mode and this is possible through the imple-
mentation of modal ﬁltering on the response to obtain the modal coordinates. The quality of
the modal coordinates depends highly on the initial identiﬁcation applied on the entire signal.
This was particularly evident on the time-variant system, since the isolation of mode 1 was
necessary in order to avoid the existence of noise into the signal. To increase the accuracy of
the identiﬁcation, stability criteria are applied. Regarding the shortest stable window across
the systems, this is maintained lower than 2.5sec, except for the Heritage Court Tower (HCT)
and the second mode of the non-linear scenario, which result to a window length between
83
==PAGE:92==
4-8sec. The developed methods manage to maintain high adaptability, since the MATLAB code
requires minor adjustments when applied to different systems. With regard to the sensitivity of
the method, it is shown that a change in some parameters can highly affect the result of the
identiﬁcation. In terms of reliability, this proved to be different for each developed method, with
the RD method maintaining high reliability across all systems, while the RDHT2 method fails to
maintain its reliability, especially for the TV system.
Concluding this thesis, the main question that formed the basis of the present study will be
answered.
Can OMA-based time domain identiﬁcation techniques accurately estimate the modal parameters,
using short time-series?
– Through the development of the RD and the RDHT2 methods applied on a time-invariant,
a time-variant and a system exhibiting non-linear response, it is shown that the RD method
can accurately estimate the frequency across all systems, while the RDHT2 method exhibits
lower accuracy.

9
Further work
Since this thesis along with [9] were the initial step on using OMA-based identiﬁcation techniques
on short time-series to track the modal properties, there is clearly future work to be done. The
present thesis addressed various challenges but also highlighted the limitations posed on this
study. Therefore, focusing on the limitations, this chapter outlines a few possible future studies
regarding this ﬁeld.
The sensitivity analysis conducted here was applied mainly on one response from each system. It
was considered more preferable to investigate multiple parameters and the time allotted did not
allow the consideration of multiple responses for each of these parameters. However, different
responses or different types of frequency variations may have a different impact on the ﬁnal
result. Thus, to obtain a more robust conclusion regarding the sensitivity and reliability of the
method and determine its optimal settings, multiple responses should be simulated and different
frequency variation scenarios may be explored.
Since the method developed in this thesis is still at a proof of concept phase, the method was
mainly applied on responses of a rather simple dynamic system. Therefore, some possible
limitations maybe have not been revealed and it would be beneﬁcial to test the limits of the
method by utilising experimental data from more complex dynamic systems. Instead of simulated
a time-variant system, real data where the response’s characteristics are varying over time may
be used. Reference on such experiments can be found in [8]. A more detailed study on non-linear
responses, from several earthquake excitations with different kinds of frequency content may
result to more solid conclusions for the non-linear regime.
It was shown that the removal of the damping, for the RDHT2 method did not have the desired
results for the time-variant scenario. This was due to the existence of anomalies in the undamped
response that arose from the parts of the response with low excitation. It is seen then that the
modal coordinates criterion did not manage to exclude every low excited part. Therefore, an
improvement of this criterion may improve as well the accuracy and reliability of the RDHT2
method.

time-variant system and the script adapts to each system according to the type of response that
