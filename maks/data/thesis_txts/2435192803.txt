Abstract 
Glucono delta lactone (GDL) or Gluconic acid (GA) has been used to produce cottage cheese and 
feta cheese since it gives excellent control and reproducibility of pH decrease. However, limited 
studies investigated the inhibitory effect of GA on L. monocytogenes in dairy products.  
The aim of this study was to develop and evaluate a GA cardinal parameter term to include in the 
L. monocytogenes model developed by Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2009) in order to predict growth 
in chemically acidified white cheeses. Bioscreen experiments were performed to determine 
minimal inhibitory concentrations (MICs) for undissociated gluconic acid (GAU). GAU was shown 
to have an inhibiting effect on growth of L. monocytogenes in broth. MICs of GAU in broth was 
shown to be temperature dependent; 17.43 mM, 30.67 mM and 34.98 mM at 8, 20 and 37 ˚C, 
respectively. Challenge tests were performed on commercial Feta cheeses under static temperature 
and on acidified white cheese at both static and dynamic temperatures in order to evaluate the 
extended model. There was no clear inactivation nor growth of L. monocytogenes in commercial 
Feta cheese without GDL added. However, growth of L. monocytogenes was observed for the 
acidified feta cheese and white cheeses stored at 25, 20, 15 and 10 ˚C and dynamic temperature. 
Growth of L. monocytogenes in acidified white cheese (n=4) resulted in an unacceptable bias factor 
(Bf) of 0.40 and an accuracy factor (Af) of 2.36, giving a fail dangerous model. The model 
predicted growth of L. monocytogenes to be slower than observed. 
In conclusion, the extended model is incomplete and further experiments and determinations of 
MICs for GAU in cheese are necessary to investigate the reliability of the model.

1 Introduction    
The interest in developing mathematical models to predict growth or no growth of pathogenic 
microorganisms in food has increased in the last years (Velugoti et al., 2011). These models can 
be a useful tool for product development, documentation of food safety, day-to-day decision 
making in Hazard analysis and critical control point (HAACP) implementation and risk assessment 
(Elliott, 1996). Such models should be evaluated whether their predictions provide valid 
description of growth in foods. Existing cardinal parameter models for growth of Listeria 
monocytogenes have been developed over the years (te Giffel & Zwietering, 1999; Augustin & 
Carlier, 2000; Gimenez & Dalgaard, 2004; Augustin et al., 2005; Zuliani et al., 2007; Mejlholm & 
Dalgaard, 2009; Østergaard et al., 2014). L. monocytogenes is a foodborne pathogen and has been 
a major concern in the food industry due to serious outbreaks of listeriosis. Health effects 
associated with listeriosis range from mild flu-like symptoms, such as nausea, vomiting and 
diarrhoea to more serious infections such as meningitis. In particular RTE-foods, such as cheese, 
heat-treated meat products and fishery products are often sources of Listeria infections (EFSA, 
2013a). L. monocytogenes has been shown to have the highest fatality rate (21 %) and highest 
hospitalization rate (90 %) of all foodborne pathogens. L. monocytogenes can occur in both raw 
and processed foods, which are contaminated during or after processing. In 2006-2007 an outbreak 
linked to a commercial acid curd cheese made from pasteurized milk, caused death of 26 out of 
189 patients in. Food business operator must therefore be able to document compliance with this 
microbiological criterion in product characteristics and different reasonably foreseeable storage 
conditions (EC, 2005). Food that are not exceeding the limit of 100 CFU/g during shelf life are 
considered safe for a healthy population in the EU (EC, 2005). L. monocytogenes must be absent 
if the foods are able to support L. monocytogenes growth and the food processor cannot 
demonstrate that this limit is not exceeded during shelf life.  
Feta cheese is a RTE food and it is highly important, that this product will not exceed the limit of 
bacteria to cause illness, as the product will not be further processed or heat-treated by the 
consumer. It is one of the most popular, internationally known, snow-white brine cheeses with a 
pH of about 4.5. The major characteristics of Feta cheese are the pleasant slightly acid taste with 
and a smooth, creamy and firm texture. The contents for moisture and fat-in dry-matter are 49.6 % 
and moisture % of 54.6 and salt (% of the aqueous phase) (Polychroniadou-alichanidou, 2004). 
Storing food at low temperatures is the most common preservation method, whereas other ways 
==PAGE:11==
Page 11 of 65 
 
can be reduced pH and adjustment of salt content. A slow pH reduction is required in many 
production processes in the dairy industry. This is traditionally obtained by adding lactic acid 
bacteria (LAB) or common food acids such as GA are used in the food and dairy industry with the 
E-numbers E 574 and E575, respectively.  Even though hydrochloric acid and lactic acid have 
been used experimentally to acidify milk for cheese production, GDL is more suitable, because it 
hydrolyses to GA at a rate convenient for cheese making resulting in a final pH below 5 in cheese, 
depending on the amount used. GDL is readily available, nontoxic, and free from objectionable 
properties such as insolubility of calcium salts and an unpleasant taste (Ramachandran et al., 2006). 
Studies have shown that growth of L. monocytogenes can be inhibited with short chain-organic 
acids in their undissociated from, however limited information is available on the effects of GA or 
GDL on growth of L. monocytogenes in dairy products (El-Shenawy & Marth, 1990; Møller et al., 
2013) . 
1.1 Aim 
The aim of this study is to determine a GA cardinal parameter term (GAC) that will be included 
in the Mejlholm and Dalgaard, 2009. Bioscreen experiments will be performed to determine a 
MIC- term for GAu. Challenge tests under static and dynamic temperatures will be performed to 
determine L. monocytogenes growth parameters in acidified white cheese and chemical product 
characteristics will be analysed in order to evaluate the new extended model.

2 Theory  
2.1 Listeria monocytogenes  
Listeria is the genus representing more than 20 species, which can be divided into three clades, of 
which only L. monocytogenes and L. ivanovii are pathogenic (Robinson et al., 2000). L. 
monocytogenes infects both man and animals, but L. ivanovii is principally an animal pathogen 
that rarely occurs in man.  L. monocytogenes is a foodborne pathogen that is widely distributed in 
soil, water, effluents and foods. The bacterium has been isolated from foods such as milk (Gaya et 
al., 1996), dairy products (Harvey 1992), chicken (Soultos et al., 2003), meat (Samelis & 
Metaxopoulos 1999) and  RTE foods (Guerra et al., 2001). Since L. monocytogenes is not a thermo 
resistant bacterium it does not survive pasteurization and is presence in pasteurized milk, due to 
contamination after the heat treatment process. In cheese made from raw milk, L. monocytogenes 
can originate from the milk itself. Several studies reported that L. monocytogenes has been isolated 
from cheeses made either from raw milk  (Bemrah et al., 1998; Little et al., 2008; Loncarevic et 
al., 1995) or pasteurized milk (Rudolf & Scherer, 2001). Studies have shown that the behaviour of 
L. monocytogenes in different kind of cheeses is dependent on the L. monocytogenes strain the 
different conditions of cheese during manufacture, ripening, storage composition, pH and ripening 
temperature, etc (Ryser & Marth, 1989). 
 Morphology and growth characteristics 
L. monocytogenes is a gram-positive facultative anaerobic, non-spore-forming rod-shaped 
bacterium 0.5 µm in width and 1-1.5 µm in length (Liu 2006). L. monocytogenes is a psychrotropic 
bacteria, which means it can grow in temperatures ranging from -0.4-45 ºC (Junttila, 1988; Walker, 
1990). L. monocytogenes is halotorelant and can survive at high salt environments (10 to 12 %) as 
shown in Table 1. The pH range is 4.3-9.6 (Farber, J. M., & Peterkin, 2011).

 Listeriosis associated with cheese 
Cheeses, fishery and meat products tended to be the origin of most human infections (EFSA, 
2013a). Even though listeriosis is rare, the disease is often severe with high hospitalisation and 
mortality rates for the so called YOPI group (Young, old, pregnant and immunocompromised) 
(Nørrung et al., 2009). In 2011 EU reported about 1,470 human cases with a mortality rate of 
12.7 %. From 2010-2011 the EU prevalence of L. monocytogenes in chesses at retail was 0.47 % 
determined as 16 positive samples out of 3393 at the end of shelf life. Two samples out of 3393 
exceeded the critical concentration of 100 CFU/g (EFSA, 2013 a). Consuming foods that contain 
low levels (<102 CFU/g) of L. monocytogenes is unlikely to cause clinical disease (Chen, 2003), 
but levels of 102-104 CFU/g have been associated with listeriosis in the immunocompromised 
population (Välimaa et al., 2015). The biggest review of meta-analysis on the prevalence of L. 
monocytogenes in European cheeses was reported by Martinez-Rios and Dalgaard (2018).

2.2 Cheese  
There are several cheese-making protocols that result in the many different cheese varieties. pH 
values, types of cultures and acidification, the salting method, the time and temperature of the 
make procedure and the ripening conditions influence the characteristics of the cheese. Cheese 
making involves the coagulation of liquid milk by added rennet and by acid developed by starter 
lactic and bacteria. The coagulum has a natural tendency to contract and expel whey, so fat 
globules and bacteria will be retained in the shrinking coagulum, the cheese curd. The fresh cheese 
curd contains valuable nutrients and can therefore quickly be spoiled by bacteria, yeast and moulds 
if not preserved. The main factors contributing to preservation are low moisture content, 
acidification by adventitious or added LAB and salting. To prevent spoilage of cheese by moulds 
on the surface the surface can be dried and cleaned by immersion in brine by coating the cheese 
with wax or synthetic film material. Cheese can be categorized into several groups:

homogenization may be applied in order to whiten the cream and increase the water-binding 
capacity of the cheese curd.  
 Spoilage bacteria 
The rate of spoilage of cheese is determined by several factors like water activity, pH, salt 
/moisture ratio, temperature, characteristics of the lactic starter culture, types and viability of 
contaminating microorganisms and characteristics and quantities of residual enzymes. These many 
variables explain why cheeses vary widely in spoilage characteristics. Unripen cheeses or soft 
cheeses spoil most quickly due to their highest pH values, along with the lowest salt to moisture 
ratios. On the other hand aged, ripened cheeses retain their desirable eating qualities for long 
periods because of their low pH, low water activity and low redox potential. Rapid cooling and 
quick use of raw milk are accepted as best practices and can affect the spoilage ability of 
Pseudomonas spp. present in milk. Cheese has been shown to have a pH ranging from 4.9 to 5.5 
in most hard-ripened varieties three or seven days after manufacture, and 4.4 to 4.8 in fresh lactic 
and most soft-ripened varieties. 
If the milk is not stored at low temperatures below 5 ˚C psychotropic organisms like Pseudomonas, 
Enterobacter, etc. can spoil white soft cheeses with a high pH 5.0-6.5 and a moisture content 50-
80%  (Dogan & Boor, 2003). Pseudomonas spp. are aerobic, gram-negative, non-spore forming, 
rod shaped bacteria and represent typically 65-70 % of the psychrotrophs isolated from raw milk 
(Garcia 1989). They can spoil dairy products due to production of heat-stable extracellular 
enzymes when they reach concentrations above 7 log CFU/g in for example raw milk, pasteurized 
==PAGE:17==
Page 17 of 65 
 
milk, fresh fish and beef products. Brocklehurst & Lund (1988) reported that growth of P. 
flourescens, P.putida and Enterobacter agglomerans was a major cause of spoilage of certain 
cottage cheese varieties with a pH in the range 4.9-5.2. 
 Organic acids 
Growth of L. monocytogenes can be inhibited with short-chain organic aids and their undissociated 
form. This is because undissociated acids can pass through the membrane and dissociate in the 
cytoplasm, which results in an increased hydrogen ion concentration in the cell. The hydrogen ions 
are pumped out to restore the intracellular pH, but this disturbance of the proton motive force is 
an energetically unfavourable process for bacteria (Mitchell, 1961). The ratio between the 
dissociated and undissociated form can be calculated from the pKa- value of the acid and the pH 
value of the food. MICs of the undissociated forms of lactic, acetic, citric and propionic acid for 
L. monocytogenes have previously been reported (Ahmad & Marth, 1989); Conner et al., 1990; 
Coroller et al., 2005; Houtsma et al., 1993; Van Der Veen et al., 2008). Fermentation by LAB 
results in the production of different organic acids, such as citrate, lactate and acetate with lactic 
acid is the major end product (Wright & Axelsson, 2012). The inhibitory effects of these weak 
acids are listed in the following order: citrate<lactate<propionate. Citric acid has the lowest 
inhibitory effect since it has the lowest pKa of 3.13 compared to the pKa of 4.87. 
2.3 Gluconic acid

addition of acids such as HCL, or the utilization of (GDL), which is hydrolysed to gluconic acid, 
resulting in a reduction in pH (Lucey et al., 1998) . 
Gluconic acid; (C6H12O7) pentahydroxycaproic is a natural constituent in honey, fruit juices, wine 
and many fermented foods. GA is listed in the European Parliament and Council Directive 
No.95/2/EC as a generally permitted food additive (E575) (Ramachandran et al., 2006). Table 3

2.4 Predictive microbiology  
Predictive microbiology relies on the assumption that the response of microorganisms to 
environmental factors is reproducible against several extrinsic and intrinsic environmental factors 
(Ross, 2000). Growth curves should therefore be determined in relation to different combinations 
of factors affecting growth. Predictive models can be split into three groups; primary, secondary 
and tertiary models (McMeekin et al., 2002). Primary models aim to describe the kinetics of a 
process with as few parameters as possible while being able to accurately define the growth and 
inactivation phases. They are represented as the increase/decrease in population density against 
time.

differential form of Eqn. 1.

3.1 Bioscreen C experiment 
BioScreen C (Labsystem, Helsinki, Finland) is an instrument that measures growth of 
microrgnisms by measuring turbidity of liquid growth medium in all wells of two 100 well-
microplates (honey comb). BiosScreen C has been for several purposes, such as development of 
new antibiotics, protein expression, development of new preservatives and to study the effect of 
pH or chemicals on microorganisms. This method has been used successfully to determine MIC 
values of organic acids for L. monocytogenes (Dalgaard & Koutsoumanis, 2001). The BioScreen 
C can control the temperature, whereas a shaker assures mixture of the wells. The incubation 
temperature can range from 1 to 60 ˚C, whereas the maximum temperature of 60 ˚C is reached 
when the lid is 60 ˚C and the cassette holding the samples has reached 59 ˚C. Bioscreen can reach 
6 ˚C below and 30 ˚C above ambient temperature.

 Preparation of media for inoculation  
The desired amount of D-GAsodium salt (0-37.5 %) (See Appendix I for concentrations of 
undissociated gluconic acid ) was transferred to 100 ml BHI (Brain Heart Infusion) broth 
(CM1135, Oxoid, Basingstoke, UK) with adjusted pH of 5.5. The pH was then adjusted to 5.5 
again if the pH was <5.46 or >5.55 before autoclaving (121 °C, 15 min) and after autoclaving if 
necessary. The four L. monocytogenes strains (Table 5) were sub-cultured individually in BHI 
(normal pH) tubes from frozen cultures kept at -80 ˚C and incubated at 25 ˚C overnight. 
 Preparation of inoculum  
The sub-cultured isolates were pre-cultured by transferring 10, 100 and 1000 µl of each culture 
into 9 ml tubes (with pH 5.5) of BHI-broth. Three different concentrations were chosen to increase 
the probability that at least one tube will be ready for inoculation. The initial absorbance for each 
tube was measured by a spectrophotometer at 540 nm. (Novaspec II, Pharmacia Biotech, Allerød, 
Denmark). Air was used as blank. The strains were pre-cultured at 10 ˚C for the experiment at 8 
˚C and at 15 ˚C for the experiments at 20 and 37 ˚C. When an increase in absorbance was reached 
within the range of 0.05-0.1 the tubes were placed on ice. The relative change in absorbance was 
calculated for each of the four tubes, then it was determined which four tubes were within the 
specified range to be used to prepare the mixture of isolates with volumes of each isolate dependent 
on their relative increase in absorbance. The cell density (CFU/ml) of the inoculum was determined 
by using a phase contrast microscope (Olympus soft imaging solutions GMBH, Münster, 
Germany). Ten randomly fields of view were chosen and the cell density was calculated as an 
average from the assumption that of one cell per field of view would correspond to around 106 
CFU/ml.

 Inoculation 
Isolates for the Bioscreen C experiment were diluted in 10-fold series of following desired

run at the desired temperatures of 8, 20 and 37 ºC.  
 Data analysis of Bioscreen results 
At the end of run the absorbance detection time (DT) was found as an increase in absorbance of 
0.05 from the initial absorbance measurement. The concentration in each well was calculated from 
the dilution factor and the total bacterial aerobic count. DT was plotted against the natural 
logarithm (Ln) cell concentration at the investigated dilution levels (106, 105, 104, 103, 102) giving 
the maximum specific growth rate µmax as the linearly part of the growth curve.

 Organic acids 
The method used for the organic acid was a modification of the method introduced by Jørgensen 
& Dalgaard (2000). The concentration of GA and other organic acids (benzoic, lactic, sorbic, citric 
and acetic acid) was determined by a high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis. 
5 g of homogenised cheese in a centrifuge glass was extracted with 20 ml cooled 0.6 M perchloric 
acid by further homogenising with (Ultra Turrax, IKA, Germany) for one min. The samples were 
neutralised by adding 2.5 M KHCO until reaching a pH value in the range of 6.2-6.8. The samples 
were then placed on ice at 2-3 ˚C for 1 hour followed by a centrifugation step at 4500 rpm for 20 
min. The solution was then transferred to a 50 ml volumetric flask and demineralised water was 
added until a total volume of 50 ml was reached. The samples were then filtrated using coffee filter 
and standard filter and transferred to an Erlenmeyer flask.  30 ml of filtrate was sufficient for 
further analysis.  The samples were frozen in plastic containers overnight (-20 ˚C). The following 
day the extracted samples were thawed at 5 ˚C for 24 h, afterwards the samples were heated at 50 
˚C for 15 min followed by another centrifugation step at 15000 rpm (Pro-Reseach centurion 
Scientific Ltd). Finally the samples were filtrated using a 20 𝜇m sterile filter before they were 
transferred to HPLC vials for analysis. Organic acids were separated using a 300 x 7.8 mm HPLC 
column (Aminex BIORAD HPX-87H, BioRad, USA). The eluent (mobile-phase) consisted of 
0.008 H2SO4 (95-97 % Sulfuric acid, Merck, Germany).

 Fitting of data, Primary model 
Growth data obtained from CT 1 and CT 2 were fitted to the logistic growth model with delay 
(four parameter model) or without delay (three parameter model), modelling log CFU/g data as a 
function of time (Eqn. 3 and Eqn. 4). 
To minimise the Residual Sum of Squares (RSS) of the fitted data, the solver-add in function 
available in MS-Excel was used. The value of goodness-of-fit (RSS) of the three and four 
parameter logistic model was compared with and without lag. The value should be as small as 
possible. A small RSS indicates a tight fit of the model to the data (Ratkowsky, 2004).  
In case of no colonies on the plates, an estimation was given as less than the spreaded dilution, e.g.  
if the dilution 105 was plates and colonies appeared on the plate the estimation is < 105. Such data 
points were excluded from the primary fitting of data. Colonies above 250 were not counted. An 
example of fitting of data is given in Figure 7.

 Performance evaluation of new developed model 
Evaluation of the expanded model predicting the growth of L. monocytogenes in the acidified white 
cheeses was performed by comparing µmax predicted at static temperature by the expanded model 
to fitted observed µmax for L. monocytogenes to the primary model obtained from the challenge 
test. Predicted µmax were calculated based on the product characteristics that were inserted as an 
input. Evaluation was also performed on external data describing the growth of Listeria in acidified 
milk/ cheese. Data were extracted from the graphs in the articles and fitted to the primary logistic 
model with and without delay to obtain growth. Bias and accuracy factors (Eqn. 7 and 8) were 
used in this study to evaluate the model performance.      
 ASZ for growth at static and dynamic temperature 
Acceptable Simulation Zone (ASZ) was used in this study to compare the observed µmax from the 
challenge tests with predicted data at constant and dynamic temperatures. The ASZ was set to be 
± 0.5 CFU/g and the simulations were considered acceptable when at least 70 % of the observed 
log CFU/g values were within/inside the corresponding acceptable zone (Østergaard et al. 2014). 
For the simulation at dynamic temperature product characteristics obtained from the challenge test 
were inserted in a excel sheet in the extended model as in input to get a temperature dependent  
predicted 𝜇max for each change in incubation temperature.  The predicted growth rate for the first 
incubation temperature was inserted in a new sheet, whereas Nmax and the observed initial level 
was also inserted. The temperature dependent growth rates calculated from the first excel sheet 
were imported to the next sheet to the first temperature profile. The initial concentration of L. 
monocytogenes was the initial concentration observed from the challenge test. Nmax was in this 
study set to 8.5 CFU/g. The final predicted 𝜇max at each temperature step was inserted as the initial 
concentration for the next temperature step.

6.1 MIC-term for gluconic acid 
Studies have shown that a low pH and the presence of short-chain organic acids are important 
factors contributing to inhibition of the growth of L. monocytogenes. There is no studies yet on 
MIC values for GAU in cheese, and data on MICs of undissociated acids for L. monocytogenes 
available in the literature are limited, varying from two to 46 data points per acid (Wemmenhove 
et al., 2016). Several studies reported MIC values for undissociated lactic acid, acetic acid, benzoic 
acid, citric acid, propionic acid and sorbic acid for L. monocytogenes (Mejlholm & Dalgaard 
2009). 
The present study showed as expected that µmax–values of L. monocytogenes decreased with 
increasing concentrations of GDL in broth (Figure 8 and Appendix I). This is in agreement with 
the study from (El-Shenawy & Marth, 1990) studying the effect of increasing concentrations of 
GDL and GA in broth and milk at 13 ˚C.  
However, they did not determine MIC values for GA to compare with. El-Shenawy & Marth, 
(1990) showed that addition of 0.25% GA to TB broth decreased the pH from 7.0 to 5.3. 
Furthermore, they observed that by adding 0.75% of GA to TB broth the pH was reduced to 4.13 
and L. monocytogenes growth was inhibited for four weeks. Additionally, they repeated the 
experiment but using milk instead of TB media. For these experiments, they observed that the 
addition of 0.25% GA in milk decreased the pH from 6.4 to 5.6 but the decreased of growth rate 
at 13°C from 0.157 to 0.104 was lower than compared with TB broth.  
Moreover, it was necessary to add 1.5 % GA in milk to completely inhibit growth of the bacterium, 
however, the pH was decreased to 4.3. The L. monocytogenes growth inhibition might be the result 
of low pH, rather than the addition of GA. For instance, Koutsoumanis et al., 2004  observed that 
the minimum pH permitting growth of L. monocytogenes in broth was 4.45. This was considered 
in this study since the pH of the broth was adjusted after adding the acid to see if the GA had an 
inhibition of L. monocytogenes, rather than being just a pH reducing agent. 
 
.

The MIC values in the present study increased with increasing temperatures as seen in Figure 8 
and 9. No studies previously showed the effect of temperature on MIC values for GAU but studies 
of Wemmenhove et al., (2016) showed that the MIC of undissociated lactic for L. monocytogenes 
was lower at 12 ˚C than at 30 ˚C. 
Other studied the effect of other organic acids on other microorganisms. For example  (Martinez-
Rios et al., 2016) showed that increasing concentration of lactic acid concentrations inhibits the 
growth rate of Pseudo monads spp.  The reported MICU Lactic acid was 5.39 ± 0.17 mM. 
The activity by the organic acids has been related to interference with porousness of the microbial 
cell membrane. This causes uncoupling of both substrate transport and oxidative phosphyloration 
from the electron transport system, where the anion of the organic acids is then metabolized by the 
cell wall with the release of H+ and leads to acidification of the cell contents (Mitchell 1961). This 
may be the main cause of inhibition and death of microorganisms by organic acids.  
Citric and lactic acid have higher dissociation constants which leads to weaker bactericidal or 
inhibitory action than did acetic acid. The effect of medium pH on the MIC of monolaurin against 
L. monocytogenes was investigated.  It was shown that when the pH of the medium was reduced 
from 7 to 5, the MIC of monolaurin was reduced from 10 to 3 𝜇g/ml for three of four strains (Oh 
& Marshall 1992).  
Wemmenhove et al., (2016) reported that undissociated lactic acid was evaluated as the most 
important factor for growth inhibition of L. monocytogenes compared to other generic growth 
inhibiting factors, such as T, aw,pH on the growth of L. monocytogenes

concentrations of GAu with increasing protein content. E.g., 14.6 % protein in the cheese resulted 
in 6.26 mM GAu in the water, whereas cheeses containing 20.8% protein resulted in 2.02 mM GAu 
in the water phase.

6.3 Hydrolysis of GDL to GA 
In the present study there was a tendency that the GDL was hydrolyzed less in some of the white 
acidified cheeses (Table 9) even though the same amount of GDL was added and the pH of the 
samples did not vary that much neither did the other product characteristics (Table 9). These 
variations in the final concentration of GAU in the cheeses may be due to variations in the HPLC 
extractions. Another reason could be variations in the homogenization of the GDL mixture in the 
milk. 
The pH of UF- milk is known to be around 6.59. In this study after adding 2 % GDL to the milk, 
the pH was decreased to~ 4.98 after approximately 3 h and a pH of 4.78 was reached after 48 h 
(Appendix 4). This is in agreement with the study from El-Shenawy and Marth (1990) in milk. 
They showed that 0.75 % GA reduced the pH from 6.4 to 4.98. However the hydrolysis of GDL 
was showed to be higher in TB since the buffering action of milk was greater than of TB. The pH 
decreased from 7.0-4.3. They reported that the GDL in milk continued to hydrolyze during the 
first 5 to 6 h and then only a small further decrease in pH was observed by 24 h. Martin et al., 
(2009) reported that adding 1.3% GDL to milk gave a final pH of 4.6 after approximately 3.5 h.  
Another study by (Fetahagic et al., 2002) tested the influence of milk dry matter content, protein 
composition, heat treatments and acidification temperature on milk pH value change during 
acidification with GDL. The acidification was carried out at 25, 35 and 45 ˚C during 240 min with 
different concentrations of GDL; 0.5, 0.75, 1, 1.25, 1.5, 1.75, 2.0 and 3.0 % used for acidification 
for both non-heat-treated and heat-treated milk. The study showed that the pH of milk decreased 
faster when the concentration of GDL was increased. The pH value of non-heat-treated milk 
samples decreased faster during acidification at 25 ˚C after 60 min than pH of heat-treated samples 
regardless of the content and composition of dry matter, which indicates a greater buffer capacity 
of heat-treated samples. The reduction of pH value was greater at higher acidification temperature 
during the first 60 min, since the rate at which GDL hydrolyses increases at higher temperatures. 
The study also showed that the increase of acidification temperature from 25 ˚C to 35 ˚C had a 
greater influence on the rate of pH decrease than increase from 35 ˚C to 45 ˚C did.  
GDL has been used previously as an ingredient in preventing Salmonellosis and staphylococcal 
food poisoning when filled bakery products are subjected to elevated temperatures and manual 
handling. The study showed that GDL lowers the pH and retard the growth of acido-phobic 
==PAGE:53==
Page 53 of 65 
 
bacteria in bakery fillings and icings. GDL has also been used to acidify pork and results showed 
that the most effective treatment at both 4 and 10 °C was the combination of 1.8 % SL with 0.25 % 
Sodium-diactetate (SD) followed by the combination of 1.8% Sodium lactate (SL) with 0.25 % 
SD and 0.125% GDL (Barmpalia et al., 2005). 
6.4 Growth of L. monocytogenes in commercial cheeses 
In the present study L. monocytogenes was inactivated in commercial feta cheeses with lactic acid 
(Figure 10a and 10b). A study by Papageorgious examined the ability of L. monocytogenes to grow 
in Feta cheese-making (with starter cultures; Streptococcus thermophiles (ST), and Lactobacillus 
bulgaricus (LB)) process and to survive during ripening, and storage. The study showed that L. 
monocytogenes survived during the storage of Feta cheese at 4 ºC for more than 90 days, indicating 
that L. monocytogenes is more tolerant than other pathogens of conditions in Feta cheese. 
This is in agreement with the study of (Wemmenhove et al., 2018) showing that the growth of L. 
monocytogenes in Gouda cheese was not supported, when the undissociated lactic acid 
concentrations was above 6.35 mM. The concentration of L. monocytogenes was decreasing in 
WM1, but an increase was observed after ~400 h (Figure 10b), when the pH reached 4.62 
(Appendix 4). This can be attributed to the presence of fungi and yeast on the surface of the cheese. 
The environmental conditions can be changed by digesting lactic acid and increasing the pH, which 
favours survival of L. monocytogenes. This observation is in agreement with Belessi et al.,(2008) 
who showed that the initial pH of Feta cheese was between 4.43 and 4.56 but increased to above 
5.0 within 6, 10 or 14 days of storage at 15, 10 and 5 ˚C. They reported that the increase collated 
with fungal growth on the surface of samples (Belessi et al., 2008). 
Neither inactivation nor growth was observed for WM3 in (Figure 10c). This can be due to the 
Jameson effect, where the population density of L. monocytogenes is restricted by LAB, when 
these are present in high concentrations (Mejlholm and Dalgaard 2007). This phenomenon has 
previously been shown/observed for L. monocytogenes in cold smoked salmon (Gimenez & 
Dalgaard 2004) and in meat product and cottage cheese (Grau and Vanderlinde, 1992;Østergaard 
et al., 2014). The growth and survival of L. monocytogenes in fermented dairy products has shown 
to be affected by several factors such as the type and concentration of starter cultures, product 
characteristics and processing. Beside their function of acidifying the cheese, their other 
components like bacteriocins, peptides, organic acids, fatty acids, volatile compounds and H2O2 
==PAGE:54==
Page 54 of 65 
 
also inhibits the growth of L. monocytogenes (Østergaard et al., 2014). Unexpected growth of LAB 
was observed on NAP plates for cheese WM2 (Figure 10b). This may be the result of 
contamination during the preparation of cheese for inoculation with L. monocytogenes or be false 
positives counts for LAB in the culture media as a consequence of lack of pH adjusted to 6.2.  
6.5 Growth of L. monocytogenes in acidified cheese/food  
In the present study the acidified white cheeses supported growth of L .monocytogenes at 25, 15 
and 10 °C and the growth appeared to stop when the background microflora (BM) reached their 
MPD as seen on Figure 11(a-d). It was observed that L .monocytogenes did not reach its maximum 
density and this might be due to the microflora competing with L. monocytogenes. It was not 
expected to observe growth of L. monocytogenes in the acidified white cheeses, but this could be 
due to the background flora increasing the survival of the underlying population of pathogen 
microorganism as previously observed for Salmonnella typhirium (Aldsworth 1998). 
The growth rate of the bacterium in the acidified white cheeses in this study was as expected 
decreasing with decreasing incubation temperatures (Table 10). This is in agreement with a study 
by Uhlich et al., (2006) showing that the growth rate of L. monocytogenes in Queso Blanco cheese 
was increasing with increasing incubation temperatures. When temperature rises, chemical and 
enzymatic reactions will proceed at a higher rate resulting in a faster growth. In this study the 
target concentration of the inoculum was 102 CFU/ml. Several studies suggested that the inoculum 
size on L. monocytogenes growth in food had no effect. Generally it is expected that the growth 
rate of L. monocytogenes is independent of the inoculum size (Gnanou Besse et al., 2006). Though, 
Gay et al., (1996) and Coleman et al., (2003) reported that the growth rate of both L. 
monocytogenes and E.coli in broth were dependent on the inoculum size. The study by (Gnanou 
Besse et al., 2006) investigated the effect of inoculum size on lag time, 𝜇max and Nmax, Their study 
showed that there was no effect on the inoculum size on the growth rate of L. monocytogenes. On 
the other hand they found a significant effect of the inoculum size on Nmax in both synthetic media 
and cold-smoked salmon. Especially for the cold-smoked salmon with high levels of background 
microflora, where the Jameson effect  is stressing the initial size leading to a lower Nmax of L. 
monocytogenes (Ross 2000), which was observed in the present study too (Figure11a-d). The 
initial background microflora was high in the acidified white cheeses at concentration of 105 
CFU/ml (Figure 11 a-d), which may be a contamination of the cheese, since the cheese was not 
==PAGE:55==
Page 55 of 65 
 
prepared under a laminar flow.  
Møller et al., (2013) was the only study reporting the growth of L. innocua at 9 °C in acidified 
white cheese with different added concentrations of GDL, NaCl and instant skim milk powder. In 
the present study the observed growth rate for the acidified cheese at 10°C was in agreement with 
the growth rate for acidified cheese at 9 °C from Møller et al., (2013) that was 0.017 1/h with aw 
of 0.982, a mean pH of 6.01 and a dry matter of 57.1. The GAu was 1.01 mM compared to 12.99 
mM in the white cheese in this study (pH = 4.78 and aw of 0.973). Furthermore, the growth rates 
of L. innocua in the acidified cheese from Møller et al., (2013)  at 9 °C was ranging between 
0.00010 and 0.017 1/h. Samples with the same amount of NaCl and GDL but with different 
textures suggested that more viscous texture resulted in lower growth potential of L. innocua. µmax 
and log increase of L.innocua was higher in cheeses with added instant skim milk powder 
indicating that the observed effect on growth potential could be linked to protein/water properties 
of the white cheeses. 
The study of (Mejlholm & Dalgaard 2013) compared MIC values of acetic, benzoic, citric and 
sorbic acids from µmax values of Lactobacillus sakei in challenge test with shrimps and in broth. 
MICs determined from challenge test were two to three times higher than MIC from APT). The 
reason for that is still unknown and need further examination. This is interesting to investigate in 
this study, since L. monocytogenes was inhibited in broth but not in the acidified cheeses. 
6.6 Growth of Pseudomonas spp. in acidified cheese  
Pseudomonas spp. did not grow in this study in the acidified white cheese at 10 °C and this might 
be attributed to the mean pH of 4.73 (Figure 11d). There is no available studies on growth of 
Pseudomonas spp. in acidified cheese with GA in the literature yet, but there are studies 
investigating the growth of Pseudomonas spp. in cheeses. The study by Martinez et al., (2016) 
showed that Pseudomonas grew in cottage cheese at 5, 10, 15 and 20 °C. Reducing the storage 
temperature of cottage cheese inoculated with Pseudomonas spp. from 7 to 5 °C increased the 
shelf life of cottage cheese with cultured cream dressing by seven days, and three days for non-
heated milk and by a day for heated milk. 
(Jackson 1978) cited experiments in which a pseudomonad from spoiled cottage cheese grew at 
pH 5.3, but was inhibited at pH 4.8 when incubated at 4 or 10 °C. Another study by    (Brocklehurst 
& Lund 1988)showed that Pseudomonas grew at both 7 and 20 °C at pH 4.8 but not at pH 4.6.

They reported that the presence of organic acids rather than HCL could be expected to cause 
greater inhibition. (Buchanan & Bagi 1999)studied the effect of P. flourescens on the growth of L. 
monocytogenes at different temperatures, NaCl concentrations and pH. L. monocytogenes was 
suppressed by P. flourescens at lower temperatures and salt concentrations. Though, higher

Pseudomonas has been shown to be the dominating spoilage bacteria of aerobic stored meats at 
chilled temperatures  (Dominguez & Schaffner 2007). 
6.7 Performance evaluation of new developed model  
Validation is an essential step in the modelling process. Models cannot be applied if a validation 
process is not previously accomplished, which typically consists of confirming the predictions 
experimentally by using any quantitative method (Dym, 2004). In this study only four growth rates 
were obtained Table (11).  WM2 was excluded from the evaluation since the model predicted no 
growth of L. monocytogenes.That can be because the concentration of the GAU found in the cheese 
was higher than the MIC-value. The Bf and Af factor were calculated to be 0.42 and accuracy 
factor of 2.36 (Table 11). The model predicted growth of L. monocytogenes in acidified cheese to 
be slower than the observed growth of the bacterium. The model was fail dangerous. In this study 
it was observed that, GAU had an effect on the growth rate on L. monocytogenes in broth, but not 
in the acidified cheeses from the challenge tests (Figure8a-c, Figure 10b, Fig11a-d, Figure 15 and 
Table 10). Therefore the MIC-term was excluded from the model and it was seen that GA had a 
little effect on the growth rate. Simulation with predicted growth rates without the effect of GAU 
gave similar ASZ with and without the GAC-term. Evaluation performed on data by El-Shenawy 
& Marth, 1990 on growth of L. monocytogenes in milk resulted in a good performance with Bf and 
Af of 1.37 and 1.39, respectively (Table 11). The data from Møller et al., (2013) did not give 
satisfactory results even when cheeses were categorized after their different structures; viscous, 
firm and hard. This might be attributed to the pH values that were very high in their study above 
5 (See table 11). Møller et al., 2013 measured the pH of the acidified white cheeses after 4, 8, 13 
and 17 weeks and used the average of the four measurements for modelling. It is unknown if they 
used the mean pH as in this study and this could have a big effect on the predicted 𝜇max. Changing 
==PAGE:57==
Page 57 of 65 
 
the pH of the white acidified cheeses to 4.76 in (Møller et al., 2013) gave an acceptable Bf of 1.26.  
Another question could be if the pH of the acidified white cheeses was distributed equally or if the 
pH in the water phase was lower than the pH in the acidified cheeses in this study. But this was 
taken into account since the pH was measured directly in the homogenized cheese samples and in 
5 g sample with 25 ml water. There was a tendency to a little higher pH values of the acidified 
white cheeses when measured in water. When using the pH values in 5 g sample in 25 ml water in 
the model it gave a Bf of 1.51 and Af of 1.51. 
Previous studies showed that data from at least 20 experiments are needed to obtain relatively 
stable Bf and Af  values (Mejlholm et al., 2010). This means that more data are needed to obtain a 
reliable validation of the new developed model in this study. 
The average ASZ of 52.54 % in this study and unacceptable Bf < 0.87 indicates that the new 
extended model with the included GAC-term is not complete yet and can therefore not be used to 
predict growth in acidified cheese with GA.

7 Conclusion  
The aim of this study was to determine a GA cardinal parameter term (GAC) to include in the 
existing L. monocytogenes growth model by Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2009).  
MICGAu values in broth were shown to be temperature dependent: 17.43 mM, 30.7 mM and 34.98 
mM at 8, 20 and 37 °C, respectively. L. monocytogenes grew in commercially acidified cheese in 
challenge test performed at 25 °C and in all acidified white cheeses at 20, 15 and 10 °C and at 
dynamic temperatures. It was observed that L. monocytogenes exhibited increased growth rate with 
increasing temperatures. However, it cannot be concluded whether GA had an effect on growth of 
L. monocytogenes in cheese, since other factors may have been attributed to the survival of the 
bacteria in this study. The concentration of undissociated GAU in the white cheeses was shown to 
be depending on pH and dry matter of the white acidified cheeses, whereas the pH was the major 
factor affecting the concentration of undissociated GA in cheeses.  The most important thing is to 
determine MIC values for GDL and GA in cheese.  
The combination of an unacceptable Bf of 0.40 (< 0.87), an accuracy factor of Af 2.36 and an 
average ASZ of 52.45 % (<70%) indicated that the model is fail dangerous, predicting growth of 
L. monocytogenes to be slower that what was observed.  
Finally, it can be concluded from this study, that the extended model is incomplete and more 
growth data at a larger spn is necessary to optimize the model.

8 Further perspectives  
As previously mentioned further experiments are necessary to assess the extended model with the 
GA cardinal parameter, since evaluation in this study was based only on four growth rates. The 
study of Mejlholm & Dalgaard (2013) showed that MICs determined from challenge test were two 
to three times higher than MICs from broth. This was not known in this study since MIC values 
were only determined for GAU in broth and it could therefore be interesting to see if the MIC 
differs from broth and white cheeses. Increasing concentrations of undissociated GAcould be 
added to the milk, to be sure to have the acid in the undissociated, since it is known to pass through 
the cell membrane. Additionally, it could be interesting to see the effect of adding GDL at different 
temperatures on the hydrolysis to GA and thereby the final concentration of GAU in white cheeses. 
To eliminate contamination during the preparation of cheese, more strict sterile conditions must 
be considered.  An identification of the microbial background flora could be applied to know if 
some of the microorganisms were inhibiting growth of L. monocytogenes. Observing the growth 
of L. monocytogenes in cheeses with different fat content and NaCl or other antimicrobials to test 
if a combination would provide a better effect. The amount and time for adding the rennet could 
be interesting to further examine. Including Di-phosphate (P2) and Tri-phosphate (P3) could also 
be considered. A different inoculation method could be applied or another homogenization of the 
GDL in milk to make sure that it is well distributed in the milk. To prevent growth of fungi in the 
surface, which gives a wrong estimation of the pH, spraying with a layer of Natamax solution, that 
contains natamycin could be an option. Finally, to be able to actually see if there is an inhibiting 
effect of GAin white cheese, the milk could be acidified with HCL and GA to see if GA has an 
antilisterial effect in cheese.  The effect of temperature and pH on the growth rate of L. 
monocytogenes could be determined at different temperatures in broth with different adjusted pH- 
values.
