Summary (English)
Even in the modern world of today, food waste and food safety is still a major
problem. It is estimated that each year roughly 1 in 6 Americans (48 million
people) gets sick, 128,000 are hospitalized, and 3,000 die of foodborne diseases.
A lot of these infections could be prevented if not for the currently limited ﬂow
of product information. In addition to the health risks, food waste is at an
all-time high. It is estimated that one third of the world’s food is wasted, which
amounts to about 1.3 billion tons per year. The estimate covers the entire sup-
ply chain, but in medium- and high-income countries food is to a signiﬁcant
extent wasted at the consumer level.
As a result, this project propose the KitchenHub platform to increase food safety
and limit food waste. It does so by detecting and identifying unique products
and present associated product information to the user in a user friendly manner.
Existing systems, which tracks and traces products throughout the supply-chain,
focuses mainly on business-to-business communication. The KitchenHub plat-
form is intended to extend these systems to also include business-to-consumer
communication.
The platform consist of Android-based KitchenHub, which is located in the home
of the user, a scalable number of data sources (e.g. RFID readers) and three
cloud services. The cloud services consists of a Lookup, a Product Information
and a Recall service.
The user conﬁgures data sources to be linked to various containers (refriger-
ators, freezers, cupboards etc.).
Once the data sources detect products and
deliver unique product identiﬁers to the KitchenHub, it will link the products
to the correct container. The product identiﬁers uses a GS1 standardized for-
mat called SGTIN, which contain a company number, item number and serial
number. Various GS1 standardized formats are used throughout the project.
==PAGE:4==
ii
From the product identiﬁers, the KitchenHub will request product information
from a product information service using the REST web service architecture. To
ﬁnd the correct product information service for a speciﬁc product, the Kitchen-
Hub will use a lookup service, which is implemented using the GS1 standardized
Object Name Service (ONS). Once product information is returned, it will be
presented to the user through a user friendly touch interface. Among the prod-
uct information, an expiration date is included, which is used by the KitchenHub
to warn the user if a product is about to expire or already has. This will help
the consumer use products before expiration and limit accidental food waste.
Once products have been detected and the product information have been re-
ceived, the KitchenHub will open a WebSocket connection to a recall service
and register the products. The correct recall service is once again found using
the ONS service. The WebSocket connection will remain open and is used to
return a recall warning in case a product is recalled.
These methods are used to keep the consumer from ingesting expired or recalled
products.
Using the requirements, which were created at the beginning of the project,
and the use cases, which were created during the design and analysis process,
an integration and acceptance test is executed. The use cases are linked to the
requirements through a use case realization. All use cases are tested to conclude
if the system acts as intended. In addition, a scalability test were done to the
recall service to test how it handles many connections. Up to seventeen thou-
sand WebSocket connections were opened to the recall service and the memory
and CPU usage were noted. All tests were considered to be successful.
There are several parts of the system which needs improvement before an ac-
tual usable platform is available, but with the limited timeframe, the project is
concluded as a success.

Summary (Danish)
Madspild og fødevaresikkerhed er stadig et stort problem i vores moderne ver-
den. Det anslås, at hver sjette amerikaner (cirka 48 millioner mennesker) bliver
ramt af fødevare relatede sygdomme årligt. Af disse bliver 128.000 indlagt, og
3.000 mister livet. Ved optimisering af produktinformationstrømmen fra produ-
cent til forbruger, ville en stor del af disse infektioner kunne undgås. Madspild er
ligeledes på det højeste niveau nogensinde. Det anslås, at en tredjedel af verdens
fødevarer bliver spildt, hvilket svarer til cirke 1,3 milliarder tons årligt. Skønnet
dækker hele forsyningskæden, men i mellem- og højindkomstlande bliver føde-
varer er i betydelig grad spildt på forbrugerniveau.
Derfor foreslår dette projekt KitchenHub platformen, der har til mål at øge føde-
varesikkerheden og begrænse madspild. Platformen gør dette ved at opdage og
identiﬁcere unikke produkter og præsentere relevante produktoplysninger til for-
brugeren med fokus på brugervenlighed. Eksisterende systemer, der sporer pro-
dukter igennem hele forsyningskæden, fokuserer primært på business-to-business
kommunikation. KitchenHub platformens mål er at udvide disse systemer til og-
så at omfatte business-to-consumer kommunikation.
Platformen består af en Android-baserede KitchenHub, der er placeret i for-
brugerens hjem, et skalerbart antal datakilder (f.eks RFID-læsere) og tre cloud-
tjenester. Cloud-tjenesterne består af en "lookup", en "product information"og
en "recall"tjeneste.
Brugeren konﬁgurerer datakilder til at være knyttet til forskellige beholdere
(køleskabe, frysere, køkkenskabe osv.). Når datakilder opdager produkter, vil
de levere et sæt af unikke produktindikatorer til KitchenHubben, der vil knyt-
te produkterne til den korrekte beholder. Produktindikatorerne bruger et GS1
standardiseret format kaldet SGTIN, som indeholder et selskab nummer, pro-
duktnummer og serienummer. Forskellige GS1 standardiserede formater anven-

stemet. WebSocket forbindelsen vil forblive åben og bruges til at returnere en
"recall"meddelelse i tilfælde af et produkt bliver tilbagekaldt.

tilbagekaldte produkter.

der blev skabt i løbet af design og analyse processen, blev en integration og
acceptance test udført.Use cases er knyttet til de relevante krav gennem en "use
case realization". Alle use cases testes for at konkludere, om systemet fungerer
efter hensigten. Derudover blev en skalerbarhedstest udført på "recall"tjenesten
for at teste, hvordan den håndterer mange forbindelser. Op til 17.000 WebSock-

blev noteret. Alle test blev betragtet som en succesfulde.
Der er ﬂere dele af systemet, der skal forbedres, før en egentlig brugbar platform
er tilgængelig, men med den begrænsede tidsramme, bliver projektet betragtet
som en succes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Even in the modern world of today, food waste and food safety is still a major
problem. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) estimates that
each year roughly 1 in 6 Americans (or 48 million people) gets sick, 128,000 are
hospitalized, and 3,000 die of foodborne diseases [1]. Reasons for these infec-
tions are many. A lot of them could be prevented by optimising the ﬂow of
information.
Most people have tried ﬁnding an expired product at the back of the fridge or
a cupboard. This might result in contamination of other products or acciden-
tal consumption of the expired products. I most cases, the expiration date is
marked with a small print on the packaging. It is easy to overlook and once you
notice it, the warning may be too late.
A 2009 estimate suggests that one third of the world’s food is wasted, which
amounts to about 1.3 billion tons per year[2]. The estimate cover the entire
supply chain, but in medium- and high-income countries food is to a signiﬁcant
extent wasted at the consumer level. One of the reasons is that consumers throw
out food due to shopping mistakes (buying products already bought) and will
accidentally allow food exceed their expiration date before consumption. Im-
proving the information channel to the users would help limit the waste.
The current recall procedure is troublesome as well. Once a product is have
been ﬂagged as "in risk", an investigation start. If the investigation results in a
recall, retailers and the media are contacted. The last ﬂow of information from
media/retailer is very ineﬃcient. In many countries, it’s primarily the job of

the media to relay the information to the consumer. The problem is that far
from every consumer constantly read news. In the time from when the recall is
ﬁrst announced to the time it reaches every consumer could potentially be days.
This ﬁnal link of communication has a huge potential for optimisation.
The lowering costs and increasing sophistication of RFID (Radio-Frequency
Identiﬁcation) tags coupled with the EPCglobal C1G2 standard have led to
signiﬁcant and renewed interest in the technology[3, 4]. RFID tags can act as
an alternative/replacement for bar codes and allow manufacturers to store more
information. It will allow for easier readings as well, where line of sight is no
longer required (as opposed to bar codes). Each tag can be ﬁtted with an unique
EPC (Electronic Product Code), which contains both a product number and a
serial number. RFID is day-by-day being used on a larger scale in the industry.
Areas of use includes the supply chain, retail, transportation, healthcare, bag-
gage processing, access control and wireless commerce[5].
GS1[6] and EPCglobal[7] have developed a large standardized framework for
enabling supply management, which includes ﬁltering and aggregation of RFID
data, reader conﬁguration, name service, persistent storage and sharing of tag
events.
This framework is already on full way to be implemented by the industry, but
mainly with focus on business-to-business (B2B). The EPCglobal standards were
mainly developed for this purpose.
By the use of these standards, this project will focus on developing a platform,
which includes new services, as well as extending existing, to include business-
to-consumer (B2C) communication and show consumer oriented practical uses
of the gained information.
The proposed solution results in a KitchenHub platrorm, which is controlled by
a central unit (the KitchenHub). The KitchenHub is to collect product infor-
mation and present it to the consumer in a user friendly manner.

Chapter 2
Related work
Traceability of food product is nothing new. Most projects tend to limit them-
selves to a business-to-business (B2B) scope and not explore the business-to-
consumer (B2C) aspect. This is understandable as it is important that B2B is
properly designed and implemented before expanding to B2C.
One of these projects is the "RFID from Farm to Fork" (F2F)[8]. The project ex-
plored the possibilities of tracing food products from manufacturer to consumer
using GS1/EPCGlobal RFID/bar-code standards. The project allowed for data
generation, which is shared between manufacturers, logistics, retailers and con-
sumers. Consumers would be able to scan a product with their smartphone, or
use a scanner located at the retailers, to gain detailed product information. The
KitchenHub project diﬀers in having its main focus at the consumers point of
view and is to present a platform where user can access information directly in
their home. Data generation and B2B communication will not be the focus of
the KitchenHub project.
In 2010 a group of researchers at the Zaragoza Logistics Center (Zaragoza,
Spain) investigated the possibility of RFID tagging food containers[9].
The
project focused on reliability of RFID tagging reusable food containers at restau-
rants and catering companies. Several uses were discussed, including real time
inventory control within a kitchen. The idea was only theorized, where as the
KitchenHub project is to present a usable platform where the original packaging

In 2012 a researcher at Fudan University (Shanghai, China) proposed a low-cost
RFID enabled smart fridge[10]. In comparison to the KitchenHub project, the
article propose many identical ideas and aspects. The main diﬀerence is that the
KitchenHub project does not intent to limit itself to refrigerators, but instead

GS1 have deﬁned multiple standards for supply chain management. The GS1
Global Traceability Standard[11] was ﬁrst released in September 2007. It deﬁnes
a standard for track and tracing products of all types.
The standard relies
heavily on the EPCIS (EPC Information Services)[12] standards, which have
been widely accepted the industry. At the current time 26 EPCIS systems have
been certiﬁed[13], including the open source Fosstrak Project (ETH Zurich &
Auto-ID Labs)[14] and the BizTalk Server (Microsoft)[15].
Several GS1 standardized Recall systems are already implemented as well. The
American Rapid Recall Exchange[16] and the Australian Recallnet[17] are exam-
ples of these. A common feature of these are that they, once again, only focus
on business-to-business. The KitchenHub project aims to extend the system to
business-to-consumer. Therefore the recall sub-project of the KitchenHub recall
platform is to be designed as an extension, as opposed to a replacement, of ex-
isting recall systems. Existing systems are able to handle the entire complicated
process of tracking aﬀected project using the event data normally stored in a
EPCIS system[12]. At the point when a product range have been identiﬁed and
are ready to announced to consumers, the existing systems would be able to
push the recall to the KitchenHub recall system, which is to handle the B2C
communication.

Chapter 3
Problem Deﬁnition
At the current time the communication between the industry and the consumer
is very old fashioned and lacking. Product information is printed, often in a
small font, on packaging, which is hard to read and easily damaged. Product
are often left to spoil and might be consumed accidentally as the expiration
information is not relayed optimally.
Project recall isn’t handled very well either. The time from when a manufac-
turer recalls a product to when the consumer receives the information could
potentially be days.
Based on these problems, The KitchenHub aims to detect products in a con-
tainer (e.g. cupboards, refrigerators, freezers) using data sources (e.g. RFID-
readers), retrieve detailed information and present it to the user.
It should
inform the user on expiring and recalled products within seconds of them ap-
pearing.
As several relevant GS1/EPCglobal standards have been developed for these
purposes, they should be used where applicable, as well as open source project
based on these standards.

LGTIN
The LGTIN is a format, which extends the GTIN format. In addition to the
properties of GTIN it adds a batch number, which identiﬁes products with
identical properties (e.g. products made from the same batch of ingredients).
A GTIN-14 is always used (padded with zeroes if necessary), though the check
digit is removed as the overlaying protocols of more recent technologies (e.g.
RFID, 2D bar codes) have better error detection. The ﬁnal GTIN have a length
of 13 digits, while the batch number can be as long as 20 digits.

4.3
Lookup system
The Lookup service is the only cloud service initially known by the KitchenHub.
Using a Lookup service, a Lookup client is able to request the addresses of
services based on a product identiﬁer. This system is used by the Recall and
Product information sub-projects.
GS1[6] standards deﬁnes such a service. It is named the Object Name Service
(ONS)[18] and builds upon existing DNS technology.
From the GTIN of a product, a FQDN (Fully Qualiﬁed Domain Name) is gen-
erated using the following procedure:
1. The checksum of the GTIN is stripped.
2. Start the FQDN by the indicator digit.
3. Reverse the remaining GTIN and separate digits by a ’.’ (period). Append
the result to the FQDN.
4. Append ".gtin.gs1.id.".
5. Append a valid ONS Peer Root domain name.

4.4.1
Data sources
A data source can be any kind of product detection unit (e.g. RFID reader or bar
code scanner). The Data sources are responsible for detecting products and
presenting them to the KitchenHub via the Product handler. The conﬁgura-
tion is largely hardware dependent. The Low-Level Reader Protocol (LLRP)[19]
was developed by EPCGlobal and have been adopted by many large manufac-
turers, but due to its complexity it is often implemented as a secondary protocol.
Although several interfaces are available from varies data sources, the project
will initially only support TCP/IP connections.
To save energy and optimally use data sources, they should be started by a
trigger, do a complete scan and deliver their results to the Product handler. As
a change in the content of containers (refrigerator, freezers, cupboards etc.) are
relatively rare, this will save a lot of energy as opposed to continuous detection.
Closing a container (e.g. refrigerator door), should trigger a scan. This feature
is already implemented in many electric containers in the way that refrigerator
doors turn on a light once opened and turns it oﬀupon closing it. To start, the
data source should simply listen to the falling edge of the light signal.
4.4.1.1
Format
This project will focus on RFID-readers, which will deliver an Electronic Product
Code (EPC) unique for each RFID-tag. For trade items, the standard format of
the EPC is SGTIN-96, which is based on the SGTIN format (see section 4.2.3)
and contains three important elements: company preﬁx, item reference number
and serial number.
An LGTIN EPC will not be supported as products of the same batch would
be identiﬁed by the same EPC. This would be result in a Data source only
detecting one product even though several products of the same batch is placed
in the container (e.g. only one carton of milk is detected, even though you put
in three cartons, which belongs to the same batch).
The task of decoding the EPC is the responsibility of the Product handler and
will be explained in the next section.

4.5.1
Product information service
GS1 have developed a EPC based system called the Electronic Product Code
Information Services (EPCIS)[12]. EPCIS is able to provide event and master
data.
Event data is generated by recording events that relates to an EPC as it passes
through the supply chain. This information is very valuable for businesses (e.g.
manufacturers, logistics and retailers), but not for the consumer.
Master data is the general information related to an EPC. This could be product
information and is exactly what this project need. This isn’t without problems
though. The EPCIS was designed for business-to-business (B2B) and does not
take into account the necessity of a business-to-consumer (B2C) system. Busi-
nesses will often only request for product information once, while consumers will
do it a lot more often. EPCIS does not allow for proper versioning and ﬁltering
of information, which results in a lot of redundant work. Due to the problems, it
was decided that another Product information system is to be designed. In
the future the advantages of this system might be incorporated into the EPCIS
design. The new Project information service is to oﬀer retrieval of project
information based on either an URN (SGTIN or LGTIN, see section 4.2).

4.6
Recall
The recall sub-project was brieﬂy explained in section 4.1.4.
The sub-project contains ﬁve sub-systems, which are the Shared storage,
Lookup client, Lookup service, Recall service and Recall client sub-
systems. The Lookup service and client was explained in section 4.3. Recall
service and Recall client will be designed in the following sections as well as
the data requirements for the Shared storage, which will be designed section
4.8.
The Recall client is responsible for registering all detected items to a Recall
service, which is found using the Lookup system (see section 4.3). In case of a
recall the Recall service will notify the Recall client, which will save the
details to the Shared storage and notify the rest of the system.
As mentioned earlier, products can be traced on three levels:
Generic, Speciﬁc and Unique (see section 4.2).
Generic is used when a manufacturer wish to recall all products of a certain
type. This might occur if an used ingredient is suddenly found to be dangerous.
Speciﬁc is used when recalling a set of products, which have similar properties.
E.g. metal splinters from a manufacturing machine was found in a batch, which
results in the batch being recalled.
==PAGE:45==
4.6 Recall
31
Unique is interesting as well as it would allow the manufacturer to recall every
unique product.
The problem is that the amount of work and information
required to handle registrations of all products is infeasible. In the large majority
of recall instances, the two previous levels of traceability will suﬃce.
When tracing an item at the Speciﬁc level, a LGTIN is used. As explained
in section 4.2, the LGTIN format inherits the properties of the Generic level.
Therefore products should be registered by an LGTIN.
Potentially the recall system could be implemented as an attribute with a speciﬁc
key value in the Product information sub-project, which the User interface
is to look for and warn the user. The system wasn’t designed for this though,
as item information is considered to be relatively static. As a result the time
between inserting the attribute to the Product information service to the
Product information client receiving it might be hours or days, resulting in
the risk of preventable dangerous consumption. Therefore the recall system is
designed as its own system.
The system contains two main tasks: registration and notiﬁcation.
The following sections will explain how the service and client will handle these
tasks.
4.6.1
Recall service
Registration is done when the client delivers a list of LGTINs to the service.
The service will register the speciﬁc client to these LGTINs.
When a product is recalled, the service will send a recall notiﬁcation to the
client detailing the recall.
When sending a recall notiﬁcation, from the service to the client, the methods
push and pull can be used.
A push is a style of communication where the
request is initiated by the publisher (service). This is in contrast to pull, where
the client initiate the request and "pull" the needed information. As only the
service will know when a recall notiﬁcation is available, push should be used to
transmit it to the client in the shortest time possible.
==PAGE:46==

1. The client will act as a server and accept connections, where the service
can push the information.
2. The client open up a connection to the service, which the service can use
to push the information.
Both methods have pros and cons.

5.1
Platform/framework
As explained in section 4.1, software will be implemented on two types of sys-
tems. The KitchenHub and several Cloud services. The platform/framework
for these two will be decided in this section.
5.1.1
KitchenHub
The aim of the KitchenHub is to make it as cheap and simple as possible. Today
the ARM architecture is the most widespread low-power/low-cost platform for
application processors. The vast majority of ARM systems are UNIX based,
notably Apple’s iOS and several Linux distributions (including Android).
As iOS is closed source and with only limited, hight-cost hardware conﬁgura-
tions, it was discarded.
As a result the choice was between the many diﬀerent Linux distributions.
==PAGE:56==
42
Implementation
Of these Android was chosen for the following reasons.
• Open source.
• Stable.
• Well-known by users.
• Touch oriented UI framework.
• Very large amount of hardware conﬁgurations available (from low to hight
cost).
• Vast experience from developer in both Android system and app develop-
ment.
5.1.2
Cloud services
All cloud services will be implemented on Linux based systems.
Three diﬀerent cloud service are to be implemented.
• Lookup service (ONS)
• Product information service
• Recall service
As decided in section 4.3, the Lookup system is to based on ONS[18], which is
based on DNS. For this reason BIND[20] was chosen as the platform, as it is
one of the most used and stable DNS distributions.
The Product information service and Recall service are both developed
from scratch. Due to the skills and proﬁciency of the developer a Java frame-
work was to be chosen. Apache Tomcat[21] was chosen, due to it being well-
documented, popular and open source.
5.1.3
Data Source
As a data source, an Alien ALR-9800 RFID reader is used. This model doesn’t
support the Low-Level Reader Protocol (LLRP), but instead the proprietary
Alien Reader Protocol (ARP). Due to time constraints, previous experience
with ARP and the complexity of LLRP, it was decided to only support ARP at
the current time. Support for LLRP is high on the list of future development.

5.6.3
Lookup client
Lookup Client is implemented as a library used by both the Product
information client and Recall client. It is implemented using the
dnsjava[26] library to connect to the Lookup service.
From the requesting party, it is handed a GTIN and a service identiﬁer. The
GTIN will be converted to a FQDN (Fully Qualiﬁed Domain Name). The FQDN
is used to look up all relevant NAPTR records from the Lookup service. The
existing caching mechanisms of the dnsjava library is used.
Once a list of
records is returned, the client will remove those who does not match the service
identiﬁer. The resulting list will be sorted by priority. The ﬁnal list will returned
to the requesting party.
To summarize, the following process is executed:
1. Receive GTIN and service Identiﬁer.
2. Convert GTIN to FQDN.
3. Request results from Lookup service using FQDN.
4. Remove results, which does not match service identiﬁer.
5. Sort remaining results
6. Return ﬁnal list of results.

Product information client
The Product information client module is implemented as an Android Ser-

another broadcast is received, while the thread is still running, another thread
will be queued. There can be no more than one thread queued at a certain time.
The process of the thread was described in section 4.5.2. It starts by retrieving a
list of products, which have been ﬂagged present, from the Shared storage. For
each product, the client will request a list of Product information services,

it will have a list of Product information service URLs. It will generate the
URN of the product and retrieve version numbers of previously held information
related to all four information levels (see section 4.5.1). Using these as parame-
ters, the client will send a request to the Product information service. The
URL of an request will look similar to the following:
http://localhost:8080/product-information-service/rest/query?
urn=urn%3Aepc%3Aclass%3Asgtin%3A9577813.41700.2945072&cVersion=1

used to decode it. The information will be handled according to the attached
message value (see section 4.5.2). If the message value indicates the data is OK,
old information will be deleted and the new information will be saved to storage
by the use of the Shared storage module.
Once all products have been cycled through, the client will send out the following
broadcast:
kr.kaist.resl.kitchenhub.PRODUCT_INFORMATION_UPDATED

5.6.5
Recall client
The Recall client module is implemented as an Android Service and will
continuously run in the background.
The design of the recall client was described in section 4.6.2. It starts by re-
trieving a list of products, which have been ﬂagged present, from the Shared
storage. For each product, the client will request a list of Recall services,
using the Lookup client (see section 5.6.3).
It will then bundle up products of which have an identical Recall services.
For each Recall service, the client will generate a list of URNs from the
associated products. A SGTIN without a serial number and, if a batch number
is available, a LGTIN (see section 4.2 for format descriptions). It will try to
retrieve previous version numbers of associated recall notiﬁcations for each URN.
Last will then open a WebSocket to the Recall service and send the list of
urn/version number to register the products.
It will keep the WebSocket connection open indeﬁnitely. In case the connection
is lost, or the kr.kaist.resl.kitchenhub.PRODUCT_INFORMATION_UPDATED
broadcast is received, the client will close the connection, open new connections
and register all products again.
In case a recall is issued, the client will receive an JSON encoded recall noti-
ﬁcation through the WebSocket connection (see section 5.5.1 for format). The
recall notiﬁcation will be saved to storage by the use of the Shared storage
module. Once the recall notiﬁcation have been stored, the client will send out
the following broadcast:
kr.kaist.resl.kitchenhub.RECALL_UPDATED
Attached to the broadcast is the primary key of the newly inserted recall no-
tiﬁcation. The broadcast is caught by the User interface, which will all act
accordingly.

Chapter 7
Future work
At his point the KitchenHub project is only in a very early state. Many im-
provement are required before a usable platform is available. A few of these are
listed below.
Support for more data sources
At the current state, the KitchenHub only supports RFID readers using the
proprietary Alien Reader Protocol (ARP). A high priority improvement is to
add support for the standardized Low Level Reader Protocol (LLRP)[19].
Security
At the current state, the system have very limited security features. In the future
cloud service authentication should be implemented. In addition, manufacturer
authentication for issuing recall notiﬁcations should be implemented in the recall
service. A general security analysis should be done to the entire system.
==PAGE:92==
78
Future work
Integration to existing systems
It should be investigated whether it would be possible to integrate the product
information service into existing services such as the EPC Information Service
(EPCIS). The recall system should be integrated into or at least agreed on a
standardized interface with existing GS1 standardized recall systems such as
Recallnet[17] or Rapid Recall Express[16].
Better use of product information
Product information should be expanded to include other properties such as:
• Images - Support for an image property.
• Temperature - Indicate optimal project storage temperature. Display
warnings in case a product is placed in a container, which doesn’t match
the optimal temperature, which might aﬀect product quality/lifetime (e.g.
bread placed refrigerator/freezer, when it should be stored at living room
temperature).
Additional container sensors
Install sensors in container, which would report back to the KitchenHub. Such
as temperature sensors, which warns the user if the power goes out or a refrig-
erator/freezer is suspected to be defective.
Mobile access
Allow the user to access and edit the KitchenHub form their mobile device (e.g.
Smartphoe, Tablet).
Automatic conﬁguration of Data sources
At the current time Data sources have to be conﬁgured manually through a
terminal or similar. When the user conﬁgures the KitchenHub to accept a data
source, it should connect to the data source and apply the correct conﬁguration.
==PAGE:93==
79
Other uses
The KitchenHub platform might be able to be expanded outside the kitchen.
For example installed in the garage/workshop, where the user could register all
tools and have easy access to speciﬁcations/ manuals/inventory. Or just as a
general inventory management system, where new products are scanned when
bought. The user could register his/her TV or computer and again have easy
access to speciﬁcations or manuals.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
A KitchenHub platform has been developed during this project. The result is an
Android-based KitchenHub, which is located in the home of the user, a scalable
number of data sources (e.g. RFID readers) and three cloud services. The cloud
services consists of a lookup, a product information and a recall service.
The user can conﬁgure the data sources to be linked to various containers (re-
frigerators, freezers, cupboards etc.). Once the data sources detect products and
deliver unique product identiﬁers to the KitchenHub, it will link the products
to the correct container.
The KitchenHub will then retrieve detailed product information from the Prod-
uct information services and register the Products to a Recall service.
The
Recall service is then able to send Recall information to the registered clients.
GS1/EPCGlobal formats has been used throughout the system.
Using the requirements, which were created at the beginning of the project, and
the use cases, which were created during the design and analysis process, an
integration and acceptance test was executed. The use cases are linked to the
requirements through a use case realization. All use cases are tested to conclude
if the system acts as intended.
==PAGE:96==
82
Conclusion
In addition, a scalability test were done to the recall cloud service to test how it
handles many connections. Up to seventeen thousand WebSocket connections
were opened to the recall service and the memory and CPU usage were noted.
All test were considered to be successful.
There are several parts of the system which needs improvement before an ac-
tual usable platform is available, but with the limited timeframe, the project is
concluded as a success.

I underestimated my initial project proposal, which turned out to be too big for
the limited timeframe. At the beginning of the project I expected to design and
implement most of the extra features explained in "Future work" (chapter 7).
As I came to realize the problem I limited the project in areas which weren’t
vital.
Communication between myself and my advisors hasn’t been without troubles
either.
Due to the small size of the KAIST-DTU dual degree program, not
much experience is yet attained by either universities. As the project was done
at KAIST, my advisor Professor Daeyoung Kim was to handle most of the
project guidance, while my DTU advisor, Professor Jan Madsen, was supposed
to have a more passive role. The communication between myself and Professor
Daeyoung Kim has been lacking. Neither of us have the full blame. I could have
been better at providing statuses and keeping the communication alive, while
Professor Daeyoung Kim was very busy with other projects and not always
great at replying to emails or arranging meetings. In the relationship between
myself and Professor Jan Madsen, I could have been better at providing project
statuses.
The cooperation between advisors have been almost non-existent. I should have
pushed more to establish a better cooperation.
I also believe I should work on my skills of project planning and time estimates.
Several parts of the project have been much more time consuming than originally
planned. The result was all my buﬀer time was exhausted and at the end of the
project I had to put in many more hours than originally planned.
Even though all these problems have arisen, I believe that I have handled the
project quite well.
I have been quite ﬂexible and successfully adjusted the
project when needed.
Given all these circumstances I am happy with my performance. The project
has shown me several of my skills that I need to work on improving.
