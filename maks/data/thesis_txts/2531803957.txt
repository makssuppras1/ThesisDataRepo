Abstract
The Plastics Economy is here, and it is here to stay. Thus the need to ﬁnd optimized routes for recycling
this recalcitrant material is needed. Global production of 359 million metric ton of plastics are produced
each year. Due to the low production cost, improved purity, and quality of virgin plastics, synthesis of new
polymers are the preferred way. As a result, an average global recycling rate less than 10% has led to the
accumulation of plastics in the natural environment with a minimum of eight million metric tonnes ending
up in the ocean, giving rise to terms such as "The Plastic Ocean". A conservative business-as-usual scenario
predicts a 1:1 ratio of plastics to ﬁsh we don’t act now.
Recent discoveries by industrial and academic forces have established proof of concepts for the enzymatic
hydrolysis of PET and subsequent bioconversion by engineered cell factories such as P. putida for the pro-
duction of value-added chemicals. In this study, we outline and examine the rational design of a PET-
degrading Pseudomonas putida. We focus on establishing a terephthalate utilization phenotype as this is
the limiting step from a complete valorization of PET. Through the joint efforts of industry, metabolic- and
protein engineers, we envision a concept for a plastic-based bioprocess industry mediated by the strong
metabolism of P. putida. This work provides the tools through FACS coupled evolution to develop an efﬁ-
cient Ideonella sakaienis PETase secretion and PET utilization phenotype in the well known GRAS status P.
putida strain. Throughout this thesis, we aim to close the loop in the life cycle of polyethylene terephthalate
and pave the way for a NEW Plastics Economy.

1 . Introduction
Plastics serve as a diverse group of synthetic polymers derived from the petrochemical industry, with many
beneﬁts such as their low weight, high durability, and low production costs (Ellen-MacArthur-Foundation
2016). Plastics show a broad range of properties depending on the polymer type. As a result, several in-
dustries use plastics in one way or another, with the "Building & Construction" and "Packaging" sectors
representing 20% and 40% of the total European plastic demand (PlasticsEurope 2019). An industry an-
nually, contributing 30 billion to public ﬁnances and welfare in Europe, thus commonly referred to as the
"Plastics Economy" (PlasticsEurope 2019). Polyethylene terephthalate is the fourth most used type in plastic
packaging and commonly used for plastic bottle production. Although methods for successfully closed loop
recycling of PET bottles have been established, it is far from optimal as globally, only seven percent of PET
bottles are recycled bottle-to-bottle. In fact, most of the plastic packaging material is lost after single usage
(Ellen-MacArthur-Foundation 2016). Furthermore, the established recycling methods often lead to inferior
properties of the recycled plastic compared to virgin plastic resin (Hopewell et al. 2009; Faraca et al. 2019).
Estimation of the production and fate of all plastics made i.e. from their introduction in 1950 to 2015,
predicts a global accumulative production of 2500 Mt (2.5 ∗109kg), in which 10% have been recycled,
30% currently in use and 60% landﬁlled, incinerated or in other ways lost to the Plastic Economy (Geyer
et al. 2017). Terms such as "The Plastic Ocean" (Cressey 2016), "The Great Paciﬁc Garbage Patch" (Howell
et al. 2012) and "Plastisphere" (Zettler et al. 2013) all reﬂects the fact that eight Mt of plastic annually end
up in the marine environment (Ellen-MacArthur-Foundation 2016).
Recent studies suggest that Pseudomonas spp. have natural capabilities for degradation and utilization of
synthetic polymers as microbial consortia often contain species from this genus (Wilkes et al. 2017; Vague et
al. 2019; Feng et al. 2020). In fact, Franden et al. (2018) proved that P. putida KT2440 can be engineered for
efﬁcient ethylene glycol metabolism, one of the monomers in PET. Genes for terephthalate utilization have
been characterized as well (Sasoh et al. 2006), hence enabling complete valorization of PET monomers to
high-value compounds as suggested by (Wierckx et al. 2015; Salvador et al. 2019). This strategy, however,
is limited by the demanding, inefﬁcient and costly state-of-the-art recycling of PET through mechanical and
chemical methods(Sinha et al. 2010; Faraca et al. 2019). The discovery of a PET hydrolase (PETase) from
the bacterium Ideonella sakaiensis paves the way for PET hydrolysis at moderate temperatures, increasing
the economic feasibility of the recycling process and potentially leads to improved competitiveness to virgin
PET (Yoshida et al. 2016; Faraca et al. 2019). Heterologous production of PETase, independent of host, has
shown far from industrially relevant levels.
In this thesis, we investigated the use of P. putida KT2440 and derivatives thereof for heterologous produc-
tion of two PETase mutants, I179F and DuraPETase, based on a selection of ﬁve different promoters (Ma
et al. 2018; Cui et al. 2019). Moreover, we attempted to establish a terephthalate utilization phenotype for
the potential of a circular PET economy as advocated by Ellen-MacArthur-Foundation (2016).

2 . Background
Rational Design of a PET-degrading Pseudomonas putida strain requires the crucial understanding of each
element in the reported PET utilization phenotype. In the section below, we initially advocate for the use of
P. putida as host for the engineered phenotype. Hereafter, we deﬁne the substrate in which the bacterium
is intended to act upon and an overview enzymatic PET hydrolysis. Next, we explain the observed PET-
utilizing phenotype discovered in Ideonella sakaiensis by examining the secretion of the novel PETase in
addition to the intrinsic aromatic carbon metabolism supporting this phenotype. The section is concluded
by stating which two PETase mutants were chosen for expression in P. putida.
Due to an extended and thorough result section and discussion, which should provide the required informa-
tion for the understanding of the acquired results and the discussion thereof, this background section is a
short review of the essential information and can be considered as an extension of the introduction. For the
expert in PET utilization, this section may seem redundant.
2.1
Pseudomonas putida:A promising host for the future plastic-based bio-
process industry
Pseudomonas putida is a soil-bacterium like species of Comamonas, Burkholderia, Rhodococcus etc. With
its GRAS status (Generally Regarded As Safe), strong aromatic metabolism and a vast selection of molecular
tools (Martínez-Gar´cía et al. 2015; Volke et al. 2020a; Volke et al. 2020b; Wirth et al. 2020). P. putida being a
proteobacterium like the newly identiﬁed species of Ideonella sakaiensis, makes this bacterium a promising
host for re-creation of the PET-utilization phenotype as described by Yoshida et al. (2016). A phenotype
which is highly remarkable since plastics, especially polyethylene terephthalate (PET) through its semi-
aromatic structure, was thought to be recalcitrant for enzymatic hydrolysis (Kawai et al. 2019). Classes of
enzymes that have shown to have activity towards PET are generally known as Esterases. These esterases
are then subdivided based on their recognition of the substrate: long carbon chains generally found in lipids
are thus classiﬁed as a lipase; Medium chain-length is recognized by cutinases, known for their hydrolysis of
plant material cutin; And lastly, esterases for the recognition of short ester-linked chains (Kawai et al. 2019).
The ester bond in PET is found between the monomer of terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol (Kawai et
al. 2019). The ester reaction between terephthalic acid and the alcohol group of ethylene glycol is stimulated
at 280°C, where the rapid polymerisation happens (Sinha et al. 2010). These polymers are through various
techniques, which are not a scope of this project, packed tightly. Depending on the tightness of the polymer,
the crystallinity of the plastics is determined (Sinha et al. 2010). In general, the higher the crystallinity, the
harder it is for the enzyme to access the ester linkages, and with the low water absorbency of PET ranging
from 0.1-1%, the accessibility is further halted (Kawai et al. 2019). Therefore, to facilitate PET hydrolysis
reaction temperatures are elevated. Kawai et al. (2019) recently proposed that the classiﬁcation of a PET
hydrolase should be the efﬁcient endo-acting activity of an esterase at 65°C.
3
==PAGE:17==
This classiﬁcation, however, leaves per deﬁnition PETase from I. sakaiensis a mere polyaromatic-esterase
if the deﬁnition should be followed. Nonetheless, PETase has shown activity towards mostly low crys-
tallinity PET less than 10%. No one has reported PETase activity at a high crystallinity percentage of 50%.
Through several analysis methods, genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics the PET phenotype of I. sakaien-
sis has been found to be mediated by the secretion of a PETase that releases primarily mono hydroxyethyl
terephthalate (MHET) residual amounts of BHET (bis(hydroxyethyl) terephthalate) and terephthalate (TPA).
Through porin structures in the outer membrane of I. sakaiensis MHET enters the periplasmic space of the
Gram-negative bacterium, and a periplasmic residing enzyme, MHETase, catalyzes the conversion to TPA
which has been proposed to be transported inside the cytoplasmic space for conversion into hub metabolite
of protocatechuic acid (Figure 2.1) (Sasoh et al. 2006; Hosaka et al. 2013; Yoshida et al. 2016; Taniguchi
et al. 2019).
Figure 2.1: PET-phenotype in Ideonella sakiensis. Picture is from (Taniguchi et al. 2019)
PCA is further metabolized through general ways of aromatic cleavage into the succinyl CoA or acetyl-CoA
for utilization in the citric acid cycle releasing CO2. Secretion of PETase is mediated by a signal-peptide
that through the activity of ribosome bound molecular chaperones transports the produced PETase unfolded
through the general secretion pathway of Sec (Huang et al. 2018; Seo et al. 2019).

another organism such as P. putida. A two-step prokaryotic secretion system of proteins, has shown to
mediate secretion of PETase in heterologous expression by E. coli, and is based on the initial transport of
the unfolded protein, via Sec, to the periplasm and subsequently mediated by a complex structure, T2SS,
with narrow substrate speciﬁcity to the extracellular environment (Natale et al. 2008; Korotkov et al. 2019).
Indeed, the T2SS systems is a common pathway for lipases, which require the activation by a corresponding
lipase chaperone for catalytic activity (Verma et al. 2020). In addition, it has recently been argued that the
overall structure of PETase resembles a lipase (Austin et al. 2018). Therefore it is an exciting ﬁnding that
heterologous PETase expression in E. coli, when translocated to the periplasm through a Sec-signal peptide,
seems to be a natural substrate for T2SS in E. coli (Seo et al. 2019). Although signal-peptides, guiding
the protein to the periplasm through the correct machinery with alternative to Sec being, Twin-Arginine
Transport(TAT) system or Signal-Recognition-Particle(SRP) mediated, an overall similarity of the signals
is found (Figure 2.2). The improved PETase secretion obtained by deletion of TAT in Bacillus, may in fact
show a lipase with a degenerated signal-peptide (Huang et al. 2018). The ﬁnding that two disulﬁde-bridges
are present in PETase, and the fact that the oxidative environment of the periplasm favors disulﬁde-bridge
formation, further suggests a desired two-step secretion route of PETase. Indeed, the stability of PETase
is highly controlled by a disulﬁde bridge close to the catalytic triad of Serine-Histidine-Aspartic Acid. A
disulﬁde bridge essential for the activity of PETase (Fecker et al. 2018). Upon mutation of this cystein-
bridge, the activity of PETase is reduced to 10% of the WT indicating that the distance exceeds the limitation
of the charge-relay dependant activation of the Serine mediated nucleophilic attack of PET esters (Fecker
et al. 2018; Taniguchi et al. 2019).
We have selected to work with the two PETase mutants recently engineered for a 2.5 fold increased activity
for PET at 30°C, PETaseI179F (Ma et al. 2018) and an engineered PETase for remarkable thermostability
at 50°C, i.e. DuraPETase (Cui et al. 2019). As these seem as promising candidates for enzymatic hydrolysis
of PET.

3.5
Production of heterologous proteins & assessment by SDS-PAGE
To compare different expression schemes on the production of the PETase mutants and expression of the tph
cluster, respective plasmids were transferred to competent E. coli BL21 (DE3) or P. putida strains KT2440,
EM42 or SEM10. Competence and transformation of E. coli BL21 (DE3) was achieved using the procedure
as previously described for E. coli DH5α. Competence of P. putida strains were achieved by sucrose-
washing of P. putida cell pellets obtained after 24 h growth in LB medium, following the protocol of Choi
et al. (2006). Washed P. putida cells were hereafter transformed by electroporation following guidelines
of Iwasaki et al. (1994). Initial transformations using pure plasmids were veriﬁed by colony PCR using
universal primers p108 and p109 as described previously.
Once conﬁrmed, the heterologous production of the proteins was obtained by the following scheme: A 10
mL liquid preculture of either bacterium, was created by using a single colony from the transformation plate
as inoculum in minimal medium or LB medium supplemented with kanamycin and grown for 24 h at op-
timal conditions. The day after, 50 mL fresh liquid medium was poured in a 250 mL shake-ﬂask and was
inoculated to a ﬁnal OD600 of ≈0.1 using the newly made preculture. The optical density (OD) had been
determined by measuring the absorbance at 600 nm of 1 mL cell culture contained in a 1.5 mL BRAND®
semi-micro cuvette [Sigma-Aldrich, USA] using a standard spectrophotometer.
The 50 mL liquid culture was then allowed to grow until the exponential growth phase of the culture, which
was expected to occur at OD600 of ≈0.5-0.6. Next, the exponentially growing cells were placed on ice for
30 min, and subsequently, expression from the plasmids was induced using 1 mM of 3-methyl benzoate,
cyclohexanone or 3-chloro-4-hydroxyphenylaceticacid with cultures containing pS238, pS2311 or pS2312,
respectively. For the production of PETase mutants from a T7 based expression system, 0.1 mM of the
inducer IPTG was used instead. After induction, liquid cultures were transferred from the optimal growth
temperature (30° for P. putida & 37°C for E. coli) to 16°C and left for 20 h. Liquid cultures were transferred
to 50 mL centrifugal tubes and centrifuged at 4000g for 10 min in a 4°C precooled "SL 40R" centrifuge
[Thermoﬁsher Scientiﬁc, USA]. Growth was terminated by transferring the generated supernatant to new
tubes and storing the cell pellet at -20°C.
Production of the desired protein was assessed using sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel elec-
trophoresis (SDS-PAGE) with either sample from the supernatant or the pelleted cells.
Samples of former origin were centrifuged an additional time and ﬁltered through 0.22µm to obtain a cell-
free supernatant. A 50x concentrated sample was made using Amicon ® Ultra - 15 centrifugal ﬁlters [Merck
Millipore, Germany] to remove excess water from 50 mL to 1 mL.
A 500x concentrated version of the supernatant was subsequently made from the 50x concentrated super-
natant by vacuum evaporation using "Concentrator Plus" [Eppendorf, USA], at 60°C for 1hour, obtaining
a ﬁnal volume of 100 µL. If required, the supernatant was completely evaporated, using 2 h evaporation
instead. Supernatant samples were prepared for loading on SDS-PAGE by mixing 5 µL with 2.5 µL 4X
Laemli Buffer [BIO-RAD, USA] and 2.5 µL 600 mM β-mercaptoethanol. Pelleted cells from 50 mL cul-
tures grown for 20 h (OD600 ≈3.0) were resuspended in 1 mL LB medium and 5 µL from this solution
(0.5% (v/v)) was transferred to a new tube and re-pelleted. This 0.5% (v/v) was dissolved directly in double
concentrated Laemli Buffer and β-mecapthoethanol. If required, pelleted cells from less incubation times

4 . Results
Rational design of a PET degrading Pseudomonas putida strain requires (i) the enzymatic machinery needed
for degradation of the high molecular weight polymer into its constituents and (ii) efﬁcient incorporation of
PET monomers into the carbon metabolism. These two properties are mandatory to make PET degrada-
tion feasible in P. putida, (and other microbial platforms), and were thus attempted to tackle the problems
through the following efforts:
Part I (Section 4.1) will introduce the initial construction of plasmid vectors, enabling the expression of two
newly engineered mutants of the original PET hydrolase (PETase) discovered by Yoshida et al. (2016), that
either showed to improve the hydrolytic activity or the thermostability of the enzyme. Moreover, the ex-
pression of a putative PETase chaperone will be investigated as well to see if PETase expression is improved.
Part II (Section 4.2) examines the differences in activity and protein stability of the newly identiﬁed mutants
of PETase: PETaseI179F and DuraPETase.
Part III (Section 4.3) delves into the construction of a terephthalate utilizing P. putida strain, by the intro-
duction of a gene-cluster.
Each part of the results will ﬁnish with a summary section to discuss the main ﬁndings of each module in
the project.

4.1.1
Design and construction of PETase expression vectors
Several expression systems were designed to assess P. putida ability to produce the novel PET hydrolase,
PETase, which was recently identiﬁed by Yoshida et al. (2016). These plasmids were designed according
to the Standard European Vector-Architecture (SEVA) Database (http://seva.cnb.csic.es/). In brief,
this database was developed to introduce a three digit nomenclature for plasmids (pS- - - ) and to facilitate
quicker construction of expression vectors by standardizing the architecture of DNA plasmids. Minimum
elements of a plasmid i.e. antibiotic resistance marker, origin of replication, and the desired cargo are thus
all ﬂanked by unique restriction sites, and the type of each element is reﬂected in the name by unique num-
bers at position 1, 2, 3, respectively (Silva-Rocha et al. 2013; Martínez-Gar´cía et al. 2015; Martínez-García
et al. 2020).
Four well-known expression systems used in metabolic engineering of Gram-negative bacteria were cho-
sen from the SEVA database. These were the three inducible expression systems XylS/Pm (SEVA#- -
8), ChnR/PchnB (SEVA#- - 11), CprK1/PDB3 (SEVA#- - 12) and the constitutive synthetic promoter PEM7
(SEVA#- - 13), which already were available in the laboratory, and shown to work in both E. coli and P.
putida (Gawin et al. 2017; Benedetti et al. 2016a; Benedetti 2014; Zobel et al. 2015). It was also ensured
that the replication origin of the plasmid (SEVA#- X -) was compatible with the enterobacterium E. coli
and the soil bacterium P. putida, as evaluation of PETase gene expression, was intended to be carried out
in both bacteria. Replication origins pBBR1 (SEVA#- 3 -) and RSF1010 (SEVA#- 5 -) were chosen as they
are both broad-host-range (BHR) and they display mid to high plasmid copy number per cell (Silva-Rocha
et al. 2013). Finally, the antibiotic resistance gene, aphA, was chosen as a selection marker on all expression
systems (SEVA#2 - -), since this gene enables resistance towards kanamycin, which is routinely used as an
antibiotic for selection in either host (Silva-Rocha et al. 2013). Thus, the backbones for the construction of
several PETase expression systems were vectors, pS238, pS2311, pS2312, and pS2513. In addition to these
expression systems, the strong T7 phage promoter (pS231T7) was chosen as a positive control for PETase
expression, as this system already had been shown to work in E. coli (Yoshida et al. 2016).
Next, the coding sequence of PETase was retrieved from the whole genome shotgun sequence of the origi-
nal strain reported by Yoshida et al. (2016) (NCBI Reference sequence: NZ_BBYR01000074.1, Gene ID:
ISF6_RS23955) and codon-optimized towards proper expression in P. putida. Since the original PETase was
reported to have a slow PET hydrolysis rate and to be very sensitive towards temperatures above 30°C, we
searched the literature for already characterized mutations known to increase the aforementioned properties.
Recent work done by Ma et al. (2018) showed that exchanging the isoleucine at position 179 to pheny-
lalanine increased the activity of PETase towards PET ﬁlm by 2.5 fold (PETase179F). Cui et al. (2019)
addressed the issue of thermostability and showed a dramatic increase in the melting temperature of PETase
(∆Tm = 31°C) by introducing ten mutations S214H-I168R-W159H-S188Q-R280A-A180I-G165A-Q119Y-
L117F-T140D. Thus, two PETase mutant coding sequences were created by introducing the aforementioned
mutations into the codon-optimized coding sequence of PETase, while paying attention to the numbering
with/without the 27 amino acid (aa) signal peptide. In addition to the original ten mutations of the mutant
described by Cui et al. (2019), the I179F mutation was included as well, in the hopes of creating a PETase
mutant with combined traits (DuraPETase).

is subsequently reacted to a second antibody, which binds the heavy chain of the primary antibody. This
secondary antibody is fused to a Horse Raddish Peroxidase that catalyzes the oxidation of luminol to a ﬂuo-
rophor that can emit light. Since this reaction only occurs at proteins containing both antibodies bound, the
background signal will be highly reduced (GE-Healthcare 2017).

Cloning of the TPA-cluster and design of a TPA-biosensor

transporter (tphC), respectively (Figure 4.16). In addition to amplifying the region of interest primers were
designed to introduce restriction sites or USER-elements at the end of the amplicon for downstream ligation
to a compatible vector. In brief, unlike restriction enzymes that recognize the presence of a unique pattern
of nucleotides before introducing a double-stranded break, USER (Uracil Speciﬁc Excision Reaction) on
the other hand only requires the presence of a single uracil in the DNA of interest. Treatment by a USER
enzyme mix, consisting of uracil DNA glycosylase and DNA glycosylase-lyase Endo VIII, the uracil is
excised from the DNA by a single-stranded nick, and the adjacent small DNA fragment is removed in a
3’-5’ direction, generating a single stranded DNA overhang (Nour-Eldin et al. 2010). After USER treatment
fragments, designed to contain complementary overhangs, are allowed to bind to each other by Watson-
Crick base pairing and ﬁnally ligated once successfully transformed into E. coli DH5α. Thus, in theory,
any DNA fragment independent of intrinsic restriction sites can be fused after successful PCR generation
of the fragments. As mentioned before several primers were designed to amplify different sections of the
TPA-cluster from B. gladioli using Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) indicated in Figure 4.16.

Heterologous expression of TPA catabolic genes in P. putida & E. coli
The TPA utilization phenotype of B. gladioli, encoded by the TPA-cluster, is catalyzing the conversion of
terephthalate (TPA) to protocatechuate (PCA), which some bacteria are capable of metabolizing, including P.
putida. The tphA1-3 genes of the cluster encode a terephthalate 1,2-dioxygenase, that is a tripartite oxidore-
ductase complex, TPADO, with two oxygenase components, tphA2 (large) and tphA3 (small), along with
a reductase component tphA1. This complex converts TPA to the intermediate DCD (1,2-dihydroxy-3,5-
cyclohexadiene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid) using the cofactor NADPH in a oxidation reaction ([EC 1.13.11]).
DCD is then converted to PCA using NADP + as co-factor through the action of tphB in the cluster, encod-
ing terephthalate dihydrodiol dehydrogenase that removes water ([EC 1.1.1]).
Thus by a transformation of pS238-TPAcatabol into P. putida SEM10, growth in minimal medium sup-
plemented with TPA was expected. To ensure that the genes were expressed, prior inoculation in minimal
medium, P. putida had been grown in LB-medium containing 1 mM of the inducer 3-methyl benzoate (3m-
Benz). After growth O/N, the cells were washed in minimal medium and then inoculated into de Bont
Medium supplemented with 10 mM TPA. As a positive control, the same cells were inoculated into DeBont
Medium supplemented with 0.2% (w/v) citrate. The expected growth was monitored for 104 h by measuring
at OD600. The use of P. putida SEM10 instead of other strains, were the ∆benABCD genotype which dis-
ables inducer metabolism of 3m-Benz, as observed in prolonged incubations in previous experiments (See
Figure A.7 in Appendix). However, the OD600 value was only increasing with time in the positive control
(data not shown). Samples were kept for an additional three days in the incubator for a total cultivation time
of seven days, however, no visual growth was observed in the TPA supplemented samples.
A quick experiment was performed using cell extracts of E. coli DH5α expressing the pS238-TPAcatabol
plasmid, prepared as with PETase cell extracts (See Section 4.2). An in vitro TPA conversion reaction con-
taining 150µM TPA, 200 µM NADPH, 50 mM Tris-HCL buffer (pH 8) and 200 µg of protein extract were
performed O/N. All samples were subsequently analyzed using High Performance Liquid Chromatography
(HPLC), which enables the detection of individual organic compounds. In brief, HPLC consists of a sta-
tionary phase and mobile phase, that are chosen based on the physicochemical properties of the analytes.
Most often reverse phase HPLC is used with an apolar stationary phase and a polar mobile phase, thus non-
polar analytes such as terephthalate preferably interact with the stationary phase and are retained. Gradually
changing the composition of the mobile phase to mimic the stationary phase, allows the slow release of
similar non-polar analytes and analytes are thus separated according to their interaction with the stationary
phase which is determined by their polarity.

A discussion of the results obtained in our attempt to construct a PET degrading Pseudomonas putida strain,
will be discussed below according to their corresponding part in the results section. This section concludes
by summarising key points from each partial discussion and lists future experiments to be done.
5.1
Part I: Expression of PETaseI179F and DuraPETase in P. putida
A total of 13 mutant PETase expression vectors (with/without signal-peptide(SP)) were constructed using
four different inducible expression systems i.e. XylS/Pm, ChnR/PchnB, CprK/PDB3, PT7 or the constitu-
tive expressing PEM7. Three of the plasmids contained the modiﬁed consensus Shine-Dalgarno sequence
(Miller et al. 1997) compared to the BCD2 translation initiation region of the remaining plasmids (Mutalik
et al. 2013a). Transcription from these three plasmids was also under the control of the strong PT7 routinely
used for protein production in E. coli (Rosano et al. 2014). Adequate protein production from the different
expression systems was assessed by SDS-PAGE and subsequent staining for visualization, in which only
the T7 based system provided a detectable protein concentration when produced in E. coli. This observation
was only evident when using histidine speciﬁc methods for visualization i.e. InVision™or Western Blotting.
Using the Coomassie based, InstantBlue™, detection method (routinely used in this study), unfortunately,
masked the protein band from both PETase mutants. The same masking effect was observed only for the
DuraPETase mutant when InVision™staining was applied. Estimating the theoretical molecular weight of a
protein should be straight forward i.e. by summarizing the individual molecular weights of each amino acid
residue, plus the addition of one mole of water (for the unpaired termini), and ﬁnally subtracting the molec-
ular weight of water (n-1) times for total condensation reactions. However, the theoretical molecular weight
calculated using the "Quest Calculate™" (AAT Bioquest 2019) estimated DuraPETase to be the heaviest of
the mutants (∆0.14kDa), although the opposite was experimentally determined. Moreover, a difference of
∆0.14kDa, would not be observable in an SDS-PAGE gel (BIO-RAD, date unknown), however, a clear dif-
ference was seen in our gel experiments, with PETaseI179F being the heaviest. Although certain limitation
in detection, a sufﬁcient protein concentration was detected using InVision™staining of the pSEVA231-
T7Pr-PETase/DuraPETase samples (Revisit Figure 4.8, page 25). Even a low protein concentration (less
intense band) was seen 3 hours after induction, compared to the intense band in the sample taken 20 hrs
after induction, indicating that small protein concentrations are, in fact, detectable using the less sensitive
InVision™method.
Therefore our reasoning is the following: Poor protein production from the non-T7 based plasmids should
be detectable by InVision™. In fact, the applied sample preparation method used in this study for SDS-
PAGE does not necessitate correctly folding of PETase. This is due to the samples being prepared from the
total protein fraction of the cell pellet. This would include protein aggregates arising from misfolded PETase
(i.e. inclusion bodies) or unfolded proteins that otherwise would be lost through the Bead beating applied
for cell-free extractions.

These misfolded proteins would lead to a 30kDa visible band on the SDS-PAGE gel, as samples of total pro-
tein were treated with denaturing elements such as high temperature, SDS and β-mecapthoethanol leading
to unfolding of individual proteins and protein aggregates (Laemmli 1970). Thus, detection limitation and
band masking can not fully why a PETase band was not observed from total protein samples derived from
non T7-based plasmids. The detection method, however, relies heavily on the successful decoding of DNA
to protein, as the assessment of promoter-activity was purely based on the protein level of gene expression.

Transcription from T7 promoter 3) Different element for translation initiation. Hence every element that
inﬂuences successful protein production is different in the T7-based plasmids. Since the experimental ev-
idence is limited, a putative explanation based on the theoretical knowledge on protein production will be
derived below, gradually ruling out possible explanations.
The genetic code: A coding sequence from any gene, is transcribed into mRNA and translated by a ri-
bosome that recognizes ribonucleotides in pairs of three(codon). Translation begins at the ﬁrst ATG near
a ribosomal binding site and through the base pairing of tRNA anti-codons delivering the amino acid in
the mRNA code, a polypeptide chain starts to form. As the anticodon for each amino acid is degenerate
the same amino acid can be acquired to the emerging polypeptide chain through additional tRNAs. Since
the synthesis of activated tRNAs is energetic, any organism has evolved a preference for selected few an-
ticodons/tRNAs for the translation of each codon over the course of time. As a consequence, a general
approach codon-optimization tools, such as the one from Integrated DNA technologies Karacay (2018) ap-
plied in this study, tunes the coding sequence of a given gene, into matching the frequencies of the desired
host. From an evolutionary point of view, it is therefore very logical that the pool of available tRNAs, is
reﬂected in the translation speed of a ribosome, and therefore indirectly correlated to the folding properties
of a protein.
Correctly folded proteins can, therefore, arise from different translational speeds of a ribosome. For illustra-
tion, three stereotypical proteins exist according to their translational speeds: 1) A mRNA encoding, tight
secondary structures, ribosome pausing sites or rare tRNAs will be translated at a slow pace, and as a con-
sequence give time for local thermodynamic energetic minima stabilizing protein intermediates as they fold
co-translationally; 2) An unstructured mRNA with no ribosomal pausing sites and use of high frequency
tRNAs would lead to a rapidly produced polypeptide chain, that through its intrinsic primary amino acid
sequence readily ﬁnds the favorable thermodynamic energetic minima and folds post-translational; 3) The
mRNA encodes a mixture of both extremes and leads to functional protein (Tsai et al. 2008; Kramer et
al. 2018; Hanson et al. 2018).
Therefore codon-optimization is tricky, as an expression of distantly related organisms requires the codon-
optimization for compatibility of the available tRNAs while jeopardizing the thermodynamics of its pro-
tein folding. Thus, a logical consequence by codon-optimizing changing group type would lead to mis-
folded/unfolded proteins (Hanson et al. 2018). Therefore a common strategy for heterologous protein pro-
duction is upon induction transfer the liquid culture to lower temperatures, such as 16°C done in this study
and other PETase expression (Rosano et al. 2014; Ma et al. 2018; Cui et al. 2019).
An interesting observation from codon-optimized PETase expression using the commercial expression sys-
tems as Bacillus subtilis, Pichia pastoris and Aspergillus oryzae, expression in E. coli seems to work the
best (Personal Communication, Kenneth Jensen). Another observation is that Huang et al. (2018) used an
active codon-bias towards E. coli when expressing PETase in B. subtilis indicating that the codon frequency
of E. coli and therefore the protein folding properties mimics the original PETase host, Ideonella sakaiensis.

Posttranscriptional issues: Based on the above discussion we speculate that the inadequate PETase pro-
duction from non T7-systems is due to impairment at the posttranscriptional or translational level. Indeed a
study by Volke et al. (2020a) used the BCD2 element coupled to constitutive expression of ﬂuorescent pro-
teins and showed an improved ﬂuorescent signal, compared to a standard ribosomal binding site. Comparing
the constructs, pSNW2 and pS228R, used in Volke et al. (2020a) show that the genetic make-up is different
from the plasmids constructed in this study. In fact, a consistent and average spacing((63+39+78+45bp)/4)
of 56bp and 67% ((65+74+57+73%)/4) GC-content between the promoter and BCD2 is found in the non
T7-based, PETase/PETase-Chaperone containing plasmids. A study by Chan et al. (2009) showed that
structured RNA molecules have a strong positive correlation between the stability of secondary structures
and their GC percentage, as structured RNA molecules with high GC-content were more resistant to mi-
croRNA guided degradation, which is based on RNA-RNA interactions. Since the initial recruitment of
the ribosome depends on the 16SrRNA mediated contact with the mRNA at the ribosomal binding site i.e.
Shine-Dalgarno, this would indicate a reduced interaction due to secondary structures. Duval et al. (2013)
showed that the effect of structured RNA molecules near the Shine-Dalgarno sequence were determining
translation initiation, and reﬂected in the ability to recruit the ribosome (Boni et al. 1991). Indeed a strong ri-
bosomal binding site showed efﬁcient recruitment independent of secondary structures immediately around
it. On the other hand, weak Shine-Dalgarno sequences required the aid of available regions for ribosomal
protein S1 binding (Duval et al. 2013). Independent of RBS strength Smit et al. (1990) showed a strong
negative correlation of secondary structures directly at or covering the RBS, indicating that RBS availability
is detrimental for recruitment of the ribosome. The T7 system in pS231T7-PETase/DuraPETase contain
the additional basepairs, however this strong translation unit comprises 14bp in total (Miller et al. 1997),
AGGAGGAAAAACAT , while the BCD2 element is a long stretch of 77bp upstream the coding sequence
(Mutalik et al. 2013a).
The region between the transcriptional start site and the ﬁrst start-codon, AUG, downstream RBS is deﬁned
as 5’-UTR (5’-Untranslated region). Indeed, engineering of 5’-UTR has shown to affect positively on pro-
tein production (Berg et al. 2012; Mutalik et al. 2013a). Indeed the BCD2 element is an attempt to control
the 5’-UTR effects on gene expression as a parallel study by Mutalik et al. (2013b) proved that this element
was causing the highest contribution to the measured values of ﬂuorescence and mRNA abundance, indicat-
ing this region as a prime controller of efﬁcient gene expression. Moreover, they showed that the promoter
region was the element that provided the highest variation regarding the amount of mRNA actively trans-
lated to protein i.e. translation efﬁciency. This, however, can be explained by the above discussion regarding
the masking of translation initiation. To overcome the contribution of 5’UTR affects on gene-expression led
(Mutalik et al. 2013a) to developed BCD2. BCD2 is a highly tuned region developed through computational
methods, a consensus junction between promoter:5’-UTR and 5’-UTR:GOI, to eliminate contributors for
secondary RNA structures.
The overall design of the bicistronic design (BCD) 2 was inspired by single mRNAs encoding translation
of more than one gene(cistron), and where the stop codon in the ﬁrst gene is frame-shifted +1 in the down-

available. Thus generating a translation scheme tolerant to secondary structures generated from fusion to
any gene with a variance of the signals measured of 0.14, indicating very low noise.

ture generated from different promoter additions and downstream genes.

element can be achieved through FACS and FENIX, which are available at hand. Any potential gain of
function mutations in PETase, would through conventional and reverse Michaelis-Menten kinetics enable
us to describe if the improved activity is due to an increase of more viable attack-sites, or from the protein
itself. If PETase, in fact, is a degenerated lipase, with a putative broad protein mutability landscape as ar-
gued by the many beneﬁcial mutations successfully engineered in PETse, the putative lipase foldase directly
upstream of PETase, and the presence of lipases substrate T2SS secretion in P. putida (Taniguchi et al. 2019;
Meer et al. 2016; Putker et al. 2013), an improved secretion phenotype could be selected for by FACS in P.
putida, as shown in to improve secretion phenotype in E. coli (Eichmann et al. 2019). In combination with
our newly improved genome engineering platform functional genetic elements can be integrated in P. putida
for higher genetic stability (Wirth et al. 2020).
With the many successful proof-of-concepts for enzymatic recycling of PET (Kawai et al. 2019) and con-
version of monomers for production of value added chemicals (Kim et al. 2019), we envision thatP. putida
has an important part to play. Especially by the recent ﬁndings of a PET depolymerase with 90% hydrolysis
of recycled low quality PET in 10 hours (Tournier et al. 2020) in addition to an engineered ethylene glycol
metabolism (Franden et al. 2018; Li et al. 2019), we argue that an engineered terephthalate utilization would
help close the loop in the PET economy, and open the door to potential plastic-based bioprocess industry,
adding to the already existing and growing bio-based production of chemicals (Lee et al. 2019).

6 . Conclusion & Perspectives
In this study, we examined three major areas regarding the rational design of a PET degrading bacterial cell
factory: PETase expression, PET hydrolysis and synthetic aromatic metabolism. We show the importance
of optimal vector design, and that routine screening methods are prone to discarding false-negative results.
We have conﬁrmed previous attempts of E. coli BL21 (DE3) derived production of, Ma et al. (2018) PETa-
seI179F and Cui et al. (2019) DuraPETase, by the strong T7 RNAP systems, which led to protein amounts
sufﬁcient for biochemical studies. We conclude that the single amino acid (aa) mutant PETaseI179F has a
3-fold higher PET hydrolysis compared to the 11aa mutant DuraPETase at 30°C, while near glass-transition
temperatures the DuraPETase is superior in both thermostability and activity, as reported by Cui et al. (2019).
The two PETase mutants represent a classical dilemma in protein engineering as mutations that beneﬁt ac-
tivities may alter the stability and vice versa. Indeed argued by Fecker et al. (2018) the PETase seems to be a
protein at the brink of instability, i.e. the thermodynamic local minimum of the native conformation is close
to an alternative conformation. Indeed promiscuous enzymes, such as cutinases, have long been known to
be the driving factor of the evolutionary development of new catalytic activities (O’Brien et al. 1999). The
many protein engineering studies of PETase Taniguchi et al. (2019) could be evidence that this PET hydro-
lase has a broad plethora of alternative local minima in its protein mutability landscape (Meer et al. 2016).
Selected studies already showed by inserting single-to-few mutations here and there enabled the improve-
ment of either thermostability (Son et al. 2019), enzymatic activity (Ma et al. 2018), or broadening substrate
selectivity (Austin et al. 2018). The existence of a transcriptional regulator, TphR, sensing the presence of
terephthalate(TPA), enables the coupling of PET degradation to any potential protein/phenotypic trait, as
TphR activates transcription from any gene of interest(GOI) upon the presence of TPA. Through coupling
TphR regulation to GFP, an ultrahigh-throughput screening method such as FACS (Fluorescence Activated
Cell Sorting) would allow the selection of mutations leading to enhanced TPA generation (Blagodatski et
al. 2011; Markel et al. 2020). Through rationally designed selection schemes for in vivo evolution, the pro-
tein mutability landscape of PETase could be explored.
This potential exploration initially requires successful transport of PETase to the extracellular milieu either
through complete secretion (Seo et al. 2019; Huang et al. 2018) or attached in outer-membrane display
systems (Chen et al. 2020). Indeed such a display system has recently been developed in P. putida KT2440
(Tozakidis et al. 2020), and combined with recent evidence that Pseudomonas spp. readily colonize PET-
ﬁlm and microparticles enable the possibility of generating an efﬁcient cell factory for PET hydrolysis
through catalytic bioﬁlms (Vague et al. 2019; Feng et al. 2020; Benedetti et al. 2016b). The existence of a
protocatechuate(PCA) transcriptional regulators (Jha et al. 2018), would allow the tight ﬁne-tuning of the
chosen PET degrading phenotype of P. putida through FACS, as coupling of both transcriptional regulators
to ﬂuorescent proteins would allow dual selection schemes. Mutations leading to PCA accumulation could
improve the production of PCA-derived high-value products, thus adding to the map of chemicals derived
from bioprocesses (Lee et al. 2019).
