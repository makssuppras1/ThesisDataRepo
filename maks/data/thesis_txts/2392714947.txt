Abstract
This study aims to assess the application potential of the streambed point velocity probe (sbPVP)
to inform about heterogeneous ﬂows in sediments, as well as to determine contaminant mass discharge
at the groundwater-surface water interface. The sbPVP is an in-situ direct push method that injects a
small saline tracer into the streambed to measure vertical ﬂow in streambeds. To date, sbPVPs were
mainly tested in laboratory studies or rather homogeneous sandy sediments. This study investigated
the use of the sbPVP in particularly heterogeneous sediments at two contaminated sites posing a risk
to streams in Denmark.
Heterogeneous sediments may cause complex velocity and localized concentration patterns. Results
show that the sbPVP provides very detailed information about the complexity of ﬂows in the sediment
on a cm-scale and is applicable in various sediment types, including homogeneous sandy, gravelly, and
organic-rich sediments, sediments covered by macrophytes, as well as stream reaches containing waste
material and stones in various sizes. Results show that the sbPVP injection tests are highly dependent
on the streambed structure and material and did not always show the overall groundwater ﬂow to
the stream. Therefore, it was found useful to perform injections at several depths and to combine
methods to quantify groundwater surface water interactions.
This was done by comparing sbPVP results to more conventional methods, which are two methods
based on Darcy’s Law, i.e. permeameter tests and grain size distribution analysis, and an analytical
solution of the heat conduction-advection-equation by means of measuring the temperature distribu-
tion in the streambed. Comparisons showed large diﬀerences in velocities by the diﬀerent methods
applied. The sbPVP stood out with greatest independence from external parameters, such as hydraulic
conductivities, hydraulic head measurements, or streambed temperatures.
At the former knife factory in Raadvad, previous investigations showed discharge of chlorinated
ethenes to the nearby stream. sbPVP injections and sampling campaigns of streambed and stream
water could determine the contaminant mass discharge of mostly Vinyl chloride from the streambed.
The results showed very localized highly concentrated mass discharge of contamination, which was
mainly due to heterogeneous concentration distribution in the sediment rather than diﬀerences in
velocities.
Moreover, the application of the dilution and volatilization model (DILVOL), which is based on
in-stream completely mixed concentrations, showed that the mass discharge of chlorinated ethenes
from the streambed originates from a small discharge area and is relatively low compared to the
contaminant mass discharging from the identiﬁed point sources along the stream bank.
In conclusion, this study shows the great potential of the sbPVP to investigate the complex be-
havior of the water ﬂuxes and contaminant discharge at the groundwater-surface water interface.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Chlorinated solvents are xenobiotic organic compounds that were used in the 20th century in a wide
range of industrial processes covering mainly metal, dry cleaning, aerospace, and military industry
(Morrison et al., 1964). These compounds are of concern, as they and their degradation products
may pose a risk to ecosystem and human health when released into the environment (Conant et al.,
2004; Ellis and Rivett, 2007; McKnight et al., 2010). Once in the environment, chlorinated solvents
can be transported over long distances and into several of its compartments, such as the hydrosphere,
biosphere, and atmosphere (Freitas et al., 2015). Especially in aquifers with high redox potential and
low sediment carbon content, plumes can migrate distances up to several kilometres (Benker et al.,
1997; Jackson, 1998). The interface between groundwater and surface water especially interesting
as contaminated groundwater may enter stream water and have adverse eﬀects on the stream water
quality or vice versa, i.e. polluted surface water may inﬁltrate and aﬀect groundwater quality (Kalbus
et al., 2007). There has been increasing agreement between authorities that contaminant mass ﬂuxes
and mass ﬂow rates, e.g. the contaminant mass discharge (CMD), should be taken as an indicator for
water quality and support site assessment for further treatment and remediation measurements rather
than limit concentrations (Basu et al., 2006). This is expressed in the European Water Framework
Directive that highlights the necessity for an “improved process-oriented understanding" of contam-
inant transport between these two bodies of water (McKnight et al., 2010, 2012; Weatherill et al.,
2014, p. 4).
To determine the contaminant mass discharge, the groundwater velocity and contaminant concen-
tration in the streambed have to be known (Kalbus et al., 2007). In the past, several ﬁeld method-
ologies have been developed to measure water velocity in the streambed, an important transmission
zone for groundwater and surface water. More conventional methods include heat tracer methods and
approaches based on Darcy’s Law, which require estimates for hydraulic gradient and conductivity for
use in the Darcy equation (Kalbus et al., 2006). A more recent technology is the point velocity probe
(PVP) developed by Labaky et al. (2007), which is a reliable, inexpensive, and time-eﬃcient method
of measuring the groundwater velocity and direction at a cm scale (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a). An
adaptation of this method lead to the development of the streambed point velocity probe (sbPVP),
to be able to measure vertical velocities at the groundwater surface water interface (Cremeans and
Devlin, 2017a). The sbPVP technology aims to characterize streambeds eﬃciently and permits a more
detailed description of the ﬂow heterogeneity in the shallow streambed (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a).

==PAGE:14==
Until now, this technology was subject to extensive laboratory testings (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017b)
and used to determine the mass discharge distribution of chlorinated ethenes in a predominantly sandy
streambed located in Grindsted, Denmark (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a).
sbPVPs have not been applied to diﬀerent streambed settings at other sites. The use of the method
thus still needs to be tested for heterogeneous streambeds and compared and validated by conven-
tional methods in those new settings. At a former knife factory in Raadvad, Denmark, investigations
showed discharge of chlorinated ethenes to the nearby stream, assumingly partially discharging from
the streambed, which has not been assessed before.
1.1
Objectives
The overall aim of this thesis is to assess the application potential of sbPVPs as a tool to determine
contaminant mass discharge at the groundwater surface water interface at contaminated sites posing
a risk to streams. This is done by fulﬁlling the following objectives:
- To investigate the use of the sbPVP particularly for heterogeneous streambed settings and to
link results to sediment characteristics.
- To compare sbPVP results to methods based on Darcy’s Law and streambed temperature.
- To quantify the contaminant mass discharge using sbPVP measurements in combination with
streambed contaminant concentrations at a selected stream reach.
- To evaluate the estimated contaminant mass discharge by comparing sbPVP results with those
calculated from a model based on in-stream completely mixed concentrations.

Chapter 2
Groundwater surface water interactions
When investigating the transport and fate of contaminants from the subsurface to streams, it is
important to understand the processes that occur in the transmission zone where groundwater and
surface water interact, the hyporheic zone. The hyporheic ﬂow system can be very complex, which is
caused by, for instance, heterogeneous sediment materials and structures, as well as groundwater and
stream water ﬂow rates. This chapter introduces the processes related to groundwater surface water
interactions (GSI) in the hyporheic zone, as well as the inﬂuence of sediment heterogeneity and material
on contaminant fate and transport. The chapter ends with an introduction to the contaminant mass
discharge and an overview of methods to be used to quantify velocities at the groundwater surface
water interface.
2.1
Water exchange across the hyporheic zone
Various deﬁnitions of the hyporheic zone can be found in the literature. It is commonly referred to
as an ecologically important transmission zone between the larger scale processes in the groundwater
and the small scale processes in the stream (Bencala, 2000; Harvey, J.W.; Bencala, 1993; Hutchinson
and Webster, 1998; Kalbus et al., 2006; Nützmann and Moser, 2016; Sophocleous, 2002). The zone
where groundwater inﬂow and surface water exchange ﬂows are mixing is characterized by a variety
of processes that can result in transport, degradation, transformation, precipitation, or sorption of
compounds, which will further be described in Section 2.4 (Conant et al., 2004; Kalbus et al., 2006;
Nützmann and Moser, 2016). The hyporheic ﬂow or hyporheic exchange is referred to as the down-
and subsequent upwelling of stream water into the shallow sediments (Findlay, 1995; Schmidt et al.,
2006).
The groundwater discharging into the hyporheic zone strongly inﬂuences the exchange ﬂow system,
i.e. inﬂuences the extent of the hyporheic zone, and thus the hyporheic ecology and biogeochemistry
(Cardenas and Wilson, 2007; Fox et al., 2014; Schmidt et al., 2006; Storey et al., 2003). The impact
of groundwater on the hyporheic exchange has been modelled and was part of a laboratory study by
Fox et al. (2014), which found that hyporheic ﬂows under gaining or losing ﬂow conditions have a
similar extent. Increasing the gaining or losing water ﬂux decreased the hyporheic ﬂow extent, i.e. if
groundwater ﬂuxes are low, the hyporheic exchange is dominant (Fox et al., 2014), whereby a high
groundwater discharge might cause the hyporheic ﬂow to disappear (Bayani Cardenas and Wilson,

==PAGE:16==
2006).
It is important to consider the interaction between hyporheic and groundwater when measuring
the groundwater discharge to streams, as the results may not only represent the groundwater ﬂux,
but a mixture of both waters (Cardenas et al., 2004; Kalbus et al., 2006). Investigations showed that
the overall inﬂow is nearly twice the actual groundwater discharge (Harvey, J.W.; Bencala, 1993).
High variability and spatial heterogeneity of ﬂuxes in the shallow streambed is commonly encoun-
tered during ﬁeld investigations (Chen et al., 2009; Conant et al., 2004). For instance, Chen et al.
(2009) found that the magnitude of ﬂux and even vertical ﬂow direction varied greatly within "a short
distance".
2.2
Heterogeneity and the inﬂuence of scale
Interactions between groundwater and surface water can be very heterogeneous and complex systems,
where properties and characteristics change on a small temporal and spatial scale (Kalbus et al., 2006,
2007; Nützmann and Moser, 2016; Sophocleous, 2002). The term "heterogeneity" refers to a mixture
of "grain sizes or types, spatial diversity in sediment and bedforms, and/or rapidly changing sediment
characteristics" (Holland and Elmore, 2008, p. 1). Palmer and Poﬀ(1997) ﬁnd a shorter deﬁnitions
and describe heterogeneity as the "variability in a process or pattern over space and time" (Palmer
and Poﬀ, 1997, p. 1).
For instance, hydraulic exchange processes are mainly inﬂuenced by local hydraulic gradients,
which themselves depend on larger climatic and anthropogenic changes, seasonal variations in precip-
itation patterns, or even on single rain events (Kalbus et al., 2006; Nützmann and Moser, 2016).
On a catchment scale, heterogeneity can be expressed by the aquifer type and stratiﬁcation of the
aquifer (Nützmann and Moser, 2016). Spatial heterogeneities on a reach scale are mainly caused by
local pressure variations due to geomorphological features in the streambed, i.e. pool-riﬄe sequences,
discontinuities in slope, obstacles in the streambed, and/or vegetation (Hutchinson and Webster, 1998;
Nützmann and Moser, 2016).

Various investigations focused on streambed shapes and morphological induced hyporheic ﬂow,
including meanders, tributaries, pool and riﬄe sequences, stream splits, etc. (Schmidt et al., 2006).

fast and in alternating directions, forming vertical circulation cells ranging in pathway lengths from
a few centimeter to a few meter, which may reach deep into the streambed and aquifer (Nützmann
and Lewandowski, 2009; Nützmann and Moser, 2016; Thibodeaux and Boyle, 1987). Stonedahl et al.
(2010) concluded that all scales of topography from riﬄes to meanders have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence
on the pore water ﬂow and residence time of stream water in the hyporheic zone. They found that
complex interactions, between diﬀerent features control the net interfacial ﬂux and spatial distribution
of hyporheic exchange. For instance, an increased wavelength of stream features like pools and riﬄes
can lead to longer and deeper pathways of hyporheic ﬂows (Schmidt et al., 2006). Tonina et al. (2016)
concluded that heterogeneity has a net eﬀect of compression on the hyporheic zone, which changes
the volume of the zone, the ﬂow ﬁeld, and residence time distribution. Heterogeneity increases the
tortuosity of the ﬂow line and leads to a longer residence time of stream water in the streambed. This
tends to keep ﬂow lines closer to the streambed, which leads to more shallow hyporheic zones (Tonina
et al., 2016). An overall reduction of hyporheic exchange is thus achieved. Heterogeneity can also
switch certain upwelling to downwelling zones (Tonina et al., 2016).
2.3
Heterogeneity due to sediment material
On a streambed scale, the composition and heterogeneity of the sediment material can also highly
inﬂuence the ﬂow in the hyporheic zone. One of the main factors of hyporheic exchange is the hydraulic
conductivity (Kv) of the streambed material (Storey et al., 2003). The hydraulic conductivity of a
sediment depends on the properties of the sediment, as well as the viscosity of the water, and controls
the permeability of the sediment, which determines the ease and velocity of water ﬂowing though the
porous media (Brassington, 2007). The hydraulic conductivity of the sediment can for instance be
determined by grain size analysis. However, this only considers unconsolidated material and destroys
the packing of the sediment structure. This can be avoided by permeameter or slug tests. Hydraulic
conductivities for massive clay to clean gravel range from hydraulic conductivities of 10E-9 m s−1
to 1 m s−1, respectively (Brassington, 2007). Generally, low hydraulic conductivities lead to a high
residence time of water in the sediment, and lower ﬂow velocities. In contrast, high Kv-values are
usually associated with low residence times and higher ﬂow velocities (Brassington, 2007). In the
ﬁeld, hydraulic conductivities are also inﬂuenced by the amount of organic material in the sediment,
indicating that lower ﬂows are detected in highly organic materials (Conant et al., 2004).
The porosity of the sediment indicates how much void space or, in saturated systems, water the
sediment contains. Generally, a higher porosity of the sediment is related to larger permeability, and
hence, higher water ﬂow rates. Porosity of unconsolidated material varies with sorting, packing and
shape of the grains (Brassington, 2007).
Concerning heterogeneity in streambeds, it has been shown that heterogeneous deposits aﬀect the
magnitude of inﬂow and outﬂow into the hyporheic zone (Cardenas et al., 2004). The diﬀerences and
alterations of ﬂow and contaminant transport through heterogeneous sediments can be explained by
dispersion, which is inﬂuenced by diﬀerent grain and pore sizes and shapes, grain size distribution and
packing, which leads to distinct ﬂow velocity proﬁles. Dispersion and/or dispersivity is strongly scale
dependent, which means that longitudinal dispersion can be relatively small in lab scale experiments

with only a few millimeter and up to 1 cm, but up to hundreds of meters in the ﬁeld (Matthes, 1990;
Nützmann and Moser, 2016).
A special case was investigated by Zhou et al. (2014) on an open
framework gravel that can occur in gravelly streambeds. They conclude, that open framework gravel,
can create preferential ﬂow pathways with a permeability orders of magnitude greater than in sandy
sediments, and much greater than caused by meanders, dunes, or riﬄes sequences (Zhou et al., 2014).
2.4
Transport and fate of chlorinated ethenes in the streambed
Conant et al. (2004) found that contaminant concentration distribution in the streambed can be very
complex on a small scale, both vertically and horizontally. They found contamination concentrations
with factors ranging from 100 to 10,000 over a lateral distance of a few meters.
Cremeans and
Devlin (2017a) also describe the discharge of chlorinated ethenes as spatially localized. The hyporheic
zone, or near-river zone, may strongly modify the distribution, concentration, and composition of
a contaminant plume consisting of chlorinated solvents prior to discharging into the surface water.
Conant et al. (2004) concluded that, among others, the streambed composition and biodegradation
processes in the streambed are a major cause for the heterogeneous contaminant distribution, which
makes the characterization and monitoring of a plume discharging to a stream challenging (Conant
et al., 2004).
Contaminants in the hyporheic zone undergo several fate and transport processes. First, the mixing
of contaminated groundwater and surface water leads to dilution (Freitas et al., 2015). Spatial vertical
variation of sediment concentrations associated with the dilution process in the hyporheic zone raises
the question of at which depth concentration measurements are reasonable to obtain a representative
sample (Conant et al., 2004).
Second, mainly anaerobic dechlorination leads to the biodegradation of chlorinated solvents (Co-
nant et al., 2004; Weatherill et al., 2014). Conant et al. (2004) found that most of the biodegradation
was taking place in the top 2.5 m of the streambed, and more speciﬁcally, Weatherill et al. (2014)
showed that dechlorination might be taking place mostly in the upper 20 - 40 cm of the sediment. This
shows high degradation potential of the subsurface zone. However, the anaerobic groundwater mixes
with aerobic surface water in the hyporheic zone, which inﬂuences the reductive dechlorination of
chlorinated compounds (Freitas et al., 2015). However, aerobic degradation pathways are also possi-
ble, especially VC can undergo alternative oxidation pathways, particularly at very low concentrations
(Freitas et al., 2015).
Combining solute transport and groundwater discharge, it can be seen that contaminant ﬂowlines
do not always follow the groundwater discharge (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a; Weatherill et al., 2014).
Explanation attempts vary. Cremeans and Devlin (2017a) propose that chemical/microbial processes
may be the reason for this divergance. Longer residence times for low ﬂow areas could cause the
redox conditions that are necessary to maintain dechlorination. Notably, the highest concentrations
of chlorinated solvents were not found in the preferential pathways, but in low hydraulic conductivity
semi-conﬁning deposits (Conant et al., 2004; Kalbus et al., 2008).
Thus, high concentrations in
streambed water did not correspond to areas with high discharge, but more to areas of low discharge.
This is likely due to sorption of contamination to the streambed material (Conant et al., 2004; Kalbus
6
==PAGE:19==
et al., 2008). Especially organic-rich streambed material lead to increased sorption of chlorinated
solvents and hence, cause retarded transportation and increased retention time in the streambed
(Conant et al., 2004; Freitas et al., 2015). Slow desorption of contamination might cause the streambed
to be a longterm contamination source even if the primary source might have been remediated (Kalbus
et al., 2008).
All these factors show that the streambed’s sediment properties, water exchange pathways and
resulting contaminant distribution can be very complex. Due to the high heterogeneity, there seems
to be much uncertainty regarding the prediction of contaminant distribution, movement and fate in the
streambed and hyporheic zone (Weatherill et al., 2014). Hence, it is very important that investigations
are performed in detail (Conant et al., 2004) and consider the spatial distribution and magnitude of
groundwater discharge to a stream. As ﬂow patterns can vary on a small scale, it will require a high
density monitoring network to not miss signiﬁcant areas (Conant et al., 2004; Schmidt et al., 2006).
There is thus a need for an inexpensive, quantitative method to characterize the spatial heterogeneity
of the GSI (Schmidt et al., 2006). As groundwater ﬂow magnitudes and directions can change on a
small scale, both Kalbus et al. (2006) and Schmidt et al. (2006) recommend a solution of multiple
measurements at multiple scales.
2.5
Contaminant mass discharge
Estimation of mass discharge from contaminated sites became more important over the last decades
and is considered useful for the assessment of natural attenuation of contaminant plumes (Basu et al.,
2006; Guilbeault et al., 2005), for giving advice on remediation strategies (Guilbeault et al., 2005;
Jawitz et al., 2005), for potential risk assessment on down-gradient receptors (Basu et al., 2006; Kalbus
et al., 2007), as well as for identiﬁcation of contaminant sources on a catchment scale (Troldborg
et al., 2008, 2010). There has been increasing agreement between authorities, that contaminant mass
discharge should be taken as an indicator of water quality rather than limit concentrations (Basu
et al., 2006).
CMD is deﬁned as the contaminated mass per unit time that travels through a cross-sectional area,
i.e. the control plane, located perpendicular to the groundwater ﬂow and downstream of the source
zone (Troldborg et al., 2010).
The mass discharge through the cross-sectional area can be calculated by Equation 2.1 (Cremeans
and Devlin, 2017a), where J is the mass discharge in kg year−1, C the contaminant concentration in
kg m−3, v the seepage velocity in m year−1, n is the porosity, and A is the cross-sectional discharge
area in m2.

Regarding contaminant mass discharge through the streambed assuming vertical water ﬂow, the
cross-section perpendicular to the water ﬂow is represented by the streambed. Usually when deter-

discharge is calculated for each of the sub-areas. All summed up result in the overall plume discharge
(Guilbeault et al., 2005).

2.6
Methods of measuring groundwater surface water interaction
The demand for methods to investigate ﬂuxes and contaminant mass discharge across the GSI lead
to the development of various ﬁeld methods, which are summarized in Table 2.1 and are extensively
reviewed by for example Cremeans and Devlin (2017a) and Kalbus et al. (2006). Therefore, only a brief
description is given in the following sections. Descriptions of the methods used as part of this study
are presented in Chapter 4. Table 2.1 contains suggestions for review references and cross-references
to the method description in Chapter 4.
The methods used to measure water ﬂow across the groundwater surface water interface can be
categorized into two large groups: indirect and direct methods. Indirect methods are dependent on
external parameters, which are subsequently converted to ﬂow rates (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a).
For instance, methods based on Darcy’s law depend on hydraulic head, porosity, and hydraulic conduc-
tivity estimations. Heat tracer methods use one dimensional analytical solutions to convert streambed
temperatures into quantitative ﬂuxes, which are subject to limitations associated with the assump-
tions related to the solutions (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017b; Irvine et al., 2016). However, it is an
inexpensive, simple method, which makes high density measurements possible and provides insight
into the spatial heterogeneity of a stream reach (Schmidt et al., 2006).
To overcome some of the limitations of indirect measurement methods and to ﬁnd a solution for
reliable, inexpensive and time-eﬃcient measurements, the point velocity probe (PVP) was recently
designed to measure groundwater ﬂow on a cm-scale in aquifers (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017b). An
adaptation of the PVPs lead to the development of the streambed point velocity probe (sbPVP) to
enable the measurement of water ﬂuxes at the GSI (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017b).

Chapter 3
Description of study sites
Two contaminated sites posing a risk to stream water are chosen for ﬁeld work and assessment as part
of this thesis. Both sites are currently investigated as part of the testing of a new screening tool for
the Danish EPA, which is tested at 30 sites contaminated with chlorinated compounds that might
pose a risk to nearby surface waters in Denmark (Orbicon, 2016).
This chapter introduces the sites Skjern and Raadvad, and presents brieﬂy their historical back-
ground, abundant contamination, geological and hydrogeological characteristics, as well as already
performed and ongoing investigations at the sites.
3.1
Skjern
The contaminated site is located at a former dry cleaning facility and disposal site in Bredgade 1 - 9
and Klostervej 1 -3 , which operated from 1939 - ca. 1970. Furthermore, a car workshop was located at
the site from 1945 to 1962 (Figure 3.1).
The groundwater of the secondary aquifer and soil pore water is contaminated by chlorinated
solvents, BTEXs and PAHs. The plume moves with the groundwater in a southwestern direction
and discharges into the stream, Kirke Å, approximately 60 m south of the hotspot area (Lemaire,
2016). The plume’s width entering the stream is approximately 100 m. Additionally, a drain close to
borehole B202 contributes to the contamination of the stream water (Orbicon, 2016). Stream water
and streambed water shows contamination by chlorinated solvents.
3.1.1
Geology and hydrogeology
The geology of the site consists of 1 m peat and 40 m sand with occasional peat patches. Hydropotential
maps show groundwater ﬂow in the unconﬁned primary aquifer in the southern direction towards Kirke
Å (NIRAS, 2012).
Kirke Å is characterized as a medium sized stream with a width of 4 m and a depth of 1 m
(Lemaire, 2016). According to Orbicon (2016), mean water ﬂow is Q = 0.37 m3 s−1 in October 2015.
The groundwater temperature was found to be ca. 12.2 ◦C at 4 m depth in borehole B202 (Orbicon,
2016).

3.2
Raadvad
The contaminated site Rådvad is a more than 200 years old industrial site that is located on the
Mølleåen river system on Zealand, Denmark. Contamination at the site originates from an old metal
works factory, mainly producing knives, whose facilities were running until the 1970s (NIRAS, 2012,
2017). The contamination mainly consists of chlorinated solvents, oil products including BTEXs, and
heavy metals (Orbicon, 2016; Bigi, 2017). The area aﬀected by the contamination covers an area of
300 m2, which is the area north of the main channel, with an average chlorinated solvent concentration
of 2500 µg L−1 in shallow boreholes moving towards Mølleåen (Orbicon, 2016).
The history of the site goes back to when a mill was established at the site around 1700. The knife
factory was in operation from 1879 to approximately 1972. During that time there was additionally
a bronze foundry and a metal workshop located at the site and operating for an unknown period.
Moreover, the site hosted a smithy and a grindery amongst other small businesses.
Nowadays it
is home to several professions, including a bronze workshop, a smithy, oﬃces, and a nature school.
Historical investigations revealed locations of two former oil tanks north of the boiler house with
12,000 L and 16,000 L storage capacity, which are probably the main cause of hotspot A in Figure 3.2.

3.2.1
Geology and hydrogeology
The geology of the area is a moraine landscape formed within the last ice age. The river Mølleåen is
running in a former meltwater valley, which is now forming the Mølleåen river system.
The area is located approximately 5 to 6 m above sea level. The geology of the site consists of
diﬀerent layers of glacial deposits including layers of gyttja, peat, sand, and gravel located above
Danian limestone. The aquifer system consists of a conﬁned primary (lower) limestone aquifer and a
secondary (upper) aquifer that mainly consists of sand, gravel and organic material. The primary and
secondary aquifer are separated by a compact gyttja layer with low hydraulic conductivity (NIRAS,
2012). Borings reveal sand and gravel below the compact gyttja layer until the end of the core sample
at approximately 30 m depth (NIRAS, 2012).
Hydraulic potential maps show groundwater ﬂow in the eastern direction towards the Øresund in
the primary aquifer (NIRAS, 2012). Due to a higher hydraulic head gradient in the primary aquifer,
groundwater ﬂow is assumed to occur from the primary to the secondary aquifer through the low
permeable gyttja layer. It is not known to what extent the two aquifers or the secondary aquifer and
Raadvad Dam are in hydraulic contact (NIRAS, 2012). Shallow groundwater was found in the soil

Instrumentation and methods
4.2.1
sbPVP
The streambed point velocity probe (sbPVP) is a further development of the point velocity probe
(PVP) ﬁrst described by (Labaky et al., 2007). The sbPVP is developed to measure the vertical velocity
and direction of water in streambeds with GSI. It is an in-situ direct push method injecting a small
tracer into the environment (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a). The design of the sbPVP allows measuring
the diﬀerences in electrical resistivity between a tracer and the surrounding water. Figure 4.3 a shows a
schematic view of the sbPVP. Injection of a tracer into the injection line leads to a discharge of tracer
at the injection port on the probe surface. The discharged tracer is transported up or downwards
by the ambient water ﬂow. Two detector plates, either above (HB1 and HB2) or below (HB3 and
HB4) the injection port, detect electronic resistivities. To get a good detection contrast in fresh water
streams, a saline tracer is used (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a). Generally, tests were performed with
a 1 g L−1 tracer solution and an injection volume of 0.1 to 0.25 mL. Tests lasted from approximately
10 minutes to 3.5 hours.
To only measure the vertical component of the ﬂow, as well as to prevent the tracer from being
ﬂushed away by horizontal hyporheic ﬂow, a "hyporheic shield" is placed around the probe (Fig-
ure 4.3b). For the purpose of installing the probe in heterogeneous streambeds, a new shield was
manufactured and equipped with 1 cm large teeth.
As a result of modelling and laboratory testing, an optimal injection depth of 7 cm to 11 cm was

VelProbePE
The spreadsheet program VelProbePE (velocity probe parameter estimation) is speciﬁcally developed
to process and analyze tracer breakthrough curves detected by PVPs (Schillig, 2012) or sbPVPs
(Cremeans and Devlin, 2017a). VelProbePE is programmed using Visual Basic for Applications (VBA)
within Microsoft Excel and can process up to 16 detector signals at a time (Schillig, 2012). VelProbePE
uses a nonlinear optimizer program to ﬁt observed data to an adapted transport equation, i.e. the 1D
advection-dispersion equation (ADE). Best ﬁt between measured and calculated data is reached by
minimizing the residual sum of squares (RSS) (Schillig, 2012).
VelProbePE oﬀers several methods for the breakthrough curve ﬁtting. Diﬀerent weighting options
20
==PAGE:33==
include the bisquare method, which forces anomalies in the curves, i.e.
data with high RSS, e.g.
injection spikes, to get less emphasis during the curve ﬁtting process (Schillig, 2012).
A newly introduced option uses the method of moments, which can either be used for making
initial guesses for parameter input into VelProbePE and subsequent optimization, or it can be used
purely mathematically based on the tracer’s mass. The method of moments can be used especially
when breakthrough curves are not gaussian-shaped, which makes ﬁtting to the ADE challenging. The
method of moments calculates the mass of tracer and sets the peak of the breakthrough curve where
half of the mass is passed by the detector. The calculations for the method of moments are based on
a paper by Freyberg (1986) (Cremeans and Devlin, 2017b).
In this thesis, all injection data is analysed by the bisquare weighting method, the "method of
moments only", and the method of moments for optimization.
The ﬁnal selection of one of the
methods was made based on the following considerations:
If breakthrough curves were clearly gaussian-shaped, the method of moments for optimization
was chosen as a ﬁrst analysis attempt. The method estimated key parameter input values for the
modelled breakthrough curve, which can then be optimized by altering the dispersivity and injection
pulse in an interative way until the best ﬁt is achieved, paying special attention to the peak. If the
dispersivity was unreasonably high (above 0.27) and the bisquare weighting method showed good ﬁt
with dispersivity values below the critical values, the latter method was chosen. The bisquare method
was furthermore chosen, when injection experients were not completed, i.e. leading to an incomplete
BTC. The "method of moments only" was chosen if either method gave unsatisfactory dispersivities.
The "method of moments only" was also selected when a non-gaussian injection curve did not give the
opportunity to determine the breakthrough curves’ tracer peak or if several peaks were abundant due
to tracer splitting, which is common in heterogenous streambeds.

Hydraulic conductivity: Grain size analysis
Another way to estimate the hydraulic conductivity of the sediment is to analyze the grain size

Four out of ﬁve sediment cores taken at Skjern are chosen to determine the grain size distribution.
The analysis is done by sieving the samples using 10 sieve sizes ranging from 2 mm to 0.063 mm. The
ﬁne fraction below 0.063 mm was not determined as its amount was less than 2 % of the total sum.
Samples were subdivided into sub-samples according to visual delimination of sediment types in the
cores. The sample were weighed on a scale with a precision of two digits and dried at 60 ◦C for
72 hours. After drying, all sieves and the bottom pan were weighed once again. The samples were
sieved for 15 min in a sieving tower with a magnitude shaking of 1.5. The sieves and the bottom pan
including the mass of the sediment were weighed and a fraction according to the overall dry weight
was determined. The grain size distribution data was analysed using the Excel based spreadsheet
HydrogeoSieveXL developed by Devlin (2017).
Beneﬁts and limitations of the grain size distribution are listed in Table 4.3. Devlin (2017) aims

4.2.4
Water sampling and chemical analysis
After installing and clean pumping piezometers as described in Section 4.2.2, sampling was done using
a perestalic pump, if piezometer gave enough water, or by mean of a syringe, if water ﬂow was low. For

oxygen content, pH, and electric conductivity.
The surface water samples were collected manually in the stream. The sampling vials were rinsed

taken at approximately half of the water depth, which was measured additionally. After sampling,
vials were put on ice and stored at 4 ◦C prior to analysis. The analysis for chlorinated solvents was
carried out by the laboratory Euroﬁns following the ISO15680 P&T-GC-MS method (Lemaire, 2016).
4.2.5
OTT MF Flow meter
Flow water velocity was measured using the OTT MF ﬂow meter in the main channel in Raadvad. A
transect was chosen according to the following recommendations by OTT Hydromet (2015): the ﬂow
directions at each measurement point is parallel to the bank and perpendicular to the cross section,
the streambed is stable, free of large rocks, or weeds. The selection of the measurement location
was challenging due to rocks and macrophytes along the southern half of the stream width. Transect
SB was chosen for the measurement as it is located in the centre of the investigated area and has
comparably smaller amounts of macrophytes. A three point measurement with 3 points at 0.2, 0.6,
and 0.8 per vertical water depth was chosen. Measurements were done starting from the northern
bank at spatial intervals of 0.5 m, which lead to 13 intervals from a total stream width of 7.1 m.
4.2.6
DILVOL analytical model
The DILVOL (dilution and volatilization) model is a 2D steady state advection-dispersion model with
the aim to assess and predict the dilution and volatilization of a continuous contaminant plume in
streams introduced by contaminated groundwater inﬂow (Aisopou et al., 2015; Lemaire, 2016). The
model aims to be used as a screening tool for streams and has recently been implemented in the risk
assessment tool of the Danish EPA as a response to the European Water framework directive (Aisopou
et al., 2015). The model can describe the main trends and processes in the dilution of a contaminant
plume, including peak concentrations and mixing lengths in a stream (Aisopou et al., 2015).
Changes in the model described by Lemaire (2016) include the consideration of background con-
centrations, assuming that the stream was fully mixed at the point of plume discharge into the stream
(Lemaire, 2016). Moreover, it is possible to include the dilution of a clean water input in the form
of a tributary. A Microsoft Oﬃce Excel input ﬁle makes it possible to combine all input information
to be loaded into a MATLAB code. Further refurbishment of the code includes the addition of one
additional point source to the diﬀuse source, which allows the entry of several sources at a single
contaminated site (Lemaire, 2016).
For the application of the model in this study, changes lead to the input of up till ﬁve additional
point sources. Furthermore, it is now possible to enter either a mass discharge, J in µg s−1, as an
28
==PAGE:41==
input value for the main contaminant source, or to make a separate input of the key mass discharge
factors, i.e. the area, A, velocity, v, and concentration, C, required to determine J.

5.1.2
Testing diﬀerent injection tracers
To run a successful injection experiment, the tracer needs to show a suﬃcient contrast to the sur-
rounding environment, i.e. the stream water or streambed water. Moreover, the injected volume must
be large enough (danger of not being able to track the response of the injection), but not too large
in order to perform experiments in reasonable time frames. Every environment is unique, hence, the
tracer concentration and amount has to be evaluated for each site.
The aim of this section is to present results from injections with diﬀerent tracers and volumes,
and to observe their inﬂuence on the breakthrough curves. Moreover, the objective is to ﬁnd the best
tracer concentration and volume for Raadvad.
Injection experiments were performed at location PN101 in the contaminated area.
First, in-
jections with the same injection volume V = 0.25 mL and diﬀerent tracer concentrations were per-
formed. The tracer concentrations used were 1 gNaCl L−1, 2 gNaCl L−1, and deionized water. Velocities
with 2 gNaCl L−1 and 1 gNaCl L−1 indicate downwelling water ﬂow with velocities between 236 and
240 cm d−1. The breakthrough curve’s magnitude is inﬂuenced by the diﬀerent tracer concentrations.
Figure 5.2a shows the highest magnitude for 2 gNaCl L−1, with a peak reaching up to 0.016 mV. The
tracer 1 gNaCl L−1 peaked at 0.008 mV. Using MilliQ showed a slightly larger response on halfbridges
1 and 2, indicating an upward ﬂow. However, data from these injections, however, show a very low
contrast in electrical conductivity and are therefore not reliable for use at the site (see Appendix C.2).

5.2.2
Application of the sbPVP in heterogeneous streambeds
This section aims to summarize the ﬁndings on sbPVP injections conducted during this thesis on
several streambed substrates. This is done by ﬁrst presenting diﬀerent characteristic breakthrough
curve forms and, second, by presenting advantages and disadvantages of the sbPVP with special focus
on its application in more rocky streambeds.
Very nice gaussian-shaped breakthrough curves can be obtained in sandy sediments or homoge-
neously distributed sediments. Figure 5.5a shows an injection performed in June 2017 at location
T1RB3.
Similar curves were obtained during several tests in both Skjern and Raadvad.
Even if
outside environmental inﬂuence and/or heterogeneous sediment structure aﬀected the breakthrough
curves and did not results in gaussian-shaped ﬂow, they could still be analysed by means of the method
of moments in VelProbePE and, hence give estimations of velocities. One example of this type of curve
is shown in Figure 5.5d, which shows showing a combination of a fast injection and a shifting baseline.
A very common curve form in heterogeneous systems is tracer splitting, which can for example
happen when the tracer has to ﬁnd its ways around obstacles. This can often be seen in two or more
peaks as shown in Figure 5.5b. A very slow ﬂow is shown by a pronounced response on either of the
vertical detectors and stable or slowly decreasing electric conductivity, Figure 5.5c.
From the results obtained in this thesis, several beneﬁts and challenges of the sbPVP application,
especially in heterogeneous rocky streambeds, are identiﬁed and summarized in Table 5.10. Some are
highlighted in the following section.
Tracer injections using the sbPVP measures the vertical water ﬂow in a direct manner, that is
independent from temperature, geology of the aquifer, hydraulic conductivities of the sediments, etc.
One single injection is suﬃcient to obtain the full information about the ﬂow, which can easily be
analysed using the software VelProbePE. The set-up and principle behind the probe’s functionality is
straightfoward, which allows relatively easy repair and maintenance works. The rather short injection
tests (a range of 10 minutes to 3 hours experienced in this project) allow multiple injections during a
ﬁeld day. It was found in Section 5.2.1, that the tool can be applied in various streambed types and
that it can provide valuable information on streambed structure, ﬂow patterns, and ﬂow complexity.
Streambeds in Raadvad and Skjern cause several practical challenges, such as the installation
of the tool in the streambed.

analytical solution on various parameters. For instance, the thermal conductivity of the sediment

(2011) used an equlibration time of 10 minutes. Hence, overestimation of streambed temperatures
might contribute to the observed diﬀerence.

to compare. Field methods deal with the dynamic systems occurring in the environment, showing

and are highly dependent on the method chosen. The goal is to reduce uncertainties as much as
possible, as absolute velocities are required as an input for contaminant mass discharge calculations

sites (Basu et al., 2006). Hence, it is important to perform experiments and analyses as accurate as
possible to rule out as many uncertainties as possible. It can be said that results from sbPVP show
measurements with the least uncertainty and are therefore considered to deliver the best estimate for

DILVOL model application
The DILVOL model is used to identify sources of contamination within the stream reach and to
quantify the contaminant mass discharge from the streambed and from the additional point sources
identiﬁed along the bank. This was done ﬁrst by inserting a probable contaminant mass discharge
value, in an iterative way, for the streambed and additional point sources in order to get the best ﬁt
between modelled and measured stream water concentrations along transects SB and SC. Next, the
results from the ﬁeld experiments and the best ﬁt for discharge from the streambed were fed into the
model to ﬁnd a probable mass discharge area. Lastly, an attempt was made to model an additional
point source upstream of transect SA.

Contaminant mass discharge determination

that mainly VC is discharging from the streambed, and cDCE from the Crack in the wall as a ﬁrst
additional point source followed by Drain 1/Recess as a second additional point source.
The ﬁrst step implied ﬁtting the observed concentrations in transect SB, by assigning mass dis-

5.6
Discussion of main ﬁndings
Using the results from ﬁeld investigations (Section 5.4) and modelling (Section 5.5), two new conceptual
models are developed, which aim to present the complex contamination at the site, including sources,
mass discharge, and pathways to the stream.
Figure 5.24a shows the ﬁve contamination sources
that could be identiﬁed, i.e. the unknown upstream source, Crack, Recess/Drain1, Drain 2, and the
streambed. Diﬀerent colors are used for the presentation of the highest contaminant fraction.
The main component discharged from the bank is cDCE. Interestingly, Drain 2 is an exception, as
it seems to form a preferential pathway for parent compounds of chlorinated ethenes to the stream.
The lack of degradation products in Drain 2 indicates no or little degradation of PCE or TCE in the
subsurface, due either to a low retention time of PCE and TCE using preferential pathways, or due
to unsuitable conditions for degradation.
Concentrations and application of the DILVOL model have shown, that most of the contamination
is entering the stream from the stream bank and especially downstream of transect SB. Drain 1 is used
as a point source for the model application. However, concentrations from June 2017 show that the
Recess and Drain 1 show similar absolute concentrations and molar ratios. Hence, it is not proven that
Drain 1 is the only additional discharge location, but mass discharge from close to Drain 1 is assured
by sample analysis and the model application. Results show that contamination is also entering from
the Crack or from close to the Crack. However, mass discharge is many times lower than for Drain 1.
The main component discharging from the streambed is VC, which, according to mass discharge
determination, originates mainly from a small area at location PN106. Moreover, rather higher con-
centrations are found in locations PN101, PN103, and PN105, which present a corridor-like shaped
area consisting of rather permeable sediment composed of sandy, gravelly material, that is high in
organic matter. This corridor seems to be curtailed by a more impermeable streambed close to the
wall in the northern and southern half of the stream. Especially the southern half of the stream,
which is covered by large rocks, seems to present a concrete-like layer in approximately 25 cm depth
below the streambed. The partly sealed area seems to force the contamination to discharge from the
corridor stretch.
A cross-sectional perspective is presented in Figure 5.24b, including an illustration of the distribu-
tion, migration and pathways of the contaminant plume entering from the subsurface to the stream.
The plume distribution delivers an explanation for cDCE discharge from the bank, as contamination
is discharged through openings in the concrete wall before continuation in degradation.
The heterogeneity of the streambed in the form of rocks, cracks in impermeable layers, or waste
material might cause longer pathways (Tonina et al., 2016) of discharging groundwater, leading to
longer residence times in the subsurface and sediments. A longer residence time in the streambed can
maintain the anaerobic conditions needed for dechlorination of chlorinated solvents (Cremeans and
Devlin, 2017b; Weatherill et al., 2014) and hence allows degradation to VC. Moreover, the hyporheic
ﬂow at Raadvad seems to be relatively small due to absence of larger morphological features and an
overall slow stream water ﬂow. The shallow hyporheic exchange ﬂow seems to maintain anaerobic
conditions in the streambed, so that preferable redox conditions for dechlorination are apparent at
40 cm depth (Freitas et al., 2015; Weatherill et al., 2014).

due to the high variability and complex ﬂow in shallow streambeds, induced by hyporheic exchange
from down- and upwelling stream water. However, it is more likely the composition of the streambed
initiates the complex concentration distribution. For instance, Conant et al. (2004); Kalbus et al.
(2006); Freitas et al. (2015) all found that the highest concentrations were detected in low hydraulic
conductivity areas and semi-conﬁning deposits. These low conductivity areas are due to the large
amount of organic matter in the sediment (Brassington, 2007; Conant et al., 2004). The presence of
organic matter enables sorption of chlorinated solvents in the sediments (Conant et al., 2004; Kalbus
et al., 2006).
In the case of Raadvad, the secondary aquifer consists mainly of a mixture of sand, gravel and
organic material in the form of gyttja and peat. Hence, altered and retarded contaminant concentra-
tions may cause the very local contamination patches and highly concentrated mass discharge locations
determined in this study.
Likewise, the DILVOL model results suggest a small discharge area for VC of 0.05 -0.1 m2, which
comforms with the sediment characterizations and localized streambed concentrations. However, this
calculation is based on a concentration measurement from samples taken in 40 cm depth. The deter-
mined area assumes that the contamination moves along with the discharging water not considering
the sorption of VC to the organic material in the streambed. Hence, the mass actually discharging
into the stream might be overestimated. Moreover, it is uncertain whether the contaminant mass is
discharged vertically above the source detected in the ground or enters the stream at another loca-
tion, for example inﬂuenced by horizontal hyporheic ﬂows or due to diﬀerential pathways within the
sediment.
In conclusion, the site Raadvad reveals a complex system of contamination pathways, that leads
to a variety of discharge sources to the stream. Most of the contamination is detected and modelled to
discharge from Drain 1, which comprises mainly the degradation product cDCE. High concentrations
of VC could be detected in the streambed, which seem to be very localized spots with a small discharge
area. However, discharge from the streambed is low, which is mainly due to the slow water discharge.
There is evidence of temporal and spatial dependency regarding contaminant concentrations in the
streambed and stream water, mass discharge from the bank or streambed, stream water velocities and
discharge, and even vertical water ﬂow directions. All factors form a complex and unique discharge
system.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The study investigated the application of the streambed point velocity probe (sbPVP) to determine
contaminant mass discharge at the groundwater-surface water interface at contaminated sites posing
a risk to streams.
The study results showed that the sbPVP has high potential to quantify contaminant mass dis-
charge from streambeds. The sbPVP could quantify streambed ﬂow velocities in all of the investigated
stream reaches and could provide deeper understanding of the complexity of ﬂows in the hyporheic
zone of particularly heterogeneous streambeds.
A comparison of sbPVP experiments with methods based on Darcy’s law and multi-depth tem-
perature measurements showed the least uncertainties and greatest independence concerning sbPVP
measurements.
At the investigated reach, it was possible to determine very localized high concentrations and
mass discharge of chlorinated solvents from the streambed, which is mainly due to a heterogeneous
concentration distribution rather than diﬀerences in velocities. The concentration distribution can
be explained by a complex sediment structure and material composition and is subject to temporal
changes.
However, the contribution of mass discharge from the streambed to the overall contamination in
the stream is low, which mainly originates from point sources along the stream bank.
Altogether, sbPVPs have great potential to investigate the complex behavior of water ﬂuxes and
contaminant discharge at the groundwater-surface water interface.

Chapter 7
Future outlook
sbPVP application and heterogeneity
To improve applicability of the sbPVP on heterogeneous streambeds, more studies are necessary to
gain experience on injection and data analysis of various streambed types. In addition to ﬁeld studies,
laboratory experiments with various streambed materials and degrees of heterogeneity might be useful
to improve the understanding of injection behavior and support the interpretation of the results. Since
skrewing or hammering was mostly necessary to install the sbPVP in the streambed, it would be useful
to further investigate the eﬀects of this disturbance on the streambed in the results. Moreover, a more
robust design of the sbPVP would make the handling with heterogeneous streambeds easier and
prevent breakage.
Comparison of ﬁeld methods for GSI
Concerning the permeameter test, it would be interesting to investigate the applicability of the for-
mula used for interpretation of various sediment columns shorter than the installation depth of the
piezometers.
With respect to the analytical solution for the temperature proﬁle interpretation, stream water
temperatures are handled diﬀerently throughout the literature. It seems necessary to reach a consensus
on how to include the stream water temperature in the equation. Maybe a comparison study could
be performed. Moreover, investigations could be made on the equilibration time of the multi-point
thermocouple probe to deﬁne a minimum adaptation time.
Contamination in Raadvad
Regarding investigations in Raadvad, it would be interesting to locate the upstream source of contami-
nation to the main channel. Moreover, identiﬁcation of the source of the relatively high concentrations
of parent compounds at Drain 2 could improve the conceptual understanding of the site. Furthermore,
additional investigations of the streambed material concerning organic matter fraction and a high den-
sity screening for contamination of the contaminated zone, especially around PN106 and PN101, could
improve the understanding of contaminant distribution and processes at the site.
