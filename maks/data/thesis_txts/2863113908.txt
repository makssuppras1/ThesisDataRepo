1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Underwater robots are increasingly equipped with advanced observation technology to
explore and monitor aquatic environments.
These robots possess different degrees of
autonomy and are often referred to as Autonomous Underwater Vehicle (AUV) or Re-
motely Operated Vehicle (ROV). They come with various sensors including high-resolution
optical cameras such as monocular cameras or laser scanners, acoustic sensors such as
echo-sounders, side-scan sonars, or multi-beam sonars, and oceanographic sensors like
conductivity-temperature-depth sensors. These sensors enable robotic platforms to col-
lect detailed imagery, precise maps of underwater terrain, and the ability to monitor the
physical and chemical conditions of the aquatic environment. In addition, unmanned sys-
tems have improved the safety and efficiency of such operations. Human risk is minimized
by reducing the need for human presence in hazardous and inaccessible environments. Ex-
tended operations can be carried out without the need for complex manned submersibles
or large dive teams and their associated logistics. Hence, AUV and ROV have become an
increasingly useful tool for research and industrial applications.
For marine research, these technologies facilitate more extensive data collection from deep-
sea ecosystems to track biodiversity, assess climate change impacts, or conduct ocean
exploration. For the industry, underwater robots have become essential in their main-
tenance procedures to inspect platforms, pipelines, and other offshore infrastructure for
safety and efficiency. Furthermore, these underwater robots are used to monitor coral
reefs for conservation efforts, optimization of aquaculture production, or surveillance for
security purposes.
These sensors are often used in silos without the integration of information from other
types of sensors. Traditional optical sensors offer higher spatial resolution and color infor-
mation above water. However, underwater, it suffers from the effects of light scattering,
absorption, and color distortion in turbid waters and is susceptible to variation in illumi-
nation. In contrast, acoustic sensors primarily obtain depth information and are effective
for long-range detection. They work well in low-visibility scenarios, albeit with coarser
spatial resolution. Thus, multimodal sensor fusion of these two modes provides comple-
mentary sensor information that can be used to enhance the observation capability of the
system in an underwater environment.

1.2
Problem statement
1.2
Problem statement
Exploration and mapping of unknown underwater environments is critical for applica-
tions ranging from marine ecology to security surveillance and infrastructure maintenance.
However, the ability to observe and reconstruct these environments is limited by the in-
dividual sensor technologies. Optical sensors offer higher spatial and color resolution but
are impeded by the effects of turbid waters. Acoustic sensors perform well in long-range
scenarios and turbid waters, but lack the detailed resolution needed for mapping. Thus,
a fusion of sensor data collected from optical and acoustic sensors collects complementary
information that leverages the strength of both modalities.
Hence, opti-acoustic sen-
sor fusion systems can collect higher-quality data to visualize and reconstruct unknown
underwater environments.
1.3
Projection description
This project aims to develop, test and verify novel sensor fusion methods and tools for
the integration of optical and acoustic sensor technologies poised to map partly and fully
unknown marine underwater environments. Departing from the understanding of tech-
nologies for optical and acoustic sensing, the project will achieve the following objectives:
1. Review of the state-of-the-art in opti-acoustic sensor fusion with an application in
underwater mapping and three dimensional (3D) reconstruction
2. Design and execution of experimental campaigns aimed at generating relevant datasets
for the subsequent development and verification of algorithms
3. Development, implementation and testing of one or more methods for the 3D map-
ping of objects in marine environments, where the focus should be on state-of-the-art
methods like Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) and other machine learning methods.
4. Evaluation of the robustness of the proposed methods in relation to tuning parame-
ters of the adopted sensor technologies and operational or environmental conditions
5. Writing of a technical report documenting the development process and the achieved
results

State of the art
Sensor fusion is the integration of data from multiple sensors to achieve higher quality
information about the system or environment. It could lead to lower uncertainty, higher
accuracy, and a more comprehensive understanding of the state of the system. These
sensors can vary in modality and the entities that they are mounted on. A traditional
example is the use of a gyroscope, accelerometer, and magnetometer to estimate the
motion and orientation of a body.
In the field of underwater robotics, sensor fusion is used to enhance the capability of
observation technologies. Monocular cameras, depth cameras, and sonars are popular
choices for such tasks. Sonars take the form of single-beam or multi-beam echo-sounders
or imaging sonars. This report focuses on active multi-beam forward looking imaging
sonars.
Opti-acoustic sensor fusion is the integration of optical cameras (monocular images) and
acoustic sensors (sonars) to observe an environment. It has been used in various fields to
improve the quality of information given to the autonomous system.
2.1
Applications
In the field of localization and mapping, Raaj et al. used hybrid sonar and camera particle
filter-based calibration and fusion to localize 3D objects in 3D space on board of an AUV
[7]. William et al. fused sonar and vision information to identify and classify rich natural
features of coral reefs and the sea floor, which are subsequently used for Simultaneous
Localization and Mapping (SLAM) of the AUV [8]. The downward facing pencil beam
imaging sonar scans the sea floor to initialize new features, and the higher resolution
vision system is used to track them across frames. Forouher et al. merged depth camera
data and ultrasound measurements for obstacle detection and robot navigation to create
an occupancy grid map [9]. Each sensor creates its own grid map before merging into a
combined map. Its strength lies in its ability to detect transparent objects, such as glass.
Finally, Singh et al.
incorporate semantic slam to identify, classify, and map marine
objects without a priori knowledge of object classes to reduce trajectory error [10]. In
the mapping phase to obtain the 3D coordinates, the pixel values of objects detected by
an unsupervised segmentation model in the visual image are used to calculate the closest
azimuth beam. Then, the object range is extracted from the azimuth beam, albeit subject
to erroneous noisy range measurements.
In other areas, Boudhane and Benayad took an industrial approach in their sensor fusion
application to monitor and track fish species [11]. The two modes of sensors were used

3D reconstruction
Most relevant to the topic of this report, opti-acoustic sensor fusion has been used for
surface modeling and 3D object reconstruction.
2.2.1
Classical methods
Obtaining multiple views from optical cameras alone has been widely studied and well
developed as described in [13]. The concept is widely used in triangulation, photogram-
metry, and Structure From Motion (SfM) [14, 15]. SfM estimates a sparse point cloud
from visual features in a collection of photos during the camera calibration process. In
the underwater realm, feature-based stereo imagery was used to reconstruct and visualize
detailed 3D models of the seafloor [16]. While Roznere et al. developed a multiview pho-
tometric stereo framework to reconstruct high-quality and dense scenes from a dynamic
underwater robot by incorporating Oriented Fast and Rotated Brief (ORB) SLAM for
monocular systems [17].
For opti-acoustic systems, Babaee and Negahdaripour [18] used structural features such
as occluding contours and edges of objects in the optical and sonar image to construct
3D occluding rims.
These 3D rims are subsequently used for surface interpolation to
obtain the 3D object model. This method relies on the epipolar constraint and stereo
triangulation of optical and camera images, as derived earlier by Negahdaripour [19].
However, it requires a full 360° view of the object of interest or scene and the point-to-
point correspondences. Then, a bundle adjustment process specialized for opti-acoustic
systems is applied to register the images and estimate the 3D motion trajectory of the
opti-acoustic system [20]. This technique was explored in greater detail with a Python
simulation for a previous special course (see Fig. 1).

2.2.2
Novel view synthesis
Recently, deep learning approaches have been adopted in 3D scene reconstruction in
computer vision and graphics. Breakthrough modern techniques such as Neural Radiance
Field (NeRF) [21] and Gaussian splatting [1] have been developed in the field for novel
view synthesis.
3D scenes in NeRFs are implicitly defined by a large fully connected Multi-Layer Percep-
tron (MLP) neural network. The input to the network is a 3D point (x,y,z) in space and
the viewing angle (θ, ϕ), while the output is the volume density and the view-dependent
emitted radiance (color and transparency) at the position. These scene representation
parameters are optimized and used to reconstruct accurate view-dependent scenes from a
set of images with known camera poses. The success of NeRF led to a proliferation of its
variants that improved its speed and quality. This technique was adapted for opti-acoustic
sensor fusion purposes by incorporating sonar data in A Neural Rendering Framework for
Acoustic-Optical Sensor Fusion (AONeuS) where it demonstrated its strength in heavily
restricted baseline scenarios [22]. It is noted that 3D reconstruction was performed using
multiview imaging sonars in Neural Implicit Surface Reconstruction using Imaging Sonar
(NeuSIS), where they introduced a novel acoustic differentiable volumetric renderer was
introduced and its geometry was represented by a neural implicit function [23].
On the other hand, 3D scenes in Gaussian splatting are explicitly represented by anisotropic
3D Gaussians without neural components. The 3D Gaussians primitives are defined by
their 3D positions (x,y,z), colors (RGB), opacities (α), scale (Σ), and spherical harmonic

Technical Background
The following sections go through the relevant theories and concepts covered in 3DGS [1]
and Z-splat [2].
Gaussian splatting is a computer vision and graphics technique used to represent a 3D
scene with thousands of 3D anisotropic Gaussians. It is a learning approach that optimizes
the sizes, color, and positions of the Gaussians. It “overfits” the Gaussians to the training
images, and the trained Gaussian model is only used to represent that trained scene with
no generalisability to other scenes. These 3D Gaussians are then rendered into an image
on the image plane to generate different views of the scene.
3.1
Scene representation
The scene is represented by a volumetric radiance field. It is a continuous representation of
the 3D space and describes how light is emitted from each point. This is in contrast with
traditional discrete and non-differentiable representations like point clouds and meshes.
Each point in the volumetric radiance field has an associated density and opacity that
affect light scattering and attenuation.
In Gaussian splatting, anisotropic 3D Gaussians are used as primitives to represent ra-
diance fields without neural components. They are explicit, unstructured (not restricted
to a predefined grid), continuous, and hence differentiable. These 3D Gaussians are ren-
dered into a 2D image by projecting them into 2D splats and performing α-blending to
composite the effects of multiple Gaussians.
Firstly, the solution takes a set of calibrated cameras, with known intrinsics and extrinsics,
and images as input. The initial set of 3D Gaussians could be initialized randomly or
with the sparse point cloud produced from the Structure-from-Motion (SfM) process.
A 3D Gaussian is parameterized by its position (µ ∈R3), opacity (α ∈R), color (c ∈R)
and covariance (Σ ∈R3×3). A Gaussian defined in the world space centered at point µ is

3.6
Optimization
3.6
Optimization
Figure 6: Operation and gradient flow during optimization.
The optimization process involves projecting the 3D Gaussians onto the 2D image plane
and rasterizing them into image pixels to form a rendered image. This rendered image
is compared against the ground truth captured image to compute a loss. The gradients
of this loss are computed using backpropagation, and the model parameters are updated
using stochastic gradient descent. A custom CUDA kernel was developed by the 3DGS
authors and subsequently modified by Z-splat authors for fast differentiable rasterization
and optimization. During optimization, the number of Gaussians and its density per unit
volume are adaptively controlled by densification and culling. The strategies modify the
Gaussians in under-constructed and over-constructed regions of the scene.
Densification Strategy to increase the number of Gaussians by cloning or splitting.
Splitting Replace a large Gaussian with two smaller ones with their positions initialized
from the PDF of the original Gaussian.
Cloning Create a copy of a Gaussian with the same size but its position slightly offset
in the direction of the positional gradient.
Transparency culling Remove any Gaussian that is practically transparent with an
opacity value α below a threshold ϵα.
Size culling Remove any Gaussians that are very large in the world space or view space.

Loss
In the original 3DGS and other novel view synthesis methods, the results are often com-
pared at 7K and 30K iterations as benchmarks. The number of times each camera view-
point is used for training is dependent on the total number of camera viewpoints. Thus,
the tests conducted for this report were modified to be based on the number of epochs
per camera viewpoint instead of the total number of iterations. The tests were done with
500 epochs, such that each camera viewpoint was used for training 500 times.
3.7
Loss
The losses for each sensor modality were computed separately in different iterations of
the training. The camera loss Lc is defined as a weighted combination:
Lc = (1 −λ)Lc,1 + λLD-SSIM
(10)
where Lc,1 = ∥I(x, y)−Igs(x, y)∥1 is the L1 loss and LD-SSIM is the differentiable structural
similarity index measure. I(x, y) refers to the captured ground-truth RGB image, and
Igs(x, y) refers to the rendered image from Gaussian splatting. λ = 0.2 was used as the
weight during training. The structural similarity index measure (SSIM) is an image qual-
ity assessment tool based on human perception ability to extract structural information
from images [38]. It compares two images and gives a score between -1 and +1, with
+1 meaning more similar. The measure compares local levels of pixel intensities with
Gaussian windows for its structural information, luminance, and contrast.
The FLS loss is defined as an L1 loss:
Ls = ∥S(x, z) −Sgs(x, z)∥1
(11)
where S(x, z) refers to the captured ground truth sonar image and Sgs(x, z) refers to the
rendered sonar image from Gaussian splatting. The L2 loss was also tested, although it
did not cause substantial changes to the accuracy of the reconstruction.
At each iteration, one camera viewpoint, RGB or FLS, is used for training. Thus, Lc and
Ls are evaluated separately depending on the type of camera used for that iteration.

3.9
Limited baseline scenarios
The baseline refers to the extent of the scene that is captured by different camera view-
points. In this report, it is often in degrees, where a full 360° baseline means that there are
camera viewpoints from all around the object of interest. In limited baseline scenarios,
the information about position, covariance, and variances along the depth axis are not
well recovered when using camera images only. This section seeks to demonstrate that the
opti-acoustic fusion of RGB cameras and FLS is advantageous in small baseline scenarios
using the Fourier slice theorem.
The Fourier slice theorem is applied in the field of computed tomography like medical CT-
scans where 3D reconstructions are produced from multiple 2D scans. A 2D illustration
is provided as an explanation, and the theorem can be extended to higher dimensions.
The camera measurements are projections along the x-axis. The 1D Fourier transform
of the 1D camera measurements results in a line or slice through the origin of the 2D
Fourier domain (Fig. 7). According to Fourier slice theorem, the 1D Fourier transform of
a projection of a 2D image at a certain angle θ is equivalent to a slice in the 2D Fourier
transform of the image at the same angle θ. Thus, when the camera is shifted to many
different viewing angles in a many-shot scenario, it produces multiple slices of the Fourier
transform (Fig. 8b). With sufficient slices, a 2D inverse Fourier transform of the Fourier
domain will produce an accurate 3D reconstruction of the physical domain. However, in
a few-shot or limited baseline scenario (Fig. 8a), there is a conic region in the Fourier
domain that is not captured by the cameras, leading to the “missing cone” problem [39]
that causes poor reconstruction of the physical domain.
In opti-acoustic sensor fusion, sonar measurements are projections along the z-axis, and
its 1D Fourier transform also results in a slice of the 2D Fourier domain that is orthogonal
to the camera slice (Fig. 9). The complementary information from the two different sensor
modalities produces more slices in the Fourier domain and captures the “missing cone”.
Thus, in a few-shot scenario (Fig. 10), having an additional sonar sensor will improve the
reconstruction process.

The training optimizer uses PyTorch and CUDA extensions in a Python environment

System for Image Based Rendering (SIBR) [42] interactive viewer not in the Docker image
are still needed to process the training input and output, respectively. The interested

4.3.2
Forward-looking sonar
Mitsuba 3 has a depth integrator that computes the distance of scene for each pixel, re-
sulting in a depth map of the rendered view. An FLS scan was simulated from the depth
map by taking row- or column-wise histograms, each histogram representing an azimuth
ray from the FLS. A column-wise histogram results in an xz-plane sonar scan (mounted
horizontally), while a row-wise histogram results in a yz-plane sonar scan (mounted ver-
tically). An example of the process is elaborated in the following sections.
A LEGO Bulldozer model was chosen as a test model due to its rich features and pop-
ularity among NeRF and 3D Gaussian splatting datasets. This model was downloaded
from the Mitsuba 3 gallery and Blender [45, 46].
4.3.3
Pre-processing
The RGB images generated with Mitsuba did not require any pre-processing. However,
the FLS images had to be processed to remove salt noise. Creating a histogram of the
entire depth map in Fig. 12a shows that most of the depth values are between 1.0m and
1.75m. However, there is some noise below 1.0m. This is present because of how the
depth map was created. To illustrate the origin of such noise, a cropped region of the
depth map is taken in Fig. 12b.
Each Mitsuba camera has a samples per pixel property which is the number of samples
traced from the pixel into the scene to render the final image. For pixels that lie along
the edge of the model, some of the samples will intersect the model correctly, while others
will not intersect but read the empty background, returning a value of zero depth. The
average of all these samples are computed to return as the depth value of the pixel. Thus,

as expected, outliers with depth values less than 1.0 m lie along the edge of the object,
as seen in Fig. 13c. Normalizing along each azimuth ray without removing these outliers
results in salt noise being generated erroneously, as seen in Fig. 12.
In this example, the input depth map has the same resolution as the RGB image, that
is, 512 × 512, with a horizontal and vertical field of view of 40°. Each row and column
are treated as azimuth rays in the row and column-wise histogram, respectively. The
assumption is that noisy pixels are scattered and not clustered together, while true pixels
are clustered together as they are in the actual model. Thus, a majority filter was applied
to determine the number of nonzero points in the neighborhood of the pixel (see Fig. 15).
The approach taken was to remove only pixels that were noise and not modify any existing
values. Hence, other methods for the single image enhancement technique for multi-beam
sonar images such as the median and the Gabor filter suggested by [12] tested with limited
success.
The majority filter counts the number of non-zero pixels in the n × n window centered
around the pixel. If the count is smaller than the cut-off value, the pixel is treated as noise
and has its value set to zero. Most of the noise is successfully removed in the first pass
of the majority filter with a 3 × 3 window and a cutoff value of 3 as seen in Fig. 16a. A
larger second pass with a 10 × 10 window and cut-off value of 10 was used to remove the
final few blobs as seen in Fig. 16b. The window sizes and cut-off values were empirically
selected. Finally, the images were transposed to match the axes required by the data
loader for the training scripts.

4.5
Camera calibration
4.5
Camera calibration
In the simulation scenarios, the camera parameters like field-of-view, rotation matrix, and
translation vector are fully known. However, in real-world scenarios, the camera intrin-
sics and extrinsics are not directly known, thus requiring additional steps to be taken.
COLMAP was used to calibrate the camera viewpoints using Structure From Motion
(SfM) and Multi-View Stereo (MVS). The camera intrinsics of the OAK-D Lite stereo
camera were previously obtained by DTU Aqua using Kalibr [49, 50]. The camera intrin-
sics can be found in the tools/oak_d_lite_calibration/calibration-left-rgb.yaml
directory of the main repository. COLMAP takes a set of RGB images as input, and out-
puts the following files (see documentation for more details).
• Intrinsic parameters of all reconstructed cameras.
• Pose and keypoints of all reconstructed images.
• Information of all reconstructed 3D points such as position and color.
During feature extraction, select "PINHOLE" as the camera model, and check "Shared
for all images" if only one camera was used to capture the images. The default settings for
feature matching are sufficient. The camera parameters can be modified in the "Database
management". Copy the parameters from calibration-left-rgb.yaml into the relevant
fields.
After reconstruction, COLMAP may not use all images to reconstruct the scene as there
may not be matching features between all pictures. Thus, the user should determine
which images were used by parsing through the output files and finding the camera IDs
that are used in the sparse model.
Scaling issue
After the outdoor data set was collected, the Gaussian models produced were evaluated.
However, it was noticed that the scale was wrong. COLMAP uses an arbitrary scale to
define the camera extrinsics and 3D points. To obtain the accurate scale, geo-registration
should be done with COLMAP’s model aligner to input the Global Positioning System
(GPS) coordinates of the images. An attempt was made to solve the Perspective-n-Point
(PnP) problem, where the camera’s pose is estimated from a set of known 3D points and
its corresponding 2D image positions. Four corners of the milk crate were used to define
the known 3D positions (see Fig. 23). However, the reconstructions produced with the
PnP poses did not turn out well compared to the COLMAP poses. COLMAP offers a
more robust workflow that uses more visual features and performs a global optimization
on all cameras, compared to the single image pose estimation for PnP.

4.7
Geometric evaluation
4.7
Geometric evaluation
Geometric evaluation is performed by comparing the Gaussian model against a reference
point cloud. The Gaussian model is converted into a point cloud by taking the mean
positions of each Gaussian.
Poisson-disk sampling is applied to ground-truth meshes
that are not stored as points (e.g., Standard Triangle Language (STL) files) to obtain a
reference point cloud.
The number of vertices in the predicted and reference point cloud is matched by changing
the number of points in the reference point cloud. This creates a fairer comparison between
the methods as the FLS method typically generates half the number of splats as compared
to the RGB method due to the additional FLS constraint. The number of splats affects
the metrics as the Chamfer distance (see Section 4.7.1) computes an average.
The two point clouds are also aligned using Iterative Closest Point (ICP) [54], a point
cloud registration algorithm. An initial transformation is obtained through trial and error
and applied to the predicted point cloud. The ICP algorithm minimizes the mean square
distance metric between the two point clouds by iteratively applying a transformation to
the predicted point cloud until a distance threshold is reached. This process results in a
predicted point cloud that aligns with the reference point cloud.
The simulated model is resized so that its maximum dimension corresponds to 1. In the
real-world data set, the ground truth model of the crate is resized to have its length equal
to 1. Two corners along the length of the crate are manually selected from the Gaussian
splats to measure the distance between them. The Gaussians are multiplied by a scale
factor so that the distance between the two corners is equal to 1. Hence, the distance
measures computed henceforth follow an arbitrary unit scale.

Hypothesis 3: sonar resolution
Hypothesis: Finer sonar resolution improves reconstruction.
Sonar resolution is the distance per pixel of a sonar image. It is affected by the number of
range bins and the minimum and maximum distances captured by the sonar. A finer sonar
resolution should capture more details along the depth axis and result in an improvement
in reconstruction. In this setup, the depths of the LEGO Bulldozer model are between
1.0 and 2.0m depending on the viewing angle. Thus, the minimum range was fixed at
0m, while the maximum range was varied at 1.0m intervals from 2.0m to 7.0m. The
number of range bins was fixed at 512.
In this experiment, only the FLS method is
relevant. Geometric and photometric scores are found in Appendix C.5. The Chamfer
and Hausdorff distances are illustrated in Fig. 41.
Finding: The best performing configuration is with the lowest maximum range that
has the smallest pixel resolution. The Chamfer and Hausdorff distances grew with the
maximum range of the test. The photometric scores also reflect a similar trend in the
performance of the Gaussian model, with the lowest maximum range having the best
scores. Hence, the hypothesis was supported.

Hypothesis 4: weight scheduling
Hypothesis: Weight scheduling improves reconstruction.
As mentioned in Section 3.8, weight scheduling focuses on different sensor modes at dif-
ferent stages of the training process. This should lead to better reconstruction along the
depth axis in the 1st step (t1), while being able to reconstruct details along the x and y
axes from the RGB images in the 2nd step (t2). In this experiment, only the FLS method
is relevant. Geometric and photometric scores are found in Appendix C.6. Key results
are illustrated in Fig. 42.
Finding: The two-step weight scheduling generally leads to an improvement in recon-
struction, as seen from the lower Chamfer distances and the higher photometric scores.
Hence, the hypothesis was supported. It is noted that the simple variation of reducing
Wfls in t2 to 0.1 or 0.01 suffices.

5.1.8
Underwater image distortion
RGB images taken underwater undergo different types of distortion. In this experiment,
three types of common distortion were applied to the RGB images while keeping the FLS
images unchanged. Geometric and photometric scores are found in Appendix C.8.
• Gaussian blur: 5 × 5 windows were applied to reduce the quality of visual features.
• Particles: added salt and pepper noise to 0.05% (about 130) of the pixels.
• Scattering: depth-based light scattering based on the normalized depth such that
points further away experience more scattering (lower contrast).
Finding: The underwater image distortions did not have a significant impact on recon-
struction quality, as Chamfer distances varied within 0.001 of the control test. There were
no significant differences in the other geometric and photometric scores, as well. However,
it is unclear why the RGB method still outperforms the FLS method in these scenarios.
The additional particles were not seen in the trained Gaussian model. This could be due
to the small size of the particles and the random nature of the particles such that the
same particle is unlikely to appear in the same position again in another image. Thus,
splats are not created in the Gaussian model to represent particle noise. From the metrics,
depth-based light scattering had minimal impact on the geometric and photometric scores.
Gaussian splatting captures color well, so it is able to reconstruct the colors in the training
images. It is unable to recover the original image colors before underwater distortion, as
it is not designed to do so. Thus, depth-based light scattering has minimal impact on
reconstruction.

6
Conclusion
This project has implemented an opti-acoustic sensor fusion approach on both simulated
and real-world datasets. It detailed the pre-processing steps required to enhance the qual-
ity of RGB and sonar images by effectively reducing noise, thus generating higher-quality
inputs for the Z-splat framework. Furthermore, it showcased the practical application
of Z-splat on a ROV equipped with a stereo camera and a multi-beam forward-looking
sonar. The simulation tests conducted were unable to replicate the superior performance
of the FLS method in the limited baseline scenario described in Z-splat, highlighting areas
for further investigation. However, the models produced by the FLS method in real-world
tests were better constrained along the depth axis.
Z-splat and 3DGS are approaches that fundamentally rely on computer vision techniques
and RGB images to produce Gaussian splats. It requires high-quality photos with minimal
motion blur and clear background segmentation for the reconstructions to be accurate.
6.1
Future works
More work can be done to build on the foundation of current research by exploring several
key areas for advancement.
Preprocessing: Further advances can be made by implementing more robust methods
tailored for underwater image processing, effectively addressing challenges specific
to this area such as noise, distortion, and other lighting inconsistencies. Similarly,
the exploration of advanced techniques for sonar image processing will be beneficial
in enhancing the interpretation and utility of FLS images.
Training: One avenue involves experimenting with different loss schemes for the RGB
and FLS images, which could enhance the model’s capability to learn effectively from
each data modality. In addition, other training hyperparameters can be optimized
through systematic sweeps to uncover configurations that could further improve the
performance and efficiency of the model.
Applications: The captured scene can scale up to go beyond a single object and capture
a larger environment with a minimal trade-off on storage of the Gaussian model.
Multiple models of a specific scene can be produced over time to monitor its state.
Using geometric or photometric measures, a scene can be flagged if a change is
detected (e.g. hole detection in a pipeline).

Future works
Other works: 3DGS is a rapidly advancing domain, and research in this area is being
released quickly. Integrating recent advances will likely yield innovative solutions
that push the boundaries of this framework. For example, Mip-Splatting mitigates
aliasing and dilation issues by reducing the size of the Gaussian created [56] and
Splat-Nav is a pipeline to navigate Gaussian splatting environments in real time
[57].
