Abstract
This research investigates on the surface hardening of large cylindrical samples (◦300
mm) by heat treatments, on a specific grade of ferritic ductile cast iron, EN-GJS-400-
18-LT, used in critical wind turbine components. Manufacturing advantages related
to a ferritic matrix have to comprised with a pearlitic matrix if the former cannot
be surface hardened to resolve wear related failures. However, a ferritic matrix has
poor response to heat treatments and is well-documented in literature. Setting the
scope of hardening to induction heating, the research started with the investigation
of oven treated smaller specimens to comprehend microstructural response of the
material. The results from oven treated experiments indicate that the dissolution
of carbon from graphite into matrix, at critical temperatures and sufficient holding
time, can be quenched to form a hard layer of martensite. Consequent use of induc-
tion hardening demonstrates the importance of composition modification by involving
a pre-heating stage before final heating. Results from induction hardening suggests
that a pre-heating employed at 950 ◦C for 15-20 seconds, followed by air cooling to
500 ◦C, can create a conducive microstructural response to form martensite upon a fi-
nal heating temperature of 950 ◦C, followed by spray quenching. Furthermore, results
from Thermo-Calc + DICTRA and COMSOL modelling are in good agreement with
experimental observations. These introductory modelling experiments can therefore
be employed in future, for the optimization of various decisive parameters concerning
induction heating.
Keywords: surface hardening, large cylindrical specimens, heat treatment, fer-
ritic ductile cast iron, ferritic matrix, induction hardening

Introduction
This chapter acquaints the reader with the project background. It outlines the genesis
of the problem and translates it into a research question which is then established as
a project aim. The scope is thereafter defined while examining for similar activities
in the past.
Anticipated challenges are also evaluated which aids in formulating
the problem. Finally, a few delimitations are also set upfront for an in-depth study.
”Where is the problem?”, ”Why is it a problem?” and ”What can be done?” are a few
questions answered in this section.
1.1
Background
Wind energy is a rapidly growing segment within energy sector in Europe, where 16.8
GW of gross additional wind power capacity has been installed in 2017 [1]. With a
net installed capacity of 168.7 GW, wind energy remains the second largest form of
power generation in Europe [1]. In light of uncertain governance towards achieving
EU climate and energy binding targets, European Wind Energy Association (EWEA)
updated its vision to 2030, by when it expects to install 320 GW [2]. This is almost
twice as much as the total installed capacity in 2017. To meet these requirements,
industrial trend is towards multi- megawatts offshore wind farms.
Siemens Gamesa Renewable Energy (Denmark) is in the process of responding
to such energy initiatives by building larger offshore turbines, details of which are
confidential and therefore undisclosed. Nevertheless, these wind turbines would use
significantly larger components, here among main shaft, which is the subject of this
report. This main shaft is expected to have dimensions in the order of meters, and is
made from a soft ductile cast iron (DCI) having a ferritic microstructure (available
for further reading in Section 4). It is anticipated that the bearing housing would be
jeopardized due to wear-related failures, if the inherent hardness of the material is
not increased. However, only a few millimeters on the surface is to be hardened so
as to preserve the toughness at its core.
Structural castings of DCI provide adequate stiffness, during which they are sub-
jected to dynamic and/or cyclic loading. Hence, a vast majority of research is towards
==PAGE:20==

the improvement of fatigue properties. Ferritic microstructure, of the material under
investigation, is not sufficiently hard and neither is it desirable for many of the indus-

1.2
Project aim
The aim of this project is to increase the hardness of a 2-3 mm thick surface layer in
ferritic ductile cast iron cylindrical samples with a diameter of 300 mm to above 300
HV as compared to the current hardness of ≈150 HV. The original toughness has to
be maintained in the core and hence the case depth should not exceed 5 mm.
1.3
Scope of hardening
Thermochemical treatments, cold working processes, induction heating, flame and
laser heating are a few available hardening techniques.
As the process might ul-
timately be applied to large shafts, thermochemical treatments and many of cold
working processes cannot be utilized. Flame and laser heating could be interesting
options. However, constraints of time and accessibility, bar their use in this project.
Consequently, the scope of hardening is defined to induction heating.
1.4
Previous activities
Various smaller parts made of cast iron grades, used in vehicles, are currently surface
treated using induction heating. However, no information has been found about the
use of such treatments on large specimens of ferritic DCI. There are also a number
of reports on effective surface laser hardening of high strength grades of cast iron.
1.5
Challenges and problem formulation
This work encounters both technical and practical challenges. The former corresponds
to the unfavorable composition of material that precludes thermodynamic/kinetic
changes for hardening, and the latter is related to the size of the specimen which
limits the available hardening techniques. To be defined in section 1.6, only technical
challenges are addressed in this report. A few of them are as listed below.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
A theoretical framework about ductile cast iron (DCI) and induction hardening (IH)
is presented in this chapter.
The purpose here is to perform a literature survey
on the metallurgical behavior of DCI on induction heating. However, the chapter
begins by giving a basic understanding on cast iron and its classifications, so that the
readers can comprehend the origin, production complexity and importance of DCI.
An understanding of the various concepts highlighted in this chapter will also serve
as a knowledge base and reference to many of the experimental activities that follow.
2.1
Cast Iron and its classifications
Cast iron, like steel, includes a large family of ferrous alloys.
They contain iron,
carbon and silicon as major alloying elements. Additionally, various other minor al-
loying elements are added depending on the required microstructure. Cast iron (CI)
can solidify into thermodynamically metastable Fe-Fe3C or stable iron-graphite sys-
tem based on cooling rate, composition and melt treatment [5]. Referring to a binary
system, CI can be defined as an Fe-C alloy containing more than 2 %C. However,
silicon and other minor alloying elements can change the maximum solid-solubility
of C in austenite (γ) [5], where, in exceptional cases, alloys with less than 2% C can
solidify with a eutectic structure and can still fall into the category of CI. Though
historical classification of CI was into white and grey iron, based on its fracture, the
most common classification scheme categorizes cast iron into four types; white iron,
grey iron, ductile iron and malleable iron.
2.2
Ductile cast iron
Ductile cast iron (DCI), also known as nodular iron or spheroidal graphite (SG) cast
iron, is cast iron in which graphite is present in the form of tiny nodules or spheres.
They are used for many structural applications, those which require a combination of
strength and toughness. The relative industrial success of DCI comes from its ease
==PAGE:24==
6
2 Theory
of casting and machinability, in conjunction with low cost. The following sections
discuss on the various aspects of DCI which is of relevance to this project.
2.2.1
History of DCI
Until the first half of the last century, malleable iron formed from an extended anneal-
ing treatment of white iron (in which carbon exist as cementite), allowed to obtain a
combination of good castability and high toughness [6]. During the process, carbon
precipitates in the form of irregular graphite. This form of graphite is called as ”tem-
per carbon” because it is formed in the solid state during heat treatment [7]. It was
however, an expensive process owing to the extended annealing treatment and the dif-
ficulty to cast sound white iron. A breakthrough happened in 1947 when spheroidal
graphite was first observed in an as-cast structure from the melt [8]. H. Morrogh
and W.J. Williams studied the solidification of Ni-C and Co-C alloys for a long time,
during when they found that an addition of Ca and Mg alloy or increasing cooling
rate promoted the precipitation of spheroidal graphite [8]. In 1948, a similar result
was obtained by the addition of cerium [6] and other rare earth elements (REEs).
2.2.2
Metallurgy of DCI production
Graphite in DCI precipitates in the form of nodules from the melt. Spheroidal/n-
odular morphology is attributed to the reduction of sulphur content. Sulphur is an
element that is known to favor the formation of graphite flakes [7] and is removed
by calcium carbide treatment followed by a small addition of spheroidizing elements.
While there are many spheroidizing elements, magnesium (Mg) and cerium are con-
sidered as the most influential [8]. They poison the preferred growth directions of
graphite, and contribute to an isotropic growth resulting in spheroids of graphite
[7]. Additionally, various REEs are also used for nodulization. They are generally
employed in DCI to accomplish the following: (a) neutralize tramp elements like Ti,
Pb, Bi, As etc; (b) assist in nodulizing or provide a supplementary effect to Mg in
imparting a spherical shape to graphite and (c) assist in nucleating graphite [9].
The amount of residual Mg required to produce spheroidal graphite is generally
0.03-0.05% [5]. The exact amount depends on the cooling rate, i.e; a higher cooling
rate would require less Mg. However, Mg is rarely added in its pure form due to
various reasons;
1. Boiling point of Mg is below the molten temperature of iron. Therefore, volatiliza-
tion is a severe problem when Mg is treated with iron [8].
2. Mg is an anti-graphitizing element and a carbide promoter [7].
3. Excessive addition of Mg deteriorates mechanical properties and increases de-
fects such as dross, porosity and sub-surface pinhole [8].
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Ductile cast iron
7
Various alloys have been developed to overcome the above problems; Mg-Ni alloy,
Mg-Cu alloy, Mg-ferrosilicon alloy, to name a few [8].
2.2.3
Graphite shape, size and distribution in DCI
Metallurgical processing of CI can be subjected to the influence of many anti-spheroidizing
(deleterious) minor elements. They can cause minor changes in graphite spheroidal
morphology to complete deterioration. According to the ISO 945-1 standard [10],
there are six forms of graphite in cast iron and are illustrated in the Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Principle graphite forms in cast iron [10]
Shape of graphite has a decisive influence on the mechanical properties of the
material. The presence of graphite in the form of flakes (type I in figure 2.1) impart
excellent machining and lubricating properties. But these graphite flakes are detri-
mental in case of loading. They act as stress risers causing premature localized plastic
flow at stresses less than critical levels [7]. On the other hand, graphite in the form
==PAGE:26==
8
2 Theory
of spheroids (type VI in Figure 2.1), in case of DCI, interrupt the matrix much less
than graphite flakes [7]. Therefore, ductile cast iron has higher strength and tough-
ness than grey cast iron. The percentage of graphite particles that are spheroidal or
nodular in shape (of form V and VI) is represented by a term known as ”nodularity”.
Additionally, size and distribution of nodules also influence properties, but to a lesser
extent than shape [11]. Requirements of form, size and distribution vary with appli-
cation, industry etc. For wind turbine applications, spheroidal graphite must ensure
that 90% of graphite has been segregated into spheroidal form V and VI [12].
2.2.4
Heat treatment of DCI
DCI’s are heat treated to obtain microstructures that are not readily obtained in
an as-cast condition. Depending on the alloy composition and section size, as-cast
structures are mostly ferritic, pearlitic or a combination of both. Annealing, normal-
izing, stress relieving, hardening and tempering, austempering and surface hardening
are some of the most important heat treatments applied on DCI. Heat treatment
provides strength, wear resistance and high temperature properties which cannot be
attained from an as-cast microstructure [13]. The following discussion will only focus
on hardness, which is the major mechanical property governing wear resistance.
As in all ferrous alloys, it is the amount of carbon in the matrix that determines
the final hardness. The phrase ”carbon in matrix” is the key metallurgical difference
between steels and DCI [14]. Carbon content in steels are a definite value that is
fixed by the chemical composition, and will not exceed this value in a fully austenitic
condition [14]. On the contrary, cast irons have graphite in various forms that act
as ”source” and ”sink” for carbon. Depending on the heat treatment parameters,
the matrix carbon content in DCI can be extremely heterogeneous, as the matrix
in proximity with the nodules have a greater advantage of being carbon enriched.
Different levels of carbon enrichment can yield martensite with varying amount of
carbon dissolved in it during quenching, and therefore, non-uniform hardness.
Alloying elements other than carbon affect ”hardenability”, and have little affect
on hardness [13]. Hardenability is the depth to which a material can be hardened
when quenched from an austenitizing temperature. Certain alloying elements con-
tribute to better hardenability in DCI when compared to others. A study conducted
by Lee et al. shows that manganese and molybdenum are more effective in increasing
hardenability, per wt.% added, than nickel or copper in DCI [15]. The effects of these
alloying elements on DCI austenitized at 870 ◦C is illustrated in the figure 2.2. The
contribution of tin (Sn) to hardenability is also widely studied in the literature. Pet-
richenko et al. [16] concludes that 0.08% Sn makes it possible to lower the quenching
temperature by 25-30 ◦C while retaining the same hardenability.

Thermal conductivity of material is another important parameter that govern me-
chanical properties of a material after heat treatment. Thermal properties are more
significant in a heat treatment like induction hardening, where cycle time of heating
is much shorter compared to alternative heating processes. As an example, thermal
conductivity of CI is typically lower than that of carbon steels. This results in a much
weaker “soaking” action in the former during surface heating [14]. If these materials
are allowed to cool by itself, carbon steels can produce a shallow case depth, unlike
CI [17]. This is due to the effect of thermal conduction away from the surface by the
cold core which provides sufficient cooling to miss the nose of the continuous cooling
curve [14]. Therefore, in contrast to surface hardening of steels, self-quenching is
practically never used in surface hardening cast irons [14].

Alloying elements play an important role during heat treatment. Their effect on
various properties of CI would be cumbersome to infer, if evaluated for individual
alloying species. To dwindle the complexity, a terminology known as ”carbon equiv-
alence (CE)” is used for steels and CI. It is an empirical value in wt.%, where the
alloying elements other than carbon are converted into equivalent carbon percentage.
CE essentially coverts an iron-alloy phase diagram into an equivalent iron-carbon
phase diagram, which is more established. This allows for a better understanding on
the influence of alloying elements on casting behavior and heat treatment. CE can
be used to predict thermal conductivity, shrinkage, specific heat, linear expansion
coefficient, hardness and various other properties [18]. Several equations have been
developed to calculate CE, cited in the order of equations as [19, 20, 21]
CE = %C + 0.33(%Si)
(2.1)
CE = %C + 0.317(%Si) + 0.33(%P)
(2.2)
CE = %C + 0.3(%Si) + 0.33(%P) −0.027(%Mn) + 0.4(%S)
(2.3)
The co-relation between CE and hardness is such that a higher CE yields a lower
hardness. An example for this relationship in the case of induction hardened grey
irons can be found in the ”Handbook of Induction Heating” by Rudnev et al. [22].
It could appear contradictory on a glimpse as to why a higher CE reduces hardness.
A contextual (with relation to hardness) description of CE found in [5], can clear
the ambiguity, where it states that ”CE defines how close a given analysis is to a
eutectic composition”. A CE of 4.3 corresponds to a eutectic alloy [5], and values
lesser than and greater than 4.3 makes a hypoeutectic and hypereutectic composition
respectively. The microstructure of hypereutectic composition (higher CE) usually
has coarser grains which reduces hardness [23]. It is based on this rationale that a
higher CE yields lower hardness.
2.3
Induction hardening
Induction hardening (IH) is non-contact heating process that uses the principle of in-
ductive heating to form a ”hard” microstructure upon quenching. In this process, an
alternating current is passed to an induction coil to generate a time-varying magnetic
field in it, which in turn induces an eddy current that heats up the material. IH is
an environment-friendly and cost efficient heat treatment technology frequently used
for axisymmetric and flat steel elements [24]. It can be used in a number of heat
treatment processes such as surface hardening, through-hardening, tempering, stress
relieving and annealing [17].
A typical induction hardening process involves heating the material to the austen-
itizing temperature, holding the temperature for a short duration (if required) and
==PAGE:29==
2.3 Induction hardening
11
rapidly cooling it below the martensite start (Ms) temperature. Rapid cooling re-
places the time dependent diffusion process by martensite which is the product of
a shear-type transformation [17].
The formation of martensite and the depth to
which it forms will depend on material properties (chemical composition, starting mi-
crostructure, thermal conductivity etc) and the choice of parameters for the induction
heating system (frequency, power density, coil features, quenching conditions etc). A
few important points are discussed below.
2.3.1
Influence of Si and starting microstructure on IH
The response of CI to induction heating is strongly dependent on silicon content. In
addition to altering the eutectic and eutectoid temperature, it can also influence the
formation of different phases. Si in CI can decrease the solid solubility of carbon in
austenite (γ), increase the upper critical temperature and make a δ-ferrite + liquid
+ graphite (G) three phase region and α-ferrite + G + γ three phase region [25]. A
section through the Fe-Fe3C-Si ternary equilibrium diagram at 2% Si is shown in the
figure 2.3. The three phase region of γ + α + G defines a lower and upper critical
temperatures (Tcu) for the formation of austenite on heating or ferrite during cooling,
confirming an ”inter-critical range” [26]. Tcu must be exceeded so that the austeni-
tizing temperature is in the two-phase field (γ + G). An incompetency in achieving
Tcu will result in a lower hardness due to the presence of pre-eutectoid ferrites.
Figure 2.3: Section through the Fe-Fe3C-Si ternary equilibrium diagram at 2% Si [5]

Type of matrix is another influential factor that determines the ability to be
induction hardened. There are five subgroups of ductile cast iron matrix; marten-
sitic, pearlitic, pearlitic-ferritic, ferritic and austempered1. Induction heating is usu-
ally applied to martensitic, pearlitic and, to a lesser extent, pearlitic-ferritic DCI
[14]. Ferritic DCI is not a typical choice for IH due to the inability to transform
low carbon-containing ferrite into martensite [14, 28].
Additionally, spheroidized
structures (graphite nodules, for example) generally have a poor response to induc-
tion hardening, resulting in the necessity of having prolonged heating and requiring
higher hardening temperatures [29]. In case of ferritic DCI, intense induction heating
and quenching is required to create localized martensitic areas near the boundaries of
graphite nodules [30]. Depending on the starting microstructure, surface temperature
ranges between of 860 to 960 ◦C is typical of induction hardening of cast iron castings
[28]. The time of holding at the austenitizing temperature is imperative for a ferritic
DCI, as too short or too long a holding time can unfavorably reduce hardness. While
the former reduces hardness due to the unavailability of sufficient carbon in the solid
solution, the latter affects by grain coarsening. Readers are directed to the work by
Voigt et al. [31], which presents a nice overview on the induction hardening response
of ductile iron at 900 ◦C at various holding times and starting microstructure.
2.3.2
Influence of induction system parameters on IH
Induction heating is a multiphysics phenomena that couples electromagnetism with
heat transfer. The material properties, discussed above, are mostly related to physics
of heat transfer, while the induction system parameters affect the physics of electro-
magnetism.
When an alternating current flows through a conductor, there is a non-uniform
current distribution within it. The maximum value of current density is concentrated
at the surface, with a decrease in its value from the surface towards the interior [17].
This non-uniform current distribution within the cross-section of the conductor is
termed as ”skin effect”. It is considered as a fundamental property of any process
that relies on electromagnetic induction heating [17]. According to this phenomenon,
eddy currents induced in the material will primarily flow across a thin surface layer
known as ”skin” [32]. ”Skin”, by definition, is where approximately 86% of the all
the power induced by the coil gets saturated [17], and where 63% of the current
generated by the electromagnetic induction flows [28]. The degree of skin effect is
dictated by the frequency of alternating field, magnetic permeability and material
resistivity, given by the equation [32]:

where, δ is the skin depth or current penetration depth, ρ is the electrical resistivity of
the material in Ω-m, µr is the relative magnetic permeability and F is the frequency
in Hz. It can be assessed from the above mathematical equation that the material
properties (resistivity and relative magnetic permeability) couple with the induction
system parameter (frequency) in determining the current penetration depth.
An important inference from equation 2.4 is corresponding to the skin depth,
which is inversely proportional to the square root of frequency. Thus, according to
the electromagnetic skin effect, lower frequencies will heat deeper while higher fre-
quencies will favor a shallow case depth. If the chosen frequency is much lower than
desired, the heating will be more than what would be intended. This will result in a
deeper heat-affected zone, energy wastage and additional work piece distortion [32].
The phrase ”low frequency” and ”high frequency” could sound extremely vague and
relative, but a clear distinction between the two cannot be found in the literature.
Nevertheless, it is useful to know that the frequency selection for surface-hardening
application ranges from 60 Hz to greater than 2 MHz [17, 33]. Kevin mentions in his
article [33] that a medium frequency falls in the range between 500 Hz to 10 kHz, and
those above 100 kHz fall into the category of high frequency. The desirable frequency
range is mostly determined by the dimensions of the work piece, material type and
penetration depth [33].
Power is yet another factor, in conjunction with frequency, that affects the case
depth. Rudnev [32] states that in many instances it is possible to achieve the same
desired case depth by using different combinations of power density and frequency.
This signifies that a lower-than-optimal frequency can be chosen in combination with
higher power to achieve a shallow case depth. The statement holds true for a deeper
case depth, where higher-than-optimal frequency can be chosen in combination with
lower power density. Co-relating the literature to the present work, a higher power
would impart a higher temperature in the case which enables enhanced dissolution
of carbon from the graphite nodules (if held for sufficient time) into austenite. The
martensite thus formed will be much harder, owing to a greater amount of tetragonal
distortion by carbon in the lattice.

and hardness measurements. Literature review from previous chapter contradicts the
relation between initial microstructure and final desired properties of the investigated
material. Evidently, surface hardening of a predominantly ferritic ductile iron has not
captured the interest of many researches. Associated dearth of literature compelled
this project to start the proceedings by experimentally understanding the microstruc-
tural behavior of the material upon heat treatment. It was clear from literature that
free carbon (graphite) is the sole source for hardening the investigated material by
heat treatment. Subsequently, following research questions were raised.
1. Can carbon be dissolved into matrix from graphite nodules?
2. What temperatures and treatment times are required for the carbon dissolution?
3. What quenching conditions are required to impart required hardness, from the
dissolution of carbon in matrix?
4. If the carbon dissolved in the matrix, at an experimentally determined temper-
ature and treatment time, can impart hardness, what is the possible depth of
hardening?
5. If the depth of hardening is above 5 mm, how can the case depth be constraint
within this value?

3 Methodology
Following experiments were planned, and are mentioned in the chronological order
of the raised research questions.
1. The primary research question to be answered was if carbon can be dissolved
into matrix from graphite nodules. To answer this question, ”thermally thin”1
specimens were investigated after heat treatment at various temperatures fol-
lowed by water quenching. This experimental package is referred to as ”thin
sample experiments”.
2. ”Thermally thick” samples were investigated at various treatment times at a
chosen temperature, to comprehend the influence of holding time. In such a
sample, temperature is a function of time. This experimental package is referred
to as ”thick sample experiments”. The temperatures above which the treatment
was performed on both thin and thick samples were decided on a metallurgical
understanding of the investigated material.
3. Quenching conditions dictate cooling rates and the related hardness of the mi-
crostructure formed.
Thin and thick sample experiments were quenched in
water.
The hardness of these samples were related to another experimental
package where air cooling was employed, and is referred to as ”air cooling ex-
periments”.
4. Depth to which the material can be hardened is a decisive parameter to be
determined, if the material responds positively to the above three experiments.
This information is included in the experimental package called ”hardenability”.
NOTE: Heat treatment on all the four experimental packages: thin sample exper-
iments, thick sample experiments, air cooling experiments and hardenability, were
performed in laboratory oven. It is not expected to achieve the required case depth
using oven treatments. Heating rate in an oven is so slow that it will not create a large
thermal gradient within the material that can be exploited under cooling conditions
to form a case depth.
5. To constrain case depth within 5 mm, rapid heating using induction treatment is
used. These treatments are first utilized on small samples and later on upscaled
samples. The latter of the experimental package is referred to as ”Induction

very short treatment time. With further increase in time, surface and bulk temper-

Despite temperature well above Ac3, a short treatment time in case of thick

diffuse into regions far from nodules, resulting in a concentration gradient. This con-

deviations during measurement corresponds to this fact, and is largest for the sample
held for 10 min. From this experiment, it can be inferred that the process of homog-
enizing austenite with carbon is limited by the dissolution of carbon from graphite
nodule at the chosen treatment time.

conventional induction treatment, which otherwise would have hardened a surface
layer in many steels. Such a behavior can be ascribed to two plausible reasons; a. in-
sufficiently low austenitizing temperature b. very short holding time. Cast iron may
be moderately similar to steels in its reaction to hardening [43]. However, because of
graphite and high percentages of silicon, cast iron is more difficult to austenitize than
steels [44]. Silicon promotes graphitization which results in graphite, a phase which
is much more difficult to dissolve than carbides [5]. Additionally, the presence of
silicon is also claimed to retard the absorption of carbon by austenite [44]. Therefore,
it is crucial to resort to higher austenitizing temperatures for remaining specimens.
Importance of holding time is also not to be neglected. An extremely high heating
rate provides insufficient time for dissolution and diffusion of carbon into austenite.

It is included in the
project as an attempt to validate experimental observations. Thermodynamics soft-

6.1
Thermodynamics modelling
6.1.1
Property diagram of the material
As a primary step, it is important to gain knowledge on the amount of phases and
their stability across various temperatures. The mole fraction of the phases formed
under equilibrium conditions at the defined temperature ranges is shown in the figure
6.1. The plot also shows the phase transformation temperatures, (marked as A1 and
A3 ). The stability window of different phases can hence be comprehended. As an
example, it can be interpreted from the plot that the stability window of α-ferrite
is until 735 ◦C, corresponding to (A1). However, it is more interesting to observe
the behavior of graphite phase with the increase in temperature. It can be seen that
graphite is stable at low temperatures until A1. With the nucleation and growth of
austenite, graphite content decreases. A further kink in the stability of graphite can
be seen at the boundary which marks complete austenitization (A3). 2% reduction in
the amount of graphite is indicated as the temperature passes from the A1 through
inter-critical region to A3. This explains why graphite acts as a ”source” and ”sink”
for carbon during heating and cooling respectively.

6.1.2
Thermodynamic modelling challenges of the material
Graphite is entered as a non-diffusion phase by DICTRA. Its presence poses numer-
ical challenges during simulation. For example, an initial simulation was tried using
a low carbon austenite and graphite as the starting phases. Austenite was intended
to inherit the composition of low carbon ferrite during the transformation, which is
also accompanied by the dissolution of carbon from graphite into austenite matrix.
Such a simulation is numerically tormenting as the scenario is extremely far from
equilibrium when the system is defined with graphite and very-low carbon austenite.
Carbon being a fast diffusion element, is interstitial in austenite and substitutional
in graphite which further complicates the problem. These shortcomings urge for a
simplification of the problem.
6.1.3
Defining diffusion model
A single-phase diffusion problem was set up in the graphical interface of DICTRA, to
alleviate modelling difficulties. A planar interface was assumed for the binary alloy
single-phase diffusion problem. Global condition of temperature was set as 950 ◦C,
which corresponds to the temperature at which the set-2 samples were held during
pre-heating stage.
It was decided to start with an austenite phase rather than a ferrite phase, as the
latter would instigate DICTRA to nucleate austenite and have a diffusional trans-
formation of ferrite to austenite, which will not occur during rapid heating in a real
scenario. Therefore, an austenite with very low carbon content of 0.001 wt.% was
defined1 at the start of simulation. The width of austenite region in the model envi-
ronment was set as 100 µm.
A linear-uniform composition of 0.001% C was fixed along a geometric type of grid
consisting of 100 grid points with a ratio of 1.04. This grid definition implies that the
density of grid points along the left boundary will be more than the right boundary.
Such a geometry is assumed on the left boundary because it is expected that the com-
position will vary a lot when the left boundary condition is set. A counted carbon
concentration of 1.32 wt.% is defined as the left boundary condition. This value was
chosen as it corresponds to the maximum solid-solubility of carbon in austenite at
950 ◦C2. The right boundary condition was set as ”zero flux” which signifies that the
defined species are not allowed to enter or leave the system. An illustration of the
model is shown in figure 6.3.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
On the basis of the investigations including microstructural observations, hardness
measurements, thermodynamics and COMSOL modelling, following conclusions may
be proposed:
1. An extremely rapid heating rate during induction hardening provide insufficient
time for dissolution and diffusion of carbon into austenite even at ≈60 ◦C above
upper critical temperature. The upper critical temperature was calculated as
806 ◦C.
2. The hardenability of ferritic ductile iron can be significantly improved by em-
ploying a two stage induction heating process; ”pre-heating” well above AC3
followed by air cooling below AC1 and ”final heating” above AC3 followed by
spray quenching. Pre-heating employed at 950 ◦C for 15-20 s followed by air
cooling to 500 ◦C formed a total case depth of 4 mm and an effective case
depth of 2 mm, upon a final heating temperature of 950 ◦C followed by spray
quenching.
3. Heating the material above AC3 and sustaining this temperature in the required
case depth is uncomplicated. Challenges are more with regards to imparting a
sufficient cooling rate to form martensite in these intended regions.
4. The calculation of diffusion distance from graphite nodule during a pre-heating
temperature of 950 ◦C for 15 s by DICTRA shows that carbon diffuses 70 µm
into the matrix, and 82 µm when the pre-heating time is 20 s. Experimental
observation yielded very close results of 68 µm for the former and 81 µm for
the latter case, respectively.
5. Interpretation of critical temperatures (AC1, inter-critical temperatures and
AC3) from COMSOL at various depths as a function of time was analyzed
against microstructural observations. Results demonstrated that the compli-
mentary use of heat transfer modelling, using COMSOL, and optical microscopy
is a powerful tool to predict the series of microstructural changes happening
within the material during heat treatment processes.

CHAPTER 8
Future Work
The interesting results and conclusions established in this thesis provided an un-
derstanding about the feasibility of induction hardening on the investigated material.
Various misconceptions, prior to the start of this work, about the response of a ferritic
matrix to induction hardening has gained clarity and perspectives that can address
new research questions in future. A few suggestions to the future development of this
thesis are discussed below.
1. It was possible to achieve the required case depth in the investigated material
of ◦300 mm.
Attaining similar results on an upscaled dimension could be
challenging as the inductive heat generated within the material can quickly
flow into the core because of larger thermal gradient. Therefore, it would be
inevitable to resolve this issue without resorting to dual-frequency arrangements.
The best solution would be to use a low frequency in the pre-heating stage
(larger skin depth which heats deeper) followed by a medium frequency during
the final induction hardening stage.
Metallurgical response of the material
under the application of dual-frequency during induction hardening is a vital
study that must be considered in future.
2. It is extremely time and resource consuming to conduct experimental trials on
large specimen dimensions. COMSOL modelling used in this work was limited
to the application of a single physics; ”heat transfer in solids”. It validated ex-
perimental results. A further step would be to take advantage of multiphysics
coupling features in the software. This can be done by selecting ”Induction Heat-
ing” predefined multiphysics (which consists of a Magnetic Fields physics inter-
face) in conjunction with ”Heat Transfer in Solids physics interface”. COMSOL
provides ample opportunities to optimize a rather complex induction hardening
parameters.
3. Anomalies in microstructure was observed at many instances during LOM inves-
tigations. Considering the interest of the project, advanced characterizations
involving diffraction studies, scanning/transmission electron microscopy etc.,
were compromised. A closer look into the microstructure will expand current
knowledge of the material and open more interesting research questions.
==PAGE:92==
74
8 Future Work
4. Importance of pre-heating, followed by air cooling, before final induction hard-
ening has been elucidated at multiple instances in chapter 5.
Due to time
constraints, the influence of different pre-heating temperatures were not stud-
ied. Austenite grain growth is an important aspect that features at AC3. As
coarser grains increase hardenability, it would be interesting to set a study in-
volving different pre-heating temperatures. Additionally, the effect of cooling
rate and cool-down temperature after pre-heating stage can also be studied.
5. Austenite can exist as a metastable phase at temperatures below its usual stabil-
ity window with the increase in carbon content of the matrix. Characterization
of the amount of carbon required to stabilize austenite can be performed as a
function of time and temperature. These data will be valuable if experiments are
conducted to fabricate an austempered ductile iron (ADI) microstructure using
induction hardening. ADI contains metastable austenite as the iron-bearing
matrix with finely dispersed ferrite, and is a very popular microstructure in
ductile iron castings due to its unique combination of strength and ductility.
