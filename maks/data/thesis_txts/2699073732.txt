Abstract 
The structural variant CCR5-∆32 is a 32 bp deletion located in the CCR5 gene. It is known that individuals 
who are homozygous for this deletion, are resistant to HIV-1 infection. The allelic frequency of CCR5-∆32 is 
estimated to be 0.10 - 0.15 in the Northern European populations, whereas in the African populations it is 
almost zero. A few ancient DNA studies with small sample pools have dated the CCR5-∆32 allele back 4900 
years and documented an allelic frequency between 0,05 -0,12 in the Norman age (11–14th century, CE). 
Those results indicate there is a genetic evolutionary history behind the CCR5-∆32 allele that is yet to be 
discovered. No large ancient NGS data studies concerning the CCR5-∆32 allele have been published. This is 
perhaps due to the degraded state of ancient DNA, which lead to low genome coverage with shorts reads 
(read-lengths 40-60bp) and mis-incorporated nucleotides. These features decrease read mapping accuracy 
in the alignment process and increase reference bias, making the CCR5-∆32 allele difficult to detect.  
In this project, we develop a new approach to detect the CCR5-∆32 allele in ancient NGS datasets. We 
identify 23 ancient samples with the CCR5-∆32 allele. We also discover a CCR5-∆32 haplotype comprising 
86 SNPs associated to the CCR5-∆32 allele, in both present and ancient time. We show, for the first time, 
that the CCR5-∆32 allele and the CCR5-∆32 haplotype were scattered around in Northern Europe in the 
Viking age. We also document that the CCR5-∆32 allele was present in Denmark and Western Siberia in the 
Neolithic age. And not least, we date the CCR5-∆32 allele longer back than previous studies. Further, we 
see that the CCR5-∆32 allele, the CCR5-∆32 haplotype and its haplotype blocks have population-specific 
fingerprints in present time, with traces pointing back to the haplotype blocks genesis in Africa.

Introduction 
The active chemokine system (chemokine receptors and chemokines) mediates both the traffic of 
inflammatory cells and pathogen-associated immune responses.1 The C-C chemokine receptor 5 (CCR5) is a 
member of the G-protein-coupled receptor family and is expressed on the surface of various immune cells. 
Upon activation, mainly by the CC chemokines CCL3, CCL4, and CCL5, CCR5 becomes a regulator in 
inflammatory responses, by directing leukocytes to sites of inflammation.2–4 In 1996, it was discovered that 
CCR5 is a necessary co-receptor for the infection of immunodeficiency virus type 1 (HIV-1) (Fig. 1A).5 Thus, 
reduction or the complete absence of CCR5 caused by loss of function mutations, can lead to HIV-1 
resistance. The 32 base pair frameshift deletion within the CCR5 (CCR5-∆32, rs333), lead to a truncated 
CCR5 protein and thereby its absence on the cell surface. Individuals homozygous for this variant do not 
express any CCR5 receptors and are therefore resistant to HIV-1 (Fig 1B). Individuals heterozygous for the 
CCR5-∆32 allele will express lower amounts of CCR5 relative to the CCR5-wt carriers and thereby have 
some level of HIV-1 resistance and slower progression towards AIDS when infected.6 In late 2019, a Chinese 
court had sentenced the biophysicist, He Jiankui, who announced that he had created the world’s first 
gene-edited babies, to prison for “illegal medical practice”.7 The aim was to give a set of twins babies  
lifetime immunity to HIV infection, by introducing  a CCR5 mutation in genomes of the embryos, to 
mimicking the effect of the CCR5-∆32 allele.

The CCR5-∆32 allele has also been associated with other pathological conditions including viral diseases, 
immune-related diseases, and various types of cancer.2,4,9,9,10 These studies all indicate that the CCR5-∆32 
allele can act as a modulator of a given phenotype expression, with both advantages and disadvantages, 
depending on the medical context. Lately, the focus has been turned toward the possibility of including 
CCR5 as a therapeutic target for SARS-CoV-2 infection and thereby the treatment of severe COVID-19 
patients.11–14 In a study by Panda et al., the CCR5-Δ32 allele was found with a significantly lower frequency 
among the COVID-19 patients, especially in the severe-ICU group (intensive care unit), compared to the 
control group (the background population).15 
The CCR5-∆32 allele frequency among ethnic groups, has been studied extensively.9,16–18 A defined 
geographic pattern has emerged, with the highest CCR5-∆32 minor allele frequency (MAF) between 0.10- 
0.164 in northern European populations and with a gradual decrease moving towards the South - and 
South-East Europe. Outside Europe, the CCR5-∆32 allele can be identified in countries where the 
population includes inhabitants of European descent, in particular countries in America (MAF between 
0.036 - 0.120), South Africa (0.132) and Australia (MAF 0.123), which support the theory of a single 
Northern European origin followed by migration.19 The current high MAF has led to the debate of the 
evolutionary history and age of the CCR5-∆32 allele. Positive selection, negative selection, balancing 
selection, and genetic drift have each been proposed as an explanation for the present gene frequency.19–22 
 Theoretical approaches using linked markers estimated the allele’s age to range between 700–3,400 
years,18,20 whereas a later study in 2005,21 combining linked markers with genetic maps, proposed an 
estimate of 7,000 years (2,900–15,750 years, 95% confidence interval).  
The debate has further been challenged by the degraded nature of ancient DNA (aDNA) samples and 
contaminations (Fig. 2), small geographic sample pools, and the specific PCR assaying of the CCR5-∆32 
alleles concerning sparse DNA content.  
On the other hand, the implication of using a larger ancient NGS dataset is the decreased read mapping 
accuracy, and the increased reference bias, especially when analyzing structural variations (SV), such as the 
CCR5-∆32 allele. Recently the variation graph software (vg) has addressed this issue by replacing the linear 
reference sequence in the alignment approach with a variation graph that includes known alternative 
variants at each genetic locus.23

The aim of the project  
Aim: To test different bioinformatic tools for detection of the CCR5-∆32 allele in ancient human genomes. 
Further to identify a possible associated CCR5-∆32 haplotype and characterize this haplotype among the 26 
populations in the 1000 Genomes Project dataset. Finally, to illustrate a likely evolutionary history of the 
CCR5-delta32 allele and its haplotype.

Data  
The Ancient dataset 
The Ancient dataset consist of whole genome shotgun-sequenced samples (NGS approach) from the 
Neolithic Age (NEO, 281 samples), Bronze Age (BA, 100 samples), and Viking Age (VK: 442 samples). The 
NEO dataset is unpublished and belongs to the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen, Denmark, 
whereas the BA and VK datasets are published.29,30 For this project, the Ancient dataset includes adaptor-
trimmed FASTQ files and their associated BAM files. The average read length of the aDNA reads is 60 bp 
and the distribution of coverages of the samples is shown in Fig. 4.

Methods 
All annotations in this study refer to the human reference genome GRCh37 assembly.32 
Detection of the CCR5-∆32 allele in the 1KGP samples 
In total, 12 paired-end sequencing samples were selected and their Whole genome sequence (WGS) and 
Whole exome sequence (WES) fastq files: (ftp://ftp.1000genomes.ebi.ac.uk/vol1/ftp/phase3/data/), with 
associated data statistics (ftp://ftp.1000genomes.ebi.ac.uk/vol1/ftp/data_collections-
/1000_genomes_project/1000genomes.sequence.index) were downloaded. The average read length for 
the datasets were 98 bp and 87 bp for the WGS and WES respectively. The 12 samples include 4 references 
(HG00179, HG00185, HG01506, HG01512), 4 heterozygous for the CCR5-∆32 allele (HG00171, HG00267, 
HG01537, HG01605) and 4 homozygous for the CCR5-∆32 allele (HG00320, HG00323, HG01684, HG01762). 
The origin of these specific samples were Spain (IBS) and Finland (FIN). 
Reads were aligned to the human reference genome GRCh37 assembly using Burrows–Wheeler aligner 
(BWA), the BWA aln and BWA mem.33 Files were sorted and indexed using Samtools (v.1.9) 34 and merged 
into libraries using Picard-tools (v.2.9.1).35 Duplicate reads were marked using Picard-tools MarkDuplicates 
(v.2.9.1).35 Indel realignments and base recalibration were performed with GATK (v.3.8-0)36 to reduce false 
positive variants calls. The VCF files were created using HaplotypeCaller36 both as individual VCF files and as 
a joint VCF files. The CCR5-∆32 genotype was viewed using bcftools.34  
Downsizing the sample depth of coverage to identify the CCR5-∆32 read threshold  
To investigate the CCR5-∆32 read threshold in a given sample that was required for BWA (mem and aln) 
and HaplotypeCaller to call the CCR5-∆32 genotype, the 12 samples initial depth coverages were calculated 
from the equation:37 
Depth of coverage(𝐶) = Read length(𝐿) ∗Number of reads (𝑁)

For downsizing the samples’ reads covering of the genomes (depth of coverage), DownsampleSam (Picard 
2.9.1)35 was applied, resulting in a dilution scale for each sample: 10x, 5x, 4x, 3x, 2x, and 1x target coverage 
(Fig. 5). The position 186066045 on chromosome 4 (SNP ID rs886041081), was used as an internal 
reference call.38

Detection of possible contamination of the ancient CCR5-∆32 samples 
The Schmutzi software40 was used to estimate a possible contamination degree (in relation to the 
mitochondrial genome) in each of the CCR5-∆32 ancient samples. The tool assembles an endogenous 
mitochondrial genome sequence, based on the recognitions endogenous/consensus nucleotides or possible 
contaminated nucleotides. The differentiation of a given nucleotide (ancient or present time) is relying on 
information from the deamination patterns, length distribution of the fragments, quality scores and 
whether the given nucleotide has matches in a database of putative contaminants (197 human 
mitochondrial). In brief, raw reads were aligned to the mitochondrial revised Cambridge Reference 
Sequence (rCRS) and MD tags were added to the BAM file. The schmutzi software was run with default 
parameters and an est output file was created including contamination estimates for each sample. The data 
parameters XYZ are assigned to: X average estimate of contamination, Y lower estimate of contamination 
and Z upper estimate of contamination. Depending on further downstream analysis, Z values larger than 
0,03 is to be considered as edge cases.40

In silico identification of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype 
Linkage disequilibrium (LD):41,42 The association between two alleles A and B at different loci, can be 
regarded as completely random if they are in linkage equilibrium, indicating that the probability that the 
two alleles occur together (pAB) is given by their individual frequencies (pA and pB). Linkage disequilibrium 
(LD) is the nonrandom association of the alleles at different loci and can be quantified by the coefficient of 
linkage disequilibrium DAB.  
DAB = pAB − pApB 
When DAB ≠ 0 for a given haplotype (allele pair AB), the DAB describes the deviation of observed haplotype 
frequency (pAB) from its corresponding allelic frequencies (pA and pB) expected under equilibrium.  
Depending on allele frequency of two loci, the value of D can be negative. Therefore, a normalized version 
of D is used (D’), where D is divided by the theoretical maximum difference between the observed and 
expected haplotype frequencies (range between 1 to -1). In this thesis the square of the correlation 
coefficient, r2 is used. The r2 is the correlation between two indicator variables (here between pairs of loci) 
– one representing the presence or absence of a particular allele at the first locus and the other 
representing the presence or absence of a particular allele at the second locus.  
𝑟2 = 
𝐷2
𝑃𝐴(1 −𝑃𝐴)𝑃𝐵(1 −𝑃𝐵) 
The r2 value describes exclusively whether the variation at two given loci are statistically dependent on 
each other. The r2 value range between 0 and 1 and when the two markers provide identical information 
the r2 = 1 and is said to be in perfect LD.   
LDLINK43 is a web-based toolset, including the application, LDproxy Tool, which is designed to 
query pairwise LD between variants in the different 1KGP population groups. LDproxy Tool was applied on 
1KGP’s five different European population groups; CEU, FIN, GRB, IBS, and TSI, where CCR5-∆32 was the 
query variant for identification of a haplotype.

Statistic 
Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE): HWE is used to compare allele frequencies in a population over a 
period of time.44,45 To consider a population to be “in equilibrium”, the allele frequencies should remain 
constant from generation to generation and there must not be any evolutionary influences.  
The Hardy-Weinberg equation is expressed as:  p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1 
In the population, p is the frequency of the "A" allele q is the frequency of the "a" allele. In the HWE, p2 
represents the frequency of the homozygous genotype AA, q2 represents the frequency of the homozygous 
genotype aa, and 2pq represents the frequency of the heterozygous genotype Aa. Further, the sum of the 
allele frequencies for all the alleles at the locus must be 1, so p + q = 1. 
The assumptions for Hardy-Weinberg principle are as following: (1) random mating, (2) the absence of 
natural selection, (3) a large population size, (4) no gene flow or migration, (5) no mutations.44 
Chi-squared test (χ2): If a human population evolves, their allele frequencies will change from one 
generation to the next. How far a population deviates from HWE can be measured using the chi-squared 
test (χ2).46

The p-value applied in this study is 0.05 and in relation to the HWE with df = 1, the critical value will be 
3.84.  The chi-squared test (χ2) is also applied to compare the different MAF between the 1KGP 
populations.

Inspection of the coverages among the ancient samples (Fig. 4), indicates that some of the samples had 
almost zero coverage, making the detection of the CCR5-∆32 allele impossible. New sample pools were 
created based on the threshold from the lowest coverage ancient CCR5-∆32 sample (coverage >= 0,398 x).

rearrangements and associated haplotype blocks the following imputation criteria can be applied to 
impute the CCR5-∆32 genotype.

Discussion  
Development of an approach to identify the CCR5-∆32 allele in ancient samples.  
Mapping reads to a reference graph can be a challenge if the reads differ substantially from the reference 
at the read’s location. This is the case with reads from the CCR5-∆32 allele. Table 2 shows that a minimum 
of 3 reads covering the deletion (WGS data) is required to call CCR5-∆32 with HaplotypeCaller. Ancient 
single-end reads with low sequencing quality scores, nucleotides from deamination and an indels 
variation (like CCR5-∆32) can lead to poor mapping quality score, causing the HaplotypeCaller allele depth 
(AD) to become too low, to detect a structural variation. For detecting the CCR5-∆32 allele in ancient 
samples with X1 coverage or even lower, we developed an approach that directly targets the reads 
covering the CCR5-∆32 allele. We restricted a positive CCR5-∆32 read to cover a least 10 bp on each side 
of the break point, to balance the sensitivity and specificity of a truthful CCR5-∆32 read. The CCR5-∆32 
allele was detected in 21 VK and two NEO samples. With these results we could show, for the first time, 
that the CCR5-∆32 allele was scattered around in Northern European countries at ancient time. We can 
document that the CCR5-∆32 allele was present in Denmark and Western Siberia in the Neolithic age. And 
not least, we could date back the CCR5-∆32 allele longer than previous studies.48 
Preparing and extracting aDNA usually comes with the risk of contamination of present-day DNA. All 23 
samples were investigated for present-day DNA, where only one sample showed signs of contamination. 
Running more contamination analysis (e.g., on nuclear DNA) and on the whole ancient data set would 
have been preferrable.  
Improvements on the described approach for detecting CCR5-∆32 in aDNA should include benchmarking 
with respect to sensitivity and specificity. This could be achieved with simulated ancient reads and 
statistical evaluation.  
Identification of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype in 1KGP samples 
The word "haplotype" is derived from the word "haploid," describing cells with only one set of 
chromosomes, and from the word "genotype," the genetic composition of an individual 
organism.49 Haplotypes can be defined as combination of alleles at different markers, where the LD 
patterns are in the absence of recombination. As the CCR5-∆32 allele can be traced back to ancient time 
and to a single Northern European origin, we aimed to look for a possible CCR5-∆32 haplotype. Using 
1KGP data and LDproxy Tool we discovered a CCR5-∆32 haplotype in the CEU population, comprising 86 
SNPs (Fig. 6A). Investigation of all 1KGP CCR5-∆32 samples showed that the CCR5-∆32 allele is correlated 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
with the CCR5-∆32 haplotype. Further we could identify CCR5-∆32 haplotype rearrangements and we 
were able to trace a specific CCR5-∆32 haplotype rearrangement in the Spanish population to the South 
America populations Peru and Colombia. The findings truly support the hypothesis that CCR5-∆32 and the 
CCR5-∆32 haplotype is of European origin and have followed the European migration pattern over the 
world, probably in this case, starting when the explorer Christopher Columbus set foot in America 
1492.50,51 
Investigation of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype in 1KGP samples 
The breakdown of a given haplotype will lead to haplotype blocks, which over evolutionary time will 
further break down to new smaller haplotype blocks. The breakdown event is mainly caused by 
recombination, as a haplotype is passed from one generation to the next during gametogenesis. The rate 
of a haplotype breakdown will furthermore depend on the presence of recombination hot-spots.42,52,53 
Interestingly, we did not see CCR5-∆32 haploblocks in CEU and FIN, only in TSI, IBS, GBR and AMR. Further 
investigation of the haplotype blocks did not reveal recurrent blocks involving only our Top2 SNPs without 
CCR5-∆32 and vice versa. Detailed investigations of the haploblocks lead us to the discovery of a new 
haplotype including 84 SNPs, but without CCR5-∆32 and the Top2 SNPs: The minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype, 
which is more frequent in IBS and GBR. 
With this knowledge we investigated all 2436 1KGP WGS samples. In the South Asian Ancestry (EAS) and 
East Asian Ancestry (EAS) populations, the few CCR5-∆32 alleles detected were also associated with the 
CCR5-∆32 haplotype and only 2 alleles with sporadic small haplotype block. In the samples with pure 
African Ancestry the pattern was completely different. Many different sizes of haplotype blocks were 
identified and, in some cases, the MAF of the SNPs were so high (> 0,5), that the reference nucleotide 
could exchange place with the variant nucleotide (Fig. 7C). In a study by Micheletti, et al. the authors 
found genetic evidence of Atlantic African ancestry in Latin America, where the majority of the 12.5 
million enslaved people arrived between the 16th and 19th centuries.54 With this knowledge we 
reinspected the MAF plots from the AFR and AMR populations (Fig. 7B and C) in relation to the 
significantly more frequently haploblocks in AMR compared to EUR ( Fig. 8 and Table 5). As a result, some 
of the observed haploblock in AMR could be inheritance from Atlantic African ancestry. Detailed analysis 
of both continents’ CCR5-∆32 haploblocks should be performed to clarify this hypothesis. 
 
 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
In summary the results from the investigation of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype in 1KGP samples are the 
followings: 
• 
The CCR5-∆32 allele correlates with the CCR5-∆32 haplotype or rearrangements of the haplotype 
• 
A defined minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype exists (84 SNPs, including the 2 supportive SNPs) 
• 
A haplotype with neither CCR5-∆32 nor TOP 4 SNPs exist 
• 
Recurrent haplotype blocks of all three haplotypes exist in EUR and AMR 
• 
Both the CCR5-∆32 haplotype and the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype have spread from EUR to AMR 
• 
CCR5-∆32 and the CCR5-∆32 haplotype is significantly more frequent in EUR than in AMR  
• 
The minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype is significantly more frequent in AMR than in EUR 
• 
There are more frequent and diverse haplotype blocks in AMR than in EUR 
• 
FIN: no breakdown of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype and the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype has not been 
found 
• 
AFR: the CCR5-∆32, CCR5-∆32 haplotype, the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype or haplotype blocks 
involving the TOP 4 SNPs does not exist 
• 
EAS showed no sign of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype, the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype nor haplotype 
blocks 
With these results a hypothesis could be stated, in relation to the CCR5-delta32 allele’s evolutionary 
history: the evolution of CCR5-∆32 haplotype started in Africa with the formation of haplotype blocks, 
which got expanded into the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype in Southern Europe. Hereafter, probably in 
Northern Europe, the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype turned into the CCR5-∆32 haplotype after a single 
genetic event, where the Top2 SNPs and CCR5-∆32 emerged on the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype. 
To justify this hypothesis, the fundamental proof would of course be that all the identified CCR5-∆32 
ancient samples harbor the CCR5-∆32 haplotype. 
Detection of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype in ancient samples and the imputation of CCR5-∆32 
genotype 
Calling the CCR5-∆32 haplotype in the 23 CCR5-∆32 ancient samples did indeed show that the whole 
CCR5-∆32 haplotype, including all 86 SNPs was present (Table 6) and the number of SNPs from the 
haplotype correlated with the average depth of the individual samples (Fig. 9). Imputation in ancient 
samples is possible, based on our new knowledge of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype, if a high-quality read did 
not cover the CCR5-∆32 break point in a given sample. The imputation criteria can be refined. Including 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
the presence of low-quality reads. The imputation criteria should also be benchmarked using other large 
databases with MAF data and use of more samples, including simulated ancient date.   
The MAF of the CCR5-∆32 allele and Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE)  
The Hardy-Weinberg principle states that allele frequencies in a population will remain constant from 
generation to generation in the absence evolutionary influences.44  As human populations evolve over 
time, their receptively allele frequencies will be subjected to changes. Applying the Hardy-Weinberg 
principle to the 1KGP dataset, with the null hypothesis being that the population is in Hardy–Weinberg 
proportions, and the alternative hypothesis being that the population is not in Hardy–Weinberg 
proportions, we could show that all the different 1KGP populations with respect to the CCR5-∆32 allele 
had no deviation from the HWE. This indicates that the CCR5-∆32 allele is at present time not under 
extensive evolution. Testing for HWE in the ancient data, chi2 test show that the different ancient 
populations were in equilibrium (Table 7). These results can only elucidate possible genotyping errors in 
our CCR5-∆32 detecting approach, as our ancient dataset violates all the assumptions for the Hardy-
Weinberg law (methods section). The CCR5-∆32 MAF from our dataset shows an increase during the 
different time periods; MAF 0,02 in the Neolithic age to 0,06 in the Viking age to 0.106 present-date, 
which could indicate a slight evolution force.  
A possible evolution hypothesis of the CCR5-∆32 allele  
Lactose tolerance caused by the dominant rs4988235 allele is an example of micro evolutionary changes 
in human lineages during historical times.55 The rs4988235 allele can be molecularly dated back to 5000 
years ago14, the same age we now have dated the CCR5-∆32 allele back to. Today, in some Northern 
European countries, the rs4988235 allele is moving towards fixation, as the rs4988235 allele had a clear 
phenotypic advantageous outcome during ancient European history.56  The rise in the CCR5-∆32 MAF 
appeared much earlier than the advantageous phenotypic outcome for HIV-1 virus, as the origin of the 
virus dates back only to the early 1900s in Cameroon, Africa.57 Different evolutional scenarios have been 
debated, such as Yersinia pestis 20 and the smallpox variola virus58 or other known/unknown diseases, 
which could give an evolutionary advantage to individuals bearing the CCR5-∆32 allele (in heterozygous or 
homozygous form) and consequently, an increase of the CCR5-∆32 MAF and spread in the European 
population.3 Comparing CCR5-∆32 MAF from the few aDNA studies, regional differences in the recent 
evolutionary history appears (Fig 3), however these results are limited by sample sizes and small numbers 
of sites. Today the NGS technologies are the new golden standard to investigate aDNA, which make it 
possible to interrogate many thousands of phenotypically variants simultaneously in large dataset. The

validation that the rs4988235 had been exposed to positive selection was demonstrated in two large 
ancient NGS dataset from the Bronze age and Viking age.29,59  
Investigating the CCR5-∆32 allele in ancient NGS dataset is challenged by the decreased read mapping 
accuracy, and thereby the increased reference bias. This can lead to wrong genotyping towards the 
wildtype allele. In contrast, the conventional PCR techniques are challenged by reference allelic dropout, 
leading to genotyping towards the CCR5-∆32 allele in homozygous form. Here, we have developed a new 
approach to identify the CCR5-∆32 allele in ancient NGS datasets. This approach led us to the discovery of 
the oldest origin for a CCR5-∆32 allele (Western Siberia) and furthermore to the proof that the CCR5-∆32 
allele was present in Denmark during Neolithic age. We identified the CCR5-∆32 haplotype, which we 
could trace back to the two CCR5-∆32 Neolithic samples. We could also show that the CCR5-∆32 allele 
with its associated haplotype was scattered around in Northern Europa at the Viking age. Further, that 
the CCR5-∆32 allele, CCR5-∆32 haplotype and haplotype blocks have a population-specific fingerprint in 
present time. Together these results support our evolutionary hypothesis of the CCR5-∆32 allele (Fig. 10). 
The cause of the increase of the CCR5-∆32 allele MAF, and its associated CCR5-∆32 haplotype, during 
ancient evolutionary time is yet to be discovered.

The CCR5-∆32 haplotype started in Africa with the formation of haplotype blocks, which got expanded into the 
minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype in Southern Europe. Hereafter, probably in the Northern Europe, the minus CCR5-∆32 
haplotype turned into the CCR5-∆32 haplotype, by a single genetic event, where the Top2 SNPs and CCR5-∆32 
emerged on the minus CCR5-∆32 haplotype.

Conclusions and Future Perspectives 
The aim of the project has been achieved. An approach for the detection the CCR5-∆32 allele in NGS 
aDNA samples, was developed. All the positive CCR5-∆32 samples were verified with the discovery of the 
CCR5-∆32 haplotype. Characterization of the CCR5-∆32 haplotype led to the finding of the CCR5-∆32 
minus haplotype and its possible genesis in Africa.  
The results from this project generate a lot of new questions. How old is the CCR5-∆32 and the CCR5-∆32 
haplotype? One striking result is the lack of CCR5-∆32 haplotype blocks, which could indicate no 
degradation of the haplotype. The Top2 SNPs (including CCR5-∆32 between them) span almost >74000 bp 
and yet, out of >1000 EUR alleles, we could only identify a single haplotype block with a single Top2 SNP. 
Only 3 different CCR5-∆32 haplotype blocks, including CCR5-∆32, were identified among 5000 alleles with 
a CCR5-∆32 MAF 0.029. These results suggest that the CCR5-∆32 haplotype is younger than the minus 
haplotype, as we observe significantly more haplotype blocks from the minus haplotype.  
The results suggest a slight evolutionary force on the CCR5-∆32 haplotype. CCR5-∆32 is the main suspect 
for this force. But the Top2 SNPs are dark horse suspects as well, even though they haven’t been 
identified with any clinical significance. LD data for the tree SNPs are all equal to 1, making them 
indistinguishable, regarding this evolutionary force. 
The uncovered historic information on CCR5-∆32 and fingerprints of the CCR5-∆32 haplotypes and 
haplotype blocks can form the basis for new population genetic studies, with the use of powerful 
bioinformatic tools.
