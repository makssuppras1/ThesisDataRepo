Abstract
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is one of the fastest-growing neurodegenerative disorders in
the world, characterized by the progressive degeneration of dopaminergic cells in the
substantia nigra and dopamine depletion in the striatum. Despite extensive research,
the exact causes of PD remain unknown, and there are no treatments to reverse the
disease progression. Current animal models often fail to replicate effectively human
neurophysiology, highlighting the need for new alternatives to explore neural connec-
tivity, disease mechanisms and potential therapies.
The current study focuses on the development of an early-stage in vitro model of
substantia nigra to enable a controlled, precise and on-demand modulation using
a light-based approach. Optogenetically modified human neural stem cells (hNSCs)
were differentiated into dopaminergic neurons, and the light effect on cell response was
investigated in real-time by integrating optogenetics with electrochemical detection.
By systematically optimizing light intensity, pulse duration, and stimulation patterns,
the influence of blue LEDs on neurotransmitter release was studied.
The results
confirmed successful neural differentiation and the system’s electrochemical sensitivity
to light-induced dopamine exocytosis. Additionally, the LED setups proved effective
in eliciting dopamine release with high precision, demonstrating their potential for
optogenetic stimulation of dopaminergic neurons. Higher light intensities generally
led to stronger responses, and peak amplitude remained consistent across recordings,
proving the reproducibility of the setup.
These findings set a foundation for the development of advanced neural models, where
controllable, scalable, reproducible and selective light-induced stimulation of intercon-
nected organoids will pave the way for advanced neurodegenerative disease research.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Parkinson’s Disease
According to a study of the global burden of diseases, from the Institute for Health Metrics
and Evaluation [1], neurological disorders are currently the leading source of disability
around the world, and the fastest-growing of these disorders (in age-standardized rates of
prevalence, disability, and deaths) is Parkinson’s disease.
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused mainly by
the degeneration of dopaminergic cells within the substantia nigra and its consequent
dopamine depletion in the striatum. This results in primarily motor problems, causing
symptoms like resting tremor and bradykinesia, or rigidity and akinesia [2, 3].
This neurodegenerative process is not only limited to motor functions, but also encom-
passes a range of non-motor symptoms, including cognitive decline, mood disorders, and
sleep disturbances, which significantly impact the quality of life of affected individuals.
Right now, the disease affects millions globally. From 1990 to 2015, the number of people
in the world with Parkinson’s disease doubled, reaching 6.2 million cases. In the next 15
years, the estimated cases are expected to increase, from 6 million in 2015 to 12 million
by 2040. Its prevalence is expected to continue growing in the next years significantly due
to ageing and by-products of industrialization like pesticides, solvents, and heavy metals
[2].
The causes of PD remain complex and multifactorial, involving both genetic and envi-
ronmental factors; there are some genes linked to PD predisposition but these only affect
5-10% of cases. The cause of idiopathic PD, which makes up most cases (90-95%), is still

1.1.1
Basal Ganglia
The basal ganglia is a group of interconnected subcortical nuclei that primarily regulate
motor control. It includes the striatum, the globus pallidus, the substantia nigra, and the
subthalamic nucleus.
Figure 1.1. (a) Illustration of motor control processes showing different stages of movement planning and
basal ganglia connectivity. The blue region illustrates the interaction between the thalamus, substantia
nigra, and cortex in movement planning and execution. (b) Section of the brain showing the basal ganglia
structures. Adapted from Kandel et al. and Virameteekul et al. [4, 5]
These structures are part of a neural circuit that originates in the cortex, where voluntary
motor decisions are made and signals are initiated. The motor signals are then translated
and processed through the basal ganglia nuclei, the striatum, the globus pallidum, and the
thalamus. Eventually, the motor signals travel back to the motor cortex and, through the
lower neurons and the spinal cord, synapse with motor neurons that innervate muscular
tissue, eventually executing the desired movement.
The basal ganglia interact with the motor cortex by regulating the activity of upper motor
neurons, although they do not form direct synapses with lower motor neurons, as can be
observed in Figure 1.1.
The regulatory motor nature of the basal ganglia is the reason why PD symptoms are
due to abnormalities within these circuits [6, 7].

Introduction
Functional Pathways of Basal Ganglia
In terms of projections, the basal ganglia are divided into afferent, efferent, and intrinsic
nuclei, and independently, substantia nigra pars compacta. The afferent nuclei receive
the input from cortex, thalamic, and substantia nigra regions. The information is then
further processed by intrinsic nuclei, the caudate nucleus and the putamen. The output
is primarily projected to the thalamus through the efferent nuclei, sending output signals
back to the cerebral cortex (mainly the frontal lobe). The last nucleus, substantia nigra
pars compacta, serves mainly as a projection to the basal ganglia circuit, supplying the
striatum with dopamine and modulating the overall motor response.
Dopaminergic Modulation: Substantia Nigra pars compacta
Substantia Nigra pars compacta is a nucleus in the basal ganglia that plays a key role in
motor execution. It is located in the midbrain and contains neurons with a high melanin
content, giving the region its characteristic dark colour. These neurons are dopaminergic
and use dopamine as a neuromodulator, i.e. signalling molecule synthesised by tyrosine
hydroxylase that modulates the function of other neurons. Dopamine binds to dopamine
receptors on postsynaptic cells, mainly G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), which upon
activation, trigger a biochemical signal cascade that either increases or decreases the
activity of neurons in the targeted brain regions.
Motor signals travel through a complex network of neural pathways that involve multiple
brain regions. While the overall process is highly complex and sometimes unclear, a sim-
plified view is that cortical signalling (primarily using glutamate) and striatal signalling
(primarily using GABA) either enhance or suppress dopamine release in SNpc. The re-
sponse from dopaminergic (DA) neurons is innervated to the striatum, where it plays a
key role in modulating the activity of striatal neurons and the motor output, as explained
later.
The dopaminergic signalling is essential for the proper functioning of the motor pathways,
as it affects both the initiation and correct execution of movements.
Afferent and Efferent Pathways
Striatum is the main afferent nuclei and is subdivided into three structures: the cau-
date nucleus, the putamen, and the nucleus accumbens. It is the first input nucleus in
basal ganglia, and primarily receives excitatory glutamatergic projections from the cortex
and thalamic regions, crucial for initiating voluntary movements [8], and dopaminergic
projections from SNpc, key to accurate and regulated execution of the movement.

Introduction
Striatum neurons, called medium spiny neurons, are inhibitory neurons that use GABA
as the neurotransmitter [9]. They have a high density of dopamine receptors, and are
classified into two types (Type 1 and Type 2) based on the predominant dopaminergic
receptors they express.
When substantia nigra pars compacta releases dopamine to the striatum, it can bind
to medium spiny neurons with D1 receptors, which trigger the direct pathway, or spiny
neurons with D2 receptors, which trigger the indirect pathway. The two circuits provide
the necessary balance for smooth motor function[8].
Direct and Indirect Pathway
When D1 receptors are activated, medium spiny neurons in the striatum project to the
internal segment of the globus pallidus (GPi). These projections are primarily GABAergic
and they suppress the activity of the GPi, [10]. In parallel, the GPe, which is not inhibited
in this pathway, inhibits the STN, preventing it from exciting the GPi too. [8]
All in all, the inhibition of these nuclei disinhibits the thalamus, allowing it to excite the
cortex and therefore the execution of voluntary movements[9]. This cascade is known as
the direct motor pathway.

2.1
Stem Cells in Neurodegenerative Diseases
2.1.1
Stem Cells
Stem cells are undifferentiated or partially differentiated cells characterized by two prop-
erties: can divide and differentiate into diverse specialized cell types and can self-renew
to produce more stem cells [17]. They are found in both embryonic and adult organisms,
but depending on the development stage, their capacity to differentiate into different cell
types varies, an ability known as cell potency.
Totipotent stem cells, only present in the zygote, have the highest differentiation potential,
and are able to divide and differentiate into cells of the whole organism and placenta.
Pluripotent stem cells, present in the blastocyst’s inner cell mass, can differentiate into
the cells of all germ layers but not extra-embryonic structures. Embryonic stem cells are
but only those of a closely related family, like hematopoietic or mesenchymal.
Adult stem cells are more limited and only found in a few locations in the body, like
the central nervous system or bone marrow. They can be multipotent, or unipotent, the
latter meaning they only differentiate into one type of cell.
In normal conditions, the potency is rarely reversible, meaning that once a cell is dif-
ferentiated, it typically cannot revert to a stem cell state. However, in 2006 a Japanese
team led by Shinya Yamanaka discovered a method to reprogram somatic cells back into
pluripotent stem cells, creating what are now known as induced pluripotent stem cells
(iPSCs). iPSCs are artificially generated, and they function similarly to PSCs but have
the ability to be patient-specific and available at any time.

2.1.2
Neural Stem Cells
Neural stem cells (NSCs) are multipotent stem cells that can differentiate into neurons
and glial cells of the nervous system, contributing to neurogenesis. During embryonic
development, NSCs are more widespread and develop into most of the growing brain.
However, in adults, their activity is significantly reduced.
Initially, it was believed that neural stem cells were not found in adult brains, but in the
early 1990s, it was demonstrated that some neural cells were capable of self-replicating and
giving rise to both neurons and glia cells. Now, it is known that some neural progenitor
stem cells can persist in highly restricted regions in the adult brain, primarily in the
subventricular zone and the hippocampus, and continue to produce neurons throughout
life, even though it is a rare process, and occurs at a very slow pace, meaning their
presence, proliferative and regenerative capacity is highly restricted in adults [18].

The ventral mesencephalon, also known as the ventral midbrain, is a small region in the
upper part of the brainstem, as shown in Figure 2.1. It is rich in dopaminergic neuroblasts,
and these stem cells are the primary source of dopaminergic neurons during embryonic
development and early life.

2.1.3
Differentiation of Stem Cells into Neurons
In human development, the differentiation of neural stem cells into dopaminergic neu-
rons is a complex process that requires precise temporal and spatial regulation of several
intrinsic and extrinsic factors like the presence of sonic Hedgehog (SHH), or activation
of Wnt Signalling. In vitro, these cells can be simply manipulated to expand and differ-
entiate in response to mitogenic factors such as fibroblast or epidermal growth factors,
added to the fact that the ability of dopaminergic neurons and their precursors to secrete
neurotrophic factors seems to further support the survival of endogenous dopaminergic
neurons [14, 20].
Different strategies and signalling cues have been used in vitro for dopaminergic differenti-
ation. In previous studies relevant to this work [14, 21, 22, 23], Glial-Derived Neurotrophic
Factor (GDNF) and dibutyryl-cAMP (db-cAMP) have been used for the differentiation of
neural stem cells (NSCs) into a dopaminergic phenotype. GDNF is a potent neurotrophic
factor that promotes the survival and morphological differentiation of dopaminergic neu-
rons. Dibutyryl cyclic AMP (db-cAMP) is a known inducer of TH activity, a phenotypic
marker of dopaminergic neurons and catecholamine synthesis. Both compounds have been
extensively studied and well-characterized for their role in dopaminergic differentiation.
Beyond biochemical cues, substrate composition has also been shown to influence dopamin-
ergic differentiation. Amato et al. found that culturing human neural stem cells on top of
pyrolysed carbon electrodes pre-treated with oxygen plasma and coated with PLL, trig-
gered dopaminergic differentiation and significantly enhanced the expression of TH and
β-tubulin (III) on the cultured cells, even in the absence of differentiation factors [14].

2.2
Stimulation Techniques in PD models
To better understand disease mechanisms and develop effective treatments, stimulation
techniques to modulate dopamine (DA) levels are widely used in in vitro, ex vivo, and in
vivo PD models. Various stimulation strategies have been studied, including electrical,
optical, or pharmacological. In this study, we will broadly classify them in (a) models
that mimic disease progression by inducing neurodegeneration (like neurotoxin models),
useful for studying PD physiopathology, and (b) models that can modulate dopamine
release on demand (like electrical, chemical or light-based models), which provide insights
into dopamine dynamics and therapeutic interventions.
2.2.1
Toxin-based Models
Toxin-based animal models aim to trigger damage in the substantia nigra by injection of
exogenous chemicals, to study the PD physiology, as well as test potential therapies to
reverse or slow down neural degeneration.
There are two main neurotoxins used, 6-hydroxydopamine (6-OHDA), which is injected in
the substantia nigra or striatum and induced selective degeneration of dopaminergic neu-
rons; and 1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine (MPTP) which causes dopamin-
ergic cell death, simulating advanced-stage PD in one week [26].
However, these models show limitations, as they cannot represent the dynamic and pro-
gressive stages of the disease in a single subject and often require irreversible cell damage
to induce symptoms.
2.2.2

Theoretical Background
Under normal physiological conditions, neurons maintain a negative resting membrane
potential, where the intracellular environment remains negatively charged relative to the
extracellular space.
When a neuron receives a stimulus above a certain threshold, such as an incoming signal
from another neuron or direct electrical stimulation, different channels open, allowing
positive ions to get into the cell. This influx of positive charge causes the membrane
potential to become less negative, a process known as depolarization. Depolarization is
achieved when the membrane potential becomes positive.

terminal, where it triggers dopamine release. The graph (B) shows the changes in membrane potential
during an action potential, including depolarization, repolarization, and hyperpolarisation phases.
The consequence of depolarization is the generation of an action potential, a rapid elec-
trical signal that propagates along the neuron’s axon toward the synaptic terminal. Upon
reaching the axon terminal, the depolarization can lead to the fusion of synaptic vesi-

Theoretical Background
Electrical and Chemical Stimulation
Electrical stimulation in the field of PD can be used both therapeutically and as a tool to
study and modulate neural circuits. Electric-based therapies such as deep brain stimula-
tion are very used to treat motor symptoms. However, it is highly invasive and risky, as
it involves electrode implantation in the basal ganglia. Other electric techniques, such as
trans-cranial electrical stimulation are non-invasive but lack specificity, as they activate
several types of neurons within the heterogeneous brain tissue. In brain models, electrical
stimulation also lacks precision, as it is applied with a stimulation electrode and it results
in the depolarization of all the neurons in the stimulation field.
Similarly occurs with chemical stimulation.
According to the literature, neurons can
undergo depolarization with exogenous stimulation such as buffers with high potassium
concentration. Potassium-based solutions have been already used as a model to study
dopamine release in neurons and their effect well-documented [14, 16, 27].
In both cases, the stimulation triggers cell depolarization on demand, but non-specifically,
and cannot be reliably used in complex models where other neurons, such as GABAergic
and cholinergic, are present, since their simultaneous activation has shown to lead to
unintended synaptic interactions that can alter the overall signal interpretation, as shown
by Melchior et al. [28]. Moreover, chemical stimulation setups involve the addition of
external solutions, creating the need to replace the media after every stimulation. This
could limit the integration of this stimulation into closed systems such as microfluidic
devices.
These methods can provide a baseline control to understand how dopamine exocytosis
works in simple models, but lack spatial precision and single-cell resolution. Therefore,
it would be ideal to develop a strategy that would allow precise stimulation, both spatial
and temporal, and have a reversible effect, being able to mimic different stages of the
disease in the same model.

wavelength [30].

Understanding ChR2
Opsins are membrane-bound proteins sensitive to specific wavelengths of light that can
induce different cellular responses. One of the most commonly used opsins in optogenetics
is a light-gated cation channel called channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2), which can be activated
by blue light.
Nagel et al. tested the specific excitation wavelength of Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2) by
measuring photocurrents at different wavelengths to determine its action spectrum. They
used oocytes expressing ChR2 and measured light-induced inward currents under voltage-
clamp conditions. They found out that ChR2 has a peak absorption at 460 nm (blue light)
but can shift up to 485 nm in more alkaline media (pH 9) [30].

Theoretical Background
Applications of Optogenetics
Since it was discovered, optogenetics has been widely applied in the field of neuroscience
for the precise stimulation of neural populations in vitro and in vivo.
On the one hand, optogenetics serves as a tool for understanding physiopathology, offering
the possibility to unveil hidden mechanisms of the development of diseases. For example,
Crompe et al. combined in vivo electrophysiological recordings in optogenetically modified
rats, to study both healthy and parkinsoninian physiology. By targeted light stimulation,
they activated or inhibited different components of the basal ganglia, such as the cortex,
globus pallidus, and sub-thalamic nucleus.
This allowed them to analyse the specific
contributions of each structure in the imbalance of firing rates of striatal neurons involved
in the affected pathways of movement [32].
On the other hand, optogenetics can be highly useful in modelling specific disease char-
acteristics, and several optogenetic preclinical models have been developed to study dif-
ferent hallmarks of PD. For example, Kim et al. developed a human model with induced
pluripotent stem cells (iPSC), and used optogenetic techniques to investigate the role of
alpha-synuclein aggregates in the development of PD. They used blue LEDs (470 nm, 70
mW) in vitro to trigger the release of the protein aggregates and study the consequences
in the surrounding neural tissue in real-time [33].
Furthermore, optogenetics enables unparalleled experimental control, providing the flex-
ibility to dynamically modulate cell responses and replicate different stages of PD within
the same model, facilitating the study of the disease progression without time constraints.
For instance, Lee et al. developed an optogenetic rat model of PD that could be induced
to different severities of the disease through optical stimulation of the substantia nigra,
by optimizing light parameters such as frequency (5-50Hz) and duration (10-100ms), and
assessing the effects caused in the degeneration and malfunction of neurons, proving that
the severity of rigidity correlated with the illumination exposure [26].
The implications of optogenetics also extend beyond basic research; the ability to activate
or inhibit specific neuronal populations with light, together with its minimally invasive
nature, positions it as a promising tool for the development of advanced and personal-
ized therapeutic strategies for PD that could potentially restore normal function in the
affected brain circuits [29]. Cell therapy, combined with light, could enable the selective
stimulation and release of dopamine on demand, reversing PD condition while preventing
dyskinesia. For instance, Vasudevan et al. proposed the transplantation of optogenetically
modified stem cell-derived dopaminergic neurons to enable the restorement of dopamine
levels in patients in the future [16, 24].

2.3.1
Light Source in Optogenetics
When selecting a light source for optogenetic experiments, two critical factors must be
considered: the wavelength and intensity of light.
Opsins have specific activation spectra, each with a peak wavelength at which it is most
eﬀiciently activated. For example, as mentioned before, Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2) is
optimally activated by light with a wavelength close to 470 nm. Choosing a light source
that matches this peak wavelength is critical to achieving effective cell modulation [34].
On the other hand, the required light intensity depends on different parameters related
to the cells and setup. On one side, the activation threshold of the opsins defines the min-
imum energy needed to generate a response. On the other side, setup-related parameters
to be considered include the area and distance to the cells to be stimulated, and beam’s
collimation. For instance, larger areas might require higher power to reach the necessary
intensity across the field due to beam divergence. Similarly, light intensity decreases with
distance, so as distance increases, more power will be needed. Additionally, the use of
collimation lenses can lead to more uniform stimulation with lower energy output, while
wider beams may require higher intensity to achieve the same effect.
However, it is key to consider that excessively intense beams due to high power, too
close proximity, or the use of a collimated lens, can lead to over-stimulation and heat
generation, causing cell damage or death. Albers et al. have shown the risks of local
heat resulting from the light application and demonstrated how even low light intensities
could alter neuronal activity [35]. This emphasizes the importance of using experimental
controls to differentiate between the optogenetic effects and the light-induced artifacts
and highlights the need to carefully select the parameters to achieve a balance between
the desired neural activation while avoiding unintended detrimental effects.
Among the available light sources for optogenetics, lasers and LEDs are the most common.
Lasers provide high collimation and intensity, minimising beam divergence and allowing
precise and effective light delivery. However, they are expensive, have limited wavelength
options, restrict activation to a highly localized region and pose a higher risk of tissue
damage due to phototoxicity and thermal effects caused by the high energy output and
power consumption. Additionally, they present a limitation for neural implantation due
to their bulkiness and reliance on external power sources. Conversely, LEDs are cost-
effective, durable, and versatile due to their compact size and ability to function wirelessly.
Their low operating temperature minimizes the risk of heat-induced tissue damage, and
the emitted light can be easily modulated to provide precise control over light intensity
and pattern, enabling reduced phototoxicity [36].

However, lasers frequently have a narrower spectral width (around 1 nm) which means

Alternatively, Liu et al. proved the use of blue LEDs (470 nm), for remote control of ChR2

They
aimed to study dopamine dynamics in real-time, and develop neuromodulation strategies
for real-time dopamine regulation, advancing therapeutic interventions. In the study, they
optimized light pulse duration and frequency [39].
Overall, all light sources show their advantages and inconveniences depending on the
experimental context. Lasers have highly collimated beams enabling highly precise tar-
geting but they are bulky, more expensive, and can induce thermal damage. LEDs reduce
photo- and thermal toxicity and are more versatile and cheap, but their broader spectral
width could reduce opsin activation and lead to cross-stimulation.
However, despite these differences, both types share a common need: they must be inte-
grated into hybrid systems combining optogenetics and monitoring techniques for their
application to neural models. This will allow a closed-loop system to study the effects
of stimulation, optimize the parameters accordingly, and gain deeper insights into neuro-
transmitter dynamics and brain function.

2.4
Biomarkers and Detection Techniques
To fully understand dopamine signalling in brain models, it is key to integrate controlled
stimulation with real-time monitoring of the excitability of cells. Assessing cell viability
and response to light in real-time is crucial to control the modulation of target brain
regions and to understand the physiology of the disease or response to drugs.
This monitoring could be achieved by constantly measuring different biomarkers of dopa-
minergic neurons, such as tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) expression, the enzyme that cataly-
ses the conversion of the amino acid L-tyrosine to L-DOPA, the precursor of dopamine,
or by visually assessing neuromelanin, the black pigment characterizing substantia nigra
cells. However, the quantification of these biomarkers requires time-consuming assays
limiting their applicability for real-time assessment. Similarly, techniques like immunocy-
tochemistry do not provide immediate feedback on neuronal phenotype or viability and
additionally contain compounds that damage irreversibly the cells.
Given the challenges associated with phenotypic biomarkers, dopamine itself emerges as a
more suitable candidate for monitoring cell viability and response of dopaminergic neurons
in real-time. Dopaminergic neurons are defined by their ability to synthesize and release
dopamine, and hence measuring the levels of this neurotransmitter can directly reflect the
functional state of these neurons.
Moreover, the role of dopamine in the pathophysiology of Parkinson’s disease makes it
particularly relevant as a biomarker: the loss of dopamine is the main hallmark of PD,
and monitoring its levels can provide insights into the progression of the disease and also
the outcomes of any therapy tested in PD models.
Various techniques, including neuroimaging, microdialysis, or enzymatic assays, have been
developed for DA quantification. However, they show some limitations such as invasive-
ness, delayed response, high cost, complexity or the need for sample pre-treatment [40].
Electrochemistry emerges as a potential alternative to solve these challenges. Electro-
chemical methods are among the most used biosensing techniques due to their simplicity,
versatility, ease of use and high sensitivity. Additionally, due to the electroactive nature
of dopamine, it is possible to dynamically detect its release from neurons in real time [14].
2.4.1
Electrochemical Methods for Dopamine Detection
There are different electrochemical measures for dopamine detection, such as chronoam-
perometry, cyclic voltammetry, fast-scan cyclic voltammetry, or impedance.
Among
these, cyclic voltammetry and chronoamperometry are the most widely used techniques.
22
==PAGE:35==
Theoretical Background
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) allows the study of dopamine’s oxidation-reduction behaviour
by measuring current as a function of a sweeping potential. CV is less sensitive than
chronoamperometry and variations in scan rate or electrode surface conditions can affect
the voltammogram morphology, making CV more complex for quick quantification across
different samples. Also, CV measurements do not directly provide real-time data, and
preprocessing is needed to convert voltammograms into time-dependent signals [41].
In contrast, chronoamperometry (CA) is a simple technique that involves applying a con-
stant potential to trigger the reduction or oxidation of the analyte and measuring the
resulting current, enabling real-time monitoring of DA release with temporal resolution.
Nevertheless, chronoamperometry cannot provide detailed information about the electro-
chemical processes involved in the generated current [42].
A third electrochemical measurement is electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS).
It works by applying an alternating current potential (AC) over a range of frequencies to
obtain the impedance (resistance to flow of electrical current). The results obtained are
displayed in a Nyquist plot. EIS is useful to study how coatings affect the conductivity
of materials such as pyrolytic carbon, as changes in charge transfer resistance caused by
a functionalization step can be detected but it is not well-suited for detecting dopamine,
as it lacks the sensitivity and selectivity required for neurotransmitter quantification [43].
Chronoamperometry
The first amperometric biosensor was developed by Clark in 1956. It was an oxygen sen-
sor that generated currents proportional to oxygen concentration when a potential was
applied to a platinum electrode. Since then, amperometric measurements have evolved
significantly and have been applied to multiple fields, including neurotransmitter detec-
tion.
As mentioned earlier, chronoamperometry consists of applying a constant potential to
trigger the oxidation or reduction of the analyte of interest. For biosensing, it is important
that the potential is slightly higher (for oxidation) or lower (for reduction) than the redox
potential of the electroactive molecule, to ensure a suﬀicient driving force for oxidation
(or reduction).
For example, in dopamine biosensing, the applied potential needs to be higher than the
dopamine oxidation peak, to effectively trigger the oxidation of dopamine into dopamine-
o-quinone. The triggered oxidation can then generate a measurable current that can be
detected in real-time and is correlated to dopamine concentration [42, 44]. A diagram of
how the chronoamperometry works in the context of dopamine is shown in Figure 2.7.
23

Theoretical Background
Figure 2.7. Schematic representation of chronoamperometric detection of dopamine release. The top

Another advantage of chronoamperometric recordings is that they allow dopamine mea-
surement from a single-cell level to whole populations. The latter measures the average
response of a large number of cells, while single-cell studies allow the measurement of
DA in individual cells and can provide more insights into the biology of neural responses.
However, it has some challenges, since the size of the cell is small, the content of DA in a
single cell is very low and the cells contain many components, which may interfere with
the accuracy of the detection [45].
However, chronoamperometry also has limitations, as other electroactive compounds in
body fluids, such as ascorbic or uric acid, epinephrine or norepinephrine are oxidized
at the same potential as dopamine, generating a detectable chronoamperometric current
that could interfere with the signal of interest. In the past 50 years, extensive research
has focused on studying electrode modifications to overcome this limitation. While some
interfering compounds, such as ascorbic acid, have been already addressed, the detection
of structurally similar neurotransmitters with overlapping redox activity remains still a
challenge.
Recently, 2nd generation biosensors have emerged as a potential solution: by incorpo-
rating receptors, aptamers or enzymes such as laccase on the electrodes, these systems
24

Theoretical Background
can improve dopamine selectivity against other neurotransmitters. In these biosensors,
dopamine acts as a mediator of electron transfer between the redox centre of the enzyme
and the electrode, while a secondary substrate (such as oxygen or hydrogen peroxide)
can be either reduced or oxidized by the enzyme, facilitating a mediated electron transfer
mechanism [46].
Additionally, with the progression of research, chronoamperometric techniques have also
improved in other areas. For instance, the incorporation of nanomaterials, such as gold
nanoparticles or nanostructured surfaces has allowed the achievement of enhanced selec-
tivity, sensitivity, lower detection limits, faster responses and selectivity, and application
to more complex setups [14, 16].
For example, Abe et al. developed a device for a 3D spheroid culture with integrated
amperometric platinum sensors to visualize dopamine release after chemical stimulation
with potassium buffer. They obtained a rapid detection of dopamine spikes within less
than one second after stimulation [47].
Similarly, Vasudevan et al.
[16], developed a complex omnidirectional fiber for both
dopamine detection and optogenetic control for cell therapy applications.
The device
consisted of a laser diode coupled to an optical fiber coated with conductive pyrolytic car-
bon and micro-windows that allowed omnidirectional light emissions. The electrochemical
detection was performed by the pyrolytic carbon, whose surface acts as an electrode for
real-time chronoamperometric measurement of dopamine release.
2.4.2
Electrodes
For any electrochemical measurement, it is essential the use electrodes, conductor mate-
rials that are used as an interface between the potentiostat and the electrolyte solution,
enabling the transfer of electrical signals in the system. Electrodes are usually presented
in a three-electrode configuration, consisting of a working (WE), a counter (CE), and a
reference electrode (RE).
In the WE the potential is applied through a potentiostat (as a function of the RE), and
the electrochemical reaction of interest occurs. It must be made of a redox-inert material
in the potential range of interest. The CE completes the circuit by allowing current to
flow from the WE. The RE maintains a stable and well-defined potential to provide a
reference against which the potential of other electrodes can be measured.
25
==PAGE:38==
Theoretical Background
To record electrochemical signals, some additional elements are needed. First, the elec-
trolyte solution, which is the conductive medium that allows the ion movement between
electrodes. The amount of ions in the solution determines its ionic strength. The elec-
trolyte must be stable over the applied potential range, and inert to redox processes.
Secondly, the electroactive species of interest are the molecules that undergo oxidation
or reduction through electron transfer reactions at the surface of the WE, generating
measurable current signals. Each electroactive molecule has a characteristic oxidation
and reduction potential, that depends on 1) the molecule itself, 2) the electrode material
and its related electron transfer kinetics and 3) the electrolyte and its ionic strength [48].
Pyrolytic Carbon
The use of pyrolytic carbon in electrode development has recently gained significant atten-
tion in electrochemistry due to its excellent conductivity, stability, and biocompatibility
properties.
Pyrolytic carbon is produced through the pyrolysis of organic precursors like SU-8. The
composition of the precursor and the pyrolysis temperature determine properties such as
electrical conductivity, mechanical strength and structure of the final material.
Pyrolytic carbon electrodes have a high electroactive area and eﬀicient charge transfer
kinetics due to their inherent 3D layered structure, which enhances surface-to-volume
ratio compared to other substrates. Furthermore, it allows the fabrication of structures
with high reproducibility and cost-effectiveness up to micro/nanoscale, further improving
sensitivity and conductivity when integrated into a substrate. This high sensitivity makes
pyrolytic carbon electrodes very effective in the detection of low analyte concentrations,
an essential factor for dopamine detection [49].
Several studies, such as that of Vasudevan et al., support their application in neurotrans-
mitter detection by proving the successful detection of DA release using pyrolytic carbon
integrated into optical fibers, which in turn also proves their potential for optogenetic
studies [16].
Beyond their electrochemical properties, pyrolytic carbon is also biocompatible. As men-
tioned earlier in this section, Amato et al. showed how pyrolytic carbon triggers the
spontaneous differentiation of hNSC into dopaminergic neurons [14], making it even more
suitable for cell-biosensors and neural interfaces.

3.4
Electrochemical Electrode Characterization
As mentioned in Section 2.4.2, the oxidation peak potential of dopamine is not strictly
constant; it can vary depending on several factors, including the electrode material and
its electron transfer kinetics, the electrolyte composition and its ionic strength, or the
nature of the analyte, which can lead to adsorption events or changes in mass transport,
which may slightly alter the redox peak characteristics [48].
To study DA behaviour under the specific experimental conditions of this work, an elec-
trochemical characterization was performed to assess the system’s ability to detect the
analyte. The protocol was adapted from Amato et al. and consisted of three distinct
parts [14].
First, the functionality of the electrode and scan rate dependence was analyzed using
cyclic voltammetry (CV) with 10 mM potassium ferrocyanide (K4Fe(CN)6) and 10 mM
potassium ferricyanide (K3[Fe(CN)6]) as the redox couple in phosphate-buffered saline
(PBS, pH 7.4).
This redox probe was selected because it is a well-studied, reversible redox couple, ensuring
reliable electron transfer and minimal fouling for accurate and reproducible posterior
dopamine measurements [48]. Different scan rates were tested to observe their influence on
the detected current. The scan rate determines how fast the applied potential is scanned;
higher scan rates lead to higher amplitudes, likely due to a thinner diffusion layer thickness
due to insuﬀicient time to widen, leading to steeper concentration gradients and higher
currents. Conversely, at low scan rates, the diffusion layer has time to widen, resulting in
steadier concentration gradients and lower currents [48]. However, excessively high scan
rates can compromise the precision of the potential scan, as capacitive current increases
and can hinder signal interpretation.
Second, cyclic voltammetry scans were performed at different concentrations of dopamine
( 25 µM, 100 µM, and 250 µM) in nitrogen-purged PBS (20 min) prepared immediately
before use. This step was crucial to (1) validate the electrode’s sensitivity to the neu-
rotransmitter, (2) assess any difference in the cyclic voltammogram and oxidation peak
caused by concentration shifts, and (3) to determine the oxidation potential for dopamine,
which was used for the later recording of the chronoamperometric signal.
Lastly, the effect of poly-L-lysine (PLL) coating on dopamine oxidation potential and
conductivity was investigated. On the one hand, CV was performed to study any change
in the electron transfer properties and effect on the voltammogram, i.e. to assess whether
a coating alters the redox behaviour of dopamine. On the other hand, EIS was performed

3.5.1
Chemical Stimulation
Previous studies by Amato et al. and Vasudevan et al. have reported the use of 150 mM
K+ potassium solution to cause depolarization in neurons and trigger the release of
dopamine vesicles [16]. Higher potassium concentrations should be avoided, as they could
cause irreversible depolarization block [51].
In this study, chemical detection was used as a baseline control under well-known con-
ditions to establish a reference framework to understand how dopamine exocytosis and
detection should work [14, 16]. Additionally, the suitability of differentiation media for the
detection of dopamine was tested, as to our knowledge, previous experiments have primar-
ily conducted measurements in buffer solutions. Establishing whether the differentiation
media functions as an optimal electrolyte to perform electrochemical detection without
the need for solution replacement would be particularly relevant for future experiments,

where more complex cultures, such as organoids or connectoids, may be more sensitive to
media changes. Additionally, the possibility of integrating seamlessly dopamine measure-

would facilitate and streamline the execution of the experiments.
The decision was made to start with chemical stimulation to test the viability of hNSC

light pulse could lead to misinterpretation, initially suggesting insuﬀicient light intensity
or ineﬀicient gene expression, while potentially indicating cell death, meaning that no

Immunostaining
ICC requires immunostaining, which consists of the use of antibodies combined with
fluorophores to detect target antigens with high specificity.
Antibodies are proteins made from four peptides connected by disulphide bonds. They
have a constant region (Fc) which is species-specific and serves as the binding site for
secondary antibodies, and a variable region (Fab) which binds to the epitope of the antigen
[52]. The regions can be observed in Figure 3.12.
Figure 3.12.
Illustration of indirect antibody staining in immunocytochemistry.
The primary an-
tibody binds specifically to the antigen of interest through the variable region (Fab), while the sec-
ondary antibody, conjugated to a fluorescent label, binds to the constant (Fc) region of the primary
antibody. This indirect method amplifies the signal and provides flexibility in detection. Created in
https://BioRender.com.
The immunostaining process consisted of several steps. First, cells were fixed to pre-
serve their morphology and immobilize the target antigens. A common fixation agent,
paraformaldehyde (PFA), was used to cross-link proteins to maintain their structure.
Then, to allow antibodies penetration to the cell, the cell membrane was permeabilized
using the detergent agent Triton X [53]. To prevent non-specific binding (which would
lead to a fluorescence signal in areas where the antigen is not present, resulting in false
positive results), a blocking solution (horse and goat serum, (STPBS)) was applied, as
their proteins can bind to surfaces and block sites where the antibody could stick.

For the antibody staining, two antibodies were used: primary and secondary. The primary
antibodies were raised elsewhere against the target antigen (TH or β-tubulin III), and
derived from a specific host species such as rabbit or mouse. The secondary antibodies
were raised elsewhere to recognize and bind to the constant Fc region of the primary
antibodies.
They must be raised in a different species than the primary antibody to
recognise and bind to them (e.g., a goat anti-mouse secondary antibody will bind to any
mouse-derived primary antibody). The process is illustrated in Figure 3.12.
This approach, known as indirect immunostaining, allows signal amplification and enables
multiple primary antibodies from the same host species to be detected with a single-
labelled secondary antibody. This eliminates the need for custom fluorescently labelled
primary antibodies, which significantly reduces costs.

A final electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurement was performed to
assess the effect of the PLL coating on the impedance of the electrode.
According to previous literature by Heiskanen et al., coating an electrode with poly-
L-lysine would be expected to reduce the charge transfer resistance (Rct) when using
ferrocyanide/ferricyanide as the redox probe. Due to its positively charged nature, PLL
would be expected to electrostatically attract the negatively charged species of the ferro-
cyanide/ferricyanide, facilitating the probe access to the electrode and leading to enhanced
electron transfer and lower impedance, regardless of the coating layer thickness [54].
However, as can be observed in Figure 4.4, the obtained results show the opposite. After
the coating with PLL, the semicircle diameter in the high-frequency region of the Nyquist
plot was increased, indicating a higher Rct consistent with the idea that PLL acts as a
barrier to electron transfer between the electrode and ferrocyanide/ferricyanide probe.
Additional replicates were performed (data not shown), showing consistent behaviour.
Figure 4.4. Nyquist plots of impedance before and after PLL treatment. The increase in both real (ZRe)
and imaginary (ZIm) components after PLL treatment indicates an increased charge transfer resistance
and altered electrochemical behaviour, explained by reduced electron transfer eﬀiciency.
There is no plausible explanation for the obtained results, except that previous studies
have been conducted using gold or platinum electrodes, whereas this study used oxygen
plasma-treated carbon, which may have introduced additional effects. However, further
investigation will be required to address this issue.
The increased resistance could limit the detection of dopamine, but previous results sug-
gested that this unexpected increase in impedance is acceptable as dopamine oxidation
peaks remained well-defined after the PLL coating.

For the differentiation media (DM) test, DM in the cell culture was used as the baseline
medium, and a solution of 450 mM KCl in DM was used as stimulation solution. Upon so-

gradual depolarization and repolarization, showing a slower return to baseline.

several molecular components, such as proteins, which could interact with dopamine or

The electrode behavior observed in our results was contrasted with published literature.
Modestov et al. [55] attribute the photoelectric effect observed in pyrolytic carbon elec-
trodes to the electronic properties of sp2-hybridized systems and their associated π or-
bitals, which have relatively low energy gaps and are therefore easier to excite. When
light of suﬀicient energy irradiates the electrode, it excites electrons from the π-bonding
orbitals to the π∗-antibonding orbitals, leaving behind positively charged holes. The ex-
56
==PAGE:69==
Results and Discussion
cited electrons, now carrying higher energy, are driven across the space charge layer (SCL)
by its electric field, which under unstimulated conditions, separate and prevent the charge
exchange between the electrode and the electrolyte.
When these high-energy electrons reach the electrode surface, they reduce substances in
the electrolyte such as oxygen, forming reactive oxygen species (ROS). Meanwhile, the
positively charged holes left in the electrode can oxidize molecules such as water, producing
protons and oxygen gas.
Together, these redox reactions create a continuous flow of
electrons between the electrode and the electrolyte, which is recorded as a measurable
photocurrent.
This mechanism could explain the peaks observed in our control experiments, emphasizing
the importance of considering the intrinsic photoelectric properties of materials used as
electrodes in optogenetic experimental setups.
These findings demonstrate that light stimulation alone can generate a current at the
electrodes, emphasizing the importance of including controls in optogenetic experiments to
distinguish between cellular responses and background signals. The < 1nA peak observed
in these control experiments represents the baseline amplification due to the electrode’s
photoelectric properties, which should be considered in future experiments.

2. The small LED has a wide angle, as has been discussed, due to which it may primarily

between continuous pulses should be further optimized.

Conclusion
5.1
Conclusion and Final Remarks
This study aimed to test and optimize the light parameters for controlled light stimulation
of optogenetically modified dopaminergic neurons while evaluating their dopamine release
in real time. By systematically optimizing light intensity, pulse duration, and stimulation
trains, we effectively studied the influence of different LEDs and light parameters on
modulating neurotransmitter release, while proving that the system has electrochemical
sensitivity to light-induced dopamine exocytosis.
First, the results obtained during the electrode characterization confirmed the electro-
chemical activity of the pyrolytic carbon electrode and provided insights into the influence
of dopamine concentration and poly-l-lysine coating on dopamine detection. Neverthe-
less, future experiments should focus on refining dopamine quantification, to establish a
clearer correlation between pulse amplitude and neurotransmitter concentration. More-
over, further characterization would also allow to establish the linearity and sensitivity of
the electrode, as well as any limits in the detection of dopamine levels.
Second, the chemical tests confirmed the ability of the cells to release dopamine, confirm-
ing successful dopaminergic differentiation. They also helped understand the effect of the
electrolyte during dopamine measurements. While they proved the suitability of differen-
tiation cell media (DM) as an electrolyte for the real-time chronoamperometric recording
of dopamine release, the signals obtained were less defined compared to reference stud-
ies that used buffer solutions, where peaks appeared sharper. On the one hand, these
wider peaks could result from the composition of the media, which might affect electron
transfer, increase viscosity, or interact with dopamine or the electrode surface, leading
81
==PAGE:94==
Conclusion
to broader signals. In the future, further experiments would be needed to elucidate the
exact reasons for these variations. Additionally, significant variations were observed in
peak height across tests, likely due to variations in cell seeding, or manual pipetting of
the stimulation buffer. In the future, a more automated and streamlined process could
be developed to minimize or solve these variations.
The different control rounds, including positive controls with unmodified cells and nega-
tive controls with blank electrodes, provided some key insights. First, they validated that
all the recorded signals and results obtained were induced only by blue light stimulation.
Second, they revealed the presence of background signals, whose amplitude is dependent
on the duration of light exposure, revealing unexpected photoelectric properties of the
pyrolytic carbon electrode. Although low in amplitude, these signals could pose a limi-
tation in some studies, mostly when the concentration of the released dopamine is very
low. In these cases, obtaining a clear response peak could be challenging as it could be
overshadowed by the background signal. This effect was more pronounced with the big
LED, which produced larger background peaks, suggesting that background noise may
become greater with higher light intensities or collimated beams. Also, the pulse length
should be critically considered, as models that require longer stimulation times, might be
compromised in the threshold of dopamine concentration detection, potentially limiting
sensitivity in low-concentration conditions. In the future, further investigation into the
background signals should be performed, and it would be needed to validate whether
background peaks increase with light power in the same trend as with pulse length or
LED type. This would provide a clearer understanding of the compromise between cell
stimulation and background noise, enabling better design of future experiments.
The different optimization tests allowed to better understand the effects of light stimu-
lation patterns, intensity, and pulse duration on cell response and the recorded signal.
For example, optimization of the pulse duration determined the maximum duration of
pulse capable of eliciting a measurable dopamine response without inducing saturation;
excessively long pulses were found to be suboptimal, as they triggered a plateau in cell
stimulation and resulted in peak saturation.
Additionally, pulse train optimization revealed the importance of adequate spacing be-
tween stimulations to prevent signal accumulation and baseline shifts: too short stimu-
lation intervals led to signal aggregation and a misleading effect of increased amplitude
due to signal overlap. In the future, it would be needed to fully optimize pulse trains,
and fully adjust stimulation patterns to generate individual peaks without signal overlap,
to determine for different light intensities the exact interval able to trigger individual
measurable dopamine responses. In the future, a detailed big LED optimization remains

to be done, as only one round of optimization could be performed due to time constraints.
Although similar behaviour to the small LED was expected, this was not validated during
this research, so further tests are necessary to assess if the two LEDs have truly the same
behaviour or whether the collimation or higher intensities lead to unexpected events.
The main experiments confirmed the sensitivity to light of the dopaminergic neurons.
Cells were able to depolarize in response to light pulses and release dopamine, and the
neurotransmitter could successfully be measured in real time.
Furthermore, it was observed that higher light intensities generally led to higher responses,
and peak amplitude was found to be consistent across recordings, proving the reproducibil-
ity of the experiments.
However, comparative analysis revealed significant differences
across the average of the signals between replicates and LED types, likely due to the
random seeding and distribution of cells and the yet undefined light differences between
LEDs. In future experiments, a method should be developed to (1) detect and quantify
the concentration of the dopamine released, (2) measure the light intensity reaching the
electrodes and cells and (3) implement a systematic background correction to accurately
assess the real signal differences between the two LEDs. This would allow to establish
the linearity between pulse amplitudes, applied light energy, and response generated in
cells, and identify the source of variability across signals.
The results demonstrated that both the big and the small LED setups were effective in
eliciting dopamine release with high precision, confirming their potential for optogenetic
stimulation of dopaminergic neurons.
However, background noise remains a limiting
factor. Notably, the small LED, contrary to our initial expectations was found to be
suﬀicient for this purpose, successfully achieving control in hVM1 stimulation, while min-
imizing background signals that could hinder dopamine detection in experiments involving
low neurotransmitter concentrations, compared to the big LED. Therefore, it can be con-
cluded that for the stimulation of 2D cultures of hNSC, the small LED emerged as the
optimal choice. Nevertheless, for more complex systems such as organoids or in vivo mod-
els, it remains to be determined whether the small LED provides suﬀicient stimulation,
or whether the big LED will be necessary to ensure a detectable cell response, despite the
trade-off between increased background signal and higher power delivery. Future studies
should be performed to optimize signal detection, balancing cell response and background
interference.
Although the results obtained in this study were promising, it is important to note that
many tests remain to be performed. Most of the challenges hindering the execution of
more complex and complete tests during this project were due to the complexity of the

experimental design. One of the main factors slowing down the obtention of results was

times, intense cleaning after each use, and manual manufacturing and assembly, and the

replicates and in most of the rounds. Overall, each of these issues resulted in substantial
delays, as every failure required further discussions and an additional three weeks before
new experiments could be initiated.

the electrode holders will allow to increase the number of replicates, which is essential

results.

and different experimental parameters.
Building on these, future experiments should
explore further refinements in experimental design to optimize optogenetic stimulation
and electrochemical detection in more complex models.

Conclusion
5.1.1
Future Research Directions
Optogenetic stimulation of dopaminergic neurons in a controlled manner in vitro is possi-
ble, which demonstrates its potential for the development of Parkinson’s disease models.
By adjusting light parameters it is possible to precisely modulate the neuronal responses,
opening new possibilities in neurodegenerative disease research and the development of
future therapies based on neuronal stimulation.
However, there are still challenges to be addressed. As mentioned before, the next studies
should focus on improving system stability, refining data analysis methods, and extend-
ing electrochemical characterization to more accurately determine dopamine detection
thresholds. Once these aspects are optimized, the next step will be the exploration and
translation of these findings to more advanced models that more closely recapitulate the
complexity of brain physiology and Parkinson’s disease.
For example, the next step could involve the implementation of this approach into more
complex cell models such as pluripotent stem cells (iPSC) or even transitioning from two-
dimensional (2D) cultures to three-dimensional (3D) brain organoids, which could more
closely resemble human physiology and replicate in vivo conditions.
Further advancements could lead to their implementation into multi-chamber microfluidic
devices, such as connectoids envisioned by OpenMIND, which mimic the basal ganglia and
allow precise control of dopamine release on-demand into different compartments repre-
senting brain structures such as the striatum. This would provide unprecedented insights
into substantia nigra function and effect, neurotransmitter dynamics, and Parkinson’s
disease physiology, while also enabling the study of new therapeutic strategies.
Ultimately, successful results in this field could be the first step towards the extrapo-
lation of optogenetics tools to in vivo models, where the transplantation of genetically
modified dopaminergic neurons that respond to controlled light stimulation, could have
the potential to restore the lost dopamine levels in patients suffering from Parkinson’s
disease.
Overall, the present study provides a foundation for research in optogenetics in the field of
Parkinson’s disease, opening new opportunities for disease modelling and the development
of novel treatment strategies. Despite the limitations and technical challenges that still
need to be addressed, the findings achieved in this study provide a robust starting point
for the development of more precise and personalized therapeutic strategies in the field
of neuroscience and regenerative medicine.

stimulation): dopamine was successfully detected in the pyrolytic carbon upon optical
stimulation and could be compared with chemical stimulation assessing the differences in
cell response.

Detailed Cell Expansion and Culture Protocol
A.3.1
Preparation of Culture Flasks
Preparation of culture flasks for cell expansion was done following Amato et al. protocol
[14].
hNSCs require adhesion factors, for example, poly L-Lysine (PLL), to adhere, grow and
differentiate. Therefore, prior to cell seeding, culture flasks were prepared by coating
them with sterile PLL hydrobromide solution (10 µg/mL; Sigma-Aldrich Cat No. P6282)
in PBS, and left incubated for at least two hours in the humidified incubator (5% CO2 /
95% air) at 37 ◦C.
Right before cell seeding, the coated flasks were rinsed twice with PBS. The PBS was
kept in the flask until right before cell seeding.
A.3.2
Cell Thawing
HVM1 cells, including naïve and genetically modified clone C19, were kept frozen at
-150◦C until the start of the experiments.
For thawing, cells were kept on dry ice and quickly thawed using a warm water bath until
only a small frozen core remained in the vial.
The thawed cell suspension was added drop-wise to a T25 flask containing pre-warmed
media to minimize stress on the cells. The flask was left flat and unmoved for approxi-
mately 30 seconds to allow the cells to settle and minimize cell damage. After that time,
the flask was labelled accordingly and placed in a CO2 37◦C incubator.
The next day, the attachment and viability of cells were subsequently verified under a
microscope, and the cell media was replaced to avoid cell toxicity due to DMSO.
During the whole cell expansion, hVM1 cells were cultured in growth medium, containing
DMEM/F12 with GlutaMAX and additionally 6 g/L glucose, 5 mM HEPES, 0.5% (wt%)
AlbuMAX, 40 µM of non-essential amino acids, L-alanine, L-asparagine, L-aspartic acid,
L-glutamic acid, and L-proline, 100x diluted N-2 supplement, penicillin/streptomycin, and
20 ng/L of EGF and FGF (R&D Systems). Cultured cells were kept in the humidified
incubator (5% CO2 / 95% air) at 37 ◦C and the GM was replaced every two days.

A.3.3
Cell Passage
Cells were passaged after they reached approximately 70-80% confluence. Basal medium
(BM) containing Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10%
fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (pen-strep) and GM were pre-
warmed to 37◦C.
Trypsinization was performed by adding 1mL of TrypLE™Express Enzyme (1X), phenol
red (Gibco™, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) for cell detachment from culture substrates.
1ml was added to the T25 flask, followed by incubation at 37◦C for 7–10 minutes. Mean-
while, a fresh PLL-previously-coated T25 flask was prepared by removing PLL and wash-
ing twice with 5mL of PBS. After trypsinization, 5mL of BM was added to the cell flask
to facilitate detachment (ratio tryple:BM is 1:5). The final cell suspension, consisting of
5mL of BM and 1mL of tryple, was resuspended gently and transferred to a 15mL Falcon
tube, ensuring no cells remained in the flask.
To determine cell density, 200µl of the cell suspension was transferred to an Eppendorf
tube under sterile conditions. The cell density of the 200µl sample was determined using
a cell counting machine.
The remaining suspension was centrifuged at 700 rpm for 7 minutes. After centrifugation,
the supernatant was removed, and the cell pellet was resuspended in GM. The number
of cells required for seeding was calculated based on the experimental plan and usually
ranged between 300.000 to 700.000 cells/ml. The appropriate volume of cell suspension
was added to the pre-warmed GM and transferred to the T25 flask. The flasks were then
incubated at 37◦C, and proliferation continued.
A.3.4
Cell Freezing
For cryopreservation, cells were trypsinized as described previously. The detached cells
were transferred to a Falcon tube and centrifuged at 700rpm for 7 minutes. The cells
were resuspended calculating the final density to be 4-7 million cells/ml, considering 1ml
total volume per cryovial. The freezing solution was prepared with 10% dimethyl sulfox-
ide (DMSO) in GM, with the volume adjusted to the total number of cells (e.g., 0.7mL
of DMSO and 6.3mL of GM for 7mL of freezing medium).
After centrifugation, the
supernatant was removed, and the cell pellet was resuspended in the calculated freezing
medium volume. Cryovials were filled with 1mL of cell suspension per vial and labeled ac-
cordingly, including passage number. The cryovials were stored in isopropanol-containing
freezing containers to ensure a gradual cooling rate of -1◦C per minute to -80◦C. After
24-48 hours, the vials were transferred to a liquid nitrogen freezer for long-term storage.

A.4
Cell Seeding on Electrode
To culture cells on the electrodes after PLL coating, the basal medium (BM) and growth
medium (GM) were pre-warmed for 20 minutes.
Cells were trypsinized with Tryple,
centrifuged, and counted as mentioned above.
To calculate the number of cells required on the electrode, a desired cell density of 50,000
cells/cm² was targeted, resulting in 32.000 cells per electrode in a volume of 80µl. The
circular area of the electrode was calculated using the formula Area = πr2, where r =
4.5mm, given a diameter of 9 mm and a height of 3mm. This geometry corresponds to
a total volume of 80µl, used as initial seeding volume to ensure just the coverage of the
electrode well and avoid cells seeded on the walls of the well.
After centrifugation and cell counting, the cell suspension was prepared according to the
needed parameters, and 80µl of this suspension was added to each electrode. The cells
were allowed to rest on the electrodes for 2-4 hours to ensure proper attachment. After
that, up to 1.5ml of pre-warmed GM media was added to the electrodes and left in the
humidified incubator (5% CO2 / 95% air) at 37 ◦C overnight.

A.7
Chemically-Induced Depolarization
A.7.1
Stimulation in Differentiation Media
Chemical stimulation experiments were conducted using differentiation media (DM) as
the baseline solution and as solvent for the potassium solution to stimulate the cells.
The existing electrode media was removed and the electrodes were filled with 140µl of
pre-warmed fresh differentiation media.
A 450 mM KCl stock solution was prepared in DM, and electrodes were connected to
CHI1010 potentiostat (CH Instruments, Austin TX).
Chemically induced depolarization of neurons was triggered by adding 70µl of 450 mM KCl
solution to the electrode, ensuring consistent positioning in all electrodes and experiments,
to stimulate the neurons in a consistent way among replicates. This addition resulted in
a total volume of 210µl per well, achieving a final K+ concentration of 150 mM .
A.7.2
Stimulation in Chemical Buffer
A validation test with potassium buffer was performed to compare our setup to results
from the literature that had already been reported. The same solution reported by Va-
sudevan et al. [16], consisting of a stable pH 7.4 buffer, was used to perform the test. This
also allowed to prove that any obtained signal with DM was due to cell depolarization
and not a reaction between potassium and DM.
A baseline buffer (5 mM Glucose, 1,2 mM MgCl2, 2 mM CaCl2, 150 mM NaCl, 5 mM
KCl and 10 mM HEPES), and stimulation buffer (5 mM Glucose, 1,2 mM MgCl2, 2 mM
CaCl2, 5 mM NaCl, 450 mM KCl and 10 mM HEPES) were prepared, sterile filtered
(0.2-µm syringe filter) and stored at 4◦C.
The same measurement procedure was followed as in differentiation media stimulation.
Instead of using DM as the baseline solution and KCl as the stimulation solution, baseline
and stimulation buffer were used, respectively.

A.9
Detailed Immunocytochemistry Protocol
A.9.1
Immunostaining and Fluorescence Microscopy
At the experimental endpoint (differentiation day 12), the electrodes were prepared for
cell characterization, following Vasudevan et al. [16] protocol, used to confirm neural and
dopaminergic phenotype of differentiated hNSCs, and optogenetic phenotype.
Cells were fixed with freshly prepared 2% paraformaldehyde for 2 minutes, followed by
4% paraformaldehyde for 10 min. Afterwards, they were thoroughly rinsed with PBS.
Cells were incubated for 1h with 5% goat serum, 5% horse serum, and 0.25% Triton X-100
in PBS (STPBS solution) in order to permeabilize cell membranes and prevent unspecific
binding of antibodies.
After that time, cells were incubated with primary antibodies, anti-tyrosine hydroxylase
(rabbit, PelFreez Biologicals P40101, 1:1000) and anti-β-tubulin (mouse, Sigma-Aldrich
T8660, 1:1000) diluted in STPBS, overnight at 4◦C.
ICC was used for that purpose, measuring tyrosine hydroxylase (TH; the rate-limiting en-
zyme for catecholamine synthesis) and β-tubulin (III) (an indicator of neural cytoskeleton
development).
The next day, the cells were cleansed with STPBS for 10 minutes, followed by 10 minutes
wash with Triton 0.3%, and lastly thoroughly rinsed with PBS. Cells were then incu-
bated for 1h at room temperature with secondary antibodies, Alexa 546 goat antimouse
(ThermoFisher A-11030, 1:500) and Alexa 647 goat antirabbit (ThermoFisher A-21245,
1:500), and cell nuclei counterstaining Hoechst 33342 (ThermoFisher H1399, 1:2000) for
1h, followed by thorough rinsing with PBS.
The samples were left in the freezer to dry until the moment of analysis.
Fluorescence laser scanning microscopy was performed using Zeiss Axio Observer Z1 in-
verted epifluorescence microscope equipped with 40x (LD Plan-Neofluar 40x/0.4 Korr
M27, ZeissTM, Germany) objective. Images were processed using ImageJ (NIH) soft-
ware.
