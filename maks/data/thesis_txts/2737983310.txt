Abstract:
The application of Chaos Engineering principles is explored to identify and assess
the resilience of automotive communication systems.
The aim is to identify
andunderstand the system’s tolerance to various external disturbances, thereby
improving its overall reliability.
The method involves a structured three-phase
approach: an exponential phase to escalate disturbance levels and find initial tolerance
thresholds, a binary phase to refine these thresholds, and a validation phase to verify
the results. Network disturbances were systematically introduced and their impacts
were analyzed. The results demonstrated varying levels of system resilience to the
fault injection. This study highlights the importance of comprehensive disturbance
modeling and systematic resilience testing, laying a foundation for future work to
enhance the reliability and safety in the automotive sector.

Introduction
In this thesis, the primary goal is to improve systematic quality management within
the automotive industry by harnessing the principles of chaos engineering. Coined by
Netflix in 2015 [1], chaos engineering is about testing how well systems can handle
unexpected situations. It involves running experiments in settings similar to real
world environments to make sure systems can handle challenges. This research
will help the automotive industry understand how well the systems can cope with
disturbances that affect system continuously and to find out their threshold.
The objective of this study is accomplished by deliberately introducing and
modeling disturbances within the system.
This enables a thorough evaluation
of the system’s ability to recover from problems and enhances its fault tolerance.
This approach is inspired by [2].
Furthermore, the insights gained from these
evaluations, particularly regarding system availability, play a significant role in
refining system specifications. By explicitly aligning disturbance tolerance with
functional requirements, organizations can optimize their systems.
This thesis aims to connect chaos engineering theories with real-world practices in the
automotive field. Through a systematic approach to fault injection and evaluation,
this research aims not only to enhance system performance but also to develop better
ways to design and test automotive systems.
At the core of chaos engineering lie four fundamental principles, each critical for
effective implementation:
• Build a hypothesis around steady-state behavior
• Vary real-world events
• Run experiments in production
• Automate experiments to run continuously
In the realm of chaos engineering methodology, experiments follow a structured
process. Initially, a "steady state" is defined, as representing a measurable output of
the system under normal operating conditions. The next step involves forming
a hypothesis and deliberately introducing faults into the Ethernet networking
plane of vehicles as part of the designed setup. The aim is to evaluate system
resilience and enhance fault tolerance mechanisms. Fault injections are performed
to observe the system’s response to such situations by introducing variables that
mimic real-world events, such as hardware malfunctions or network faults. Finally,
the results are compared, and the system’s responses to the fault injections are
observed to potentially refute the formulated hypotheses. These evaluations inform
improvements in system specification by aligning disturbance tolerance with system
requirements.
Within networks, fault tolerance refers to the capacity of a network to endure a

3.1
Chaos Engineering
The technique of testing distributed networks under unpredictable environments
to increase confidence in their ability to survive stressful situations when used
is known as chaos engineering. Experiments to confirm or contest theories are
the fundamental concepts of chaos engineering[1]. In this context, a hypothesis
is a system’s predicted or presumed behavior under particular circumstances. In
real-world scenarios, turbulence, such as faults, is injected to test theories about chaos
engineering while system behavior is monitored. The behavior that has been seen
provides fresh information since it sheds light on how the framework will function
and if it will support or contradict the established theories[6].
3.1.1
Principles of chaos engineering
Following are the principles of chaos engineering[19].
Build a Hypothesis around Steady State Behavior By using Chaos Engineering
to run experiments in the test framework, performance indicators can be tested to
ensure the system works correctly without needing to examine its internal workings
in detail. Focusing on measurable outputs like error rates, and latency helps in
understanding how the system is performing in its normal state.
Vary Real-world Events In Chaos experiments, the focus is on emulating real-world
occurrences, encompassing various types of events. These events are sorted based on
their potential consequences and how frequently they occur. This involves examining
scenarios such as hardware breakdowns, software malfunctions, and non-critical
incidents like sudden increases in user traffic or system scaling. Any event that could
potentially destabilize system equilibrium is regarded as a possible factor in Chaos
experimentation.
Run Experiments in Production Intermittent traffic patterns and external
conditions can cause abrupt changes in a system’s performance. Acquiring real-time
traffic data is essential to comprehending request pathways, as utilization patterns
can change suddenly. An essential way to verify that system testing is realistic and
consistent with the current operating environment is to do experiments directly on
live production traffic, as highlighted by Chaos Engineering.

3.3.1
VCU-Vehicle Control Unit
The electronic control unit in charge of managing a car’s different subsystems is
called the Vehicle Control Unit, or VCU. Its main duties involve interfacing with,
obtaining information from, and deciding choices depending on data from several
Electronic Control Units (ECUs)[23]. Data is exchanged between the VCU and these
ECUs via a range of data transfer protocols, such as CAN, LIN, and Ethernet. The
responsibility of every ECU is to keep an eye on the condition of each of its parts and
report back to the VCU. By doing this, the VCU can evaluate the vehicle’s overall
condition and functioning state.

4.2
Raspberry pi setup
As the initial step of the thesis, a Raspberry Pi model 4B [47] was employed as the
intermediary device between the VCU and VIU. Bridging was initially attempted
to establish data flow between the devices by assigning a static IP address to
the Ethernet interface connected to the Raspberry Pi. However, due to certain
restrictions, IP routing could not be achieved, resulting in unsuccessful bridging.
Subsequently, the Scapy tool was utilized, and a forwarding script was developed to

5.3
Method
The thesis utilizes chaos engineering to identify issues and evaluate the system’s
response to intentionally induced disturbances. Through the application of chaos
engineering principles, this research aims to assess the system’s tolerance for
fault injection using an external disturber termed an assessment measure. This
measurement facilitates an understanding of the system’s response to disturbances.
The term ’analysis’ emphasizes the thorough evaluation process used to ensure
the system’s compliance with predefined criteria, highlighting the essential role of
analytical processes in maintaining its functionality and performance. This approach
is implemented following the outlined principle.
To achieve the thesis aim, three steps are taken:
Firstly, chaos engineering is employed to analyze the steady-state, system analysis is
conducted under normal conditions, without any hardware modifications or injected
disturbances. These results are stored for comparison with the hypothesis state
outcomes, which are recorded during and after disturbances are introduced into the
system.
In the second step, which is the hypothesis states that continuously inject faults into
the system to analyze its response to the disturbances. This testing continues until
the system’s tolerance to each disturbance for the selected test cases is determined.
Finally, results from both the steady state and the hypothesis results are collected
and stored for analysis, simulating a production-like system performance. During
hypothesis testing, certain factors may indicate inaccuracies, such as failure to meet
predefined requirements, or unexpected behavior, such as prolonged test completion
times. By observing differences in the hypothesis results, more precise insights into
how disturbances impact system performance are gained.

Methods for Injecting faults:
To inject faults into the system, two main components are required: disturbances and

5.8
Modeling Disturbances
Disturbances are modeled to induce network congestion, simulate varying loads,
and assess system responses.
To generate congestion, disturbances such as
packet delay and burst are utilized, introducing delays and bursts of traffic to
assess system performance under different load conditions. Additionally, packet
reordering disturbance is implemented to replicate the real-world scenario of packet
rearrangement in network traffic.
In the context of data transmission, disturbances like packet loss, bit errors, and
packet duplication are introduced to inject errors into transmitted packets. This
allows for the evaluation of error-handling mechanisms and the overall resilience of
the system.
Initially, various ideas were considered for creating disturbances, such as malformed
headers and implementing variable delays instead of static ones. However, due to
time constraints, this thesis only covers six disturbances. Nonetheless, in the future,
more disturbances will be developed using this implementation to assess system
resilience across a broader spectrum of disturbers. All implemented disturbances are
based on probability-based models, providing approximate tolerance values. Types
of Disturbances that are designed :
1. Delay
2. Packet Drop
3. Packet Reordering
4. Packet duplication
5. Burst Queue
6. Burst Timeout
7. Bit Error Rate
The delay disturbance, designed to inject disturbances into the framework, introduces
a static delay in packet transmissions from the VCU to the designated VIU. This
delay traffic injection takes place as packets flow through the device in the middle,
aligning with the exchange between the VIU’s request and the VCU’s response.
Utilizing the TC netem tool, the delay is measured in milliseconds, representing
a time-based delay. In addition to the delay disturbance, other disturbances are
created using code that operates on the device in the middle.

5. Approach
Packet loss arises from transmission errors, often encountered in wireless networks or
congested network environments. Typically, the degree of packet loss is assessed as a
percentage in relation to the total number of packets transmitted[36]. The packet
drop mechanism is designed such that a random number between 0.0 and 1.0 is
generated for each packet. The probability of packet drop is determined for each
disturber, where the decision to drop or forward a packet is based on this generated
number compared to the disturber parameter. If the generated number is less than
the disturber parameter, the packet is dropped. Conversely, if the generated number
exceeds the disturber parameter, the packet is forwarded.
Packet reordering pertains to a situation in network traffic where packets may arrive
in a different order than they were originally sent [37]. This disturbance is simulated
by collecting packets and setting a timer to determine when they should be released.
Packets are sent once they reach the maximum time set, following a "last in, first
out" approach, with the unit of time being milliseconds.
The burst queue operates by collecting packets traveling through the network until
it reaches a predefined queue depth threshold. Upon reaching this threshold, all the
gathered packets are simultaneously released into the system to assess its response.
Similarly, the burst timeout mechanism collects packets and releases them collectively
when a predetermined timeout period elapses. Both mechanisms are configured with
integer values for defining the queue depth and timeout duration.
The bit error rate disturbance is designed to inject bit errors into packets as they
flow through the system. This custom code utilizes the numpy library for array
manipulation. It includes functions to flip both zero and one bits within frame bytes,
along with an XOR function for bitwise operations.The process begins by creating a
bit mask and determining a flip probability. Packet lengths are defined within an
array, with the length set to accommodate larger values. As packets flow through
the device in the middle, bit flipping occurs based on the predetermined probability.
This process repeats for each packet, ensuring that errors are introduced according
to the specified probability.

5.10
Measuring the requirement tolerance
A search space algorithm was created to comprehensively test the system’s
performance and determine the system’s tolerance level for the disturbances
introduced during packet flow. "Last work" and "last fail" are two crucial parameters
that are important in determining system tolerance. The term "last fail" describes
the result that indicates the point in the test case where the system breaks down
when a disturbance is introduced; it indicates when the system is no longer able to
handle the disturbance. On the other hand, "last work" refers to the value at which
the test was last passed, showing the highest level of disruption that the system is
capable of managing. Three stages were created to find the probabilistic assessment
of the disturbances while keeping these values in mind. The system’s tolerance limit
is progressively reached during the first phase, referred to as the exponential phase,
by gradually raising the disturbance quantum with the power of 2,which gives two
values,one is last work and the other is last fail.A binary search between the last
work and the last failed values is carried out in the second phase to find the threshold
value, known as the binary phase. In order to verify the system’s tolerance and
ensure stable activity under the set-up disturbances is the validation phase concludes
with a series of tests conducted around the identified threshold.The figures presented
in this section are for a specific test case for reading the active state VIU for delay
disturbance.

data being collected when disturbances were introduced to the system. However,
this issue can be addressed by implementing the tool in test bench. Test bench,
which are stationary but simulate the functionalities of a real car, would provide
a more comprehensive testing environment Limitations: The initial plan for this
project involved using a Raspberry Pi. However, a significant amount of time was
spent attempting to make the Raspberry Pi functional. As a result, it was replaced
by a server to serve as an intermediary device. While the first step was successfully
completed using the server, the original concept could not be fully realized due to
time constraints.
The system should be able to perform numerous repeatable test runs. Given the
unpredictable nature of the disturbances introduced, a seed was implemented for
each disturbance. This approach generates random numbers to help mitigate the
unpredictability of the disturbances within the system.
The impact of networking disturbances extends beyond individual functions, affecting
all network traffic. Demonstrating that a specific test can endure certain disturbance
levels does not imply that the entire system or electrical architecture can withstand
the same levels of disturbance.
Disturber implementations are consistently applied with high intensity to validate
their effectiveness in the test suite. For practical application, it will be necessary to
incorporate time-based regulations into the framework.
To mitigate the limitations, The current initial setup provides a limited number of
results and restricts the potential to impact the system. By enhancing the setup
to encompass a broader environment, the results provided by the system may be
significantly affected. Additionally, the latency present in the current setup needs to
be addressed in future improvements to mitigate the extra latency introduced by the
setup.
Using seeds for each disturbance in the code ensures that the random numbers
generated during repeated tests are consistent, which is essential for evaluating
system resilience. Future implementations of this tool in larger-scale systems will
address concerns with the current system framework and setup. Currently, this
setup offers valuable insights into how various test scenarios react to disturbances
introduced into the system. The latency induced by the setup does not presently
impact system behavior, making it acceptable for now. However, this issue will be
addressed in future improvements.

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the application of Chaos Engineering principles to evaluate
and enhance the resilience of automotive core network. By systematically introducing
disturbances and assessing system responses, we developed a robust framework for
fault injection and analysis. The study highlighted the importance of understanding
how disturbances affect system performance.
Despite predefined requirements,
lower-level disturbances were not thoroughly considered, underscoring the necessity
of Chaos Engineering to identify and mitigate these risks. Through focused metrics
such as transparency, quality improvements, testing improvements, and usability,
we demonstrated that capturing system tolerances can significantly enhance the
understanding and robustness of automotive systems.
Utilizing a three-phase
approach—comprising the steady-state hypothesis analysis, continuous fault injection,
and result comparison—proved effective in identifying system tolerance levels. This
method allowed for a detailed examination of the system’s capacity to withstand
various disturbances.
The results indicated that while some disturbances, such as packet duplication, had
no significant impact, others like bit error rates and packet reordering did affect
system performance. The analysis showed varying tolerance levels, with the system
demonstrating higher-than-expected resilience in some cases, likely due to factors such
as traffic flow and protocol behaviors. The study addressed the research questions
by identifying methods to assess the tolerance threshold for given requirements
under various conditions and demonstrating the effectiveness of Chaos Engineering
in quantifying system resilience.
However, the study’s initial setup was limited by the scope of test cases and the
simulation environment. Future work will need to address these limitations by
expanding the test framework and refining the implementation to better mimic
real-world conditions.
Despite these limitations, the creation of a controllable
framework for fault injection marks a significant achievement. The framework’s
ability to manage and implement disturbances, along with its systematic approach
to determining tolerance levels, sets the stage for more comprehensive future studies.
Future developments will focus on integrating this tool into more complex and
operational systems, aiming to establish it as a standard for system validation in the
automotive industry. By extending testing to test bench and potentially incorporating
Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) and Fault Tree Analysis (FTA), the
tool’s applicability and effectiveness can be significantly enhanced.
This thesis has successfully laid the groundwork for applying Chaos Engineering in
the automotive core network. Since this work focuses on diagnostics, that represents
only a fraction of the whole traffic in the core network, future implementations with
