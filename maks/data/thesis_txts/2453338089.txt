Abstract
This master thesis analyzes the effect of the sea current on wave loads on a offshore bottom ﬁxed
monopile. The analysis was conducted using different models and theories and comparing the results
of each model.
The results were obtained from the simulations of the following models and theories: the modiﬁed linear
Wave theory with the addition of the current velocity, the Stream Function theory presented by Fenton
and the New Force model introduced by Schloer,Bredmose and Ghadirian. The analysis also contained a
simulation on random sea-states computed with a JONSWAP spectra, obtaining η and force time-series
which were averaged in order to compare with the different models.
Additionally, OceanWave3D, a full non-linear wave solver was also used to simulate different current
induced wave forces on a monopile. These results were given by Amin Ghadirian in lab-scaled, which
resulted in the need to post-process the data by scaling and taking out the transients of mentioned results.
The analysis was also made through experiments in the ﬂuid-mechanics laboratory at DTU, which were
performed by Amin Ghadirian and Malene Vested. The obtained results passed through a post-processes,
which involved scaling and noise-ﬁltering through the Butterworth ﬁlter. Two different experiments were
performed to assess the effect of the current: a randomization of a sea-state and the repetition of New
Force model waves. Both results are compared with the results obtained from the simulations with the
New Force model.
The aero-hydro-servo-elastic code HAWC2 helped wrap-up the analysis by analyzing the structural re-
sponse of a monopile for different current velocities. The DTU 10 MW turbine model was used to run
the simulations, and the wave forces were modelled with the Stream Function theory.
The analysis concluded that the waves riding on a following current induce higher forces than opposing
current but with less force variation. Or said it in another way, the wave force induced by an opposing
current is steeper but also weaker than the one induced by a following current. The same conclusion was
reached for other loads such as the moment on the mudline of the structure, which is also stronger for
waves with big height, as the moment highly depends on the distance to the wave force application point.
Additionally, waves that oppose a current reach higher η values than those who follow a current. And
also, waves riding an opposing current have higher η variation along the wave than when they follow a
current.
In relation to the structural response, it was concluded that when the waves are riding on a current, no
matter the direction of the current relative to the wave, the response intensity is stronger than when there
is no current.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During the past few years, climate change has become a reality, and it is now a growing concern in the
actual society. More and more countries are starting to apply laws to reduce the harmful gas emissions
to the atmosphere. As the concern grows, the need to reverse the path of constantly growing GHG emis-
sions is becoming urgent. In order to approach this problem, the use of clean energies is taking the
spotlight as the way to face the situation.
And within these energies, wind energy plays a big role. The efﬁciency of the wind turbines and the
low-cost compared to other energies makes it an attractive and plausible way to generate energy.
Since 1887 when the ﬁrst eolic turbine was built for the production of electric power [9], the Wind En-
ergy sector has seen an exponential growth and technological development.
1.1
Offshore wind energy
While the wind energy sector has been around for a long time, the concept of constructing wind turbines
in the ocean is quite recent, as the ﬁrst offshore wind farm installed was the one called Vindeby [9], in
1991 in Denmark.
Since then, the offshore wind energy sector has seen an increase as the technology keeps developing. The
reason for this is that offshore wind is steadier and has higher speeds than the onshore, which develops
in a higher production of energy as the full load hours and the energy produced by a turbine in higher
than on the shore.
As the ﬁgure 1.1 shows, offshore wind energy installation has been increasing exponentially in Europe
for the past 15 years. This growth shows the importance of the offshore wind sector and how it is gaining
more and more strength through the years.
1
==PAGE:14==
1.1. Offshore wind energy
Figure 1.1: Cumulative and annual offshore wind energy installation in Europe [3]
As its importance grows, the technology also develops and thus, as more and more offshore wind tur-
bines are constructed, the reduction of costs of the technology is of important matter. This costs can be
divided in two sections: OM costs and construction costs.
In order to decrease the costs of operation and maintenance of the offshore wind turbines, the design
of the structures is of great importance, as the resilience and life-time of the wind turbine dictates the
mentioned costs. To reduce these costs, difference researches and studies are conducted and the analysis
and study of the wave loads is one that can help reach this goal.
The better understanding of the waves and their induced loads can help improve the efﬁciency of the
structure and enlarge the lifetime of the wind turbine structure which will highly reduce the costs.
Types of foundations
Depending on the depth of the sea there can be different types of foundations on which the wind turbines
are constructed. These foundations have different characteristics and following diverse criteria the foun-
dation type for a wind turbine is chosen. This criteria is the water depth, sea bed type and climatic loads,
as the most important ones.
• Monopile foundations These foundations are applied at depths up to 45 meters. Their construction
and installation is simple and also cost-efﬁcient.
• Gravity base foundations This type of foundation is placed below 15 meters of water depth. They
are made of concrete and built at quay and then ballasted at the installation. The concept of this
foundation revolves around the overturning moment that is taken at sea bed by gravity.

1.2
Current in waves
In order for the turbines to be more efﬁcient and safe, different investigations and researches have been
carried out. As mentioned before, the loads caused by the waves are a concerning matter, as they can
damage the structure of the turbine and thus leave it disabled.
Apart from the waves, other phenomenons can affect the loads suffered by the monopile. One of these
phenomenon is the sea-current who carries the waves. The current can be driven by winds, tides or dif-
ferences in density and additionally it can profoundly affect the weather, marine transportation and the
cycling of nutrients at the ocean.
Depending on the intensity and the direction of the ocean-current in the area, the wave loads can also be
affected and as an effect, affect the structure of the wind turbine.

1.3
Project scope
The goal of this project is to assess the effect of the current on the wave loads on a monopile. The as-
sessment will be done by different models and simulations, as well as data from lab-scale experiments.
The simulated loads will be affected by different current velocities and directions, in order to determine
how the waves change with the ocean currents and how their behaviour affects the offshore structures.
Speciﬁcally, the project consists of simulations and experiment data of the force caused by the waves,
and also simulations on how the monopile is affected and its structural response. The loads will be sim-
ulated through different models and theories which will give a nice picture of the effect of the current
velocity on the wave loads.
The ﬁrst theory used is the Stream Function Theory which was ﬁrst introduced by R.G. Dean in 1965
[5] and then further developed by J.D. Fenton in 1990 [4]. The New Force model introduced by Schløer,
Bredmose and Ghadirian in 2017 [7] which was developed to compute the shape of extreme waves, is
also used to assess the effect of the current.
Lab-scale experiment results are also processed and analyzed for different current velocities. Also, sim-
ulations performed by OceanWave3D, a fully non-linear and dispersive free surface wave modelling
program developed by DTU, are analyzed for different current-velocities.
In order to evaluate the structural response of the monopile for different current velocities, the aeroelastic
code HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation) is used. The wave kine-
matics are modelled through Stream Function theory with the help of the HAWC2 code and on the DTU
10 MW model provided beforehand.

The stream function presented by Fenton in 1988 and previously introduced by Dean in 1965 gives a nu-
merical solution to the computation of steadily progressing waves. This method uses non-linear equation
through Newton’s method and all the derivatives are evaluated numerically. Additionally, if the wave
period is one of the given parameters in this method, the current on which the wave is riding needs to be
known. As the computations are made for different sea-states, the wave period is introduced as an input,
and the current velocity is also added as a parameter.
As mentioned before, the effect of the current on monopile loads is analyzed in this project, and in order
to do that the different loads for different current velocities will be compared.

Another way of calculating extreme waves was introduced in the New Wave theory and is an alternative
to the Stream Function theory. The New Wave was validated experimentally in diverse studies such as
Wittaker et. al (2016)[2] and it proved that it was similar to the linearised average shape of the biggest
waves in extreme weather for non-dimensional wave numbers down to kh=0.4, where k is the wave num-
ber and h the sea depth.
Although this can compare well with the wave shape, the forces related to these extreme waves are not
always the larger ones, as they also depend on the steepness of the wave. In order to predict the extreme
response forces on a monopile from irregular waves, the New Force model [7] was introduced derived
from the New Wave model.

The waves are created through a piston paddle that is controlled by a wave-maker software. The waves
are sent from the paddle and different wave gauges compute the different parameters through its evolu-
tion. Speciﬁcally, there are 8 different wave gauges between the wave paddle and the cylinder, which are

erative process, the targeted Hs value is inserted as an input in the wave maker software and the paddle
creates the waves. Then, the output is analyzed and the Hs measured. If this value is far from the one
that was targeted, the ratio between the Hs obtained and the one targeted is utilized and multiplied by the
Hs that was used as an input before. Then again, the same process will be performed until the output is

As mentioned before, there are two types of experiments that have been conducted. The New Force

method, the waves meet at the cylinder at the same time, as the long waves travel faster than the short
waves. The sum of all these waves create a wave force shape which is the New Force shape.

Design inconsistencies
While conducting the experiments, there have been some errors or inconsistencies in the design that may

• Oversized instruments Led to obtaining larger errors in the measurements
• Water leaks inside load-cells that may affect the circuit which sends the signals
• Experiments performed in different days Led to more leakage in the cells as days passed, which
caused a bias error in the measurements.
• Same sea-state and different current experiments As before, led to more water leakage, having
bias errors for the same sea-state measurements.

OCW3D analysis w/o current
OceanWave3D is a fully non-linear wave solver developed at Technical University of Denmark. It is
convenient for simulating non-linear wave interactions and computing the loads induced by them. This
program is used in order to insert the non-linearity in the computations and use another approach to ana-
lyze the effect of the current on the wave loads.
The simulations were performed by Amin Ghadirian. Firstly, different Hs and Tp combinations were
tried when adding the different current velocities. With the addition of the current the waves were break-
ing and the simulations could not continue. As a result, the wave peak height Hs was lowered until the
value was small enough for the wave not to break with the addition of the current.
The waves created from these simulations do not have the same steepness as the previous simulations
and experiments so they are not comparable, but it helps having another source of input to analyze the
effect of the current on wave loads.

Inputs
As mentioned above, the ﬁnal Hs and Tp values were found after testing the maximum values with the
addition of the current on the OCW3D program.
The ﬁnal input values are Hs=6m and Tp=15m, which give the maximum steepness for the waves not to
break with the current. Additionally, the peak enhancing factor γ has a value of 1, and the sea depth h is

Chapter 8
Conclusions
In this master thesis, different simulations and experimental analysis have been carried out in order to
analyze the effect of the sea current on the wave loads on a monopile. The different models used have
helped study the wave loads and the wave behaviour riding on a current and have provided different
results and conclusions which will be summarized and wrapped up in this section.
The effect of the current on the wave force has not seen a consensus in the simulations carried out. The
mean wave force is increased when the wave is following a current, while when it is riding an opposing
current, it decreases. Additionally, the opposing current induces higher wave force variations along the
wave than the following current, but again, these values are lower than the following current induced
forces. This conclusion was made after analyzing the experimental data and the non-linear analysis car-
ried out by OCW3D.
However, when observing the results from the Stream Function theory and the wave force average from
the JONSWAP random sea-state , a higher steepness or force variation in the wave force was seen when
there was a following current. These simulations however, show a consensus with the others when deter-
mining that the mean wave force is increased when following a current.
As the experimental analysis and the non-linear simulations performed by OCW3D better replicate the
reality in which the waves are non-linear and irregular, the conclusions obtained from these analysis were
prioritized. This conclusion states that the waves riding on a following current induce higher forces than
opposing current but with less force variation. Or said it in another way, the wave force induced by an
opposing current is steeper but also weaker than the one induced by a following current.
When analyzing the wave shape riding a current, a consensus was reached after all the simulations were
done: Waves that oppose a current reach higher η values than those who follow a current. Additionally,
waves riding an opposing current have higher η variation along the wave than when they follow a current.
It is also observed that in order for a wave riding an opposing current to induce the same force as a wave
on a following current, it will need higher wave height or η.
Other loads such as the moment on the mudlane of the structure are also affected by the current. Follow-
ing the same reasoning as with the force, the higher the current velocity is, the stronger the moment felt
by the turbine. Additionally, high waves will induce stronger moments than low waves, as the moment
on the mudlane is highly dependable on the height of the application point of the force.
The effect of the current on the structural response was also analyzed and it was concluded that when
59
==PAGE:72==
the waves are riding on a current, the response intensity is higher than when there is no current. This
relates to both directions of the current, so structural response intensity depends on the absolute value of
the current velocity, the higher the velocity the stronger the response intensity.
This analysis of the current effect on the wave forces can be more complete, but the time and knowledge
constraints did not allow to thoroughly investigate the case. For future work in order to complete the
study, the following analysis or simulations could be made:
• Simulation of extreme sea-states on OCW3D for different currents. It is now not possible as the
extreme waves break with the addition of the current.
• Addition of the second-order contribution for the New Force model with current in order to add
the non-linearity to the model. This addition was proved to obtain higher similarities with extreme
waves [7] without the addition of the current.
• Integration of the computed OCW3D wave force time-series into the wave kinematics on HAWC2.
This will allow to analyze the structural response of a wind turbine and the monopile under non-
linear wave forces.
