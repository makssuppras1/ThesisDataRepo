ABSTRACT
The aim of the present thesis was to develop an intermediate temperature (200-400
°C) CO2/H2O co-electrolysis using molten salt electrolyte to produce methane
without forming a toxic by-products (as CO). The project was focused on elec-
trolyte materials in order to ﬁnd a promising candidate to be implemented in the
electrolysis at intermediate temperature CO2/H2O co-electrolysis.
An experimental electrolyzer setup performed in laboratory scale was used.
That enabled an advanced characterization of the electrolysis performance, includ-
ing electrochemical and physico-chemical measurements. The physico-chemical
properties of CsH2PO4 in terms of structure and morphology have been investi-
gated along with thermal stability using polarization scan, impedance, scanning
electron microscopy (SEM), and X-ray analysis.
The observed differences between polarization curves were inﬂuenced by the
activity of the electrode, catalyst and electrolyte. Pressed nickel pellets were used
as cathode. Anodes were based on IrO2 with PBI as a binder. Composite based on
CsH2PO4 and ZrO2 blend was made to improve the properties of the electrolyte
material. Composite included mechanically strong material ZrO2 and PBI-felt
which improved the densiﬁcation of the electrolyte and further resulted to im-
proved mechanical stability of it.
The maximum performance obtained by a Ni/(CsH2PO4 - ZrO2) /IrO2 co-
electrolysis system at 300 °C was 42 mA/cm2 at 2.0 V. This activity is in good
agreement with the results obtained by Gas Chromatography (GC) where the max-
imum methane production at 300 °C reached current yield of 56% .
The results present that methane can be produced from co-electrolysis of CO2/H2O
from molten salt electrolyte with high current yield.

BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
1.1
Introduction
In these years huge efforts are being made to replace use of conventional energy
sources such as nuclear power or fossil fuels by renewable energy sources such as
wind or solar energy. This is due to limited fossil fuel sources, increasing oil prices
and environmental consideration. One of the main concerns with conventional
energy sources is the emission of greenhouse gases (GHGs) such as carbon dioxide
(CO2) and methane (CH4). Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)
under United Nations has estimated that these two gases are responsible for up
to 92 % of the total emission of GHG [1]. The contribution of CO2 emissions
from fossil fuels is estimated to be responsible for 65 % of the total. Emissions
of CO2 from the fossil fuels and industrial processes, are the main contributors
to enhanced greenhouse effect. Over the past century, atmospheric CO2 level has
increased more than 39 %. Dennis et al [2] have estimated the climate change
mitigation for the global GHG emission in 2030 will increase by 25-90 % over the
year 2000 level.
Removal of GHG emission from the atmosphere is challenging but several
strategies have been employed. Whether any of them could work at the scale
needed to deliver the goal of the standard agreement depends on three things: fea-
sibility, cost and acceptability. A crucial component of all of these approaches is
the non-climatic impacts that large-scale CO2-removal could have on ecosystems
and biodiversity [3].
==PAGE:16==
1-2
PART I
The most commonly studied technology to reduce CO2 emissions is Carbon
Capture and Sequestration (CCS) [4], which consists of CO2 capture, transporta-
tion and underground storage. Alternatively, the captured CO2 could be utilized
and converted into fuels and chemicals, such as dry reforming of methane for
synthesis gas production or CO2 hydrogenation to CH4, methanol or higher alco-
hols [5].
Methane production looks back on a history of more than 100 years research.
Methane is used as an energy carrier of signiﬁcant importance to the industry, en-
ergy, and transportation sectors worldwide. The conversion of electrical energy
from renewable sources into methane (power to gas) is called methanation [6].
Methanation reactions of carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide were ﬁrst discov-
ered by Sabatier and Senderens in the beginning of the last century 1902 [7]. These
process aimed to produce methane from hydrogen and carbon dioxide. Several
studies have investigated the methanation process in order to produce methane
from CO2 co-electrolysis to minimize global warming and maintain a high energy
production [8, 9].
To overcome the increasing levels of CO2 and open possibility for storage of
electric energy, the aim of this thesis was to accompany and facilitate new metha-
nation co-electrolysis process at intermediate temperature (200-400 °C). This tem-
perature range is of strategic importance, since it closes the gap between the cur-
rently available high and low temperature electrochemical energy conversion tech-
nologies [10–12].
The current thesis is present a laboratory scale research of
methanation process. On one hand, application-orientated research focusing on
ﬁnding promising electrolyte candidates to be implemented in the electrolysis at
intermediate temperature. On the other hand, fundamentals such as reaction mech-
anisms, morphology and catalyst activation are presented.

2.3
Methanation
The Middle East oil crisis increased natural gas needs, and dwindling gas reserves
have intensiﬁed the need to use our huge coal reserves for the synthesis of gas,
especially methane. Methane is the main constituent of natural gas, for which
both the efﬁcient mass transportation infrastructures and the combustion technolo-
gies have been well established for renewable energy storage [6]. The strategy
to remove of CO2 by reducing it with hydrogen to methane is methanation pro-
cess [17, 18].
Methanation of CO2 is a particularly promising technique for producing chem-
ical energy carrier. The methanation process is one of the crucial steps in the con-
version of electric energy from renewable sources to synthetic natural gas. Several
studies have investigated the methanation process in order to produce methane
from CO2 electrolysis to minimize global warming and maintain a high energy
production. Numerous studies, using methanation process through CO2 recycling
, have suggested different applications such as seawater electrolysis [19], adiabatic
ﬁxed-bed methanation [20–24], cooled ﬁxed-bed methanation [25], ﬂuidized-bed
methanation [26–28], three-phase methanation [29, 30], ﬁxed-bed reactors [31–
37].
Methanation research into catalytic processes focused on two options, CO2
methanation and CO methanation. If electrolysis hydrogen is supplied as power
to gas, CO2 methanation allows for the chemical storage of electricity and react
to form of CH4 and H2O [8, 38, 39]. However, CO methanation using carbon
monoxide and hydrogen as educts for the catalytic production of CH4 and H2O.
These educt gases mainly come from coal or biomass gasiﬁcation at synthetic
fuel production plants. The conversion of carbon dioxide is referred to as CO2
methanation Eq. 2.13, and the conversion of carbon monoxide as CO methanation
Eq. 2.14, respectively. These two equations are known as the Sabatier process,
however the ﬁrst catalytic reaction was published by Sabatier and Senderens [7].
CO2 + 4H2 ⇌CH4 + 2H2O
(2.13)
CO + 3H2 ⇌CH4 + H2O
(2.14)
CO + H2O ⇌H2 + CO2
(2.15)

2.4
Phosphate based materials
One of the key impediments to the development of CO2/H2O co-electrolysis in the
intermediate temperature range is a lack of suitable and practical electrolyte. Ide-
ally, the component effectively separates the anode and cathode gas feeds and me-
diates the electrochemical reaction occurring at the electrodes through conducting
a speciﬁc ion at very high rates. Considerable interest has been recently dedicated
to the development of novel proton conducting electrolytes for intermediate tem-
perature co-electrolysis, based on molten salts. [40, 41]. Molten salts were used
as the general requirements of an intermediate co-electrolysis electrolyte due to
their high conductivity and the low volatility. Since molten salts by nature have
a high concentrations of ions, and they are generally not electronically conduc-
tive, then would have a high ion conductors. Molten salts have strong interaction
between the positive and negative ions, due to low saturation vapor pressure and
high boiling points. Furthermore, the strong intermolecular forces make them to
be extraordinary solvents, dissolving a wide scope of substances, such as homo-
geneous and heterogeneous catalyst, gases, water, even metals. Some of these
molten salts were previously studies as electrolytes materials for proton conduc-
tors such as Cesium dihydrogen phosphate (CsH2PO4) [42–44] and Lithium di-
hydrogen phosphate (LiH2PO4) [45, 46], and Cesium pentahydrogen phosphate
(CsH5(PO4)2) [47].
2.4.1
Cesium dihydrogen phosphate
The assembling of the electrochemical cell is based on the most common inter-
mediate temperature proton conducting electrolyte, cesium dihydrogen phosphate
(CDP). CDP is one of the members of potassium dihydrogen phosphate (KH2PO4-
type) family [48] . Numerous studies of CDP were carried out and Nikiforov et al.
have written a review of these studies [42]. The shapes of the CDP is monoclinic
crystal (space group P22/m) structure at atmospheric pressure and room temper-
ature with low conductivity [49]. This is also called paraelectric phase although
means polarized in an electric ﬁeld. The effect of an electric ﬁeld could alter the
distribution of bonds and thus induce polarization [50]. The CDP undergoes a
transition below -100 °C, where the paraelectricity is lost compare to monoclinic
structure which is maintained [51].

Zirconia (ZrO2) has recently been recognized as a potential photocatalyst due to
its chemical stability and suitable redox potential. Zirconia exists in three different
crystalline form: monoclinic (M), tetragonal (T) and cubic (C). In the monoclinic
phase the Zr4+ ion has seven-fold coordination. In the tetragonal phase and cubic
phase the Zr4+ ion has eight-fold coordination.

initially been viewed in a light microscope but subsequently found to require SEM

The principles of SEM operation, including the formation of an electron beam,

Cesium dihydrogen phosphate (CsH2PO4) was synthesized from Cs2CO3 (Ce-

The yield was washed at least twice with methanol, since the H3PO4 dilutes in
methanol, wherein H3PO4 is soluble but CsH2PO4 is not soluble. The solution
placed to dryness overnight at 90 °C in a ventilated oven until the remaining water
was evaporated. The solution (CsH2PO4) was dissolved in Milli-Q® water and
then placed at room temperature ( 22 °C) by slow evaporation in a ventilated hood
for several days. To be ensure complete dryness of the solution the (CsH2PO4)
was kept at 90 °C for one day more.

3.2.4
Synthesis and preparation of anode electrode
The synthesis of IrO2 was based on the procedure by Adams fusion procedure [62].
A normal batch size for the catalyst preparation would be using approximately 3 g
of the precursor salt. The reaction schemes for the synthesis of IrO2 can be seen
in Eq. 3.10 and Eq. 3.11:
H2IrCl6.4H2O + 6NaNO3 −→6NaCl + Ir(NO3)4 + 2HNO3
(3.10)
Ir(NO3)4 −→(500°C) IrO2 + 4NO2 + O2
(3.11)
As precursor salt dihydrogen hexachloroiridate (IV) hydrate, H2IrCl6 . 4H2O
(Alfa Aesar, 99 % metal base) is used. The precursor salt is stored and weighed in
a protective argon atmosphere (glove box) to keep its nominal level of hydration.
The hexachloroiridate is brought into complete solution by magnetically stirring
in approximately 56 mL of 2 propanol (Riedel de Haen) equal to concentration
of 8.10 −2 mL . L−1. After dissolution of the iridium salt a large surplus (9.5
grams, approximately 25 times the stoichiometry) of ﬁnely ground sodium nitrate
is added to the solution, a little at the time. After addition of NaNO3 the mixture
is magnetically stirred at 50 °C in air until completely dry. The salt placed in
a preheated furnace at 500 °C for 1 hour to ensure the complete decomposition
of the Ir(NO3)4. When the furnace is slowly cooled to room temperature, and
then iridium oxide is collected and washed 3 times with demineralised water. The
washed IrO2 is dried in air and a typical yield of this synthesis is 80 %.
The dihydrogen hexachloroiridate (IV) hydrate grained to ﬁne powder in an
agate mortar, and then placed in a porcelain crucible. 14 mL of propanol was added
and the salt was dissolved under stirring. 2.4 grams of ﬁnely ground NaNO3 was
added and then mixture was heated to 50-60 °C under stirring in air until dried.
The crucible placed in the ceramic furnace and heated at 500 °C/h up to 500 °C,
and kept this temperature for 1 hour. Then the powder slowly cooled down to
room temperature overnight. Iridium oxide powder collected from the furnace and
washed 3-4 times each time with 400 mL of demineralised water. Then the product
dried in air at 90 °C overnight. Catalyst was collected and weighed and the yield
calculated.

3.3
Electrolysis setup
As it has been presented in section 2.1, the electrochemical reaction needs two
electrodes: anode as a positive electrode, cathode as a negative electrode and thin
electrolyte between setup. This combination is called the Membrane Electrode
Assembly (MEA), which the core component of a electrolysis produce the elec-
trochemical reaction needed to separate electrons.
Figure 3.12: Sketch of experimental setup for electrolyzer which has been build at Proton
Conductors Section of DTU Energy
Fig. 3.12 shows the sketch of electrolyzer which has recently been build at Pro-
ton Conductors Section of DTU Energy and used in this thesis. Operation of the
electrolysis cell follows a standard procedure where the CO2 ﬂow is run to steam
into cathode compartm. The HPLC pump has function to pump water into the
cell and the water evaporated at temperature range at 170-190 °C then follows to
anode compartm. The heater has slightly higher temperature than evaporator. The
temperature of both evaporator and heater are decided based on water boiling tem-
perature at 8 bar pressure which is the standard operating pressure of the system.
The electrolytes temperature is between 300 to 350 °C. The valve has function to
control the ﬂow of CO2 which are equipped with pressure measuring tools. The
connection between the cathode and GC is a manometer where it can measured
the pressure on the outlet of the cathode. Finally the pressure of the electrolysis
cell can be controlled via the autoclave that is connected to a N2-ﬂask.

Polarization
The most common method used to characterize the performance of electrochem-
ical systems is to examine current-voltage polarization curve. A polarization curve
shows the relationship between Cell Voltage (V) and current/current density (mA/cm2)
across the cell. The voltage- current curve is a simple and efﬁcient way to eval-
uate properties of electrochemical devices. Nonetheless, the basic mechanisms
are difﬁcult to analyze because the contributions from different processes overlap.
However, at low, medium, and high current density ranges, the dominant processes
are kinetic, ohmic, and mass transfer, respectively.
By measuring polarization curve certain parameters such as composition, gas
ﬂow rate, temperature, and relative humidity on cell performance can be charac-
terized and compared. Generally, the voltage increases consist of four factors:
• increase due to activation resistance
• increase due to ohmic resistance
• increase due to mass transport limitation

DISCUSSION
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the chemistry of a methanation process at
intermediate temperature (200-400 °C) co-electrolysis of CO2/H2O. The primary
challenge for co-electrolysis of CO2/H2O is the improvement of stability, which is
directly related to the structural features and material properties of the electrolytes.
Since a lack of suitable and practical electrolytes is one of the key impediments
to the development of co-electrolysis from CO2/H2O. Ionic resistance, capillary
action and the compatibility of the support materials would be the three crucial
factors for good performance and stability of the electrolytes.
In this thesis, CsH2PO4 was used as electrolyte, due to its possibility of at-
taining simultaneously transport and mechanical property suitable for intermedi-
ate temperature in co-electrolysis. The physico-chemical properties of CsH2PO4
in terms of structure and morphology have been investigated along with thermal
stability using polarization scan, impedance, scanning electron microscopy, and
X-ray analysis.
Reported results show that methane can be produced associated with high cur-
rent yield from co-electrolysis of CO2/H2O from CDP/ZrO2 electrolyte. A sys-
tematic increase in methane production correlated with increased cell voltage dur-
ing measurement was observed by gas chromatography (Figs. 4.14, 4.15, 4.16 and
4.17). Numerous studies, reported the solid acids have high conductivities, due to
their phase transitions [43, 68, 69]. In 2001, Haile et al. [44] were the ﬁrst study to
test CsHSO4 electrolyte. They generated a high cell voltage of 1.1 V with current
density of 44 mA/cm2 at 160 °C with a plantinum loading of 18 mg/cm2 in fuel
==PAGE:68==
5-2
DISCUSSION
cell. They proposed that solid acids were able to be used in fuel cell assembly, due
to their proton conductor properties. Although, several factors such as softness in
superprotonic phase, solubility in water and a narrow working temperature interval
had a discouraging effect on the research of these compounds. In 2004, this idea
was revived by Boysen et al [70] where the viability of CsH2PO4 as electrolyte
was studied.
Polarization method was used to characterize the performance of the electro-
chemical system and measure current-voltage polarization curves. Using polariza-
tion curve inﬂuence of certain parameters such as composition, gas ﬂow rate, tem-
perature, and relative humidity on cell performance were characterized and com-
pared. The polarization curves (Figs. 4.7, 4.8, 4.9, 4.10, 4.11, 4.12, 4.13) reported
that the cell voltage (V) increased due to increased current density (mA2). The
fundamental factors of increased cell voltage during measurements could be origi-
nated from electrolytes, activation resistance, ohmic resistance, and mass transport
limitation. The observed differences between polarization curves were the activity
of the electro catalyst and electrolytes. By using XRD and SEM techniques, sev-
eral potentially inﬂuencing factors on the activity of the electrocatalyst and elec-
trolytes can exist. One of the factor was blocking of gas ﬂow plate by melting of
CDP/ZrO2 on surface on electrodes. Another factor was a partial deactivation of
the catalyst by further oxidation of anode at high potentials to less active oxidation
states.
Theoretical value of the cell voltage at 300 °C was calculated to be 0.97 V
(section 2.2). By comparing the theoretical value with the polarization curves
results of the tests, it can be seen that Test-4 (Fig. 4.9), Test-10 (Fig. 4.10) and
Test-11 (Fig. 4.12) were obtained the theoretical value at 300 °C to be 0.97 V.
Although, Test-1 (Fig. 4.7) and Test-3 (Fig. 4.8) were showed to have practical
value of 0.52 V at 300 °C, while Test-9 (Fig. 4.11) and Test-12 (Fig. 4.13) were
showed to have practical values of 1.5 V at 300 °C. The big differences between the
theoretical value and the practical values were indicated by several factors. Firstly,
it was possible to the gases were mixed at beginning of the process. Secondly, the
currents had to low value then were not possible to obtain the theoretical value for
some the tests. Thirdly, the cathode side of electrolysis was ﬁlled with O2 at the
start of the process thus it was reduced to oxide. These factors have inﬂuence to
why we have not achieved the theoretical value for all tests.

5-3
The temperature ﬂuctuation during the process was one of the most critical
conditions which had inﬂuence over the whole process. The small peaks from
Chronopotentiometry results (Fig. 4.6) can be a good explanation of the tem-
perature ﬂuctuation in the process. In addition, CO2 and water were not added
regularly from the valve to the system, therefore cell voltage over 80 mA seems to
have uneven peaks.
The preparation conditions of the cell electrolyte have a lot of signiﬁcance on
our methanation processes. In order to improve the CDP/ZrO2 electrolytes we de-
cided to use ultrasound and hot pressing in order to let the composites pass through
the PBI membrane. The XRD analysis of CDP/ZrO2 composites w/wo ultrasound
and hot pressing indicated patterns similar to the published data [63], with no
changes in morphology (Figs. 4.5). However, GC- and polarizations results in the
presented co-electrolysis system with CDP/ZrO2 composites using ultrasound and
hot pressing were not measurable, as has been shown in Table 4.1. It is well-known
that ultrasound can improve the electrolysis activity and increase the electrical con-
ductivity [65]. We speculated whether water evaporation may be the key factor for
the outcome of the measurements, due to molten CDP conductivity decreases after
hot pressing (Eq. 2.17).
Not only do the preparation conditions of the cell materials inﬂuence the pro-
duction of methane, also support, active catalyst and electrolysis setup are impor-
tant to obtain a high methane production rates.
Commercial ZrO2 powder was used as support electrolytes. Zirconia has ben-
eﬁt for improving the catalytic activity and stability [71]. Jensen [58] reported in
her thesis as well that the composite formation with ZrO2 increased conductivity
at higher temperatures probably due to the physical stabilization of the high con-
ducting phase. The obtained results (Fig. 3.10) showed that the addition of ZrO2
as a support can affect the electrolytes interaction and leads to an improvement of
both activity and stability of CDP electrolytes.
To obtain thermochemical stability and improve mechanical properties PBI-
felt was used as reinforcement. PBI is an electronic and ionic insulator, thus pos-
sess endurance both in alkali and acid at intermediate temperature. In the presented
thesis, PBI-felt was used as the matrix material for electrolytes. It was found that
CDP was distributed evenly in the matrix of PBI, and free or combined CDP par-
ticles provided ionic conductivity of CDP/ ZrO2.
To fabricate anode electrode with high performance IrO2/PBI ink to the Stain-
less steelfelt, coated with tantalum was used. In this thesis, IrO2/PBI tantalum
anode was used due to it has greater stability and a reasonable activity compared

to electrocatalysts, such as Ru-based [72].
In an earlier theoretical work, Sabatier and Senderens [7] discovered that nickel
was able to catalyze the reaction between carbon monoxide and hydrogen to form
methane and water. Based on Sabatier and Senderens study, nickel electrode was
used as a cathode in our electrolysis system due to its high activity, high tem-

CONCLUSIONS
A novel procedure for producing of methane is presented in this thesis. The differ-
ent conﬁgurations of electrode, electrolyte, and also preparation techniques have
been investigated.
CsH2PO4 (CDP) was prepared and investigated for use as an electrolyte in the
electrolysis cell at intermediate temperatures (200-400 °C). The purity of CDP was
found to be 99.7 ± 0.3 wt % by using thermogravimetric analysis. Commercial
ZrO2 powder was used as electrolyte support. For preparation of electrolytes sev-
eral molar ratios of CDP/ZrO2 were used, e.g. 1:1, 1:2, 2:1 and 3:1. The attempts
to obtain a thinner and more stable electrolyte indicated that the most promising
molar ratio of CDP/ZrO2 blend was 2:1. Pressed nickel pellets were used as cath-
odes and the anodes were based on IrO2 with PBI as a binder.
It is concluded that due to the promising results reported for CsH2PO4 as
electrolyte, this material, and presumably the other phosphate-based solid acids,
MH2PO4 (M= Cs, Li, Na, K, Rb, Fr), are considered as potential electrolytes for
CO2/H2O co-electrolysis. Furthermore, the presented thesis has demonstrated the
feasibility and the potential of using CDP/ZrO2 electrolytes for good performance
of methane production in a co-electrolysis system within the intermediate temper-
ature range which was mainly attributed to the maximum methane production at
300 °C and had current yield of 56 %. In addition, the methane production by
the reaction of carbon dioxide with hydrogen proceeded without forming a toxic
by-product, such as carbon monoxide and also without high production rates of
hydrogen, which is competitive reaction in this setup.
