Abstract
This thesis focuses on developing robust grasping capabilities for mobile manipulators
through the integration of vision­based tactile sensors, specifically aimed at enhancing
pick­and­place tasks in industrial environments. The primary objective is to achieve ac­
curate estimation of tri­axial forces (Fx, Fy, Fz) and effective response to slip conditions
during object manipulation. The research also involves implementing a navigation algo­
rithm optimized for obstacle avoidance to enhance the robot’s autonomous navigation
capabilities.
To achieve these goals, the thesis presents several key contributions:
1. Autonomous Navigation: Development and testing of a navigation planner based
on the nav_core ROS package to enable the mobile base to navigate typical indus­
trial settings autonomously.
2. Manipulation Algorithm: Creation of two distinct manipulation control algorithms
tailored to different tactile sensors (Gelsight Mini and CompdVision), focusing on
robust grasping without inducing slip.
3. Grasp Algorithm: Utilization of tactile data during the critical phase when the grip­
per has secured but not yet lifted the object, refining the feedback loop and response
actions.
4. Deep Neural Networks for Force Estimation and Slip Detection: Implementation
of a method using deformation images as inputs to a Convolutional Neural Network
(CNN) for force estimation, followed by a Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN) to
detect static or slipping states of the grasped object.
The methodology involves a decoupled approach where navigation and manipulation are
executed sequentially but independently. This separation simplifies control architecture
but poses challenges in operational efficiency and accuracy, especially in varying illumi­
nation conditions affecting visual markers.
Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms in im­
proving the performance of mobile manipulators in pick­and­place tasks, particularly with
rectangular objects like hard drives. Despite some limitations, such as the dependency
on visual conditions and manual labeling in slip detection datasets, the research shows
promising advancements in the integration of tactile sensing and deep learning for robotic
manipulation.
Future work aims to unify navigation and manipulation tasks into a cohesive movement
strategy using Model Predictive Control (MPC) and automate the labeling process for slip
detection to enhance model accuracy and resolution.
This thesis contributes to the field of robotics by advancing the integration of tactile sensing
and vision­based systems, offering improved strategies for robust and reliable robotic
manipulation in dynamic and unstructured industrial environments.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The integration of robotics into industrial processes is evolving with an increasing expec­
tation that robots will operate with human­like dexterity in unstructured environments. This
shift is driven by the need to adapt swiftly to changing manufacturing demands without the
reliance on static production lines [1]. Traditionally, robotic arms have been anchored to
factory floors, limiting their operational range to predefined workspaces, while advances
in mobile robotics significantly enhance production line capabilities. By enhancing mobile
platforms with serial manipulators arms, a broader set of complex operations in dynamic
and less structured environments can be accomplished, often in proximity to human op­
erators [2]. However, despite their versatility, mobile manipulators have yet to be adopted
widely in manufacturing settings [3]. The complexity of such systems presents escalated
challenges in design, modeling, sensory integration, planning, and dexterous manipula­
tion [4].
For robots to be truly effective in human­centric environments, they must execute highly
dexterous tasks under varied conditions, including interaction with unfamiliar objects [5].
Recent advancements in computer vision and range sensing technologies have signifi­
cantly improved object detection reliability. However, even with precise detection, the act
of grasping remains fraught with difficulties. The deployment of tactile sensors can en­
hance the monitoring of interactions between the gripper and objects, which is crucial for
effective manipulation [6]. To achieve a level of performance comparable to human dex­
terity, robots will likely need sophisticated tactile sensors and robust algorithms to process
such information in appropriate way that is useful for the target task [7]. The development
of better and more robust algorithms based on tactile information represents a promising
avenue to significantly augment the robustness and reliability of robotic manipulation.
1.2
Problem description
Despite the versatility of mobile manipulators, tasks such as pick and place demand high
levels of precision and efficiency concerning speed, maneuverability, and grasping robust­
ness [8]. It is clear that significant enhancements are required in the design and control
of mobile manipulators [9], especially when they are deployed in industrial applications.
These robots are capable of executing complex tasks traditionally performed by humans
[10]. However, the nuanced manipulation of everyday objects, especially with limited con­
tact points such as two­fingered grips, remains a formidable challenge in the development
of service and collaborative robotics [11]. Accurate control is crucial to maintain contact
with an object without causing it to slip or be dropped, involving complex contact states
like rolling or slipping.
Vision systems enable robots to detect and identify the optimal pose for object interaction.
However, the final act of grasping remains problematic. Stable and successful grasping
requires not just visual input but a nuanced understanding of the tactile interactions at play
between the gripper and the object, an area where current vision systems offer limited
assistance [12]. This necessitates sophisticated tactile sensors and the development of
algorithms capable of predicting and enhancing grasping stability.
This thesis focuses on developing robust grasping capabilities through the integration of
vision­based tactile sensors for pick­and­place tasks, with the ability to accurately esti­

1.3
Objectives
The primary goal of this thesis is to develop a robust grasping algorithms for mobile ma­
nipulators through the implementation of vision­based tactile sensors. Specific objectives
are as follows:
1. Autonomous navigation: Test and implement an adequate navigation planner on
the mobile base, based on the nav_core ROS package. This will allow the robot to
navigate typical industrial environments autonomously such as warehouses.
2. Manipulation algorithm: Develop two distinct manipulation control algorithms tai­
lored to different tactile sensors (Gelsight Mini and CompdVision experimental sen­
sor). These algorithms will focus on achieving robust grasping capabilities, address­
ing the critical challenge of maintaining a grip on an object without inducing slip.
3. Grasp algorithm: Utilization of tactile data at the critical moment when the gripper
has secured the object but has not yet lifted it. This approach aims to refine the
feedback loop and response actions during this delicate phase of object manipula­
tion.
4. Application of Deep Neural Networks for Force Estimation and Slip detec­
tion: Propose a method that employs deformation images as inputs to estimate
tri­axial forces using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). Subsequently, a Tem­
poral Convolutional Network (TCN) will be utilized to ascertain the static or slipping
state of the grasped object.
1.4
Scope and limitations
This project is dedicated to developing a decoupled approach for the integration of the
mobile base and the manipulator within the context of pick­and­place operations. This
approach will involve executing navigation and manipulation sequentially but indepen­
dently. The use of tactile sensors will be pivotal in achieving dexterous manipulation by
sensing slip, force, and even color information when interacting with objects. This sensory
feedback will inform the development of effective strategies for robustly grasping objects,
particularly hard drives. The project’s efficacy will be evaluated through a grasping and
insertion task. This task, essential in various practical applications, will serve as the pri­
mary evaluation framework for the mobile manipulator system. However, the project faces
inherent limitations:
1. Operational Efficiency: The method of first moving the robot’s base to a desired
position before actuating the arm simplifies the control architecture but results in
slow and discontinuous task execution.
2. Accuracy and Reliability in Grasping: The standard procedure involves the robot
grasping objects from preset locations based on positional and pose information
provided by an aruco marker. Challenges arise when this information is compro­
mised by inadequate illumination or visual distortions, potentially leading to inac­
curate grasping. Such conditions can obscure the spatial relationship between the
robot and the object, complicating the insertion process and impacting the overall
system reliability.

1.5
Proposed method
Two deep learning models are trained. The first model uses CNNs to accurately estimate
tri­axial forces, with the input to the network consisting of images of the sensor surface
deformation when in contact with an object. The second model, based on TCNs, re­
sponds instantly to slip conditions, using the tri­axial forces estimated by the previous
model as input. The combination of both models creates a robust grasping algorithm for
manipulation that dynamically adjusts the manipulator’s grip to accommodate variations
in object dimensions and to counteract potential slips caused by movement or external
disturbances.
For navigation, the robot uses a comprehensive set of on­board sensors, including IMUs,
laser scanners, and cameras, to fuse real­time sensor data and plan a feasible, collision­
free path using global and local planners while considering mechanical and user­defined
constraints.
To naturally grasp the object, the manipulator computes a feasible trajectory based on
a multi­query or single­query motion planner, taking into account both kinematic and dy­
namic constraints.
The core innovations of this work include a force estimation model that matches state­of­
the­art performance and a slip detection model capable of identifying slip events, thereby
increasing robustness in manipulation.

Literature review
This chapter provides a comprehensive review of existing literature about mobile manip­
ulators, tactile sensors, force estimation, and slip detection. It’s purpose is to provide an
examination that sets the current state of development and boundaries for this thesis. A
deep context about the tools and frameworks available for developing the methodologies
proposed in chapter 4 will be covered in chapter 3.
2.1
Mobile manipulators
Mobile manipulators represent a class of robotic systems that combine the mobility of a
mobile robot with the dexterity of a manipulator arm [13]. This combination allows robots
to perform a wide range of tasks in different environments, from construction, industrial
automation, warehouse logistics to healthcare and service. The synergy between move­
ment and manipulation in mobile manipulators introduces unique challenges in planning,
control and interaction with the environment.
Figure 2.1: HKUST mobile manipulator.
The main advantage of mobile manipulators is their ability to navigate complex environ­
ments while performing manipulation tasks [14]. This dual capability allows mobile manip­
ulators to interact with objects beyond the reach of fixed manipulators, thus improving their
operational efficiency and flexibility. For example, in a warehouse, a mobile manipulator
can autonomously navigate to a storage location, retrieve an item using its manipulator
arm, and transport it to another location, all perfectly done [15].
An effective integration of mobility and manipulation requires complex coordination be­
tween the mobile base and the manipulator arm, this coordination ensures that the robot
will maintain stability and precision while performing the tasks. To achieve such coordina­
tion, the control system of a mobile manipulator must consider the kinematic limitations of
Intelligent Mobile Manipulator and Dexterous Manipulation for Smart Assembly and Manufacturing
5
==PAGE:14==
both the mobile base and the manipulator arm to ensure smooth and collision­free move­
ments[16]. Additionally, the control system must be able to make dynamic adjustments to
respond to changes in the environment, such as moving obstacles or variations in the task
demands. For example, Xu et al. [17] developed a planner that optimizes the sequence
of base positions for a mobile manipulator, enabling it to efficiently collect objects from
multiple trays. This planner utilizes inverse kinematics to identify the base regions where
the manipulator can grasp the target objects. By moving the base to the intersection of
these regions, the operation time is minimized. However, this approach has a notable
limitation: the motion is not fully optimized, resulting in a relatively low success rate.
Mobile manipulators face several challenges, to ensure robust and reliable operation in a
dynamic and unstructured environment a sophisticated perception and control system is
required [18]. Choophutthakan et al. [19] developed a cascaded visual object localization
architecture that enables extensive area searches and progressively improves localization
precision. This system allows the mobile platform to accurately position itself between
the target and the designated parking position. These systems must be able to process
and make decisions in real time to deal with obstacles and unexpected changes in the
environment. Additionally, the design of mobile manipulators have to balance trade­offs
between mobility, dexterity, and payload capacity, to ensure that the robot can perform its
tasks efficiently.
2.2
Tactile sensors
Tactile sensing is becoming an essential element for robotic manipulation, providing in­
sights that cannot be obtained through distant sensors such as cameras or lidars. This
capability becomes particularly crucial when dealing in unstructured environments where
the exact characteristics of the objects being manipulated are often unknown or ambigu­
ous [20].
The data from tactile sensors on the gripper can allow the robot to have a better under­
standing of the grasp state, making possible to handle delicate objects more safely, re­
ducing the likelihood of causing damage during interaction [21]. These sensors enhance
the robot’s manipulability by detecting contact force, depth and other features depending
on the sensor. For example, tactile feedback enables the detection of potential object
slippage, allowing for real­time adjustments in gripping force [22].
There are many different types of tactile sensors available in the market with different
functionalities, but the ones explored in this thesis are vision based.

2.3
Slip detection and force estimation
Tactile sensing is crucial for autonomous systems to achieve stable grasping of objects.
Tri­axial force estimation and the effectiveness of slip detection methods is significantly
influenced by the type and configuration of the tactile sensor employed.
He et al. [27] developed a tactile sensor with a 3x3 array of sensing cells that can measure
tri­axial forces. This sensor detects slip by monitoring abrupt changes in the feedback
signals. When the sensor’s surface experiences a sudden slide during a stable grip, the
signal shows a sharp spike due to the tension from the sliding motion. As the sliding
transitions to smooth slipping, the signal levels out. This spike in the signal provides
useful feedback for adjusting the grip.
In the realm of vision­based tactile sensing, Sui et al. [28] proposed a method for slip
detection that combines contact force distribution and deformation information. The force
distribution is calculated using a pre­established relationship between deformation and
force, defined through a series of constraint equations. This method works well on sim­
ple surfaces but struggles with objects that have irregular features like bumps and dents.

Theoretical framework
This chapter explores the current applications of deep learning in this field and examines
the benefits of combining Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Temporal Convo­
lutional Networks (TCNs). It also delineates the main differences between regression
and classification, outlines optimization strategies for deep learning models to enhance
inference performance, and reviews current algorithms for Simultaneous Localization and
Mapping (SLAM). Furthermore, it contrasts the use of global and local planners for navi­
gation, as well as the distinctions between multi­query and single­query planners for ma­
nipulation tasks.
It’s primary objective is to provide an extensive review of current technologies, serving as
a foundation to comprehend the proposed methodologies presented in the subsequent
chapter.
3.1
Deep learning
Deep learning has revolutionized the field of artificial intelligence by enabling the develop­
ment of highly accurate and efficient models for a wide range of tasks. Among the various
architectures, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Temporal Convolutional Net­
works (TCNs) have emerged as powerful tools for handling spatial and temporal data,
respectively. This section delves into the application of CNNs and TCNs in time series
analysis, highlighting their roles in classification and regression tasks. By leveraging these
techniques, we can achieve significant advancements in predicting patterns, recognizing
anomalies, and making informed decisions.
3.1.1
Classification
Classification is a fundamental task where the objective is to predict the categorical label
of a given input. It can be utilized in many applications ranging from image recognition
and speech processing to medical diagnosis and sentiment analysis [30]. Classifiers are
handled by training deep neural networks on large datasets with pre­labeled examples,
these networks learn to map input features to the appropriate class labels, effectively
modeling the underlying patterns in the data [31].
The architecture of neural networks for classification usually involves several layers of
processing. Early layers are responsible for feature extraction, for example, in image
classification, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) utilize convolutional layers to capture
spatial relationships of features (e.g., edges leading to shapes, then to complex objects)
[32]. Another example, in natural language processing, recurrent neural networks (RNNs)
or transformers are used to handle sequential data, extracting contextual relationships
within texts [33].
Once features are extracted, they are passed through one or more fully connected lay­
ers, often ending with a softmax layer if the classification is multiclass. The softmax layer
converts the outputs of the network into a probability distribution over the predefined cat­
egories. This final output layer ensures that the sum of the probabilities for all classes
equals one, making the output interpretable as confidence scores for each class.

3.1.2
Convolutional neural networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a specialized type of neural network primar­
ily used for processing structured array data such as images. These networks excel at
capturing spatial information, with lower layers identifying basic features like edges and
textures, and higher layers learning more complex elements such as shapes and specific

To further improve their performance, CNNs incorporate specialized techniques such as
padding to maintain the spatial size of the output and prevent loss of edge information,
thereby enabling deeper network architectures. They also adjust stride parameters to
manage output size, which is essential for reducing computational load and expanding
the filters’ field of view. Modern CNNs integrate batch normalization and dropout layers
to accelerate convergence and mitigate overfitting [38]. Batchnorm layers standardize
inputs to each neuron across a mini­batch and dropout layers randomly omit units during
training to avoid feature co­adaptation [39]. Together, these features significantly improve
the robustness and efficacy of CNNs in complex tasks like image recognition and classi­
fication.
3.1.3
Regression
Regression is another core task in deep learning, where the objective is to predict contin­
uous values rather than discrete class labels [40]. Deep learning models for regression
tasks typically employ neural network architectures similar to those used for classification
but with modifications to accommodate continuous output [41]. A common architecture
includes multiple hidden layers, each comprising numerous neurons activated by non­
linear functions such as ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit). The final layer, however, differs in
that it often has a single neuron (or multiple neurons in the case of multivariate regression)
and uses a linear activation function to produce a continuous output.

Navigation Planners
Navigation planning is a critical component in the operation of differential robots, which
are commonly used in various applications such as logistics, service robotics, and au­
tonomous vehicles. Effective navigation planners are essential for enabling these robots
to move from one point to another safely and efficiently [61]. This section provides a broad
overview of the fundamental concepts and methodologies used in navigation planning for
differential robots.

A navigation planner for differential robots typically consists of several interconnected
modules that work together to achieve the desired movement. The primary components
include:
• Perception: This module involves gathering and interpreting data from sensors
such as lidar, cameras, and ultrasonic sensors. The perception system constructs
a map of the environment and identifies obstacles that the robot must avoid.
• Localization: Accurate localization is crucial for effective navigation. Localization
methods, such as the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) and Particle Filter, help the
robot determine its precise position within the environment by fusing data from var­
ious sensors.
• Path Planning: The path planning module generates a feasible path from the
robot’s current location to the target destination. This involves selecting a series
of waypoints that the robot will follow while avoiding obstacles. Common algorithms
used in path planning include A*, Dijkstra’s algorithm, and Rapidly­exploring Ran­
dom Trees (RRT).
• Motion Planning: Motion planning focuses on generating the specific motor com­
mands required to follow the planned path. This module takes into account the kine­
matic constraints of the differential drive system, ensuring smooth and collision­free
movement.
Path planning algorithms are at the heart of navigation planners. They can be broadly
categorized into two types: global planners and local planners.
3.4.1
Global planners
Global planners are essential for guiding robots from a starting point to a target destination,
they take into account the entire environment, typically represented as a detailed map.
This map can be grid based, where the space is divided into cells, or graph based, where
nodes represent positions and edges represent possible paths [62]. The main goal of
global planners is to find the most efficient path, minimizing travel distance or time while
considering factors like energy use and safety [63]. Because these environments can be
large and complex, global planners also need to be computationally efficient, so they can
ensure to generate paths quickly.
Some common global planners:
• Dijkstra’s Algorithm: This algorithm finds the shortest path between nodes in a
graph by exploring all possible paths in order of increasing cost. While guaranteed
to find the optimal path, it can be slow for large maps as it does not use heuristics
to guide the search.

Local planners continuously update the robot’s path based on sensor data, allowing the
robot to react swiftly to new obstacles or changes in its environment.
Dynamic obstacle avoidance is a key feature of local planning. Unlike global planners,
local planners handle moving obstacles and sudden changes, adjusting the robot’s tra­

3.5
Manipulation Planners
Manipulation planning is an essential aspect of robotic control, particularly for robot arm­
s/manipulators engaged in tasks that require precise and dexterous movements. These
planners are responsible for generating the sequence of movements that a robot arm must
execute to manipulate objects, navigate through its workspace, and accomplish complex
tasks [66]. This section delves into the principles of manipulation planning, focusing on
the strategies used to address the challenges of planning for robot arms.
Figure 3.23: Motion planning of mobile manipulator [67].
Manipulation planners must consider several critical factors, including the kinematic con­
straints of the robot, the dynamics of the task, and the presence of obstacles in the en­
vironment [68]. These planners typically operate within high­dimensional configuration
spaces, where each dimension corresponds to a degree of freedom of the robot arm.
The complexity of these spaces necessitates sophisticated algorithms capable of effi­
ciently exploring and mapping feasible paths [69]. Two main categories of manipulation
planners have emerged to address these challenges: multi­query planners and single­
query planners.
3.5.1
Multi­query planners
Multi­query planners are designed to efficiently handle multiple planning requests within
a given environment. These planners are particularly advantageous in scenarios where
the environment remains relatively static, and the robot is required to perform a variety of
tasks over an extended period [70]. Multi­query planners invest significant computational
resources upfront to preprocess the environment, constructing a comprehensive roadmap
or graph that represents feasible paths within the configuration space [71]. Once this
roadmap is constructed, subsequent planning queries can be resolved quickly by search­
ing the precomputed structure.
One of the most prominent algorithms in this category is the Probabilistic Roadmap (PRM).
PRM operates by randomly sampling points in the configuration space and connecting
these points to form a network of valid paths [72]. This network, or roadmap, serves as
a reusable structure that can be queried multiple times, making PRM highly efficient for
environments where numerous tasks need to be performed.

The primary advantage of multi­query planners lies in their efficiency for repeated tasks.
Once the initial roadmap is established, planning times for subsequent queries are signifi­
cantly reduced, making these planners ideal for applications such as automated manufac­

preprocessing phase can be computationally intensive, and the planner’s performance is
highly dependent on the quality and completeness of the constructed roadmap [72].
3.5.2
Single­query planners
In contrast, single­query planners are tailored for scenarios where planning requests are
unique or infrequent, and the environment may be dynamic or partially unknown. These
planners do not rely on extensive preprocessing but instead focus on quickly finding a
feasible path for each individual query [74]. This approach makes single­query planners
more flexible and adaptive to changes in the environment.
A well­known algorithm in this category is the Rapidly­exploring Random Tree (RRT). RRT
builds a tree of feasible paths by incrementally exploring the configuration space from the
starting position towards the goal [75]. This method is highly effective in quickly identifying
viable paths, even in high­dimensional spaces with complex constraints. RRT’s ability to
dynamically adapt to new information makes it particularly useful in environments where
obstacles may move or where the robot must interact with unpredictable elements [76].

Learning slip detection
To detect the moment when an object slips from the gripper, it is essential to understand
its temporal context. For this purpose, a continuous sequence of the magnitudes of the
three measured forces will be used as input. The length of this sequence will be 100
seconds, with a sampling rate of 70 Hz. The sequence will consist of three features
represented by lags of 5, 10, and 15 seconds. This vector will serve as the input to our
temporal convolutional network, and the output will be a probability indicating whether the
next timestep will represent a static state or a slip.

extract temporal features. In this context, d represents the dilation, and k represents the
kernel size. Following the DCC layers, the architecture includes two fully connected layers

Robust grasp algorithm
It is important to note that this research developed two algorithms for two different visual­

force feedback and focuses on slip detection to achieve a robust grasp. In contrast, the
algorithm based on the CompdVision sensor utilizes both force feedback and environ­

difference is due to the fact that the force feedback from the CompVision sensor is not as
reliable as that from the Gelsight Mini.
The algorithm depicted in Figure 4.7 outlines a process for ensuring a secure grasp of
an object using a robotic gripper and the Gelsight Mini sensor. The sequence begins
with the ”Start” state, which is represented by the current state (pose) of the gripper. The
first step involves incrementally closing the gripper around the object. The algorithm then
checks whether the magnitude of the gripping force (Fm) exceeds a predefined thresh­
old. If the force measure exceeds this threshold, the gripper stops closing; otherwise, it
continues to close. This ensures that the minimum sufficient force is applied to hold the
object securely. If the force threshold is met, the algorithm proceeds to pick up the object.
Following this, it continuously monitors for any slip detection. If a slip is detected, the
algorithm responds by incrementally closing the gripper by a predetermined percentage
to increase the grip strength and prevent the object from slipping further. This loop of
checking for slip and adjusting the grip continues until no slip is detected. Once the object
is securely held without any slip, the algorithm transitions to the ”Done” state, indicating
the successful completion of the grasping task. This process ensures that the object is
held firmly, dynamically adjusting the grip based on real­time feedback.

5.6
Discussion
The results demonstrate the capabilities of the various components that comprise the mo­
bile manipulator (visual­based tactile sensor, mobile base, and manipulator). The Com­
pdVision sensor exhibits functional issues due to the lack of an internal light source to illu­
minate the blobs when the sensor surface is covered by an object. This problem prevents
the sensor from being used with the proposed methodology for achieving a robust grasp
with deep learning. Nevertheless, its camera remains useful for detecting other types of
visual cues, such as colors. The main differences between CompdVision and GelSight
Mini lie in two aspects: first, the surface of GelSight Mini is not transparent; second, it has
an integrated light source, allowing the sensor to use blob dispersion as features for train­
ing the suggested models, whereas CompdVision cannot. These limitations are evident
when analyzing the graphs [5.8, 5.7, 5.9].
Regarding the force estimation models trained to predict the three forces (Fx, Fy, Fz), it
is noteworthy that among the three different architectures, MidNet provides the most ac­
curate predictions compared to the ground truth, while VGG16 is the least accurate. The
advantage of using MidNet is that it is a relatively small model, occupying approximately
120MB, and when optimized with the OpenVINO toolkit, its inference time is 11ms, mak­
ing it suitable for real­time prediction applications. Combining it with the slip detection
model it shows that the TCN architecture can generalize well and accurately classify mo­
ments when an object is about to slip from the gripper. With both models functional, we
can apply the proposed robust grasping algorithm. It is important to note that GelSight
Mini was used to validate the proposed methodology, where deep learning is employed
to train the mentioned models. However, for the practical demonstration of the pick­and­
place activity, CompdVision must be used as this project is subject to client requirements.
Therefore, the alternative methodology has been implemented, utilizing visual feedback
to primarily detect colors as cues for controlling the grasping process.
Regarding the navigation planners, the best local planner for the mobile platform is defi­
nitely TEB. By the nature of the algorithm, it includes the functionality to move in reverse
like a vehicle and to rotate according to the spatial circumstances. It is also the planner
with the fewest collisions and a success rate of 80% for the five different poses com­
manded on the map. The best global planner is undoubtedly Global (Base), as it gener­
ates the shortest path and adequately considers the obstacle field for trajectory genera­
tion, allowing the mobile base to navigate between obstacles regardless of how narrow
the area is.
Comparing the different manipulation planners, it is evident that for pick­and­place appli­
cations in dynamic environments, single­query planners perform better than multi­query
planners. This is confirmed by the results showing that the three fastest planners in cal­
culating the trajectory belong to the single­query category: RRT, SBL, and RRTConnect.
Any of these planners allow the manipulator to complete the entire pick­and­place task in
approximately one minute.

6
Conclusion
This thesis proposes two algorithms for robust grasping to manipulate objects, particularly
hard drives, based on various vision­based tactile sensors.
The CNN­based algorithm used to estimate tri­axial forces performs satisfactorily when
trained with three primitive geometric shapes. Additionally, the TCN­based algorithm for
slip detection achieves an accuracy of over 90% with just 40 minutes of stored sequence
data. The proposed method for generating robust grasping by using both networks to­
gether can operate at 70Hz. The sensor’s sensitivity is high enough to detect disturbances
with just 100 points of historical data.
Among the four global and local planners, Global(Base) and TEB provide the best perfor­
mance for navigating narrow environments, such as warehouse aisles. They also enable
the robot to maneuver effectively, performing complex configurations to avoid obstacles
and reverse like a common vehicle. The generated path always considers the inflation ra­
dius of obstacles, ensuring the robot has sufficient space to avoid contact with surrounding
surfaces.
Single­query manipulation planners are the most suitable for pick­and­place tasks that
require the robot to move across various locations within the plant. These planners gen­
erate a faster trajectory than multi­query planners because they do not need to construct
an entire roadmap. RRT, RRTConnect, and SBL are ideal for completing the task in ap­
proximately 60 seconds.
Despite the fact that the force feedback signal from CompdVision is unreliable due to
the lack of its own light source to distinguish blobs on the surface when in contact with
an object, the image feedback signal still provides significant value. It can detect other
features such as colors, allowing for interruptions in object manipulation based on the
observed colors.
Overall, the proposed methodologies for robust grasping and autonomous navigation per­
form adequately with the mentioned technologies, allowing for the successful execution
of pick­and­place tasks for rectangular objects like hard drives.
6.1
Future work
Instead of separating navigation and manipulation tasks, this approach seeks to combine
them into a single, more natural, and robust movement. This integration aims to further
reduce operation times while maintaining the safety and stability needed to grasp objects
without allowing disturbances to affect performance.
A Model Predictive Control (MPC) based algorithm could effectively unify the aforemen­
tioned components by leveraging the kinematics of the mobile manipulator and the robust
grasp algorithm proposed in this research.
Additionally, increase the robustness of the dataset for slip detection. Currently, each
data point is labeled manually, and the goal is to automate this process to avoid limiting
the model’s performance with incorrect manual labels and capture a higher resolution slip
moments for labeling it.
Finally, the aim is to integrate all tasks together (navigation, manipulation, and robust
