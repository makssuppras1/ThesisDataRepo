Abstract  
Antibiotics are among the greatest discoveries of mankind and they combat dangerous infections, 
improved survival rate in operations, and increased lifespans. However, the use of antibiotics has 
been high and careless. Due to the overuse of antibiotics, an epidemical rise in antibiotic resistant 
bacteria has arisen. The situation is so dire, that many healthcare organizations are calling it a threat 
to humanity. With the rise in resistance there is a need for new and improved antibiotics. Where to 
look then? 
Actinobacteria have been a predominant resource in finding drugs and especially antibiotics. The 
genus Streptomyces is one predominant source of antibiotics. Streptomyces has a vast pharmacologic 
potential that remains unopposed among other microbial groups. One newly marine derived Strepto-
myce from Xinghai Bay produced a compound with excellent antibacterial activities. The structure 
given for the compound seemed to be wrong, and thereby still novel. To find the correct structure or 
other interesting compounds small-scale fermentations with different media were performed. Three 
different media named as MTSB, coconut mannitol and soya mannitol media were used. All media 
with or without sea salts were tested. The condition testing led to no new compounds detected, but 
two pink compounds appeared only in coconut mannitol with sea salt. Through HPLC-MS analysis, 
they were proposed to be zincphyrin and coproporphyrin III, respectively. To confirm the proposed 
structures, a large-scale fermentation was performed. The first attempt did not lead to finding the pink 
compounds, instead three other compounds were found. The proposed structures for two of the three 
were xiamycin and oxiamycin, with the third compound being unknown. The compounds were sep-
arated by semi-preparative HPLC, where only the proposed xiamycin and oxiamycin were fully sep-
arated and by 1HNMR spectrum, structure of xiamycin could be confirmed. 
A second attempt of a large-scale fermentation was performed to find the structure of the pink pig-
ments. Both pink compounds could be detected. Analysis of the HRMS data proposed that they are 
zincphyrin and coproporphyrin III, respectively. Due to the low amounts, NMR experiments were 
unsuccessful.

Antibiotics  
The definition of antibiotics was described by S. A. Waksman in 1947 as: “An antibiotic is a chemical 
substance, produced by micro-organisms, which has the capacity to inhibit the growth of and even to 
destroy bacteria and other micro-organisms”1 Now the meaning of antibiotics has changed into mul-
tiple meanings: 
1. An organic chemical of natural or synthetic origin that inhibits or kills pathogenic bacteria 
2. Any antimicrobial substance  
3. Any antimicrobial substances of microbial origin, like what Waksman said1 
The modern era of antibiotics started off with the discovery of penicillin by Sir Alexander Fleming 
in 1928. Since that discovery, antibiotics have saved millions of lives.2,3 Antibiotics have not only 
saved lives but have also played a big role in medicine and surgery. Antibiotics make it possible to 
treat or prevent dangerous infectious diseases in patients who are receiving chemo therapy, who have 
a chronicle disease such as diabetes or people with any sort of immune system deficiency.2–4  
Antibiotics have also been used to extend life span by changing the outcome of a bacterial infec-
tion.2,4,5 In 1920, people in US were averaging a lifespan on 56.4, but now they are expected to have 
a lifespan on 80 years.2 Antibiotics have also helped developing countries where the sanitization is 
not very high, by decreasing the morbidity and mortality caused by food-borne and other poverty-
related infections.4  
Both fungi and bacteria are prolific antibiotic producers. In bacteria, the predominate species that 
produce antibiotics are actinobacteria.6  
 
Actinobacteria- prolific source of antibiotics  
Actinobacteria  
Actinobacteria comes from a distinct clade of Gram-positive bacteria which contains many genera.7 
The earliest descriptions of Actinobacteria were those of Streptothrix foersteri in 1875 by Ferdinand 
Cohn and in 1877 Actinomyces bovis by Carl Otto Harz. Harz described A. bovis, causing a disease 
called ‘lumpy jaw’ in cattle.  A. bovis has thin filaments which ended in club shaped bodies that Harz 
thought to be ‘gonidia’ from fungi. The assumption of the bacteria being fungi gave the name

Actinomyces. Actinomyces in Latin means ray fungus. This was then a wrong assumption because 
the gonidia were only host cells. The phylogenetic position of Actinomycetales as bacteria was first 
officially established in 1960s. At this point of time, actinobacteria is one of the largest phyla of the 
bacteria kingdom. The actinobacteria have a diverse morphological and can either be coccoids like 
Micrococcus or rods like Mycobacterium or to the branching mycelium like Streptomyces. Actino-
bacteria are not bounded to one type environment and can be isolated in both aquatic and terrestrial 
habitats. Actinobacteria species are now isolated from very different places such as medieval wall 
paintings, desert soil, butter, marine sponges, and radon-containing hot springs. Actinobacteria can 
adapt to many different environments because, they are able to produce a variety of extracellular 
hydrolytic enzymes. The ability of producing different types of enzymes has particularly been seen 
in soil, where they are able to breakdown dead plants, animals and fungal materials. Some actinobac-
teria breaks down more complex molecules, where the Rhodococcus species is a good example. This 
type of actinobacteria can degrade nitro-, di-nitrophenol, pyridine, and nitroaromatic compounds.7 
Actinobacteria lifecycle  
The most described life cycle of actinobacteria is the one of Streptomyces, because of its commercial 
value. Streptomyces differentiate taxon to perform their growth with the formation of branching hy-
phae which then form a vegetative mycelium. In favorable conditions, spores will start to germinate 
and tends to form hyphae. Hyphae grows by a tip extension and finally forms a mycelium. If there 
are not sufficient nutrients or any other signal is encountered, many different types of morphological 
differentiations start to occur. One of these morphological differentiations is the development of aerial 
hyphae. Aerial hyphae are spores that form on specialized reproductive structures for the Streptomy-
ces. Aerial hyphae are then made into prespore chain through a controlled cell division process. From 
here on, the mature spores with thick coats are formed to continue the lifecycle of Streptomyces. (see 
Figure 1)8

Actinobacteria and antibiotics  
Over the past few decades, interest in actinobacteria and their ability to produce interesting secondary 
metabolites with potential of being bioactive, especially marine actinobacteria. Marine actinobacteria 
are thought to be treasure houses for new drug discoveries.9 The genus Streptomyces is the most 
harnessed actinobacteria and has found out that it contains many novel secondary metabolites.10 80 
% of all the natural products from actinobacteria comes from Streptomyces.11 Each strain of actino-
bacteria is assumed to have the potential to produce 15 to 25 secondary metabolites.12 Streptomyces 
could produce varied range of biomolecules, including antibiotics. Streptomyces have a vast pharma-
cologic potential that remains unopposed among other microbial groups. The vast diversity and un-
derutilized potential of secondary metabolites makes actinobacteria a great candidate for making new 
antibiotics.9 
Abyssomicin C (see structure on Figure 2) is a novel polycyclic polyketide antibiotic, produced by a 
marine actinobacterium named Verrucosispora sp.13 Abyssomicin C works potently well to inhibit 
aminobenzoicacid biosynthesis and thereby inhibits the folic acid biosynthesis at an earlier stage than 
most of the sulfa drug.14 It has potent activity against Gram positive bacteria including clinical isolates 
of multiple-resistant and vancomycin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus.15  
Bonactin(Figure 2) has been isolated from a Streptomyces sp. Bonactin showed antimicrobial activity 
against both Gram positive and negative bacteria.16 Another new antibiotics is Chlorinated dihydro-
quinones(Figure 2), which was discovered from a new marine Streptomyces sp.  This compound has 
a new carbon skeleton but it is similar to several previously reported compounds of the napyradio-
mycin class.17   
Diazepinomicin(Figure 2) with  a unique farnesylated dibenzodiazepinone produced by a Micromon-
ospora strain has shown to have antibacterial, anti-inflammatory and antitumor activity. Diazepinom-
icin showed a broad spectrum of activities against glioma, breast and prostate cancer in mouse mod-
els.18  
Frigocyclinone(Figure 2) isolated from Streptomyces griseus strain NTK 97. This is a new an-
gucyclinone antibiotic that has showed promising results against Gram positive bacteria. Frigocy-
clinone contains a tetrangomycin moiety attached through a C-glycosidic linkage with the aminode-

Mechanisms of Antibiotics, Drug-resistance and Current Situation 
Mechanisms of Antibiotics 
Antibiotics targets the main functions in bacteria that are needed for the bacteria’s own survival, the 
fundamentals of inhibiting bacterial growth.  The process is complex that starts by physical interac-
tion of the antibiotic and its specific target. This involves biochemical, molecular, and structural 
changes that will act on multiple cellular targets such as: DNA replication, RNA synthesis, cell wall 
synthesis, and protein synthesis.21 (See Figure 3) 
 
Figure 3 Schematic representation of the target and mechanism of action of certain antibiotics.21 
==PAGE:9==

DNA replication is important for the development of cell division. DNA gyrase(topoisomerase) func-
tion by controlling the topology of the DNA. DNA gyrase catalyzes the cleavage pattern and DNA 
binding. The reaction is important for DNA synthesis and mRNA transcription. Antibiotics named 
fluoroquinolones inhibit DNA gyrase, and there by stopping it to incisions the double-stranded DNA. 
DNA gyrase contains four different subunits, where two of the subunits are A and two are B. Subunit 
As function is to carry out the incision of DNA and the resealing of the strands, where B subunit 
introduces negative supercoils. The fluoroquinolones inhibit DNA gyrase by binding to A subunits, 
which results in blocking of sub unit As function.5,21–23 
Transcription process and the main regulator of gene expression in prokaryote is mediated by DNA-
dependent RNA polymerase. This enzyme is critical for cell growth, which makes this a lucrative 
target for antibiotics. An example of antibiotic targeting RNA polymerase is rifamycin. Rifamycin 
inhibits the synthesis by binding to β-subunit in the RNA/DNA channel of RNA polymerase. This 
leads to a separation of the active site, and thereby inhibiting the transcription and blocking the growth 
of ribonucleotide chain.24  
Bacterial cell walls are made of peptidoglycan, which contains long sugar polymers of transglyco-
sides crosslinks peptidoglycan of glycans strands. The peptide chains extend from the sugars in the 
polymers and form cross links, one peptide to another.25 The peptide has a D-alanyl-alanine portion 
which is cross linked by glycine residues in the presence of penicillin binding proteins(PBPs). The 
cross-linking function is to strengthen the cell wall.26 Antibiotics targeting this cell wall synthesis are 
β-lactams and the glycopeptides.

β-lactams works by targeting PBPs. The β-lactam ring mimics the D-alanyl-alanine portion of the 
peptide that normally binds to PBPs. The PBPs interact with the β-lactam ring and will be blocked 
and thereby not be able to undergo the synthesis of new peptidoglycans. The disruption in the pepti-
doglycan synthesis leads to lysis of bacteria cells (see Figure 4).27   
The glycopeptides bind to D-alanyl D-alanine portion of peptide side chain of the precursor pepti-
doglycan subunit. An example of this is the large drug molecule vancomycin which prevents binding 
of this D-alanyl subunit with the PBP. This will then lead to a prevention of the peptidoglycan syn-
thesis and thereby killing the cell.27,28 
Disrupting the proteins synthesis is done by interfering with the translation process. This process has 
three phases: Initiation, elongation and termination. This process is catalyzed by cytoplasmic ribo-
somes (see Figure 5). The bacterial ribosome 70Ss contains two subunits, 50Ss and 30Ss which are 
the main targets of antibiotics.21,29

Antibiotics resistance epidemic 
Antibiotics resistance is widespread across the world.2 A 2011 survey conducted by the Infectious 
Diseases Society of America(IDSA) Emerging Infections Network found out that 60% of the partic-
ipants had witnessed a pan-resistant, untreatable infection within that year.30 The description of the 
rapid emergence in resistant bacteria by many public health organizations as “crisis” or “nightmare 
scenario” that could have “catastrophic consequences”.31 The Centers of Disease Control and Pre-
vention(CDC) came out in 2013 and said human beings are in the “post-antibiotic era”. The World 
Health Organization(WHO) sent warnings in 2014 that the antibiotic resistance is becoming dire.32 
Multi drug resistant bacteria have been declared as one of the most substantial threats for U.S. public 
health and national security by the IDSA and the institute of medicine.2 The spread of resistance is 
seen in both Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria.  
Antibiotics resistance in Gram positive bacteria 
In Gram positive pathogens there is a global pandemic of resistant S. aureus and Enterococcus species 
that currently possess the biggest threat.4 Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus(MRSA) has a 
higher death toll of Americans each year than HIV/AIDS, Parkinson’s disease, emphysema, and hom-
icide combined.33 MRSA has been known for a while and the first time it was spotted was five decades 
ago.30 Since the first instant of MRSA discovery, the infections by MRSA have spread worldwide. 
Incident predominantly occurred in Europe, North- and South America, and Asia Pacific region.4 The 
infections occurring from MRSA can be very serious and is one of the more frequently happening 
infections of all antibiotics resistant infections.2  
MRSA is normally only resistant towards penicillin-like beta-lactam antibiotics.2 This thereby leaves 
the chance for other types of antibiotics, such as glycopeptides, linezolid, tigecycline, daptomycin, 
and even some new beta-lactams.4 The problem with MRSA is, that they have shown a high level of 
versatility in emerging and spreading in different epidemiological over time, such as in hospitals, the 
community, and, more recently, in animals.4

The rise in the number of Vancomycin resistant enterococci(VRE) and additional pathogen are de-
veloping resistant to many of the common used antibiotics is giving a lot of complication in health 
care.2 Enterococci causes many different types of diseases and mostly among patients in hospitals or 
other healthcare sites such as bloodstream, surgical-site, and urinary tract infections.34 VRE infec-
tions are often caused by Enterococcus faecium and less frequently by Enterococcus faecalis. VRE 
infections have a lower prevalence and epidemic than MRSA does worldwide, except in the U.S. and 
some European countries.4 An estimated number of 66000 hospital associated infections are caused 
by Enterococci each year in the U.S.2 The proportion of the infection that vancomycin resistant de-
pends on which species it is. 30 % of hospital-acquired enterococcal infections per year are vanco-
mycin-resistant, with a death toll on 1300 deaths.2 
The spread in antibiotic resistance of common respiratory diseases is on an epidemic level, especially 
Streptococcus pneumoniae and Mycobacterium tuberculosis.4 Streptococcus pneumoniae causes se-
rious infection and in some cases, they can be life threatening.2 Streptococcus pneumoniae causes 
bacterial pneumonia, meningitis, bloodstream, ear, and sinus infections.33 Streptococcus pneumoniae 
resistance infection can complicate medical treatment and resulting in nearly 1.2 million illnesses and 
7000 deaths each year.2 The deaths occurring by Streptococcus pneumonia happens normally among 
adults at the age of 50 or older, where the highest occurring at age of 65.2 Streptococcus pneumoniae 
resistance is still under control because of a new version of pneumococcal conjugate vaccine that 
protects the most resistant pneumococcus strains. The other bacteria Mycobacterium tuberculosis is 
a serious threat worldwide and needs urgent attention. WHO is reporting in 2012 that 170000 people 
died from drug resistance tuberculosis infections.2 The infection can happen all through the body, but 
mainly it starts in lungs and spreads from there. In the total case of 10528 tuberculosis in US 2011, 
9,9% of the cases were found to be drug resistant.2 The reason why tuberculosis drug resistance is 
emerging as a threat is incomplete, incorrect, or unavailable treatment and a lack of new drugs for the 
bacteria.2 
Antibiotics resistance in Gram negative  
The problem of resistance in Gram negative bacteria is worrisome because they are getting resistant 
to nearly all good antibiotics.4 The occurring cases of drug resistant Gram-negative bacteria in all 
healthcare and medicine fields are alarming.2 In healthcare the most critical drug resistance bacteria 
are enterobacteriaceae, pseudomonas aeruginosa, and acinetobacter.4

Reasons for antibiotic resistance  
As mentioned before, antibiotics has been one of the greatest discovery. But as predicted by Sir Al-
exander Fleming that public demand will lead to an era of abuse.2,30 This overuse of antibiotic has led 
to enhance the rate of bacteria resistance towards antibiotics. Epidemiolocal studies have shown a 
direct correlation between the use of antibiotics and the emergence of resistant bacteria strains.35 
Bacteria do not necessarily need to be descendent of resisting bacteria but can gain resistance through 
genetic elements such as plasmids. This phenomenon is called horizontal gene transfer and can hap-
pen between different species of bacteria.36 Resistance can also happen through spontaneous muta-
tions. Despite the warnings of over use, antibiotics are over prescribed worldwide without caution.35,36 
In USA the number of prescription is so high that it is a must to do work on reducing the number of 
prescriptions.33  IMS Health Midas database made an analysis to give a number estimation on the 
consumption based on the volume of antibiotics sold in retail and hospital pharmacies. The analysis 
indicated there were given 22 standard unit antibiotics in 2010 in USA per persons, where one unit is 
equaling one dose, i.e., one pill, capsule, or ampoule.37 In some other countries antibiotics are even 
==PAGE:14==

given without a prescription and unregulated. The lack of regulation leads to antibiotics being acces-
sible, abundant and cheap which leads to over use. The ability to order them online has increased the 
accessibility.35  
The problem doesn’t necessarily lay only in the over prescription of antibiotics. Incorrectly prescrib-
ing antibiotic also leads to overuse. It has been shown through studies that treatment indication, choice 
of agent, or the length of antibiotic therapy is incorrect in 30% to 50% of the time.38 In addition, 
antibiotics prescribed in intensive care units have been deemed unnecessary, inappropriate, or subop-
timal in 30% to 60% of the time.38 The incorrect prescribed antibiotics have some questionable ben-
efits but can lead to expose the patients to potential complications of antibiotic therapy.39 The sub-
therapeutic antibiotic concentrations can give a rise in the development of antibiotic resistance by 
supporting genetic alterations, changes in gene expression, horizontal gene transfer, and mutagene-
sis.31  
Agriculture have been using antibiotics widely as growth supplements in both the developed and 
developing world.35 80% of all antibiotics sold in USA are used in animals, primarily to promote 
growth and to prevent infection.33 Farm animals have been noted 35 years ago to be able to transfer 
resistant bacteria to farmers, when high amount of antibiotic resistant bacteria were found in the in-
testinal flora of both farm animals and farmers.40 Not only do the resistant bacteria reach farmers, but 
also it has been found out the resistant bacteria reaches consumers through meat consumption.2  
The overuse of antibiotics also has an effect with environmental microbiome.40 90 % of the used 
antibiotics given to farm animals are excreted in urine and stalls.2,40 The excreted material is then 
wildly spread through fertilizer, groundwater, and surface runoff. This will lead to much more expo-
sure of antibiotics to bacteria and will lead to more them getting resistance to antibiotics.2 
 
Current state in antibiotic research  
To combat the rise of antibiotic resistance there is a need to find some new and better antibiotics, 
which was the go to strategy in the past.40 This has gone down due to the large economical cost and 
regularity obstacle (see Figure 6).40 The 15 out of the 18 leading companies in pharmaceuticals have 
dropped the antibiotic research.40

Detection of new antibiotics 
Dereplication techniques 
Actinobacteria are a rich source for finding new antibiotics, but that does not mean that products you 
find will always be novel. Dereplication of known compounds and their analogs is an important and 
==PAGE:16==
12 
 
time saving part of discovering new compounds. Dereplication is a technic that combines chroma-
tography and spectrometry method with a database.  An example of such a database is Antibase, 
which is constructed by Laatsch and co-workers. One preferred way of dereplication is using Liquid 
chromatography(LC) combined with UV/vis diode array detection and mass spectrometry(MS). 
UV/vis diode array detection is a cheap and reliable way to see if the compound contains conjugated 
chromophore. LC-HRMS dereplication provides accurate mass, which can be searched in any given 
database for natural products, so with one simple search you can see if the product is novel or not.42     
Chromatography 
Chromatography has the same principles as in extraction with one difference. The difference is that 
one phase is moving through a solid phase or a phase that is held on its place. Shown in Figure 7 is a 
column packed in solid particles and filled with solvent. In this column solutes A and B are placed. 
When the column is opened the solute A and B pass through. Solvent is again added at the top, so 
there is a constant flow of solvent. If solute A interacts more with the solid particles then solute B, 
solute A will go down slower than solute B. This is the way to separate particles through chromatog-
raphy. 
 
Figure 7 The idea behind chromatography: solute A, with a greater affinity than solute B for the stationary phase, remains on the

1930s. Bands marked by horizontal lines are different pigments. The lower stationary phase is Ca(OH)2 and the upper stationary phase 
is CaCO343. 
Mobile phase in chromatography is either a liquid or a gas. The stationary phase is commonly a 
viscous liquid chemically bonded to the inside of a capillary tube or onto the surface of solid particles 
packed in the column. In Figure 7 the stationary phase is the particles the solutes should go through. 
Columns are either packed or open tubular. A packed column is filled with particles of stationary

phase, like in Figure 7. A tubular column is a narrow, hollow capillary with the stationary phase 
coated on the walls.43 
HPLC 
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) uses high pressure to force solvent through closed 
columns containing fine particles that give high-resolution separations. HPLC contains autosampler, 
a column department, a sample injection valve, a high-pressure chromatography column, and a UV, 
which serves as a detector.43  
The Chromatographic Process 
To get the best chromatography result the rate at which solute equilibrates between stationary and 
mobile phases needs to be increased. In gas chromatography it is done by reducing the diameter of 
the tubular column, so the molecules can diffuse quickly between the channel and the stationary phase 
on the walls. That is not possible in liquid chromatography, because reducing the diameter will make 
it 100 time slower than gas chromatography and the diameter of the solvent channel is too great to be 
traversed by a solute molecule in a short time. Liquid chromatography is done in packed columns so 
that a solute molecule can diffuse with the stationary phase. The effectiveness of the packed column 
depends on the particle size, the smaller the better. 43 
Stationary phase 
The most common support is highly pure, spherical, microporous particles of silica that are permeable 
to solvent and have a surface area of several hundred square meters per gram. Bare silica can be used 
as the stationary phase for adsorption chromatography. Most commonly, liquid-liquid partition chro-
matography is conducted with a bonded stationary phase covalently attached to the silica surface. 
There can either be bounded a polar stationary phase or a non-polar stationary phase. The most com-
mon attached stationary phase is the octadecyl (C18), which is a non-polar stationary phase.43 
Elution phase 
Solvent molecules compete with solute molecules for sites on the stationary phase. The relative abil-
ities of different solvents to elute a given solute from the adsorbent are nearly independent of the 
nature of the solute. Elution occurs when solvent displaces solute from the stationary phase. 
A bare silica will be an example of normal phase chromatography, in which there is a polar stationary 
phase and a less polar solvent. A more polar solvent will be able to eluate more. Reverse phase and 
the more common technic is where the stationary phase is nonpolar or weakly polar and the solvent

is more polar. In reverse phase, the polar molecule will go through the column fast, because they are 
not interacting with the non-polar stationary wall. The opposite will happen to non-polar compound, 
and thereby extending their retention time. This is the most common way of performing HPLC.43 
Mass Spectrum 
Has been used for a longtime to measure isotopes and determining organic structures. The technic is 
to study the masses of atoms or molecules or fragments of molecules. Gaseous species desorbed from 
condensed phases are ionized, then accelerated by an electric field and separated by their mass to 
charge ratio. When the charge is 1, it means mass-to-charge ratio is equal to the mass. If there is a 
charge of 2, then it would be equivalent to half the mass. Example of a mass spectrum is shown in 
Figure 8, where the area of each peak is proportional to the abundance of Pb different isotopes.43   
 
Figure 8 Mass spectrum showing natural isotopes of Pb observed as an impurity in brass. The variability of isotopic abundances in 
Pb from natural sources creates a large uncertainty in the atomic mass (207.2 ± 0.1) in the periodic table.43 
In this project we used Bruker maXis, which is a Hybrid Quadrupole/Atmospheric Pressure Ionization 
orthogonal accelerated Time of Flight mass spectrometer. The instrument includes the Apollo II Elec-
trospray Ion Source, a quadrupole MS/MS-stage, a vertically arranged Time of Flight mass spectrom-
eter, a vacuum system (including the rough pump) and complete electronics. Route through the TOF-
Mass Spectrometer is shown in Figure 9. The Brucker Apollo source introduces the solved sample 
through a nebulizer into the spray chamber. Nebulizers function is to achieve reasonable sensitivity 
in the mass analysis of liquid samples. The sample must be sprayed into fine droplets, which can be 
easily evaporated when entering the vacuum system. After the nebulizer receives the sample, it will 
pass through a thin needle. The needle is placed inside a tube that delivers pressurized nebulizer gas 
that are usually nitrogen. In the end of the tube, the sample and the nebulizer gas interact in such a 
way, that small droplets are formed.44

and TOF spectrometer (orthogonal accelerator, dual stage reflector, detector)44 
In the spray chamber the sample will be introduced to the electrospray ionization process. This pro-
cess happens by means of an electrical field between the inner chamber wall and the spray shield with 
the aid of a nebulizer gas. During the process solvent will evaporate that results to the droplets being 
reduced in size and built up of the surface charge ratio. The built-up surface charge ratio will result 
in columbic forces, which will further break up the droplets (see Figure 10). 
 
Figure 10 Principle of the ESI process44 
A high voltage is then applied to the spray shield to attract the ions. The charged droplet is accelerated 
by the electrical field between the nebulizer and spray shield. Depending on which ions are made, the 
==PAGE:20==

spray shield can be negative charged or positive charged. A charge on -500V between the spray shield 
and the capillary cap directs the ions in to the glass capillary.  
The glass capillary then leads to ion transfer stage that contains the first three of the five vacuum 
stages in the mass spectrometer. At the first stage a roughing pump 28m3 reduces the pressure to 
around 3mbar.The aim of the ion transfer is to separate analyte ion from rest of the mixture and with 
least amount of ion loss to the quadrupole stage, which requires pressure lower than 3x10-5 mbar. The 
ions are guided by stacked ion rings with inner profile shaped as funnel. The stacked ion rings are 
applied with radio frequency to generate an effective potential that confines the ion beam inside the 
funnel. From the ion transfer stage comes the subsequent multipole, that is connected to the first turbo 
stage of the triple stage turbo pump. At this stage a multipole is used to focus the ion beam.  Radio 
frequency voltage is applied so there will be a radially increasing effective potential. The effective 
potential will focus the ions onto the multipole axis. This stage ends with a gate lens and a focusing 
lens. The next stage is the analytical quadrupole, which is the mass analyzer in in the maXis. The 
quadrupole consists of four parallel metal rods. The metal rods are applied with both a constant volt-
age and a radio-frequency oscillating voltage. The analytical quadrupole in maXis consist of three 
quadrupole segments. The middle segment is used as a mass filter, while the outer segments are used 
to optimize the ion transfer. In the quadrupole stage there is also a collision cell and a cooling cell.

Genomics-driven drug discovery 
The potential and fast development of genome sequencing methods has revolutionized nearly every 
aspect of biology including aspects in natural product research. In the last three decades the tech-
niques of identification and manipulation, from the first secondary metabolites genes to whole ge-
nome sequencing of thousands of bacterial genomes. The amount of genetic data available now, 
means there is no shortage of secondary gene clusters. The challenge is now to find a way to effec-
tively mine the data, connect the detected Biosynthetic Gene Clusters to the already known molecules 
and predict the ones encoding the promising compound. There have been developed tools to enable 
computationally mining genetic data. The data can then be connected to know secondary metabolites. 
The various strategies developed over time is shown in Figure 12.45

Classical way of genome mining was the mining of enzymes or more precisely genes encoding en-
zymes putatively involved in secondary metabolite biosynthesis. Examples of classes coming of sec-
ondary metabolites are polyketides, biosynthesized by polyketide synthases, non-ribosomally synthe-
sized peptides, produced by non-ribosomal peptide synthetases, ribosomally and post-translationally 
modified peptides, aminoglycosides, and many more.45 Softwares have been developed to aid scien-
tists in finding the enzymes involved in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites in (meta)genomic 
sequences.46 Softwares with the ability to do so have been one of the main innovation in natural 
product research.47 Further refinement in this, so the focus not only lies in single genes, but on partial 
or complete gene clusters. This type of functionality is found in the antiSMASH software.48 An-

library of other Biosynthetic Gene Clusters from different microorganisms and the curated MIBiG

HPLC and NMR 
Semi-preparative HPLC was carried out on an RP18 column using acetonitrile and H2O as mobile 
phases. Four compounds were collected and dried off (see Figure 39).  
 
 
Figure 39 Collecting vials and the dried of compounds 
Compound 1 (~0 mg) and 4 (1.0 mg) were colorless, while compound 2 (0.4 mg) and 3 (1.6 mg) had 
a pinkish color (see Figure 39). The amount of compound 1 was rather low, which means that the 
compound has been lost in the transfer process. The drying happened on rotary evaporator in round 
bottom flask. The compound was dried till no liquid was left and then transferred by methanol to 
smaller vials. If the compound is not dissolved in MeOH it will be left in the round bottom flask. The 
first peak could be the contamination from fraction 4 and 5 that were in fraction 6, because it came 
out first during the HPLC. 
Compound 2 and 3 both were pinkish, but amount of both is low. Compound 2 is the proposed copro-
porphyrin III and compound 3 is proposed to be zincphyrin structure. The peak of the zincphyrin was 
less intense then coproporphyrin III in the HPLC-MS before separation, but the mass was still higher 
(see Figure 38). Compound 4 was colorless and showed a small peak in the HPLC-MS before sepa-
ration, but still had a greater mass than coproporphyrin III.   
The compounds were send to 1HNMR to confirm the structure. However, no signals appeared in the 
NMR spectra which might be due to the poor solubility in MeOD.

Conclusion on largescale fermentation part 2 
The pink pigments were present in organic ethyl acetate phase and water phase. The ethyl acetate 
was discarded after the HP 20 diaion column, because the compound was not present. From the wa-
ter phase three compounds were isolated and two of them were the same pink compounds from the 
condition testing. 1HNMR was not good, so structure of the three isolated compounds could not be 
identified. Even though, solid HRMS data combined with the UV data strongly suggested that they 
are possibly zincphyrin and coproporphyrin. 
 
Conclusion  
It can be concluded that the purpose of the project of finding a new drug was not successful. No new 
compounds were found. Instead, the proposed xinghaiamine A from literature was not found even 
when using the same medium as in literature. The compound showed excellent results in bioactive 
test, but the structure in literature is not correct. The idea was to find this compound or another com-
pound that would have antimicrobial effect. This was not possible and instead two red compounds 
were found. The red pigment was only found in coconut mannitol with sea salt medium. The other 
medium did not have any signs of it. After viewing the HPLC-MS for the condition there were two 
peaks that gave the pink color. The compounds are expected to be zincphyrin and coproporphyrin III, 
because of the given mass spectra. It was interesting that color only came in that medium, so a 
largescale fermentation was carried out.  
After first attempt of a largescale fermentation, it can be concluded the purpose of large scaling the 
fermentation was not met. The pink compound did not show, but three other compounds appeared. 
Viewing the HPLC-MS results two of the compounds seemed to be xiamycin A and oxiamycin, where 
the third one seemed unknown. Separation of the three compounds was done through HPLC, where 
the proposed xiamycin A and oxiamycin did come out good, but not the third unknown compound. 
The third compound had contamination and did not separate properly, so was not sent to 1HNMR 
measurments. Only one of the two NMR was good enough to use for structure identification, and that 
was for the one for the proposed xiamycin A. The NMR can be concluded to confirm the proposed 
structure. 
The second attempt to largescale production of the pink compound can be concluded to nearly be 
successful. It is successful in the way that the pink compounds was seen, but the NMR for the 
==PAGE:47==

compounds was not good enough to do a structure identification. There were isolated 4 compounds 
from the fermentation, where two can be concluded to be the two pink compounds from condition 
testing. The mass of the four compounds were very low, where the first compound that was isolated 
had a mass on 0, the second 0.4 mg, the third 1.6 mg and the fourth 1 mg.  
 
Perspectiv 
Xinghaiamine A was the needle in a hay stack that was looking for during this project. Even though 
it was not found, some other compounds were isolated. To find xinghaiamine A a different approach 
during fermentation maybe needed. Xinghaiamine A could have been toxic for the host cells, which 
could have led to a rather low production of the toxic compound. To avoid this, adsorbent resins could 
be used to eliminate toxic effects on growth and reduce feedback repression of production. In one 
case HP-20 diaion was directly added in the fermentation and helped eliminate toxic effects on growth 
and in the purification.57 This could maybe be implied to improve the production of secondary me-
tabolites production.  
The pink compound did show in the final attempt of largescale fermentation, but the amount was too 
low. It can be that the pH had an influence on the stability or ionization of the compound. Maybe with 
lower pH the compound would not be ionized and thereby not salting out. It depends on the compound 
itself, but a difference in pH could lead to better extraction of the pink compound.
