Abstract
A quarter of the Spanish wind turbines will be reaching the end­of­life stage in 2024, a challenge
the wind industry will have to face in the upcoming years if they want to reach the climate tar­
gets set by the EU. The old wind farms are in the best locations but require more maintenance
and are less efficient than wind farms with newer wind turbines. So, by repowering, this wind
resource would be used more efficiently without the need to build new infrastructures around
it. Furthermore, it is not extensively studied how these projects are seen by the local com­
munities surrounding them, which makes it a focus area of this thesis. This thesis performs a
socio­technical analysis of the repowering scene in Spain, providing an overview of it through
a snapshot with interviews with relevant people in the sector and a case study located on the
Balearic Island of Menorca named Milà Wind Farm. The results show the extent of the repower­
ing potential in Spain now and in the coming years. The snapshot also provides a clear picture
of the state of repowering in Spain, highlighting the lack of a specific regulatory framework, the
lengthy permitting process and the negative impact these have on implementation as the main
challenges. Additionally, in the case study, a significant improvement in energy production is
achieved by increasing the total capacity of the wind farm and reducing the number of wind
turbines by half, considering the requirements for wind energy in the regional energy strategy.
Finally, the social study of the case provides insights into the importance of the local context
while studying the social acceptance of repowering projects, concluding that the extrapolation
of these results to a national level would not be representative due to the many particularities
in the case.

1
Introduction
The current climate emergency has highlighted the importance of the transition towards renew­
able energy generation. Therefore, technologies like solar and wind will play a crucial role in
reaching the established climate targets. The implementation of wind energy has been present
in Spain for over 25 years, making it a mature market with a solid industry and installed capacity.
However, if the sector wants to maintain the progress made so far, it faces the challenge of the
ageing of existing wind farms and all the potential problems that this entails.
As these wind farms reach the end of their useful life, their efficiency decreases and mainte­
nance costs increase. In addition, they often struggle to meet the requirements of the new grid
codes, their availability decreases over time due to obsolescence of wind turbine technology,
and then their installed capacity does not translate into as much electricity generation as it could.
A convincing solution to that is presented with the repowering, which is broadly defined as the
replacement of older wind turbines with newer, more efficient and powerful models. However,
the exact definition and scope of repowering can vary, sometimes creating confusion with life
extension operations aimed at prolonging the lifespan of existing turbines. This thesis analyses
the various approaches held under the umbrella of repowering, discussing the distinctions be­
tween repowering and life extension activities. Repowering not only lengthens the lifetime of the
wind farm but, among other things, also increases the amount of energy that can be extracted
at the same location as before, but with the knowledge that comes from operating the wind farm
there for 20 years or more. The opportunity to increase the economic performance of the wind
farm is one of the main reasons why owners are interested in repowering their ageing assets.
Although repowering is considered one of the key initiatives to contribute to the energy transition,
there are other aspects to take into account besides technical and economic, such as social
acceptance and public involvement in the process. Public acceptance plays a crucial role in
ensuring the successful implementation of these projects but is often overlooked or, in the case
of repowering, taken for granted.
Despite the urgent need to renew the wind turbines’ fleet in Spain, which is the oldest on aver­
age in Europe, and given that 25% of the capacity has already been operating for 20 or more
years, not many repowering projects are being carried out. So, this thesis investigates which
are the main challenges that repowering initiatives are facing, the repowering potential, what
factors have led to this situation, and what could be improved to move forward. This is done
through the development and study of the main research questions: What are the critical ele­
ments that contribute to the success of wind farm repowering initiatives in Spain, including the
dynamics of the wind energy industry, social perceptions, technical requirements, and the cur­
rent limitations existing in the repowering sector? How can these insights contribute to future
sustainable energy strategies?
The first chapter provides a ground understanding of the general status of the sector, delving
into the historical development, the present state and how the future trends look. The next
chapter dives into the social dimension, reviewing existing literature on social acceptance of
repowering projects. After that, the definition of repowering itself is analyzed, together with the
requirements, the permitting process and the distribution of costs. In the following chapter, an
explanation of the methodology used in the social, technical and economic studies is made,
facilitating the comprehension of the later results. After that, a snapshot of the current land­
scape of repowering is made through interviews with relevant profiles and the main findings are
analyzed. After exploring this broad vision of repowering, the next chapter is dedicated to a

for future repowering policies, wind farm operators and owners and all stakeholders in general
invested in Spain’s energy transition.

2.1
Energy policy framework and climate targets
The European Commission sets the climate strategies and targets through its 2030, 2040 and
2050 climate and energy frameworks, aiming to reach climate neutrality by 2050 [1]. The Eu­
ropean Green Deal is where the European Commission has aligned its strategy with the com­
mitment to global climate action under the Paris Agreement. One of its key points is a clean
energy transition since the production and use of energy account for more than 75% of the EU’s
greenhouse gas emissions [2]. To reach the 2030 climate objectives, it is critical to decarbonise
the EU’s energy system, which will be done according to each EU member’s national energy
and climate plans (NECP). These NECPs intend to address energy efficiency, energy security,
decarbonization, internal energy market, and research, innovation and competitivity [3]. In the
case of Spain, the final version of the NECP with the objectives for the period of 2021 ­ 2030
was published in December 2019 and the latest update was submitted in June 2023.
In terms of electrification and decarbonization of the energy system, as well as in the improve­
ment of energy security and energy dependency, the Spanish NECP foresees a total installed
capacity in the power system of 214 GW, of which 160 GW will be renewables and 22 GW
of energy storage by 2030. Additionally, participation of 48% of renewable energy in the final
usage of energy and 81% of renewables in the total of electricity generation is expected. The
main technologies envisioned to help achieve this value are solar photovoltaic and wind energy,
with 76 and 62 GW of installed capacity respectively [4]. In the case of wind power, given its
historical importance in the energy sector, the estimated evolution consists of constant growth
without needing huge yearly increases.
2.2
Evolution and future trends
Spain started its wind energy industry early on, by installing the first wind farms in the 1990s and
the first 15 GW between 15 and 25 years ago [5]. Since then, the sector has grown, reaching a
total of 30.7 GW of installed capacity at the end of 2023 [6][7], being able to cover the 23.5% of
the annual electricity demand [6]. Currently, Spain is positioned as the second EU country with
more installed wind power, only surpassed by Germany and the fifth in the world [8].
As seen in Figure 2.1, from the first years until 2007, the sector had a positive evolution, show­
ing a continuous increase. However, this growth decreased and the tendency changed in 2013
when the government applied the Royal Decree­Law 1/2012, of January 27, ”which suspends
the procedures for the pre­allocation of remuneration and the abolition of financial incentives
for new electricity production facilities from cogeneration, renewable energy sources and waste”
[9]. It stated that due to the current economic situation, the tariff deficit was a barrier to the devel­
opment of the sector. Furthermore, the generation capacity installed at the time was considered
enough to cover the expected demand, so it was not essential to continue with the annual im­
plementation rates of these technologies to reach the objectives for 2020. Consequently, for
four years, the sector was at a standstill.
Eventually, in 2017, initiatives were launched for the period 2017­2019 for new renewable elec­
tricity generation facilities. Through Royal Decrees 359/2017 [10] and 650/2017 [11], two auc­
tions were held to award 3 GW each, where the objective was to reduce the standard value of
the initial investment, granting economic retribution to eligible renewable electricity generation

Regarding future trends, according to Wind Europe, Spain is rated as quite likely to be able to
meet its 2030 wind energy target [7]. To achieve that, it is of high importance that repowering
starts to become a common practice for the oldest wind farms, together with offshore wind.
Moreover, as seen in Figure 2.1, the capacity installed is predicted to grow at moderate­high
rates, from 2.3 GW to approximately 10 GW per year, through the Recovery and Resilience
Facility (RRF) and National Recovery and Resilience Plans (RRPs). These funds from the
NextGenerationEU investment program are allocated to each country’s specific needs [12].
Specifically, in Spain, a Circular Repowering Program was launched at the end of 2022 to
boost investments in the repowering of old wind farms, renewal of mini hydropower plants and
facilities to recycle wind turbine blades [13].
2.3
Repowering potential
As has already been pointed out, repowering plays an important role in the Spanish energy
transition and in meeting the objectives set out in section 2.1, as almost half of the installed
wind power is about to or will reach the end­of­life stage within the next five years. Currently,
the average age of wind turbines’ fleet is 14 years old, and 7 GW of wind power is between 20
and 24 years old, shown in Figure 2.2. This positions Spain as one of the EU countries with the

oldest wind turbines on average, together with Denmark and Portugal [14].
Repowering is a good option to extend the operational lifetime of these wind farms, given that
they increase their energy production output by improving their performance, availability and/or
power while cutting down on the number of turbines. For this reason, repowering represents a
quick way to ramp up wind energy production in Spain. In addition, the wind turbines reaching
the end of their lifetimes exhibit a lack of relevant spare parts (generator, gearbox, blades...)
since they are old models that are no longer manufactured [15], which makes their maintenance
more complicated than repowering.

The amount of repowering potential is influenced by the criterion used to define which wind
farms are candidates to go under this process. When deciding on repowering options, different
parameters can be taken into account including technical, economic, environmental, political
and social [17]. In this case, the technical parameter of the age at which wind farms are consid­
ered to have reached their lifetime is the one chosen. So, following the standard IEC 61400­1
[18], wind turbines should be designed for at least 20 years of operation. If properly maintained,
their mechanical availability will be almost 100% for the first 5 to 10 years and over 92% for the
rest of their lifetime, it is after this threshold that they should be considered part of the repowering
potential.
Consequently, considering an ideal situation, the total market opportunity in 2024 is around 8.5
GW, which will only increase in the following years reaching 18 GW in 2029. This value for 2024,
however, does not take into account the latest resolution from the Circular Repowering Program,
which is expected to help repower 713 MW of capacity in the following years. Then, the market
size is reduced to 7.8 GW, representing still a significant amount of wind farms. An interesting
fact about most of the projects that have participated in the Circular Repowering Program is that
their installed power has not significantly increased. This may be due to the existing regulation
regarding grid access and connection permits, which only considers the same installation as
those that do not increase by more than 5% the installed capacity. Otherwise, the developer
must apply for a new permit, which is extremely difficult to obtain due to the congestion of the
Spanish transmission and distribution grid.
All this previous capacity that is up for repowering is also seen as one of the key players in the
energy transition because the majority of wind farms are located in areas with high levels of wind
resources. This is what can be concluded given that despite having less efficient wind turbines,
using less advanced technologies, and expecting a decline in their performance over the years,

In conclusion, the old wind farms were built at the best locations available at that moment. As it
can be seen in Figure 2.4, the wind farms constructed until 2004 are located in the areas where
the mean wind speed at 50m is the highest, excluding the protected areas. This height has
been selected to locate the best locations since the average height of a wind turbine hub in the
1990s and early 2000s fluctuates around that value [20].
Due to these reduced hub heights and the available technology, the rated power of the wind
turbines was not very high, taking an accumulated average value of 755 kW until 2004. In
Figure 2.5 the evolution of the rated power over time is shown, which puts into perspective the
size of the old wind turbines compared to the current ones and how the technology has evolved.
The average unitary power of the fleet is around 1.3 MW but the yearly averages have gotten
significantly higher since then, with the latest data from 2022 being 3.6 MW.
Thus, the large number of wind turbines needed had to be taken into account by developers who
wanted to build wind farms with a lot of capacity. Given that the average power of a wind farm
until 2004 is around 22 MW, there are numerous cases of that. In these situations, the distance
between wind turbines, the wake effect, and the limited available space play an important role.

Literature review of social acceptance in
repowering projects
A substantial amount of literature exists on the social acceptance of energy projects. Many of
these studies have established that social acceptance is a key factor in achieving energy tar­
gets[22], particularly in the case of wind energy, where local opposition is considered a signifi­
cant barrier to gaining permission [23][24]. The evolution of this field has been well­documented,
with analysis of the past, present and future of the study of social acceptance. In this context,
[25] offers an overview, suggesting that one of the lines of work should be around empirically
studying the dynamics of people’s responses to renewable energy projects over time, at var­
ious levels (local, national, and global). There is broad consensus about the complexity of
social acceptance, which is classified as multidimensional, culturally conditioned, and socially
constructed [26]. Consequently, further research will need to adopt a multifaceted approach, ex­
amining the influence of the actions of various stakeholders at different levels on the responses
of individuals to renewable energy technologies at the local level [25].
Much research is also available exploring the factors that influence the social acceptance of new
wind farms, which has been a topic of discussion since the 1980s. These factors include vi­
sual impact [27][28], place attachment [29], developer­community relationships [30], community
trust in the developer [31], trust in the planning process (particularly the ability for communities
to participate and have a meaningful impact) [32][22], and community benefits [33] [34]. These
insights are valuable, yet they overlook the temporal aspect of how these factors evolve over
time considering the lived experiences of the local community from the implementation phase
to the wind farm’s end­of­life [35]. It is often assumed that the social acceptance of wind energy
projects tends to increase after projects are constructed and operational [36], having a U­shape
[37], going from very positive when people don’t have to face a local proposal, to being more
critical during the siting phase, and returning to more positive again after the wind farm is in
operation. Some studies explore wider timeframes that support the increase of favourable atti­
tudes towards the project over time [38] or report an increase in the number of residents who find
the wind turbines more attractive than before but with no reliable changes in opinions regarding
acceptance [23].
It has been hypothesized by Frantál ([34]) that the local community will be more accepting of
repowering due to the technical and aesthetic advantages of the new wind turbines. However,
others argue that it has not been the case in locations where there is no trust between develop­
ers and the local community. Considering the substantial challenge that repowering will present
in the coming years, there is a notable disparity in the comprehension of its social acceptance,
largely due to the absence of social studies that consider a limited temporal frame for the wind
farm operation and the subsequent repowering or life extension [35]. It is important to acknowl­
edge and address this gap because local acceptance is often used by developers or authorities
as a justification for carrying out projects on existing sites without empirical evidence.
There is research on this topic regarding procedural fairness and public participation in the
decision­making, to understand how the maintenance of trust and fairness after wind turbines
are operational is a key issue for new and repowering developments. In [34], the analysis identi­
fied two central factors influencing support, diversified economic profit for local communities and
landscape impacts. It suggests that meeting expectations of economic compensation in exist­
ing projects will have a significant impact on the acceptance of future projects, rather than other
Socio­technical study of the repowering potential of old wind farms in Spain
8
==PAGE:16==
factors like knowledge or risk perception. In [39], procedural fairness is also considered impor­
tant for end­of­life decision­making, together with public participation, adequate information to
the local community, respect, and justice. Another important aspect to consider, especially for
repowering, is the role of land ownership since not all of the land owners from the old wind farm
will remain as such after the implementation [40].
Finally, the study in [35], identifies relevant factors that influence social acceptance over time
and are related to end­of­life applications (life­extension or repowering), by comparing two cases
of repowering and life­extension implementation in the UK. The results suggest that the com­
munity benefits from the existing wind farm appear to influence positively the perception the
end­of­life applications, however, these benefits need to be recognisable by the community.
Additionally, it is found that developer transparency and trustful developer­community relation­
ships are also important. The study implies that this long­term relationship is a path to create
long­term support for the wind farm and its repowering. Visual impact is also one of the factors
that this paper considers, stating that it has been one of the most important aspects influencing
the perception of wind farms, for both new and repowering projects. The findings highlight how
the increase in height of the wind turbines can be a problem and how, because of that, a smaller
number of wind turbines might not be always seen as positive. The last noteworthy factor is
the involvement of the local community in the planning process, by having clear and open com­
munication channels, allowing public participation and engaging with communities. From the
surveys conducted, it is extracted that greater consideration of these matters is necessary to
increase the support.
Regarding the Spanish research on social acceptance, there are several studies that look at the
most common factors influencing it. In [41], the short and long term effects of renewable energy
generation on the local communities are studied, comparing the hopes associated with the
areas with newly planned wind farms and the reality for those areas with previous experience
of wind energy development. The results show that people recognise the contribution of the
wind farm to the economic development of the region, but also consider the visual impact to be
the worst long­term environmental impact. In addition, [42] mentions how the local acceptance
of renewable energy is strongly influenced by its location and size. The results suggest that
people prefer medium­sized plants in locations with low natural value.
In conclusion, future research could focus on specific areas to provide valuable contributions
to the existing literature and inform the development of more effective policies and strategies to
improve social acceptance in repowering projects. Given the complexity and evolving nature of
social acceptance, an empirical study of its dynamics over time would help to understand how
social acceptance evolves from the planning stages (or even before) through the operational life
and the potential end­of­life applications. The research should adopt a multi­layered perspective
to understand how the different stakeholders at different levels impact local acceptance. In
addition, by addressing the temporal and contextual factors, a better understanding of how
lived experiences and changing community dynamics influence social acceptance would be
achieved. As previously stated, there is a need to enhance community involvement, so the
exploration of effective strategies for engagement and transparent communication to foster long­
term support would provide insightful knowledge. Finally, a promising approach to achieve
context­sensitive insights and evidence to support the justification of projects is to conduct social
studies in specific locations.

Specifics of repowering projects
4.1
Repowering definition
To know what repowering entails, is important to know first what is the current definition of it in
Spain. According to the Official State Gazette (BOE), repowering is defined as [43]:
”Technological renewal of existing wind farms consisting of the complete replace­
ment of the wind turbines with new, previously unused ones, without prejudice to
the fact that such replacement may partially affect the number of existing wind tur­
bines as opposed to the total number, regardless of whether or not the power of the
previous installation is increased. These actions involve the dismantling of at least
the tower, rotor and nacelle of the existing wind turbines affected by the action and
the construction and commissioning of new wind turbines on the previously occupied
site, even if the position coordinates of the machines are modified and potentially
additional installations are included.”
However, implementing a repowering project as the definition suggests, implies a large num­
ber of processes and new permits because the ones granted to the previous wind farm are
not valid. This is due to the varying parameters that the authorities use to evaluate what is
considered repowering and what is not during the permitting process, explained in more detail
in section 4.3. Additionally, the government itself uses the terms partial and total repowering.
Partial repowering is defined as the replacement of certain key components of the wind turbine
and the keeping of the foundations and most of the tower [44], so it does not fit the definition
above from the BOE. This causes confusion between the terms of repowering and life extension
actions.
In the case of life extension, the aim is to extend the life of the wind farm from 5 to 10 years
by replacing some parts or introducing technological improvements that allow the wind farm to
achieve similar production to when it first started operating. The required investment is signifi­
cantly lower but it is estimated that if the cost of the modifications per wind turbine exceeds by
15% the value of a new one, it is no longer feasible [45]. For the repowering, partial or total,
since there is a replacement and modernization of the oldest sites with better wind resources, it
allows for considerably higher energy production with fewer wind turbines. This requires larger
investments but they are considered feasible until they represent around 80­85% of a new wind
turbine’s value [45].
Therefore, the main differences between life extension and repowering are: the increase in effi­
ciency and electricity production by repowering since the power of the wind turbines increases,
the lower investment costs for the life extension actions, the high regulatory sensitivity of the re­
powering due to longer amortization periods than life extension, the technical viability of repow­
ering to be implemented in wind farms with obsolete technologies, and finally the administrative
requirements that repowering entails while life extension actions are faster to implement.
Regarding the EU definition of repowering [46], it can be considered a simpler and more open
definition, which reflects the range of possibilities that repowering offers and the need for it to
have an efficient permitting process, like the European directives suggest and is explained in
more detail in section 4.3:
“Renovation of power plants producing renewable energy, including the total or par­
tial replacement of installations or operating systems and equipment, with the ob­
jective of replacing capacity or improving the efficiency or capacity of the plant.”

4.3
Permitting process
Currently, the permitting process in Spain for repowering projects is the same as for new ones.
Therefore, the only differing part is the consideration of the decommissioning inside the overall
planning. The decommissioning does not require an additional environmental impact assess­
ment, so only local permits are needed to carry out the civil works. In the case of the commis­
sioning, several main permits need to be obtained, which are:
• Grid access and connection permit (IVA, acronym from the Spanish term): defined as
the right of use and right of connection to a point on the electricity grid under specified
conditions.
• Favourable DIA (acronym from the Spanish term): this milestone represents the authori­
ties’ positive evaluation of the project’s environmental assessment.
• Prior Administrative Authorisation (AAP, acronym from the Spanish term): grants the right
to carry out a specific type of installation, subject to certain conditions.
• Administrative Authorisation for Construction (AAC): allows the installation to be con­
structed in accordance with the technical requirements.
• Administrative Authorisation for Exploitation (AAE): once the project has been completed,
this permit allows the installed equipment to be commissioned.
For repowering projects, there is an option to retain the grid access and connection permit if
several requirements are met to ensure that the installation remains ”the same”.

4.5
Electricity prices
Electricity prices are a key factor in the feasibility of renewable energy projects, due to their
impact on revenue generation. It is important to understand the price dynamics and be able
to forecast as accurately as possible the future fluctuations of this value, which has proven
difficult given the changes and uncertainties of the market during the last years. Electricity
prices are so volatile because they are tied to many uncertain parameters such as regulatory
changes, weather conditions, the price of conventional fuels and technological advances, which
can complicate the feasibility assessment.
In the case of repowering, it is important to mention that the original projects were planned
considering the existing subsidies for wind energy at that moment, as mentioned in chapter 2.
These would not apply to the new wind farms, so there is a possibility that the economic feasi­
bility of the projects is not as good as expected.
To mitigate the uncertainty and variability of electricity prices, Power Purchase Agreements
(PPAs) are an effective option. These long­term contracts between electricity producers and
consumers, typically utilities or large companies, set the terms for selling and purchasing elec­
tricity. PPAs provide price stability, ensuring long­term project success and attracting banks
and investors due to their predictable cash flows. However, when market electricity prices are
low, PPAs become less attractive to consumers, who can obtain cheaper electricity from the
market, making a high fixed price offer difficult to justify for producers.
Currently, across Europe, the reduced gas prices, the decrease in demand, and an increased
renewable energy penetration are reducing wholesale electricity prices. Futures contracts show
that they will stay stable for the next few years. Far from the volatile and high prices in 2023
and especially 2022, the prices in 2024 are predicted to stabilize around 40 and 70 €/MWh
around Europe [58]. However, Spain is experiencing a particular situation where the average
price is leaning towards lower values such as 35 €/MWh over the past 5 months. This is mainly
due to the high share of renewables in the national energy mix, benefiting from solar, wind
and hydropower. These three sources account for nearly three­quarters of Spain’s electricity
generation, while the decrease in demand has also diminished the relevance of other fossil fuel
options, such as combined cycle technology. In the case study’s sensitivity analysis, variability
and uncertainty of the electricity prices are considered but a forecast provided by European
Energy is used for the main calculations.

Methodology
This chapter describes the methodologies employed in each section in detail. The first sec­
tion deals with social analysis and its associated procedures and structures. It is divided into
two sections, which present the objectives, research questions, and profiles selected for each
study in greater detail. Subsequently, a description of the wind energy production analysis is
provided, which offers an overview of the process involved in obtaining the final results and the
most crucial calculations. The economic analysis is then presented, in which the key economic
parameters that the results provide are described to assess the profitability of the different sce­
narios and the steps necessary. Finally, the financial study is delineated, accompanied by an
explanation of the key parameters employed. In both financial and economic studies, a sensi­
tivity analysis is conducted by varying critical inputs.
5.1
Social analysis
In the social analysis, both the general status of the repowering and the site­specific situation
are targeted. To achieve this, a common procedure for both studies is established, taking into
account the differing needs of each. In the case of the general status of repowering, a snapshot
is the chosen social study typology, since it is most used to analyze the state and the process at
the time of the investigation by collecting expert knowledge in the field through interviews and
comparing them. Regarding the study at the wind farm location, a case study is the selected
method, as it is intended to provide a detailed description of a specific situation or problem that
is significant for the thesis research question [59].
Despite differing social studies typologies, a common approach to interviewing is employed, the
semi­structured focused interview. The central focus of these interviews is the pre­determined
subject or topic of conversation, with the aim of collecting reactions and interpretations in an
interview with a relatively open format [59]. The interview guidelines and specific research
questions used during each study are further detailed on subsection 5.1.1 and subsection 5.1.2,
along with the profiles selected.
As part of the methodology, once the interviewee had consented to participate in the study,
the interviews were scheduled to last for one hour, at a convenient time for the interviewee.
However, the final length of the interview was dependent on the dynamics of the conversation
with each participant. In advance of the scheduled interview, all interviewees were provided with

Governance of repowering
”The challenge of repowering is on the regulatory side, in other words, there is no
specific regulatory framework for repowering. Therefore, it is like starting a new
project. [...] With the speed at which things go in Spain, with less than 4 years you
don’t have time to arrange all the permits for repowering, which does not comply

— Director of Energy Policy and Climate Change at AEE
Throughout all the interviews, there was consensus between all the participants regarding the
current state of the regulatory framework of the repowering projects. All of them appointed the
lack of specific regulations and procedures for repowering its main challenge. This is an aspect
that affects, not only the number of permits needed but also the time that it requires to obtain

them if the process goes smoothly. Currently, the Spanish regulation does not comply with
the European directives that intend to shorten the timings of permitting obtention to 1 year or,
in some cases, 6 months. When talking about the definition of repowering, there were some

Some of the interviewees stated that the companies in the Spanish wind energy sector have a
lack of trust towards the robustness of the legal system when it comes to investing in repowering.
This is caused by the fear of starting a repowering project and not being able to execute it
due to the current difficulties in obtaining permits for repowering. Currently, there are only 17
repowering cases in Spain, which is quite low compared to the amount of ageing wind turbines.
These 17 projects correspond to the oldest wind farms of long­established companies such as
Iberdrola or EDPR, and no company has more than two repowering cases.
A specific problem with the EIA requirements was raised by almost all participants. Only one was
in favour of repowered wind farms having to comply with the same environmental requirements
as new projects. This differs from the predominant view that the EIA should be carried out to
different standards and cover different impacts than the EIA required for new projects. This is
due to the time, resources and complications that this could mean for the repowering, as the
projects are already in place and some degree of impact has already been caused. Therefore,
its repowering could be seen as a measure to avoid having to implement projects in other areas

existing wind farm.
From a technical point of view, there are also limits to its implementation, for example, if the
same grid access and connection permit is to be maintained, the total power increase cannot
be greater than 5% and there can be no modification of the conditions of the evacuation lines
or the substation, which was also appointed during the interviews as a great limiting factor.
Compliance with the grid codes is another important aspect stressed by one of the interviewees,
who states that it is very important for the grid to comply with the requirements as much as
possible, which should be relatively easy due to the technological advances since the original
project was built.
Nevertheless, the general opinion of the companies that have repowered projects is that they
are satisfied with the outcome, as the resulting increase in production, efficiency and revenue is
significant. All the interviewees agreed on the technical benefits that this type of project brings.
So the technical challenge does not lie in these, but rather in the grid’s ability to integrate them.

ment as the central supporter of developers who decide to repower their assets. Additionally,
they agreed that it is the government’s responsibility to create awareness about potential future
problems if repowering is not undertaken, through educational campaigns.
Interviewees highlighted other issues related to the political framework, noting that various in­
terests influence the promotion of different energy sources. In recent years, technologies like
solar PV have been prioritized. However, now that the level of implementation of solar PV is
considered to be closer to its full potential, the focus is shifting back to wind energy, which is
crucial. In this context, the director of energy policy and climate change says that the AEE is
already in talks with the ministry to find ways to accelerate the permitting process, which has
been a significant barrier to implementing repowering projects. Simplifying this process would

6.2.4
Conclusion
Through the conducted interviews, it has been possible to get an overall understanding of the
current state of repowering in Spain, along with the main challenges it faces and the recom­
mendations from a wide range of experts. Among the main takeaways, the highlights are the
identification of the lack of a specific regulatory framework as the main challenge, along with
issues related to the permitting process and political instability around the implementation of
renewable energy.
For future projects, it is vital that political support for repowering is demonstrated by developing
specific economic benefits for these types of projects, making investment more attractive to de­
velopers. The technical benefits are clear, and if implemented well, repowering can significantly
contribute to national decarbonization targets.
Therefore, the next steps should focus on resolving the lengthy permitting procedures, adapting
and unifying the environmental requirements, and aligning with European directives as fast as
possible.

sufficiency of renewable energy generation on the island. Therefore, environmental sustainabil­
ity is at the forefront of the strategy, ensuring commitment to mitigate climate change, improve
air quality, and decarbonize the energy system. Moreover, it has a strong focus on social in­
volvement requiring it to be both a driving force and a beneficiary of the change. For the strategy
to succeed it must also be economically sustainable by creating local industry related to renew­
ables, attracting professionals in areas within the sector and stimulating the local economy.
Finally, it is important for the island that there is a landscape integration of the infrastructures
associated with the energy transition, helping to preserve the island’s agro­forest mosaic and
historical heritage.
Due to the relevance of the criteria and principles of the strategy, developers looking to imple­
ment new renewable energy projects on the island must consider and use this document as
best practice guidelines to ensure social and institutional acceptance.
Focusing on wind energy production, the strategy focuses on onshore wind and the potential
to cover up to 25% of the total demand with it. The main challenge presented is the amount
of territory that is under protection, leaving little space available for these kinds of projects.
IDAE conducted a study to determine the most suitable areas for wind project development
within the locations identified as having wind aptitude, as defined by the Energy Sector Master
Plan (PDSE) of the Balearic Islands Government, the result being the one in Figure 7.2. The
zones have been selected considering environmental, visual, and accessibility factors, limiting
the search to areas on non­developable rural land with minimal agricultural value.

The energy consumption of Menorca has a regular pattern through the weeks but has a heavy
seasonal variation in magnitude. From June to September, the consumption doubles the val­

consumption has an average value of 32 GWh/month while for summer months it is 55 GWh/­

index in the summer months and a higher wind speed index in the winter. Depending on the
final total power of the repowered wind farm, it could contribute by covering between 2 and 2.8%
of the total demand on a yearly average.

Technical study
So that an appropriate technical assessment can be made, the first part is dedicated to the
analysis of the old wind farm. This creates a clear picture of the situation and allows future
comparisons between results. The repowering will be implemented after that. For this purpose,
the annual energy production in different scenarios is calculated using real wind measurements

carried out, and a feasibility study is conducted. A sensibility analysis is performed to check the
variability and dependency of the results. Finally, the environmental impacts are considered in
the EIA and visualisations are done to show how the project will look after the implementation.
7.2.1
Old Milà wind farm
As mentioned, the Milà wind farm is located on the island’s east side, where the favourable
orography improves the wind resource available at the wind turbine locations, even at a hub

original wind farm had 4 Gamesa MADE AE­59 wind turbines of 800 kW rated power each,

Extreme wind conditions
Using WAsP Engineering, the Generalized Extreme Wind Climate (GEWC) can be obtained by
providing the Observed Extreme Wind Climate (OEWC)(calculated in WAsP Climate Analyst)
as input or by using the Spectral­Correction (SC) method. The first one is based only on the
observed data while the other one allows for a combination of local measurements and long­
term data available in the server. For this reason, the SC method is chosen. The GEWC is
obtained at all the hub heights and site locations of the scenarios. So, here is where the value
of the 50­year extreme wind speed is obtained to be used in the wind turbine selection, which
is 37.2 m/s for scenarios 1 and 2, 37 m/s for scenarios 5 and 6, and 37.3 ­ 36.3 m/s for the two
wind turbines in scenarios 3 and 4.
In section B.2, the GEWC direct results from WAsP Engineering can be consulted. The values
obtained have been contrasted with the Global Atlas for Siting Parameters, to validate that the
values are within the expected range. For this site location, the range is between 35 and 42 m/s
(Figure B.1) which matches the values seen in all the cases. Therefore, they are considered a
correct input for the WAT, together with the wind turbine model and the GWC.

Wind farm design and planning
Wind turbine technology
The wind turbine generator selected for the Milà repowering case is the Vestas V120 2.2MW,
which corresponds to scenario 4. This wind turbine is suitable for the wind farm location as
it has been designed to operate efficiently at low to medium wind speeds with relatively low
turbulence. According to the manufacturer, the maximum noise that can be produced has a
power of 110.5 dB. However, Vestas has a Serrated Trailing Edge technology available for the
model that allows the reduction of the sound power level. Furthermore, some characteristics
and additional features available to enhance the wind turbine performance while reducing its
environmental impact are:

Grid strength
The concept of grid strength refers to the ability of an electrical power grid to maintain its voltage
stability (and frequency) even when there are changes in the loads or the generation units. The
short circuit power characterizes the grid strength at a point in the network (Sk).
To evaluate the grid strength for new projects in Spain, the Short Circuit Ratio (SCR) was used,
which establishes the ratio between the short circuit power at the connection point and the nom­
inal power of the project according to Equation 7.1. The ratio for non­manageable generation
power connected to a node of the grid was set at 1/20 Sk, which was imposed to achieve the uni­
form distribution of renewable generation across the grid. However, this ratio treats the project’s
connection to the grid as independent without considering the influence of other generation units
in neighbouring and electrically close nodes, among other lacking considerations [76]. There­
fore, it has been recently replaced by the Weighted Short Circuit Ratio (WSCR) which is a zonal
criterion and incorporates other nodes in its calculation, as seen in Equation 7.2.

Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)
To be able to carry out a repowering project, the elaboration of an EIA is a compulsory require­
ment. The current permitting framework of repowering in Spain does not contemplate a specific
format of EIA for this kind of project, so the expected content is the same as for a completely new
one. Therefore, the EIA evaluates the direct and indirect main impacts of the project based on
a wide range of environmental factors (biodiversity, human health, material assets, landscape,
climate...). The project developer must submit a report to the approval authority containing de­
tails about the project, potential significant effects, reasonable alternatives, and measures to
avoid, prevent, reduce, or offset likely notable environmental impacts. Additionally, there are
strict rules on public notification about the project, its subjection to an EIA procedure, and how
those affected can participate in the decision­making process. The public is informed of the
decision afterwards and has the right to challenge it in court [79].
Environmental restrictions
It is common practice in the wind energy sector in Spain to carry out an initial screening of the
environmental sensitivity of the project site before proceeding to the next stages. This can be
done through a tool called Geo Portal, which is a public database that provides multiple layers
of geographic information.

Affected fauna
Birds
Although Menorca is a small island, it is classified as a Biosphere Reserve, which means that
it has a large number of bird species. During the interview with the Ecologists Association of
Menorca, important information was extracted, which is that the main problem with the wind
farm is that it is next to the waste treatment plant, which attracts birds that come in search of
food and increases the mortality of these endangered species. Therefore, some measures are
being taken to prevent this, such as the opening of a feeding station in the S’Albufera d’Es Grau
Natural Park, to divert the birds that come close to the wind farm in search of food. Additionally,
the blades of the new wind turbines will include red markings to increase their visibility for the
birds. The critically endangered species are: Balearic shearwater, bittern, red kite, osprey, reed
bunting, red­crested pochard and white­headed duck [80].
Bats
Bats serve as excellent bioindicators of ecosystem changes due to environmental degradation.
Therefore, they are monitored and registered every year. These species rely heavily on their
refuges, forming colonies of varying sizes in natural cavities, caves, abysses, and man­made
structures like mines or other underground sites. As mentioned before in subsubsection 7.2.2,
in Menorca, three of the six cave­dwelling species are classified as vulnerable. The map in
Figure 7.21 shows all the caves on the island that have been surveyed through the years and
show evidence of bat activity. As there are some cases close to the wind farm, the preventive
measure of acquiring the bat protection system is being taken.

Decommissioning
One of the peculiarities of repowering projects is the need to plan a decommissioning of the
previous wind farm. Therefore, there has to be a plan to manage the waste generated by it,
as Figure 7.19 suggests. The management of the waste is divided by the material since there
are some easier to treat, like the concrete or the metals and others more complicated, like the
composites of the blades. The plan for the main waste materials is the following:
• Concrete: due to its high potential for recycling and reuse, it has a high resource value.
In order to be sold it should be transported over a long distance due to the site location,
so the recycling option is best. This way it could be used locally after being crushed into
aggregate.
• Metals: the main ones are iron, steel, copper and aluminium. There is a high demand for
them in Europe so they could be sold to be reused.
• Composites: since their recycling and recovery are expensive and underdeveloped, the
most common option is to repurpose. They could be used in the municipality or the island
without the need to be shipped to the mainland. For example, for playgrounds or street
furniture, and some specific parts for building structures like bike shelters or walkways.
• Rare earths: their recycling and reuse are important to prevent the environmental effects
that their extraction implies. They can be stored as replacement parts to reuse or re­cast
as new magnets with the same composition.
With these considerations, the carbon footprint of the project can be reduced.

7.3
Social study
While the technical and feasibility studies are important for the development of repowering
projects, their success can depend on public acceptance which is often overlooked. This study
focuses on the local perception of a repowering project in an area where the wind farm has been
co­existing for a long time with the local community. The main objective is to investigate further
the wind farm’s interaction with the different stakeholders after 20 years of operation, since as
explained in chapter 2 there is still not much research on it.

Results interpretation
Energy transition and local implementation on the island
”It is quite accepted that we have to be responsible for what we consume and there is
a preference for local generation instead of importing it. On the other hand, the con­
cern is also to protect our landscape. [...] Therefore, the strategy and planning done
in Menorca are always along the lines of increasing renewables but in a way that is
as environmentally and socially correct as possible, integrating social participation
and avoiding large external capital.”
— Head of the environmental department at OBSAM
Throughout all the interviews, there was a consensus on the importance of the island being
energy self­sufficient, due to past incidents related to the existing electrical connection with
Menorca and the population’s awareness of the climate emergency. In this direction, the energy
strategy for the island is clearly defined and its contents and priorities are clear for everyone.
Moreover, some of the interviewees highlighted the participative process that the Menorca 2030
energy strategy went through, ensuring that it was a consensual document including the main
concerns and interests of the majority of the inhabitants. The willingness of local people to
be part of the energy transition is unanimous, ranking third in their priorities after housing and
specific issues, according to a survey by the Confederation of Neighbours’ Association.
About the specifics of the energy strategy, all the interviewees stated that solar energy has
played a more central role than wind energy, mainly because it is easier to implement on the
island uniformly without causing as much commotion as wind energy proposals. However, some
of the interviewees stressed that the framework for its implementation has been strongly defined
by the different organisations on the island, in order to prevent developers from exploiting the
available terrain without taking into account the priorities of the inhabitants.
In the case of wind energy, there were differing opinions on the need of additional definition
for its implementation. Some argue that it is enough to limit the accepted areas for it and the
preferred characteristics while others think that when a proposal is presented in one of these
areas, the implementation model should be revised. This debate comes from the need to pro­
tect the biosphere of the island at the same time that the limited space is used for renewable
implementation, so it can be concluded that there has to be an equilibrium between these two
aspects for the energy strategy to be accepted.
Another issue around implementation is local participation in terms of investment. Only one of
the interviewees drew attention to the lack of regulation in this area, stating that it is not well­
defined how it can be done and at what percentage. It is clear, as all interviewees mention, that
there is a willingness to move towards local implementation, either with local investors or with
projects that offer local participation, so this should be one of the lines of action for improvement.
Therefore, the rejection of foreign developers that try to impose their interests over the island’s
is mentioned.

7.3.3
Conclusions
Through the interviews of this case study, it has been proven the importance of local context
when studying social acceptance of energy projects. While the specific project has a long rela­
tionship with the local community and has been received positively, the implementation of wind
energy in other areas of the island might not be seen as favourably.
The takeaways include the low effect on the local community in the social dynamics, along
with the environmental implications of the repowering, which would imply an increased visual
impact but would allow reusing the degraded space next to the landfill, the willingness of the
local community to participate actively in the energy projects, and the importance of having a
well­defined strategy of implementation for the different technologies.
There seems to be a clear difference between the areas of the island that have been exposed
to wind energy and those that have not. However, the specific reasons for this are still partly
unknown, as no research had been carried out on the island before and the opinions of the
interviewees were based on specific memories. The effect of the news on the perception of the
wind farm’s operation is also noticeable, it created an awareness around the technical issues
that might not have been that important otherwise. However, this helped make people see that
this action was necessary to keep it in operation.
The fact that the wind farm was developed on mainly public land and was managed by the
administration had a positive effect, it demonstrated the willingness of the public authorities to
lead the energy transition. This project also helped to open up the debate on green energy
generation on the island, which so far is the only example of wind energy implementation in the
Balearic Islands.

Discussion and future work
The main objective of this thesis is explore the main challenges that repowering initiatives face,
to understand what factors have led to this situation, and what can be improved to move forward
while also investigating the importance of the local context during its implementation. Repow­
ering plays a key role in the Spanish government’s energy targets, so optimising it from various
perspectives is important. However, some assumptions, uncertainties and challenges need to
be acknowledged in this section.
A fundamental assumption in this study is that the data collected from interviewees represents
different perspectives of the generalised status of repowering and the specific case study, i.e.
the participants have responded to the questions in good faith according to their expertise. It
was also assumed that technical, regulatory and economic factors would remain stable over
the study period, allowing for a less complex study in terms of financial feasibility and permitting
timelines. Additional technical assumptions have also been made, including the selection of
which parts of the existing infrastructure are in acceptable enough condition to be kept, like the
substation and its components.
Therefore, this thesis is subject to several uncertainties. Firstly, the evolving regulatory frame­
work and the accuracy of current electricity forecasts could affect the feasibility and attractive­
ness of repowering projects. Secondly, even though it is not considered an uncertainty as such,
the bias of the interviewees when talking about their own opinions and perspectives is also ex­
istent and acknowledged. In fact, this bias and differing opinions helped the studies gather
valuable insights into the problems, concerns and particular interests that the stakeholders en­
counter or have. Thirdly, due to the specific characteristics of the repowering case studied in
Menorca, there are many uncertainties regarding the possibility of extrapolating conclusions to
other cases in the Iberial peninsula or considering it a representative example of repowering in
Spain. Finally, there are other minor uncertainties about the data collection of wind measure­
ments due to the lack of official documents explaining the methodology used to obtain them.
In terms of challenges, on the technical side, the most difficult task has been to find real wind
measurements of the wind farms that are being considered for repowering. This data is consid­
ered highly confidential in Spain and companies tend to keep it to themselves in case they want
to use it in their projects. It was the main input needed for the modelling, so it is remarkable
that it was possible to obtain this data from a particular wind farm. On the social side, the main
challenge was getting relevant, available and willing participants to take part in the process, as
the social research strongly depended on their participation.
In addition, there are other factors to consider before moving on to future research and conclu­
sions. In the case of the social studies, all the interviews were conducted online, which makes
non­verbal communication more difficult and can make it more challenging to create a comfort­
able atmosphere for the interviewees. However, the fact that the interviews were all conducted
in the language that each participant felt more comfortable with helped a lot to get more sincere
and honest opinions, as Catalan and Spanish were the languages used. In addition, the num­
ber of interviews conducted is not considered to be a determining factor, as it was decided as
part of the methodology to stop conducting interviews if the newer ones did not bring any more
insight to the research. Therefore, collecting more information in the way that was done would
not lead to different results. It would be part of future work to use other methods to complement
the current findings.
The recommended future lines of work extracted from this thesis are:

Conclusions
This thesis has been dedicated to investigating the main challenges faced by repowering in
Spain and implementing a complete study of a repowering case in Menorca from the social,
technical and economic perspectives. This research has provided important insights into the ap­
plicable regulatory framework and the importance of social dynamics, stakeholder engagement
and the role of political stability in repowering investments. The key results and conclusions are
summarized below:
In the first place, the economic and technical results of the case study demonstrate the feasibility
of the repowering implementation. The difference in energy production and efficiency that the
wind farm would experience compared to the previous one is significant. It shows repowering as
an option to solve the technical problems that the wind farm had during the previous operation
while helping Menorca reach its own energy targets. All of these, without increasing the impact
on land occupation and reusing some of the existing infrastructure. It is important to note that
Menorca has very specific guidelines for the implementation of wind energy and the fact that
the project complies with them is crucial for its success.
Regarding the local acceptance of the Milà wind farm, the results highlight the importance of
the local context and the need to investigate specific cases of implementation. In this particular
case, it is clear that the local community supports the repowering of the obsolete wind farm but
may not see favourably the implementation of wind energy in other regions of the island. The
western region, which has not been exposed to wind energy, is more sceptical about its devel­
opment and the effects that it would have on their social dynamics, while it can be confirmed
that the local community near the site location has accepted the wind farm as part of the skyline.
This could be attributed to the particular siting of the wind farm next to the landfill and the small
number of wind turbines, as the results showed that people were not affected in any way by it.
The location of the wind farm is highly visible, but the presence of the thermal power plant in the
harbour reduces the visual impact of it. Moreover, the reuse of degraded land is considered to
be one of the most important factors in favour of this initiative. The technical problems operating
the wind farm have also influenced public opinion, as the repowering is seen as a necessity for
the project rather than something imposed by an external investor. Due to the importance of the
local context, it is difficult to affirm that the results can be extrapolated to other cases or serve
as evidence of the general acceptance of repowering in Spain.
The results of the snapshot of repowering can be considered representative of the current sit­
uation. The profiles participating in the study came from a diverse range of backgrounds and
interests, from both the public and private sectors, and yet they all agreed that the main chal­
lenge faced by repowering is the current lack of a regulatory framework and the problems asso­
ciated with the permitting process, including timings and requirements for repowering projects
specifically. This is particularly problematic if the repowering projects cannot keep the previ­
ous grid access and connection and are located in areas where the grid is highly congested
and has no available capacity. In these cases, when the old wind farm releases its capacity,
it is automatically allocated to the first project waiting in the queue, rather than to the repower­
ing project. This not only creates an issue with the timing of permits but also jeopardizes the
entire development of the project. Another factor that has not been considered, and to which
this study has drawn attention, is the effect of instability in the political environment, leading
to abrupt changes in support mechanisms and policy implementation through the years. This
slows down any progress that may have been made in the past and prevents the planning of
long­term strategies like the one repowering requires.

Finally, addressing the research questions formulated at the beginning of the study, the critical
elements that have been identified for the repowering initiatives and should be improved in order
to develop future sustainable energy strategies are:
• Lack of a consistent and uniform definition of what repowering entails throughout the public
administration system.
• Lengthy authorisation timings that reduce the attractiveness of the investment for the asset
owners.
• Lack of specific procedures for repowering that consider the differing characteristics and
impacts that these projects have compared to new ones.
• Lack of preference in granting grid capacities in case of repowering in congested grid
areas.
• Lack of specific support mechanisms or tenders.
• Lack of consideration of the complex social context at the site location since the accep­
tance of repowering depends, to some extent, on the long­term relationship between the
developers and the local communities.
In order to improve these previously mentioned areas, some recommendations are made. The
first one addresses the problem with the grid capacity allocation when a repowering project is
not considered ”the same” according to the existing criteria and cannot keep the grid access
and connection. This issue could be solved by establishing preferential processing for grid
access to repowering projects, excluding them from the ordinary process ruled by the ”first come,
first served” principle. To tackle the lack of attractiveness of repowering projects for investors,
it would be advantageous to include repowering in the current category of technologies that
receive additional points in the grid capacity tenders. This category currently includes self­
consumption, energy storage and hybrid projects.
To address the lack of a specific framework for repowering, it would be necessary to create
a guide or timeline that includes all the necessary administrative procedures. It should start
by describing the decommissioning process and link to the commissioning process of the new
project at all levels, i.e. local, regional and national. Currently, there is no defined process and
this leads to confusion when developers need to refer to specific regulatory terms to implement
repowering.
Technical requirements of energy production, efficiency, maintenance and compliance with the
grid codes are factors that do not represent a challenge for the repowered wind farms due to
the technological advances. Therefore, the challenge in future sustainable energy strategies of
repowering is considered to be elsewhere, as mentioned before.

Technical Description
GE’s 2 MW Platform is a three-blade, upwind, horizontal axis wind turbine with a rotor diameter of either 
116 or 127-meters. The turbine rotor and nacelle are mounted on top of a tubular steel tower. The 2 
MW-127 is offered at an 89-meter hub height, and the 2 MW-116 is offered at 80-meter, 90-meter, and 
94-meter hub heights. To keep the blades pointed into the wind, the 2 MW-116 uses a passive yaw control 
system, and the 2 MW-127 uses an active yaw control system. GE’s 2 MW Platform operates at a variable 
speed and uses a doubly fed asynchronous generator with a partial power converter system.
