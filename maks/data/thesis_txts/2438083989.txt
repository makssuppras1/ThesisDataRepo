Abstract
As the installed capacities of wind turbines increases every year, there is a need for faster
and cheaper methods of Operations and Management. Furthermore, in case of oﬀshore wind
farms the operations and maintenance are a major concern as oﬀshore wind farms have poor
accessibility. Hence, the need for reliable and cheap wind farm monitoring methods is critical.
Conventional methods for monitoring performance of wind farms have usually relied upon
power curve analytics of SCADA data. The major challenge is, such models have relied upon
the nacelle anemometers for quantifying wind speeds. The quality of data obtained from nacelle
anemometers can have large uncertainties. Furthermore, such methods need the anemometers
to be in wake free sectors which is a very unlikely situation for wind turbines in the middle of
large oﬀshore wind farms. Hence, wake models become very important in estimation of wind
speeds for wind turbines under wake.
The thesis proposes a new method for evaluating performance of wind turbines in large
oﬀshore wind farms based on high frequency SCADA data and fast wake models. It has been
identiﬁed that fast wake models have increased uncertainties when quantifying wake losses
accumulated from multiple upstream wind turbines, hence the thesis proposes a new wake
summation method that is based on SCADA data and Larsen wake model. The signiﬁcant
reduction in uncertainties from applying the new wake model is shown. The improved method
for calculation of wake losses is used to identify possible cases of underperformance.

Layout of Horns Rev-I wind farm and the nomenclature used in the thesis: the wind turbines are placed
in a grid like structure with 8 rows and 10 columns, the North-South grid direction is referred to as rows

from north to south directions, the columns are named as C1, C2,... C10 from east-west directions. The
co-ordinate system for wind direction is such that the wind coming from north to south is at 0o, west to
east is at 90o, south to north is at 180o and east to west is at 270o, the wind turbines along the rows are
under full wake when the wind direction is 90o and 270o, the wind turbines in the columns are under full
wake when direction is 6.05o and 186.05o.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As of February 2018, the total installed capacity of wind turbines in the world has reached
to 540 GW, of which 52.6 GW was installed in 2017 alone [2]. As the installed capacities of
wind turbines increases faster and cheaper methods for operations and management of wind
farms becomes more and more important hence newer operations strategies for reducing the
operation costs are being explored. Furthermore, in the context of Oﬀshore wind farms the
operations and maintenance are a major concern, as oﬀshore wind farms have poor accessibility.
In case of failures of wind turbines the operations and maintenance cost is expected to reach
upto 30% of life time costs of wind turbine. Hence, it is extremely critical that reliable methods
for monitoring the operations of wind are developed [3].
The key aspects of system reliability of a wind turbine are the operational availability
and the achievement of the predicted power output [3]. The traditional SCADA (Supervisory
Control and Data Acquisition) based research for validating the performance of wind turbines
is largely based on using SCADA data and the reference curves to validate the performance of a
wind turbine [4]. The major challenge with such models is that the quantiﬁed wind speeds are
based on nacelle anemometer. The quality of data obtained from nacelle anemometer depends
upon the quality of the anemometer in use and the transfer function that is being used to
account for ﬂow distortion due to the wind turbine [3].
Besides the uncertainty from the anemometer and the transfer function the wind speed
quantiﬁcation methods also require the wind turbines to be in a wake free sector [5]. For wind
turbines in the middle of a wind farm it is very unlikely that the wind turbines are in a wake
free sector hence wake calculations become very important for estimation of wind speeds of
downstream wind turbines in a wind farm. In cases when wind turbines are under wake one
could resort to use of fast wake calculation models for estimating the wind speeds at the wind
turbines, see [5].
This thesis focuses on modelling fast, computationally cheap wind farm monitoring tech-

2.1.2
Wake Modelling
This section gives a general introduction to wake characteristics, followed by an introduction
to the wake models used in this study.
The purpose of wake models is to characterize the wind ﬂow behind a wind turbine. A wake
behind a wind turbine typically reduces the momentum of the inﬂow wind causing reduction
in power and increases the turbulence in wind leading to unsteady loads on the downstream
wind turbines [6].
The wake behind a wind turbine can be categorized into two regions, the far wake and the
near wake regions. The near wake region starts right behind a wind turbine and extends up
to 2-4 rotor diameters. The wind ﬂow in near wake region is highly inﬂuenced by the rotor
geometry i.e. vertices created by the blade tip [6].
When moving further downstream of a wind turbine the eﬀect of rotor geometry becomes
minimal, the rotor inﬂuences in the far wake region are reduced wind speeds and increased
turbulence.
Furthermore, the far wake region is inﬂuenced by the atmospheric turbulence.
The interaction between the atmospheric turbulence and the rotor inﬂuenced turbulence leads
to turbulence mixing, turbulence mixing accelerates the wake recovery. The nature of wake
recovery is such that the wind deﬁcit would approach a Gaussian proﬁle when moving further
downstream [6].
This study uses Larsen wake model calibrated using non-linear least square estimation (see
[7]) to calculate the wake created by each wind turbine. The Larsen wake model is chosen be-
cause it is a simple and fast method for calculating the wake created by wind turbines, designing
a solution that would use Larsen wake model would mean that the wind farm operators can
actively monitor the wind turbines for identifying anomalies.
The tuned parameters of the Larsen model for the wind farms under investigation is obtained
from, [7]. Where the parameters are tuned using 1-Hz eﬀective wind speeds derived from the

PossPOW
PossPOW (Possible Power) algorithm is a frame work that puts together the eﬀective wind
speeds and the wake model to estimate the wind speeds and possible power for each wind
turbine in the entire wind farm.
Illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1 is the work ﬂow of PossPOW algorithm. The SCADA is used to

normalized for standard temperature and pressure conditions. The data thus gathered is used

The eﬀective wind speeds are used as inputs of re-calibrated Larsen model as discussed in
section 2.1.2 to estimate the wind speeds at downstream wind turbines. The estimated wind
speeds thus calculated are used to calculate the Possible Power produced by the downstream
wind turbines.

2.2
Literature
2.2.1
Wake Impacts on Yawing
The losses to wind velocity due to wake from upstream wind turbines has been discussed in
numerous papers, this phenomenon is discussed in section 2.1.2. Besides inﬂuence of wake on
wind velocity and turbulence intensity, an interesting phenomenon of the wake inﬂuencing the
yaw of wind turbines is observed in the SCADA data, see [9].
Th study in [9], studies the wake losses and yawing of a single array of 6 onshore wind
turbines that are placed at distance of 5 times the rotor diameter. When observing the SCADA
of the wind turbines the study has found that when the upstream wind turbine is experiencing
a wind direction that must result in the downstream wind turbines to be under complete wake,
the nacelle direction of downstream wind turbine is observed to be misaligned. In case of the
six wind turbine array, it has been observed that the misalignment could reach up to 10. The
study states that this eﬀect is likely due to wind turbines experiencing higher wind speeds from
lateral inﬂux of winds when under wake. The study goes on to state that in such cases the wake
losses at downstream wind turbines could be overestimated when using estimation models that
do not take such an eﬀect into consideration.

2.2.2
High-frequency SCADA for Wind Turbine Performance
Monitoring
Some insight into wind turbine monitoring using high frequency SCADA is obtained from [4].
The study uses high-frequency SCADA to identify underperformance in wind turbines, the
ﬁndings are summarized in the current sub-section.
The traditional performance of wind farms using SCADA is monitored using the power
curves.
The widely used approach is to model reference power curves from wind turbines
operating under normal conditions along with the conﬁdence limits. The reference power curves
thus obtained along with the conﬁdence limits are used to identify cases of abnormal operation
conditions in the wind turbines. Although the conﬁdence limits are a key element to identify
the abnormal operation conditions there has been little emphasis in earlier cases of research to
identify the conﬁdence limits [4].
Furthermore, the state-of-art methods for detecting the underperformance rely on the nor-
mal distributions of the modelling error to evaluate wind turbine performances. Contrary to
the assumption it has been observed that the residuals of model in general are heteroscedastic
in nature, i.e.
the error residuals are characterized by diﬀerent variance for diﬀerent wind
speeds [10], the author of the current thesis has also made similar observations in the data.
The heteroscedastic nature of the data could become a draw back to the normal distribution
assumption leading to poor fault detection capabilities. Hence, the study advocates use of
quantile regression approach to detect faults. Quantile methods have a clear advantage as they
are based on probabilities in the distribution rather than assuming a normal distribution.
The key ﬁndings from the study in [4] are that: The quantile methods yielded results
that indicated errors which were cross validated using the alarm data of the wind turbine. The
cases of faults stand out more in case of the quantile methods in comparison to the non-quantile
methods. The detected cases of faults when compared to the alarm data indicated failures such
as gearbox failure.

4.2
Data Moving Mean
The SCADA data used for analysis in the thesis is collected at 1 [Hz] frequency. As the data
is recorded at a high frequency a certain level of noise is expected in the data. The aim of this
section is to ﬁnd the appropriate period for which a moving mean can be applied to the data
in order to eliminate the noise in the data.
The approach used is to compare the estimated wake losses by the Larsen model with the
observed wake losses occurring at a downstream wind turbine for diﬀerent time intervals. The
assumption here is that for shorter time intervals the wake loss error returned by the Larsen
model contains model related errors and noise related errors, by gradually increasing the time
period of the moving mean the author expects to arrive at a time period at which the noise in
the data is signiﬁcantly reduced with a minimum compromise in the resolution of the data.
Illustrated in ﬁgure 4.2 is an example of aforementioned comparison of wake losses using
HRData-1 where, the absolute mean of the ratio of the estimated wake losses to the observed
wake losses created by wind turbines 5 and 6 on their immediate wind turbines is calculated for
diﬀerent time periods. The examples illustrated in ﬁgure 4.2 are from the upstream most wind
turbines, the upstream most wind turbines are selected in order to eliminate the uncertainties
arising from the wake superposition that would arise in the wind turbines further downstream.
It can be observed from the ﬁgure 4.2 that as the averaging time period increases the error
returned by the model decreases and the error seems to stabilize at around 60 seconds, it is
assumed that improvement in the results is due to the reduced noise in data from applying
a moving mean. Based on such plots for the wind turbines in the upstream it is subjectively
decided by the researcher to use 60s [1 minute] as the appropriate averaging time period to
smoothen the data under investigation.
Conclusion: It is hypothesized that for high frequency data there could be noise related
errors along with the model related errors. Hence by gradually increasing the averaging time
period of moving mean the reduction of noise in data is observed. High magnitude of noise
related errors are observed for averaging time periods of less than 50 seconds after which the
errors do not seem to signiﬁcantly change. After observing the noise vs moving mean periods at
diﬀerent time periods, it appears that a choice of 60 seconds of moving mean period is optimal.
Such a moving mean period would signiﬁcantly reduce the noise in the data with minimum
compromise in the resolution of the data.

Quasi-Dynamic Wake Summation
When examining diﬀerent wake superposition methods in section 4.3, it is inferred that al-
though the Larsen model showed good results for single wake conditions, there is an increased
uncertainty in wake losses calculated when using diﬀerent wake superposition methods. Fur-
thermore, if a wind turbine in the upstream is behaving diﬀerent to the downstream wind
turbines the wake superposition methods could carry the related errors to the wake estima-
tion of the downstream wind turbines. Hence the Quasi-Dynamic Wake Summation (QDWS)
is developed to take advantage of the data available and Larsen model. The QDWS method
estimates wake losses empirically for a downstream wind turbine based on the wake losses
observed at the immediate upstream wind turbine (du1) and the wake losses created by the
immediate upstream ind turbine (du2).
The hypothesis for developing the QDWS is that the total wake losses experienced by each
wind turbine are a result of the superimposed wake from each of the upstream wake creating
wind turbine. Based on the said hypothesis, the wake losses at a wind turbine is calculated
by ﬁtting the total wake losses experienced by the immediate upstream wind turbine and the
wake created by the immediate upstream wind turbine calculated using the Larsen model.
The intention here is to assume the wake losses experienced by the immediate upstream wind
turbine to be a result of superimposed wake from rest of the wind turbines further upstream
and superimpose the Larsen wake losses of the immediate upstream wind turbine empirically
to the existing wakes. The empirical coeﬃcients thus developed could account for eﬀects such
as wake degradation, wake superposition and possible biases.

turbine wt21 the total wake losses at wind turbine wt21 would be represented as duwt5−21
(meaning total wake losses between the upstream most wind turbine wt5 and the current wind

the immediate upstream wake creating wind turbine wt13 i.e. duwt5−13 is calculated as the
diﬀerence between the eﬀective wind speeds at wt13 and the upstream most wind turbine wt5
as in 5.1 this attribute is assumed to have the superimposed wakes of all the wind turbines

Conclusion
The major take away from training the QDWS model using data from diﬀerent TIeff at the

• The TIeff results of the wind turbines under wake shown in ﬁgures 5.5, 5.7, 5.9 and 5.11.

more data at diﬀerent turbulent conditions and discretize the intervals of turbulence into
smaller intervals. In order to prove the concept of the QDWS model is trained using
TIeff intervals in section 5.1.1, 5.1.2, 5.1.3 and 5.1.4.
• The potential of reducing the errors and uncertainties by using QDWS model is proven in
HRData-2. With the reduced uncertainties the possibility of identifying underperforming
wind turbines is explored in the chapters to follow.

Anomaly Wind Turbines
This chapter uses diﬀerent data based approaches to identify the anomaly wind turbines de-
veloped for the thesis, the anomaly wind turbines are then examined for performance in chapter
7. Three anomaly detection techniques are developed namely, Quasi Dynamic Wake Summa-
tion Model, Correlation Coeﬃcient Model and Ueff similarity model. Each of these techniques
are based on diﬀerent hypothesis explained in detail in respective sections.
The Quasi Dynamic Wake Summation(QDWS) model will serve as the primary method of
reference for anomaly detection, while the aim of developing correlation coeﬃcient6.2 method
and the Ueff similarity2.1.1 method is to have simple alternative methods for cross validation
and to provide supplementary information to help understand the nature of anomalies identiﬁed
using Estimated wake model.
6.1
Quasi Dynamic Wake Summation Model
Quasi Dynamic Wake Summation Model (QDWS) is based on the QDWS [see, chapter 5] model
explained in Chapter 5. The step by step process followed to identify the anomaly wind turbines
is explained below.
• The eﬀective wind speeds at each wind turbine for full wake conditions are calculated
based on QDWS method in section 2.1.2, UQDWS.
• The UQDWS and Ueff at each wind turbine is used to derive the normalized error for each
observation in the data as Uerror,QDWS = UQDW S−Ueff
Ueffinf
. Where Ueff is the eﬀective wind

based on the QDWS method, and Ueffinf is the ambient eﬀective wind speeds based on
the eﬀective wind speeds of the wind turbines that are not under wake (i.e. the upstream
most wind turbine).
• Ideally positive value of Ueff means a wind turbine has lesser Ueff than expected hence
under performing, the observer must keep in mind that the model is being trained with

6.2
Correlation Coeﬃcient Model (CCM)
Coeﬃcient of correlation is a parameter that can be used to measure the linear relationship
between two attributes. The value of coeﬃcient of correlation could fall in range of [-1, 1] such
that ’0’ means no linear relation at all, 1 means a perfect linear relation of direct proportionality
and a value of -1 means a perfect linear relation with negative proportionality, see [11].
The hypothesis behind this model is that adjacent wind turbines in similar wake conditions
have a correlated eﬀective wind speeds, hence the degree of correlation of Ueff between adja-
cent wind turbines under similar wake is measured. Once the correlation coeﬃcients between
adjacent wind turbines are calculated they are compared with the other correlation coeﬃcients
between wind turbines under similar wake, similar to the procedure followed in section 6.1.
Among the compared correlation coeﬃcients, the correlation coeﬃcients that are less than 15
% quantile are categorized as anomalies.
Illustrated in ﬁgure 6.4a and 6.4b, are the correlation coeﬃcient calculated between pairs of
wind turbines in HRData-1 and HRData-2 respectively. When correlation coeﬃcient between
two wind turbines is categorized as an anomaly, such cases are marked with a double headed
arrow pointing towards the wind turbines from which the correlation coeﬃcients are calculated.
The pairs are chosen such that the wind turbines are adjacent to each other and are under similar
number of wakes. When a correlation coeﬃcient is less than 15 [%] quantile value the double
headed arrows are shown in red. The correlation coeﬃcients are always calculated from north
to south for western/eastern winds and from east to west for northern/southern winds.
For example ﬁgure 6.4a shows the coeﬃcients of correlation calculated in HRData-1 with
westerly wind directions, for wind from west/east the wind turbines in the columns are under
similar wakes. In each column the correlation between Ueff of wind turbines in R1-R2 , R2-R3
...R7-R8 are calculated (R1..R8 refers to rows of wind turbines see ﬁgure 3.1) i.e. northern
row to southern row. Similarly, for wind coming from north/south, the correlation coeﬃcients
are calculated for turbines in each row from east to west. In the current case as it has been
observed that the wind turbines in the edge of the wind farm along the direction of the wind
i.e. wind turbines in R1 and R8 go into partial wake the data from this wind turbines is not
usd in the identiﬁcation of anomaly wind turbines.
If a correlation coeﬃcient between two wind turbines is identiﬁed to be an anomaly it is
likely that one of the two wind turbines could be an anomaly as the data from both the wind
turbines is involved in the calculation of the coeﬃcients, hence both the wind turbines are
categorized as anomalies. Although the correlation coeﬃcient method is simple and may not
give us much information about the performance of the wind turbine, this method can be a
simple cross validation method to the QDWS model [see, section 6.1].

6.3
Ueff Similarity Model (USM)
This model is similar to the coeﬃcient of correlation model in section 6.2. In this case the
hypothesis is that wind climate between wind turbines under similar wake conditions is same
and hence the wind turbines must experience very similar eﬀective wind velocities. The wind
turbines are again chosen in pairs just as in section 6.2. In each pair the eﬀective wind speed
at the each wind turbine Ueff,cwt is estimated to be same as the eﬀective wind speed at the
adjacent wind turbine Ueff,adj, followed by which the error of the predicted eﬀective wind
speed is calculated from the observed wind speeds i.e. Ueff,cwt −Ueff,adj. The error-similarity
calculated is normalized by dividing with the eﬀective speeds of the current wind turbine to
which the model is applied, Uerror,USM = (Ueff,cwt−Ueff,adj)
Ueff,cwt
. The mean error at each wind turbine
is calculated as the mean of Uerror,USM, the mean of Uerror,USM is represented as U error,USM.
In the pair of wind turbines chosen for westerly/easterly winds the current turbine is always
northern and the adjacent wind turbine is the southern wind turbine, similarly for north-
ern/southern winds the current wind turbine is always the eastern and the adjacent wind
turbine is the western wind turbine . The mean errors of Ueff similarity model for wind tur-
bines under similar wake conditions are compared with each other. The wind turbines that
have an error greater than 85 [%] quantile values are categorized as anomaly wind turbines.
In order to observe cases when the wind turbines are under similar wake, for a known wind
direction the data corresponding to full wake condition +/- 100 has been used for each wind
turbine i.e. for western winds the chosen data will correspond to 2700 +/- 100 .

Chapter 7
Anomaly Case Analysis
The anomaly cases identiﬁed in chapter 6 are analyzed in this chapter to show the applications
of QWDS method for identifying anomaly wind turbines. The nature of anomaly behaviour
identiﬁed using QDWS model are studied in the chapter. The potential application of the
QDWS model as an anomaly and underperformance wind turbine detection tool is discussed
in the current chapter. It is observed from the timeseries of the Uerror,QDWS that some wind
turbines are identiﬁed as anomalies due to the wind turbine operating in unsteady conditions
for a short interval and in some cases wind turbines are identiﬁed to have consistent underper-
formance behaviour throughout the dataset that is being studied (the examples are discussed
on the text that follows). Hence the anomaly behaviours are categorized into 1) Short term
anomalies, 2) Long term underperformance, the aforementioned cases of anomaly behaviours
are explained with examples in section 7.1 and 7.2.
7.1
Short Term Anomalies
Shown in ﬁgure 7.1 is the times series of wt44 rotor rotational speed ω in HRData-2. It can be
observed from the ﬁgure 7.1 that the wind turbine either operates at about ω ≈1.4rad/s or
ω ≈1.8rad/s, the inference from such a plot is that the wind turbines use two asynchronous
generators, i.e. generators running at ﬁxed rotational speeds one that is more eﬃcient at low
speeds (1.4 [rad/sec]) and the other that is more eﬃcient at higher wind speeds (1.8 [rad/sec]).
Based on the availability of pitch data it can be inferred that the wind turbines are pitch-
regulated to operate using one of the generators. For more reading on asynchronous generators,
see [12]. The important take away for the reader is that the wind turbine is being controlled
by pitch regulation, the goal of the pitch regulation below the rated wind speeds is to operate
the wind turbine in the most eﬃcient of the two asynchronous generators.

Chapter 8
Summary and Major Contributions
As of February 2018, the total installed capacity of wind turbines has reached 540 GW, of
which 52.6GW was installed in 2017 alone [2]. With increased installed capacities of wind
turbines around the world there is a need for faster and cheaper methods for operations and
management of wind farms. The operations and management of oﬀshore wind farms is a major
concern. The management of oﬀshore wind farms with poor accessibility coupled with instances
of wind turbine failures could escalate the cost of operations and maintenance upto 30 % of
the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) [3]. Furthermore, the downtime of wind turbines during
maintenance results in reduced annual energy production from wind turbines. Hence, given
the rapid increase of installed capacity of wind turbines the need to explore cost eﬀective wind
farm operational strategies becomes more and more important and even more so for oﬀshore
wind farms that have poor accessibility.
The study focuses on evaluating the performance of wind turbines using wind turbine
SCADA data so as to remotely monitor the health of the wind turbines. The earlier research
on evaluating the performance was largely based on using the SCADA of wind turbines along
with the reference power curves to evaluate the performance of wind turbines [4]. The wind
speed data used by such models is largely obtained from the nacelle mounted anemometers.
The uncertainty of wind speeds quantiﬁed by the anemometers directly depend on the quality
of the anemometers and the transfer functions used to account for the ﬂow distortion from the
wind turbine. While the uncertainty of the anemometers is itself a challenge the performance
evaluation methods based on the power curves are not suitable for wind turbines under wake.
In a typical oﬀshore wind farm the wind turbines in the middle of park experience wakes from
upstream wind turbines most of the time there by making such methods that evaluate perform-
ance based on nacelle anemometers not suitable, see [3]. Hence, along with the SCADA data
the wake calculations are an integral part of the thesis for performance evaluations of wind
turbines.
The SCADA data and wake calculations are two key elements of the thesis for successfully
quantifying the performance of wind turbines. The wake calculations are based on PossPOW
67
==PAGE:81==

algorithm, see [1]. The PossPOW algorithm is the choice for wake calculations in the current
study due to it being a simple and rapid method which can calculate the wake losses with
uncertainties less than 10 [%]. In addition the the availability of PossPOW algorithm with
tuned parameters speciﬁc for the Horns Rev-I wind farm from which the data is available to
the author makes it a strong candidate among other fast wake calculation methods.
The main body of thesis constitutes ﬁve diﬀerent Chapters 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7. The chapters
3 and 4 are largely SCADA related. Chapter 3 provides description of the wind climate at
the Horns Rev-I during the period at which the data was collected and the interpretation of
bias in the wind direction data based on the wake depth on of the upstream wind turbines.
Chapter 4 deals with the processing carried out on the data and the constraints used to select
the data that will be used as inputs for wake loss calculations. The objective for processing the
data is primarily to reduce the uncertainties in the wake calculation inputs. Additionally wind
directional constraints are set for data selection as the study majorly analyzes the full wake
conditions, the full wake conditions are deﬁned in order to reduce the uncertainties that could
possibly arise from applying the wake meandering condition to partial wake conditions. The
wind directional constraints are solely laid so the author can prove the concept of the thesis, in
the future such constraints may be relaxed when building on the ﬁndings of the current study.
While investigating the source of uncertainties and error in the chapter 4, the author found
that although the uncertainties investigated for single wake conditions in [7] were less than 10
[%], the wake accumulation methods added more uncertainty and bias in the errors, see section
4.3.
It is understood that wake accumulation is an area of improvement that needs to be con-
sidered before calculation of wake losses for the downstream wind turbines that are under
multiple wakes. Hence, in chapter 5 a new method for wake accumulation is developed, the
new method is coined to be Quasi-Dynamic Wake Summation [QDWS] method. For a wind
turbine under wake the QDWS calculates the eﬀective wind speeds empirically based on the
total wake losses that have accumulated until the immediate upstream wide turbine and the
wake created by the nearest upstream wind turbine for more detail, see chapter 5
Once the inputs and the wake model are established in the chapters 3, 4 and 5, in chapters 6
and 7 focus upon the anomaly wind turbines. The chapter 6 establishes three diﬀerent methods
for identifying anomaly wind turbines. The anomaly wind turbines are established based on
the QDWS model the identiﬁed cases of anomaly wind tribunes are cross validated based
on two alternative models namely Ueff Similarity Model and Correlation Coeﬃcient Model.
The alternative models are superﬁcially built to serve as a cross validation to the interpretation
made when using QDWS and possibly provide additional notes regarding the behaviour of wind
turbines. While the anomaly wind turbines are directly identiﬁed when using QDWS model,
the alternative methods require more objective assessment from the observer. Examples of
anomaly cases identiﬁed in the chapter 6 are further assessed in the chapter 7 to understand
the nature of anomalies detected and possibly concluding the anomaly cases that are due to

underperformance.
From assessing the errors in QDWS model, it has been identiﬁed that the increased error in

The wind turbines in the Horns Rev-I appear to have two asynchronous
generators, one for lower speeds and the other for higher speeds. The wind turbine appears to
be controlled to operate in the more eﬃcient of the two generators for an incoming wind speed,
the wind turbines are controlled through pitch regulation. It has been observed that the short
term anomalies were caused during the state of unsteadiness when the pitch regulation does
not completely succeed in operating the wind turbine at the rotational speed of one of the two
generators. Hence, it has been inferred that the QDWS method can have successful applications
in identifying unsteady states of wind turbine operation, prolonged cases of unsteady state loads
on wind turbines can have deteriorating eﬀects on the health of wind turbines. It has been
identiﬁed that one of the wind turbines that has high error in QDWS model, as the wind
turbine appears to operate at a lower wind speeds than estimated wind speeds using QDWS
model. Furthermore, the QDWS model appears to work well for other wind turbines in the
same column. Hence, the wind turbine is concluded to be an underperforming wind turbine.
The major takeaways from the thesis are as follows,
• Any model is only as good as the inputs that are being used, hence it is very import-
ant that one analyzes and scrutinizes the data inputs before using them to make any
interpretations.
• The wind direction inputs are critical in wake calculations whether using the full wake
conditions as in current thesis or when applying methods such as wake meandering models.
Hence one has to ensure the bias in the wind directional data are properly corrected. In
the current thesis the bias correction method used in [6] is employed, the method proposes
ﬁxing the bias in the upstream wind turbines using wake depth and assuming that the
mean wind direction in the downstream wind turbines is same as the upstream wind
turbines. Caution has to exercised when applying mean of upstream wind directions,
while using wind directions in the range of [00, 3600] or [-1800, 1800] if the data contains
values closer to the end of the range the results can be misleading.
• The current study proposes use of eﬀective wind speeds Ueff as inputs for wake loss
calculations. The eﬀective wind speeds are calculated based on the reference power curve

data. When using Ueff one has to keep in mind that in the partial control region also
referred to as the shoulder of the power curve the Ueff can have increased uncertainties.
The increased uncertainties can be due to control mechanisms such as the pitching of the
blades.
• A certain level of noise is expected in data recorded at high frequency, hence as a best
practice the noise in the data has to minimized to eliminate noise related errors. The
approach used is to calculate the single wake error in the data and measure the mean
error for diﬀerent lengths of moving mean periods. At a certain time period of moving
mean applied to the timeseries of the data then noise related errors can be minimized,
see ﬁgure 4.2 where the elimination of noise in the data by increasing the moving mean
periods is exhibited. Although high frequency data can contain information regarding
short lived events reducing the noise in the data can provide clearer interpretations for
the observer, hence the noise reduction as in section 4.2 is recommended.
• The current thesis analyzes the data in the full wake condition, the reason is to prove the
concept in the thesis for full wake condition. Ideally the next step after developing the
model in the thesis will be to include wake meandering model. As the data is available
while integrating the wake meandering model one has to assess the wake depth and width
of wake region interpreted by the wake meandering model and compare it to the wake
depth and wake depth interpreted in the data as in section 4.4. In case deviations are
observed, the parameters of the wake meandering model must be appropriately tuned.
For the data used in the thesis, the appropriate time period for applying moving mean
period was calculated to be 60[s].
• The QDWS model was developed in order to take advantage of available data and reduce
the errors and uncertainties developed when using the wake accumulation methods, see
section 3. QDWS method develops parameters that could account for degradation wake
from upstream wind turbines and the wake recovery for diﬀerent eﬀective turbulence at
the wind turbines. Although the reduced errors and signiﬁcant reduction in uncertainty
when applying QDWS model is shown in chapter 5, it has been observed that the reduced
uncertainties are subject to the quality of the training data set. While the QDWS model
trained based in HRData-2 showed signiﬁcant improvement in results, the uncertainties
in wake calculations could not be reduced in HRData-1.
Uncertainties can be good
indicators of the performance of training data, given the positive results shown when
using HRData-2 it would be reasonable to pursue a more robust model. A more robust
model can be pursued by further collecting data from diﬀerent turbulence intensities and
further segregating the turbulence intensities into shorter intervals (see chapter 5) for
notes related segregation of eﬀective turbulence for training the QDWS model.

• The proposed application of QDWS model include, wind turbine performance evaluations
and monitoring wind turbines for unsteady loads. Alternatively, the QDWS model can
also be used for wind power forecasting although a diﬀerent approach must be employed
for selecting the training data than the method used in the current thesis.
• The application of QDWS model parameters for wake summation methods in PossPOW
