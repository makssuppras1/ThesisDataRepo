Summary 
Worldwide 322 million tons of plastics are produced each year not including fibres with a yearly increase of 
around 4 % in the last decade. The European parliament has passes a suggestion that 60 % of all post-
consumer plastic packaging must be prepared for recycling by 2025 (DAKOFA, 2017; Europaudvalget, 2017). 
From surveys in European cities it has been identified that 50 – 60 % of all plastic waste is packaging (Larsen, 
et al., 2012; Toft, et al., 2016). The main resins found in the plastic packaging is polyethylene terephthalate 
(PET), high and low-density polyethylene (HDPE/LDPE), polypropylene (PP) and a little polystyrene (PS) 
(Hestin, et al., 2015). 
Most Danish waste for beverage packaging is collected by Dansk Retursystem under the laws determined of 
the Danish environmental protection legislation (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2015). By way of a deposit 
scheme the consumer is encouraged to return used packaging. Although the concentration of metals is 
slightly lower when PET is collected through a deposit system rather than through source segregation no 
statistical significant difference was observed (Snell, et al., 2017). Plastic recovered from mixed residual 
household waste could be contaminated from contact with other waste. Open loop recycling of PET bottles 
to fibres indicates a 40 – 85 % saving of abiotic resources compared to virgin PET and a 25 – 75 % saving in 
global warming potential (Shen, et al., 2010). 
The goal of the study is to investigate how the setup of post-consumer plastic collection and treatment 
schemes influence the quality of the recycled plastic and the environmental effects hereof. 
Three base line scenarios are compared: 1) Collection of plastic though source segregation 2) Collection of 
plastic through a deposit system and source segregation of residual plastic 3) incineration of plastic alongside 
residual waste.

The study showed that increasing the volume of post-consumer plastic collected for recycling have a positive 
environmental impact. Savings in all impact categories were obtained. Furthermore, increasing the volume 
of collected plastic for high-quality flakes through a deposit system increased the savings due to a higher 
substitution ratio. Moreover, increasing the amount of plastic recycled results in an increased saving in the 
crude oil reserve and emissions of greenhouse gases.   
To reach the targets set by the European union of recycling 60 % of post-consumer plastic packaging 
incentives to promote recycling of packaging from households, since none of the investigated scenarios could 
guarantee that the legal requirements are met. Highest recovery rate was achieved when PET one-way 
packaging currently covered by the Danish legislation for deposit, PET and HDPE packaging not yet covered 
by the legislation are collected by a deposit system. Hard plastic is source segregated and residual plastic are 
suggested to be collected by a comingled bin. Dependent of the source segregation efficiency between 84 % 
and 91 % of the total plastic was collected, while 55 % and 67 % was prepared for recycling. 
As such setting targets for recycling only invites the municipalities to push the consumers to sort out the 
packaging waste but if challenges in the management chain are not solved simultaneously, as well as creating 
a demand from the producers, not enough recycled plastic will end up in new products.

1 Introduction 
Worldwide 322 million tons of plastics are produced each year, not including fibres, with a yearly increase of 
around 4 % has been observed the last decade. The basic raw materials for virgin plastics are oil and natural 
gas. 4-6 % of the resources extracted globally are used for production of plastic, while 45 % are used for 
transport and 42 % for heat and electricity (Plastics Europe, 2016). According to an estimate from British 
Petroleum the world has a proven crude oil and natural gas reserve equivalent to approximately 50 years’ 
consumption at 2015 rates (British Petroleum, 2016). 
The demand for recyclable plastic is linked to the marked price of virgin plastic (Shen, et al., 2010). While the 
price for virgin plastic which is linked to the crude oil and as such promoting reuse and recycling can be 
challenging at low oil prices (O'Leary & Manavalan, 2015). 
1.1 Political Perspective and Challenges 
Limiting or fully stopping of the flow of virgin materials entering the production of new packaging materials 
is the obvious solution to reduce the need for extraction of the raw materials needed for production. Up until 
now the mantra for protecting resources and generation of large quantities of waste has been the waste 
hierarchy. But proposed issues with the hierarchy include limited specification and implementation of 
prevention, a lack of guidance for choosing amongst the levels of the hierarchy and the absence of a 
distinction between open-loop and closed-loop recycling (Van Ewijk & Stegemann, 2016). A British study 
estimates that by recycling 40 – 60 % of PET bottles a 32 – 48 % reduction in GWP could be obtained, 
compared to landfilling (Amienyo, et al., 2013).

circular the recovered material has to be reused in products of same quality.  
1.1.2 Amendment of the European Waste Directive 
According to the Danish authorities a larger share of the packaging waste is to be recycled by 2027, a strategy 
adopted from the European waste directive (Danish Government, 2015). In parallel a directive from the 
European parliament and the council amending the European directive on packaging and packaging waste is 
being prepared. Initially the European Commission (EC) proposed a minimum limit of recycling and reuse for 
plastic packaging at 55 % in 2025 while the targets for 2030 will be evaluated at a later point in time (European 
Commission, 2015). But by the Parliament's environmental committee the targets were raised to 60 % of 
plastic packaging to be prepared for recycling by 2025 (DAKOFA, 2017; Europaudvalget, 2017). 
Some member states including the Danish government and the Slovakian and Maltese precedency suggested 
reduction of the proposed targets by 5 % to 50 %, as the targets were thought to be too ambitious. Generally, 
there is support for the new binding targets from Danish parties, but the targets were said to a bit ambitious 
as it was not clear what deposit systems could be included. Moreover, it was noted that the plastic collected 
by the Danish municipalities has to be made available to the marked (Europaudvalget, 2017)..  
Furthermore, some member states oppose to let deposit systems count towards the recycling targets, as it 
is the impression that countries like Denmark and Finland easier will meet the targets. For the moment, the 
proposal from the environmental Parliament's environmental committee has been passed by the parliament 
and negotiation with the Council is needed to finalize the directive. 
1.2 Plastics of Interest 
From surveys in European cities it has been identified that 50 – 60 % of all plastic waste is packaging (Larsen, 
et al., 2012; Toft, et al., 2016). The main resins found in the plastic packaging is polyethylene terephthalate 
(PET), high and low-density polyethylene (HDPE/LDPE), polypropylene (PP) and a little polystyrene (PS) 
(Hestin, et al., 2015). In Denmark, currently only 33 % of light weight plastic packaging is recovered for 
recycling, with the rest being recovered as heat and energy and a small fraction is being landfilled (Kranzinger, 
et al., 2017; Toft, et al., 2016). 
1.2.1.1 
PET 
PET also known as polyester, is a clear lightweight polymer that is wildly used in packaging materials for food 
and beverages, but is also applied for packaging for mouthwash, liquid hand soap, window cleaner and tennis 
balls (PETRA, 2015). PET is easily recyclable by washing and re-melting, or by chemically breaking it down to 
its monomers to make new PET resin. Products made from recycled PET can be bottles, industrial strapping, 
automotive part, textiles for clothes or insulation material for winter jackets. 
==PAGE:9==
Page 8 of 85 
 
1.2.1.2 
PE 
Polyethylene is categorized by density into either high density or low density (HDPE or LDPE). HDPE is a 
relatively stiff plastic and can be used for packaging for milk, laundry detergents or garbage bins, where LDPE 
is a flexible material widely used in films and plastic bags. A variation of LDPE is LLDPE (Linear Low-Density 
Polyethylene) which is quite similar to LDPE, although production is less energy extensive 
(creativemechanisms, 2016). LLDPE has a higher tensile strength than LDPE and is commonly used in films. 
HDPE is non-biodegradable and can take centuries to decompose, so it is necessary that it is recycled and 
used again (Thomas, 2012). 
1.2.1.3 
PP 
Polypropylene has a wide range of applications, from consumer packaging, garbage cans to plastic part in the 
industry like parts for cars. PP is very resistance to fatigue and has demonstrated certain advantages in 
improved strength, stiffness and higher temperature capability over polyethylene. Polypropylene is one of 
the lightest plastics available making it good for decreasing weight and saving money for transport. 
1.2.1.4 
PS 
Polystyrene can be either solid or foamed, is normally clear but can be easily coloured. Foamed PS is used for 
protective packaging, while solid PS can be used for containers, lids or disposable cutlery. Polystyrene is 
slowly biodegradable and is therefore a focus among environmentalists. Especially in its foam form It is 
increasingly present as in form of litter in the environment, predominantly along shores, and also in 
increasing volumes in the Pacific Ocean (Kwon, et al., 2014). 
1.2.1.5 
Additives 
More than plastic monomers are used for producing plastic. Nearly all producers add compounds to plastic 
to improve the properties of the plastic, i.e. flame retardants stabilisers, fillers, glass fibres, colorants. 
Additives can also improve ageing and processing properties of the plastic. Many plastics used for single-use 
packaging have sufficient properties without any addition additives, as such only a small number of additives 
are used to reduce oxidation and improve slip properties. For other applications, it is necessary to improve 
the basic properties of the plastic polymer by using higher amounts of additives.  
Some additives are added to lower the price of the polymer rather than to improve the performance. 
Additives do not, for most uses, bind to the polymer and can migrate to the surface. This is unwanted in food 
packaging as additives can be toxic or bad tasting  (Hansen, et al., 2013). 
1.3 Management and Collection of Plastic  
Recovery of the targeted types of plastics are crucial to the process of implementing a circular economy. 
Different approaches can be applied depending on the targeted materials and desired output quality. Three 
ways of collecting recyclables have been identified for this study, either by a deposit system, source 
segregation or as a part of the residual waste stream. To move towards the targets set by the European Union 
the collection need to be directed towards collection schemes that favour high return rates but also minimize 
losses throughout the management chain.  
1.3.1.1 
Deposit 
Most Danish packaging for beverage is collected by Dansk Retursystem under the laws determined of the 
Danish environmental protection legislation (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2015). By way of a deposit 
scheme the consumer is encouraged to return used packaging, securing that the loss of material on packaging 
sold in Denmark is kept to a minimum. With a high return ratio and quality, the deposit system secures that 
==PAGE:10==
Page 9 of 85 
 
the recovered material can be recycled in products of at least the same quality with a minimum of virgin 
material needed. The responsible for carrying out the Danish deposit system has a policy that buyers of the 
recyclables must guarantee that they are used in products with the same quality (Dansk Retursystem, 2015).   
Additionally, increasing the volume of recyclables going into a deposit scheme removes them from the 
general waste collection, potentially creating less heterogeneity in waste going to incineration. 
1.3.1.2 
Source Segregation 
Source-segregation systems can be introduced as mandatory or voluntary systems and affects the number 
of fractions that is collected separately. The systems differ for different fractions depending on volume and 
value.  
The waste collection systems dependents not only on physical factors, but also political issues such as wanted 
services and willingness to pay. Increasing source segregation schemes, should be followed by an advisory 
and consultancy service (Nilsson & Christensen, 2011). 
Changes in collection scheme influences the quality of the recovered plastic. Contamination from packaging 
or other waste that enters the household collection system cannot be completely separated during the 
recycling process. This leads to contamination of the plastic flakes that are manufactured (Snell, et al., 2017). 
Different versions of the source segregation for plastic is applied by Danish municipalities (Larsen, et al., 
2012). Some collects rigid and soft plastic together, while other collect only the ridged packaging combined 
with some smaller items. Finally, some municipalities collect plastic packaging as a part of a mixed recyclables 
bin alongside glass, paper and metal packaging (Cimpan & Wenzel, 2013).  
An Austrian study of a unification of the collection of plastic recyclables by the ‘catch-all’ bin illustrated an 
improvement of the global warming potential as well as an improvement of the collection efficiency 
(Kranzinger, et al., 2017). 
The different collection schemes across the municipalities creates different composition of recyclables across 
Denmark. But it should also be noted that composition of household packaging waste is not expected to be 
uniform distributed in urban and rural areas, and as such collection strategy must be evaluated from 
municipality to municipality. A simple and easy to understand system is desired by the consumer (Mueller, 
2013). This potential increases the level of heterogeneity, by mixing different fractions in the same bin, 
increasing the need for sorting and risk of contamination and devaluation of material quality. Studies 
indicates that introduction of a separate collection system increases the total quantity of collected 
recyclables (Kranzinger, et al., 2017).    
1.3.1.3 
Quality of Collected Recyclables 
As mentioned deposit systems provide the most homogeneous recovery of a targeted polymer. This is 
confirmed by a study on the quality of the collected PET from various collection schemes in Germany. Flakes 
obtained from PET collected by a deposit system contained fewer impurities of other polymers. Although the 
concentration of metals is slightly lower when PET is collected through a deposit system rather than through 
source segregation no statistical significant difference was observed (Snell, et al., 2017). In comparison, 
plastic recovered from mixed residual household waste could be contaminated from contact with other 
waste like organic solvents, pesticides or detergents, this would make closed loop recycling nearly impossible 
(Chilton, et al., 2010). Recycling of PET bottles to fibres in an open loop scheme, indicates a 40 – 85 % saving 
==PAGE:11==
Page 10 of 85 
 
of abiotic resources compared to virgin PET and a 25 – 75 % saving in global warming potential (GWP) (Shen, 
et al., 2010). 
1.3.1.4 
Sorting 
The collected plastic is no matter the collection scheme bound to contain foreign objects. Metal and 
aluminium are sorted out by magnets and eddy current separators, while films, which can also be removed 
mechanically, cardboard and larger objects are manually removed (Larsen, et al., 2012). Plastics are sorted 
according to type and colour by near inferred (NIR) optical sorting. In some cases, pre-sorting is done by 
density separation although this is not commonly used in big scale.  
Plastic collected as commingled waste may need primary sorting primary before being sorted into polymers. 
The sorting efficiency of clean plastic by NIR has been shown to be between 87 and 96 % dependent of 
polymer type (DEPA, 2015), but efficiencies for PET NIR sorters has also been found to as low as 70 % (Jansen, 
et al., 2015). Due to this some sorting facilities only accept clean plastic, while a limited amount of residual 
food waste on packaging waste is accepted at other facilities and washing is carried out prior to processing. 
As the sorting rely on NIR some challenges occur when dirt or labels prevents a clear identification. 
Furthermore, objects composed of more than one type or on object concealed inside another can be difficult 
to distinguish. Another problem is the sorting of black plastic which currently cannot be sorted in large 
volumes and as such is lost as a resource (Hestin, et al., 2015; Larsen, et al., 2012). It is assumed that around 
10 % of hard plastic in the Copenhagen area is black and is only partially collected. The technology for sorting 
out black plastic is available but is still under development and is still rather expensive (Bigum, et al., 2014). 
1.3.1.5 
Processing of Recycled Plastic 
Impurities in targeted polymers by other unwanted polymers can have unwanted consequences. For 
instance, will PVC contamination of PET burn at the processing temperature creating black spots, while small 
quantities of PE will not have influence on use and processing of PET flakes (Snell, et al., 2017). The 
mechanical properties of the processed plastic are dependent on the level of contaminants, where manually 
sorted PE and PP have been shown to have slightly better tensile strength than for mixed plastic from 
household waste, though both are worse than virgin materials (Turku, et al., 2017). The quality of the recycled 
plastic is of importance as more packaging is becoming one-way packaging and producers use less material 
to reduce cost for material and transport. 
1.3.1.6 
Alternative to Recycling of Plastic 
Not all collected plastic or other recyclables is of a quality where recycling is the preferred solution. Currently 
more than 50 % of the waste steam from households in Denmark ends in the waste to energy (WtE) sector 
(Toft, et al., 2016). This converts the collected waste to an energy source. Currently electricity production is 
usually not reaching more than 30 % with the rest being recovered as heat for district heating. Increased 
electricity production is possible if seawater is used for condensation. Although this is not thought to be a 
sustainable solution as 40 % to 70 % of the energy is lost (Norman, 2011). Another barrier to achieve high 
electricity yields from incineration of waste is the large variation in composition. Increasing the level of plastic 
recycling reduces the amount of high energy material in the WtE plants, but frees capacity for residual waste.

2.1.2 Functional Unit 
The functional unit is defined as management of 1000 kg plastic waste from the Danish consumers. Included 
in the 1000 kg is all plastic waste produced in Danish households including the PET one-way packaging for 
beverages. Included in the management is collection, sorting, transport and treatment of residues for 
recycling and heat recovery. 
2.1.3 Time Horizon 
The time frame of this study is linked to the targets set by the European Union that minimum 60 % of all 
plastic packaging must be prepared for recycling by 2020. The effects of this is evaluated by use of the 
projected marginal energy changes in 2035 published by the European Commission in 2013 (Capros, et al., 
2013).  
2.1.4 System Description 
The full system for managing the collected plastic is described by Figure 4 while a simplified version is seen 
in Figure 3. The model follows the material flow from collection to the final processing step, whether it is 
processing of pellets from recycled plastic or energy recovery. From the model three qualities of the 
recyclable plastic are defined, a grade A coming out of the deposit stream, a grade B from source segregation 
of only hard plastic and a grade C which corresponds to a bin with mixed plastic or mixed recyclable including 
metal, glass and cardboard. 
The system is divided into three major streams. Stream I including the collection and handling of plastic 
included in deposit system, steam III and VI including two types of source segregated curbside collection for 
recovery of the remaining plastic and stream X for curbside collection of the residual household waste. In 
common for the schemes, where plastic is collected, is that all plastic is processes outside the Danish borders 
(for this study assumed to be primarily Germany) an as such the transport is identical for all streams. 
Incineration of residues takes place in the country were the residues ends whether it is Denmark or Germany.  
For plastic collected via source segregation it is pre-treated and targeted polymers are recovered for direct 
recycling. The term direct recycling covers the plastics sorted out from sorting facilities as ready for recycling 
material. For both source-segregated streams after recovery of some hard plastic a mixed plastic fraction is 
baled and shipped to secondary treatment on German sorting facilities for further recovery of plastics. 
Processed resins varies from grade to grade. Resins of interest originated from the deposit system is PET from 
one-way bottles, some HDPE packaging and PP caps. Recovered resins of medium grade are hard plastic, 
namely PET, HDPE, PP and PS. Lower grade recyclables also include LDPE recovery, while other types of plastic 
will not be recovered for this study. Choice of plastics include in which grade is based mainly on a estimate 
of what types could be possible to include in as deposit system (grade A) and what is categorised as hard 
plastic (grade B). Furthermore, mixed resins such as HDPE and PP are not assumed as a final product in this 
study. For flow charts used in the different scenarios se Appendix F.

2.1.5 System Boundaries 
The systems modelled have their origin at the generation of the waste in the households, as such impacts 
from production of the plastic prior to use or transport are not included in the assessment, see Figure 3. 
Included in all scenarios are transport associated with collection (I, III VI and X) and transport to sorting (V 
and VIII) or processors outside the Danish border (II and IV). Transport of residues to WtE facilities or 
transport, of plastic sorted in Germany, to processing is not included as it is assumed that the distance from 
sorting of residues is little compared to other transport distances. Contamination by other plastics than the 
desired is assumed to be highest for plastics similar to the targeted polymer. This means that when targeting 
hard plastics, such as PET or HDPE, other hard plastic like PVC is more likely to end up in the bin that soft 
plastic such as LDPE. Impact of plastic left in the environment by littering is not included in the study, this is 
also the case for imported beverage packaging from Germany that are not included in the Danish deposit 
system at the moment.

covered by the Danish legislation. Finally, for baseline scenario 3 no separate collection of recyclables is 
applied beside energy recovery from incineration of mixed municipal waste. How the individual parameters 
are obtained is further explained after the scenario description.

2.3.2 System Expansion and Allocation 
The system is expanded to credit the savings obtained by substitution of production of virgin plastic, and for 
energy recovery by thermal treatment of non-recyclable plastic residues. For the energy substitution, the 
avoided emissions and materials for the marginal electricity production is based on changes in marginal 
energy production from 2010 to 2035. The marginal energy is allocated to electricity and heat is assumed to 
be produced locally from natural gas. The avoided production of virgin plastic pellets the processes are 
obtained from the Ecoinvent database v. 3.3. 
To illustrate the different qualities of recovered plastic through the three collection methods the B factor is 
applied to represent the market response. This is don as recycling can result in loss of quality, difference in 
properties and market elasticity (Rigamonti, et al., 2014). 
An internal study has evaluated the sorting schemes and their influence on usability of the recovered plastic 
showed that only PET and HDPE were possible to collect for closed loop recycling if a state of the art MRF 
was applied while all other polymers will lose quality already after one recycle circle. From an environmental

2.3.5 Treatment and Sorting 
The post-consumer plastic is depending on the collection scheme received by either a deposit system, a 
recovery centre or a WtE facility. 
2.3.5.1 
Danish Deposit System 
The Danish deposit system is a non-profit company handling the collection and distribution of the empty one-
way packaging for carbonated beverages and mineral water placed on the Danish marked. Packaging 
included in the deposit system is brought back to grocery stores by the consumer and the compacted 
materials are collected and brought to a centralized facility for preparation for shipping. The Danish deposit 
system handled 45,000 tons glass, plastic and aluminium in 2015 and had an energy consumption of 2.5 GWh 
(Dansk Retursystem, 2015). As such an energy consumption of 55 kWh per ton and a forklift using 1 L diesel 
per ton for compaction and preparation is assumed to be a good approximation. 
2.3.5.2 
Material Recovery Facility (MRF) 
The recyclables collected by source segregation has to be sorted or baled before being shipped off to 
processers. In Denmark only the easy to recover polymers are sorted out or in some cases source segregated 
plastic is only baled prior being shipped off. It is assumed for all scenarios with material recovery that some 
amount is recovered prior to the mixed plastic being shipped to Germany for further sorting (DEPA, 2015). 
2.3.5.2.1 Sorting efficiencies 
For the collected recyclables to become a usable resource for production of new products they have to be 
split up into specific polymers and colours and unwanted objects has to be removed. For this study, primary 
sorting of collected plastic is assumed. For the source separated hard plastic recovery of PET, HDPE, PP and 
PS are assumed in most scenarios while only PET is recovered when it is collected as mixed plastic. This 
assumption is done because some Danish resource facilities only recover one type of plastic and some have 
no recovery at all when handling commingled plastic (DEPA, 2015). 
Sorting efficiencies are estimated based on two reports from the Danish EPA (DEPA, 2015; Møller, et al., 
2013) and are grouped into four groups depending on the activity: recovery of targeted polymers from source 
segregated hard plastic, recovery of targeted polymers from source segregated mixed plastic, recovery of a 
mixed plastic fraction for further processing and recovery of polymers in German plastic MRF’s.

3 Results and Discussion 
According to EU waste directive on circular economy only the share that enters the final recycling step counts 
toward the targets. Thus, it is essential not only to have a collection system that favors high collection but 
also an infrastructure that makes sure that the collected recyclables also end up as new products, and are 
not lost due to inefficient sorting or logistic.

that do not understand the difference between hard and soft plastic a chance to separate plastic from the 
residual waste stream. From a British study, it was shown that the more fractions collected in separate bins 
the higher the collection efficiency was expected to be (Woodard, et al., 2006). It was proven that the 
participation rate is proportional to the number of bins, at least until 6 bins, while no information on the 
impurities of the collected material was mentioned. This was backed up by an American study where the best 
recovery rates were achieved when more recyclables are targeted. Although, the highest recovery rates were 
seen with a single stream due to the simplicity (Mueller, 2013). The reason can be because more items are 
targeted and as such the individual household have a bin combination that better fit their waste generation. 
A downside is that there most likely is a peak for the number of bins that any consumer is willing to use due 
to convenience, just as the collection will be complex.

3.5 Contribution analysis for partly closed loop recycling 
The contribution of the selected impact categories, seen in Figure 9, is analogue to the open loop scenarios, 
only difference is that deposit of PET one-way bottles constitutes the largest share in most categories. The 
larger impacts by the deposit system are due to the fact that the collection efficiency and treatment system 
secure that a larger and more homogenous share ends up at the final processing step. The deposit system 
also secures a better quality of the plastic ending up at the processing step. 
When comparing the scenarios 2, 2A and 2B there is a trend that increasing the quantity of items included in 
the deposit system overall decreases in all selected impact categories are observed. Furthermore, a larger 
share is ending up as higher quality recycled plastic. IT is not possible to say if there is a significant gain from 
including HDPE in the deposit as the results are almost identical to when only expanding with previously not 
include PET bottles. This can be due to smaller volume of HDPE and the assumption that only half of the 
collected HDPE packaging was of a quality that can directly substitute virgin plastic.  
The impact from substitution from deposit is always larger than substitution of lesser quality. This can be 
explained by the chosen technical efficiency (a factor) and marked response (b factor) where substitution of 
plastic from the deposit system is assumed to be of a better quality. Furthermore, the loos of material due 
to sorting is less in assumed less in the deposit system than for source segregation.

3.8 Marked for plastic.  
As the marked price of virgin plastic is highly dependent on 
the price of crude oil, recycling of plastic is expected to be 
favoured when the oil price is high. Looking at the trend 
within the last year, illustrated by Figure 12, the oil price has 
increased from around 33 £/barrel to around 45 £/barrel. In 
the same period virgin PET increased from 900 £/ton to 1100 
£/ton. The increase in price could be linked to the 
devaluation of the British pound du to the British decision to 
leave the European union, but on a global marked the oil 
price has followed a similar trend with an increase in 
October and December 2016. Looking at the marked for 
recovered plastic bottles the prices for clear PET, coloured 
PET and mixed polymers have remained relatively stable in 
the last year while prices for natural and mixed HDPE have 
been increasing. Furthermore, comparing Figure 11 and 
Figure 12 one challenge arises when comparing prices for recycled plastic with those of virgin. According to 
Figure 11 the price of recycled PET and virgin PET is almost identical while in Figure 12 there is a big difference. 
This poses the question of which quality to compare? But one thing is certain, when the oil price is at 2015 
levels the expenses for energy and process water means there is no financial incentive to recycle plastic 
(Kramer, 2016; O'Leary & Manavalan, 2015).  
Looking back at the assumed marked response applied for the substitution of virgin plastic; what happens if 
marked response drops below the assumed values for all types or if the marked is saturated by especially the 
lower grade plastic making the product worthless. The model illustrated that from an environmental point 
of view the higher the quality and quantity of recycled material the greater savings can be expected. 
The main question is then if recycling should only be driven by the oil prices or if the save in fossil fuels, new 
job opportunities and environmental benefits can be a factor?

One of the main assumption is that the participation rate in any new deposit scheme will be analogue to the

The product substitution, implies a change in demand by the industry that needs to replace virgin plastic with

quality commingled recyclables are currently being purchased by re-processors from outside of Europe

In fact, there is no guarantee that the commingled bin will increase the collection rate, a European report 
suggests that the amount of waste per capita collected though a comingles system is comparable to that of 
a separate system (Seyring, et al., 2015). The hope is that having two bins for plastic can increase consumer 
awareness and increase sorting quality at participation for both bins. 
Looking back on the producers responsible for making recycling possible, careful use of recyclable plastic and 
avoidance of excessive use of chemicals and other additives in plastics will make recycling possible for a larger 
share of plastic products placed on the marked. So, to fully implement a circular value, chain a dialog with 
the industry to limit the additive put into products as well as colours to make the applied plastic as similar as 
possible. 
Incorporating recycling in the production chain creating a consistent demand for recycled plastic of a certain 
quality can be the base for designing the collecting scheme, e.g. if producers of car parts and garden furniture, 
where additives like flame retardants and UV-stabilizer are used, do not need plastic of food grade quality. 
But still high-quality plastic is needed (Murray’s Recycled plastic, 2017). Producer responsibility be a part of 
the solution to make the producer responsible for the recyclability of the end of use plastic packaging, this 
can increase collection rates (Hanisch, 2000; Nahman, 2010).

As such, decision on closed versus open loop recycling can be influenced by the type of recycling chosen, i.e.

Expanding the volume of fractions being recycled from residual waste saves resources, energy and potentially

5 Conclusion 
The study showed set out to investigate how the decision of recycling in an open or partially closed loop 
affects the quality of the recovered plastic and the environmental effects hereof. 
From the literature review it was clear that it is not possible to recycle plastic in food packaging if it was 
recovered by source segregation alongside other plastic. This loss in quality was not seen in plastic collected 
in a single stream deposit system. 
The impact assessment showed that increasing the quality and volume of plastic recycled positive impacts 
were obtained. Reductions in emissions of greenhouse gasses and lowered human toxicity were indicated. 
Furthermore, recycling of plastic kept the fossil resources in the ground. The highest savings was obtained 
from recycling through a deposit system, due to a higher recovery rate and the assumption of a better marked 
response than of source segregated plastic. 
Performing sensitivity analysis on all parameters included in important processes in the model, it was seen 
that sorting efficiencies at MRFs, technological efficiency and marked response was sensitive when the 
process was the dominating impact.  
Finally it can be concluded that the targets set by the European parliament on recycling of packaging waste 
can prove to be overly ambitious. As such setting targets for recycling only invites the municipalities to push 
the consumers to sort out the packaging waste but if challenges in the management chain are not solved 
simultaneously, as well as creating a demand from the producers, not enough recycled plastic will end up in 
new products.
