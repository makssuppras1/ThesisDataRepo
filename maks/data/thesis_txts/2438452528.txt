Summary (English)
Drinking water has been abstracted in Bagsværd since 1909, as the ground-
water there is of a natural high quality. The high groundwater quality was
aﬀected when the use of chlorinated solvents from the business district con-
taminated the aquifers. Abstraction of water from the contaminated sites,
lead to the closing of multiple drinking water abstraction wells.
Various
remedial measures have been introduced, but chlorinated solvents are still
found in the drinking water abstraction. This thesis investigates the eﬃ-
ciency of the remedial measures, through the development of a geological
model, a steady state 3D ﬂow model and a 2D contaminant transport model.
The geological model was set up in GeoScene3D as a nine-layer pancake
model. Comsol Multiphysics was used to create the steady state ﬂow model,
where the ﬁnal ﬂow model was achieved through a gradual increase in model
complexity. The geological model was implemented in the ﬁnal ﬂow model.
The contaminant transport model was set up in Comsol Multiphysics using
hydraulic parameters from the 3D ﬂow model. Three alternative remedial
pumping strategies were developed to ensure the quality of the abstracted
groundwater. Each scenario has three sub scenarios varying in extensive-
ness. The ﬁrst scenario alters the pumping rates for the existing remedial
abstraction wells. The second scenario includes three existing wells in ad-
dition to the current remediation. The third scenario includes three new
wells, with optimal placement and screen length, in addition to the current
remediation. When the groundwater ﬂow model was calibrated, a RMSE
for the diﬀerence between simulated and observed hydraulic heads of 0.33
m was found, indicating a good ﬁt. Capture zones and particle tracking for
the the current remediation wells, obtained from the ﬂow model, concluded
that particles from known contaminated sites escapes the remedial measures.
The eﬃciency of the contaminant transport models for diﬀerent scenarios was
compared. The eﬃciency was quantiﬁed as the decrease in contaminant up-
take by the drinking water abstraction well, after 20 years of operation. The
ﬁrst scenario yielded a 44 % decrease, the second scenario yielded a 36 %
decrease and the third yielded a 95 % decrease. The modelling suggested the
third scenario is a possible solution to improve the remediation.

Summary (Danish)
Drikkevand har været indvundet i Bagsværd siden 1909, da grundvand-
skvaliteten i området er af naturlig høj kvalitet. Brugen af klorerede opløs-
ningsmidler i erhvervsområder, samt deponering i grusgrave, har ført til lukn-
ing af drikkevandsindvindingen. Flere afværgeforanstaltninger er blevet im-
plementeret, men de klorerede opløsningsmidler bliver stadig fundet i ind-
vindingen. Denne afhandling undersøger eﬀektiviteten af afværgeforanstalt-
ningerne gennem udviklingen af en geologisk model, en steady state 3D ﬂow-
model og en 2D stoftransportmodel. Den geologiske model er lavet som en
ni lags pandekagemodel ved brug af GeoScene3D. Comsol Multiphysics blev
brugt til at lave ﬂowmodellen. Den færdige ﬂowmodel blev udviklet gennem
løbende forøgelse af kompleksitet. Den geologiske model er implementeret i
den færdige ﬂowmodel. Stoftransportmodellen blev også sat op ved brug af
Comsol Multiphysics. Stoftransportmodellen benytter de samme hydrauliske
parametre som ﬂowmodellen.
Tre afværgestrategier er formuleret som et
alternativ til den eksisterende afværge. Hver strategi, præsenteret som et
scenarie, indeholder tre underscenarier med varierende ekstensivering. Det
første scenarie ændrer pumperaterne på de eksisterende afværgeboringer. Det
andet scenarie tilføjer tre eksisterende boringer til den nuværende afværge.
Det tredje scenarie inkluderer tre nye boringer, med optimal placering og
ﬁltersætning, til den nuværende afværge.
Når ﬂowmodellen er kalibreret
ﬁndes der en RMSE på 0.33 meter for de simulerede potentialer, hvilket
indikerer at modellen er repræsentativ.
Oplandszoner og partikelsporing,
genereret med ﬂowmodellen, konkluderer at partikler fra kendte forurenede
grunde løber forbi den eksisterende afværge. Ved at implementere de nye
afværgestrategier i stoftransportmodellen kan deres eﬀektivitet kvantiﬁceres
og derved sammenlignes. Kvantiﬁcering er beskrevet som det procentvise
fald, sammenlignet med den eksisterende afværge, i den årlige oppumpning
af forureningsmasse efter 20 års kørsel. Den første strategi resulterede i et
fald på 44 %, den anden strategi resulterede i et fald på 36 % og den sidste
strategi resulterede i et fald på 95 %. Modellering viste at den tredje strategi
er en mulig løsning til optimering af afværgen.

Introduction
Groundwater is the main source of drinking water in Denmark, thereby giving reason to
protect it. Groundwater is pumped from deep aquifers and supplied by water works to
the end user. The water works prepare the water with simple treatments to improve the
quality. The processes include aeration, where in addition to increase in oxygen levels,
iron and manganese precipitate while hydrogen sulﬁde and methane are stripped. When
aerated the water goes through a sand ﬁlter to separate the precipitated particles from the
drinking water. As the treatment of the water is simple, contaminants are not removed,
thus demanding a high quality for the abstracted groundwater (Edzwald, 2011).
The quality of the Danish groundwater varies all over the country. Types of contami-
nation are typically determined by the surroundings. Contamination with pesticides is
often found in rural areas where the usage has been extensive. In urban districts, the
contamination is typically determined by the use of chemicals from diﬀerent businesses
in the area. Many types of contamination are found in urban areas. Chlorinated solvents
and petroleum hydrocarbons are often the reason for contaminations in urban areas.
Groundwater is primarily pumped from rural areas, where the natural water quality is
high. The high natural quality is ensured by few potential sources for contamination,
high groundwater recharge, protective conﬁning layers and highly conductive aquifers.
The Danish EPA has pointed out areas with a special interest for abstraction of ground-
water, these areas are called OSD (areas with special drinking water interest). Areas
around well ﬁelds are classiﬁed as BNBO (near well protection area). Despite being clas-
siﬁed as BNBO, new businesses and potential polluters are still allowed to be established
in the area around the well ﬁeld.
This study focuses on the contamination at the business district of Bagsværd, located
in Gladsaxe. Bagsværd has a long history of drinking water abstraction and has under-
gone major changes in both quality, quantity and surroundings. As the business district
in Bagsværd expanded, more contamination was found in the drinking water. Many inves-
tigations have been conducted in the area to map the sources. These investigations lead
to the establishment of remedial wells to avoid pollutants reaching the drinking water
abstraction. Despite various attempts to optimise the remediation, contamination still
bypasses the remedial pumping and contaminates the drinking water. This study will
therefore assess the ﬂow from the sources through the remedial measures. Rambøll has
already created a large scale 3D ﬂow model of the area to map the capture zones for the
well ﬁeld, as a part of redesigning the pumping strategy. These previous investigations
calls for a more advanced model, with a focus shifted towards the remedial pumping
instead of the capture zones of the well ﬁeld.

1.1
Objectives
The continuous abstraction of chlorinated solvents for Bagsværd water works, indicates
a problem with the remedial measures. This study assesses the remedial pumping at
Bagsværd well ﬁeld and proposes strategies to improve the eﬃciency of the remediation.
The objectives are the following:
• Achieve an overview of the historical development for the remediation and drinking
water abstraction in Bagsværd with the use of previous investigations
• Obtain a good understanding of the geology to develop a high quality geological
model
• Obtain an understanding of the ﬂow in Bagsværd, the impact on pumping and the
contamination in the area
• Outline the extent of the contamination in Bagsværd through historical and new
chemical data
• Develop a steady-state groundwater ﬂow model to assess the eﬃciency of the reme-
dial measures
• Develop a contaminant transport model to quantify potential impact of the contam-
inants in the water works
• Model-based assessment of alternative remedial pumping strategies to obtain a more
eﬃcient remediation

Site Description
Bagsværd is one of four districts in Gladsaxe. Bagsværd holds around 3700 residences
and a population of 7500, which corresponds to 10.8 % of the population of Gladsaxe.
Bagsværd accounts for 20 % of the area of the municipality (Gladsaxe, 2018).
Figure 1: Location of the site in Gladsaxe, eastern Zealand, Denmark
2.1
Drinking water abstraction in Bagsværd
Bagsværd is an area with a high natural quality of groundwater and is therefore an OSD
area. Water has been abstracted in Bagsværd since 1909, despite the ﬁrst water works
was constructed in Bagsværd in 1921. The well ﬁeld was placed in a rural area with no
potential sources to contamination of the groundwater. The establishment of new busi-
nesses resulted in the formation of the business district in 1948. In 1987 three of the six
active drinking water wells were contaminated with the chlorinated solvents TCE and
PCE. The aﬀected wells were BA5A, BA12 and BA13, see Figure 2 for placement and
Table 1 for active periods (JNU and MBMJ, 2004).
All aﬀected wells kept pumping as a remedial measure, while the three remaining drinking
water abstraction wells maintained the abstraction of drinking water. As the six drinking
water abstraction wells supplied 0.9 million m3/year, the demand could not be met. The
county of Copenhagen, now the Capital Region, initiated an investigation of the possi-
bility to move the well ﬁeld to obtain a higher groundwater quality. The investigation
concluded that it was not possible to move the well ﬁeld. There were no locations in
Bagsværd, where the abstraction could remain on the needed level to comply with the
supply in a sustainable manner. To ensure the quality of the groundwater in the existing
3

well ﬁeld, an investigation of the contamination and sources were conducted. In 1993 the
concentrations in the contaminated drinking water abstraction wells reached 100 µg/L,
giving rise to the need of an implementation of remedial measures to ensure the quality
and the continuation of the drinking water abstraction. The establishment of the reme-
dial measures was not eﬃcient as contamination was found in BA11 in 2001. BA11 was
removed from the drinking water abstraction without continuing as a remedial measure,
thus leaving BA14 and BA7A as the only active drinking water abstraction wells.

In 2012, it was decided to build a new water works and to implement activated carbon
ﬁlters, thereby allowing the inclusion of the contaminated wells. The water works is oper-

wells was performed. It was concluded that all the drinking water abstraction wells, ex-
cept BA13, were in such a bad condition that a replacement was needed. As a part of
the establishment of the new well ﬁeld, a 3D ﬂow model was created to assess the new
capture zones and best distribution of pumping rates.

2.2
Historical use of chlorinated solvents
Chlorinated solvents have been used in the area of Bagsværd for multiple purposes. The
business district holds many diﬀerent types of businesses, which uses chlorinated solvents.
There are many mechanical workshops in the business district that use chlorinated sol-
vents for the degreasing of diﬀerent metal components. Chlorinated solvents are very
eﬀective for the removal of oil components and other impurities. Spray painting work-
shops and auto repair shops use the chlorinated solvents daily. The most frequent source
for PCE is dry cleaning facilities where PCE is used for cleaning clothes. All the diﬀerent
sites where the chlorinated solvents have been used, are potential sources for contamina-
tion. Spills are known to occur thus creating potential sources. The exact amounts for
the spills are not quantiﬁed (TMB, 2003).
There are many gravel pits located in Bagsværd. The largest gravel pit is “Grusgraven”,
which translates to "The Gravel Pit" in English. Grusgraven is located in the business
district and spans from the main road Vadstrupvej all the way down to the road, Smør-
mosevej, covering about 15 % of the business district.

The gravel pits in the area were excavated for gravel and left behind large unused pits.
Grusgraven was later used as a landﬁll to deposit various types of waste from the busi-
nesses. The waste deposited was never registered, but it is assumed to consist of waste
from diﬀerent chemical substances such as containers for chlorinated solvents. Grusgraven
has waste deposited to a depth of 9 m. The majority of the depositing is assessed to have
happened in the period 1953 - 1955, but it can be assumed that the depositing has hap-
pened over a longer period. A large contamination of TCE was discovered at Grusgraven
in 1988, which lead to its registration as a landﬁll. Several investigations of the site have
been conducted. Despite ﬁndings of old drums containing chlorinated solvents, the site
never has been remediated.
The business district holds many potential point sources, but only a few of them are
known contaminated sites. There are four known contaminated sites that could pose a
risk for the drinking water abstraction. North of the business district are two known con-
taminated sites where there have been dry cleaning facilities. All known sources which
can pose a risk to the drinking water abstraction are shown in Figure 4.

2.3
Remediation in Bagsværd
In 1987 the ﬁrst three drinking water abstraction wells (BA5A, BA12 and BA13) were
changed to abstract for remedial purposes. The abstracted water was discharged into
Bagsværd Lake. Since contaminants from an unknown source was spreading to the drink-
ing water abstraction, a remedial measure was needed. The main contaminants found in
the abstracted drinking water were TCE and PCE. The contamination was believed to
origin from the business district where the chlorinated solvents are used. Measurements
validated the suspicion and indicated a large portion of the contamination to origin from
the gravel pit, Grusgraven. G3 was the ﬁrst remedial abstraction well drilled for remedial
purposes.G3 has a long screen placed in the limestone to protect the drinking water ab-
straction. Later J1 was established inside the gravel pit to abstract closer to the source.
When it was discovered that contamination still spread to the drinking water abstraction
wells, a third well (M6) was drilled in the sand. As the three remedial abstraction wells
were implemented, a remedial installation with activated carbon ﬁlters was placed to clean
the water from G3 and M6. Water from J1 is the only remedial abstraction water that
does not get ﬁltrated. The abstracted water is discharged into the sewer. A discharge
permit allows the water to have a sum of chlorinated solvents up to 5000 µg/L. The re-
medial installation was constructed in 1993 and named "Grusgraven" after the gravel pit
(CGKI, 2016).
9
==PAGE:26==
Despite the implementation of the remedial measure at Grusgraven was the concentra-
tion of TCE still increasing for the drinking water abstraction. Two new wells, AFV1
and AFV2, were constructed to improve the eﬃciency of the remedial measure. The new
wells were placed in the western part of the site to prevent contaminants ﬂowing West of
the remedial measure.A discharge of abstracted water to Bagsværd Lake was no longer
suitable as the abstracted water had high concentrations of chlorinated solvents. The two
new wells, INFA and INFB, were established to inﬁltrate the ﬁltrated abstracted reme-
dial water. All remedial abstraction wells should run at a constant pumping rate, but the
pumps have to be changed regularly due to clogging of precipitated iron. M6 is the most
problematic well as the pump has to be changed every 3rd month. The remedial installa-
tion "Grusgraven" has 12 wells that are used for monitoring the eﬀect of the remediation.
All monitoring wells are sampled yearly.As a result of the monitoring it was discovered
that one of the monitoring wells (GI-1) increased rapidly in concentration. When the
monitoring well reached 160 µg/L in 2016, it was included in the remedial pumping. The
current remedial installation is removing approximately 10 kg of chlorinated solvents per
year. The main compound removed is TCE (ANL and LHS, 2015a).

pumping rate resulting in a large removal of mass. BA13 is maintained by the water
supply company Novafos. It is not standard practice for the water supply company to op-
erate remedial measures, but the well is necessary as it captures most of the contaminants

The Capital Region sat up a remedial installation at Bagsværd Hovedgade in 2009. It

3
Geology
This chapter focuses on the various geological layers and their occurrence in Bagsværd.
Knowledge about the geological formations, helps conceptualising the geologic ﬁeld which
enhances the understanding of the geology in the area.
3.1
Sand and gravel layers
There are three large sand layers in Bagsværd. The layers are named with increasing
numbers according to the depth. The upper sand layer, Sand1, is a very silty sand layer
which is a gradual transition from the top soil. Sand1 varies in thickness and is for most
parts directly connected to the underlying sand layer. Sand1 is not continuous throughout
the area, in some places the underlying clay layer disrupts Sand1. The top of the layer is
following the surface and is located at 20 - 30 masl. (DVR90).
The second sand layer, Sand2, is the largest sand layer and continuous throughout the
area. T. Sand2 is a result of glacial deposition where sediments have been distributed
from the glacial movements during the ice age. Sand2 is mostly an extension of Sand1
and does not have a clear transition between the two layers. Sand2 is heterogeneous thus
having an uneven distribution throughout the layer. The distribution of grains follows a
pattern due to the glacial deposition. When the glacier melted the largest grains were
distributed ﬁrst, which leads to an increase in grain size with increasing depth. Sand2
holds large silt lenses where the layer is suddenly disrupted by a change in geological mate-
rial. These lenses are very local and are thereby hard to implement for a geological model.
Sand2 has a thickness varying from 10 - 30 m. Sand2 is the dominant layer in the area,
as it is the thickest of the geological layers. Sand2 is in direct contact with both the top
soils and the limestone. The top of Sand2 varies mostly in the latitudinal direction and
is located between 15 - 25 masl. (DVR90). The bottom of the layer is constant through
most parts of the area, the only place where the bottom diﬀers is near the lake where
the limestone is descending. The bottom of the layer is placed directly on the top the
limestone at 0 - -5 masl. (DVR90).
The distribution of the grains through Sand2 leads to a gravel layer at the bottom. The
gradual transition between layers makes it hard to outline the delineation. The gravel
layer has large grains which yields diﬀerent properties than the overlying sand layer.
Sand2 and the gravel layer are always connected as the gravel layer is the product of large
sand grains. As the transition from sand to gravel is gradual, the exact thickness of the
gravel can be hard to determine. The thickness of the gravel layer is determined to be
between 0 and 3 m.
Sand3 is the deepest of the sand layers. It is only present when Sand2 and the gravel layer
are not directly connected with the limestone. Sand3 is located beneath the conﬁning clay
layer, and is only present in the northeren part of Bagsværd.

3.2
Limestone layers
The limestone layers are a result of the glacial deposition of organic shells and plants.
Shells of various size are forming a cohering layer as they are exposed to high pressure.
The deposition of the diﬀerent shells is creating limestone with diﬀerent geological prop-
erties. The Danish limestone consist of the Danian limestone, which is deﬁned as the
limestone deposited in Denmark. The Danian limestone was formed in the Danian period
60 to 65 million years ago. The Danian period is the oldest period in the Paleocene period
(Vangkilde-Pedersen et al., 2011).
The Danian limestone can be split into multiple diﬀerent limestone layers. In the Copen-
hagen area the Copenhagen limestone is the upper limestone layer. The Copenhagen
limestone consists of three layers, the upper Copenhagen limestone, the middle Copen-
hagen limestone and the lower Copenhagen limestone. The upper Copenhagen limestone
is known to be crushed. Glacial movements have resulted in very high pressure applied
to the layer, which has ended up crushing it. The middle Copenhagen limestone is a
less fractured part of the limestone where the structure is holding together better than
the crushed layer on top of it. The last layer of the Copenhagen limestone is the lower
Copenhagen limestone. The layer is fractured like the upper Copenhagen limestone layer,
but it is not crushed. Many fractures in a solid structure makes the layer able to trans-
mit a large volume of water as the fracture structure is intact without the surrounding
limestone collapsing.
Beneath the Copenhagen limestone is the Bryozo limestone. The Bryozo limestone is
a limestone that consist of the moss animal Bryoza. Bryozo is the deepest of the Danian
limestone layers. The structure of the layer is solid and have large fracture systems where
fractures are connected. The fractures in the layer are primarily in the horizontal direc-
tion as they are formed due to high pressure relief where vertical fractures are a product
of tectonic movements. The Bryozo limestone does not have as many fractures as the
lower Copenhagen limestone, but the fracture system is more advanced and often spans
over larger areas.
Danian limestone is located on top of the chalk. The chalk was formed 65 to 71 mil-
lion years ago in the Maastrichtian period. The chalk consist of unicellular organisms
such as coccolithophores and Foraminiferas. The chalk is a uniform and dense layer as it
consists of small organisms. The chalk has very few fractures while the structure is soft
(Larsen. et al., 2012).
There are only three signiﬁcant limestone layers in Bagsværd, the lower Copenhagen
limestone, the Bryozo limestone and the chalk. The two other Copenhagen limestone
layers are missing due to the Carlsberg fault. The Carlsberg fault zone is located directly
beneath Bagsværd Lake and is the reason why the lake was formed. A fault is a rise in
tectonic plates due to vertical movements. The rise of the limestone formation created
a valley which was ﬁlled with glacial sand and gravel. The valley is known as Søndersø
valley. Figure 7 is an illustration of how the Carlsberg Fault aﬀects the geological layering.

The lower Copenhagen limestone layer is a continuous layer. The layer thickness decreases
in the northern part of Bagsværd. The thickness of the layer varies between 0 and 10 m.
The top of the layer is located between 2 and -5 masl. (DVR90).
The Bryozo limestone layer is considered as the main limestone layer in Bagsværd as
it is the thickest water bearing limestone layer. The Bryozo is continuous throughout
the area and has a thickness varying between 30 and 40 m. The bottom of the Bryozo
limestone layer is constant at -40 masl. (DVR90).
The chalk is located beneath the Bryozo limestone at -40 m in height reference DVR90.
The chalk is several hundred meters deep thus considered the bottom layer for the con-
ceptualising of the geologic ﬁeld.
3.3
Clay layers
There are two clay layers in Bagsværd. The clay layers are named with increasing numbers
according to the depth. The upper clay layer, Clay1, is the delimiting layer of Sand1.
Clay1 is discontinuous and has several disruptions throughout the area. Clay1 is not
present in the business district but present at variable thickness throughout the rest of
the area. The clay layer increases in thickness when near Bagsværd Lake and the forest
of Hareskoven. Clay1 has a thickness varying between 0 - 15 m. The top of the clay layer
is located between 20 and 30 masl. (DVR90).
Clay1 is a boulder clay which is a glacial deposit, where moraine materials are form-
ing a clay with various composition, consisting of a mixture of sand, gravel and clay.
Clay1 is a fractured clay. Fractures are formed when water seeps into the clay and satu-
rates the material. When the temperature decreases and the water freezes, water expands
creating vertical fractures in the otherwise solid material. As a boulder clay is a mixture
15
==PAGE:32==
of several sediments, lenses with sand or gravel occur, giving the clay a heterogeneous
structure where the conﬁning properties of the layer are aﬀected.
The deepest clay layer, Clay2, is also a boulder clay as a result from glacial deposition.
The deep clay layer does not have vertical fractures as it is not exposed to temperature
variations. Clay2 is discontinuous and is primarily present at the same places as the over-
lying clay layer. As for Clay1, Clay2 is not present at the business district in Bagsværd.
The thickness of the layer varies between 0 and 20 m, but is rarely thicker than 2 m. The
presence of the clay layer yields an underlying sand layer. The top of the clay is located
between 5 and -5 masl. (DVR90).
3.4
Geological distribution and transects
A conceptualisation of the geologic ﬁeld is formed with the use of bore logs for the con-
structed wells in Bagsværd. The conceptualisation consists of nine layers, the top soil, two
clay layers, three sand layers, one gravel layer and two limestone layers. For most parts
the sand layers are the dominant part with a coherent thickness of 30 m. The clay layers
are dominant in the North near Bagsværd Lake. The top soil represents the diﬀerent kinds
of soil that pose the top layer in Bagsværd, such as ﬁll or organic peat. Despite diﬀerent
properties for the top layers, the layers are considered as one. The conceptualisation can
be seen in Figure 8.
The geology has been modelled as a pancake model in GeoScene3D. The geology started
from the coarse Copenhagen model which was provided by Geo. The model was reﬁned
with the use of geologic information imported from GEUS’s (the National Geological In-
vestigations for Denmark and Greenland) Jupiter database. Jupiter holds approximately
1000 wells for Bagsværd which form the basis for the model interpretation. Geological
layers are formed with spatial interpolation between layer-deﬁning points. Additional in-
formation about the geology was added in the form of wells that are not registered in the
Jupiter database. The number of layers from the conceptual model are suﬃcient, as the
geological layering in the area is simple.

Hydrogeology
Bagsværd has a heterogeneous geology which results in aquifers with diﬀerent properties.
The Sand1 and Sand2 form the secondary aquifer, while the limestone layers represent the
primary aquifer. As the conﬁning clay layer is discontinuous, the secondary and primary
aquifer are hydraulically connected. The hydraulic connection between the layers results
in a combined water table.
4.1
Flow ﬁeld
To obtain information about the ﬂow ﬁeld in Bagsværd, the ﬂow direction must be deter-
mined. The water table is directly related to the geology of the aquifer and its hydraulic
parameters. The velocity of groundwater is dependent on multiple variables such as pres-
sure gradient, hydraulic conductivity and porosity.
The Sand1 is mostly unsaturated and contributes to the ﬂow with inﬁltration of pre-
cipitation. Sand2 is fully saturated and for most parts an unconﬁned aquifer. Clay is
often the aquitard conﬁning the sand aquifers. The sand aquifer is only conﬁned in the
western, northern and far eastern part of Bagsværd. Some parts of Bagsværd have a ter-
tiary aquifer located near the surface. The aquifer is the result of inﬁltrating precipitation
with an underlying aquitard, limiting the draining capacity.The tertiary aquifer is leaky
due to the fractures in the underlying clay layer.
The primary aquifer is much like the overlying sand aquifer with regards to aquifer con-
ditions. An aquitard is conﬁning the aquifer in the North. Unlike the secondary aquifer
the ﬂow in the primary aquifer depends on fracture sizes instead of the properties for
the porous medium. The primary aquifer consists of three diﬀerent limestone layers with
diﬀerent properties. The lower Copenhagen limestone is the ﬁrst occuring limestone layer
and is fractured. The fractures yield a high hydraulic conductivity, resulting in a high
ﬂow. The velocity in the fractures is dependent on the fracture width. The second lime-
stone layer is the Bryozo limestone. The fractures in the Bryozo limestone are diﬀerent
from the Copenhagen limestone as they are more complex often spanning over great dis-
tances. The Bryozo has fewer fractures than the Copenhagen limestone but are wider and
longer.
Flow in fractures is not comparable to ﬂow in porous media. Flow in fractures is de-
termined by the fracture direction and interconnection of the fractures.
Like ﬂow in
porous media, the ﬂow in fractures is moving from high pressure towards low pressure,
but the direction of the fracture determines the ﬂow direction and velocity as the gradient
is changed according to the tangent law (Pouya, 2011).
The ﬂow ﬁeld can be determined by hydraulic head measurements.
With the use of
measurements representing all of the area an ispotential map can be produced.
The
isopotential map shows isolines for the hydraulic potential thus yielding hydraulic gradi-
ents as the change in pressure with the change in distance. It is important that the wells
used for the measuring have screens placed in the same aquifer, as pressure could vary if
the aquifers are not hydraulically connected.

Hydraulic parameters
Pumping tests were conducted in connection with the construction of the new water works
and the establishment of new drinking water abstraction wells. The wells were tested with
various pumping rates for diﬀerent durations. The wells were tested with four diﬀerent
rates: 5 m3/hour for 1 hour, 10 m3/hour for 1 hour, 20 m3/hour for 1 day & 30 m3/hour
for 6 days.

conductivity when divided by the aquifer thickness in meters. Table 5 shows the tested

wells thus not having screens. The limestone wells were tested after cleansing to open the
fracture systems, thereby getting a more representative test.

hydraulic conductivities for a single geological layer. The hydraulic conductivities for the

5.2
Degradation
Degradation of the chlorinated compounds in Bagsværd is directly linked with the redox
conditions in the aquifers. TCE is degraded to cis 1,2-DCE through anaerobic reductive
dechlorination at Grusgraven due to manganese- and nitrate reducing conditions. The
TCE does not seem to degrade further than DCE as the redox conditions change to be
more aerobic in the North, while a degradation from DCE to vinyl chloride, in addition to
anaerobic conditions, requires the presence of the halorespiring bacteria Dehalococcoides
(Bjerg. et al., 2006).
1,1,1-TCA is degraded to both 1,1-DCA and 1,1-DCE. TCA is degraded to DCA through
anaerobic reductive dechlorination like TCE. The degradation to 1,1-DCE happens through
dehydrohalogenation, where a double bond is formed transforming the the chlorinated
ethane to a chlorinated ethene.
PCE is degraded to TCE at a low rate through anaerobic reductive dechlorination at
Bagsværd Hovedgade. The redox conditions in the aquifer at Bagsværd Hovedgade are
not as reduced as the conditions at Grusgraven.

6.2
Model construction
Comsol Multiphycis is chosen as software to model the groundwater ﬂow in Bagsværd.
The model is set up as a numerical model with ﬁnite elements. The groundwater ﬂow
equation shown in equation 3 is solved in Comsol Multiphysics. The modelled area is 1.6
km wide and 2.2 km long. Sources and sinks are added to the mass balance equation
inside Comsol Multiphysics.
6.2.1
Approach
The ﬁnal groundwater ﬂow model is achieved through gradual enhancement of complex-
ity, starting from a simple model 2D ﬂow model. The 2D ﬂow model is set up for the
sand and limestone aquifer. To increase the model complexity the top Dirichlet boundary
is set to follow Bagsværd Lake. By combining the two 2D models a simple two layer 3D
model is created. Bagsværd Lake is incorporated as the northern Dirichlet boundary for
the 3D model. A third layer is added to the simple 3D model thus increasing the model
complexity. The third layer is a two meter thick gravel layer between the sand and the
limestone layer.
40
==PAGE:57==
The last step for the construction of the geometry for the ﬂow model is the implementa-
tion of the advanced geology created in GeoScene3D. The advanced geology consists of
nine diﬀerent geological layers implemented as the volume between interpolated surfaces
in Comsol. Each geological layer is imported as 6141 x, y, z coordinates indicating the
placement of the top surface for the geological layer. The points are distributed equally in
the modelled area, 69 points in longitudinal direction and 89 in the latitudinal direction.
The points are used to create a surface through linear interpolation. The interpolation is
stored as a function and then implemented in the geometry as a parametric surface with
a relative tolerance of 1 × 10−7 and the use of 600 knots. The parametric surfaces are
used to partition the geometry thus forming the nine layers.
Gravel cylinders covering the screens of the wells are implemented in the model. The
gravel cylinders have a diameter of 1 m with the screen in the centre. the entire length
of the screen is covered by gravel with an additional meter of gravel above and below the
screen. The high hydraulic conductivity of the gravel cylinders allow ﬂow to distribute
according to the hydraulic conductivities.
Sand2 is featured as a single geological layer despite the variation of the hydraulic con-
ductivities in the layer. As earlier described Sand2 is the result of glacial deposition which
resulted in a spatial distribution based on grain sizes. This distribution is implemented
as a function where the hydraulic conductivity increases linearly down through the sand
layer. Figure 30 shows the spatial distribution of the hydraulic conductivities and the
function used to implement the distribution. Int5 and Int6 are interpolation functions
that return the z coordinate for the top and bottom of the layers. Z2 is the top of Sand2,
while Z1 is the bottom Sand2.

6.2.2
Assumptions
Assumptions for the groundwater ﬂow model are listed as follows:
• Steady state is assumed thus not accounting for variations in pumping rates
• All geological layers are assumed to be homogeneous
• Nine layers are included to describe the geology
• The geological distribution is assumed to follow a pancake model
• Inﬁltration is assumed to be uniform for all of the modelled area
• Flow in the limestone aquifers is assumed to behave as in a porous media aquifer
thus neglecting potential fracture systems
• Darcy’s law is assumed to be valid
• No-ﬂow boundaries are assumed present West and East of Bagsværd
• The Dirichlet boundaries are assumed to be the same for the sand and limestone
aquifer
• Flow deeper than -100 masl. (DVR90) is disregarded

7.3
Assumptions
Several assumptions are made for the contaminant transport model. Most assumptions
is formed due to the lack of available data. The assumptions is listed as follows:
• A reduction by a factor of 25 is assumed for the pumping rates to obtain the observed
isopotential distribution
• The used dispersiverties are estimated with no observations to support it
• The sorption coeﬃcient is assumed to be constant for all geological layers as the
mobility for TCE is high and no data were available
• A ﬂux from the source with a constant concentration is assumed
• Fractures are not included and the ﬂow is assumed to be as in a porous medium
• Inﬁltration from precipitation is assumed to be uniform and constant over time
• Pumping rates are assumed to be constant thus disregarding variations over time
• Degradation is disregarded assuming the contamination is conservative

Discussion
This chapter discusses the ﬁndings from the previous chapter. The eﬃciency of the reme-
diation for the diﬀerent scenarios is assessed and a recommendation is given on its basis.
In addition, the shortcomings and simpliﬁcations are addressed. A recommendation for
future investigations is listed to give perspectives on how to improve the future remedia-
tion and protect the drinking water.
The 2D ﬂow models formed capture zones dictated by the pumping rate to screen length
ratio. As the model is in two dimensions the pumping rate is presented as the abstraction
per meter of screen. BA13 is the dominant capture zone and covers most of Bagsværd as
BA13 has the shortest screen while abstracting with the same rate as the long-screened
drinking water abstraction wells, see Figure 33. The remediation at Grusgraven is not
covering all of the contaminated site thus not being eﬃcient. Simulated capture zones are
changed to abstract from a more southern direction when the Bagsværd Lake is added as
a Dirichlet boundary, see Figure 34.
When the two 2D ﬂow models are combined the simple 2-layer 3D ﬂow model is formed.
The simulated capture zones, shown in Figure 35, resembles the simulated capture zone
from the ﬂow model created by Rambøll in 2012, see Figure 21. The similarities of the
models are due to the two thick layers. Sand2 is represented as a 30 m thick layer while
the underlying limestone is 60 m thick. The simulated capture zones do not change sig-
niﬁcantly when the Dirichlet boundary for Bagsværd Lake is implemented. The remedial
abstraction at Grusgraven is shown to cover all of the site thus indicating an eﬃcient
remediation. None of the drinking water abstraction wells that abstracts high concentra-
tions are shown to abstract water from the known sources. This indicates that the capture
zones for the drinking water abstraction might be diﬀerent. The simulated capture zones
do not change when the gravel layer is added as the third layer for the 3D model. The
diﬀerences between the two model are small as the the thick sand layer has a relative
high hydraulic conductivity and the two aquifers are hydraulically connected. The dif-
ference in hydraulic conductivities is not large enough to initiate a signiﬁcant vertical ﬂow.
The implementation of the advanced geology is not aﬀecting the simulated capture zones
signiﬁcantly compared to the simple 3D model. The small diﬀerence is the result of the
homogeneity and thickness of Sand2. As the thickness of Sand2 is 30 m while being ho-
mogeneous is the abstraction from the aquifer similar to the abstraction from the sand
layer in the simple 3D model. The simulated capture zones are overlapping when the
linear distribution of the hydraulic conductivities is implemented. BA13 changes its sim-
ulated capture zone to abstract from a more southern direction while also abstracting
underneath the capture zones of the remedial abstraction at Grusgraven. The overlap
of the capture zone indicates that the remedial abstraction is not eﬃcient since BA13 is
abstracting water from the site. M6 changes from a very wide capture zone to a thin cap-
ture zone thus allowing BA111 to abstract water from Grusgraven. The changed capture
zones for the model are explaining the contamination found in BA111 and BA13, but the
contaminants found in BA112 is still explained. The calibration of the model leads to a
signiﬁcant changes for the simulated capture zones. All capture zones are changed to a
more southern direction. These capture zones could explain the contaminants found in
both BA13, BA111 and BA12, See Figure 37.

9.1
Model simpliﬁcations
The geology implemented in the ﬂow model assumes nine homogeneous geological layers
presented as a pancake model. When a layer is absent for a part of the area the thickness
is set as low as possible. The modelling software Comsol Multiphysics has troubles with
separating layers when the thickness of a geological layer is smaller than the accuracy of
the interpolation as the layers might intersect. To avoid intersecting layers a safety of 0.1
m is added to the thickness of all layers thereby making it more diﬃcult for vertical ﬂows
to occur.
When the model is set up it is based on available data. Bagsværd holds a lot of in-
formation about the geology for the business district but are lacking data for the North
and northwestern part of Bagsværd. There are only few wells in Bagsværd that are deep
enough to be in the limestone thus making a calibration for the limestone diﬃcult.
Groundwater recharge is implemented as the inﬁltration of precipitation in the model.
The model assumes the inﬁltration is uniform throughout the area. This assumption is
not considered a big uncertainty as the model is a steady state model thus not accounting
for seasonal variations in the inﬁltration.
Boundaries of the model seems to inﬂuence the capture zones. Remedial abstractions
are not aﬀected by the no-ﬂow boundaries in the East and West as they are located in the
centre of the model. The capture zones for the drinking water abstraction wells are inﬂu-
enced by the no-ﬂow boundaries since they cannot abstract water from the sides. When
the model is calibrated the inﬂuence of the no-ﬂow boundary decreases as the capture
zones changes to a more southern direction. The eﬀect of the no-ﬂow boundary before
and after calibration is shown in Figure 37.
75
==PAGE:92==
The Dirichlet boundary in the South is assumed to be straight, this assumption might
not be accurate as Figure 18 indicates. The largest error in the calibration is in the heads
in the range of 20.5 - 21.5 masl. (DVR90) which are located at the southern Dirich-
let boundary. If a more correct isopotential map is created a polygon could be used as
the Dirichlet boundary lowering the RMSE for the model. The assumption of a straight
southern Dirichlet boundary is not considered to have a big inﬂuence on the model as the
error for most calibration points in the range of 20 - 21.5 are smaller than 0.3 m.
Flow in the Bryozo limestone layer is known to be controlled mainly by fracture sys-
tems while it is assumed to be a porous media in this model. The assumption is aﬀecting
the capture zones for the drinking water abstraction wells and could lead to changes
for the capture zones. As there are little information available about the limestone in
Bagsværd is the assumption necessary for the model. The ﬂow logs show that there are
a level speciﬁc ﬂow variation in the Bryozo limestone. The ﬂow log for BA105, BA114
and BA107 shows that they take up most of their ﬂow from the Bryozo while the model
shows they are abstracting primarily from the lower Copenhagen limestone. The histor-
ical evolution of concentrations support the claim that BA105, BA107 and BA114 are
abstracting water from deeper fracture systems as they do not detect contaminants. The
plots of the concentrations can be seen in Appendix F.
All constructed groundwater ﬂow models are steady state.
The ﬂow ﬁeld is not in a
steady state as the pumping rates for all abstraction varies. Appendix H shows the total
yearly abstraction at Grusgraven and the abstracted amount are smaller than the ex-
pected volume if the pumping rates were constant. Figure 20 shows the variation of the
water table in both the primary and secondary aquifer. The variation in the water table
is a clear indication of the variations in the pumping rates. The simpliﬁcation of creating
solely steady state models is considered acceptable as seasonal variations is disregarded.
9.2
Recommendations
When choosing the remedial measures for the abstraction of chlorinated solvents in
Bagsværd it is important to get the priorities straight. It is recommended that the reme-
dial measures should focus on limiting the contamination of the drinking water abstraction
wells instead of focusing on the removal of mass at the sources.
If no new wells should be implemented in the remedial measure, is Scenario I.1 a good
possible solution to increase the eﬃciency. An increase of the pumping rates for the cur-
rent remedial abstraction wells is recommended instead of including existing wells to the
remedial abstraction. The total increase in abstracted water at Grusgraven would yield a
need of an increased capacity for the remedial installation. The instability of the pumping
rates would most likely result in lower operational pumping rates.
The inclusion of at least two new wells is found to ensure the quality of the abstracted
drinking water in Bagsværd. NWWest and NWMid is considered to be of highest im-
portance as their implementation would decrease signiﬁcantly the contamination taken
up by BA111 and BA112. NWEast is not needed as contamination in BA105 is still low
and the focus should be on ensuring the quality of the drinking water and not removing
contamination from the sources.

Recommendations for future investigations
This study gives an overview of ﬂow and eﬀects for remedial abstraction in Bagsværd
based on the assessment for multiple ﬂow models. The assumptions made and short-
comings found for this study helps outlining the investigations needed to obtain an even
more precise prediction and assessment of the eﬃciency of the remedial measures and the
transport of the contamination. Future investigations should be performed with the goal
to:
1. Obtain a better understanding of spreading of the contamination through investi-

(b) Outline the expected residual sources to assess the extent of the contamination

(a) Investigate the concentrations West and East of the remedial measure at Grus-

(b) Re-measure the surface elevation at the wells in Bagsværd to obtain a higher
quality of the hydraulic head measurements

Conclusion
Groundwater ﬂow and transport of contamination in Bagsværd were evaluated in this
study to assess the eﬃciency of the remedial pumping near Bagsværd well ﬁeld.
Available geological data formed the basis for a new geological interpretation described
with nine homogeneous layers. A new gravel layer was introduced through analysis of
borelog descriptions for the the bottom glacial sand layer. Linearization of the hydraulic
conductivities for Sand2 was implemented through the understanding of glacial deposi-
tion. The implementation leads to a more complex and descriptive geological model. A
discretization of the limestone layers was introduced to distinguish the ﬂow for the diﬀer-
ent layers. Available ﬂow logs provided information about the higher conductive limestone
layer on the top, while indicating ﬂow through fracture systems for the deeper limestone
layers. The general understanding of the geology in Bagsværd increased signiﬁcantly.
A better hydrogeological understanding was achieved by the analysis of several historical
isopotential maps. A new interpretation of the hydraulic head distribution was intro-
duced to enhance the understanding of the ﬂow in Bagsværd. A northern ﬂow direction
was found with local drawdowns at the remedial measure at Grusgraven and the drinking
water abstraction wells.
New water samples combined with analysis of available data yielded a better under-
standing of the distribution of the contamination in Bagsværd.
The PCE and TCE
plumes were delineated through interpolation. High PCE concentrations were found at
the known sources Bindeledet 9A, Bagsværd Hovedgade 79 and Aldershvilevej. TCE is
mainly found at Grusgraven where it is found in both Sand2 and limestone aquifers.
The interpolation of level speciﬁc concentrations for the North-West transect concep-
tualised the contaminant transport giving an understanding of how the contamination
escapes the remedial measures and contaminates the abstracted drinking water.
A new and more complex steady state groundwater ﬂow model was created yielding a
RMSE of 0.33 m which indicates a good ﬁt. The new ﬂow model improved the capture
zones for the abstractions which helps explain the contamination found in the drinking
water abstraction wells. BA13 changed its capture zone to a more southern direction.
The change in simulated capture zones indicates that abstracted contaminants possibly
origins from the known source at Brudelysvej 3.
The new and optimised model was used to assess the eﬃciency of the existing reme-
dial measures. The ﬂow model for the current scenario showed that particles escaped
the remedial measures at Grusgraven thus not being suﬃcient to protect the abstracted
drinking water. When implemented in the 2D contaminant transport model, was it shown
that M6 is the most important remedial abstraction well. When M6 was excluded from
the remedial measures, the contaminant mass uptake by BA111 increased 379 %.
Three new remedial pumping strategies were assessed, where capture zones and parti-
cle tracking were obtained with the ﬂow model while its eﬃciency was quantiﬁed by the
contaminant transport model through contaminant mass uptake in the drinking water
78
==PAGE:95==
abstraction well BA111. Scenario I had altered pumping rates for the existing remedial
abstraction wells. Scenario II includes three existing wells in the current remedial abstrac-
tion. Scenario III includes three new wells with optimal placement and screen length.
Scenario I showed that most particles from the known sources are abstracted. The in-
crease in BA13’s pumping rate is additionally securing that contamination escaping the
remedial measures is abstracted before reaching the drinking water abstraction. When
implemented in the contaminant transport model the contaminant uptake in BA111 is
decreased with 44 % compared to the current scenario. Scenario II shows to abstract
most contaminant like Scenario I, but the contaminant transport model shows that the
contaminant uptake in BA111 only decreased with 36 % compared to the current scenario.
Scenario III shows that contaminants from the known sources are escaping the remedial
abstraction at Grusgraven but is abstracted by the newly implemented well NWMid and
BA13 before reaching the drinking water abstraction. When Scenario III is implemented
in the contaminant transport model a decrease of 95 % for the contaminant uptake in
BA111 was found, compared to the current scenario.
Simulations indicated that Scenario III was the most eﬃcient remedial scenario. Only
two of the three remedial abstraction wells in scenario III, NWWest & NWMid, indi-
cated an increased eﬃciency. The implementation of the third remedial abstraction well,
NWEast, was not found to be of signiﬁcant importance. Further investigations of the
distribution of the contamination are recommended along with the investigation of the
hydrogeological parameters in order to obtain a lower uncertainty and thus a better pre-
diction for the ﬂow- and contaminant transport model. An implementation of the found
remedial strategy could help protect the quality of the abstracted drinking water and
eventually eliminate the need for activated carbon ﬁlters.
