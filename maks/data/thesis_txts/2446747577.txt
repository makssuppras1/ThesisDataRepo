Abstract
The thesis is about analyzing cyber security level in the transportation systems, speciﬁcally installed
in maritime ships. The M.Sc. project contributes to the project CyberShip, which run for 2 years. The
thesis was supervised by professor Christian D. Jensen together with two PostDocs Rishikesh Sahay
and Daniel Alberto Sepulveda Estay. Additionally, six companies co-operated within the project. In
this thesis are placed ﬁndings of:
• Current cyber threats and why they even exist.
• How viruses are working and how much ﬁnancial damages they cause.
• Size of the problem with cybersecurity in maritime and ships.
• Why the maritime is vulnerable and reasons why hackers are and will attack it.
• Description of pirates and how they operate.
• Incidents of cyber attacks in maritime.
• Reactions of IMO, SOLAS, UN, EU, US to arising cyber threats.
• General Risk Management strategy description and Risk Assessment Methods which are suitable
for such analysis.
• Model of the ship.
• Analysis, results, and discussion of each component by using the Asset Based Risk method.
• Presentation cyber security awareness among people working in maritime.
Result of the analysis shows that the ships on average are really badly protected. It is possible to attack
almost every single system or component on the ship and some systems placed ashore with successful for
the hackers’ result. Decisive steps must be taken as soon as possible in order to improve cybersecurity,
otherwise, the number of compromised systems will grow.

Reasons why the IT technologies were/are vulnerable
Question from the title of this section seems to be straightforward. Unfortunately, the answer is
quite complex. First fully programmable computer was developed in 1942 called Electronic Numerical

Integrator and Calculator (ENIAC) [33]. A couple of years later in 1949 John von Neumann, as probably
the ﬁrst man, has done theoretical work about self-replicating programs, which might have a potentially
negative impact, allowing to execute malicious operations. Around that time he was only giving lectures
about this problem in classes called ”Theory and Organization of Complicated Automata”. Finally, in
1966 his work was published known as ”Theory of Self-Reproducing Automata” [34].
It is important to state that vulnerabilities in the context of computers are seen as leftover unintended
or indented errors in hardware or software while developing process. These errors allow to exploit the
ﬁnal product in unnoticed and often in a malicious way. Even though there are companies on the market
which focuses on discovering, patching and protecting against vulnerabilities, the total amount of them
seems to rise every year. There is at least couple of reasons for it [35] [36] [37] [38] [39]:
• Money - Around this factor the whole world operates. Due to a classical business model, companies
do the least possible work to achieve possibly the biggest proﬁt. Because of it, security in IT is
treated as an unwanted obligation which needs to be done only to extend that is absolutely
necessary. Very often security is not improved in companies as long as nothing bad happens.
Moreover, often due to limited resources, teams of developers focus on new functionality ﬁrst and
the security itself become the only afterthought.
• Time - Programmers often work on the edge of deadlines being constantly pushed to release more
and more code and reach demanded goals. It is very easy to make unintended security ﬂaws.
• Unsecured underlying infrastructure - Backbone of the internet and operating systems were not
built with security in mind. Requirements back then for operation and connectivity were not
including such thing as security.
At that point in time (1950-1980) engineers, managers and
politicians were convinced that computers will be used only for the army, government, and uni-
versities needs. As example two well known inaccurate predictions [40]:
– ”I think there is a world market for maybe ﬁve computers.” Thomas Watson, president of
IBM, 1943.
– ”There is no reason anyone would want a computer in their home.” Ken Olsen, founder of
Digital Equipment Corporation, 1977
By default, Internet Protocol (IP) provide end to end connection and no built-in security. As the
next step, SSL/TLS and VPN were invented as purpose to secure to some degree ﬂowing data
inside the internet.
• Complexity - The computers and the internet each on their own are very complex creatures. Both
works in the multi-layer hierarchy of software. In other words, there are many moving parts, built
as separate mechanisms communicating through various interfaces but at the same time depended
on each other. It means that it is very easy to screw things up.
• Development - Big competition, constant customer’s demand for better products, creates a prob-
lem, where new solutions very often are not compatible with the previous generation of products
either hardware and software wise. Moreover, customers expect that new products will not only
provide new functionality but as well will work with whatever they use already. This creates a
big problem. The companies do what they can, in order to accomplish that demand... at least
to some possible extent. Unfortunately often it is occupied by fast ﬁxes or bypassing some of the
mechanisms, which may be safe time and money, but unfortunately exposes them to vulnerability.
In the same time increasing complexity.
• Changing paradigms of security - The internet traﬃc has changed drastically over the years, from
”north-south” (client-server) type to ”west-east”(server-server) type. It means that much more
traﬃc congestion nowadays ﬂows within networks and between servers or data centers.
In the old days, the central point of ﬁltering in some data center was done through one big bulk
ﬁrewall with tens and hundreds of policy rules.
Well deﬁned Demilitarized Zone (DMZ) was

As a consequence of that, it might happen that after tens of such alerts nobody will react, or

security systems even though the system reacted correctly.
On the other hand, false negative actions present situation when the alarm is not raised when the
attack is performed. Simply it means that the system is vulnerable.
Either way, when the attacker will get access to the system, the game is over. Such a bad actor
might learn how the system operates and make further damages without notice.
• Indented errors - There are situations were bad people leave back-doors to the software inten-

2.6
Reasons why the hackers are targeting the shipping industry
This thesis would not have sens, if there would not be a reason to hack these systems. The hackers
are clever people, sometimes group of people. They would not be interested in penetrating maritime

[93] [94] [95] like:
• Growing number of interconnected systems. It means that bigger number of systems implemented

• Blindness and fear to realize problem by the maritime industry.
According to the way how
maritime companies behave at the moment, seems that cybersecurity is not treated seriously and
awareness is low. That leads to situations like having embarrassingly easy to hack passwords for

• Money - this is a big reason. As mentioned around 90% of the world’s trade occurs at the oceans.
It means that transfers of money in this part of the economy are insanely big. Money transfers
are often counted in millions of US dollars on a daily bases. According to sources the hackers
steal on an annual basis on average of 6.7 million US dollars and this number might rise soon.
• As mentioned in Section 2.5 reason might be as well old, outdated hardware and software, that is
an easy target even for script kiddies (an unskilled person who uses scripts or programs developed
by others in order to perform some attacks).
• Slow reactions - Based on experience (in the period of this project), research, stories told be
people working in this the maritime industry, it is clearly visible, that companies are super slow
in decisions, reactions and are not open for changes. In the next Section 2.8, are placed maritime
cyber attacks examples, showing that often companies react after months or years after an attack
occurred unless it disrupts it’s daily operations or signiﬁcantly hit their ﬁnance. Moreover, it
might happen that if the attacker is clever and silent enough, his malicious actions will last longer
without notice.
• Causing chaos might another reason. Some terrorists, activists or simply maritime competitors
would like to break the reputation of the other company. It is quite clear that the company which
cannot meet delivery deadlines, has regular breakdowns, are a victim of multiple cyber attacks or
often has dangerous situations like in the worst case ship collisions, for sure can lose credibility,

never been higher.

itime cybersecurity. Lots of things have to be ﬁxed, introducing well-known standards like for example
aviation did. On top of that, the United Nations (UN) support IMO and SOLAS initiatives as well.

serves a purpose to be interactive knowledge platform for transport and logistics, quite big part of it is
maritime.

on experience there is a lack of urgency to get things in order. Mentioned updates of ISPS and ISM
will get enforced in 2021, seems until then it is not mandatory to do anything by ship owners.

Types of attacks need to be shortly explained:
• Brute force - Attack done by trying all possible options. In case of cracking a password, this attack
boils down to blindly trying every single password in hope ﬁnally the correct is found. There are
tools which speed up this process, so it is not as stupid as it seems.
• Port Scanning - Hacker scans targeted network, the aim is to ﬁnd out all connected devices
and what services/ports are opened.
Based on basic communication within the network, the
hacker can get a quite accurate overview what devices are running, what operating systems are
installed on the devices, what software/services are installed with access to the network. After
test communication with speciﬁc devices, more information can be deduced. The next step is to
ﬁnd out vulnerabilities and create/use viruses capable of exploiting bugs on such a device.
• Subverting the supply chain - This type of attack is about getting access to the company or ship
through compromising software/hardware/products delivered by third-party companies.
• Phishing - Sending messages, usually through e-mail, which tries to trick the reader into sharing
credentials or other sensitive data without knowing that this is fake. Such attacks are more and
more sophisticated, often faking whole websites or other services which helps the attacker to steal
data. Usually, such attacks are untargeted, send to many end-users.
• Spear-Phishing - Is similar to phishing, but targeted to a speciﬁc person. Usually done through
e-mail, but because it is a targeted type of attack, the hacker knows much more about the victim.
The whole action is much more advanced.
• Denial of Service - Is the type of attack where the attacker ﬂoods the service with a huge amount
of request so that the service becomes unreachable for legitimate users.
• Penetration tests - These test are legal, fully controlled made on purpose and under full approve-
ment. Through these tests, it is possible to ﬁnd vulnerabilities and patch them.
• Social Engineering - These attacks are done in a non-technical way. The strategy is based on
tricking people into bypassing security procedures and therefore give access credentials to the
hacker.
• Water Holding - This type of attack works in a way, that the hacker observes the victim or group
of people, in order to ﬁnd out what other services, websites, products they use. Then such hacker
attacks these services for example with malware. The idea is that sooner or later target person

28

or group will be infected as well by using hacked services which they use on a regular basis. This
attack is not direct and involves other actors/servers/services.
• Malware - It is malicious software designed to damage computer or steals data like all private
ﬁles, passwords, email, geolocation, eavesdropping etc. without knowledge of the owner. There
are many types of such software like these described below worms, spyware, trojans, ransomware.
Each of these types has some special characteristic of action but it is important to point out that
such viruses might have combined functionalities across diﬀerent types.
• Worms - This type of virus as the main target has own replication and fastest possible spread
across all available machines. If the worm is advanced it might cause additional damages like
deleting ﬁles or injecting other types of viruses into the compromised computer.
• Spyware - The main target of such software is to spy on the user and steal as many private

scanning is not helping.
• Trojans - This type of software works in a way that it pretends to be harmless tools.
Often

actually bad things like capturing all passwords and secretly send it to the hacker.
• Ransomware - Such an attack is based on breaking into a targeted device or devices and encrypting
disks. Next step of such an attack is to demand from the user to pay a ransom. Grate example
is NotPetya.

3.2.1
Fault Tree analysis
According to [137], [138], [139] Bell Laboratories as ﬁrst presented fault tree analysis. Additionally,
it is one of the most widely used method due to its reliability. This method through its deductive
style allows to determine various failure combinations, which leads to the main considered system error.
It is possible to ﬁnd out all causes of particular system error, moreover, it is possible to evaluate
the probability of the considered top event by statistical or analytical methods. Executing fault tree
analysis starts from deﬁning a general conclusion or system error (top event). Then by constructing a
logic diagram tree, the causes of the higher level conclusion should be determined. Logic tree is build
up out of logic gates presented in Figure 3.5. There are gate symbols and event symbols. Two basics
types in use are ”AND” and ”OR” gates. For ”AND” gates the output will be true only if two inputs
will occur simultaneously. The ”OR” gate has output true even if only one input is true.In the ﬁgure
letters correspond to ”T” - true and ”F” - false.
In the Figure 3.6 is presented an example of such a fault tree. This fault tree is based on hospital
surgery risks. Presented ”AND” gate where the output is called ”Electrical shock” appears only if two
other factors occur in the same time, that is ”Patient grounded” and ”Pathway to current source”. Place
where the output is called ”Hazard to patient” has ”OR” gate where any of occurring inputs like ”Blood
damage”, ”Electrical shock”, ”Vacuum to bloodstream, ”Thromboembolism” will give a ”true” output,
which is a hazard to the patient.
Translating this to maritime terms may with such example:
• ”AND” gate, with the top event, is ”Changing ships course unnoticed”.
• The inputs of that gate are ”Spooﬁng the GPS from outside”, ”Corrupted ECDIS system display-
ing swapped values looking like correct once”, ”Oversight of responsible crew member”.
• An assumption can be considered that rest of the navigation devices like electronic compass due
to some reason might not work at the moment.
Having all three inputs simultaneously, as a result, the ship might start changing heading direction
without notice, an additional factor could be performing this attack at night where visibility form the
bridge is weak. Of course, this is only an example given for a better understanding of the method.
Fault Tree Analysis help to determine in a graphical way, many potential scenarios and threats.
Making more complex structures can lead to ﬁnding out those elements which repeat the most in other
scenarios, and that can lead to more accurate analysis, better security as a result.

systems in a proper way.
• Professor and former director of Information Security Group, Keith Martin [30] says that there
might be even 50,000 ships vulnerable out of 90,715 used as tankers, cargo, passenger, service,

should start protecting them self much better before viruses like NotPetya will attack.
• All other attacks described in mentioned Section 2.8 brings more light to the scale of attacks.

• Loss of integrity, data relating to the safety might be destroyed.

• Loss of availability and control over the vessel.

NMEA 2000 protocol
This communication standard is a successor of NMEA0183. Other common names on this protocol
are ”N2K” or”NMEA2k”. The NMEA2000 is based on a well known industrial protocol called Controller
Area Network (CAN), widely used for example in the automotive industry. It means that instead many
cables which NMEA0183 required in this case only one ”backbone” cable is used and consist of [198]
[201] [202] [203] [204]:
• Two lines are data cables CAN_High and CAN_low.
• Ground cable.
• Two power cables for power +12V and -12V.
Comparing to NMEA0183, this one allows having multiple talkers and multiple listeners. Equipment
connection looks as presented in Figure 4.12. It is much compact and clear compared to the predecessor.
CAN is designed to provide time-sensitive and fault tolerant functionalities.
==PAGE:83==

NMEA2000 consist of couple communication layers similar to classical Open Systems Interconnection
model (OSI) model [194] [205]:
• The Physical layer - electrical and mechanical standards.
• Data Link layer - Media Access Control (MAC) for network access protocol, timing, error detec-
tion.
• Network layer - seems to be deﬁned by ISO 11783-5 for routing in the network.
• Application layer - caring actual data to transfer, usually, it is a proprietary solution
According to sources, the ﬁrst three layers form the list are the same as in CAN network.
Along
with this research, it was not found solid information about encryption for CAN, except this paper,

widely implemented feature. Nevertheless there this brings a chance for better security. The most
likely problem is due to time sensitivity, in CAN network communication has to have smallest possible
delays and perform as close as possible to full synchronization between devices in the same time without
having one speciﬁc master clock device to which other devices would synchronize their work. This is
a totally separate big topic, at this point not the part of this thesis. Lack of encryption is only part
of the problem in regards to NMEA2000/CAN security [207] [208] [209] [210] [211] [212] [213]. The
study shows that 50% of found vulnerabilities can be considered as "critical" and 71% of vulnerabilities
found can be categorized as "medium" in relation to the likelihood of them happening. A couple of
vulnerabilities found in CAN (therefore in NMEA2000 as well) are the following:
• Easy to perform DoS attack which leads to loss of availability to devices.
• Unprotected header messages (as far as understood in Data Link layer) by CRC check, means
that signal corrupted bits can cause the CRC checker to look in wrong place in the message.
• Faults in acknowledgment mechanism, it is possible to produce a scenario where some nodes will
get two copies of ”ack” messages considering second such message as independent than a copy or
re-transmission while other nodes do not get ”ack” at all. It is an interesting problem since CAN
is working as a broadcasting type network. It has to be analyzed further.

Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS)
Satellite Navigation in maritime is a convenient and desirable tool. Many papers has been created
describing in great detail all aspects of GNSS [228] [229] [230] [231] [232] [233] [234] [235]. These systems
are very widely used in many places. Precise localization of a vessel helps with many problems and
various process optimization.
The GNSS on its own is a big topic, which includes lots of physical
properties.
In this section are placed information about the general operation, attacks, and some
countermeasures.
Most satellite receivers do not get any secure data except high-frequency signal from the satellite
with atomic precise time. Positioning is done on the receiver side by an applied couple of mathematical
methods like:
• Static method
• Kinematic method
• Pseudo-kinematic method
• Diﬀerential measures method
• Real Time Kinematic (RTK)
• Real Time Network (RTN)
Along with these methods it is important to take into account various error measurements aﬀected by
natural sources like for example changing the speed of signal due to changing the density of electrons
in the ionosphere. There is a couple of these natural eﬀects which aﬀects the satellite signals, therefore
localization is aﬀected too. Except for natural sources of interference, are these unintended coming
from other radio transmitters. Finally, the third type of signal interference is intentional attacks and
these can be divided on:
• Spooﬁng - As presented in Figure 4.23 principle in this type of attack is to create fake satellite
signals appearing as multiple satellites which will convince the GNSS receiver to be real once. As
a result GNSS receiver would calculate the wrong position. For civilian usage, signals coming
from the satellites are not encrypted called C/A-code. The military uses special encrypted signals
called M-code or Y-code. There is a couple of methods of spooﬁng:
– Simplistic spooﬁng
– Intermediate spooﬁng
– Sophisticated spooﬁng

track of correct path like, speed and log device, gyro cope compass, magnetic compass.

attack is more complicated and requires high-quality radio devices. The method itself involves
quite a lot of work as well.

4.4.1
Engine Governor System
The engine system is a vital part of the ship. No engine, no transportation, no electricity. The
engine is probably one of the most expensive component on the ship. Figure 4.27 presents example
schematics of engine control. In the ﬁgure are placed such mechanical parts like air ﬁlter, gas Mixer,
gas Supply, Turbo Compressor, Inter Cooler, and the engine itself. All of these are controlled by two
controllers.
The ﬁrst controller presented to the left on the picture ”Koock Control Ariadne” is responsible for
valve timing (open/close at correct moment) ignition timing, amount of fuel injected, amount of air
injected. Controller adjust these properties depends on the fuel quality, it’s octane/cetane value (how
much the certain amount of fuel can be compressed before autoignition), amount of air which can get to
the engine, the temperature of the air, amount of fuel which can get to the engine , speed of engine. It
is done to avoid ”knocking” which is fuel auto-ignition before the piston gets to the optimum position.
Another reason to use this controller is to get the best possible eﬃciency of the engine.
The second controller, placed in the middle of the ﬁgure ”Dual Fuel Controller”, is responsible for
general engine control. For example, it controls engine speed, controls which type of fuel is injected,
has abilities to enable emergency shut down or send diagnostic data to the other systems placed in the
bridge or to the server like in this case Marorka, Sertica software. Communication with the rest of the
ship’s system is done through the NMEA network. As far as understood, the engine has own inner
CAN network, marked in the ﬁgure by the purple line. This would have to conﬁrmed in further analysis.
Additionally, it is presented that that controller has two other connections: Diagnose Communication
and Modbus (Operating interface). Probably one of these extra connections is available at the Engine
Room Control (ERC).
As presented in Section 4.1.1 NMEA is totally plain text network with many potential vulnerabilities
to exploit. If the hacker has access to NMEA network, then he can manipulate ”Dual Fuel Controller”
without hacking anything further.
Even if CAN network presented by purple lines in the ﬁgure is
independent form NMEA ship’s network, it is not a problem as well since NMEA and CAN are very
similar, the hacking process would be very the same.
Tweaking properties of the engine can lead to many diﬀerent problems:
• First problem could be that the hacker would compromise the injecting system, therefore engine
could get more fuel than it should so the ship’s fuel eﬃciency drops. Maybe due to this, the ship
would run out the fuel couple of hundred kilometers on the sea, before it reaches destination port.
• Another scenario could be that hacker would compromise and edit ”knocking controller” so that
the slight knocking would appear therefore create negative forces inside the engine, which in long
run would damage it faster.
• Probably in some way information about oil pressure which ”dual fuel controller” has to be sent
to the oil lube system. Hypothetically it is possible to hack these messages so that the oil lube
system would provide less oil to the engine, therefore at some pointed engine would be stalled.
• Interesting point is a turbo compressor. Its duty is to compress incoming air in order to get more
power out of the engine and better general eﬃciency. Turbo compressor runs by usage of engine’s
exhaust fumes. So higher engines RPM means more exhaust fumes, which means a bigger speed
of turbo compressor. It is possible to control how much air gets to the engine as well. A more
interesting case is that the turbo compressor is lubed by the oil from the engine, at least this is how
it works in cars. This has to be investigated deeper. Anyway, turbo compressor needs a noticeable
amount of oil, not only to lube its rotors bearings but as well to keep optimum temperature. If
the sealants will break then the oil will get to the air intake and then straight to the engine
cylinders where is burnt. This creates cascading reaction and the engine gets out of control, goes
to maximum RPMs as long as the oil from damaged turbo compressor is delivered. This can lead
to the engine explosion or pistons welding to the cylinder wall due to high temperature. This can
happen due to omitting maintenance check of the turbo compressor but PROBABLY can happen
due to malicious, precise conﬁguration of the turbo and engine properties as well. This would

Stabilizers
The stabilizers are devices which in principle should reduce swaying of the hull on the sea. It is
possible to distinguish a couple of types like:
• Dynamic ﬁns stabilizers presented in Figure 4.32 and Figure 4.33. These are actively changing
the angle of the ﬁn, depends on the direction of the waves on both sides of the hull.
• Fixed ﬁn stabilizer presented in Figure 4.34. Nothing special just ﬁxed positioned ﬁns.
• Active tank stabilizer presented in Figure 4.35. According to the source [245], this system should
work in a way that incoming wave from the side of the hull automatically ﬁlls tanks on the side
with help of powerful pumps while on the other side of the hull tanks are emptied.
• Gyroscope stabilizer presented in Figure 4.36. Gyroscopic force is helpful for ship stabilizers and
it is a quite famous solution. The ﬁgure presents the general principle of work. The gyroscope
dynamically responds to the waves which are ﬂowing around the hull, as result ship’s tilting is

4.5
Cargo management systems
Cargo management is made for an easier, faster and more reliable way of controlling and tracking
cargo. It consists of multiple smaller systems which cooperate together. Depends on a ship they can be
more or less automatized. Often the status of cargo is synchronized over the internet. The Section 3.3
presents list of these systems. Usually on the ship is reserved dedicated place like Cargo Control Room
(it can be just a placed in the bridge as well) and computer which is responsible via dedicated software

Crane Equipment and Main Ballast System. According to the sources usually, the crane is not connected
to any ship’s network, managed directly by the worker via dedicated independent controllers. However,
the Main Ballast system is quite heavily integrated into the ship’s infrastructure. Components of the
Main Ballast system are the following:
• Level indication system - As far as understood this system measures the total load of the ship.
• Valve Control System - Valves which can be open for allowing water to ﬁll ballast tanks.
• Main Bilge System [191]- This system helps with pumping out the excess of water and sludge
from spaces like cargo, rooms below the main deck, engine room, other machinery spaces.
• Water ingress alarm system - as far as understood the system controls how much total amount of
water is inside the ship or controls what is the ship level deepness in regards to sea level.

Printers
These devices are used almost in every oﬃce around the globe. Employees print various documents,
often these which include personal data or quite important data to the companies like invoices with
bank account numbers with amounts transferred. Generally, speaking printers seems to be quite simple
devices, nobody pays much attention to them as long as they do their job correctly. Omitting physical
security problem by leaving printed documents on a tray, there are plenty of issues which comes along
with printers connected to the network.
• Often printers are the oldest devices working in companies network, Which means that some
printers might be left without further support form supplier.
• Their placement in the network might be close to the critical systems.
• Firmware of printers is rather rarely updated, which might lead to exposing vulnerabilities.
• Often protocols used between end user’s computer and printer are not encrypted. There is plenty
of printing protocols like LPR/LPD, "Raw" TCP/IP printing, IPP, WSD. It is important to ﬁnd
out which one is used and then ﬁnd out vulnerabilities.
• Most of the printers are not integrated within one bigger administrative scheme like Active Direc-
tory which means management of login and passwords has to be done manually. Old passwords
might be an easy target to break.
• Passwords stored in the printer might be poorly secured like hash MD5 only. It might happen
that inside the printer’s software some administration passwords are embedded as a leftover after
development.
As presented there is a couple of things to consider while implementing printers into the broader
network. Often such printer might be an easy target to propagate onto other devices in the network by
compromising often quite weak security mechanisms. If the attacker will not be able to break into the
printer, but communication with the printer is not encrypted then any way such attacker can intercept
documents which should be printed [255] [256] [257] [258].
Based on the sources, own knowledge and experience, estimation of risk looks as follows and it is
assumed that:
• Lack of Active Directory (AD) support included for printers, therefore weak login and password.
• Stored login and passwords on a printer might have rather poor security level (might hash only)
• Old ﬁrmware might be without further support form supplier.
• No encryption while regular communication, except web-interface when login then probably some

4.7.5
E-mail
E-mail is a fundamental way of information exchange in maritime industry. There are many diﬀerent
products/solution in use like Mark5, Dualog, regular Outlook with Microsoft Exchange servers, Google,
etc. Travel process of every e-mail message has a couple of stages.
The ﬁrst stage is to send a message from the user’s computer to the e-mail server. This connection
usually is done through protocols like POP3, IMAP. These protocols are not encrypted in any way
by default. For regular programs like Outlook, it is not mandatory to use any encryption. However,
the encryption option might be implemented in these e-mail clients applications. When the user uses
e-mail account through web page then often it is done through HTTPS, so TLS encryption is used. It
is important to pay attention to these small diﬀerences.
As long as e-mails are exchanged internally within the same e-mail server, then there is no problem
with further message exchange. Unfortunately often it might happen, if e-mail server has to send a
message outside, to the other e-mail server through the internet, then it uses simple SMTP protocol.

possible to encrypt such traﬃc, but it must be conﬁgured in advance by e-mail servers. Usually, regular

On crew’s computer is installed anti-virus which might scan USB
devices before mounting and using them.
The ﬁrst approach is that companies have on a vessel dedicated computer with antivirus and every
USB pendrive must be scanned ﬁrst by this antivirus, then it can be used with other devices on a vessel.
Unfortunately, this solution does not prevent anybody to skip scanning and place USB directly to the
ship’s equipment.

USB devices with antivirus as well. Additionally, if the USB device is free of viruses, this security device
signs this USB as secure and add it to the vessels database. When such a USB device is inserted to the
other devices on board, at ﬁrst USB is scanned for a ﬁle with the signature created by scanning antivirus
device/box. If such ﬁle exists and it is placed in the database too, then USB device can be used for
whatever purpose it was inserted. Otherwise, end-device will not allow using that USB memory.

but as well physical card would be required to sign in. Next step would be to use these three factors to
sign any data exchanged with other machines in the network, so it would solve the problem with the
hacker who sends fake messages as whatever device he would like to impersonate.

After analysis 28 out of approximately 90 found systems and components installed on the vessel, it
is time to sum it up. For sure the list presented in Section 3.3 might not be completed, because access
to data was limited. Moreover, there is many various class of vessels which has diﬀerent needs and law
requirements for equipment. Below in the Table 5.1 are presented in ascending order results, based
on ”Asset Based Analysis” method. It is worth recalling that this method has been created by ISACA
[146]. Detailed description on this method is placed in Section 3.2.3. The applied method uses a bigger
number of factors for calculating the result, comparing to the classical approach where only two factors
are considered like probability factor and consequence factor.
Results are divided between three categories marked in a speciﬁc color:
• Color green: Acceptable risk for threshold: 0-540
• Color yellow: Warning, not acceptable risk for threshold: 541-1215
• Color red: Critical risk for threshold: 1216-3375
It is important to notice that yellow color does not mean that the risk is low enough to not take care
of the components. It is exactly the opposite. The yellow color can be understood that either the asset
does not have enough value or... there is the smallest glimmer of security applied but in most cases,
not enough. The red color means that it is critical and security has to be applied right now.
Another important case to notice are assumptions. For every single analyzed component, certain
assumptions are stated, not only for clarifying approach for grading but as well due to lack of data and
lack of time.
Grading is done based on information found in the sources, stated assumptions and personal knowl-
edge from university or work experience.
Other things to recall are values presented in the table with the results. Simple observation out of
these values is that closer both of values are together, less security implemented. For some components
both values are equal and still are green colored, simply the value of an asset is low.
• Mitigated Risk Value - is the ﬁnal result, namely Risk Impact Value decreased by implemented
security.
• Risk Impact Value - is an indicator of how big risk is associated with the usage of a speciﬁc asset.
General impression based on the results is very disturbing. Unfortunately, most systems and compo-
nents require immediate actions, which will patch present vulnerabilities. In the table below out of 28

Passenger entertainment network) implemented on the ship. As long as messages which ﬂow through

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
The main question to answer is, whether the thesis objectives are fulﬁlled. The answer is yes. Based
on found sources it turns out that the cybersecurity in maritime leaves a lot to be desired. Most of the
systems are more or less vulnerable.
The big part of the time during the period of this thesis took research on the ship’s model. This
thesis seems to be the ﬁrst document which gathers big part of components and systems of a vessel,
which potentially can be implemented. Due to lack of data access and limited time, probably not all
of possible systems and components are presented in Section 3.3. Nevertheless, research on this topic
suggests that there is no other similar work done yet, or simply such document was overlooked.
The strategy for risk management was taken from BIMCO [119]. They presented in a compact and
clear way what steps should be taken in order to proceed successful risk management strategy. This
thesis contains their ideas as a foundation to further analysis. Extended in some aspect description of
their work is placed in Section 3.1. According to Figure 3.1 main activity for this thesis was distributed
between identiﬁcation of threads, identiﬁcation of vulnerabilities and assessing risk exposure. Naturally,
the next steps still need to be done in order to fulﬁll the strategy.
Risk estimation methods are presented in Section 3.2 in this case four of methods are described:
Fault Tree Analysis, Attack Defence Tree, Asset Based Risk, and Propagation Analysis. The purpose
was to show various approach taken by these methods.
Finally when the ship model and risk assessment methods were ready. It was possible to perform
analysis of attack surface along with risk estimation. Unfortunately, a couple of limitations had to be
considered like time plan and poor data access. This required to deﬁne what can be done. As a result, it
turned out that only one risk assessment method can be taken. After considering all pluses and minuses
of each, it turned out that the Asset Based Risk method is the best. This method is straightforward
and compact. Outcomes of the method performed on various components and systems are in the form
of numbers, which easily can be compared between each other. This method contains 12 well-deﬁned
factors which are taken under consideration while analysis, which suggests that results are much more
precise comparing to other methods.
Finally, the analysis of the ship’s model with Asset Based Risk method has been performed and
placed in Chapter 4.
Each component independently was analyzed and discussed, based on found
sources and own knowledge from university or work experience. The outcome of this analysis is rather
alarming than soothing. The former director of Information Security Group Keith Martin [30], said
that in the sea at the moment is around 50,000 highly vulnerable for cyber attacks can be true. Results
of this thesis support her words. Maritime companies have to take ruthless actions in order to patch
security vulnerabilities as fast as possible. Otherwise attacks like the one performed on Maersk in 2017
when they lost approximately 2 billion of Danish Kroner will repeat. The whole Chapter 2 presents
how much destruction, counted in millions of US dollars, the viruses can cause. Additionally, that
chapter presents many cyber attacks in maritime. All of this cannot be unnoticed. Security on the
ships can be understood in a way how it is presented in Figure 6.1. The intention of this ﬁgure is to
show, that currently implemented security in maritime is close to the way how Swiss cheese looks like.
It consists of many layers of various software and hardware, which almost at every layer has multiple
security holes. These systems require fundamental changes, cosmetic addition of some security feature
or top layer isolation from the outer world will NOT help, the hacker will bypass such extra security
sooner than later. At the moment compromising any inner device (it can be computer, printer, server,
connected to NMEA anemometer, connected to NMEA compass or any other such device) makes that

3. Extend the list and knowledge about the systems and components placed in Section 3.3.
a) For more advanced security in this area it would be worth to analyze all communication
protocols, software and hardware used nowadays in maritime environments. There is a big
chance that software/ﬁrmware which is being used, is not supported anymore and therefore
might be really old and do not keep any acceptable security level. Tools like "Nmap" [275],

of how that software works and what/how data is sent over networks. Importance of such
analysis is big because the number of devices which are connected into the network on the
ship and networks ashore (the ports like cargo systems, ship owners systems) is growing.
b) Additionally to these network analyzers, more tools exist which might improve ongoing
upgrade in security in the maritime industry.
"OFMC" (Open-source Fixed-point Model
Checker for Symbolic Analysis of Security Protocols) [278] [279], ProVerif [279] [280]. These
programs are protocol veriﬁes. It means that they are able to tell whether the used protocol
is secure enough. Since in maritime industry, strict standards are not implied it means that
some software might be using communication protocols which are not secure. These tools
analyze all possible misuse scenario of a given protocol and based on the result, tell whether
it is possible to leak data sent over the network.

a) Development of protection and detection measures - Some of the basic actions which can be
taken:
• Go through BIMCO guide at ﬁrst [281].
• Make sure that satellite internet connection is not on the public internet.
• Make the networks are secure. All communication has to have encryption, authentication
and integrity mechanism present.
• Integration as much as possible Active Directory with components. Avoiding a situation
where there is to many logins and password for too many various services, otherwise
good password management.
• Update software and hardware, so it meets security requirements.
• Hardware and software backups.
• Check if networks on the ship are logically segregated.
• Secure USB ports on all ship systems.
• All communication has to have encryption.
• Do not blindly trust electronics.
• Train your crew.
• Make your tech suppliers prove that their products or services are secure.
• Regular security audits.
• Design systems in a way that anybody can come in 15 years and still update, improve
it like open-source at least in operating systems and infrastructure protocols.
b) Establish contingency plans.
c) Respond to and recovery from cybersecurity incidents.

6.2
Takeaways
• In the sea at the moment is around 50,000 ships which are highly vulnerable to cyber attacks and
this is not an exaggeration.
• Vulnerabilities are appearing due to: limited amount of money, limited about of time, unsecured
underlying infrastructure, rising complexity, changing paradigms of security, false positive and
false negative actions, indented errors, better hacking tools, possibilities vs demands in develop-
ment.
• Viruses all together causes billions of US dollars damages, it is a serious problem.
• Maritime industry has to wake up or otherwise more economic losses will appear in rather short
future. At the moment ships and maritime in general for hacker seems to be an easy target.
• Pirates are getting more sophisticated and start to use electronics to hack and make the ship
easier to take over.
• Maritime, in general, is no exception comparing to other industries. Many successful cyber attacks
have been performed, for example, Maersk in 2017 lost 300,000 of US dollars (approximately 2
billion of Danish Kroner).
• Seems IMO and SOLAS are trying to introduce new maritime cybersecurity law.
• The most of analyzed systems in this thesis are medium or highly vulnerable.
Most of the
information is proceed as plain text, no encryption, no authentication, no integrity.

anemometer, connected to NMEA compass or any other such device) makes that, the hacker can
